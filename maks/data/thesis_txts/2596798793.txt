Abstract
Traditionally, topology optimization has been performed using iterative optimization
methods in which the design is evaluated using a physical design criterion, such as the
compliance, and updated using gradient-based methods. As the resolution of the design
ﬁeld increases, this method incurs a high computational cost due to the combination of
an iterative optimization process, and the use of a ﬁnite element solver to resolve the
physics.
The aim of this thesis is to approximate the iterative optimization process, on a large
domain of problems within linear elasticity, through a deep neural network. Contrary to
previous approaches, training the neural network does not rely on target data. Instead,
the same physical design criterion as used within iterative topology optimization serves
as the loss function. Thus, the neural network can be trained in an unsupervised manner.
To the author’s knowledge, the proposed model is the ﬁrst within deep learning-based
topology optimization capable of being trained in an entirely unsupervised manner.
The proposed deep learning model is trained and tested on two datasets. One contain-
ing only a single boundary condition and the other containing a diverse set of boundary
conditions. In both cases, the load and volume fraction is varied for each problem in
the dataset. The network architecture is inspired by a U-net, and Adam with standard
parameters and gradient clipping is used for training. As inputs to the network physical
ﬁelds calculated on an initial homogeneous density ﬁeld are used. Speciﬁcally, the Von
Mises stresses, a streamline representation of the principal stresses and a ﬁeld represent-
ing of the allowed volume fraction are used. On the single boundary dataset, the deep
learning model has almost similar performance to the iterative topology optimization base-
line, while performance is signiﬁcantly worse on the multiple boundary conditions dataset.

Resumé
Traditionelt set er topologi optimering blevet udført ved hjælp af iterative optimer-
ingsmetoder hvor designet evalueres af et fysisk designkriterie, såsom strukturens ﬂeksi-
bilitet, og opdateres med en gradient-baseret metode. Grundet kombinationen af en itera-
tiv optimeringsproces, og brugen af en ﬁnite element solver til modellering af fysikken, øges
beregningsomkostningerne markant for denne type metoder, når den rummelige opløsning
af designet stiger.
Målet med dette speciale er at approksimere den iterative optimeringsproces, inden for
et stort domæne af lineære elasticitets problemer, ved hjælp af et dybt neuralt netværk.
I modsætning til tidligere fremgangsmåder beror træningen af det neurale netværk ikke
på empirisk data. I stedet bruges den samme tabsfunktion som ved iterativ topologi op-
timering, og dermed kan det neurale netværk trænes uden brug af empirisk data. Ifølge
forfatterens viden er den foreslåede model den første inden for dyb læringsbaseret topologi
optimering der kan trænes helt uden empirisk data.
Den foreslåede dybe læringsmetode er trænede og testede på to dataset. Et hvor kun
en enkelt randbetingelse fremstår, og et hvor mange forskellige randbetingelser betragtes.
I begge træningssæt varieres kraft-vektoren og den tilladte volumenfraktion for hvert
problem.
Netværksarkitekturen er inspireret af et U-net, og læringsalgoritmen Adam
med standard parametre og klipning af gradienterne bruges til træning. Som input til
netværket bruges fysiske felter beregnet på et homogent densitetsfelt. I dette tilfælde er
disse felter Von Mises spændingerne, en streamline repræsentation af hovedspændingerne
og et felt der repræsenterer den tilladte volumenfraktion. På datasættet med en enkelt
randbetingelse opnås næsten ens ydeevne mellem den dybe læringsmodel og den itera-
tive topologi optimeringsmetode der bruges som basismodel. På datasættet med ﬂere
randbetingelser har den dybe læringsmodel derimod signiﬁkant værre ydeevne end basis-
modellen.

Chapter 1
Introduction
For a given design space, subject to a set of boundary conditions, external forces, and
constraints, topology optimization seeks to determine an optimized material layout that
maximizes or minimizes a given quantity [11]. Traditionally, this has been achieved by
using the ﬁnite element method [97] to evaluate the current design, and then updating
the design through a gradient-based optimization method until a stopping criterion is
met. However, as the resolution of the design domain increases this method faces high
computational costs due to the combination of an iterative optimization process, and the
evaluation of a large system of equations in the ﬁnite element solver.
Another challenge within topology optimization is the need for discrete designs, as these
are often favored due to ease of manufacture. This is in stark contrast to the need for
continuous design variables within gradient-based optimization methods. The problem
becomes particularly nasty when considering structural optimization where discrete de-
signs give rise to checkerboard artifacts [80] if a simple physics model, such as linear
elasticity, is used. The most common way to counteract this problem is by applying a
smoothing operator to the density ﬁeld during optimization. Unfortunately, this approach
introduces grayscale material near the edges of the design and thus leads to a non-discrete
design. Various post-processing techniques, such as thresholding or image ﬁltering [78],
may be applied to the ﬁnal design in order to regain discreteness. However, this often
leads to sub-optimal designs or designs which violate the volume constraint.
Approximating the iterative optimization process through deep learning [48] is an inter-
esting potential solution to the problems encountered by iterative topology optimization,
i.e. high computational cost, and design discreteness. In particular, the non-linear ﬁl-
ters learned by Fully Convolutional Neural Networks (FCNNs) [74] present an interesting
prospect in terms of learning a mapping between the provided input and a discrete density
ﬁeld.
While neural networks have been a research topic since the early 1940s [55] the rapid
adoption of deep learning to a multitude of tasks was sparked by Krizhevsky et al. [45]
winning the ImageNet challenge [72] on image classiﬁcation in 2013 using a deep neural
network. Since then neural networks have shown state-of-the-art performance within a
variety of tasks outside computer vision, such as natural language processing [83], ma-
chine translation [7] and speech recognition ([57],[36]). In mathematics, neural networks
have been used to perform basic arithmetic operations [41], symbolic integration [47], and
Page 1 of 64
==PAGE:16==
even learn certain properties of diﬀerential equation systems [17]. Within physics, deep
learning has helped solve or accelerate complex problems, such as chaotic 3 body prob-
lems [14], turbulence modeling [53], and Eulerian ﬂuid simulation [86]. In a collaborative
study between eight universities Kasim et al. 2020 [42] showed that for scientiﬁc simula-
tions, within 10 diﬀerent domains, accelerations of up to 2 billion times may be achieved
using a custom network architecture called Deep Emulator Network Search (DENSE).
The success of these previous studies, especially those within complex physical modeling,
implies that deep learning may indeed be successfully applied to topology optimization.
The question then remains in what form, and using which type of inputs.
There is a multitude of ways in which deep learning may be applied to topology opti-
mization. Either to speed-up or replace sub-components of the traditional iterative opti-
mization framework, or perform end-to-end topology optimization. In general two overall
approaches may be considered; data-driven and reparameterization. In the data-driven
approach, a neural network is used to approximate the iterative optimization process.
Here a large computational cost is paid upfront to train the neural network, in order to
generate near-instant designs during test time. In the supervised case designs generated
by existing topology optimization approaches are used to guide the network during train-
ing. However, semi-supervised or unsupervised learning scenarios may also be considered,
in which the neural network is trained on a criterion which is not based on having access
to target data.
The reparameterization approach does not seek to approximate the optimization process
by learning through training examples. Instead, it seeks to leverage the strong representa-
tional capabilities of neural networks in conjunction with the ease of gradient computation
through back-propagation [71]. In terms of topology optimization, this can be done by
representing design variables as weights in a neural network instead of densities on a grid.
Ideally, this reparameterization leads to a stronger representational capacity, or fewer
design variables needed to generate an equivalent design. One indication that neural net-
works are a good representation of spatial features is the Deep Image Prior [87], which
shows that the structure alone of a convolutional neural network is enough to capture
many low-level image statistics.
1.1
Previous work
Deep learning was ﬁrst applied to structural optimization by Sosnovik et al. 2017 [81],
to accelerate the convergence of iterative topology optimization. The idea behind this
application was based on the observation that after a few iterations of the optimization
procedure the design often resembles the ﬁnal design, but with large areas of interme-
diate densities. By formulation the problem as a binary image segmentation task they
were able to signiﬁcantly accelerate the optimization process, while generating designs
closely resembling fully optimized designs. Here prematurely terminated designs from
the iterative optimization method were use as inputs to the CNN. Banga et al. 2018 [8]
generalized this approach to 3D and included additional inputs, such as density gradients,
and spatial representations of boundary and load conditions. Zhang et al. 2019 [95] got
entirely rid of the prematurely terminated designs as input and instead used an input
tensor consisting of displacement and strain data calculated on an initial density ﬁeld. To
Page 2 of 64
==PAGE:17==
train the network a U-net architecture [69] was used in conjunction with a loss function
based on the Kullback-Leibler divergence. This approach showed promising results, but
also highlighted the risks of using an objective function based on target data. Roughly
4% of the designs showed signs of structural disconnects, even though the mean pixel-wise
accuracy over the test-set was above 96%. This discrepancy was attributed to the fact
that each of the pixels is weighted equally in the loss function. Thus, a high accuracy may
be achieved even though critical pixels, e.g. those connecting structural components, are
erroneously predicted.
Li et al. 2019 [52] applied deep learning to a heat transfer based topology optimization
problem. Here a convolutional encoder-decoder was used to generate low-resolution opti-
mized designs. These designs were then passed through a second network based on the
SRGAN architecture [49], to upsample the low-resolution designs to high-resolution. As
inputs to the network spatial ﬁelds representing the heat source, heat sink, and volume
fraction were used. Yu et al. 2019 [93] used a similar setup, but applied it to structural
optimization instead, with spatial representations of boundary and load conditions along
with volume fraction as input.
Instead of using GANs to upsample a low-resolution design, they may also be used to
generate designs independently. Rawat et al. 2019 [67] used a Conditional Wasserstein
GAN ([59], [6]) to generate designs conditioned on a prescribed volume fraction, for a
ﬁxed load and boundary condition, while Nie et al. 2020 [61] took this approach one step
further and conditioned on both volume constraint, boundary and load condition. Oh et
al. 2019 [63] used a BEGAN [12] in a deep generative design process to help generate
new designs based on a reference design while maintaining structural integrity through
iterative topology optimization.
Up until now all of the mentioned approaches have relied on target data to evaluate the
loss function. However, a loss function based on a physical criterion, such as compliance,
is also possible.
This approach was pursued by Hoyer et al.
2019 [38] where a deep
neural network was used to reparameterize the design variables from densities on a grid
to weights in a neural network.
Both the network weights and the input were then
iteratively updated through backpropagation, and evaluated by a minimum compliance
loss function. This type of optimization only allows one to train a neural network for one
speciﬁc design example at a time and is thus in many ways similar to traditional topology
optimization. Chandrasekhar et al. 2020 [16] used a similar approach, but instead of
using a learnable input vector a ﬁxed input of (x, y) coordinates in a mesh were used.
Furthermore, sensitivities were analytically derived from the minimum compliance loss
function and explicitly provided to the back-propagation algorithm leading to a more
computationally eﬃcient gradient computation.
1.2
Motivation
So far all data-driven approaches have utilized a loss function based on target data, even
though this type of loss function in many cases does not ensure the structural integrity
of the generated designs. Furthermore, the neural network may never generate designs
better than those used as target data, although these are in many cases local minima.
Reparameterization approaches, on the other hand, have utilized a loss function based on
Page 3 of 64
==PAGE:18==
Figure 1.1: Conceptual schematic of the proposed framework
a physical criterion, but focused solely on a single design example at a time.
Motivated by the shortcomings of the current data-driven approaches, and the iterative
nature of the current reparameterization approaches, a data-driven model using a loss
function based on a physical design criterion is proposed. The basic idea of such a model
is shown in ﬁgure 1.1. First, an initial density ﬁeld and the boundary conditions, load,
and volume fraction of the desired problem are parsed to a ﬁnite element solver. Based on
the displacements, strains, and stresses obtained from the ﬁnite element analysis a set of
physical ﬁeld is generated. These ﬁelds are then used as input to a CNN which generates
a new design. Similar to the initial density ﬁeld a ﬁnite element analysis is performed on
the generated density ﬁeld, and the loss is calculated based on the solution of the ﬁnite
element analysis. In practice, the physical ﬁelds can be generated prior to the training
phase, such that only the ﬁnite element analysis related to the loss function evaluation
has to be run recurrently.
The choice of input ﬁelds is an important aspect of the proposed model. Input ﬁelds
have to provide meaningful information to the neural network about how an optimized
design should look. While load and boundary conditions do provide some information in
this regard spatial representations of these are often extremely sparse, and thus not well-
suited for a CNN. As an alternative to spatial representations of the load and boundary
conditions, physical ﬁelds, such as strains and stresses, seem an obvious candidate, as
these provide information about how the load is absorbed in the structure. Here two
types of input, which can be derived from the stress tensor, are proposed; the Von Mises
stresses and the principal stresses and directions. Von Mises stresses show the highest
stress concentrations in the domain and give a good indication of the amount of material
which should be distributed in each region of the domain. Principal stresses and directions
provide information on how stresses propagate through the domain and generally have a
high correlation with the orientation of structural members in optimized designs.

Chapter 2
Topology Optimization
Topology optimization is a mathematical method for optimizing material layout within a
given design space. For a set of boundary conditions, external forces, and constraints the
goal is to optimize the performance of the design. The physics of a given material conﬁg-
uration is resolved using e.g. the ﬁnite element method [97] and evaluated by an objective
function. To evolve the design non-linear programming methods, such as the Method of
Moving Asymptotes [84] or the optimality criteria [13, Chapter 5.5], are generally used.
Two main branches exist within topology optimization; level-set based and density-based.
In level-set based methods the design variables are nodes in a level-set ﬁeld from which the
material distribution can be controlled through a zero iso-contour. In density-based meth-
ods, the densities are used directly as the design variables in the optimization procedure.
Traditionally topology optimization was developed for generating optimal topologies in
structural design [9], but has since been applied to a wide variety of areas, such as ﬂuid
mechanics, thermodynamics, acoustics, electromagnetics and various couplings in-between
as presented in the monograph by Bendsøe and Sigmund [11].
This chapter starts by giving a brief introduction to density-based topology optimization.
It should be noted that traditional density-based topology optimization is not the main
focus of this project, and therefore only the most relevant concepts are introduced. For
a comparative review of the diﬀerent iterative topology optimization approaches see the
review article by Sigmund and Maute [79].
Next, the minimum compliance problem
and sparse linear solvers, hereunder the Multi-grid Preconditioned Conjugate Gradients
method, are presented. Finally, a summation of the current deep learning approaches
within topology optimization is given.
2.1
Structural optimization
Due to the vast amount of literature, and a well-deﬁned set of benchmark problems e.g.
[11, Figures 5.1 and 5.5], structural optimization is considered in this thesis. However, the
outlined approach should be easily extendable to other domains given that the underlying
physics can be resolved using the ﬁnite element method.

2.1.1
Density-based topology optimization
In density-based methods, each element in the ﬁnite element mesh is assigned a density,
which is used as the design variable for the optimization problem. By interpolating the
material properties of each element from its density the overall performance of a given
structure can be evaluated. One of the main challenges within structural optimization is
the need for discrete designs, which is counteractive to the need for continuous design vari-
ables within gradient-based optimization methods. Without regularization, the density
approach often leads to a large amount of intermediate densities. One way to punish inter-
mediate densities is to assign them a lower stiﬀness in the interpolation scheme. The most
popular interpolation scheme incorporating this kind of penalization is known as SIMP
(Solid Isotropic Material with Penalization) and was originally suggested by Bendsøe in
1989 [10] and further developed by Zhou and Rozvany in 1991 [96].
Ee = E(ρe) = Emin + ρp

Young modulus of solid material. Typical values for Emin, E0 and p are 1e-9, 1 and 3,
respectively.
Unless a very high penalization factor is used areas of intermediate densities may still
occur, which makes the design less feasible production-wise. Therefore, further methods
for driving the optimization towards solid-void designs, such as geometric constraints or
projection ﬁlters [78], are often used.

2.3
Deep learning approaches
Deep learning allows a computational model to learn a mapping between an input and a
target through a series of processing layers. In deep learning-based topology optimization,
the desired mapping can be expressed as
f(x0, x1, ..., xn) →ρ
(2.7)
Where f represents the deep learning model and {x0, x1, ..., xn} represents a set of input
ﬁelds, which could be related to physical properties, such as stresses, strains, and dis-
placements, or spatial representations of the load and boundary conditions.
The key thing to note about deep learning-based topology optimization is that it switches
the design variables from densities on a grid to weights in a neural network. These weights
allow the optimization procedure to learn a model of how to map a set of input ﬁelds to
a topology optimized design, rather than iteratively updating a single design ﬁeld.
The weights of the neural network can be learned in two manners; supervised and unsuper-
vised. In the supervised case designs generated by existing topology optimization codes
e.g. [77] and [39] are used to guide the learning of the neural network. This is typically
done through a loss function which rewards the neural network for generating designs

similar to the target design, such as the mean squared error or binary cross-entropy.
In the unsupervised case, a loss function that does not require target data is used. Here
a physical criterion, such as the compliance in eq. 2.2, may be used. The upside to this
approach is that the costly procedure of generating target data is not required. Further-
more, designs are directly evaluated on a physical design criterion, and thus fatal error
modes, such as disconnects, are greatly punished during the optimization. In this thesis,
an unsupervised learning approach with a compliance-based loss function is pursued.

Chapter 3
Deep Learning Framework
While section 2.3 introduced the general idea of how deep learning may be applied to
topology optimization no speciﬁc models were considered. This chapter takes a deeper
look at some of the applicable network architectures. Speciﬁcally, models used within im-
age segmentation and generative modeling are considered, as topology optimization bears
a close resemblance to both tasks. Next, commonly used building blocks of Convolutional
Neural Networks (CNNs), such as ResNets and dilated convolutions are introduced, and
lastly, the optimization procedure itself is outlined.
3.1
Network models
A wide array of neural network models exist, and choosing the right model greatly depends
on the task.
Below the overall structure of segmentation and generative models are
described, along with their pros and cons.
3.1.1
Segmentation model
Image segmentation is the task of processing and image into multiple diﬀerent segments,
to simplify or extract a more meaningful representation of the image. Applications could
be to identify anomalous regions in a brain MRI scan [22] or objects such as planes or
bicycles in an image [25]. Although deep learning-based topology optimization tries to
transform the input features into a binary segmentation, and not create a segmentation
mask on the input, the two tasks are closely related. Figure 3.1 shows the overall structure
of a convolutional segmentation network.
First, the input image is parsed through an encoder. The main job of the encoder is to
downsample the input while extracting the most important features of the input image.
The purpose of downsampling is twofold, it reduces the computational cost of the con-
volution operator while increasing the receptive ﬁeld of the network. Once the input has
been downsampled it is parsed through the bottleneck layer, which extracts deep image
features, typically through a large number of ﬁlters. The deep image features are then
upsampled through the decoding part of the network and used to create a segmentation
mask. In most cases, the model is trained using supervised learning with ground-truth
segmentations as the target.

==PAGE:30==
Figure 3.1: Graphical illustration an Encoder-Decoder segmentation network.
Segmentation-like models has been used by Sosnovik et al. 2017 [81] and Banga et al.
2018 [8] in order to generate topology optimized designs. Here designs obtained by itera-
tive topology optimization methods were used as ground truth data. In terms of topology
optimization, segmentation models have two downsides; 1) designs which are better than
the ground-truth cannot be obtained 2) the generated design is only evaluated with re-
spect to its resemblance to the ground truth design, and thus structural integrity is not
ensured.
3.1.2
Generative model
Another type of deep learning model closely related to the topology optimization task
is a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) [28]. Here two neural networks, a generator
G, and a discriminator D play a minimax game. The job of the generator is to generate
samples that are indistinguishable from the real data distribution, while the job of the
discriminator is to distinguish between real and fake samples. This type of learning is
known as semi-supervised learning, as the only requirement on training data is that it
should match the desired data distribution.
The loss function for this type of network can be expressed as
min
G max
D V (G, D) = Ex [log(D(x))] + Ez [log(1 −D(G(z)))]
(3.1)
Where z is a noise vector and x is a sample from the real data distribution. Eq. 3.1
can be interpreted as the generator trying to maximize the number of false positives of
the discriminator, while the discriminator tries to maximize the number of true positives
while minimizing the number of false positives. Once the network has been trained the
generator should be able to generate samples closely resembling the real data distribution.
Figure 3.2 shows a graphical illustration of the GAN network.
One problem with GANs is that it is hard to control the type of samples generated by the
generator. Both due to the randomness of the noise vector used to generate the samples,
and biases in the real data distribution. For example, if one class of the real data distribu-
tion is much easier to generate than the others, the generator will be biased towards this
class. In the context of topology optimization, this problem becomes even more severe,
as the generated samples have to adhere to the boundary and load conditions, along with
the volume constraint. To remedy this issue networks such as Conditional GAN (c-GAN)
Page 16 of 64
==PAGE:31==

[59] and InfoGAN [19] have been proposed. In the ﬁrst case, the generated samples are
conditioned on known properties of the real data distribution, such as class labels. In the
latter case, a prior on the latent noise vector z is utilized to control certain aspects of the
generated samples.
GANs, in particular c-GANs, have been used for two purposes within topology opti-
mization; 1) generating designs conditioned on properties such as volume fraction [67]
or boundary and load conditions [61] 2) upsampling a low-resolution design to a high-
resolution design ([52], [93]). Here the latter case seems the most appropriate utilization
of a generative model, and represents a true semi-supervised learning scenario, i.e. the
only information needed about each sample is whether it is a low- or high-resolution de-
sign. The ﬁrst case on the other hand does not seem well-suited for a generative model.
Very special conditions, i.e. load and boundary condition, along with volume constraint,
are needed in order to generate useful designs. However, conditioning the network on
such speciﬁc properties essentially turns the task into a supervised learning task, as only
one optimal/optimized design exists for each of these conditions.
3.2
Convolutional neural networks
So far an overall explanation of the presented models has been given, without going into
detail with how diﬀerent sub-components, such as the generator in the generative model
or the decoder in the segmentation model, could be implemented. In this section, some
of the most popular CNN architectures are introduced. The basic idea of a CNN is to
stack multiple convolutional layers in order to hierarchically build more and more complex
feature maps. The convolutional layers may consists of multiple diﬀerent operations used
to up- or downsample the input, or make the network more stable. Appendix A provides
a brief look-up section for those not familiar with CNNs already.
3.2.1
ResNets
Residual networks (ResNets) were introduced in 2015 by He et al. [33] to enable the
training of even deeper neural networks. When training neural networks with a large
number of layers, typically around 30-50 layers, it was observed that the results became
Page 17 of 64
==PAGE:32==

worse than for shallower networks. This counter-intuitive ﬁnding was attributed to the
vanishing gradient problem [37], e.g.
as partial derivatives are recursively multiplied
during the back-propagation step the gradients become near zero, and the network stops
learning. ResNets remedy this problem by introducing a residual block with two paths;
one is a weight layer, while the other is an identity mapping. The identity layer helps
the network learn in two ways; 1) if deeper features do not add more meaningful data
representations they can simply be skipped, and 2) gradient magnitude is preserved during
back-propagation. Figure 3.3 shows a graphical illustration of a ResNet block.
The name residual networks originate from the fact that the weight layer part of the
residual network tries to predict the residual between the input x and the output H(x)
H(x) = F(x) + x
(3.2)
The idea is based on the assumption that learning the residual is often easier than learning
the true mapping.
By stacking multiple ResNet blocks deep network structures can be built without facing
the vanishing gradient problem. ResNets became increasingly popular when He et al. [33]
won the ImageNet challenge [72] in 2015 using a ResNet with 152 layers.

factor is learned for each of the channels in the input feature map. This is done through a
global average-pooling layer and two fully connected layers and applied using a Hadamard

U-net
The U-net network architecture was ﬁrst applied to biomedical image segmentation [69]

3.2.3
Dilated convolutions
As mentioned earlier one use of downsampling is to increase the receptive ﬁeld of the
network. The downside to this approach is that the spatial size of the generated features
becomes smaller. An alternative way to increase the receptive ﬁeld, without decreasing
the spatial size of the features, is through dilated convolutions, also known as atrous con-
volutions. In dilated convolutions the spacing between kernel weights is increased, leading
to a larger receptive ﬁeld. See section A.2.2 in appendix A.2.2 for more information on
dilated convolutions.
A common architecture consisting of multiple dilated convolutions, and often used within
image segmentation, is Atrous Spatial Pyramid Pooling ([32], [18]). Here dilated convo-
lutions with diﬀerent dilation rates are pooled together, in order to represent the input
features at diﬀerent scales. Figure 3.6 shows a graphical illustration of this process.
One downside to dilated convolutions is that they, similar to transposed convolutions,
create an uneven overlap. Thus, checkerboard artifacts are a concern. To remedy this
issue a series of non-dilated convolutions may be applied after the dilated convolutions to
act as anti-gridding ﬁlters. This is the approach taken by Yu et al. 2017 [92], which uses a
combination of ResNet blocks and dilated convolutions to achieve state-of-the-art results
on a set of image classiﬁcation, object localization, and semantic segmentation tasks.

4.3
Post-processing scheme
There are various diﬀerences between the iterative topology optimization process and the
deep learning-based one that makes a direct comparison between the generated designs
unfair. First of all, the iterative method enforces the volume constraint down to a very
low tolerance through a bi-sectioning scheme, while a penalty term is used to implicitly
enforce it in the deep learning approach. Thus, one cannot be sure that the volume con-
straint is strictly enforced in the deep learning case. This leads to an unfair evaluation
if the generated design uses more material than prescribed. Furthermore, in many cases,
a lower penalization power of around p = 2 generally leads to better results for the deep
learning approach, whereas p = 3 is usually used for the iterative method. This again
makes a fair evaluation hard, as the choice of penalization power at evaluation time will
favor one or the other approach.
To create a fair evaluation the designs should ideally be entirely discrete, and adhere
strictly to the volume constraint. A post-processing scheme, consisting of two steps, is
deployed in order to obtain these type of designs:
1. Thresholding based on density sorting
2. Connected component analysis to remove any disconnected regions
The thresholding is performed by ﬁrst sorting all densities. The n largest densities are
then set equal to 1, while the rest are set equal to 0. Here n is chosen based on the desired
volume fraction. For this type of thresholding to work properly the designs must already
be close to binary, as a large number of small disconnects may otherwise appear in the
structure.
As already mentioned, after having applied the thresholding small disconnected regions
may appear.
The connected component analysis is used to clean-up these small dis-
connected regions, and remove them from the design. After the post-processing scheme

5.2.4
Conclusions
The U-SE-ResNet model trained with a relaxed continuation of the volume constraint
yielded almost similar performance to the baseline when evaluated on the test-set, with
designs on average only being 1.1% worse. On the other hand, the model did not generalize
well to problems outside the training domain, such as high volume fractions, unseen load,
and boundary conditions, or higher-resolution inputs. The deep learning model’s bad
performance in these cases indicates that the model is most likely over-ﬁtting to the
training domain. If the network had a good understanding of the physical interpretation
of the input ﬁelds it would not generate nonsense designs in the mirrored training domain
case. The most eﬃcient way of preventing this type of over-ﬁtting is to present the model
with a larger training dataset incorporating multiple boundary conditions, and with loads
distributed across the entire domain.

Training the model on a larger dataset makes the model capable of generalizing to bound-

a cost in terms of performance. When applying the network trained on the MBC dataset

which motivates further research.
5.4
Computational eﬃciency
As a ﬁnal note on the results, the matter of computational eﬃciency is discussed. One of
the most common applications of deep learning, within scientiﬁc computing, is to speed-
up existing implementations, by approximating computationally heavy tasks. When the
network is trained inference can be performed very quickly, usually in a matter of mil-
liseconds, and these lightning-fast values are often what is reported when comparing to
alternative approaches. Hence there is a vast amount of articles claiming a million times
speed-up or greater when using a deep learning model. However, the computational cost
of training the network is often also worth reporting. Especially, if the application requires
that many diﬀerent models are trained, or the training time signiﬁes a major computa-
tional expense, such as multiple days on a cluster of GPUs.
As touched upon in section 2.2 evaluating the minimum compliance loss function is ex-
pensive as a large system of linear equations has to be solved. This process dominates the
training cost of the network entirely with the ﬁnite element solver taking up 98% of the
total training time, while the forward pass, gradient computation, and back-propagation
account for less than 2%. Having a loss function which is this expensive to evaluate is
very uncommon within classical deep learning tasks, e.g. computer vision and natural
language processing, and makes the network much more expensive to train.
As solving the ﬁnite element problem dominates both the iterative and deep learning
approaches the number of FE evaluations is a good measure for comparing the compu-
tational expense of the methods. In the iterative approach, a ﬁnite element analysis is
performed in each iteration of the optimization loop, and it normally takes around 200-
300 iterations to converge if the Heaviside projection ﬁlter is used. In the deep learning
approach, a ﬁnite element analysis is performed for each entry in the training dataset
every epoch. Table 5.9 reports the number of FE evaluations needed to train a model
on the SBC and MBC datasets for 100 epochs. Furthermore, the number of designs the

clearly puts into perspective the huge computational cost associated with training this
type of model. Even though the end-user ends up being able to generate new designs in a
matter of milliseconds, the same computational resources could have been used to generate
6667 designs with the baseline model, while waiting for the deep learning model to train.
Thus, for the deep learning approach to make sense the network must either create state-
of-the-art results or be distributed to a large number of users. One such use case could
be a commercial CAD or 3D printing software, where the company uses a large amount
of computational resources to train a large neural network. Once the network is trained
the end-users are relieved of having to wait for the iterative optimization procedure each
time they want to generate a new design. This near-instant feedback could signiﬁcantly
increase the incorporation of topology optimization in the design process.

Chapter 6
Outlook and Discussion
While results look promising for smaller problem domains, such as the single boundary
case, the neural network performance is not satisfactory for larger problem domains where
more boundary conditions are considered. Especially, the large number of disconnected
designs, up to 8%, is troublesome, as avoiding these were one of the main selling points
of using a physical design criterion as the loss function. Below ideas for how the cur-
rent deep learning framework could be improved are suggested. First, some general, and
relatively easy to implement, improvements are suggested. Next, the essential topic of
network inputs is discussed and interesting alternatives are discussed. The topic of model
interpretation is then considered. Here more advanced tools for understanding the net-
work behavior are presented which may help improve future iterations of the network
architecture. Finally, the, arguably, utopian idea that a neural network should perform
end-to-end topology optimization is reconsidered, and ways in which deep learning can
be used to accelerate existing iterative methods are discussed. To ﬁnalize the discussion
a list of future work from which researches within the ﬁeld can get inspired is compiled.
6.1
General improvements
In this section, a few general improvements of the overall framework, from dataset gener-
ation to design evaluation, are discussed.
6.1.1
Distributed loads and boundaries
One improvement could be to get rid of point loads and boundaries as these create sin-
gularities in the stress ﬁeld, i.e. if a force is applied to a single point with no area the
stress becomes inﬁnite. These singularities dominate the stress ﬁeld if no precautions are
taken. In this project, clipping was used to make the stress ﬁelds smoother, but a more
elegant way of solving the problem would be to slightly distribute the load or boundary on
neighboring nodes to avoid the singularity entirely. One argument against this approach
is that end-users often use point loads and boundaries, and thus the network needs to be
able to handle these types of inputs.
Page 51 of 64
==PAGE:66==
6.1.2
Supervised learning
Another idea is to combine the supervised and unsupervised learning methods.
Here
supervised learning, as described in section 3.1.1, could be used to perform the initial
training of the model and quickly guide the network towards a good solution.
Once
this has been achieved the minimum compliance objective could be used to ﬁne-tune the
model and identify small pixel inaccuracies that signiﬁcantly inﬂuence the compliance.
This approach combines the best of both worlds but requires the generation of a large
number of target designs, which are computationally expensive to generate. Especially, if
high-resolution designs are desired.
6.1.3
Post-processing
As observed in ﬁgure 5.3a the simple post-processing used to acquire entirely discrete
designs in some cases leads to structural disconnects. Especially, if the design does not
already have a low non-discreteness value, or slightly violates the volume constraint. One
possible solution to this problem is to use the robust formulation proposed by Wang et al.
2011 [90]. Here an eroded, intermediate, and dilated version of the design is considered,
and the objective function is a composite of the performance of all three designs. While
initially designed to ensure better manufacturability, by being able to take into account
machining constraints, the robust formulation inherently makes the design more robust to
erosion and dilation processes, such as the applied post-processing scheme. One downside
to this approach is that it requires solving three ﬁnite element problems instead of one,
which would make the training much more time-consuming. Alternatively, a bi-sectioning
scheme could be used to determine the best thresholding value which satisﬁes the volume
constraint. However, since this would rely on a ﬁnite element analysis for each iteration in
the bi-sectioning loop this would also be a costly procedure. One last idea could be to use
a low thresholding value and then run a few iterations of shape optimization to satisfy the
volume constraint. In this way the volume constraint is enforced, while small deviations
in the boundary, caused by the thresholding, are remedied by the shape optimization.
6.1.4
Network architecture
While the input ﬁelds, which will be the topic of the next section, are most likely the
main reason for the unsatisfactory results on the MBC dataset there are several interest-
ing modiﬁcations that could be made to the network architecture. These more advanced
network architectures would most likely not make a diﬀerence with the current input
ﬁelds.
However, if input ﬁelds with a higher information density regarding the ﬁne-
scale structure of the ﬁnal design could be generated, these alternative network structures
might help the network learn even stronger representations of topology optimized designs.
The most simple extension to the current model architecture would be to consider a multi-
task learning scenario [70], in which the model has two heads. One head of the model
predicts the density ﬁeld, while the other head could produce a physically meaningful ﬁeld
such as the Von Mises stress or the strain energy density. The idea behind this approach
is that the two tasks positively supplement each other, i.e. by understanding how to
generate a strain energy density ﬁeld the model would also understand the importance of
Page 52 of 64
==PAGE:67==
these strain energies to the compliance of the ﬁnal design.
A more sophisticated network architecture is Deep Emulator Network Search (DENSE)
presented by Kasim et al. 2020 [42]. In this type of network, a so-called super-architecture
is ﬁrst built, in which all layers of the network have a set of operations to choose from.
These operations could be convolutions with diﬀerent ﬁlter sizes, or zero and identity
layers. In each layer, only one of these operations is chosen. During training the network
then has to learn both the network weights and the best path through the network.
This helps alleviate the time-consuming process of model selection along with making
the learning algorithm able to tailor the network architecture speciﬁcally to the given
problem. DENSE was applied to 10 diﬀerent scientiﬁc simulation cases ranging from X-
ray spectroscopy to ocean modeling. Once the network was trained accelerations of up
to 2 billion times were observed compared to normal simulation time, while maintaining
acceptable accuracy.
6.2
Improving neural network inputs
For an end-to-end topology optimization neural network to work the input features must
be of high quality and contain dense information on how to construct the ﬁnal design.
Two ideas for such inputs are principal stresses and directions (which have already been
partly utilized), and optimal micro-structures.
6.2.1
Encoding of principal stresses and directions
As mentioned in section 2.3.3 principal stresses, and especially principal stress directions,
play an important role in well-performing topology optimized designs. However, experi-
ments using the principal stress magnitudes and angles directly as inputs to the neural
network showed poor results. Most likely, due to the notion of angles not being as intu-
itive to a neural network as to a human, and the inherent singularity of angles due to the
2π-periodicity. Encoding the angles into x and y components of a vector was also tested,
but using these vector-ﬁelds as inputs again showed little to no performance increase.
Finally, an encoding where the ﬁrst and second principal stresses were encoded into a
streamline representation, with the thickness of the streamline equivalent to the principal
stress magnitude in that part of the domain, was settled on. The streamlines were then
rasterized into a grayscale image and used as input to the neural network. Although,
these images, when evaluated by a human, seemed to provide an intuitive understanding
of how a well-optimized design should look, little to no performance gain was observed
when using this additional input.
In hindsight, there are several issues with the streamline representation.
First of all,
the Euler integration scheme used to generate the streamlines is not very robust, and
required ﬁne-tuning of hyper-parameters, such as step-size, number of streamlines, min-
imum streamline length and seed points, to provide nice results. This made the scheme
ill-suited for the MBC dataset, where seed points were not always intuitive and diﬀer-
ent hyper-parameters were needed for each type of boundary condition to provide nice
results. Furthermore, the end-user of the trained network would have to go through the
Page 53 of 64
==PAGE:68==
same procedure of ﬁne-tuning the hyper-parameters in order to generate a meaningful
streamline input to the network. Another problem with the streamline encoding is that
to represent segment thicknesses of the streamlines faithfully a relatively high-resolution
grayscale image is needed. In this case, the needed resolution was 4 times higher than the
corresponding Von Mises stress ﬁeld input. This meant that in order to concatenate the
streamline features with the stress features a heavy downsampling scheme was needed.
Although learned ﬁlters were used for the downsampling some ﬁne-scale features were lost
in this process, as can be observed from the activation maps in ﬁgure D.2c in Appendix D.
Based on the discussion above the most suitable principal stress encoding seems to be a
vector ﬁeld encoding, with an associated neural network architecture specialized in ana-
lyzing vector ﬁelds. However, to the author’s knowledge only very few such architectures
exist, with the Vector Field Based Neural Networks approach by Vieira et al. 2019 the
most obvious candidate [89]. Unfortunately, the literature and research on these types of
specialized networks is still far from being as developed as the research community around
CNNs.
6.2.2
Optimal microstructure
As mentioned above there are several complications with using a streamline represen-
tation of the principal stresses and directions. One possible solution is to leverage the
knowledge about optimal/optimized micro-structures gained through the homogenization
method [4]. Here a microstructure is deﬁned as a composite material that is assumed
to be periodic on the microscopic length-scale. To create a manufacturable design these
micro-structures have to be evaluated on a global scale in which the periodicity assump-
tion does not hold. Here the homogenization method provides a way of mapping the
microstructure into a set of eﬀective material properties that can be interpreted on a
global scale. The homogenization approach may also be turned around to determine an
optimized microstructure from a set of eﬀective material properties. This process is known
as inverse homogenization and has been used to create materials with negative Poisson’s
ratio [75] or maximum shear and bulk modulus [76].
A composite material may be described as a series of laminations with diﬀerent orien-
tations θn and layer widths µn.
For two-phase composites, e.g.
solid-void, a class of
sequential laminates with a ﬁnite number of layers N can be shown to achieve the the-
oretical upper bounds for maximum strain energy ([58] [26]). Besides being optimal the
eﬀective material properties of these so-called rank-N laminates can also be derived ana-
lytically. For a single-load case, rank-2 laminates with orthogonal layers can be shown to
reach the optimal energy bound. Given a ﬁxed volume fraction ρ the optimal rank-2 lam-
inate may be constructed by aligning the laminate orientations with the principal stress
directions and the relative layer widths µ1 and µ2 can be determined from the principal
stresses [3]. Figure 6.1 shows how a rank-2 laminate may be constructed from a set of
layer widths and orientations.
Having given a very brief introduction to homogenization and optimal micro-structures
their application as inputs to a neural network is now discussed. The main idea behind the

generating input ﬁelds. First, a homogeneous density ﬁeld is considered meaning that
the density within each element can be considered ﬁxed. Secondly, only single load prob-

The rank-2 laminates can be determined analytically by considering the principal stresses
calculated on the homogeneous density ﬁeld. While the simpliﬁcation of a homogeneous
density ﬁeld of course renders the generated micro-structures sub-optimal, from the per-
spective of the ﬁnal design, it drastically reduces the computational eﬀort as topology
optimization is avoided altogether. As an alternative approach, a crude approximation of
the optimized density ﬁeld could be made based on the Von Mises stress ﬁeld.
One problem with using micro-structures as input ﬁelds is how to restrict the size of
the input ﬁeld. If each element in the global mesh is assigned a corresponding micro-
structure the input ﬁeld quickly becomes very large, as each microstructure needs a mesh
that is suﬃciently large to represent the corresponding topology. As an example, the
mesh currently used for both the SBC and MBC dataset of 120x60 elements would result
in a 12000x60000 input ﬁeld if each micro-structure was discretized on a 100x100 unit
cell. One way to get around this issue is to calculate the principal stress ﬁeld on a low-
resolution mesh, say 30x15. This would results in a 3000x1500 input ﬁeld, in which case
multiple samples could ﬁt on a modern-day GPU. The downside to this approach is, of
course, a loss in accuracy of the principal stress ﬁeld.

6.3
Feature visualization
As brieﬂy mentioned in the previous section feature visualization [64] is a powerful tool
for gaining insights as to what type of inputs the network is highly stimulated by. Sev-
eral diﬀerent tools exist for examining the behavior of a neural network. The simplest
approach is looking at the activation maps when parsing an input through the network as
shown for the SBC dataset in Appendix D. If this is done for diﬀerent inputs one might
begin to reason about what type of inputs stimulate certain neurons or channels in the
network. However, this will always be on a sample by sample basis and to make general
assumptions about the network behavior one would have to evaluate the feature maps
of hundreds of samples. A slightly more sophisticated approach is to iterate through the
dataset and determine which samples stimulate a certain neuron or channel the most.
Based on these maximum activation examples one might recognize certain patterns, such
as a given neuron only reacting strongly to examples with certain types of edges or pat-
terns. However, this only explains the model behavior in terms of dataset examples, i.e.
a neuron might react strongly to images of cats, but which explicit features in the images
is it reacting strongly to? Furthermore, the procedure is computationally expensive if a
dataset with hundreds of thousands of samples is considered.
An alternative approach is to ask the question; if the input ﬁeld could be chosen freely,
what type of input would cause the maximum amount of stimulation. This task can be
phrased as an optimization task, where the input ﬁeld acts as the design variables

While feature visualization provides interesting prospects in terms of model interpretation
the main research focus has generally been within neural networks trained on images.
The application becomes a bit more tricky when considering neural networks trained on
multiple inputs as is the case in this project. Here two options exist; either all other
inputs are kept constant while optimizing a single input ﬁeld, or all inputs ﬁelds are
optimized simultaneously. In the ﬁrst case, the input ﬁelds which are kept constant may
severely bias the optimization of the input ﬁeld which is being optimized. In the second
case one input ﬁeld may end up dominating the optimization and the other input ﬁelds
will end up being noisy. Thus, extensive testing would be needed to determine the best
optimization procedure and regularization when considering a multi-input case. As with
many other things, the best way to understand model behavior is probably to look at
the model through diﬀerent lenses.
Here activation maps help understand the model
through the lens of a single sample. Maximum activation examples help understand the
model through the eyes of the dataset, while feature visualization by optimization helps
understand the general type of inputs that stimulate a given neuron, channel, or layer in
the model. By combining the knowledge of these three methods a strong intuition about
the model behavior can be obtained. The DeepViz framework presented by Yosinki et al.
2015 [91] is an excellent tool for this job, as it combines both feature activations, maximum
activation examples, and deconvolution [94] into a single interactive tool. Unfortunately,
this tool currently only has direct compatibility with the Caﬀe1 framework.
6.4
Deep learning accelerated topology optimization
Perhaps the idea that deep learning should replace iterative topology optimization in an
end-to-end fashion is a bit too optimistic. Maybe the best results could be obtained by
combining the strengths of the deep learning and iterative optimization approaches. In
this section ideas for how neural networks could be used to help accelerate or improve
certain aspects of the iterative optimization procedure are suggested.
6.4.1
Heaviside projection ﬁlter
Topology optimization utilizing a Heaviside projection ﬁlter is capable of generating al-
most entirely discrete designs. However, the continuation scheme used to gradually in-
crease the β factor adds a signiﬁcant amount of additional computational expense. The
idea of learning a neural network to directly apply a Heaviside projection ﬁlter, without

==PAGE:74==
any iteration, seems both interesting and feasible. Here a supervised learning scenario,
in which designs optimized using a density ﬁlter could be used as inputs to the network,
while designs optimized with the Heaviside projection ﬁlter could be used as target data,
seems suitable. As a ﬁnal step one could consider ﬁne-tuning the network with a mini-
mum compliance objective, similar to the one used in this project, to reduce the risk of
disconnected designs. It seems feasible that even when trained on a pretty small subset of
boundary and load conditions the network should be able to generalize to problems out-
side the training domain. The reasoning behind this hypothesis is that the network has to
learn a local mapping. This local mapping should be dependent on the local stress condi-
tions, and while these are of course a product of the global load and boundary conditions,
the same local conditions may occur for many diﬀerent load and boundary conditions.
6.4.2
Matrix preconditioning
In terms of computational cost, the ﬁnite element analysis is by far the most expensive
procedure for both the deep learning and iterative topology optimization approaches.
Thus, speeding up the ﬁnite element analysis would be beneﬁcial for both approaches.
The most naive way of having a neural network assist this process would be to feed it the
system matrix and right-hand side of the linear system and have it predict the solution.
However, this is not an easy task for a neural network as it requires global information,
i.e. the solution to each equation in the system depends on other equations in the sys-
tem, and permutation invariance, i.e. the ordering of the equations in the system matrix
should not inﬂuence the solution. A neural network utilizing fully connected layers and
permutation invariant operations, such as max-pooling, might be able to solve these types
of problems. The downside to such an approach is that large system matrices and the use
of fully connected layers would lead to a very high training cost. Furthermore, while the
neural network might perform well on the training-set it is hard to tell what the accuracy
on unseen problems would be. A low accuracy solution vector would in turn lead to bad
sensitives and, in the end, might result in a misguided optimization procedure. One way
to have neural networks assist the linear solver while maintaining an error estimate on the
solution vector is through an iterative solver, such as the Jacobi or Conjugate Gradient
(CG) method. Both of these methods have much better convergence rates if the sys-
tem matrix is well-conditioned. In section 2.2.2 the Multi-grid Preconditioned Conjugate
Gradient (MGCG) method was described, explaining how the multi-grid method can be
used to perform preconditioning when using the Conjugate Gradient method. However,
using the multi-grid method for preconditioning adds signiﬁcant computational overhead,
and thus having a neural network eﬃciently perform preconditioning would signiﬁcantly
reduce the computational cost of the CG method.
Several attempts at using neural networks to speed-up CG and MGCG have already been
made. Katrutsa et al. 2017 [43] used a deep learning model to generate the restriction
and prolongation matrices used for the multi-grid method, while Sappl et al. 2019 [73]
attempt to create the preconditioning matrix directly by utilizing matrix properties such
as sparsity patterns, symmetry, and positive deﬁniteness. In the latter case, a factor 2-3
speed-up was achieved on urban water CFD problems. However, the approach has yet to
be validated on truly large-scale problems with millions of degrees-of-freedom.

6.4.3
High-resolution designs
Another interesting prospect for using deep learning to accelerate topology optimization
is to upsample low-resolution designs to high resolution through a neural network. The
most commonly deployed architecture for this type of task is the Generative Adversarial
Network described in section 3.1.2. Li et al. 2019 [52] obtained reasonable results by using
the SRGAN [49] architecture to upsample 40x40 designs to 160x160 resolution, while Yu
et al. 2019 [93] used a conditional GAN to upsample 32x32 designs to 128x128 resolution.
However, none of these applications can truly be considered high-resolution, as the ﬁnal
design resolutions are nowhere near the resolutions normally used for large-scale topology
optimization [1]. Furthermore, both approaches rely on a GAN loss in a combination with
a MSE-loss which is based on target data. Thus, for these approaches to scale to higher
resolutions, target data, at the desired resolution, is required.
One way to get around the need for high-resolution target data is to use a patch-based
approach. Here patches of the low-resolution design are upsampled separately and patched
together into a full design. Since a large number of samples can be drawn from just a
single high-resolution design this approach is much more data-eﬃcient. High-resolution
designs could be generated by using the approach outlined by Groen et al. [30], which
is able to generate a well-optimized 1600x800 design in a matter of minutes. The key
thing needed to make this approach work is to make sure that neighboring patches are
connected. No obvious solution to this problem currently exists, but augmenting the GAN
loss function with a heuristic loss function penalizing non-identical edges on neighboring
pixels could be one possible approach.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
A deep learning model capable of performing topology optimization for single load case
linear elasticity problems, subject to a volume constraint, has been developed. To train
the model a loss function based on a physical design criterion, the compliance, has been
used. The compliance is evaluated by performing a ﬁnite element analysis on the designs
generated by the neural network, while the volume constraint is enforced by augmenting
the loss function with a penalty term. Since the loss function does not rely on target
data the network can be trained in an entirely unsupervised manner. As inputs to the
neural network physical ﬁelds calculated on an initial homogeneous density ﬁeld are used.
Speciﬁcally, the Von Mises stresses, a streamline representation of the principal stresses
and a uniform ﬁeld representing the volume constraint is used. The deep learning model
is tested on two datasets; one containing only a single boundary condition, while the other
contains 47 diﬀerent boundary conditions. To evaluate the performance of the deep learn-
ing model a density-based iterative topology optimization method is used as a baseline.
The neural network is based on a U-net architecture with SE-ResNet blocks in the bot-
tleneck layer and was trained using Adam and gradient clipping. On the single boundary
dataset, the U-SE-ResNet model only performed 1.1% worse on the test-set compared to
the iterative baseline. Furthermore, no fatal structural disconnects were observed on the
400 problems in the test-set. When evaluated on problems outside the training domain,
such as diﬀerent volume fractions or a mirrored version of the training domain, the model
showed signs of overﬁtting and a signiﬁcant decrease in performance. On the multiple
boundary conditions dataset, the U-SE-ResNet model performed 36.7% worse on the test-
set than the baseline model, and almost 8% of the designs showed signs of fatal structural
disconnects. Especially, problems containing a point boundary and a uni-directional wall
constraint concurrently were identiﬁed as troublesome, with structural disconnects in up
to 25% of the designs for these types of boundary conditions. The model trained on the
multiple boundary conditions dataset did, however, show better generalization abilities
as none of the boundary problems in the test-set were included in the training-set.
For the multiple boundary conditions dataset, the compliance loss function does not seem
to ensure structural integrity. Most likely due to the large problem domain the neural
network has to perform inference over. Furthermore, the compliance loss function is ex-
pensive to evaluate compared to a target-based loss function, as a ﬁnite element analysis

==PAGE:78==
has to be performed for each of the generated designs. One possible solution could be to
guide the network towards a good local minimum using supervised learning, and then use
the compliance based loss function to ﬁne-tune the network weights.
Another important task is to identify the most eﬃcient encoding of the principal stresses
and directions for the multiple boundary conditions dataset. There is no doubt the prin-

representation outlined in this project is not a suﬃciently accurate way of represent-

Appendix A
Convolutional Neural Networks
Lots of information, with high-quality illustrations and animations, on the basics of con-
volutional neural networks (CNNs) already exists. Thus, this appendix mainly serves as
a quick look-up section. For a more thorough introduction to CNNs chapter 9 of the
book by Goodfellow, Bengio, and Courville [27] or one of the online articles by Medium1
or TowardsDataScience2 serves as a good starting point.
A.1
Encoder-Decoder
Many diﬀerent CNN architectures exist, and, in most cases, architectures are slightly
tuned towards the task they are used to solve. One of the most universal architectures is
the encoder-decoder architecture. Here the input data is ﬁrst parsed through an encoder
that downsamples the input, typically through a series of convolutional and pooling layers.
The downsampled input is then parsed through a bottleneck consisting of convolutional
layers with a large number of ﬁlters to create a latent space representation. Depending on
the task the decoder may have diﬀerent structures. For a classiﬁcation task, the decoder is
typically one or multiple fully connected layers which maps the latent space representation
to a set of class labels. For other tasks, such as object detection or image segmentation,
the decoder upsamples the latent space representation to the desired spatial resolution
through a set of upsampling layers.
Examples of upsampling layers could be bilinear
interpolation or learned mappings such as transposed convolutions. Figure A.1 shows a
graphical illustration of the encoder-decoder architecture.
A.2
Convolution
The convolution operator consists of a kernel of weights, often referred to as a ﬁlter, which
is applied to an input in a sliding window manner. This operation creates a new output
which in deep learning terminology is often referred to as a feature. Sequentially applying
convolutional ﬁlters is the basic idea behind a CNN.

A.2.1
Kernel size, padding and stride
Three of the most important parameters to consider when performing convolution are
the kernel size, padding, and stride. A larger kernel size means more weights, and thus
a higher computational load, but a bigger receptive ﬁeld. Larger ﬁlter sizes are typically
used at the start of a network, while smaller ﬁlter sizes are favored deeper in the network.
When performing convolution the input size is reduced, as each window the ﬁlter is
Page A2
==PAGE:81==
applied to only produces one value (see ﬁgure A.2.). Within many applications, it is often
desirable to keep the input and output sizes identical. In order to do so, padding may be
applied. Many diﬀerent padding strategies exist, and which one to choose highly depends
on the type of input data. The two most common strategies are to pad with zeros, or
mirror the edge values. To keep input and output size equal the padding width should be
chosen according to the kernel size
p = w −1
2
(A.2)
Where p denotes the padding width, and w the kernel width.
Contrary to padding, the stride is often used to reduce the output size. By default a
stride of 1 is often assumed for the convolution operator, i.e. during the sliding window
operation, the ﬁlter window is translated one pixel. This creates a high degree of overlap
between neighboring output features.
By increasing the to stride to 2, and choosing
the appropriate padding, a downsampling of factor 2 can be achieved. There are two
main reasons for downsampling in neural networks; 1) to reduce memory consumption
and decrease the computational load 2) to increase the receptive ﬁeld. By reducing the
output size by a factor of two, the receptive ﬁeld is eﬀectively doubled.
A.2.2
Dilation
Similar to increasing the stride of convolutions dilation is another approach to increasing
the receptive ﬁeld of a neural network. By increasing the spacing between kernel weights a
larger receptive ﬁeld can be achieved, while the sliding window nature of the convolution
operator ensures that the whole domain is still covered. Figure A.3 shows a graphical
illustration of convolution with two diﬀerent dilations, and ﬁgure A.4 shows the receptive
ﬁelds for three diﬀerent dilation factors.
While dilated convolutions can achieve a larger receptive ﬁeld, with an equal amount of
computation to non-dilated convolutions, two limitations remain. Firstly, since the output
size remains the same a larger memory capacity is needed, when compared to using strided
convolutions. Secondly, due to the uneven coverage of the input data, gridding artifacts
are created. This is especially pronounced for large dilation factors, see ﬁgure A.4c. To
remedy the issues with gridding artifacts a set of non-dilated convolutions is often applied
before the ﬁnal output when using dilated convolutions.

A.2.3
Pooling
Pooling layers are used to reduce the dimensions of feature maps. They do so by summariz-
ing features present in a region of the feature map. The most common pooling operations
are max-pooling and average pooling. In max-pooling, the largest value within a window
is returned creating sharper features. In average-pooling, the values within the window
are averaged resulting in more smooth features. Figure A.5 shows a graphical illustration
of the max-pooling operation.

A.4
Regularization
When training neural networks with a lot of learnable parameters, and thus a high degree-
of-freedom, a common problem is overﬁtting. In the worst cases of overﬁtting, the network
simply learns a speciﬁc path through the network for each data sample, instead of learning
a meaningful representation of the data samples. This section explains a few of the basic
regularization techniques used to avoid overﬁtting and stabilize the training procedure of
a neural network.
A.4.1
Batch normalization
It is a very common procedure to standardize input data by subtracting the mean and
dividing by the standard deviation. Batch normalization generalizes this procedure to
activation maps inside the network. Batch normalization has been shown to speed-up
convergence signiﬁcantly [40]. The batch normalization procedure is shown in eq. A.5.
ˆxi ←xi −µB
σB
yi ←γˆxi + β
(A.5)
Here µB is the batch mean, σB the batch standard deviation, and γ and β are learned
parameters that allow the network to learn any mean and standard deviation independent
of the weights in the previous layers of the network.
A.4.2
Weight regularization
A common indication of overﬁtting is some network weights becoming very large. One
way to remedy this issue is weight regularization. Here the loss function is augmented by
a penalization of the weight magnitudes
L(w) = Lorg(w) + λw||w||p
(A.6)
Where λw is a penalty coeﬃcient, and p indicates the norm. Common choices are p = 1
and p = 2, also known as the Manhatten and Euclidean norm respectively.
A.4.3
Dropout
Another strategy that can be deployed to prevent overﬁtting is dropout [82].
When
applying dropout a speciﬁed percentage of the weights are dropped, i.e. excluded, during
training. Dropout can be thought of as an approximation to an ensemble method, in which
multiple neural network structures are trained in parallel. During test time no dropout
is applied. Dropout is usually applied to fully connected layers, while its applicability
to convolutional layers is still questionable. Although, recent research has shown that
dropout applied to convolutional layers can lead to a performance increase [15].

Activation Maps
One way to gain insights into model behavior is by looking at the activation maps, i.e. the
intermediate outputs between each of the sequential layers in the CNN. Since there are a
large number of convolutional layers in the network, and each produces an activation ﬁeld
for each ﬁlter in the layer, it is not possible to show all activations. Ideally, one would use
an interactive tool, such as the one presented by Yosinki et al. 2015 [91], to easily evaluate
how the activation maps of diﬀerent layers change depending on the input. Unfortunately,
we’re constrained to the limitations of paper in this case. Thus, the maximum activations
for a single sample are shown for a few chosen layers. First, activations from the input
layer are shown and later activations from the encoder and bottleneck part of the network
are shown. Figure D.1 shows the input ﬁelds used for the forward pass of the network.
Here the volume ﬁeld has been excluded since it is just a homogeneous ﬁeld.
Figure D.1: Input ﬁelds used to generate the activation maps.
The input layer is divided into two pipelines due to the diﬀerence in resolution between
the Von Mises stress ﬁeld and the streamline image. This allows for a good understanding
of what the network focuses on in the individual input ﬁelds. Figure D.2 shows the acti-
vations for two convolutional layers in the streamline pipeline and one in the Von Mises
and volume ﬁeld pipeline. The ﬁrst layer of the Von Mises pipeline mainly seems to focus
on extracting low and high-stress regions, while a few channels (middle bottom and top)
seem to focus on more intricate features around the load. Similarly, all activations in
the upper row for the ﬁrst layer in the streamline pipeline seem to focus on the black
foreground of the streamline image. Activations in the lower row on the other hand tend
to focus on the transition between the fore- and background. This is indicated by the
high activations along the curves of the streamlines. In the ﬁfth layer of the streamline
pipeline, the streamlines have been downsampled to a resolution of 120x60 like the stress

==PAGE:97==
ﬁeld. This downsampling is visible in the activation maps as many of the thin streamlines
have been thickened, but the streamlines are still clearly distinguishable in the feature
maps. Most of the high activation channels in this layer tend to focus on the curves of
the streamlines, with high activations in-between the intersection of the curves.
Figure D.3 shows the activations for chosen layers of the U-net part of the network. Here
two layers of the encoder and the ﬁnal layer of the bottleneck are shown. In the second
layer of the encoder, the concatenation of the stress and streamlines features can clearly be
observed, with most activations resembling a smoothed version of the streamline image.
At the eighth layer of the encoder, the input ﬁelds are barely recognizable any longer,
and activations seem to be more focused on certain parts of the domain, such as the
boundaries or near the load position. In the ﬁnal layer of the bottleneck, the input ﬁelds
have been downsampled to a very small feature map of 15x8 pixels. While some structure
can be observed in these ﬁelds they are generally hard to interpret, and should in many
ways be seen as a latent code description of the input ﬁelds.
While activation maps do provide some information as to what the network is looking for
in the input ﬁelds, it is hard to draw any general conclusions by looking at a single sample.
To understand what the network is looking for, in more general terms, more advanced
techniques, such as deconvolution [94] or feature visualization by optimization [64], could
be applied. While not applied directly in this work these techniques were discussed in
section 6.3 of the discussion.
