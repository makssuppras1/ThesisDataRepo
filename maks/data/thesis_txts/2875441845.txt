Abstract
This thesis evaluates how demand-side flexibility (DSF) can contribute to verifiable CO2 reductions in
the Danish electricity system and whether such reductions can justify a dedicated certification scheme.
Using high-resolution data for DK2 and optimization models of two representative flexible assets—an
industrial freezer and a residential heat-pump fleet—the study quantifies avoided emissions under differ-
ent activation patterns and marginal-emission-factor assumptions. Three accounting methods are com-
pared: declaration-based (Method T), price-band (Method P), and average-factor (AEF) attribution.
The models simulate day-ahead commitments, real-time balancing, rebound effects, and policy-driven
activation incentives, enabling consistent estimation of additional and verifiable tonnes of avoided CO2.
Results indicate that upward activations during fossil-marginal hours can deliver measurable emission
savings, while downward activations often provide limited or negative effects. A conservative certifi-
cation framework is proposed, aligned with WBCSD “avoided-emission” guidance, and tested through
a welfare and policy feasibility assessment for Denmark. The findings suggest that DSF-based CO2
certificates are technically feasible and auditable, but not welfare-positive on avoided-tonnes alone un-
der conservative crediting in today’s Danish conditions; NPV turns marginally positive only under an
optimistic, price-based mapping over a long (15-year) horizon. Hence, cost-effectiveness would require
greater scale, lower overheads, stronger additionality, and/or complementary system benefits beyond
the core tonne accounting.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Background and motivation
Achieving climate neutrality requires reliable operation with high shares of wind and solar.
Their
weather-driven variability increases operational uncertainty and balancing needs, now pronounced in
systems such as Denmark with annual RES shares around 50–60% [1]–[4]. Historically, conventional
generators followed deterministic schedules; by contrast, RES output is stochastic and less predictable,
adding a new layer of operational complexity.
Traditional responses—grid reinforcement and fossil peak units—are being complemented and in-
creasingly displaced by cleaner, decentralized flexibility: battery energy storage systems (BESS), heat
pumps, electric vehicles, and other electrified loads [5]. Demand-Side Flexibility (DSF) is central: indus-
trial, commercial, and mobility loads adjust consumption in response to prices or system constraints,
with aggregators pooling assets for wholesale and balancing markets [6]. This marks a paradigm shift
from “generation follows demand” to “demand adapts to generation,” with consumption increasingly
modulated to the stochastic availability of renewable supply. Digitalization and updated market de-
sign enable real-time alignment of load with intermittent resources, underpinning emerging European
flexibility frameworks [7], [8].
Despite strong operational value, DSF’s carbon effects are rarely quantified or recognised in carbon
accounting and markets. As a result, verifiable CO2 benefits remain largely off balance sheet, limiting
incentives to scale low-carbon flexibility.
In collaboration with the Danish Energy Agency (DEA)
and the Technical University of Denmark (DTU), this thesis examines whether DSF’s CO2 reductions
are measurable, verifiable, and large enough to justify regulatory action, including a market-based
certification scheme.
1.2
Problem statement
DSF can reduce power-sector emissions, yet most accounting and market frameworks lack a credible
mechanism to evidence and reward those reductions. Three frictions block recognition: (i) avoided
emissions are diﬀicult to observe at activation level due to partial visibility of marginal units, directional
and short-lived activations, and rebound effects; (ii) without a standard, audit-ready method, claimed
tonnes risk over-crediting or double counting—especially under cap-and-trade regimes; and (iii) DSF’s
effects often fall outside corporate Scopes 1–3, leaving no widely accepted instrument to convert proven
performance into a verifiable claim for buyers, financiers, or regulators.
This thesis addresses that gap for Denmark. It tests whether a CO2-linked certificate for DSF can be
made credible under conservative measurement rules and welfare-positive at realistic scale, recognising
that in a rapidly decarbonising system the value may lie as much in building dependable flexibility
markets as in the absolute tonnes credited today.
1.3
Research objectives and questions
The aim is twofold: quantify DSF’s avoided CO2 under real market conditions and test a certification
framework that recognises those effects without blurring inventory accounting.
Objectives.
(i) Develop an activation-aware measurement approach that issues only net, conserva-
tively counted avoided tonnes. (ii) Estimate how an explicit “carbon weight” in dispatch alters portfolio

behaviour and avoided emissions in mFRR. (iii) Translate physical results into a welfare CBA including
programme administration, MRV costs, realistic scale-up, and EU ETS leakage. (iv) Specify gover-
nance, registry, and claim rules that keep the attribute auditable and separate from Scope 2. (v) Assess

Research questions.
1. Based on detailed modeling of two representative DSF technologies, what is the defensible range of
net avoided CO2 per MW·year that can be extrapolated to the broader DK DSF portfolio under

at system scale?
2. Which issuance and governance rules (baselines, rebound, spill gates, ETS interaction) minimise
over-crediting and double counting while keeping MRV practicable?
3. If the pure CO2 case is weak, can the certificate be justified as market infrastructure that acceler-
ates DSF participation, and which KPIs should a pilot track to evidence system value?
4. How should certified avoided emissions relate to existing accounting (Scope 2, GOs) and the EU
ETS (e.g., allowance cancellation) so that claims remain credible and non-overlapping?
1.4
Structure of the thesis
The thesis is structured into eight chapters:
• Chapter 2: Literature Review
Reviews electricity market actors, the role of demand-side flexibility, existing carbon market
mechanisms, and previous research on CO2 reduction from flexibility.
It also examines how
demand response affects marginal CO2 intensity.
• Chapter 3: Methodology
Describes the modeling approach used to estimate CO2 reductions from DSF, including the emis-
sion factor logic, scenario design, and data sources.The final section provides an overview of the
methodology used to conduct the cost-benefit analysis of implementing the proposed policy.
• Chapter 4: CO2 Quantification from Demand Flexibility
This section outlines the key assumptions of the model, explains the logical framework guiding
the analysis, describes the implementation process, and briefly discusses the resulting findings.
• Chapter 5: Certification Framework Design
Proposes the key elements of a certification mechanism, evaluates design trade-offs, and discusses
integration into corporate carbon accounting systems.
• Chapter 6: Policy and Market Feasibility
Analyzes the regulatory landscape and potential barriers to recognizing DSF-based CO2 reduc-
tions in existing carbon markets or reporting schemes. Then conduct a cost–benefit analysis of
implementing such a policy.
• Chapter 7: Discussion
Reflects on the results, considers implementation feasibility under different policy environments,
and identifies key limitations and uncertainties.
• Chapter 8: Conclusion and Recommendations
Summarizes the findings, provides policy recommendations, and outlines implications for the DEA
and other stakeholders, as well as suggestions for future research.

CHAPTER 2
Literature Review
2.1
Overview of the electricity markets
2.1.0.1
Structure of the Electricity Market and Its Role in System Balancing
General Market Structure.
European electricity markets operate over three harmonised timeframes—
Day-Ahead (DAM), Intraday (IDM), and Balancing (BM)—with distinct operational goals aligned to
the physical delivery timeline. Governance is set by EU Network Codes, notably the Electricity Balanc-
ing Guideline (EB GL), developed by ENTSO-E and enforced by ACER [9]. ENTSO-E’s 2024 Market
Report structures analysis across the same timeframes [10].
Day-Ahead Market (DAM).
The DAM clears zonally and is the primary mechanism matching
expected supply and demand. Denmark participates via Nord Pool under Single Day-Ahead Coupling
(SDAC). Participants submit hourly, block, and flexible orders between 09:00 and 12:00 CET; at 12:00
the EUPHEMIA algorithm determines prices and volumes for DK1 and DK2, with publication typically
by 13:00. Clearing uses a uniform-price auction; results set firm day-ahead schedules for 00:00–24:00
[11]. DAM captures the expected balance; it does not cover near-real-time deviations.
Intraday Market (IDM).
The IDM enables continuous portfolio adjustment closer to delivery,
critical for VRE forecast updates [12], [13]. In Denmark, trading opens at 15:00 CET D–1 and con-
tinues up to 60 minutes before delivery under Single Intraday Coupling (SIDC); orders update/match
continuously and cross-border capacity is dynamically allocated [14], [15].
Balancing Market (BM).
TSOs maintain real-time balance with reserves: Frequency Containment
Reserve (FCR), automatic FRR (aFRR), and manual FRR (mFRR). FCR stabilizes frequency within
seconds (FCR-N for normal operation; FCR-D for large deviations); aFRR restores balance on the
order of minutes; mFRR resolves larger imbalances on 15–60+ minute horizons [10]. Upward regulation
(generation up / demand down) covers deficits; downward regulation (generation down / demand up)
covers surpluses; both needs rise with variable renewables [16].
DSF’s position. DSF is technically eligible and increasingly used, but products and prequalification
are still evolving. In practice, industrial loads and aggregated residential fleets already contribute in
mFRR (capacity and energy) where activation windows align with load flexibility; aFRR participation
is expanding as telemetry, control, and verification improve; FCR from demand exists but is more
constrained by speed and stability requirements [10], [17].
This thesis focuses on DSF in mFRR,
where today’s Danish/Nordic market design provides the most tractable pathway for activation-aware,
conservative CO2 issuance.
CO2 implications of market timeframes.
Link to emissions metrics.
Day-Ahead Market
(DAM) clearing reflects the average grid mix over hours (AEF). Intra-Day Market (IDM) reacts to
within-day deviations and sits between average and short-run marginal effects.
Balancing Market
(BM—aFRR/mFRR) activates on scarcity or surplus signals and therefore aligns most closely with
marginal emissions (MEF), because the activated unit is typically at the operating margin [10], [16].
Direction matters. In BM, UP regulation (gen↑/load↓) tends to displace marginal fossil generation
and yields positive avoided tonnes; DOWN regulation (gen↓/load↑) during VRE surplus can increase

fossil re-dispatch later if it induces rebound or reduces curtailment headroom. Conservative issuance
should therefore avoid crediting DOWN and net rebounds against UP credits.
Product granularity. FCR has sub-minute dynamics and negligible net energy; attribution to
MEF is noisy and depends on frequency quality and control stability [10]. aFRR has minute-scale ramps
and non-trivial energy; MEF linkage improves but still mixes with cycling effects. mFRR activates in 15–
60+ minute blocks with clear energy volumes and is most tractable for activation-aware CO2 accounting
[17], [18].
System context qualifiers. MEF depends on (i) interconnector flows and import marginality, (ii)
the set of online thermal units and starts, (iii) curtailment incidence for VRE/CHP, and (iv) reserve
saturation. Periods with non-fossil import or must-run CHP can mute avoided tonnes; scarcity hours
with peakers amplify them.
DSM mechanisms. Demand-side management (DSM)—shedding, shifting, or temporally increas-
ing load—reshapes residual load, reduces peaker starts, dampens ramps, and can defer starts of mid-
merit units [16], [19]. In BM, industrial and aggregated residential DSF provide UP by curtailing or
pre-cooling/thermal banking, and can provide DOWN by controlled catch-up. Net CO2 depends on
activation energy, rebound size/timing, and whether rebounds occur in low-MEF periods.
Measurement frame used in this thesis. (i) Treat DAM effects as AEF-linked background. (ii)
Attribute IDM/BM activations using hour/quarter-hour MEF series with conservative rules. (iii) Credit
only UP; net out rebounds; no positive credit for DOWN. (iv) Report both intensity (tCO2/MWh
activated) and scale (tCO2/MW·year via observed activation rates).

policy-relevant avoided-tonne signal under conservative, activation-aware issuance.
Merit order, marginal units, and price–emissions linkage.
Short-run CO2 effects of DSF de-
pend on the marginal unit set by the merit order (ascending SRMC): when lignite (∼1200 gCO2/kWh),
hard coal (∼820), or CCGT (∼490) are marginal, UP activations displace fossil tonnes; when nucle-
ar/hydro/wind/solar are effectively marginal (<50 gCO2/kWh lifecycle), avoided tonnes are negligible
[20]–[22]. The marginal unit shifts intra-day and seasonally [21], [23], [24]. Because TSOs—including
Energinet—do not publish unit-level marginal emissions, prior work infers marginal emissions factors
(MEFs) from price signals and operations data: high DAM/BM prices and positive BM–spot spreads
correlate with fossil marginality; persistent negative prices indicate surplus/spill [25]–[27]. This thesis
adopts that proxy strand with conservative, activation-aware mappings: price bands and system states
(imports, thermal online, curtailment) determine UP/DOWN eligibility and MEF assignment; DOWN
receives no positive credit; rebounds are netted. Full rules are in Chapter 3 and Appendix A.
2.2
Demand-side flexibility and carbon attribution: literature
relevant to this study
Power-system flexibility is the capability to adjust to variability and uncertainty in demand, supply, and
grid constraints [28]. EU flexibility needs are projected to nearly double by 2030 [29]. This thesis focuses
on short-duration DSF archetypes (industrial DSR, aggregated residential heat pumps, BESS) providing
mFRR capacity and energy, because activation blocks (15–60+ minutes) and observable energy volumes
enable conservative, activation-aware CO2 attribution against marginal emissions. We exclude aFRR
and IDM from core quantification and treat DAM effects as background (AEF). Regulatory evolution
under EMDR that formalises flexibility needs and access is covered in Chapter 6.
2.2.1
Role of DSF and evidence
DSF covers energy shifting, reserve provision (UP/DOWN in balancing), and selected ancillary services
[30], [31].
Empirically, DSF smooths residual load, cuts ramps, and can lower operating costs and
emissions when steered by carbon-aware control [16], [19], [32], [33]. In this thesis the operational focus
is mFRR, where activations are block-energy with observable volumes, enabling conservative, activation-

aware CO2 accounting. Because short-run effects hinge on marginal rather than average conditions,
attribution is time-resolved and price/state-linked (see §2.1.0.1); DOWN receives no positive credit and
rebounds are netted.
2.2.2
Participation requirements and barriers
mFRR participation hinges on minimum bid sizes, telemetry/metering resolution, aggregation rights,
response-time envelopes, prequalification tests, and settlement/accountability rules [31], [34], [35]. Ag-
gregation (VPPs or independent aggregators) is the practical route for small assets; BRP-facing obliga-
tions shape measurement, forecasting, and imbalance treatment.
Relevance to this thesis. These constraints directly bound what DSF can be credited under the
conservative, activation-aware issuance used here, and they parameterise both the activation rates and
the programme costs in the CBA: (i) Metering/telemetry granularity determines whether activations
are verifiable at the 15-minute block level assumed in the models. (ii) Minimum sizes and aggregation
rights determine which device classes can reach mFRR thresholds, governing the scalable MW base used
in extrapolations. (iii) Response-time and prequalification filter which loads can deliver block-energy
without violating process constraints, affecting feasible UP energy and rebound risk. (iv) Settlement/ac-
countability (BRP/aggregator) define data, forecasting, and baseline obligations; these drive fixed and
variable IT/operations costs included in the programme cost curves. (v) Barrier set—fragmented rules,
telemetry/IT CAPEX, volatile revenues, static tariffs, low end-user engagement—enters the adoption
path as friction; in scenarios S1–S4 this is represented via capped uptake rates and cost adders (Chapter
6).
Conclusion for scope: we quantify only mFRR where verification and block-energy attribution are
tractable; aFRR/FCR are excluded from core results because current participation and verification
conditions would not support conservative issuance with comparable confidence. Operational checklists
and an expanded EU barrier inventory appear in Appendix C.
2.2.3
Carbon and attribute instruments: implications for DSF
The EU ETS caps emissions; DSF reduces dispatch but does not change capped totals unless EUAs
are cancelled. Claims must resolve ETS overlap [36]. VCM offsets require additionality, conservative
baselines, robust MRV, and clear scope boundaries. GOs/RECs are energy attributes for Scope 2 and
lack temporal marginality. For DSF, credible valuation requires activation-aware baselines, marginal
emission factors, and conservative treatment of DOWN/rebound (developed in Chapter 3).

CHAPTER 3
Methodology
This chapter presents the framework used to quantify CO2 effects from demand-side flexibility (DSF). A
Julia-based stack replicates activation-driven load shifting and applies time-sensitive, activation-aware
marginal emission factors to realized deviations from baseline. The outputs (avoided, added, and net
emissions) underpin the viability of a flexibility-based CO2 certification scheme in later chapters.
3.1
Research Strategy and Framework
We couple a fixed device-physics plant box with a market wrapper and an emissions layer:
1. Physics: feasible up/down headroom under comfort/safety for each asset.
2. Market: day-ahead scheduling of reserve and quarter-hour delivery against mFRR signals (di-
rection, price).
3. Emissions: activation-aware mapping of realized deviations to marginal factors on the same
15-min grid (formal definition in Appendix A.1).
Goal: quantify avoided and net CO2 in DK2 and show how program levers move both money and
emissions.
3.2
CO2 Attribution Under Partial Observability
One-for-one fossil substitution per reduced MW is not guaranteed. Indivisible/linked bids, timing/ramp
rules, cross-zonal limits, and special regulation make committed MW sticky; public data hide unit-level
dispatch. Identical prices can map to very different physical margins. We therefore (i) prefer marginal
over average factors, (ii) present results as bands rather than points, and (iii) adopt conservative issuance
when observability is weak. See the market features and citations in Appendix C.3 and the institutional
rationale for asymmetric UP/DOWN impacts in Appendix C.4.

CHAPTER 5
Certification Framework
Design
5.1
Purpose and scope
This chapter converts the quantified CO2 effects from DSF (Chs. 3, 4) into a conservative, auditable
avoided-emissions certificate. It adapts WBCSD avoided-emissions guidance [39] to DSF and specifies
eligibility, calculation, issuance, and reporting rules. Certificates sit outside Scopes 1–3 (no offsetting).
Welfare appraisal and policy feasibility use the same rules (Ch. 6).
Where the details live. Method logic and per-interval factors: App. A; accounting engine and issuance
math: App. A.1; parameter calibration protocol: App. B.4; registry fields and market timings: App. C;
reproducibility: App. A.6.
5.2
Principles adapted from WBCSD (concise)
1. Climate alignment. Credit only when DSF plausibly displaces fossil/import marginal output or
demonstrably reduces spill/curtailment risk; treat ambiguous intervals conservatively (App. A).
2. Inventory-first. Firms reduce Scopes 1–3; avoided-emissions are reported in a separate ledger and
never netted against inventory (Sec. 5.6).
3. Conservativeness & uncertainty.
Activation-aware methods with spill gates, AEFt fallback,
rebound counted; publish the method band (T baseline; AEF floor; P sensitivity) with thresholds
set by the calibration protocol (App. B.4).
4. No lock-in. Do not credit patterns likely to prolong fossil minimum-runs; if risk is material and
unobservable, set credit to zero or disclose as upper bound only.
5. Uniformity. Rules apply uniformly across DSF classes (HPs, cold stores, EV smart charging/V2G,
BESS) with common time resolution and accounting.
5.3
Eligibility and scope of issuance
Scope. Energy-based, settled activations only (aFRR/mFRR energy). Capacity-only availability with-
out energy delivery is out of scope at current observability.
Eligibility gates.
G1 Credible climate posture. Public Scopes 1–3 inventory and strategy (prevents substitution of
inventory reductions).
G2 Recognised mitigation lever. DSF qualifies under IPCC/EU Taxonomy; product participation
and prequalification satisfied (App. C).
G3 Attribution legitimacy. Activation-aware EF method; directional netting; explicit rebound treat-
ment; uncertainty band disclosed. If effects cannot be distinguished from neutral redispatch, no
issuance (Apps. A, A.1).

6.1
Regulatory gap analysis
European power markets were designed around centrally dispatched generation and energy-only signals.
As VRE shares rise and flexibility moves to the demand side, this design leaves a gap: markets procure
capacity and energy, but they neither measure nor consistently recognise the carbon value of verified
flexibility activations. This creates weak incentives for DSF that actually reduces marginal emissions.
Recent reforms begin to address the flexibility gap. The Electricity Market Design Reform (EMDR,
in force since July 2024) requires Member States to run regular national flexibility-needs assessments and
tasks ENTSO-E together with the EU DSO Entity to deliver a common methodology across transmission
and distribution levels [28]. ENTSO-E’s strategic vision likewise calls for a market design fit for a
carbon-neutral system [40]. Operational gate times and product details are summarised in App. C.
This chapter positions a CO2 certificate for DSF as a disclosure and KPI layer that complements,
not replaces, EMDR’s flexibility machinery: it translates settled, activation-aware performance into
audited avoided-emissions metrics, enabling transparent comparison across providers while keeping
claims outside Scopes 1–3.

6.3.1
Market mechanisms to scale certified DSF
Rationale.
A verified CO2 certificate turns the climate performance of flexibility into a clear, au-
ditable attribute. This reduces due-diligence frictions for buyers and financiers and lets aggregators
compete on verified quality, not only on price [41]–[43].
Main channels.
• Procurement signal.
Large buyers increasingly require audit-ready environmental attributes.
Registry-based certificates (unique serials, time-stamps, cancellation, claim rules) align with ESRS
and Scope 2 documentation and can raise eligibility and win-rates in tenders [41], [42], [44], [45].
• Financing and adoption. A standard, third-party-verifiable KPI (tCO2 avoided) fits sustainability-
linked finance and, together with predictable per-activation revenues, shortens payback for enabling
tech (smart EVSE, HP gateways, BTM batteries), accelerating enrolment [43], [46].
• Interoperability and portability.
Common serialisation and chain-of-custody support multi-
vendor integration and cross-border recognition, lowering integration costs for OEMs/ESCOs and
simplifying channel partnerships [41], [44].
How to verify (non-model evidence).
In practice these effects are observable via simple checks: (i)
share of RFPs explicitly requiring or rewarding certification; (ii) win-rate/price differentials for certified
vs. non-certified offers; (iii) use of certificate-based KPIs in loan/bond documentation. We report these
qualitatively; they are not monetised directly.
Welfare relevance.
These mechanisms count in the CBA only insofar as they increase certified
capacity St or shift activations toward high-MEFt hours, thereby raising ∆COadd
2
. Any pure price
premium is a transfer and excluded from welfare.
Design hooks (kept minimal).
15-min alignment; conservative issuance (Method T, directional
netting, rebound counted, no DOWN credit); unique serials with cancellation; clear claim language.

flating claims [44], [45].

When certificates cause additional UP in scarcity
windows, residual peaks fall, reliance on fossil peakers declines, and net import exposure can drop;
EU/IEA analyses link scaled flexibility to deeper RES integration and greater energy independence

6.3.3
Other Benefits
Balancing-cost savings (not in core).
If the programme induces extra UP energy that truly
displaces fossil marginal generation, operating costs could fall. Crediting this requires hour-level causal
evidence of (i) policy-induced actions and (ii) a fossil marginal unit. To avoid double counting with
CO2 benefits and given the attribution challenge, we do not monetise this in the core CBA; at most we
discuss price-based upper-bound proxies as sensitivity.
Capacity value / adequacy deferral (not in core).
Policy-induced, dependable UP during peak
hours could defer capacity needs. This would require demonstrated dependable MW over a qualifying
duration and a credible capacity credit. Lacking that evidence—and to avoid overlap with reliability
metrics—we do not include a capacity value in the core; it is treated as a qualitative upside only.
Reliability / resilience (not in core).
If additional DSF reduces expected unserved energy, there
is a potential resilience benefit (often valued via VOLL). Because this may overlap with capacity value
and depends on rare-event modelling that is out of scope, we do not monetise it; we note it as an
additional, unquantified benefit.
In short, the headline results are driven by certified tonnes and programme costs; balancing, adequacy,
and reliability effects are acknowledged as plausible but are excluded from the central welfare calculation
and, where useful, referenced only as sensitivity bounds.

CHAPTER 7
Discussion
7.1
Interpretation of modeling results
The modeling focused on two device classes—heat pumps and refrigerators—to explore how demand-side
flexibility can affect grid emission intensity. Despite different technical constraints, both classes showed
broadly similar patterns: the strategy that maximizes avoided emissions can diverge from the one that
maximizes revenue, and technology choice matters for abatement potential (refrigerators tended to
deliver more avoided tonnes per MW than heat pumps under the same rules). These results provide a
realistic sense of trade-offs aggregators face when targeting system value rather than purely commercial
outcomes.
7.2
Feasibility of certification CBA
Objective and approach.
We tested whether a CO2–linked certificate for demand-side flexibility
(DSF) can pass a welfare test in Denmark. The policy enters the aggregator’s optimization as a weight
κ on hourly marginal emission factors MEFt, tilting dispatch toward high-MEFt up hours.
As κ
increases, the fleet shifts energy until physical limits bind (headroom, comfort, defrost windows, ramps).
The response is monotone but saturating, with a clear plateau around κ ≈75 e/tCO2: beyond that
point, additional up and avoided tonnes are small.
From dispatch to CBA inputs.
From the policy runs we compute an additionality metric ∆Iport:
the incremental, portfolio-normalized avoided CO2 versus the baseline schedule. The CBA then mone-
tizes only these policy-induced tonnes and compares them with programme costs (fixed administration
plus per-tonne MRV). Benefits are restricted to monetized avoided CO2.
Headline results.
Under Danish mFRR conditions, the welfare case is weak under conservative
issuance: with Method T the NPV is negative at 5/10/15 years and becomes only modestly positive in
the most optimistic combination (Method P, 15 years; cf. Fig. 6.3).
Implications of regional decarbonisation.
As neighbouring systems decarbonise, hourly MEFt
falls and the same DSF actions generate fewer credited tonnes. Even with today’s intensities, our 10-year
Method T case is already negative NPV; if intensities decline further, NPV becomes more negative. In
short, a pure “tonnes programme” is unlikely to pass a strict welfare test in Denmark unless dependable
up grows materially and/or programme overheads fall.
7.3
Why the certificate can still be worthwhile (beyond CO2)
What the certificate buys (conceptual benefits)
A DSF CO2 certificate gives a clear, auditable way to report avoided emissions outside corporate
Scopes 1–3 (often discussed as “Scope 4”) [82], [83]. Used as a disclosure/KPI layer—not an offset—it
adds three things:
• Verifiability: MRV-tied, time-stamped activations link claims to measured performance.
• Comparability: common rules reduce noise and double counting risk across providers [84].

• Recognition: a traceable attribute that procurement/finance can reference, similar in governance
(unique serials, cancellation) to GOs—though certifying impact, not source [85]–[87].
Our CO2-only appraisal is deliberately conservative and, under current Danish conditions, not com-
pelling. Yet this is only part of the picture. A certificate can play a different role: not just pricing
tonnes today, but building the market infrastructure that pulls flexible demand into the system at scale.
First, a certificate turns “climate performance of flexibility” into a clear, checkable claim. That
simple, common language lowers frictions in procurement and finance and allows aggregators to compete
on verified quality, not only on price. In practice, this means fewer bespoke audits, faster contracting,
and a cleaner signal for investors and large customers about what they are buying.
Second, attaching a small, predictable cashflow to each verified activation can accelerate adoption
of enabling technology—smart chargers, heat-pump gateways, batteries, industrial controls. Shorter
paybacks and simpler onboarding matter more for participation than the exact carbon intensity in a
given hour. This “participation accelerator” effect is largely orthogonal to decarbonisation: even as
hourly emissions fall, the value of having more controllable, connected load rises.
Third, in a very clean grid the tonne opportunity shrinks, but reliability needs grow. Windier, more
solar-heavy systems require dependable up at the right hours to limit curtailment, keep adequacy mar-
gins, and contain balancing costs. A certificate that genuinely brings additional, dependable flexibility
onto the system helps on those margins—even if the credited tonnes are modest.
Credibility is the price of admission. The certificate should be hard to game and conservative when
uncertain: net effects only, tight treatment of rebound, no (or sharply discounted) credit for down or
surplus hours, and clear fallbacks when marginality is ambiguous. With those guardrails, the attribute
remains useful for procurement and finance without overstating climate gains.
When does this move from a neat label to a societal gain? Only when it measurably increases
connected flexible capacity, delivers dependable up during peak windows, or reduces activated balancing
volumes and network reinforcements versus a no-policy path. A pilot should therefore come with a short
list of evaluation metrics and an explicit learning plan. Transfers are not counted as benefits unless
they induce those outcomes.
Takeaway. A strict CO2 test under current assumptions is tough to pass, but a well-governed
certificate can still be justified as market infrastructure: it standardises claims, accelerates DSF entry,
and prepares the system for high-VRE operation where reliability depends on much deeper, dependable
demand response. If pursued, it should start as a tightly scoped pilot with clear guardrails and success
metrics focused on additional participation and system value.
7.4
Limitations and uncertainties
Method and data.
Marginal emission factors are partly inferred, and timing plus cross-zonal flows
introduce noise; results are best read as bounds, not point truths.
Baselines and additionality.
Counterfactual load is modelled, not observed. Additionality depends
on baseline design, rebound treatment, and the rule to exclude (or heavily discount) down; our choices
are conservative but not unique.
Scale and transferability.
Findings hinge on today’s fleet size and Danish market specifics. Extrap-
olation to larger portfolios, other zones, or different device mixes carries risk; fixed and per-tonne costs
may not scale linearly.
Behavioural response.
The “carbon weight” approximates how aggregators respond to a certificate.
Real-world adoption, comfort constraints, and commercial incentives could attenuate or amplify that
response.
Policy interactions.
Future market reforms and allowance rules may shift effective tonne values and
leakage. Our welfare tests reflect today’s rules.

Potential benefits for balancing costs, adequacy, and resilience are dis-

effects push NPV downward relative to our mFRR results.

8.1
Summary of findings
This thesis tested whether a CO2–linked certificate for demand-side flexibility (DSF) can pass a welfare
test in Denmark and, if not, whether a tightly governed certificate still makes sense as market infras-
tructure. Under conservative, activation-aware issuance the optimiser shifts load toward high-intensity
hours but quickly hits physical limits. In mFRR, the welfare case is negative at 5/10/15 years for the
conservative mapping; only the most optimistic mapping breaks even at 15 years. The shortfall is driven
by (i) limited scale relative to fixed administration and (ii) relatively clean up margins, with further
tightening as the region decarbonises.
8.2
Answers to the research questions
1. Net avoided tonnes per MW under conservative, activation-aware issuance (mFRR).
Order of magnitude: low single-digit tCO2 per MW-year, technology-dependent. In our runs, re-
frigerators outperform heat pumps; portfolio increments are modest once rebound and directional
netting are enforced.
2. Welfare test and break-even conditions. With conservative issuance, NPV is negative at
5/10/15 years.
Break-even requires a generous mapping, a long horizon, and near-maximum
certified capacity; otherwise fixed administration dominates. At today’s Danish scale, a pure CO2
certificate does not pass a strict welfare test.
3. Issuance and governance for integrity with practicable MRV. Directional netting (no
positive credit for down); explicit rebound accounting and caps; spill/clean-hour gates; activation-
aware MEFs with AEF fallbacks; simple, frozen baselines; unique serials and anti-stacking across
instruments; explicit EU ETS treatment via allowance cancellation or a disclosed leakage factor.
4. If the CO2 test is weak, is there a market-infrastructure case—and what should a pilot
measure? Yes. A certificate standardises a verifiable claim and turns each verified activation
into a small, predictable cashflow that accelerates DSF participation. A pilot should track: (i)
added certified MW and device counts vs. a no-policy trend; (ii) dependable up in defined peak
windows; (iii) changes in activated balancing volumes and any named network deferrals causally
linked to certified DSF.
5. Relation to existing accounting and the EU ETS. Keep avoided-emissions certificates out-
side Scopes 1–3 and separate from GOs; prohibit neutrality/offset claims.
Address EU ETS
overlap explicitly: either cancel EUAs one-for-one for credited tonnes (full neutralisation) or dis-
close a fixed cancellation share used in valuation; otherwise treat credited tonnes as in welfare
terms.

8 Conclusion and Recommendations
8.3
Recommendations
Denmark is already a very clean, renewables-rich system moving toward near-zero marginal emissions.
At the same time, today’s DSF base is not yet large enough to dilute fixed administration costs. Read
strictly as a CO2 programme, the scheme therefore struggles to pass a welfare test here. That does
not mean it should be abandoned. The near-term role in Denmark is to build the plumbing: prove the
mechanics with a tight pilot, then take what works to settings where scale and grid intensities make
the welfare case stronger.
Start with a tight Danish pilot.
Keep scope narrow (mFRR, ex-post issuance), integrity high (net
up only, explicit rebound cap, spill/clean-hour gate, activation-aware factors with AEF fallback), and
costs low by re-using existing metering and settlement data. The pilot’s job is not to “mint tonnes”
but to standardise the claim, simplify MRV, and test whether a small, predictable attribute payment
pulls additional DSF into the market. Publish simple, pre-agreed success metrics: added certified MW
and device counts vs. a no-policy trend; dependable up in peak windows; and any measured reduction
in activated balancing volumes.
Then scale where conditions are favourable.
At EU level, the same ruleset can be more than a
label—it can be an eﬀicient incentive where (i) there is enough DSF to reach economies of scale and (ii)
marginal generation is still fossil for a meaningful share of stress hours. Countries with coal/gas-heavier
mixes and large flexible loads (electric heating/cooling, cold-chain, EV charging, industry) are natural
early adopters. In those contexts the certificate can be an effective, low-cost way to draw more DSF
into balancing services and to lower system emissions per euro spent.
Why this matters for the energy transition.
A deeper DSF base creates room for more RES:
fewer curtailments, smaller residual peaks, and less reliance on peaking plant. Power systems are shifting
from “supply follows demand” to “demand follows variable supply.” Policies that ease first-stage entry
for DSF (standardised claims, simple cashflows, low onboarding friction) accelerate that shift. The
benefit to society is not only short-run operating savings; it is a system that can run reliably on very
high shares of RES.
Outside the EU, the upside can be larger.
In grids where RES growth is held back by weak
networks, costly peakers, or high balancing costs, verified DSF can substitute part of the flexibility that
would otherwise be met with new fossil capacity. A simple, conservative certificate—issued ex-post on
settled activations—provides both a practical incentive and a governance layer that is portable across
jurisdictions.
Make the reporting value explicit.
Treat the certificate as a distinct, verifiable KPI for avoided
emissions—separate from Scope 2 and not an offset. That separation gives buyers, lenders, and regula-
tors a clean, audit-ready attribute they can reference in procurement and KPI-linked finance without
confusing it with inventory accounting.
Clear wording plus unique serials and cancellation prevent
double counting and make cross-border use straightforward.
For any Danish pilot: issue ex-post on settled activations; credit net-up only with explicit rebound
treatment; use activation-aware MEFs with AEF fallback; prevent double claims via registry cancella-
tion; decide the EU ETS handling upfront; and judge success by added participation and dependable
up, not just tonnes.
Bottom line.
In Denmark, use the scheme to build the plumbing and accelerate DSF participation;
in higher-emissions, larger-scale systems, use the same conservative design to deliver real abatement
and faster RES integration. Either way, the mission is the same: give DSF providers a clear, credible
value signal—not only for the euros they can save today, but for the future system they enable.

A.2.3
Scaling to portfolio size
The freezer model implemented in this study originates from the formulation by [38], where it was
parameterized for a 3 kW reference unit. To simulate an aggregated industrial portfolio of approximately
500 kW, the physical parameters are scaled to reflect the non-linear relationships between device size,
thermal mass, and heat transfer properties, following thermodynamic and engineering scaling principles
widely discussed in the literature [88]–[90]. This step is necessary because simply multiplying the original
3 kW parameters by a constant factor would lead to unrealistic thermal dynamics and flexibility margins
[91], [92]. The adopted scaling preserves the physical meaning of each parameter while maintaining
consistency with empirical observations of large industrial refrigeration systems [93], [94].
• Food thermal mass (Cf) — super-linear scaling. The mass of stored products increases
more than linearly with the installed cooling capacity. Large cold stores not only accommodate a
greater volume of goods but also tend to store denser packaging configurations, which increases
the overall thermal mass [88], [95]. To capture this, the food-side thermal capacity Cf is scaled by
a factor of 1.2·s, where s = Pnom,new
Pnom,ref , reflecting a 20% super-linear adjustment above proportional
scaling.
• Cabinet air mass (Cc) — linear scaling. The air volume in the cabinet grows approximately
linearly with system size. Unlike the food mass, the air fraction does not increase disproportion-
ately with size. Therefore, Cc is scaled linearly with s, preserving the original air-to-power ratio
[89].
• Heat transfer resistances (Rcf, Rci) — sub-linear scaling with exponent 0.7. Larger
cold rooms have proportionally more heat exchange surface area, improving thermal conductivity
[90], [96]. This means the resistance between compartments decreases with size, but not strictly
linearly due to geometric effects and surface-area-to-volume ratios. A scaling exponent of 0.7 is
applied, consistent with thermodynamic scaling laws and validated in prior work on aggregated
demand-response refrigeration models [38], [91]. Thus:
Rnew = Rref
s0.7 .
• Turndown ratio — fixed at 20%. The minimum controllable load (Pmin) is set at 20% of
nominal capacity. This reflects operational constraints of industrial compressor technology [92],
[93] and ensures that, during partial load operation, the model maintains feasible power bounds
aligned with industry practice.
This scaling strategy ensures that the aggregated 500 kW portfolio behaves in a thermodynamically
realistic manner. The increased thermal inertia from larger food mass allows for longer up-regulation pe-
riods (load reductions), while lower thermal resistances support faster recovery during down-regulation.
These adjustments result in a model that accurately captures the operational flexibility potential and
thermal dynamics of a realistic industrial freezer installation, consistent with observations in the field
[38], [89].

A.3.7
Optimization formulation for market participation
The daily optimization is formulated from the perspective of an aggregator operating a heterogeneous
fleet of heat pumps. The aggregator bids aggregated upward and downward flexibility in the mFRR
market while ensuring that each dwelling remains within comfort and operational limits. The goal is
to maximize the fleet’s net revenue across the day-ahead and balancing markets, while accounting for
thermal dynamics and grid constraints.

B.5
Robustness, Sensitivity, and Limits
Scope and setup.
We work on DK2 full-grid quarter-hour data (day-ahead + balancing)
from March to July 2025. The MILP (HiGHS) solves one day at a time with a fixed random
seed, 96 steps per day, presolve on, and thread-parallel execution. Emission factors come from
EmissionsDK2.build_ef_table_15min: hourly AEFs are gap-filled; state-aware MEFs are ap-
plied per quarter-hour with a safe AEF fallback and a spill rule. We align all series to common
overlap and drop partial days. In this run, down-service dispatch is disabled (p_dn_srv=0), so
directionality findings rest on system volumes and MEFs, not on mFRR-only traces.
What is actually robust. Inputs are hardened with a path finder, header normalization, tolerant
time parsing, UTC→DK checks, and explicit DK2 filtering. Food safety is protected with tight
absolute temperature bounds, slew constraints, a rebound window around directional switches,
and high penalties that dominate economics when limits are challenged. Feasibility is monitored
via daily optimality gaps and terminal temperature slack. These guards make the optimization
reproducible and conservative.
Sensitivity in one line.
Stressing prices, EF levels, door-opening load, turndown, penalty
weights, rebound windows, and thermal parameters moves magnitudes (revenue, rebound energy,
net CO2) but not the sign: UP remains cleaner and DOWN dirtier because emissions are driven
by state-conditioned volumes and thermal co-movement, while import EF shifts are second-order
over this window.
Limits (deeper view)
System scope, not mFRR-only. Results reflect full-grid flows, not isolated mFRR activations.
The UP-cleaner/DOWN-dirtier signal transfers to mFRR because activations sit inside the same
system states and import mix, but numeric EFs for activation windows can deviate when con-
straints bind on specific interconnectors. Without activation-resolved EF and flow data, our EF
assignment is a principled proxy, not a measurement.
MEF construction and attribution. MEFs are derived from hourly features (thermal gen-
eration, imports by zone) and mapped to quarter-hours. Intra-hour redispatch, ramping, and
constraint changes are not observed. The spill rule zeros MEF in rare negative-price low-AEF
hours; if negative prices arise from non-emitting but scarce flexibility rather than true overgener-
ation, this rule can understate marginal emissions. AEF fallbacks dilute marginal effects in hours
with missing thermal/import signals. Overall, the direction of impact is credible; the exact size
inherits these modeling choices.
Plant physics abstraction. The freezer is a two-node thermal mass with fixed COP, heuristic
C/R scaling to reach 500 kW, and simplified defrost (two daily half-hours). Door openings are a
binary proxy that cuts Rci by a fixed factor; humidity, ice load, latent heat, and spatial stratifica-
tion are omitted. Compressor cycling, minimum on/off times, and part-load eﬀiciency curves are
not modeled; the MILP treats electrical limits as continuous within bounds. These abstractions
bias timing and size of rebound but are kept in check by strict temperature and slew constraints.
Market realism and dispatch. The model maximizes a surrogate of market cashflows using
reservation prices and balancing prices seen in the data; it does not clear a coupled market or
==PAGE:88==
76
B Data Tables and Assumptions
enforce acceptance constraints, and it disables down-service mechanically. The bid-shape surro-
gate (α, β) approximates dynamic opportunity but is not a full bid stack. As a result, revenues
are indicative under perfect acceptance and perfect tracking; real acceptance, gate closure, and
portfolio constraints would reduce both revenue and some extremes of rebound.
Data coverage and alignment. The window is March–July only. Winter wind, heating demand,
and interconnector patterns can shift both volumes and EFs. All EF features are hourly while
activations are quarter-hour, so alignment assumes within-hour homogeneity.
UTC/DK time
handling is guarded but cannot fix hidden timezone defects in raw files. Any residual gaps are
filled by construction (AEF fallback), which smooths tails.
Scaling and generalization. The 500 kW asset is obtained by scaling a small reference via
rules-of-thumb (capacitance super-linear, resistance sub-linear).
This preserves qualitative dy-
namics but not all second-order effects. Results generalize best to similar cold-storage plants with
comparable envelopes and duty. Multi-asset coordination, shared suction headers, and site-level
electrical constraints are outside scope.
Interpretation boundary. Use the model for directionality, relative comparisons, and order-of-
magnitude impacts. Do not quote absolute CO2 to the kilogram or revenue to the euro without
activation-resolved EF data, acceptance modeling, and a plant-identification campaign that fits
COP, C/R, defrost, and OD from measurements.
Bottom line. Within these limits, the directional claim is stable: UP aligns with lower effective
emissions, DOWN with higher, because system volumes and thermal output dominate the signal
over the study window.

C.2.1
Market Timeline and Gate Times (EU)
Layering and governance.
European markets clear across Day-Ahead (DAM), Intraday (IDM),
and Balancing (BM) timeframes harmonised under the EB GL [9], [10].
Day-Ahead (SDAC).
Nord Pool DAM operates zonally with uniform-price clearing. Partici-
pants submit bids between 09:00–12:00 CET for hourly delivery next day; EUPHEMIA runs at
12:00, typically finishes by 12:45, and results publish around 13:00. Bids include hourly, block,
and flexible orders [11]. DAM captures the expected balance but not real-time deviations.
Intraday (SIDC).
Continuous cross-border trading allows portfolio adjustments up to gate
closure (often T−60). In Denmark, trading opens at 15:00 CET D-1 and runs continuously to
T−60 per delivery hour; capacity is dynamically allocated [12]–[15].
C.2.2
Balancing Products and Activation Characteristics
Product stack.
TSOs procure and activate FCR, aFRR, and mFRR to maintain 50 Hz [10].
FCR-N (continuous) and FCR-D (events) respond within seconds (FCR-D ∼30 s).
aFRR re-
stores frequency in minutes (activation ∼5 min). mFRR replaces reserves and addresses larger
imbalances on 15–60+ min horizons.

Participation Pathways for DSF (Operational Detail)
Eligibility and aggregation.
Industrial processes, HVAC, EVs/V2G, and BESS can meet

Metering and telemetry.
Sub-hourly metering (5–15 min) and real-time telemetry are com-

Response capabilities.
Products require activation within minutes; latency reduces reliability

BRP planning (Nordic).
BRPs submit day-ahead plans (hourly and 5-min resolutions), with
the final 5-min plan due 45 min before the operational hour; deviations >10% persisting >10 min

C.2.4
CO2 Implications by Timeframe (Extended Notes)
DAM aligns with average system intensity; IDM/BM align with marginal intensity because they
respond to surprises. Fast thermal units or imports often set the marginal EF under scarcity; deep
surplus/negative prices correlate with spill and near-zero marginal EF [10], [16]. This motivates
the activation-aware modelling used in Chapter 3.
C.2.5
Expanded Barriers to DSF in the EU
• Regulatory fragmentation: uneven aggregator access, unresolved supplier/BRP interac-
tions, complex prequalification [29].
• Technical entry barriers: sub-hourly metering/telemetry costs; non-harmonised standards
limit scalability [29].
• High minimum bid sizes: typical thresholds (1 MW or more) exclude smaller assets
absent aggregation [29].
• Weak/volatile revenues: insuﬀicient stable signals to cover IT, forecasting, and integration
costs.
• Consumer engagement and tariffs: low awareness and static pricing dampen participa-
tion.
• Lack of local flexibility markets: few DSO-level platforms for congestion/voltage services.
These barriers justify complementary instruments such as conservative, auditable CO2 certification
for DSF (see Chapter 6).

C.2.6
EU ETS Operational Mechanics (Background)
Core design.
Cap-and-trade with EUAs (1 tCO2e each); cap declines over time (Phase III:
−1.74%/yr; Phase IV: −2.2%/yr). Free allocation for leakage exposure; auctioning via EEX for
most Member States. Non-compliance: €100/t plus make-good [36].
Registry and EUTL.
Union registry hosts accounts; transactions are logged/validated by the
EUTL, which can reject non-compliant transfers. Verified emissions and surrenders are published
annually.
Voluntary cancellation.
Holders may cancel EUAs by transferring to designated deletion
accounts, permanently removing units from circulation; cancellations are distinct from compliance
surrenders. Mechanics and governance are detailed in [36]. Interactions and double-counting risks
with DSF certificates are analysed in Chapter 6.
C.2.7
Supplementary Notes on Price Signals and Marginal EF
Electricity prices reflect marginal plants via the merit order. High spreads in mFRR/aFRR are
indicative of fossil marginal units; sustained negative prices signal surplus/curtailment. Empirical
mappings (clustering/regression) have been used by system operators (e.g., PJM, CAISO) and
consultancies to estimate real-time marginal EFs [25]–[27]. In the absence of dispatch transparency
(e.g., Energinet), price-based proxies provide a reproducible interim tool; we formalise this as
Method P in Chapter 3.
C.3
Market features driving partial observability
One-for-one fossil substitution is not guaranteed in DK2 due to:
• Block bids and indivisibilities. Divisible vs. indivisible bids in the common merit order
(mFRRIF, Art. 2) [97].
• Time-linked activations. Technical/conditional links across consecutive MTUs (mFRRIF,
Art. 2) [97].
• Ramps and timing. Activation around the point of scheduled activation; committed MW
are sticky within the MTU [97], [98].
• Cross-zonal limits. Allocation of interconnector capacity to balancing (EBGL Arts. 38–41)
[99].
• Special regulation. Out-of-merit actions for security/local constraints [100].
Because these mechanisms are not visible in public datasets (e.g., indivisible block sizes, security
tags), identical prices can correspond to different physical margins. Claims should therefore use
marginal factors where possible and report uncertainty bands.
C.4
Institutional context for UP/DOWN asymmetry
Energinet outlooks indicate scarce dependable domestic fossil capacity for upward mFRR in DK2
(order ∼250 MW vs. 623 MW requirement), while downward mFRR has ample electric boilers and
heat-bound CHP. Cross-border exchange (DK–SE–FI) is foundational, so shortages are typically
covered by imports; conversely, down-regulation often lands on CHP/thermal. DK2 is a structural
net importer (e.g., ∼10 TWh imports vs. ∼7.5 TWh exports in 2022), with substantial inflows
from hydro/nuclear systems. This institutional setting explains low MEF↑and often higher MEF↓
and supports the conservative issuance approach described in Chapter 3. [101]–[103]
