Summary (English)
The thesis explores possibilities of developing a Voice based assistant (Alexa
skill) for Insurance Industry. An exploratory design, development and test ap-
proach along with lean methodology, is adopted to build a foundational Alexa
skill. This thesis is based on four releases. The key results and outcomes from
the thesis determines and validates foundational and technical aspects of
a conversation with voice interfaces like:
• Response time thresholds, when user expects a response in a new conver-
sation
• Response time thresholds, when conﬁrmation on an earlier response, needs
to be sought from the user
• User acceptability to processing time and time beyond which progressive
response prompt is expected by users in a conversation
• Eﬀectiveness of human like gestures in a conversation using SSML tags
e.g. ’emphasis’
• User perceptions of voice interfaces in terms of eﬃciency and convenience
as compared to other interfaces
After extensive user testing, the results validate existing hypothesis and high-
lights new ﬁndings. Certain challenges of security and privacy in terms of getting
conﬁdential information through voice interface were observed. By designing
proper security validations, voice interface has the potential to provide huge
eﬃciency over any text based interface.

Summary (Danish)
Afhandlingen undersøger mulighederne for at udvikle en stemmebaseret assi-
stent (Alexa-færdighed) til forsikringsbranchen. En udforskende design, udvik-
ling og test tilgang sammen med magert metodologi, vedtages for at opbygge en
grundlæggende Alexa færdighed. Denne afhandling er baseret på ﬁre udgivelser.
De vigtigste resultater og resultater fra afhandlingen bestemmer og validerer
fundamentale og tekniske aspekter af en samtale med taleinterfaces som:
• Lydhørhed, hvad angår svarstid, når brugeren forventer et svar i en ny
samtale
• Lydhørhed, hvad angår svarstid, når man søger bekræftelse fra brugeren
om et tidligere svar i en igangværende samtale
• Acceptabilitet til høj behandlingstid og behov for et progressivt svar, når
brugeren forventer svar på en forespørgsel og backend behandlingstid er
høj
• Eﬀektivitet af menneskelige lignende bevægelser ved at bruge SSML tags
som ’emphasis’
• Eﬀektivitet, ved at forstå brugerens opfattelser af eﬀektivitet og bekvem-
melighed ved hjælp af et voice interface
Efter omfattende brugertest validerer resultaterne eksisterende hypoteser og
fremhæver nye resultater. Visse udfordringer med hensyn til sikkerhed og pri-
vatliv med hensyn til at få fortrolige oplysninger gennem voice interface blev
observeret. Ved at designe rigtige sikkerhedsvurderinger kan voice interface give
stor eﬀektivitet over enhver tekstbaseret grænseﬂade.

Preface
This thesis was conceptualized at Technical University of Denmark with col-
laboration from Principal Global Services, Pune. As part of the thesis and to
fulﬁll the requirements for acquiring M.Sc. in Digital Media Engineering, the
Alexa skills ’PRIA’, was designed and developed at Principal Global Services
with extensive guidance from the Cognitive Systems Section of DTU Compute.
It has been prepared under guidance of Jakob Eg Larsen (DTU Compute) and
Gangadhar Gaonkar (Chief Operating Oﬃcer, Principal Global Services) in the
spring of 2018. This thesis accounts for a total of 35 ECTS points.
The thesis involves research, design, development and user testing of voice based
interaction solution that could potentially assist Principal customers in receiving
information related to their 4O1K Pension fund balance, Bank account balance
etc. The thesis describes application of voice user experience principles and lean
tools to research, design, develop and test Alexa skill as a prototype.
The goal of the thesis has been to provide an instant, convenient and human
like personalized service to Principal customers, through voice based interac-
tion device, by sharing valuable information and engaging them in a long term
relationship.
Lyngby, 02-July-2018

Chapter 1
Introduction
Direct customer interaction, service and support within any organization has
always been one of the most critical function for retaining clients and generat-
ing new business. This function has undergone disruptive changes throughout
years.
With technological developments as discussed by [Lee06] and success
in text analysis, natural language processing, speech recognition and compu-
tational capacity, conversational interfaces are being widely accepted and con-
sumed as convenient means of customer interaction. These interfaces are embed-
ded within smart-phones, smart-watches and even new generation smart-devices
and engages customers with a text chat or a voice conversation.
Voice interfaces have the advantage of enabling users with a hands-free and ef-
fortless natural communication. Amazon and Google as mentioned by [Sim17],
have embraced the voice conversation trend by launching voice interaction based
products in the form of Amazon Echo and Google Home. The devices are de-
signed such that these could be placed in living spaces e.g. kitchen tables, dining
rooms or living rooms. These devices rely on speech recognition technology to
engage customers and support them in a broad range of activities to control
smart appliances such as lights, thermostats, and kettles, or broadcast news
feeds, order online or even play lullaby for kids.
==PAGE:18==
2
Introduction
1.1
Objective
As part of the thesis, the objective and primary goal will be to understand the
technology that enables voice conversation, then design and develop conversa-
tions that are engaging to customers, and ﬁnally research on usability param-
eters that enables voice interfaces to deliver eﬃciency and convenience to its
users.
1.2
Current Challenges and Opportunities
Speech recognition technology is still maturing and challenges persist when it
comes to conveying the message to voice assistants, visibility of the system
status, its proneness to errors while understanding user’ intent etc. These chal-
lenges have restricted usage of voice assistants to easy and simple tasks like
checking weather, playing music, controlling smart-devices or posing a random
query on the internet.
However, opportunities exist to leverage the capability of speech recognition
technology for complex business use-cases, design and develop smart business
solutions to provide a completely new experience to customers and generating
new businesses.
1.3
Scope
As part of the thesis, the scope has been limited to:
• Identifying relevant business use cases for voice based interactions
• Understanding the voice based technology
• Researching relevant applications of voice based technology
• Designing and developing voice conversations
• Testing usability parameters of the developed skill and its features to de-
termine:
– Responsiveness, in terms of response time, from Alexa in a new con-
versation, when user expects a response from Alexa
==PAGE:19==
1.4 Learning Objectives
3
– Responsiveness, in terms of response time, from Alexa in an ongoing
conversation, when it is important to seek a conﬁrmation or ask a
question back to the user, to ensure that the response earlier shared
by Alexa is understood by the user
– Acceptability to high processing time and need for a progressive re-
sponse prompt, to ensure user attention in an ongoing conversation,
when user expects an answer to a query and the backend processing
time for the query is relatively high
– Eﬀectiveness of human gestures using SSML tags like ’emphasis’ and
determining user acceptability
– Eﬃciency, by understanding user’s perceptions on eﬃciency and con-
venience in getting work done through a voice interface as compared
to other interfaces
• Discussing future applications of developed skills and learnings from the
thesis
1.4
Learning Objectives
The research during the thesis would provide enriching learning experience in
terms of
• understanding an evolving technology that has the capabilities of impact-
ing user interactions
• application of user experience principles while designing and developing
voice based interactions
• application of technology to meet practical business use-cases
1.5
Project plan
While initiating research on the thesis approach and the key objectives, a high
level project plan with outcomes expected every month was devised, discussed
and followed. A high level Thesis plan that was submitted, initially, can be
referred at A.2. The plan evolved throughout the diﬀerent phases of thesis and
the interim as well as ﬁnal plan of thesis could be referred at A.3 and A.5.

Chapter 2
Customer Relationship and
Service Function
Customer Relationship and Service Function has been the front face for every
organization and has essentially been part of every successful business. Initially,
customers used to walk to oﬃces in case of any queries or needed support. How-
ever, with advancements in technologies, businesses are leveraging the capabil-
ities of Artiﬁcial Intelligence (AI) based interfaces to improve their customer
service functions. As discussed by [Ven17] and with the advent of advanced

From technical feasibility perspective, the thesis would go deeper into the foun-

voice interface.
As George [And17] mentions in his article, voice-based AI appeals to its con-
sumers because of the expectations that voice AI would conform to humans, will
respond the way humans speak and think without requiring typing on a key-
board or screen. These expectations also makes it technically diﬃcult to build.
The diﬃculty arises from the fact that humans are not orderly or structured
when they talk. Instead, humans interrupt themselves, dangle thoughts, use
words, nods, and grunts in odd ways, and assume that they are making sense
even when they aren’t.
Whenever humans talk, a response is expected. Based on cultures, familiarity,
purpose of conversation, age diﬀerences, relationship between the humans as
well as context, expectations and tolerance to the response may vary. When
parents talk to kids, kids may take longer or may be immediate in responding.
However, the tolerance is higher and the conversation continues. But, if the
conversation is between an annoyed customer and a customer service agent, if
the agent is not trained well, the conversation could be a disaster impacting the
brand of the organization.
Voice user interfaces would engage users (humans) in a synchronous conversa-
tion. Hence it becomes important to understand the technical nuances of a voice

round trip time.
The research concluded that cloud based conﬁgurations are 44% faster than edge
conﬁgurations. Fog-edge and fog-cloud conﬁgurations exhibits higher latency
resulting in 17.5% and 2% higher response latencies than edge conﬁgurations.
From skill complexity perspective also, the research concludes that cloud model
performance is consistent irrespective of skill complexity and performance of the
edge model is proportional to skill complexity. For medium and large complexity
skills, fog model is more promising than the Edge. It also observed a diﬀerence
in translation accuracy for diﬀerent phrases. Low translation accuracy require
users to repeat phrases several times before accurately identifying the skill to
execute.

problems were found, and the general case was poor. It also demonstrated that
most of the evaluators (testers) do average, few do very well and few do rather
badly.
An important ﬁnding was that people are usually willing to concede
something as a usability problem if pointed out to them by others. Another
important ﬁnding was that the number of usability results grows rapidly from
one to ﬁve evaluators and then reaches tipping point for around ten evaluators.
The recommendation is to conduct heuristic evaluation with 3 to 5 evaluators.

6.1
Conversational Skills
Klein [Kle16] suggests that for the conversation between VUI and user to be
rated as high quality, it has to be normal and natural, very similar to a con-

• Map conversation ﬂows - to help users complete speciﬁc tasks through

• Use correct contexts and build trust with the users through helpful expla-

6.1.1
Designing for better conversations
To make the voice interfaces sound right, Laura Klein [Kle16] suggests that
the below mentioned rules are important to be considered and learned while
designing voice interactions.
6.1.1.1
Rule1: Keep it short and sweet
As conversations are written, dialogs have a tendency to become longer and
diﬃcult. It is important to keep all utterances shorter as if kept longer, users
may get frustrated and cut oﬀthe system and may miss important information
from the conversation. Also, it is important to not omit anything that is critical.
If the responses from system become too fast, it adds more pressure on listeners,
again frustrating or confusing the users, as there is not suﬃcient time provided
to process the information. So it is important to keep conversations short but
useful.
6.1.1.2
Rule2: Personify it
It is a normal tendency to treat things that talk back as humans and every
human has a personality. The same principle applies to VUIs. Siri has a very
diﬀerent persona as compared to Microsoft’s Cortana and both of them are

diﬀerent from Amazon Alexa.
Just as a consistent personality builds better
predictability and comfort with other humans, having a consistent personality
also helps in designing better dialogs and using the same vocabulary and accent.
For example users prefer Alexa over Google since it is quite natural to address
Alexa with a name, as compared to mentioning ’Ok Google’ for Google assistant.
It builds a connect with the user. On hearing a voice, humans would assume
the person that includes assumptions around the intelligence of the person, race,
nationality, culture etc.
With personiﬁcation of VUI, decisions could be made on the interactions and
conversations with the users. A voice persona is similar to a style guide for a
visual product, helping to determine the tone and words to use, remembering
that VUIs and users will build a relationship for a long time and hence the
persona needs to remain engaging and not become grating over time. On the
other side, the risk of personifying VUIs as humans is that users, on ﬁnding the
limitations of the system, may get disappointed, frustrated and later may get
disengaged with the VUI.
Hence, creating a well-crafted persona should be given appropriate time and
backed by research. George [Geo17] suggests that aspects, as mentioned below,
should be considered while designing a persona for voice interface:
• The Role of VUI : What user problem is the VUI going to solve? Is it
going to be an assistant for bank related activities, a ﬁnancial portfolio
manager, a stock broker or a bank clerk? Having a clear understanding
on the role, the VUI would play, helps in creating the right persona
• Company Brand and Image: It is extremely critical that the persona being
designed for the VUI is compatible with the brand or company’s image as
it will virtually represent the organization
• Familiarity and Target Audience: The persona being created needs to
align culturally well with the users. Therefore, for a compelling persona,
considering demographics, attitudes, culture, the frequency of usage and
the lifestyle of the user is critical. A persona that works well in one culture
might not work in other.
6.1.1.3
Rule 3: Let it talk like a Human
As Weng [Wen18] argues, it is important to understand that users would expect
human-understandable voices, a voice that speaks coherently human and not a
voice that sounds human.

For VUIs to be more discoverable, so that users know about all the features, it

The way Cortana triggers user prompts
depending on the context, VUIs can help users in discovering features that

6.3
Error Strategies
George et al [Geo17] argues that every interaction has errors but it is important
to consider and think of errors as opportunities to create meaningful conversa-
tions. Some of the key aspects to consider during the design are:
• Curious re-prompts: With the curious re-prompt approach detailed infor-
mation is not provided right away. This is similar to the kind of statements
humans engage in a typical conversation expressing doubts with the un-
derstanding. e.g. “What was that?” “Say it again?” “I’m sorry?”
• Escalating Errors: This is the technique that helps in saving time and
expediting task completion. e.g. in case of expecting date of birth, if the
user is not sure in what format, an immediate prompt mentioning to share
date of birth- using 2 digits for the month, 2 digits for the day, and 4 for
the year - may be extremely helpful to avoid errors.
• Be Cooperative: As part of writing prompts and error strategies, it is im-
portant to sound cooperative - leveraging the Grice’s Maxims principles.
This will help not only in the user experience, but also creates conﬁdence
and trust in users when interacting with VUIs. The Grice’s Maxims prin-
ciples are:
– The maxim of quantity : be as informative as one possibly can, and
share as much information as is needed, and no more.
– The maxim of quality : be truthful, and don’t share information
that’s false or not supported by evidence.
– The maxim of relation : be relevant, and say things that are pertinent
to the discussion.
– The maxim of manner : be as clear; avoid obscurity and ambiguity.

Concerning aspect with voice interfaces, especially voice-only, is the obvious fact

Accessibility could be other concern. The environment in which users interact

• at least 100 words per minute (wpm) input
• close to 200wpm output
• under 250ms response time

6.5 Technical Considerations

6.5.1
VUX Law1: Maximize Natural Input
Considering the facts that
• average writing speed is 25wpm
• average typing speed is 40wpm
• average talking speed is 100wpm
it is suggested that VUI input speed must be at least 50% higher than existing
UI. Voice input is potentially much faster than typing implicitly setting the ex-
pectation that voice software engineering will need to leverage machine learning.
Since even humans tend to use the wealth of data to arrive at better outcomes,
the same will be applied to Machine learning and VUIs.
6.5.2
VUX Law2: Minimize Output Tradeoﬀ
The average accepted known listening comprehension speed is 150wpm-200wpm
whereas the the average reading speed is 250wpm. Considering these facts, it
is suggested that VUI output speed must be able to go up to around 200wpm,
or 33% more eﬃcient than regular listening. This is supported by the fact that
for any podcast if the output speed is ramped up to 2x playback, users can still
listen comfortably, and only when it goes to 3x-4x times, user experience really
starts to matter. Considering average talking speeds of 100wpm, 2x speed comes
to at 200wpm natural listening speed.
6.5.3
VUX Law3: Constrain by Conversational Constant
Some interesting facts as mentioned by [Sen16] to notice are that:
• the “feeling of being instantaneous” barrier is 100ms
• the global average time between two participants in a conversation is
200ms

7.10
Speech Synthesis Markup Language (SSML)
Amazon introduced SSML [Tay97] within Alexa to provide contextual informa-
tion for speech synthesis and deliver a more human like conversation. In certain
cases having additional control over how Alexa generates the speech from the
text in response may be helpful. For example, a longer pause within the speech,
or a string of digits read back as a standard telephone number could provide
a far better experience. These features are provided through Speech Synthesis
Markup Language (SSML) support.
To use SSML, the output speech has to be constructed using supported SSML
tags. When responding through the service it needs to indicate that SSML is
being used and not plain text. The SSML provided has to be wrapped within
<speak> tags. For example:
<speak>
Your <say-as interpret-as='characters'>4O1K</say-as>
Fund Balance is <say-as interpret-as='cardinal'>12000.90
</say-as> Rupees."
</speak>

course of thesis, design of conversation and appropriate response of the system
plays a very important role. The time within which Alexa should respond to
user query, or should conﬁrm on the understanding, determines the degree by
which the conversation is natural and seamless. As natural the conversation is,
better is the user experience and hence higher the user acceptability.
The observations could be very well related to the model of system response
time as proposed by Hoxmeier et al [HP10], in their System Response Time and

periment more with PRIA was clearly evident. This observation could be very
well related to Hoxmeier’s [HP10] observations as well, where they mention that

and user feedback varied accordingly in consecutive experiments.

terfaces. For PRIA to deliver consistent user experience, across the world, it
will require testing with users from diﬀerent demography and continents. This
is another targeted future work for the PRIA. Research and development will
be required in future to improve user experience, identify new features and con-
tinuously deliver eﬃciency and convenience to customers.
Alexa voice proﬁles will need to mature so that in business use-cases like PRIA
where sensitive and conﬁdential information may be available for sharing with
users, user identiﬁcation process is accurate and right information is shared with
right users. Implementing appropriate security checks during conversations will
have to be an important aspect for future.
For further proliferation of Alexa services, the skills will need to be integrated
with Alexa voice services so that the skills are available for consumption on
user’s smart-phones. With this integration even, buying Echo devices will not
==PAGE:107==
91
be mandatory and skills could be consumed by users by simply downloading
Alexa app on their smart-phones.
It has to be noted that Alexa, although successful, is still a platform under
continuous development and there had been multiple challenges when Alexa
couldn’t identify the accents of users. Moreover, the platform still has time till
it becomes stable and many a times, although, every feature was working before,
there are chances that suddenly Alexa doesn’t recognize the utterances.
It could however be concluded that the step by step approach to designing and
developing Alexa skill and the ﬁndings from the experiments and research,as
provided through this thesis document, should be a promising start in this
domain.

Conversation Flow
PRIA skill would comprise of multiple conversations with the user. Some of
the conversations would be very simple and be more informative, while other
conversations would be very engaging. The ﬁrst conversation involving PRIA
and user would be the invocation of PRIA (A.7).
Another conversation would be greeting the user. In this conversation, the user
would request PRIA to greet user with a name. This conversation has been
described through the ﬂow in A.8.
PRIA would also provide a feature of sharing a motivational quote with the user.
This conversation would be a more engaging conversation where the user could
ask multiple times for sharing a motivational quote and PRIA would every time
share a new motivational quote. PRIA will keep on checking if the user would
like to listen to one more quote and once user says ’No’ then PRIA would stop
the conversation with a ’Good Bye’ and wish message. The conversation ﬂow
has been depicted in A.9.
