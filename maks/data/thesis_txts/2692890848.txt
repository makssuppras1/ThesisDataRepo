Abstract
Abstract
A mechanistic code utilising semi-analytical solutions was developed to simulate heavy, slow-
moving, non-standard agricultural vehicles equipped with tracks and quantify their effects on
asphalt pavements. Non-circular loaded areas caused by belt ribs were modelled by multiple
micro circles of equal diameter. In general terms, the procedure was to attain full layered elastic
solutions for a single circle and subsequently employ interpolation techniques and superposition
to compile a full track imprint. This utilised approach saved computational time substantially.
Multiple ﬁeld investigation components were additionally carried out for code validation and
close study of tracks. The work focused on utilising the mechanistic code in performing a syn-
thetic analysis for three different multilayered pavement systems under ﬁve loading conditions
– three of which were based on tracks. Five key responses used in pavement design codes were
investigated and discussed. Based on the analysis, it was found that there are distinct differences
in trace shapes for near-surface responses in a comparison between vehicles utilising tracks and
tyres. It was further discovered in this work that tracked vehicles loaded the pavement for an
extended period of time with lower response peak magnitudes. Overall, the developed mech-
anistic code addressed tracked vehicles reliably and seems applicable to any vehicle utilising
belts, with the capacity also to represent any rib shape.

1.1
Background
Denmark is a nation famous for its long prominent agricultural traditions. The sector holds
high importance in the society and brought an export earning of 186B DKr in 2019 (Dansk
Planteværn, 2019). According to the Danish agriculture and food council (2020) more than
60% of all the area in Denmark is currently being used for agricultural purposes, and it is a
leading interest to exploit it as efﬁciently as possible. The most recent trends are to complete
the seasonal tasks both fast but also in a sustainable way. The latter is thus related to utilising
machinery that is cost-effective and follows modern standards.
Tracks as a vehicle propulsion system are in many areas greater than tyres – especially within
agricultural jobs that require high traction rates combined with manoeuvrability. The deﬁnition
of the track is broadly accepted as a continuous belt equipped with ribs and at minimum two
rollers. In agricultural practice, the rollers are located on the inside of a rubber belt and can
either vary in size or be uniform. Conventionally, larger-sized rollers are used at the ends of
a track, acting as the forward driving mechanism with smaller rollers laying in-between and
supporting the belt.
Due to a long belt spanning multiple meters, tracks provide a greater contact area that is ad-
vantageous on challenging ground conditions from, e.g. unfavourable weather conditions. This
potential was already noticed in 1904 when the Holt Mo. 77 was invented to cope with Califor-
nia’s very soft soils (Backus, 2014). Furthermore, tracks operate with lower slip percentages as
the contributing rollers have the ability nowadays to individually adjust their height levels fol-
lowing the ground contours. Such systems are getting even more effective when implemented
on new modern machines as rollers are set up in advanced boogie conﬁgurations (Fendt, 2018).
Tracks thus become beneﬁcial due to lower fuel consumption as less power is required to pro-

Motivation
The motivation for this report originates from the scarce amount of existing knowledge regard-
ing pavements exposed to non-traditional mechanistic conditions in terms of; irregular non-
circular shape of loaded area, direction of stresses and abnormal intensity of stresses. Since rural
roads are gradually being utilised by heavy non-standard vehicles – especially within the agri-
cultural ﬁeld – it is an interest to obtain an enriched understanding of how their non-traditional
loading affects a pavement.
Furthermore, there is also motivation behind the development of a corresponding mechanistic
computer code. It is advantageous to be able to simulate tracked vehicles without executing
physical ﬁeld tests that cost money and time for involved parties. If a code can provide the
needed answers, then it becomes much more cost-effective. As of now, there is no existing
open-source linear elastic code for this very speciﬁc application, which has raised an incentive
to develop one. Such an adaptable code will also hold capabilities of including future vehicles
introduced to the market, which may be equipped with different drive conﬁgurations and other
types of tracks and ribs.

Literature Review
Because of agricultural vehicles’ infrequent use of the pavement network, a higher emphasis
is put on designing pavement structures with regards to everyday trafﬁc and carry out analyses
with standard tyre sizes and inﬂation pressures. Minimal research has been done regarding
agricultural machinery on rural roads where a part of their daily activity takes place.
This chapter intends to summarise what is known from former studies of relevance to this project
– both in regards to general design rules and the more speciﬁc procedures applied for tracked
vehicles. In total, 11 studies are reviewed considering agricultural machines of various types
and functionality with the common feature of using tracks or wide, low-pressured single tyres.
The literature review is separated into three parts: (i) common pavement design & approaches;
(ii) pavements under agricultural vehicles’ loading; and (iii) modelling of non-standard tyre-
pavement interactions.
2.1
Common Pavement Design & Approaches
Pavement design in its basic form consist of constructing a structure often made up of multiple
layers that is able to transfer applied surface loads down to the subgrade. The design should
also consider essential functions such as having a rigid top layer for optimal driving quality and
acquiring a sufﬁcient pavement thickness, so trafﬁc loads get distributed adequately. The latter
is important in preventing failure of underlying granular layers.
2.1.1
Empirical
Prior to 1930, the design procedures did not have many guidelines and pavements were con-
structed from past experiences and know-how knowledge i.e. from heuristic methods. In 1928
and through 1929, the California Division of Highways began looking into sections of roads
that were damaged due to stability failures and started investigating which factors that were in
play of causing that (Porter, 1950). The California Division of Highways began drilling holes
in pavements and analysing samples of subgrade layers, which eventually led to the formal test
method named California Bearing Ratio (CBR) for doing such investigations. The CBR was
developed in the 1930s by AASHTO (American Association of State Highway and Transporta-
5
==PAGE:24==
2.1
Common Pavement Design & Approaches
tion Ofﬁcials, 2003) and was made the standard test for assessment of subgrade strength. In
the following years, engineers were able to evaluate any unbound granular layers and assess a
pavement’s status.
In late 1950s, the AASHO Road Test was invented by the same association, which consisted
of two-laned pavements distributed across six major loops with 836 individual road sections
in total. The sections were all different in terms of their structural abilities and had various
thicknesses, layer compositions and granular materials (Highway Research Board, 1962). The
whole experiment took place over a two year period with repeated loading from trucks. The idea
was to use empiricism and ﬁeld measurements in research of how pavements are deteriorated
by trafﬁc loads and how they fail. The AASHO Road Test results were later incorporated in
national pavement design guides such as the AASHTO Interim Guide for Design of Pavement
Structures (AASHTO, 1972) where the evaluation of displacements, stresses and strains from
moving loads was provided. The Road Test’s loops can be seen in Fig. 2.
Figure 2: Plan view of AASHO Road Test with six test loops. Picture courtesy: Highway Research
Board (1962).
In order to have a common design foundation, the concept of an equivalent load also arose from
the AASHO Road Test as a mean to analyse mixed trafﬁc (Highway Research Board, 1961). The
repeatedly driven loops from the trucks in the Road Test provided single-load equations, which
had to be transformed into a multi-load form. The equivalent load did just that by converting
various wheel loads, repetitions and magnitudes across different vehicles and tying them to one
relative factor. It was settled to deﬁne the equivalent load as a single axle with dual wheels with
80 kN of load (AASHTO, 1993). This would become known as an Equivalent Single Axle Load
(ESAL) portrayed in Fig. 3.

The downside of basing pavement design on empirical approaches like the AASHO Road Test,
is, that experiments have to be run to gather sufﬁcient knowledge about any new design pro-
posal. Besides, the executed ﬁeld tests are only truly valid for the geographical area in which
they are developed. The same pavement structure built in another region would deteriorate
differently due to alternative weather conditions and other factors. Therefore, when empirical
data from a given ﬁeld test is extrapolated to be used for some other location, it provides many
statistical uncertainties.
2.1.2
Empirical-Mechanistic
An empirical-mechanistic pavement design approach began receiving attention in the 1980s
when the digital era of computer technology emerged. At that time, there was a need for new de-
sign procedures more adaptable than the empirical performance equations from AASHO Road
Test (AASHTO, 2008).
The mechanistic part revolves around using numerical calculations in ﬁnding stresses, strains
and displacements for a pavement system when exposed to external loads. The pros of this is
that it easily can be adapted to new pavement constructions where other types of loads, materials
and material properties are used. The empirical element of this approach is to deﬁne at which
pavement response values failure occurs i.e. the computed mechanistic output is calibrated
with observed real-life pavement performance. As so, the empirical-mechanistic method has, in
contrast to the empirical one, the ability to extrapolate with high precision from a sparse amount
of ﬁeld results.

2.2.1
Tyres
The agricultural industry heavily relies on bigger and wider tyres when their vehicles have to
work in the ﬁelds. Such tyres are named ﬂotation tyres as they have lower inﬂation pressures
in contrast to road trucks and hereby ’ﬂoat’ on loose soil and mud. Moreover, their prominent
tyre tread further changes the interaction with the underlying ground surface i.e. how loads are
transferred.
A study from Bilodeau et al. (2019) has investigated what the effects are from usage of ﬂotation
tyres on a ﬂexible pavement by constructing a full-scale pavement structure. The experiment
intended to replicate a rural road in Canada with a 100 mm asphalt-concrete (AC) layer. A
total of ﬁve passes were performed at 25 km/h with 7 different tyre types, each mounted on a
single axle. The various test cases were executed with different axle loads of 8000 kg, 9000 kg,
10000 kg and 11000 kg. One axle with truck tyres at 100 PSI was used as a reference, while the
rest were types of ﬂotation tyres used for liquid manure spreaders at 15-30PSI.
Bilodeau et al. (2019) ﬁrstly made a comparison of the average surface contact stress, σ0, with
the ﬂotation tyres being at approximately 30-60 % of the recorded values from the reference
tyre throughout the cases. The difference was caused by the signiﬁcantly larger gross surface
areas that ﬂotation tyres obtain. The relative σ0 was found as

2.3
Modelling of Non-Standard Tyre-Pavement Interactions
This subsection reviews techniques for modelling non-standard tyre-pavement interactions i.e.
interactions from non-circular loads. Such interactions cover both the special footprints from
e.g. ribs but also include tyres. It is a common sight that factors like tyre treads, inﬂation

how ribs on a belt are constructed for tracked vehicles – so some resemblance was to be found.
The studies’ choice of including pure asphalt layers for their ﬁeld experiments makes it easier
to draw parallels to Danish roads.
The obtained ﬁndings for the wide, low-pressured ﬂotation tyres are somewhat cloudy without

any clear conclusion. Whether these tyres are less-damaging to a pavement depends on many
external factors and the speciﬁc pavement composition.
As for the tracked vehicles, there is a severe lack of analyses performed for today’s commer-
cially available machines despite their recent rise in interest. The latter includes tracked tractors,
tracked harvesters and hybrid vehicles that utilise tracks on one axle and ﬂotation tyres on the
other. As so, there is a knowledge gap that has to be addressed when only the single study from
Sebaaly et al. (2002) looked at a Case IH tracked tractor. Lack of knowledge is seen within the
ﬁeld of how tracks disperse their load. Busch (2013) did a rough research on the latter down
to a level where individual rollers were addressed – although no further discretisation was car-
ried out. Moreover, the load distribution presumptions in Busch (2013) are very simplistic by
expecting the roller layouts to be symmetrical and always in contact with the ground.
The analysed studies revealed the following main points:
• It is important to design a pavement structure with a sufﬁciently thick surface course.
• Thin surface courses result in more extensive cracking, rutting and other types of failures
from loaded agricultural vehicles.
• Small rural roads are vulnerable to very severe damage from heavy surface loads.
• Induced damages can in general be reduced greatly if sensitive pavement conditions are
avoided. These are; high asphalt temperatures and a saturated base and subgrade.
• Pavement responses were lower during tests performed late fall and in the mornings in
comparison to tests carried out during the summer and in the afternoons.
• Axle weights must be the key parameter to examine in pavement analyses rather than
the total vehicle weight. Increasing the number of axles while maintaining an even load
distribution across them is the primary recommendation to incorporate for heavy vehicles
on rural roads.
• Driving in the middle of a road is of uttermost importance, as both Busch (2012) and Lim
et al. (2012) presented in their studies how edges experience accelerated destruction from
agricultural vehicle trafﬁc.
The studies of both Busch (2012) and Siddhartan et al. (2005) utilised some kind of a ﬁnite
discretisation technique to represent non-standard loading imprints, but no easy numerical ap-
proach for modelling such advanced shapes are currently existing.

3 Objectives & Methodology
3
Objectives & Methodology
CHAPTER 3
Objectives & Methodology
The objective of this work is three-fold: (i) experimentally investigate and document the loading
type and intensity exerted on asphalt pavements by tracked vehicles; (ii) develop and validate
a new mechanistic-based code for calculating asphalt pavement responses under loadings of
tracked vehicles; and (iii) quantify key pavement responses caused by different types of tracked
vehicles and contrast the results (also) against ESAL responses.
For investigating the loading of tracked vehicles, three different vehicle types will be pre-
selected – targeting extra big and heavy Danish farming equipment. The investigation will be
based on driving the vehicle belts over a series of thin wheel load scales. Additionally, a colour-
changing pressure-sensitive paper (and ruler tools) will be utilised to visualise the belt and rib
contact geometries (i.e., area, spacing, orientation). Lastly, the vehicles will be brought to a
large outdoor weighbridge – commonly employed for routine weight measurements of trucks
– for assessing overall weight. For investigating the effects on pavements, some of the tracked
vehicles (and a semi-truck) will be driven over an instrumented asphalt pavement section. Me-
chanical responses will be collected from the sensors for subsequent code validation.
The pavement analysis code will be founded on layered-elastic theory wherein all layers are
homogeneous, linear-elastic, isotropic, and weightless. Tracked vehicle loadings will be ap-
proximated by means of superposing a large number of small circular areas (micro circles),
each exposed to a uniform vertical stress. To avoid lengthy calculations, all micro circles will
be chosen to have the same diameter. This will allow the sought solution to be constructed from
the solution of a single circular load. Interpolation techniques will be utilised to further improve
the computational efﬁciency of the code. The new code will be veriﬁed against closed-form
analytic solutions and validated by attempting to synthetically reproduce the sensor readings
obtained in the ﬁeld experiments.
The quantiﬁcation of key pavement responses under tracked vehicles will be a purely synthetic
investigation. Three model pavement systems will be assumed, differing in layer thicknesses
and elastic properties. Key responses, commonly considered in pavement design codes, will be
generated and contrasted in terms of peak intensities. Moreover, trace shapes and durations for
the key responses will be investigated for vehicles travelling at a constant speed along a straight
line.

Weight Distribution
As described in the literature review, the whole weight of the vehicles is transferred to the
pavement surface through ribs. All ribs are loaded directly by the rollers inside the track, and
since these vary in size, the complete weight distribution is complex. The plan of action to
acquire a full picture of the latter is listed in these four scenarios:
Scenario 1: Measure the contribution from each roller individually by a corresponding
load scale on both sides of a vehicle. In the case a rear tyre is present it is
considered a roller.
Scenario 2: Measure a continuous weight distribution along a whole track on one side
of the vehicle by grouping multiple load scales together. Rear tyres are not
included.
Scenario 3: Simulate a local road depression or hump by placing load scales under some
of the rollers of the track.

CHAPTER 5
Mechanistic Code for Tracked Vehicles
In this section, a detailed explanation is given for the construction of the mechanistic code for
tracked vehicles and how the used algorithms work. Subsequent veriﬁcation and validation
procedures are also reviewed.
From the very beginning of this code’s development, it was settled to build it on top of an
already existing software with LET implemented, which then could be used as a foundation.
The choice fell on the open-source software package, ALVA, developed by Skar et al. (2020b).
ALVA stands for Adaptive Layered Viscoelastic Analysis and runs on the programming plat-
form MATLAB. The different components of ALVA are described in depth in Skar and Ander-
sen (2020) where a visual overview is given. For this project, only the LET module in ALVA
will be used where it is activated as a background routine to solve the fundamental equations
for a layered elastic system as described by Burmister (1945) in Sec. 2.1.2. Moreover, ALVA
is a preferable choice due to its high adaptability and transparency in regard of applying any
suitable modiﬁcations. There is thus no limit on e.g. how many surface loads or pavement
layers a user wants to simulate (Skar et al., 2020b). The listings of the full mechanistic code are
provided in App. J.
5.1
General Inputs
The user inputs for the mechanistic code consists of information about the tracked vehicles and
various selections needed to run ALVA. The inputs are listed below:
1.a. Loads from track rollers if a tracked vehicle is modelled
1.b. Loads from tyres if a non-tracked vehicle is modelled
2. Dimensions of the vehicle and geometry of ribs from the belts and/or ﬂotation tyres
3. Material parameters of the pavement system (layer thicknesses, elastic moduli values,
horizontal spring stiffness to model interface conditions and Poisson’s ratio)
4. Numerical parameters to run ALVA in terms of no. of Bessel zero points and Gauss
points for evaluation of Bessel functions

Modelling Assumptions
In the mechanistic code, LET is applied for micro circular loads of a ﬁnite radius, a, that are
grouped together with drawn inspiration from Busch (2012) and Fig. 11. In such a way, the

The modelling of the ribs is done by assuming rectangular patches as a well-approximated
substitution. The patches’ lengths, L, and widths, W, are corresponding to the real dimensions
of the ribs, and a rotation, θ, is also applied to give them the right orientation matching the
layouts on the belts as e.g. seen in Fig. 23. Once a patch has been deﬁned it is ’ﬁlled’ with the
non-overlapping, micro circles. The procedure is visualised in Fig. 39.

In addition, to obtain consistent pavement simulations from the code, the following set of re-

• Vehicles traverse the pavement at crawling speed so inertia effects can be neglected as
proposed by Iowa Department of Transportation (2000)
• The moving speed is constant
• Surface loads are modelled quasi-statically and viscoelastic effects are not included
• Surface loading is exerted on a smooth, linear and non-inclined pavement

connection is made by providing a corrected elastic modulus for a layer with a static loading as

5.5.2
Modelling of Synthetic Traces
The synthetic traces for LVDT are modelled based on the relative horizontal displacement be-
tween two points at the surface along the y-axis. It is assumed that the LVDT hole only elongates
and compress uni-directionally, and any additional skewing is neglected. The two surface points
are placed opposite each other at either end of the LVDT hole, as illustrated in Fig. 28. The
relative horizontal displacement is estimated by
∆uy(x,y0) = uy,A(x,y0)−uy,B(x,y0)
(59)
where ∆uy(x) is the difference in horizontal displacement in y, uy,A(x) is the horizontal dis-
placement in y at point A and uy,B(x) is the horizontal displacement in y at point B. The ﬁrst
calculation is made at the maximum x-coordinate. Thereupon, the x-coordinates are changed
in a repetitious procedure to simulate a backwards movement across the 2-D grid from Fig.
44 along the x-axis. For the LVDT sensor, this resembles a passing load. The y-coordinate is
constant and set to y0 i.e. the sensor offset.
Tiltmeter traces are modelled by determining surfaces slopes along the x and y axes for the
roll and tilt responses, respectively. The slopes are created by the deﬂection bowl of a passing
load, and the tiltmeter’s rotations are calculated by the relative changes in vertical displacement
between two points. By consulting Fig. 28, it has been marked that points A and C are em-
ployed for the roll, and points A and B for the tilt. The slope angles are determined by using

5.6
Summary
5.6
Summary
In general, the veriﬁcation of the mechanistic code against closed form half-space solutions and
ELLEA1 (Sec. 5.4) has proved good results with minimal absolute errors. A few cases have
resulted in an absolute error above 50%, but those are only for very small values where the
relative differences are nominal. Moreover, for these solutions, it was further discovered that by
including more micro circles to numerically represent a rectangular patch, it provides evidently
a better approximation. The latter is noticed both in Tab. 15 and for the ﬁrst results near surface
in Tab. 16. The veriﬁcation also issued the interesting observation of how a setup with 4500
micro circles in contrast to just ﬁve is not contributing to noticeably better approximations for
evaluations at large horizontal offsets or at great depths – matching well with the principle
of Saint-Venant. This discovery implies that by using the mechanistic code for computing
responses at points of interest e.g. in the granular pavement layers, a more rough discretisation
of the patches can be sufﬁcient, and it would help the code run faster.
The computed synthetic traces used for validation in Sec. 5.5 are able to accurately mimic the
measured responses from the road side sensors – with the utilised pavement model parame-
ters. It is also clearly observed that the tiltmeters record more noise than the LVDTs, which,
especially for the rotations in the x-direction (roll), buries the underlying signal since tiltmeters
approach their sensitivity limit. The effect of the latter is witnessed for the two tracked vehicles
and also for the rear tyres of Grimme Maxtron 620 II as the most notable example. On the other
hand, the greatest comparison quality was provided by the Scania semi-truck due to its much
higher induced loading in the pavement and bigger effect on the sensors.

6.3
Summary
By consulting Tabs. 23 and 24, numerous patterns are observed across the three synthetic
pavement systems. For the maximum tensile strain at top of the asphalt layer, the highest peak
values are caused by the semi-truck and the ESAL – both for the strong and medium systems
in which the values are ∼145µm/m and ∼270µm/m, respectively. The peak values for the
tracked vehicles are between 17 %-36 % lower, respectively, for the two systems. As for the
weak pavement system, the semi-truck solely induces the highest peak tensile strains at 481
µm/m, which in comparison are 21 % lower for the ESAL, and 31 %-67 % lower for the tracked
vehicles.
For the maximum tensile strain at bottom of the asphalt layer, the same pattern emerges for the
strong and medium systems as in the abovementioned: the semi-truck and the ESAL are nearly
equal regarding their highest peak values at ∼210µm/m and ∼395µm/m respectively for these
two systems. In contrast, the same tensile strains are 28 %-58 % lower for the tracked vehicles
in the two systems. In the weak pavement system, both the semi-truck and the ESAL cause the
highest tensile strains at ∼600µm/m, whereas this response’s peak values are in the range of
32 %-63 % lower for the tracked vehicles.
For the maximum vertical stress at top of the base layer, a clear trend in the peak values is
detected with the semi-truck inducing the highest stresses across the three pavement systems
(strong, medium, weak) at 11.4 ·10−2MPa, 19.3 ·10−2MPa and 26.5 ·10−2MPa, respectively.
In second, the peak values for the ESAL are consistently 15 % below. For the Grimme Maxtron
620 II and Ploeger EPD540, the peak values are 35 %-55 % below those of the semi-truck –
while a further reduction of 20 %-25 % is caused by the Fendt MT1100 across all systems.
For the maximum vertical stress at top of the subgrade, there is a much more even distribution
for the peak values in each of the three pavement systems. The highest differences are topping
at 25 % internally between the vehicles and the ESAL. Hereby, the peak values across all these
loading conditions are ∼2.5·10−2MPa, ∼3.5·10−2MPa and ∼5.5·10−2MPa for the strong,
medium and weak systems, respectively.
For the maximum octahedral shear stress in the asphalt layer, the peak values are consistently
occurring at the top of the layer for the tracked vehicles and at the bottom of the layer for the
semi-truck and the ESAL. The latter results are valid throughout all three systems. The highest
peak values are caused by the semi-truck and the ESAL at ∼45·10−2MPa, 55·10−2MPa and
75 · 10−2MPa for the strong, medium and weak systems respectively. The tracked vehicles
induce, in comparison, 30 %-50 % lower peak values regardless of the pavement system.
Furthermore, an apparent trend seen across all the key responses (except peak vertical stresses
at top of the base layer), is, that peak values despite the pavement system are consistently lower

7 Conclusion
7
Conclusion
CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
7.1
Summary and Main Findings
This work was focused on quantifying the effects of tracked vehicles on asphalt pavements. The
investigation included a review of literature, (see Sec. 2), after which the speciﬁc objectives
were deﬁned (see Sec. 3). Subsequently, ﬁeld work was carried out (see Sec. 4), followed
by the development and validation of a new mechanistic pavement analysis code (see Sec. 5).
Ultimately, the new code was applied to synthetically study the effects of tracked vehicles on
different pavement systems (see Sec. 6).
From the literature review, it was found that layered elastic theory is the most common pave-
ment design and analysis tool. This theory is semi-analytic and solves for stresses, strains,
and displacements in a layered half-space under loads applied over circular areas that represent
standard tire imprints. It was not possible to ﬁnd many studies investigating non-traditional
loads on pavements. There exist semi-analytical solutions for a rectangular loading and an el-
lipse, but the associated codes are not readily available. One identiﬁed alternative is to represent
non-circular loadings with a large number of small circles.
From the ﬁeld work, it was found that loads from tracked vehicles are non-uniformly distributed
across belts, i.e. ribs are loaded unevenly (see Fig. 23). The non-uniformity in loading origi-
nates from the individual rollers; it also seems to be related to the condition of the belt material.
The sensor readings from the full-scale ﬁeld test indicated time-dependent pavement response,
most likely due to asphalt viscoelasticity. During sensor data processing, it was further no-
ticed that tiltmeters recorded signiﬁcantly more noise than the LVDTs and performed worse
overall.
The newly developed pavement analysis code was based on the standard solution for a layered
elastic system under a circular loading (via the ALVA MATLAB code). Non-circular loaded
areas representing belt ribs, were modelled as groups of micro circles of equal diameter. The
ALVA code was called as a sub-routine to solve for one circle, and the overall solution was
compiled by superposition. In this context, an interpolation scheme was employed to solve for
the single micro circle and then utilise the results to many circles – without the need for re-
calculation in ALVA. Doing so provided precise and veriﬁed results and saved computational
91

effort proportional to the total number of circles employed. The code has been validated by
synthetic reproduction of sensor readings from the ﬁeld work. The code was able to generate
responses that resemble the sensor readings in both shape and magnitude.
The pavement analysis included three different synthetic pavement systems representing rural
danish road conditions. Agricultural vehicles were all simulated with the same constant speed.
Key responses used in pavement design codes were computed. Detailed results are provided
in Tabs. 23 and 24. It was found that, regardless of the pavement system, the semi-truck and
ESAL had the highest peak tensile strains (both at surface and bottom of the asphalt layer)
compared to the tracked vehicles. This ﬁnding was also seen for the peak vertical stresses at
top of the base layer and for the peak octahedral stresses in the asphalt layer. For the vertical
stresses at top of subgrade, the differences in peak values between vehicles and the ESAL were
roughly even for all three pavement systems. Lastly, the response traces for tracked vehicles are
more evenly spread out over a larger area in contrast to the semi-truck, which creates distinct
individual peaks (see Fig. 53).
7.2
Conclusions & Recommendations for Future Work
Based on the work in this project, it is concluded that the mechanistic code developed for ad-
dressing tracked vehicles is reliable/validated, and therefore applicable to any vehicle utilising
belts. This also means that the approach taken for representing ribs with multiple micro circles
and the interpolation technique employed for saving computational effort is accurate. Ulti-
mately, the code has utility in quantifying and comparing pavement responses under various
tracked vehicles.
The employed algorithm in the mechanistic code provides an easy way to compiling any ar-
rangement of micro circles to represent a track imprint. Any new shapes of ribs or other pro-
truding features on belts that may arise in new modern conﬁgurations will hereby also be cov-
ered. The unrestricted modelling freedom also presents opportunities for the code to be used in
alternative applications targeting other than tracks.
Based on the synthetic pavement analysis, it is concluded that the trace shapes for near-surface
responses are different when comparing tracked vehicles to those equipped with tyres. In gen-
eral terms, tracked vehicles generate prolonged traces.
In light of the experiences of this work, suggestions for improvement and possible future work
ideas are provided in the following:
(i) for conducting similar future ﬁeld experiments:
• Utilise gyroscopes in addition to tiltmeters to improve the information for calibration and

validation of pavement models
• Place LVDTs in larger holes to improve the signal-to-noise ratio
• Utilise a wide tape to help identify the lateral position of passing vehicles
• Employ stationary cameras for recording of vehicle passes
• Carry out multiple experiments throughout the year to better capture thermal sensitivity
(ii) for improvement of the mechanistic code:
• Extend the code to include viscoelastic effects to capture time and temperature layer
behaviour
• Model ribs with various sized circles to optimise the computational effort and accuracy
• Investigate semi-analytical solutions for square and elliptical loads
• Extend the code to consider pavement edge effects
(iii) for further researching tracked vehicles:
• Experimentally investigate pavement edge effects
• Utilise the code to investigate response durations for different vehicle speeds
• Make the code easily accessible to practitioners
