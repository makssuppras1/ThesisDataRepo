Abstract
With the increasing adoption of human-robot collaboration, robotic systems are incorporating
more advanced features to meet market demands, leading to increased system complexity and
associated safety concerns. In addition to complying with legal requirements and standards,
safety-critical robot systems must be thoroughly analyzed for potential safety flaws and hazards
before deployment, particularly to ensure the safety of human operators working directly or
indirectly with robots in shared workspaces.
This thesis aims to validate that a robotic system adheres to the pressure and force-limiting
safety design specified by ISO 15066:2016. It demonstrates an Automata-based, risk-guided
search algorithm to identify potentially unsafe robot responses triggered by human operator
behaviors. This hazard identification approach is validated through experiments in the domain
of human-robot collaboration. In the experimental procedure, simulation models of test scenar-
ios are created, after which feasible human action sequences, derived from regular expressions of
Finite State Automata, are executed within the simulator. The parameters resulting from these
simulations are then input into the risk-guided search algorithm, which calculates a risk value
for each specific work sequence, considering additional parameters influencing human behavior.
Early validation of safety designs through simulation models shows promising results. The
Automata-based, risk-guided search approach also yields encouraging outcomes; however, it
involves a trade-off between accuracy and exhaustiveness. Other factors, such as the robustness
of the simulator and limitations inherent in simulation models, further challenge the accuracy
of the results. This method aims to provide insights into hazard identification without relying
solely on human reasoning, but it is intended to complement rather than replace existing
methods.

Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation of Research
Collaborative robots are designed to enable direct, cooperative interaction with human opera-
tors, supporting shared tasks in a common workspace. These robots integrate the adaptability
and decision-making capabilities of human workers with the efficiency and precision of robotic
automation, aiming to enhance productivity in complex, partially automated processes. The
research and development of cobots is a relatively new field of robotics. The first successful
collaborative robot product was the Universal Robot(UR), which was commercially available
in 2008. However, the application of collaborative robots has expanded rapidly, and the market
size of cobots was $649 million in 2019, and it is expected to grow at the rate of 45% till 2025.
It is expected to be widely adopted by small and medium-sized enterprises to increase working
capabilities itesafety assurance.
In contrast to conventional industrial robots, collaborative robots operate with a level of de-
cision autonomy that allows them to detect and adapt to environmental changes. They have
advanced control mechanisms to respond dynamically, avoiding interference and ensuring safety
when objects or humans are detected in shared spaces. Factors such as structure, operating con-
ditions, movement capabilities, control mechanisms, communication protocols, and workspace
design differentiate collaborative robots from traditional robots, adding layers of complexity
that support real-time interaction with human operators [1].
In these collaborative settings, the coupling of human and robotic actions heightens the com-
plexity of collaborative robots, as they must accommodate unexpected changes and uncertain-
ties that may arise during human interaction. Successful collaboration depends on multiple
factors, including effective communication, context awareness, decision-making capacity, clear
role assignments, task planning, and task allocation. Despite careful design to ensure that both
humans and robots can act simultaneously without compromising safety, the unpredictability
of human behavior and the potential for human error introduce significant challenges for safety
assurance[2].
Collaborative robots thus have stringent safety requirements, particularly as they operate in
dynamic and often unstructured environments. The coexistence of humans and robots in a
shared workspace inherently presents a hazardous scenario that demands robust safety mech-
anisms and standards. Industrial safety standards outline key requirements for collaborative
robot design. For instance, components must meet Type A standards, such as functional safety
under IEC 62508:2010, risk assessment per ISO 12100:2010, and safety mechanisms specified
by ISO 12100. Additionally, cobots must adhere to ISO 10218:2011 standards, supplemented
by ISO 15066:2016, which addresses collaborative safety specifically.
Functional safety is essential in human-robot collaborative (HRC) systems, ensuring that hu-
mans interacting with robots are not subjected to unacceptable injury risks. However, merely
equipping robots with safety measures according to standards is insufficient to guarantee func-
tional safety on its own. The deployment of these collaborative robots is in wide areas like
manufacturing, construction, service, personal care, etc., it is crucial to ensure that the imple-
mented safety measures are adequately address specific use-case hazards. Risk Assessment is
the procedure to identify hazards and analyze the associated risk. The current risk assessment

Research Objective
This project aims to validate robotic systems against established safety standards- particularly
the pressure and force-limiting requirements outlined by ISO 15066:2016—to ensure robust
safety performance in human-robot collaborative scenarios. To perform a risk assessment of a
system, hazard identification has to be identified first, so this project also explores an alternative
method to traditional methods relying on human reasoning and then presents how such a
method can be used to ensure functional safety for a complex human-robot collaborative system
by leveraging the existing simulation models.
1.3
Research scope and limitations
To meet the research objectives of this project, the literature review will examine the research
in areas of the role of human-robot interaction, the safety designs of a collaborative robot
system, the risk assessment in the design phase, and current hazard identification methods
and their challenges, and the latest method that are being experimented to identify emergent
hazards, and the importance of verification and validation process. Based on the literature
review, an appropriate hazard identification method effective for the context of human-robot
collaboration will be identified. The developed methodology will then be validated using the
simulated environment.
Though it presents simulation-based methods, it assumes that existing simulation models are
suitable models for effectively identifying hazards. This project does not focus on developing
the models, especially the digital modeling of humans, which is a separate field of research
with its challenges. The simplified application examples will be demonstrated, focusing on the
conceptual solutions for hazard identification. The future work that can be extended to meet
the limitations will be discussed at the end of this project.

2.1
Safety Design of the Collaborative Robots
A robot is considered collaborative when humans and robots work together on the same tasks,
with their actions affecting each other directly, sometimes requiring physical contact.
The
robot’s features include one or more of the four safety modes specified by the standards. The
four safety modes that are stated in ISO/TS 15066 are Safety-Rate monitored stop(SRMS),
Hand guiding(HG), Speed and separation monitoring(SSM), and Power and force limiting [3].
As collaborative interaction may require physical contact between the robot system (robot,
robot tools, the workpieces, and the handling system) and human body parts, most of the
existing Speed Separation Monitoring(SSM) applications and studies have shifted toward Power
and Force Limiting(PFM) collaborative methods.
In human-robot collaboration, Power and force should be below the threshold limits to reduce
risk and avoid detrimental consequences. The contact situation between the robot and the
operator is either quasi-static or transient.
As per ISO/TS 15066:2016, robots supporting
operations with power and force limits can be supplied with the means to configure limiting
thresholds. The risk from these contacts can be limited by passive or active safety design.
The passive safety design is the mechanical design of the robot system, such as increasing the
contact surface area, absorbing/reducing the energy transfer time during contact, and limiting
moving masses. The active safety design methods include reducing the dynamics like forces,
torques, velocities, momentum, mechanical Power, axis ranges, or space ranges, using safety-
related monitored stop functions, and using sensors to anticipate or detect contact or proximity
[4],[5].
These safety designs are the adaptive control system design that allows a robot to adjust the
speed and force in real-time based on proximity to humans. The standards provide general
guidelines for force and pressure limits but lack detailed instructions for specific applications
or industries.
This absence of context-specific guidance can lead to inconsistencies in risk
assessments and safety implementations across collaborative robot applications. The standards
don’t provide comprehensive methodologies for these measurements of forces and pressures
during human and robot interactions, leading to potential inaccuracies.
So, verifying and
validating parameters like speed, proximity, and force must be done specifically for collaborative
interaction, considering the dynamic environment.
2.2
Human-Robot Interaction and Human-Robot Collaboration
The demand for automation in human-centric applications has grown significantly, driving
collaborative robot manufacturers to focus on enhancing robots’ interactive and responsive
capabilities [6]. Human-robot collaboration involves physical and social human-robot interac-
tions(pHRI and sHRI). To foster safer collaboration, robots require capabilities that enable
them to analyze and access both the physical and the social properties of the environment,
such as human gestures and behaviors, various touch stimuli [7], human positions, and their
movement, and the presence of the objects and operating tools [8]. This approach reduces
system hazards and increases system reliability[9].
Several researchers have worked on integrating robots into collaborative frameworks that pro-

2.3
Current approaches to ensure safe human-robot collaboration
Researchers in HRC safety have developed multiple approaches to ensure safe interactions,
which can be broadly classified into four categories: safety through control, safety via motion
planning, safety through prediction, and the integration of psychological considerations. These
approaches work to reduce risks in various ways, whether by controlling robot actions to avoid
collisions or by predicting human movements to enable safe interaction zones.
[13] has proposed Adaptive safety-critical control with uncertainty estimation to a variety and
unpredictability of human behavior leading to potential dangers for human coworkers. This
approach combines safety-critical control with uncertainty estimation to manage the unpre-
dictability of human actions. Robots can switch flexibly between path tracking and collision
avoidance by integrating a time-varying integral Barrier Lyapunov function (TVIBLF) and an
adaptive exponential control barrier function(ABECBF), ensuring safety even when humans
move unexpectedly.
The sudden, uncontrolled motions of humans can jeopardize safety. Such reactions may be
induced by robots’ behavior, which startles or surprises humans.
The Expectable Motion
Unit(EMU) concept [? ] models the relationship between robot velocity, distance to humans,
and the likelihood of involuntary movements, enabling real-time adjustments to robot behavior,
reducing the risk of startling humans, and enhancing psychological safety.
The article [14] presents a framework for real-time safety evaluation of collaborative robot
tasks. The Safe Task Generation Module evaluates the safety of instructions by computing
a subjective logic-based safety score for each command. This module combines a perception
system, program analyzer, safety evaluator, and safety tuner to optimize configurations based
on risk. Simulations show that higher safety scores lead to safer task execution, while risky
configurations are identified before execution.
Risk-aware human Modelling [15] is another approach incorporating models from behavioral
economics called Cumulative Prospect Theory to predict suboptimal human behaviors influ-

behavior in human-robot collaboration; however, several critical gaps remain. A standard lim-
itation is their assumption of specific uncertainties, restricting adaptability in highly dynamic
or unforeseen situations. Additionally, reliance on intensive real-time computational resources
can limit their practical deployment in rapidly evolving or resource-constrained environments.
Furthermore, many methods depend on accurate human behavior modeling, a task complicated
by individual differences, contextual variations, and limited data availability. Dependence on
predefined safety rules or expert-generated safety knowledge reduces adaptability to novel or
evolving collaborative scenarios. Together, these limitations highlight the necessity for com-
prehensive and robust frameworks that effectively integrate human behavioral dynamics, com-
putational efficiency, and flexibility in addressing uncertain scenarios, thereby enhancing the
safety and reliability of human-robot interactions.
2.4
Traditional and Emerging Hazard Identification Methods
Risk assessment is a critical procedure for ensuring functional safety in HRC systems. It involves
identifying, estimating, and evaluating potential hazards and their associated risks to reduce or
eliminate them. Risk assessment is mandated by the EU Machinery Directive 2006/42/EG and
strongly recommended by ISO standards such as ISO 12100, ISO 10218, and ISO/TS 15066.
Effective hazard identification is a foundational step within this process, directly contributing
to the safe integration of collaborative robots in shared workspaces. Several methodologies are
currently employed for hazard identification in HRC systems [20], including:
• Predefined hazard checklists derived from ISO standards.

• Safety engineering methods such as Hazard and Operability Study (HAZOP), System
Theoretic Process Analysis (STPA), and Fault Tree Analysis (FTA).
• Task-oriented approaches, where main tasks are divided into subtasks, each analyzed
separately for hazards.
• Simulation-based approaches utilizing tools like Human-In-Robot Interaction Testing

• The Function-Centric Hazard Identification Approach (F-CHIA) combines HAZOP and

Traditional safety assessment tools, widely applied in industrial contexts, include Failure Modes
and Effects Analysis(FMEA), HAZOP, SHARD(Software Hazard Analysis and Resolution in
Design), and STPA. These methodologies are supported by comprehensive risk assessment
practices outlined in standards such as ISO 31010 and IEC 60812:2018 [22].
However, traditional methods face significant limitations in highly complex, dynamic, and non-
deterministic environments typical of human-robot interactions. Their reliance on extensive
expert input and substantial computational resources limits their effectiveness and practicality
as the complexity of interactions grows [23], [24],[25] [25].
Simulation-based hazard analysis has emerged as a promising solution to address these chal-
lenges. For instance, [26] framed hazard analysis as a search problem, employing simulation
to systematically explore a broad range of potential human behaviors to identify high-risk sce-
narios. This approach efficiently targets likely unsafe interactions by varying human behaviors
while fixing initial states and system models, avoiding computationally infeasible exhaustive
searches.
Additionally, [27] utilized Adaptive Stress Testing (AST) with reinforcement learning, employ-
ing Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) to generate adversarial human-robot interactions. This
method proactively identifies hazards by realistically simulating and challenging robotic sys-
tems with unpredictable human behavior. Despite its advantages, modeling realistic human
behaviors and selecting relevant scenarios remain significant challenges in effectively applying
this method.
2.5
Verification and Validation technique
Another critical aspect of achieving safety and reliability objectives in human-robot collabora-
tion is the verification and validation(V&V) processes. When we use different safety designs,
solutions, approaches, methodologies, and algorithms to ensure safer human-robot interaction,
V&V processes demonstrate trustworthiness through corroborative evidence of their safety and
functional correctness.

Verification involves assessing whether a robot’s design and implementation meet the specified
requirements. This process typically employs formal methods, such as model checking, to math-
ematically analyze system models and ensure adherence to desired properties. For instance,
formal verification can systematically explore all possible states of a robot’s control system to
detect potential errors or safety violations before deployment. This approach is particularly
beneficial in HRI scenarios, where the complexity of interactions necessitates rigorous analysis
to prevent unforeseen behaviors[28] [29]. Conversely, validation evaluates the robot’s perfor-
mance in real-world scenarios to ensure it fulfills its intended purpose safely and effectively.
Simulation-based testing is a prevalent validation technique that allows developers to observe
how robots behave under various conditions that mimic real-life interactions [30].
We can comprehensively assess a robot’s safety and functionality by integrating formal veri-
fication with simulation-based validation. This combined approach addresses the limitations
inherent in using either method alone, leading to more robust and trustworthy robotic systems.
For instance, [31] methods have been demonstrated in the case study in how formal methods,
simulation testing, and testing in real scenarios all three methods can be applied to check the
res ult of one another, increasing the level of certainty in the results of the verification and
validation process.
2.6
Conclusion
From the literature review, several critical gaps exist in the current state of the safety design of
collaborative robots and safer human-robot interactions. The safety standards, hazard identifi-
cation methods, and verification techniques for collaborative systems show significant progress.
Still, they are the general requirements and do not comprehensively describe methodologies and
solutions for the human-robot interaction scenario where collaborative robots are deployed. Fur-
ther, the experiments and case studies mainly focus on robot manipulators, and few focus on
autonomous mobile vehicles.
Power and force-limiting safety measures are crucial for collaborative robots as there might
be intended physical contact between humans and robots during interaction. Further, it is
necessary to eliminate the risk of injury due to unintended contact. The proximity, velocity
of a robot, the relative velocity of a human and a robot, and the payload must be measured
to ensure that the force generated does not exceed the biomechanical thresholds for different
human body parts. However, the challenges persist due to the dynamics of the human body.
This project will use a simulated scenario to calculate the force with varying speeds and payloads
of the robot. This will validate the limiting robot’s safety design and highlight the scenario-
specific validating process to get the measurements of threshold values of speed and payload to
achieve the force limitation for the safer deployment of the robot for a particular human-robot
interaction.
Most existing studies concentrate on static hazard identification and risk assessment approaches
that assume idealized human behavior. This oversight limits the applicability of these methods
in real-world settings, where deviations in human actions can introduce unforeseen risks. The
robotic system’s complexity, where several systems integrate together to enrich the performance,
effectiveness, and functional safety, and the elimination of hazards listed in standards do not
fully make the system reliable. The risk of emergent dangers that can be observed only during

3.1
Power and force Limiting
ISO 10218 formalizes collaborative robots’ safety requirements in different operation modes.
It is complemented by the technical specification ISO/TS 15066:2016, which specifies the safe
impact scenarios between humans and robots. Physical contact between humans and robots is
allowed when a robot moves within collaborative power and force limitations. Pressure depends
on the contact area and can vary significantly, whereas force directly measures the impact energy
that could cause injury so that the focus will be on force calculation. Force is a critical factor in
assessing safety during a collision, so we delve more into it to ensure optimal robot performance
with minimal adverse impact on the human at the time of unavoidable collisions[32].
The collaborative method of pressure and force limiting involves two types of contacts:
• Quasi-static contact in which humans can’t retract from a robot, such as crushing or
clamping. In such a situation, the robotic system will continuously apply force or pressure
on the operator’s body part until the robotic parts, like an arm, are removed. Even with
a small force, the pain threshold will be reached. Such a scenario becomes hazardous,
and the degree of hazard depends on the robotic system’s kinematics configuration, the
contact area’s size, and the operator body part that comes in contact [3].
• Transient contact describes a situation where a moving robotic system hits an operator’s
body part with the possibility to retract or recoil without being trapped or clamped
between the robotic system and the operator’s body part. Such short-duration impact
is characterized as a dumper-spring system [3]. As per Table A.2 of ISO/TS 15066, the
transient force is allowed to be twice as high as the quasi-contact force.

Unsafe human behavior in HRC
We have chosen simulated-based testing to explore complex elements like human behavior dur-
ing the interaction of a human and a robot in complex HRC. In addition, testing-based methods
allow for the identification of hazards by exposing the system to various testing conditions and
observing the system’s response. Real testing is more realistic but limited by cost, time, and
availability of physical prototypes, so we have chosen simulating-based testing as a tool for
hazard analysis. The hazard analysis problem is framed as a search problem. The violation of
the safety specification is explored for the simulated HRC system, and if it exists, the specific
state where these violations occur is identified.
The problem is to find at least one human behavior that, when performed in interaction with
the robot in a simulated environment, causes the system to reach an unsafe state. Such human
behavior is hazardous/unsafe.
To find the unsafe state, we need some user-defined safety
specifications in relation to the simulated model.

4.2
Agent-based Simulation
The goal is to identify if a system can reach an unsafe state among the set of reachable states.
In complex 3D-simulation models of HRC systems, the search space is too vast, which makes it
difficult to compute all reachable states. Calculating all the reachable states is impractical, so a
sampling-based approach is taken to discover the unsafe states. Each simulation run generates a
trajectory through the system’s state space, and every state lying on this trajectory is checked
for any violation in the state specification; this way, we can identify the simulation run in
which the system enters an unsafe state. The agent-based approach is adopted to create a
simulation scenario that meaningfully depicts the HRC system [27]. One or multiple agents
interact with a simulation model and create varying simulation conditions that cause the system
to respond in varied ways. Thus, we get different state space trajectories. The agent-based
approach is enabled where the simulation model is partitioned into two sub-models: the robot
system(System Under Test) and the virtual human model (agent). Since the robot is assumed
to respond deterministically to the given agent’s behavior, the agent’s behavior is input to the
system, and the robot’s behavior is the emergent behavior that arises within the simulation as
a consequence of the agent’s behavior and the robot’s programming.

4.9
Implementation of Optimization methods
This project uses two optimization methods, random sampling and hill-climbing. The candidate
human behavior is generated using the feasible action sequences from the DFA implementation
and randomly generated velocity and position.
Random velocities between 0 and 1.6 m/s
and a random item position within a small offset around the center are generated. This adds
variability to the behavior and influences the cumulative risk score. The MCTS algorithm
evaluates the cumulative risk score for that candidate’s behavior. Then, the optimization is
done:
1. Random Sampling: This method selects many behaviors at random. The method can
identify candidates that inherently lead to high-risk scores by evaluating many random
samples. Thus, it explores human behavior globally and in a non-directed manner.
2. Hill Climbing: This is one of the simplest optimization algorithms in artificial intelligence.
It belongs to the category of local search algorithms and finds the solution by making
incremental improvements. This method starts with an initial random feasible sequence
and iteratively mutates it. The mutations include insertion, deletion, substitution, or
transposition of certain tokens (specifically ’t’ and ’w’) in the sequence. After each mu-
tation, the candidate is re-evaluated. If the mutation leads to a higher risk score, the
algorithm accepts the new behavior as the current state and continues the search. This
process aims to refine the candidate toward a local optimum where the risk is maximized.
Because the behavior space is high-dimensional, exploring every combination would be infeasi-
ble, so optimization is used to find the most hazardous scenarios efficiently. Further, it helps
to identify behaviors that lead to higher risk when paired with certain velocities and positions.
The hill climbing method climbs towards the more hazardous behavior. This refines the final
candidates and exhibits the highest cumulative risk, which is crucial for hazard analysis.
4.10
Results and Discussion
Implementing the Risk Guided MCTS algorithm efficiently evaluates the risk score for the
candidate hazard behavior(actions, velocity, position). In the simulated scenario, safety flaws
can be deliberately introduced into the simulated scenario to create hazards. The risk-guided
search can be deployed to find the simulation runs leading to uncovering these hazards. Suppose
the particular feasible sequence exhibits the dangers intentionally introduced to the system. In
that case, it validates that the feasible sequence is the hazardous sequence at the existence of the
parameters(velocity, position). This means the unsafe state is reached, and the threshold risk is
exceeded. However, in this project, a hazardous state is not visually exhibited in the simulation.
Due to some limitations in the human model used by Webot and technical complications linked
with the model, the full implementation of the simulated scenario was not achieved. Thus,
verifying the results obtained from the Risk Guided MCTS search algorithm is not completed.
MCTS algorithm evaluates the risk score for the candidate sequences generated from the Hill
Climbing and Random Sampling. The top 10 hazardous behaviors are shown in the bar charts
10 and 11. The random sampling method shows that sequences with prolonged waiting time or
unnecessary transfer of humans may lead to an unsafe state. This may be due to the prolonged
waiting with fast approaches of the agent/human. It may also be due to a quick human approach

the cost of the computation budget and by using a more powerful modeling formalism.

and does not consider the probabilities of future events. Although the risk metric is built under
the safety specification for unsafe states, it may not actually indicate an actual risk for the
simulated situation. It might be misguiding the search.

reasoning, and it does not claim to be the best alternative method. Deploying this methodology

5
Challenges and Future work
Simulation has been the most challenging part of this thesis. Building the prototype of the
available robot model of the Webot simulator added difficulties in satisfying the need for collision
force detection. However, the hindrance was overcome by continuously optimizing the model
using different nodes in the simulator. Further, in the exploration of hazardous behavior, the
human model does not support some of the movements like bending the upper body, sitting
down, or sitting up, due to which all the required action sequence was not simulated, leading
to the incomplete verification process. In the future, other human models that are provided by
Webot simulators or other simulators can be experimented with to see the required movements.
Due to time constraints, the different models were not explored. In some of the experiments,
the graphical user interface allows the atomic actions of humans walking, sitting, standing,
and bending. This can be parameterized with respect to specific properties like path or goal
coordinates. This can be an alternative to having simplified human models since the focus is
on concept. By using the detailed human model, a spatial safety map can be created, which
can help to determine the critical collision points and the required safety distances.
Another future work can be the integration of methodologies like distance-based prioritiza-
tion to reduce the search space of feasible sequences further; such a method can help identify
sequences that are in close proximity to the nominal sequence. This way, we can check the se-
quences that do not deviate far from the nominal sequence and have chances of being missed in
actual deployment. Different optimization algorithms like genetic and evolutionary algorithms,
simulated annealing, and particle swarm optimization [35] can be used, and the results can be
compared to get more effective hazard identification methods.
This thesis has demonstrated the developed methods in the context of Human-Robot Collabora-
tion that can be applied to industries like picking and placing items in warehouses. In addition,
the standards and regulations for HRC systems are well defined, though some challenges and
gaps still persist. This method can also be transferred to other industry domains like service,
delivery, and healthcare. Similar to our scenario, these industries have clearly defined tasks
and structured environments. Mobile robots outside the industrial domain, such as assistance
or delivery robots, have unstructured environments with a larger range of events and environ-
mental conditions. This makes it difficult to define the action spaces and simulation scenarios.
So, simulation-based methods can be applied to industries with a known set of events and
operators.
6
Conclusion
This thesis has presented test-based methods, especially simulation-based testing, for industry-
specific case scenarios in human-robot collaboration. It tests the safety specifications provided
by the standards for the deployment of AMR in the industry. It also shows ways to determine

When reflecting back on the research questions, the methodologies to identify the risk due
