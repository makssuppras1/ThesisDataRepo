Abstract
The objective of the present thesis is a numerical modelling of large-scale bed forms
in shallow water sea. For a given bed perturbation a non-linear numerical model is
used to calculate the response in the ﬂow and sediment transport ﬁeld. The analysis
is reduced to a linear stability analysis by assuming the amplitude of the perturbation
small compared to the water depth.
Three models are used to perform this study.
The ﬁrst one gives an estimate of the parameters governing the bed form growth. The
second model has been created to reproduce uniform ﬂow conditions and the third
one has been developed to simulate the converging and diverging ﬂow eﬀects. For the
uniform ﬂow the linear stability analysis gives good estimates of the length scale of
the bed forms in shallow water sea. The results obtained in the converging diverging
ﬂow case may be inﬂuenced by numerical errors. The ﬁndings show that the divergent
type of ﬂow is the one generating the largest ampliﬁcation for most of the bed form
lengths. In all the types of ﬂow conditions considered in this study the perturbation
which develops faster is aligned with the ﬂow direction and shows a ratio between the
bed form lengths around 3.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
The object
The object of the present thesis is a linear stability analysis of large scale sand bed
forms in shallow water sea.
It is often observed that the sea bed is never uniform
but presents always deformations from the ﬂat bed condition.
These irregularities,
vary according to the surrounding ﬂow conditions. They can form separately or repeat
themselves for several distances. They can have small dimensions like ripples or extend
for meters or even kilometres forming sand banks whose wave lengths reaches 100
times the undisturbed water depth. The ﬁeld observations lead to several questions
about why these bed forms appear at some places instead of others, why in some cases
they show clear regularities in the pattern and not in others, what are the mechanism
governing their growth and development and what is the temporal scale for that. The
variety and complexity of the scenarios in nature often make it hard to answer all
these questions. This subject has been studied for many years and it is still an object
of interest. The occurrence of sand bed forms has been interpreted by many authors
as a morphodynamic instability of a horizontally ﬂat bed in an oﬀshore environment,
subject to speciﬁc types of ﬂow and sediment transport conditions (Roos et al. (2007)).
The evolution of these bed forms may be explained as a free instability of a dynamic
morphological system in which the ﬂow and the sea-bottom interact. It is from this
mutual interaction that small instabilities in the form of perturbations are generated
and than develop in larger bed features.
Engineering applications
The present work focuses primarily on large scale-bed
forms. The research conducted on these bed features is not only motivated by academic
purposes but it also has high practical relevance in many engineering ﬁelds. For exam-
ple the presence of bed forms may lead to navigation problems in shallow water sea. In
the oﬀshore industry the growth and development of the sea bed assumes importance in
many situations. Pipelines on a bed are directly exposed to the morphological changes
of the sea bottom. In those areas where high bed activity can be observed, pipelines
are often buried to prevent any possible damage. A better knowledge on the bed form

understanding on the mechanism governing the growth and development of bed forms in
shallow water sea. The focus is particularly on large scale bed features whose dimensions
are on the order of kilometres. The analysis conducted here relies on a linear stability
analysis of a small perturbation applied to a ﬂat sea bed. The perturbation growth
is studied assuming ﬁxed ﬂow conditions and sediment proprieties.

1.2. BASIC CONCEPTS OF LARGE-SCALE BED FORM GROWTH
5
1.2
Basic concepts of large-scale bed form growth
As it is seen in the previous section the parameters governing the growth and migration
of bed forms can be several. Treating this problem can therefore be complex. In studies
like this, in order to keep the problem as simple as possible, some simpliﬁcations are
needed. Usually the physical phenomena is reduced to an idealized mathematical model
that, despite the approximations used, can describe the basic mechanism and the role
of the various parameters involved in the system with a good level of accuracy.
1.2.1
General approach
The idea behind the mechanism generating the bed form is that the sea bed is never
ﬂat but always presents some irregularities. A perturbation in the bed, initially small
disturbs the ﬂow ﬁeld in a way that vertical ﬂow is created. This ﬂow will move sedi-
ment from trough to the crest amplifying the bed perturbation (Gerkema (2000)). The
opposite eﬀect induced by the force of gravity will tend to move the sediment down-
ward. It is by the interaction of these two mechanism that bed form grows or decays.
One way to proceed in this analysis is ﬁrst to consider a ﬂat bed under steady and
uniform ﬂow. Under these conditions the ﬂow, the sediment transport and the bed are
in equilibrium. A small perturbation is then applied to the bed. The response induced
by the bed perturbation to the ﬂow and sediment transport ﬁeld are investigated. In
particular it is the perturbed sediment transport ﬁeld which will determine whether the
given perturbation will amplify or decay. The procedure just described above is often
called stability analysis. Usually the stability analysis is supposed to be linear meaning
that the perturbation is small enough so that only linear terms prevail. By using this
method the bed form growth rate can be determined. In section 2.3 the linear stability
analysis is explained in detail.
Among the several variables governing the bed form growth and development, of par-
ticular importance is the dependence of the bed form changes on the length scale of
the bed perturbation. Those bed forms with the largest growth rate are expected to
increase and expand faster and predominate over the others. The interest is then to
ﬁnd those wavelengths for which the rate of bed level change is maximum as it is often
assumed that these bed forms are, at least qualitatively, the ones determining the fea-
tures of the ﬁnal development of the bed topography (Deigaard et al. (1999)).
1.2.2
Morphological modelling
Once the linear stability analysis has been performed, the research continues with the
morphological modelling. In the present work this step has not been reached, but in this
section the general approach is explained, in order to give a better understanding of the
advantages and limitations of the linear stability analysis. As outlined by Østergaard
Madsen (2002), the linear stability analysis is a powerful tool in bed form modelling
==PAGE:24==

because it gives the parameters that governs the problem and their order of magnitude.
The output of the linear stability analysis should predict the initial migration speed,
growth rate and wave length scale of a given bed perturbation. However the limitation
of this approach is that no information is provided after the initial time step. It is not
possible, with this method, to predict the ﬁnal equilibrium conditions or the time-scale
to reach them. The presence of non linear terms that are neglected in the linear study,
inﬂuences the bed form growth and development to the point that in some cases the
wavelength scale and growth rate of the initial bed form are not necessarily the same
at the ﬁnal stage. The use of the morphological model should obviate the limitations
of the linear stability analysis and give a solution to the ﬁnal equilibrium conditions.
1.2.3
Review
The basic hydrodynamics theory refers to the work of Fredsøe (2008) and Montefusco,
Luigi (2005). An extensive work has been carried out by Fredsøe (1992) on the growth
and generation of bed dunes. The basic concept governing the development of a one
dimensional bed form is explained here.
Without any previous knowledge on linear stability analysis, the ﬁrst approach was
made by following the procedure described by Deigaard et al. (1999) that studied the
development of a longshore bar for a long straight coast.
An important source of information for the present thesis was the study conducted
by Østergaard Madsen (2002). In his study Madsen considers the ﬂow and sediment
transport over a non-uniform bed under steady current and wave-current conditions.
The relevant part for the present thesis work was the linear stability analysis applied
to the steady current case. Some of the results were tried to be compared with the
ﬁndings on the present project.
In their studies Dyer and Huntley (1999) proposes a classiﬁcation of sand banks and
ridges in terms of their location and morphology. They studied how the diﬀerent coastal
constriction aﬀect the bed topography. In Fig.1.2 a classiﬁcation of the sand banks
associated with the ﬂow conditions at the mouth of the river estuary are presented. The
study underlines how some eﬀects, like tidal ﬂow the Coriolis force and the presence of
the nearby coast aﬀect the orientation of the bed forms.
In their studies Gerkema (2000) and Hulscher (1996) a linear stability analysis was
applied to study the dynamic of bed forms under tidal currents.

1.3. OBJECTIVE AND METHODOLOGY
7
1.3
Objective and methodology
The linear stability analysis conducted in the present study is based on the result of
numerical simulations. Three diﬀerent models were developed by the author. They
will be referred in the following of this paper work as: single bed form model, multiple
bed form model and converging-diverging ﬂow model. The ﬁrst model created in the
beginning stage of the project work aimed to simulate the growth eﬀect on a single
bed perturbation under uniform ﬂow conditions. This ﬁrst bed form representation
has been mentioned because, despite some wrong assumptions adopted, it created the
basic background knowledge for the next steps. The second model, which is a corrected
version of the ﬁrst one, consists of a series of bed forms with peaks and depressions
under uniform ﬂow conditions. It was possible to obtain the ﬁrst reliable results with
this model. The last model, which represents the most relevant work in the present
study, consists on a single bed form under diﬀerent type of ﬂow conditions. Each section
of the present paper work considers the three models described above.
In chapter 2 an explanation regarding the growth and development of bed forms is
given. The linear stability analysis applied to large scale bed forms is also explained
in this chapter.
A short review on the equations governing the ﬂow and the sedi-
ment transport is presented in chapter 3. The description of the set up of the three
diﬀerent models and a sensitivity analysis are discussed in chapter 4 and chapter 5.
A dimensional analysis section has been introduced on chapter 6 in order to explain
the procedure used to make the parameters governing the bed form growth rate non
dimensional. The results are presented, explained and commented in chapter 7.

1.3.1
Reference system and terminology
The reference system used in this project refers to a Cartesian coordinates system
(x, y, z) with origin at the center of the domain and with the x axis parallel to the ﬂow
direction and the y perpendicular to it. The z coordinate is oriented upwards with
the origin on the bed. As shown in Fig.1.3, a generic perturbation can be expressed
as a sinusoidal function with peaks or crests, that are the maximum values of the
perturbation and depressions or troughs which indicate the negative bed behaviour
respect to the ﬂat bed case. When a perturbation is introduced to the bed, the variation
of the bed from the ﬂat condition is expressed as h(x). The sinusoidal perturbation is
characterized by the highest point at the crest which deﬁnes the amplitude A of the
perturbation. Sinusoidal functions are usually used in this kind of studies because of

Linear stability analysis
In this section the linear stability analysis applied to a sandy bed is presented. First
an erodible ﬂat bed under steady ﬂow conditions is considered. The ﬂow ﬁeld and
the sediment transport ﬁeld are in equilibrium. When a inﬁnitesimal perturbation is
applied to the ﬂat plane bed see Fig. 2.3, the ﬂow and the sediment transport ﬁelds
will be aﬀected by this disturbance. The system can then amplify the perturbation or
reduce it. In the ﬁrst case the system is under unstable conditions meaning that the
small perturbation will grow and develop, at least in theory, in bigger bed forms; in the
second case the system is under stable conditions and the perturbation will stay the
same or decay until the initial ﬂat bed conditions are restored.

and in the sediment transport ﬁeld are small compared to the ﬂat bed case. In general
a generic perturbed quantity K, can be expressed as a Taylor expansion series of the
unperturbed case:

The sediment transport module of MIKE21 is based on the hydrodynamic conditions.
In the ﬁrst model runs the boundary conditions for the sediment were set as equilibrium

accurate representation of the sediment transport ﬁeld can be obtained with the zero
gradient condition at the boundaries. With the equilibrium condition the suspended
sediment starts to be transported from the inlet boundary through the entire domain.
When the zero gradient conditions are set, the suspended sediment starts moving in
each point of the domain according to the local ﬂow conditions.

diﬀerent bed friction in the ﬂow direction which, in turn, produces a positive sediment
transport gradient, ∂q

Dimensional analysis
In the following section the main parameter governing the ﬂow and sediment transport
ﬁeld are described. The rate of bed level change is then made dimensionless.
In order to measure a physical quantity, a relationship between other quantities
called units of measure is needed. All the units of measure are derived from the funda-
mental dimensions (such as length, time, mass). Diﬀerent units of measure can express
the same physical quantity keeping the physical dimensions unchanged. Converting
from one dimensional unit to another can be complex. Dimensional analysis is a pow-
erful technique that allows the description of a physical law in terms of dimensionless
quantities. The advantage of this procedure is that complex physical problems, like the
one described here can be reduced to the simplest: the variables governing the physi-
cal law can be reduced so that the physical phenomena can be expressed only by the
dominating parameters. There is no speciﬁc rule on how to determine the governing
variables but each case should be studied separately. Dimensional analysis applies well
in ﬂuid dynamics where the ﬂow depends on many quantities. Dimensional analysis
ﬁnds its basis in the Buckingham’s π -theorem which will be shortly described here.
Any physical quantity Q0 can be expressed by a set of independent quantities
q1, ..., qn
Q0 = f(q1, ..., qn)
(6.1)
The independent variables q1, ..., qn can be expressed in terms of a dimensionally inde-
pendent subset Q1, ..., Qk k ≤n. The remaining quantities Qk+1, ..., Qn are expressed
also in terms of the dimensions Q1, ..., Qk. The Buckingham’s π -theorem states that
Eq.6.1 can be expressed in the dimensionless form of the n −k remaining independent
variables Π1, ..., Πn
Π0 = f(Π1, ..., Πn)
(6.2)

The most controversial point is to determine the cause of maximum growth am-
pliﬁcations for small angles amplitudes. From a physical point of view it is hard to
believe that whenever θ is slightly changed from zero the growth rate is ampliﬁed and
reaches the peak of the curve. When θ = 0 or θ = 1, the ﬂow conditions are almost the
same. This fact has been proven by checking the eﬀects of the bed form corresponding
to the dimensionless parameters kxD = 0.02, kyD = 0.06. The ﬂow velocities induced
by the bed form when θ = 0 and θ = 0.5 were compared. The diﬀerence between
the ﬂow velocities in the two cases is on the order of 10−06 m/s which is a very small
value. The reason of peaks in the stability curves for small angles can be several. The
growth ampliﬁcation might be induced by numerical errors. The mesh resolution that
has been used to perform these tests might not be accurate enough. Another reason
could be that for such small angle variation the amplitude of the perturbation does not
allow a precise analysis. The most plausible answer can be found in the linear analysis
conducted in section 5.1. The presence of a diverging or converging angle may give rise
to non linear terms in the numerical model.

Chapter 8
Conclusions
In the present thesis stability on large-scale bed forms has been studied by use of a
numerical modelling system. The study of this natural phenomenon is not driven only
by academic purposes but has many practical engineering implications. The present
work was based on linear stability assumptions. The large scale bed form has been
chosen small enough so that the response in the ﬂow ﬁeld and sediment transport
ﬁeld were linear. The advantage of using this approach is that the calculations were
performed by a non-linear numerical model which was behaving as it was linear. The
purpose of this analysis was to ﬁnd under which conditions the largest ampliﬁcations
of the bed form are obtained. The parameters governing the perturbation growth are
many but the analysis can be simpliﬁed by ﬁxing the sediment and ﬂow conditions so
that the only variables changing are the bed form wavelengths. The output of the linear
stability analysis is the initial growth rate of the small perturbation. The stability curves
obtained in this analysis should give the dimensions of that bed form which generates
the fastest growth rate. The ﬁndings of this analysis can then be set as input parameters
to use in the morphological model. In the present project work three diﬀerent models
were created. Most part of this work was based on developing the third model, the
converging-diverging ﬂow model which has the advantage of reproducing diﬀerent ﬂow
conditions that can be observed in the ﬁeld. By varying the ﬂow pattern with an angle
θ, divergent or convergent currents can be obtained. The ﬁrst model set up was not as
accurate as the other two models used in the present work. However some qualitative
information about the bed form behaviour could be observed. The ampliﬁcation of
the bed perturbation is mainly driven by the suspended sediment transport. The most
unstable situation is generally found when the largest bed form dimension is aligned
with the ﬂow.
From the second model the ﬁrst stability curve has been obtained.
The same results have been obtained with the third model which has been used to
conduct most of the investigations. From the linear stability analysis conducted when
θ = 0 it could be concluded that the most unstable situation is obtained roughly for
(kxD,kyD)=(0.02,0.06). The results obtained in the converging diverging case show
maximum growth ampliﬁcations when the diverging angle θ is close to zero. The linear
assumptions in this case may be not completely fulﬁlled and the results can be aﬀected
71
==PAGE:90==

by the presence of non-linear terms. The restricted period available to conduct this
research and the time requested to perform the numerical simulations did not allow
to go further in this investigation. However, the ﬁndings do show some interesting
information:
• Qualitatively it can be said that the converging diverging eﬀects play an important
role in the bed form growth. it can be noted that there is a certain tendency to
have higher growth rates under diverging ﬂow conditions. The reason could be
that in the diverging ﬂow the velocities decrease when approaching the bed form
crest inducing a positive net growth rate.
• When the diverging angle θ is changed from 0 the stability curves seem to follow
diﬀerent behaviours. The maximum growth rates for the diverging and converging
ﬂow show a maximum for diﬀerent bed form wavelengths.
• The growth rate obtained by averaging the results of the converging and diverging
ﬂow model aims to reproduce tidal ﬂow patterns. The fact that the bed form
ampliﬁcation under these conditions diﬀers from the case when θ = 0, suggests
that tidal currents play a role in the bed form development.
8.1
Recommendations for further work
The results obtained in this project work suggest a deeper investigation when a con-
verging or diverging type of ﬂow is considered. The following aspect should be taken
into account
• The linearity behaviour of the model should be analysed again when the angle θ
is diﬀerent from zero. This could be one of the reasons of the results obtained in
the present project work.
• It would be interesting to investigate the mesh resolution in the converging-
diverging ﬂow model and see how this aﬀect the results.
• For the uniform ﬂow conditions the choice of periodic boundary conditions in the
numerical model would be useful.
There are several possible extensions to the present work. One is to conclude the
study started here and ﬁnd the stability curves for diﬀerent kxD, kyD, θ combinations
that generates the maximum growth rates. As mentioned by ( Østergaard Madsen,
2002) bed resistance plays a determinant role in the bed form growth. By changing the
Chezy number the stability curves show diﬀerent behaviour. The same can be said when
the eﬀects of secondary ﬂow, like helical motion are considered. It seems that these two
eﬀects have a decisive inﬂuence on the wavelength of the most unstable perturbation.
It could be interesting to investigate these eﬀects of when converging-diverging ﬂow
conditions are applied.

8.1. RECOMMENDATIONS FOR FURTHER WORK

The model can then be implemented by considering other factors. As mentioned by
many authors and many ﬁeld evidences the orientation and development of bed forms
are inﬂuenced by waves, the Coriolis force and by tidal currents (Hulscher (1996),
(Gerkema, 2000),(Dyer and Huntley, 1999)). Again, it would be interesting to imple-
ment the research conducted here with these eﬀects. From an engineering point of view
it is also interesting to investigate the growth rate eﬀect when structures are applied to
the bed form. By changing the boundary conditions in the numerical model it is also
possible to reproduce the eﬀect of the land on the bed form growth. This conditions
could give further insight to the phenomena governing the presence of large-scale bed
forms near coasts and river estuaries.
