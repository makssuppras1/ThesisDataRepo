Abstract 
 
Antimicrobial resistant bacteria is major health threat according to the World Health Organization. 
Wastewater treatment plants have been suggested to be hotspots for the transfer of antimicrobial 
resistance genes to pathogenic bacteria. Horizontal gene transfer is mechanism in which bacteria 
can transfer mobile genetic elements among each other. One of these mechanisms, plasmid-
mediated conjugation, is investigated in this study. The aim is to figure out to which degree WWTP 
microbial community members from activated sludge is able to act as intermediate hosts. Solid filter 
mating assays were performed to maximize the cell-to-cell contact and hence create an 
environment for the bacteria to allow conjugation to occur. A dual-labelling technique was utilized 
to track if the IncP-1 plasmids, pKJK5 and RP4, have been transferred. In the first filter mating assay 
a known Escherichia coli (E. coli) donor was used chromosomally-tagged with red fluorescence and 
containing a plasmid tagged with green fluoresence. In this way, transfer to WWTP microbial 
community members could be observed as green fluorescent microcolonies. The high-througput 
method  fluorescence activated cell sorting could in a culture-independent way psysically isolate the 
green fluorescent transconjugants. The novelty of this research was to find out wheter the 
transconjugant were able to further transfer the plasmids to two medically relevant model 
pathogens, E. coli and Enterobacter cloacae (E. cloacae). The study showed that the WWTP microbial 
community members that have taken up the plasmid were able to further transfer it to the model 
pathogens. This implies that WWTP microbial community members from activated sludge can act 
as intermediate hosts and that WWTP is hotspot for dissemination of antimicrobial resistent genes. 
Furthermore, 16S amplicon sequencing was performed to identify the microbial community 
composition in the samples. The class Gammeproteobacteria belonging to the phylum 
Proteobacteria is bar far the dominating phylum across all samples. The top five genera represented 
across all transconjugant pools contain pathogenic species which emphasizes the risk that transfer 
of ARGs to pathogens is a possibility. Sequence analysis of the transconjugant sample that has been 
regrown contained to families that are gram-positives which means that gram-positive bacteria are 
able to replicate and maintain the IncP-1 plasmids pKJK5 and RP4.

Introduction 
 
According to the World Health Organization (WHO), uptake of antimicrobial resistance genes (ARGs) 
by human pathogens is one of the major global health threads humanity is facing (WHO, 2014). 
Hundreds of thousands of deaths are caused due to antibiotic resistance (Review et al., 2014). 
Antibiotics and the proteins protecting against them have excisted millions or perhaps billions years  
(Baltz, 2008). Antibiotic resistance appears to be due to Darwinian selection and permits niche 
exploitation (Stokes and Gillings, 2011). Microorganisms in soil have been found to produce 
antibiotics and therefore the idea of inhibiting growth of competitors has been suggested as a 
primary ecological role (Martínez, 2008).  
 
Horizontal gene transfer (HGT) is a process in which organisms are able to transfer MGEs in natural 
environmental bacterial populations. The mechanisms of HGT have long been recognized and 
involve three mechanisms; transformation, transduction and conjugation  (Lederberg and Tatum, 
1946; Zinder and Lederberg, 1952). Transformation is the uptake of free exogenous DNA directly 
from the environment. Transduction is packaging of DNA into bacteriophages during replication  
accidently happening by mistake. Conjugation is a mechanism which involves cell-to-cell contact. 
The cell-to-cell contact is made through a pilus which is the mating-pair formation apparatus. Once 
the plasmid is in the recipient cell the plasmid can autonomously replicate itself (Sørensen et al., 
2005; Thomas and Nielsen, 2005). This study investigates plasmid-mediated conjugation which is 
assumed to be the main mechanism bacterial populations adapt to local environmental changes 
(Sørensen et al., 2005; Grohmann, 2011; Heuer et al., 2011). Several advantages of plasmids have 
been suggested including genetic variation, increased survival probability and metabolic advantages 
(Sørensen et al., 2005). Plasmids can contain antimicrobial resistance genes (ARGs) and thus is a 
dissemination route between microorganisms.  
 
The two plasmids, pKJK5 (IncP-1𝜀) and RP4 (IncP-1𝛼), belonging to the incompability group IncP, are 
conjugative plasmids, which means that they are able to facilitate their own transfer. RSF1010 
belonging to the incompability group IncQ and is a mobilizable plasmid, which means transfer needs 
to be facilitated by a co-residing conjugative plasmid or a transposon (Sørensen et al., 2005). All 
three plasmids are broad host range (BHR) plasmids and a host range is definded by the 
phylogenetic diversity of the host that the plasmid can be transferred and maintianed in (Klümper 
et al., 2015). The host range has an influence on the ability to spread and persist in bacterial 
communities (Bahl et al., 2009). Plasmid stability systems have evolved to maintain the transferred 
plasmids (e.g. post-segregational cell killing, stable partitioning (par) systemEX, multimer-resolution 
systems (mrs)). Maintenance of plasmids is poorly understood and plasmid loss can be due to the 
metabolic burden and thus losing the plasmid will provide a growth advantage (Sørensen et al., 
2005).

Wastewater treatment plants (WWTPs) are anthropogenic ecosystems that receive sewage from 
households, agriculture, industry, and hospitals with the risk to contain antimicrobial resistant 
bacteria (ARB) (Basin et al., 2016). WWTPs have been suggested to be hotspots for the exchange of 
mobile genetic elements (MGEs) in environments with high cell densities, where gene transfer 
events are likely because of increased likelihood for cell-to-cell contact (Rizzo et al., 2013).  
 
To investigate plasmid-mediated conjugation a permissiveness assay has been developed (Musovic 
et al., 2010). Permissiveness is a way to determine the fate of a plasmid and is defined as “the 
fraction of a community that is able to receive and maintain a target exogenous plasmid” (Musovic 
et al., 2010; Klümper et al., 2014). The recipient community that takes up the plasmid is the 
transconjugant pool.  
 
Solid surface filter mating assays were chosen to maximize the cell-to-cell contact and thus increase 
the probability of plasmid-mediated gene transfer. The experimental approach is divided into two 
steps. The first step involves a permissiveness assay to obtain a transconjugal pool of WWTP 
bacteria via solid surface filter matings and is also referred to as primary transfer. The second step 
involves transfer of permissive plasmids from the transconjugant pool to two model pathogens 
Escherichia coli (E. coli) and Enterobacter cloacae (E. cloacae) and is also referred to as secondary 
transfer. They both inhabit the human gastrointestinal tract, thus are part of the WWTP microbial 
commmunity and are both medically relevant. These strains were chosen because both species have 
been found as multidrug (antibiotic) resistant bacteria (Moreira et al., 2009). The secondary transfer 
has not been investigated previous and it is crucial to know of WWTP microbial communities can 
transfer ARGs on to pathogenic bacteria.  
 
Fluorescently tagged plasmids provide an identifiable phenotype that permit visualization and 
quantification of conjugation events by epifluorescence microscopy. Transconjugant cells will then 
be physically separated through fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS). This is a high-throughput 
method that can isolate the green fluorescent transconjugants in a culture-independent manner. 
The use of fluorescence reporter-genes (Figure 1) and FACS makes it possible to trace the 
transferred plasmids.

This study aims to understand the fate of plasmids carrying ARGs by taking advantage of reporter- 
gene systems combined with FACS and subsequently identify isolated transconjugants by 16S 
amplicon sequencing. Furthermore, evaluation of plamid transfer frequency will be quantified by 
epifluorescence microscopy. Two sequential assays, primary and secondary transfer, will be 
performed to investigate up to what extent WWTP microbial community members can act as 
intermediate hosts for two IncP plasmids (pKJK5 and RP4) and one IncQ plasmid (RSF1010). It is 
crucial to understand if WWTP microbial community members can transfer ARGs to pathogenic 
bacteria. It is important to know if WWTPs functions ARG dissemination routes into the 
environment and if that is the case we need to reassess how to clean our water in WWTPs. 
 
 
Materials and Methods 
 
Sample collection and recipient community preparation 
 
WWTP samples from the activated sludge basins were collected in September 2018 from two 
Swedish WWTPs in Ellinge and Klagshamn. The two WWTP microbial communities were spun down 
in an ultracentrifuge and the bacterial pellets were resuspended in sterile 0.9% NaCl solution. 
Sonication was performed to get rid of any cell aggregates according to Klümper et al., 2014. 
Quantification of the cell densities were performed under a microscope using a Thoma counting 
chamber to adjust cell densities to 3.38 ∙10)cells/ml. The two microbial communities, E2 (from 
Ellinge) and K2 (from Klagshamn) were used as recipients in the solid surface filter mating assay for 
the primary transfer.

Primary transfer via solid surface filter mating 
 
The three donor strains (Table 1) and the WWTP recipient communities, E2 and K2, were mixed at 
a 1:1 donor to recipient ratio and filtered onto the 0.2 𝜇𝑚 Cyclopore membranes (Figure 1). Filters 
were subsequently placed on plates containing synthetic wasterwater medium, all samples carried 
out in quadruplicates. All filters were incubated 48 hours at 37oC (filters facing up to ensure that 
they do not fall off). The plates were subsequently stored at 4oC for 48 hours to let the fluorescence 
mature (GFP and mCherry). Successful plasmid transfer events were detected by epifluorescence 
stereomicroscopy. Donor strains were grown in duplicates to verify mCherry fluorescence. 
 
 
Visualization and quantification of transfer events 
 
Transfer frequencies are quantified by counting transconjugal microcolonies by using the program 
Image Pro Plus 7.1 Transconjugants per picture is the average amount of cells quantified by the 
automated image analysis program. Fluoresecence objects larger than 4𝜇𝑚. are counted and the 
community’s transfer frequency was calculated according to equation 1 as previously described 
(Musovic et al., 2010). Visualization of conjugation events was performed with a Leica MZ16 FA 
epifluoresence stereomicroscope. Fifteen GFP epifluorescence pictures were taken of each donor-
recipient combination. Reference mCherry epifluorescence pictures were taken for all three donor 
strains.

FACS, cell lysis, amplification and sequencing 
 
Flow cytometric detection of cells were carried out using a FACSAria IIIu instrument (Becton 
Dickinson Biosciences, San Jose, CA, USA). Cells from two filters per mating combination were 
detached from the filter and resuspended in 0.9% NaCl solution. Triple gating was carried out 
according to (Klümper et al., 2015) to ensure that particles smaller than bacteria are excluded, 
another gate is sorting for green fluorescent events and the last gate excludes all red fluorescent 
events. For all samples, 10,000 gfp-expressing cells were sorted for 16S sequencing and another  
10,000 cells were sorted for sencondary transfer experiments. Double sorting was performed for 
the samples used for 16S sequencing to ensure more pure cultures which was done by sorting 
100,000 gfp-expressing transconjugant cells and then further sort 10,000 cells. DNA extration was 
performed using GenePurgeDirect agent (Nimagen). 16S rRNA sequencing was performed on an 
Illumina® MiSeq® platform (Illumina, San Diego, CA, USA) and PCR amplification was performed of 
the V3 and V4 region. The primers 341F and 806R and the thermal cycle program can be found in  
Uli Klümper et al., 2015. PCR amplification was verified by running a 1% agarose gel electrophoresis. 
 
 
Growth of the transconjugant community to increase cell density 
 
The transconjugal pools of 10,000 cells were grown on fresh filters containing solid synthetic 
wastewater medium. The transconjugant cells from E2 and K2 were regrown in triplicates with and 
without antibiotics (20 𝜇𝑙/𝑚𝑙 kanamycin). All samples were grown twice on fresh filters to reach a 
sufficient amount of cells, grown at 25oC for 48 hours. Fluorescence microscopy was performed to 
verify successful propagation of transconjugants. 
 
 
Secondary transfer via solid surface filter mating 
 
Seconday solid surface filter matings were performed with the regrown transconjugant 
communities supplemented with antibiotics as donors in combination with the two model 
pathogens E. coli and E. cloacae as recipients. Filter matings were carried out in a 1:2 ratio, where 
the donor cells were diluted to 3.38 ∙10) cells/ml and recipient cells were diluted to 6.67 ∙10) 
cells/ml. The ratio ensures that the donor cells were surrounded by multiple recipient cells. After 48 
hours of incubation at 25oC the plates were moved to 4oC for 24 hours to let the fluorescence 
mature. Microcolonies that show fluorescence on both channels (red and green) have successfully 
taken up the plasmid. All samples were carried out in triplicates.

Sequence analysis 
 
R studio was used to evaluate the 16S sequencing results. Paired end reads of 16S rRNA gene 
amplicon sequencing were analyzed using DADA2 pipeline to infer exact sequence variants. 
Trimming and chimera removal was performed. BLAST was used to align the closets relatives to the 
seqeunces was used. MEGA for doing aligment and creating a phylogenetic tree was used. Alpha 
and beta diversity was carried out in R. 
 
Results 
 
GFP is properly repressed in the E. coli donor strains for pKJK5 and RP4, but not for RSF1010 
 
In this study, solid filter mating assays were performed with an E. coli  donor containing a gfp-tagged 
plasmid which was mixed with recipients sampled from WWTPs in Ellinge (E2) and from Klagshamn 
(K2). Epifluorescence microscopy was performed for the three gfp-tagged donors containing one of 
the BHR plasmids, pKJK5, RP4 and RSF1010  to ensure that the dual-labelling technique worked 
(Figure 3). Growing the donors without adding the WWTP recipients should only express red 
fluorescence due to gfp-repression. Red fluorescence was detected for the two IncP-1 plasmids 
pKJK5 and RP4. Red and green fluorescence was detected for the IncQ plasmid RSF1010. The flow 
cytometry results indicated that 25% of the E. coli donors containing plasmid RSF1010 were counted 
as both red and green cells (Appendix ). This observation eliminated the donor containing RSF1010 
from further experiments due to poor GFP repression.

necessary to reach a sufficient amount of cells for the sencondary transfer experiments. 10,000

(Figure 10). Under the fluorescence microscope it was clear that not all transconjugant cells 
remained green without antibiotics. As a result, regrowth of the transconjugant communities from 
E2 was performed with the antibiotics. Antibitocs used in these experiments were kanamycin and 
tetracycline both with a concentration of 20 𝜇𝑔/𝑚𝑙. A couple of pictures of each plate was taken 
which means quantification of the green fluorescence cells is not possible. The amount of cells 
expressing the GFP is in the same order of magnitude for all samples.

Discussion 
 
 
Plasmid RSF1010 was excluded due to improper GFP expression 
 
The three E. coli donor strains were grown on a synthetic wastewater medium to verfy gene-
expression in absence of recipient communities. Green fluorescence was observed after growing 
the E. coli donor strain containing plasmid RSF1010. This indicated that GFP was not properly 
repressed in all donor cells. One possible explanation could be leaky gene expression of the GFP 
reporter gene. All three plasmids carries a lacIq repressible promoter upstream of the gfpmut3 gene 
that encodes the GFP. Leaky gene expression of the lac operon could be because the transcriptional 
control of the operon is not 100% efficient and binding of the repressor to the operator sites in the 
DNA are not 100% efficient (Nielsen et al., 2007). Furthermore, skimmed milk powder is a 
component in the synthetic wastewater medium which has a high occurrence of lactose. This could 
also contribute to the expression of the reporter gene GFP because the lac operon is activated in 
the presence of lactose and thus induces the expression of GFP. The two IncP-1 plasmids pKJK5 and 
RP4 are both low copy number plasmids, whereas the IncQ plasmid RSF1010 is a high copy number 
plasmid (Bagdasarian et al., 1981; Nielsen et al., 2007). Mid-to-high copy number plasmids have the 
possibility to increase the leaky expression because the number of genes, promoters and operator 
sites are higher (Nielsen et al., 2007).    
 
 
Higher transfer frequency for plasmid pKJK5 and Gammaproteobacteria being the 
dominating class 
 
Previous studies have observed higher transfer freuencies for filter mating experiments carried out 
with plasmid pKJK5 than for plasmid RP4 (Li et al., 2018). This observation was observed despite 
that the plasmids pKJK5 (IncP-1𝜀) and RP4 (IncP-1𝛼) belong to the same incompability group (IncP-
1). Activated sludge is not a homogeneous mass but according to Jacquiod et al., 2017 WWTPs show 
high phylogenetic similarities  regardless of geological locations and abiotic fluctuations. Worldwide 
WWTP habitats share a core microbiome dominated by Fimicutes, Bacteroidetes, Proteobacteria 
and Actinobacteria (Jacquiod et al., 2017). All four phylums are represented in this study to a greater 
or lower extent. Several studies have shown that IncP-1 plasmids have a BHR (Selbitschka, 1999; 
Musovic et al., 2006, 2010; Klümper et al., 2015; Jacquiod et al., 2017; Li et al., 2018). Proteobacteria 
is by far the most dominating phylum in all samples (activated sludge, transconjugal pools and the 
regrown sample). The plasmid pKJK5’s preferred host is the phylum Proteobacteria Jacquiod et al., 
2017 which is consistent with the findings in this study. This could be why plasmid pKJK5 show higher 
transfer frequencies than plasmid RP4 although both belonging to the same incompability group 
IncP-1.  
==PAGE:22==
 
18 
 
The most permissive families in the transconjugal pools were dominated by the families  
Aeromonadaceae and Enterobacteriaceae after filter matings, both belonging to the class 
gammaproteobacteria. This agrees with previous findings that used the same E. coli donor tagged 
with the same plasmids, namely pKJK5 and RP4 (Li et al., 2018).  The transconjugant communities 
that carry the BHR plasmid RP4  showed a similar high increase in the family Moraxellaceae which 
may be related to the plasmid, a family that belongs to the class gammaproteobacteria. The nine 
permissive genera shared by the transconjugant pools represent the classes gamma- and 
betaproteobacteria. The three transconjugant pools; E2 (RP4), K2 (pKJK5) and K2 (RP4) share more 
than 88% of the permissive genera. The shannon index clearly shows that the transconjugal pool E2 
(pKJK5) is distinct from the other transconjugant pools (a significant drop in the shannon index, see 
figure 12). The top five genera represented across all communitites are Aeromonas, Raoultella, 
Acinetobacter, Pseudomonas and Enterobacter which all belong to the class gammaproteobacteria. 
Pathogenic species are found in all five genera which emphasizes the risk that transfer of ARGs to 
pathogens is a possibility.  
 
 
Regrowth: diversity loss and transfer in gram-positive bacteria 
 
Regrowth of the transconjugant pools from the WWTP K2 was performed to ensure a sufficient 
amount of cells for the secondary filter mating. Very few transconjugants grew on most filters and 
not enough fluoresence pictures were taken to quantify the exact amount of green fluorescent 
microcolonies. The calculation of the amount of microcolonies was based on maximum six 
fluorescence pictures and hence is only an estimate. Regrowing the transconjugant pools with and 
without antibiotics in the medium resulted in growth of non-fluorescent cells that did not have the 
expected phenotype. The phenotype was supposed to be green fluorescent cells expected that they 
maintained the gfp-tagged plasmid. It is up for debate if antibiotics are necessary as selection 
pressure for maintenance of the plasmid. In the absence of antibiotics the bacteria stopped 
expressing the plasmid which could be due to that they cannot replicate and maintain it. Loosing 
the plasmid during cell division is possible because there are not more copies than the initial copy. 
According to Karkman et al., 2019 ARG abundance correlated strongly to fecal pollution and that 
ARGs posses selective pressure if the environment is polluted with very high levels of antibiotics. 
Furthermore, same incompatibility groups cannot stably share replication and partition systems in 
the host. If any of the hosts (transconjugants) contain a plasmid beloning to the same incompability 
group then this could interfere with the uptake and maintenance of the donor plasmids pKJK5 or 
RP4 (Shintani et al., 2015)..  
  
The relative abundance of the transconjugant communities for both plasmids experienced a 
significant diversity drop after regrowth because only a few microcolonies from the first filter 
==PAGE:23==
 
19 
represent the community composition. Three families are present in the community (K2-pKJK5); the 
dominating family Enterobacteriaceae constituting more than 80% of the community and the 
families Jonesiaceae and Microbacteriaceae. The families Jonesiaceae and Microbacteriaceae 
belong to the phylum Actinobacteria that are gram-positive bacteria and hence inter-gram transfer 
was observed. This means that gram-positive bacteria are able to replicate and maintain the IncP-1 
plasmids pKJK5 and RP4. 
 
 
Secondary transfer is possible 
 
Seondary filter mating assay was performed with two model-pathogens, E. coli and E. cloacae, and 
the transconjugant pools as donors. The amount of Cells were too high to quantify the transfer 
frequency (Figure 13). Even though the quantification is not possible secondary transfer is observed. 
Yellow arrows in figure 13 represent examples of successful transfer events. Detection is technically 
challenging because successful transfer events have to be detected on both channels (red and 
green) and therefore different strategies needs to be explored. Quantification of transfer 
frequencies would be possible by making a serial dilution and thus decreasing the amount of cells 
in the filter mating assay. 
 
 
Conclusion 
 
This study show that Gammaproteobacteria is by far the most dominating class for all samples (the 
activated sludge samples, all the transconjugant pools and the regrown sample) which may be 
related to the incompability group (IncP-1) of the two plasmids pKJK5 (IncP-1𝜀) and RP4 (IncP-1𝛼) 
which has been shown in other studies (Gelder et al., 2005; Klümper et al., 2015; Jacquiod et al., 
2017; Li et al., 2018).  
 
The top five genera (Aeromonas, Raoultella, Acinetobacter, Pseudomonas and Enterobacter) 
represented across all transconjugant pools contain pathogenic species which emphasizes the risk 
that transfer of ARGs to pathogens is a possibility.  
 
Regrowth of the transconjugant pool, K2 (pKJK5), without antibiotics show non-fluorescent 
microcolonies, the expected phenotype is green fluorescent microcolonies. This could be due to 
bacteria that cannot replicate and maintain the plasmid. Karkman et al., 2019 suggests that 
antibiotics only have an influence on the selective pressure in the presence of high levels of 
antibiotics. To test the hypothesis future studies should measure the concentration of antibitics and 
see if the levels of antibitics have an influence.  
 
==PAGE:24==
 
20 
A significant diversity loss is observed for the regrown sample (K2-pKJK5) because only a few 
microcolonies from the first filter represent the community composition. Interestingly, the families 
Jonesiaceae and Microbacteriaceae belong to the phylum Actinobacteria that are gram-positive 
bacteria. This means that gram-positive bacteria are able to replicate and maintain the IncP-1 
plasmids pKJK5 and RP4. Furthermore, the plasmid pKJK5 show higher transfer frequencies than 
plasmid RP4 for the primary filter mating assay.  
 
Plasmid transfer was observed for the secondary filter mating assay which means that WWTP 
microbial community members from activated sludge were able to act as intermediate hosts. The 
amount of cells were to high to quantify the transfer frequency used in the secondary transfer assay. 
The filters were completely overgrown. As a suggestion, a serial diltution could be carried out to 
decrease the amount of cells and hence being able to quantify the transfer frequency. Secondary 
transfer was technically challenging to detect because transfer events had to be detected on both 
channels (red and green). The fluorescence expression of microcolonies that have taken up the 
plasmid should be reconsidered to prevent detection of transfer events to happen on both channels.
