Abstract
One of the major corrosion mechanisms in the oil and gas industry is CO2 corrosion,
called "sweet corrosion" due to the absence of oxygen. Consequentially, an FeCO3
layer is formed leading to the passivation of the surface; hence the decrease of the
corrosion rate . This corrosion mechanism is widely occurring in the production tub-
ing (wells) in the Danish sector of the North Sea. According to a previous study; iron
oxide such as hematite Fe2O3 was found on the scale formed on production tubing
retrieved from the North Sea. Based on the assumption that oxygen is absent in the
reservoir, the transformation of the FeCO3 passive layer into iron oxides is the focal
point of this project.
This research consisted of studying the eﬀects of time and temperature on this trans-
formation. In order to do so, an electro-chemical formation of a passive FeCO3 layer
was conducted in the lab for 5 days on L80 steel with a martensitic structure, gener-
ally used in the manufacturing of the production tubing pipes. Later on, the samples
were placed in a climatic chamber to study their transformation when exposed to
the following temperatures 25◦C, 40◦C and 80◦C at 99% relative humidity. The ex-
posure time were chosen as follows; 1 day, 3 days and 7 days. A Scanning Electron
Microscopy (SEM) was used to observe the various layers formed after the transfor-
mation (The morphology). Energy Dispersive Spectroscopy (EDS) was then used to
do a mapping and site analysis for the chemical composition of the chosen site or area.
X-ray diﬀraction (XRD) analysis were conducted on the surface of the sample in order
to label the diﬀerent phases constituting the layer along with the X-ray photoelectron
spectroscopy (XPS) depth proﬁle analysis.
The diﬀerent analyses revealed the formation of two distinguished layers; an outer-
most surface layer rich in FeCO3, and another layer beneath it, which is a stabilized
layer containing FeCO3 along with FeO and Hematite Fe2O3. However, it is believed
that the wustite FeO and the hematite Fe2O3 constitute a magnetite Fe3O4 phase.
The (XPS) showed a higher binding energy on the surface which indicated higher
oxidation levels. It was also revealed that the transformation was accelerated with
time and temperature. As a matter of fact, it was noted that the 25◦C and 40◦C
temperatures were not enough to accelerate the kinetics of the transformation, even
after 7 days, while a temperature of 80◦C was able to accelerate the kinetics of the
transformation after 3 and 7 days.

Preface
This master thesis is built upon an extensive and exhaustive research conducted in
the department of mechanical engineering in DTU. It granted the completion of 35
ECTS points required to graduate with a Master of Materials and Manufacturing En-
gineering from DTU. The presented report has been conducted under the supervision
of Prof. Rajan Ambat and the co-supervision of Researcher Magdalena Rogowska and
Ph.D student Riccardo Rizzo between February 1st, 2019 and July 31st, 2019. The
prime focus of this research is investigating the unlikely presence of iron oxides in the
production tubing (wells) retrieved from the Danish sector of the North sea given the
absence of oxygen in the reservoir. Theoretically, the corrosion mechanism consists of
the formation of FeCO3 layer. Thus the transformation of the FeCO3 into iron oxides
on L80 steel is studied.
Kongens Lyngby, July 31st, 2019

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1 Corrosion in the Oil & Gas production
The ﬁrst production of hydrocarbons in Denmark from the North Sea goes back to
1972. The production continued rising till reaching a plateau in 2004 as depicted in
ﬁgure 1.1 [1]. This decline in production is caused by the frequent need of maintaining
Figure 1.1: Oil and gas production in Denmark between 1975 and 2025 (projected) [1]
the well and the infrastructure such as the pipeline systems. Hence, a huge amount
of money is being reallocated to the optimization of the design and the durability
of the infrastructures [2]. The harsh nature of the service conditions of the oﬀshore
and the subsea oil ﬁelds can promote corrosion [3][4].
By deﬁnition, corrosion is
the mechanical removal of a metallic material when reacting with its environment[5].
Figure 1.2: Schematic of the diﬀerent structures used in the
oil and gas industry [6]
These service conditions can be listed
as follows; high temperature, high pres-
sure, acidic pH levels, as well as oxide
concentration and salinity of the water.
There are multiple forms of corrosion
that occur in the oil and gas industry
such as the Carbon Dioxide (CO2) corro-
sion sometimes referred to as sweet cor-

One source of oxygen in the oil and gas industry is the water based drilling mud.
It represents more than 80% of the drilling ﬂuids used in the oil and gas industry
[3][19]. A drilling ﬂuid is used to prevent the oil and gas recovered from the formation
ton enter to the well bore during the drilling process as well as to collect the drill

Another source for the oxygen is the dissolved oxygen (DO) in the water injected in the

the deaeration process is taking place before each injection, lack of accuracy (real time
monitoring) of the monitoring stage can cause unknown levels of DO in the injected
water; hence severe corrosion can take place in the production tubing [14].

When the normal angle X-ray

on the surface of a specimen. The x-ray beams hit the surface of the sample and

Diﬀerent diﬀraction angles are detected by the rotation of the x-ray detector while
the x-ray source is kept stationary in the case of the grazing incident measurements.

Chapter 5
Discussion
The transformation of FeCO3 into iron oxides was studied in this report. In order to
understand the eﬀect of time and temperature on the FeCO3 layer transformation;
SEM, XRD and XPS analysis were conducted on diﬀerent samples exposed to diﬀer-
ent temperatures and exposure time in a climatic chamber at 99% relative humidity
level. In the beginning, Corrosion was induced in a corrosion cell in order to deposit
a uniform FeCO3 layer as shown in ﬁgure 4.3.
The SEM analysis revealed the formation of two distinct layers diﬀerent in morphol-
ogy and chemical composition as depicted in ﬁgure 4.4 as well as in all the SEM
micrographs for all the exposure time at 25◦C, 40◦C and 80◦C. It was believed that
the outermost layer on the surface of the sample (layer 2) was not a stable layer com-
pared to the layer beneath it (layer 1). This conclusion was derived from the fact that
the thickness and the morphology of layer 1 was not changing nor with the increase
of temperature nor with the exposure time in the climatic chamber; in addition to
the presence of residues of layer 2 on the epoxy resin after the grinding process which
indicated that its adhesion to the surface is low.
Another observation made that
emphasized the weak adhesion was that with the increase in exposure time, the less
presence of the outermost surface layer was observed. This might had been caused
by the airﬂow inside the climatic chamber. The outermost layer was clearly rich in
FeCO3 as the cross section SEM micrographs showed a similar morphology of the
crystal structure of the iron carbonate which is a calcite structure or trigonal [54][41].
The EDS mapping was used in order to study the concentrations of oxygen, carbon
and iron in layers 1 and 2. Figure 4.6 as well as all the other mapping studies revealed
a very high concentration of oxygen in layers 1 and 2 with a slightly lower concentra-
tion of carbon. This signiﬁcantly high concentration of oxygen aﬃrmed the fact that
layers 1 and 2 were corrosion products from the electrochemical formation of FeCO3
and not formed prior to the corrosion test. Two distinct areas were identiﬁed dark
and bright within the stable layer (layer 1) as shown for example in ﬁgure 4.10.
In order to identify the chemical composition of the diﬀerent areas observed in layers
1 and 2, an EDS point-ID was conducted. The theoretical values for the ratio be-
tween the diﬀerent weight percentages of each element in the iron carbonate and the
iron oxides were calculated as shown in appendix A and compared to the calculated
values. Figure 4.10 and table 4.2 depicted the presence of hematite Fe2O3 in the
dark areas and the presence of FeCO3 in the bright area. Concerning the chemical
composition of layer 2, the outermost layer on the surface, table 4.1 showed a ratio

The thickness of the outermost layer (layer 2) was not aﬀected nor by the increase of
temperature nor by the exposure time. The average thickness of the rich FeCO3 layer
(layer 2) was estimated to be around 7 to 9 µm. Similarly to layer 2, layer 1 was also

it ranged from 25 to 28 µm. The possible explanation of this increase of the thickness
of the layer was that the absence of the passive layer rich in FeCO3 (layer 2) exposed
the surface of the sample to the oxygen in the air made it easily oxidized.
The SEM micrographs showed the change of the morphology of the FeCO3 layer as it
became more porous with the increase of temperature and the exposure time. How-
ever, these changes were not observed except for the 3 and 7 days exposure at 80◦C
as shown in ﬁgure 4.37 and ﬁgure 4.41. This change of morphology might had been
caused by the diﬀerences of densities between iron oxides and FeCO3. According to
literature, the density of FeCO3 is euqal to 3.9 g/cm3 while the density of the iron
oxide, more speciﬁcally hematite is equal to 5.3g/cm3 [55][56]. Upon transformation,
a volumetric shrinkage of the FeCO3 occurred when it transformed into iron oxide,
hence the porous morphology.
The XPS depth proﬁle analysis was used in order to identify the diﬀerent phases
present from diﬀerent depth levels from the surface of the sample. The presence of
Iron carbonate was conﬁrmed in all the samples except for the 7 days exposure at
40◦C as shown in ﬁgure 4.32 and in the 3 days and 7 days exposure at 80◦C as shown
in ﬁgure 4.40 and ﬁgure 4.44. Furthermore, peaks of metal oxides such as FeO and
hematite Fe2O3 in the Fe2p spectrum and the O1s spectrum were present. The XPS
analysis proved the presence of FeO and Fe2O3 with the FeCO3 which was consistent
with the ﬁndings of the EDS point-ID. Another observation made from the Fe2p spec-
trum was that at the layer closest to the surface, a high binding energy was recorded.
According to literature, the higher the binding energy the higher oxidation levels [49].
Finally, the XRD analysis depicted in section 4.6 displayed strong FeCO3 peaks along
with Fe2O3 and FeO peaks. However, some peaks for the Fe2O3 and the FeCO3 are
located at the same 2θ angle which might lead to a misinterpretation of the spectrum.
This might have been caused by the similar crystal structure of the FeCO3 and the
Fe2O3 and the existence of similar lattice parameters such as the (110), (024) and the
(018) planes [50][52][51]. Furthermore, a magnetite phase could also be present as it
has peaks that coincide with hematite such as the 36, the 57 and the 72 2θ angles [53].
However, since magnetite phase was not present in the XPS analysis, the conclusion
that hematite exists was assumed.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
The main aim of this project was to study the transformation of the FeCO3 into iron
oxides under the eﬀect of time and temperature. For this purpose a thick layer of
FeCO3 was electrochemically deposited on the surface of L80 steel samples. After-
wards, the diﬀerent samples were exposed to diﬀerent temperatures; 25◦C, 40◦C and
80◦C in a climatic chamber for 1,3 and 7 days at 99% relative humidity level.
• The reference sample depicted two diﬀerent layers formed during the electro-
chemical formation of the FeCO3. The outermost surface layer (layer 2) showed
a crystal structure similar to to the FeCO3 trigonal crystal structure.
The
(EDS) and the (XPS) depth proﬁle analyses aﬃrmed the presence of FeCO3. A
hematite Fe2O3 phase along with wustite FeO were present in the second layer
(layer1) formed beneath the FeCO3 rich layer.
• The morphology of the two layers did not change when exposing the samples to
the 25◦C and 40◦C. The same phases as the reference sample, hematite Fe2O3
phase along with wustite FeO and FeCO3, were present in all of the etched
layers. The FeCO3 rich layer was seen to be reduced with exposure time. It was
believed that it had been caused by the airﬂow inside the climatic chamber as
it was not well adhered to the surface.
• The morphology of the FeCO3 rich layer (layer 2) was seen to change signiﬁcantly
at 80◦C after 3 and 7 days of exposure. The layer was becoming porous as a
result of the density diﬀerence between the iron oxides and the iron carbonate.
In addition, according to the (XPS) analyses; the FeCO3 was no longer present,
only peaks indicating hematite Fe2O3 and wustite FeO were depicted.
• Given the fact that FeCO3 decomposes under temperature levels signiﬁcantly
higher than the experimental temperatures, it can be suggested that it was the
high level of relative humidity that accelerated its kinetics.
• The formation of Fe2O3 and FeO along with the FeCO3 can be explained by
the increase of the pH level during the experiments leading to the formation of
magnetite Fe3O4 which can be present in low temperatures in the form of FeO
and Fe2O3

Chapter 7
Issues & Future Work
7.1 Experimental Issues
One of the major issues faced during this thesis was that, for an unknown reason, the
XRD experiments conducted on the 80◦C samples had to be redone multiple times,
and was not successful. Another issue was a result of the limited availability of the
XRD and the XPS apparatus, the samples were exposed to the environment for longer
than they should have been. This may have caused oxidation of the samples before
the tests were conducted.
7.2 Future Works
• Since the transformation of FeCO3 into iron oxides only started at 80◦C after
a 3 and 7 days exposure, it is recommended to start the experiments at higher
temperatures and expose the samples for longer time in the climatic chambers.
• As the conclusion suggested, the relative humidity might have accelerated this
transformation, therefore, examining the eﬀect of relative humidity would be
beneﬁcial.
• Glow Discharge Optical Emission Spectroscopy (GDOES) could additionally be
used as a validation technique for the conducted XRD and XPS experiments,
as it allows the study of the diﬀerent phases in diﬀerent layers, up to 50 nm
thickness.
• A transmission/scanning transmission electron microscope (TEM/STEM) with
electron energy loss spectroscopy (EELS) can be used to obtain the phases of a
thin layer, particularly iron oxides. Furthermore this method allows the study
of a speciﬁc known region within a layer.
• Given that the results suggested a change in the pH during the electrochemical
formation of the FeCO3, which could have lead to the formation of magnetite
Fe3O4, it is recommended to measure the pH throughout the experiments to
make sure that it is within the range. Another suggestion would be to stop the
experiments as soon as the passivation occurs, in order to avoid the reduction
of the anodic reaction (Fe cations).
