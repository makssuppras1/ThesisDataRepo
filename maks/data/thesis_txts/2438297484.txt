Abstract  
The overall aim was to investigate; whether the substrate, given to the mealworms (Tenebrio molitor), needs 
to be free of microbial hazard and contaminants in order to obtain mealworms that are safe for consumption.  
 
To investigate this, the mealworms are exposed to Salmonella enterica Typhimurium Rif and Listeria 
monocytogenes in three different concentrations 102, 104 and 106 CFU/g given to them via the wet substrate 
(spent grain). To prepare for the exposure experiment, different selective plates and combinations of disin-
fection method were tested. The disinfection method chosen to remove external bacteria on the mealworm 
surface was washing for 1 minute in the disinfection agent 0.26 % Sodium hypochlorite and rinsing for 1 
minute with 0.9 % NaCl saltwater. For enumeration of Salmonella enterica Typhimurium Rif the selective 
plate Nutrient agar with 50 µl/ml Rifampicin (Na Rif) was found to be the most selective and had the best 
recovery. For enumeration of Listeria monocytogenes the selective plate Polymyxin Acriflavine Lithium 
chloride Ceftazidime Aesculin Mannitol (PALCAM) was found to be the most selective and had the best 
recovery.  
 
The disinfection method only removed a portion of the external bacteria; therefore the results found in the 
exposure experiment do not reflect the internal concentration but rather a combination of the internal and 
external concentration. But as the whole mealworm is eaten, the results still indicates that substrate contami-
nated with Salmonella enterica Typhimurium Rif and Listeria monocytogenes constitutes a safety risk. The 
results showed that the concentration of Salmonella enterica Typhimurium Rif and Listeria monocytogenes in 
mealworms and substrate depended on the spiked contamination level and the concentration did not decrease 
notably over time.  
 
It was therefore not recommended to give substrate with a high risk of microbial hazard to the meal-
worms, as the mealworms do become contaminated for a period of at least seven days.

1. Introduction 
The global demand for proteins is increasing worldwide as a consequence of the global population growth. 
Especially animal proteins are requested more and more worldwide [Van der Fels-Klerx et. al. 2016; Nor-
dentoft et. al. 2017]. The production of meat is not very environmentally friendly and is associated with cli-
mate changes [Houbraken et. al. 2016]. Therefore, a new source of protein is needed to fulfill the need for 
protein in future generations.  
 
An excellent candidate to fulfill this need is the yellow mealworm Tenebrio molitor. The mealworm has a 
high nutritional value in protein, minerals and fat [Houbraken et. al. 2016; Van der Spiegel 2016]. Further-
more, one kilo of protein made from mealworm only requires 10% of the space that cattle needs for the same 
amount of protein [Oonincx & De Boer 2012]. The production of mealworms has a much smaller impact on 
the environment than other sources for animal protein [Oonincx & De Boer 2012]. 
 
Today the Danish legislation allows the production and sale of the mealworm for consumption [FUST, 
2017]. Due to concern for consumer safety, all risk and hazard regarding eating the mealworm must be min-
imized. Therefore, legislation dictates that the substrate given to the mealworms is of the same quality and 
standard as the feed given to other livestock animals [FUST 2017]. Therefore, the substrate must be free 
from pathogens and come from certified producers of feed. Furthermore, the substrate must not contain 
meat-residues, fish, kitchen waste, manure or other residues [FUST 2017].  
 
The aim of this thesis is to investigate if the substrate, given to the mealworms, needs to be free of micro-
bial hazard and contaminants, in order to obtain mealworms that are safe for consumption. The focus is the 
microbiological hazard in the wet substrate (vegetables) for mealworm, and the consequence of this hazard 
being present when eaten by the mealworms. Do the pathogens accumulate in mealworm? Do they pass 
through or do they disappear completely? Does the pathogen concentration have an effect on the outcome? 
  For this investigation, two pathogens are chosen, which are normally found in vegetables; Salmonella and 
Listeria [Beuchat 2002; FAO/WHO 2008]. Both pathogens are commonly known to have outbreaks in dif-
ferent salads, fruits and other vegetables, worldwide [Beuchat 2002]. For investigation of the aim, different 
experiments were conducted to find a suitable method to detect and count internal Listeria and Salmonella in 
the mealworm that are fed spiked substrate. 
 
The culturing method to best find Listeria or Salmonella in a mealworm matrix was determined. This is 
done by comparing the methods on selectivity and ability to recover the smallest amounts of inoculated bac-
teria. This is tested on four serovars of Salmonella enterica (Weltevreden, Typhimurium, TyphimuriumRif 
and Typhimurium Nal+Rif) and two strains of Listeria monocytogenes (two isolated from different salads). The 
selective plate used for culturing Salmonella is BGA, XLD and NaRif. The selective plate used to for cultur-
ing Listeria is PALCAM and Oxford.  
 
To be able to ensure that the microbial count from homogenized mealworms only reflects the internal gut 
microbiota a suitable disinfection method was assessed. Different disinfection combinations were tested to 
find one that could remove the highest amount or all of the external bacteria from the mealworm’s surface. 
The reasoning for this is to be able to conclude that the microbial count found has indeed come from the 
intestines of the mealworm. 
 
Lastly, an exposure experiments were done to investigate a production environment where mealworms 
were fed with spiked substrate in different concentration of Listeria and Salmonella. This was investigated 
by introducing groups of mealworms to spiked substrate of either Salmonella or Listeria. The concentration 
of the bacteria in the mealworm and substrate was observed over a time period of 7 days.

2. Yellow mealworm Tenebrio molitor 
To mimic the real world in the experiments that is conducted, it is necessary to understand the mealworm 
and the production of mealworms for consumption. This is to understand at what developmental stage the 
mealworm will be most relevant to use for the exposure experiments. But also, what conditions the worms 
normally live under in production. Besides the lifecycle and the physical aspect of the mealworm, the micro-
biota of the mealworm is investigated. This is done as to indicate what other bacteria that could interfere in 
the experiment, and therefore should be prepared for. 
2.1. Physical properties 
The Tenebrio molitor is commonly known as the yellow mealworm and is the larvae of either the Tenebrio 
molitor Linnaeus beetles or the Tenebrio molitor Fabricius beetles [Abdalbasit et. al. 2017]. The larvae un-
dergo metamorphosis to become a beetle during its life cycle, seen on figure 1 (a) [Andersen et. al. 2017]. 
The mealworm has four stages in this life cycle; eggs in the first couple of days, larvae stage in 6-8 weeks, 
pupa stage and then adult beetle stage in 1-3 months before the life cycle ends [Abdalbasit et. al. 2017 and 
Andersen et. al. 2017]. The female beetle starts to lay eggs between 4-17 days after they have copulation 
[Abdalbasit et. al. 2017]. They lay 5-8 eggs a day and around 300 eggs over its adult lifespan [Andersen et. 
al. 2017]. 
 
 
 
Figure1. (a) The life cycle of the Tenebrio molitor. (b) Tenebrio molitor [Abdalbasit et. al. 2017] 
 
The mealworm is yellow or brown in color as seen on figure 1. Just before pupa stages the mealworms 
are between 25-35 mm long and weigh about 0.2 g [Abdalbasit et. al. 2017]. A living mealworm consists of 
13-15 % fat, 20-22 % protein, 3% carbohydrate, 57-62 % moisture and 1 % ash [Abdalbasit et. al. 2017 and 
Jones et. al. 1972]. It has a mean pH value of 6.7 ± 0.02 and a mean water activity of 0.96 [Vandeweyer et. 
al. 2016].  
2.2. Gut microbiota 
A study by Wang and Zhang (2015) looked at the mealworm gut microbiota. The gut was separated into four 
parts and then the microbiota of each part was identified. The study used the following methods to identify 
the microbiota: Cultivation of bacteria, genome DNA extraction, PCR amplified DGGE analysis and Nucleo-
tide sequencing [Wang and Zhang 2015]. The study shows that the abundance of the four parts is different 
and some areas of the intestine have more types of bacteria than other areas of the intestine. In total, the 
study found 9 different genera of microbes: Enterobacter, Pantoea, Bacillaceae, Clostridium, Lactobacillus, 
Bacillus, Lactococcus, Spiroplasma and some uncultured bacterium [Wang and Zhang 2015]. Another study 
by Wynants et. al. (2017) was studying the mealworm gut microbiota before and after starvation at different 
temperatures. They used classic microbiological analyses and metagenetic analyses to identify the gut mi-
crobiota [Wynants et. al. 2017]. They found that the relative abundance of gut microbiota was different be-
fore and after starvation, but mostly consisted of the same microbe’s genera. The microbes genera found

sarily have the same relative abundance. This indicates that during experiments with mealworm that the re-
sults could differ as the microbiota differs from mealworm to mealworm.   
2.3. Breeding and farming  
Mealworms have been bred and farmed for a long period of time as fodder for pets like reptiles [Andersen et. 
al. 2017]. So, the production of mealworms is not new in itself but the study of how to get the worms to grow 
big in the shortest amount of time is still new and not yet well known [Andersen et. al.2017].  
 
Figure 2. An overview of the cycles of production of mealworms. A box of eggs that go into larva stage. The mealworms are fed 
and maintained, then separated and some larvae are taken to become beetles. The rest of the production group is then separat-
ed between frass and larvae. The larvae are then euthanized. [Andersen et.al.2017]. The figure is taken and translated from 
[Andersen et.al. 2017]. 
 
The production cycle is shown on figure 2. The production of the mealworm starts with a batch of larvae. 
This batch is called the mother culture. The mother culture is allowed to enter the beetle stage where they 
start laying eggs. Every day the eggs and mother culture are separated to control the age of the eggs. The 
optimal production of eggs is achieved with a density of 0.84 beetles per cm2 [Andersen et. al. 2017]. Other 
factors to consider when trying to get a higher egg yield are the surrounding temperature, humidity, access to 
food and access to water [Andersen et. al. 2017]. 
 
In the transfer to the production stage beetles are removed from the box to ensure the highest amount of 
eggs. The box now only containing eggs then enters the production stage. To control the concentration of 
==PAGE:10==
[10] 
 
 
eggs and thereby get a more stable production, the eggs can be separated from the substrate using a 0.5 mm 
mask filter.  
 
The larvae should have a start density of 10 larvae per cm2 in the first month and then change to a density 
of 5 larvae per cm2 until harvest [Andersen et. al. 2017]. This is to achieve the best growth rate, size, survival 
and to avoid cannibalism. Harvest should happen after 70 days if they are growing in 25°C and after 50 days 
if they are growing in 30°C [Andersen et. al. 2017]. The humidity should be around 50%-70% to ensure the 
best production [Andersen et. al. 2017].   
 
Harvesting a separation is done by first separating worms from substrate and frass, then to take the pupas 
or 5-10% of the larvae to create a new mother culture [Andersen et. al. 2017]. Residues are discarded, the 
mother culture is taken back in the production cycle, the rest of the mealworms are then sent to be eu-
thanized.  
 
The euthanizing happens by first letting the worms starve in a 15°C room for 24 hours. Secondly, they are 
frozen for 48 hours at -18°C [Andersen et. al. 2017].

3. Food safety 
 
When having a food production, several things are important; beside a product with a high quality and a 
good taste, the product needs to be safe for consumption. More than 200 diseases are foodborne and caused 
by different bacteria, viruses, parasites and prions [WHO 2015]. Some of the diseases are very harmful to 
specific groups of consumers where there is a risk for long lasting disabilities or even death. Uncooked or 
undercooked food products are particularly at risk for containing foodborne diseases [WHO 2015]. There-
fore, identifying the microbiological hazards for the food product during the production and the production 
line itself is a priority as to minimize the risk of contamination.  
 
Eating insects is the new way of getting animal protein in a more environment friendly way [Oonincx and 
de Boer 2012]. But there are not many final regulations for production of insects for human consumption yet 
[Van der Spiegel 2016]. Different hazards that can occur naturally when eating insects have been identified 
and are illustrated in table 1. There is both what the insects contain naturally, what can be introduced in the 
processing and in the feed given to them. Therefore, all these factors stated in table 1 should be accounted for 
during a production to prevent foodborne illnesses.

3.1. Safety in mealworm production 
The mealworms today are rinsed and cleaned in lukewarm water before being frozen and later freeze-dried 
and assigned a 52-week shelf life. This means that spore forming bacteria are a particular hazard as they can 
survive in freeze-dried products [Opperhuizen 2014].  Mealworm gut microbiota is affected by what the 
mealworm eats and its surroundings [Dillon and Charnley 2002]. Therefore, there is a risk of pathogens to be 
present in the substrate or the surroundings. As the mealworms live in relatively high temperatures, there is a 
potential risk for undetectable amount of some pathogens to multiply into hazardous amounts [Opperhuizen 
2014].  
 
Heat-treating mealworms with roasting, does not kill all internal Enterobacteriaceae [Klunder et. al. 
2012]. But if boiled or heat-treated for 10 minutes the Enterobacteriaceae was reduced to acceptable levels 
of less than 10 CFU or removed all together [Klunder et. al. 2012; Opperhuizen 2014]. Opperhuizen (2014) 
also indicates that there is no documentation for the postulate that mealworm freeze-dried products stay safe 
in the 52-week long shelf life. Furthermore, fresh mealworms sold for human consumption were found to be 
highly contaminated with potential pathogens and spoilage organisms [Stoops et. al. 2015]. 
 
To avoid contamination in the mealworm production line, hygiene is essential, and all regulation of feed 
hygiene and food production hygiene must be adhered too. All facilities needed for the production need to be 
placed in an appropriate manner to allow cleaning and disinfection [FUST 2017]. According to the Danish 
environment and food ministry a production company must have all the necessary equipment needed for 
mealworm production such as: euthanizing, storages for insect products and substrate and processing equip-
ment [FUST 2017]. The facility should also comply with the requirements for avoiding pests. The most 
common pests are birds, outside insects and rodents and should be avoided. Therefore, various pest control 
equipment should be installed.  
 
To ensure hygienic standards in the production line, use of equipment with a CE certification is a mini-
mum requirement. As this mark does not ensure quality but ensure the minimum safety requirements such as: 
material use, surface standards and easy to clean design [Europa-commission 2018]. Another certification 
that ensures high hygienic standards is the EHEDG certification. Besides hygiene it ensures quality in the 
equipment used for production [EHEDG 2018]. Both certifications ensure easy to clean equipment; which 
should be cleaned every day.  
 
The water source and the substrate to the mealworms used in the production should follow legislation. 
The Danish legislation states that feed used for mealworms should follow the same legislation as feed used 
for any other domestic animals. Therefore, kitchen scraps and residues from animals are not allowed as feed 
[FUST 2017].  
 
As mentioned in table 1, there are different hazards to be aware of. Heavy metal like cadmium and lead 
can accumulate in the mealworm’s body [Vijver et. al. 2002]. The substrate could contain pathogens; the risk 
with pathogens could either be the disease some of them cause or the mycotoxins some of them produces. 
Additionally, using conventional substrate where pesticides have been applied during the process of making 
the substrate, pesticides can pose a health risk to humans. Mealworms have been shown to accumulate, de-
grade, metabolize and excrete some fungicides [Gao et. al. 2013; Gao et. al. 2014; Liu et. al. 2013; Lv. et. al. 
2013; Lv et. al. 2014; Van der Spiegel 2016]. Some antibiotics can be added to the feed to remove bacterial 
hazards. Genta et. al. (2006) showed that this could potentially damage the mealworm’s own gut microbiota. 
When mealworms were reared on feed with antibiotics they would get a midgut without bacteria and fungi 
which could end up damaging the growth of the mealworms [Genta et. al. 2006]. There is a risk of the insect 
feed being contaminated with transmissible spongiform encephalopathies if the feed has been in contact with 
meat from ruminant animals. Transmissible spongiform encephalopathies can be a vector for prion disease 
and it has been shown that flies can transmit the disease [Charlton et. al. 2014, Post et. al. 1999]. 
 
The water sources for mealworms are often fruits and vegetables that contain a high water activity. Focus-

4.1. Listeria monocytogenes 
Listeria monocytogenes was described for the first time in 1926 in Cambridge, United Kingdom and was 
found in rabbits [Murrayr et. al. 1926]. Listeria monocytogenes is small rod-shaped organism, about 0.5 µm 
in width and 1-2µm in length [Farber and Peterkin 1991; Murrayr et. al. 1926].  It is gram-positive, non-
spore forming and aerobic [Farber and Peterkin 1991]. When well spread unto a plate and incubated at 37 °C 
for 3-4 days, the colonies grow to 1.5 to 2.5 mm in diameter and have a smooth surface that is slightly flat-
tened and round [Murrayr et. al. 1926]. Listeria monocytogenes can grow in temperatures between -0.4 °C 
and 50 °C and pH values between 4.3 and 9.6 [Farber and Peterkin 1991; Tirziu et. al. 2010]. It is an oppor-
tunistic pathogen that multiplies intracellularly and infects the enterocytes in the gut [Larsen et. al. 2002]. 
4.1.1. Human health risk, outbreaks and infection rate for Listeria monocytogenes 
Listeria monocytogenes is commonly found in surface water, plants and soil samples and has been isolated 
from slaughterhouses, animal feces and domestic animals and it is found infrequently in wild animals [Farber 
and Peterkin. 1991]. Even though it is found in so many different places infection of Listeria monocytogenes 
is primarily from foodborne outbreaks [Farber and Peterkin. 1991; Tirziu et. al. 2010]. The percentagewise 
distribution of Listeria monocytogenes found in ready-to-eat food products in the EU in 2016 is found in 
figure 3. Only 10 % of the samples tested positive from ready-to-eat food category in 2016 were from vege-
table and salads [European Food Safety 2017].

The minimum infection dose for Listeria monocytogenes is unknown as it is dependent on the infected 
persons immune system [Farber et. al. 1996], but the probability of infection based on dose has been calcu-
lated and can be seen in figure 4. The minimum infection dose (ID) is found to be 10.000 CFU and ID50 is

is because Listeria monocytogenes invades enterocytes and multiply intracellularly and the CD8T-cell is the 
innate immune response to combat this type of infection [Hess and Kaufmann 1996; Larsen et. al. 2002]. 
However, infants, the elderly and pregnant women have a higher risk of getting infected and have a higher 
mortality rate of 20-30 % then others have when infected with Listeria monocytogenes [Tirziu et. al. 2010].

4.2. Salmonella spp.  
Salmonella was described for the first time by Carl Joseph Eberth in 1880 and was isolated from cadaveric 
tissues [Franco-Paredes et. al. 2016].  The bacteria are rod-shaped organisms that are gram-negative and 
anaerobic. They have flagellum that makes them motile in water, which is essential for their infection mech-
anisms [Johnson 2018; Wingstrand and Aabo 2014]. Salmonella is a part of the Enterobacteriaceae family 
and has two species; Salmonella enterica and Salmonella bongori, where Salmonella enterice has 2500 
serovars [Brankatschk et. al. 2012; Grimont and Weill 2007; Wingstrand and Aabo 2014]. Salmonella can 
grow in 5 °C to 46 °C with the optimal temperature between 35-37 °C and a pH between 4.5 and 9.0 with the 
optimal between 6.5-7.5 pH. Salmonella cannot grow in reservoirs with a water activity below 0.93 [Wing-
strand and Aabo 2014].  Salmonella is a zoonotic and intercellular pathogen that infects its host cell; the in-
testinal epithelium [Hess and Kaufmann 1996; Johnson 2018]. 
4.2.1. Human health risks, outbreaks and infection rates for Salmonella spp. 
The most common reservoirs are livestock, pets, feces, contaminated soil and contaminated water. It is most 
commonly known and associated with poultry, pigs and other livestock [Johnsen 2018; Wingstrand and 
Aabo 2014]. Contamination of soil or vegetables often happens when wild animals like rodents or birds get 
contaminated and shedding it in their feces, or when a farmer uses contaminated manure to fertilize the 
farm's fields [Wingstrand and Aabo 2014]. Outbreaks of Salmonella often happens in food vectors like meat 
and eggs but there has been increased observations of non-animal foods like vegetables and herbs due to risk 
of contaminated soil and irrigation water [Wingstrand and Aabo 2014]. On figure 6, the percentagewise dis-
tribution of Salmonella found in different food products in EU 2016 divided in four categories [European 
Food Safety 2016]. It was found that fruits and vegetables only contributed with 2 percent of all the food

Salmonella has a high minimum infection dose of 105-108 units to infect a healthy person [Wingstrand 
and Aabo 2014; Johnson 2018]. It infects the intestinal epithelium in the gut and causes symptoms like fever,

can last up to 4 weeks and the infected person can keep shedding Salmonella for up to two month [Johnson 
2018, Wingstrand and Aabo 2014]. In some rare cases Salmonella can pass into the bloodstream, where it 
can infect other body parts like the liver or joints. If it enters the blood stream there is risk of it causing men-

4.2.2. Salmonella spp. detection methods 
The Danish ISO standard for detection and isolation of Salmonella spp. dictates that either XLD or BGA 
should be used [ISO 6579, 2002].  
 
XLD was designed for isolation of Salmonella and Shigellae and was developed in 1965. XLD contains 
the following inhibitory component; Sodium deoxycholate. It has three indicator systems; xylose, lactose and 
sucrose combined with lysine HCI phenol red, sodium thiosulphate and iron [JFM 00M41 1987]. Results 
give three kinds of colonies. Colonies with red zone and black centers are; Salmonella, Arizona and Ed-
wardsiella. Colonies with yellow haloes and center are different bacteria like the genera Escherichia and 
Enterobacter. Colonies with red zone and red center are Providencia, Shigella and sulphide negative Salmo-
nella. Colonies should be tested biochemically to confirm the findings [JFM 00M41 1987]. The composi-
tions of XLD agar can be found in appendix A3.  
 
BGA is a selective and diagnostic agar to isolate Salmonella from food and feed which was developed in 
1968 [JFM 00M22 1987].  The indicator compounds in BGA are lactose, sucrose, phenol red and brilliant 
green. The Salmonella spp. colonies are smooth, moist, very low convex and pinkish or red. The colonies 
should be tested biochemically or serologically [JFM 00M22 1987]. The compositions of BGA ager can be 
found in appendix A4.  
 
Serological tests are done on the Salmonella to find the specific serogroup the tested Salmonella belongs 
to. The method works by using an agglutination reaction subjected to antigens structure [Grimont and Weill 
2007; Lee et. al. 2014]. The reaction happens to the surface antigen on the Salmonella. If the reaction hap-
pens, the fluid becomes grainy which indicates a positive. Serogroups have different antigens and therefore 
this method can be used to indicate the Salmonellas serogroup. But Salmonella can lose or change surface 
antigen due to mutations, which can reduce the sensitivity of the serological test [Grimont and Weill 2007; 
Lee et. al. 2014].

5.3. Detection limit for Salmonella and Listeria in mealworms  
5.3.1. Experimental design 
This experiment is to find a selective plate where the smallest amount of bacteria of interest can be found in 
a mealworm matrix. There are two factors that are important here; the concentration of bacteria and the con-
centration of the mealworm. Optimally a small concentration of bacteria could be found in a minimally dilut-
ed mealworm.  
  To test variance between the same species, two Listeria strains and four different Salmonella strains were 
chosen, shown in table 3. For Listeria the following two types of plates were chosen; PALCAM and Oxford. 
These plates are the ISO standard for detection of Listeria monocytogenes [ISO 11290-1, 1997] and are 
therefore chosen for the experiment. PALCAM is somewhat selective for Listeria and Listeria turns the agar 
from red to black [IUMS-ICFMH Working Party, 2003]. Oxford turns Listeria black and should be mostly 
selective towards Listeria with a couple of exceptions, like staphylococci or enterococci [FOOD 00M09, 
1989].  
   For Salmonella the following three types of plates were chosen; Brilliant green ager (BGA), Xylose 
Lysone Deoxycholate (XLD) and Na Rif. BGA and XLD are the ISO standard for detection of Salmonella 
[ISO 6579, 2002]. BGA is a diagnostic agar that indicates different kinds of bacteria by reacting differently 
to them. It turns the Salmonella colonies pink or red [JFM 00M22, 1987]. The XLD is used for isolation of 
Salmonella and works by an indicator system that dyes the colonies black [JFM 00M41, 1987]. Results 
from “detection limit for Salmonella and Listeria in mealworms” indicated that both XLD and BGA were 
not selective enough to suppress background flora so a third plate was chosen, Na Rif, which contained the 
selective factor, the antibiotic Rifampicin to select Salmonella with Rifampicin resistance.

5.5. Mealworm exposed to substrate contaminated with Salmonella and Listeria    
This experiment was done twice independent of each other.  
5.5.1. Experimental design 
The exposure experiment is done to investigate the spiked Salmonella or Listeria concentration in three dif-
ferent spiked concentrations of 102, 104 and 106 CFU/g over a period of 7 days in a mealworm production 
environment. This is done by following the concentration of Salmonella and Listeria in the substrate and the 
mealworms by sampling and analyzing both. 
   The experimental setup is 7 cups that are divided into 3 groups; three cups in the Salmonella group, three 
cups in the Listeria group and one cup in the control group. There are three different concentrations of bacte-
ria in each bacteria group, which are aimed to be 102, 104 and 106 CFU/g. Only one strain of each bacterial 
strain was tested. The Listeria and Salmonella groups each got their own box to contain the bacteria. Fur-
thermore, the box was transported from the climate cabinet to the laboratory in a sealed container and a tin-
foil sheet with air holes was also laid over the box to contain the bacteria further. The physical setup can be 
seen on figure 11. Only one box at a time was taken out from the climate cabinet to minimize the risk of 
cross contamination. Just after sampling the box should return to the climate cabinet as the surrounding envi-
ronment of 27 °C and humidity of 60% is important for simulation of a real production setup.

5.5.2. Method 
5.5.2.1. Day -3 normalization of mealworms 
Seven cups (A= 44.15 cm2, h = 11.5 cm) were prepared by adding 25 g of substrate. 25 g of mealworms was 
then added to each cup. The mealworms were fed 10 g of spent grain for each cup. The two boxes and the 
negative control cups were placed in the climate cabinet with temperature around 27 °C and humidity around 
50 %.    
5.5.2.2. Contaminating samples day 0 
On day 0 the cups were contaminated. Listeria monocytogenes 7378 and Salmonella Enterica Typhimuri-
umRif were prepared as in prep 04. The spent grain feed to the mealworms on day 0 was contaminated to 
reach the concentrations 102, 104 and 106 CFU/g in the cups in each group.  
5.5.2.3. Sampling 
Four tubes were prepared for duplicate samples of the mealworms and substrate. A sterile tweezer was used 
to transfer 1 ± 0.05 g of mealworm to the mealworm tubes. A single use table spoon was used to transfer 1 ± 
0.05 g of mixed substrate and spent grains to substrate tubes, as seen in figure 13. This was done for all 7 
cups. 
 
The mealworm tubes were then washed one time in 1 ml Sodium hypochlorite (prep07) and vortexed for 
1 minute and then rinsed one time in 1 ml NaCl (prep07) and vortexed for one minute. Mealworms in tubes 
were then homogenized with a rod mortar inside the tube. The rod mortar was sterilized in virkon s (OU 
PONT 17080255). To the tubes was added 9 ml of dilution fluid (prep03). The tubes were vortexed for 10-20 
seconds to mix the dilution. 
 
The feed tubes were also added 9 ml dilution fluid (prep03). The tubes were vortexed for 10-20 seconds 
to mix the dilution. The sampling was then repeated for all the cups. Sampling was done on day 0, 1, 2, 4 and 
7 in the experiment.

that the colonies seen on the plates are indeed Salmonella. 
5.6. Statistical analysis 
 The observed concentration from the exposer experiment was analyzed with an ANOVA test. Only data for 
Salmonella was analyzed as the Listeria results are too questionable to do an analysis on. The following 
three null hypotheses were set up to be checked for in the ANOVA analysis. H1: That the means of the con-
centrations observed in mealworms or substrates are the same in the different spike groups, H2: That the 
means of observed concentration over time are the same, and H3: There is no interaction between spiked 
concentration and time. The Analysis was done in the plugin data analysis, ANOVA two-factor with replica-
tion in Microsoft Excel. The alpha is set to 0.05 and there are two samples for each day, therefore rows per 
sample are set to 2. The F, Fcrit and P value were used to accept or reject the hypotheses.  If F ˃ Fcrit the hy-
potheses are rejected. If F < Fcrit the hypotheses are accepted. If P value ˃ 0.05 the hypotheses are accepted 
and if P value ˂ 0.05 the hypotheses are rejected.

7. Discussion 
To investigate if the mealworms would be safe to consume after exposure to the Listeria and Salmonella, a 
method was made to detect the mentioned bacteria in the mealworms. The purpose was to investigate if the 
Listeria or Salmonella will accumulate in the mealworm or whether it will pass through the mealworm. To 
investigate this, a method was established by testing different selective plates and different ways to remove 
external bacteria were tested. The established method was combined with a mealworm production environ-
ment with different concentrations of the bacteria to investigate the concentration of Listeria and Salmonella 
in a 7-day period.  
7.1. Selective plates with the best recovery of Listeria and Salmonella in mealworms 
The selective plates were tested for their ability to suppress mealworm gut microbiota in high quantities and 
detect target bacteria in low quantities. Initially there were only two plates tested for Salmonella and Listeria, 
but both plates (BGA and XLD) for Salmonella failed to meet minimum criteria of finding 102 CFU/g Sal-
monella in the recovery test. Therefore, Na Rif was introduced together with two species of salmonella that 
were resistant to Rifampicin. 
 
The ability to suppress the mealworm gut microbiota was done in two different dilutions 10-1 and 10-2. 
PALCAM and NaRif were selective enough to suppress all of the gut microbiota the mealworm dilution 10-1. 
Oxford, BGA and XLD were not able to suppress all the gut microbiota, with BGA plates as the least selec-
tive and Oxford as the most selective of the three plates.  
 
The detection limit testing was done for six different concentrations from 102 - 107 CFU/g, as seen in 
tables 5 and 6. BGA detected Salmonella above 106 CFU/g but the results were unreliable, as the amount of 
detected Salmonella colonies increased when the mealworm matrix got diluted. This indicates that BGA 
plates were not selective enough and the mealworm needed to get diluted for the Salmonella colonies to be 
detectable. This would prove ineffective for low concentrations of Salmonella as this would dilute the Sal-
monella to undetectable levels.  
 
XLD plates were not able to detect Salmonella ˂ 104 CFU/g. At 107 CFU/g and 106 CFU/g XLD recov-
ered around 108 CFU/g and 107 CFU/g. But the lower the concentration the less XLD recovered, as in the 
contaminated concentration of 6.5 x 104 XLD only recovered 1 x 103 CFU/g as shown in table 5. This indi-
cates that XLD ability to recover the inoculated Salmonella is inaccurate and it had a low recovery.  
 
Na Rif plates were able to detect Salmonella in all concentrations tested, as seen in table 5. The recovering 
ability was different for the two Rifampicin resistant Salmonella. As for S. e. Typhimurium Rif+Nal the Na Rif 
plate recovered for 107 CFU/g and 106 CFU/g a slightly higher concentration than inoculated. For S. e. 
Typhimurium Rif, Na Rif recovered around the same amount of bacteria as the inoculated amount.  
 
The plate with the best detection and selectivity was Na Rif with no CFU in the selectivity test and Na Rif 
was able to detect Salmonella in all concentrations. The best and most accurate recovery was found to be 
with S. e. Typhimurium Rif bacteria. Therefore, the Na Rif and S. e. Typhimurium Rif were used moving for-
ward.  
 
The Oxford plate was not able to detect 102 CFU/g Listeria for any of the Listerias tests, as seen in table 
6. For ˂ 103 CFU/g the recovery was off with a factor 10, from ˃ 104 CFU/g the recovery was more accurate. 
 
PALCAM was able to detect Listeria for all concentrations tested. The recovery was most accurate on 
Listeria 7378 where it finds 1.2 x 102 CFU/g at 1.5 x 102 CFU/g. For Listeria 6793 it finds 6.9 x 101 CFU/g 
at 1.2 x 102 CFU/g.  
 
The plate with the best selectivity and recovering ability was PALCAM with no CFU in the selectivity 
test, and PALCAM could detect all concentrations of Listeria. The bacteria with the best and most accurate 
recovery were Listeria 7378. Therefore, the PALCAM and Listeria 7378 were used for further experiments.  
==PAGE:43==
[43] 
 
 
 
According to the ISO standards 6579, the XLD is the primary plate for detection of Salmonella, where 
BGA is more for secondary plating [ISO 6579 2002]. This also matches the data from table 5 and the selec-
tive data that XLD is more selective and better at detecting the Salmonella than BGA. XLD was used in 
2012 to selective counts of Salmonella Typhimurium 101 – 107 CFU/g concentrations in human feces [La-
lander et. al 2013]. Lalander et. al. (2013) results matched what was found in the recovery experiment for the 
high concentration, but XLD was not able to suppress the background flora in recovery experiment in the 
low concentrations of ≤ 103 CFU/g.  
  The use of an antibiotic plate with the concentration of 50 µg /ml Rifampicin for selection of Salmonella 
has been shown to work before. Nordentoft et. al. (2017) uses a BABRif plate with a concentration of 50 µg 
/ml Rifampicin to suppress the background flora of Musca domestica larvae (flies). They were able to detect 
Salmonella Enteritidis (9978239) concentration down to 1 CFU/g [Nordentoft et. al. 2017]. The NaRif plate 
was tested to detect in a low concentration of 102 CFU/g, as seen in table 5.  
  According to the ISO, 11290-1 PALCAM and Oxford can be used equally for the purpose of detection 
[ISO 11290-1 1997]. None of the media have proven to be superior compared to each other and the plates are 
chosen out of preference [Farber and Peterkin 1991]. However, this study shows a clearer difference between 
Oxford and PALCAM, both in the recovery and selectivity test. Oxford was not able to repress the entire 
background flora as it cannot suppress Staphylococci or Enterococci and Wynants et. al. (2017) found those 
genera in mealworms. Therefore, PALCAM was superior of Oxford in suppression of mealworm back-
ground flora and recovering the Listeria in a mealworm matrix.   
7.2. Removal of external microbiota 
The disinfection method was evaluated on the ability to remove the added external bacteria from the surface 
of the mealworm. The method was also evaluated on the effect it had on the internal microflora, as any effect 
on the internal bacteria was unwanted. It was desired to find a method with the ability to remove all external 
bacteria but have no effect on the internal microflora.  
 
The results from the two disinfection experiments were very inconclusive, as seen in table 7. The first set 
of experiments showed that 1 x sodium hypochlorite + 1 x NaCl and 1 x sodium hypochlorite + 2 x NaCl had 
removed all Listeria, as there was no CFU on the plates. But in the second experiment the same two disinfec-
tions were less effective with the highest CFU counts, as 1 x sodium hypochlorite + 1 x NaCl had 261 CFU 
the highest CFU and 1 x sodium hypochlorite + 2 x NaCl the second highest with 200 CFU.  
 
For removal of Salmonella the same was seen as in the Listeria results, that a disinfection method with a 
promising CFU count in the first experiment (78 CFU), failed to remove the bacteria in the second experi-
ment (494 CFU). On the other hand, the result showed that disinfection had an effect, as the No wash and 1 x 
NaCl had between 483 – 102+ CFU counts. And almost all the disinfection methods had below 275 CFU 
counts with two exceptions in the first and second experiment for removal of Salmonella with a count of 103 
and 494 CFU respectively. This also indicates that the disinfection agent was the sodium hypochlorite and 
NaCl only served as rinsing agent.  
  None of the disinfection methods had notable effects on the internal microbiota. It is seen that in the first 
trial of the three disinfection methods: 1 x sodium hypochlorite + 1 x NaCl, 3 x sodium hypochlorite + 1 x 
NaCl and 1 x sodium hypochlorite + 2 x NaCl had visible less CFU, but this was not the case in the second 
replication. As the harsher disinfection method had no visible effect, it is noted as a microbial uncertainty but 
further experiments should be conducted to conclude anything. 
  As the results indicated, the disinfection had an effect but there was no consistency on the amount of 
times the disinfection agent or rinsing agent was used and removed bacteria. The simplest disinfection com-
bination of 1 x sodium hypochlorite + 1 x NaCl was chosen for further experiments. 
==PAGE:44==
[44] 
 
 
  Both agents have been used before for disinfection and rinsing of the fly Musca domestica [Nordentoft et. 
al. 2017; Bahrndorff et. al. 2014].  None of these studies had commented on the effect of the disinfection in 
the article. It is therefore an indication that the sodium hypochlorite is an accepted disinfection agent for 
removal of external bacteria from insects. Crippen and Sheffield (2006) tried 11 different disinfection proto-
cols on mealworm beetles (Alphitobius diaperinus) with three primary chemicals; EtOH, NaOCl and H2O2. 
The worst cleaning method they found was 95% EtOH and H2O with only 48% of the CFU being removed. 
The best method they found was either 95% EtOH-evaporation and H2O2 or 95% EtOH-evaporation and 
H2O2/peracetic where 100% of the CFU was removed [Crippen and Sheffield 2006]. Crippen and Sheffield 
(2006) used beetles and not the worms for their disinfection protocols. Crippen et. al. (2018) tried to disinfect 
the lesser mealworm (Alphitobius diaperinus). They used 95% EtOH as disinfection agent and sterile H2O as 
rinsing agent. They compared the disinfected lesser mealworm with unwashed lesser mealworm. Crippen et. 
al. (2018) had worms living in spiked manure where the unwashed samples had a lower CFU than the disin-
fected samples with one exception. The removal of external bacteria is possible on beetles but has not been 
shown successful in worms yet.   
7.3. Observed concentration of Listeria and Salmonella in mealworm and substrate  
To understand the interaction between the mealworm and the inoculated bacteria in the substrate to see if the 
inoculated bacteria accumulate or pass through the mealworm, the concentration of the inoculated bacteria 
was observed over a period of 7 days. This was done by using the selective plates; Na Rif and PALCAM. In 
an attempt to only observe the internal concentration of Salmonella or Listeria, the mealworm was disinfect-
ed with 1 x sodium hypochlorite + 1 x NaCl.  
 
The observed concentrations for both mealworms and substrates in the different spiked levels for Salmo-
nella were compared in an ANOVA test. No statistical analysis was done for the Listeria results, due to the 
fact that high quantities of data points was either estimated or not found. 
 
Dead and sick mealworms were observed during the first experiment, as shown in table 9. The negative 
control group had approximately the same amount of sick and dead mealworms as the highest concentration 
of Listeria and Salmonella. This indicates, together with the fact that none of the mealworms got sick in the 
second experiment, that the sickness was not caused by the inoculated bacteria.  
 
The other experiment also had some complications as most of the mealworms were almost going to pupa 
stage and some of the cups ended up with beetles that also were eating the spent grain. Another problem was 
the climate cabinet which had some issues, as seen in figures 22 and table 14, with the humidity ending up 
on almost 90 %. This combined with the inactive worms that did not eat the spent grain made the spent grain 
mold. The mold dominated the plates and made it impossible to read the results from the affected cups. In 
appendix N the cup containing mold is shown and a plate overgrown with mold from the cup is also shown 
there. The mold contaminated cups were the negative control cup and the cups spiked with Listeria. Accord-
antly, this is seen in Listeria data for day 7 in figure 21 a-f. This is also seen in table 12 and 13 where the day 
7 observed concentrations all are noted as not possible to detect in the second replication, as the plates could 
not be counted due to mold growth.  
7.3.1. Observed concentration of Salmonella Enterica Typhimurium Rif 
The ANOVA test was conducted on observed concentrations of Salmonella Enterica Typhimurium Rif in both 
mealworms and substrate.  
 
The observed concentrations for all the spiked levels in mealworms are seen in figure 20 (a), (c) and (e). 
The ANOVA test for the first and second replication for observed concentration in mealworm is seen in table 
15 and 17. For the first replication seen on table 15 it was found that the observed concentration was differ-
ent at different spike levels and that the observed concentration in the mealworm at different times showed 
==PAGE:45==
[45] 
 
 
no significant changes. There was no significant interaction between the observed concentration in meal-
worms over time and spiked levels. In table 17 the ANOVA test for the second replication is shown. The 
ANOVA test for the second replication is similar to the first replication. This indicates that the observed 
concentrations in mealworms are dependent on the spiked levels. There were no tendencies in the observed 
concentration in the mealworms over time; the ANOVA tests establish this as both P values are above 0.5. 
The lack of effect over time may be due to large standard deviation that is observed in table 10 to be above 1. 
The ANOVA tests establish that there is no interaction in the time and spiked level which means that there is 
no pattern in the observed concentration. This indicates that the observed concentration in the mealworms 
increased in concentration if the spiked concentration increased but was unpredictable if the observed con-
centration in the mealworm would slightly increase or decreases over time. Therefore, there was not ob-
served any pattern and by that no interaction on the effect of the observed concentration between spiked level 
and time. 
 
The observed concentrations in the substrate are seen in figure 20 (b), (d) and (f). The ANOVA test was 
also done for the first and second replication for substrate, as seen in table 16 and 18. For the first replication 
the ANOVA test in table 16 established that the observed concentration over each spiked level is different, 
the observed concentration over time is different and there is no interaction between observed concentration 
over time and spiked levels. The second replication ANOVA test is found in table 18 and established the 
same as in first replication except that the observed concentration over time is the same. The ANOVA tests 
confirm that the cups spiked with different concentrations had different observed concentrations. The first 
replication had a significant change of concentration over time, as the P value is 0.03. This significance is not 
observed in the second replication with the P value being above 0.1. This indicates that there is a pattern for 
observed concentration over time, but the pattern is not statistically significant for the second replication. 
The ANOVA tests establish that there are no significant interactions between observed concentration over 
time and spiked level but, the P value for the second replication is 0.09. The observed standard deviation for 
both replications is mostly below 1.  
 
This indicates that the observed concentrations in mealworms are mostly random, but the observed con-
centrations in the substrate are following a pattern. This is backed up by the variation of the observed con-
centrations. The variation of observed concentrations between trial replicates and the duplicates from the 
same experiment is observed be low for the substrate, or almost no variation at all (figure 20 (d)). The varia-
tion of observed concentrations between replicates and the duplicates from the same experiment is observed 
to be more varying in the mealworms than in the substrate.  
 
No other studies were found where the observation of Salmonella concentration in mealworms over a 
time period of 2 days was the topic. One study was found, that looked into concentration of Salmonella over 
a time period of 24 hours in mealworm beetles and a couple of studies was found that observed Salmonella 
concentration over different time periods in different fly species. 
 
Crippen et. al. (2018) observed that after 24 hours of exposure to Salmonella, the internal Salmonella 
concentration in the beetle (Alphitobius diaperinus) was increased to around 103 CFU/beetle after it was 
exposed to 103 CFU/ml and 105 CFU/ml Salmonella. The observed concentration in the beetle after 4 hours 
of exposure was 101 CFU/beetle and 102 CFU/g for spiked concentration 103 CFU/ml and 105 CFU/ml re-
spectively [Crippen et. al. 2018]. The results seen in table 10 show the observed concentration in mealworms 
in exposure 104 CFU/g for 0 hours was around 102 CFU/g and after 24 hours it was 103-104 CFU/g. This 
indicates that the observed concentration in mealworms matches what is found in Crippen et. al. (2018) as 
the observed concentration seen in exposure 104 CFU/ml was around the concentration Crippen et. al. (2018) 
found in exposure 103 and 105 CFU/beetle around the same time frame. 
 
To compare the Salmonella observed concentration over time in mealworms or substrate, the studies had 
a substrate (feces from either animals or humans) contaminated with Salmonella and fly larvae living in the 
==PAGE:46==
[46] 
 
 
medium. The flies used were often in maggot stage and varied from study to study; some of them used were 
Hermetia illucens (black soldier flies) Musca domestica (House fly) Phaenicia sericata (green bottle fly). 
The studies showed that the concentration of Salmonella in the feces and maggots was going from 106-108 
down to non-detectable levels after 4-8 days [Greenberg and Klowden, 1972; Lalander et. al. 2013; Lalander 
et. al. 2014 Nordentoft et. al. 2017]. Neither was the case in this study as both the concentration of Salmonel-
la in mealworms and substrate did not decrease noticeable much over time.  Nordentoft et. al. (2017) showed 
with 6 replicates that after 4 days the Salmonella was under the detection limit in both the maggots and in the 
feces. Lalander et. al. (2014) recontaminated the feces each week but Salmonella concentration was under 
the detection limit when they tried to recover it during the same week. This indicates that the maggots inacti-
vate Salmonella and mealworms do not, as seen in this studies that after 7 days the observed concentration is 
around the same concentration as day 0 in the substrate.  
7.3.2. Observed concentration of Listeria 7378 
Not only was the data point of day 7 in the second replication uncountable due to mold the PALCAM plates 
were not selective enough during the first experiment which led to overgrown plates, as seen in appendix O. 
It was attempted to count these plates, but not all were countable. Therefore, the results from these plates 
were the reason for the Listeria results to be uncertain, as not all Listeria was visible or able to emerge into 
colonies. Therefore, ANOVA was not done for further comparison of the results. The microbial counts were 
only evaluated on the observed concentrations between the replicate and the duplicate samples from the same 
experiment.  
 
For all three observed concentrations seen in mealworms it is observed that for all detected CFU/g meal-
worms had no consistent pattern. The three graphs seen in figure 21 (a), (c) and (e) had variants between the 
two replications. There are very few duplicates in the individual experiments, but the standard deviation ob-
served is less than 1. This argues that the duplications observed concentration is the same, but this is not 
observed in the different replications. This indicates a difference in the mealworm feed intake and thereby 
the uptake of Listeria. The first batch of mealworms was sick, and the second batch of mealworms was al-
most at pupa stage. This could have an effect on the eating habits. The difference between replication of the 
observed concentration could also be explained by the estimation of CFU/g as the PALCAM plates appeared 
less selective in this replication. 
 
The observed concentration in the substrate for all three spiked level for two of the three graphs in figure 
21 (b), (d) and (f) do not vary for the replications. The standard deviation for the duplicates varies a little but 
most of the standard deviations are below 1. This indicates that the observed concentration is not random 
values when both duplicates and replicates affirm each other.  
 
The observed concentration for both mealworms and substrate seems to be dependent on the spiked con-
centration.  
7.4. Overall comparisons  
 
Based on Nordentoft et. al. (2017) and Lalander et. al. (2013) results, it was expected to have observed a 
decrease in concentration in both the mealworms and the substrate. This was not what was observed, instead 
both the mealworms and the substrate had detectable levels of Salmonella and Listeria that both decreased 
and increased over the 7-day period 
 
Lalander et. al. (2013) found that Salmonella a gram-negative bacterium rapidly deceases in concentration 
when living in feces and fly larvae. But Enterococcus a gram-positive bacterium did not decease under the 
same conditions as the Salmonella. This indicates that gram-negative and gram-positive bacterium behave 
differently when eaten by insects. As there is a lack of statistical data for Listeria it is hard to compare the 
two experiments. But there is not observed an obvious difference in the two experiments.

The observed concentration in the substrates spiked with Salmonella indicates that the chosen selective 
plates Na Rif worked. 
 
The somewhat random behavior of the mealworm concentration observed in both Listeria 102, 104 and 
106 CFU/g and Salmonella 102, 104 and 106 CFU/g could be caused by uncertainty of the disinfection step. 
As the disinfection method only removed some of the external bacteria and the removed amount of bacteria 
varied from disinfection to disinfection, as seen in table 7. This is backed by the mealworm time 0 sample in 
figure (20 (a) and (c) 21 (a), (c) and (e)) not starting at 0 CFU/g or spiked concentration. As the samples for 
Salmonella and Listeria were taken right after spiking the substrate, the mealworm would not have time to 
eat the spiked substrate before sampling. This indicates that the disinfection step removed some of the exter-
nal bacteria but not all, as mealworms were sampled just after spiked feed was added. Therefore, there 
should not be any internal Listeria or Salmonella at time 0. But the amount of removed external bacteria was 
also varying, an example of this is seen in figure 20 (a) for spiked concentration 106 Salmonella. Here is 104 
CFU/g removed in the disinfection step for the first replication as the observed concentration in time 0 is 102 
CFU/g. For the second replication only around 102 CFU/g is removed as there is observed 104 CFU/g at time 
0. This indicates that the variation of the amount of removed bacteria has a large variation which can give the 
concentrations observed in the mealworms in figure (20 (a) and (c) 21 (a), (c) and (e)). 
 
As mentioned, the PALCAM plates were not selective enough during the first replication and late in the 
second replication, especially when the cups were contaminated with mold. The PALCAM that was tested in 
“Determination of background flora in mealworm” had no problem suppressing the background flora. But 
this was not the case later on. There could be three things that cause this difference in plate selectivity. First 
the plates could have expired or have been treated wrongly to reduce the selectivity of the plate. Secondly 
different powders from different companies were used to create the plates which could have influenced the 
final selectivity. Lastly the mealworm gut microbiota or environment could have been different enough from 
the first mealworm tested so that the tested matrix consisted of two completely different microbiotas. The 
sickness from which the worms suffered in the first replication and the mold in the second replication are 
two examples of what could cause the microbiota to be different from the original selectivity test. It could 
also be a combination of the three that gave the overgrown plate in the first replication of the exposure exper-
iment. 
 
The length of the experiment and the time between samples are also two factors that could be adjusted to 
better understand the interaction between observed concentration of Listeria and Salmonella and the meal-
worms. But as nothing really happened to the observed concentration it indicates that sampling was done 
outside the concentration changes. Crippen et. al. (2018) showed that the internal concentration rises to 104 
CFU/g in only 24 hours. This indicates that the uptake of Salmonella and Listeria happens within the first 
day. This study indicates the decreases in Listeria and Salmonella concentration happens after 7 days.

8. Conclusion  
The aim of this study is to answer if mealworms Tenebrio molitor are safe to consumption if fed contaminat-
ed substrate. For the purpose of investigation mealworms was exposed to Salmonella enterica Typhimurium 
Rif and Listeria monocytogenes and then the internal concentration was retrieved by disinfection of the meal-
worms and plated on selective plates. The mealworms lived in conditions of 27 °C and humidity around 60 
% and were fed spent grain to replicate a normal production environment. 
  The selective plates Na Rif and PALCAM were used to retrieve Salmonella enterica Typhimurium Rif and 
Listeria monocytogenes respectively. Furthermore, a disinfection method was tested to remove external bac-
teria on the mealworms’ surface. Na Rif plates were able to suppress the background flora and recover 102 
CFU/g from a 102 CFU/g exposure of Salmonella enterica Typhimurium Rif during both the detection limit 
trails and during the exposure test in the mealworm. PALCAM plates were able to suppress the background 
flora and recover 102 CFU/g from a 102 CFU/g exposure of Listeria monocytogenes during the detection 
limit trails and the second replication of the exposure test in the mealworm. None of the tested disinfection 
methods were superior to the others, and none of them showed any promising results as to remove all exter-
nal bacteria.  Nonetheless, the disinfection method had an effect when compared to the control group and the 
not disinfected but rinsed group.   
 
The observed concentration of Salmonella enterica Typhimurium Rif has a significant difference in ob-
served concentration in both mealworm and substrate over the different spiked levels. Therefore, the spiked 
levels had an effect on the observed concentration of Salmonella enterica Typhimurium Rif in the meal-
worms. The observed concentration for Listeria monocytogenes was unreliable but it was indicated that the 
concentration of Listeria monocytogenes in mealworms and substrate depends on the spiked level.  
 
In conclusion Na Rif is recommended for further research on the subject, whereas PALCAM is recom-
mended even though it was unreliable during the first replication of the observation of concentration in 
mealworms as it was reliable in both the selective test and in the exposure experiment. The disinfection 
method is not recommended for further research as it does not remove all external bacteria. The unreliability 
of the disinfection makes it uncertain if the observed concentration in the exposure experiment reflects the 
external concentration or rather a combination of the internal and external concentration. The statistic estab-
lished that the concentration for Salmonella enterica Typhimurium Rif was depending on the spiked concen-
tration. These observations indicate that the mealworms would not be safe for consumption when exposed to 
pathogens as the whole mealworm is meant for consumption. Even though more research needs to be con-
ducted for a final conclusion it is according to these observations not recommended to give substrate with a 
high risk of microbial hazard to the mealworms, as the mealworms do become contaminated for a period of 
at least 7 days.  
8.1. Further investigation  
To finalize the conclusion of the safety of consuming of mealworms given contaminated substrate, further 
research needs to be conducted. Finding a reliable method to disinfection of the external surface of the 
mealworms that does not remove internal microbiota is needed. For finding a reliable disinfection method it 
is recommended to take basis in Crippen et. al. (2006) research on this subject.   
 
Furthermore, the timeline for the exposure study should be adjusted so sampling is performed 3-4 times 
within the first 24 hours, and it is recommended that the sampling of mealworms continues every 2-3 days 
for a longer period to find when the observed concentration of Salmonella enterica Typhimurium Rif and 
Listeria monocytogenes decrease.
