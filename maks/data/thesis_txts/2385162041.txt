Summary
Registration of incoming documents is a costly affair and time consuming.
It is
estimated that the cost per invoices can be as high as 8$. This cost is mainly due to
man hours spent in data entry. By automating the process, businesses can allocate
these resources for other, more important tasks.
Assuming that for every invoice all the relative information is known, this study
investigates the automatic categorization of every line (transaction) and registration
into an Enterprise Resource Planning system. The scope of the project is consisted
of two parts. The first part is the selection of a reliable model for the categorization
of the transactions. Thereafter, on the second part, this model is integrated into
Microsoft Dynamics NAV.
Special attention is given to logistic regression framework, as different settings
of the algorithm as well as, different combinations of the input features are tested.
Further, a new version of the logistic regression algorithm is proposed to face issues
with over-fitting. This version exploits the sparsity structure of the input matrices
that is a result of transforming a nominal feature, taking many different values, with
the one hot encoding.
Finally, a fair amount of time was spent on familiarizing with Microsoft Azure
Machine Learning studio, which was used for deploying two web services; one for
training a model and one for predictions.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
The history of accounting or accountancy is thousands of years old and can be traced
to ancient civilizations. The early development of accounting dates back to ancient
Mesopotamia, and is closely related to developments in writing, counting, money and
early auditing systems by the ancient Egyptians and Babylonians [1].
According to Steven Brag in [2] there are seven different types of accounting noted
below:
• Financial accounting:
This field is concerned with the aggregation of financial information into external
reports. Through this process several reports are generated for people outside
the company. These reports mainly document how a business is doing for a
given period and they are used when the company is seeking for a loan or an
investment.
• Public accounting:
This field investigates the financial statements and supporting accounting sys-
tems of client companies, to provide assurance that the financial statements
assembled by clients fairly present their financial results and position.
• Government accounting:
This field uses a unique accounting framework to create and manage funds,
from which cash is disbursed to pay for a number of expenditures related to the
provision of services by a government entity.
• Forensic accounting:
This field involves the reconstruction of financial information when a complete
set of financial records is not available.
• Management accounting:
This field is concerned with the process of accumulating accounting information
for internal operational reporting. Management accounting generates detailed
reports for the business decision makers of the company.
• Tax accounting:
This field is concerned with the proper compliance with tax regulations, tax

filings, and tax planning to reduce a company’s tax burden in the future. It
ensures that the company obeys the tax laws and it is not paying any more
taxes than it should be.
• Internal auditing:
This field is concerned with the examination of a company’s systems and trans-
actions to spot control weaknesses, fraud, waste, and mismanagement, and the
reporting of these findings to management.
From the perspective of a company, unless it is an accounting consultant company,
accounting involves the following five processes: Bookkeeping, Financial accounting,
Management accounting, Tax accounting and Internal auditing.
Public and Gov-
ernment accounting are not relevant in this case, while the Forensic accounting is
replaced with Bookkeeping. The Bookkeeping process records the financial effects of
business transactions.
In the normal course of business, a document is produced each time a transaction
occurs. Sales and purchases usually have invoices or receipts. Bookkeeping firstly
involves recording the details of these source documents into multi column journals.
For example, all credit sales are recorded in the sales journal; all cash payments are
recorded in the cash payments journal. Secondly it involves the process of posting
a journal line into the relevant accounts. Usually the double entry system is used,
meaning that every business transaction will involve two accounts (or more). If a
company pays 200$ for an advertisement, its Cash account will decrease and its
account Advertising Expense will increase. In this case, the first account indicates
which resources ware used, while the second account indicates how they were used.
Note that every other process depends heavily on Bookkeeping. The more accurate
each transaction is recorded and classified the better the resulting reports/decisions
are going to be.
Furthermore, all the modern accounting software (or Enterprise
Resource Planning software) have all these processes automated except Bookkeeping
and Internal auditing.
1.2
Problem Formulation
Businesses need to hire a bookkeeper (or usually an accountant) to manually register
and audit every transaction. According to [3] even though using an accounting soft-
ware system dramatically decreases the cost per Invoice from 8 euro to 1.89 there is
still a lot of space for improvement. This is because this cost is a result of man hours
spent on registering these documents into the system. One has to fill several fields
for every document, such as the two parties involved (buyer-seller), the date of the
transaction, the due date of the payment, a short description of the item or service
that was exchanged or provided, it’s quantity and finally the total amount.
In order to reduce this cost, we need a system to automatically analyze each trans-
action and decide in which account each transaction should to be posted. Imagine

1.3
Assumptions
Businesses tend to use the same supplier again and again and they rarely seek for an
alternative. Further, if they do look for an alternative supplier, it is more likely to
use one they already do business with and trust.
A supplier is expected to provide similar products or services to a given company.
Thus, only a small set of accounts is expected to be used for every supplier.
Furthermore, the transaction’s description is expected to be informative, as it is
the main resource a human would use to classify it. What is more, ERP systems
usually offer a functionality of matching a specific text to an account.
This fact
strengthens our intuition regarding the importance of the transaction’s description.
Last but not least, all the details of each transaction are assumed known, either
by Optical Character Recognition (OCR) or manually typed.
1.4
Objectives
The research hypothesis can be formulated as follows: The matching between accounts
and transactions, accountants use to complete this task, can be discovered by a clas-
sification algorithm. The main subject of this project is to explore the opportunity
of applying classification models for assisting the invoice registration process. Ana-
lyze the procedures that should be used to get satisfactory classification results and
reliability. Special attention is dedicated to the preprocessing and feature selection
steps.
A successful result of this thesis would be an algorithm, that would reduce the
work load of the bookkeeper significantly. Given the transaction details, the algorithm
should return the account number and a confidence level. For the system to be reliable
I would expect that, when it is confident about a mapping it’s accuracy would be
high. In addition, this needs to happen frequently enough. Therefore, the following
criteria should be satisfied. The final algorithm should give a confident answer (with
a probability ≥80%) for at least 50% of the test data. In addition, it’s accuracy for
this subset of observations must be greater than 90%. Finally, for the non-confident
predictions the right account should be on the top 5 accounts with accuracy greater
than 90%.

1.5
Limitations
The mapping we are looking for is different from company to company. For example,
gas expenses for a consultancy could be classified as a travel expense, while for a
transportation company as an operating expense. Thus, the resulting model cannot
be used for any other company than the one provided the data. Although it is possible
to train a model for companies of a specific industry, given that they use a similar
accounting schema and a set of accounts. This is not something unusual in practice,
as the majority of the accounting software offer a set of accounts per industry and
people do not tend to heavily alter this schema.
Finally, only one algorithm is thoroughly investigated (Logistic Regression). The
overall strategy followed was to investigate a simple linear model, which is easy to
interpret and gradually improve it.

2.1
Feature transformations
Most of Machine Learning algorithms, if not all, have limitations on the kind of
features they can process.
In order to use a certain type of feature in a certain
algorithm, a feature transformation may be necessary.
2.1.1
Feature Types
The authors in [4] define four types of attributes: Nominal, Ordinal, Interval and
Ratio.
• Nominal:
The feature takes values from a specific set and only uniqueness matters.
• Ordinal:
The feature takes specific values and there is a natural order of those values.
• Interval:
The feature takes a range of values it is ordered and the relative magnitude of
the variable has a physical meaning.
• Ratio:
The difference of two values and the value 0 of the variable have a physical
meaning.
2.1.2
Normalization
Normalization is applied on an interval and ratio features. To normalize a feature, we
subtract the mean and divide with the standard deviation. This way the new feature
will have mean 0 and standard deviation of 1.

2.2.1.2
Minimizing J
The minimum of J is achieved where it’s derivative is equal to 0.
Unfortunately
solving for J = 0 results in a series of non-linear equations and cannot be computed
in a closed form. Newton’s method can be applied to find the solution iteratively.

2.3.3
Document Term Matrix (DTM)
The document term matrix simply contains all the above vectors.
Each row cor-
responds in a document of the collection and each column in a term.
When the
document collection is large and diverse (contains documents with a variety of topics)
this matrix presents a high degree of sparsity, since the number of distinct terms is
usually a lot higher of the average terms per document. Although the matrix can be
stored in a sparse format saving memory resources, the matrix operations that are
needed by several Machine Learning algorithms are expensive. Furthermore, the fact
that it presents a high degree of sparsity can be interpreted as it holds too little infor-
mation for its size. Thus, it is usually approximated with a lower rank representation.
In the sub sections below we present two widely used techniques, Feature Hashing and
Latent Semantic Indexing. There could be various reasons for these approximations:
• The original document term matrix is presumed too large for the computing
resources; in this case, the approximated low rank matrix is interpreted as an
approximation (a ”least and necessary evil”).
• The original document term matrix is presumed noisy: for example, anecdotal
instances of terms are to be eliminated. From this point of view, the approxi-
mated matrix is interpreted as a de-noisified matrix (a better matrix than the
original).
• The original document term matrix is presumed overly sparse relative to the
”true” document term matrix. That is, the original matrix lists only the words
actually in each document, whereas we might be interested in all words related
to each document-generally a much larger set due to synonymy.
2.3.4
Stemming
The main problem with the document term matrix is that similar words, words origi-
nate from the same root or different cases of the same word, are treated as completely

different terms. Stemming is the process of removing each words ending before we
construct the document term matrix. This way the word’s ”happen” and ”happening”
are both represented by the ”happen” term.
2.3.5
Feature Hashing
Feature hashing is a technique often used to reduce the size of the document term
matrix.
Note that due to the way it is constructed, it presents a high degree of
sparsity. Every document is a long vector with only a few non-zero elements.
As the authors in [10] state, the main idea behind the hashing trick is simple and
intuitive: instead of generating a bag-of-word feature vectors through a dictionary
that maps tokens to word indices, one uses a hash function that hashes words directly
into a feature vector of size b. The hash function h : {Strings} →[1..b] operates
directly on strings and should be approximately uniform.
A second independent hash function ξ : {Strings} →{-1, 1}, determines whether
the particular hashed dimension of a token should be incremented or decremented.
This causes the hashed feature vectors to be unbiased, since the expectation of the
noise for any entry is zero. The algorithm 1 shows a pseudo-code implementation of
the hashing trick that generates a hashed bag-of-words feature vector for a document:

2.3.7
Latent Semantic Analysis
Latent semantic analysis (LSA), also known as Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI), is
a technique in natural language processing of analyzing relationships between a set
of documents and the words they contain. LSA produces a set of concepts related

to the documents and terms.
It is important to note that this technique is used
on the Document Term Matrix and is not affected on the way this matrix is con-
structed. Therefore, it can be applied for both ”bag of words” and ”bag of character
ngrams” representations. LSA assumes that terms which are close in meaning will oc-
cur in similar pieces of text (the distributional hypothesis). With a technique called
singular value decomposition, described below, on the Document Term Matrix we
can reduce the number of columns while preserving the similarity structure among
rows(documents). In other words, we can represent each new feature(topic) as a lin-
ear combination of the old features(terms). This linear combination of terms is given
by the singular vectors of the Document Term Matrix.
Sometimes the resulting combinations are easily interpreted, for example a combi-
nation of the form 20% car + 33% truck + 17% bus + 30% bicycle can be interpreted
as ”vehicle”. This mitigates the problem of identifying synonymy, as the rank low-
ering is expected to merge the dimensions associated with terms that have similar
meanings. It also mitigates the problem with polysemy, since components of polyse-
mous words that point in the ”right” direction are added to the components of words
that share a similar meaning. Conversely, components that point in other directions
tend to either simply cancel out, or, at worst, to be smaller than components in the
directions corresponding to the intended sense. However, not all combinations make
sense as a combination of the form 20% ground + 33% flower + 17% bus + 30%
people is also possible [14].
As mentioned in [15] for any two entities present in the text collection of inter-
est, the proximity of their representation vectors in the LSA space provides a direct
measure of their degree of contextual association in the collection. This feature can
be exploited to provide rapid overviews of the aggregate implications of the relations
present in large collections of unstructured information.
The author successfully
demonstrated, in his work, that Latent Semantic Analysis can be used to identify
associations of interest in large text collections in a completely automated fashion.
This technique has several important characteristics that make it particularly well-
suited for intelligence analysis applications:
• It is independent of topic, genre, or language.
• Identified relationships represent the aggregate implications of high-order asso-
ciations.
• A textual description of an entity or concept of interest may be chosen as
a definition of an item of interest. The LSA space can be derived from the
description documents and not necessary from documents to be indexed. This
is an extremely important characteristic in our study and it is highly exploited.
Since the documents to be indexed in our study are small (50-100 characters),
they cannot be used directly to extract any relationships between the terms.
• There is no need for predefined auxiliary structures such as taxonomies or on-
tologies.

2.5
Feature selection
Surprising including a feature into the model is not always beneficial. Sometimes
the noise introduced by a new feature is greater than the signal. Thus, including a
feature can be harmful. The process of finding the optimal set of features to train
a model is called feature selection. There are two main techniques for selecting this
set, the forward selection and backward selection [4]. However, none of the above
methods is guaranteed to find the optimal feature set. They are very popular though,
because the only examine a practical feasible number of combinations, only n2 from
the overall n! possible combinations.
2.5.1
Forward selection
In forward selection the feature set is gradually increasing as long as adding a feature
leads to a better performance. We start with an empty feature set M. Hereafter,
K (the total number of features) new models are examined and evaluated with the
preferred cross validation method. Each new model uses the feature set from the pre-
vious iteration, augmented with a new feature. The feature that results by including
in the lower test error, if that is lower than before, is added to the feature set. The
process continues until there is no feature that can be added into the feature set and
reduce the test error.
2.5.2
Backward selection
Backward selection is the opposite process. Here we start with all the feature in the
feature set M. Then, a model is examined for every feature like before, with the
only difference that now this feature is removed from the feature set. The process
continues until there is no feature left that can be removed resulting in a reduced test
error.

Our dataset is composed of 55.000 purchase transactions from 2010 to 2016, each de-
scribed with 8 attributes: vendor number, description, expected receipt date, posting
date, due date, amount, account number and payment method.
3.2
Feature extraction
Sometimes it is beneficial to combine features into new features. For example, the
difference between due date and posting date shows us the payment window, how
much time we have available before we pay for the goods.
Another useful technique is to extract several features out of a feature. For exam-
ple, from the date features we can extract the features: the day of the month, day of
the week, month and year. This was only done for the Posting date. The expected
receipt date feature is totally ignored, as it gets the same values with Posting date.

Challenges
The fact that there are several different wordings for the same concept makes the text
processing challenging. All these different wordings need to be represented with the
same term in the document term matrix. Furthermore, there are a lot of compound

of the character ngrams. The variable length ngram procedure described in chapter 2

CHAPTER 4
Model Selection
In this chapter, we examine several models, different settings of the Logistic regression
algorithm, in order to find the best performing setup.
4.1
Evaluation
The measure used for the evaluation of the models is accuracy. Between two models
the one with the highest accuracy is considered better. The error of a model is defined
as:
Error = 1 −Accuracy
(4.1)
Generally, it is recommended to use K-fold cross-validation with for instance K =
10. However, the selection of the method is dependent on the size of the dataset, the
algorithmic complexity of the models and the time we can afford to spend. In this
project a two layer cross-validation was used with the hold-out method in both layers,
since with the available resources some of the models required up to 8 hours to train.
Although it is also recommended to use a random split of the dataset, a temporal
split was used. Given that we have a temporal dataset and our final goal, to predict
the billing account of feature transactions using historical data, this split felt more
natural and closer to our objective. More precisely, the transaction of the years 2010,
2011, 2012 and 2013 were used to build the training set, the transactions of the
year 2014 for the validation set and finally the transactions of the years 2015 and
2016 for the test set. The resulting sets contain 30680, 8295 and 12633 observations
respectively.
4.2
Baseline
Our baseline is defined as the model that selects the most frequent account for each
vendor, or the most frequent account if there is no training example for the current
vendor. The vendor number is a very informative feature and this simple baseline
model results in 66.83%. accuracy.

4.3
Motivation
Our analysis started with exploring Azure Machine Learning Studio and the building
blocks it provides. Several algorithms were tried including: Random Forest, Random
Jungle, Naive Bayes, Support Vector Machines, Logistic Regression and Feed Forward
Neural Network. All of the algorithms above except Logistic Regression and Neural
Network had similar performance, spanning from 60 to 70% on accuracy. What drew
our attention was the poor performance of Logistic Regression and Neural Network,
around 20% on accuracy. Apparently, the main cause of this poor performance was
the lack of preprocessing. The most serious mistakes were not transforming the vendor
number and payment method with the ”one hot encoding” and not normalizing the
numeric features. However, even after those transformations these two algorithms
were still under performing and failed to beat our simple baseline. Thus, we decided
to investigate this issue further starting with Logistic Regression as it is easier to
interpret and maybe extending to Neural Networks. Although later in the process
we decided we did not have enough training examples to efficiently train a Neural
Network.
4.4
Feature selection
In this section, the description feature is modeled with the binary representation of the
document term matrix considering words as terms. The vector space model cannot
achieve its full potential in our case, due to the nature of the documents. Words
appear only once in the documents or not at all, thus the binary representation feels
more natural.
Apart from that, the Vendor number feature, even though it is a
numeric feature needs to be treated as nominal. Thus, for the Vendor number and
Payment Method the one hot encoding transformation has been used. What is more,
the full document term matrix for the description as well as the matrices for vendor
number and payment method are treated as one feature. Further, the year feature
was left out, due to the way the dataset was split into training, validation and test
sets. Finally, the numeric features were normalized. The normalization of the numeric
features helps Logistic Regression to converge faster.
As described in chapter 2 we first consider a model with no features. This results
in predicting always the most frequent class and in an accuracy of 21.8%. Hereafter,
we consider all the models with only one feature. Note that the date features do not
seem to distinguish the classes at all. Thus, they are not considered in the subsequent
steps. Table 4.1 presents the validation errors for all the different models. The feature
that distinguish the classes the best is description, as it results in the lowest validation
error.

We believe that one of the sources responsible for the over-fitting is the high
number of parameters compared to the number of training examples. In fact, after
transforming our vendor feature with the one hot encoding technique, we end up
with more than 140,000 parameters. This is because the vendor feature takes approx-
imately 2,000 unique values, which if combined with the 70 different classes (figure
4.1(a)) will result in 140,000 parameters only for the vendor feature. And if we con-
sider that in our case the total number of examples is 55.000, it may lead in a very
complex model, that describes the noise instead of the underlying relationships.
In order to reduce the number of parameters, we can trim down the weights
that do not correspond to a pair vendor-account on the training set. We call this
version of the logistic regression, Sparse Logistic Regression.
This is achieved by
constructing a binary mask. For every pair vendor-account of the training set we set
the corresponding element in the mask to 1 while the rest are set to 0. Thereafter,
we element wise multiply this mask with the gradient matrix before each update. In
addition, the starting weights are set to zeros, this way the part of the weight matrix
that corresponds to the zero elements of the mask remains zero.
There are three main benefits for using this version of logistic regression. Firstly,
a slight improvement in the validation accuracy is observed. Secondly, the model is a
bit faster to train since the weight matrix can now be represented by a sparse matrix.
Finally, the vendor feature is no longer important in excluding classes. Normally if
there is no example pair Vendor number - account, the corresponding weight would
be negative, driving the probability of this class closer to 0. With that modification,
the contribution of the vendor variable will be zero and the prediction would be based
on the rest of the features. This way we are exploiting our intuition that if a business
has to choose an alternative vendor, it will prefer one they already do business with
and trust.

5.1
Azure Machine Learning Studio
Azure Machine Learning Studio lets you create a machine learning experiment by
combining modules together with drag and drop. These generic modules allow you
to manipulate data, initialize, train and evaluate Machine Learning algorithms and
even execute your own R code.
Once your experiment has been finalized Azure
Machine Learning Studio automatically creates a web service for you. Depending on
the modules your experiment is using the resulting web service can be stateless or
not.
Actually, if you are not executing custom R code and you are only using the
building blocks it provides, Azure Machine Learning Studio generates two linked
experiments, a training and a predictive one. The training experiment results in a
stateless web service, which every time it is invoked generates a state-full web service
for the predictive experiment linking it with the generated model. This approach
though is not scalable since there is a limit on the number of endpoints(web services)
per subscription. In our case since we need a model per company that would mean
that only an X number of companies would have access to this feature.
To overcome this issue, we followed a different approach. Two experiments were
created one for training and one for predictions. From both experiments a stateless
web service was created. The training web service gets as input the data to train the
model on and a blob name. Hereafter, it creates the model and stores it in an Azure
Blob Storage account. The predictive web service gets as input the data to make
predictions on and the blob name in which the model was saved. This way only two
endpoints are needed regardless of how many models we need to store.
Those experiments are shown in figures 5.1 and 5.2. As you can notice the exper-
iments are similar. The R code for each experiment is presented in the appendices
G and H. The scripts for preprocessing and logistic regression algorithm used in the
above scripts can be found in the appendices E and F respectively.

Proposed system
Two new features are introduced. The first to the ”Set Text To Account Mappings”
procedure on the original workflow (figure 5.3) and send the transactions to the pre-
dictive web service. The second is available at the creation of a new purchase invoice,
now the user can select a vector and only type a description for each transaction in
order to get a billing account suggestion. There are two phases for both features.
The first phase, the setup phase, collects all the historical purchase transactions and
sends them to the training web service. At this point, we need to choose a free blob
name, so we do not override any existing model. Hereafter, we can track when the
training phase is done by simply checking if the blob for the model has been written.
The second phase calls the predictive web service passing the blob name, used for
training, as a parameter and the data to predict on. Depending on the confidence
level of the prediction the whole process can be completely automated. Given our
analysis we can only trust the prediction when the confidence level is high enough.
If the confidence level is less than 80% the system prompts the user to select one of
the top five accounts as shown in figure 5.9. What is more, the prediction and the
selection of the user are saved in a database table for faster suggestions in the future
and to save computing resources. Unless the model is updated the prediction is not
going to change, if the model is updated all that is needed, is to clear this table.
Finally, the C/AL code to contact the web services in Microsoft Dynamics NAV
is presented in appendix I.

6.1
Conclusions
The final chapter of this report presents the general observations from the experiments
conducted during this research.
• Understanding the data you are working with is a very crucial step in data
mining process.
Exploiting domain knowledge is also very important.
The
main benefit of the sparse logistic regression is that it makes the vendor feature
less important in excluding classes, giving the impression of intelligence on the
second proposed feature. The classic version tends to always suggest the same
account for certain vendors, regardless what description the user types in. On
the other hand, the sparse version relies more on the description feature and
generates suggestions for accounts even though they have never been observed
before for the specific vendor.
• Sometimes combining non-beneficial techniques can be highly beneficial. For
example, the use of LSA and character ngrams were not beneficial on their own,
but when combined they offered a significant performance boost. The same
could happen with the date features that were excluded in the first iteration
of the forward feature selection. However, since the posting date is not always
available there is no point in investigating whether including them is beneficial
or not.
• The best performing model was deployed in a web service with the help of Azure
Machine Learning Studio and integrated into Microsoft Dynamics NAV.
• The proposed architecture allows for seamlessly updating the model on the
background as a periodic activity without the user to have to worry about it.
All that is needed, is to call the training web service once a critical mass of
examples has been gathered.

6 Conclusions and future work
6.2
Future Work
In this section the limitations of the whole solution and several suggestions for future
improvements are provided.
• Unarguably the biggest limitation of the suggested model is that it is language
specific. However, the whole architecture of the proposed solution is language
independent (excluding the stemming phase which it is not necessary), all that
is needed to generate a model for a different language, is to provide the training
web service with new training examples. Currently we are working in a data
gathering feature that would allow us to gather examples from different compa-
nies and different languages. Once this is complete and enough examples are
collected the suggested features could be released in Microsoft Dynamics 365
for Financials.
• The vector space model is a more robust mathematical model and its perfor-
mance should be investigated. This only makes sense in combination with the
character ngrams, as the resulting ngrams can be present more than once in a
given example.
• The combination of feature hashing and ngrams extraction could also be in-
vestigated. Even though I would not expect an improvement in the models
accuracy, there will be an improvement on training and prediction times. Thus,
it is worth considering, if the loss is not significant.
• The suggested solution is company specific, a new model must be generated
for every company and historical data must be provided. This is very limiting,
since this feature generates no value for new customers. Ideally a generic model,
generated with data from different companies, offering broad suggestions would
be more valuable. However, this could only be available for companies that
have kept the default account schema (chart of accounts). Thereafter, using
the users input(selection of the proper account for each transaction) a company
specific model can be generated in the background.
