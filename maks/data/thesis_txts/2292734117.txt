ABSTRACT
1
Abstract
The most abundant group of secondary metabolites in fugi are the polyketides. Many of
these compounds are bioactive, which has led to their use as pharmaceutials and other
valuable applications. Some of them are however toxic and can cause great damage to
human and animal health, e.g. when contaminating food and feed. The objectives of this
study was to characterise and elucidate the biosynthetic pathway of polyketide compound
Antibiotic Y and identify the role of a predicted type III polyketide synthase (PKS) in
Fusarium avenaceum.
Earlier studies have suggested a link between the biosynthetic pathway of Antibiotic
Y and PKS12, the PKS responsible for Aurofusarin. To determine if PKS12 is indeed
responsible for production of Antibiotic Y, this project aimed to created deletion strains
of PKS12, aurP and aurE and analyse these chemically. Type III PKSs have not been
characterised in F. avenaceum before, and were only recently discovered in fungi. To
investigave the role of PKSIII this project aimed to validate deletion strains from a pre-
liminary study and chemically analyse these.
A hypothetical biosynthetic pathway of Antibiotic Y was proposed, based on literature
studies. Antibiotic Y levels of F. avenaceum on diﬀerent media was compared and Bells
was found to be the best media and used for the study of Antibiotic Y levels over time.
The study over time did unfortunately have very high standard deviations, but an increase
in Antibiotic Y over time was observed.
Deletion plasmids of PKS12, aurP and aurE were created, but only ΔaurE was suc-
cessfully transformed into the fungi but not completely validated. One of the proposed
intermediates, CS-INT-1, was however identiﬁed, strengthening the proposed biosynthetic
pathway.
Additionally two unknown compounds that could possibly be related to be
biosynthesis of Antibiotic Y identiﬁed. Structural elucidation is however needed to con-
ﬁrm these ﬁndings.
Deletion strains for PKSIII were validated and chemically analysed. Chemical analysis
of the PKSIII deletion strains did not however shown any obvious changes, and further
research was not possible due to the time constraint of this project.

INTRODUCTION
4
Introduction
Fungi are known to have very diverse secondary metabolism and produce a wide array of
bioactive compounds. These natural products are called secondary metabolites because
they are not essential for growth [Dewick, 2012] but are thought to give the organisms an
advantage over competing organisms and serves functions such as signalling or chemical
warfare [Klejnstrup et al., 2012]. The impressive range of bioactivity has led to their use as
anticancer agents, antibiotics and other pharmaceutically valuable products[Smedsgaard
and Nielsen, 2005; Nielsen et al., 2011b; Liou and Khosla, 2003]. A great example of
the importance of natural products and derivatives thereof is the fact that they continue
to play a signiﬁcant role in drug discovery and makes up a great part of small-molecule
pharmaceuticals [Newman and Cragg, 2012].
Fungi are also known for their negative consequences as human pathogens and feed-
and food spoilers due to their production of mycotoxins [Smedsgaard and Nielsen, 2005].
Due to the properties of these organisms and their secondary metabolites an eﬀort has
been made to elucidate the genetic basis for the formation of these compounds [Andersen
et al., 2012]. Knowledge about these metabolites and their biosynthetic pathways can be
used to ﬁnd novel compounds, improve cell factories and improve our understanding of
their toxic properties. Polyketides are the most abundant group of fungal metabolites
and are produced by polyketide synthases [Keller et al., 2005].
As genomes has become available for many fungi, it has become clear that the metabolic
diversity and potential is greater than previously thought and a lot of discoveries are
to be made. Unfortunately it is not possible to extract all the necessary information
to predict the resulting compounds using bioinformatics alone [Keller et al., 2005]. The
elucidation of biosynthetic pathways is further complicated by the tight metabolic control
these compounds are under as many polyketides are only produced in very small amounts,
under very speciﬁc conditions or in developmental stages that are rarely observed in the
laboratory [Nielsen et al., 2011b].

BACKGROUND
5
Background
5.1
Fusarium avenaceum
Fusarium avenaceum is a plant pathogen most known as the cause of root rot and ear
blight as is among the most frequent fungal species encountered on small grains and maize
in temperate climate zones[Sørensen and Giese, 2013]. The infection with Fusarium usu-
ally results in reduction of crop yield and contamination with mycotoxins [Uhlig et al.,
2007]. Despite F. avenaceums impact on agriculture and health there has only been con-
ducted very few studies on the molecular basis of its secondary metabolism [Sørensen
et al., 2014].
F. avenaceum is known to produce many secondary metabolites including moniliformin,
aurofusarin, antibiotic Y, fusarin C, 2-amino-14,16-dimethyloctadecan-3-ol, chlamydosporol
and enniatins [Sørensen and Giese, 2013]. A genomic study has recently been conducted
on three F. avenaceum isolates by Lysøe et al. [2014]. One strain was isolated from Finnish
barley (Fa05001) and two from Canadian spring (FaLH03) and winter wheat (FaLH27).
The study showed that F. avenaceum has a very large potential for producing secondary
metabolites with between 75 and 80 enzymes belonging to the polyketide, non-ribosomal
peptide, terpene, alkaloid and indole-diterpene synthase classes. Out of these 25-27 of
these were iterative type I PKSs and 2-3 were type III PKSs [Lysøe et al., 2014].
Furthermore analysis of the genomes revealed a 91-96% alignment between the three
strains and BLASTn analysis revealed more than 95% of the predicted genes had a signif-
icant hit within the two other genomes, which suggest a high degree of similarity. However,
the three strains possibly have diﬀerences in their secondary metabolisms, as there were
instances of unique PKSs and NRPSs [Lysøe et al., 2014].
Phylogenetic studies has suggested F. avenaceum to be closely related to F. graminearum
and a reciprocal BLAST revealed 76.7% of the predicted genes in F. avenaceum 05001
genome to have orthologs in the F. graminearum genome [Lysøe et al., 2014]. In another
genomic study 10 diﬀerent Fusarium species were compared, including F. avenaceum. A
total of 52 diﬀerent PKS genes were identiﬁed, of which only two, PKS3 and PKS27,
were conserved in all the genomes. PKS8 was also found to be highly conserved and was
also found in F. avenaceum. Furthermore F. avenaceum was found to be the species that
had the largest number of PKSs and out of the 25 PKSs, 2 were unique [Hansen et al.,
2015]. These numbers are however only based on prediction of type I PKSs, as this study
did not take type III PKSs into consideration. The study by Lysøe et al. [2014] found 2-3
type III PKSs in the three genomes included in their study.

5.2
Polyketides
Polyketides is a very diverse group of molecules and can be found as small as six carbon
compunds, such as triacetic acid lactone, and bigger compounds with 164 carbons in their
chain like maitotoxin [Weissman, 2009]. Despite the great diversity within the group of
polyketides, they are all derived from poly-β-keto chains formed by claisen codensations
and assembled in a manner similar to that of fatty acid synthases (FASs) [Dewick, 2012;
Weissman, 2009; Cox and Simpson, 2012].
Polyketides are synthesized by polyketide synthases (PKSs). The PKS is typically clus-
tered together with the genes encoding transcription factor, transporters and tailoring
enzymes required for the biosynthesis of the ﬁnal compound [Frandsen et al., 2011; Weiss-
man, 2009]. The reason why PKSs produce such diverse endproducts is because unlike
FASs the PKSs are not only controlling the chainlenght, but also selection of starter and
extender units, number of iterations, the extent of reduction and dehydration, the extend
of methylation and cyclisation of the polyketide chain. Finally the polyketides can also be
fused with other compound groups, such as amino acids [Cox and Simpson, 2012; Keller
et al., 2005].
Polyketide synthases can be divided into three diﬀerent groups, according to how the
polyketides are assembled; type I, II and III [Weissman, 2009].
5.2.1
PKS type I
Type I PKSs are found in bacteria and fungi and are characterised as large multifunc-
tional proteins with individual functional domains and are found in bacteria and fungi
[Cox and Simpson, 2012; Dewick, 2012]. Type I PKSs can be subdivided into modular
and iterative. The modular PKSs consist of several modules that are used consecutively
[Dutta et al., 2014], whereas the iterative PKSs use the same module repeatedly and are
homologues to Fatty Acid Synthases (FAS)[Cox and Simpson, 2012; Dewick, 2012]. A
module is deﬁned as the active sites needed for extension of the polyketide chain by one
unit [Weissman, 2009].
A module consists of the core domains β-ketosynthase (KS), acyl transferase (AT) and
acetyl carrier protein (ACP)[Crawford and Townsend, 2010; Keller et al., 2005]. The AT
loads the starter and extender units onto the ACP that holds the growing chain and al-
lows for loading of extender units. The KS catalyses the C-C bond formation via Claisen
condensation [Crawford and Townsend, 2010].

In a FAS module there are three additional domains; ketoreductace (KR), dehydrase (DH)
and enoyl reductase (ER) that reduces the β-ketone in a three step process. In polyke-
tide biosynthesis these domains and the full reduction are only optional and the iterative
PKSs can be further subdivided into non-reducing, partially reducing or highly reducing
according to their level of reductive processing during the chain assembly [Crawford and
Townsend, 2010]. In fungi the type I PKSs are iterative and they are furthermore the the
most abundant type of PKS in fungi [Cox and Simpson, 2012; Crawford and Townsend,
2010].
5.2.2
PKS type II
Type II PKSs are composed of a complex of individual monofunctional proteins, with one
active site each [Dewick, 2012; Liou and Khosla, 2003]. All type II PKSs are iterative and
typically produce aromatic compounds. They have so far only been observed in bacteria
[Dewick, 2012].
5.2.3
PKS type III
Type III PKSs are found in bacteria, plants and fungi. They are homodimeric proteins
consisting of two identical KS monomeric domains with one active site that iteratively
performs of decarboxylation, condensation, cyclization and aromatization and uses acyl-
CoA without any auxiliary enzymes and are thus ACP independent[Austin and Noel,
2003; Crawford and Townsend, 2010; Dewick, 2012]. Type III PKSs are also known under
the name chalcone synthase-like PKSs. The chalcone synthases (CHSs) responsible for
forming ﬂavonoids in plants are a superfamily under the type III PKSs, and the most well
studied but also the most frequently occuring [Austin and Noel, 2003].
Unlike type I and II PKSs, type III PKSs utilizes a diverse set of larger starter units
[Austin and Noel, 2003]. The type III PKSs has also developed three distinct polyketide
cyclization mechanism, while maintaining their simple homodimeric structure [Austin and
Noel, 2003].
They are less abundant in fungi than type I PKSs and were only recently discovered
[Seshime et al., 2005]. Phylogenetic studies of the type III PKSs by Juvvadi et al. [2005]
showed a clear division into plant, bacterial and fungal type III PKS. Additionally the cat-
alytical residues Cys-His-Asn was shown to be conserved in the fungal type III PKS genes
and were therefore considered to be enzymatically active [Juvvadi et al., 2005; Seshime
et al., 2005].

5.2.4
Cytochrome P450 and Epoxide Hydrolases
As mentioned earlier, the PKSs are typically clustered with tailoring enzymes required
for the biosynthesis of the ﬁnal compound. Cytochrome P450 and epoxide hydrolases are
probably best known as being an important part of the catabolism of harmful metabolites
in mammals [Berg et al., 2007]. However, the two enzyme classes are also known to work
together to form ring structures in secondary metabolites, such as polyketides [Dewick,
2012].
Cytochrome P450 monooxygenase can perform epoxidations of aromats [Wong, 1998]
and epoxide hydrolase are co-factor independent and catalyses the hydrolysis of epoxides
to their corresponding vicinal diols [Barth et al., 2004; Faber et al., 1996]. Vicinal diols
are when two hydroxyl groups are placed on two adjecent atoms. The hydroxylation im-
proves solubility of the compound and facilitates secretion. An epoxide has at least one
chiral center and a ring opening can occur at either of the two carbon atoms [Spelberg
and de Vries, 2012]. The catalytic domain of a nucleophile (aspartic acid), which attacks
the epoxide and forms a covalent ester-intermediate, a catalytic histidine and a carboxylic
acid, which subsequently activate a water molecule and hydrolyze the esterbond to release
the product [Barth et al., 2004].
Figure 1: Degradation of an aromatic hydrocarbon. First an epoxide is added to by a monooxygenase.
Subsequently an epoxide hydrolase cleaves the epoxide to yield a trans-diol.
Cytochrome P450 is primarily found in the endoplasmatic reticulum of the liver and small
intestine [Berg et al., 2007]. In humans the epoxide hydrolases are membrane bound and
abundant in the liver and small intestine [Zou et al., 2000]. In plants the cytochrome
P450 systems plays a role in the biosynthesis of secondary metabolites, such as toxic com-
pounds and pigments of ﬂowers [Gonzalez and Nebert, 1990].
Both Cytochrome P450 and epoxide hydrylases are found in fungi, such as the Aspergillus
genus. The main diﬀerence between mammalian and microbial epoxide hydrolases is that
the mammalian versions has an anchor to bind them to the membrane, while the microbial
ones do not [Arand et al., 1999]. In fungi they are known to metabolise a wide variety
of polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons. Fungi such as Aspergillus niger and Phanerochaete
chrysosporium have been shown to be able to metabolise hydrocarbon rings by initiated

5.3
Secondary Metabolism in F. avenaceum
5.3.1
The Aurofusarin Cluster
Many Fusarium species appear reddish due to the production of mycelia pigments such as
Aurofusarin, Bikaverin and Fusarubin [Hansen et al., 2015]. Aurofusarin is known to be
produced by many Fusarium species; F. acuminatum, F. avenaceum, F. cerealis, F. cul-
morum, F. graminearum, F. poae, F. sambucinum, F. sporotrichiodes, F. tricinctum and
F. venenatum [Samson et al., 2010]. In F. graminearum the aurofusarin cluster consists
of 11 co-regulated genes [Frandsen et al., 2011] and PKS12 is the PKS responsible for
aurofusarin production [Kim et al., 2005; Malz et al., 2005] and YWA1 has been shown
to be the primary product of PKS12 [Frandsen et al., 2011].
The cluster is regulated by two transcriptions factors, aur1 and aur2 and encodes two
laccases (GIP1 and AurL2), a monooxygenase (AurF), an O-methyltransferase (AurJ),
an oxidoreductase (AurO)[Frandsen et al., 2006], a rubrofusarin pump (AurT), a dehy-
dratase (AurZ) and a protein of unknown function that is essential for the completion of
the pathway (AurS) [Frandsen et al., 2011]. The cluster is seen on Figure 2.
In F. avenaceum the aurofusarin cluster has two additional annotated genes; aurE and
aurP. Their functions have currently not been characterised, but they have been pre-
dicted to be an epoxide hydrolase and a cytochrome P450 class enzyme, respectively. The
Aurofusarin Cluster in F. avenaceum can be seen on Figure 3.

known either, but the inﬂuence of carbon sources has been tested. A study tested three
F. avenaceum strains, IBT 5001, IBT 40847 and IBT 8500, were all able to grow pro-

between the diﬀerent carbon sources, than in each strain, indicating that carbon source
could inﬂuence the regulation of antibiotic Y biosynthesis [Sørensen and Giese, 2013].
Another study has analysed the change in secondary metabolite production as a function

the yeast extract from one supplier (MP Biomedicals) [Sørensen and Sondergaard, 2014].

5.4
Genetic Engineering
In molecular biology a common way of characterising the function of a certain gene or
identifying the genes behind a speciﬁc phenotype is by modifying the genome. Two overall
approaches forward and reverse genetics. In the two following sections the principles of the
two approaches will be explained with typical approaches of introducing the modiﬁcations
in Fusarium.
5.4.1
Forward Genetics
The underlying principle of forward genetics is random mutagenesis.
A phenotype is
observed, compared to the wild type and the responsible genes are identiﬁed. For this
naturally occuring mutants can be used, or mutants with the desired phenotype can be
acquired from a mutant library [Snyder and Champness, 2007]. The advantage of this
strategy is that it does not require any prior knowledge or assumptions. The disadvan-
tage of this strategy is that creating a mutant library and screening it for the desired
phenotype is labour-intensive and the site of the mutation may not be an active gene or
a gene with very low expression levels.
There are several ways of creating a mutant library. One method is to induce mutations
with chemicals or UV radiation, but these tools are diﬃcult to work with. Alternatively
the gene can be disrupted by insertion of DNA into the genomic target DNA [Hensel
and Holden, 1996]. How this can be achieved depends on the recombinant DNA methods
available for the organism of interest.
Agrobacterium tumefaciens mediated transfer (ATMT) is a method for introducing DNA
into the genome of organisms such as Fusarium species F. avenaceum and F. graminearum
[Sørensen et al., 2014]. It can also be used to create random insertional libraries, where
it typically results in single insertion events. This makes it easier to assign the observed
phenotype to the right gene. Furthermore it comes with a molecular tag, that makes
it easier to identify where in the genome the insertion happened [Frandsen et al., 2011,
2012]. ATMT is however not a method that leads to truly random insertions, as it has
been show to display a bias towards promotors and the start of coding sequences. Addi-

complicating the characterisation [Frandsen et al., 2011].
5.4.2
Reverse Genetics
In reverse genetics a gene is modiﬁed and the eﬀect is analysed and compared to wild
type. In this way a function can be assigned to a gene [Snyder and Champness, 2007].
This targeted modiﬁcation of a gene requires precise molecular tools for the organism
of interest and prior knowledge to guide the design of the modiﬁcation [Frandsen et al.,
2012]. Gene targeting is however not only suitable for deletions or disruptions of genes,
it can also be used to create over expression mutants [Frandsen et al., 2011], which could
be one strategy of overcoming the challenge of inactive genes or genes with very low ex-
pression levels.
ATMT can be used for gene targeting in F. avenaceum.
The DNA can be inserted
into the genome by homologous recombination in a double crossover event between the
genomic DNA and complimentary sequence ﬂanking the selection marker [Frandsen et al.,
2012]. The gene targeting frequency and transformation frequency for ATMT in fungi
has been shown to vary, but great results has been obtained for F. graminearum and F.
oxysporum [Frandsen et al., 2011].
The targeting eﬃciency of gene targeting can be improved in several ways. One way
is to create mutants with defects in the non-homologous end joining (NHEJ) system and
force the organism to integrate the DNA with the homologous recombination system in-
stead [Krappmann, 2007; Frandsen et al., 2011]. Improvement can also be achieved by
negative selection and bipartite gen targeting. Negative selection is performed by placing
a negative selection marker outside of the homologous recombination site. The marker
will be lost if the DNA is integrated by homologous recombination, but will be inte-
grated if it is mediated by NHEJ [Frandsen et al., 2011]. Bipartite gene targeting utilises
two plasmids, where each of them contains two thirds of the selection marker and one
of the homologous recombination sites. The selection marker will only be functional if
both parts are fused and integrated by homologous recombination [Frandsen et al., 2011;
Nielsen et al., 2006].

5.5.1
Chemical Analysis of Secondary Metabolites
It can be very challenging to identify natural products, as some have identical elemental
composition. Thus, high resolution MS without authentic standards is not enough to
conﬁdently identify each metabolite [Nielsen et al., 2011a]. To solve the structure of a
compound further analysis using NMR is needed. This is however very time consuming
and labour intensive. Furthermore NMR analysis requires a pure sample of the compound
of interest [Nielsen and Larsen, 2015].
An important method when analysing HPLC-MS data i deconvolution.
This can be
used to determine if two masses belong to the same compound. By extracting the Ex-
tracted Ion Chromatogram (EIC) for each mass, the retention time of the maxima can be
compared. If the rentention time is for the two EIC are diﬀerent, then the masses belong
to two diﬀerent compounds. Another important method is dereplication. Dereplication is
a method that aims to rapidly identify all known compounds in a sample [Nielsen et al.,
2011a].
Dereplication is a powerful tool in the search of novel natural producats and can ensure
that work with isolation and structure elucidation is focused soley on novel compounds
[Cordell and Shin, 1999]. An absorption spectrum provided by a DAD can be used as a

Another important aspect of proﬁling secondary metabolites is sample preparation, as

OBJECTIVES AND HYPOTHESIS
6
Objectives and Hypothesis
This thesis is about polyketides in F. avenaceum based on two case studies of PKSIII and
Antibiotic Y, respectively. PKSIII is a predicted type III PKS in F. avenaceum. Type III
PKSs have not earlier been characterised in Fusarium, but an earlier study has predicted
the genome of F. avenaceum to contain two of these [Lysøe et al., 2014]. An objective of
this project is to identify possible products of PKSIII. This will be done by cultivating
wildtype and ΔPKSIII strains on diﬀerent media and subsequently chemically analysing
them.
Antibiotic Y is a polyketide compound produced by F. avenaceum. This project seeks to
characterise Antibiotic Y production and elucidate its biosynthetic pawthway. To charac-
terise the production of Antibiotic Y a suitable media should be identiﬁed. This project
will try to determine if PKS12 is responsible for the biosynthesis of Antibiotic Y. Labelling
studies have shown that the precursor YWA1 is incorporated into Antibiotic Y. YWA1 is
the ﬁrst stable intermediate formed by PKS12 [Klitgaard et al., 2015].
PKS12 is known to be the PKS responsible for production of the red pigment Auro-
fusarin[Malz et al., 2005]. The Aurofusarin gene cluster has two additional genes in F.
avenaceum compared to the non-Antibiotic Y producing species F. graminearum. These
genes, aurE and aurP, has been predicted to encode an epoxide hyrdrolase and cytochrome
P450, respectively. These enzymes and reactions are known from the degradation of poly
carbon aromatic rings but could possibly also be utilized in biosynthetic pathways.
On the basis of the background literature presented in this thesis, a hypothetical biosyn-
thetic pathway of Antibiotic Y was constructed and is shown on Figure 5. To determine
if PKS12, aurE, aurP and the proposed intermediates are involved in the biosynthesis
of Antibiotic Y, this project aims to create knock out strains of the three genes and
characterise these chemically.

DISCUSSION
9
Discussion
9.1
Choice of Media for Detecting Changes in Antibiotic Y Pro-
duction
The media chosen for this study performed very diﬀerently. The internal standard Chlo-
ramphenicol was easily detected in all samples, but apart from that, only Linoleic Acid
was detected in all samples and were the only compounds of interest to be found in
SNA. It was possible to detect all the compounds in the day 14 DFM sample except
Nor-rubrofusarin. Additionally DFM did not seem like a good choice, as Antibiotic Y,
YWA1 and Rubrofusarin could not be identiﬁed in the day 7 DFM sample.
Even though Antibiotic Y could be detected in the MEA day 7 sample, only Fuscofusarin
and Aurofusarin could be detected in both the 7 day and 14 day samples in addition to
Chloramphenicol and Linoleic Acid. YES and PDA performed very similarly to MEA,
with only Chloramphenicol, Fuscofusarin, Aurofusarin and Linoleic Acid detected on both
the day 7 and day 14 samples. The fact that Antibiotic Y could not be detected on YES
is consistent with an earlier study by Sørensen and Giese [2013] who found that Antibi-
otic Y was only produced when grown on YES media from MP Biomedicals and not on
YES media from any of the other suppliers. Furthermore YES is a complex media, its
composition is known to vary even between batches from the same supplier.
Bells was the only media where Antibiotic Y could be easily detected on both the day 7
and day 14 sample. It was chosen as best suited media for studying changes in the for-
mation of Antibiotic Y. Furthermore, it was also possible to identify compounds known
to be produced by PKS12, such as YWA1, Aurofusarin, Rubrofusarin and Fuscofusarin.
Some of the compounds were harder to identify than others. Rubrofusarin was initially
very hard to identify as the UV peak was very hard to integrate properly and looked like
something else with a quite strong UV signal eluted approximately the same retention
time.
YWA1 was only detected on DFM day 14, OAST day 14, Bells day 7 and Bells day
14. As it is an intermediate in the biosynthesis of many polyketide compounds, such
as Aurofusarin and Rubrofusarin, it is possible that it is very quickly metabolised. The
separation on the UHPLC might however also play a role, as the identiﬁed peaks were
not very well separated.

9.2
Possible Intermediates
9
DISCUSSION
Analysing all these samples would not have been possible without some sort of automa-
tion. In this study Qualitative Analysis and Quantitative Analysis has complimented
each other. The great strength of Qualitative Analysis, besides its many kinds of auto-
mated searches, is that UV spectras can be examined and used as a second identiﬁcation
criteria. Quantitative Analysis on the other hand is great for compounds where more
prior-knowledge is available. It is however all very dependent on how the methods are set
up.
Quantitative Analysis identiﬁed Fuscofusarin in more samples than the automated method
in Qualitative Analysis. This might however partly be due to the fact that smaller peaks
drown in the noise on the BPC and are deemed insigniﬁcant.
9.2
Possible Intermediates
In this study possible intermediates in the biosynthesis of Antibiotic Y were uncovered.
The ﬁrst compound was CS-INT-1, which is a theoretical compound based on what an
intermediate in the Antibiotic Y biosynthesis might look like.
The samples were searched for the mass of CS-INT-1 in Qualitative Analysis, where
a peak with the right mass was identiﬁed. Compounds from the same biosynthetic path-
way often have very similar chromophore systems and interestingly the UV spectra for
the peak of CS-INT-1 proved to be highly similar to that of Antibiotic Y. This supports
the claim that this compound is associated with the biosynthetic pathway of Antibiotic
Y, however positive identiﬁcation will rely on future puriﬁcation of the compound and
structural elucidation.
To investigate if the retention time of CS-INT-1 was indeed plausible, the LogP was
calculated for CS-INT-1 and Antibiotic Y. LogP can be used as a measure of molecular
hydrophobicity. Antibiotic Y had a greater LogP than CS-INT-1 which means that it is
probably more polar than Antibiotic Y and thus will have elute earlier. This is consistent
with CS-INT-1 eluting at approximately 4.9 minutes and Antibiotic Y eluting at approx-
imately 8 minutes. The calculated LogP values are however only theoretical and should
be interpreted with caution. It does however seem possible that CS-INT-1 would elute
before Antibiotic Y.
Apart from the Epoxide intermediate, two interesting peaks were identiﬁed, CS-unknown-
1 and CS-unknown-2. The masses were determined but no compounds in the database
matched the mass and the UV spectras. The two compounds are probably structurally

9.3
Growth Study
The data from the growth study was summarised in a number of graphs, Figure 23, 24,
25 ,26, 27, 28 and 29. It is evident that the standard deviations are very high, which
makes it diﬃcult to draw reliable conclusions. It should however be safe to conclude, that
under these conditions, the levels of Antibiotic Y are much higher than the levels of the
other compounds investigated. This could however be due to diﬀerences in ionization,
and Antibiotic Y may be easier to ionize than the other when using negative mode MS,
than the other metabolites.
Despite the high standard deviations, it seems that Antibiotic Y levels in the middle
of the colony increases, but evens out after approximately 6 days. Antibiotic Y levels
in the growth front of the colony increases over time as well. Fuscofusarin was present
in very small amounts in both the middle and growth front of the colony. Rubrofusarin
levels seemed somewhat constant in middle of colony, but increasing in the growth front
of the colony.
Another possible issue of the study of Aurofusarin, Fuscofusarin and Rubrofusarin, apart
from the standard deviation, is the limit of quantiﬁcation. Especially Fuscofusarin was
only present in very small amounts relative to Linoleic acid. The extraction method can
also inﬂuence how much of the individual compound is extracted and consequently present
in the sample.

The great diﬀerences in metabolite levels between colonies with the same age, could
also be explained by the unusual phenotype all of the colonies to some degree shared.
This phenotype has been described in an earlier study by Wellman and Blaisdell [1941],

As the phenotype this study was conducted on showed great variation in size and shapes

normal phenotype and compare it to what was found in this study with the degenerated
phenotype.
9.4
Construction of Deletion Strains
During the construction of the plasmids for the deletion strains, ampliﬁcation of the re-
combination sequence U1/U2 for PKS12 proved to be very diﬃcult. Several attempts to
optimize the PCR protocol and master mix, did not yield any improvements. The U1/U2
recombination sequence is situated between PKS12 and aurE and the diﬃculties could
possibly be explained by a miss assembly of the genome in that area.
The digest of the ΔPKS12 plasmids showed that two of the four pladsmids were smaller
than anticipated. The band that was smaller than expected was the band where the
U1/U2 recombination sequence was located. This might indicate that miss assembly is
not the only possible explanation, and that the sequence between PKS12 and aurE, or
part of it, might be present somewhere else in the genome.
During the validation of the ΔaurE deletion mutant, the RF-1/T3 ﬂank proved to be
very diﬃcult to amplify. The ﬂanking region that this primer pair was supposed to am-
plify into the same region that was diﬃcult to work with, when creating the ΔPKS12
plasmid. Many diﬀerent approaches were tried to validate the last ﬂank of the ΔaurE
transformants but none of them were successful.
Transformation of ΔaurP and ΔPKSIII into F. avenaceum were attempted twice, but
without succeeding. In the ﬁrst transformation, no growth was observed on the sterile
ﬁlter control. Normalt growth was observed on the wildtype F. avenaceum and the A.
tumefaciens plates. At ﬁrst the nitrogen source was suspected to be the issue and the
transformation was repeated with a fresh one. This did however not help and no changes
were observed on any plates. Alternative methods of validating ﬂanking region could be

Chemical Analysis of ΔPKSIII Strain
The precultures of the ΔPKSIII strains showed some interesting phenotypes. It is hard
to tell what the phenotype is caused by without further investigation, but possible expla-
nations could be that the colony is heterokaryon or aﬀected by the inoculation of more
than one single conidiae.
Despite these interesting phenotypes no immediate diﬀerences in the produced metabo-
lites between the wildtype and the ΔPKSIII strains were found. There are however many
possible explanations to this. The extraction method plays a big role, and the compounds
produced by PKSIII may not have been extracted and therefore present in the analysed
samples or simply that PKSIII is not active or produce very small mounts of product
under the conditions these strains has been grown under. A molecular biological strategy
to over come this challenge could be creating an over-expression strain, that expresses the
PKSIII under a constitutive promotor.
It is very diﬃcult to ﬁnd the product of a PKS in LC-MS data ﬁles like these, when
no prior knowledge about the products are available. A method to overcome this chal-
lenge is to identify all known peaks by dereplication in a automated way and then inspect
the remaining peaks. Multivariate data analysis can be used to ﬁnd signiﬁcant peaks and
correlations between them. The time constraint on this project did however not allow for
these analyses.

CONCLUSION
10
Conclusion
The ﬁrst case of this study concerned Antibiotic Y. This study found that Bells is a a
suitable media for detecting changes in the levels of Antibiotic Y in F. avenaceum cul-
tures. Despite the very high standard deviations, the experiment showed that Antibiotic
Y was produced in much higher levels than Aurofusarin, Fuscofusarin and Rubrofusarin
and that there were diﬀerences in the levels of Antibiotic Y in the middle of the colony
and the growth front of the colony when cultivated on Bells.
PKS12 was proposed to be the PKS responsible for Antibiotic Y production and a hy-
pothetical biosynthetic pathway of Antibiotic Y was presented, involving aurE and aurP
from the Aurofusarin cluster in F. avenaceum. Plasmids for creating deletion mutants
of PKS12, aurE and aurP were created, but only the ΔaurE plasmid was successfully
transformed into the fungi. The ΔaurE transformants were however not fully validated
due to time constraint and therefore not chemically analysed.
A compound was however found in all samples of F. avenaceum 05001 cultivated on
Bells media, that could be the proposed intermediate CS-INT-1. This supports the hy-
pothetical biosynthetic pathway proposed in this thesis, but the compound should be
puriﬁed and analysed by NMR to conﬁrm that the compound is indeed CS-INT-1. Two
other possible intermediates were identiﬁed, based on their UV spectrums that showed
similarities to those of Antibiotic Y and Aurofusarin. Like CS-INT-1, more research is
needed to conﬁrm this association.
The second case of this thesis concerned the predicted type III PKS PKSIII. Two deletions
strains from a preliminary study was successfully validated and analysed with UHPLC-
DAD-MS. The chemical analysis did not reveal any clues to which compounds the PKSs
produce. Due to the limited time it was not possible to analyse the data any further.
This project contributes with new knowledge and material for future research on polyke-
tide products from F. avenaceum, including type III PKSs, which were recently discovered
in fungi. Thus many discoveries are to be made and future research into polyketide syn-
thases and their biosynthetic pathway may lead to new antibiotics and therapeutics.
