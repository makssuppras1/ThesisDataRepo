Abstract
The presentation of peptides on Major Histocompatibility Complex (MHC) molecules is an es­
sential step in the activation of the immune system. Studies have shown that only a fraction of
the human proteome is sampled by MHC. This peptide repertoire presented on MHC, termed
the immunopeptidome, is affected by multiple complex processes and mechanisms that are not
yet fully elucidated.
This project focused on the investigation and characterization of protein characteristics that con­
tribute to the protein MHC class II presentation enrichment. The aim was to understand what
characterizes proteins that serve as more frequent sources of MHC class II ligands compared to
others. It utilized an MS­immunopeptidomics dataset comprising HLA­DR ligands, which were
mapped to the human proteome. Peptides from this dataset were organized into nested sets
to quantify protein MHC class II presentation enrichment. To explore the factors influencing
MHC class II presentation enrichment, a comprehensive collection of features relevant to MHC
presentation was employed. These features, including protein abundance, mRNA expression,
chemical characteristics, subcellular localization, and secondary protein structure, were inte­
grated into various machine learning frameworks, starting from a simple generalized random
forest model and progressing to ensemble random forest models that incorporated additional
features obtained through enrichment analyses. The culmination of this research was the de­
velopment of a novel random forest predictor for protein MHC class II presentation enrichment,
which is, to the best of our knowledge, the first of its kind. Overall, this thesis contributes to our
understanding of protein MHC­II presentation enrichment and sheds light on the importance
of different protein characteristics and processes involved in MHC­II ligand generation. The
findings have implications for immunology and provide a foundation for future research in this
field.

Introduction
1.1
Major Histocompatibility Complexes
Major Histocompatibility complexes (MHC) play a crucial role in the development of adaptive
immune response, and thus the understanding of the underlying mechanisms is valuable for
research in infections, cancer, and vaccine development [1]. MHC serves the purpose of pre­
senting antigens in the form of peptides on the cell surface for recognition by an appropriate T
cell. By doing so, they enable the immune cells to survey the status of the cells in the body and
continuously gain information on which and when an immune response is in need of activation
or suppression. Thus, they constitute a crucial step in the immune system’s ability to combat
pathogens, as their T cell interaction can elicit several responses including killing virus­infected
cells, activating macrophages to kill bacteria living in their intracellular vehicles or active B cells
to produce antibodies for elimination or neutralization of the given pathogen [1, 2].
They exist in two flavors: MHC class I and MHC class II (shortened MHC­I and MHC­II in the
following). They are said to be both polygenic and polymorphic. This implies that there are
several genes encoding the molecules, which means that every individual possesses a specific
set with different ranges of peptide binding specificity and that there are multiple variants of each
gene within a population, respectively. More specifically, the human MHC molecules are named
Human Leukocyte Antigen (HLA), and the two classes are primarily encoded by three gene
regions (MHC­I: HLA­A, ­B, and ­C, MHC­II: HLA­DR, ­DP, and ­DQ). The allelic variation arising
from this highly affects and enables the diversity of MHC binding specificities for pathogenic
peptides and thus the overall and specific responsiveness of the immune system to antigens
[2]. MHC­I and MHC­II have important functional distinctions: MHC­I presents peptides on
nucleated cells and is recognized på cytotoxic CD8+ T cells, whereas MHC­II presents peptides
on the surface of antigen­presenting cells (APC), e.g., dendritic cells (DCs), macrophages, and
B cells, and are recognized by CD4+ T cells. They are both heterodimeric and transmembrane
glycoproteins and consist of two domains that form the peptide­binding cleft. This cleft originates
from a single heavy alpha chain in MHC­I and two chains, alpha­chain, and beta­chain, in MHC­
II. The binding cleft in MHC­I is closed at both ends leading to a peptide size restriction of 8­10
amino acids. On the other hand, MHC­II has a more open binding cleft allowing peptides of
13­25 amino acids with a binding core often consisting of 9 amino acids and the remaining
residues extending from the ends of the binding clefts [3]. As the subject of this thesis is the
protein enrichment related to MHC­II, this will be the focus of the following.

The MHC­II is synthesized in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) and is bound to the invariant
chain (Ii) protein for stabilization and restriction of peptide loading. When translocated to the
endosomal compartment and thus entering the endocytic pathway, endolysosomal proteases
cleave the Ii leaving a residual peptide called CLIP in the peptide­binding cleft of the MHC­II.
Peptide exchange factors (called HLA­DM and HLA­DO) then catalyze the removal of CLIP
and potentially later MHC­II bound peptides, thus allowing multiple peptide exchanges to occur.
This leads to an exchange of low­affinity peptides to peptides with higher affinity for MHC­II.
Finally, the MHC­II can present the peptide on the cell surface to interact with the T­cell recep­
tor [3, 5]. The acquisition of these peptides happens through distinct cellular mechanisms that
allow the APCs to sample their external environment to enter the endocytic pathway. Examples
include macropinocytosis, phagocytosis, receptor­mediated endocytosis, and more unconven­
tional pathways. Macropinocytosis is a nonspecific endocytic process, where invagination of the
cell membrane allows for a fluid­phase uptake of extracellular material, often seen in immature
DCs and macrophages. Phagocytosis is characterized by the internalization of relatively large
(> 0.3µm) particles seen predominantly in DCs and macrophages. Seen mostly in B cells is

receptor­mediated endocytosis, where internalization occurs as a result of binding to the B cell
receptor or other surface receptors. More unconventional pathways include autophagy, where
cytoplasmic components are degraded and processed inside the cell, and trogocytosis, where
intercellular transfer happens between cells through immune synapses. However, studies show
that peptides presented by MHC­II mostly originate from endogenous rather than exogenous
source proteins. For instance, the cell membrane and endo/lysosomal compartments have been
shown to be frequent origins. Other cellular compartments including the nucleus, mitochondria,
ER, Golgi apparatus, and the cytosol are also recurrent sources of peptides [5]. Throughout
this process, numerous factors including source protein expression, post­translational modifi­
cations, enzymes, chaperones, and transporters can influence the final selection of presented
peptides [6]. The actual binding of a peptide to MHC­II involves several hydrogen bonds be­
tween the residues in the binding site and the peptide backbone and contacts between specific
peptide side chains and pockets in the binding cleft. A classic feature of the MHC­II peptide
repertoire is that it’s clustered in so­called nested sets. These are characterized by peptides
with a common binding core, but different lengths of the extended residues, also termed peptide
flanking residues [7, 8, 9]. Overall, numerous complex mechanisms and factors influence which
peptides end up being presented on a given MHC­II molecule. The increasing amount of data
available and the generation of computational methods to investigate have greatly contributed
to its understanding. However, several aspects of these mechanisms underlying the processes
happening prior to the actual peptide binding to MHC­II remain unclear, including the charac­
teristics explaining why some proteins seem to deliver more peptides for MHC­II presentation
than others [10].
1.2
Identification of Immunopeptidomics Data
The repertoire of peptides presented by MHC is termed the immunopeptidome. The identifi­
cation of the immunopeptidome is challenging for several reasons, including the complex and
unclear antigen processing mechanisms for peptide­MHC complex generation and its low abun­
dance [11]. Recent advances in experimental technology and immunoinformatics have provided
powerful tools for identifying MHC­II peptides and predicting their potential immunogenicity.
1.2.1
Liquid Chromatography Tandem Mass Spectrometry
Liquid chromatography tandem mass spectrometry (LC­MS/MS) is a powerful and commonly
used method for identifying MHC peptides, as it enables the detection of several thousands of
peptides in a single experiment while implicitly preserving information of antigen processing.
The experimental setup initiates with collecting and culturing a cell of interest [7, 12]. The next
step involves lysing the cells in the presence of protease inhibitors (to preserve proteins of in­
terest), followed by purification of the peptide­MHC complexes with MHC­specific antibodies.
Acid elution is then used to detach the peptide from the MHC molecules, which are then sep­
arated from higher molecular weight components by e.g., high­pressure liquid chromatography
(HPLC). The peptides can then be separated by nano­flow chromatography and ionized. A
mass spectrometer can identify the mass­to­charge ratio of the peptides, which provides spec­
tra that can be compared to in silico­generated spectra based on suitable protein sequence
databases. From this, it’s possible to completely or partially obtain the peptide sequences that
were originally bound to the MHC­II molecules.

The method has provided a great source of information in the field of immunopeptidomics, yet
it still suffers from some limitations. Although advances have been made, limited sensitivity is
relevant to mention, as the method requires large sample sizes to avoid only detecting the most
abundant and ionizable peptides. Furthermore, suitable antibodies specific for the particular
HLA alleles need to be available, and contaminants (e.g., from co­eluted peptides or wrongly
annotation spectra) are unavoidable. MS­data has also demonstrated an over­representation
of so­called ‘flyable’ peptides and a neglect of cysteine­containing peptides. Nevertheless, the
method has enabled researchers to do large­scale sequencing of the immunopeptidome and its
contribution to data availability has had a great influence on e.g., computational methods within
immunology [1, 13].
1.2.2
Immunoinformatics
Immunoinformatics is an interdisciplinary field that combines immunology and computer science
to develop computational tools to understand and predict immune reactions. A fundamental as­
pect of this is antigen presentation by MHC. Due to the previously mentioned allelic variety
associated with MHC, mapping all peptide­MHC complexes would be a substantial endeavor,
and for this reason, the development of computational tools capable of predicting this is es­
sential. The tools available include a great variety of prediction methods, such as motif­based
models (e.g, SYFPHEITHI), linear regression models (e.g., stabilized matrix model (SMM)), and
artificial neural networks (ANN) (e.g., NetMHC). The availability of such tools is relatively scarce
for MHC­II compared to MHC­I, which is partly explained by its open binding cleft and the asso­
ciated allowance for longer peptides [4]. As the binding core of these longer peptides can vary
between peptides, and the peptide flanking residues can have different lengths and interactions
with both MHC­II and T cell receptors, the understanding and prediction of the peptide­MHC­II
interaction is highly complicated. An example of a computational tool seeking to combat this

challenge is the state­of­the­art predictor NetMHCIIpan­4.0, which incorporates multiple data
types (including MS­derived data as described above) and encoding of proteolytic ligand con­
text into a model consisting of an ensemble of ANNs. It is pan­specific and is thus able to deliver
predictions across multiple MHC alleles, also outside the set of MHC molecules included in the
training data [14]. However, computational predictions of MHC­II peptide binding still demon­
strate a slight shortfall in specificity (false positive rate) when compared to the actual bound
peptides from the MS experiments, and the performance of the MHC­II predictors lack behind
corresponding models for MHC­I (e.g., NetMHCpan­4.1) [4, 15]. Thus, making models that can
aid in this prediction and provide a better understanding of the complex mechanisms in antigen
presentation by MHC­II is beneficial.
1.3
Protein MHC­II Presentation Enrichment
It has been previously suggested that only a smaller fraction of the human proteome is sam­
pled by MHC. Moreover, some proteins appear to be more sampled to be presented on MHC
than others [10, 16]. This can be termed as a protein’s MHC presentation enrichment. Many
methods have been developed focusing on the peptide binding to MHC, where less effort has
been made towards providing optimal protein candidates from a selection of proteins [10]. For­
mer researchers have shared methods to identify this, although mostly focusing on MHC­I.
Examples include simple models such as logistic regressions to combine different processes
involved in peptide processing in order to quantify the protein’s contribution of peptides to MHC­
presentation [16].
In this project, we aimed to contribute to this knowledge on protein MHC­II enrichment through
the use of machine learning. For this, we have conducted an extensive collection of protein fea­
tures considered to relate to this phenomenon. Our goal was to identify the key features that are
most strongly associated with MHC­II protein presentation and incorporate these findings into
predictive models. Specifically, we integrated the features in different machine learning frame­
works utilizing random forest to present a final model that is both able to predict the MHC­II
presentation enrichment of a protein and contribute to the lacking knowledge on the character­
istics of proteins containing MHC­II ligands.

2
Materials and Methods
2.1
Data Description
The data used in this study was obtained from an in­house MS­immunopeptidomics data set
[15]. It contained MS­eluted HLA­II ligands obtained from 221 experiments in 40 donors. The
number of experiments per donor varied from 2­12 experiments with the majority being 5­6 ex­
periments per donor (visualized in Appendix A). These experiments were constituted of PBMC­
isolated monocytes which were pulsed with LPS to differentiate into DCs. These DCs were
lysed, followed by L243 mAb immunoprecipitation to isolate the HLA­DR molecules (the alle­
les are provided in Appendix A). The peptides were eluted from the HLA­DR and sequenced
with LC­MS/MS. The resulting spectra were mapped to the human proteome using FDR 1%, by
which 5926 ligands did not map to the reference proteome and were thus discarded. The final
peptides were then filtered to include peptides of length 13­21 amino acids (length distribution
provided in Appendix A). This resulted in a total of 1,323,868 peptides.
2.2
Mapping Peptides to Human Proteome
The obtained sequences from the LC­MS/MS experiment were remapped to the Swiss­Prot
reviewed human reference proteome with 20,387 proteins from UniProt (UP000005640, [17])
with a python script utilizing Biopython’s SeqIO [18]. We did this by searching for each peptide
in all protein sequences, and if an exact match was found, the given protein was annotated as
a source protein for that peptide. In the case of peptides mapping to completely new proteins
than what was originally assigned in the data, this was investigated with UniProt and blastP. If
a given peptide was mapped to multiple source proteins, all the peptide­protein combinations
were kept. We furthermore did a brief inspection of the source proteins of peptides mapping to
multiple source proteins with blastP similarity assessment.
2.3
Data Filtration
2.3.1
Removal of Contaminants
We removed peptide contaminants from the dataset originating from the LC­MS/MS experi­
ments. We did this by predicting the likelihood of HLA binding to all possible alleles related
to each donor with MHCMotifDecon [14]. MHCMotifDecon deconvolutes the individual ligands
and assigns them to the most likely HLA molecule (by lowest rank score). We defined eluted
peptides as contaminants if they had a predicted rank score above 20% to all expressed alleles
by the donor. These peptides were then removed from the data.
2.3.2
Protein Filtration
We then filtered the data to remove proteins that would interfere with prediction tools and calcu­
lations in the downstream analysis. This included proteins in either of the following categories:
• Proteins containing invalid amino acids in their sequence (“X” or “U”)
• Very short (< 50 amino acids) or very long (> 5000 amino acids) proteins
• Proteins marked as putative in Uniprot (meaning that characteristics of the proteins have
a varying lack of experimental verification [19])

2.4
Nested MHC­II Enrichment
The protein MHC­II presentation enrichment refers to the degree to which a protein is a source
of peptides presented by MHC. We quantified this term by enumerating the number of MHC­II
peptides originating from a given protein in the data.
To do this, our initial course of action was limiting this count to being unique on donor level, i.e. a
peptide detected in a donor across multiple experiments would only be counted once. This was
done as a way of taking the multiple and varying numbers of LC­MS/MS experiments between
the donors into account. More specifically, the re­occurrence of a peptide in the same donor
and protein was viewed as ”confirmative” and simply a result of the repeated experiments. On
the other hand, if the same peptide sequence occurred in multiple donors, these were counted
accordingly, i.e. a protein that delivered peptides presented by a broader range of people would
indicate a higher level of enrichment than a protein that delivered peptides presented by only a
few individuals.
Furthermore, as earlier mentioned, peptides presented by MHC­II are normally found in nested
sets. We reflected this in our final data set by first defining nested sets based on three principles:
1. They must map to the same source protein
2. They must share a common motif of at least 9 amino acids with a full overlap. If they
overlap beyond these 9 amino acids, these remaining overlapping amino acids must also
be identical
3. They must share a common binding core
This is also illustrated below, where only the fourth case would be considered a nested set.

This was done by first sorting all the peptides by length. Each peptide would then be checked
against the previous (and thus longer) peptides for a common 9­mer motif with a full overlap. If
this was found, these two peptides would be noted as having a common motif. Subsequently, we
predicted the 9­mer binding core for each peptide and its associated HLAs with MHCMotifDecon
on default MHC­II settings. Here only binding cores predicted with a rank score <= 20% were
considered (the rest corresponded to contaminants that had been filtered out). This information
was then used to “pool” together all peptides mapping to the same source protein, that share
both a common motif with full overlap, and the same binding core into nested sets. Peptides
that did not share these criteria with other peptides were considered to be so­called “singletons”.
With this, we looked into the distribution of peptides in nested sets vs. singletons.
We used the above­described data to convey the MHC­II presentation enrichment for the pro­
teins. This was done for each protein by counting the number of nested sets and singletons
mapped to it. More specifically, a count for a protein would indicate that a specific nested set
or singleton had been mapped to the specific protein sequence in one donor. This target value
will in the following sections be noted as ”nested MHC­II enrichment” to illustrate that the num­
ber quantifies how much each protein is enriched in its MHC­II presentation as a count for the
nested sets (or singletons), not as a count of individual peptides.

The following section presents how and which features were collected to be included in the

2.5.1

Protein abundance was obtained for human proteins through PaxDb, which is a meta­resource

converted Ensembl IDs to UniProt IDs for all proteins in the data with biomaRt in R [22, 23]. For
tissue expression, we averaged the expression across all tissues per protein. For cell expres­

2.5.2
Chemical Protein Features
For each protein, basic chemical protein characteristics were calculated with a Python script
utilizing Bio.SeqUtils.ProtParam’s ProteinAnalysis included in Biopython [18]. With this, we first
calculated the protein molecular weight and protein length. We computed the amino acid com­
position of each protein. These were converted to be a column for each amino acid as the
absolute amino acid count divided by protein length, thus corresponding to the proportion of
the protein constituted of the given amino acid. Protein aromaticity was calculated as the rel­
ative frequency of aromatic amino acids (Phe, Tyr, and Trp) utilizing the findings by Lobry et
at., 1994 [24]. The calculation of the protein instability index was based on the work by Gu­
ruprasad et al., 1990 [25]. This originated from the principle that stability characteristics are
governed by an arrangement of amino acids in a specific order, where dipeptides must be the
smallest unit defining that order. To implement this as an instability measurement, an impact of
the 400 possible dipeptides (20·20 amino acid combinations) on unstable proteins was obtained
by comparing their observed occurrence in both unstable and stable proteins compared to the
expected value. From this, a weight value of instability could be assigned to each of the dipep­
tides. Utilizing these, we could obtain the instability index of a protein as the summarization of
its dipeptide instability weight values normalized by the length of the protein. We also computed
the hydropathic character of the proteins. This was done utilizing the Kyte­Doolittle scale which
contains a hydrophobicity score for each amino acid based on experimental observations from
the literature. A moving segment then averaged these scores for the entire protein sequence,
and the GRAVY (grand average hydropathy) score was then obtained as the sum of these av­
erages normalized by protein length [26]. We calculated the isoelectric point and the charge of
the proteins, the latter at pH 5 and 7 to mimic the environment in the cell cytosol and lysosome
[27]. Lastly, we computed the molar extinction coefficient in both the reduced and oxidized form.
With the amino acid composition, we conducted an analysis of enriched proteins (i.e., those
containing MHC­II peptides) by comparing their amino acid frequencies to the average amino
acid frequencies across the human proteome: For each of these proteins, we divided the fre­
quency of each amino acid by the corresponding proteome average. With the obtained ratio
values, we investigated if some amino acids showed alternate frequencies than the background
(i.e. the human proteome). We enhanced this analysis by further investigating this in the most
enriched proteins. More specifically, we ordered the proteins according to their nested MHC­
II enrichment and extracted the 100 proteins of the highest values, with which we utilized the
same calculations to obtain a ratio of amino acid frequency compared to the human proteome.

2.5.4
Secondary Protein Structure and Relative Accessible Surface Area
We computed the secondary protein structure and relative accessible surface area (RSA) in
two ways, namely through AlphaFold and NetSurfP­3.0 predictions. Alphafold consists of two
main layers: a novel neural network (the “EvoFormer”), which produces processed multiple
sequence alignments (MSA) and residue pairs representation, and a structure module that in
short introduces a 3D structure of initial set rotations and positions, which are then refined into
a highly accurate protein structure [29]. We obtained the predicted Alphafold structures for our
proteins by downloading PDB files corresponding to the same reference proteome from the Al­
phaFold predictions database [30]. For each PDB file, we then used the Bio.PDB.DSSP module
from Biopython [18] to extract structure information from each PDB. From the object created,
we extracted the DSSP codes for the secondary structure (corresponding to α­helix, isolated
β­bridge, strand/β­sheet, 3 −10 helix, π helix, (h­bonded) turn, bend, and coil) and RSA for
each amino acid. We collapsed this data to for each protein obtain two sequences: one with
the short letter code for each of the predicted secondary structures, and one with RSA values.
NetSurfP­3.0 takes as input the primary protein sequence only and can annotate proteins in a
time­efficient way compared to similar methods. From this, it generates sequence profiles and
uses embeddings from a pre­trained language model that are fed into two convoluted neural
networks (CNN) layers, and a final two­layer biLSTM provides the output for the given protein
sequence. Using our protein sequences as input, we obtained the eight­state secondary struc­
ture and relative solvent­accessible area (RSA) for each amino acid in each protein [31]. As
with the AlphaFold structures, we converted this output to a secondary structure and an RSA
sequence for each protein in our data.
For both prediction outputs, we applied a second step of filtering with a sliding window ap­
proach. For the secondary structure sequence created for each protein, we used a sliding
window corresponding to 7 amino acids. Within a sliding window, we would count the occur­
rence of the different secondary structures. If the frequency of a particular secondary structure
within the window was above a certain threshold, we would add it to the overall protein count for
that particular secondary structure. These thresholds for each secondary structure were deter­
mined based on the minimum number of amino acids required for the corresponding structures
to form: 5 amino acids for α helix, 1 amino acid for isolated β­bridge, 3 amino acids for β sheet,
3 amino acids for 3­10 helix, 6 amino acids for π helix, 1 amino acid for turn, 1 amino acid for
bend and 1 amino acid for coil [32]. To prevent secondary structures and RSA at the ends of
each sequence from being counted differently than those in the rest of the sequence, we ex­

tended the sequence at both ends with the length of the window. Overall, by introducing this
filtering step, a single amino acid predicted as e.g., an α­helix with no other α­helix amino acids
in proximity was not considered a valid prediction and thus not counted. A similar approach
was applied for the RSA values utilizing the same window: An average of the seven RSA val­

and accessible surfaces. We furthermore investigated which structures were most frequently
predicted by the two tools and how well they aligned in their predictions.
2.5.5
Protein Family, Domain, Molecular Function, and Biological Process
Besides our base features described above, we integrated a broad set of features into a par­
ticular modeling framework. These were related to the protein family and domain as well as its
molecular function and biological process. This data was obtained through different sources,
which could be incorporated into a collected download for the chosen human proteome from
UniProt. The sources used included:
• InterPro: InterPro is a comprehensive resource combining multiple signature databases
into one resource containing protein families, domains, and functional sites. It reduces
redundancy between the databases by having one InterPro entry for the same biologi­
cal entities, and new entries are carefully checked by the curators prior to being publicly
available [34].
• Reactome: The Reactome Knowledgebase contains manually curated molecular details
across a broad range of biological processes in humans. It contains entries for human pro­
teins, whose literature­derived reactions are grouped into 2546 pathways (e.g., interleukin­
15 signaling and receptor­mediated mitophagy), which are then collected under 28 super
pathways (e.g., immune system and metabolism) [35]. We selected the former more spe­
cific option for our analyses.
• Gene Ontology: The Gene Ontology (GO) Consortium provides a comprehensive re­
source of gene functions and products. Most of the available data is derived directly from
published experiments and contains three categories of GO annotations: Biological pro­
cess, molecular function, and cellular component. Biological process refers to a biological
objective to which the protein contributes. The molecular function is the biochemical ac­
tivity of the proteins and the capability it carries as a potential (e.g., enzyme, transporter,
and ligand). Cellular component refers to the cellular location [36, 37]. As we had already
included cellular localization, we extracted entities related to the first two annotations.
As the above databases contain numerous protein annotations that are not all relevant to the
human proteome and the scope of this project, we performed an enrichment analysis to extract
only the most relevant features. This was done based on partitions of the data comparing the
proteins of a given partition with the proteins of high MHC­II enrichment of that group. As this
data extraction is included in the modeling framework, it will be described in Section 2.7.6.

2.6
Data Partitioning
2.6.1
BlastP and Minimum Spanning Tree Clustering
We clustered proteins sharing sequence similarity utilizing blastP E­score and a minimum span­
ning tree (MST) approach. We did this by aligning all protein sequences against each other
using blastP, where each protein sequence was aligned against a database of all protein se­
quences (a leave­one­out database). From this, an E­value for each protein­protein comparison
was obtained. This E­value corresponded to the number of expected hits of similar scores that
could be found just by chance and was thus a measurement of the biological validity of the sim­
ilarity [38]. Even though the use of the E­value score is dependent on the size of sequence and
database, it’s more robust and independent of the alignment length when the database is fixed
compared to the percentage of identity. These values were used to construct a binary similarity
matrix based on a certain E­value threshold. If below this threshold, we would assign a value
of 1, and above a value of 0. Based on this matrix, we could utilize the MST algorithm, which
clusters together sequences that share similarity to at least one common neighbor (i.e. a value
of 1 in the matrix).
In order to determine an optimal E­value threshold for when proteins should be considered
“neighbors” in the binary matrix, we repeated the above­described framework with E­value
thresholds in range [1 : 1 · e−18]. For each threshold, we looked into the size of the biggest
cluster and the total number of clusters obtained. This approach allowed us to settle on a
threshold that balanced the trade­off between cluster size and the number of clusters, avoiding
both a main cluster exceeding the wished partitioning size and an excessive number of small
clusters. In extension to this analysis, we also looked into the main clusters obtained through
different thresholds. The nature of the MST algorithm allows for a cluster where multiple clus­
ters are only connected through individual proteins with a neighbor in both. Thus, the removal
of these few proteins would split the main cluster and allow the possibility for a higher E­value.
Based on these considerations, a threshold was selected and clusters were made accordingly.
Finally, we randomly distributed the clusters obtained through MST into five partitions of the
same size.

Cross­validation
With the five partitions obtained from the methods described above, we held one out as a test
set to include in our final model evaluation. The four remaining partitions would partake in a

constitute the training set, and one partition constitute a validation set, to which each trained
model would be evaluated on. Hyperparameter tuning was carried out utilizing grid search,
which trained models on each of the four training sets with different combinations of parameters.
The best­performing model (based on the calculated r2 from its validation set predictions) would
then be extracted. When the final model had been developed and selected, the performance
was evaluated on the held­out test set. This CV scheme constituted the basis for all modeling

2.7.2
Evaluation Metric
The predictive performance of all our final models was evaluated on the test set by the area
under the receiver operating curve (AUC and ROC). For model performance comparison, we
pairwise bootstrapped prediction values 1000 times (i.e. with replacement), and calculated 1000
AUCs from these. We then calculated the p­value for the first model outperforming the second
by counting the occurrences of AUC from the second model greater than the first model. This
was then divided by the number of samplings. In the case of multiple testing, we FDR adjusted
the p­values. We furthermore evaluated the feature importance from a selection of our models.
More specifically, we calculated feature importance as the mean decrease of Gini impurity for
each specific feature in all the trees.
2.7.3
Random Forest Regressor
In our project, we employed a random forest regression algorithm [39] for our predictive mod­
eling of nested MHC­II enrichment based on the collected features. Random forest trains an
ensemble of decision trees based on different samples and features of the data [40]. To ensure
the optimal performance of our models, we utilized a grid search approach to tune the hyperpa­
rameters of the algorithm. This included the number of trees in the forest (n_estimators), the
maximum number of levels in each decision tree (max_depth), the minimum number of sam­
ples required to split a node (min_samples_split), the minimum number of samples required
at each leaf (min_samples_leaf), and whether or not to bootstrap the samples when building
trees. In order to limit the search space for these parameters, we initiated the tuning with a
random search with a wide range for all parameters (provided in Appendix A, Table A.1). More
specifically, we randomly sampled 100 combinations from the chosen ranges of hyperparam­
eter values, from which the best model across all CV folds and its hyperparameters could be
extracted. We repeated this several times to get an indication of appropriate hyperparameter
ranges to concentrate our grid search on.

2.7.5
Ensemble with Base Features
Ensemble learning is an umbrella term for methods that combine multiple models to solve a
machine learning task, and has been shown to improve predictive performance compared to
using a single model [41]. We incorporated this concept into our modeling framework by extract­
ing sub­models from our CV scheme. More specifically, we would still in each CV fold perform
grid search hyperparameter tuning and evaluate it on the validation set. However, for each
fold, the model with the most optimal hyperparameters for that particular training and validation
set would be extracted, thus obtaining four models with different hyperparameters. These four
models would partake in an ensemble, in which the final prediction would be the average of
their predictions. We evaluated the performance of this ensemble model on the test set and
compared it to the performance of the more simple model described above.

3.1
MS­immunopeptidomics Data Preparation
3.1.1
Mapping Peptides to Human Proteome
We initiated our work by collecting an MS­immopeptidomics dataset containing MHC­II peptides.
We mapped these peptides to the most current version of the human proteome (Swiss­Prot re­
viewed human reference proteome from UniProt, UP000005640 [17]). We did this by searching
for the peptide sequence in all of the human protein sequences, and if an exact match was found,
this protein would be annotated as a source protein for the particular peptide. From this, we
found that 97.3% of the original unique peptides were found in source proteins from the current
human proteome. This corresponded to 97.6% of the peptides unique on donor level (the con­
cept of unique on donor level is described in Section 2.4). As only the peptides that mapped to
the newest proteome will be included in the final dataset, the following analyses were performed
on these only.
The vast majority of the peptides (93.2%) mapped to just one source protein. With the remaining
peptides, the number of source proteins varied with up to 22 proteins. As a sanity check, we
evaluated the similarity between the source proteins of some of these ”multiple­mapping pep­
tides”. This was done to confirm our hypothesis that this happened as a result of the human
proteome containing proteins of similar sequences and functionality rather than being only iden­
tical in this peptide region, which would require further inspection. With blastP, we concluded
that the proteins had percent identities of at least 50% with the majority being 80% and above
(results can be seen in Appendix B). Furthermore, we found that the newly annotated source
protein (compared to the source protein originally annotated in the data) was mainly due to the
renaming and deletion of proteins (analysis and results provided in Appendix B).
As contaminant peptides can arise through LC­MS/MS, we removed these peptides from the
data by utilizing MHCMotifDecon (described in greater detail in Section 2.4). 7% of the peptides
unique on donor level were predicted with rank scores above 20%. These were considered
contaminants and were consequently removed from the data.

We used these nested sets to construct our final target value. This was done by for each
protein counting how many nested sets and/or singletons had mapped to it. Note that this
was counted on a donor unique level, that is nested sets seen in multiple donors would be
counted accordingly, whereas nested sets seen in multiple MS­experiments within the same
donor would only be counted once (thus, they are ”unique on donor level”, as earlier described).
This was done to reflect that proteins containing peptides seen in many donors were viewed as
more enriched for MHC­II presentation than one seen in only a few donors, whereas peptides
found across multiple experiments were viewed as confirmatory rather than as a level of MHC­II
presentation enrichment. We termed this target value the ”nested MHC­II enrichment” for each
protein to illustrate that this count specified the enrichment counted from nested sets rather than
counting the complete number of unique peptides. This showed that only 29% of the proteins
were sources of MHC­II peptides (i.e. a nested MHC­enrichment count above 0, Figure 3.2).
We will differentiate between proteins containing and not containing any MHC­II peptides as
enriched or not­enriched proteins, respectively, in the following sections. This count for nested
MHC­II enrichment would then form the basis of this project and be the value that we wished to
predict with multiple machine learning frameworks.

As proteins are degraded into peptides to then be presented on MHC­II, we looked into the

and proteins with high nested MHC­II enrichment but low abundance values. We did a further
inspection by looking into abundance values between the proteins with no enrichment (i.e. the

3.2.2
Chemical Protein Characteristics
To include basic characteristics for protein descriptions, we included a selection of chemical
features. By doing so, we could examine whether features of the proteins such as size, charge,
stability, and amino acid composition were of importance, working from a hypothesis that large,
unstable proteins would be more enriched for MHC­II enrichment by ”containing” more pep­
tides and be more likely to undergo protein degradation. This was separated into two parts:
First the overall chemical features including protein length, weight, instability index, charges,
isoelectric point, molecular extinction coefficients, aromaticity, and hydropathic character, and
second amino acid composition. From the first, we saw some relation between a selection of
these features and nested MHC­II enrichment, where the features with a ROC curve and AUC
furthest from random included instability index, charge at pH 7 (corresponding to cytosolic pH),
and isoelectric point. These are all factors somewhat related to protein stability or binding pref­
erences: The instability index is based on the occurrence of stable or unstable dipeptides, and
the latter two might affect the overall charge and thus binding preferences of the protein. On
the contrary, the features least important included charge at pH 5 (corresponding to lysosomal
pH) and the protein length and weight.

Subcellular Localization
We applied DeepLoc­2.0 to all protein sequences to obtain a probability and the most probable
subcellular localizations for each protein across 10 subcellular compartments. We investigated
three formats of integrating these results as features in our model: 1) as the predicted probabil­
ities, 2) one­hot­encoded format denoting only the localizations of highest predicted probability,
or 3) one­hot­encoded format denoting the subcellular localizations suggested by Deeploc­2.0
(which could be more than one localization per protein). For the second format, four proteins
(corresponding to 0.02% of the data) had two subcellular localizations of equal probability. For
these, we selected a random of the two probabilities.
We evaluated the predictive power of these three formats related to nested MHC­II enrichment.
Looking into their ROC curves and AUCs, we first found a very weak relation to nested MHC­II
enrichment with the last approach (i.e. the one­hot­encoded combinations of subcellular local­
izations). Only the nucleus had an AUC outside the interval [0.48; 0.52] with a value of 0.435
(ROC curves and AUCs provided in Appendix C, Figure C.2­C.5). With the first format (i.e.
using all subcellular localization predicted probabilities), we did see a relation to nested MHC­II
enrichment: Especially the nucleus, ER, Golgi apparatus, and lysosomes/vacuoles had AUCs
furthest from random.

3.2.4
Secondary Protein Structure and Surface Accessibility
We wanted to include the secondary structure of a protein, as it can provide clues of how rigid
or flexible a protein is, and thus possibly how likely they are for e.g., proteases to access and
degrade. In relation to this, we also wanted to include RSA to evaluate if proteins with more
exposed surface areas were more likely to undergo proteolytic processing and thus possibly
end up as peptides presented on MHC­II. We decided to obtain these features from two distinct
prediction sources. One was AlphaFold, a well­known tool to obtain highly­accurate protein
structure predictions. PDB structure files containing AlphaFold predictions for almost all hu­
man proteins could be downloaded and were converted into secondary structure proportions
for each protein [30]. However, since AlphaFold’s prediction performance was related to a cost
in terms of running time, we also included the faster NetSurfP3 to have a more sustainable op­
tion for large data sets where pre­predicted structures were not available. We filtered outputs
from both predictors to extract only structures of high confidence (as described in Section 2.5.4).

3.3
Data Partitioning
High similarity between certain proteins in the human proteome exists [46]. A great challenge
in machine learning concerns ”data leakage”, which refers to the situation where the model is
evaluated on data containing information linked to the data it was trained on [47]. With this
in mind, we wished to reduce our risk of overestimating our model performance as a result of
evaluating our model on a test set containing proteins highly similar to proteins it was trained on
and thus also compromise its generalization to unseen data. Furthermore, this could hopefully
contribute to making more clear subgroups in the data that contribute differently to nested MHC­
II enrichment for our models to learn. We did this by obtaining a measurement for sequence
similarity through blastP, aligning all protein sequences through a database of the entire human
proteome. These results were integrated into multiple MST analyses with different E­values as
a threshold for cluster generation. With this, we sought to identify an E­value that would ensure
a balance between producing a large number of tiny clusters and the formation of excessively
large clusters that would exceed the desired partition size. We evaluated this by plotting the
size of the main cluster and the number of clusters obtained in total. This indicated that an
E­value of 1 · e−14 was optimal.

3.4
Machine Learning Models to Predict Nested MHC­II
Enrichment
We predicted the nested MHC­II enrichment based on the collected features with the random
forest algorithm. This was the selected approach due to its good performance accuracy and
its ease of feature importance interpretation [40]. Our first course of action was to hold one of
our data partitions as our final test set. This was done to evaluate our models on completely
unseen data. The four remaining partitions would partake in a CV scheme, where one partition
would constitute the validation set and three partitions constitute the training data, alternating
between each CV fold. To initiate our different machine learning frameworks with the random
forest regressor, we performed a random search with broad settings for the parameters to obtain
a more narrow parameter range for our actual hyperparameter tuning. The ranges obtained by
this that were then used in grid searches to tune hyperparameters are provided in Appendix D.
These were used in the models presented.
3.4.1
Generalized Random Forest Model
The first model was made with the four­fold CV, where we in each fold used grid search to tune
the hyperparameters of the random forest model. The overall best­performing model (according
to r2 when evaluated on the validation set in each CV fold) was selected as the final model, and
the performance was evaluated on the held­out test set with a ROC curve and AUC. Although
similar at first glance to the ROC curve seen with protein abundance, we got an increase in
AUC. This indicated that we actually did benefit from several features and that the model was
able to make better predictions than solely using protein abundance.

3.4.3
Introducing Partition­specific Enriched Features in Ensemble Model
We enhanced our random forest ensemble framework by providing each sub­model with an
additional set of features that were specifically relevant to the data they were trained on in re­
gard to nested MHC­II enrichment. These features were obtained using the DAVID functional
annotation tool, through which we performed enrichment analyses on features obtained from
the InterPro, Reactome, and Gene Ontology databases. Note that enrichment analysis refers
to features enriched for a specific set of proteins compared to a background and not the MHC­II
enrichment. We evaluated which features were enriched for the most MHC­II enriched proteins
compared to all proteins separately for each training set in the CV folds. In addition, we applied
this framework four times for each training set based on different combinations of thresholds.
These thresholds were based on whether the 5% or 10% most MHC­II enriched proteins were
used as input in the DAVID enrichment analysis, and if 3 or 5 proteins were used as the minimum
count for the number of proteins a feature should be enriched for to be included (as described
in greater detail in Section 2.7.6). This resulted in four feature sets for each sub­model in the
ensemble. Thus, each sub­model would undergo four rounds of grid search hyperparameter
tuning, one for each of these feature sets, and the overall best­performing model and its cor­
responding features would be extracted to partake in the ensemble. Ultimately, this resulted
in an ensemble of four models utilizing different features to predict nested MHC­II enrichment.
Looking into the performance of this more sophisticated ensemble model with our test set, we
did obtain an increase in performance compared to the previously described ensemble model,
with an AUC value of 0.847 compared to 0.840.

4
Discussion
In this project, we integrated MS­immunopeptidomics data containing HLA­DR ligands from
monocyte­derived DCs to obtain a measurement for a protein’s MHC­II presentation enrich­
ment. We sought to mimic the biological phenomenon characterized by the presence of MHC­II
peptides within nested sets by pooling together peptides sharing a 9­mer common binding core,
a full overlap, and mapping to the same source proteins of the human proteome. By doing so,
we obtained a quantification of the protein MHC­II presentation enrichment, which we termed
the ”nested MHC­II enrichment”. Additionally, we did a broad collection of features to include
in machine learning models for the prediction of a protein’s nested MHC­II enrichment. This in­
cluded protein abundance, mRNA expression, chemical features, subcellular localization, and
secondary protein structures. Beyond analyzing these features and their relation to nested
MHC­II enrichment individually, we integrated them in multiple random forest modeling frame­
works made with four­fold CV. We based this on conscious partitioning of our data determined
by protein sequence similarity MST clustering, which was utilized to decrease the risks of intro­
ducing data leakage into our machine learning evaluation. Our final machine learning models
evolved from a more simple single generalized random forest model to an ensemble based
on the best­performing model in each CV fold. We furthermore introduced features related
to protein family, pathway, and Gene Ontology annotations identified through comprehensive
enrichment analyses to produce partition­specific feature sets. We also explored the same
frameworks with XGBoost and concluded that random forest exhibited better predictive abilities
in terms of nested MHC­II enrichment. From these, we could extract feature importance and
reveal protein abundance to be the most important feature by far, followed by features such as
the cell membrane or extracellular space as the subcellular localization, the monocyte mRNA
expression, neutrophil degranulation, and protein size. From this, we were also able to extract
the most important features to train additional models on, and thus present simpler models still
capable of predicting the nested MHC­II enrichment of human proteins.
MS­immunupeptidomics has revolutionized the field of immunoinformatics by allowing for a
comprehensive and precise identification of MHC­II ligands [1]. An additional quality related
to the MS­data utilized in this project was the repeated experiments with all donors. This is
a great advantage when considering the limited sensitivity related to MS, as a peptide that is,
as a result of this, not found in one experiment might be detected in another, thus increasing
our confidence in the credibility of the data. This was also the reason behind our decision of
keeping our data unique on donor level, i.e. considering peptides seen across multiple experi­
ments as more redundant and confirmatory information rather than a sign of enrichment signal.
However, despite the qualities that can be presented in relation to our data, important notes
concerning its limitations must also be mentioned. The limited sensitivity is unavoidable with
this type of data and as a result, our models might be biased. Particularly, one should be aware
that the model might exhibit a bias against the inclusion of cysteine­containing peptides, as it’s
made with data that neglects these peptides. Furthermore, by using NetMHCMotifDecon to
filter out contaminants, it can be argued that we strengthen this bias, as this model contains the
same bias (as MS data is integrated into its model training, thus eliciting the same limitation)
[14]. In this regard, our analysis of the amino acid frequency of enriched proteins compared to

==PAGE:59==
the human proteome revealed a lower cysteine content in the enriched proteins, indicating the
presence of this bias in the MS data. However, this bias may not fully account for the difference
since the analysis covered the entire protein sequences whereas the MS­data bias affects only
the amino acid content of the peptides. Thus, it could seem like the cysteine content of a protein
is of relevance for its MHC­II enrichment, a signal which would be interesting to explore if data
not suffering from this bias was available.
Another aspect of our data worth mentioning is that the MHC­ligands were obtained through
just one cell type, a finite set of donors, and thus limited sets of MHC­II alleles (both determined
by the donor and the MHC­specific antibodies used for purification). This should of course be
taken into consideration when we make conclusions concerning the biological mechanisms of
MHC­II based on our findings, as these are based on monocyte­derived DCs and HLA­DRB.
The limitation regarding the limited selection of HLA­II alleles is common in the field due to a lack
of data availability [49, 14], but future implementations of our work could include more extensive
data. Here, it would be interesting to explore whether different APC types exhibit differences in
the protein MHC­II enrichment.
In our project, we have made a great effort to provide trustworthy performance results and
a model able to generalize by conscious partitioning our data utilizing MST. For model evalua­
tion, we have from the beginning held out data as a test set based on the partitions we made.
By doing so, we would argue that we have worked against the risk of overestimating our model
performance due to data leakage, as we only present the final performance on completely un­
seen data not containing proteins similar to what it was trained on. However, we should mention
that sequence similarity is only one way of defining protein similarity, and other measurements
such as structure, function, etc. could be explored. As sequence similarity was easy to obtain
and quantify, we utilized this as our similarity measurement. Furthermore, with the held­out
test set, the most optimal approach would be to introduce entirely new data for the final model
evaluation. However, working with the data available to us, we will argue that we have taken
great care to ensure that our analysis is sound and that our conclusions regarding model per­
formance are well­supported.
We did several models of varying complexities to enhance the prediction capability in regard
to nested MHC­II enrichment. The more complex framework consisted of an ensemble of ran­
dom forest models, in which each sub­model was trained on four different feature sets based
on enrichment analyses made with different thresholds. This allowed each sub­model to uti­
lize the most optimal feature set without completely pre­defining the best thresholds. However,
from this, we saw that an ensemble model where all sub­models were trained on the same set
of features had similar performance. We see several reasons that can explain these results:
Firstly, by looking into how much the obtained feature sets overlapped between the different
training partitions for specific thresholds, it could be seen that only a small fraction (1.0 −5.8%)
were unique for each training partition. This could thus indicate that the features found specific
for each training partition only differed marginally from the others, why training models on these
different sets will not contribute greatly. Secondly, we believe the models could suffer from
having too many irrelevant features included. The model made with these partition­specific fea­
tures utilized 978 features in total (including the base features), being almost five times as many
features as the model made with the strict overlap (with 208 features in total).
This leads to another limitation of our models. Although we have been able to build models

with good predictive capabilities in terms of nested MHC­II enrichment, they were also based
on extensive feature collection. In other words, in order to extend or reconstruct our findings,
it requires work with different predictors and especially the availability of data such as protein
abundance, expression, etc. for the particular data set. To accommodate this, we initiated
an exploratory analysis to assess if models with fewer features could elicit adequate perfor­
mance. This was done based on the 5, 10, 15, and 20 features found to be most important from
our random forest ensemble model (made with the strict overlap of enriched features). This
showed that models made on reduced feature sets could indeed compete with the full models.
This also supported our belief mentioned above that our models might suffer from including
many irrelevant features. However, this was a preliminary analysis, and more elaborate work
could be conducted in order to determine an optimal feature set. Furthermore, the most impor­
tant features covered all our feature collection sources except the InterPro and Gene Ontology
databases. This is naturally a limitation to the reduced models, as many of these features can­
not be obtained without conducting the entire computation (e.g., to obtain cell membrane as
a feature, one still needs to conduct the computation of all other subcellular localizations with
Deeploc­2.0). However, this result also illustrates that our initial hypotheses concerning which
features were relevant for a protein’s MHC­II presentation enrichment were confirmed.
We also explored the predictive capabilities of another machine learning algorithm: XGBoost.
This showed significantly lower performance than the corresponding random forest model. We
evaluated if this could be a result of the XGBoost models suffering from the many irrelevant fea­
tures. However, even when reducing the number of features, it was still out­competed by our
random forest models. More extensive hyperparameter tuning could be applied to investigate
this further, but our work suggests random forest as the most optimal predictor of the two. In
future work, other more advanced algorithms could be applied.
Through our feature collection and implementation in our random forest models, we observed
some interesting tendencies with specific features.
Firstly, protein abundance stood out as the feature with the strongest impact. Through our in­
vestigations of the relationship between protein abundance and nested MHC­II enrichment, we
concluded that high protein abundance was only seen in the enriched group (i.e. the proteins
containing MHC­II peptides). This could confirm a rather logical hypothesis that high abundance
of a protein simply increases its likelihood of being sampled by the MHC­II pathway. However,
this did not apply in the opposite case as lower abundance was seen in both groups. In fact,
a set of proteins elicited high values of nested MHC­II enrichment although being of very low
abundance. Inspecting these outliers revealed that the functionalities of these proteins were
all related either directly to the MHC­II molecule itself or the endocytic pathway through with it
obtains its ligands (including APC uptake, protein degradation, or endocytic processes). A pos­
sible explanation might be that their proximity to or inclusion in the MHC­II sampling machinery
allows for efficient processing and presentation of even small amounts of the protein, leading
to enhanced MHC­II presentation. Here, an interesting extension to our work could be to do a
separate model specializing in these outlier proteins.
We did see similar tendencies with the mRNA expression, where especially monocyte expres­
sion presented as one of the most important features as well. Protein abundance and mRNA
expression are both measurements of the amount of protein available, why we expected simi­
larities between them. However, the models elicited a clear preference for protein abundance ­
a preference seen in earlier research on protein MHC presentation enrichment prediction (how­

==PAGE:61==
ever for MHC­I) by Hoof et al, 2012 [16]. This could be attributed to the fact that protein abun­
dance is a more direct measurement of the actual protein content in a cell, whereas mRNA
expression can be influenced by various factors such as post­transcriptional modifications and
mRNA degradation [50]. Additionally, the same outliers as described with protein abundance
were seen with expression. However, two additional outliers were also detected, which were
not related to the MHC­II sampling pathway.
To this, we should acknowledge the presumable presence of noise introduced by the way we
obtain both protein abundance and mRNA expression. The protein abundance dataset was de­
rived from a meta­analysis across multiple tissues and cells in the human organism, obtaining
just one value per protein although variability between tissues and cells might exist. We utilized
this approach to simplify the final feature set and based on literature stating that the abun­
dance set mainly contains housekeeping genes in which tissue­to­tissue variability is limited
[51]. Nonetheless, this limitation should still be acknowledged. Likewise, with mRNA expres­
sion, we computed a grand average over a broad range of tissues and cells. However, we also
extracted the expression for monocytes only, which elicited stronger predictive power in regard
to nested MHC­II enrichment. This could be indicative of two possibilities: Firstly that we benefit
from limiting the possible noise introduced by averaging across the cells, and secondly that the
experimental setup is actually evident as the peptides in our data were obtained from monocyte­
derived DCs. Although not necessarily providing great biological insights, this is confirmative
that we can actually capture logical patterns in our data. This also supports that the inclusion
of data based on other APCs could provide nuances.
From investigating the subcellular localizations of the proteins, we found that the nucleus stood
out as one of the most prominent. Inspecting this further showed negative correlations to all
other cellular compartments than the cytosol. The other most important subcellular localizations
included the ER, Golgi apparatus, and lysosomes/vacuoles, which are all part of the MHC­II
assembling and loading pathway. A possible explanation could be that the nucleus could be
viewed as the subcellular localization furthest from the endocytic pathway. In other words, the
proximity of a protein to the MHC­II sampling pathway is the most important factor when de­
termining its nested MHC­II enrichment. However, when looking into the feature importance
from our models, the prediction of the cell membrane or extracellular space as the protein origin
showed to be of higher importance. The importance of these aligns with findings from previ­
ous studies, e.g., Ciudad et al., 2017, who found the cell membrane, extracellular space, and
lysosomes to be the most frequent source [7]. Other studies have also mentioned the cell mem­
brane as a prominent cellular origin in regards to MHC­II [5, 10].
After the inclusion of protein features from InterPro, Reactome, and the Gene Ontology Consor­
tium, we discovered additional important features regarding neutrophil degranulation, interferon­
gamma signaling, and clathrin­mediated endocytosis, with the first presenting as the second
most important feature in our model. The two latter features align with our understanding, as
endocytosis is related to the peptide loading pathway of MHC­II, and earlier studies have shown
that interferon­gamma is related to MHC­II expression on cells [52]. To the best of our knowl­
edge, the direct relationship between neutrophil degranulation and protein MHC­II enrichment
in humans has not been proposed. However, one study in bovine tissues has demonstrated
that the presence of neutrophil degranulation product PMN­DGP during the differentiation of
monocytes into macrophages displayed decreased MHC­II expression [53]. Further analysis
of this relation would be necessary to make final conclusions, but it is certainly noteworthy that
this feature presents as one of the three most important features of the final model.

Conclusion
Our project successfully developed a, to the best of our knowledge, first­of­its­kind random
forest ensemble model for predicting protein MHC­II presentation enrichment. By integrating
MS­immunopeptidomics data and constructing nested sets of MHC­II peptides, we uncovered
that only 29% of the human proteome is presented on MHC­II, with the majority of these pep­
tides, specifically 96.8%, forming nested sets. To explore the factors influencing nested MHC­II
enrichment, we considered various features including protein abundance, mRNA expression,
chemical properties, subcellular localization, and secondary protein structures. We conducted
individual analyses of all features and included them in different machine learning models, in
which we incorporated additional features collected through enrichment analysis. From these,
we could present the most important features in relation to protein MHC­II presentation enrich­
ment, including protein abundance, neutrophil degranulation, expression (of the cell used for
data preparation), and subcellular localization as the cell membrane or extracellular space. We
integrated this into various random forest modeling frameworks. Here, we also presented and
utilized a method to consciously partition our data utilizing MST clustering based on protein se­
quence similarity. By utilizing our modeling framework with XGBoost, we could conclude that
our random forest models outperformed in their ability to predict the nested MHC­II enrichment.
Our final random forest ensemble model based on a reduced feature set of 20 features was
able to predict the nested MHC­II enrichment with an AUC of 0.848. The results of our work can
hopefully contribute to the understanding of the complex machinery forming the basis of why
some proteins are presented more on MHC­II than others.
