Abstract
Hospital visits can be a distressing experience for young children, particularly in emer­
gency settings where anxiety is often exacerbated by unfamiliar environments, medical
procedures, and lack of understanding. This thesis explores how interactive, play­based
systems can be designed to reduce anxiety in children aged 4­7 visiting the Pediatric
Emergency Department (PED) at Herlev Hospital. Three low­fidelity concepts were tested
with children in clinical settings, and ”Ambulance Adventure,” an interactive, wall­mounted
board game, was selected for high­fidelity development. The final prototype used RFID­
triggered interactions and audio­based explanations to guide children through a simplified
hospital journey. A mixed­methods evaluation with 13 participants indicated a statistically
significant reduction in self­reported anxiety, as measured by the Wong­Baker FACES
scale (mean score decreased from 2.46 to 1.54, p = 0.027). Observational assessments
using the modified Yale Preoperative Anxiety Scale (m­YPAS) showed a smaller, non­
significant reduction (mean score decreased from 28.39 to 26.60, p = 0.152). Even so,
the prototype was positively received by both children and caregivers, indicating strong
potential and warranting further exploration and refinement.

1
Introduction
1.1
Context and Motivation
In the domain of pediatric healthcare, visiting an emergency department can be a dis­
tressing and anxiety­inducing experience for children and their families. This anxiety not
only affects children’s cooperation during treatment but may also impact their long­term
emotional well­being.
Recognizing the importance of supporting children beyond clinical procedures, this Mas­
ter’s Thesis investigates the design, development and evaluation of interactive, play­
based solutions aimed at reducing pediatric anxiety during emergency visits. By targeting
the waiting period in the PED, a time often overlooked, the project focuses on develop­
ing a physical intervention that is intuitive, developmentally appropriate and engaging for
children aged 4 to 7.
Various strategies to alleviate pediatric anxiety are implemented by different hospitals,
such as employing medical clowns or specially trained nurses. This project, conducted in
close collaboration with Herlev Hospital, explores the specific approaches implemented
there. This partnership provides a unique opportunity to combine real­world observations
and clinical insights from healthcare professionals with human­centered research and
technology development methods.
A key area of interest lies in examining different types of interventions and their potential
to mitigate anxiety. Ultimately, the motivation behind this thesis is to contribute to the
development of a more child­centered emergency care environment, one where anxiety
is recognized and addressed as a central aspect of the patient experience.
In order to design effective interventions, it is first necessary to develop a comprehensive
understanding of pediatric anxiety, its causes, impact, and how it can be reliably mea­
sured.
1.2
Understanding Pediatric Anxiety in Emergency Settings
1.2.1
Definition and Types of Anxiety
Anxiety is a fundamental brain response to perceived danger, manifesting as an emotion
that organisms actively attempt to avoid. This response, present from infancy, exists
on a continuum from mild to severe and is not inherently pathological, as it serves an
adaptive function by promoting avoidance of potential threats [1]. Physiologically, anxiety
is characterized by increased tension, worried thoughts, and bodily responses such as
elevated blood pressure [2].
Anxiety can be categorized into state anxiety and trait anxiety, each representing distinct
aspects of the anxiety spectrum. State anxiety refers to a temporary emotional response
triggered by adverse events or specific stressors. It is characterized by heightened sym­
pathetic nervous system activity, leading to physiological responses such as increased
heart rate and alertness. However, state anxiety is transient and does not necessarily
indicate any underlying pathological condition [3].
In contrast, trait anxiety is a stable personality trait that predisposes individuals to respond
with persistent concerns, troubles, and worries across various situations. Unlike state
anxiety, trait anxiety is considered an enduring characteristic of an individual’s personality

and is often linked to psychopathological conditions and chronic high physiological arousal
[3]. High trait­anxious individuals tend to experience heightened state anxiety in stressful
situations, indicating a strong interaction between these two forms of anxiety [4].
Anxiety is distinct from fear, though the terms are often used interchangeably. Anxiety is
a future­oriented, long­lasting state broadly focused on diffuse threats, whereas fear is a
present­oriented, short­lived response to a specific, identifiable danger [2]. While signifi­
cant research has been conducted on pediatric pain and its management [5, 6, 7], fewer
studies focus explicitly on anxiety assessment methods and the differentiation between
pain and anxiety. The lack of clear differentiation has led to challenges in effectively ad­
dressing anxiety in pediatric emergency care [8].
1.2.2
Causes and Triggers of Pediatric Anxiety in Emergency
Departments (EDs)
Preventive clinic visits, acute care, medical procedures, and hospitalization can be emo­
tionally threatening and psychologically traumatizing for pediatric patients, as they often
experience heightened anxiety in emergency medical settings due to a combination of
environmental, psychological, and physiological factors, Figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: Anxiety factors in PEDs, based on findings synthesized from the literature
The environmental factors of emergency medical settings contribute significantly to height­
ened anxiety in pediatric patients. The unfamiliar and chaotic atmosphere, filled with
medical equipment, unfamiliar healthcare providers, and a fast­paced nature of care, can
induce fear and uncertainty. Additionally, prolonged waiting times increase distress in
children [9]. Going to the hospital can intensify anxiety, as children must leave their home

and caregivers, disrupting their routines. Hospital wards are often perceived as “cold and
medical” environments associated with pain, particularly for elementary school­aged chil­
dren undergoing critical developmental adjustments [10]. Moreover, due to their lack of
control children may develop a sense of helplessness, which coupled with fear and pain
can cause them to feel powerless in healthcare settings. All these can delay treatment
and increase procedure times [11].
The psychological factors contributing to pediatric anxiety in emergency medical settings
stem largely from children’s cognitive development and limited understanding of medical
procedures. Misconceptions about treatments and interventions can amplify fear, particu­
larly when explanations about their necessity and process are unclear. Without adequate
information, children may experience heightened distress, as uncertainty can make med­
ical experiences more intimidating [12].
The physiological factors of anxiety can significantly contribute to distress in pediatric pa­
tients. Physical symptoms such as increased heart rate and hyperventilation can be par­
ticularly alarming for children who may not understand these bodily responses. For those
with pre­existing medical conditions, such as asthma or gastrointestinal illnesses, anxiety
can be further heightened by fears about their health and potential medical emergencies.
This creates a cyclical effect, where anxiety intensifies physical symptoms, which in turn
exacerbates emotional distress [13].
Understanding, identifying, and addressing these factors are essential for improving pedi­
atric patient care in emergency medical settings by effectively mitigating anxiety in young
patients.
1.2.3
Methods for Measuring Anxiety in Children
The measurement of anxiety in children can be broadly categorized into self­report meth­
ods, observational methods, and physiological assessments, Figure 1.2. Each of these
approaches has its strengths and limitations, and their applicability depends on the child’s
age, verbal ability, and the clinical setting.
1.2.3.1
Self­Report Methods
Self­reported anxiety assessments rely on children’s ability to understand and express
their emotional state.
• State­Trait Anxiety Inventory for Children (STAI­C): Developed by Spielberger et
al. (1973) [14], the STAI­C is widely regarded as the gold standard for measuring
childhood anxiety. It consists of 40 items, scored on a 3­point rating scale, with
higher scores indicating greater anxiety [15, 16]. The STAI­C is divided into two
subscales: state anxiety (20 items), which assesses temporary symptoms, and trait
anxiety (20 items), which measures chronic or generalized anxiety. It is designed
for children 9 to 12 years but can also be used in children as young as 5 years old.
It takes approximately 15­20 minutes to complete. Despite its validity, the STAI­C
is lengthy and not suitable for younger children or fast­paced environments like the
ED [15].
• Revised Children’s Manifest Anxiety Scale (RCMAS): Originally derived from the
Children’s Manifest Anxiety Scale (CMAS), the RCMAS developed by Reynolds and
Richmond (1978) [17], comprises 37 dichotomous (Yes/No) items assessing gen­
eralized anxiety in children. A total anxiety score is computed based on 28 anxiety
items (divided into 3 subscales: physiological anxiety, worry/oversensitivity, social
concerns/concentration). The remaining 9 items on the RCMAS constitute the Lie
subscale. It takes approximately 10­15 minutes to complete. It has been widely

• Spence Children’s Anxiety Scale (SCAS): Developed by Spence (1998) [19], the

time constraints are a concern [15].
• Children’s Anxiety Meter­State (CAM­S): Recognizing the need for brief and effi­

visual tool resembling a vertical mercury thermometer, in which children are asked
to show how much worry or anxious feelings they have by showing how far up the
thermometer their feelings are.Its brevity and simplicity make it particularly useful in

• Faces Pain Scale (FPS): The FPS, Figure 1.5, developed by Bieri et al. (1990) [25]

1.2.3.2
Observational Methods
Since young children may struggle to articulate their anxiety levels, observational methods
provide an alternative means of assessment. These methods evaluate visible behaviors
and signs of distress, such as facial expressions, body movements, and vocalizations.
• Modified Yale Preoperative Anxiety Scale (m­YPAS): Originally designed for chil­
dren aged 2­6 years undergoing surgery, the m­YPAS [28] has since been adapted
for older children and ED use. It assesses anxiety based on five behavioral cat­
egories: activity, vocalization, emotional expressivity, state of arousal, and use of
parents. Each item rating is 1 to 4 or 1 to 6, according to the category. The total
m­YPAS score ranges from 23.33 to 100, with higher values indicating greater anx­
iety. The m­YPAS is advantageous in emergency settings due to the fact that it can
be completed in approximately 5 minutes [15, 29].

• Child Behavior Checklist (CBCL): The CBCL is a parent­report questionnaire con­
sisting of 113 items that assesses a broad range of emotional and behavioral prob­
lems in children. There are separate versions for preschool (ages 1.5­5) and school­
age (ages 6­18) children. The checklists consist of statements about the child (such
as “argues a lot”). Responses are coded as 0 (not true), 1 (somewhat or sometimes
true), or 2 (very true or often true). Completion time is approximately 15­20 minutes.
[21]
• Behar Preschool Behavior Questionnaire and Behavior Rating Scale for Blood
Sampling: These tools involve adult observers rating children’s behaviors to assess
anxiety levels, especially in specific medical contexts like blood sampling. Teachers,
parents, or clinicians rate children’s behaviors to determine anxiety levels. [8]
While observational methods are useful, the lack of self­reporting is a limitation as anxiety
is an inherently subjective experience and these methods may not always reflect that
experience. [29]
1.2.3.3
Physiological Assessments
Anxiety also manifests through physiological responses, making biometric measures a
useful complementary tool in anxiety assessment. Common physiological indicators in­
clude [8]:
• Heart Rate Monitoring: Increased heart rate is a well­documented physiological
response to anxiety. Studies have used heart rate variability as a reliable marker of
anxiety states in children.
• Palmar Sweating (Electrodermal Activity): Increased sweating is a reliable indi­
cator of sympathetic nervous system arousal in response to anxiety, though it is less
commonly used in pediatric settings.
• Electromyography (EMG) and Eye Blink Rate: These measures have been em­
ployed in laboratory settings to assess anxiety but are rarely practical for clinical use
due to the complexity of administration.
While physiological measures provide objective data, they assess only one component of
anxiety and often require specialized equipment, making them less feasible in fast­paced
ED environments.
1.2.4
Impact of Anxiety on Medical Procedures and Long­Term Effects
Anxiety in pediatric medical settings can have profound short­ and long­term consequences.
In the ED, heightened anxiety can lead to increased distress, lack of cooperation with
medical personnel, and greater difficulty in performing necessary procedures, such as
venipunctures or sedation [16, 5]. Children experiencing severe anxiety may also require
higher levels of sedation, leading to increased medical risk and extended procedure times
[16].
Beyond the immediate setting, repeated exposure to distressing medical experiences can
contribute to long­term psychological consequences, including the development of pho­
bias related to medical staff or hospitals, avoidance of medical care, and emotional dis­
tress that persists into adulthood [15, 5, 11]. Pediatric patients with anxiety are also at
higher risk of noncompliance with medical recommendations, potentially affecting their
long­term health outcomes [15].

1.3
Thesis Overview
This thesis followed the IMRaD structure (Introduction, Methods, Results, and Discus­
sion) which has become the standard for research reports due to its consistency, and
adaptability. It serves primarily as a framework and it is not rigid but flexible and adapt­
able. Modern research papers often include additional chapters beyond the core IMRaD
structure to improve clarity, organization, and completeness [30]. In this project, two such
chapters were added, one describing the current state of pediatric emergency care at
Herlev Hospital, and one reviewing related work. These additions help contextualize the
study and connect it to existing literature.
The structure of the thesis is as follows:
• Chapter 1 ­ Introduction: Presents the background and context of pediatric anxiety,
explores causes and assessment methods, and explains the motivation behind this
project.
• Chapter 2 ­ Current State of Pediatric Emergency Care at Herlev Hospital: Pro­
vides an overview of the Pediatric Emergency Department, its layout, the patient
journey, and current practices for identifying and managing anxiety.
• Chapter 3 ­ Related Work: Reviews existing non­pharmaceutical interventions
aimed at reducing children’s anxiety in healthcare settings and categorizes them.
• Chapter 4 ­ Methods: Explains the overall design approach and methodology used
to research, design, develop and test the interventions.
• Chapter 5 ­ Results: Presents the outcomes of the design process and user test­
ings.
• Chapter 6 ­ Discussion: Interprets the results, reflects on the study’s limitations,
and outlines opportunities for future work.
• Chapter 7 ­ Conclusion: Summarizes the contributions of the thesis and the po­
tential impact of the developed intervention on pediatric emergency care.

Current State of Pediatric Emergency
Care at Herlev Hospital
This chapter provides an in­depth look at the Pediatric Emergency Department (PED)
at Herlev Hospital, which offers specialized treatment for children and adolescents. The
content is informed by research from the hospital’s official website, on­site observations,
and interviews with medical personnel.
2.1
Overview of Herlev Hospital’s PED
Herlev Hospital [31] has been a key institution in Denmark’s healthcare system since its
establishment in 1976. The Department for Children and Adolescents (Afdeling for Børn
og Unge) operates as a ”hospital within a hospital,” with its own reception area dedicated
to the examination, treatment, and care of young patients.
The department handles both acute and chronic conditions in children from premature
birth to 17 years old and manages approximately 12,000 inpatient sessions and 26,600
outpatient consultations annually, with 80% of cases being acute and 20% chronic con­
ditions. The medical team includes doctors, nurses, psychologists, social workers, and
other specialists. Additional staff such as dietitians, physiotherapists, occupational ther­
apists, and hospital clowns contribute to creating a supportive environment for young
patients.
For emergency cases, children aged 0­12 years are referred to the Pediatric Emergency
Room (Børnelægevagten) by general practitioners or via the emergency hotline (1813),
while those over 12 years old are directed to the main Emergency Department. Certain
cases, such as injuries to the arms and legs or lacerations requiring stitches in children
over two years old, are treated in the general emergency department rather than the
pediatric unit.
2.2
General Layout of the Department

The triage room, Figure 2.2, is where nurses first meet their patients, typically within

In this room, children undergo pre­

receive medication or any other necessary medical treatment. Each room contains a
hospital bed equipped with a tablet, essential medical equipment, and a couple of chairs
to accommodate the parents.

2.3
Patient Journey
The patient journey at Herlev Hospital’s Pediatric Emergency Department (PED), Fig­
ure 2.6, follows a structured process. From arrival to discharge or hospitalization, chil­
dren and their families follow a predetermined path through check­in, triage, waiting and
consultation.
Upon arrival at the hospital, children with their families follow a white line on the floor lead­
ing to the PED reception area. At the Children and Youth Reception (Børne­ og Unge­
modtagelsen), families check in with a reception secretary, where the child’s name, age,
symptoms and referral details are validated.
After check­in, families wait in the common waiting area until a nurse is available to take
them for an initial triage assessment. Triage typically occurs within 10 minutes of arrival,
where nurses assess the child’s temperature, breathing and pain levels using standard­
ized pain scales. If a child has a fever for example, they may be given paracetamol to
reduce discomfort while waiting ( Appendix A.1).
Following triage, patients are either sent to a single­patient room if space is available or
return to the waiting area if their condition is stable but no rooms are available. The waiting
time varies by triage category:
• Green: Up to 2 hours wait
• Yellow: Seen within 1 hour
• Orange: Requires a doctor within 15 minutes
• Red: Immediate care needed
However, due to high patient volume, children may sometimes wait 4­5 hours before see­
ing a doctor. In such cases, families are allowed to leave the waiting area, and nurses
communicate with parents when it is time for their child’s consultation (Appendix A.1).
Once called in for a medical consultation, the child is either moved from the waiting area
to an examination room or seen in their single­patient room if already placed there after
triage. The doctor meets with the family only once or twice during their visit and, in some
cases, may not see the child until after diagnostic tests like blood samples are completed.

2.4
Recognizing and Managing Anxiety
To gain a deeper understanding of how anxiety manifests in children, as well as the strate­
gies used to assess and manage it at Herlev Hospital’s Pediatric Emergency Department
(PED), interviews were conducted with healthcare professionals, including experienced
nurses. This section presents the key findings from these interviews, while the full inter­
view transcripts can be found in Appendix A.1.
2.4.1
Identifying Signs of Anxiety
Recognizing anxiety in children at the Pediatric Emergency Department (PED) requires
experience and observational skills. Nurses rely on both verbal and non­verbal cues to
assess a child’s emotional state. Common signs include:
• Verbal expressions: Some children explicitly state their anxiety by saying, “I am
nervous” or “I do not want to do this.”
• Body language and facial expressions: Children may avoid eye contact, look
down, withdraw into themselves, or physically move closer to their parents.
• Physical behaviors: Signs such as crying, repeatedly saying “No”, or resisting
medical procedures can indicate distress.
• Learned professional intuition: Experienced nurses develop the ability to distin­
guish between fear and other emotions based on subtle cues, such as changes in
tone of voice or behavior patterns.
2.4.2
Measuring Anxiety Levels
Healthcare providers at Herlev Hospital use self­assessment tools to gauge anxiety levels:
1. Wong­Baker FACES Pain Rating Scale: A widely used tool (see Section 1.2.3.1)
that asks children to point to a face that best represents their pain, Figure 2.7.
Nurses must interpret whether high pain scores may actually reflect fear.
2. Scary Scale by Stephanie Thurillet [32]: A scale, Figure 2.8, that allows children to
self­report their fear levels, helping healthcare providers distinguish between pain
and anxiety. While it is not yet a globally standardized tool, it is currently being
piloted at the hospital to explore its clinical usefulness. This scale rates fear with a
score ranging from 0 to 10 (rated two by two, gradually) so that is consistent with
pain rating scales.
Although biometric tools such as smartwatches could potentially be used to track physio­
logical indicators of anxiety, such as elevated heart rate, nurses at the hospital note a key
limitation. In clinical practice, an increased heart rate is typically interpreted as a sign of a
medical issue rather than as a symptom of anxiety, making it less reliable as a standalone
measure of emotional distress.

2.4.3
Triggers of Anxiety in Children
Several environmental and procedural factors contribute to children’s distress at the hos­
pital according to nurses:
• Medical procedures: Blood samples, injections, and stitches are among the most
feared experiences.
• Fear of the unknown: Not knowing what will happen next increases fear levels.
• White coat syndrome: Many children develop anxiety in response to hospital uni­
forms and the sterile white environment.
• Visual environment: The predominantly white hospital setting can feel cold and
intimidating.
• Parental anxiety: Children often mirror their parents’ emotional states; if a parent
is visibly distressed, the child is likely to feel more anxious.

3
Related Work
A literature review was conducted to identify non­pharmaceutical interventions aimed at
reducing children’s anxiety in healthcare settings. More details about this process can be
found in the Methods chapter (see Section 4.1.1). Various interventions were identified
and categorized to enhance understanding. The most relevant ones were examined in
greater depth to gain a clearer picture of existing approaches targeting children in the
specified age group within emergency settings.
3.1
Categories of Interventions
Anxiety in children receiving medical care can lead to distress, reduced cooperation,
and physiological responses that may complicate treatment. It is commonly addressed
through a range of structured interventions.
Based on a synthesis of findings from the literature, the interventions identified were or­
ganized into types according to their primary mechanisms for anxiety reduction. This
categorization includes three main types: Educational Interventions, Relaxation­Based
Interventions, and Distraction­Based Interventions. In practice, these are often combined
to enhance effectiveness, giving rise to what could be considered a fourth, integrative
category.
• Educational Interventions: These provide children with age­appropriate informa­
tion about medical procedures to reduce fear and uncertainty. By offering clear
explanations and familiarization techniques, such as instructional videos/games,
comic information leaflets, and role­playing at the Teddy Bear Hospital (TBH), chil­
dren gain a sense of control and emotional preparedness.
• Relaxation­Based Interventions: These aim to activate the parasympathetic ner­
vous system to counteract anxiety symptoms like increased heart rate and muscle
tension. Techniques include breathing exercises, guided imagery, mindfulness ac­
tivities, aromatherapy, hypnotherapy, and calming sounds such as nature noises or
a recorded mother’s voice.
• Distraction­Based Interventions: These redirect attention away from medical pro­
cedures through engaging and expressive activities. Examples include digital dis­
tractions (e.g., virtual reality, games), play­based methods (e.g., therapeutic play,
balloon inflating, toy­making), and expressive tools like storytelling, music, or art.
• Combined Approach: Many effective pediatric interventions integrate educational,
relaxation, and distraction techniques to address different facets of anxiety simulta­
neously, enhancing both emotional and physiological coping.
This initial categorization can provide a clearer understanding of available interventions,
aiding clinicians, caregivers, and designers in selecting or developing suitable approaches
for anxiety reduction in children undergoing medical care. The diagram in Figure 3.1
visually presents this classification, including examples of specific interventions within
each category that were identified through the literature review process.

The above 22 studies illustrate a wide range of non­pharmaceutical interventions for re­
ducing anxiety in young children in healthcare environments. The full table of the studies
can be found in Appendix A.3.
Play­based interventions, such as puzzle solving, playdough manipulation, building blocks,
and storytelling, were the most common, emphasizing the critical role of engagement and
distraction through play. For example, Yildirim et al. [54] found that toys made from
medical materials significantly reduced anxiety in hospitalized children aged 4­6, with a
notable reduction in Children’s State Anxiety (CSA) scores after the intervention. Another
example, signifying that storytelling is particularly impactful is from Abdi et al. [34] that
conducted a randomized controlled trial comparing storytelling, play therapy, and a con­
trol group, and found that storytelling had the greatest effect in reducing anxiety, especially
in children aged 3­8.
Digital solutions, including virtual reality and robot interactions, represent a growing trend
in technology­assisted anxiety management. For example, research by Bernaerts et al.
[37] supports the use of immersive technologies (VR) in pediatric care settings concluding
that it can reduce anxiety, pain and tension (stress), and increase happiness in a hospital
setting.
The studies varied widely in sample size, from single case studies to larger randomized
controlled trials. For example, the study by Siti Fadhila Sepfiani [51] included only one
child, while Li et al. (2016) involved over 300. While many interventions reported pos­
itive outcomes, those with smaller sample sizes may offer less generalizable evidence
compared to the larger­scale trials.
It is also important to note that each study was conducted within a single hospital setting
in a single country. However, some intervention types do appear repeatedly across differ­
ent regions. For example, both Andi et al. [35] and Siti Fadhila Sepfiani [51], conducted
studies related to puzzle play therapy in different islands of Indonesia. This limited cul­
tural and institutional diversity means that findings may not generalize universally across
healthcare systems, especially when considering variations in clinical workflows, parental
roles or how healthcare professionals interact with child patients.
An important observation across the reviewed studies is the diversity in anxiety mea­
surement tools. Most studies employed standardized scales such as the Spence Chil­
dren’s Anxiety Scale (SCAS), Visual Analog Scale (VAS), Hamilton Anxiety Rating Scale
(HARS), and the Modified Yale Preoperative Anxiety Scale (m­YPAS), and some stud­
ies did not specify their instruments at all. This variation complicates direct comparison
across interventions.
A key gap identified in the literature is the limited number of studies conducted specifically
in emergency department settings. From the aforementioned studies that were included
in the final synthesis after full­text screening, only three of them are conducted in emer­
gency settings, including Stein Duker et al [52], Van der Heijden et al. [53] and Yilmaz &
Demir [55]. Although the findings were positive, the limited number of PED­specific stud­
ies suggests that this area is still underexplored. This trend was already apparent during
the initial screening of titles and abstracts for the broader pool of 180 studies, where very
few were situated in emergency contexts. The unique nature of the PED, marked by acute
medical needs may partly explain why research in this setting remains limited. Nonethe­
less, given that emergency visits often involve more uncertainty, less preparation time and
a lack of continuity in caregiver support, it is crucial to develop and evaluate interventions
that are tailored to the unique emergency context.

Methods
This thesis adopted a human­centered design approach structured around the Double Di­
amond framework [56], Figure 4.1, which supports iterative problem­solving through four
distinct phases. The Double Diamond design process comprises two diamonds. Diamond
One focuses on Discovering and Defining the problem, often referred to as the “Problem
Space.” Diamond Two involves Developing and Delivering the solution, often referred to
as the “Solution Space.”
Figure 4.1: The Double Diamond design process as followed in this project
Discover: This phase involved extensive research, including literature review and field
studies, interviews and observations conducted at Herlev Hospital. The goal was to gather
as much relevant information as possible to understand the problem space.
Define: This phase synthesized the insights gathered into a clear and actionable problem
statement that provided a focused direction for developing solutions aimed at reducing
pediatric anxiety.
Develop: This phase involved ideating and exploring multiple potential solutions through
brainstorming, sketching and low­fidelity prototyping. Minimum viable products (MVPs)
were used to visualize and test ideas, allowing for the exploration of various solutions and
approaches.
Deliver: This phase focused on finalizing and implementing the most promising interven­
tion and evaluating its effectiveness in reducing anxiety. The most promising prototype
was refined and developed into a final solution. This involved testing, validation and it­
eration to ensure the final outcome is both engaging and effectively addresses the initial
problem.

4.1
Problem Discovery
4.1.1
Literature Review Methodology
A literature review aims to provide an overview/synthesis of existing research. The litera­
ture review for this thesis was conducted following the Preferred Reporting Items for Sys­
tematic Reviews and Meta­Analyses (PRISMA) guidelines [57, 58] and aimed to address
the research question: What non­pharmaceutical interventions have been implemented
to reduce children’s anxiety in healthcare settings?
The review focuses on studies published in English from 2015 until the beginning of this
project in February 2025. Literature was sourced from PubMed and DTU FindIt, using a
carefully designed search string to ensure a comprehensive exploration of relevant stud­
ies. The PRISMA flow diagram, screening criteria and other details of this process are
available in Appendix A.4.
To manage the review process efficiently, Silvi [59] was used as a screening and data
extraction tool for Systematic Literature Review. Initially, 1,964 references were imported
into Silvi for screening. The process involved multiple stages, starting with the removal
of 503 duplicate references. Next, the titles and abstracts of the remaining 1,461 studies
were screened, leading to the exclusion of 1,281 papers that did not align with the research
focus (Irrelevant). From these studies, a categorization of the different interventions used
to reduce anxiety in children in medical settings was made (see Section 3.1).
The third step involved re­screening the titles and abstracts of the 180 remaining papers to
further refine the selection. Here, studies were excluded based on various criteria, such
as focusing on a different age group or addressing pre­ or post­surgery anxiety. This
process helped narrow the results to better align with the scope of our thesis, ultimately
resulting in 63 relevant papers.
A fourth step involved a full­text eligibility assessment. The remaining 63 studies, which
appeared relevant based on their titles and abstracts, were thoroughly reviewed to de­
termine their suitability for inclusion. Eligible studies focused on evaluating, measuring
the effectiveness and testing an intervention in mitigating anxiety in children within the
target age group, specifically in emergency departments or among hospitalized children.
In the end, 22 studies met the defined eligibility criteria and were included in the litera­
ture review, providing valuable insights into reducing anxiety in pediatric care settings for
children aged 4­7.
4.1.2
Interviews and Observations
This study employed a combination of semi­structured interviews and informal observa­
tions to gather insights into pediatric anxiety within the emergency department at Herlev
Hospital. Two one­on­one interviews were conducted with nurses working in the Pediatric
Emergency Department, using a semi­structured format that included prepared questions
while also allowing space for open­ended discussion and follow­up. These can be found in
Appendix A.1. In addition, observations were carried out in the waiting area by informally
inspecting the physical environment and noting the behavior of children and their care­
givers. These observations focused on how families interacted, how children responded
to the setting, and the availability of distractions or comforting elements in the space.

4.2.1
Brainstorming
Brainstorming is a foundational method in creative problem solving and design thinking,
particularly during early concept development. After the literature review, the design di­

rection was oriented towards developing physical interactive solutions for reducing pe­
diatric anxiety in emergency settings. To support this focus, a structured brainstorming
session was conducted. Although brainstorming is often characterized by its open­ended
and informal nature, evidence suggests that sessions guided by clear structure and pre­
defined categories tend to yield more focused results [60]. In this case, four key design
dimensions were used to guide ideation:
• Interactions / Mechanisms: Define the physical actions through which a child en­
gages with a toy, such as pressing, squeezing, blowing, or turning. These actions
are central to toy­based design, shaping how children explore, manipulate, and de­

• Sensors: Enable the toy to respond to user input. A wide range of sensors (such
as motion detectors, pressure sensors, RFID readers, and heart­rate monitors) can
support personalized and adaptable interaction patterns.
• Type of Toy/Game: Refers to the overall form or typology of the intervention, such
as plush toys, board games, interactive books, or digital hybrids. The physical form
influences both the possibilities for interaction and the toy’s emotional appeal.
• Feedback / Stimuli: Captures the sensory outputs the toy provides, including sound,
vibration, light, or movement. Feedback mechanisms help close the interaction loop,
enhance understanding, and sustain engagement by signaling responsiveness.
These dimensions were chosen because they reflect fundamental building blocks of in­
teractive toy design. Each category offers a high degree of variability and supports sys­
tematic combination of elements.
The rapid ideation technique, in which participants independently write down their thoughts
before sharing them, was chosen to efficiently produce a large volume of concepts for
each dimension within a short timeframe. This method supports divergent thinking and
can foster more creative outcomes by reducing performance anxiety and promoting cog­
nitive independence.
The brainstorming session contributed to the early­stage exploration of possible design
directions and laid the foundation for selecting and refining concepts for prototyping. The
outcomes of the session were organized into a morphological matrix, which enables struc­
tured recombination of ideas and serves as a tool for the concept generation phase.
4.2.2
Concept Generation
The morphological matrix developed from the brainstorming session was constructed us­
ing four design dimensions (Interactions/Mechanisms, Sensors, Type of Toy/Game, and
Feedback/Stimuli) identified as key components in toy­based interactive systems.
Rather than attempting to generate all possible permutations across these categories,
which would have resulted in an unmanageably large number of combinations, a semi­
random recombination strategy was employed. Exploring the full factorial set of combi­
nations would not only have been infeasible but also likely to result in overly simplistic
concept pairings without sufficient functional integration [61].
To balance creative exploration with feasibility, a small number of combinations were cre­
ated by selecting a few elements from each dimension. Some combinations included
more than one feature from a single dimension, which helped introduce greater functional
richness. This process resembles the approach of grouping highly coupled functions and

4.3
Prototyping
The prototypes were informed by early design research, including a literature review on
pediatric anxiety, informal field observations at Herlev Hospital and research on age­
appropriate toys.
Age­appropriate toys are the ones that comply with the cognitive and physical abilities
of the kids. According to Jean Piaget [63], children aged 4 to 7 are in the preopera­
tional stage of development. At this age, they are able to use symbols (such as words,
drawings, or objects) to represent other things, which allows them to engage in imagina­
tive play and role­playing. They begin to understand basic cause­and­effect relationships
and can follow simple rules and sequences. Physically, they have developed better mo­
tor coordination and are able to perform actions like grasping, squeezing, stacking, and
manipulating small objects. They are also capable of basic problem­solving and enjoy
activities that involve constructing, sorting, and exploring. Based on these cognitive and
physical abilities, the prototypes were designed to be age­appropriate.
4.3.1
Low­Fidelity Prototypes
To explore the potential of interactive, play­based systems for reducing anxiety in children
in the Pediatric Emergency Department, three low­fidelity prototypes were developed.
The concept of each prototype was chosen through the process described in Section
4.2.3. Each prototype was intentionally low­fidelity to allow for rapid iteration and user
testing with minimal resources so that a final idea can be chosen to be further developed.
In order to set the stage for each prototype, a template for Lo­Fi Prototype Plan was
developed including the following sections:
• Title
• Concept summary
• Purpose
• Interaction overview (input, feedback and used materials)
• Storyboard (the steps/actions of child needed to complete the game)
• Potential Testing Questions
• Notes for final prototype (any ideas that could be added in the final prototype)

4.3.2.1
Design
The high­fidelity prototype was developed with reference to Don Norman’s Design Princi­
ples [64], ensuring usability, intuitiveness, and emotional appropriateness for young chil­
dren in high­stress environments like the PED. These are the following:
• Visibility: Refers to how easily users can see and understand available actions.
Important elements should be noticeable and self­explanatory.
• Feedback: Provides users with immediate and clear responses to their actions so
they know what effect they’ve had on the system.
• Constraints: These are design elements that restrict possible actions, helping to
prevent errors and guide the user toward desired behavior.
• Mapping: Refers to the relationship between controls and their effects. Good map­
ping ensures that users can intuitively understand what actions do based on spatial
or symbolic cues.
• Consistency: Ensures that similar actions yield similar results across different con­
texts. This reduces the learning curve and promotes intuitive use.
• Affordance: Refers to the qualities of an object that suggest how it should be used.
Good affordance makes it obvious what users can do with an object.
These principles are especially relevant when designing for young children, who rely on
direct, concrete cues to understand and engage with unfamiliar environments
4.3.2.2
Planning
Similarly to the Low­Fidelity Prototype planning, some important elements of the concept
were outlined so that it can be defined in detail before fabrication.
• Title
Ambulance Adventure
• Goal
The main goal is to reduce anxiety in children aged 4­7 visiting the Pediatric Emer­
gency Department (PED) by familiarizing them with the hospital environment through
playful, interactive engagement. The toy aims to inform children in a non­threatening
and developmentally appropriate manner about the hospital journey, including the
purpose of different rooms, commonly used medical tools and procedures, and the
roles of various healthcare professionals they may encounter.
• Concept and Storyboard
Ambulance Adventure is a wall­mounted interactive board game designed for chil­
dren in the PED waiting area. It features a toy ambulance that follows a route through
a simplified top­down floor plan of the hospital.
The toy car starts from “Home”, and the child can move it manually along the route
at their own pace. When the ambulance enters a room, the buttons in that room
are activated, this is indicated by lights turning on next to each button. In each
hospital room, the child can press the buttons, featuring large icons, that trigger
audio messages explaining what the depicted item does (e.g. “The thermometer
shows if your body is warm or cold”). After visiting all the rooms, the ambulance
returns to “Home”. The child can repeat the journey or interact with the rooms in
any order.

4.4
User Testing and Evaluation
To evaluate the proposed interventions, two testing phases were planned: an initial low­
fidelity prototype testing phase to compare and assess early concepts, and a later testing
of the selected final prototype to determine its effectiveness in reducing anxiety.
4.4.1
Selected Anxiety Assessment Methods
To evaluate the proposed interventions, a combination of self­report and observational
tools was used in order to capture both the child’s subjective experience and observable
indicators of anxiety based on parents. This allowed for cross­validation of the measured
anxiety response.
Two self­report measures were employed: the Wong­Baker FACES Pain Rating Scale
(WBFPRS), which is traditionally used to assess pain but previous studies have demon­
strated its effectiveness in capturing emotional distress, and the Scary Scale (Stephanie
Thurillet), a visual analog scale representing fear levels, which is already in clinical use at
the pediatric emergency department of Herlev Hospital (see Section 2.4.2). These scales
were chosen for their visual simplicity and suitability for children aged 4­7.
For the final testing phase, the Modified Yale Preoperative Anxiety Scale (m­YPAS) was
added to complement the self­reported tools (see Section 1.2.3.2). This validated obser­
vational measure is widely cited in the literature for assessing situational anxiety in young
children. It offers a structured behavioral assessment that captures anxiety manifestations
beyond what children may be able to express verbally.
The use of the m­YPAS was limited to the final prototype testing, where reducing anxiety
is the primary focus. Earlier stages involve testing multiple low­fidelity interventions, so

4.4.2

This initial testing phase was conducted using low­fidelity prototypes, primarily to assess

4.4.2.1
Type of Testing
The user testing conducted in this phase followed a Wizard of Oz methodology. Wizard
of Oz testing is an established approach in Human­Computer Interaction (HCI) research
in which a human operator simulates the behavior of a system that has not yet been fully
implemented [67]. This technique allows for early exploration of interaction patterns, user
expectations, and emotional responses without requiring a fully functional system. This
was especially useful in the present case since the prototypes were low­fidelity and did
not have full technical functionality.
During testing sessions, in response to user actions, visual changes and interactive feed­
back were manually simulated, for instance, by triggering a visual change or mimicking
an interactive feedback mechanism. This allowed children to experience a version of the
interaction flow as it was envisioned in the final system design, while allowing us to col­
lect authentic user reactions and engagement patterns. The Wizard of Oz method was
particularly appropriate given the nature of the interventions, where the illusion of respon­
siveness could be effectively created through real­time facilitation.
4.4.2.2
Participants
The participants were children aged between 4 and 7 years, each accompanied by at
least one parent or caregiver. Recruitment was conducted informally within the Pediatric
Emergency Department (PED) of Herlev Hospital, primarily in the waiting area or single
patient rooms. Due to the low number of children visiting the PED during the testing
period, it was decided to also include children from the outpatient clinic, located across
the hall from the PED. This decision was supported by the rationale that, although these
children visited the hospital by appointment and were likely less stressed or not in acute
pain compared to those in the emergency department, they were still situated in a hospital
environment and required to wait for treatment in the same waiting area. A total of six
children participated in the testing of each of the three prototype interventions.
All participants were recruited on a voluntary basis. Prior to the testing session, parents
were informed about the study and were asked if they wanted to participate. After the
session, the parents were given a digital consent form which they accessed by scanning
a QR code. This form included a short demographic questionnaire, to collect information
on the child’s age, gender, previous ED visits and previous hospitalizations. The form can
be found in Appendix A.6. Participation was anonymous, and no identifiable personal data
were collected (video/audio).

were employed. First, the modified Yale Preoperative Anxiety Scale (m­YPAS) was used

developed and used. This form consisted of several sections, as described below, that
capture important metrics for the child­toy interaction.

time spent with the toy, number of buttons pressed, number of audios listened to, whether
the full ambulance route was completed, and the number of parent­child interactions.
These indicators were selected in line with the User­Engagement Framework by O’Brien
and Toms [68], which highlights total time, interaction count, and task completion as direct
markers of engagement in child­computer interaction. These markers are independent of
the modality and can also be applied whenever a child engages with any goal­directed
activity. The inclusion of parent­child interaction follows Rogoff’s guided­participation the­
ory [69], which emphasizes the role of shared attention in sustaining engagement and
supporting learning.
Intuitiveness: It was noted whether the child independently understood the mechanics of
the game or required verbal explanation or support from the caregiver. Recording these

5.1
Problem Discovery
5.1.1
Literature Review
The literature review conducted with the help of Silvi AI resulted in 22 studies that provide
valuable insights into reducing anxiety in pediatric care settings for children aged 4­7.
The literature showed that play­based interventions are the most commonly used and
consistently effective in reducing anxiety in young children. However, very few studies
have been conducted in emergency settings, and most interventions do not rely on active,
hands­on participation by the child.
An overview of these studies, along with detailed commentary, is provided in Section 3.2.
5.1.2
Interviews and Observations
Along with the Literature Review, two interviews with nurses were also conducted. Most
of the interviews focused on the current state of recognising and managing anxiety at
Herlev Hospital (see Section 2.4). Four significant points extracted from the interviews
are the following:
Several systemic and environmental factors contribute to heightened anxiety among chil­
dren in the Pediatric Emergency Department (PED). One recurring theme is the strong
influence of prior hospital experiences. According to Gitte, a researcher and Clinical Nurs­
ing Specialist at the hospital, children who have previously visited often exhibit elevated
anxiety levels during subsequent visits. In addition, the emotional state of parents plays
a significant role in shaping the child’s response to medical environments. She noted that
children often mirror their parents’ emotions. For example, when mothers cry or fathers
appear visibly upset, the child’s anxiety tends to increase.
Another challenge concerns the limitations of existing anxiety assessment tools. While
the Wong­Baker FACES Pain Rating Scale and the Scary Scale are used for subjective
self­reporting, concerns were expressed about their validity, particularly among younger
children who may not yet have the cognitive maturity to accurately articulate or quantify
their emotions. Moreover, although biometric measurements such as heart rate are some­
times captured using smartwatches, it was reported that elevated physiological responses
are often interpreted as medical symptoms rather than indicators of anxiety.
An additional complexity lies in the time constraints inherent to emergency departments.
While nurses recognize the value of allowing children time to adjust and feel secure, the
demands of room turnover and limited staff availability often limit their ability to provide
extended emotional support. As a result,interventions intended to reduce anxiety are often
applied reactively, once a child is visibly distressed, rather than proactively. This reactive
approach may lead to increased use of sedation or procedural restraint, especially when
children are too anxious to cooperate with necessary procedures.
Finally, examples from other hospitals, such as programs where kindergartens are in­
vited to tour the facility and familiarize children with the hospital environment and medical

equipment, were mentioned by Gitte as promising, yet currently unavailable, strategies
for proactive anxiety prevention, aiming to reduce fear before a visit becomes necessary.
The transcripts from the interviews can be found in Appendix A.1.
Additional on­site observations were made during multiple visits to the hospital throughout
the duration of this project, helping to build a comprehensive understanding of the clinical
environment. Most children tend to remain close to their parents, with only a few mov­
ing around freely. Parents often take an active role in managing their child’s emotional
state by using food, toys brought from home, or reading books and answering questions
together. Despite these efforts, the waiting area itself offers limited support for distraction
or engagement. Aside from a small aquarium, a few wall stickers, and a table football
setup, there are few environmental features designed to comfort or entertain children.
5.1.3
Problem Definition
The knowledge gained from the literature review, combined with insights from fieldwork at
Herlev Hospital, forms the foundation of the first diamond in the Double Diamond design
process. Based on these findings, a focused problem statement was developed to guide
the direction of the project. This thesis aims to address the identified gaps by designing,
developing, and evaluating interactive, play­based solutions specifically tailored to the
PED environment, with the goal of reducing anxiety in young children during hospital
visits.
Problem Statement: How can we design an interactive solution to reduce pediatric anx­
iety in the waiting area of the Pediatric Emergency Department at Herlev Hospital?
Subquestions:
1. How can we design interactive solutions that are compatible with the spatial and
procedural characteristics of the Herlev PED environment?
2. What different interactive solutions can be designed to reduce pediatric anxiety at
Herlev Hospital and which one shows the highest potential?
3. How effective is the selected solution in reducing anxiety in real­life settings and
how is it experienced by children and caregivers?

5.3.1
Prototyping
The following sections provide an overview of the three low­fidelity prototypes that were
developed, including their design goals, materials, and interaction flow. Additional mate­
rial can be found in Appendix A.11.
5.3.1.1
Prototype 1: Air­Balloon Ride
This concept explored the use of breathing and squeezing as calming and empowering
interactions. The prototype, Figure 5.1 consisted of a cardboard board featuring two mov­
able elements: a cloud, made of foam, and an air balloon, constructed from colored paper
sheets. The board included two icons labeled with numbers: ”1” showing “Pust!” (blow)
and ”2” showing “Klem!” (press), providing visual guidance for the interaction sequence.
Since the prototype’s mechanics were not functional at this stage, all movements were
performed by the facilitator to simulate the intended interactions.
The goal of the game was to move away the clouds and clear the sky so the air balloon
could rise. The visual and tactile design aimed to engage young children through vivid
colors and diverse textures, including felt details, to stimulate sensory interest.
The interactive elements were connected to the board via cables: a cup for blowing and
a stress ball for squeezing. Each time the child blew into the cup, the cloud was man­
ually moved by the facilitator one step along a numbered horizontal axis, symbolizing a
gradual shift from anxiety to calm. Similarly, with every squeeze of the stress ball, the air
balloon was manually moved one step upward along a vertical axis, offering a sense of
progression or reward. These numbered “steps” on the board provided visual feedback
and reinforced the connection between the child’s actions and the resulting movement.
The sequence was designed to support regulated breathing and focused motor control,
drawing inspiration from breathing techniques used in pediatric anxiety management.

5.3.1.2
Prototype 2: Match the (E)motion
This concept explored the use of distraction through problem­solving and physical en­
gagement as a strategy for reducing anxiety in young children. The prototype, Figure 5.2,
took the form of a mechanical gear puzzle and consisted of multiple custom­designed,
3D­printed components. A board and a set of ten gear pieces, varying in size, were cre­
ated. The largest gears, each with 10 teeth, served as the start and end points of the
puzzle and were fixed on the board (i.e., they could rotate but their positions could not be
changed).
The objective of the game was to connect one of the emotion­themed starting gears to the
end gear with the crank, forming a complete mechanical chain by selecting and placing
the correctly fitting gears. This process symbolized a journey from a particular emotional
state toward resolution and completion.
Children could choose between three emotion­themed gears: happy, neutral, and sad,
which were also color­coded. These starting gears were placed at one end of the board.
The child then had to complete the corresponding colored path by selecting and plac­
ing four out of the six available gears onto the color­matched pegs, linking the chosen
emotion gear to the crank at the other end. When assembled correctly, turning the crank
set the entire gear chain in motion, providing satisfying visual and tactile feedback. The
mechanical movement reinforced a sense of accomplishment.
During early prototyping, several issues arose. The gear pieces were initially designed
using an online gear generator. The original idea was to create the gears by carving them
out of cork, but it became clear that for the gears to interconnect and rotate properly, there
should be minimal friction, and the margin for error was very small. As a result, 3D printing
was chosen instead.
The gears generated online were created in Fusion 360, along with the board and its
pegs. Printer inconsistencies led to warping and failed prints, necessitating multiple it­
erations to ensure the components were durable and fit together smoothly. Achieving

5.3.1.3
Prototype 3: Ambulance Adventure
This concept explored the use of educational play and narrative sequencing to reduce
anxiety by familiarizing children with the hospital journey in a playful and engaging way.
The prototype, Figure A.14, took the form of an interactive hospital map built from laser­
cut wood, engraved with illustrations of key locations a child might encounter during a
typical visit to the PED. A route was cut through the board, guiding the child from one
room to the next using a movable wooden toy car attached to the track. In each room,
there were icons with animated­style illustrations, mounted on small cardboard squares
to give them a slight 3D effect, representing buttons for objects relevant to that specific
room.

(b) Toy car stopped at the Reception

The objective of the game was for the child to drive the car around the hospital floor plan,
passing through four numbered key stops: Reception, Triage Room, Waiting Area, and
Examination Room, while learning information about each room along the way. The route
concluded with the child “leaving” the hospital, represented by a home icon at the end of
the track.
The child could start moving the car along the track and stop at pieces of tape labeled
”STOP” in each room. There, they would press the cardboard elements in that room
one by one. When the child pressed one of these elements (acting like buttons), a short
”audio” explanation was triggered (the transcript was read aloud by the parents in Dan­
ish) describing the object’s purpose and use in simple, child­friendly terms. The small
cardboard elements represented relevant medical items (e.g., thermometer, oximeter),
objects (e.g., aquarium, sofas), and hospital staff (e.g., doctors, nurses). The specific

icons placed in each room were chosen to reflect what a child might encounter at each
step of the patient journey (see Section 2.3) . Once the child had pressed and listened
to all the information in a room, the “stop” tape was removed, allowing them to continue
driving the car to the next room and repeat the process until they reached the end, marked
by the home icon.
During the prototyping process, the car was designed to stay securely on the track using
a 3D­printed piece glued to its bottom. The piece consisted of two parts put together and
was shaped to slide along the track while preventing the car from being lifted out. Initially,
the plan was to laser­cut the design into cardboard, but testing showed that it would not
be stable enough to withstand the handling and movement by children. As a result, it
was laser­cut from wood to ensure greater durability. Finally, the border of the board was
spray­painted blue to add a touch of color and make the game visually more appealing.
The prototype aimed to support predictability and emotional preparedness. By moving
through the hospital journey at their own pace and receiving explanatory input, children
could build cognitive familiarity with potentially anxiety­inducing procedures and spaces.

Reflections
The following reflections highlight important considerations and limitations observed dur­
ing the testing process. Most of the participating children were not visibly anxious, stressed,
or in pain during their hospital visit, which limits the reliability of the anxiety scales as in­
dicators of emotional impact. Additionally, all children completed the games, as each
prototype featured a clear final goal. As a result, the duration of engagement may not
accurately reflect the level of interest or immersion, since the tasks were structured rather
than open­ended.
Furthermore, children’s cognitive and interactional abilities varied considerably and did not
always correspond with their chronological age. While some younger children needed
more support from parents, others demonstrated skills beyond what might typically be
expected for their age. This variation suggests that dividing the age group or tailoring
separate interventions for narrower age ranges may not be necessary.

Based on the results, no single prototype emerged as a clear winner, and the testing did

Comparison
It is important to acknowledge that the three prototype games differed significantly in both
design and purpose, which complicates direct comparisons. Firstly, the type of gameplay
varies: one game was designed primarily for relaxation, another for distraction, and the
third for educational engagement. These differing intentions inherently influence how chil­
dren interact with the games, affecting metrics such as engagement time, attention focus,
and emotional response.
Secondly, the total play duration required to complete each game’s intended interaction
flow varied considerably. For example, the Air Balloon Ride game involved five blowing
and five squeezing actions, resulting in a relatively short and straightforward interaction.
50
Developing Interactive Solutions to Reduce Children’s Anxiety in the Pediatric Emergency Department

In contrast, the Ambulance Adventure included a navigational element and the sequential
presentation of information across 11 items, leading to a significantly longer play session.
Finally, the Match the (E)Motion game featured three levels of difficulty, encouraging re­
peated play within a single session and potentially increasing both time­on­task and depth
of engagement.
Taking all of the above into account, and in order to choose one of the prototypes for
further development, especially since all prototypes performed well and received similarly
high scores based on the initial evaluation criteria, newly defined criteria are needed to
allow for a validated comparison of the high­fidelity solutions:
Gameplay Resilience: Evaluates whether the game can still be played as intended in
cases where parts are lost, misplaced, or not used as expected.
Durability: Assesses how well the prototype withstands repeated or rough handling, in­
cluding drops, pressure, or twisting.
Cleanability: Assesses how easily the prototype can be cleaned and maintained.
Wall­scale Potential: Evaluates whether the concept could be adapted into a wall­mounted
or large­scale format.
Ease of Fabrication: Assesses how easily the prototype can be built using available ma­
terials, tools, and skills, considering time and resource constraints during the development
phase.
Cost: Evaluates the total material cost of the prototype.

5.4.1
Prototyping
The following sections provide an overview of how the high­fidelity prototype was devel­
oped, Figure 5.5, including the design process, construction steps, materials and com­
ponents used, and the overall outcome. While a complete new design iteration was not
conducted, several design elements were revisited and refined during the building phase
as practical limitations and usability issues emerged. These adjustments were made to
improve functionality and durability.
Figure 5.5: Final look of the ”Ambulance Adventure” game board
5.4.1.1
Design
Board
To model the entire game board, Fusion 360 [75], a cloud­based 3D CAD, CAM and CAE
software developed by Autodesk, was used. The final design of the game board was
based on the initial low­fidelity prototype (see Section 5.3.1.3), with several improvements
introduced.
Most notably, the toy­ambulance used in the interaction was designed to resemble an
ambulance, reinforcing the hospital context and enhancing narrative immersion. The ori­
entation of the layout was adjusted, and the path was redesigned to form a continuous
circular route. This not only enabled uninterrupted, repeatable play without requiring man­
ual resetting, but also ensured that if a child stopped playing midway, another child could
seamlessly continue the activity. While the game still has a logical starting and ending

==PAGE:62==
point at ”Home”, the circular layout avoids enforcing a strict progression, allowing chil­
dren to engage with the experience in a more self­directed manner. The overall size of
the board was increased to provide children with more space for movement, encouraging
more embodied interaction. Additionally, 3D walls were added to separate the different
rooms, clearly signaling transitions between areas of the hospital.
For the toy­ambulance, its proportions were chosen to be relative to the size of the board
while also small enough to be easily grasped by a child’s hand. An approximate size of 6
cm × 4 cm × 5 cm was targeted. A design found online [76], Figure 5.6, measuring 7 cm
× 3.9 cm × 4.8 cm was selected as a close fit.
Figure 5.6: 3D render of the toy­ambulance
The base of the board was designed based on a simplified representation of the actual
layout of the PED (see Section 2.2), including the main rooms that a patient typically visits
during their time in the department, Figure 5.7. Engraved outlines were used to mark the
wall positions so that vertical wall pieces could later be fitted securely into those grooves.
The walls were designed to be 8 cm tall, sufficiently high to provide clear spatial separation
between rooms, yet low enough to maintain visibility of other elements on the board.
Door­shaped openings were incorporated to allow the ambulance to pass easily between
rooms. For structural stability and ease of assembly, finger joints were used at the corners
of the outer walls, while mortise and tenon joints connected the inner walls to the external
structure, Figure 5.8.
To simulate the patient journey (see Section 2.3), a continuous path, Figure 5.9, was
modeled connecting the different rooms. The path avoided sharp 90­degree corners to
support smooth movement of the ambulance. It also featured curves and twists to cre­
ate a more engaging play experience and to maximize the length of the route within the
given space. Adequate spacing between the path and the walls was ensured to allow the
ambulance to pass unobstructed.
Further details were added to enhance usability and visual clarity. The names of the rooms
and the hospital were designed to be engraved using font sizes and styles that could be
read from a distance. A hole for a speaker was positioned at the mid­height of the board’s
right side, approximately aligned with the average height of a child’s head to optimize
audio directionality. Additional holes were included for buttons and corresponding LEDs

Electronics
To make the game functional, the system was powered using Arduino boards. Arduino
boards were chosen due to their suitability for rapid prototyping, as they offer a low­cost,
user­friendly, and highly customizable environment for building interactive systems. The
system schematics shown in this and subsequent sections, were developed using Fritzing
[77], an open­source electronics design software known for its ease of use and extensive
component library.
Figure 5.11: Overview of system’s components
The system’s main electronic components included RFID readers, an MP3 module, a
speaker, buttons, and LEDs, Figure 5.11. The initial concept involved placing a single
RFID reader on the ambulance and embedding RFID tags at the entrances of the rooms.
This setup allowed the reader to scan tags upon entering a room and detect its corre­
sponding ID. However, this approach presented mechanical challenges: due to the cir­
cular route and the presence of supports underneath the board, the wires from the RFID
reader would get entangled. An alternative design, attaching the board’s “island” to the
main structure via the walls without supports, was also considered but was ultimately
deemed insufficiently stable.
To address these challenges, the system was redesigned to invert the RFID logic. Instead
of placing a reader on the ambulance, five RFID readers were mounted on the underside
of the board, each associated with a room. The ambulance itself carried a tag, allowing
the system to identify the room it entered by detecting which reader was triggered as the
ambulance passed by.
The complete system design required 5 RFID readers, an MP3 module, a speaker, 11
buttons, and 11 LEDs, Figure 5.12. A single Arduino board could not accommodate all
necessary components, so the design incorporated two Arduino boards to expand pin
availability and separate system responsibilities. Even with the distribution of components
across two microcontrollers, the number of available pins remained limited. To resolve
this, a multiplexer was used to manage the button inputs, reducing the number of required
pins from 11 to 4.
As shown in Figure 5.12, the system utilizes two Arduino boards. Arduino A, positioned
on the left side of the schematic, is responsible for managing the RFID readers and the
MP3 module. Arduino B, located on the right, controls the LEDs and buttons. This division
of tasks allows for better distribution of processing load and simplifies wiring by grouping
related components under each microcontroller.
To facilitate communication between the two Arduinos, several methods were considered
(Serial, I2C, and SPI). Serial communication was selected due to its simplicity and ease
of implementation, as system scalability (that I2C could offer) was not a primary concern.

The finalized design includes all component connections, appropriate resistors for LEDs

Figure 5.12: Overview of system schematic
The audio files that play when the button is pressed were created by asking one of the
student nurses at the hospital to read and record the transcripts, which can be found in
Appendix A.11.2. Since the content is in Danish, a native speaker was chosen instead
of an AI voice to ensure a more natural delivery and a warmer, more engaging tone for
children to listen to.
The logic of the game was translated into code for the two Arduinos, Figure 5.13. Arduino
A continuously monitors its five RFID readers and when an RFID tag is detected, it iden­
tifies which room it corresponds to (e.g., ”Room 3”) and sends this room name to Arduino
B. Once Arduino B receives the room message, it activates the corresponding LEDs to
reflect that the current room is activated. It then monitors whether a button that belongs
to that room is pressed and, if so, sends the button index (e.g., ”3”) back to Arduino A.
Upon receiving this value, Arduino A plays the associated audio file.
The complete Arduino code can be found in Appendix A.13.
Overall, the design of the board and electronics was carried out with the intention of com­
plying with Norman’s Principles, (Section 4.3.2.1):
1. Visibility: When the child drives the ambulance into a room, the corresponding
buttons light up, clearly signaling that they are active and ready to be pressed. Ad­
ditionally, the spatial layout of the game (including 3D walls, icons, and the ambu­
lance track) enhances spatial understanding and makes the structure of the hospital
environment understandable.
2. Feedback: The lights in the room light up when the ambulance enters, giving visual
confirmation that the room is active and responsive. When a child presses a button
in a room, an audio message plays, offering information about a hospital object or
person. These responses help the child understand that their actions are having a
direct effect.

3. Constraints: The physical track constrains the ambulance’s movement to a pre­

Additionally, only the buttons in the room where the ambulance is currently located
are activated, which constrains the interaction space and minimizes potential con­
fusion. While the system allows freedom of exploration in terms of room order and
pacing, these embedded constraints help ensure that interactions remain meaning­
ful and context­specific.
4. Mapping: The layout of the hospital on the board mirrors a real­world hospital envi­
ronment, with buttons in each room designed as visual icons representing medical
staff, equipment or objects.
5. Consistency: All rooms share the same interaction pattern, with buttons that are
visually and physically consistent in size and shape, helping children predict what
to expect in each space. Additionally, the style of icons and the tone of the audio
explanations remain uniform across the game.
6. Affordance: The buttons invite physical interaction, as their design clearly suggests
they should be pressed.
The toy ambulance on the track naturally encourages
movement along the path, since children easily recognize it as a drivable object.
Additionally, the layout of the board, along with the 3D walls and openings, clearly
communicates that the toy is intended to be explored.
5.4.1.2
Build
Board
For the construction of the board, the design was precisely cut using a 5­axis CNC ma­
chine [78] on a 6 mm plywood sheet.
Since the board features a circular path, this creates an ”island” in the center that is not
physically connected to the rest of the board. To support this isolated section and ensure
the structural integrity of the overall design, a layered construction was implemented.
Specifically, a backboard was added and foam spacers were used between the front
board and the backboard to provide the necessary depth for housing the electronics on
the reverse side of the front board. This layered structure also helped keep the central ”is­
land” in its intended position. The entire assembly was built like a sandwich, Figure 5.14,
consisting of a plywood front, a middle foam layer, and a plywood back. Wooden blocks
were placed within the foam layer to act as anchors, allowing all three layers to be securely
screwed together.
Figure 5.14: 3D render of the layered board construction

The development of the electronic system began with testing the components (includ­
ing the MP3 module, the multiplexer, the RFID readers and the communication between
the two arduino boards) individually, using small breadboard circuits. This helped verify
their functionality and allowed for an understanding of their behavior prior to full system
integration.
Following successful individual tests, a proof­of­concept circuit was built using all essen­
tial components and two RFID readers. Standard Arduino jumper wires (maximum 20
cm) were used. The successful operation of this prototype validated the approach and
confirmed readiness for scaling up to the full version.
Once the layout of the board and its build process were done, assembly of the system
on the physical board began. The primary elements, two Arduino boards and two bread­
boards along with the battery packs, were positioned according to the planned layout,
Figure 5.18. Assembly started with fixing the buttons and LEDs on their specified places
onto the board, trimming wires to optimal lengths for neat routing.
Buttons and LEDs were secured using a hot glue gun. The wires were stabilized using
paper tape. The Arduino boards and RFID readers were mounted onto 3D­printed holders
and fastened with screws. Breadboards and mounts were attached to the baseboard
using double­sided adhesive tape.
During the first test of the fully assembled system, none of the RFID readers responded.
Investigation revealed that signal degradation occurred due to excessive wire length. The

==PAGE:73==
Figure 5.18: Blueprint of the position of key components on the back side of the board
MFRC522 RFID reader uses the SPI communication protocol, which is highly sensitive
to signal integrity over longer distances. It was found that SPI communication became
unreliable when wire lengths exceeded 75 cm. This required a redesign of the wire routing
to keep connections within this functional limit.
Even after adjusting wire lengths, system stability remained an issue. The RFID readers
would intermittently fail to initialize or respond to scans. After further research, it became
apparent that power instability was a major cause. The MFRC522 module is particularly
sensitive to fluctuations in the 3.3V supply. Voltage noise or sagging can cause SPI com­
munication errors, microcontroller crashes, or silent failures of the RFID module.
To resolve this, the power strategy was revised. The RFID readers were decoupled from
the Arduino’s onboard 3.3V regulator and instead powered via an external regulated 3.3V
source. A breadboard power supply module was introduced for this purpose. Additionally,
decoupling capacitors (220 μF) were placed between the 3.3V and ground pins of each
RFID reader to suppress high­frequency noise. A larger bulk capacitor (1000 μF) was
also added to the power rail to buffer current surges. This significantly improved system
reliability.
Further challenges arose with the DFPlayer Mini MP3 module and its speaker. One is­
sue was incorrect audio playback order, even though the filenames appeared correctly
sorted on the SD card. This occurred because the DFPlayer does not use filenames for
playback sequencing. Instead, it relies on the FAT filesystem’s internal file index, which
is determined by the order in which files are copied to the card.
To fix this, the SD card was reformatted using the official SD Card Formatter, then popu­
lated with correctly named files (e.g., 0001.mp3, 0002.mp3, etc.) and finally loaded with
files in the intended sequence by dragging them all at once into the card’s root directory.
This ensured consistent indexing and fixed the audio order.
Another problem was insufficient speaker volume. This was addressed by processing the
audio files in Audacity [79], an open­source audio editor. Dynamic range compression
was applied to reduce the volume difference between loud and quiet sections of speech.
Additionally, a makeup gain of 10 dB was applied during compression to boost overall

==PAGE:74==
loudness without distorting the audio.
Although the system was now mostly functional, intermittent audio cutouts appeared dur­
ing playback. These were traced back to brownout conditions, a drop in voltage when the
DFPlayer module experienced sudden current spikes, causing it to reset or stop playback.
This issue was mitigated by adding a decoupling capacitor (220 μF) between the power
and ground pins of the DFPlayer module, absorbing transient current spikes, ensuring a
more stable voltage supply during audio playback.
Finally, two toggle switches were added to the battery power sources supplying the Ar­
duinos, allowing the game to be turned off, since the breadboard power supply already
includes a built­in switch.

6
Discussion
The final prototype, Ambulance Adventure, was developed to explore how interactive,
play­based systems can reduce pediatric anxiety in the waiting area of the Pediatric Emer­
gency Department at Herlev Hospital. The following discussion interprets the testing out­
comes in greater depth, outlines the main contributions of the project in response to the
initial research questions, addresses the limitations and proposes directions for future
work and further development of the concept.
6.1
Interpretation of Results
Looking more closely, beyond the numbers and statistics collected during testing of the
high­fidelity prototype, can offer insights into how children experienced and understood
the intervention. Interpreting these observations helps uncover the underlying factors that
shaped their engagement, behaviors and performance, as well as provide indications of
whether and how the intervention may have contributed to reducing their anxiety in the
hospital setting.
Initially, when it comes to the children’s engagement with the game, all participants com­
pleted a full route. In general, the children pressed most, if not all, of the buttons and
most of those times stayed to listen to the entire audio before pressing the next button or
moving along. This behavior indicates that the narration and explanations were effective
in capturing and maintaining their attention.
Regarding the intuitiveness of the intervention, all children appeared to understand that
they could drive the ambulance around the board and began doing so independently,
without the need for explanation. However, this was not the case for the buttons. A
number of participants did not recognize the buttons as interactive components and only
pressed them after prompted by either their parents or by the facilitators.
In addition to providing such guidance, parents often supported their children by respond­
ing to their questions. When children encountered unfamiliar or intriguing audio content,
many turned to their parents for clarification or to share their curiosity, resulting in mo­
ments of joint engagement. In general, when parents were physically close, they tended
to participate in the experience much like they would in any other play activity. However,
there were also cases where parents allowed their children to explore the intervention in­
dependently. This range of involvement reflects common patterns in how children interact
with toys and games and suggests that the intervention remains functional and engaging
both with and without adult participation.
Another relevant observation concerning parents that can be noted is that when the game
was placed in the waiting area, some parents passing by either alone or with younger chil­
dren, outside the targeted 4­7 age group, briefly stopped to interact with it. In the latter
instances, the parents often took the lead, engaging with the game while their children ob­
served or participated minimally. This suggests that for younger age groups, the interven­
tion may become a shared experience, with the parent guiding the interaction, explaining
what is happening and using the opportunity to initiate a conversation that supports the
child’s understanding of the hospital environment.
From an ease­of­use perspective, all participating children in this age group were able to
physically access the entire game board. Some of the older and taller children even chose
Developing Interactive Solutions to Reduce Children’s Anxiety in the Pediatric Emergency Department
75
==PAGE:84==
to kneel in order to better reach the bottom areas, suggesting that the game could also
be accessible for slightly older children who may choose to interact with it. For example,
when a 12­year­old boy passing by began interacting with the game, he remarked that it
had caught his attention and that he would be willing to play with it in a hospital setting.
However, all participants, regardless of age, experienced difficulty when attempting to
move the toy­ambulance along the track as it frequently became stuck. This mechanical
issue may undermine the intended enjoyable nature of the experience and could poten­
tially lead to disengagement, particularly if a child becomes tired or irritated by repeatedly
attempting to slide a component that does not function as expected.
When assessing knowledge retention following interaction with the game, participants
performed notably well on the post­intervention questions. Particularly Question 3, which
was about which object (oximeter) performs which function (showing pulse rate). The high
accuracy in responses suggests that participants retained information presented during
gameplay. Notably, the wording of the question closely mirrored the phrasing used in
the corresponding audio when the oximeter button was pressed, indicating that verbal
cues may have played a significant role in supporting memory. Nevertheless, it cannot be
excluded that some children used a process of elimination, selecting the correct answer
by ruling out the more familiar items (thermometer and syringe, which served as the other
multiple­choice options) that they knew did not match the described function.
Similarly, performance was strong on Question 1, which assessed the ability to match an
object (the aquarium) to the room (the waiting area) where it can be found. Some par­
ticipants had not yet learned to read, which likely contributed to difficulties in interpreting
the written labels on both the board and the answer sheet. As a result, some children
gave incorrect or seemingly random answers, while others, despite selecting the correct
location, pointed to the icon on the game board or even to the actual aquarium in the room
instead of marking the answer on the sheet. This behavior suggests a reliance on visual
recognition and spatial memory rather than reading comprehension.
Question 2 focused on conceptual understanding of spatial layout and sequencing in the
hospital setting by asking how many rooms (4) the patient passes through during the
journey. Most participants answered correctly. Among those who selected the option with
one more room than the correct number, it appears that the ”home” icon on the board was
mistakenly included in their count as part of the hospital path. For those who selected
the option with one less room than the correct number, the reasons are less clear, but it
is possible that they failed to count a room where no audio was played, perhaps because
they did not press the button in that room or due to confusion or inattention.
It is also worth noting that a few children did not provide answers to certain questions.
In some cases, this was expressed through a verbal response such as “I don’t know”
or by nodding. For example, one seven­year­old child with ADHD did not answer any
of the questions and his play session was noticeably more rushed and less structured
than those of other participants. In other instances, such as with the oximeter question,
non­responses appeared to be linked to the fact that the children had not pressed the
corresponding button during gameplay and therefore had not heard the relevant audio
information.
Overall, no clear correlation could be observed between participants’ ages and their per­
formance on the quiz. This may be attributed to the varying developmental levels among
children, which are influenced by factors beyond age. For instance, some younger chil­
dren demonstrated reading abilities, while some older participants had not yet learned to

read. Additionally, a few children spoke multiple languages, which may have influenced
both their cognitive development and their ability to understand or interact with different
aspects of the intervention. These variations highlight that developmental differences
within this age group can be shaped by a range of individual factors such as the family
environment.
When asked whether they would like to play the game again, the majority of children re­
sponded positively. This indicates a general willingness to repeat the experience, which
is a promising indicator of the prototype’s perceived playability and acceptance. Interest­
ingly, even among the few children who responded negatively, some had already com­
pleted more than one route through their play session, suggesting that their decision may
have been influenced by having already explored the content rather than a lack of interest.
Notably, it was observed that all children enjoyed moving the ambulance around the track.
So in cases where a child has already pressed all the buttons and listened to the corre­
sponding audio content, they could possibly continue to engage with the game by sliding
the vehicle along the track and through the doors resembling the kind of tactile play seen
with bead maze toys, where the satisfaction comes from the movement itself rather than
achieving a specific outcome. This suggests that the physical interaction component of
the prototype holds standalone play value, which may help sustain engagement even after
the primary informative elements have been explored.
Parents’ general satisfaction was reflected in the high Net Promoter Scores (NPS) they
provided, indicating that they found the intervention interesting, appropriate for the hos­
pital environment, and a promising concept overall. These impressions were further sup­
ported by the optional feedback they were invited to share at the end of the session. One
mother, whose child participated in the testing, noted that she believed the intervention
could also be beneficial for her son, who is on the autism spectrum. She explained that he
prefers to explore things at his own pace, is drawn to tactile and interactive experiences,
and feels somewhat uncomfortable with social interactions. In this context, she felt that
receiving information through the game, rather than directly from a nurse, for example,
could be more suitable and supportive for him.
Regarding the pre­ and post­intervention anxiety measurements, the results were modest
but encouraging. Both the WBF scale and the m­YPAS indicated a decrease in anxiety
levels. Although the small sample size limits the strength of statistical conclusions, the
consistency across both measures suggests a potential calming effect of the prototype.
Notably, the WBF scale revealed a statistically significant reduction in self­reported anx­
iety, whereas the observational decrease measured by m­YPAS did not reach statistical
significance. This discrepancy may be attributed to the difference in timing between the
two assessments: the WBF scale was administered immediately following the play ses­
sion, while m­YPAS observations were conducted a couple of minutes later, once the child
had returned to their pre­intervention context. Additionally, potential ceiling effects may
have contributed to the limited change in m­YPAS scores, as baseline anxiety levels on
this scale were already relatively low. It is noteworthy that on several occasions, the m­
YPAS indicated no observable anxiety, while children self­reported anxiety levels above
the lowest possible score on the WBF scale.

Main Contributions
The main contributions of this thesis include answering the problem statement and sub­
questions.

1. How can we design interactive solutions that are suitable for young children in the
context of Herlev’s PED?
To gain a comprehensive understanding of the pediatric patient journey and the proce­
dures that children undergo in the emergency setting, observational studies and semi­
structured interviews were conducted with medical staff at Herlev Hospital. Additionally,
literature and market research were carried out to identify developmentally appropriate
toys for children aged 4 to 7 years.
These efforts informed the creation of specific criteria used to evaluate and select potential
solution concepts suited to the hospital context. The selected evaluation criteria included:
Developmental Fit referring to cognitive, emotional, and physical appropriateness of the
solution for the target age group, Engagement, defined as the solution’s ability to sustain
attention and encourage curiosity and a sense of control through play, and Hospital Com­
patibility, encompassing practical factors such as portability, ease of cleaning, durability,
low noise output, and minimal setup time.
Accordingly, the interactive solution to be developed should fulfill all three criteria to en­
sure it is both effective in reducing anxiety and feasible for use in a pediatric emergency
environment.
2. What different interactive solutions can be designed to reduce pediatric anxiety at
Herlev Hospital and which one shows the highest potential?
Literature review revealed that there are numerous types of interventions capable of re­
ducing pediatric anxiety. Building on these insights, a brainstorming session was con­
ducted, followed by an evaluation of the generated concepts based on the previously
defined criteria. As a result, three low­fidelity prototypes were selected for development
and testing: AirBalloon Ride, a concept that explored the use of breathing and squeezing
as calming and empowering interactions, Match the (E)motion, which explored the use of
distraction through problem solving and physical engagement and Ambulance Adventure,
which incorporated educational play and narrative sequencing as a means of familiarizing
children with medical procedures.
To assess the effectiveness of these prototypes, user testing was carried out. Although the
results were inconclusive with regard to anxiety reduction, the process informed the need
for additional evaluation criteria to guide the selection of the most promising concept for
further development. Based on this extended evaluation, Ambulance Adventure emerged
as the strongest candidate for continued exploration.
3. How effective is the selected solution in reducing anxiety in real­life settings and how
is it experienced by children and caregivers?
Testing of the high­fidelity prototype indicated that the solution led to a significant reduc­
tion in self­reported anxiety among participating children. However, it did not produce
satisfying results regarding observational indicators of anxiety. Despite this limitation, the
prototype was positively received and successfully captured the attention and interest of
both children and caregivers in the waiting area, including those who were not directly
involved in the testing. Overall, the findings suggest that the prototype holds promise and
warrants further exploration and refinement.
Considering the problem statement:
How can we design an interactive solution to reduce pediatric anxiety in the waiting
area of the Pediatric Emergency Department at Herlev Hospital?

6.3
Limitations
Several limitations should be acknowledged in relation to both the testing process and the
prototype itself.
Although testing was conducted over multiple days, it took place during the summer, a pe­
riod when fewer children typically visit the Pediatric Emergency Department. As a result,
the available sample was limited in both size and diversity. This reduced the likelihood
of including children who were experiencing elevated levels of anxiety, which may have
affected the evaluation of the prototype’s effectiveness in reducing distress. Furthermore,
the nature of this thesis project imposed time constraints that limited the overall duration of
data collection. While behavioral observations and self­report methods were employed,
these approaches may not have captured more subtle or internalized expressions of anxi­
ety, especially in children who appeared calm or were not particularly anxious during their
visit.
The high­fidelity prototype also presented some technical limitations. While the system
was generally functional, minor instability was observed during testing. This was likely
caused by the combined power demands of the RFID readers and the MP3 module, both
of which can draw high peak currents. These demands occasionally resulted in brief
performance issues, which were resolved by rebooting the game. Fortunately, these in­
terruptions did not significantly affect the testing sessions.
6.4
Future Work
The primary aim of this prototype was to help reduce anxiety in children visiting the Pe­
diatric Emergency Department. To address the current limitations, future studies should
involve a larger and more diverse sample, with particular emphasis on including children
who exhibit higher levels of anxiety prior to hospital visits. Additionally, incorporating
physiological measures such as heart rate monitoring or eye­tracking could offer a more
objective assessment of emotional responses and engagement during gameplay.
Also, building on the insights gained during testing, several improvements and extensions
could be made to the prototype. First and foremost, the prototype’s minor power­related
issues should be addressed by optimizing power distribution, possibly through the use of
more stable power supplies or lower­energy components.
Additionally, the button interface could be refined to feel more intuitive. Making the buttons
non­removable and visually more button­like would improve durability and recognition.
Additionally, integrating a blinking effect around the pressed icon (e.g., a light ring) while
the audio is playing, could provide children with clearer feedback.
To support multisensory engagement, incorporating audio cues throughout the experience
is recommended. These could include a bell sound when the child ”arrives home,” as well
as an engine sound while the ambulance is moving. Furthermore, when the ambulance
enters a room, a short audio clip could play that announces the room name, particularly
beneficial for children who cannot yet read.
In terms of navigation mechanics, first and foremost, the ambulance should glide smoothly
along the track, providing a satisfying and seamless experience. To enhance physical in­

In terms of localization, introducing a language selection option (e.g., Danish/English)
would increase accessibility for families with different language backgrounds.
Finally, the scope of the play environment could be expanded by adding new areas, such
as the garden, to enrich the narrative and provide further opportunities for exploration.

Conclusion
This thesis explored the design and evaluation of interactive, play­based interventions
aimed at reducing anxiety in children aged 4­7 in the Pediatric Emergency Department
(PED) at Herlev Hospital.
Recognizing the limitations of passive or pharmacological
strategies, the project followed a human­centered design approach grounded in the Dou­
ble Diamond model, emphasizing empathy, iteration, and real­world testing.
A review of existing literature revealed a gap in interactive solutions suited to emergency
settings. Observations and interviews with hospital staff highlighted that pediatric anxiety
often goes unaddressed early in the patient journey due to systemic and time­related
constraints.
In response, the project introduced Ambulance Adventure, a wall­mounted interactive
board game informed by developmental psychology and embodied interaction principles.
Through tactile play and audio feedback, the intervention familiarizes children with the
hospital journey. Two testing rounds, low­ and high­fidelity, demonstrated strong engage­
ment and a statistically significant reduction in self­reported anxiety, as measured by the
Wong­Baker FACES scale. Although observational measures (m­YPAS) did not yield sig­
nificant changes, caregiver feedback confirmed the intervention’s relevance, and potential
value in a clinical setting.
This work shows that interactive, developmentally appropriate tools can enrich pediatric
emergency environments, offering children agency, comfort, and emotional support. It
contributes to the growing body of research at the intersection of interactive user inter­
faces, pediatric healthcare, and affective design, and provides a scalable foundation for
future interventions.
In conclusion, this project demonstrates that thoughtfully designed interactive experiences
can support young patients’ emotional needs in acute care settings, contributing to child­
friendly hospital environments.

A.1.2
Interviews on Anxiety Reduction
Questions to Gitte (Researcher and Clinical Nursing Specialist):
What strategies do you use now to reduce anxiety? Are there any digital tools
used?
In cases of procedural pain, they numb the skin and sometimes give sedatives, use virtual
reality solutions, or use the screen in the room. Otherwise, we just sing and try to distract
the children. Sometimes, clowns come in.
It has become very common to sedate the children when we think they are scared. This
is because, for example, if we use laughing gas with a mask, then 2 nurses are needed
per child. This makes it a resource­intensive solution.
How much of a problem is anxiety for nurses/doctors?
Anxiety is a problem because we would like to do the best for the child, give them the best
experience. But we are time­constrained because we might need the room for another
patient. So we cannot give all children all the time they need to relax before treating them.

Studies show that if you come to the hospital multiple times, your anxiety level is higher
after the first visit.
Children can be anxious or fearful about not knowing what is going to happen. In this
case, nurses assure them: “Nothing bad is going to happen to you” or “We are not going

Does anxiety cause a delay in medical procedures?
Sometimes fear causes a delay in medical procedures, and there is sometimes a need
to restrain the children. It is not easy to give sedatives to children above the age of 8­9
because it numbs their awareness. The older the child, the more difficult it is to reach this

Do you think that most nurses can recognize and address anxiety? What are some
signs?
Sometimes nurses come in after being with the child and say: “Oh, this child is very
anxious.” There are some things that can be noticed to reach that assumption: Is the child
crying? (It could also be fear, but sometimes, with experience, you can tell.) Is the child
saying ”No”? Is the child looking down? Can I calm the child in any way? Recognizing
anxiety is a skill you have to learn.
Maybe if the child rates 10 for pain and 8 for fear, and we work on the fear, then the pain
level will go down.
What aspects of the hospital experience trigger anxiety in young children?
What children fear the most is getting blood samples or other stitches. Another thing is the
”white coat syndrome”: being afraid of the clothes nurses wear at the hospital. (Maybe in
the US there have been studies about clothing color.)
When/if/how much do the kids interact with doctors?
A doctor sees the family once or twice. Sometimes the nurse communicates the problem
to the doctor without the need for them to see the child. They might only see the child
after the samples are taken. They are in the room for 10­15 minutes doing an additional
examination. Then they leave to plan further processes (e.g., X­ray, ultrasound), and the
nurses arrange it, but the doctor informs the family. Also, the doctor talks to the nurse
before seeing the child.

A.5.1
Prototype: Ambulance Adventure
1. Concept Summary:
Children move an ambulance on a simplified hospital map to simulate a visit to the
Pediatric Emergency Department. At each stop (reception, triage, waiting room,
single­patient room), they receive visual and audio feedback that explains the role
of that space, what happens, and what is there in a child­friendly way.
2. Purpose / Hypothesis:
Familiarity with the hospital journey will reduce anxiety by making the environment
more predictable and less threatening for children through a fun and playful process.
3. Interaction Overview:
Interaction Type
Description
Input
Child moves the car to different spots along a path.
Output
Audio clips play at each stop when the buttons next to
the images are pressed.
Materials
Board with drawn hospital map, small toy car with
magnet, audio clips played via smartphone, buttons.
4. Storyboard / Steps of Use:
The board is placed on a surface in front of the child.
(a) The child is introduced to the board and map.
(b) They are asked to drive the car from the entrance along the stops.
(c) At each stop, they press the buttons next to the images and hear audio feed­
back explaining the stop.
(d) The child reaches the final stop and completes the journey by reaching their
“home.”
5. Testing Questions:
• Does the child understand what they have to do?
• Do they understand what each stop represents?
• Do they hear the whole audio for the info before moving to the next stop?
• Can they recall some of the info after the game?
6. Notes for Final Prototype:
When the buttons are active, they light up. While the audio is playing, the buttons
blink.

A.5.2
Prototype: Match the (E)motion
1. Concept Summary:
A gear puzzle where children match and assemble gear pieces. There are multiple
initial gear pieces that resemble emotions, and there is one final gear. Once the
puzzle is fully assembled, a crank allows the child to move the entire mechanism,
leading to the rotation of the corresponding emotion gear.
2. Purpose / Hypothesis:
Building the gear system engages problem­solving. Seeing the final motion offers
emotional closure, reinforcing a sense of control and completion. By choosing a
specific emotion at the start of the game, the child is given the chance to express
their emotional state. Also, this game acts as a form of distraction that requires the
child’s engagement to complete the mission.
3. Interaction Overview:
Interaction Type
Description
Input
Child places gears in position to complete a pathway.
Output
Final gear triggers visual motion ­ the emotion gear
rotates.
Materials
Plastic gears, board with predefined gear positions.
4. Storyboard / Steps of Use:
The board is placed on a surface in front of the child.
(a) The child is introduced to the board and gears.
(b) They are asked to choose an emotion.
(c) They place the gears on the corresponding path to complete the puzzle.
(d) Once the path is complete, turning the last gear triggers all to move, rotating
the initial emotion gear.
5. Testing Questions:
• Does the child understand the goal of completing the gear system?
• Are they curious about what will happen at the end?
• Do they express satisfaction upon completing the puzzle?
6. Notes for Final Prototype:
Light up the emotion once the path is complete and moving.

(c) If the cloud does not reach the end of its route and the child stops blowing, the
cloud returns to its original position.
(d) When the cloud is moved away and the sky is ‘clear’, the air balloon can take
off.
(e) The child moves the air balloon by squeezing the ball.
(f) The game ends when the goal is reached (the cloud and air balloon are moved
fully).

• Is the breathing action intuitive and engaging?
• Does the child repeat the action voluntarily?
• Do they associate the action with calming or fun?
• Does the visual feedback reinforce the effort?
• Is the structure/timeline of the game clear? (e.g., understanding they need to
move the cloud first)

_ 1. Alert, looks around occasionally, notices people around.

Notes: Each time point is scored separately. Typical administration is 4 time points. For

Scoring: Divide each item rating by the highest possible rating (i.e., 6 for the “vocaliza­

multiply by 100. This produces a score ranging from 23.33 to 100, with higher values
indicating higher anxiety.

starting gears following different paths that converge on a single final gear. This struc­
