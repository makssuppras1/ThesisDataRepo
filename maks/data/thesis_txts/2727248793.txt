Abstract
Waste management is one the main concerns of our generation, but
important steps forward are being made in the developed countries.
In Denmark, residual waste is currently disposed of in Waste­to­Energy
(WtE) facilities producing energy. Nevertheless, some materials could
still be recovered from residual waste to be recycled. In this study, a
Material Recovery Facility (MRF) is introduced to retrieve recyclable
plastics and metals from the residual waste flow. The recyclables un­
dergo recycling processes producing secondary materials, while the
MRF’s reject is disposed of in the WtE plant. The goal is to evaluate
the environmental aspects of the two waste management solutions
with a comparative LCA with focus on the Climate Change impact
category.
The study is performed considering four scenarios (no MRF, ideal,
realistic ­ manual, realistic ­ automatic) and two waste compositions
(current and future). The study presents and discusses the Life Cy­
cle Inventory (LCI) results, including the recovery and quality of re­
covered fractions, energy requirements of the system and changes
waste properties, such as Lower Heating Value (LHV), and fossil car­
bon content. The LCIA results show that the current disposal solu­
tion produces a burden on the Climate Change category equal to 203
kg CO2­eq/Mg ww. On average the introduction of the MRF reduces
the overall burden on the Climate Change category by around 145­
167%. Specifically, as reported in the contribution analysis, the impact
of the WtE plant is reduced by 58­70%, while material recycling pro­
duces benefits between ­188 and ­205 kg CO2­eq/Mg ww. The same
analysis is also performed on a future waste composition to assess
the effect of MRF introduction in a future scenario. The reliability of
the results is evaluated considering an average, worst­ and best­case
layout in each scenario to account for the unknowns in the recycling
processes. Furthermore, a sensitivity assessment study is also per­
formed.
Giacomo Putti

==PAGE:6==
The results are partly based on assumptions, mainly related to re­
covery processes in the MRF and to material recycling. Therefore,
it is recommended that further analysis are conducted to reduce the
uncertainties linked to these assumptions. Nevertheless, the analysis
on the effect of material recovery on the WtE impacts is reliable since
those results are not affected by the recycling processes. Finally, it is
recommended to consider the introduction of Carbon Capture technol­
ogy to evaluate its impact on the model. The findings would contribute
to a broader understanding of modern waste management practices
and their impact on the environment.

1.1
Waste Generation and Management – a
Global Insight
Global waste generation was estimated to have reached 2.01 billion
tonnes in 2016, which corresponds to an average of 0.74 kilogram of
waste per capita per day. The average global composition presents
mostly organic waste (44%), followed by dry recyclables (plastic, pa­
per and cardboard, metal, and glass) which amount to 38%. Waste
composition also vary significantly in the world and is highly depen­
dent on the wealth of the region. In high­income countries the percent­
age of organic waste is lower, while the rate of plastic, paper, card­
board, etc. is higher. Waste generation is expected to increase as the
population increases and as the GDP growth of developing countries
rises. Based on future projections, the world is expected to gener­
ate 2.59 billion tonnes of waste annually by 2030 and is expected to
reach 3.40 billion tonnes by 2050. Globally, around 40% of waste
is disposed of in landfills, while about 19% undergoes materials re­
covery through recycling and composting and 11% is treated through
modern incineration. 33% of waste is still openly dumped, but govern­
ments are increasingly recognizing the risks and costs of dumpsites
and are shifting towards more sustainable solutions. Waste disposal
varies significantly by income: high­income countries depict high recy­
cling, incineration and composting rates, while low­income countries
rely heavily on open dumping. All the information mentioned above is
obtained from the report ”What a Waste 2.0 – A Global Snapshot of
Solid Waste Management to 2050” [1].
The ever­increasing production of waste to be managed is one of the
main struggles of our generation. Decades of indiscriminate landfilling
Giacomo Putti
1
==PAGE:20==
and open dumping have caused enormous problems to the ecosys­
tems, putting a huge toll on all living creatures, including humans. This
has been caused by the drawbacks of the linear economy, which con­
sists in extraction of raw materials, production, use and direct disposal
of goods, creating large amounts of waste. The linear economy has
been proven environmentally unsustainable, putting huge stress on
Earth’s limited resources. Today, the developed countries are shifting
towards a circular economy, which implements multiple principles to
reduce the environmental stress on the planet’s finite supplies.

Among the different principles, waste recycling plays a major role.
It produces secondary materials, which can be used to partially sub­
stitute raw materials in the production of goods, thereby reducing the
amount of virgin materials that needs to be extracted. In the Euro­
pean Union (EU), waste recycling is currently managed according to
the Waste Framework Directive [3].

1.2
Waste Management Policies in EU –
History and Goals
Different Directives were implemented during the years to regulate
waste management in the EU Member States.
Directive 75/442/EEC [4] aimed to homogenize waste management
in EU giving common definitions, goals and rules to be followed by
all the Member States. The main point of this Directive, which is later
recalled in the following ones, is to “ensure that waste is disposed
of without endangering human health and without harming the envi­
ronment” (Art. 4, [4]). The Directive introduces the main aspects of
what would later be called “Waste Hierarchy”. As stated, “Member
States take appropriate steps to encourage the prevention, recycling
and processing of waste, the extraction of raw materials and possibly
of energy therefrom and any other process for the re­use of waste”
(Art. 3, [4]). The concept of “polluter pays” is also introduced (Art. 15,
[4]). Nevertheless, no targets were set regarding recycling, preven­
tion or reduction of waste.
Directive 75/442/EEC has been amended on several occasions and
was repealed in 2006 by Directive 2006/12/EC [5]. Despite being an
improved version of the previous one, with modern approaches to the
waste issue, the 2006 Directive does not provide any new information.
Directive 2006/12/EC was repealed in 2008 by the current Directive
2008/98/EC [3], also known as “Waste Framework Directive”. The Di­
rective should help the EU to shift toward a circular model of growth
by setting recycling targets and by encouraging the Member States to
“support the use of recyclates (…) in line with the waste hierarchy and
with the aim of a recycling society” [3]. For the first time, resource use
and its relative impact are mentioned with the other main scopes of
the Directive in Article 1, which states: “This Directive lays down mea­
sures to protect the environment and human health by preventing or
reducing the adverse impacts of the generation and management of
waste and by reducing overall impacts of resource use and improving
Giacomo Putti
3
==PAGE:22==
the efficiency of such use” (Art. 1, [3]). The Directive defines point
by point the Waste Hierarchy and sets recycling targets for waste. As
reported in Article 11, all Member States have to establish “preparing
for re­use and the recycling of waste materials such as at least paper,
metal, plastic and glass from households and possibly from other ori­
gins (…) to be increased to a minimum of overall 50% by weight” by
2020 (Art. 11, [3]). The targets were later modified so that minimum
recycling corresponds to 55%, 60% and 65% by weight to be reached
by 2025, 2030 and 2035, respectively [6].
1.3
Waste Separation in EU – Typologies
and Main Characteristics
In order to comply with the above­mentioned Waste Framework Direc­
tive, EU Member States have introduced waste sorting of municipal
waste. According to Article 11 of the EU Waste Directive, all Mem­
bers “shall set up separate collections of waste (…) to meet the nec­
essary quality standards for the relevant recycling sectors” and collec­
tion “shall be set up for at least the following: paper, metal, plastic and
glass” (Art. 11, [3]). Nevertheless, separate waste collection systems
vary based on cost, people’s involvement and objective. It’s not easy
to determine which one is better than the other. Also, the fact that
a collection method produces good results in a specific region does
not directly imply that the same solution can be successfully applied
in another region. There is not a solution that works well in all situ­
ations for various reasons, such as cultural behaviour, habits, social
awareness, technical issues, etc. Separate municipal waste is mostly
collected in:
• Single­stream (commingled) flow: system in which all recyclables
are mixed together during disposal and later separated for recy­
cling.
Giacomo Putti
4
==PAGE:23==
• Double­stream flow: users are required to pre­sort recyclable items
into subcategories, such as fiber and non­fiber stream, in different
bins.
The single­stream system is the easiest solution for the general
user since the sorting responsibility is entirely transferred to the re­
cycling facility. In this way, users are more likely to take part in the
recycling effort, enhancing the overall collected recyclable rate. Col­
lection costs are also reduced. On the other hand, the sorting facilities
are required to put a lot of effort in the separation of the different frac­
tions, increasing the cost [7]. Furthermore, separation of commingled
recyclables produces low quality outputs [8] .
The double­stream system switches the responsibility of sorting waste
on the users. Sorting facilities receive multiple streams of materials
which are easier to separate, increasing the quality of the outputs.
Nevertheless, this solution burdens the public, which is required to
know the difference between materials and spend time doing the sep­
aration at home. Because of this, participation is generally lower com­
pared to the single­stream system, meaning lower collection rate and
higher loss of recyclables, which inevitably end up in the residual bin
[7].
Another more recent solution is the Deposit Return Systems (DRS).
It consists in adding a fee on some items, such as PET bottles, alu­
minum cans, glass bottles, etc., which is paid by the consumer when
purchased and refunded back when the item is returned. Items are
brought back to grocery stores and collected by a machine. This so­
lution provides the best results in terms of quality [9]. Currently, DRS
are implemented in 11 EU countries (Norway, Sweden, Finland, Den­
mark, Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania, Germany, The Netherlands, Croatia
and Malta) [10], but many others are considering its implementation.

1.4
Current Material Recycling in EU and
the Potentiality of MRF and Residual
Waste
Despite the multitude of solutions to enhance material recycling, the
ever­increasing EU recycling share are difficult to comply with. Ac­
cording to Eurostat [11], in 2020 only 9 countries of the EU­27 area
recycled at least 50% of the waste as required by the Waste Directive,
from Italy (51.4%) to Germany (70.3%). The EU­27 average is slightly
lower than 50%, while Denmark rate was 45% [11].
In order to meet the requirements, new solutions need to be imple­
mented, such as sorting out recyclables from residual waste as an
extra step to recover more materials. Residual waste is defined as the
fraction of waste that is left after recyclables and other valuable mate­
rials are taken out. Based on the situation and national laws, residual
waste might or might not contain organics. It is usually considered
to have little­to­no value and, because of that, gets rid of in landfills
or incinerated. In Denmark, residual waste is disposed of in waste­
to­energy plants, which produce electricity and thermal energy from
the incineration of the waste. This is possible thanks to Denmark’s
long history of disposing waste in incineration plant and the realiza­
tion of heating districts to supply heat to companies and houses [12].
This solution avoids direct landfilling and the use of fossil resources
for energy production. Nevertheless, since Denmark’s electricity mix
heavily relies on renewable sources [13], especially wind energy, the
benefits gained from waste incineration are limited. In this scenario,
residual waste can play an important role in reducing the gap to re­
source circularity. In fact, residual waste still contains some valuable
recyclables as an effect of missorting, such as plastics, glass and met­
als, while paper can’t be recovered because of contamination [8] [14].
Nevertheless, implementing a material recovery facility (MRF) prior
incineration would increase the recovery rate of materials to be sent
to recycling, increasing Denmark’s overall recycling rate.

1.5
MRF History and Main Typologies
Material recovery facilities, usually called MRFs or sorting facilities,
consist in a wide range of combinations of mechanical­treatment unit
processes. They are usually employed to homogenize the input ma­
terial (e.g. in industries or with mining materials), to improve stability
(e.g. in biological or thermal processes) or remove impurities and in­
crease quality (e.g. in solid waste management).
Mechanical separation of waste has a recent history. It was first in­
troduced on large scale around 1970, using operation elements taken
from different industries (e.g. mining industry, pulp and paper indus­
try, agricultural industry, etc.) with the goal to recover recyclable mate­
rial fractions from mixed waste. Nevertheless, very poor results were
obtained for a variety of reasons, partly because the used equipment
was not geared to deal with the high heterogeneity of the waste, the
varying water content, and partly for low material quality obtained,
producing less revenue than initially anticipated. Other issues also
reduced its popularity, dooming its use in the waste management sec­
tor.
However, by 1990 mechanical sorting and MRFs came back and are
today a common element in waste management. This “revival” was
possible for several reasons, among which the development of sen­
sor technology, which allows high quality fraction recovery, and the
spreading of segregated waste streams, which reduce waste hetero­
geneity.
The functions of a MRF mainly depend on its role in the waste man­
agement system, which is usually to produce valuable materials for
recycling, the types and qualities of the materials that are to be re­
covered (obtaining high quality streams reduces the overall quantities
and requires multiple steps, increasing the total cost and the environ­
mental impacts) and the amount and in which form the material to be
recovered enters the MRF.
Material recovery facilities can be categorized in:
Giacomo Putti
7
==PAGE:26==
• Pre­sorted recyclables MRF, which receive separately collected
waste fraction to be upgraded into defined qualities.
• Single (commingled) stream MRF, which receive all recyclables
in a single stream that needs to be separated in the individual
fractions for recovery.
• Dual stream MRF, which receive recyclables separated into a fiber
and non­fiber stream. It provides higher quality outputs compared
to the single stream MRF.
• Mixed waste MRF, also called “dirty MRF”, which treat mixed or
residual waste containing both recyclables and non­recyclables.
Since the quality of the recovered material is low, mixed waste
MRF are usually used to sort out a refuse­derived fuel (RDF) frac­
tion with high energy content to be employed for energy produc­
tion.
• Mechanical­biological treatment (MBT) plants, which receives mixed
or residual waste which is later sent to landfills. It employs com­
posting processes to degrade organic waste and dry the waste to
reduce its potential emissions after being landfilled. This typology
of plant became popular in Europe in the 1990s following the EU
Landfill Directive.
Given the multiple types of MRF, the different inputs and role they
might have, there is not a single fixed design to be employed every
time. The information presented above has been obtained from the
textbook ”Solid Waste Technologies and Management” by Thomas H.
Christensen [15].

1.6
Literature Review of MRF – Main
Aspects and Limitations
MRF have gained interest in the developed countries as the main so­
lution to sort material fractions to enhance recycling. This interest is
confirmed by the large amount of scientific literature being produced
in the last decades on the argument. Nevertheless, the available arti­
cles on MRF are usually case­specific, making it difficult to compare
results or draw conclusions that can be applied to other scenarios. In
fact, there are far too many variables to be considered in a MRF study,
such as inlet waste composition and quantity, waste collection meth­
ods, used technologies, legislation, as well as assumptions, system
boundaries, etc. Furthermore, authors usually focus only on some
features linked to the MRF, such as economical and technical assess­
ment or environmental impacts, while other aspects are not covered.
Some articles focus on the MRF performances, usually evaluated in
terms of material recovery and quality rates ([14] [16] [17] [18] [19] [8]
[20]). These articles are usually case­specific, referring to the specific
plant being studied, and present different “initial conditions”, such as
inlet waste composition, target materials and technologies. For ex­
ample, Gadaleta et al. (2019) [16] and Antonopoulos et al. (2021)
[17] studies focus on plastic waste flows, while Feil et al. (2015) [18]
study has lightweight packaging as inlet waste. Furthermore, despite
providing overall efficiency and quality rates of MRFs for the recov­
ered material fractions, these studies generally fail to provide detailed
step­by­step information. In fact, MRFs are made of series of differ­
ent equipment, each one having its one target fraction(s) and recovery
rate, which are usually not provided.
Exception to this can be found in Combs (2012) [19], Ip et al. (2018)
[21] and Damgacioglu et al. (2019) [8]. Combs (2012) relied on expert
opinion of MRF managers and engineers to estimate material through­
put and separation efficiency of the sorting equipment [19], while Ip et
al. (2018) and Damgacioglu et al. (2019) could gather data from sort­
Giacomo Putti
9
==PAGE:28==
ing facilities in Spain [21] and Florida [8], respectively.
Some articles focus on the economic aspects related to MRF, such
as operating cost, initial investments, labor cost, etc., usually consid­
ering the revenues from the sale of the separated fractions ([20] [22]
[23] [19] [24]). Nevertheless, global market for recycled materials, es­
pecially plastics, is not stationary. For example, following China’s an­
nouncement in July 2017 that it would ban the import of most recycled
plastics from 2018, the price has significantly decreased. Further­
more, there are different grades and qualities of recycled materials.
As reported by Damgacioglu et al. (2019), while fractions recovered
from pre­sorted recyclables or dual­stream flows are desirable and
considered high quality, material collected from single­stream flows or
mixed waste has little­to­no appeal to the recycling industry because
of its generally low quality and higher cost compared to the virgin ma­
terials [8].
Damgacioglu et al. (2019) present an extensive literature review on
the effect of contamination on material recycling, exposing the main
problems and limitations of single­stream flow collection of waste. It
is reported that the source of waste and its collection method play a
key role in the market of recycled materials, as the recycling industry
is reluctant to accept contaminated recyclables because of the higher
operating cost of the facilities and the lower revenue caused by lower
quality [8].
Some articles ([23] [25] [19] [26] [24] [27]) focus on the environmental
aspects of the MRF, presenting the benefits of material sorting and
recycling, comparison of scenarios with different disposal solutions or
initial conditions, etc. Comparisons and savings are usually presented
using LCA tools ([23] [26] [24]). These environmental savings derive
from recycling, which create products that can re­enter the market as
secondary materials, reducing the requirements for virgin ones. Nev­
ertheless, material substitution is not an easy task. As reported in
Gala et al. (2015), most of the studies regarding recycling assumed
1:1 substitution ratio of recycled to virgin materials [28], which “implic­
itly means that recycled materials are supposed to replace the same
amount of virgin materials with the same quality” ([28]). The authors
Giacomo Putti
10
==PAGE:29==
argue that when a recycled material re­enters the market it does not
substitute the primary production of virgin material, but rather repre­
sents the average combination of technologies used to produce an
average unit of the material [28].
Among all the reviewed literature, Burnley & Coleman (2018) [24]
study is the only one addressing residual waste with material recy­
cling.
The aim is to assess the environmental impact of reducing
the fossil carbon content of residual waste by removing some plas­
tics prior incineration with energy recovery. The baseline scenario in
which the residual waste is entirely sent to WtE plant is compared
with a scenario in which plastic is separated and collected for recy­
cling with an LCA study [24]. Their conclusion states that removing
plastic from the waste has a positive impact on climate change, which
derives from the reduction in CO2 emissions associated with the man­
ufacturing plastics from virgin materials and from the lower fossil car­
bon content of the WtE plant input [24]. Nevertheless, the authors
realize that “the benefits from recycling mixed waste plastics stated

2.1
Introduction to LCA Methodology
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) is a systematic and iterative approach
used to assess the environmental impacts associated with a product,
process, or service over its entire life cycle (cradle­to­grave). It in­
volves a series of distinct phases that help in evaluating the potential
environmental burdens from raw material extraction to end­of­life dis­
posal.
The first phase of LCA is the Goal and Scope Definition.
During
this stage, the purpose and boundaries of the assessment are es­
tablished. The system under study is defined, and the specific goals
and objectives of the assessment are identified. Also, the functional
unit is determined, which quantifies the performance or functionality
of the product or service being assessed.
The second phase is the Life Cycle Inventory (LCI). In this step, com­
prehensive data is collected on the inputs and outputs associated with
each stage of the product’s life cycle. This includes information on
Giacomo Putti
12
==PAGE:31==
raw materials, energy consumption, emissions, waste generation, and
other relevant elements.
The third phase is the Life Cycle Impact Assessment (LCIA). Here,
the inventory data is transformed into potential environmental impacts.
Different impact categories, based on the selected LCIA methodology,
are considered. Various impact assessment methods and models ex­
ist to evaluate the inventory data and estimate the environmental con­
sequences of the system being analyzed.
The final phase of LCA is Interpretation. During this stage, the results
obtained from the previous phases are analyzed and interpreted. The
significant contributors to environmental impacts are identified (contri­
bution analysis), and limitations and uncertainties associated with the
assessment are considered (sensitivity and uncertainty analysis).
By following these phases, LCA provides a comprehensive under­
standing of the environmental performance of a product, process, or
service. It enables decision­makers to identify areas of environmental
concern, compare alternative options, and make informed decisions
towards more sustainable practices.
2.2
Goal and Scope
The aim of this LCA study is to assess and quantify the environmental
impacts of different residual waste disposal options in Denmark. The
current treatment, consisting in direct incineration with energy produc­

cility (MRF) to retrieve recyclables prior incineration. The conclusion
of this study might offer new insights to the management of waste in
Denmark.
The model presents a MRF, a Waste­to­Energy (WtE) plant and ma­
terial recycling processes. Nevertheless, the focus is on the MRF,
which is presented in three different configurations. The WtE plant

2.3.2
Characterization
Another important piece of information is waste size.
Some MRF
equipment, namely trommels, screens and sieves, separate waste
based on its dimensions. It is a crucial aspect for facility design and
to assess performances, but is generally omitted in MRF literature.
Moreover, this data is site­ and time­specific, meaning that having
outdated statistics and/or referred to another area doesn’t provide re­
liable information.
In fact, waste can vary over time due to a variety of factors, including
changes in consumption patterns, advancements in technology, and
shifts in societal attitudes and behaviors. Waste generation type is
site­specific because it depends on a range of factors that are unique
to each location, such as characteristics of the population (e.g. age,
income, education) or economic activities present in the area. Overall,
the factors that influence waste generation type are complex. This is
why waste management strategies and solutions need to be tailored
to the specific needs and circumstances of each site.
Size data can be best obtained with manual sampling [15]. This pro­
cedure provides reliable input data but it’s costly and requires time
since large amount of waste are needed for it to be representative.
In this study, assumptions were made regarding the dimension of
waste. Four sizes were considered: small (<50 mm), medium (50­
150 mm), large (150­300 mm) and oversized (>300 mm). As reported
in Table 2.4, each fraction dimensions are depicted as rates based
on mass. For example, the fraction “vegetable food waste” presents
60%, 30%, 10% and 0% of the total inlet mass as small, medium,
large, and oversized size, respectively.

2.4.1
Sorting Equipment
A variety of equipment is employed in waste­sorting facilities, based
on inlet waste, level of automation, target materials, etc. In this sec­
tion, only the equipment required to this study and applied to the model
is described below:
• Bag­opener: it opens the plastic bags containing the waste. It is
the very first step of the MRF.
• Conveyor belt: it consists of a continuous belt that moves materi­
als from one piece of equipment to another.
• Coarse sieve: it is used to separate waste based on its size. In
this study, it is used to remove the oversized fraction of the waste.
• Trommel sieve: it consists of a rolling cylinder with openings. It
is used to separate waste based on its size. In this study, it is
applied to separate the small fraction of waste from the rest and
then the medium fraction from the large one.
• NIR: it is a typology of optical sorters which rely on near­infrared
(NIR) spectroscopy to identify materials from one another, which
are removed from the waste flow using pressurized air. In this
model, NIRs are used to remove fiber materials and identify and
collect the different plastic polymers.
• Ballistic separator: it makes use of distinctive characteristics si­
multaneously, such as size, density, and rigidness, to separate
two­dimensional waste from three­dimensional waste. In this study,
it is used to separate PE film and some unwanted 2D fractions
from the waste flow.
Giacomo Putti
26
==PAGE:45==
• Magnet: it uses magnetic forces to attract magnetic ferrous met­
als out of the waste flow. In this study, it is used to recover steel
fractions.
• Eddy current separator: it allows the separation of non­ferrous
metals by the induction of eddy current in non­magnetic metal
objects in response to an electromagnetic field. In this study, it is
used to recover the aluminum fraction.
• 2­way baler: a baler machine that can compact waste materials
from two different directions. This means that the machine has
the capability to compress and bind waste from both the top and
bottom, resulting in denser and more tightly packed bales. It is
used to bale the recovered recyclables.

2.4.3
MRF Design
A Material Recovery Facility is a system of sorting equipment that are
linked together with conveyor belts. The waste is carried through var­
ious steps of separation and ultimately into collection units. The pur­
pose of a MRF is to segregate a mixed material stream into distinct
categories, including recyclable materials that are then transformed
into secondary raw materials, as well as residue that is sent to land­
fill or incineration. Apart from mechanized sorting procedures, manual
sorting may also be employed to separate materials that are challeng­
ing for machines to handle or for post­separation quality check. Given
the different composition of the inlet waste, the possible goal materi­
als to be recovered and the level of automation of the plant, there is
not a fixed design for these facilities. Nevertheless, there are some
similarities that can be found in multiple MRF plants. For example,
the first part of the MRF usually consists of bag openers and sieves,
and the magnet always comes before the eddy current separator.
Giacomo Putti
28
==PAGE:47==
The proposed design features a bag opener, followed by a pre­sorting
manual step meant to remove bulky or problematic elements from the
waste flows. Then, a series of three sieves divides the waste flows by
size in four fractions: small, medium, large, and oversized, as previ­
ously described. The oversized waste is not further recovered and is
directly sent to incineration. The other waste flows go through ballis­
tic separators, manual sorting, NIRs, magnets and eddy current sep­
arators to recover recyclables, which are baled using a 2­way baler
to be sent to recycling. Residues and unwanted items are sent to
the Amager Bakke incineration plant, which is described in Section
2.7. A simplified graphic representation of the MRF is shown in Fig­
ure 2.2, where post­separation quality check steps and baling are not
presented. Complete and detailed schemes of the different scenarios
are found in A.1.5.

tions only. In fact, all types of paper and cardboard are not recovered
because of contamination by the organic waste and directly sent to in­
cineration [22], while glass is not economically convenient to recover
given its low price and availability.

2.5
EASETECH
The waste­LCA model EASETECH [33] version 3.4.4, developed at
DTU Environment, was utilized for this assessment. With EASETECH,
it becomes possible to model the material flow in the life cycle as­
sessment (LCA) by considering different material fractions, such as
plastics, and tracking their physico­chemical properties (e.g., energy
content, fossil carbon content) throughout the various stages of the
modeled life cycle. This approach allows for the representation of re­
source consumption and production based on the physico­chemical
properties of the inlet material. It also enables to express the emis­
sions to air, water, and soil during the end­of­life phases, taking into
account the chemical composition of the materials (e.g., emissions of
fossil CO2 during incineration). Furthermore, it allows the assessment
of the results robustness by providing built­in sensitivity and uncer­
tainty analysis.
In this study, already built­in processes as well as new ones are em­
ployed to model the different scenarios. A variety of sources have
been employed to update these processes, such as previous studies,
literature and the EcoInvent v3.8 database (https://ecoinvent.org/).

2.6
Energy System
All the processes modeled in EASETECH require or produce energy
while operating. Material sorting in the MRF requires electricity to em­
ploy the different pieces of equipment, while material recycling needs
electricity to transform waste materials into secondary resources. Fur­
thermore, the WtE plant runs on electricity to treat the residues, as
well as producing energy both in the form of heat and electricity from
the incineration of waste. Therefore, the energy system in which the
model operates affects the results of the LCA study.
In this study, as previously reported, marginal energy is applied to
all the above­mentioned equipment and processes to consider future
energy scenarios. In the context of energy systems, marginal energy
often refers to the energy technologies that are most responsive to
changes in demand or supply [29]. Based on the methodology pro­
posed by Weidema et al. (1999) [34] and as reported in Bisinella et
al. (2021), ”long­term marginal energy technologies are defined as the
technologies whose planned installed capacity is expected to change
within the time frame covered by the analysis” [29] . This feature en­
ables the environmental evaluation of the current waste disposal so­
lutions in a future energy scenario, in which renewable energy pro­
duction plays an even higher role, and vice versa [29].
In this study, the same considerations as reported in Bisinella et al.
(2021) [29] are applied. There, the selection of marginal technologies
for electricity and heat generation have been applied to produce an
energy mix describing an average situation between 2025 and 2055.
The proposed energy mix relies on projected energy scenarios for the
Nordic countries, as outlined in the Nordic Energy Technology Per­
spective (NETP) report [35], the latest version available.

3.1.1.4
Lower Heating Value (LHV)
The Lower Heating Value (LHV) is one of the main properties of
waste. It is defined as measure of the amount of heat generated from
full combustion considering the energy needed to evaporate all the
water, and is usually expressed as MJ/kg ww. The LHV is used to
evaluate the energy that can be expected to be recovered from WtE
plant. Specifically, the energy recovered in the form of electricity and
heat is usually expressed as a fraction of the LHV. In this study, the
LHV is obtained from the EASETECH model from the WtE inlet and
is dependent on the composition of residual waste. Nevertheless, in
the scenarios featuring waste separation, the composition of waste
changes following the recovery of recyclables in the MRF, which re­
sults in a change in the LHV. The values, obtained at the WtE inlet,
Giacomo Putti
44
==PAGE:63==
are presented in Figure 3.3.
Figure 3.3: LHV (MJ/Mg ww) in each scenario for both waste compo­
sitions, measured at the WtE inlet.
As shown, for the current waste composition case, compared to the
baseline scenario with no MRF, the lower heating value decreases
by 24%, 22% and 20% in the ideal, realistic­manual, and realistic­

materials that are the target of recovery in the MRF. In fact, plastic

4
Conclusions
Nowadays the disposal of residual waste in the Amager Bakke inciner­
ation plant produces a negative effect on the Climate Change impact
category, despite the efficient energy recovery. The impacts worsen if
the composition of residual waste changes as a consequence of bet­
ter segregation of recyclables.
The introduction of a MRF and subsequent material recycling pro­
cesses significantly reduce the impact of the system on the climate
because of the decreased emissions of the WtE plant and the gener­
ally positive effects of material recycling.
For the current waste composition, on average the impact of the base­
line scenario without MRF is equal to 203 kg CO2­eq/Mg ww, while it
is equal to ­137, ­108 and ­91 kg CO2­eq/Mg ww for the ideal, realistic­
manual and realistic­automatic scenarios, respectively, which is equiv­
alent to say that the impact is reduced by ­167%, ­153% and ­145%,
respectively. As shown in the contribution analysis, the impact of the
WtE plant is significantly reduced, and material recycling plays a ma­
jor role in generating savings. The sensitivity assessment displays
how much the results are effected by plastic materials recycling pro­
cesses, indicating that those processes should be studied further to
produce realistic and reliable results. Nevertheless, the impact reduc­
tion compared to the baseline scenario is still noteworthy.
The system was also modelled to assess its impact in a future waste
composition layout. In this case, on average the impact of the base­
line scenario without MRF is equal to 245 kg CO2­eq/Mg ww, while it
is equal to 86, 99 and 109 kg CO2­eq/Mg ww for the ideal, realistic­
manual and realistic­automatic scenarios, respectively, which is equiv­
alent to say that the impact is reduced by ­65%, ­60% and ­56%, re­
spectively. The contribution analysis shows that the impact of the WtE
plant is reduced, but the reduction is more contained compared to the
Giacomo Putti
65
==PAGE:84==
previous waste composition. Furthermore, since less recyclables can
be recovered, the savings obtained from material recycling are lower.
Regarding the other impact categories of the EF 3.0 wo LT model, for
both waste compositions the MRF introduction generally enhances
the savings in the given categories, with the exception of the Land
Use impact category.
The results presented above are partly based on assumptions, mainly
related to recovery processes in the MRF and to material recycling.
Therefore, it is recommended that further studies are conducted to
reduce the uncertainties linked to these assumptions. It is recom­
mended to assess the recovery of non­target materials in the MRF
sorting equipment to properly evaluate the quality of the retrieved frac­
tion, as previously defined. Quality of the recovered waste affects the
recycling outcome, since high­quality secondary materials can only
be obtained from highly pure waste flows. Furthermore, it is recom­
mended to precisely assess the material recycling processes in terms
of material loss, energy and material requirements, and market ac­
ceptance of the secondary material. In this way, it would be possible
to reduce the uncertainties linked to the impact of material recycling
on the different impact categories. Nevertheless, the analysis on the
effect of material recovery on the WtE impacts is reliable since those
results are not affected by recycling processes that follows and are
modeled using previous studies (Pressley et al. (2014) [20] for mate­
rial recovery, Bisinella et al (2021) [29] for WtE plant modelling).
Finally, the results presented in Section 3.1.3.3 depict that CO2
emissions can be further reduced. For example, carbon capture tech­
nologies could be implemented to store the collected emissions avoid­
ing their release in the atmosphere (Carbon Capture & Storage) or to
convert and use them for a different purpose (Carbon Capture & Uti­
lization). Bisinella et al. (2021) [29] assessed the effect of adding
Carbon Capture to the Amager Bakke incineration plant using MEA
scrubbing technology. It was concluded that around 90% of the CO2
Giacomo Putti
66
==PAGE:85==
emissions could be absorbed, greatly reducing their impact on Cli­
mate Change [29]. In this study, avoiding the release of fossil CO2
would reduce the impact of the WtE plant on the system, while collec­
tion of biogenic CO2 would produce significant savings in the Climate
Change category. Nevertheless, severe changes in the model would
be required, which could produce unforeseen results. Therefore, it is
recommended that further analysis are conducted to assess the pos­
sibility of adding carbon capture technologies to the proposed system.
