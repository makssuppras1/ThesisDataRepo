Abstract 
This master thesis examines the opportunities and challenges of Carbon Capture, Utilisation, 
and Storage (CCUS) in the Nordic region, focusing on Denmark, Finland, Norway, and 
Sweden. The study compares various CCUS technologies, analyses the industries driving CO2 
emissions, assesses the geological storage capacity, and explores the measures required to 
establish CCUS leadership in Europe. Norway emerges as the frontrunner in CCUS, thanks to 
its technological maturity and investment interest, derived from its experience in the oil and 
gas industry. Denmark stands out for its decisive policy-making and investments in renewable 
energy. Finland and Sweden present significant potential for Bioenergy with Carbon Capture 
and Storage (BECCS) due to their reliance on bioenergy and abundant forest resources. 
Collaboration among the Nordic countries is advantageous, given their history of cooperation, 
political stability, strong economies, and existing cross-border energy networks. To achieve 
CCUS leadership in Europe, the Nordic countries must align their intentions and actions. 
Public-private partnerships, supportive regulatory frameworks, and infrastructure development 
are crucial. Collaboration and joint ventures can mitigate risks, share costs, and drive 
innovation. Long-term investment decisions should prioritise operational efficiency and 
economies of scale. This thesis concludes that while Norway leads in CCUS advancements, 
the collective strengths of the Nordic countries present significant potential. By leveraging their 
unique advantages through collaboration, the Nordic region can accelerate CCUS development 
and contribute to achieving climate goals. The insights from this thesis are valuable for 
policymakers, industry stakeholders, and researchers seeking sustainable and low-carbon 
solutions.

1.1 Motivation  
The challenge of climate change poses a pressing issue for humanity, with greenhouse gas 
emissions, particularly carbon dioxide, significantly influencing the climate. Reducing the 
impact of these emissions has become a paramount priority globally, and carbon capture, 
utilisation, and storage (CCUS) offer a promising approach to mitigate climate change. 
 
The Nordic region, comprised of Denmark, Finland, Norway, and Sweden, has demonstrated 
a strong commitment to sustainability and has taken substantial measures to reduce its carbon 
footprint. However, there is still room for further action, and this thesis aims to contribute to 
the region's efforts by exploring and comparing different CCUS methods. The primary 
objective is to identify each country’s most effective carbon capture methods, considering their 
unique challenges, including limited natural resources, diverse climates, and varying 
economies. Understanding the most effective methods for each country is crucial in supporting 
their transition towards a low-carbon future. 
 
Furthermore, this thesis will investigate storage options, which are essential for the 
effectiveness of CCUS, and identify industries that could incorporate CO2 in their value chains 
in the future, thus promoting sustainable development in the region. 
 
The motivation behind this thesis is to provide valuable insights into the most effective CCUS 
methods, thereby contributing to the Nordic region's efforts to mitigate climate change. This 
research can serve as a valuable resource for policymakers and industries, enabling them to 
make informed carbon capture and storage decisions. By assessing various parameters, 
including the economy, technology, regulatory and societal aspects, natural and geological 
capital, and potential impact, the aim is to provide recommendations on how the Nordic 
countries can leverage their unique position as frontrunners in achieving net-zero emissions. 
The Nordic region has a proven track record of excellence in areas such as education and 
standard of living, making it well-positioned to lead in the development and implementation 
of cutting-edge technologies necessary to achieve ambitious climate objectives. According to 
European Commissioner for Energy, Kadri Simson, the EU will need to capture, utilise, or 
store between 300 and 640 million tonnes of carbon dioxide per year by 2050 to meet its climate 
goals (Kadri, 2022). However, as of 2022, the global capacity for carbon capture stands at only 
45 million metric tons, highlighting the urgent need to accelerate the development of carbon 
capture and storage technologies to successfully achieve emission reduction targets 
(International Energy Agency, 2022a).

1.2.1 Carbon capture 
Carbon capture is a method that involves the removal or capture of CO2 before it is released 
into the atmosphere, thereby reducing global warming. The three primary technological 
approaches for carbon capture are industrial-point-source CCUS, Direct Air Capture (DAC), 
and bioenergy with carbon capture and storage (BECCS), with ongoing developments in other 
methods. Among these approaches, industrial-point-source capture holds significant 
importance for short- and medium-term decarbonisation efforts due to its availability and 
potential to capture substantial amounts of CO2 emissions from challenging-to-decarbonise 
industries. 
 
1.2.2 Utilisation 
CO2 can be utilised in processes such as Enhanced Oil Recovery (EOR), fertilisers, 
construction materials, fuels, and plastics. Globally, approximately 230 Mt of CO2 is used each 
year, with the fertiliser industry accounting for 130 Mt alone (International Energy Agency, 
==PAGE:8==
 
 
 
3 
2022a). CO2 utilisation offers the potential for mitigating emissions while creating economic 
value. However, it should be noted that CO2 utilisation is not a standalone solution for climate 
change mitigation. Its potential for reducing greenhouse gas emissions is limited, and it must 
be considered alongside other carbon mitigation strategies to achieve a low-carbon future. 
 
1.2.3 Storage 
Carbon storage, or carbon sequestration, involves capturing and storing CO2. Various carbon 
storage technologies exist, including geological storage, ocean storage, mineralisation, and 
utilisation. Geological storage is currently the most viable method, which entails injecting 
captured CO2 into deep underground rock formations for long-term storage. The global CO2 
storage capacity is estimated to exceed 14,000 Gt (OGCI, 2023). To put this into perspective, 
global energy-related CO2 emissions for 2021 were 35 Gt (International Energy Agency, 
2021a). 
 
1.3 Added Value 
CCUS is a relatively new concept in sustainability and is still in the early stages of 
commercialisation. However, it is recognised as a necessary and relatively low-risk component 
of the decarbonisation puzzle to achieve the objectives of the Paris Agreement by limiting 
global warming to well below 2 degrees Celsius and exert efforts to limit it to 1.5 Celsius. 
Many reports and leading governmental organisations, including the European Commission 
(EC), International Energy Agency (IEA), Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change 
(IPCC), and others, have conducted extensive research on the best utilisation of this technology 
to move closer to global net-zero goals. Incumbents in the Nordic region have also shown 
increasing interest in investing heavily in CCS, positioning the Nordics as leaders in CCS 
within Europe and setting common goals across the region (Nordic Council, 2022). 
 
This report aims to synthesise and expand upon the existing work and research conducted by 
incumbents and research institutions while customising the framework and scope to the unique 
circumstances of the Nordics. The ultimate objective of this research project is to develop 
tailored recommendations for fully harnessing the potential of CCUS, a nascent technology 
that has yet to be fully explored and integrated at a comprehensive region-specific level.

2.2 The Strategic Value of CCUS 
There is an urgent need to address emissions from the industry and power sectors, which 
together account for 60% of global emissions (International Energy Agency, 2020b). These 
sectors play a crucial role in our economic growth, technological advancement, and overall 
quality of life. However, they are also significant contributors to environmental pollution and 
==PAGE:13==
 
 
 
8 
the climate crisis. Mitigating their environmental impact is essential for achieving a sustainable 
future. 
 
While investment in new technologies to reduce emissions is important, the timeline for 
addressing the climate crisis is limited. These industries, often classified as hard-to-abate due 
to their complex nature, require immediate attention to accelerate emissions reduction. Carbon 
capture, utilisation, and storage (CCUS) can play a vital role in supporting the decarbonisation 
of the energy sector. Some of the ways in which CCUS can contribute to emissions reduction 
in the industry and power sectors are as follows: 
 
• Reducing Emissions from Existing Power Plants: Coal and gas power plants still account 
for two-thirds of global power generation, with new investments continuing to emerge, 
particularly in Asia. Carbon capture technology offers a solution to retrofit existing power 
plants, enabling owners to recover their investments while facilitating the cost-effective 
transition to sustainable and decarbonised power systems (International Energy Agency, 
2020b). 
 
• Flexibility for Stable Power: As renewable energy sources become a larger part of the 
energy mix, their intermittency poses challenges for grid stability. CCUS-equipped fossil 
fuel power plants can provide stable and predictable power generation, acting as a reliable 
backup while maintaining carbon neutrality. 
 
• Net zero and Negative Emissions – BECCS and DAC will enable carbon negative 
emissions by drawing out the CO2 from the atmosphere and, by doing so, lowering the 
concentration in the atmosphere. It will also make it possible to offset residual emissions 
from harder-to-abate sectors that may have limited technological solutions or be more 
expensive to decarbonise. This approach can help to reduce the overall cost of 
decarbonising the energy sector. 
 
By incorporating CCUS technologies into the industry and power sectors, a significant progress 
can be made in reducing emissions and mitigating their impact on the environment. This will 
contribute to achieving the climate goals set out by the Paris Agreement and securing a 
sustainable future for generations to come.

2.3 Value Chain of CCUS 
The value chain of CCUS consists of four major steps:  
2.3.1 CO2 Sources  
CO2 sources for carbon capture, utilisation, and storage (CCUS) encompass concentrated CO2, 
biospheric sources and sinks, and diffuse CO2 fields, each with varying gas compositions 
(Patricio et al., 2017). High-purity CO2 is generally preferred for its effectiveness and cost-
efficiency, as it eliminates the need for additional purification processes. Blue hydrogen, 
produced through the combination of natural gas with CCUS, and ethanol production are 
considered crucial technology drivers that can serve as low-emission alternatives to 
conventional energy sources such as coal, oil, and natural gas (Howarth and Jacobson, 2021). 
While the actual impact of these technologies on total emissions reduction is still uncertain, 
commercialisation efforts are already underway, with current production of blue hydrogen 
facilities (Howarth and Jacobson, 2021). Blue hydrogen production is considered one of the 
more mature use cases for mitigating emissions. However, it is important to note that capturing 
high-purity point sources contributes to less than 5% of the global emission volume (Baylin 
and Berghout, 2021). On the other hand, low-purity point sources, including cement 
production, petrochemical production, and power generation, are major emitters that require 
more extensive processing, leading to a higher cost per tonne of CO2 captured (Baylin and 
Berghout, 2021). Figure 2 illustrates the cost differential between high-purity and low-purity 
point sources, which can vary by up to a factor of 10, highlighting the significant impact of 
purification complexity on overall cost.

2.3.2 CO2 Capture Technologies 
There are two main capture technologies: Point source and direct air capture. While point 
source is the most effective as it’s easier to capture the CO2 at the source and is much less 
energy-intensive, DAC provides some considerable advantages; DAC enables us to reduce the 
concentration of CO2 and potentially reverse the current levels of 400 parts per million (PPM) 
in the Earth's atmosphere, which is the highest concentration recorded in approximately 3-5 
million years (Stocker, 2014). For point source capture, can the most typical methods be 
divided into four categories (Niass and Kislear, 2017): 
• Solvent – a high-performance chemical that selectively dissolves CO2 from effluent 
stream and releases to regenerate themselves 
• Sorbents – Typically solid materials that “absorb” CO2 from the effluent stream via 
temperature or pressure swing 
==PAGE:16==
 
 
 
11 
• Membranes – “Filter-like” technology that separates gasses from industrial streams 
• Novel concepts  – Refers to a broad range of approaches targeting a step change process 
enhancement 
 
Solvent and membranes currently have the highest maturity levels, with both having a 
Technology Readiness Level (TRL) of 9. 
 
Typically, CO2 can be captured in three different states of the combustion process: 
• Post-combustion – techniques are employed to isolate CO2 from exhaust gases 
generated by combustion, enabling the capture of CO2 using a liquid solvent 
• Pre-combustion – processes convert fuel into a gaseous mixture of hydrogen and CO2, 
allowing for clean combustion and the CO2 to be compressed for transport 
• Oxy-combustion – The oxyfuel with post-combustion method utilises pure oxygen 
instead of air during fuel combustion, resulting in an exhaust containing a high 
concentration of readily separable CO2

2.3.3 Compression and Transportation 
Both compression and transportation are a significant share of the total cost. Evaluations where 
the advantages of compression such as enabling larger quantities for transport must be weighed 
against the disadvantage of the increased energy demand for such a process. Transport options 
include the following: 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
 
12 
• Pipeline – CO2 pipelines have been in operation for decades, capable of transporting 
carbon dioxide over long distances in both gaseous and liquid states. However, pipeline 
costs tend to escalate in regions with high population densities, mountainous terrains, 
nature reserves, or areas with obstacles such as rivers and freeways. The technical 
aspects of CO2 pipelines include determining the route, metallurgy, and establishing 
minimum quality standards for the transportation of CO2 
• Shipping – CO2 transportation by ship is presently limited due to the low demand. 
However, shipping CO2 can be a more cost-effective option than pipelines, particularly 
when the distance is significant or when the transportation is needed overseas. The 
transportation of liquid CO2 necessitates the use of pressurised and refrigerated cargo 
tanks, as it must be kept at low temperatures and pressures 
• Road transport – In comparison to pipelines and ships, CO2 transportation by trucks 
is not cost-effective except for rare cases where the scale is very small

2.3.4 Storage and Utilisation 
Storing CO2 refers to injection of the captured CO2 into an underground geological reservoir 
of porous rocks overlaid by an impermeable layer that prevents the upward migration that 
would lead to CO2 entering the atmosphere. The are several options that are well-suited for 
this both on- and offshore. The two options with the highest capacity are deep saline formations 
and depleted oil and gas reservoirs. Deep saline formations are made up of porous and 
permeable rocks that are saturated with brine and are commonly found in sedimentary basins 
onshore and offshore. Depleted oil and gas reservoirs are porous rock formations that have 
trapped oil or gas for millions of years and can be repurposed to trap CO2 (IEA, 2021). It’s 
typically stored at 800-1,000 m below the surface. The CO2 becomes naturally compressed at 
this depth, reducing its volume to less than 1%. This compression gives the CO2 a denser 
composition, similar to a liquid rather than a gas. As mentioned in 1.2.3, is the storage potential 
manifold of what is needed to reach net zero. Therefore, in the larger context, storage is not 
expected to be a constraint in achieving net zero emissions. Nevertheless, storage still entails 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
 
13 
potential risks and liabilities. These may be attributed to local conditions, injection rates, 
leakages, or contamination of subsurface resources, among others (Gholami, Raza and Iglauer, 
2021). 
 
CO2 has the potential to be used as a crucial component in a diverse array of products and 
services. Its applications encompass direct use, where the CO2 is not chemically altered (non-
conversion), and transformation involves converting CO2 into a functional product through 
chemical and biological processes. The use and storage options can be grouped into five main 
categories: 
 
• Enhanced Oil Recovery (EOR) - CO2 EOR is the process of injecting CO2 into a 
reservoir to increase the oil that can be recovered by increasing the pressure and 
reducing the viscosity of the oil 
• Construction materials - By permanently converting CO2 to stable calcium 
carbonates, carbon curing of concrete offers an alternative to the steam curing process 
and results in a significantly faster curing time compared to conventional methods 
• Fuel - Synfuels that can be utilised as a substitute for traditional fossil fuels, such as 
gasoline, jet fuel, and diesel, can be produced by chemical processes that convert CO2 
and hydrogen. These synfuels are compatible with combustion engines 
• Plastics, chemicals, and new materials - Through the use of heterogeneous catalysts, 
methanol synthesised from CO2 can be transformed into polyethylene and 
polypropylene using conventional techniques that circumvent the need for ethane and

2.4 An in-depth look at use cases for CO2 
As briefly touched upon previously in the report, CO2 can be used as an input in a wide array 
of products and services. It is currently used mainly in the fertiliser and EOR industries. 
However, new pathways to use CO2 in the production of other products are generating global 
interest, and early markets are starting to emerge. IEA is estimating that the near-term potential 
for the use of CO2 will increase by 10 Mt per year (International Energy Agency, 2019b). 
However, this amount represents only a small portion of what most pathways expect CCUS to 
be able to provide in the emission mitigation (IPCC, 2022b). This underlines that utilisation is 
only a complement, not an alternative to CO2 storage for large-scale emissions reductions.  
 
There are five key factors that are driving the increase of interest  in commercial applications: 
 
• Generating a fuel from hydrogen that has similar ease of use and handling as gaseous 
or liquid fossil fuels but with significantly reduced CO2 emissions 
• Incorporating the carbon from CO2 into chemical products that already contain carbon 
results in lower overall CO2 emissions compared to their fossil counterparts 
==PAGE:19==
 
 
 
14 
• Creating cement and concrete that outperform traditional building materials while 
producing fewer CO2 emissions 
• Utilising waste products as feedstock to produce high-value building materials, thereby 
reducing waste disposal expenses 
• Increasing the efficiency of biological processes to improve crop yields 
 
The technological development of the use of CO2 in these applications is primarily in the early 
phases, and their potential for future market growth hinges on scalability, competitiveness, 
climate benefits, and, lastly, policy and regulatory frameworks. 
Looking further at the climate benefits, the considerations that must be made to get an overall 
assessment of the impact of CO2 use are: 
 
• 
The source of CO2 (fossil fuels, biomass, or air etc.)  
• 
The type of product or service that the CO2-based product or service is replacing 
• 
The amount and form of energy required to convert the CO2 
• 
The duration the carbon is retained in the product  
• 
The size of the opportunity for CO2 utilisation 
 
 
2.4.1 Concrete 
Concrete is a mixture of cement, water, and aggregates. It is estimated that between 100 Mt-
1,400 Mt CO2 / year can be utilised in this sector alone (Hepburn et al., 2019). Many alternative 
cement processes are substitutable with traditional cement and have equivalent or superior 
properties. CO2 has the potential to serve as a constituent of the filler (aggregate), as a raw 
material in the manufacturing of the binding material (cement), and as an input for the concrete 
curing process.  
The primary benefit of using CO2 in cement is that the performance and properties of the 
concrete will not be compromised by utilising CO2. If the production process is properly 
attuned, the CO2-derived concrete would actually perform better when looking at its building 
properties (International Energy Agency, 2019b). IEA also estimates that the climate benefits 
of CO2-derived concrete could be up to 80% compared to conventional; however, further 
verification is still needed.  
 
2.4.2 Fuel 
Fuel consists of a wide range of products that can incorporate CO2 as a feedstock in their 
manufacturing process. Two prominent examples of CO2-derived fuels are synthetic fuel and 
microalgae-based fuels. Synthetic fuels are produced through a reverse water gas shift reaction, 
where CO2 is converted to CO and subsequently combined with hydrogen (H2) using the 
Fischer-Tropsch process to produce the fuel (Dry, 2002). On the other hand, microalgae are 
cultivated in CO2-rich environments and harvested to obtain algae oil, which can be processed 
into fuels, along with protein by-products that can be used as feed (Dry, 2002). 
 
==PAGE:20==
 
 
 
15 
The fuel market holds significant potential for CO2-derived products, as they offer a dual 
advantage. Firstly, they can help reduce emissions associated with conventional fuel sources, 
contributing to overall emission reductions. Secondly, these products can serve as a solution to 
address the intermittent nature of renewable energy sources by providing energy storage 
options, such as methanol. The ability to store and utilise energy derived from renewable 
sources can help address the challenges of intermittency and ensure a more reliable and stable 
energy supply (Gray et al., 2021). 
 
However, CO2-derived fuels also face certain challenges. Currently, their production is not 
economically competitive compared to conventional production methods. The cost of 
production remains a significant barrier to widespread adoption and commercial viability. 
Additionally, CO2-derived fuels generally have a lower energy density compared to fossil 
fuels, which limits their overall performance and applicability. This aspect is particularly 
critical for certain high-energy-demand sectors such as aviation, where high energy density is 
essential for efficient operations (Gray et al., 2021). 
 
While CO2-derived fuels present opportunities for emission reductions and renewable energy 
integration, addressing the economic and performance challenges is crucial for their 
widespread adoption. Further research and development efforts are needed to improve 
production processes, enhance energy density, and optimise the cost-effectiveness of CO2-
derived fuels. Overcoming these challenges will be instrumental in realising the full potential 
of CO2-derived fuels and their contribution to a more sustainable and decarbonised fuel 
industry.

2.5.1 Investment Interest  
Governments are implementing policies to support the development of CCUS to meet their 
emissions reduction commitments. For instance, the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) (117 th 
United States Congress, 2022) in the United States offers an improved tax credit for the 
permanent sequestration of CO2. The IRA expands the federal income tax credits for domestic 
CCUS projects, commonly known as "45Q credits," from $50 to $85 per ton for sequestering 
CO2 generated by industrial activities. This initiative could accelerate the adoption and scaling 
of CCUS facilities in the US alone. In the EU, The EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) is 
established and covers approximately 40 % of the EU’s emissions. Starting in 2026, a carbon 
border levy, called Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM), will come into force, 
effectively imposing charges on non-EU manufacturers and importers for the carbon emissions 
resulting from the sale of their products or materials within the EU (European Commission, 
2021). Policies such as these will all benefit the adaptation of CCUS as the carbon pricing 
becomes higher and higher, and so will the attractiveness of CCUS as a potential emissions 
reduction technology business case that will provide a stable revenue stream. However, to 
enable CCUS to scale at the pace expected for us to reach net zero, nonsubsidy revenues are 
critical.

The different approaches and technologies for CDR are still far from commercialisation, and 
there still have to be significant technological advances before this is realised; however, the 
CO2 removal potential is enormous and could become a vital role in reaching net zero. 
Nonetheless, it is important to consider the potential side effects associated with the use of 
these technologies: (International Energy Agency, 2019a):  
• BECCS and certain land management approaches can lead to land use changes 
• DAC capture costs can be quite high but has a small land footprint 
• Ocean fertilisation/alkalinisation may cause ocean eutrophication 
 
DAC has been gaining momentum both from investors and from research institutions. 
Climeworks and Carbon Engineering, two cleantech scale-ups, have received €730 million and 
€100m, respectively, in funding. Both are working on making DAC economically feasible 
through large-scale DAC plants.  Currently, there are two approaches used for DAC: solid and 
liquid DAC. Solid DAC, also known as S-DAC, utilises solid adsorbents operating at ambient 
to low pressure (i.e., under a vacuum) and medium temperature (80-120°C). On the other hand, 
liquid DAC, also known as L-DAC, uses an aqueous basic solution (such as potassium 
hydroxide) that releases the captured CO2 through a series of units operating at high 
temperatures (between 300°C and 900°C) (Custelcean et al., 2019).

Overall, these approaches and technologies are expected to play a decisive role in reaching net 
zero. There are some uncertainties regarding the feasibility and potential environmental impact 
that it may have: 
• Capture cost: In order for DAC and BECCS to become commercially viable, 
significant cost reductions must be achieved through technology advancements and 
economies of scale 
 
• Performance: DAC is still in its early stages of development, and its performance has 
not been fully evaluated. BECCS is more mature and has been shown to be effective at 
capturing and storing CO2. However, the performance of BECCS is highly dependent 
on the availability of sustainable biomass and the type of land used for biomass 
production 
 
• Speed of commercialisation: the slow pace of commercialisation is a significant 
barrier to the wide-scale adoption of these technologies. While BECCS is more mature 
and has already been deployed in some locations, DAC is still in its early stages of 
development. Both technologies require significant investment and development to be 
deployed at a commercial scale 
 
• Public understanding and acceptance: people may view the technologies as risky or 
unproven, while others may be concerned about the potential side effects, such as land 
use changes or higher energy costs. Another concern is that DAC might prove 
increasingly controversial if it erodes global efforts at mitigation. If carbon can 
effectively and affordably be removed from the air, it may slow the rush to eliminate 
fossil fuels (Gertner, 2021) 
 
• BECCS: Availability of sustainable biomass: access to sustainable biomass can 
prove a huge challenge for BECCS.  Social and environmental conflicts can arise from 
the use of biomass. 1st generation biomass poses the challenge of ‘food vs. fuel’; while 
2nd generation biomass is made from non-food crops, it still requires land use that can 
negatively impact biodiversity, soil quality, water use, and more (Gao et al., 2019)

There are different overarching regulations for the different regions. The matureness of the

is Directive 2009/31/EU that outlines a set of fundamental principles and regulations for the 
geological sequestration of CO2, which member nations must adhere to and follow (Zhang,

substantive guidelines that govern the entire life cycle of CCS projects, from a regulatory 
perspective. However, if looking at China that many predicts to become a massive market for 
CCUS and develop into a global hub (Zheng, 2022), there are no explicit legislations or 
regulations specifically for CCUS, even though various government policies emphasise the 
significance of using CCUS to reduce emissions. There are international laws, inter alia the 
London Convention on the Prevention of Marine Pollution by Dumping of Wastes and Other 
Matter and London Protocol provides transboundary regulatory framework CO2 sequestration 
in the seabed. Under the London Protocol is all dumping prohibited except for possibly 
acceptable wastes on the so-called “reverse list”, which includes CO2 sources from capture 
processes for sequestration. This legislation allows countries with collective interests in CCUS 
to work together on using the same storage sites. An example of this could be that European 
nations may form an alliance to utilise the highly potential storage locations situated in the 
North Sea instead of relying on comparatively inferior storage options available within their 
respective borders (Shogenova et al., 2014). 
 
A more holistic approach to law and regulation that fully integrates and addresses all of the 
core elements of CCUS technology is necessary for commercialisation and large-scale 
deployment of CCUS. Even though there has been some momentum on creating regulatory 
frameworks and that a significant portion of the value-chain can be covered by already existing 
frameworks from similar industries such as oil and gas.

Storage capacity is, in general, considered abundant. The range of estimated theoretical 
capacity varies between 3,000 Gt (Luderer et al., 2015) to 11,000 Gt CO2 (Chen and Tavoni, 
2013). As current global emissions are around 40 Gt, would even the lower estimate be more 
than enough for many centuries to come. These estimations are based on high-level estimations,

and it must be assumed that not all global storage potential is well-suited when considering 
accessibility, technology and economic perspectives. To visualise this, can a Techno-

Figure 13 - The techno-economic resource pyramid for capacity of CO2 storage depicts three levels of estimates: theoretical, 
realistic, and viable (Bachu et al., 2007) 
 
1. Viable capacity is the maximum possible storage capacity but is unrealistic due to 
technical and economic limitations. Representative capacity estimates consider 
geological and engineering factors but exclude the basal parts of the resource pyramid. 
Viable capacity estimates also consider economic, legal, and regulatory barriers, and 
include source-sink matching and social and environmental aspects 
 
2. Realistic capacity is where a  more practical assessment applies technical cut-off limits 
to factors such as reservoir quality, depth, pressure, and potential impacts on existing 
resources or interests. This estimate is more precise and indicates the technical 
feasibility of CO2 storage 
 
3. Theoretical capacity assumes the entire geological formation can store CO2, whether 
in its pore space, formation water or coal mass, at maximum saturation levels. While 
this provides an upper limit estimate, it is not practical due to technical and economic 
constraints that limit access to and utilisation of the entire formation. The theoretical 
limit represents the entire resource pyramid but is unrealistic as not all parts of the 
formation can be fully utilised

2.5.5 Potential Impact 
The near- to mid-term technical potential for CCUS remains noteworthy. CCUS does not 
remove the necessity to persist in reducing CO2 emissions through alternative means, such as 
augmenting the use of renewable energy sources. Nonetheless, it presents substantial potential 
for curbing emissions in sectors that are challenging to decarbonise: such as cement and steel 
production. This potential abatement that CCUS could bring has already been discussed in 2.1, 
therefore the environmental impact will not be further discussed in this section.  
 
CCUS is expected to bring new green jobs to the market. Many of these are associated with 
construction of the facilities, but there will still be a significant part of the job share linked to 
operation. According to the IEA Sustainable Development Scenario, by 2050, the 
implementation of CCS technology could result in the employment of approximately 80,000 
to 100,000 individuals in the construction of projects and 30,000 to 40,000 employees in the 
==PAGE:32==
 
 
 
27 
operation of facilities. These job opportunities would fit well for individuals already working 
in industries such as oil and gas, as their expertise in drilling and underground storage could be 
transferable.

2.6.1 Investment Interest 
The Nordics plan to incorporate CCS as a component of their long-term net zero strategies. 
Furthermore, an examination of the SDG-Index score reveals that the Nordic countries occupy 
the topmost positions on the list (Sachs et al., 2022). Nordic countries are widely regarded as 
in a favourable position to benefit from the increasing focus on sustainability from the 
government and the general public, with some already having established themselves as 
pioneers in this field. In the period spanning from the present time to 2050, Nordic companies 
have the potential to tap into vast global value pools by adopting an offensive approach to the 
green transition. This entails concentrating on developing expertise and solutions that can aid 
the global community in decarbonising rather than merely addressing their own 
decarbonisation obstacles defensively. In some ways, the Nordics can be compared to 
sustainability today what Silicon Valley was to technology in the 1990s. (Aagaard et al., 2022). 
In addition, the Nordics have a leading clean-energy system and environmentally conscious 
consumers. Another driver of Nordic investment interest in CCUS is the need to maintain the 
competitiveness of key industries. The Nordics is home to several energy-intensive industries, 
including steel production, cement manufacturing, and pulp and paper production. These

emissions. CCUS technologies can help these industries reduce their carbon footprint while 
maintaining their competitiveness in the global market. These mentioned industries consist of 
large installations and are considered hard to abate, making them significant sources of 
emissions with the technical potential for deploying CCS. From an economic perspective, these 
sectors are also the largest industrial sectors in the Nordics, which further highlights their

2.6.3 Regulatory and Society 
Denmark, Finland, and Sweden are members of the European Union, whereas Norway is not 
part of the EU. On an EU level, there are presently 13 regulations in force, with a predominant 
emphasis on storage-related aspects. Norway targets climate neutrality by 2030, Finland by 
2035, Iceland by 2040, Sweden by 2045, and Denmark aims for 70% emissions reduction by 
2030 on the path to climate neutrality by 2050, with Denmark and Sweden adopting legally 
binding net zero targets as some of the only countries globally (University of Oxford, 2023). 
This demonstrates their political will to enact comprehensive legislations and regulations, 
highlighting their commitment to fostering a sustainable future. However, when looking at 
policy measures at the national level across the Nordic countries reveals a notable disparity. 
 
Denmark has implemented national policy measures to support the development of CCUS 
technology. These measures include the establishment of a market-based, technology-neutral 
pool with an annual budget of EUR 109.5 million starting in 2024 (State of Green, 2022). The 
country's national CCUS strategy includes the preparation of permits for CO2 storage, funding 
allocation of EUR 2.2 billion for CCUS projects, and support for the utilisation of captured 
CO2. A broad  political agreement has also been made stating that he Danish state as a starting 
point participates with an ownership of 20% of licenses for all Danish CO2 storage projects 
(Energistyrelsen, 2022). 
 
Finland has not implemented any direct national policy measures. However they mention in 
their official net zero strategy that: “The development and use of carbon capture and 
utilisation(CCS/CCU) technologies and solutions will be accelerated” as well as “The 
legislative framework for CCS/CCU regulation at the EU level will be promoted” (Ministry of 
Economic Affairs and Employment of Finland, 2022). This underlines that Finland does not 
have any specific legislative frameworks for CCUS yet; however as they are part of EU, they 
will adopt those. However, Finland has, through their Sustainable Growth Programme for 
Finland allocates EUR 150 million to hydrogen projects and carbon capture and utilisation 
projects. 
 
Norway already presented its strategy for CCS in 2014 (Norwegian Ministry of Climate and 
Environment, 2014) that consisted of a broad range of measures and policy instruments in 
research and development, demonstration, work on realising a full-scale demonstration facility 
and international work. Norway, as a non-EU country has also adopted The EU CO2 storage 
directive (Directive 2009/31/EC) that regulates both the exploration of storage sites and the 
storage of CO2 in Europe.

Sweden has not implemented any national policy measures, nor have a formal CCS-strategy.

In general, Norway and Denmark are the countries in the Nordics with the most exhaustive and 
mature policy framework.  
 
In terms of society and public perception, Norway has been globally used as a case in several 
studies investigating the public perception of CCS (Merk et al., 2022). They are, in general, 
have a positive attitude toward CCS. However, when evaluating a project in which foreign-
sourced CO2 is stored in Norway, there is a significantly more negative perception than a 
project where domestically sourced CO2 is stored in Norway, particularly compared to its 
peers. Studies have also been conducted on Sweden and Denmark, revealing a weaker 
acceptance due to previous failed projects, which has resulted in the absence of current large-
scale projects. This situation has made the technology and its impact more ambiguous. (Haug 
and Stigson, 2016). Factors that also impact acceptance in the Nordics are the project's 
proximity to the population and whether the project is located onshore or offshore. No 
extensive study on public acceptance in Finland has been conducted. However, one study 
indicated a relatively neutral attitude towards CCS (Pihkola et al., 2017). 
 
2.6.4 Natural and geological capital 
Denmark and Norway are the only countries in the Nordics that have oil and gas (O&G) 
production, with Norway producing significantly more (BP, 2022). Both countries have 
recently expressed their plans for how they will phase out the O&G sectors. Norway will not 
issue licences for energy companies to explore oil and gas in frontier areas during the life of 
the current parliament, which ends in 2025 (Buli and Adomaitis, 2022). Denmark has already 
stopped granting licenses since 2020 and plans to cease all new oil and gas extraction by 2050. 
(Murray, 2020). The restriction on oil and gas exploration in Norway and Denmark presents 
an opportunity for utilising abundant wells for CO2 storage. Research suggests that repurposing 
depleted oil wells can offer advantages such as expedited implementation and reduced risks 
compared to utilising saline aquifer sites for storage. Consequently, Denmark and Norway are

strategically positioned to leverage their existing oil and gas sector for potential CO2 storage 
initiatives (Hannis et al., 2017).

properties. It estimates that the total European storage capacity is 358 Gt, with 114 Gt onshore

3.1 Research Question 1 
”What are the relative merits and demerits of various CCUS technologies, and how do they 
compare across the parameters of the economy, technological matureness, regulation, 
natural and geological capital, and impact?” 
 
 
The technologies that will be compared are Industrial-point-source, DAC, and BECCS. Their 
merits and demerits will be evaluated across the Nordics, building upon the previous 
discussion in section 2.6. 
 
3.1.1 BECCS 
The potential for BECCS in the Nordics is significant. Especially Finland and Sweden are 
positioned well to capitalise on this technology as both countries have many large industrial-
point sources of biogenic CO2 emissions, mainly CHP plants burning wood waste from the 
forest industry and pulp and paper plants (Karlsson et al., 2017). BECCS does however require 
a significant upfront investment, but currently, there is limited momentum from incumbents, 
start-ups, and the government in the Nordics. BECCS can be viewed by the general public as 
interventions that interfere with nature, as indicated by certain studies. Compared to 
afforestation and reforestation, support for BECCS tends to be lower. This reduced support 
may stem from public scepticism regarding the manipulation of natural ecosystems and 
concerns about the long-term effectiveness and unintended impacts of BECCS (Wolske et al., 
2019).  
 
3.1.2 DAC 
DAC presents a promising approach to extracting CO2 from the atmosphere in the Nordics, 
albeit with considerable financial and energy expenditures, marking it as one of the costliest 
technologies per unit of CO2 removed. Nevertheless, could the Nordics be frontrunners in the 
deployment of DAC on an commercial scale. Norway is one of the worlds’ leading energy 
exporters (Eurostat). As DAC technology and carbon markets mature, Norway may leverage 
its energy surplus to fuel DAC operations, consequently selling the resulting carbon offsets. 
This strategy could create a paradigm shift, transforming Norway from a traditional energy 
exporter to a leader in cleantech technologies and solutions. Norway also have the only start-
up in the Nordics working with DAC, called Removr. Striving for a significant impact,  Removr 
aims to have a commercial scale facility with a capture capacity of 1 Mtpa CO2 in 2029.

Industries such as steel, cement, and power generation that the Nordics has and still rely heavily

3.2.1 Denmark 
Denmark is present in several industries that currently use and in the future are expected to use 
CO2 to achieve a greener end-product. The following industries Denmark is most known for, 
will be briefly explored below. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Fertiliser 
Even though Denmark is a relatively small country, it is one of the world's 
largest exporters of agricultural products. The agriculture industry is 
currently the biggest off-taker of CO2 through the need for urea in fertiliser. 
In addition to this, the production of biochar, a high-carbon, fine-grained 
residue that is produced via pyrolysis from captured CO2, is seeing 
momentum. This could again serve Danish agriculture as a way to 
decarbonise itself through sustainable fertiliser and feedstock (Schmidt et 
al., 2019). 
Cement 
Currently, the biggest point-source polluter in Denmark is a cement-
producing company called Aalborg Portland. They produce 2.2 Mt of CO2 
annually, responsible for around 8% of Denmark’s total emissions (Lund-
Hansen, 2022). Carbon curing of concrete as an alternative to the steam 
curing process that permanently converts CO2 to stable calcium carbonates, 
resulting in a significantly faster curing time than conventional methods, 
thereby enhancing the properties. This is a new use case for CO2 being 
explored. This technology is currently in the early adaptation phase but 
could prove essential to the decarbonisation of this industry (Liang et al., 
2020). 
e-fuels 
Denmark is gearing up for the execution of numerous ambitious projects 
that focus on the production of e-fuels. A notable initiative is the ammonia 
production project in Esbjerg by Copenhagen Infrastructure Partners. The 
objective is to utilise wind energy for ammonia production. Production is 
set to begin in 2026, and the expected output will be sufficient to not only 
substitute Denmark's current agricultural fertiliser imports but also offer a 
sustainable alternative to conventional fuels used in marine vessels, trucks, 
and other heavy transport. The Danish maritime sector is very large and 
can play a key role in setting the standards for the fossil-free shipping 
(Nordic Energy Research, 2022).

3.2.2 Finland 
There are a diverse set of on-going commercialisation and piloting activities related to 
utilisation by Finnish technology companies in the fields of methanation, protein production, 
and the pulp and paper industry. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Pulp and paper 
Finland, rich in forest resources, is one of the world's leading pulp and 
paper producers. The industry represents nearly 50% of the annual 
industrial energy use and around 23–24% of the total energy demand. 
(Furszyfer Del Rio et al., 2022) provides an incentive to decarbonise 
as fast as possible. One way would be for the industry to utilise CO2 
in a process known as mineral carbonation, where CO2 reacts with 
calcium or magnesium to form carbonates. Carbonates are used widely 
in paper production, meaning that captured CO2 could be put to 
productive use in the existing industrial infrastructure. 
Protein 
CO2 utilisation in protein production is an emerging and 
transformative concept in sustainable industry practices turning CO2 
into a valuable input for protein generation. The methods most often 
used are microbial protein production or algae cultivation. A notable 
example in Finland is Solar Foods, a company that has developed a 
process to produce a protein-rich food source called Solein from water, 
CO2, and electricity. The company uses a bioprocess, where a 
microorganism is grown in a liquid culture medium, fed with carbon 
dioxide and hydrogen. This process can be carried out anywhere 
renewable electricity is available. The product is a protein powder that 
can be used in various food applications.

3.2.3 Norway 
Norway is a base for prominent global oil and gas industry entities such as  Shell, Equinor, and 
Aker. Concurrently, the country is also recognised for housing Yara International, a corporation 
that stands at the forefront of global fertiliser production. Given this industrial profile, it is 
reasonable to infer that Norway presently constitutes a significant sink for CO2 within these 
sectors. However, demand from other industries will also arise as the technology matures.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Aquaculture 
Norway has one of the world's largest aquaculture industries. CO2 could be 
used in controlled algal cultivation systems to feed farmed fish. The 
microalgae consume CO2 and are then used as a sustainable feedstock. CO2 
captured from industrial processes is introduced into photobioreactors 
where microalgae are cultivated. The microalgae utilise the CO2 for growth 
and are then harvested to create nutrient-rich feed for farmed fish  (Jones et 
al., 2022).  
Fertiliser 
While Norway is not traditionally recognised for its agricultural sector due 
to its geographical and climatic constraints, the projected surge in global 
demand driven by population growth (Ros et al., 2020), may present an 
intriguing opportunity for the country. By scaling up its existing agricultural 
production, Norway has the potential to establish a symbiotic relationship 
between its CO2 emissions and the fertiliser industry. This expansion could 
serve to establish a circular economy, wherein the CO2 emissions generated 
within the country are captured and subsequently utilised within the 
domestic fertiliser industry, thereby establishing a closed-loop system.

3.2.4 Sweden 
While the possibilities for CO2 utilisation in Sweden are largely consistent with those of its 
neighbouring countries, the unique composition of Sweden's industries provides distinct 
opportunities for CO2 usage. These specific intersections between various industries pave the 
way for custom strategies to integrate CO2 utilisation within Sweden's industrial framework.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
e-fuels  
Sweden is globally renowned for its advanced transport manufacturing 
industries, such as Volvo, Scania, Saab, and more. The prominence of these 
industries naturally generates increased attention and potential opportunities 
for related sectors, such as the fuel industry, to leverage this established 
platform for their own growth and development. E-fuels is a potentially 
interesting option for all transport modes, especially shipping, aviation, and 
long-distance road transport, where the potential for other renewable fuel 
options, such as electricity and hydrogen, may be limited. Research suggests 
that using recoverable CO2 to produce e-methane could yield 224 TWh 
yearly, over twice Sweden's current transportation fuel demand (Hansson et 
al., 2017). 
Plastics 
The plastic production industry in Sweden encompasses the manufacture of 
both raw plastic materials and the subsequent processing into finished 
products. PET bottles, construction industry, vehicles, electronics, and 
healthcare are the biggest demanders off plastic in Sweden. Companies such 
as Nolato, Trelleborg, and Trioplast are the lead producers of plastics in 
Sweden. CO2 can be transformed into a range of carbon-based compounds, 
which can serve as the building blocks for polymers, including various types 
of resins. An example is Polycarbonate can be produced directly from CO2, 
with 50% of the product’s mass from CO2. Polycarbonate applications 
include electronics, construction materials and vehicle components (Van 
Heek, Arning and Ziefle, 2017).

3.3 Research Question 3  
“What would it require for the Nordics to achieve their goal of becoming European 
frontrunners in CCUS, and what measures would incumbents and policymakers have to make 
to support this?” 
 
The overarching attitude towards CCUS in the Nordics is positive. All the countries are 
counting on CCS to be an essential part of their individual zero strategies set by each country, 
as presented in 2.6.3. There have been specific legislation deployed to help nurture CCS on 
national levels. Concrete initiatives have been implemented in Denmark and Norway to further 
promote CCS’s large-scale development. However, for the Nordics to become global 
frontrunners in CCUS and catch up to the current leading countries, like the US and UK, further 
engagement from policymakers and incumbents would be needed (Consoli, Havercroft and 
Irlam, 2017). IEA states that the rapid deployment hinges critically on a massive increase in 
government support and new approaches to public and private investment (International 
Energy Agency, 2020).  
 
3.3.1 Measures for Nordic Policymakers 
Even though the measures for policymakers and incumbents often overlap and influence both 
parties directly or indirectly, it remains essential to differentiate them due to their significantly 
distinct roles, capabilities, and responsibilities. 
Policymakers establish legal and regulatory frameworks that guide, incentivise, or mandate 
activities within a sector or industry, in this case, CCUS. They can design and enforce policies 
that promote the development and implementation of CCUS. They also have the power to 
allocate public resources, establish public-private partnerships, and negotiate international 
agreements.  
 
1. Increase R&D development 
Referring back to Table 8, showing the patent activity in each of the Nordic countries, 
it is evident that Norway alone demonstrates potential as a global frontrunner, while the 
remaining three countries lag behind. With each country having technical universities 
ranking highly based on research (World University Research Ranking), it must be 
assumed that the technical and research capabilities are adequate to enable the Nordics 
to become a hub for R&D in CCUS. Policymakers should increase funding either 
directly to research institutions or indirectly through grants and other subsidies for 
private companies, including start-ups. This practice is already implemented at the 
European level through the EU Horizon 2020 project, but it could be further enhanced 
with additional support at the national level. 
 
2. Create a cross-border policy framework 
The Nordics are uniquely positioned to capitalise on a mutual collaboration on CCUS 
due to their history of regional cooperation, political stability, strong economies, and 
an existing cross-border energy network. However, they also have some key 
==PAGE:50==
 
 
 
45 
differences, such as geography, geology, energy mix (Chen et al., 2021), and industries, 
e.g. Integrating these diverse factors across borders could lead to a CCUS solution that 
is markedly more flexible and versatile than separate, country-specific approaches. 
Implementing cross-border legislation would facilitate the framework for an optimal 
cross-border solution and allow for a future unified solution that would be beneficial 
for all participating parties.  
 
3. Incentivise public-private partnerships 
Public-private partnerships (PPP) are increasingly used to address financial, legal, 
political and social challenges in the public infrastructure procurement (Roehrich, 
Lewis and George, 2014). The Longship/Northern Lights CCS project will be the first 
full-scale project covering the whole value chain expected to be operational in 2024. 
This project is PPP and will be a precedent for the future of PPP in CCUS (Northern 
Lights, 2023). An arrangement consisting of PPP can bring a lot of value for both 
parties. From the public perspective, it can attract private investors by offering 
incentives such as grants, tax benefits, and other financial instruments. For private 
investors, the risks associated with complex and novel projects like CCUS can be 
mitigated with public support, thereby increasing the projects' attractiveness. 
 
 
 
3.3.2 Measures for Nordic Incumbents 
Established entities or companies working with CCS, incumbents possess significant 
operational resources and practical industry experience. The policies directly influence their 
activities in place, and they have the capacity to invest in new technologies, adapt their business 
models, and deploy solutions at scale. These incumbents include established energy and 
industrial companies, power producers, and oil and gas corporations. 
 
1. Reduce independence of subsidies  
The few commercial operating CCS facilities globally are all heavily dependent on 
subsidies making it economically feasible even with the increased EU ETS price 
(Biniek, De Luna and Di Fiori, 2022). Companies should leverage the Nordics' 
ambitious sustainability goals as well as a higher willingness to pay for lower-carbon-
intensity products (Reyes, 2021). Incumbents would be able to serve other industries, 
most likely hard-to-abate industries such as steel and cement, with a solution as to 
where they could make more sustainable products that a premium paid by the customer 
would enable.  
 
2. Engage in partnerships 
The inherent risks associated with first-mover initiatives can often be excessively high 
for a single company to bear, particularly when the venture involves a complex and 
volatile market, unpredictable prospects, and necessitate significant upfront capital. 
==PAGE:51==
 
 
 
46 
Partnerships through Joint-Ventures (JV) or Memorandum of Understanding (MoU), 
could help mitigate risks by providing a form of financial security in case projects fail 
or unforeseen costs emerge. Both private entities and public institutions can effectively 
employ this collaborative approach. An example of such collaborations can be seen in 
an adjacent industry, offshore wind, where Nordic developers are engaging in JV’s to 
leverage each other’s individual capabilities and expertise (Ørsted, 2021). While the 
approach to JV’s in Carbon Capture, Utilisation, and Storage (CCUS) differs from that 
of the more mature offshore wind sector due to its emerging status, considerable 
parallels can still be identified and leveraged. A unique challenge that CCUS is facing, 
and where collaboration could be the solution, is the deployment of needed 
infrastructure. Infrastructure is a significant share of the total cost for CCS (Middleton 
and Yaw, 2018). Optimal long-term investment decisions can promote operational 
efficiency, including pinpointing opportunities for economies of scale via infrastructure 
"super-sizing" to meet anticipated future demand. This means that incumbents, in 
collaboration with public decision-makers, could look into creating clusters for CCUS 
to leverage economies to scale and greatly decrease the development costs of CCS (Sun 
et al., 2021).  
 
3. Actively engage in Pilot Projects  
Participation in pilot projects, either conducted internally, by research institutions or 
start-ups, will most likely not initially yield tangible financial returns. Instead, it may 
initially present as a seemingly inconvenient task that does not immediately bring the 
results and benefits that CCUS is rumoured to bring. However, it could bring valuable 
data and information that could make the transition to full-scale implementation much 
more efficient and mitigate risk. A significant part of this solution lies in adaptable and 
scalable technology. In this context, investing in pilot projects can yield considerable 
long-term dividends. This proves particularly significant in hard-to-decarbonise 
sectors, where the path to emissions reduction is largely reliant on effective CCUS 
strategies (Adam Bryant, 2022).

in other renewable sources like wind and solar. Norway's energy mix, on the other hand, is 
largely based on hydropower, according to the Nordic Statistics Database. When distinguishing 
between renewable energy sources, such as wind and solar, from bioenergy, it becomes 
apparent that each presents unique opportunities and challenges. Wind and solar power, while 
emitting no CO2 during energy production and demanding minimal maintenance, grapple with 
the major drawback of intermittency, which diminishes their reliability. Conversely, bioenergy 
ensures a consistent energy supply but poses environmental challenges. Unless integrated with 
CCS, bioenergy results in CO2 emissions during combustion. Moreover, bioenergy production 
can exacerbate deforestation and incite competition with food crops without sustainable 
management. In terms of CCS, this opens up opportunities for Denmark and Norway to 
capitalise on the intermittent nature of their renewable energy sources by integrating them with 
DAC technologies. One of the primary ways that high intermittency renewable energy sources 
could be combined with DAC is by using the DAC system to balance the grid. When there is 
an excess of renewable energy (for instance, on a particularly sunny or windy day), rather than 
wasting that energy or having to curtail production, it could be used to power the DAC system 
and capture more CO2. Conversely, the DAC system could be ramped down when less 
renewable energy is available. However, this surplus of energy needs to be assessed against 
other potential uses, such as exporting it to other countries or perhaps utilising it for hydrogen 
production through electrolysis, which is also an energy-intensive process. 
 
If the Nordics are to become European frontrunners on CCUS, could BECCS and DAC enable 
the Nordics to capitalise on providing services such as carbon offsetting and storage services, 
enabling them to turn their environmental commitments into economic benefits. The market 
for carbon offsetting alone is expected to grow to €230 billion by 2050 (Morgan Stanley, 2023). 
The Nordics could provide high-quality offsets due to their rigorous governance, transparency, 
and respect for environmental standards. These attributes can enhance the credibility of their 
carbon offsets, which is crucial in a market where the quality of offsets can vary significantly. 
Buyers of offsets can have greater confidence that projects in these countries are genuinely 
reducing emissions and not simply displacing them elsewhere. The Nordic countries will 
require further political commitment and financial support to leverage their close collaboration 
and disparities effectively and create an optimal infrastructure.

4.2 Comparison with Prior Work 
Much research has been done on CCUS, both academically and by large institutions such as 
IPCC, IEA, and others. However, a comparison between the Nordic countries and 
recommendations on how they could potentially use this technology to reach net zero has not 
been done in the last 15 years. In 2007, Norden, an organisation facilitating interparliamentary 
cooperation among the Nordic countries, published a report suggesting significant potential for 
establishing a CCS chain in the North European and North Sea region. This initiative would 
involve major infrastructure and storage components within the Nordic countries (Nordic 
Council of Ministers, 2007). While this report might seem outdated, it still provides valuable 
insights into the potential for CCS. However, it does not delve into areas such as utilisation, 
the potential for DAC or BECCS. Instead, it offers a comprehensive examination of transport 
and storage. Hence, this study has chosen to concentrate more heavily on these aspects. 
Norden has also recently published a report touching upon e-fuels, CCU, and CCS in a Nordic 
context (Nordic Energy Research, 2022), which also briefly touched upon CCU and each of 
the countries current engagement and future potential. The report underlines ongoing CCUS 
projects and offers brief recommendations for each country on using CCUS for 
decarbonisation. While it presents valid recommendations, which have been taken into account 
in this study, it does not delve into detailed explanations of how to implement these 
recommendations. 
This study seeks to offer informative insights and recommendations on the most efficient 
CCUS approaches for the Nordic countries. It accomplishes this by collating studies and reports 
from leading organisations and research institutions. While much existing work delves into 
areas pertinent to our study's objective, very few are directly focused on the Nordics. The most 
recent publication dedicated to this subject dates back to 2007, underscoring the need for a 
fresh perspective on CCUS in the Nordic context.  
 
4.3 Limitations  
As mentioned before, CCUS is a highly researched subject. Due to limitations of time, it has 
been necessary to evaluate which publications were the most relevant. Therefore, potential 
relevant information could have been missed.  
 
4.4 Future research 
Future research could involve conducting a techno-economic analysis of CCUS. Specifically, 
the study could investigate the economic viability of harnessing excess renewable energy, due 
to intermittency, for DAC instead of exporting this surplus energy or producing hydrogen. The 
proposed techno-economic analysis could consist of the following: 
 
 
 
==PAGE:55==
 
 
 
50 
• Operational Costs Comparison: A detailed assessment of the operational costs of 
DAC, energy export, and hydrogen production could provide a clear financial picture. 
This would take into consideration factors such as energy prices, technology and 
maintenance costs, and energy storage costs. 
 
• Efficiency Analysis: The study should analyse the efficiency of using excess 
renewable energy for DAC versus hydrogen production. This analysis would take into 
account the energy conversion rates and overall system efficiency. 
 
• Infrastructure Needs: The analysis should evaluate the infrastructure requirements for 
all three scenarios: DAC, energy export, and hydrogen production. This includes DAC 
facilities, power grid connections, and hydrogen production and storage facilities. 
 
• Regulatory and Policy Implications: The study should also consider the policy and 
regulatory environment surrounding DAC, energy export, and hydrogen production. 
This includes potential subsidies, carbon pricing, and regulations around energy export 
and hydrogen storage and transport. 
 
• Environmental Impact: The environmental implications of DAC, energy export, and 
hydrogen production should be compared. This analysis should quantify potential 
reductions in CO2 emissions, consider the lifecycle emissions of each option, and 
evaluate the impact on sustainability goals. 
 
• Market Analysis: An exploration of potential market opportunities and challenges for 
each option could inform decisions about future investments. As markets for carbon 
capture and hydrogen technologies grow, understanding the potential opportunities and 
risks is crucial.

5 Chapter 6 – Conclusion 
The field of CCUS holds immense potential for mitigating greenhouse gas emissions and 
accelerating the transition to a low-carbon economy. This master's thesis has explored the 
prospects and challenges of CCUS in the Nordic region, specifically focusing on Denmark, 
Finland, Norway, and Sweden. By analysing the current state of CCUS technologies, the 
industries driving CO2 emissions, the geological storage capacity, and the necessary measures 
to achieve CCUS leadership in Europe, this study has provided valuable insights for 
policymakers, industry stakeholders, and researchers. 
 
The findings of this thesis underscore the unique strengths and opportunities that each Nordic 
country possesses in the context of CCUS. Norway emerges as the leading country, driven by 
its extensive experience in oil and gas exploration and production. This background has 
positioned Norway at the forefront of technological maturity and investment interest in CCUS. 
The country's commitment to phasing out the oil and gas sectors and its strategic location with 
abundant offshore storage capacity further enhance its potential for leading CCUS initiatives. 
 
Conversely, Denmark has excelled in policy-making and has made significant investments in 
renewable energy sources. While the country's technological maturity in CCUS may not match 
that of Norway, its proactive approach to regulation and supportive frameworks create a 
favourable environment for CCUS development. Denmark's investments in offshore wind 
energy and its potential for integrating intermittent renewable energy with DAC) technologies 
present unique opportunities for driving CCUS innovation. 
 
Finland and Sweden, with their heavy reliance on bioenergy, offer substantial potential for 
implementing BECCS to achieve negative emissions. These countries possess abundant forest 
resources and a strong industrial base, making BECCS a viable option for both emissions 
reduction and utilisation of captured CO2. While their technological maturity in CCUS may be 
comparatively lower, the opportunity to leverage their bioenergy infrastructure and industries, 
such as the pulp and paper sector, provides a strong foundation for scaling up BECCS projects. 
 
A crucial aspect highlighted by this thesis is the potential for Nordic cooperation in CCUS. The 
Nordic countries share a history of regional collaboration, political stability, strong economies, 
and an existing cross-border energy network. These factors create a favourable environment 
for cross-border initiatives and the development of optimal CCUS solutions. Collaborative 
approaches, such as Joint Ventures (JVs) or Memoranda of Understanding (MoU), can help 
mitigate risks, share costs, and foster innovation in CCUS projects. Leveraging each Nordic 
country's unique strengths and resources through collaborative efforts could yield substantial 
benefits in technological advancements, cost efficiencies, and knowledge sharing. 
 
The Nordic countries need to align their intentions and actions to achieve their goal of 
becoming European frontrunners in CCUS. While significant progress has been made in some 
areas, further engagement from policymakers and industry incumbents are required. Public-
==PAGE:57==
 
 
 
52 
private partnerships can play a vital role in driving CCUS development by attracting private 
investors through incentives such as grants, tax benefits, and financial instruments. 
Governments should also provide a clear and supportive regulatory framework, allocate public 
resources, and establish public-private partnerships to accelerate CCUS deployment. 
 
Infrastructure development is a critical aspect of CCUS implementation. The Nordic countries 
should prioritise long-term investment decisions that promote operational efficiency and 
identify opportunities for economies of scale through infrastructure "super-sizing." By creating 
CCUS clusters and leveraging economies of scale, development costs can be significantly 
reduced, making CCUS projects more economically viable. Public decision-makers and 
industry incumbents must collaborate to plan and develop the necessary infrastructure to 
support widespread CCUS deployment.
