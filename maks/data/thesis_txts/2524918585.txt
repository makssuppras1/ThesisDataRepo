Abstract
The geometry between the wellbore and the formation layering can have a major impact on the
logging tool response. In deviated wells (Low-Angle, High-Angle and Horizontal Wells), resistiv-
ity measurements can be complicated by environmental effects, such as approaching boundary,
dip angle, abnormal response such as polarization horn and resistivity anisotropy. Thus, using
traditional vertical well evaluation method on deviated wells may not give qualitative results
due to geometric effects which could cause erroneous, estimation of hydrocarbon in place. This
study has attempted petrophysical evaluation of deviated wells using data from the Volve Field
in the Norwegian Continental Shelf, with focus on Hugin and Sleipner Formations. The method-
ology employed was initial computation of basic petrophysical parameters to determine the sat-
uration using the raw data and subsequent correction of the raw logs in the deviated wells and
re-computation of saturation. Two cases were investigated; a low angle well and a high angle well
and 3D petrophysics workﬂow within the Sclumberger Techlog software platform was used to
derive the square logs from the model logs after correction of geometrical effects.
The results showed that the geometric effects are more pronounced in the high angle well
than low angle well. The water saturation computed from squared logs and measured log for
both models showed differences as a result of the geometrical effects corrected using squared logs
generated from 3DP modeling. Further sensitivity analysis on the water saturation computation
showed that m and n had the highest positive inﬂuence on the computed water saturation in
terms of petrophysical parameters. Hugin formation was found to have better hydrocarbon sat-
uration when compared to Sleipner with average water saturation 0.16 . Porosity was observed
to be decreasing with depth which as result of possible compaction. Furthermore, petrophysical
property modeling was done using Sequential Gaussian Simulation to interpolate the variation of
the porosity and water saturation across the reservoir in a 3D grid. The results were consistency
with well logs. However, due to limited data, especially core data in the model wells, we could
==PAGE:6==
vi
not validate the result of saturation differences observed in the measured and model square logs
after the removal of geometric effects.

Introduction
Summary: This chapter talks about the research problem, why it is necessary to correct the geometric effects on resis-
tivity log acquired in deviated wells. The study objectives is also introduced and chapter outline is drawn
1.1
Statement of Research Problem
Over years, there has been a signiﬁcant improvement in the technology to drill high angle, hor-
izontal and extended reach wells, and with the recent technology, HaHz well can be drilled up
to 9km. Horizontal wells accounted for 96% of the oil production from tight geological forma-
tions, such as shale, in 2018, up from 15% in 2004, according to data from the federal Energy
Information Administration (EIA) [49]. Economics and environmental restrictions play a role and
necessitate drilling deviated wells of complex trajectories, especially in offshore prospect, where
wells are drilled from a central structure, thus enabling development of satellite reservoirs. Also,
the greater reservoir coverage, increased drainage and smaller footprint made possible by devi-
ated/horizontal wells, are an added advantage.
Similarly, there has been a signiﬁcant improvement in technologies to log wells. Sophisticated
tools like the borehole image tools and Array Resistivity Compensated (ARC) with greater resolu-
tion are available which can provide more information about the wellbore. Modern technologies
allow standard petrophysical measurements to be acquired in deviated wells, technologies such
as coil tubing and drill pipe assisted logging, and coupled with development of LWD tools and
assemblies, have become the primary means of acquiring logging data in HaHz wells [45].
Difﬁculty arises, since the LWD data acquisition in HA/Hz wells is primarily designed for
geo-steering purpose, and can be unstable to be used directly for petrophysical evaluation [30].

Petrophysical models are normally created and calibrated using vertical (semi- perpendicular)
wells which have been cored. In areas where there is no calibration, the behavior of most well
logs in vertical wells is understood sufﬁciently to make a reasonable analysis possible. However,
when the log readings and character changes, as the wellbore becomes more parallel to the bed-
ding planes, these calibrations and theoretical interpretations fail to give quantitative results [36].
Moreover, in vertical well, where the logging tools are often perpendicular to the formation bed, a
single layer is sampled, which is the layer adjacent to the borehole as shown in ﬁgure 1.1, whereas
in deviated and horizontal wells, multiple layers are sampled since the logging tool has become
more parallel to the formation beds (the angle between the wellbore and formation bed decreases,
becomes zero for horizontal well) [37].

In such HA/Hz wells, the propagation resistivity logs are severely affected [22]. Extended
boundary effects like when crossing a boundary with high resistivity contrast (polarization horn
) are more profound. Depending on the formation properties and their geometry relative to the
wellbore, formation evaluation may be signiﬁcantly over or under-estimated, if these geometric
effects are not corrected [22]. So, due to bed boundary effects, proximity or uncrossed layers,
resistivity anisotropy, polarisation horns and compounding with asymetric invasion proﬁle which
prevails in deviated and horizontal wells, it becomes difﬁcult to apply vertical well interpretation
to deviated well and horizontal wells.[30].

2.2.1
Effects of Borehole Deviation on 2-MHz Resistivity Log
The 2-MHz array tools like the ARC5 (array resistivity compensated by Schlumnberger), prop-
agates a high frequency (2-MHz) of electromagnetic wave into the formation. A propagation
measurement is made by taking the difference between the phases (phase shift) and amplitudes
(attenuation) of the voltages recorded at the two receivers. Attenuation increases as a function of
increasing conductivity, while the wavelength decreases as conductivity increases. Thus, the two
measurements are proportional to formation conductivity and can be used to generate resistivity
logs.
For these measurements, axial and radial response functions vary with the resistivity of the for-
mation being measured. The measured currents induced around the tool follow the path of least
resistance, preferentially responding to low resistivity layers.
As the angle of deviation increases, the development of polarization horns on the resistivity
tool response becomes more profound. The polarization horns is a spike to high resistivity as the
tool crosses bed boundaires, which is caused by the resistivity contrast between adjacent beds. In
this case, current loops circulating around the tool are forced to cross boundaries where a resistiv-
ity contrast exists ﬁgure 2.7
The polarization is caused by a discontinuity of the electric ﬁeld crossing the bed boundary. In
the absence of dip, for both 2-MHz and induction tools, the induced electric ﬁelds and their cur-
rents are parallel to the bed boundaries. However, when a dipping-bed boundary is encountered,
the induced currents must cross the bed boundary. The currents must be the same for a given
current loop because the electrons or ions cannot simply vanish [5]. The boundary separates two

2.3
Corrections for Geometrical effects.
Over the years, a lot of research had been put in to develop models that would effectively de-
termined the true formation resistivity Rt from deviated wells which is free from shoulder bed,
anisotropy, invasion, and geometric effects. 1-D and 2-D resistivity modeling techniques have
been used to analyze log data and the results used to solve for H-C saturation [26].
==PAGE:39==
2.3. Corrections for Geometrical effects.
17
Group of standard correction charts and algorithms have been developed to compensate for theses
parasitic effect. For instances, the skin effect on induction logs can be corrected using the boosting
algorithm [39], borehole and invasion effects can be corrected using charts [41], and shoulder bed
effect are corrected using a deconvolution algorithm [16].
Several work abound in literature which demonstrated the corrections for the geometrical ef-
fects on logs particularly the resistivity log. For instance, K.A Saﬁnya et.al (1998) [3] developed a
code for computing induction and 2-MHz tool response in highly deviated wells, using iterative
forward modelling , which signiﬁcantly improved Rt estimation [3] [6]. Gaugliu et al (2009) [29]
presented a deep learning and convolutional neural network learning frame work to automate
formation geometry determination and resistivity inversion for high angle and horizontal wells,
which would produce a more accurate formation resistivity. Also, Zhiyi Zhang et.al (2002) [55]
developed a constrained petrophysical inversion algorithm which uses gamma ray, neutron, den-
sity, acoustic and resistivity data in a single uniﬁed interpretation process. They described the
interpretation steps from estimation of bed boundary position which best represents the subsur-
face geology and lithology to incorporate a resistivity bounds into the inversion algorithm. The
inversion process they described was able to handle effectively systematic noise in the data, and
thus producing a more accurate results when applied to real data. A number of case studies were
also described by S.Syofan et.al (2017) [48] for deviated wells in Abu Dhabi, where an innova-
tive modeling workﬂow was adopted to generate the true formation resistivity from LWD logs
using an image-constrained inversion technique. They validated the results using data from near
by vertical wells, obtaining a more realistic formation resistivity. Similarly, A fast forward model
was developed by Hui Xie et. al (2016) [54] for nuclear measurement, which is based on a ﬂux
derived sensitivity function maps from Monto Carlo modeling. They described a workﬂow for
determining the layer properties (layer earth model) from density and neutron logs in high angle
and horizontal wells, which was further validated with synthetic and ﬁeld data.
Furthermore, a dip correction algorithm based on ﬁltering was developed for both phasor and
array induction tool, the algorithm is limited to dip below 60◦[7]. More recently, a maximum
entropy inversion [9] was applied to array induction logs to estimate Rt, Rxo and di in invaded
thin beds at high relative dip angles, and validating the accuracy of the results using 3D modeling

[8].
Most of all these methods involves forward modeling of the tool response and inversion. The
forward modeled logs are a function of the structural model and layer properties of the formation,
which are compared to the measured logs. Several iteration are performed until a good agreement
between the measured and the modelled log is obtained. Forward modeling of the tool response
provides an effective way to correct the geometric effects on the resistivity log, and it would be
used in this study to generate the true layer properties of the formation understudy. The Schlum-
berger forward modeling program is reviewed below.
2.3.1
Forward Modeling and Resistivity Inversion.
To correct the resistivity logs for geometric effects, a forward model of the tool response is being
computed, which takes into account the formation dip and the bed boundary. The forward mod-
eling code is based on a schlumberger program named ANISBEDS [23] (see [4] for a description
of the modeling of the Schlumberger tool response ), which computes the electromagnetic ﬁelds
of a point magnetic dipole radiator in a layered, dipping, and transversely isotropic anisotropic
medium. The program basically computes the complex vector-valued magnetic ﬁeld and electric
ﬁeld at any position with respect to an arbitrarily oriented magnetic dipole transmitter. In this
study, the in-house program described by [4] would be used to forward model the tool response
for an induction-type measurements.
Inversion for Rh and Rv, uses a parametric inversion algorithm described by [4] for a layered-
earth model. The inversion algorithm is an iterative approach based on Gauss-Newton method
that employs a quadratic model of the cost function. The cost function is the square of the sum
of the relative residual errors given by the difference between measured and predicted responses
(by a forward model of the tool response) after each iteration.
The basic theory of parametric inversion based on a layered-earth model for a 2-MHz tool
response in an anisotropic media as described by [4] is highlighted below.

Checking the quality of the acquired logs, is a painstaking and rigorous process, which is very
important for a better evaluation. Drilling is a dynamic process and the log environment is never
ideal and well logs are prone to environmental effects ranging from borehole conditions to inva-
sion effects. Thus, log quality check (LQC)is an essential procedure before interpretation, and it
involves checking the acquired logs for environmental effects and applying corrections if effects
are identiﬁed.
Although, tool engineering and well site instrumentation may have already corrected the logs
for environmental effects, but as a convention, the logs were cross checked to validate their data
quality in terms of accuracy, clarity and repeatability [44]
Some of the procedure for the LQC were adopted from [17], and the include the following
steps.

Conclusion
In this study, the effect of borehole deviation on 2-Mhz propagation resistivity was investigated
for a low and a high angle well using measurements from the Volve ﬁeld. A basic petrophysical
evaluation was done to understand the reservoir properties of the ﬁeld.

pecially at bed boundaries. The square logs from the modeled logs were used to recompute the

tence with the well log, and there was more variation in the minor direction and vertical direction
than major direction. With additional well data, the reservoir could be improved and could pro-
vide additional information for placing injector and producer wells.
Although the water saturation results could not be validated due to lack of core data, but the
results from the modeled logs were free from geometric effects and their square log provided the
true formation properties. From this study, it could mentioned that 3D petrophysic workﬂow
could be effective in correcting the geometric effects on propagation resistivity log, and more
conﬁdence could be established by validating the results with core data.

A.4
Wellbore Logging
Welling logging is the practice of making a detailed record of the geological formation penetrated
by the a borehole [52]. The log data presents a continuous documentation of the whole proﬁle,
and gives a physical characterization of individual layers and sections in terms of resistivity ,
nuclear radiation etc. It could be either visual inspections of the cuttings or core sampled on
the surface or on physical measurements made by instruments lowered downhole. logging is an
alternate or supplement to the analysis of cores, side-wall samples, and cuttings. Although often
preferred because of the possibility of continuous analysis of the rock formation over a given
interval, economic and technical problems also limit the use of cores [20]. Cuttings recovered
from the surface are a good source for subsurface sampling, but their lithological reconstruction
are imprecise due to difﬁculty in matching the depth, so logging remains the best technique for
petrophysical measurement.
The birth of logging can be dated to the ﬁrst recorded event at Pechelbronn on September
5, 1927 where H. Doll and the Schlumberger brothers (and a few others) made a semicontinu-
ous resistivity measurement in that tired old ﬁeld in Alsace [20]. The operation was performed
with a rudimentary device (a sonde) consisting of a bakelite cylinder with a couple of metallic
electrodes on its exterior [20]. In recent years, more advanced tools have been developed to take
different physical measurement even in more challenging bore hole conditions (Temperature and
Pressure). The measurements are either taken using wire-line or simultaneously while drilling
(LWD). In the later case which is a focus of the study, the logging tools are embedded in the bot-
tomhole assembly, with the aim of saving time and aiding geo-steering. Sophisticated processing
and interpretation transforms the measured data into reservoir properties such as porosity, ﬂuid
saturation and permeability.
A.5
Log Type and Uses.
The most relevant purpose of log measurement is to provide a continuous record of the lithology
(identifying the reservoir rocks), with the exact depth of the formations/bed boundaries, to infer
reservoir proprieties like porosity, permeability and saturation (hydrocarbon), identify fractures

and tectonic element, to precisely navigate and place the well at the optimum position (geo steer-
ing) and also (depending on the motive,) indication of content and properties of other substance
other than hydrocarbon such as water, thermal water, ore etc. [45].
There are several kinds of logs available, the electric (laterlog/induction) and the nuclear (Den-
sity, Gamma, neutron) log being the most widely used. The subsequent sections will be reviewing
the relevant log for this study, principle of measurement and their basic physical equation would
be highlighted. Some of the relevant logs include:
• Resistivity/Conductivity Log.
• Acoustic Log.
• Gamma Log.

A.5.1
Electrical and Electromagnetic Logs
Historically, the Schlumberger brothers, started the development of welling logging with the ﬁrst
electrical resistivity measurement. Interpretation of these electrical logs was ampliﬁed in 1942
by Archie when he published his famous equations which forms today’s corner stone for well
logging, especially for water saturation determination.[21] The key parameter in determining oil
saturation is the resistivity of the formation Rt, as electricity can pass true rocks only if they contain
conductive ﬂuids (which is the major limitation for electric tools.). The development of electrical

tools has henceforward been intense, and the limitation of the electric tools for use only in conduc-

The electric (galvanic) and the electromagnetic (induction) logs measures the formation’s re-

This log provide a full spectrum of gamma radiation in terms of the individual contributions of
potassium, Thorium and Uranium. It is useful in identifying source rocks.
The relative abundance of Thorium and Uranium are measured in PPM (pounds per million)
and its very small compared to potassium which is roughly 1.5% of the earth crust by weight, yet
their contribution to gamma log is often of the same magnitude with Potassium. As presented
in ﬁgure A.24, the percentage fraction of radioactive Thorium and Uranium are 100% , when
compared to potassium which is 0.0017%. This means a very few mole (trace) of Thorium and
Uranium would contain a radioactive nuclides greater or of the same magnitude of radioactivity
with a corresponding higher mole of potassium.

Neutron Log
The neutron log provides a continous measurements of the formation’s reaction to the bombard-
ment of fast neutron particle, and its quoted in terms of neutron porosity units.. The neutron
tool observes the interaction of the neutron radiation from the source and the formation. These
interaction are related to the formation richness in hydrogen (which is provided by water or hy-
drocarbon) or the hydrogen index [38]. The hydrogen index is simply the number of hydrogen
atoms per unit volume divided by the number of hydrogen atoms per unit volume of pure water
at surface conditions.
Neutron logs are used for delineation of porous formations and determination of their porosity
(see Table 2.4 for qualitative and quantitative use of neutron log). They respond primarily to the
amount of hydrogen in the formation. Thus, in clean formations whose pores are ﬁlled with water
or oil, the neutron log reﬂects the amount of liquid-ﬁlled porosity[45]. Gas zones can often be
identiﬁed by comparing the neutron log with another porosity log or a core analysis. A combina-
tion of the neutron log with one or more other porosity logs yields even more accurate porosity
values and lithology identiﬁcation, even an evaluation of shale content [45].
In principle, the neutrons which are sub-atomic particles with no electric charge and the same
mass as hydrogen atom interacts with matter in two distinct ways, by collison (in-elastic and
elastic) and by absorption. These interactions, results in lose of energy or velocity over a certain
trajectory called the slowing down length [42]. The slowing down length is relatively short in a
hydrogen rich region, and the knowledge is used in logging as detectors are placed at optimum
distance from the neutron source[42].
The kind of interaction depends on the energy state of the neutron ﬁgure A.26.

The capturing nucleus becomes intensely excited and emits a high-energy gamma ray of cap-

the detector increases for decreased hydrogen concentration. and vice versa.
As mentioned previously, the neutron log response is given in neutron porosity unit. Most
neutron tools are calibrated in fresh water ﬁlled clean limestone, so the log gives a porosity value
as it would in clean limestone. For other lithologies like sandstone, a conversation factor is re-
quired. Neutron log are also corrected for bore hole effect, mud weight and temperature.

Sonic Log
The Sonic log or acoustic log provides a continous measurement of the formation capacity to trans-
mit sound waves. It measures the velocity of the sound wave as it travels through the formation,
and in most cases, it is quoted as the formations transit time ∆t (which is the inverse of the veloc-
ity of the sound wave) [38]. The velocity of the sound wave or the transit time in the formation,
depends on the formation lithology and porosity. In known lithology, this dependence on poros-
ity makes it possible to quantify the porosity from sonic logs. See table 2.4 for quantitative and
qualitative use of sonic logs.
Principle
The transmitters for the sonic tools are mostly a magnetostrictive or a piezoelectric device which
translates an electric signals into ultrasonic vibration. The receivers are also a piezoelectric device
which converts pressure waves in electromagnetic signals which is ampliﬁed to providing the
logging signal [42].
The tool propagates sound wave into the formation. The propagation of sound in the borehole is
governed by the formation type, the borehole ﬂuid column and the tool conﬁgurations. When the
propagated sound arrives the borehole wall, compressional and shear waves that travel through
the formation are created, also surface waves traveling along the borehole wall and guided wave

spacing, helps to mitigate unwanted borehole and tool effects and provides a reliable borehole
compensated measurement even in poor borehole conditions.
The output from the receivers are transmitted to the surface, as a set of digitized waveform, which
are further processed with the surface instrumentation using a full waveform technique [42].

Generally, the velocity of the sound in a given rock would decrease with increase in porosity.
In well compacted and consolidated formations with lower porosity, the sonic velocities is not
sensitive to the pore ﬂuids or even disseminated shale. However, at higher porosity, the effect of
pore ﬂuid and shale volume become more evident[42].
Also, sonic velocities are sensitive to compaction, as it increases with compaction. Since com-
paction increases with depth, the sonic velocity would tend to increase accordingly with depth.
Over pressured zone which could be due to increase in shale porosity, would reduce the sonic
velocity (higher transit time), showing a breakage in the normal compaction trend. This property
of the sonic log, makes it useful in identifying over pressured and over compacted zones as the
