Abstract  
 
Proteins are extensively utilized in food applications as gelling, emulsifying, and foaming agents in 
cake, bread, ice cream, and meringue. Animal-based protein has been a highly utilized protein in the 
food industry, however, in recent years; plant-based alternatives are greatly researched to replace 
animal protein. This tendency is mainly driven by several factors, such as animal welfare, allergen 
reduction, sustainability, environmental concern, and consumer trends. Chickpea cooking water, 
which is also known as “Aquafaba”, is a potential source of proteins with high techno-functional 
properties.  
The current study investigated the effect of different extraction conditions on the biochemical, phys-
icochemical, and colloidal properties of aquafaba protein. By using response surface methodology, 
we investigate the effect of processing parameters: temperature (60℃, 80℃, 100℃), chickpea to 
cooking water ratio (1:1, 1:1.6, 1:4), and heating time (90, 165, 240 min) on protein extraction struc-
ture-functionalities were investigated.  Extracted aquafaba was analyzed by proximate analysis i.e., 
protein composition, moisture content, and protein concentration. Protein profiles were analyzed by 
SDS-PAGE, secondary structure by Fourier Transform Infrared (FTIR) spectrometry, and thermal 
property by Differential Scanning Calorimetry (DSC). The processing temperature along with time 
showed a significant effect on glass transition (Tg), denaturation temperature (Td), and melting tem-
perature (Tm). No visible peaks at Amide I region were detected by FTIR for lower processing tem-
perature, time, and ratio. However, after peak resolution, noticeable amide I bands were observed in 
all the samples. Foams were produced by Ultra-Turrax homogenizer and by visual analysis the foam 
capacity (FC) and foam stability (FS) during the time were explored. The temperature showed a sig-
nificant effect on FC. The highest FC was obtained at 80℃, a ratio of 0.625, and a time of 165 min. 
FS followed a similar trend in all the processing conditions. Overall, the obtained results in this study 
provide deep insight into the impact of extraction process conditions on the yield, composition, and 
functional properties of aquafaba.

1. Introduction 
In the food industry, animal-based protein has been the main source of protein in a formulated food 
and are extensively used as gelling, emulsifying, and foaming agents in cakes, bread, ice cream, me-
ringue, etc. However, due to specific dietary choices, health considerations, allergens reductions, in-
creased costs, and environmental concerns (Janssen et al. 2016; Rizwan Sharif et al. 2017; Alsalman 
et al. 2020; He et al. 2019), the consumption of animal proteins is unappealing for those consumers. 
Those consumers are actively searching for healthier alternatives not only for cheaper sources of 
nutrients including essential amino acids but also for their functional properties.  
Many studies have identified a possible animal substitute from plant protein. Legumes such as soy-
beans, dry beans, peas, and chickpeas are rich sources of protein as well as fiber and carbohydrates, 
which makes them valuable sources of food (Rebello, Greenway, and Finley 2014). They can be 
incorporated as meat and dairy replacers in the formulation of different food products (Rizwan Sharif 
et al. 2017). In food products, the contribution of protein (for example egg protein) in a cake is due 
to its techno-functional properties (Mustafa and Reaney 2020). It becomes a challenge to find a suit-
able substance with similar functional protein properties, even with a combination of additives. 
Aquafaba in “Latin” means water from beans (He et al. 2021). In recent years, aquafaba has gained 
popularity in vegan society to replace eggs in many foods. Aquafaba is defined as the liquid (waste 
water) from both canned chickpeas as well as the boiling water from chickpea preparations (Buhl, 
Christensen, and Hammershøj 2019). Aquafaba contains carbohydrates and proteins that leach out 
during soaking and cooking operations (Sayar, Turhan, and Köksel 2011). This liquid is characterized 
as emulsifying, gelling, and foaming agents in several studies. As a foaming agent, it has been used 
to make meringue, ice cream, mousse and eggless cakes (Stantiall et al. 2018; He et al. 2021).  
Protein concentration is known to provide functional properties such as foaming. Foaming properties 
depend on protein concentration.  It is reported that higher protein concentration provides higher foam 
capacity and stability (Stantiall et al. 2018).  Subsequently, it has also been reported that protein 
concentration is affected by processing parameters (temperature, pressure, and time) (Sayar, Turhan, 
and Köksel 2011; Alsalman et al. 2020). 
The efficiency of aquafaba protein as functional protein can be adversely or favorably affected by the 
processing conditions, such as heating temperature, pressure, heating time and ratio (seeds to water 
proportion). It is therefore important to take into consideration the factors that can affect the compo-
sition and functionality of aquafaba.

1.1 
Objectives   
Currently, information on the effect of processing conditions of functionality of aquafaba is limited. 
Therefore, the objectives of this thesis were as follows. 
i) 
To establish an aquafaba extraction protocol, to evaluate aquafaba yield, and to investigate 
the optimal extraction of aquafaba by studying the chickpea to cooking water ratio (CWR), 
heating time and temperature.  
ii) 
To characterize aquafaba physicochemical properties and to determine the impact of ex-
traction condition on the aquafaba protein profile.  
iii) 
To investigate aquafaba foaming properties obtained from different extraction conditions. 
iv) 
To correlate the effects of extraction conditions on composition and foaming properties 
of aquafaba.

2. State of the Art 
This chapter presents understanding regarding protein, aquafaba, soaking and cooking of chickpea, 
and foam.  The first section provides a review of protein and its necessity followed by aquafaba, 
soaking and composition in the second section. The techno-functional properties are presented in the 
third section of this chapter. 
2.1 
Proteins  
Proteins are chains of 20 amino acids linked together via peptide bonds, which are commonly found 
in plants and animals (Wu 2016; Hou and Wu 2018). An amino acid is an organic compound, which 
contains both an amino (NH3) and carboxylic acid (COOH) and a side chain (Lopez and Mohiuddin 
2020; Haque, Timilsena, and Adhikari 2016). A general structure of amino acid is depicted in Figure 
2.1. Amino acids are classified into essential and not essential. Only 9 of these amino acids are con-
sidered as nutritionally essential in humans as they cannot be synthesized by the body and they must 
be supplied from an exogenous diet (Wu 2016; Hou and Wu 2018; Lopez and Mohiuddin 2020).   The 
essential and non-essential amino acids are assembled in Table 2.1. Proteins are divided into primary, 
secondary, tertiary, and quaternary, but nutritionally only primary proteins are important for humans 
(Haque, Timilsena, and Adhikari 2016). Schematic representation of protein structures is illustrated

Proteins are essential for the health, growth, development, reproduction, and survival of organisms 
(Lopez and Mohiuddin 2020). Deficiency in protein results in stunting, anemia, physical weakness, 
edema, vascular dysfunction, and impaired immunity (Wu 2016). Protein demand varies from person 
to person, where the greatest demand is during growth, pregnancy, and lactation (Kristo and Corredig 
2014).  In European countries, the average protein intake varies between 67-114 g/day in adult men 
and 59-102 g/day in women (“Scientific Opinion on Dietary Reference Values for Protein” 2012). 
Besides the nutritional value, food protein also provides functional properties. The functional prop-
erties are discussed in Sections 2.3.  Proteins play an important role in the food supply. Protein also 
provides texture and flavor to food (Kristo and Corredig 2014).  
In recent years, plant based proteins have attracted great attention as a possible candidate to replace 
animal-based protein (Rizwan Sharif et al. 2017). This interest is raised by the increased awareness 
of individual health, environmental and agricultural sustainability  (Nikbakht Nasrabadi, Sedaghat 
Doost, and Mezzenga 2021) as well as consumer (vegetarian and veganism) and food industry con-
cerns (Rizwan Sharif et al. 2017; D.J. McClements 2003; He et al. 2021; David Julian McClements 
2007). Pulses, which are edible seeds from legumes are an important source of plant-based protein 
substitutes for animal proteins (Liener 1962). According to He et al. (2021), pulses such as chickpea, 
lentil, soy and dry pea contain 20-30 % lysine rich protein. Soy protein has been used for a many 
years to improve nutritional value and functional food ingredients in food (Singh et al. 2008). Soy 
protein isolates are often referred to as the most functional soy protein due to high nutritional values 
as well as its ability to bind water, emulsify fat, create gel and form foam (Singh et al. 2008).  
Lately, the viscous liquid known as aquafaba, which is usually discarded as wastewater has gained 
popularity in the vegan community. Studies have shown aquafaba could be used as a source of protein 
to create gel, emulsion, and foam and could be used to replace the functional properties of eggs (Buhl, 
Christensen, and Hammershøj 2019; Lafarga et al. 2019; Stantiall et al. 2018). Aquafaba is discussed 
in the ensuing section.

2.2 
Aquafaba  
Aquafaba in “Latin” means water from beans (He et al. 2021). Aquafaba is described as the viscous 
liquid formed during the cooking of legumes seeds typically from chickpea (Raikos, Hayes, and Ni 
2020). Generally, aquafaba is discarded as waste both at an industrial (food industries) and domestic 
level (Lafarga et al. 2019). Aquafaba is registered as a valuable ingredient with desirable functional 
properties by researchers, which could replace eggs and milk in vegan products (Shim et al. 2018; 
Stantiall et al. 2018; Serventi et al. 2020). Emulsification, gelling, and foaming are the main func-
tional properties of aquafaba. These functional properties are mainly driven by protein content, water-
soluble/insoluble carbohydrates, polysaccharide-protein complexes, coacervates, saponins and phe-
nolic compounds (He et al. 2021). Functional properties of a protein are described in Section 2.3.  
Foaming has been the focus of this project. 
2.2.1 
Aquafaba source  
Aquafaba can be obtained from different legumes after cooking. However, aquafaba from chickpea 
has gained a lot of attention in recent years. Aquafaba from chickpea has been investigated by several 
researchers as an egg replacement (Alsalman et al. 2020; Lafarga et al. 2019; Buhl, Christensen, and 
Hammershøj 2019). 
Chickpea (Cicer arietimum L.) is a legume crop, which belongs to the Fabaceae family and is one of 
the oldest and most cultivated and consumed legumes worldwide (Sofi et al. 2020). In tropical and 
subtropical areas it is a staple food (Alajaji and El-Adawy 2006).  It is the third most cultivated legume 
crop in the world (Mustafa and Reaney 2020). Chickpea’s gross composition accounts for 66.8 % 
carbohydrates, 25.1 % protein, 4.7 % fats, 7% moisture, and 3.4 % ash (Xu et al. 2016). The protein 
fraction of chickpea constitutes mainly globulins (56 %), glutelin (18 %), albumins (12 %), and pro-
lamin (3%) and residual proteins (Xu et al. 2017). These compositions are known to leach into the 
soaking/boiling water and exert some valuable functional properties.  
2.2.1.1 
Soaking and heating 
Chickpea is traditionally stored dried and the dried pulses are not edible as they are hard, and could 
be dangerous to consume due to the presence of antinutritional compounds (phytates, tannins) 
(Chigwedere et al. 2019).  The two universal methods applied during the preparation of chickpea 
(legumes or pulses) are soaking and cooking (Stantiall et al. 2018; Buhl, Christensen, and 
Hammershøj 2019; Lafarga et al. 2019; Alsalman et al. 2020). The leftover water is the source of 
aquafaba.  
==PAGE:18==

Chickpeas are processed by cooking, often in combination with the pretreatment of soaking. Studies 
have shown that soaking before cooking is essential both in domestic and industrial canning to elim-
inate anti-nutritional factors such as phytates contained in the seeds, to decrease cooking time 
(Yildirim, Öner, and Bayram 2013; Chigwedere et al. 2019; Wood and Harden 2006; Costa, Fusco, 
and Ao 2018) and for water uptake that will enable gelatinization during boiling (Costa, Fusco, and 
Ao 2018). Soaking generally increases the hydration level of legumes, which makes them soft and 
activates an endogenous enzyme to enhance further processing during heating/cooking. According 
Roy, Ghosh, and Kundagrami (2019), there is a reduction phytic acid when chickpea seeds are soaked 
at room temperature. After 6 h of soaking, there is a reduction of 16 and 24 h of soaking, there is a 
reduction of 18 %. Conventionally, pulses are soaked in an ambient temperature over a day or two. 
However, soaking can be performed under different conditions and temperatures. This has been in-
vestigated by Yildirim, Öner, and Bayram (2013) where chickpea was soaked at temperatures of 20-
97℃ for 20-540 min. At a temperature higher than 55 ℃, gelatinization occurs in pulses (Sayar, 
Turhan, and Köksel 2011) and there can be a significant amount of solid loss (carbohydrate, protein, 
starch) if the soaking is carried out above the gelatinization temperature (Frias et al. 2000). Addition-
ally, Sayar, Turhan, and Köksel (2011) reported solid loss increases with increasing temperature due 
to a higher rate of water uptake, which leads to non-uniform swelling of cell cotyledons, creating an 
imbalance of forces, thus giving rise to tension among local tissues of chickpea. This results in seed 
damage, and consequently leads to increased solid loss. At 20 ℃, solid loss of chickpea was reported 
to be 2.5 % for 900 min, while 10.2 % was lost when cooking at 100 ℃ for 60 min.  
The structural change during soaking is the hydration leading to swelling of chickpea, which is char-
acterized by weight gain and volume gain (Wood and Harden 2006) as shown in Figure 2.3.

after soaking for at 5℃ for 16 hours.

Soaking followed by heating is one of the most common methods used for modifying the physical, 
structural and functional properties of plant-based protein (Chigwedere et al. 2019). In addition to 
electrostatic, hydrogen and disulfide bonds, heat treatment at above the protein denaturation temper-
ature causes thermal mobility of the peptide chains that could lead to separation of the inter and 
intramolecular hydrophobic interactions (Akharume, Aluko, and Adedeji 2021).  The protein dena-
turation can be reversible or irreversible, where irreversible occurs due to extreme thermal heat treat-
ment (higher temperature) (Nikbakht Nasrabadi, Sedaghat Doost, and Mezzenga 2021). It is reported 
that this irreversible denaturation is accompanied by the loss of secondary and tertiary structure of 
the protein molecules (Sun-Waterhouse, Zhao, and Waterhouse 2014). Normally, when there is a 
permanent denaturation, the hydrophobic core of the protein is exposed and there occurs new aug-
mented crosslinking through hydrophobic interactions, hydrogen and disulfide bonding, reducing the 
functional properties of protein (Nikbakht Nasrabadi, Sedaghat Doost, and Mezzenga 2021). Thus, 
controlled heat application could be used to modify the structure of protein and subsequently to im-
prove the functional properties (Sun-Waterhouse, Zhao, and Waterhouse 2014). Some of the methods 
applied to structurally change the protein structure as reviewed by Nikbakht Nasrabadi, Sedaghat 
Doost, and Mezzenga (2021) are microwave heating, high pressure heating, and  ultrasound treatment. 
2.2.1.2 
Composition of Aquafaba 
Aquafaba does not contain any fats but is comprised of a mixture of carbohydrates, starch, and protein 
that leaches from the seed to the water during cooking (Shim et al. 2018; Mustafa and Reaney 2020).   
Table 2.2 shows the chemical composition of raw chickpea, cooked chickpea, and liquid aquafaba. 
Besides carbohydrates, starch, and protein, aquafaba also contains some minerals. Minerals in 
chickpea aquafaba include calcium, copper, sodium, magnesium, molybdenium, ash (Damian, Huo, 
and Serventi 2018).  
The protein content in canned aquafaba is amphiphilic consisting of hydrophilic and hydrophobic 
groups, which are mainly low molecular weight types (≤25 kDa) (Shim et al. 2018; Stantiall et al. 
2018).  Similar protein bands of canned aquafaba and high pressure treated aquafaba have been re-
ported by Buhl, Christensen, and Hammershøj (2019) and Alsalman and Ramaswamy (2021). The 
lower molecular weight proteins (10, 12, 15, 23 kDa) are known to be surface active and have good 
foaming properties (Buhl, Christensen, and Hammershøj 2019).

2.2.1.3 
Factors affecting techno-functional properties.  
The functional properties of food arise from proteins’ chemical composition, physical structure, and 
the way they interact with other food components and the processing environment (Radivojac et al. 
2013). Depending on kind of chickpea the functional properties of protein are affected by both intrin-
sic and extrinsic factors. The intrinsic factors include the content and disposition of amino acids,  
molecular size, shape conformation, net charge, and protein to protein interactions (Massoud 2020). 
The extrinsic factors are governed by preparation or the extraction conditions (Massoud 2020). This  
extraction condition is further affected by soaking treatment, seed to water ratio, pH, time, tempera-
ture, and pressure during cooking (Alsalman et al. 2020). The overview of factors affecting aquafaba 
quality and functionality is represented in Figure 2.4. During processing, proteins are known to get 
denatured and denaturation of protein may enhance functional properties such as gelling, emulsifica-
tion and foaming (Nikbakht Nasrabadi, Sedaghat Doost, and Mezzenga 2021).  
The techno-functional property of aquafaba proteins such as emulsification, gelling, and foaming is 
described in the ensuing sections.

2.3 
Techno-functional properties  
2.3.1 
Emulsification 
Emulsification is an important process in the manufacturing of formulated foods. Emulsions are a 
mixture of two immiscible liquids, where in one the liquids are dispersed and in the other liquids are 
in the form of small droplets (Lafarga et al. 2019). There are two types of emulsions, namely oil-in-
water (O/W) and water-in-oil (W/O). A system, where the oil droplets are dispersed in the aqueous 
phase is known as an oil-in-water emulsion, which consists of a creamy texture, such as mayonnaise. 
While an emulsion containing water droplets dispersed in an oil phase is called water-in-oil emulsion 
that consists of glassy texture such as butter (David Julian McClements 2007; Mustafa and Reaney 
2020).  
Emulsifying properties of protein are evaluated by emulsifying capacity (EC), emulsifying stability 
(ES) and emulsifying index (EAI) (C. H. Tang 2017). EC of protein is the maximum amount of oil 
that can be dispersed in an aqueous solution containing a specific amount of the emulsifier without 
breaking the emulsion. Emulsifying stability is the ability of an emulsion to withstand changes in its 
physicochemical properties over time (David Julian McClements 2007). Due to gravitational separa-
tion, coalescing, flocculation, creaming and Ostwald ripening and phase inversion, ES can become 
unstable (David Julian McClements 2007; C. H. Tang 2017). The instability mechanism of emulsion 
is represented in Figure 2.5.

Conformational characteristics (flexibility and adaptability of the polypeptide chain to the environ-

The interfacial tension in emulsions is remarkably reduced by the presence of protein in the oil-water

Several environmental factors, such as pH ionic strength and temperature could produce the structural 
modification (Wagner and Guéguen 1999). It has been reviewed by Lam and Nickerson (2013) that 
the partial denaturation of protein presents better EC, which is associated with increased exposure of 
hidden hydrophobic amino acids on the surface (Lam and Nickerson 2013). ES has been shown to 
increase with an increasing surface charge of the protein (Haque, Timilsena, and Adhikari 2016). 
Furthermore, by increasing protein concentration, ES can be enhanced by the formation a thicker film 
around emulsified droplets (Zayas 1997).

2.3.2 
Gelling 
Gelling properties are important in the food industry. Protein is the main gelling ingredient used 
(Boye, Zare, and Pletch 2010). The gelling capacity of a protein plays an important role in modifying 
the quality characteristics of food, especially structural and textural properties (Ikeda and Nishinari 
2001; Haque, Timilsena, and Adhikari 2016; Zayas 1997). Gels are semi-solid systems formed when 
a small amount of solids are dispersed in relatively large amounts of liquid which impart more solid 
like than liquid like behavior (Mustafa et al. 2018). The gelation phenomenon is responsible for sev-
eral properties of food, such as viscoelasticity, increased viscosity, adhesiveness, and improved wa-
ter-retention. These specific gel characteristics are due to the presence of a three-dimensional network 
of proteins (Massoud 2020). It has been reported that the gel formation is the result of hydrogen 
bonding, ionic and hydrophobic interactions, Vander Waals forces, and covalent disulfide bonding  
(Hammann and Schmid 2014). 
 According to previous researchers, gelling ability of legume flour depends on cultivar, genotype, and 
heat treatment. For instance, Kabuli chickpea flour formed gel at lower concentration than Desi chick-
pea flour (Thushan Sanjeewa et al. 2010; Kaur and Singh 2005). Gel formation is complicated and 
gel properties (color, cohesiveness, hardness and adhesiveness) are affected by the concentration of 
protein, water content, ionic strength, time, temperature, pH, and interaction with other components 
in the food system (Raikos, Campbell, and Euston 2007; Mustafa and Reaney 2020). Li et al.( 2012) 
reported that acid induced soy protein stabilized emulsion gel had a longer storage time and shorter 
gelation time. Increasing protein concentration, acidification temperature and oil volume fraction de-
creased the gelling time. Furthermore, Papalamprou et al. (2009) stated that protein isolates gelling 
properties from chickpea depended on the method of preparation, where the isolate obtained from 
ultrafiltration exhibited lower gelling concentrations.   
However, it is reported by Stantiall et al. (2018) that aquafaba forms weak gel in the presence of 
water-binding protein, water-soluble carbohydrates, insoluble fiber, and their interactions.

2.3.3 
Foam 
Foams are defined as a two-phase system in which gas bubbles are uniformly dispersed into a con-
tinuous liquid phase (Maldonado-Valderrama et al. 2007). Foam can also be defined as “thermody-
namically unstable colloidal systems in which gas is transiently maintained as a distinct dispersed 
phase in a liquid matrix"(Rodríguez Patino, Dolores Naranjo Delgado, and Linares Fernández 1995).  
In the industry, foams are the result of whipping or shaking (Zayas 1997).  Foams are created through 
unfolding and absorption of the protein at the air-water interface. They are also formed as a film 
formation around the air bubble. These foaming properties depend on the origin of  the proteins 
(Marinova et al. 2009).  Due to their unique mouth-feel and textural properties, foams have become 
a focus for food industry and culinary arts (Asghari et al. 2016).  
The two important features of foams are foam capacity (FC) and foam stability (FS). Foam capacity 
is the volume of foam produced after homogenization of  a certain amount of protein solution (Singhal 
et al. 2016). Foam stability is an indication of the film characteristics of interfacial film strength and 
viscoelastic properties (Bovšková and Míková 2011). FS is based on the measurement taken over 
time, including the rate of liquid drainage and the rate of reduction in foam volume because of bubble 
collapse, disproportionation, and coalescence (Muthukumaran, Ratti, and Raghavan 2008).   
Due to the bubble surface moving closer together, the drainage of liquid occurs which leads to bubble 
coalescence, foam collapse and loss of foam structure and texture (Murray and Ettelaie 2004). Typical 
foam collapse as function of time during and after formation is illustrated in Figure 2.6. Drainage due 
to gravity is one of the main destabilizing mechanism in the foam (Marinova et al. 2009). High vis-
cosity is required to avoid gravity deformation of the film and to hold the liquid within the lamella 
between air bubbles (Bovšková and Míková 2011). A bubble coalescence represents a process of 
thinning and rupturing of the thin films separating two adjoining bubbles. The main factors control-
ling FS against coalescences are the colloidal surface forces acting between the film surfaces, adsorp-
tion of surface molecules, and capillary pressure of the bubbles (Cascã Pereira et al. 2003). Another 
destabilizing mechanism is gas diffusion, where the gas passes from small to larger bubbles as a result 
of the differences in the internal pressures between bubbles (Damodaran 2005). Generally, net diffu-
sion change is greatest from smaller to larger bubbles, causing foam to contain more larger bubbles, 
which is called disproportionation (Marinova et al. 2009). This causes of the shrinkage of small bub-
bles, which eventually disappear, and leads to the formation of large bubbles (Müller-Fischer and 
Windhab 2005). As the number of large bubbles increases, the film between bubbles becomes thinner, 
leading to a decrease in foam volume and eventually foam collapse. (Damodaran 2005).

The foaming properties can vary from one sample to another depending upon the above-mentioned 
factors (such as time, temperature, protein concentration). Inconsistency in foaming properties of 
commercially canned aquafaba has been reported by a previous researcher (Shim et al. 2018), which 
could be attributed due to the differences in canning conditions and chickpea cultivars (He et al. 
2019). FC of aquafaba (297%) was reported to be comparably higher than egg white liquid (281%) 
(Shim et al. 2018; Mustafa et al. 2018). On the contrary,  Stantiall et al. (2018), observed significantly 
higher FC in egg white (400 %) compared to aquafaba (39-97%), which was prepared by heating 
chickpea at 100 ℃ for 90 min. Lafarge et al. (2019) reported that lowering the pH increased the FC 
and FS of chickpea boiled at 100℃ for 190 min. On the other hand, Buhl, Christensen, and 
Hammershøj (2019) demonstrated alteration in pH did not affect FC but did affect FS. In addition, 
chickpea to cooking water ratio, pressure, and time altered aquafaba composition and foaming 
properties (Alsalman et al. 2020), where FC varied from 40-120 % and FS% from 7-58% depending 
on the ratio and time of extraction.  
This contrary observation could arise from differences in aquafaba composition and whipping time 
and speed (Shim et al. 2018). According to the same authors’ heating chickpeas for different times 
and at different temperatures and pressures, changes the composition of aquafaba, and consequently

the foaming properties. Furthermore, the same authors reported that in order obtain to comparable 
foam capacity to egg, it is essential to whip aquafaba for 5 min. Heating for longer time may results 
in cell wall breakdown and transfer fat to aquafaba, which in turn could reduce foaming properties 
(Stantiall et al. 2018). A solution of protein aggregates can have different foaming properties. In most 
cases, protein aggregates can be obtained from heat, pressure, or pH treatments. Heating globular 
proteins can lead to different types of aggregates (long semi-flexible, flexible strands, dense spherical 
particles and fractional aggregates) (Nicolai and Durand 2013). The type and the size of the 
aggregates depend on the conditions such as pH, ionic force, protein concentration, and heating 
temperature (Nicolai and Durand 2013). Depending on the nature of the protein, solution conditions, 
size, and the structure of protein aggregates, foaming properties can be different. According to 
Rullier, Novales, and Axelos (2008), the foaming procperties were best with smaller aggregates (<70 
nm) than when compared non-aggreated protein and larger aggregate (> 120 nm) had the least 
foaming properties.  
To summarize, the foaming properties of proteins are affected by rheological properties (viscosity, 
shear resistance, film elasticity) and the magnitude of disjoining pressure between protein layers. 
(Khalid and Balla Elhardallou 2015). Furthermore, the foaming properties of protein are affected by 
intrinsic, environmental, and processing factors. The intrinsic factors include size, protein structure, 
hydrophobicity, surface potential, and charge. The environmental and processing factors are protein 
concentration, pH, temperature, and other additives (Narsimhan and Xiang 2018).  
As discussed above, although aquafaba is well researched to develop foam for food items such as 
meringues, eggless cakes, etc., accessing scientific data regarding the foaming properties of aquafaba 
is very scant. The majority of studies focused on comparing aquafaba with egg white properties (Buhl, 
Christensen, and Hammershøj 2019; Raikos, Hayes, and Ni 2020). Limited number studies showed 
variation in both chemical and techno-functional properties of commercially canned aquafaba (Shim 
et al. 2018), and some obtained aquafaba from chickpea under controlled conditions (temperature and 
time and ratio) and analyzed the composition and functionality (Stantiall et al. 2018). Few studies 
investigated the effect of pH, time, and chickpea to boiling water ratio (Buhl, Christensen, and 
Hammershøj 2019; Lafarga et al. 2019) on functionality. Others  studies optimized and studied the 
effect of pressure, time, and ratio on the composition and properties of aquafaba (Alsalman et al. 
2020). Although these studies have been conducted on aquafaba composition and techno-functional 
properties, there are still some gaps to be further investigated. The literature review highlighted that 
aquafaba composition and techno-functionality is affected by heating temperature, heating time and 
chickpea to boiling water ratio.

Nevertheless, there is no study that investigated the effect all the three parameters. Therefore, research 
may need to understand the effect of processing conditions on the composition and foaming property 
of aquafaba. Based on the literature review, it is hypothesized that processing conditions affect the 
composition and functional properties of aquafaba. 
Thus, the research question here under will be investigated in this project.

3.2.2 
Extraction of aquafaba 
Soaking was performed as described by Stantiall et al. (2018) with slight modification. Chickpeas 
were weighed (100 g), washed, and soaked in tap water 1:5 weight ratio (dry weight: water) in a 
beaker for 16 hours at 5 ℃ in a refrigerator. Then the chickpeas were drained using strainer, and 
soaked water was discarded. Chickpeas were then heated at different conditions (temperature, ratio, 
and time) (Table 3.2). Extractions of aquafaba were carried out by boiling chickpea in a pot with a 
lid on at 100℃ in an electric stove, and by heating at temperatures 60℃ and 80℃ in water bath.  
When boiling the total volume (chickpea plus water) was kept constant by adding boiling water.  
Boiling time was noted from the start of heating to the end of cooking. The boiling temperatures were 
monitored by inserting thermocouples into the boiling pot and was measured every minute with the 
help of Picolog 6® (Figure 8.6 Appendix 8.4). Once the required time was achieved, the heated/boiled 
chickpeas were allowed to cool at room temperature (22-25 ℃) for 120 min, and aquafaba and chick-
peas were separated by a plastic strainer. Figure 3.1. illustrates the flow diagram of the aquafaba 
extraction. Then, samples were then frozen at -20.5℃ in petri dishes in freezer (Vestofrost Solution, 
Denmark) until freeze- drying. Finally, the frozen samples were freeze dried at -39 to-41 ℃ in a 
freeze dryer (Heto Drywinner, Thermo Fisher Scientific, US). The samples were then weighed and 
stored in an air-tight container, sealed with paraffin at 5℃ in a refrigerator for further analysis.

3.3.7 
Foaming properties 
The foaming capacity and stability of aquafaba were measured as described in Lafarga et al.( 2019) 
and the procedure is represented in Figure 3.4. For this 0.8 g of aquafaba powder and 40 ml of de-
mineralized water was homogenized in a beaker (100 ml) on a stirrer for 15-20 minutes. Aquafaba 
foams were prepared using T-25 digital ULTRA-TURRAX homogenizer (IKA, Saufen, Germany). 
The solution was whipped at room temperature at 9400 rpm for about 1 min. Immediately, the foam

Fourier Transform Infrared (FTIR) spectrometry  
FTIR spectrometry was applied to access the conformation changes of aquafaba protein prepared 
using different processing conditions. FTIR provides information about the molecular structure, func-
tional group composition, and secondary structure of a protein (Kong and Yu 2007). Generally, the 
spectra consist of several bands arising from the vibration of different groups belonging to proteins, 
lipids, carbohydrates, nucleic acid (Wei, Jiao, and Xu 2015). Figure 4.6 illustrates five distinctive 
regions: 3600-3200 (amide A), 3200-3023 (amide B), 1700-1600 (amide I), 1600-1500 (amide II), 
and 1300-1200 cm-1 (amide III) (Kong and Yu 2007; Vanga et al. 2016). All samples present peaks 
at similar wavelengths, however, some differences in intensity was observed, indicating difference 
level of water absorption. (Alsalman and Ramaswamy 2021). The band around 3360-3390 cm-1 found 
in all spectra mainly corresponded to vibration associated with N-H stretching involved in hydrogen 
bonding (Sadhana 2014). A band 3069-2940 cm-1 is related to asymmetric and symmetric stretching 
of-CH2, as reported  by (De Pascale et al. 2019; Díaz et al. 2019). 
The amide I region (1700-1600 cm-1) is the most useful for the analysis of composition and structural 
changes in the secondary structure of the protein as 80 % of the peptide linkage accounts to this region 
(Vanga et al. 2016). Hence, amide I region is assigned to secondary structure of protein (Kong and 
Yu 2007). Amide I region consist of overlapping composite bands such as α-helix, β-sheet, β-turn 
and random coil (Mune and Sogi 2016; Kong and Yu 2007). Changes in the amide I region (1700-
1600 cm-1) of the samples were observed only for condition 1, 10, and 14, this region is mainly asso-
ciated with C=O stretching of peptide backbone and N-H bending (Ahmed et al. 2018). The absence 
of Amide I bands on the other samples could be due to a lower concentration of protein in the samples 
(Wang et al. 2014). The author observed the changes in bands of heat-treated (70-90 ℃) soybean 
protein isolate (protein content ranged from 2-5%). They found that the secondary structure sample 
with 5% protein concentration was similar to that of non-heat-treated soybean protein isolate, indi-
cating that heat-treated soy protein isolates remained in their original structure. In addition, the au-
thors reported that samples with 2% protein and 5% protein with the same heat treatment did not 
follow the same trend, indicating protein structure is related to the concentration of protein in a sam-
ple. In our conditions, 1, 10, 14 contained a higher percentage of protein (22-24 %) (Table 4.1), which 
is similar to raw chickpea protein content as indicated by the producer (Table 3.1). On the other hand, 
Amide I region in the rest of the condition could be overlapped with carbohydrates and other func-
tional groups present in chickpea aquafaba. This is because according to Mustafa and Reaney (2020), 
chickpea aquafaba not only consist protein but also carbohydrates and saponins, which were not in-
vestigated in this project work. Moreover, this could also be due overlapping of the random coil and

As illustrated in Figure 4.9, both Tg and Td increased as the extraction temperature increased. Our 
results follows increasing tendency of Tg and Td with previous results obtained by  Xu et al. (2017). 
Lower Td at lower temperature could be due to partial unfolding of protein that exposes the hydro-
phobic site to the surface, but higher temperature could lead to aggregation, which is more compact 
in structure with higher thermal stability, leading to higher Td (Molina, Papadopoulou, and Ledward 
2001). Lower Td values indicate, a lower degree of denaturation during boiling, and hence some parts 
of protein remained in their native state ( Xu et al. 2017). Due to the difference in processing temper-
ature, the degree of protein denaturation may also be different between the samples (Kweon, Slade, 
and Levine 2017). The authors reported complete denaturation of 7S and 11 S protein when treating 
native soy protein at 100 ℃ for 30 min, whereas only 7S protein was denatured completely at 80 ℃ 
on soy flour slurry at 50 % w/w.  Higher Td values were observed for protein obtained at the highest 
processing temperature (100℃), denoting the higher degree of protein denaturation that has taken 
place at a higher temperature and time (Kweon, Slade, and Levine 2017). 
Furthermore, the increase in Tg and Td due to temperature could be related to differences in protein 
content and starch structure of the samples (Kaur and Singh 2005). It was observed that protein con-
tent also increased with increasing temperature and time. Similar trends in results have been reported 
by Chung et al. (2008),  where Tg of a sample increased (70-72℃) when the protein content increased 
from 21-25 %. Similarly, in this project, protein content varied (⁓12-24 %) among the samples de-
pending upon the processing conditions and showed the influence of protein content on Tg and Td. 
Both Tg and Td increased with increasing protein content (Figure 4.10). Thus, variation in Tg among 
the samples maybe due to differences in the formation of disulfide bonds and protein conformation 
(Kim et al. 2004). Higher Td is attributed to the higher thermal stability of globular protein and can 
also be related to disruption of hydrogen bonds maintaining tertiary and quandary structure of a pro-
tein, specifically tertiary ones (Ladjal-Ettoumi et al. 2016). Therefore, suggesting a more compact 
structure of a polypeptide is associated with a higher Td of protein. Higher protein stability (Higher 
Td) could be indicative of higher legumin content since legumin is structurally more compact and 
therefore harder to thermally unfold than vicilin (Ladjal-Ettoumi et al. 2016). It could be that samples 
processed at higher temperatures consisted of a higher amount of non-polar protein residues, which 
could be the reason for higher Td (Kaur and Singh 2007). Heat stability of a protein is reported to be 
regulated by polar and non-polar residues of amino acids, and higher heat stability (higher Td) for 
protein is known to contain a higher proportion of non-polar residues (Kaur and Singh 2007). Fur-
thermore, higher Td could be an indication of conformations of protein aggregates after initial dena-
turation state ( Xu et al. 2017). A Higher Td value is also an indication of more stable protein since 
half of the protein has already been denatured (Alsalman et al. 2020). Alsalman et al. (2020), further

et al. 2013; Chung et al. 2008) and reported that both Tg and Td decreased as the moisture content of 
protein increased. According to Tang et al. (2021), the moisture content of the protein sample greatly 
affects the protein phase transition and thermostability.

Protein aggregates  produced at higher temperatures could have been larger in size, and could have 
resulted in loss of solubility, thus reducing the foaming properties of the protein in this work (Moro 
et al. 2011). Protein aggregate (disordered protein) having smaller molecule (size) have reported to 
have better affinity to the interface to reduce surface tension and have good foaming properties (Báez 
et al. 2013). Protein denaturation has been associated with structural changes, resulting in aggregation 
and reactive disulfide groups and these larger aggregates do not readily form an interfacial film and 
the surface load becomes smaller, and foam rigidity becomes lower, thus negatively affecting FC and 
FS (Bals and Kulozik 2003). Foaming properties of heat-generated protein particles might be corre-
lated to aggregates state, size, and partially unfolded structure, especially at a higher temperature. 
Larger aggregates that are likely to sedimentation are reported to act against foaming properties, pro-
ducing a steric hindrance that leads to an open and weaker interfacial film (Moro et al. 2011). Lee et 
al. (2019) reported aggregation increased with increasing temperature and time on alkaline extracted 
protein from yellow mealworm larva, which were heated at different temperature (55, 75 and 95℃) 
for 20, 40 and 60 min, respectively.

lowest at 95℃. Foaming properties of aquafaba have been researched by few researchers. Shim et al.

the differences in whipping speed applied while creating foam. Lafarga et al. (2019) applied 14000 
rpm, whereas only 9400 rpm was applied in this study. Higher the whipping speed, higher the foam

5. Summary 
In this chapter, the results obtained are summarized. The variables studied during the extraction of 
aquafaba were temperature (T), ratio ((CWR)) chickpea to water), and heating time (t). The factors 
measured in order to study the optimal preparation of aquafaba were yield, protein concentration, and 
foaming properties. 
All the variables (T, CWR, t) studied showed a significant effect on one or the other response (yield, 
protein, or foaming properties). The main effect indicated that CWR and time during heating had a 
greater effect on yield. CWR had a decreasing effect, while time had an increasing effect on yield. 
The lightness of the samples was negatively affected by temperature and ratio, meaning the higher 
the temperature and ratio during heating, the darker the samples were. Moreover, the two-way inter-
action of temperature and ratio showed a positive impact on the lightness of samples. The only T had 
the main effect on the redness (a*) of samples. The samples became redder with increasing tempera-
ture. Whereas the independent process variables did not affect the yellowness of the samples (b*). 
The results as shown by RSM, protein concentrations were significantly affected by T and t as well 
as the interaction of the variables (T*T, T*CWR, CWR*CWR, T*t). Increasing T, t, T*T, and T*t 
increased the protein content. On the other hand, increasing CWR*CWR and T*CWR decreased the 
protein content. Only the variable T had a significant effect on the moisture content of the samples, 
moisture content decreased as the T increased. Ash content was affected by both T and t, ash content 
decreased when T and t were increased. 
The effect of temperature was also visible on the protein profile (SDS-PAGE). Bands were similar 
for samples prepared at 80 and 100℃. Except the bands between 21-31 kDa (11S legumin) were 
missing on the samples treated at 80℃. Similarly, for the samples treated at 60℃, bands between 21-
31 kDa and 6-14.4 kDa (2S albumin) were not present. Furthermore, only bands of 7S vicilin were 
observed for the samples treated at 60 ℃ for a shorter time. For the samples that were extracted at 
the highest temperature (100℃) and with a longer processing time (165-240 min), bands between 6-
14.4 kDa (2S albumin) were not visible. Whereas these were visible on the samples that were treated 
at the same temperature (100℃) but for a shorter time (90 min).  
The modification in the structural changes of the protein of aquafaba samples were observed in FTIR, 
the main observations were at amide A, amide B, amide I, amide II, and amide III region. Temperature 
showed an appreciable change on the spectra of the samples, the amide I region was visible only on 
the samples that were treated at the highest temperature (100℃), which also had the highest protein 
content.  In general, β-sheet, β-turn, and β-strands were the predominant secondary structures in the

samples. 
DSC results showed that time and temperature had a significant effect on the thermal properties of 
the samples. Both Tg and Td increased with increasing temperature and time. These thermal properties 
can be correlated with the protein content and moisture content of the samples. Thermal properties 
(Tg, Td, and Tm) increased with increasing protein content, while it decreased with increasing moisture 
content of the samples.  
Temperature showed the highest effect on the foaming properties of aquafaba. The temperature had 
a parabolic effect on the foaming properties. FC% increased with  increasing temperatures until 80℃ 
and then declined until the temperature reached 100 ℃. Foaming properties are often related to the 
protein content of the samples. Even though protein content was highest for the samples obtained at 
100℃, foaming properties were lowest and unstable for those samples. It could mean that the protein 
in those samples was completely denatured or destroyed due to high temperature and prolonged pro-
cessing time.  
Overall, the yield was affected by the ratio, meaning that increasing the ratio decreased the yield of 
extraction. The temperature and time had a profound effect on both the composition and foaming 
capacity of aquafaba.

6. Conclusion and future perspective  
As aquafaba becomes increasingly central to consumers’ need for a sustainable food system due to 
environmental concerns, allergens reductions, and increasing proportion of the population becoming 
vegan, it is important to understand how to maximize aquafaba production. It is also essential to 
maximize the production parameters that affects aquafaba composition and functional properties. A 
more sustainable production could be achieved that would benefit manufacturers, consumer health, 
and environmental concerns by managing both the time and temperature required during production. 
In this study, the effect of processing parameters such as temperature, ratio, and time on composition 
and foaming properties of aquafaba have been in focus.   
By testing the effect of processing parameters, this study established that temperature, ratio, and time 
do indeed have a significant effect on the yield, composition, and foaming properties of aquafaba. 
The results obtained herein indicated that ratio (chickpea to cooking water) affected yield negatively, 
meaning it is beneficial to use a higher proportion of water to obtain a higher yield. It is advantageous 
to have heating performed at a lower temperature (80℃) than 100℃ for a shorter period. Although 
the highest FC% was obtained at the longest heating time (240 min), a similar and stable foam can 
be obtained at a lower processing time since a longer processing time is associated with higher cost 
and lower efficiency. The results obtained in this study predicted optimal heating temperature at 80 
°C with a heating time of 165 min at a ratio of 0.625. At these conditions, a better-quality protein is 
obtained, which assisted in producing high and stable foam. Mostly, the temperature had a profound 
effect on color, protein profile and functional properties. Overall, the project was carried out success-
fully and the project goals were achieved.  However, some areas may need to be further investigated 
to allow the research outcome to be more effectively commercialized. For instance, protein profile 
(SDS-PAGE) may be further analyzed or investigated to obtain exact molecular weight as well as 
other unknown bands present in the SDS-PAGE.  
Future research into aquafaba and its functionality should focus not only on foaming properties but 
also on emulsification and gelling. Furthermore, while the experiment measured foaming properties 
in terms of protein content, studies on other functional compounds such as carbohydrates and saponin 
are required to understand whether they contribute to the functional properties of aquafaba. It may 
also be interesting to investigate other methods of extraction, which requires shorter processing time 
such as ultrasound, ohmic heating and microwaving. An additional analysis regarding molecular size 
of protein such as SEC-MALS might give better insight of protein size. The size of protein aggregates 
may be investigated as well. Regarding the foaming properties, other analytical methods such as Tur-
biscan could be used that may provide more precise measurements.
