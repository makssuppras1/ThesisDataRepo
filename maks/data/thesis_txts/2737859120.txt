Abstract
The need for analyzing maritime traffic patterns for safety and security purposes is be­
coming increasingly critical. As a result, Vessel Traffic Service operators require effective
and context­aware tools to identify abnormal maritime behaviors. Existing models often
lack interpretability in their predictions and are applied mainly in areas with heavy traffic.
This study addresses these gaps compiling a dataset that includes trajectories from the
remote region of Svalbard. This area gained particular attention due to a disruption of crit­
ical infrastructure, which enabled the opportunity to validate our model using suspected
abnormal trajectories. Our methodology for maritime anomaly detection utilizes a two­
step clustering framework, which initially utilizes positional data (longitude and latitude)
and then focuses on an analysis of kinematic data (speed and course). This approach
focuses on kinematic behaviors corresponding to specific geographical locations, thereby
distinguishing between positional and kinematic features. This differentiation makes be­
havioral patterns more distinct, which helps in identifying clusters defined by kinematic
behaviors and offers context by providing the most similar behaviors to the predictions.
Several clustering methods and similarity measures for the trajectories were tested to
determine the most effective combinations for our scenario. The best performing setups
achieved relatively high silhouette scores for both positional and kinematic clustering, par­
ticularly within the cluster that included the incident. The best kinematic clustering config­
uration did not produce a large number of clusters, which somewhat limits explainability,
it identified effectively several suspected abnormalities from the incident. This outcome
showcases the model’s capability in adapting and functioning within the challenging re­
mote region of Svalbard, contributing to advancements in anomaly detection methods for
maritime surveillance

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Water has always been important to human civilization as it provides the foundation for
farming, sanitation and industrial production. Proximity to water has enabled the growth
of communities and the facilitation of trade of ideas. Maritime transport, which is more ef­
ficient and cost­effective than land transport, continues to play a crucial role in connecting
and sustaining the global economy. Today, maritime shipping accounts for over 90% of
the world’s trade by volume and around 70% by value [1]. Recent geopolitical tensions
in Europe have highlighted the vulnerability of critical infrastructure and the importance of
secure trade routes. Interruptions in the transport of essential goods such as food, fuel
and raw materials have caused crises and showed the need for reliable maritime chan­
nels [2]. Significant incidents like the sabotage of the Nord Stream gas pipelines in the
Baltic Sea and the disruption of the Svalbard Undersea Cable System in Norway validate
this need for improved maritime security measures. These incidents which are explored
in the documentary The Shadow War (Skyggekrigen) [3] show the susceptibility of critical
infrastructure to acts of sabotage and have directly inspired the focus of this thesis.
Maritime Surveillance Command and Control systems are essential for maintaining safe
navigation and ensuring marine traffic safety. Operators oversee large ocean areas and
are responsible for detecting and responding to potential threats such as collisions, piracy,
illegal fishing, smuggling, pollution and territorial violations. These systems depend on
human operators who utilize a large volume of data from sensors such as radar, sonar
and the Automatic Identification System (AIS), along with weather data. As the number
of data sources increases, operators face significant challenges including informational
overload, time pressure and fatigue which are all compounded by the complexity and
diversity of the data. Consequently, enhancing operator support with effective anomaly
detection techniques has become a critical matter within maritime surveillance and has
drawn significant research interest [4]
Human operators develop situation awareness from experience which is essential for re­
sponding to emergencies [5]. However, increasing automation brings a challenge, known
as the Automation Conundrum [6], where operators may over­rely on systems and make
biased decision. Automation should enhance and not replace human situation aware­
ness. Operators use models of typical maritime behavior to identify anomalies and au­
tomated systems help in this by clustering AIS data and analyzing trajectory similarities
[7]. Nonetheless, the definition of what makes ship trajectory abnormal brings additional
challenges. Distinguishing between normal and anomalous behaviors requires an un­
derstanding of the diverse operational contexts of different vessels, which makes harder
the development of universally applicable models [8]. Examples of abnormal trajectories
might include deviations from typical routes, unexpected stops or irregular speed pat­
terns which could indicate scenarios like smuggling or mechanical failures. This shows
the need for advanced anomaly detection techniques that are adaptable to varying mar­
itime conditions and behaviors.
In recent years, the availability of extensive AIS data has significantly advanced the field of
maritime anomaly detection. AIS provides comprehensive data on vessel identities, GPS

has allowed for detailed modeling of normal navigational behaviors and the identification

anomaly detection techniques.
1.2
Research Objectives & Contributions
In this thesis, we address the challenge of detecting abnormal maritime trajectories using
AIS data whic particularly focus on a remote challenging area with less traffic patterns on
a period where a suspected sabotage event occurred and resulted in several anomalous
ship behaviors. A main objective of this research is to explore the feasibility of utilizing
raw AIS data extracted from an open­source API in building a clustering model for identi­
fying these trajectories and then validate it using the data from the incident. Additionally,
we aim to research whether our method offers a comprehensive contextualization of de­
tected abnormalities compared to deep learning models. To succeed that, we assess our
approach’s ability to aid operators by presenting the closest normal behaviors as context
to the predictions. This would enhance maritime surveillance with a reliable and inter­
pretable tool for detecting abnormal maritime behaviors.
We adopted and adjusted a framework that employs a two­step clustering approach which
initially clusters ship trajectories based on the positional data of vessels and then refining
these clusters using their kinematic data of speed and course. To implement this, we
set up a data­fetching mechanism and preprocessed the data into a well­defined dataset
comprising around 10,000 trajectories. Our contributions to the field include presenting a
workflow for extracting and preprocessing raw AIS data into usable trajectories and apply­
ing a robust anomaly detection model. We experimented with various similarity measures
in the clustering steps to determine which measures best explain the detected anomalies.
Our findings suggest that this multi­step clustering approach can effectively disentangle
positional and kinematic information and provide a clear description of behavioral patterns
in a challenging ROI.
1.3
Structure of the thesis
The structure of the thesis is organized across seven chapters. Chapter 1 lays the ground­
work by discussing the motivation behind the research, along with the objectives and con­
tributions of this study. Chapter 2 shows the related work, presenting a literature review
relevant to the field of maritime anomaly detection with AIS data. In Chapter 3, there is
an introduction to AIS data, the process of data collection, preprocessing and the vali­
dation data from the suspected sabotage event in Svalbard. Chapter 4 introduces the
methodology employed in this study which including various clustering methods, similar­

the experiments that were conducted and the results obtained ans examines the efficacy
of different clustering methods and similarity measures for both positional and kinematic
clustering. Chapter 6 provides a general discussion on the experimental findings. Finally,
Chapter 7 concludes the thesis with key takeaways and suggests aspects for future work.

Literature Review
Maritime surveillance has undergone a significant transformation from traditional rule­
based anomaly detection methods towards more advanced data­driven approaches. This
shift is attributed to the availability of Automatic Identification System (AIS) data which has
enabled the development of advanced models capable of detecting abnormal maritime
behaviors with high accuracy and efficiency.
Historically, maritime anomaly detection relied on predefined rule systems to identify devi­
ations from expected behaviors. While these knowledge­based methods [10] provided in­
terpretable results, they struggled to adapt to the dynamic and complex nature of maritime
environments. The introduction of data­driven approaches with AIS data has advanced
anomaly detection by offering models that learn from large datasets to identify patterns
and deviations from normality without the need for specific rule definitions.
Building on this foundation, one significant advancement from data­driven approaches is
the application of deep learning methods which enhance further the capability to discern
patterns and anomalies in maritime behaviors. GeoTrackNet [11] is a groundbreaking ap­
proach for maritime anomaly detection that utilizes probabilistic neural networks to learn
complex vessel behaviors from AIS data. This method analyzes over 4.2 million AIS
messages to create a probabilistic representation of vessel movements. This enables
the model to identify abnormal behaviors in maritime traffic more accurately than tradi­
tional methods. The neural network captures heterogeneous patterns in vessel behavior,
while the a­contrario detection mechanism accounts for variations that depend on the lo­
cation. Experiments on real AIS datasets showcase GeoTrackNet’s ability to process data
streams in real­time which makes it a robust solution for operational maritime surveillance.
In a study by [12], a deep learning approach involving a Recurrent Variational Autoen­
coder (RVAE) was utilized to encode trajectories for reconstruction. These latent encod­
ings were then clustered using hierarchical DBSCAN which revealed groups that corre­
spond to primary shipping lanes. These clusters were used as training data for neural
networks which were then assigned with predicting future positions. On another front,
a graph­based contrastive trajectory model was developed. This method employed a
Graph Neural Network encoder which was trained with contrastive learning using five dis­
tinct trajectory augmentations. This setup enabled the calculation of trajectory similarities
by evaluating the distances between them in the latent space. The effectiveness of this
new similarity measure was confirmed through the use of downsampled random trajec­
tories from the training set as test cases and outperformed traditional trajectory distance
measures.
Another noteworthy advancement in the field of maritime anomaly detection using AIS
data is the development of the TrAISformer model [13]. This framework uses a modified
transformer network to predict vessel positions hours in the future. It does this by utilizing
a representation of AIS data and a loss function designed to handle the heterogeneity
and multimodality of maritime data. The TrAISformer model marks a significant change
from traditional approaches by extracting long­term correlations from AIS trajectories as
it outperforms state­of­the­art methods in trajectory prediction. This development shows
the potential of transformer networks in improving maritime surveillance systems.
Recently, an ensemble method that relies only on deep learning was developed. This
approach incorporates a Variational LSTM AutoEncoder and a Graph Variational AutoEn­

coder [14]. Each component of the ensemble was trained to reconstruct input trajectories
and their reconstruction errors were aggregated to yield a final binary abnormality pre­
diction. Meanwhile, a Variational Recurrent Neural Network (VRNN) for abnormality de­
tection through trajectory reconstructions was introduced [15]. This work also proposed
an A­Contrario detection approach to address regional discrepancies in reconstruction
accuracy. Despite the promising performance of VRNN, feedback from VTS operators
highlighted a significant drawback which was the lack of interpretability. This lack of inter­
pretability has been recognized as a crucial challenge in automated abnormal maritime
behavior detection using exclusively deep learning methods [16].
Various clustering algorithms have been applied to the analysis of trajectories. Techniques
like K­means [17] and K­medoids [18] have been used with different similarity measures.
The first one integrates k­medoids clustering with a Naïve Bayes classifier while the sec­
ond one utilize trajectory compression and alignment kernels which incorporates domain
knowledge to improve clustering, classification and outlier detection. Furthermore, a K­
Means clustering method was proposed to study ship behavior based on AIS data in the
inlet waters of the Port of Rotterdam [19]. It performed K­Means clustering of dynamic
AIS data (speed, position, etc.) to classify different ship behaviors. The results were then
processed through a Bayesian classification algorithm with static AIS data (ship’s length,
width, etc.) to estimate which ship behavior class the ship belongs to. The results showed
that there were six ship behavior classes with great variability in spatial distribution and
speed. It was also found that ship characteristics were closely related to behavior patterns
which provided valuable references for traffic management in port areas.
Despite this, density­based clustering techniques have remained the most effective in
maritime trajectory analysis. A notable method in this field is the TREAD method [20]
which clusters trajectories into traffic routes for anomaly detection and trajectory predic­
tion. TREAD is a point­based approach and it extracts coordinates of new entries, exits
and stops within a ROI. These points are clustered using DBSCAN to form waypoints that
indicate where ships enter, exit or stop within the ROI. Then when the trajectories are
formed, their similarities are then calculated using measures such as the Hausdorff dis­
tance and DTW before being clustered with DBSCAN. Another work based on AIS data
near Portuguese port waters [21], implemented trajectory compression and the DBSCAN
clustering algorithm to simplify ship trajectories and a ship trajectory probability model
based on vectorized ship trajectory was obtained for anomaly detection. Moreover, an­
other density based clustering approach was implemented by extracting the turning points
in AIS data in the Three Gorges Dam area and applied the DBSCAN algorithm to help
generate the optimal ship routes [22]. After comparing with the actual routes, it was found
that the method still has room for improvement in more complex areas.
Given that many of the aforementioned studies use deep learning methods which lack
interpretability, the two­step clustering method proposed by [23] presents a different per­
spective by incorporating both positional and kinematic data at different stages which en­
ables the discovery of distinct vessel behaviours and provides context by presenting the
most similar trajectories to the prediction of the model. It initiates the analysis by clustering
trajectories based on spatial coordinates of longitude and latitude. Subsequently, it adds
a more detailed layer focused on kinematic analysis which focuses on changes in speed
and direction. Similarity measures such as the Hausdorff, Average Haversine, DTW and
another measure proposed in this work tailored to kinematics are used along with DB­
SCAN and hierarchical clustering to discover the best combination for the specific ROI
around Zealand in Denmark. This method, which forms the core of our research, stands
out for its ability to uncover additional insights and provides a deeper comprehension of

3.1
Introduction to AIS
Automatic Identification System (AIS) is an autonomous tracking system that helps in the
safe navigation of ships and maritime traffic management. It does this by exchanging
data such as vessel identity, position, speed and course among nearby ships and VTS
stations. This is succeded by that using transponders on ships and is used by VTS. AIS
helps by exchanging data such as vessel identity, position, speed and course among
nearby ships and VTS stations. Developed by the International Maritime Organization
(IMO), AIS became mandatory equipment under the amendments to the Safety of Life at
Sea (SOLAS) Convention in order to enhance maritime safety by providing a means to
exchange navigational information automatically [24].
AIS data can be collected though onboard transponders that all sizeable maritime vessels
are required to carry. These devices broadcast information to terrestrial receivers along
coastlines to other ships and via satellites that capture global AIS data they expand their
coverage beyond the reach of terrestrial networks [25]. This data is typically available
through APIs by service providers who offer it either as a free service for basic tracking
or with a subscription for more advanced analytics.
AIS transponders transmit data which includes the Maritime Mobile Service Identity (MMSI),
vessel position via longitude and latitude, navigational status, speed over ground and
heading. The data is formatted using the NMEA 0183 standard which is a text­based pro­
tocol developed by the National Marine Electronics Association [26]. AIS data is broadcast
at various intervals depending on the vessel’s status and speed. For instance, a moving
ship typically broadcasts every few seconds to a few minutes.
3.2
Data Collection
The data collection for this study was based on the extraction the of maritime Automatic
Identification System (AIS) data. This data was sourced through a REST API from Kyst­
datahuset [27] which is a provider known for its maritime data services on Norwegian
waters. The specific API endpoint used allowed for the retrieval of data of vessels within
a predefined polygonal area, which included the area with the critical infrastructure around
Svalbard during the period of interest.
The data request was structured as follows:
• Endpoint and Headers: A RESTful API was accessed using the URL: https://kystdatahuset.
no/ws/api/ais/positions/within-geom-time. The request headers specified the data
type as JSON and received plain text responses for easier processing.
• Temporal and Spatial Parameters: The coordinates in the request define a polygon
around Svalbard, with date parameters spanning from July 1, 2021 to March 1,
2022. This period was chosen specifically to include the dates surrounding the
Svalbard incident which took place on January 7, 2022 and makes it highly relevant

3.3
Data Preprocessing
The preprocessing of the raw AIS data was an essential step in preparing for effective
anomaly detection in maritime trajectories. This section shows the methodology used to
transform raw AIS data into structured and analyzable trajectories.
3.3.1
Data Cleaning & Structuring
The data aggregation process was initiated by compiling daily vessel data from multiple
JSON file into a single data structure. Each JSON file was structured with columns as
specified by the API and provided the following maritime navigational data:
• mmsi: Maritime Mobile Service Identity, a unique number assigned to a vessel for
identification.
• date time utc: Timestamp of the AIS data record in Coordinated Universal Time.
• longitude: The east­west position of the vessel on Earth.
• latitude: The north­south position of the vessel on Earth.

==PAGE:17==
• course over ground: The direction in which a vessel is moving ground, given in
degrees.
• speed over ground: The speed at which the vessel is traveling oven ground, given
in knots.
• AIS message number: This field indicates the specific type of AIS message that is
being reported.
• calc_speed: The calculated speed of the vessel, which might be derived from
changes in position over time rather than reported directly by the vessel’s AIS equip­
ment.
• seconds to previous point: The amount of time in seconds that has elapsed be­
tween the current and the previous AIS data record for a given vessel.
• distance to previous point: The distance in knots between the current position of
the vessel and its position at the last recorded AIS message.
For the analysis, the dataset was refined to include only: mmsi, date time utc, longitude,
latitude, course and speed which were the attributes that were defined critical in this
analysis for understanding vessel behaviors in our framework.
3.3.2
Defining Trajectories
Segmenting Data into Trajectories
Initially, the data was divided into distinct vessel trajectories based on continuous AIS
message transmissions. If there was a gap that was exceeding 45 minutes between
consecutive transmissions for the same MMSI then it was considered the start of a new
trajectory. This approach is critical for analyzing movement patterns within the maritime
data.
Refining Trajectory Segments
Trajectories were refined by their duration:
• Minimum Duration: Trajectories shorter than 10 minutes were discarded as they
provide insufficient data for analysis.
• Maximum Duration: Trajectories longer than 12 hours were split into smaller seg­
ments to ensure that the data reflects consistent operational phases and trajectory
durations do not have a very high variance which would skew the data.
Data Resampling and Interpolation
Each trajectory was resampled at two­minute intervals to normalize the time series data.
Missing data points from resampling were interpolated based on nearby values to ensure
the trajectory’s continuity.

Vessel Type Identification
The raw AIS data retrieved from the API initially lacked specific vessel type information
which could be beneficial for detailed behavior analysis. To enrich the dataset with this es­
sential attribute a manual search was conducted for each MMSI number using the website
Marine Vessel Traffic [29], which provides detailed information about vessels, including
their type (e.g., tanker, cargo, fishing). This website was selected due to its extensive
database and reliability in offering updated vessel information.
From the analysis of AIS data during the period and area of interest, 206 unique ships were
identified. Among these, 20 distinct ship types were found. However, for some vessels
the specific type was not available and these were categorized as Other. To ensure more
clarity and enable the identification of more general patterns, these 20 ship types were
grouped further into six general categories based on their primary function:
• Commercial: Includes Tankers, Cargo ships and Supply ships.
• Fishing: Comprises all types of Fishing Vessels.
• Recreational: Yachts, Pleasure Crafts and Sailing Vessels.
• Passenger: Includes Passenger Ships and High­Speed Crafts (HSC).
• Service: Covers a range of vessels including Research Vessels, Patrol Vessels,
Military Operations ships, Pilot boats, Port tenders, Ice breakers, Lighthouse Ves­
sels, Pollution Control Vessels and Offshore Support Vessels.
• Other: Includes vessels that do not fit into the above categories, such as those
classified as Resolution 18 ships and other unspecified types.
This categorization helps in a more structured analysis that allows the assessment of
traffic patterns, operational behaviors and potential anomalies within each category more
effectively.

In the preprocessing phase of our study, we employed trajectory compression to improve
computational efficiency due to the extensive volume of AIS data. To achieve this, we
utilized the Douglas­Peucker algorithm [30], which is highly effective in reducing the num­
ber of points in a curve while maintaining its essential shape. This method was crucial for
managing the large datasets without losing significant geographic detail.
The parameter epsilon, which controls the algorithm’s sensitivity, was calibrated after sev­
eral experiments with various values. The aim was to determine the smallest epsilon that
still preserved the necessary information of the trajectories’ shapes. After several trials,
we selected an epsilon value of 0.0005 that gave an optimal balance between simplifying
the trajectories and retaining their critical geometric properties.

4
Methodology
To tackle the neglect of kinematic behaviour in previous clustering methods that were re­
viewed in the literature review, the abovementioned two­step approach involving separate
sequential clustering of trajectories based on both positions and kinematics is employed.
Different similarity measures are utilized for each step which enables a comprehensive
analysis of vessel movements. In this section, we begin by exploring the various similarity
measures for spatiotemporal trajectories and the clustering algorithms that were utilized
in our experimental setup. Subsequently, we introduce the abnormality detection model
utilized in our study.
4.1
Trajectory notation
An AIS trajectory, denoted by A, spans a duration TA. The trajectory A is defined as a
four­dimensional time series A = (a1, a2, . . . , aTA) where each element at in the series
represents the vessel’s state at time t. Specifically, each element at is a tuple that is con­
sisted of of four components: longitude (lont), latitude (latt), speed (speedt) and course
(courset). These components are extracted from the AIS messages transmitted by the
vessel. We can express the state of the vessel at any given time t using the notation
A(t) = at, where A(t) representes the tuple (lont, latt, speedt, courset).
In this section, we use as reference two AIS trajectories, A and B, with lengths TA and
TB respectively. We consider timestamps from the ranges t in {0, 1, 2, . . . , TA} and τ
in {0, 1, 2, . . . , TB}. We also assume that the trajectories are sampled regularly with no
missing data points. The distance function d can represent a generic distance in one­
dimensional or two­dimensional space depending on the application.

4.3
Clustering and Outlier Detection methods
The categorization of trajectories relies heavily on the choice of clustering algorithm once
the similarities have been measured. In this project two clustering algorithm are used in
our experiments, the agglomerative hierarchical clustering and the density based cluster­
ing algortithm DBSCAN which are briefly described below.
4.3.1
DBSCAN (Density­Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with
Noise)
DBSCAN is known for its effectiveness in identifying clusters of different shapes in spatial
data without needing to know the number of clusters in advance. It relies on two key
parameters: epsilon (ε) and minPts [35]. Epsilon defines the radius around each data
point within which nearby points are considered part of the same cluster. Larger epsilon
values result in larger clusters. MinPts sets the minimum number of points required within
the epsilon radius to form a dense cluster core. Points that do not meet these criteria are
labeled as noise or outliers. By adjusting these parameters appropriately, DBSCAN can
detect effectively clusters of various densities and sizes within the dataset [36].
4.3.2
Agglomerative Hierarchical Clustering
Agglomerative Hierarchical clustering was utilized as an additional clustering method.
This technique initially handles each data point as an individual cluster and merges the
nearest clusters until one cluster includes all data points. The merging process is deter­
mined by a linkage criterion such as such as single, average and complete linkage:
• Single linkage measures the shortest distance between points in different clusters
and often results in large clusters that are influenced by outliers.
• Average linkage computes the average distance between all pairs of points in two
clusters and promotes more balanced clusters
• Complete average considers the maximum distance between points in different
clusters and leads to compact spherical clusters.
The selection of the linkage criterion influences significantly the resulting clustering ar­
rangement [37]. Agglomerative hierarchical clustering doesn’t predefine the number of
clusters necessarily. For the identification of the optimal number of clusters based on

4.3.3
Kneedles algorithm
The Kneedle algorithm is used to identify the knee points in curves that represent system
behaviors [39]. These knee points show critical shifts in the behavior or characteristics of
the system and help in determining optimal parameters in various applications. The al­
gorithm functions by normalizing the curve of interest and calculating a differential curve
to find the precise location where there is a sharp increase in the rate of change. This
point, known as the knee, is crucial for optimizing performance parameters in automated
systems and makes the algorithm a significant tool in system analysis. The algorithm was
employed to determine the optimal linkage method in hierarchical clustering and hyper­
parameters of minimum samples and epsilon values in DBSCAN.
4.3.4
Silhouette score
The silhouette score is a metric that is used to assess the quality of clusters that is pro­
duced by various clustering algorithms. It shows the intracluster similarity and intercluster
separa tion. The intracluster similarity indicates how closely are related members within
the same cluster. Higher similarity increases the silhouette score. While, the intercluster
separation shows how distinct clusters are from one another. Higher separation similarity

A score close to +1 indicates high intracluster similarity and good intercluster separation

groupings and it is employed to define the optimal clustering mode in our experiments.
4.3.5
Local Outlier Factor (LOF)
Local Outlier Factor (LOF) was employed as an outlier detection method. The LOF is a
technique used to detect outliers by analyzing the local density variation of each data point
compared to its neighboring points. This approach enables the identification of outliers
as data points with lower local density. LOF differs from distance­based methods due
to its ability to detect outliers across regions with different data densities and complex
geometries [41]. The contamination parameter is a key aspect of the LOF as it specifies
the expected proportion of outliers in the dataset. Additionally, adjusting the neighbor­
hood size parameter offers flexibility in outlier detection and adaptability to the specific
characteristics of the dataset [42].
4.4
Two­step Clustering framework for Abnormality
Detection
In our approach for abnormality detection, a framework by [23] was adopted and adapted
in the ROI of this study. This method uses a two­step algorithm aimed at identifying
effectively anomalies in trajectory data.
The initial step involves grouping trajectories based on their overall positional behavior
within a Region of Interest (ROI). This preliminary clustering aims to categorize trajectories
broadly, while a subsequent phase focuses on distinctions in local kinematic behavior.
This algorithm aims to minimize the false identification of trajectories as outliers during
the initial clustering and reserve this classification mainly for the next step. The purpose
behind this is to provide VTS operators with a decision support tool that gives contextual
insights into trajectory anomalies and aides in informed decision­making.
Anomalous trajectories are identified based on either their similarity to other abnormal
trajectories or their deviation from the most similar non­abnormal trajectories. Importantly,
the characterization of vessel behavior incorporates not only spatial positions but also
speed and course information. Therefore, any similarity metric used must be suitable for
both spatial and temporal characteristics.
Our approach comprises three main stages:
Anomaly Detection for Maritime Traffic
19
==PAGE:27==
1. Positional Clustering
Initially, trajectories are clustered based on their positional attributes of latitude and lon­
gitude. The AH was chosen as a distance metric which ensured computational efficiency
since it has linear complexity and considers synchronized positions. So, the positional
distance matrix is calculated using the AH and then is utilized during training for assign­
ing trajectories positional clustering. The Hierarchical clustering with average linkage was
employed for assigning the trajectories into positional clusters which enables an easy ad­
justment of cluster numbers for outlier isolation. During inference, trajectory assignment to
clusters was determined using a K­Nearest Neighbors (KNN) classifier with k = 5 nearest
neighbors which was trained on the positional clustering.
Figure 4.2: Flowmap of the two­step clustering approach for training and inference [23]
2. Anomaly Detection
As there are not any labeled data, all historical trajectories are assumed to be normal.
Consequently, abnormality was defined as a deviation from the training set trajectories
within the assigned positional cluster. So for each positional clustering a kinematic dis­
tance matrix was computed using the Hausdorff distance as similarity measure for the
kinematic attributes of course and speed. Then a Local Outlier Factor (LOF) model was
trained on the kinematic clustering and was utilized for outlier detection. As positional
clustering already categorizes trajectories abnormality detection was based on variations
in kinematic attributes. The LOF model using a KNN with k = 5 nearest neighbors was
used for making the predictions based on behaviors of the local neighborhood. A rela­
tively low value for the contamination hyperparameter was recommended as it results in
a small proportion of outliers.
3. Kinematic clustering
This second step of clustering is based on kinematics and is performed on each posi­
tional cluster separately.
This step was used to provide explanatory context for LOF
predictions. This involved extracting similar historical trajectories from a precomputed
hierarchical kinematic clustering based on the Hausdorff distance. During inference, A
KNN classifier with k = 5 nearest neighbors trained on this kinematic clustering was used
to assign trajectories to clusters. Presenting the five most similar trajectories to the pre­
diction using the KNN enables the VTS operators to examine further if an outlier prediction
should be classified by themselves as an abnormality by comparing the behaviours of the
prediction and its closest normal trajectories.

6
Discussion
The findings of this work show the adaptability and effectiveness of the two­step clustering
approach in a maritime environment with variable traffic densities. Originating from a
methodology previously applied by [23] in the heavily trafficked and well­defined shipping
lanes around Zealand, this approach has been adapted to the more isolated and less
predictable waters of Svalbard. Despite Svalbard’s limited maritime activity, the study
demonstrates that meaningful clusters can still be achieved. This adaptation is significant
as it highlights the flexibility of the clustering methods to different traffic conditions which
is essential for broader application in maritime surveillance.
Kinematic clustering in these low­traffic areas presented fewer clusters compared to areas
with more regular and dense maritime traffic. This aspect of clustering is particularly useful
for explaining the detection of outliers in the two­step abnormality detection framework. In
Svalbard, where traffic patterns are less consistent and more influenced by environmental
factors rather than human activity, the clustering could still assign detected outliers or
abnormalities to specific kinematic clusters. However, given the result of fewer clusters,
explainability could be limited as fewer clusters could indicate less behavioral patterns
which the predicted abnormalities could be compared to. This capability is crucial as it
allows for a better interpretation of anomalous behaviors and aids in the identification and
analysis of potential unusual activities.
Notably, even the detection of a single anomaly among a series of trajectories in an critical
abnormal scenario can be pivotal in critical scenarios as it can justify alerts and prompt
further action to handle potential threats. In our case, the two­step abnormality detection
model at contamination level of 0.10 was able to identify sucessfully the first suspected
abnormality from the validation case and at 0.14 it detected two suspected abnormalities.
This shows that when 10% of all trajectories are identified as outliers, the model detects
successfully the one out of the nine trajectories used for validation. Although this might
seem significant, it’s important to note that not all these outliers are automatically consid­
ered abnormal. Instead, they are flagged for further investigation by VTS operators who
can explore the context of these predictions and make the final decision. Essentially, the
level of contamination represents a balance between how many cases are flagged and
the additional workload for the operators to handle.
The clustering methods showed adaptability in the challenging Arctic maritime environ­
ment. Despite the generally medium to low silhouette scores in the case of kinematic
clustering, which typically suggest less optimal clustering, the approach managed to find
distinct patterns and groupings. The challenging maritime environment of Svalbard, which
is characterized by its confined waters and limited traffic, presents several constraints that
are not found in more trafficked regions. Yet, the effective application of advanced clus­
tering techniques here offers critical insights into maritime behavior and showcased the
potential of these methods to enhance maritime safety and operational planning in simi­
larly challenging environments.

Conclusion & Future Work
7.1
Conclusions
In this study, we created an AIS traffic dataset to explore maritime anomaly detection
around the Svalbard archipelagos, consisting of approximately 10,000 defined prepro­
cessed trajectories. This dataset included trajectories of particular interest related to the
suspected sabotage event of critical infrastructure in Svalbard on January 7, 2022. Our
approach incorporated a two­step abnormality detection method where the initial step
utilized positional data (longitude and latitude) and the subsequent step used kinematic
data (speed and course). This dual­phase method gave a refined focus on the kinematic
behaviors expected at specific geographical positions which enabled the separation of po­
sitional and kinematic features. This separation improved the clarity of behavioral patterns
and leaded to the identification of clusters characterized by distinct kinematic behaviors.
These clusters are used in order to provide VTS operators with context and help them
make informed decisions regarding the acceptance or rejection of algorithmic predictions.
Our exploration into various clustering techniques revealed that agglomerative hierarchi­
cal clustering with the average Haversine similarity measure was particularly effective for
the positional clustering as it was shown by the high silhouette scores. These scores
are indicative of how well each object lies within its cluster suggesting a good degree of
separation from other clusters. For kinematic clustering, the main focus was on the clus­
ter related to the sabotage event, where DBSCAN with the Hausdorff similarity measure
showed satisfactory performance compared to other combinations which performed rela­
tively lower. These experimental results showed the adaptability of the clustering frame­
work in the challenging Arctic maritime environment. This method detected three out of
nine defined abnormal trajectories as outliers during training and a subsequent LOF anal­
ysis based on the kinematic distance matrix, which is used as an abnormality detector in
this method, identified successfully two out of nine trajectories as outliers at a contamina­
tion level of 0.14 and one out of nine at 0.10. This level of detection could be considered
sufficient, as even the identification of a single trajectory in a series of abnormal trajec­
tories can be pivotal in critical scenarios. Such detections can justify alerts and prompt
further actions to prevent potential threats and thereby enhance the overall security and
response strategies in maritime environments.
7.2
Future Work
Despite these successes, constraints remain due to the nature of the data which in some
cases does not allow for a large number of well­defined combinations, potentially restrict­
ing the explainability of anomaly detection. Future research could benefit from incorpo­
rating additional contextual factors like weather conditions, seasonal variations and less
frequent traffic patterns which are relevant in remote areas like the Svalbard archipela­
gos. Moreover, extending the analysis to include ship types may yield insights into ac­
tivity patterns as ships engaged in similar activities are likely to have similar behaviors
and be clustered together. Studying how ship type data can be integrated at the training
Anomaly Detection for Maritime Traffic

stage might improve further the detection capabilities of maritime anomaly detection sys­
tems. Such advancements could provide deeper insights and improve the robustness of
anomaly detection methods in maritime surveillance, particularly in challenging environ­
ments.
As maritime anomaly detection continues to evolve, it is clear that both the refinement
of existing methods and the incorporation of broader contextual data will be pivotal in
enhancing the robustness and applicability of these systems in critical unique maritime
environments like Svalbard. These advancements could not only improve anomaly de­
tection but also contribute to more proactive and informed decision­making in maritime
operations worldwide.
