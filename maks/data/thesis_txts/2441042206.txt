Abstract
ALMA is the largest radio telescope in the world and operates on wavelengths 0.32 - 3.6
mm. The light at these wavelengths is coming from dense clouds where the visible light
is blocked by dust grains and re-emitted in infared. In these clouds, the star formation
occurs. The James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) is the successor of Hubble Telescope,
will be operating at long-wavelength visible light to mid-infared (0.6 - 27 mm) and will
be launched in 2020. With JWST and ALMA, we will be detecting star-forming galaxies
at high-z. However, observations and interpretation of the observed ﬂuxes in the mm at
high-z, as probed by ALMA, could be aﬀected by the contribution from the CMB. In
particular, the far-IR ﬂuxes can be used to infer the dust mass of the galaxies that can
then be converted to gas mass( a key physical parameter that dictates the evolutionary
path of a galaxy). The detectability of the sub-mm ﬂuxes of high-z galaxies against
the CMB but also the true intrinsic dust heating from star formation rather than the
CMB is still an open question. This is what has been tested, by taking into account
the observed evolution of the dust temperature of the galaxies as a function of redshift
that has been shown in several recent works. As a result, it has been concluded that
for the sub-mm observations of high redshift galaxies the eﬀects of CMB must not be
disregarded.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Since the discovery of Cosmic Microwave Background(CMB) radiation which is the ﬁrst
light of the Universe and the theory of the Big Bang, many questions arise about the
Universe. Thanks to constantly evolution of our observational tools, bigger and high
resolution telescopes, eﬀorts have been made to achieve these two last decades, to reach
fainter sources back on the cosmic time. What happened to the Universe after Big Bang
and how we ended up to today’s complex Universe? How do the ﬁrst stars look like?
How do the ﬁrst galaxies look like?
The ﬁrst luminous objects is believed that appeared in the end of Dark Ages, denoting
a new era, Reionization era ( 6 < z < 15), in a Universe that has built up from smaller
objects to larger luminous objects to today’s structure(”Bottom-up” model). As a result,
the signiﬁcance of the understanding the star formation is very important in order
to understand how the galaxies formed and evolved. The stars are formed at dense
molecular clouds where dust and gas play primary role. The sub-mm emission from
these galaxies is produced by both the excitation of gas molecules(line emission) and
by dust grains(thermal emission) with the dust grains absorbing the UV radiation and
re-emit at infrared wavelengths. In the local Universe the temperature of the diﬀuse
ISM is about 20K. However, it has been observed an increase of dust temperature to ∼
40K at redshift z ∼5.
The key feature that includes the information of a galaxy’s structure is the Spectral
Energy Distribution (SED). The SED of a galaxy can give information about the age,
stellar mass, the metallicity, the Star Formation Rate(SFR) and the gas mass (Mgas) of
a galaxy. Combining all these information for various galaxies at diﬀerent redhsifts and
using the established scaling laws a well contraint picture of the cosmic star formation
history has been obtained up to redhsift z∼4.

With the launching of JWST which will be operating at 0.6 - 29 µm, detecting light
from UV to near mid IR and followed up observations with ALMA, detecting light at
the FIR and sub-mm regime, will be possible to reach these ﬁrst galaxies (z> 4) and
fully understand the assembly of Galaxies. Fitting of UV to mm SED of the galaxies,
can provide estimations for the physical properties of the galaxy.
However, at these redshifts(z> 4), the temperature of the CMB has also increased, reach-
ing or even exceeding the temperature of these galaxies, contributing to high redhsift
observations. The purpose of this thesis is to quantify the impact that CMB has on these
high redhsift star forming galaxies as a function of redshift, taking under consideration
the observed evolution of the dust temperature for various scenarios. As a result, for
the various assumptions that have been made for the evolution of dust temperature of
a galaxy the following work obtained. Firstly, it was needed to be checked after which
redshift the aﬀect of CMB in the dust temperature becomes signiﬁcant. From the con-
struction of the FIR and sub-mm SEDs, all the relevant information collected in order
to investigate the eﬀect of the CMB on the observed ﬂuxes in all the ALMA bands,
the detectability of the ﬂuxes against CMB background and provide corrections for the
monochromatic luminosities in common used atmospheric windows from the peak of the
SED and beyond. Based on the scenario that has been used, the eﬀect of the CMB in
the derivation of the SFR and Mgas has been quantiﬁed from sub-mm observations at
the peak of the SED (rest-frame 100 µm) and the R - J tail ( rest - frame > 250 µm).

Chapter 2
History of the Universe
Seconds after the Big Bang happened, the Universe inﬂates very rapidly and for these
ﬁrst 300.000 years old Universe is a hot soup of charged particles and bright and energetic
photons, but it’s all conﬁned and opaque and they can not escape. However, Universe is
cooling, is expanding and about 380.000 years after the Big Bang, is expanded and cooled
enough that all these charged particles, all these protons, and electrons start to combine
and form the very ﬁrst atoms.
The ’Recombination’ era starts.
At this point, the
Universe goes from completely opaque to completely transparent. The Universe is full
of neutral atoms (Figure 2.1). As a result, the boundary between the pre-recombination
era and the recombination era is the Cosmic Microwave Background. The reason that
CMB is the ﬁrst light to the Universe, it’s that at that point, photons are freed up. Before
that, everything is charged but the Universe is opaque and photons cannot escape, due
to the abundance of free electrons and protons, but as the universe cools and electrons
and protons form atoms, photons are then free to travel through the Universe towards
us. Those ﬁrst photons that we see, are the light to CMB radiation, that’s why the
patterns on the light denote how the Universe was like at that time.
So, clumps of dark matter start to form. The ﬁrst congregations of matter to form are
aspects of dark matter. While the luminous matter is subject to a lot of diﬀerent forces
that kind of resisting clumping together under gravity, dark matter has that kind of
behavior, clumping together under gravity, and these tiny condensations that we see on
the CMB are from these gravitational aggregations. They will start gathering into dark
matter and it’s only later on that neutral gas start being pulled in and become part of
the structures. Of course, this is not observable, the Universe is not luminous yet, but
from simulations how matter congregates onto gravity, whether there is dark matter or
luminous matter, one scenario most profound than others has obtained. The structure
of the Universe assembles in a hierarchical fashion. It’s called ”Bottom up”. As a result,

The Cosmic Microwave Background(CMB)
The Cosmic Background Radiation(CBR) which was freed during the epoch of recom-
bination (z ∼1300) is the ﬁrst light to the Universe which traveled almost undisturbed
until today.
The discovery of the CMB was the validation for the hot ”Big Bang”
model. It was ﬁrst detected from FIRAS(Far infrared Absolute Spectrometer) which
was an instrument on board of COBE. Scanning the sky COBE also made a map of the
temperature of the CMB radiation which was not quite uniform. These variations in the
temperature are so small, in a scale of microKelvin ∼10−5 K, which then lead to the fact
that these warmer regions are denser regions. The exact characterization of these tem-
perature variations obtained thanks to the better resolution of the CMB map of modern

temperature of ∼3 K (more speciﬁc 2.73 K) while the Rayleigh-Jeans tail of the spectrum
lies to the sub-mm regime (Figure 2.3).

galaxies along with it.

The era that stretches from the point of CMB to when the Universe is probably a few
hundred millions years old, is known as ’Dark Ages’. Although the Universe has moved
from opaque to transparent, still nothing can not be seen back in that time, because
it’s dark. It’s full of neutral gas. Here nothing is observable due to the fact that dark
matter starts to pull in the neutral gas and in somewhere at that stage that the ﬁrst
luminous objects will begin to form. So, hydrogen gas cools as dark matter ﬂuctuations
collapse to form ’minihalos’, in which the very ﬁrst generation of stars formed. There
is no expectation these ﬁrst stars to have any resemblance to the stars that exist in the
Universe today.
These ﬁrst stars are believed, that are more massive stars (hundreds of solar masses)
than today’s stars, due to the fact of the blob of the gas without heavy elements in(
any elements heavier than hydrogen and helium - metallicity).
So it’s very possible
when there is a slightly warmer interstellar medium that current galaxies don’t have,
and have the primordial composition, it can accumulate much more mass into a clump
before it switches on and it begins to shine. These hypothetical generations of stars
called Population III stars which are the very ﬁrst stars ever formed and they click on
the end of the Dark Ages.
Since the Population III stars are such massive stars, to counteract the pull of gravity,
they have to burn very brightly, burning all this material through nuclear fusion. So
the more mass there is there, the more gravity there is, the hotter the star has to burn,

2.3
Star formation
A galaxy is an assemblage of gas, dust, billions of stars and dark matter. Depending
on the number of stars and the kind of stars that they contain, can be classiﬁed as
metal-poor or metal-rich galaxies. Galaxies vary also in shape, color, and properties.
Depending on the type of the galaxy the star formation occurs in diﬀerent ways. The
two main ingredients for the stars to form are gas and dust. In spiral disks, which are
richer in dust and gas in the spirals, that’s where the star formation occurs.
The space between the stars is not empty. It’s full of matter, gas, atoms, ionized atoms
and solid particles of dust mixed together. There is a lot of hot gas, ionized plasma,
which is very diﬀused, not so dense, so even occupies a lot of volume there is not a lot of
mass on it. But, in that space, there are also cooler clouds, much denser because they
tend to carry a lot of mass.
The stars are formed in these dense, cold molecular clouds with dust converting the
atomic hydrogen to molecular (e.g. Wolﬁre et al. (1995)). Dust absorbs the ultravio-
let(UV) emission of the stars, re-emitting on in-fared, dropping the temperature of the
gas and allowing the formation of new stars. The size of these grains is proportional to
0.1 µ m and they are considered spherical, giving a blackbody spectrum.

As mentioned to previous section, the two of the main elements of the ISM are dust and

regime are possible. As a result, over the past years, indirect techniques have been ob-

Furthermore, the distributed grains of dust throughout the ISM can be heated from 15

weak. These grains are connected to the atomic hydrogen(HI) regions and the molecular
clouds(H2) and can be heated by both old and young stellar population. In between
there is also warm dust (25 K ≤Td ≤40 K) which is connected to the H ⨿regions and
it’s heated by powerful stellar objects such as O and B stars (Mathis (1990)).

One of the key tools that can express in a quantitative way the most essential properties
of galaxies is called the luminosity function.
The luminosity of the galaxies can be
considered as a measure of their stellar abundance or their present star formation activity
or even both. Thus, by connecting luminosity and abundances of galaxies, the luminosity
function yields information about the formation and the evolution of galaxies (Madau
& Dickinson (2014)).
Over the past twenty years, many deep ﬁeld surveys have been conducted trying to
understand the evolution of the galaxies. From a plethora of multi-wavelength data,
which contain ultraviolet (UV) to near-infrared (NIR) and mid-infrared to far-infrared
photometric surveys, radio surveys and Hα spectroscopic surveys, using ground and
space-based telescopes operating at diﬀerent frequencies many attempts have been made
to construct a picture of the cosmic star formation density. The most commonly used
and well-analyzed surveys are the HDF-N, HUDF, GOODS, and COSMOS.
Continuum measurements applying ultraviolet rest-frame wavelengths are the most com-
monly used since UV measures can be considered as a direct measure of star formation
rate of a galaxy. However, due to Earth’s atmosphere, such measurements ideally can
be obtained better from space telescopes especially at z<1 where shorter UV wave-
lengths can be detected, such as 0,15 µm and 0,28 µm, which are the most common
used atmospheric windows. It was around 1997 where combining measurements from
HDF (Hubble Deep Field) for z> 1 and from CFRS (Canada-France Redshift Surveys)
for z<1, it was ﬁrst recommended the ”rise and fall” description of the UV luminosity
density and that the cosmic star formation history show a maximum around z ∼1-2.
More analyses of GOODS data around 2005 showed that at higher redshifts (2< z <

5), there was a smooth increase in UV luminosity density which was evidence of the
existence of high-luminosity star-forming galaxies around z∼6.
For the measurements of the infrared luminosity(IR) density, many observational tools
have been used that operate in various wavelengths, such as ISO(90 µm and 170 µm),
Herschel(250 - 500 µm) and ground-based telescopes operating at 850 µm and 1.2 µm.
Locally, the IR luminosity is not a direct measurement of the star formation rate of a

while the cooler stars that dominate the stellar mass of the galaxy radiate mostly at
wavelengths between far - optical and NIR (Madau & Dickinson (2014)). These blue

at NIR wavelengths. Thus, the integrated stellar mass of a galaxy can be found by
observations at these wavelengths. Thanks to the continual evolution of the technology
and the higher sensitivity of NIR detectors, surveys by sampling galaxies by stellar mass

higher redshifts (z>4) deep observations are needed for the accurate derivation of stellar
masses from galaxies.

Spectral Energy Distribution
(SED)
The Universe is expanding. One consequence of this expansion is the idea of redshift, the
stretching of the wavelength of a photon. Every photon has a characteristic wavelength
and can travel from an astronomical source across the Universe but, if the space that
it’s traveling through is expanding, the photon itself gets stretched. As it gets stretched,
has longer wavelength and longer wavelengths are redder. As a result, the photon’s light
changes, from short wavelength( high energy) to longer wavelength(low energy) (Hwang
et al. (2010)).

There is a distribution of dust temperature in any galaxy, reﬂecting the diﬀerent nature
and properties of the diﬀerent type of grains. These information can be collected by
the Spectral Energy Distribution (SED) of a galaxy. There are 3 main components that
produce the FIR SED. As one can see from Figure (4.1), the short - wavelength mid - IR
range (∼3 - 20 µm) dust emission arises from complex molecules (Polycyclic Aromatic
Hydrocarbons, PAHs) and continuum,which is due to emission from both - single photon,

At each regime of the SED, the information that one can get varies. At the regime of UV

rate density (SFRD) for a ﬁxed stellar initial mass function in stellar populations that
are young.
While in old stellar population, the UV measure can be altered by the
evolved stellar types such as post - AGB stars. However, in practice dust attenuation is
generally heavy. In addition, the measurements at that region can provide information
about stellar masses, due to dependency at that regime on the mass - to - light ratio
of the diﬀerent stellar population. At longer wavelengths (Far - Infrared regime), which
corresponds to the peak of the SED, the information that one can extract is about the
temperature of the galaxy and the total luminosity. Last but not least, as mentioned
to the previous section, from the measurements at the sub-mm regime, one can convert
the dust mass to gas mass.

Star Formation Rate indicators across UV - IR wave-

The basic idea for a measurement of an SFR of an object is to predict the amount
of the luminous young stars that have a speciﬁc age. Be that as it may, the galaxies
are not spatially resolved at high redshifts (yet), especially for dust-enshrouded galaxies
(Katsianis et al. (2016)). As a result, SFR indicators, are simply either monochromatic
luminosity measures or measures of integrated luminosity over a range of wavelength
(Brown et al. (2012)) which are connected with a linear relation. The most common
methods to measure SFR are a) the conversion of IR and UV luminosities to SFRs and
b) SED ﬁtting to a range of wavelengths in order to get dust corrections and/or SFRs
(Katsianis et al. (2016)).
For the ﬁrst case, diﬀerent converters had to be adopted,
depending on the wavelength of the measurement. More speciﬁcally:
• UV luminosity: Measuring the UV luminosity, one can estimate the population of
the young O and B stars, which shine brighter in UV than at longer wavelengths.
Under the assumption that there is a linear scaling between the SFR and the
integrated continuum luminosity over a speciﬁc range of wavelength, one can easily
get the SFR of an object. Since UV luminosity comes mostly from PAHs, the
optimal integration limits are between 0,15 - 0,28 µm (Kennicutt (1998), Smit
et al. (2012)). So from these models the relation that converts the UV luminosity
to SFR obtained (Calzetti et al. (2012)) which is :
SFRUV [MJyr−1] = 1, 4 × 10−28LUV [ergss−1Hz−1]
(4.1)
where LUV is the galaxy’s UV luminosity.
• Hα luminosity: Young massive stars O,B ( ⩽15 MJ) are capable of ionize the
gas around them due to the fact of the large amount of UV photons that they
are forming. Hydrogen recombination has as result line emission with the Balmer
series lines Hα (0,6563 µm) and Hβ (0,4861 µm) be most profound, representing
the most popular SFR indicators. The relation that connects the LHα and the
SFR has obtained by Kennicutt (1998) from synthesis models and is given by:
SFRHα[MJyr−1] = 7, 9 × 10−42LHα[ergss−1]
(4.2)
where LHα is the Hα luminosity. Unfortunately, this SFR indicator is applicable for
low redshifts due to the fact that beyond the redshift z ∼0,4 the LHα luminosity
has been shifted to the infrared regime.

5.1
James Webb Space Telescope (JWST)
The James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) is the near - mid - IR successor of HST and
Spitzer. It’s a large (6,5 m) deployable space observatory that operates at wavelengths
0,6 - 28 µm and will be launched around 2020 into an orbit of the second Lagrange
point. It will carry four instruments which are a Near - infrared Camera (NIRCam) and
a multi-object Spectrograph(NIRSpec) covering the range from 5 < λ < 29 µm, while
a Tunable Filter Imager (TFI) will take coverage of the range of 0,6 < λ < 5 µm and
ﬁnally a Mid infrared Instrument (MIRI). (Gardner et al. (2006))
The scientiﬁc reasons that JWST has constructed can be categorized into four themes.
The purpose of the ﬁrst theme ’The End of Dark Ages: First Light and Reionization’ is to
ﬁnd any of these ﬁrst bright objects and resolve the Early Universe’s ionization history.
The second theme called ’ Assembly of Galaxies’ and having as a goal to examine how
a galaxy’s composition (dark matter, gas, mass, and stars) even its AGN, has changed
since the Reionization era until today (Windhorst et al. (2006)).

5.2
ALMA
Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array (ALMA) is a state of the art radio
telescope located on the Chajnantor Plateau of the Chilean Andes at an altitude of
5000m. ALMA is operating at the regime of millimeter and submillimeter wavelengths
(6 - 0,4 mm), with the transmitted light at these wavelengths coming from the extensive
cold clouds in the interstellar space and possibly by the ﬁrst forming galaxies in the
Universe. That’s why it can be used to en lightning us about the chemical and physical
conditions in these cold molecular clouds where new stars are formed. Although, these
regions of the Universe are totally obscured by dust, especially in visible light, in the
regime of mm and sub-mm their emission is very bright.
The location of ALMA in such an altitude has been selected due to the fact that the
mm and sub-mm wavelengths are strongly absorbed by water vapor from Earth’s atmo-
sphere.the atmosphere of the Earth. However, even under the driest weather conditions,
in the sub-mm regime the atmosphere is very opaque, but there are some natural atmo-
spheric windows that allow the detection at these wavelengths at 350 µm (860 GHz),
450 µm (670 GHz), 770 µm (390 GHz), 870 µm (345 GHz), 1,2 mm (250 GHz) and 2mm
(150 GHz) (Wootten et al. (2015)).
ALMA detects the emission that has been redshifted close and beyond of the peak of
the dust continuum emission. At that regime that ALMA focuses, the observations are
almost independent of redshift and especially for z ∼0,5 - 10. This is because of the
negative k - correction that in this regime counteracts the inverse square law and the
dimming of the galaxies. So except the sub-mm luminous objects, ALMA can point also
to fainter, more normal galaxies, like our Milky Way.
Along with the detection of the dust continuum, ALMA can detect both the cold and
the warm elements from distant galaxies, detecting line emission from molecular and
atomic gas. This cold gas tracer is where the new stars are born. In addition, detecting

5.2.1
ALMA’s characteristics
One reason that ALMA is the most powerful telescope in the world is that consists of
an array of 66 antennas. From these antennas, 54 of them have 12 m diameter while
the rest have 7 m diameter. The dish on each of these antennas operates like a mirror
does to an optical telescope. It’s a collector of the radiation that receives from distant
astronomical sources, gathering it to a detector which measures the radiation. Since
ALMA collects longer wavelengths than the optical ones, the dishes are covered with
metallic panels. These huge metallic dishes work as a nearly perfectly mild reﬂecting
surfaces which are focusing the sub-mm photons with extreme precision (Herter et al.
(2004)).
ALMA works as an interferometer, combining the signals from its array of antennas,
operating as one large telescope. There are two factors in which the resolution of a
telescope depends on, the wavelength in which the telescope operates and the diameter
of the dish of the telescope. As a result, there is bad resolution for long wavelengths
==PAGE:35==
The impact of the CMB on sub-mm observations
26
but with a large diameter of the dish, the resolution is getting better. For ALMA, the
resolution depends on the maximum distance that antennas can have between them and
not on the diameter of each dish. So, better resolution can be achieved placing the
antennas further apart.
The main array of ALMA consists of ﬁfty 12-m antennas, which can be placed in con-
ﬁgurations at distances from 150 m up to 16 km. That allows ALMA to work as a large
single telescope, the larger that has ever built, with resolution even better than the Hub-
ble Space Telescope, which operates at visible wavelengths. The remaining antennas are
used for diﬀerent reasons. The twelve smaller 7-m antennas can be located very close
to each other, allowing them to have a broader picture of the objects that they targeted
to observe. For the measurement of the absolute brightness of the observed objects, the
rest four 12-m antennas are used, due to the fact that an interferometer can not be used
for that reason.
Each antenna is supplied with an arsenal of detectors, which are extremely sensitive radio
receivers. Each of these receivers is sensitive to a speciﬁc ’band’ and until today only
eight receivers out of ten have been installed. The bands varying from band 3 which
corresponds to starting wavelength of 3.6 mm(84-116 GHz) and to band 10, which
corresponds to 0,3 mm (787 - 950 GHz).
In the near future band 2, which covers
wavelengths 3,3 - 4,5 mm(65 - 90 GHz) will be added while band 1 responsible for
covering wavelengths at 6 - 8,5 mm (35 - 50 GHz) is under construction. However, data
can be taken only by one band at a time.
As a result, while ALMA traces emission near and beyond the peak of the dust emission,
JWST will focus to near and mid infrared regime. Both telescopes will work comple-
mentary to each other, thus by connecting the dust continuum to the PAH emission, the
geometry of the regions where the stars are forming can be obtained and more speciﬁc
the ﬁlling element of H II regions.

Chapter 8
Conclusions
Now that ALMA can operate almost in full mode and with the launching of JWST
in 2020, the detectability of galaxies at high redshifts (z> 5) from the reionization era
(6< z < 15) will be possible. However, at higher redshifts, the temperature of the CMB
is increasing. The purpose of this thesis was to investigate how and what kind of impact
this extra heating of the CMB provides on these high redshift star-forming galaxies and
how it aﬀects their detectability in the sub-mm ﬂuxes and the corresponding estimates
of their SFR and Mgas. Several scenarios have been considered of the Tdust evolution
as a function of redshift, for these cases the eﬀect of the CMB is not that strong as in
the cases of ﬁxed Tdust at 18K and 40K. Especially for the case with the colder intrinsic
temperature (which is closer or smaller to the CMB temperature at any redshift, 18K),
the eﬀect of the extra heating and the eﬀect of the CMB as background is the strongest.
The contrast of the dust emission against the CMB declining dramatically as a function
of redshift and at redshift z=15, no detection will be available. Also, it’s the case where
the biggest correction is needed for the monochromatic luminosities, with the measure
of the monochromatic luminosity at 100 µm to be increased by a factor of 120. The
same applies to the measure of the gas mass, where correction is needed by a factor of
almost 7 at 250 µm at redshift z = 15.
For the case that the intrinsic dust temperature exceeds the CMB temperature at any
redshift the eﬀect of the CMB at high redshifts is almost negligible.
For all of the cases of the dust temperature that have been assumed, the eﬀect of the
CMB on high redshift observations is almost the same. It shifts the peak of the SED to
lower wavelengths giving a boost to the total infrared luminosity. The extra heating that
CMB provides at the predicted ﬂux densities, is stronger for the high-frequency bands.
However, the contrast of the dust emission against the CMB as background at most of
the cases is declining as a function of redshift for the lower ALMA bands. Moreover,

the monochromatic luminosity which corresponded to the peak of the SED( 100 µm),
appeared to need the bigger correction from all of the other measurements at the diﬀerent
atmospheric windows. Thus, CMB results in an overestimation of the true SFR of these
enshrouded galaxies. This overestimation of the SFRs at diﬀerent redshifts may aﬀect
the picture of the cosmic star formation history and in general, the understanding of
galaxy evolution. Mostly, the measurements at wavelengths closer to the peak of the
SED have shown the need for bigger correction for both the monochromatic luminosities
and the gas mass.
As a result, it has been concluded that the eﬀects of CMB on the sub-mm emission of the
early galaxies should not be neglected in the observing strategy for follow-up observations
of high redshift galaxies with ALMA. Also, the eﬀect of the CMB must be considered in
the interpretation of the observed ﬂuxes which lead to physical quantities like the SFR
and the Mgas. Finally, as a future step could be considered the implementation of the
eﬀects of CMB in SED ﬁtting codes.
