Abstract
The purpose of this project is to explore fracture mechanical fatigue estimations of structural compo-
nents. The desire is to give a better description of fatigue life to provide a more accurate prediction of
fatigue life for more complicated structures and load cases, such as variable loading. To do so three
diﬀerent fatigue crack growth models are considered, Paris, Walker and NASGRO. It has not been
possible to provide recommendations for a better way of predicting fatigue life. Some of the theory
behind fracture mechanical fatigue estimation is described and recommendations are made for further
work on this subject to ﬁnd an appropriate model to supplement traditional fatigue analysis based on
empirical data, giving a more accurate and eﬃcient way to predict fatigue life.

1
Introduction
In this project fracture mechanical fatigue estimation of structural components is explored. Fatigue
estimation is widely used in the industry today, where the lifetime of a structural component is found
using traditional fatigue analysis. In traditional fatigue analysis the prediction of fatigue life is based
on empirical observations. [1] For some structures and loading patterns this method only gives rough
estimates, which sometimes results either in structures being oversized or failing before predicted.
Because of this it is interesting to explore other approaches to predict lifetime. A possibility is fracture
mechanical fatigue estimation.
The aim is to construct theoretical SN-curves based on fracture
mechanics to supplement experimental SN-curves, and thus opening the possibility of making more
precise predictions of fatigue crack growth for more complex loading patterns.
A crack can have a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the strength of a component or structure. The smaller
initial crack will not cause failure but over many load cycles the crack will slowly propagate. Thus
it will become critical over time. The most common subcritical crack growth is when a component
or structure with an existing crack is exposed to fatigue. [2] Which can be described as fracture
mechanical fatigue. Fracture can be viewed as two processes, nucleation and growth. It is possible to
analyse crack growth without considering the nucleation of the crack. ([3] Section 2) In this project
crack growth is considered, but not nucleation. An initial crack width is assumed and the further crack
growth is considered.
To predict the lifetime of the structure using fracture mechanical fatigue estimations the critical crack
size is also necessary, since it describes the crack width at which the structure will fail. The critical
crack size is calculated from the applied stress and the critical stress factor, also described as fracture
toughness. A safety factor is used to determine the service life of the structure to allow for a margin
of error, since some uncertainties exists when using this method. ([3] Section 1) A common cause of
failure is negligence during design or fabrication, producing unexpected results. This also calls for
the use of a safety factor. ([3] Section 1) In this project the predicted lifetime is considered, not the
service life, hence no safety factor is considered. To predict the service life more knowledge about
appropriate safety factors in diﬀerent situations is needed.
The procedures for analyzing fracture mechanical fatigue with constant amplitude and small scale
yielding are already well established. [3] For fatigue crack growth the stress intensity is one of the
parameters used to describe the crack growth rate. ([3] Section 1) Thus this approach is used in this
project.
Existing theory will be explored in this project. Fatigue crack growth will be introduced. Diﬀerent
fatigue crack growth models are known and have diﬀerent properties.
The fatigue crack growth
theories, Paris law, Walker and NASGRO, are used throughout the project. SN-curves constructed in
Excel are used to underline the described theory behind all three models and compare the diﬀerent
models to each other. For simpliﬁcation a penny shaped crack in an inﬁnite plate is considered during
the project. The fatigue crack growth is described using all three models, illustrating the properties of
each model and the theory of fatigue crack growth.

The theoretical SN-curve based on Paris law is approximated to an existing experimental SN-curve, to
illustrate that it is possible to use fracture mechanical based fatigue analysis to predict lifetime. This
is the background for expanding the theory of fracture mechanical fatigue to make it possible to make
more precise predictions of lifetime for more complex structures.
For loading with variable amplitude and large scale plasticity some uncertainties regarding fatigue
crack growth remain. Prior loading history complicates the fatigue crack growth analyses considerably.
[3] Some existing variable loading theories are described, one of them being Palmgren-Miner. Load
interactions are sought to be described on the basis of Paris law using Excel.
This project does not give any deﬁnite guidelines to a fracture mechanical fatigue estimation of
structural components to replace the traditional fatigue analysis. It explains existing theory about
fatigue crack growth and illustrates some of the possibilities and challenges of using existing fatigue
crack growth models to describe fatigue crack growth for both constant and variable loading.

Fatigue Crack Growth
Despite being a relatively old subject, fatigue life prediction is still very much based on empirical
observations. It is diﬃcult to obtain theoretical expressions that explain the entire crack propagation
from initiation to failure. The rate of the crack propagation varies in time. Crack advance is larger for
increasing stress amplitudes and increasing crack length.[1]
From empirical observations the typical fatigue crack growth in metals can be described in a log-log
plot of da
dN vs. ∆K as shown in Figure 2.0.1.
Figure 2.0.1: Typical fatigue crack growth in metals ([3] Figure 10.2).
The typical fatigue crack growth in metals is divided into three regions as illustrated in Figure
2.0.1.
Region I: The curve approaches a vertical asymptote at the fatigue crack growth threshold. [4] At the
threshold stress intensity factor, ∆Kth, da
dN approaches zero and the fatigue crack will not grow below
this value.([3] Section 10.2)
Region II: At intermediate values of ∆K the curve is linear in the log-log plot. This region can be
described by Paris law, see Section 3.1. ([3] Section 10.2)
Region III: At high values of the stress intensity factor range the crack growth once again approaches
a vertical asymptote, for some materials. When R = 0, meaning zero to tension loading, the vertical
asymptote is the critical stress intensity factor. Cracks in this region will grow in a rapid and unstable
manner until fracture.[4]
The critical stress intensity factor, Kc, is a measure of fracture toughness. It describes a state in
which the crack grows in a rapid and unstable manner without an increase of load. Kc depends on the
3

material, strain rate, environment, temperature, thickness and to some extent the crack length. The
critical value usually represents the stress intensity factor at the last load cycle before fatigue fracture
occur.([2] Section 6.3)
Constant amplitude fatigue crack growth is mainly inﬂuenced by the environmental eﬀects such as
temperature and corrosion. Secondary to these, aspects such as frequency, wave shape, and thickness
also aﬀects the crack growth. The main inﬂuence of the thickness on the crack growth is in Region III,
due to the inverse relationship of the thickness and fracture toughness. High fracture toughness and
thus a thinner component is preferred since this allows a longer crack size before fracture. The largest
inﬂuence on fatigue crack growth comes from variable loading. ([2] Section 6.4) Three diﬀerent
fatigue crack growth laws are described in Section 3. They are usually used to describe fatigue crack
growth under constant loading.
2.1
R-ratio
As Figure 2.1.1 indicates an increasing R-ratio increases the crack growth rates in all regions of the
fatigue crack growth curve. However, the increase becomes larger and larger at the extremities. The
eﬀect of the R-ratio on fatigue crack growth strongly depends on the material. Thus the diﬀerences in
the curves shown in Figure 2.1.1 will diﬀer for some materials, for either one or more of the regions.
The main tendency will, however, be the same. [2] The dependency of mean stress eﬀects on fatigue
crack growth is relevant to consider when working with fracture mechanical fatigue estimates.

Fatigue Crack Growth Models
Three diﬀerent fatigue crack growth models, Paris, Walker and NASGRO, are described and com-
pared.
3.1
Paris law
Paris law is a power-law relationship for fatigue crack growth. Equation (3.1.1) describes Paris law as
it was formulated by Paris and Erdogan. [3]
da
dN = C(∆K)m
(3.1.1)
As seen in Equation (3.1.1) the fatigue crack growth depends on the material parameters C and m,
sometimes denoted as n, and the range of the stress intensity factor. It does not take mean stress
eﬀects, described by R-ratio, R = σmin
σmaks
, into account. ([3] Section 10.2) This is bound to aﬀect the

accuracy of Paris law depending on the stress levels. For R ratios diﬀerent from zero it is possible
to take the mean stress eﬀects into account by choosing an appropriate Paris constant, C. [4] Other
methods such as Walker and NASGRO also take mean stress eﬀects into account and these are used for
this purpose instead of using Paris law with a C-value that takes mean stress eﬀects into consideration.
Paris law assumes elastic similitude, meaning that the crack tip conditions can be described by a single
loading parameter, such as the stress intensity factor. [3] Thus it does not include history dependency.
Hence, it is only valid for constant stress intensity factor amplitude loading. ([3] Section 10.2)
C and m both depend on the stress range, the material and the environment.[5] m is the slope of
the linear part of the graph in Figure 2.0.1[4] and reﬂects mainly the dependency of crack closure
on ∆K.[5] Studies have shown that for most metals in a non-corrosive environment m has a value
between 2 and 4. ([3] section 10.2) When m = 4 the crack opening level increases linearly, but with
m = 2 it remains constant.[5]
Even today it is diﬃcult to derive an entire SN-curve from integration of crack propagation laws. This
is particularly because of the deviations from the Paris law in Region I and Region III. In Region I
short crack propagation occurs and in Region III the crack propagates fast up until ﬁnal failure. Paris
law can signiﬁcantly underestimate the rate of the crack growth of small cracks. The crack propagation
rates are signiﬁcantly larger for short cracks. In Region II the fatigue crack growth coincides quite well
with Paris law. In Region III the fracture mode becomes static and thus is governed by the maximum
stress intensity factor.[1]
Various extensions of the Paris law are made to include the R-ratio in Region I and Region III. However,
this is only done correctly for long cracks and thus it is still uncertain how to include the R-ratio for
short cracks satisfactory.[1] The assumptions of LEFM need to be fulﬁlled for Paris law to apply. One
of these are the assumptions of long cracks, thus a > a0 is required.
For high stress values the SN-curve created using the Paris law diverge from the expected linear curve
and instead the law seems to follow a horizontal asymptote. This is illustrated in Figure 3.1.1. For
larger and larger stress values larger and larger crack sizes are needed to reobtain the validity of LEFM
and thus the validity of Paris law.[1]
Today it is not common to use various crack growth approaches to predict the fatigue crack growth
in the industry. The two main reasons for this is that the initial crack size is often unknown, and the
data for the crack growth rate depending on stress intensity are more expensive to obtain. Another
uncertainty with using the fatigue laws are that due to them being empirical their constants often vary
more than expected. [1]

3.1.1.1
Initial crack width
When working with a hypothetical specimen the initial crack width
for the speciﬁc specimen is not known. Instead the initial crack size is assumed to be the minimum
detectable crack size using non-destructive testing. Only ultrasonic methods of detecting defects in
metals can measure crack’s through-wall position and size [7], hence this would be the method used

tool. However, some tools can detect cracks at a width of 20mm [8] and thus the assumed initial crack
width is, a0 = 10mm, since a denotes half of the total crack width. This will give some uncertainty,
since for lower stress ranges the propagation will initiate at larger crack sizes than at larger stress
ranges. [1] This is, however, an uncertainty that is neglected for this purpose.

6
Variable Loading
In real life structures the applied loading is usually of variable amplitude. ([2] Chapter 9) For variable
loading the crack growth may depend on the loading history. Analysis that take prior loading history
into account are far more complicated than analysis which assume similitude. [3] Similitude means
that crack tip conditions can be described by a single loading parameter, such as a stress intensity
factor,[3] which has been the case for the analysis of constant loading in this project. If a specimen
subjected to variable loading is dependent on the loading history, the crack growth can no longer be
described by a single loading parameter and similitude can no longer be assumed. ([3] Section 10.1)
The sequence of the diﬀerent loading values and number of cycles at each load value may inﬂuence
the crack growth. Fatigue crack growth life estimations for variable loading can take sequence eﬀects
into considerations or not.
42
==PAGE:48==
Fracture Mechanical Fatigue Estimation of Structural Components Stine Hald Christiansen, S134802
If it is a simple loading pattern with repeated applications or where peak loads have a small
eﬀect on the crack growth sequence loads can be neglected and a more simple model can be used
to approximate the fatigue life. [2] However, in many case history load and sequence eﬀects has a
signiﬁcant eﬀect on the fatigue crack growth and should be taken into consideration. In some cases a
more complex loading history can be altered to give a repeated application of a multiple block loading
sequence. This approximates the eﬀect of the sequence eﬀects, while making the analysis simpler.
In many cases computer software is necessary to make a reliable estimation of fatigue life for variable
loading taking history loads and sequence eﬀects into account.
6.1
Existing Variable Loading Theories
Diﬀerent methods exists to estimate the lifetime of a structure under variable loading. Some methods
are simple, some are complex. Which method to use depend on the circumstances for the speciﬁc
case. [2] Some of them are described in this section.
6.1.1
Damage Theories
One approach is cumulative damage theories such as the Palmgren-Miner rule. Cumulative damage
refers to the summation of fatigue eﬀects of diﬀerent load events. Load events that does not cause
crack growth are considered to cause zero damage and is thus omitted in the accumulation of damage.
The most common measure of damage is life fraction. ([2] Chapter 9) The most simple model of
damage accumulation is to compute the crack growth for each load cycle and accumulate the crack
growth pr. cycle until the critical crack growth is reached. [2] For some linear damage rule, the
damage at each diﬀerent load value is computed and accumulated. One such linear damage rule is
Palmgren-Miner. It is the simplest linear damage rule and is used frequently. [2] Palmgren-Miner is
expressed by the following relation [2]:

6.1.2
Load Interaction and Sequence Eﬀects
Sequence eﬀects aﬀects both the nucleation and growth of microcracks and the macrocrack growth.
Yielding resulting in residual stresses and crack closure near the crack tip are the main reasons for
sequence eﬀects. ([2] Chapter 9)
If a specimen is subjected to a few very high loads and many small loads, the eﬀect of the high loads
partially depend on whether the high load cycles ends at a tension peak or at compression. Due to the
residual stresses it will have a beneﬁcial eﬀect on the fatigue life, if the high loads end at a tension
peak and a harmful eﬀect if they end in compression. The tensile load can cause a plastic zone at the
crack tip, forming compressive residual stresses, even at small load values. This will slow down the
crack growth. A compressive load must be of greater magnitude to produce yielding, since these do
not open the crack. They result in tensile residual stresses, which accelerate the crack growth. ([2]
Chapter 9)
This underlines that sequence eﬀects does not only depend on the number of cycles, but on the details
of the load history. Such details are the tensile or compressive state of the loads when they change
magnitude. It can also depend on the interval between the load events of diﬀerent magnitude. It
is possible to make a more precise prediction of lifetime if the details of the load history is known.
However, in many cases the sequence eﬀects cancel each other out or are unpredictable. When the
sequence eﬀects are unpredictable the detailed analysis of fatigue lifetime is no longer reliable and
gives a false illusion of the accuracy of the analysis. ([2] Chapter 9) In these cases it is better to use
the simple methods and a conservative safety factor.
6.1.3
Calculation of Fatigue Crack Growth by Integration
A diﬀerent approach to estimation of fatigue life at variable loading is calculation of fatigue crack
growth by integration. The loading is considered as a periodic loading sequence. The time interval
for each period is considered to be small compared to the entire lifetime. The increments of crack
growth due to each cycle in the loading function for the loading sequence are computed for a small
number of crack widths. Based on this data a curve ﬁtting technique is used to compute function for
the crack growth per period as a function of crack length. The reciprocal of this function is integrated
to ﬁnd the lifetime. The method of summing the crack growth cycle by cycle, as mentioned earlier,
can be very time consuming. By integrating the crack growth functions the computational time can be
reduced signiﬁcantly. [15] The details of this method is not elaborated here, but would be relevant for
further work on fracture mechanical fatigue estimation for variable loading, since it shows indications
of being very eﬃcient.

7
Further Work
Some of the background for fracture mechanical fatigue estimation has been explained and some
preliminary exercises has been performed to investigate the possibilities of using fracture mechanics
when describing fatigue life. However, no recommendations has been made for a fracture mechanical
fatigue estimation to supplement or replace the traditional fatigue life prediction. In this section some
suggestions are made to further work in this subject. It has been shown that it is possible to adjust an
SN-curve based on Paris law to an experimental curve. This does, however, include some limitations.
Because of the behavior of Paris law, it only correlate with the experimental SN-curves for smaller
load ranges, as shown in Section 5. It would be relevant to approximate an SN-curve based on one
of the other fatigue crack growth models to the experimental curve. Especially NASGRO, that better
describes the fatigue crack growth at small and large stress intensity factors. Maybe this would give a
more precise and useful approximation.

For variable loading Paris law is not suitable to describe fatigue crack growth, both according to the
literature and the performed exercises in this project. It would be interesting to investigate if NASGRO
takes sequence eﬀects into consideration, and hence would give a more accurate prediction of fatigue
lifetime for variable loading.
It would be relevant to go more into details with existing theory about fatigue crack growth estimations
for variable loading. Especially calculation of fatigue crack growth by integration shows great promise.
The method is already developed, and it would be interesting to look more into this approach, since it
gives indications of being suitable and eﬃcient to describe fatigue crack growth for variable loading.
Hopefully some of the ideas presented in this section would give a better description of a fracture
mechanical fatigue estimation to supplement traditional fatigue analysis in order to give a more precise
prediction of fatigue life for complex structures and loading cases.
8
Conclusion
Because of the limitations of fatigue life predictions based on empirical data the possibility of using
fracture mechanics to estimate fatigue life has been in explored. The background of fracture mechanical
fatigue estimation has been explained. The focus has been on three fatigue crack growth models, Paris,
Walker and NASGRO. These have been described and compared. Paris law is the most simple of the
three models. It does not take mean stress eﬀects into account, which Walker does. NASGRO is the
only of the three models that describes the crack growth for small and large stress intensity factors and
is thus more precise than Paris and Walker. It is also far more complicated, making the usage of this
model more complicated than i.e. the simple Paris law.
Some preliminary exercises have been performed to investigate the possibilities of fracture mechanical
fatigue estimations. One of them is the approximation of a theoretical SN-curve based on Paris law
to an experimental SN-curve from the recommended practice DNVGL-RP-C203. The knowledge of
the Paris parameters derived from the investigation of the inﬂuence of the parameters on Paris law is
used to make qualiﬁed adjustments to said parameters when approximating to the experimental curve.
It shows that it is possible to correlate part of the SN-curve based on Paris law to the experimental
SN-curve. It indicates, that while Paris law can partly describe the fatigue life, it has some obvious
limitations. It would be better to use a diﬀerent approach to formulate a theoretical SN-curve.
Variable amplitude loading is more realistic for real life structures since these are exposed to wind and
other variable and sometimes unpredictable loads. For variable loading the crack growth sometimes
depend on the loading history. Analysis that takes loading history into consideration are far more
complicated. Thus the loading case is simpliﬁed whenever possible. It can be when the load sequence
can be simpliﬁed as a repetition of shorter load sequences. This simpliﬁes the fatigue life estimation
and is preferred. Another possibility is when sequence eﬀects cancel each other out, making it possible
to neglect them to provide a more simple analysis.

One of the simplest models to describe the fatigue life for variable loading is the damage tolerance
theory, Palmgren-Miner. Because of it’s simplicity it is used frequently in the industry despite of it’s
limitations. For instance Palmgren-Miner does not account for sequence eﬀects in any way.
It is shown that Paris law is not suitable for predicting sequence eﬀects, since it does not take the load
sequence into account. Paris law is used to illustrate the SN-curve for a loading pattern with peak
loads. It shows that the threshold load value depends on the number of load cycles, however, it does
not give an accurate description of how it depends on the number of load cycles. It would be relevant
to explore if another fatigue crack growth model such as NASGRO would be suitable to describe the
load sequence eﬀects and give a better SN-curve and fatigue life prediction.
This project does not give any deﬁnite guidelines to a fracture mechanical fatigue estimation of
structural components to replace the traditional fatigue analysis. Some preliminary exercises are
performed to give an understanding of fracture mechanical fatigue estimations and the possibilities
and challenges for some of the existing theory. It is relevant to expand these analysis to include
other fatigue crack growth models. The literature shows that calculation of fatigue crack growth by
integration gives an accurate and eﬃcient estimation of fatigue crack growth for variable loading.
Thus this method is of special interest in the pursuit for a fracture mechanical fatigue estimation
of structural components to supplement the most popular approach, fatigue life prediction based on
empirical data.
