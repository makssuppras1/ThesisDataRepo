Summary
This thesis focuses on the reaction of wooden structures to a moving
fire. There has been a significant increase in our knowledge of how
structures react in a fire over the years. Our current knowledge and
design recommendations are predicated on the localised assumption
that fire conditions within a room are uniform.
However, it has
become evident that fires can spread rapidly in large open spaces,
necessitating a new approach to band safety.
Existing building codes account for the spread of fire, but not neces-
sarily the structure’s response. Traditional methodologies and mod-
els assume that the room’s temperature and combustion rates are
uniform. Contrary to the observation that fire in vast open areas
tends to spread over the floor slabs and confine the area, this is the
opposite. Therefore, it is necessary to investigate the fundamen-
tal concept of temperature homogeneity in flames and to reevaluate
previous assumptions.
In this dissertation, the authors use current experimental data from
fire experiments on large and small sections of timber structures
to investigate and identify factors that influence structural capacity
under moving fires. A new perspective on fire behaviour has been
proposed based on the findings of real-world fires and exploratory
experiments, which have refuted previously held beliefs.In addition,
research concentrates on the characterization of moving fire in wood
structures and its impact on the load-bearing capacity of structures
during the burning and decay phases, when they are not exposed
to fire. To investigate the responses of the timber structure to a
moving fire, a case study will be conducted on an open portion of a
building that may contain a fire that is spreading.
The purpose of the thesis is to contribute to our comprehension
of spreading fires in wooden structures and to identify factors that
affect structural strength. A literature review and explanation of the
methodology behind moving fires and their relationship to timber
structures will also be included.

Abstract
In the past decades, there has been a substantial development in
our understanding of how a structure acts in a fire. These rules
dictate how a building responds to the flames.
This increase in
understanding is mostly the result of unintentional fires as well as
full-scale experimentsconducted on a variety of compartments, both
of which gave us the opportunity to investigate numerous fire scenar-
ios. On the other hand, the vast majority of our current knowledge
and design recommendations stem from the assumption that the fire
conditions within a compartment are consistent with one another.
It has become abundantly evident that flames in bigger open-plan
compartments can spread rapidly, and while this strategy may be
beneficial for smaller compartments, it is not appropriate for larger
compartments. The collapse of the World Trade Center Towers in
2001, the fire that broke out in the Windsor Tower in Madrid in
2006, and the latest fire that broke out in the Plasco building in
Tehran in January 2017 are three prominent examples of this phe-
nomenon.
The existing regulations for buildings do take into account how
quickly fires may spread but not for the structure. Experiments con-
ducted in a small rooms constitute the basis for the time-temperature
curves that serve as the standard and parametric models. These
curves imply that the temperature and combustion rates are dis-
tributed uniformly throughout a compartment.
In addition, the
usual methodologies for thermal inputs in structural fire analysis
in buildings are based on the presumption of homogeneous burning
and temperature conditions, with no consideration given to the size
of the compartment in which the fire is occurring. This goes against
what has been observed, which is that accidental fires in large open-
plan compartments have a tendency to spread over the floor plates,
thereby limiting the area of confinement at any given time.
Using current experimental data from well-instrumented fire tests
==PAGE:9==
vii
on large and small sections of wood structures, this study examines
the basic concept of homogeneous temperature conditions in fires.
Real-world findings from unintentional fires and exploratory testing
refuted the old assumptions and proposed that a new perspective
on fire behavior may be implemented instead.
In order to comprehend structural response to non-uniform fire, re-
searchers have undertaken extensive studies on timber structural re-
action in large-scale compartment fire tests in recent years. A new
design approach for non-uniform fire in structures has been created
to account for the moving nature of fires in large compartments. In
contrast to the usual fire design scenario in a confined compartment,
moving fires are characterized by a flame front that gradually travels
over the enclosure, resulting in zones of high and low temperatures
at different periods and locations.
The goal of this thesis is to explore the characterization of travelling
fires for timber structural design by identifying the factors that im-
pact their structural capacity. The thesis also aims to analyze the
impact of a moving fire on the load-bearing ability of a structure
during the burning and decay phases of a fire, as measured by the
thermal penetration depth. Furthermore, it will involve a literature
review on the thesis topic and an explanation of the methodology
behind traveling fires, as well as its general framework and analytical
information pertaining to the relationship between traveling fire and
timber construction. To support these objectives, a case study will
be conducted on the open portion compartment of a building that
may contain a spreading fire, and the reaction of a timber structure
to the travelling fire will be examined.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This chapter introduces the overview and purpose of the thesis, as
well as the advantages and disadvantages of using wood as a build-
ing material and the potential fire hazards. Even though timber is
good for the environment, it is also flammable, so fire safety regula-
tions must be observed. Due to the limited comprehension of the fire
response of engineered wood, incorporating wood-based structures
into building design poses a challenge.
Traditional design meth-
ods presume that fires spread uniformly, but in reality, fires do not
spread uniformly.
1.1
Overview
Wood has been a popular building material for many years because it
has so many benefits. These characteristics include renewability, re-
cyclability, carbon-absorbing capacity, waste-free composition, and
energy efficiency. Due to its advantageous characteristics, academics
have advocated that substituting wood for conventional building
materials such as steel and concrete could be an efficient technique
for addressing climate change and lowering carbon emissions.[1]
While Vytenis Babrauskas’s article " Ignition of Wood: A Review
of the art State of the Art" provides a comprehensive review of
available data on the ignition of solid wood, its main message is
that wood is a combustible material whose ignition properties must
be understood to reduce the riske of the fire.[2] To ensure its safe
use in a variety of applications, wood must be handled and stored
properly.
Fires can easily spread from one structure to another if they are not
kept at a safe distance or contained. In acknowledgment of this risk,

various building codes and standards have established restrictions on
or outright bans on the use of wood in construction.[1]
Despite its many advantages as an eco-friendly building material,
it is essential to recognise wood’s possible fire risks. By following
to appropriate safety protocols, the use of wood in building can
continue to be economically and ecologically feasible.[3]
In recent years, architects have investigated the potential of devel-
oping medium and high-rise wooden constructions with open spaces
as a means of lowering the quantity of material that is required to
construct the buildings. However, this strategy poses issues about
the fire safety of the building, particularly in open offices, lengthy
corridors, and auditoriums. Because all goods made from timber
are inherently combustible, the construction of medium- or high-

safety and evacuation. As a result, fire engineering is an essential
component in ensuring that safe designs are implemented.
In spite of the growing interest in wooden constructions, practi-

the safety concerns associated with wooden projects. This is pri-
marily because of the limited knowledge that exists regarding the
combustible character istics of engineered wood. This creates a sig-
nificant information gap and poses a considerable obstacle in the
way of fully realising the potential of timber as a building material.
Existing design methodologies for timber buildings are predicated
on minor compartment fires and presume uniform compartment fire
conditions. [4] While the uniform fire assumption may be appropri-
ate for small compartments, it has been observed that fires in real
structures with big open-plan compartments spread over the floor
plate and between levels. A close examination of accidental fires
in large, open-plan spaces finds that they do not spread simulta-
neously over the entire space.[5] These fires frequently spread over
floor plates as the flames grow, consuming a restricted area at any
given moment. These fires are known as "Travelling Fires".[6]

Design Methods for Fire Safety
This chapter provides an overview of the design methods for struc-
tural fire safety and introduces a novel design method known as
the "Travelling Fire Method." The chapter presents a variety of de-
sign techniques utilized in the field of structural fire protection. It
presents the standard approaches to fire safety in structural engi-

2.1
Structural Fire Engineering Challenges
Fire presents an important threat to the structural integrity of build-
ings and infrastructure; consequently, it is essential to include ap-
propriate fire safety measures for structural components during the
design phase. This requirement is founded on the premise that struc-
tural stability and integrity are the final lines of defense in the event
that other fire containment measures fail.
During an accidental fire, structural members or elements of a build-
ing may fail due to a decrease in load-bearing capacity caused by
rising temperature. The objective of structural fire engineering is to
ensure that structural members made of materials like steel, wood,
or concrete maintain their structural integrity during a fire. A build-
ing is deemed to meet structural integrity requirements when its
load-bearing elements can withstand the effects of fire for an ade-
quate period of time without losing stability. The only failure mode
==PAGE:26==
8

addressed by structural fire engineering is the loss of load-bearing
capacity, whereas the fire resistance framework evaluates additional
performance requirements. Structural fire engineering generally fol-
lows the same methodology as a structural design under ambient
conditions.
Although severe fires are statistically uncommon in the majority of
buildings, the load and resistance variables in fire limit state design
are modified to account for this fact. In fire analysis, the effects of
elevated temperature on the mechanical properties of materials and
thermal expansion and contraction are taken into account as follows:
• There may be a reduction in timber cross-sectional areas and
a decrease in material strength.
• The emergence of diverse failure mechanisms
• Thermal expansion and thermal bending result in geometric
modifications.
Understanding the core ideas of fire resistance and fire severity is
a vital component of constructing fire-resistant constructions. Fire
severity assesses the destructive impact of a fire, which may result in
structural collapse and other failures, whereas fire resistance refers
to a structure’s ability to survive collapse, fire spread, and other
types of failure.
The time, temperature, and strength domains are used to assess fire
resistance and fire severity. To ensure that structural components
can withstand the effects of a fire, they must be engineered with an
intrinsic fire resistance that is greater than the intensity of the fire.

2.2
Fire Scenarios
The thermal effects of fire-damaged structures can be analyzed using
a variety of techniques. Eurocode 1 (EN 1991-1-2) distinguishes be-
tween prescriptive and performance-based approaches, also known
as nominal temperature-time curves and nature fire models, respec-
tively. [4]
Analytical functions are utilized by prescriptive methods to define
the evolution of gas temperature over time via a curve. The term
nominal assumes that these curves are not representative of an ac-
tual fire, but are rather standard functions. Three curves are speci-
fied in EN 1991-1-2 for prescriptive approaches.[9]
• The standard temperature-time curve serves as a representa-
tion of a fully developed fire in a compartment and is adapted
from ISO 834. This curve, which was developed based on nu-
merous post-flash-over fire tests, uses a logarithmic relationship
to define the evolution of gas temperatures. This curve was
published in 1978 by Babrauskas et al.
• The external fire curve, which is used for the exterior surface
of separating external walls of a building that are exposed to
flames entering through the windows of a compartment located
below or adjacent.
• The hydrocarbon curve, which is used to represent the effects
of a hydrocarbon fire.
The application field of nominal temperature-time curves, which ex-
amine structural member temperatures over a specific time without
cooling, is dependent on the nation or region, building size, use,

Performance-based techniques take into account
the physical factors that directly affect compartment temperatures
and fire duration, such as building design, ventilation conditions,

2.3
Fuel control and ventilation control fires
The available energy of a fire is identified by its fire load, whereas the
gas temperature is determined by its Rate of Heat Release (RHR).
Due to the RHR, which drives the spread of gas and smoke, a fire
load burning rapidly or slowly can generate distinct gas temperature
curves. Depending on the availability of oxygen, a fire typically be-
gins small and develops, with two potential outcomes. If sufficient
oxygen is present, the RHR is constrained by the fire load, result-
ing in a fuel-controlled combustion. Both ventilation-controlled and
fuel-controlled fires are capable of causing flashover. Flashover is a
critical transition from a localised fire to one that involves all com-
bustible surfaces in the compartment that are exposed. [9]

2.4.1
Two-zone models
Zone models are numerical programs that are used to determine the
evolution of gas temperature within a compartment over time by
solving ordinary differential equations that express mass and energy
conservation for each zone. These models, which are based on the
assumption that temperature is uniform in each zone, provide useful
information such as gas and wall temperatures, as well as gas velocity
through openings. Geometrical data, wall material properties, and
fire data such as RHR curve, pyrolysis rate, and combustion heat of
fuel are required as input for zone models.
Two-zone models express mass and energy equilibrium for two layers
and take into account exchanges between them via air entrainment
models. The simulation yields gas temperatures, wall temperatures,
and flux through openings, as well as the evolution of layer thick-
ness over time. The thickness of the lower layer, which remains at
a colder temperature and lacks combustion products, is critical in
determining the occupant safety of the compartment.

terms. These models can also be used to analyze complex build-

2.5
Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD)
The powerful tool of Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) can also
be used to investigate the behaviour of fire in wooden structures.
CFD entails the numerical solution of governing equations that de-
scribe fluid flow, including heat and combustion product transport.
By dividing the domain into smaller volumes or cells and employing
numerical techniques such as finite difference, finite volume, or finite
element methods, the equations are solved.
CFD can be used to simulate a variety of fire scenarios in timber
structures, such as fire spread, heat release, smoke production, and
hazardous gas production. CFD models can provide insights into fire
behavior that are difficult or impossible to acquire experimentally,
such as the formation of fire plumes, the distribution of smoke, and
the interaction of fire with the environment.[10]
The ability to simulate complex fire scenarios, such as those involv-
ing multiple compartments, ventilation systems, and combustible
materials, is one of the benefits of CFD in fire safety engineering.
Additionally, CFD models can be used to evaluate the efficacy of
fire protection measures such as sprinkler systems, smoke control
systems, and fire barriers.
==PAGE:35==

CFD has the potential to be more cost-effective and time-efficient
than experimental methods. CFD simulations can provide precise
predictions of fire behavior at a fraction of the cost and time required
to conduct large-scale fire experiments.
Typically, CFD models for fire safety engineering are developed us-
ing specialized software packages with user-friendly interfaces for
defining the geometry of the problem, setting up the grid or mesh,
specifying boundary conditions, and selecting physical models. In
addition to advanced solvers that can manage complex equations
and numerical techniques, the software also includes post-processing
tools for visualizing and analyzing the results.[10]
It is essential to recognize that CFD simulations are only as accu-
rate as the data and assumptions used to contract the model. Conse-
quently, it is essential to calibrate the model with experimental data
and validate the complexity can result in substantial computational
costs, which must be carefully managed to ensure its viability.[4]
Computational fluid dynamics (CDF) is an indispensable tool for
simulating the spread of fire in wooden structures. However, this
will not be the focus of this thesis.

Standard temperature-time curve limitations:
• 1. Fire Intensity Variability
The standard temperature-time curve assumes a homogeneous
heat flux, which is not necessarily true in real-world fire sit-
uations.
The heat flux changes spatially and temporally in
compartment fires due to the presence of fuel, ventilation con-
ditions, and compartment shape. This variance can result in
localised overheating and thermal gradients, leading to a va-
riety of structural element failure types.
The conventional
temperature-time curve may therefore underestimate or over-
estimate the real fire resistance of a wooden structure.
• 2. Compartment Size
A real homogeneous fire scenario in an environment in a com-
partment is not always the case, according to what the typical
temperature-time curve suggests. Heat transport to the build-
ing is influenced by the compartment’s size and proximity to the
fire source. Due to the distance of the fire source, the heat flow
is higher in compact compartments, which can lead to localised
overheating and early collapse of structural components. Due
to the lower heat flux in large compartments and the increased
==PAGE:39==
2.7 Standard Fire Method

air volume, delayed structural failure may result. Because of
this, the typical temperature-time curve might not accurately
depict the fire scenario in various compartment sizes.[13]
• 3. Conditions for Ventilation.
The standard temperature-time curve presupposes a compart-
ment with continual ventilation, which isn’t often the case in
actual fire situations.(Actually the standard fire is the consid-
ered worst case of and under-ventilated Regime I fire) By regu-
lating the oxygen supply, which affects the oxygen combustion
rate and the oxygen release rate of the fire, ventilation condi-
tions have an impact on the heat transfer to the structure. The
fire intensity is lower and the heat transfer to the structure is
slower in compartments with poor ventilation, which can cause
delayed structural failure. The fire intensity is greater and the
heat transfer to the structure is quicker in compartments with
good ventilation, which can lead to the early failure of struc-
tural components. As a result, under various ventilation condi-
tions, the standard temperature-time curve may not accurately
depict the fire scenario.
Due to its structural qualities and popularity among engineered
wood products, cross-laminated timber (CLT) has been employed
more and more in the construction of structures. Yet, because of its
combustibility and the lack of recognized fire design methodologies
for CLT buildings, the usage of CLT in buildings has sparked worries
regarding fire safety.
Current research investigations have concentrated their attention

2.10
Steel and concrete response to fire
The reactivity of building materials to fire is a crucial factor in fire
safety design. Steel, wood, and concrete are the three most com-
monly employed materials in building construction. Each material
has distinct qualities that affect its reaction to compartment fires.
Steel has a certain behavior that enables it to sustain its structural
integrity for a while before eventually giving way to the heat when
exposed to fire. The temperature of steel rises during the start of
a fire, which causes it to expand. A partial or complete collapse
of the structure could result from this expansion, which could put
a strain on the connections and eventually cause them to break.
However, as the fire burns on, an oxidized layer begins to form on
the steel surface. This layer serves as an insulating barrier, slowing
the transfer of heat to the steel below.
Steel’s capacity to withstand fire is influenced by a number of el-
ements, including the fire size, shape, and thickness as well as its
intensity and duration. Fire resistance ratings are used by struc-
tural engineers to calculate the shortest period a steel element can
remain structurally sound during a fire. Steel constructions can also
be given fire-resistant insulation and coatings to increase their fire
resistance.
Concrete is a non-combustible material often utilized in the con-
struction of buildings. Nevertheless, rapid heating can cause spalling

cracking and spalling.
2.11

As mentioned at the start due to its natural attractiveness and envi-

structures. The behavior of wood in a fire depends on a number of
variables, including the type of wood, the moisture level, and the
size of the wood parts.
The temperature at which wood ignites depends on a number of
variables, including its moisture content, density, and ratio of surface
area to volume. According to ASTM E119-21, the standard test
technique for fire tests of building construction and materials, the

°C ) (ASTM, 2021).
When wood is exposed to fire, its strength
and rigidity diminish as the temperature rises. Over 392°F (200°C),
the tensile strength of wood diminishes significantly. Temperature-
induced thermal degradation of the material causes the rigidity of
wood to diminish as well.
Once wood reaches paralysis temperatures, it begins to char, which
is the process of its outer coat burning off. Many variables affect the
pace of charring, including temperature, moisture content, and den-
sity. The chare layer can provide some degree of fire temperature to
the unburned timber beneath it. Yet, the decrease in cross-sectional
area caused by charring can decrease the load-bearing capability of
the wood part.

The charging rate, or rate of change in moisture content, is essential
for designing wood structures. Timber is hygroscopic, meaning it ab-

in moisture content can result in dimension variations, affecting the
structural integrity and performance of the material.

safety concern. Determining the strength degradation of wood dur-

strength deterioration proceeds through three sub-processes: dry-

during which water vaporises and load-bearing wood transforms into
non-load-bearing char and volatile flammable substances.[17]
Pyrolysis occurs in the absence of oxygen while surface-igniting
volatiles provide additional heat.
The char layer penetrates the

or all the timber transforms into char, losing its load-bearing capac-

In 2019, a large-scale fire test was conducted in an existing industrial

mm/min, and Mode 3 or “fully developed.” [23]

dominating mechanisms driving flows within the compartment at

of the compartment along its length, including openings, thermal
characteristics of the boundaries, and compartment geometry. The
experiment also met one of the objectives described in this thesis,

in a large area generates high temperatures for a brief period of time.
A travelling fire, on the other hand, generates elevated temperatures
in the far field and flame temperatures in the close field, exposing
the structure to high temperatures for longer durations. Recently,
the term "travelling fire" has been used to refer to fires that burn
locally and extend across entire floor plates, resulting in uneven
temperatures within compartments. Several analytical procedures
for travelling fires have been discussed in review articles by Stern-
Gottlieb Rein and Dai, which are outlined in the following section.
Stern-Gottfried, Law, and Rein developed the Travelling Fires Method-
ology (TFM). This methodology is intended to augment rather than
replace standard design methods, as it is intended to account for
non-uniform flames in big open-plan environments. TFM takes into
account multiple potential fire dynamics, as opposed to just one
or two, and develops gas temperature-time curves at the roof of
a medium-height compartment. The model describes two moving
zones inside a design fire: the near-field, which represents flames
impinging on the ceiling and assumes peak flame temperatures, and
the far-field, which depicts smoke temperatures that drop with dis-
tance due to air mixing.
3.6.2
Clifton’s firecell model
Clifton created a model to simulate fire in large compartments in
1996 by dividing the floor plan into multiple "design areas" using
a grid-based approach. Each area of the design is represented by a
fire curve that is sequentially applied to simulate the burning pro-

3.6 TFM Experiments

cess. This method reflects the scenario of a "travelling fire" in which
a fire moves locally throughout the compartment, resulting in un-
even heating. Depending on the time step, each design area can
have various states, including fire, pre-heating, smoke-logged, and
burned-out. When a region is in the fire state, it experiences a fully
developed fire. According to Wang et al., despite being an innova-
tive model for a localised fire scenario, it was not widely adopted
due to insufficient experimental validation. Clifton himself stated
that this method should only be utilised for single-element design
checks and serve primarily as a research method. [20]

3.6.3
Rein’s Travelling Fire model
Rein first used computational fluid dynamics to construct temper-
ature fields, which were subsequently simplified for a single floor to
provide realistic findings while transferring less data to future struc-
tural analyses. Parameter sensitivity analyses were then utilised to
study the structural behaviour of a generic concrete frame. This

work examines the thermal responses of two structurally equiva-
lent steel and concrete beams to moving fires, while simultaneously
decreasing the thermal descriptions of near-field and far-field tem-
peratures utilised in TFM and the possible range of valid fire sizes.
The near field is the burning region of a fire where structural ele-
ments are directly exposed to flames and experience the most in-
tense heating, whereas the far field is the region away from the
flames where structural elements are exposed to hot combustion
gases. A uniform temperature of 1200°C is used in the near field,
while Alpert’s ceiling jet correlation is used in the far field to cal-
culate the smoke temperature. This calculation takes into account
the ceiling jet temperature, ambient temperature, heat release rate
of the fire plume, height of the compartment ceiling, and distance
from the centre of the fire plume. The fire spread is calculated from
the flame thickness, fire load density, and rate of heat release density.
This travelling fire approach, rather than a single fire scenario, pro-
vides a range of possible fires, allowing the most challenging heating
scenario to be determined for subsequent structural analysis. The
ventilation conditions are ignored, and the near field temperature is
applied uniformly throughout the height of the compartment.
Rackauskaite et al. improved the methodology in 2015 by account-
ing for localised fire dynamics, narrowing the range of possible fire
sizes, and introducing the concept of flapping angle to account for a
range of near field temperatures ranging from 800°C to 1200°C. This
method is known as iTFM (improved Travelling Fire Methodology).

3.7.3
Near Feild- Flame Flapping
The term "near-field" in TFM refers to flames that strike the ceil-
ing directly and reach their peak flame temperatures. Temperatures
between 800 and 1200 °C have been recorded in a variety of building
fires and experiments. TFM uses a near-field temperature assump-
tion of 1200 °C in order to be on the safe side. But gas temperatures
typically fluctuate continuously between 800 and 1200 °C because
of variations in the position of the flame on the ceiling caused by
natural fluctuations. [8]
Incorporating flame flapping helps iTFM take this into account.
Because of this, rather than the peak flame temperatures seen in
fires, structural members experience lower average gas temperatures.
To connect this decreased near-field temperature to fire size or os-
cillations, there is no experimental support or correlations avail-
able. TFM indirectly accounted for this by figuring out the typical
bay temperatures for the structural members, but this supposition
wasn’t thoroughly investigated. The idea of the flapping angle is pre-
sented to apply decreased near-field temperatures in iTFM caused
by flapping.[8]
The angle θ from the flame’s primary axis represents the length of
the ceiling where fluctuations of the impinging flame occur. The
ceiling length over which the impinging fire fluctuations occur is
calculated using the flapping angle, and the average temperature
over this length is calculated while accounting for both the far-field
and peak near-field temperatures. A lower near-field temperature is
produced by this blending of the cooler smoke and the varying flame.
Using this lower near-field temperature as a starting point, travelling
fire time-temperature curves are produced rather than using a fixed
peak value of 1200 °C. Alpert’s ceiling jet correlation is used to derive
the equation that determines how much the near-field temperature
drops as a result of flapping. [8]

This chapter will examine fire-damaged timber construction. The

Static system
The design of the structures is based on the intended functions of

4.7
Design timber elements
ULS Design:The column and beam timber elements are designed and
constructed with consideration given to the structural requirements
before coming into contact with any fire.
The process of design
requires doing analyses of a number of aspects, including expected
loads, design concerns, and the material qualities of timber. The
dimensions, shape, and details of the column and beam are carefully
picked to ensure that they have the capacity to withstand the loads
that are expected of them, offer stability, and contribute to the
overall structural integrity of the building.
ALS Design - Standard Fire: After a fire incident, the column and
beam timber elements’ fire resistance is evaluated using the stan-
dard fire scenario. This evaluation entails submitting the elements
to a predetermined temperature-time curve, which is typically based
on standards such as DS/EN 1995-1-2. The objective is to deter-
mine whether the elements can maintain their load-bearing capacity,
structural integrity, and fire resistance for a predetermined amount
of time, such as 30, 60, or 90 minutes. The fire resistance rating is
determined based on how well these criteria are met. In this thesis,
60 minutes will be calculated.
During the standard fire assessment, factors such as charring, cross-
sectional area reduction, and changes in mechanical properties are
taken into account.
Charring refers to the burning of the outer
layers of the wood elements, which results in the formation of an in-
sulating char layer. To assess the fire resistance rating of elements,
the extent of charring and remaining structural capacity are evalu-
ated. This information assists in determining whether the elements
can withstand the fire conditions while maintaining the structure’s
safety and stability.
ALS Design - Travelling Fire: In a travelling fire scenario, the spread
of fire throughout the building is meticulously considered when as-
sessing the column and beam timber elements.
This evaluation
considers the progressive character of the fire, including its growth
==PAGE:90==
72
Case Study
rate, spread, and heat transfer mechanisms. The elements are an-
alyzed to determine their ability to withstand prolonged exposure
to high temperatures, the effects of charring, and the influence on
their structural performance as a whole.
The evaluation of a travelling fire investigates how the spread of
fire affects vital aspects of the elements, such as their load-bearing
capacity, structural stability, and overall integrity. Potential vulner-
abilities or weaknesses can be identified by comprehending how the
fire spreads and affects the wood components.

Based on the article "Travelling Fires for Structural Design - Part
II," it is compared the behavior of travelling fires to assumptions-
based approaches for various fire sizes. Travelling fires are character-
ized by their dynamic nature, as they spread across floor plates and
burn in a limited area at any given moment, deviating from the tra-
ditional assumption of uniform burning and temperature conditions
throughout a compartment.[6]
In order to generate more plausible fire scenarios for structural fire
analysis, it is incorporated a variety of fire sizes. The investigation
evaluated the impact of fire proportions comprising 10 percent, 25
percent, and 50 percent of the floor area on the structural behavior of
wood elements. It is sought to capture the diversity of fire scenarios
that can occur in large, open-plan compartments by incorporating
various fire sizes. This was accomplished by including both small
and major fires.[6]
The study yielded valuable insights regarding the effects of differ-
ing fire sizes on structural timber elements.
Notably, it was de-
termined that fires encompassing approximately 10 percent of the
floor area posed the greatest threat to the structural integrity of
wood elements. Such fire sizes resulted in higher temperatures than
conventional fires and even surpassed the temperatures calculated
using the Eurocode 1 temperature-time curve. This demonstrates
the significance of incorporating non-uniform and time-varying fire
scenarios, such as spreading flames, into structural design in order
to ensure effective fire safety measures.
The study employed both travelling fire and standard fire method-
ologies, with an emphasis on heat transfer calculations to infer the
structural performance under various combustion scenarios. Consid-
ering the localized and time-varying nature of the fire phenomenon,
this method enabled the researchers to acquire a greater understand-
ing of the behavior of structures exposed to moving fires.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
6.1
Summary and Highlight
The current design guidelines assume that the compartment would
always have the same temperature, but in practise, flames in big
enclosures spread, which results in very non-uniform temperature
distributions across the space. The structural reaction of a GL32h
timber frame that was exposed to travelling fires and uniform fires
that were defined in the standards was explored in this research.
Travelling fire scenarios, the Eurocode parametric curve, and the
ISO standard fire have been taken into consideration.
The primary objective was to expose timber structures to several
types of travelling fire scenarios and then compare those results to
more established approaches. Both the impact of moving fires on the
structural performance of timber elements and the validity of tradi-
tional design methodologies in forecasting the behaviour of timber
structures under fire circumstances were to be evaluated as part of
this project.
According to the findings of the research project, conventional design
methodologies, which are based on the assumption that a compart-
ment will experience consistent burning and temperature conditions
across its entirety, have limits when applied to large open-plan com-
partments. Because these methodologies fail to represent the dy-
namic and non-uniform behaviour of travelling fires in an acceptable
manner, the predicted structural reaction of timber elements could
be inaccurate as a result. The conventional procedures were mostly
created on the basis of experiments conducted on a small scale, and
as a result, they are not directly applicable to big enclosures.
The incorporation of travelling fires into the analysis was necessary
in order to achieve a more precise characterization of the actual
==PAGE:120==

Conclusion
performance of the structure and to maximize the effectiveness of
the structural fire design. In large open-plan compartments, it has
been found that travelling fires do not burn concurrently throughout
the whole enclosure. Instead, the fires migrate across floor plates,
only igniting a small portion of the space at any given moment.
These hypothetical fires were shown to be more accurate depictions
of the way fires behave in real-world conditions.
6.2
Case Study and Comparison of results
The purpose of this research was to examine the thermal response
of a timber structure using two different approaches, namely the
conventional fire method and the travelling fire method, and to de-
termine the ways in which they are similar and different from one
another. In order to accomplish this goal, a case study was carried
out in which each approach was implemented, and the results were
analyzed based on the accidental limit state (ALS) by employing
the reduced cross-section method for standard fire situations.
In the case study, one of the tasks was to use a parametric formula
to determine how temperature and opening factors are related to
one another. After this step, the charring rate was calculated based
on the temperature, the amount of time, and the gamma factor for
several situations involving a travelling fire. Using these approaches
allowed for the determination of the charring rate in moving fires at
various temperatures, periods, and places. In addition, the iTFM
(Improving Travelling Fire Method) formula was applied in order to
compute the near field and far field travelling fire scenario in order
to gain an understanding of how the fire will spread through the
compartment at various periods and in different locations.
The primary objective was to monitor the temperature during the
parametric and moving fires in order to calculate the charring rate
and load-bearing capacity of the timber structure. The goal of the
research was to determine which type of fire scenario posed the great-
est threat to the structural performance of timber elements, and this
==PAGE:121==
6.2 Case Study and Comparison of results
103
was done by simulating the fire.
According to the results of the study, the rate at which timber struc-
tures were charred when they were exposed to moving fires varied
depending on the temperature and the amount of time. On the other
hand, the charring rate for smaller cross-sections did not change from
its initial value of 0.7 mm/min. This value includes the pyrolysis
zone that accounts for the initial 7 mm of char. This demonstrated
that fires that covered around ten percent of the floor surface posed
the most severe challenge to the structural integrity of timber parts
in the building.
The findings of the study highlighted how important it is to imple-
ment the new design methodology, which takes into consideration
varied fire sizes and incorporates moving fires, for more accurate and
dependable fire scenarios in structural fire analysis. This method-
ology permits a safer and more robust structural design of timber
structures by accurately simulating the behavior of fires in the actual
world when they are contained within vast compartments. Addi-
tionally, it contributes to the continuous efforts that are being made
to improve the fire performance and safety of timber construction,
particularly in the context of developing architectural trends and
the environmentally responsible utilization of lumber as a building
material.
