Introduction 
From many points of views, quaternions make a great tool for representing rotations in three 
dimensions. Indeed, to specify an orientation in space, that is to say a set of three 
orthonormal vectors, or a “frame of reference”, only three parameters are needed. Although 
both efficiency and elegancy considerations should have prompted their wide use at 
application level, this is not quite the case: the engineering world – with of course some 
exceptions – tends to prefer instead the more familiar matrix (nine scalars) representation.  
The opening chapter of the thesis will be concerned with the study of frame fields along 
curves. A tangent “moving frame” along a curve is just a frame of reference attached to a 
parametrized curve in space so that a specific frame axis keeps itself aligned with the 
curve’s tangent direction. The most notable example, taught in any introductory course in 
differential geometry, is that named after the French mathematicians Frenet and Serret.  
In this context, unit quaternions shall prove to be highly useful, and so – in the second 
chapter – they will be put into play to help us visualize the three rotational degrees of 
freedom of a moving frame.       
Following the work of professor Hanson from Indiana University, one of the key concepts 
we intend to cover is that of the space of possible (tangent) frames of a given curve. We will 
be limiting ourselves to presenting fairly basic examples (a circle and a helix) yet it turns out 
– rather expectedly – that he simplest cases are also the most revealing.   
The final chapter will be dealing with developable, or “flat”, surfaces, i.e. surfaces that can 
be ideally built by simply bending and joining together planar pieces of paper. There are 
many possibilities to look at concrete examples of these, particularly as modern works of 
architecture. For instance, take Frank Gehry’s Walt Disney Concert Hall in Los Angeles 
(Figure 1). For an historical investigation on their wide-ranging use we shall suggest the 
beautiful exposition by S. Lawrence. Finally, it is also to be mentioned their role within 
geometric modeling (see e.g. Wallner).

1.3 The angular velocity vector 
It is clear that any rotation matrix R(t) represents, in the standard basis {i,.j,.k}, a linear map 
t : ℝ3 → ℝ3 called “rotation about the origin”, that preserves length and orientation. 
Especially, if R(t) ≠ 1, such map leaves fixed all points lying along a unique straight line 
(Euler’s Theorem), the direction of which is the “axis” of the rotation.  
Then, it should come as no surprise that the set of rotations, together with the operation of 
composition, satisfies the four group axioms of closure, associativity, identity element – 
being the identity map Id – and inverse element. In fact, the 3D rotation group is isomorphic 
to the (multiplicative) group of rotation matrices, SO(3). 
It follows that each smooth path α :  ∋ t ↦ R(t) ∈ SO(3) may be identified with a “rotation 
sequence”, that is to say with a one-parameter family of rotations, i.e. a curve  : t ↦ t in 
the space of rotations. We may choose such curve  to originate at the identity for t = 0, i.e. 
we set 0 ∈  and 0 = Id, or R(0) = 1. This way, for any x0 ∈ ℝ3, the map x : t ↦ t.(x0) is a 
curve in ℝ3 with starting point x(0) = x0. 
Moreover, if (0) = Id, the tangent vector to  at t = 0 is a tangent vector at the identity, and 
thereby the collection of all functions ′(0) makes up a space isomorphic to the Lie algebra 
(3) = T1(SO(3)). Put simply, a function ′(0) is essentially the same thing as a skew 
symmetric matrix.

1.4 The Frenet-Serret frame 
We have seen that there are infinitely many possible frame fields along a curve γ, i.e. infinite 
smooth paths in SO(3) associated to it. However, among all possible choices of the vector 
field e2, there is one of special importance. 
We know in fact that the derivative of the tangent vector t, evaluated at any value of t, lies in 
the corresponding normal plane of γ. It seems natural, then, to define a unit vector field, 
which we indicate by n, in such a way that all its values are parallel to dt(t)/dt. Thus, for it is 
now clear that the length of dt(t)/dt equals the product v(t) ·κ(t), the desired vector field shall 
be given by n = (v ·κ)−1·dt/dt. Here we see that n is well defined provided κ(t) ≠ 0 for all t ∈

parallel to t(t) and b(t) will instead reveal itself later when discussing developable surfaces.

1.7 Geodesics 
We now consider the case of the curve γ being a geodesic of . Such is the case of t being 
“parallel” along γ, meaning that, for any value of t, the acceleration vector dt(t)/dt is

2.1 Introduction 
We now move to using quaternions for representing orientation frames, that is to say 3D 
rotation matrices.  
A few references, as our treatise will be fairly compact. First of all, Hanson’s book 
“Visualizing Quaternions”, which provides a very nice analysis of both properties and 
applications of quaternions. Also to be mentioned, chapter 23 “Rotation and Animation 
Using Quaternions” in Gray’s Mathematica-based differential geometry text. Further 
technical aspect are touched upon in many books. In particular, we suggest Stillwell’s 
“Naïve Lie Theory”. 
We shall commence by recalling that the set of unit quaternions, q = (q0, q1, q2, q3) = (q0, q), 
such that q·q = 1, forms a (Lie) group, isomorphic to SU(2), the group of order two unitary 
matrices with determinant +1. The group operation is the so-called Hamilton product, i.e. the 
non-commutative multiplication rule  
p ⋆ q = (p0, p1, p2, p3) ⋆ (q0, q1, q2, q3) 
 
= ( p0·q0 − p · q, p0·q + q0·p + p × q).

i.e. the set of points at unit distance from the origin in four dimensional Euclidean space 4,

q(θ, v) = {cos(θ/2), n·sin(θ/2)},

given parametrically. With this parametrization, the matrix

3 Flat Ribbons 
3.1 What is a ribbon? 
We begin proposing a very simple experiment. Only one sheet of standard office paper, a 
pair of scissors and a pen are needed. Start by drawing, on same region of the sheet, a rather 
narrow rectangle. Then, sketch the axis x and y of a Cartesian coordinate system aligned 
with the sides of the rectangle and with origin at its center. It is also a good idea to draw a 
few lines x = const and y = const. Finally, cut the strip along its borders and smoothly bend it 
until it assumes the shape you most like. Any shape is allowed – the planar one too – 
provided no crumpling, tearing or self-contacting of the paper gets involved. What you are 
now handling is a concrete example of the surface ℛ of a ribbon. We will refer to it as a 
“paper ribbon”. You obtained it through the real world equivalent of a so-called 
diffeomorphism, a bijective smooth map with smooth inverse. 
A few observations: first, the lines you drew have been (in general) transformed into curves, 
lying on the paper ribbon. However, their lengths and angles of intersection have remained 
unchanged. In fact, the same would have been true for any pair of curves you could have 
sketched on the strip in the first place. Indeed, a diffeomorphism that does not induce any 
change in the metric, as in this case, is of special type, and called an isometry. 
Having introduced the notion of “paper ribbon”, it shall not be too troublesome to give some 
paper-free definition of ℛ. However, a few preliminaries are needed. Let 1 and 2 be two 
surfaces. We shall recall that:  
1. a smooth map f : 1 → 2 is a local isometry if it takes any curve lying on 1 to a 
curve of same length in 2; 
2. an injective local isometry is a called an isometry, and is a distance-preserving 
diffeomorphism; 
3. when a (local) isometry f : 1 → 2 exists, one says that 1 and 2 are (locally) 
isometric with each other; 
4. any local isometry preserves the Gaussian curvature K – this is Gauss Theorema 
Egregium – and takes any geodesic of 1 to a geodesic of 2.

correct, we write +(t) = Lt.

We shall now make some further considerations on flat surfaces. To begin with, recall that 
any point of a developable is either a planar or a parabolic point. This shall be of no surprise.

first case (planar point), both principal curvatures, κ1 and κ2, are zero, i.e. the mean curvature 
H = ½ (κ1 + κ2) is also null, as opposed to the second (parabolic), when only one of them 
vanishes, and so H ≠ 0. It is also very well known that a developable only consisting of

Notice that any of 1-4 is a ruled surface, and that 2 and 3 also include singular points.

planar points in ℛ. Indeed, planar regions were allowed. Thereby, our ribbon, generally

definition. We argue that any point of a developable may be classified as either 
a) “essentially parabolic”, if it is the limit of a sequence of parabolic points, or 
b) “flat”, if a planar point with a neighborhood comprising only planar points (i.e. a 
neighborhood lying in a plane). 
Hence, being essentially parabolic is a weaker condition than just parabolic: a point that falls 
in a) may either be planar or parabolic, yet any parabolic point is essentially parabolic. 
Conversely, flatness represents a stronger condition for a point to fulfill than simple 
planarity.  
Admittedly, one may consider slightly inconsistent the use of the word “flat” to indicate a 
point that is “more” than just a planar point. Indeed, flatness, when referred to a surface 
instead, is a weaker property than planarity.  
Anyway, there is a theorem, generally credited to Hartman and Nirenberg, basically stating 
that 
• 
from any essentially parabolic point of a developable there passes a unique (open 
and connected) line segment, lying on the surface, called generator;  
• 
each generator runs to infinity or has one (or both) of its end points on the surface 
boundary;  
• 
the points along a generator are either all parabolic or all planar. 
The theorem, a proof of which is given in Stoker, ultimately indicates that a developable 
whose points are all essentially parabolic cannot even possess any isolated planar point. 
They come only in groups, arranged along never-intersecting lines. In loose terms, in fact, 
the most general developable surface embedded in three-dimensional space may be obtained 
by gluing together pieces of 1-4. If we discard planar pieces as valid elements for our 
assemblage – by saying that no flat point shall be present – planar points remain only 
possible along a generator separating two parabolic regions.
