Abstract
This thesis describes the experimental investigation of the general behaviour of rotor-
air-foil-bearing dynamics without rotation. Specifically, the influence of varying the
leading-edge angle. Obtaining characteristics experimentally can help to develop and
improve theoretical models used to design efficient and robust air-foil-bearings. In
this work, both static and dynamic experiments are performed on a near symmetrical
hollow rotor supported by two identical air-foil-bearings. The experiments showed
clear anisotropy of bearing stiffness along with a dependence of resonance frequency
on excitation amplitude. A clear effect of increasing dynamic stiffness closer to the
leading edge of a bump-foil segment was observed, which is a valuable characteristic
of an air-foil-bearing to know in order to model it correctly.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation and goal
Rotating machinery often has issues with wear and breakdowns. Technologies that
help to reduce downtime and breakdowns of key components is desirable. An Air-
Foil-Bearing (AFB) is a type of bearing, which uses air instead of lubricant. When
the need for lubrication is removed, the complexity, maintenance and cost is gener-
ally lower. Its benefits also include quieter operation and wide temperature operat-
ing ranges. They are not applicable in all situations however, and are mostly used
for high rotational speeds as it has to generate self-acting hydrodynamic pressure
from the surrounding air [5]. This also means that the AFB is mostly effective for
higher operating speeds as enough pressure has to be generated in order to lift the
rotor from the bearing. The low rotating speed under startup creates wear in the
foil of the bearing as the speed builds up, but this effect is only transient. Because
of the high rotational speed, the AFB is used in many advanced turbo-machinery
systems, such as high speed compressors [6].
Although the working principle of AFBs is simple, theoretical models are difficult
to develop. While great strides have been made in this regard [5], it is still relevant
to perform experiments to improve the congruence of theory and experiment.
This work aims to contribute to the scientific field of AFBs by investigating how
the rotor-bearing dynamics behave without the air-film generated from the rotor
rotation. This means that all experiments will be conducted without any rotation
of the rotor, i.e with rotational speed of zero.

2.1.1
Motor
The motor is the model e+a mSPW17/15-4-b1, and consists of a stator which by
applying a rotating magnetic field, rotates the permanent magnet fitted on the rotor.
The rotor is kept in place by two air foil bearings, one in the Drive End (DE) and
one in the Non Drive End (NDE). The housing of the motor is mounted to a heavy
steel table, with shock absorbers in between, see Figure 2.1b.
(a) Motor
(b) Shock absorbers
Figure 2.1: Motor on table with shock absorbers.
Each end of the rotor is fitted with a steel disc which is mounted with balanced
screws to ensure a centered rotation, when the motor is operating. A disc with a
dynamic force transducer can be mounted in each end in order to attach and excite
the rotor with a shaker for frequency response analysis. In Figure 2.2, a cross section
of the motor is shown to illustrate the inside of the motor.

Four proximity sensors are mounted in the housing, two horizontally and two ver-
tically, in both the DE and the NDE. The horizontally placed proximity sensors
are not visible in the illustration, due to them being mounted perpendicular to the
plane.
2.1.2
Air foil bearings
Each end of the rotor is supported by an AFB. The inside of the AFB consist of 3
segments of bump foils and top foils, see Figure 2.3. The AFB is a type of bearing
which operates at high speeds by generating a thin film of air, on which the shaft
rotates. At 0 RPM, the shaft rests on the top foil lining on the inside of the bearing.
As the shaft starts to rotate, pressure builds between the shaft and the foil lining.
When the rotational speed reaches a critical speed, enough pressure is generated in
the bearing that the shaft lifts from the lining. The lifting capability in the bearing
increases as the rotational speed increase. The AFB relies on the foil structure to
generate damping by dry friction in the contact points between the bump foils and
the bearing sleeve.
Figure 2.3: Picture of similar AFB, as the two AFB in the system.

2.1.3
Cooling system
During long runs of the system, the motor needs to be cooled. This is done by
circulating water through a connected hose. The water inside is cooled by pumping
it through a radiator which is cooled by a fan, before recirculating to the motor.
The inlet and outlet hose of the cooling system is mounted in the side of the motor
housing.

2.3
Sensors
Proximity sensor
The sensors used to measure the displacement of the rotor are four Bently Nevada
3300 3 mm proximity sensors. The proximity sensors works by generating a radio
frequency signal, in the tip of the probe, that generates small eddy currents in any
conductive materiel inside of the measuring range. By measuring the power loss in
the probe, created by the eddy currents, the distance to the conductive material
can be calculated. The voltage output of the proximity sensor is proportional to the
gap. With the calibration factor of the given probe, one can determine the distance.
Figure 2.10: Proximity sensor
Static force transducer
In order to measure static force, a HBM U9B 2 kN static force transducer is used.
The static force transducer is essentially a Wheatstone bridge which works by having
four resistors, three known and one unknown. The resistors are connected in series
in pairs, as illustrated in figure 2.11b. The potential between the pairs will be 0 V
when the transducer is unloaded, and will generate a voltage output when loaded,
which is proportional to the load.

Dynamic force transducer
To measure the forces under dynamic testing when doing frequency response the
Br¨uel & Kjær Type 8030-001 IEPE dynamic force transducer i used. The trans-

the piezoelectric effect.
When compressed, the transducer outputs an electrical
charges which is proportional to the force applied. The charge is converted into a
voltage output which can be measured. The dynamic force transducer comes with
a sensitivity directly from the manufacturer. In this Thesis, however, the sensitivity
provided by Albert Eegholm Hende [2] will be used, since the transducer has been
calibrated recently by DTU.

2.4.1
Foil stiffness program
In order to measure the foil stiffness, a program in dSPACE is is created for this
purpose. Using the voltage output from the static force transducer and four prox-
imity sensors, and applying the individual sensitivities, the program logs the data
in a file which later can be processed in Matlab.
2.4.2
Frequency Response Function program
In order to do frequency response analysis a program is created for this purpose.
Besides the output from the proximity sensors, this program logs the output from
the dynamic force transducer. The sensibility for each sensor is applied and the
output from the sensors are logged for further processing in Matlab. This program
also generates the chirp signal to excite the shaker, and is controlled by inputting
a frequency span for the chirp to sweep through and the amplitude for the shaker.
Further more the acquisition time as well as the acquisition frequency is controlled.

Calibration of sensors
Before any measurements are done, the sensors are calibrated. The four proximity
sensors are calibrated by using a purpose built calibration device. Each proximity
sensor is placed in the device and a micrometer screw controls the distance to the
sensor. The distance between the proximity and the micrometer screw is logged
every 0.05 mm and plotted in figure 2.16 as voltage as a function of displacement.
With the acquired data-points a line is fitted for each sensor, and the slope of the
lines are used as calibration factors for the sensors.

2.6
Disassembly of rotor-bearing system
When disassembling the rotor-bearing system, all sensors are removed from the
housing. Both the DE end and the NDE disc is removed, leaving the shaft free at
both ends, figure 2.18a. A support is mounted to the NDE of the shaft as seen in
figure 2.18b. This is designed to keep the shaft in the center of the bearings, making
it possible to pull the shaft out of the housing and bearings, without damaging the
DE bearing and the stator inside of the housing. Before pulling out the shaft, the
screws on the end of the housing in the NDE is loosened, so that the end of the
housing containing the NDE bearing can be removed with the shaft, figure 2.18c.
When the shaft has been pulled out, the shaft has to be carefully supported in the
middle, without damaging the magnet on the rotor. This allows for the dismantle
of the support in the NDE. The end of the housing is then removed, allowing access
to the bearing.

2.7 Design of angular support
2.7
Design of angular support
The angular support is designed to be robust and simple and consists of 5 parts.
The plate on which the motor is mounted, the angular plates and the angle-mount
plates, as illustrated in figure 2.20. The angular plates comes in sets of two, with a
slope of respectively 8°, 16° and 24° to the horizontal. This allows for a total of 7
different angles of measurement. For each angled test the motor is mounted on top of
a rectangular plate, which is then mounted on one of the three sets of angled plates.

Static characterization
3.1
Introduction
The damping in the bearing is caused by dry friction from the shaft being in contact
with the lining of the bearing and in the friction in the bump foils. This friction is
difficult to model and changes depending on the dynamics in the bearing and how
the system is excited. The foil stiffness in the bearing and the clearance between
the shaft and bearing lining does not change. The clearance and bearing stiffness
is experimentally investigated by conducting a series of static tests, where the shaft
is forced into contact with the top foil of the bearing. Then it should be possible
to determine the force/displacement relationship, i.e. the stiffness, as well as the
clearance inside the bearing. This experiment is performed in multiple directions.
3.2
Foil stiffness and clearance
The AFBs consist of three segments of bump- and top foils. The stiffness of the
bearing is dependent on position.
The stiffness of the bearings is tested in the
horizontal direction, the vertical direction and a combination of the two directions.
Figure 3.1 shows the orientation of the applied force. The shaft is loaded in each
direction by a hand operated screw jack, and the displacement is measured. A force
transducer is placed between the shaft and the jack in order to log the force applied
for each measured displacement. To mitigate the movement of the shaft in the NDE,
when measuring in the DE, the shaft is constrained in the NDE. Since the shaft is
resting on the bearing, the reaction force in the NDE will have a small impact on
the measurements in the DE, when force is applied in the negative direction. This
will however result in a small deviation compared to the relatively large movements
in the DE and can therefore be neglected. The clearance in the AFB is estimated as
described in [4], as where the regression line of the foil stiffness intersects the x-axis
for both directions, negative and positive. All results are tabulated in Table 3.1 and
Table 3.2.

Foil stiffness
The foil stiffness is more difficult to characterize.
Generally AFBs are expected
to have anisotropic stiffness characteristics, i.e. an increase in stiffness when ap-
proaching the leading edge of the bearing segment [3]. The estimated foil stiffness
in multiple directions for both shim thicknesses are listed in Table 3.1. When com-
paring tests of opposite directions, for both bearings and shim configurations, i.e.
0°-180°, this effect is clearly observed. For example, the results clearly show higher
stiffness in the 180° direction compared to the 0° direction. For an AFB with the
leading edge positioned at 30° from vertical, this is to be expected, as 180° is closer to
the leading edge than 0° as can be observed in Figure 3.1. The apparently anomalous
result of 90°-270° is arguably not anomalous. Observing Figure 2.19c, the transition
of two segments can be seen, with the trailing edge resting substantially further
away from the bearing wall compared to the leading edge. Thus 270° should be
thought of as contacting a trailing edge and not a leading edge. For 45°-225° and
135°-315°, the anisotropic effect is also present. This assumes that the individual
segments in the AFB are identical. If the results for a single segment in the AFB is
compared however, the effect is not consistently observed.
3.2.5
Discussion of static characterization
In this section, the static characteristics of the bearing system has been investigated.
The bearing stiffness along with the rotor-bearing clearance has been experimentally
estimated. The addition of shims had a clear effect on the bearing clearance, as was
expected. It is more difficult to make definitive conclusions on the effect of the
shims on the static stiffness however.
The results are heavily inconsistent, and
would change from experiment to experiment. A potential cause of the uncertainty
21
==PAGE:29==
3.2 Foil stiffness and clearance
and inconsistency in the measurements lie in the unknown position of the rotor in
the bearing. When the rotor is under no load, and does not rotate, it will rest
at the bottom of the bearing.
When the rotor is subject to non-vertical forces,
the contact point of the rotor on the top foil of the bearing is unknown, and might
vary from experiment to experiment, leading to differences in measured foil stiffness.
The method of estimating clearance by linear regression is not perfect. It assumes
perfect linear stiffness of the bearings which is a simplification of the true behaviour
of the bearing. In all the experiments, clear non-linear stiffness of the AFB can be
observed. When the shaft initiates contact with the top foil, a gradual increase in
stiffness can be seen in the results, until a near-linear force/displacement relationship
is observed. A plausible explanation of this is the fact that all elements of the AFB
are not fully engaged until the shaft is forced into the top foil with enough force.
Nevertheless, a bearing stiffness anisotropy effect was observed, although with a high
degree of uncertainty, as the variation in the experimental data was high. Including
multiple replications of the same experiment to decrease uncertainty from noise and
variation might reduce the error in the measurement.

contact with the bearing in the direction of the excitation. The point being that
there is no clearance for the shaft to move without contacting the top foil. It is also
noted that the width of the peaks shows that the system is more highly damped
compared to the horizontal direction. This can also be caused by the shaft being
in constant contact with the bearing, leading to more friction between the bump
foil and the bearing housing. As previously observed in the horizontal excitation of
the DE, a clear decrease in resonance frequency along with an increase in resonance
amplitude is present. Much less pronounced however, is the decrease in damping
which is seen in the difference in the slope of the phase. Thus, it is likely that
the stiffness of the system decreases as the excitation amplitude increases, as was
the case for the horizontal excitation. The coherence is high enough to indicate
reliable results. A peculiar peak of constant frequency through all tested excitation
amplitudes is present in the results at approximately 495 Hz.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
In this work, the static and dynamic behaviour of the rotor-bearing system was in-
vestigated. The results of the static experiments indicate bearing stiffness anisotropy
in both bearings, when comparing opposite directions for both the original shims
and with the additional shims added to the bearing. The effect was not consistently
observed for individual segments however. The presence of the effect is not com-
pletely certain for this reason.
Additional shims were inserted into the bearing to investigate the influence of
decreased clearance on the bearing characteristics. As expected, the clearance was
reduced after the additional shims was added. The frequency response showed sub-
stantially increased dynamic stiffness in the horizontal direction, indicated by the
large increase in resonance frequency, as seen in Table 6.1. This increase in reso-
nance frequency is likely caused by the reduction in clearance, as the rotor cannot
move as much inside the bearing without contacting the bearing surfaces. For the
same reason this stiffening effect was not observed for the vertical direction.
Non-linear effects of bearing stiffness and damping depending on excitation ampli-
tude was also observed. As the excitation amplitude in the dynamical character-
ization was increased, the dynamic stiffness of the system appeared to decrease,
indicated by the decrease in resonance frequency. This can be seen in Table 6.1.

Future work
For future work, it would be interesting to conduct the same experiment with vary-

effects when the air-film is present in the bearing. Some unexplained disruptions

bearing, when force applied.
