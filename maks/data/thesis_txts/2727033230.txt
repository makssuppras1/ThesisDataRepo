Abstract
The COVID­19 pandemic has accelerated the adoption of hybrid work arrangements. Un­
derstanding the relationship between hybrid work, team dynamics, and social capital has
become crucial for promoting employee well­being, as well as improving organizational
performance.
This thesis aims to analyze the relationship between hybrid work, team dynamics, and
social capital in Danish public administrations using a hierarchical clustering analysis ap­
proach. Through a large­scale survey, five clusters were identified based on respondents’
self­reported scores in work environment dimensions such as motivation or social support
from colleagues, and background variables like the number of days working from home.
Additionally, a random forest algorithm was used to provide insights into the degree of
importance of the different features used to classify respondents into clusters. This al­
gorithm was also used to predict which cluster respondents from a smaller team­specific
survey belong to, which allowed to analyze the characteristics of teams based on their
cluster distribution. Finally, an internal benchmarking method suggested by the Danish
NFA was used to further compare team characteristics and support the insights found by
the team cluster analysis.
The findings show a significant difference between the five clusters, which suggests that
social capital and team dynamics are significantly impacted by hybrid work arrangements.
Additionally, results show that in the context of Danish public administrations, work envi­
ronment dimensions have a higher degree of importance than background variables for
determining the cluster a respondent belongs to. Finally, findings in this thesis provide
insights into team characteristics based on their cluster distribution which highlighted the
importance of personalized team management strategies that focus on the characteris­
tics, challenges, and issues observed within each team.
The results of this thesis contribute to the understanding of how hybrid work arrangements
influence team dynamics and social capital in Danish public administrations and offer
insights to organizations wanting to address the unique needs of their teams and promote
a positive and productive hybrid work environment.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background
The possibility of working from home has become one of the most common forms of work­
ing arrangement in recent years. Telecommuting or hybrid work started as an experimen­
tal practice in the IT and marketing industry in the 1970s and has become a widespread
and common practice in the 21st century. The technological advancements made in the
last few decades, such as increased internet access, bandwidth, and speed, and afford­
able computers and laptops, facilitate employees to perform their work from anywhere [1].
More recently, hybrid work arrangements have experienced a drastic increase in adoption
rate due to the COVID­19 pandemic, as more and more organizations decided to adopt
hybrid work arrangements to keep their employees safe while ensuring the continued
delivery of critical services [2, 3].
After the pandemic, hybrid work arrangements have become a standard work arrange­
ment option, and organizations now face new challenges because of this transition. These
challenges are not only related to the optimization of benefits for the organization but also
to minimizing the possible setbacks of the implementation and understanding the possi­
ble trade­offs it might have, particularly concerning team dynamics and social capital [4].
If not implemented properly, hybrid work arrangements could lead to a decline in team
relationships, communication, and job satisfaction as social interactions and face­to­face
teamwork are reduced significantly at work [1, 3].
1.2
Thesis scope and goals
1.2.1
Scope
The purpose of this thesis is to investigate how the implementation of hybrid work ar­
rangements impacts team dynamics and social capital in different teams within Danish
public administrations. To achieve these objectives, the study will use a quantitative data
analysis approach, as well as a review of existing literature on hybrid work, team dy­
namics, and social capital. The quantitative data has been collected through two online
surveys. The first consists of approximately 2,000 respondents from different public ad­
ministrations across Denmark, while the second survey consists of approximately 170
respondents from specific teams selected by HK Kommunal and the SPACE project [5].
The surveys asked for employees’ experiences with hybrid work arrangements across
different dimensions, such as job satisfaction, social support from colleagues and near­
est leader, working conditions, and social interactions, among others. This data will be
analyzed using clustering techniques and classification algorithms, to determine which
areas team leaders and managers could target to improve social capital in Danish public
administrations implementing hybrid work arrangements.
The findings of this study will be of significant value to team leaders and management
teams in the different Danish public administrations who are considering implementing
hybrid work or are already using a hybrid work model. Public administrations may be able
to better support employees in hybrid work environments, and enhance team dynamics
and social capital by using these results to support policy decisions regarding these types
of work arrangements. Finally, this research will add to the increasing body of knowledge
on team dynamics, social capital, and hybrid work by offering insightful advice to leaders
and organizations.

Literature review
This section aims to describe the academic understanding of hybrid work arrangements,
team dynamics, and social capital, and provide the main definitions that will be used
throughout this thesis. Additionally, it aims to provide an overview of different algorithms
and strategies that have been used in other research projects in different contexts and

2.1
Hybrid work
2.1.1
Background
Traditionally, the concept of office work is associated with the term white­collar work[6].
This is work performed by employees who do non­manual work and interact with others
to achieve organizational goals, typically done in an office or other administrative setting.
As mentioned by Danijela Sokolic in her article Remote Work and hybrid work organiza­
tions [3], in this traditional view, workplaces or organizations are constructed around four
dimensions[3]:
• Physical dimension: physical and material spaces where work and interactions oc­
cur.
• Hierarchical dimension: power structures and hierarchical relationships with others
in the organization.
• Innovation dimension: creativity spaces where innovation and new ideas rise.
• Human dimension: all the actors interact and collaborate to accomplish work and
achieve goals for the improvement and growth of the organization. It is important
to note that these actors are immersed in a cultural, economic, social, and political
context that influences their relationships and interactions.
This traditional view associates work with physical locations, which implies that people
need to plan for and spend a specific amount of time commuting to the workplace. Over
the years, alongside this traditional view, new forms of working arrangements have been
developed, mainly because of technological developments. In particular, the development
of information and communication technology (ICT), followed by digitalization, enabled
practices of working at a physical distance from the employer’s location [3].
The idea of not working at a specific physical location is not new, but its popularity has
surged in recent years due to advances in technology, changing work preferences, and
more recently the COVID­19 pandemic. Allen et al. [1] indicate that this idea was being
explored as early as the 1970s during the oil crisis in the United States, as a way to
move work to workers to alleviate traffic problems and reduce energy consumption. At
the same time, private companies started to realize that this concept could help them
address workforce issues, especially for high­demand/low­supply positions.
With the development of ICT and advancements in technology such as personal comput­
ers, laptops, and cellphones, as well as the increase in speed and bandwidth of internet
connections, increasing demands for work­life balance, and a desire for more flexible work
arrangements, the idea of working somewhere other than the workplace started to spread

in the 1990s [1, 3]. However, its widespread adoption was propelled by the COVID­19
pandemic, which forced many organizations to adopt remote work as a temporary mea­
sure to ensure business continuity and employee safety [3].
This work practice, which allows individuals to substitute some of their typical work time
in the organization to work away from a central workplace, using technology to interact
with others as needed to complete work tasks, has been called telecommuting [1, 3].
Over the years, other terms have also been introduced to refer to this practice, such as
telework, remote work, distributed work, virtual work, flexible work, flexplace, and distance
work, among other labels [1]. While all these different terms represent alternative ways
to commuting to and from work, they also contain some distinct elements, have differ­
ent origins, and refer to different situations, which make them slightly different from one
another [3]. As Allen et al. [1] mention, even though the concept of telecommuting has
existed in the literature for decades, the different terms used to refer to it, as well as the
multiple disciplines that have approached this idea, hinder the understanding of scientific
literature about this topic, as there is no consistency nor a commonly accepted definition
[1].
2.1.2
Definition
Based on this multiplicity of concepts, it is a fundamental part of this thesis to decide on a
definition for the main terms and concepts that will be used throughout it. In this section,
three main concepts will be defined, remote work, hybrid work, and in­office work:
• In­office work will be defined, for this thesis, as a work arrangement where employ­
ees commute every day to a physical space that serves as the organization’s central
workplace and performs their work at this location. These in­office work arrange­
ments do not provide the option of working either from home or a different location
that is not directly related to the workplace.
• Remote work will be defined using the definition mentioned by Allen et al. [1] when
quoting the U.S. Office of Personnel Management [7], as, “a work arrangement in
which the employee resides and works at a location beyond the local commuting
area of the employing organization’s worksite; generally, includes full­time telework
and may result in a change in duty location to the alternative worksite” [1]. This
definition highlights the most important distinction between remote and hybrid work
to be used in this thesis, which is that remote work arrangements imply working full­
time from a location unrelated to the employer or office, such as a home, coworking
office, or other space. It could be because the employee lives far from the workplace,
or due to a work arrangement with the employer.
• Hybrid work will be used as a synonym for telecommuting as defined by Allen
et al, “Telecommuting is a work practice that involves members of an organization
substituting a portion of their typical work hours (ranging from a few hours per week
to nearly full­time) to work away from a central workplace—typically principally from
home—using technology to interact with others as needed to conduct work tasks”
[1]. This definition highlights the most important characteristic of hybrid work to be
used in this thesis, a type of work arrangement that allows the employee to decide
and adjust how much time they spend working at the office and how much time at a
different location, such as home or other space.
Allen et al. [1] indicate that this definition of hybrid work arrangements encompasses some
important characteristics for the employees that are subject to this work arrangement,
which are [1]:

• Work time that is typically spent at the central office is replaced by either work at
home (Work from Home) or other preferred location (Work from Anywhere).
• The time spent away from the central office varies from a few hours to nearly full­

• Employees are part of the organization and do not apply to external contractors or

• Employees use some sort of ICT to interact with others during the away periods.
2.1.3
Current state
Hybrid work has become one of the most prevalent forms of work arrangements in recent
years. As mentioned by Sokolic [3], what started at the beginning of the 2000’s as an
experimental practice in some companies (mainly in the IT industry) became widespread
during 2020 and 2021, due to the COVID­19 pandemic. According to the European Com­
mission [8], early estimates from Eurofound suggest that close to 40% of those currently
working in the EU began to telework full­time as a result of the pandemic [3]. As can be
seen, the pandemic played a significant role in the increase of hybrid work since, in order
to avoid destroying the entire economy, the viable path for companies was to introduce
mandatory full­time work­from­home programs for their employees to comply with social
distancing guidelines and keep employees safe [3].
However, even if the COVID­19 pandemic was the most important event to catapult hybrid
work as a work arrangement, there are several other reasons behind this increase. One
reason is the growing demand for work­life balance and flexibility. The pandemic triggered
a large­scale experiment in forcing workers around the world to work from home, and
many discovered the benefits, which typically included less time spent commuting, cost
savings, and greater flexibility [3]. As Holweg and Trevor [9] mention, the imperative
to support hybrid working is largely workforce demand. Employees — pointing to their
strong performance when they worked from home during the worst of the pandemic —
are reasonably demanding greater flexibility to work where and when they want. Many
employees are seeking more control over their work schedule and location, and hybrid
work arrangements offer them the opportunity to work from home or another location
without sacrificing the benefits of face­to­face interaction and collaboration [9].
Finally, as mentioned by Allen et al. [1] while quoting Kizza [10], as technology advances,
increasing numbers of office workers have become able to work from outside the office.
Moreover, the shift from a manufacturing to an information economy has expanded the
number of jobs that lend themselves to telecommuting [1]. More recently, the rapid ad­
vancement of technology has made hybrid work more feasible and accessible. Advances
in communication tools, video conferencing, and cloud computing have made it easier for
employees to work remotely and collaborate effectively with their colleagues.
To summarize, hybrid work is a flexible work arrangement that combines remote and on­
site work. It has become increasingly popular due to the growing demand for work­life
balance, advances in technology, and the COVID­19 pandemic. Research predicts that
the trend toward working from home will continue post­pandemic, and to a much greater
extent than pre­pandemic, but not entirely in a fully remote mode. This draws attention
to hybrid models, which offer workers more flexibility while preserving a degree of control
and stability for the employer [3].

2.2
Social capital
Social capital as a concept has been studied from multiple disciplines, such as social sci­
ences, economics, and public health; as well as at different levels, including individual,
team and organizations, and society [11, 12, 13]. In recent years, it has been increasingly
recognized as an important resource for organizations, as the COVID­19 pandemic and
the increase in hybrid work arrangements have reduced face­to­face interactions [14].
This section will explore the definition and types of social capital, with a particular focus
on bonding, bridging, and linking social capital, and their relevance to hybrid work agree­
ments.
2.2.1
Definition
As mentioned before, social capital is not a new concept. Leana III & Van Buren [12]
describe multiple initial approaches taken by researchers since the 1980s. These ap­
proaches focus on different perspectives, going from individual and small groups (micro)
to industrial networks, communities, and nations (macro).
Coleman [15], quoted by Meng et al. [16] and Brown & Livermore [17], indicates that
social capital refers to the perceived potential and direct benefits that can be obtained
by the exchange of resources and assets across relationships between people. These
relationships can occur in different contexts, such as work, organizations, communities,
and society, and have common elements that facilitate people to act together to achieve
common goals and objectives. These elements include trust, recognition, cooperation,
reciprocity, shared values and norms, and common goals [11, 16, 13].
Social capital can be seen as a form of capital, like physical, financial, or human capital.
Just like these other forms, a higher social capital is perceived as better than a lower one,
and it should be managed properly, as it changes with relationships and rewards, and
can disappear abruptly by sudden changes and events, such as contract violation and
downsizing [12].
2.2.2
Types
Depending on the type of network that connects people, the literature describes three
main types of social capital, which are bonding, bridging, and linking social capital [17,
16, 18]. These are defined as follows:
• Bonding social capital refers to the relationships and connections among individ­
uals that belong to the same group or community. These are internal ties within the
group and are characterized by strong ties and high levels of trust. Bonding social
capital aids the development of mutual support, collaboration, and collective action
or teamwork.
• Bridging social capital refers to the relationships and connections between mem­
bers of different groups of people. Bridging social capital is characterized by weak
ties and lower levels of trust. Bridging social capital helps with the expansion of
social networks, the creation of opportunities for innovation and learning, and ex­
changing of resources.
• Linking social capital refers to the relationships and connections between individ­
uals or groups who have different levels of power or authority. Individuals or groups
at the top of the hierarchy provide access to resources, information, and opportu­
nities to those at the bottom, also helping spread culture and group norms to new
members.

Organizational social capital
The focus of this thesis is the impact that hybrid work arrangements have on the social
capital of teams. Hence, I will focus on the organizational aspect of social capital as
defined by Leana III & Van Buren [12], when they mention that organizational social capital
is “a resource reflecting the character of social relations within the firm. Organizational
social capital is realized through members’ levels of collective goal orientation and shared
trust, which create value by facilitating successful collective action” [12].
Organizational social capital has two main components, associability and trust. Associa­
bility is defined as “the willingness and ability of participants in an organization to sub­
ordinate individual goals and associated actions to collective goals and actions” [12]. It
is directly related to the individuals’ capacity to work as a team and collaborate towards
common goals set by the organization. On the other hand, trust refers to the belief in
someone’s ability to accomplish a task or help the team achieve its goals. It can be clas­
sified according to the nature of the relationship between members of the organization
using two approaches[12],
Fragile vs Resilient
• Fragile: trust based on predictions or expectations that are made due to a contrac­
tual or specific situational event.
• Resilient: trust that is based on numerous links and interactions that survive past
initial transactions and is based on previous experiences with the other party (mem­
bers or organization).
Dyadic vs Generalized
• Dyadic: “trust between two parties who have direct knowledge of one another” [12].
• Generalized: trust between two parties that extends due to affiliation or reputation.
In this type, someone may be trusted without direct knowledge of them, based on
the behavior of others in relation to them.
As mentioned by Leana III & Van Buren (1999), organizations with strong social capital
exhibit resilient trust (based on previous experiences), even between members who do
not know each other directly.
A fundamental part of the development of organizational social capital is the practices and
strategies that employers implement within the organization to manage this type of social
capital. These practices can complement each other and even substitute for the need
for strong and stable relationships within the organization. The most important practices
are[12]:
• Stable relationships among organizational members: practices that promote stable
jobs and high­performance employees, but at the same time include efforts by the
organization on training and learning, job security and collaboration, and teamwork.
• Organizational reciprocity norms: in this practice, the important factor in the rela­
tionships is not the stability, but the norms and ideology in which these relationships
are developed. In this case, organizational philosophy and culture play a funda­
mental role in the building and management of social capital. It is transmitted to
new members during their onboarding, and highlights which activities and values
are important for the organization.
• Bureaucracy and specified roles: practices that define the interactions and roles

2.3.1
Definition
Team dynamics can be defined as ”the motivating and driving forces that propel a team to­

connections, and behaviors displayed by team members within a group context, and cover
how team members interact, communicate, and cooperate to accomplish shared objec­

2.3.2
Elements
Due to the scope of this thesis, I will focus on specific elements of team dynamics that are
present in the datasets. These elements can be divided into two groups: team relation­
ships, which represent and measure different interactions within the teams, and individual
factors, which represent and measures an individual’s personal opinions and desires re­
lated to the workplace. These elements are:
Team relationships
• Social support from colleagues: describes the support that team members give
to one another on an emotional and social level. It includes communication, support
for one another, social interaction, and a feeling of teamwork. Individual well­being,
work motivation, and team performance are all positively impacted by this element
[21]. This dimension differs from social capital bonding as it involves getting ad­

contrast, social capital bonding is more concerned with the common understanding,
experiences, and collective identity that create a strong sense of unity and cooper­

• Leadership: team leaders are crucial in the dynamics and performance of teams.
The role of the leader goes from motivating employees to handling stakeholders,
managing different tasks, and even serving as a role model for encouraging the
adoption of new technologies and practices in the workplace [22]. As Strömgren et
al. [13] mention, leadership influence can be split into three different forms:
1. Social support from nearest leader: similar to social support from colleagues,
social support from the nearest leader refers to the support, guidance, and
encouragement provided by the immediate supervisor. This form of support
promotes a positive work climate and increases employee motivation [22, 21].
This approach describes the focus of leadership in employee well­being and
development [13].
2. Management quality: this form describes the effectiveness of leadership and
management techniques [13]. It includes elements like effective communica­
tion, decision­making, and clarity of team structure, purpose, roles, and goals.
The success of an organization as a whole, employee satisfaction, and team
cohesion are all influenced by effective management methods [13, 22].
3. Relationships with nearest leader: This form refers to the interpersonal con­
nections between team members and their immediate supervisor, and aims
at reaching common team goals [13]. A positive and supportive relationship
with the nearest leader increases trust, open communication, and collaboration
within teams [22]. Out of the three forms, it is the most similar to social capital

Individual factors
• Job satisfaction: the subjective opinion of a person’s work experience and general

2.4
Challenges of social capital and team dynamics in hybrid
work environments
The increased implementation of hybrid work arrangements in recent years has raised
the interest of employers and researchers in understanding how these arrangements af­
fect work environments. Additionally, they have become increasingly interested in un­
derstanding the benefits, negative effects, and challenges that are associated with their
implementation. As hybrid work agreements change the way people interact and com­
municate at work, they have a significant impact on team dynamics, and the enhancing
or diminishing of the organization’s social capital.
If implemented poorly, hybrid work arrangements can diminish an organization’s social
capital due to the challenges and potential negative effects that people can experience due
to isolation and disconnection between employees, managers, and the organization as a
whole [4]. This section describes some of the challenges and possible negative effects
that hybrid work can have on social capital and team dynamics within organizations.
2.4.1
Social isolation and disconnection
As employees are constantly not present at the office there is an increased risk that they
may feel disconnected from their colleagues and the organization’s culture. Sokolic [3]
indicates that a higher degree of home­based work and reduced physical contact could
lead to weaker social ties and might contribute to psychological problems such as isolation
and depression. Additionally, the lack of social ties leads to employees identifying less
with the company, leading to organizations experiencing loyalty problems, high turnover
rates, motivation, and productivity problems, as well as a loss of corporate culture [3].

Finally, Deal & Levenson [14] highlighted that one of the main challenges companies expe­

2.4.2
Neighborhood effect
Neighborhood effect is an informal term used in some human resources circles to describe
the loss of bridging connections, and an increased dependency on bonding connections
at work, due to the reduction of in­person interactions in hybrid work arrangements. This
idea is also mentioned by Deal& Levenson [14], as they highlight that pre­established con­

arrangements. However, as hybrid work arrangements become more common, weak ties
(bridging connections) eroded considerably whilst strong ties (bonding connections) re­

working remotely, workers spend more time collaborating with their strong ties than with
their weak ties. This indicates that communication and interaction become more siloed
and less stable, with fewer connections between different teams or business units and
more connections within the team. This makes the team more dependent on their inter­

workers’ results.
As mentioned by Allen et al. [1], work is an interpersonal process within the workplace that
shapes employee attitudes and behaviors. The reduction of regular face­to­face interac­
tions could change the dynamics of work­related relationships. They continue to indicate
that the quality of relationships may be impacted by the number of days a person works
away from the office [1]. Additionally, Sokolic [3] highlights what is mentioned by McRae
& Kropp [25], that, particularly in hybrid work organizations, the lack of visibility can af­

being reduced to a specific group of employees, can affect employees’ opportunities for
promotion and career development [3].

The reduction of face­to­face interactions at the workplace can impact the innovation and
thinking processes at work. As Sokolic [3] mentions, in situations where employees are
less present at work and where bridging connections are reduced, there are concerns
about the transfer of knowledge and the sharing of ideas that lead to new value creation
[3].
Additionally, Trevor & Holweg [9] mention that, as companies switched to hybrid work
arrangements during and after the pandemic, leaders noticed a dip in the production of
new ideas, business proposals, and collaborative solutions, as mechanisms for creating,
sharing, and embedding knowledge were eroded by a reduction in network connections
[9].

2.5.2.1
Hierarchical clustering
Definition
Hierarchical clustering is an unsupervised clustering algorithm that groups similar data
points together based on patterns, structures, or relationships present in the data. Un­
like supervised algorithms, hierarchical clustering does not rely on any prior instructions
or labels to identify clusters; instead, it constructs the clusters on its own by iteratively
merging or splitting groups of data points. This algorithm provides a hierarchical struc­
ture resembling a tree that allows to visualize and interpret the resulting clusters easily,
offering valuable insights into the underlying structure of the data [28, 29].
Types of hierarchical clustering
The hierarchical clustering algorithm has two approaches depending on whether the data
is being merged or split into different clusters [28, 29, 30]:
• Divisive clustering (top­down): starts by grouping all the data points into a big
cluster. Then, it is split into smaller clusters in an iterative process until each data
point becomes its own separate cluster.
• Agglomerative clustering (bottom­up): starts by assuming that each data point
is its own cluster. Then, it merges the data points into new and bigger clusters until
it creates one final cluster that contains all data points.
Agglomerative clustering
As mentioned before, agglomerative clustering uses a bottom­up approach that starts by
placing each data point in its own cluster. It then merges different clusters into larger and
larger clusters and ends when all the data points are in one big cluster.
Agglomerative clustering requires the use of two main parameters, distance metric and
linkage method. When combined, this is called dissimilarity and is what helps the algo­
rithm to determine which clusters to combine.
The distance metric takes the form of a mathematical equation that measures how far or
close the data points in different clusters are. Some examples of the distance metrics
that can be used in the algorithm are: Euclidean distance, Manhatttan distance, and Co­
sine similarity. These distances will not be described further as their assumptions and
calculation are outside the scope of this thesis and would require an extensive section.
The linkage method is the way that the algorithm uses the distance metric equation to
calculate the distance between clusters and determines which clusters to merge. This
process can be done using different rules which aim to minimize the distance calculated
[29]:
16
Analyzing the Relationship between Hybrid Work, Team Dynamics, and Social Capital
==PAGE:25==
• Single link or nearest neighbor: the distance between two clusters is calculated as
the shortest distance between the closest two data points from the clusters.
• Complete link or farthest neighbor: the distance between two clusters is calculated
as the maximum distance between two points from the clusters.
• Average link: this method calculates the distance between two clusters as the av­
erage of all distances between the elements of the clusters.
• Centroid link: the distance between two clusters is calculated as the distance be­
tween their centroids.
• Ward method: this method minimizes the variance within clusters by evaluating the
increase in the sum of squared distances when merging two clusters.
The algorithm for agglomerative clustering uses the following steps:
1. Start by assigning each data point to an individual cluster.
2. Using the predefined distance metric and linkage method, calculate the distance or
similarity matrix of the clusters.
3. Merge the clusters that are close or similar to one another.
4. Recalculate the distance matrix.
5. Repeat steps 3 and 4 until there is only one cluster that contains all the data points.
This process can be illustrated using a graphical representation called dendrogram. This
is a tree­like structure that represents how the algorithm merges the clusters on each step.
It has a final node called root (single final cluster) and initial nodes called leaves (initial
individual clusters).

The most common hyperparameter tuning methods for random forest algorithms are Grid

• Random search: first, a random combination of hyperparameter values is selected
from a subset of all possible values. Then, the model is trained on every combination
of the values and returns the best combination. This technique randomly samples
from a grid of hyperparameters instead of conducting an exhaustive search.
• Grid search: first, a parameter space or parameter grid is defined, where a subset
of all possible hyperparameter values are included. Then, the model is trained on
every combination of hyperparameter values and returns the best combination it
could find.
Application of random forest in existing literature
Random forest and decision tree algorithms have been used in social capital research.
Gutiérrez et al. [33] used the feature importance of a random forest model with 10,000
decision trees to identify the most important variables that contributed to the success or
not of 130 co­managed fisheries in different countries. They identified strong leadership as
the most important attribute contributing to success and indicated that it was important for
the fisheries to invest more resources in identifying good community leaders and building
social capital, rather than only imposing management tactics without users’ involvement.
Another use of random forest and decision trees is the one made by Bai et al. [34]. In
their article, they explore the relationship between self­rated health and social capital in
older people with and without health conditions in the Anhui province of China. They use a
logistic regression algorithm and classification and regression tree (CART) to analyze the
information obtained from a survey that included sociodemographic information, social
capital, and self­rated health. By interpreting the results of both methods and the node
split criterion of the decision tree, they were able to determine that social support and
reciprocity were positively associated with self­rated health, suggesting that high­level
social support could be a protective factor against poor self­rated health in older people.
Additionally, they indicated that this relationship was affected by the presence of health
conditions, as older people with health conditions may be less willing to communicate or
interact with others, leading to lower social capital.

3.1
Research design
To address the research question, “How does the implementation of hybrid work arrange­
ments impact the team dynamics and the development and maintenance of social capi­
tal among workers in Danish public administrations?“ I used a quantitative research ap­
proach. This research design involves the collection and analysis of numerical data to
address research questions or test hypotheses. It places a strong emphasis on using
statistical techniques to obtain meaningful insights from the data [35].
Quantitative research design involves the analysis of previously collected data through
surveys or questionnaires. The surveys used to collect the data for this thesis were de­
signed to capture relevant variables such as the number of days working from home, job
satisfaction, motivation, social support from colleagues and nearest colleagues and lead­
ers, and social capital. These variables were measured using Likert scale items or other
appropriate scales.
The data sets used in this thesis were collected in two stages: a larger survey which
had approximately 2000 respondents and a smaller survey with 170 respondents. The
larger survey was used to generate clusters through agglomerative clustering techniques
based on respondents’ answers to the survey. These clusters were characterized based
on their mean scores and using statistical techniques such as ANOVA and post­hoc tests.
Additionally, it was used to train and calibrate a random forest model that is able to predict
the cluster label of respondents based on their answers.
The smaller survey was used to analyze specific teams of interest for the SPACE project.
This data was processed using the calibrated random forest model to predict the cluster of
each respondent. This helped to analyze team composition and characteristics based on
cluster distribution. Additionally, teams were compared using an internal benchmarking
method suggested by the National Research Center for Working Environment in Denmark
(NFA) that provided additional insights and confirmed the findings of the cluster analysis.
The sampling of the two data sets was unrelated. The larger survey was sent to 10,000
members of HK Kommunal and is a randomly selected and representative sample of the
population. The small survey was sent to 11 specific teams that were selected through a
collaboration between HK Kommunal and DTU Management.
The findings from this quantitative analysis were interpreted and discussed based on the
research questions and hypothesis. The quantitative research design provided robust
evidence to support the investigation into the relationship between hybrid work arrange­
ments, team dynamics, and social capital, offering valuable insights for both academia
and practitioners in managing and optimizing hybrid work environments.

Clustering
I used a hierarchical clustering algorithm in the SPACE project data to group the respon­
dents into clusters based on their responses. For this and the classification model and
calibration stage, I decided to remove the social capital bonding and social capital bridging
dimensions from the data set. This was done to analyze whether the remaining variables

these dimensions to analyze themselves, which could lead to biased or wrong conclusions
later in the analysis.
For the hierarchical clustering algorithm, I decided to use an agglomerative clustering ap­
proach as it is less computationally intensive and is included in many recognized Python
libraries, such as sklearn and scipy. Additionally, I used a combination of Euclidean dis­
tance and Ward’s method as this provides robust results and is one of the most common
combinations used in the literature [31]. The dendrogram obtained, and the means of the
total scores of the dimensions, were used to define the appropriate number of clusters
for this thesis. A new column was added to the data set that indicates which cluster each
observation belongs to. An Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) and Tuckey’s HDS tests were
performed on the clusters to confirm whether the variable means differed across clusters,
and if so, which clusters varied from one another.
Classification model and calibration
Using the assigned cluster for each observation of the SPACE data as a data label, I
trained a random forest algorithm to predict the cluster that a respondent would belong
to, based on their responses to the survey. This was done by splitting the SPACE data
set into two segments, a training set that accounted for 80% of the data set, and a test
set that accounted for the remaining 20%. After this initial calibration, a Randomized
Search and a Grid Search were used in the random forest model to determine whether it
was possible to find hyperparameters that could improve the model, such as the number
of trees used, the maximum amount of levels of the trees, the minimum and maximum
number of observations for each subset, and others.
Team specific survey prediction
Once the model was ready and calibrated, I ran the team­specific survey data through the
random forest model to predict the cluster that each respondent belonged to. By doing
this, I was able to analyze the general characteristics of each team based on the cluster
composition. By adding the social capital bonding and social capital bridging dimensions
back in to the data set I was able to analyze the relationship between the teams’ clusters,
characteristics, and social capital.
Each team was also analyzed using the methodology proposed by the NFA Questionnaire
[40]. I calculated the overall dimension values by averaging the values of all team mem­
bers, and the standard deviation of the dimensions. The overall score was used to analyze
the teams, and by calculating the standard deviation of the dimensions of each team, I
was able to calculate the degree of agreement between employees within the teams. Fi­
nally, I was able to compare the teams with each other using their overall scores, which
is called internal benchmarking, and against the overall scores of all the teams.

variables such as MIA or STL are on a scale of 0 to 100, whereas Job satisfaction is
on a scale of 1 to 10. To standardize each data point, the mean of the overall scores is
subtracted from the dimension score and then divided by the standard deviation. This

both identifying true positives and avoiding false positives.

Summary of results
To summarize, it can be shown that in recent years Danish public administrations’ employ­
ees have shifted from mainly working at the office to choosing hybrid work arrangements.
This can be seen by the number of employees that spend more time working from home
in both the SPACE and team­specific surveys. Additionally, it is important to notice that
the respondents in the team­specific survey tend to work relatively more days from home
than the ones from the SPACE survey. This will be used to characterize and compare
findings from both surveys in the following chapter. Using the data from the SPACE sur­
vey, I identified different clusters that group respondents according to their responses to
the survey. After reviewing the information from the possible total number of groups, 5
clusters were selected. These clusters were:
• High Social Capital (HSC): includes respondents with high scores in their answers
and who tend to work two or more days from home per week.
• Low Social Capital (LSC): includes respondents with low scores in their answers
and who tend to work less than a day from home per week.
• Mid Social Capital and High Leadership and Motivation (MSC_HL_HM): includes
respondents with mid scores in their answers and who tend to work less than a day
from home per week.
• Mid Social Capital and Low Leadership and High Motivation (MSC_LL_HM): in­
cludes respondents with low scores in the leadership dimensions and mid scores in
their other work environment dimension. Respondents from this group tend to work

• Mid Social Capital and High Leadership and Low Motivation (MSC_HL_LM): in­

and mid scores in their other work environment dimension. Respondents from this
group tend to work less than a day from home per week.
Using ANOVA and Tukey post­hoc tests, it was shown that the clusters are significantly

A random forest model was trained and calibrated using the responses from the SPACE

Discussion
This chapter will provide a more in­depth discussion and analysis of the results of this

5.1
Discussion
This thesis aims to analyze the relationship between hybrid work, team dynamics, and so­
cial capital in Danish public administrations. The key results highlight important aspects
that contribute to the understanding of this relationship and provide insights into the inter­
action of these concepts among teams in Danish public administrations. In this discussion
section, I will focus on the key findings and reflect on their implications in relation to the
literature and research problem.
5.1.1
Current hybrid work panorama
The transition to hybrid work has impacted teams’ dynamics and organizations’ social
capital. This transition has changed the concept of the workplace by dissociating it from
the concept of a central office or other administrative setting. Additionally, it has rede­
fined some of the most relevant features of how employees interact, communicate, and
cooperate with each other [3].
The initial results obtained by analyzing the information from both surveys, show a sig­
nificant shift from in­office work to hybrid work among respondents after the COVID­19
pandemic. This aligns with the broader trend seen after the pandemic, where organiza­
tions and employees have promoted the adoption of hybrid work arrangements [41], and
shows the change in the working landscape as hybrid work options are more widely avail­
able. This could signal to Danish public administrations that offering flexible hybrid work
arrangements where employees are able to choose the number of days they work from
home may be highly appreciated and valued. These options could increase motivation
and job satisfaction in employees as they could choose the number of days according
to their preferences and needs, adjusting the arrangements to have better working con­
ditions and increase their work­life balance and flexibility accordingly. This can be seen
in the desires expressed by respondents in the two optional open questions about their
experience working at home and at the office. Some answers given by respondents can
be seen below.

of mind, as well as increased work­life balance and job satisfaction and motivation, which
are highly regarded benefits in the new working environment post­pandemic. In contrast,
as Figure 4.3 shows, they indicate that working at the office is more associated with noise
and interruptions, as well as face­to­face interactions and having a room or larger place
to work. It is important to notice that the data used for this thesis relied on self­reported
measures and personal experiences, which could add a potential bias to the analysis.
It is also important to state that there appeared to be a trend of employees that work
from home 4 or 5 days per week prior to the pandemic, and now they have shifted to
fewer number of days working from home, as can be seen in Table 4.1 and Table 4.2.
This aligns with what is mentioned by Sokolic [3] that workers tend to prefer working from
home for a period of time, but not completely remote. This last trend could be a response
to social isolation and lack of face­to­face interaction, as well as the increased levels of
stress and lack of boundaries between work and home. On the other hand, this trend
could also be explained by organizations limiting the number of days that an employee
work from home in an attempt to build social connections and ease communication and
interactions within and among teams post­pandemic. Finally, this trend could be related to
changes in the respondents’ working or personal conditions prior to the pandemic which
may not be consistent with their current ones. These downsides are associated with fully
remote work arrangements or hybrid options with a high number of days working from
home.
This shows that hybrid work arrangements are not a one­size fits all approach and Danish
public administrations need to develop practices and strategies to properly offer and man­
age options that fit with employees’ needs, as well as promote teams’ associability and
improve their social capital. Some of these practices might involve providing managers
or team leaders with simple­to­use diagnostic tools to understand their teams’ personal
preferences, work context, and tasks to better support their needs of cooperation, com­
munication, and interaction toward the teams’ goals [4].

5.2
Limitations
The limitations of this thesis will be briefly discussed in this section, as they should be
taken into consideration for future research.
First, the data collection relied on self­reported answers, which may be biased as respon­
dents may have provided what they believed were socially desired answers. Additionally,
these responses could have been affected by personal experiences or situations prior to
answering the survey. Future work might benefit from repeating the survey regularly as
it could reduce the impact of personal feelings or specific situations and could provide a
way to compare teams’ scores evolution over time.
Second, the data from this thesis is related to employees of Danish public administrations,
which needs to be taken into account if trying to use these findings in other industries or
contexts. Using data from a different industry and /or work setting might yield results
that are different from the ones presented in this thesis. This also accounts for the five
clusters found by the agglomerative clustering algorithm as they are specific to the chosen
variables and clustering algorithm. Changing the variables used, the distance or linking
method, or the clustering technique might produce a different number of clusters and
group characteristics.
Finally, the internal benchmarking method provided useful insights by comparing team
mean values to the overall mean value for the work environment dimensions. However,
these comparisons and findings are related to the specific teams represented in the team­
specific responses, and in the context of Danish public administrations, which needs to
be taken into account as they may not reflect wider population trends.

Conclusions
This chapter presents the main conclusions of this thesis, obtained from the analysis of
the relationship between hybrid work, team dynamics, and social capital in Danish public

6.1
Research goal
How do hybrid work arrangements affect team dynamics and social capital among
employees in Danish public administrations?
This thesis has explored the impact of implementing hybrid work arrangements on team
dynamics and social capital among workers in Danish public administrations. Through an
in­depth cluster analysis approach, valuable insights have been gained that could help
Danish public administrations to develop better practices and strategies to improve team
dynamics and social capital in hybrid work environments. The results from this thesis
highlighted the following key insights:
• The implementation of hybrid work arrangements in Danish public administration
has changed the way employees communicate, collaborate and interact with others
within their teams and with other teams. These changes have impacted teams’
dynamics as new challenges appear in hybrid work environments. These challenges
are related to, 1) the use of technology; 2) trust and willingness to work towards a
common goal; 3) the development and maintenance of social relationships; and 4)
leadership quality and decision in these new hybrid environments. These challenges
may signal to the Danish public administrations that the practices and strategies
currently in place might need to be reevaluated to properly offer and manage options
that fit with employees’ needs, as well as promote teams’ collaboration and improve
their social capital.
• The cluster analysis approach employed in this study showed distinct underlying
patterns and profiles within the respondents based on their answers to the survey.
The five identified clusters helped to categorize respondents into different groups
and highlighted the groups’ characteristics, strengths, and challenges. This showed
the different perceptions among employees and the importance of tackling the spe­
cific needs and challenges identified within each cluster to promote a positive work
environment and improve employee well­being and performance.
• The analysis of the feature importance of the Random Forest algorithm showed that,
for the model’s logic, work environment dimensions are significantly more important
than the background dimensions in the decision of which employee should be as­
signed to a cluster based on their responses to the survey. This suggests that the
main focus of the policies and strategies developed by the Danish public adminis­
trations should be, 1) improving collaboration and communication channels through
new technologies; 2) promoting a positive work environment within the teams; and
3) improving leadership skills, and not necessarily focusing on the number of days
employees in a team work from home.

How can organizations use AI and ML approaches to analyze data related to hybrid

This thesis has explored the application of Machine Learning algorithms to analyze sur­
vey data related to hybrid work and team dynamics. The findings of this research demon­

dynamics and social capital, providing organizations with actionable insights to focus the

parameters could also provide different findings.
• Context, industry, and time: the data is related to a specific context and industry,
and collected at a precise moment in time, any change to these variables might
yield different results.
Overall, using Machine Learning algorithms, such as clustering techniques and random
forest models, might allow organizations to effectively analyze data related to hybrid work
and team dynamics. This could lead to a deeper understanding of the factors influenc­

public administrations can use a data­driven decision approach to improve employee sat­

This thesis has examined the relationship between hybrid work, team dynamics, and so­
