Abstract
The climate crisis and the global energy market disruption caused by the war in Ukraine
have amplified the need for urgent solutions in clean energy technology and cutting gas
imports. Heat pumps will play one of the key roles in achieving the ambitious energy
transition goals. The number of newly installed units will require developing new solutions
for optimizing the operation and maintenance of the heat pumps.
This study proposes a methodology for developing data-driven fault detection algorithms
using steady-state operational data and machine learning techniques. Two algorithms,
Logistic regression and CUSUM, were examined for refrigerant leakage and reduced brine
flow fault detection. The literature review highlighted a research gap in applying data-
driven fault detection methods to operating energy systems. Operational data from a
residential building in the Netherlands, obtained through collaboration with Metro Therm
A/S, was used for algorithms validation and fine-tuning. Laboratory tests simulated faults
and provided insights into the PVT heat pump system behaviour under faulty operation.
The developed algorithms showed encouraging performance outcomes. Logistic regres-
sion exhibited a high F1 score (from 0.951 to 1.0) on laboratory test datasets, but lower
accuracy on the residential building validation dataset (0.8), while CUSUM achieved high
scores on both datasets (from 0.991 to 0.999). The developed algorithms are scalable and
capable of detecting faults in a residential building with multiple PVT heat pump systems.
Further improvements and adaptations are recommended for future method development.

Introduction
The climate crisis and the global energy market disruption caused by the war in Ukraine
have amplified the need for urgent solutions in clean energy technology and cutting gas
imports. In this context, EU Commission presented REPowerEU plan, where heat pumps
will play one of the key roles in achieving the ambitious energy transition goals [1]. Such
purposes require the number of newly installed heat pumps in Europe double yearly. The
European Heat Pump Association estimates this would lead to an additional 60 million
heat pumps installed by 2030 [2].
The number of newly installed units will require developing new solutions for optimizing
the operation and maintenance of the heat pumps (HPs). The use of machine learning
(ML) methods for fault detection and diagnosis (FDD) in heating, ventilation, and air condi-
tioning (HVAC) systems is becoming increasingly popular. Researchers and practitioners
are exploring various ML techniques, such as supervised and unsupervised learning, to
improve the efficiency and accuracy of fault detection and diagnosis [3].
FDD definition, described by Katipamula and Brambley [4], is consisting of three key pro-
cesses: fault detection, fault isolation, and fault identification. The first, fault detection,
is the process of determining that some fault has occurred in the system. The second
involves isolating the specific fault that occurred, including determining the kind of fault,
the location of the fault, and the time of detection. The third process, fault identification,
includes determining the size and time-variant behavior of a fault. Together, fault isolation
and fault identification are commonly termed fault diagnosis.
Finding anomalous behavior of the HVAC systems turns out to be on the most commer-
cially important applications of ML methods, because it allows to identify buildings that
have unusually high energy consumption. Buildings affected by faults in the heating and
cooling systems, could lead to waste of about 4 % – 18 % of the total energy consumed
by HVAC systems [5]. Hence, applying FDD methods could lead to significant energy
savings and improve the reliability of the system. It is challenging to apply a FDD method
developed for one equipment to another directly [6]. Therefore, further studies of ML
techniques are necessary to develop reliable FDD methods in practical applications.
The HP system analyzed in the study was designed by the Danish manufacturer Metro
Therm A/S. The company has already deployed a number of units in the Netherlands,
and monitors their performance. This study aims to analyze faults affecting the systems
and propose how to use the operational data retrieved from the HP systems for the FDD
purposes. Based on the accident frequency and importance, the two faults for detection
are proposed by the Metro Therm A/S company: reduced brine flow rate in the heat source
loop and refrigerant leakage.
1.1
PVT heat pump system
The FDD model is developed for the liquid-water HP units with the commercial name
”METRO Delta” [7]. HPs provide space heating, domestic hot water, and passive cooling.
The HPs are mainly operating in the space heating mode. In this context, the hot water
and cooling modes are excluded from the study to simplify the analysis.
Each HP unit has a capacity of 4.7 kW and is filled with 150 grams of R290 (propane), a
natural refrigerant with a low climate impact. The COP value is 4.7 at a heat source 5 °C,
heat supply of 35 °C, and outside temperature 12 °C [7].

1.2
Objectives
The present research was initiated in order to propose a methodology for developing FDD
methods and tools, using steady state performance data for the PVT heat pump system.
The primary objective is to detect the presence of a fault or anomaly. It aims to provide an
early warning that the HP system is not functioning as intended. Fault detection involves
monitoring variables such as temperature and pump speed. If any of these variables
exceed predefined thresholds or deviate significantly from expected patterns, it triggers
an alarm or notification to signal the presence of a fault.
The thesis project focuses on FDD techniques, recognizing the severity level and indicat-
ing the time frame it occurred. A more detailed analysis to pinpoint the exact location of
the fault and the root cause is not investigated in the project.
Laboratory tests have to be performed to obtain faulty labeled data. It is needed for training
the supervised machine learning algorithm. Additionally, the laboratory training set helps
to create a more realistic representation of the data distribution. It enhances the model’s
ability to handle similar instances during fault detection in a real-world scenario.
In conclusion, the primary thesis objective of building the FDD algorithms consists of the
following steps:
• Perform laboratory testing for gathering faulty labeled data set.
• Create a new methodology for building the fault detection algorithms.
• Build data-driven FDD algorithms for detecting if/when a fault has occurred in the
HP system.
• Classify the fault type and severity level.
• Validate the fault detection model on the residential building operational data set.

2
Literature review
This chapter covers the most relevant publications within the field of FDD methods. An
overview of the most popular machine learning algorithms for the FDD is presented. Two
algorithms are described in separate sections, namely Logistic Regression and Cumu-
lative sum. They are used for building the FDD model in this study. Additionally, the
literature about the reduced brine flow rate and refrigerant leakage is reviewed. The main
indicators of these faults are highlighted.
2.1
FDD methods
Katimapula and Brambley [4] described several methods used to detect and diagnose
faults. The major difference in these approaches is the a priori knowledge used for for-
mulating the methods. It can be classified as knowledge-driven models or data-driven
models.
Methods in the knowledge driven-based subcategory rely heavily on domain knowledge.
Every fault detection rule or model is developed based on prior knowledge which gen-
erally has clear physical meanings. On the contrast, methods in the data driven-based
subcategory detect faults through finding the changes of patterns in the measurements
of selected variables. In this way, they can benefit from the powerful machine learning
algorithms, that can extract patterns for FDD automatically [6].
The data driven-based FDD methods can be further classified into supervised learning
based and unsupervised learning-based. Supervised learning requires labeled data and
focuses on predicting fault labels based on provided examples. In contrast, unsupervised
learning explores the inherent patterns in the data to detect anomalies or deviations from
normal behavior without explicit labels [6]. The graphical presentation of the main meth-
ods used in building energy systems are shown in Fig. 2.1:
Figure 2.1: Classification of the FDD methods for building energy systems
The supervised learning based methods include such algorithms as Artificial Neural Net-
works, Support Vector Machines, Decision tree, Logistic regression. The most popular
algorithms for unsupervised learning are density based clustering, K-means clustering,
Gaussian Mixture Models, Principle Component Analysis, CUSUM [3] [6].
A comprehensive literature survey by Zhao et al.[6] revealed that 79 % of the HVAC fault
detection papers published from 1998 to 2018 implemented data-driven methods. In com-
parison, only 21 % of 135 papers were related to knowledge-based methods.

There are several reasons why data-driven models are more popular. They can learn pat-
terns from data automatically, do not require deep domain knowledge, and are relatively
easy to develop. They can yield good accuracy results even if some crucial variables or
sensor measurements are missing.
For example, a variable speed heat pump study by Boahen et al. [9] showed good refrig-
erant charge detection results by utilizing Linear regression and Artificial Neural Network
algorithms. The FDD model consisted of 3 input variables: compressor discharge tem-
perature, compressor speed and condenser inlet water temperature. The Artificial Neural
Network, yielded 96 % accuracy score. The Linear regression model showed a slightly
worse score (95 %).
A logistic regression fault detection model developed by Aguilera et al. [10] could classify
an experimental chiller’s 94 % faulty and fault-free operational points. In comparison,
other supervised ML algorithms showed significantly lower accuracy results. The decision
tree and random forest classifiers yielded 74 % accuracy score, and Naive Bayes showed
the lowest score (37 %).
A method called CUSUM that identifies cumulative differences over time in specific indi-
cators has been used for fault detection for heat pumps and energy systems. Liu et al.
[11] study proposed a CUSUM method for an abnormal energy consumption and R410A
refrigerant undercharge diagnosis in a variable refrigerant flow heat pump system. The
CUSUM process control chart detected 15 % - 37 % reduced refrigerant load at a high ac-
curacy. The diagnosis efficiency presented in the study was 99.86 % - 100 %. A study by
Puranik [12] also showed high CUSUM method efficiency for detecting abnormal changes
in the energy consumption process.
Although CUSUM belongs to a data-driven FDD category it typically requires some knowl-
edge or assumptions about what constitutes normal behavior and the expected magni-
tude of the changes. Additional statistics tools are needed for the relevant fault detection
features selection. Liu et al. [11] applied the Pearson’s correlation analysis to evalu-
ate the strength and direction between the sensor measurements and power consump-
tion. Based on the correlation coefficient and domain knowledge, three features were
selected as the leading indicators of abnormal power consumption. Namely, compressor
frequency, compressor discharge temperature, and condenser saturation temperature.
The disadvantage of the data-driven methods is that large amounts of data representing
both normal and faulty operations are needed for training the model. The residential
building heating system sensors are usually installed for control purposes only. Therefore
the data for developing high accuracy fault detection data-driven models are often scarce
[4].
It was generalized by Zhao et al. [6] that most of the existing methods were evaluated us-
ing simulation or experimental data only. Hence, concluding that the same performance
can be obtained in practical applications is unreasonable. Also, too much attention was
paid to introducing more powerful machine learning algorithms. Although a lot of their
methods were very similar. Only a few attentions were on how to improve the methodolo-
gies based on a deep understanding of the energy system.

3.3.3
k-fold cross-validation
Machine learning algorithm uses one part of a dataset for training (adjust its structure
based on observations) and another one for testing or validation (compare its outputs
with unseen observations) Feature selection is generally an integral part of the FDD al-
gorithm building process. Here, it is crucial that predictive features are selected using
only the training set, not the entire dataset; otherwise, the output of a machine learning
algorithm may be incorrect when predicting unseen data [22]. Therefore, cross-validation
(CV) is applied as a data resampling method to assess the fault detection algorithm and
to prevent overfitting. Overfitting occurs when a model becomes too closely tailored to the
training data, capturing noise and irrelevant patterns. If the F1 scores in both training and
validation sets are similar, it indicates that the algorithm can generalize well to unseen
data.
The model is trained and evaluated k times. In each iteration, one fold is used as the
validation set, while the remaining k-1 folds are used as the training set. This process is
repeated k times, with each fold being used as the validation set exactly once. Illustration
of 10-fold cross-validation is shown in Fig. 3.6. The data set is randomly split into ten
disjoint subsets, each containing 10% of the data.

given in the literature, but as a rule of thumb, at least 3-4 observations are recommend if
the distribution does not depart radically from the normal [24]. The decision is based on
the probability central limit theorem, which states that the distribution of the mean values
approximates to a normal distribution if the observation sample size is large enough.
The HP operation is considered to be faulty if either C+ or C−exceeds the decision interval
H, The size of the H value defines how large the sum C+ or C−has to be accumulated
before labeling the operation as faulty. The decision interval H of the CUSUM is computed
as follows:
H = hσ

Where σ is the standard deviation. The recommended value for the decision limit coeffi-
cient h, that provides good average length performance is 2.5 [24]. The upper CUSUM
limit (UCL) is the positive H value, the lower CUSUM limit (LCL) is the negative H value.
3.5
Feature selection for the fault detection algorithm
3.5.1
Recursive Feature Elimination for LogReg algorithm
Recursive Feature Elimination (RFE) is a technique used for feature selection in machine
learning models. RFE is a recursive algorithm that iteratively eliminates less important
features until the subset of features that yields the best accuracy score is determined.
This technique is implemented in Python, by using the feature selection module in the
package scikit-learn [25].
RFE starts by considering all the features in the dataset as the initial set. Then the ML
model is trained on the entire set of features. After training the model, the algorithm
Fault detection of PVT-heat pump system using machine learning methods
21
==PAGE:36==
evaluates the importance of each feature. The feature importance in a logistic regres-
sion algorithm is derived from the weights assigned to the features. The feature with the
lowest importance is eliminated from the feature set. Afterward, the model is retrained
on the reduced feature set. The algorithm evaluates the model’s performance using a
predefined metric, such as F1 score, throughout the elimination process. Finally, RFE
algorithm identifies the subset of features that yields the best performance according to
the evaluation metric.
3.5.2
Spearman’s correlation coefficient
The Spearman’s correlation coefficient is a popular statistical measure used in data min-
ing [26]. It quantifies the strength and direction of the monotonic relationship between
continuous and ordinal variables. It is a non-parametric measure, which means it does
not assume a normal distribution for the data. It is used as one of the measures for the
feature selection for the fault detection algorithm because of it’s robustness to evaluate
datasets in which the distribution of the classes is highly imbalanced.
Spearman’s correlation coefficient ranges from -1 to +1, where a value of -1 indicates a
strong negative relationship, +1 indicates a strong positive relationship, and 0 indicates

BNR 80 heat pump. Hence, it is not known if this apartment have signaled anomalous
behaviour in the next space heating season.
The rest of the apartments had a fault fraction below 40 %. That might be flagged due to
accidental HP operational deviations from the reference measurement value. Therefore,
only HP’s over 40 % faulty sample fraction are considered as having a high fault likelihood.
This value is arbitrary and can be adjusted depending on the fault detection importance.

5
Discussion
This chapter provides insights into the effectiveness and reliability of the implemented
FDD methodologies. It aims to contribute to understanding of the reduced brine flow and
refrigerant leakage faults in the PVT heat pump systems. The methods applied in the
study and their relationship to the related literature publications are investigated. The
implications, possibilities and limitations of the proposed methods are discussed.
5.1
Literature review
It was noticed in the literature review that there is a lack of research for the practical ap-
plication of the data-driven models. The comprehensive FDD literature study by Zhao
et al. [6] concluded that most researchers are focusing on developing complex machine
learning algorithms for fault detection, which are similar to each other. Often the model
validation on the operational data from the building energy systems is missing in the pub-
lications as well. To fill this gap, the cooperation with the Metro Therm A/S company
allowed to acquire the operational data from a residential building to validate the fault
detection method developed in this study.
5.2
Refrigerant leakage
The findings in the refrigerant leakage laboratory test confirmed most of the general trends
found in literature [13][15][16]. For example, the measured electric power ˙Wel decreased
up to 13 % under faulty operation conditions. Boahen et al. [16] recorded a more signif-
icant decrease of 20 % during his laboratory test. Although it is complicated to compare
absolute values for different HP systems, the general trend can be explained: because
of the lower condensing pressure and pressure ratio, compressor did less work in the
refrigerant undercharge conditions.
However, the positive effect on compressor work was out-weighted by the reduced heating
capacity. Due to the lower mass of refrigerant in the system, the heating capacity had
decreased by up to 49 %. Eventually, the COP dropped down to 43 % in the most severe
RL40 case. The same trend was also described in the RP-1275 report [13]. The lowered
heating capacity means that the HP unit will have to operate for a longer time to generate
the same amount of heat compared to the fault-free operation.
The condenser subcooling temperature was recorded as the main indicator of the refrig-
erant leakage both in laboratory test and literature [13][15][16]. The temperature sensor
T7 fixed in the subcooling zone of the HX showed a high variation of measurements under
faulty operation during laboratory testing (Fig. 4.4).
The unexpected behavior was noticed when comparing RL20 and RL40 dT T7−T5 tem-
perature measurements, where the lower severity RL20 case showed 0.8 °C degrees
larger temperature difference than the RL40 case. Although the absolute value was not
significant, it complicated the threshold setup for detecting the RL20 refrigerant leakage
case. The RL40 severity fault would be detected before the RL20 due to the lower thresh-
old in CUSUM algorithm.
To solve this issue, a second feature, the compressor discharge temperature T6 was
selected to differentiate between the RL20 and R40 severity levels. It was recorded during
testing that in the RL40 case, the T6 temperature was 5.4 °C degrees higher than the
RL20, which was a strong leakage indicator.

The reason why the T6 temperature increased significantly only for the RL40 case, can be
related to the TXV valve component. The TXV expansion valve is able to compensate the
refrigerant loss by controlling it’s opening degree. If the refrigerant charge is slightly lower
than nominal, the valve would adjust the superheating by opening more and allowing a
larger amount of refrigerant into the evaporator. At some point, the expansion valve would
no longer be able to compensate for refrigerant loss and the evaporator pressure would
drop and the superheat would increase [14]. It is possible that in the most severe RL40
case the valve was operating fully open, similarly like an orifice valve. Eventually affecting
the compressor discharge temperature measurement.
It was also noticed in literature by Hundy [17], that low refrigerant charge might lead to
compressor overheating. Therefore monitoring the compressor discharge temperature is
beneficial for avoiding damage to the compressor in the long run as well.
The findings in the literature regarding the directional change of the T6 discharge tem-
perature in undercharge conditions were not uniform (Table 2.2). The ASHRAE RP-1275
report showed that the change in discharge temperate was insignificant [13], same as in
the RL10 and R20 laboratory test (Fig. 4.5). Boahen et al. [16] recorded the reduced dis-
charge temperature and Hundy [17] highlighted the compressor overheating issue. The
mismatch between the trends is primarily due to different types of compressors and re-
frigerants tested in the experiments described in the literature. Therefore the temperature
threshold acquired from the Metro Therm A/S laboratory test is expected to be the most
accurate for the given PVT heat pump system.
The speed of the source pump GP1 was observed to decrease under the presence of
refrigerant undercharge, except for the RL20 case. The RL20 case had a higher average
pump speed than RL10. That also explains why the total power consumption is higher
in the RL20 case, as shown in Fig. 4.2. The most likely explanation is that performing
tests on different HP modules might lead to discrepancies in the test results, even if the
component design is identical.
The measured sink pump GP2 speed looked like a strong fault indicator. Under faulty
operation, the circulation pump was working at a lower speed because of the reduced
heating capacity of the HP. The GP2 pump operated at the minimum speed (40 %) at
the highest severity level RL40, throughout all the test time (Fig. 4.10). There is no cir-
culation pump speed effect described in the literature review confirming this behaviour.
Nonetheless, using pump speed for the fault detection in residential apartments might
lead to inaccurate results, because it is affected by floor heating valves operation and
pressure drop in the heating system. The influence of floor heating control devices was
not involved in the laboratory test. Therefore, adding a sink circulation pump speed to the
fault detection model needs further investigation.
The GP2 pump speed, in combination with four additional features: T6 compressor dis-
charge temperature, dT T7 −T5, T2 source outlet temperature and dTsinkT4 −T5, com-
posed LogReg RL10 algorithm (Table 4.5). In comparison, the CUSUM RL10 fault detec-
tion showed excellent fault detection results with a single dT T7 −T5 feature (Table 4.9).
The reason why CUSUM was able to detect refrigerant leakage with a lower number of
features compared to LogReg algorithm, lies in the difference of the sample classifica-
tion. The LogReg algorithm classifies each sample separately. If the faulty and fault-free
measurements distribution overlap (Fig. 4.23) the algorithm misclassifies the observation,
when too few features are used. It is especially relevant for the low severity RL10 case,
which has many overlapping measurements. Therefore, multiple features were added to
54

==PAGE:69==
the model to capture the relationship between the faulty class and different sensor mea-
surements. The irrelevant features were penalized by a low weight coefficient to diminish
the influence on the class prediction. Additionally, the recursive feature elimination (RFE)
algorithm was applied to find the optimal number of features and reduce the overfitting.
The CUSUM algorithm, instead of classifying each observation separately, accumulates
the residuals over time. Therefore, even slight HP operation discrepancies are summed
up. As it was noticed during testing, the largest deviation from the fault-free operation was
seen when the source inlet temperature T1 and the temperature lift were high (Fig. 4.1).
Hence, the fault detection algorithm is expected to perform best during high solar radiance
and hot temperature water supply demand.
For the more severe RL20 case both LogReg and CUSUM algorithms showed high fault
detection accuracy with a single feature dT T7 −T5. As discussed previously, significant
difference between the faulty and fault-free measurements allowed to detect the fault with
the lower number of features for the LogReg algorithm.
The CUSUM algorithm was selected for fault detection in Barendrecht apartments be-
cause of the higher sensitivity for the low fault severity level. The HPs technicians’ main-
tenance log had no records related to the refrigerant leakage. Therefore the fault detection
model was not validated on the apartments data set. Nonetheless, CUSUM algorithm de-
tected one apartment as possibly having a slight refrigerant undercharge (Table 4.15).
The fault was spotted on the last day of available heat pump operational dataset. Hence
it is complicated to conclude if the fault appeared also during further HP operation.
5.3
Reduced brine flow
The initial expectation for the reduced brine flow laboratory test result was to detect the
change in the heat source GP1 pump speed during the faulty operation. Based on the
GP1 pump control strategy explained in Section 3.2, it had to operate at a higher speed
in case of a flow restriction in the piping loop to compensate for the increased pressure
drop.
During the laboratory test, it was recorded that the circulation pump worked close to the full
capacity limit at fault-free operation. Hence, the clogged filter had only a minor influence
on the pump speed (Fig. 4.14). The test results suggested of removing GP2 pump speed
from the fault detection model.
Instead, the more significant change was recorded for the dTsourceT1 −T2 temperature
difference (Fig. 4.12). On average, the faulty operation had a 1.3 °C higher temperature
compared to the fault-free dataset. It matched the fault trend described in RP-1275 report
(Fig. 2.2), where the temperature difference increased in the range from 0.5 °C to 2.5
°C. It was also noticed during the restricted flow testing that the brine temperature T2
leaving the evaporator was lower compared to the fault-free operation temperature by 1.2
°C on average (Table 4.3). It can be concluded that the brine was cooled down to a lower
temperature in the evaporator HX, eventually increasing the dTsourceT1−T2 temperature
difference.
The laboratory test findings correlated with the LogReg algorithm’s weight coefficients.
The dTsourceT1−T2 and T2 source outlet temperature had the largest weight coefficients,
accordingly 6.18 and -1.73 (Table 4.4). They were indicating that dTsourceT1 −T2 feature
had the most substantial impact on the fault probability function. The sink pump GP2
was ranked 3rd, and the GP1 source pump had a lower ranking (5th). As explained,
the source pump GP1 worked at maximum capacity for most of the laboratory test time.

Therefore, during the LogReg training, the optimization algorithm diminished the weight
coefficient for irrelevant features. The recursive feature elimination algorithm excluded
the less important features, highlighting the optimal set. Namely: dTsourceT1 −T2, T2
source outlet temperature and GP2 pump speed. The LogReg fault detection algorithm
with the three features had the maximum F1 score = 0.999 (Fig. 4.16).
The LogReg algorithm validated on the BNR 67 apartment showed lower fault detection
performance by 15 % compared to the algorithm tested on the laboratory dataset (Sec-
tion 4.6). One of the reasons for the decreased validation performance was the usage of
GP2 pump speed as the algorithm input variable. The pump speed is an unreliable fault
detection feature due to the different pressure drop and pump operation in the apartments
floor heating system compared to the laboratory setup. Hence, the LogReg algorithm
struggles to detect the fault if operating conditions differ from the training data set. The
assumption was confirmed by removing GP2 pump speed from the LogReg algorithm
and leaving only one dTsourceT1 −T2 feature for the fault detection. The single feature
algorithm had a higher F1 score than the three features model, respectively 0.695 and
0.8 (Table 4.13). The single feature model had fewer FNs, being more sensitive to faulty
operation. But the increased sensitivity also resulted in more false alarm FP predictions
(Fig. 4.32b).
The solution for improving the LogReg algorithm performance, is to change the default
probability threshold 0.5 to a lower value. It would increase the sensitivity of the model
and detect the lower severity level faults, but it can also lead to a higher false alarm rate.
The second solution, requiring more working hours, would be to perform laboratory tests
with lower clogging levels and retrain the model with the new data set.
Based on LogReg algorithm validation results, it can be generalized that the LogReg
algorithm with fewer features is more likely to detect reduced brine flow fault, but at the
cost of a higher false alarm rate. The feature selection should depend on the trade-off
between the importance of fault detection and the need for a low false alarm rate.
The CUSUM algorithm performed well on the training and validation data sets (Table 4.13).
During the validation process, the algorithm detected the reduced brine flow rate at a high
F1 score (0.991). It suggested that the novel approach of using multiple HPs operation
measurements as the reference value µ0 for residual calculation is a robust solution.
The model is easily scalable and can be adapted to heating systems with large number
of heat pumps. It also relates to the probability central limit theorem, which incites us-
ing a larger number of samples [24]. The variance of the sample mean decreases with
the increase of the number of observations. Hence, reference value µ0 would be more
accurate and less influenced by the outliers or noisy HPs measurements.
The model showed good performance results with the minimum value of 5 heat pumps
operating simultaneously at the same timestamp. The higher number of minimum value
would improve the normality, but some valuable data would also be removed from the
fault detection model. Therefore, the filter of a minimum of 5 HPs operating at the same
time stamp was chosen as the optimal value.
Suppose a sufficient number of heat pumps would not be available for calculating the
reference value µ0. In that case, CUSUM algorithm should be incorporated with an addi-
tional knowledge-based model for simulating the fault-free behavior of the heat pump. It
could be a model based on the first principles or a digital twin model with virtual sensors
as output variables [10]. Zhao et al. [6] stated that the knowledge-based models are more
robust when operating out of the training data range. Hence, it should improve the fault

computational time and more complex model development.
5.4
Recommendations for future work
In order to facilitate the next phase of the project, some ideas about the possible future
development work are proposed in this section. These suggestions are based on the
applied methodology and the results obtained during the case study analysis.
LogReg algorithm: The algorithm has the potential for improvement through fine-tuning
of weight coefficients and probability threshold values. It would increase the model sensi-
tivity; fault would be detected earlier, at a lower severity level. Techniques like Ridge and
Lasso regularization [19], could be applied to reduce overfitting and false alarm rate.
CUSUM algorithm: During the training process, the K threshold value sensitivity analysis
required a lot of manual work. Therefore it is recommended to create a threshold value
optimization algorithm. It would significantly decrease the time needed for the algorithm
development.
Fault diagnosis: Extend the FDD algorithms to provide insights into the localization of
faults within the system. Investigate anomaly localization algorithms to strengthen the
fault diagnosis [6]. Go beyond fault detection by aiming to identify the specific cause or
source of the fault [4]. It would involve a more detailed analysis and understanding of the
system behavior to pinpoint the underlying issue. Fault diagnosis would enable to answer
questions like ”What caused the refrigerant leakage” or ”Which component or process is
responsible for the fault”.

Conclusion
This study aimed to propose a methodology for developing data-driven fault detection
techniques using steady-state operational data and machine learning methods. The re-
search focused on two fault detection algorithms, Logistic regression and CUSUM. A liter-
ature review was conducted to fulfill the thesis objectives and explore the latest research
in the field of fault detection. The review revealed a scarcity of research on the practical
application of data-driven fault detection methods in residential building energy systems.
Danish heat pump producer Metro Therm A/S facilitated the acquisition of operational data
from a residential building in the Netherlands. This dataset was used to validate the fault
detection models and fine-tune the threshold parameters. Laboratory tests were also con-
ducted to simulate reduced brine flow rate and refrigerant leakage. These tests provided
valuable insights into the PVT heat pump system and identified significant fault indicators.
Labeled datasets from the laboratory tests were utilized for training the machine learning
models. The fault detection algorithm for reduced brine flow rate was validated using the
operational dataset, while the refrigerant leakage algorithm could only be verified due to
the absence of labeled faulty operation samples in the operational dataset.
The Logistic regression algorithm performed excellent on the laboratory test datasets.
It exhibited the maximum F1 score (1.0) for the refrigerant leakage and slightly lower
F1 score (0.951) for the reduced brine flow rate fault detection. But its performance de-
creased significantly on the residential apartment validation dataset (0.8). It demonstrated
fast training using Python Scikit optimization algorithms, automated feature ranking, and
elimination of irrelevant features. The accuracy of the algorithm was independent of the
number of heat pumps operating in the building. Nonetheless, its fault detection perfor-
mance significantly declined if the operating conditions in the training dataset differed from
the operational dataset.
The CUSUM algorithm achieved performance for the laboratory and validation datasets.
The F1 score ranged from 0.991 to 0.999 in refrigerant leakage and reduced brine flow
fault detection. This algorithm was easily scalable for larger heat pump systems and
capable of detecting low severity level faults due to the residual accumulation algorithm.
However, the fault detection threshold required manual fine-tuning, which slowed down
the training process. Additionally, the algorithm’s accuracy might decrease if the lower
number of heat pumps would be operating simultaneously.
In conclusion, the developed FDD algorithms exhibited fault detection capabilities for a
residential building with 30 operating heat pumps. Furthermore, these data-driven models
are easily scalable and can be applied to larger PVT heat pump systems of the same
design. However, for the new component designs, such as PVT panels, the models would
need to be retrained using new datasets or by adjusting the fault detection thresholds.
