Preface
This thesis was the final part of the Master of Science (MSc) in Architectural Engineering at
the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). The report was prepared by Emil Vang Olsen
and Trine Søs Mølgaard from February to July 2024 at the Department of Environmental
and Resource Engineering at DTU.
The report deals with the environmental footprint of office buildings and the associated
technical systems, while ensuring a healthy indoor environment. With the EN15798:2011
framework, a comparative life-cycle assessment was performed for four design variations,
in addition to building performance analyses through simulation software.
Throughout the entire project, the workload has been distributed evenly, and all decisions
have been made collaboratively.

Abstract
Reducing the environmental impact of the building sector has a substantial effect on mit-
igating climate change. In 2021, approximately 28 % of all global greenhouse gas emis-
sions was due to operational energy use and around 11 % came from the use of materials
for new buildings and renovation of existing buildings. In recent years, the life-cycle as-
sessment (LCA) framework has been utilized as part of the Danish building regulation and
multiple research studies to develop various benchmarks. This introduces a need for a
detailed analysis of the whole life carbon emission of buildings’ heating, ventilation, and
air conditioning (HVAC) systems. Existing studies are largely based on generic data and
assumptions, do not adequately consider indoor comfort, and lack a consistent approach.
This study comparatively evaluated the whole life carbon and building performance of dif-
ferent HVAC systems on the middle floor of a Danish office building, with varying thermal
mass. The resilience of each system, to overheating due to climate change, was con-
sidered based on the methodology proposed by IEA EBC Annex 80, and the robustness
through stochastically-generated occupancy loads. Two systems were investigated; a
thermally active building system (TABS), and a configuration with active chilled beams
(ACB) and wall-mounted radiators, both with a centralized variable air-volume system.
The whole life carbon was calculated using the LCA framework following the require-
ments of the Danish building regulation and with life cycle stages classified based on
EN15978:2011. Building performance simulations were carried out in IDA ICE 5.0, where
the indoor environmental quality satisfying EN16798-1’s Category II was set as a prereq-
uisite to the life-cycle assessment in this case study.
The findings showed that the whole life carbon emission of TABS was 6.5 kgCO2-eq./m2/yr
and 6.0 kgCO2-eq./m2/yr with heavy and light construction, respectively. Similarly, the
emissions with ACB and wall-mounted radiators were 6.5 kgCO2-eq./m2/yr and 4.7 kgCO2-
eq./m2/yr.
Embodied carbon emissions were higher with the TABS compared to the configurations
with ACB and wall-mounted radiators. Configurations with high thermal mass resulted
in a higher embodied carbon emission and a lower operational emission compared to
constructions of low thermal mass. The difference between high and low thermal mass
was more prominent in the configurations with ACB, which was reflected in the embodied
emissions and whole life carbon results.
Consistent with similar studies, the embodied carbon emissions from HVAC were a con-
siderable part of the total embodied emissions constituting around 11 % and 17 % for ACB
& radiators and TABS configurations, respectively.
The presented methodology and the results are highly relevant for developing a consistent
approach to evaluating the environmental impact of buildings and HVAC systems. This
methodology considers energy use, IEQ, and sustainability and can be used in similar
studies.

Introduction
Anthropogenic activity has caused the global surface temperature to increase by 1.07°C,
from 1850-1900 to 2010-2019 (IPCC, 2023). Greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions are con-
sidered to be one of the main sources to this climate change. In 2021, global energy
and process emissions from buildings, including embodied carbon emissions from new
building constructions, contributed 40 % of global emissions (IEA, 2022). Approximately
28 % of all global GHG emissions come from operational energy use for the total existing
building inventory, and around 11 % comes from the use of materials for new buildings
and renovation of existing buildings (WGBC, 2019). Thus, it is apparent that reducing the
GHG emissions associated with the building sector plays a substantial part in mitigating
climate change.
The life-cycle assessment (LCA) framework can contribute to determine and reduce the
whole life GHG emissions and other environmental impacts from all the processes of
activities related to building materials and operational energy use (Callahan et al., 2011;
Hauschild et al., 2018).
Generally, the impact of buildings is divided into operational impacts and impacts from
materials (embodied impacts). Few case studies have investigated the embodied and
whole life carbon emissions of HVAC systems in buildings, but also mention the lack of
studies exploring the impact of different technical systems (Birgisdottir et al., 2017; Shindo
et al., 2023a; Zimmermann et al., 2021).
In the process of improving the environmental impact of a building, the indoor comfort
should not be neglected. Multiple connected control parameters have a significant impact
on the operational carbon (Shindo et al., 2023a). However, studies often neglect the
indoor environmental quality (IEQ) when estimating the operational carbon footprint of a
building (Menacho et al., 2023).
Furthermore, different solutions can be utilized to meet the indoor comfort criteria. Inte-
grating systems with an activated thermal mass can increase the flexibility in operation
and effectively shift some of the peak load to outside the occupancy period (Olesen &
Dossi, 2004; Shindo et al., 2023a). Multiple studies have evaluated the thermal comfort
and energy performance of TABS versus – or in combination with – other systems (Aller-
hand et al., 2019; Gustafsson et al., 2017; Kolarik et al., 2011; Mahmoud et al., 2022;
Olesen & Mattarolo, 2009).
With an increasing focus on building performance, the occupants’ behavior play a signifi-
cant role. Overestimated occupancy levels may significantly affect the energy use of the
building and the systems within (Sun & Hong, 2017).
Another important aspect to consider, is the increased frequency and intensity of extreme
weather events due to the global effects of climate change. By 2050, more than 970 cities
could be subjected to extreme heat (WGBC, 2023). Heatwaves and power outages are
identified as major disruptions since they have a direct impact on the buildings’ cooling
systems and thermal environment of buildings. Resilience is thus seen as the future target
for building cooling design (Attia et al., 2021).
A review by Zhang et al. (2021) indicated that a combination of different active and pas-
sive cooling strategies with various capacities may be needed to achieve resilient cooling

and that the optimal solution may differ based on the contextual climate. To assess the
resilience of these cooling strategies, Zhang et al. (2023) provides a consistent approach
by dynamic simulation. Few recent studies have included similar methodologies for as-
sessing resilience (Rahif et al., 2022; Shindo et al., 2023b).
1.1
Objective
The increasing focus on environmental sustainability and energy performance of buildings
is prominent in the literature. Since 2023 it has become a Danish requirement to assess
the climate footprint of a building through an LCA. In this regard, the interconnectedness
of the HVAC systems’ embodied and operational environmental footprint needs further
investigation. Moreover, in the process of improving the carbon footprint of a building,
indoor comfort should not be neglected, and different solutions can be utilized to meet the
indoor comfort criteria.
The objective of this study is to perform a comparative evaluation of an optimized office
space with different HVAC systems utilizing detailed IEQ and energy simulations, and the
LCA framework. Additionally, the resilience of the optimized HVAC systems in combina-
tion with separate construction types will be investigated. More specifically, this includes:
• Perform indoor environment- and energy simulations for a reference office space,
including meeting rooms, optimized to maintain satisfactory indoor comfort while
reducing the environmental impact using different active HVAC solutions.
• The IEQ will be evaluated, and the model shall be optimized to fulfill the requirements
of EN16798-1 Category II.
• The LCA framework will be utilized to investigate the environmental impact of the
building, including its different HVAC systems, during its lifetime (embodied and
operational). In addition, the systems will be evaluated using a light- and a heavy
construction type.
• The LCA of the building and its HVAC systems will be explored using future energy
emission factors.
• The resilience of the model and its different HVAC systems shall be assessed using
current- and future weather data.
• The model’s robustness will be analyzed using different occupant behaviour pat-
terns.
• This study will include a literature review on current best practices and relevant
research.
• Provide strategies of how to reduce the environmental impact of the building system
without compromising comfort and long-term resiliency.

Background
The operation of a building, its energy use and thus operational environmental footprint
are linked to the required thermal environment of the building and thermal comfort of its
occupants. This chapter will provide an overview of the relevant theory and frameworks
related to the objectives of this study. The main concepts of the thermal environment and
the principles behind the investigated HVAC strategies, together with their key indicators,
are described, as well as how they are linked to the energy use of buildings. The back-
ground will also introduce the concepts used in the LCA framework, and how it is used to
calculate the environmental footprint of buildings.
2.1
Thermal comfort evaluation
Thermal comfort is that condition of mind that expresses satisfaction with the thermal en-
vironment. The thermal sensation of people is connected to the thermal balance of their
whole body. The heat balance of people is affected by physical activity and clothing,
together with multiple environmental parameters, such as air temperature, mean radiant
temperature, air humidity, and air velocity (ISO7730, 2006). Different measures, like heat-
ing and cooling systems, are often needed to maintain thermal comfort inside a building.

2.1.1
Predicted mean vote and predicted percentage dissatisfied
During the design and assessment of the indoor environment the predicted mean vote
(PMV) index is of interest. PMV describes the average thermal comfort of a group of
occupants exposed to the same thermal environment using a 7-scale index. The thermal
environment is generally split into separate parameters; air temperature, mean radiant
temperature (MRT), air velocity and -humidity. Additionally, personal parameters such as
clothing and activity levels (i.e., metabolic rate) play a role in the thermal balance of a
person. Thermal balance is thus reached when the internal heat production and body
heat loss are equal. This heat balance is used to calculate the PMV; an objective value,
where thermal neutrality corresponds to a PMV = 0. To evaluate the general thermal
comfort in the environment; the predicted percent dissatisfied (PPD) indicates the share
of a group of occupants who are expected to feel thermal discomfort (too hot or too cold)
under the same conditions. The connection between the PMV index and PDD as well as
the influential parameters is shown in Figure 2.1.
Fig. 2.1: The PMV index and PPD with their influential parameters.
The activity level of the occupants, commonly referred to as the metabolic rate, defines
the internal heat production of a person’s body. The metabolic rate is given in the unit
met, wherein 1 met corresponds to 58 W per m2 of body surface area. Additionally, the
clothing insulation level is given in unit clo, where 1 clo = 1.55 m2K/W (Fanger, 1970).
With conventional thermal comfort theory, this subjective comfort or discomfort results
in more or less no further action. However, with adaptive comfort theory, behavioural
action such as regulating one’s clothing level or physiological responses plays a role.

2.1.2
Local thermal discomfort
Thermal neutrality (PMV = 0) is an essential condition, yet insufficient, to ensure thermal
comfort. Thermal asymmetry is when parts of the human body are cooled or heated
locally. This can also interfere with comfort and is referred to as local discomfort. The most
common cause of local discomfort is draught, while radiation temperature asymmetry and
large vertical temperature differences are less frequent in today’s well-insulated buildings.
More rarely is discomfort due to warm and cold floors (ISO7730, 2006).

2023; Wargocki & Wyon, 2017). Poor IAQ is the consequence of increased exposure to

As a product of the occupants’ metabolism humans emit carbon dioxide (CO2), making
it an abundant pollutant. The indoor concentration of CO2 is thus frequently used as an
indicator of the indoor air quality (D6245-18, 2018; Fan et al., 2021). A sufficient ventilation

2.3.2
Active chilled beams
By capitalizing on water’s higher thermal capacity versus air to transfer heat, the required
airflow to sufficiently cool a space can be reduced. Utilizing active chilled beams (ACB)
instead of conventional HVAC systems can result in a reduction in energy use by up to 30
% (Latif et al., 2022). Additionally, ACBs usually require less ceiling space and ductwork.
A cross-sectional illustration showing the ACB principle and a sketched design is seen in
Figure 2.2.
The primary air, from an AHU, is supplied to the room through a pressure plenum and
nozzles along the beam. At the same time, chilled water is circulating within a cooling coil
inside the ACB. The primary air reach a high velocity from the nozzles and the secondary
air (room air) is induced, and is cooled down when passing the cooling coil. The mixed air
is then supplied into the room through slots along the sides of the ACB (Cao et al., 2008;
Latif et al., 2022). A dew point control system is normally utilized to avoid condensation
on the coil surface. A control system ensures that the chilled water supply temperature is
always at least 1.0 °C higher than the indoor air dew point.
Fig. 2.2: (a) A cross-sectional view illustrating the principle behind an active chilled beam.
(b) A 3D illustration of a 4-way active chilled beam, inspired by Swegon (2024b).
2.3.3
Radiators
Wall-mounted radiators utilize convection to exchange heat from a hydronic system to the
room air. In addition, it serves as a radiant surface in the room. Radiators can quickly
regulate changes in temperature due to their low water volume, and are a common heating
system in Danish buildings. Radiators are typically placed in the coldest parts of the
room or under windows to help reduce draught from window convection due to inefficient
windows. Thus, with the advancements in window technology, the placement of radiators
has become more flexible (Babiak et al., 2013).

2.3.4
Thermally activated building systems
Thermally activated building systems (TABS) take advantage of the thermal storage in
the building’s structure by having hydronic pipe systems embedded as its heat-carrier
(Olesen & Dossi, 2004). Utilizing the ceiling and floor building structure in a multi-storey
building, the thermal exchange occurs both upwards and downwards. An illustration can
be seen in Figure 2.4.
Fig. 2.4: (a) Cross-sectional view of the concept of TABS; with hydronic PEX tubing em-
bedded the concrete ceiling and floor building system. (b) An isometric view of the building
system. Dimensions are given in millimetres.
The heating- and cooling supply temperatures can be operated relatively close to the room
temperature because of the large heat transfer areas and thermal capacity of the system
(Olesen & Dossi, 2004; Olesen & Mattarolo, 2009). This is more commonly known as high
temperature cooling and low temperature heating. The large thermal capacity acts as a
heat storage, effectively distributing the cooling load over a longer period and reducing
the peak loads (Babiak et al., 2013). The asynchronous operation between the thermal
loads the conditioning plant enables other concepts such as nighttime cooling and utiliz-
ing cheaper electricity tariffs for operation.
To deal with latent loads and maintain good air quality, TABS often require a supplemen-
tary ventilation system. In addition, this ventilation system can optionally cover the peak
loads where the TABS deals with the base load (Behrendt, 2016).
As a radiant system, TABS is expected to perform optimally when the radiant surfaces
are exposed directly to the room. However, this can present challenges with the acoustic
environment. Thus, by retrofitting a space with acoustic panels to circumvent this, the per-
formance of TABS would be expected to decrease. Through measurements, Weitzmann
et al. (2008) found that the cooling capacity of a radiant ceiling was reduced by approx.
30 % with 83 % of the ceiling surface area covered with acoustic panels; resulting in an
acceptable reverberation time in the room.

2.5
Life-cycle assessment
Life-cycle assessment is a method to assess the environmental aspects and possible
impacts throughout the life cycle of a product, process, or activity. In the context of build-
ings, the LCA framework can contribute to evaluate the environmental impacts from all the
processes of activities related to building materials (embodied impacts) and operational
energy use (Callahan et al., 2011; Hauschild et al., 2018). This section will introduce
the LCA framework and how it is applied in determining the environmental impacts of a
building.
2.5.1
The framework of life-cycle assessment
The framework of LCA consists of four phases, as seen in Figure 2.5; a goal and scope
definition, inventory analysis (LCI), impact assessment (LCIA), and interpretation of re-
sults.
Fig. 2.5: The four phases of LCA as defined in ISO14040 (2008).
1. Goal and scope definition: An LCA starts with a well-considered definition of the
goal of the study. Key elements are determined: The function of the system, the
functional unit, the system boundaries, impact categories of interest, perspective of
relevance, and the credibility of the inventory data.
2. Inventory analysis: The LCI collects data on all input flows of resources, materials,
and products, together with the output of emissions and waste of the system.
3. Impact assessment: The LCIA translates the flows of the product system into im-
pacts on the environment, utilizing knowledge and models from environmental sci-
ence with emission factors.
4. Interpretation: The results obtained from the LCI and LCIA are interpreted and
uncertainties are evaluated to answer the question(s) presented in the goal defi-
nition. Sensitivity analysis and uncertainty analysis can be applied to support the
conclusions from the results and to identify aspects of future work.
A comprehensive LCA is achieved through an iterative process, using sensitivity and un-
certainty analysis, with many feedback loops between the different phases (Hauschild et
al., 2018; Jolliet et al., 2016).
10
==PAGE:25==
Chapter 2. Background
2.5.2
Reference unit
A detailed functional unit (FU) is normally specified when performing an LCA. According
to ISO14040 (2008) the FU is the ”quantified performance of a product system for use
as a reference unit”. It defines the qualitative and quantitative aspects of the function,
which includes answering the questions ”what?”, ”how much?”, ”for how long/how many
times?”, ”where” and ”how well?” (Hauschild et al., 2018).
EN15804 (2019) states that a declared unit may be applied if an FU cannot be defined
unequivocally. An environmental product declaration (EPD) based on a declared unit shall
be declared by applying one of the following unit types; an item (piece), an assemblage of
the item, mass (kg), length (m), area (m2) or volume (m3). LCAs for building products or
buildings today are often performed with a declared unit as reference unit as for instance
per m2 or per m2 per year.
2.5.3
System boundary and life cycle stages
LCAs within the building domain are mainly used to compare different choices of outline,
design, or materials at a single building level. LCA ensures a long-term perspective of a
building based on a selected reference study period, considering all life-cycle stages and
modules seen in Figure 2.6. The life-cycle stages within building LCAs can be divided
into three segments; pre-use, use, and after-use. Pre-use encloses the production stage
(A1-A3) and the construction process (A4 & A5), use revolves around the operational
aspects of the building (B1-B7), and after-use includes the end-of-life stage (C1-C4) and
next product system/recycling potential (D) (Hauschild et al., 2018).
Fig. 2.6: The life-cycle stages (A, B, C, and D) and the underlying modules (A1, B4,
C3, etc.) of a building. According to The Danish Building Regulation (2024a, § 297) the
modules marked in blue are required to be included and documented in an LCA.

2.5.4
Reference study period and reference service life
When conducting LCAs, a reference study period (RSP) and different reference service
lives (RSL) of the elements within the product system are chosen. The RSP indicates the
computational period over which the building’s life cycle is calculated. The environmental
impact of buildings must be assessed with an RSP of 50 years to make the calculations of
the impact from individual buildings comparable (The Danish Building Regulation, 2024a,
§ 297).
If the RSL of a specific building component or material is shorter than the RSP, it is as-
sumed to be replaced within the RSP. The environmental impacts from the replacement
are aggregated in module B4: ”Replacement”. The replacement corresponds to the sum
of modules A1-A3 and C3-C4 of the respective building component or material. Figure 2.8
shows an example of a building component with an RSL of 30 years over an RSP of 50
years. With an RSL of 30 years, the building component is replaced once (B4), and the
modules C3-C4 in the end-of-life phase are included because the lifespan ends at the
50-year observation period, even though the RSL may be longer.
Fig. 2.8: Example of a building component with an RSL of 30 years over an RSP of 50
years. The replacement (B4) happens after 30 years and is the sum of the modules A1-A3
(product stage) and C3-C4 (Waste processing and disposal). Module D (Reuse, recovery,
and recycling potential) is not included in the figure.
2.5.5
Data collection
The reliability of the LCA is based on the level of precision and detail provided in the data
and the information used to reflect the object of assessment. LCAs of buildings today often
lack data on technical installations (Birgisdottir et al., 2017; Shindo et al., 2023a). Based
on 60 case buildings in Denmark, Zimmermann et al. (2021) found that the materials of
the building installations account for up to 10% of a building’s GWP. Environmental data
of the various building components and the materials involved contain information on the
climate impact per unit of product.
According to The Danish Building Regulation (2024a, § 297-298) and EN15978 (2012)
the climate impacts from each material in the building should be calculated using generic
and average data from The Danish Building Regulation (2022b, appendix 2, Table 7) or
from relevant and valid EPDs executed in accordance with EN15804 (2019).

2.7
Recent studies
Few studies focus on the life-cycle assessment of buildings while including HVAC compo-
nents, indoor environmental quality and the energy use. Ylmén et al. (2019) performed a
post-construction analysis of a Swedish building using highly reliable data provided by the
contractors. A novel finding was that the technical installations had a significant impact
of 14-32% in 4 of 5 environmental impact categories. They mention that LCAs can ig-
nore a considerable part of the environmental impact by omitting the impacts from HVAC
systems, which is often the case due to lack of data — or by using generic data.
Rasmussen et al. (2022) and Simonen et al. (2017) pointed out the need for the estab-
lishment of general calculation rules for building LCA, and that a crucial next step would
be to evaluate the performance of the specific building design. Trigaux et al. (2021) tried
to identify this through the main approaches and methods used in existing literature.
In step with the technological development, Ramboll (2024) designed a carbon assess-
ment database, CO2mpare, for building projects with matching scopes. The database can
be used to assess carbon emissions for building projects in different countries, typologies,
and project types.
Among other uncertainties, the lifespan of the building is a sensitive parameter to the
calculated environmental impact of buildings. Using data from 124 096 cases of demo-
litions in Denmark, Andersen and Negendahl (2023) predicted that office buildings, built
between 1990 and 2010, generally have much shorter expected lifespan than previously
assumed; at an average of 41 years.
Zimmermann et al., 2021 explored LCAs of 60 DGNB1-certified building cases in Den-
mark, built from 2013 to 2021, hereof 22 cases being offices. A 50-year and an 80-year
reference study period were analysed. They found that the median value for the 50-year
reference study period, for all modules (A1-3, B4, B6, C3-4), was 9.5 kgCO2-eq/m2/yr and
that the impacts from materials were approx. 2-4 times greater than impacts from opera-
tions. Moreover, the materials of the technical installations accounted for up to 10% of a
building’s GWP. The study established a suggested common basis for the environmental
performance of buildings through benchmark values.
Often whole life carbon studies neglect the indoor environmental quality when estimating
the operational carbon footprint of a building (Menacho et al., 2023). Implementation of
innovative heating and cooling technologies is essential to reduce global emissions (IEA,
2022).
Shindo et al. (2023a) compared a radiant system and an all-air system for an office building
based on its whole life carbon in addition to the energy use and IEQ. They found that the
radiant system had a 34 % reduction in primary energy and an 11 % reduction in whole life
carbon, compared to the all-air system. They mention that due to the flexibility in operation
with an activated thermal mass, the radiant system can result in further carbon emission
reductions by implementing a dynamic carbon intensity of the grid.
A system with TABS and CAV was compared to an all-air VAV system in different climates
in a study by Kolarik et al. (2011). With optimized control parameters for TABS, it resulted
in less discomfort and a 16 % reduction of primary energy use as compared to the all-air
system. However, regardless of orientation, they found that with a light wall construction,
a higher prevalence of discomfort periods was found with TABS, in moderate and hot-dry
climates.

Allerhand et al. (2019) evaluated the thermal comfort and energy performance of TABS
and phase change materials (PCM) ceiling panels compared to that of an all-air system,
in an open-plan office. Their results showed 15 % energy savings and a reduced peak
cooling power by 30 %, while maintaining thermal comfort, and the possibility of shifting
the cooling demand, thus enabling other strategies such as night ventilation cooling. In
extension, Boccardo et al. (2019) compared these systems economically and found that
TABS was the cheapest option, and that PCM ceiling was a valid alternative to TABS in
retrofit projects.
Latif et al. (2022) investigated the performance of ACBs in office buildings through a com-
prehensive literature review. It was found that a reduction in energy use up to 30 % can be
achieved by ACBs compared to conventional HVAC Systems. However, there is a lack of
collected framework on ACBs in literature. Through the review it was found that ACB sys-
tems sometimes cannot meet high sensible cooling requirements for large spaces, such
as open-plan offices. The high heat loads can cause abnormal air circulation patterns due
to asymmetrically placed chilled beams, which may cause draught risk.
Shahsavari et al. (2019) used a variance-based sensitivity analysis to investigate the un-
certainties in a building design process. With varying thermal properties of the building
materials, window-to-wall ratio (WWR) and occupant behavior for operating windows, they
found that the most important uncertain design variable during optimization was occupant
behavior and its effect on building performance simulations (BPS).
To investigate how occupant behaviour patterns impact the performance of different en-
velope design solutions of an office, Buso et al. (2015) simulated several envelope de-
signs in different climate zones. They found the energy performance was less sensitive
to changes in occupants’ behavior when the building envelope was massive and with a
WWR of 10 %, most evidently for Nordic and continental climates.
Standards and codes tend to treat occupants simplistically, effectively resulting in model-
ers following these unrealistic default assumptions to avoid liability. O’Brien et al. (2020)
comprehensively reviewed 23 regions’ building energy codes and standards by analyz-
ing their approaches, rules and quantitative aspects. Occupants play a significant role in
building performance and design and can thus no longer be treated simplistically via codes
and standards. Quantitatively, they found that code-specified occupant densities varied
by nearly a factor of three between different codes and clarify that the occupant-related
code requirements should be updated.
In addition to evaluating buildings’ current energy- and thermal performance, and their
environmental footprint, the future comfort of occupants should be assessed, with respect
to climate changes.
Zhang et al. (2021) reviewed and assessed resilient cooling strategies. The review indi-
cated that a combination of cooling strategies with different capacities might be needed
to achieve resilient cooling and that the optimal solution will differ from climate to climate.
This corresponds with the findings of O’Donovan et al. (2021) for passive cooling strate-
gies. Additionally, both studies argue that the use of typical or historical weather files is
limiting and may be insufficient to evaluate the effectiveness of cooling strategies when
faced with climate change.
As an extension, under IEA EBC Annex-80, Zhang et al. (2023) provides a consistent
approach to evaluate and assess the resilience of cooling strategies in dynamic simula-
tion. This approach was initially investigated by Rahif et al. (2022) for an office building
with two different air-based cooling strategies in six different climates with future weather

Methodology
The following sections describe the methodologies of the case study with varying con-
struction type and two different systems; a configuration with TABS and one with active
chilled beams and wall-mounted radiators. Both systems utilize a centralized ventilation
system with VAV. Following this, the system controls are described along with input param-
eters to the building performance simulation and the different evaluation criteria; thermal
comfort, IAQ, primary energy use, environmental impact, and resilience to overheating.
3.1
Overall methodology
The building model was configured using typical construction materials and assemblies.
The quantities and volume of construction materials were used as input to the calculation
of the embodied emissions of the building. In extension to this, the building was set up
to function as an building simulation model in IDA ICE (EQUA, 2024). The heating- and
cooling demand were extracted and used to dimension the HVAC systems using avail-
able weather data and internal loads. The materials of these HVAC systems were then
included in the embodied emissions.
The HVAC controls were determined from an iterative design process based on energy
efficiency and desired IEQ (and IAQ). With a compliant design and similar indoor environ-
ment in all configurations, the operational emissions were then extracted to calculate the
whole life environmental footprint of the building.

was not included nor was the core itself included as conditioned space. The dimensions
and overall layout of the floor can be seen in Figure 3.2. This office building was set up
for analysis with four different iterations (see Figure 3.3). These iterations included two
different construction types; a typical light- and heavy construction, as well as two different
systems configurations; with TABS and with ACB including wall-mounted radiators.
The investigated floor had a window-to-floor ratio (WFR) of 20 %, with fixed windows for
all orientations. As a result, north and south facades each had 6 windows at (H x W) 1.50
x 1.83 m while east and west facades each had 5 windows at 1.50 x 1.54 m.

Results
In this study, four overall steps were taken to evaluate the four configurations of the of-
fice building. Building performance simulations in IDA ICE were performed, and data on
energy use and IEQ were extracted and analyzed. By introducing a stochastically sim-
ulated occupancy model, the sensitivity of the systems was briefly assessed. Construc-
tion types, technical systems, and their constituent parts and materials were evaluated
through the LCA framework to assess the environmental impact of the four building con-
figurations. Finally, by utilizing validated TMYs, the resilience of the different building
designs against overheating due to climate change was evaluated. This chapter provides
a general overview and analysis of the results.
4.1
Indoor environment
This section presents the findings of the indoor environment of the office building utilizing
the system with TABS and the system with active chilled beams and wall-mounted radia-
tors, each with heavy and light construction. The study aimed to achieve a similar indoor
environment with the two HVAC configurations to make them comparable when perform-
ing the LCA calculations. The input data to accomplish this are listed in Section 3.2.3.
For IEQ, the configurations were compared to each other in terms of thermal comfort and
indoor air quality.
4.1.1
Thermal comfort
Thermal comfort was evaluated in the occupied zones. Figure 4.1 illustrates the percent-
age of occupied hours within the respective comfort categories for all configurations and
rooms in the office building. All configurations, regardless of the HVAC system, construc-
tion, or room type, had the majority of indoor operative temperatures within EN16798-1
(2019) Category II, 20 to 26 °C, during occupied hours. The meeting room with the TABS
and light construction had the highest exceedance, but still close to the others, with 0.9
% of the occupancy hours above 26 °C, corresponding to 8 hours. Additionally, most of
the operative temperatures in the occupied time also complied with Category I, at 21 to
25.5 °C.

Discussion
The LCA framework is recognised as an important tool to systematically assess the envi-
ronmental impact, further enabling informed decision-making in building design (Hollberg
et al., 2020). However, different assessment elements, interpretations and inconsisten-
cies in reported carbon values can present challenges when comparing buildings’ perfor-
mances in different regions or with different approaches (Astle et al., 2023; Cabeza et
al., 2014; Ding, 2020). Thus, finding comparable LCA studies, with the EN15978 (2012)
methodology, was limited.
Indoor environmental quality was set as a prerequisite to the life-cycle assessment in this
case study. Parameters such as operative temperature and heating- and cooling setpoints
or dead-bands have a significant impact on the operational carbon emission when using
dynamic simulations (Shindo et al., 2023a).
All four investigated configurations met the EN16798-1 (2019) thermal comfort Category II
criteria for more than 99 % of the occupied hours, in both meeting rooms and landscaped
offices. Systems utilizing active chilled beams were found to have a higher energy use
due to the need for added air cooling to achieve adequate thermal comfort. Similarly,
Latif et al. (2022) found that ACB systems sometimes fail to meet high sensible cooling
requirements in large open spaces. The strength of the active chilled beams is that they
respond to the cooling setpoint quickly. With TABS, the high thermal mass allowed for
high temperature cooling and low temperature heating, closer to the room temperature.
Additionally, the absorbing properties allowed for pre-cooling of the slab at night.
TABS is expected to perform optimally when the radiant surfaces are exposed directly
to the room. However, challenges with the acoustic environment can arise. Weitzmann
et al. (2008) found that the cooling capacity of a radiant ceiling was reduced by approx.
30 % with 83 % of the ceiling surface area covered with acoustic panels. Consequently,
the low energy use of the TABS compared to the ACB configurations in this study, would
be expected to increase.
Multiple studies have investigated the environmental impact of buildings; their structures,
envelopes and technical systems (Kiamili et al., 2020; Menacho et al., 2023; Shindo et al.,
2023a; Simonen et al., 2017; Trigaux et al., 2021; Wolf et al., 2016; Ylmén et al., 2019;
Zimmermann et al., 2021). Few studies have investigated the whole life carbon of HVAC
systems, but mention that it contributes substantially to the embodied carbon emissions
(Kiamili et al., 2020; Menacho et al., 2023; Shindo et al., 2023a; Ylmén et al., 2019). In
this study, embodied carbon emissions were calculated for the different building elements,
including the HVAC components.
A strength of this study, was that it investigated four model configurations through multiple
life-cycle stages; product stage (A1-A3), replacement (B4), operation (B6), end-of-life (C3
& C4), and re-use (D) with detailed product-specific environmental data.
Additionally, the whole life carbon emissions of the technical installations were based on
dimensioned systems and material quantities extracted from the building model. With
said extraction of material quantities and product-specific environmental data, the overall
credibility of the inventory data was evaluated to be fair for the scope and level of detail of
this study. Contextually, dynamic building performance simulations were used to calculate
the operational carbon emission for each configuration, while considering the IEQ on a
multi-zonal level.

Generally, configurations with heavy constructions were found to have a higher embodied
carbon emission, but a lower operational emission, than with light constructions: Heavy
constructions resulted in an annual embodied carbon emission of 5.2 kgCO2-eq./m2/yr
and 5.0 kgCO2-eq./m2/yr for TABS and the system with ACB and radiators, respectively.
Similarly, for light constructions, the annual embodied emissions were found to be 4.7

between the two systems was 0.26 to 0.37 kgCO2-eq./m2/yr, depending on construction
type.

ment and its sensitivity. Here, after identifying impactful components, the inventory was
updated through this iterative process. Namely, the embodied impact of the AHU was ini-
tially estimated based on its ventilation rate compared to that of the EPD used. However,
after assessing its substantial impact out of all the HVAC components, it was specified
to be weight-based. This prioritization was similar to the methodology used by Buhl et
al. (2022). This example resulted in a reduction in its impact, from 0.37 to 0.30 kgCO2-
eq/m2/yr for the TABS and 0.56 to 0.33 kgCO2-eq/m2/yr for the ACB system.

5.1
Robustness
Occupant behavior can lead to substantial differences between anticipated and actual
total energy use in buildings (Buso et al., 2015; Sun & Hong, 2017). Robust building
designs are considered a solution to reduce differences in energy and thermal comfort
performance with diverse occupant behavior patterns.
This study investigated the robustness of the building configurations through stochastically-
generated occupancy schedules. Compared to the default scenario, the energy used for
cooling decreased for all configurations. As mentioned, the maximum number of people
in the rooms was higher, but the stochastic occupancy resulted in more frequent fluctu-
ations with less intensive peaks, enabling the cooling system to cope with the internal
loads. This, in turn, increased the heating demand.
The total primary energy use for the ACB system increased with 1.5 kWh/m2/year and
0.5 kWh/m2/year, for heavy and light construction respectively. For the TABS the primary
energy use decreased with 0.7 kWh/m2/year, regardless of construction type. The TABS
configurations resulted in a negligible difference in thermal comfort from the default oc-
cupancy schedule. This could in part be attributed to both construction types featuring
a concrete slab. Comparatively, the ACB system with light construction resulted in the
largest difference compared to the default occupancy schedule. Based on the energy
and thermal comfort results, the building system with TABS, regardless of construction,
indicated having the highest building robustness.
These results were consistent with the findings by Buso et al. (2015), that showed robust-
ness towards changes in occupants’ behavior increased with increasing thermal mass
and with decreasing transparent area of the envelope.

5.2
Resilience to climate change
An increasing frequency and intensity of overheating events due to the effects of climate
change are expected to continue in the future. Approaches in previous studies, for assess-
ing resilient cooling strategies, lack common boundary conditions and a generic frame-
work for resilience evaluation. To accommodate this, a consistent simulation-based ap-
proach by Zhang et al. (2023) was developed and allows a comparison of different cooling
technologies.
The three KPIs; IOhD, AWD, and CCOR, were utilized in this study with contemporary,
medium-term future, and long-term future TMY weather files. All the investigated configu-
rations were able to suppress the thermal stress of each weather scenario, with a CCOR
> 1. Configurations with a heavy construction showed a higher resistivity to overheating
due to climate change. TABS with heavy construction had the highest CCOR of 3650 and
ACB with light construction had the lowest CCOR of 287, while still resulting in a lower
energy use.
Compared to previous studies utilizing the same framework, the CCOR results of the
current study were substantially higher (Amaripadath et al., 2023; Rahif et al., 2022).
However, none of the found studies were fully comparable in terms of location, reference
building, boundary conditions, and HVAC systems. Rahif et al. (2022) tested two differ-
ent cooling strategies in a shoe box model representing a double-zone office building.
One of the most comparable locations investigated was in Stockholm, which resulted in
a CCOR of 28.46 and 14.05 for the first system with a variable refrigerant flow (VRF)
unit coupled with DOAS and the second system with VAV, respectively. The AWDs and
IOhDs for the weather scenarios of this study, regardless of configuration, ranged from
2.74 to 3.09 °C and from 0.00002 to 0.00360 °C, respectively. For the mentioned case
study the values were generally higher, spanning from 3.84 to 5.96 °C, and from 0.0088
to 0.1602 °C for AWDs and IOhDs, correspondingly. Moreover, the study in Stockholm
had a larger amount of hours above the comfort limit of 26 °C compared to this study. The
higher AWDs and IOhDs in the Stockholm study indicated a larger severity of the outdoor
thermal conditions in the cooling season, which should be kept in mind when comparing
the CCORs of the two different assessments. Moreover, the choice of software for the
building simulations was also different from this study.
The three indicators in the utilized framework, IOhD, AWD, and CCOR indicate the over-
heating degree, the severity of the outdoor thermal conditions, and the climate change
overheating resistivity of building cooling solutions.
As with any, these metrics have
strengths and uncertainties: The IOhD metric is flexible, since it represents a real zone
allocation with the multi-zonal approach having variable occupied hours, requirements,
and comfort models. The AWD metric is proficient in evaluating the severity of the out-
door thermal conditions but does not consider solar radiation. As a consequence it can
fail to accurately assess the intensity of the outdoor thermal conditions during a day with
high solar radiation.
A limitation in the assessment of resilience, was that the three periods of the TMYs (i.e.
2001-2020, 2041-2060, and 2081-2100), based on the worst case GHG scenario RCP
8.5. The intermediate periods, 2021-2040 and 2061-2080, and other emission scenarios
were ignored. Most studies, including this, assume that the cooling system is running
with no disruptions. In reality, power outages might occur, but limited studies have inves-
tigated it as a scenario. This case study did not consider the impact of power outages.
However, Rahif et al. (2023) assessed the resilience of a dwelling in Brussels assuming
the outage of the cooling system. It was found that neither of the optimized solutions was
able to suppress overheating during heatwaves.

Chapter 5. Discussion
The average global cooling demand for non-residential buildings is anticipated to increase
by around 275 % looking at the present and until 2050 (Santamouris, 2016). To accom-
modate increasing overheating discomfort due to global warming, different adjustments
and strategies could be implemented. The cooling setpoint could be decreased, but this
would result in increased primary energy use and GHG emissions. One of the sugges-
tions by WGBC (2023) to mitigate extreme heat is to implement passive design. Differ-
ent proposals consist of semi-permanent shading devices, shutters, light-colored roofs,
overhangs, utilizing thermal mass, and minimizing large glazing areas. Passive cooling
control strategies are essential to maintain comfortable indoor environments while mini-
mizing the energy use of a building. Several studies concluded that multiple combined
passive solutions would lead to the most optimal comfort and energy results (O’Donovan
et al., 2021; Rahif et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2021). Along with the limited potential of
ventilative cooling, solar protection was found to be one of the most effective adaptation
measures to reduce overheating due to climate change (Hamdy et al., 2017). Conse-
quently, the implications of building performance with passive measures, in combination
with the investigated HVAC systems, should be explored.
A more efficient heat sink, to further reduce the annual primary energy, could be utilized.
Few studies have explored the benefits of coupling a thermally activated building system
with a ground source heat pump (GSHP). In a comparison by Uponor (2013), a GSHP
was found to reduce the annual primary energy use, albeit a higher cost. Mahmoud et
al. (2022) established that a storage-integrated hybrid GSHP system reduced the annual
median primary energy and CO2 emissions by half compared to conventional systems.
This study only considered district heating, as it is more commonly used in Denmark.
The simulation and analysis workflow of this study provided a detailed overview of the
investigated configurations and their performance across several metrics. As was also
found, LCAs can present challenges when comparing building performance in different
regions, boundaries, or with different approaches. A limitation of this study is that it only
considers specific zones in an office building, namely landscaped offices and meeting
rooms. The current study also excluded the core and foundation, which contributed sub-
stantially to the embodied carbon emissions.

6
Conclusion
It is evident that reducing the environmental footprint associated with buildings has a sub-
stantial influence on mitigating climate change. This thesis aimed to comparatively eval-
uate different HVAC systems and construction types using detailed indoor environment-
and building energy simulations in addition to the life-cycle assessment (LCA) framework.
Consequently, thermal indoor environmental quality, and air quality indicated by CO2 con-
centrations, satisfying EN16798-1’s Category II was set as a prerequisite to the life-cycle
assessment in this case study.
By reviewing recent literature, the methodology and results of this study were compared
to findings and benchmarks from similar studies.
A generated multi-zone office building in Denmark was used as the case study, featuring
two different dimensioned systems: (i) A thermally activated building system (TABS) and
(ii) a system with active chilled beams (ACB) and wall-mounted radiators. Both configura-
tions utilized a ventilation system with variable air volume (VAV). In addition, the systems
were evaluated using a light- and a heavy construction type. Using product declarations
that specify environmental impacts and future emission factors, the LCA framework was
utilized to investigate the whole life embodied and operational carbon emissions of the
building and its HVAC systems. A reference study period of 50 years was applied in
accordance with the Danish building regulation.
Each system was briefly evaluated through its overheating resistivity due to climate change
(CCOR) using future typical meteorological years (TMYs) as climate data.
In exten-
sion, the robustness to changes in occupancy load schedules was investigated using
stochastically-generated occupancy schedules.
Through an iterative design process, all four investigated configurations were able to suc-
cessfully meet an indoor environmental quality of Category II (EN16798-1, 2019). An
office utilizing heavy construction was found to result in a lower primary energy use than
with light construction. More specifically, for HVAC, TABS had an annual primary energy
use of 14.7 kWh/m2/yr and 14.8 kWh/m2/yr for heavy and light construction, respectively.
Similarly, for the configuration with active chilled beams and wall-mounted radiators, the
annual total primary energy resulted in 19.2 kWh/m2/yr with heavy construction and 20.5
kWh/m2/yr with light construction. The primary energy use and thus the operational car-
bon emissions were lower for configurations utilizing TABS.
Consequently, this resulted in a whole life carbon carbon emission, excl. recycling (D),
of 6.5 kgCO2-eq./m2/yr and 6.0 kgCO2-eq./m2/yr for TABS with heavy and light construc-
tion, respectively. The operational emissions (B6) contributed with 1.27 kgCO2-eq./m2/yr,
regardless of construction, corresponding to approx. 20 % of the whole life carbon emis-
sion. Comparatively, the whole life carbon emission was 6.5 kgCO2-eq./m2/yr and 4.7
kgCO2-eq./m2/yr for the respective constructions with ACB and wall-mounted radiators.
Here, operational emissions for ACB with heavy and light construction represented 1.52
kgCO2-eq./m2/yr and 1.64 kgCO2-eq./m2/yr, respectively. This result corresponds to 23
% and 35 % of the whole life carbon emission.

Chapter 6. Conclusion
Evidently, with TABS, embodied carbon emissions were found to be higher than the re-
spective configurations with ACB and wall-mounted radiators. Configurations with high
thermal mass resulted in a higher embodied carbon emission and a lower operational
emission compared to constructions of low thermal mass.
Consistent with similar studies, the embodied carbon emissions from HVAC were a con-
siderable part of the total embodied emissions constituting around 11 % and 17 % for ACB
& radiators and TABS configurations, respectively.
Comparing the robustness of the configurations; TABS showed the smallest difference
in energy and thermal comfort results, regardless of construction, when simulated with
stochastically-generated occupancy schedules from the default scenario. Comparatively,
the ACB system with a light construction resulted in the most noticeable differences.
All configurations were able to suppress outdoor thermal stress due to climate change.
Configurations with heavy construction showed a higher resistivity to overheating due to
climate change. Consequently, configurations with a TABS were found to have a higher
climate change overheating resistivity than those with ACB & radiators. Additionally, TABS
configurations showed a lower primary energy use when simulated with future weather
data.
Few case studies have investigated the embodied and whole life carbon emissions of
HVAC systems in buildings, but also mention the lack of studies exploring the impact
of different technical systems.
This study presented additional embodied and opera-
tional emission benchmarks for dimensioned systems that consider an adequate indoor
environment. Future targets for building cooling design include its resilience to climate
change. This study evaluated the climate change overheating resistivity for two contem-
porary HVAC systems in a well-insulated building.
The methodology utilized in this study comprehensively evaluated the performance of
HVAC systems and the findings can be used to develop future benchmarks.
6.1
Future work
Based on the analysis and limitations outlined in the discussion, suggestions for potential
future research includes:
• Investigate the influence of passive design strategies, such as increased solar pro-
tection, in combination with the different system configurations.
• The incorporation of weather data including heatwaves in the evaluation of building
resilience to climate change. In addition, the building autonomy with respect to
power outages can be included.
• Introduce alternative materials and HVAC solutions to further reduce the embodied
and operational emissions, respectively.
• Consider additional environmental impact categories.
• A life cycle cost evaluation.
