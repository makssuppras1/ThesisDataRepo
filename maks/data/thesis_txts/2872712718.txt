Abstract
Lidar measurements have become increasingly popular in wind resource assessments
as an alternative to traditional mast sensors. In offshore or coastal sites, the ability to
characterize the wind resource without installation of a costly mast can save valuable
resources in a measurement campaign. However, while lidars have been shown to meaВӯ
sure wind speed reasonably well, they have consistently struggled to accurately measure
turbulence. The dual Doppler lidar technique has been proposed to produce better lidar
turbulence estimates. Nonetheless, the use of dual Doppler and the nature of remaining
biases has not been fully explored. This thesis identifies the differences between dual
Doppler turbulence measurements and cup anemometer turbulence measurements, usВӯ
ing a month of data from a nearВӯcoastal site. Possible connections between these difВӯ
ferences and siteВӯspecific conditions are explored, including stability, wind speed, and
direction. Ultimately, the results indicate limited possibility of predicting difference based
on these factors, although the signal quality strongly affects the results. The turbulence
estimates are largely consistent with cup measurements, although it is noted that a higher
signal quality is required for turbulence measurements than for wind speed measureВӯ
ments.

Introduction
Wind energy has a critical role to play in decarbonization of the energy sector. To achieve
targets of carbon neutrality by 2050, the IEA estimates that wind capacity must increase
by 17% annually, necessitating the rapid deployment of new wind farms [1]. Wind reВӯ
source assessments are a critical part of wind farm development, and require accurate
measurements of wind speed, wind direction, and turbulence in order to make predicВӯ
tions about power production and turbine loading. Understanding the errors associated
with these parameters is therefore of critical importance in developing new wind projects.
The incorrect estimation of turbulence intensity, for example, can lead to power production
overestimations on the order of 10% [2].
Measurement devices commonly used in resource assessment include cup anemomeВӯ
ters, sonic anemometers, and more recently remote sensing devices such as lidars. Lidar
(Light Detection and Ranging) devices offer some notable advantages over devices that
require installation of a meteorological mast, which is costly and at times infeasible. HowВӯ
ever, although lidars perform well in determining mean wind speed and direction, there
are significant differences in estimating turbulence compared with other sensors [3]. One
source of uncertainty is the assumption of spatial homogeneity between different beams,
which are treated as measuring identical wind vectors for the purposes of identifying mulВӯ
tiple wind components.
The dual Doppler technique removes the uncertainty associated with lateral separation,
and has the potential to improve turbulence measurements by lidar. Existing research has
shown promise for the use of dual Doppler for turbulence, although work is still needed
to understand the extent and sources of remaining errors [4]. The primary aim of this
masterвҖҷs thesis project is to further evaluate the possible use of dual Doppler lidar in the
context of turbulence through comparison to cup in situ measurements.
In addition, because dual Doppler does not eliminate biases due to volume averaging and
vertical and transverse crossВӯcontamination [5], potential methods of estimating errors will
also be investigated. Current correction strategies under research tend to rely heavily on
existing knowledge of turbulence parameters from in situ anemometers, posing problems
for fully replacing such devices with lidar [6]. Prediction of errors without extensive data of
the existing wind climate at a given site could further improve dual Doppler measurements
and use of lidar in general.
The project will use data from a monthВӯlong measurement campaign with pulsed scanning
lidars in staring mode (dual Doppler), and met mast data (cup anemometers, wind vanes,
sonic anemometers, rain sensors, and temperature sensors) [7]. The data will be used to
answer the following research questions:
1. To what extent do dual Doppler turbulence intensity estimates differ from cup estiВӯ
mates for wind resource assessments?
2. How are the errors in lidar turbulence measurements related to atmospheric stability
and other wind profile characteristics?
3. Can the dual Doppler technique be improved through better prediction of errors?

Background
The use of lidar for measuring wind speed is wellВӯstudied and shows promising results
compared with traditional anemometers. The inherent differences in how lidar devices
measure wind, however, result in error when measuring turbulence. There has been
considerable research into the use of lidar for turbulence measurements in recent years,
including how to predict differences between lidar and in situ measurements. This section
outlines the measurement principles of lidar and the impacts on turbulence measurement.
The state of current research is also discussed.
2.1
Wind Resource Assessments
Wind resource assessments are used to determine a range of parameters important for
siting wind farms, including wind speed, direction, and turbulence. The turbulence of a
site not only affects power production predictions, but is also used in fatigue analysis
for blades and other wind turbine components. Higher turbulence intensities have been
found to significantly increase turbine loading, and accurate measurements are therefore
crucial for turbine selection and placement [8].
Remote sensing has become increasingly attractive for wind resource assessments as
wind projects become larger and move offshore, where installation of a met mast is infeaВӯ
sible and costly [3]. Lidar devices are commonly used in the industry, and are capable
of measuring important parameters such as wind speed and direction by detecting the
Doppler shift of backscattered light from a laser beam [9]. The increased interest in lidars
has prompted the development of standards and recommended practices for lidar use in
site assessment [10]. Of particular interest is improving the use of lidar for turbulence
measurements, which remains challenging [11].
Lidars can be groundВӯbased or nacelleВӯmounted, and are broadly categorized by whether
the laser is pulsed or continuous wave (CW) [9]. Two common commercial lidars that
utilize these different measurement strategies are the ZX ZephIR (CW) and the Vaisala
Windcube (pulsed) [12]. Pulsed lidars involve a pulsed collimated beam that scatters light
back to a detector as each pulse meets aerosols. They are capable of measuring at further
distances than CW, and can measure simultaneously at multiple locations since the beam
is not focused at a particular point. However, the scale of changes in wind speed that
pulsed lidars can measure is limited by the length of the pulses. Smaller pulses provide
better resolution, but increased signal noise [13].

The underlying principle of lidar measurement is derived from the Doppler shift. As the
laser emitted from the lidar illuminates small particles in the air, the backscattered light
experiences a change in frequency that is proportional to the wind velocity along the line

so the resulting вҖқbeatsвҖқ can be detected. The focus of the lidar can be determined by
weighting backscatter by the intensity of the laser. For a continuous wave, the maximum
intensity occurs at the set focus distance. For a pulsed wave, maximum intensities are
centered in each pulse. The weighting functions of CW and pulsed lidar are described by
Lorentzian and Gaussian functions, respectively [14]. The width of the functions at half
the maximum value (the Full Width Half Maximum or FWHM) defines the minimum length
scale of wind that the lidar can measure. This will inevitably exclude some of the smaller
scale eddies, representing a problem for measuring turbulence.
Another possible complication for lidar turbulence measurements is that the horizontal
wind field is assumed to be spatially homogenous. Because the lidar only measures
along the line of sight, in order for the two horizontal wind components to be determined,
beams in two directions are needed. The wind vector must be identical across the two
probe volumes to extract velocity components, which may not be the case [6]. Further,
the inclination of the beam can introduce vertical contamination, resulting in a nonzero
vertical velocity that is not accounted for in velocity reconstructions.

Dual Doppler involves two lidar devices with beams that meet at a set point, as shown
at left in Figure 2.1. This technique has the potential to mitigate the problems arising
from spatial variation in the horizontal plane, although the cost of the measurement camВӯ
paign nearly doubles with the addition of another scanning lidar [7]. Availability typically
decreases with this scanning strategy, as both lidars must be functioning to produce meaВӯ
surements. Further, concerns with volume averaging remain with dual Doppler measureВӯ
ments.
Other methods of turbulence estimation using lidars include Velocity Azimuth Display
(VAD), where a beam measures conically at varying azimuthal angles, and Doppler Beam
Swinging (DBS) where the beam moves to four positions at a set elevation angle [15].
These strategies both rely on assumptions of homogeneous flow, as the measurements
are both spatially and temporally separated but are assumed to be measuring the same
wind vector. This poses problems for turbulence, which varies with position and time.
A variation of the VAD strategy is the sector scan, where a smaller range of azimuthal
angles are sampled at a constant elevation angle (at right in Figure 2.1). This allows for
a higher sampling rate, although it may still be difficult to measure turbulence due to the
low temporal resolution [16].

2.4
Existing Research
A substantial body of research has been devoted to improving turbulence measurements
in the wind industry, particularly in recent years. Early experimental comparisons beВӯ
tween lidar and cup anemometers indicated relatively good agreement of wind speeds,
but challenges in estimating turbulence from lidar data [20][21]. These errors were further
explored by Mann et al. in 2012, using a model based on sonicВӯmeasured turbulence
parameters for determining turbulence from VAD lidar data. The results indicated that
turbulence errors can vary significantly depending on height and stability [22].
Different scanning techniques have also been proposed to reduce systematic errors.
Sathe and Mann developed a 6 beam lidar technique to lower the cross contamination
errors associated with VAD strategies [23]. Newman et al. evaluated different strategies
for lidar turbulence measurement including VAD and 6 beam, and found the 6 beam to
be an improvement [15]. However, high systemic errors remained particularly in unstable
atmospheric conditions [15]. A review of methods for turbulence measurements in 2013

identified three intersecting staring lidars to be the most accurate for turbulence measureВӯ
ments, although measurement campaigns with three lidars can be expensive [24]. The
need for fewer devices has driven research into single and dual lidar campaigns.
A number of correction strategies and novel methods have been proposed for single lidar
measurements. The LВӯTERRA model suggested by Newman and Clifton combined corВӯ
rections for instrument noise, volume averaging, variance contamination, and additional
machine learning corrections [25]. This model somewhat reduced stabilityВӯdependent erВӯ
rors, but did not eliminate difference from in situ instruments entirely. Newman and Clifton
noted that the model does not sufficiently capture the complex physics of turbulence. FurВӯ
ther, the requirement of input parameters like shear profiles and turbulent length scales
depends upon site information that is likely not known at the start of a measurement camВӯ
paign. This problem was termed the вҖқturbulence paradoxвҖқ by PeГұa et al. in research
that further developed efforts to use machine learning in lidar measurements [6]. Despite
these advances, the need for correction strategies that do not rely on existing site knowlВӯ
edge remains. A key advantage of lidars is the ability to measure heights without a met
mast, so the use of sonic and cup data for error correction negates this benefit.
Dual Doppler has been explored as a means of reducing bias in turbulence measureВӯ
ments. One of the earliest papers to this effect was written by Davies et al. in 2005, who
found sufficient evidence for effectively deriving turbulence parameters from dual Doppler
lidar data [26]. PeГұa and Mann presented a method of estimating bias through compuВӯ
tation of ReynoldвҖҷs stresses based on turbulence parameters, scanning geometry, and
probe volume [5]. Another study by Shimada et al. aimed to validate dual Doppler for
turbulence compared nearВӯshore lidar data with cup anemometer and sonic data [4]. The
dual Doppler data agreed well with cup measurements, although was significantly differВӯ
ent from sonic data. Still, the work shows promise for the use of dual Doppler in wind
energy in the future.

Errors can be introduced to the lidar signal through a variety of factors including hard
targets passing the probe volume or insufficient aerosol concentrations. Filtering is necВӯ
essary to ensure accurate speed measurements. The CarrierВӯtoВӯNoiseВӯRatio (CNR) proВӯ
vides a measure of backscatter intensity, and is therefore used as a measure of data
quality. A common method of filtering is to use a CNR threshold, below which data is
excluded. Frehlich proposed a CNR of Вӯ22 dB as a cutВӯoff value [30], although as Beck
and KГјhn noted, this can be overly aggressive and exclude valid data [31].
Multiple CNR threshold filters were tested, although a relatively high threshold of Вӯ22 dB
was required to remove the majority of the spikes. This high threshold was insufficient to
fully catch the noisy data, even when used in combination with a radial speed filter and
manual removal of some data. The threshold filters were deemed ineffective and overly
strict, so several alternate filtering approaches were considered.

filter [32]. This filter requires initial filtering by CNR and radial speed to remove initial

< Вӯ5 dB, while the radial speed filter removed values higher than 20 m/s and lower than
вҲ’20 m/s. The reconstruction algorithm also requires that the elevation and azimuthal

quency eddies should correspond to lower energy, and indicates a likely artifact of noise.
The line on the plot indicates the frequency where the spectra begin to differ (0.1 Hz), and
therefore shows the target frequency for filtering. Two filtering strategies were considВӯ
ered, including a lowpass Butterworth filter and a wavelet filter. The Butterworth filter was
chosen as the noise seems concentrated at the highest frequencies, and a more complex
filtering strategy was deemed unnecessary.
To ensure accurate comparisons, both the cup and lidar were filtered using the Butterworth
filter before resampling but after other filtering strategies. A cutoff frequency of 0.1 Hz was
selected with a filter order of 2. Although this frequency is lower than where the plateau
begins, the attenuation of frequencies using the Butterworth filter is more gradual, and the
higher cutoff frequencies tested failed to remove the impact of noise from the data.

7
Conclusion
The goal of this thesis was to study the differences between a dual Doppler lidar and a
cup anemometer in turbulence intensity measurements. This investigation involved not
only characterizing the differences themselves, but better understanding the predictors
of difference, analyzing impacts of atmospheric stability, wind speed and direction, and
signal quality indicators.
The dual Doppler lidar turbulence was found to align well with the cup anemometer estiВӯ
mates, although higher scatter and a greater number of pronounced outliers was present
than in the comparison of mean wind speeds. The signal quality was the biggest predictor
of these outliers. Low CNR resulted in high amounts of noise in the lidar signal that did not
affect the mean wind speed estimates but failed to capture the turbulence accurately. The
agreement between the sensors further improved when the high frequency fluctuations
were filtered out, although the dual Doppler lidar remained consistently lower.
The other factors examined, including shear, atmospheric stability, wind speed, and diВӯ
rection were not observed to be strong predictors of errors, although the presence of
significant noise makes it difficult to study these factors robustly. In particular, the stability
classification would benefit from additional data to ensure sufficient comparison across
categories.
7.1
Recommendations
When it comes to practical implementation of dual Doppler lidar measurement campaigns,
several key recommendations are made based on these findings.
1. A relatively high CNR threshold filter should be applied when using dual Doppler for
turbulence measurements (Вӯ22 dB in this case).
2. The dual Doppler lidar technique yields similar TI values as the cup, but may produce
lower estimates by up to 10%, particularly if noisy conditions are expected and the
data must be filtered for high frequency noise.
3. Caution should be used when relying on dual Doppler measurements for high freВӯ
quency data, as erroneous measurements are difficult to identify without reference
instruments.
7.2
Future Work
There are several limitations to this work that require further investigation. First, the camВӯ
paign is from over a decade ago, and lidar technology has continued to develop in those
years. The signal quality issues observed here may have significantly improved. Studies
with more modern equipment are therefore recommended. Second, this campaign ran for
one month, and is therefore limited in terms of the amount of data. Because wind resource
assessments are typically conducted over a year or longer, longer studies are required
to build confidence in this technology. In addition, the site used in this study is relatively
flat with low turbulence. The ability of dual Doppler to measure may be significantly difВӯ
ferent in more complex terrain, where the turbulence characteristics are more variable.
Finally, this thesis focused on relevant differences between the cup and dual Doppler, but
did not examine in detail the limitations of TI measurement practice. An examination of
the effects of mean wind speed trends and a comparison to expected dual Doppler lidar
difference would be relevant for understanding sources of error in a campaign.
