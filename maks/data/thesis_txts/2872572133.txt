Abstract
Keywords: sustainability transitions, Systemic Design, transition-facilitator, socio-technical system, toolbox, Transition 
Pathway, leverage points
This master thesis explores how a Systemic Design approach can facilitate sustainability transitions within 
Copenhagen’s food system. As urban food systems contribute significantly to environmental, social, and 
economic challenges, there is a need for systemic change of the current practices.
The thesis addressed two main objectives: first, to explore the potential of Systemic Design for enabling 
sustainability transitions within Copenhagen’s food system; and second, to develop and test a framework 
that supports actors engaged in sustainability transition projects. The research was based on the 
understanding that the food system is embedded in a wider network of interconnected systems, such as 
transport, health, and policy, and therefore requires a systemic and holistic approach to transition.
Grounded in theories such as the Multi-Level Perspective, Transition Management, and Systemic Design, the 
thesis analysed the current state of Copenhagen’s food system. Supported by interviews with key 
stakeholders, the analysis revealed the absence of a dedicated actor capable of coordinating across levels 
and sectors to enable systemic change. The Transition-facilitator was therefore identified as a key leverage 
point. Copenhagen Municipality served as the case study, as it seeks to adopt this role in developing its 
upcoming Food- and Meal strategy (2026–2035). However, the role remains unfamiliar to the municipal 
team, underlining the need for targeted support. In response, a collaboration was established to co-develop 
a Systemic Design toolbox intended to support transition-facilitators in urban food contexts.
The resulting toolbox consists of six elements: a frontpage, a facilitation page, a workshop page, a transition 
pathway, individual Systemic Design tools, and a digital workspace. It translated theoretical concepts into 
practical tools that aligned with the needs and capacities of potential users. Testing showed that the toolbox 
provided value by enabling coordination, supporting learning, and structuring transition efforts without 
oversimplifying systemic complexity.
The thesis demonstrated how Systemic Design enables more coherent, collaborative, and actionable support 
for sustainability transitions in urban food systems. Although based on Copenhagen’s food system and co-
developed with Copenhagen Municipality, the toolbox offers insights and applicability for other contexts 
facing similar sustainability challenges.
This master thesis, titled A Systemic Design Approach for Facilitating Sustainability Transitions in 
Copenhagen’s Food System, is conducted by the master students Emma E. Ansel-Henry and Josephine A. R. 
Mølvang. It serves as the final of the authors’ master programmes in Design and Innovation, and Industrial

under the section Design for Sustainability, at the Civil and Mechanical Engineering department DTU

Network of elements, such as technologies, institutions, societal practices, and knowledge, that

long-term, multi-dimensional, and non-linear transition processes in which existing socio-

Systemic Design
Interdisciplinary approach that combines methods from Design Thinking and Systems Thinking.
Leverage points
Places within the Socio-technical system where a small change in one element can lead to

Lock-ins
Structural, institutional, or behavioural conditions that stabilise existing socio-technical systems

Actor

Actors at the forefront of sustainable development capable of accelerating the transition towards

Human consumption has accelerated rapidly since the mid-20th century due to industrialisation, population growth, 
urbanisation, and resource-intensive lifestyles, putting extensive pressure on Earth’s systems (Stockholm Resilience 
Center, 2015; Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2022). This negative impact is also shown by the planetary boundaries, 
where six out of nine had been surpassed globally by 2023 (Rockström, 2015; Stockholm Resilience Center, 2023). 
Although nutrition and food are essential for human survival, the food systems of today are one of the main drivers in 
current environmental, economic, and social challenges. Additionally, urbanisation has contributed to a centralisation 
of the food system to meet the demands of urban citizens, currently counting for half of the world’s population (Zou et 
al., 2023; Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2019). This highlights the urgency for sustainability transitions towards absolute 
sustainability within the urban food system.
1.1 The changing urban food system
To feed the growing population, the food system has undergone a transformation towards an industrialised and 
global system. The modern food system operates primarily in a linear production paradigm, where resources are 
extracted, processed, consumed and ultimately disposed of as waste (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2019) as illustrated 
by Figure 1.1:
Although the current food system has been able to meet rising demands from the expanding world population, the 
linear model cannot do so without posing sustainability risks to the planet (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2019). The 
linear model has contributed to various global challenges, including resource scarcity, ecosystem degradation, social 
inequalities, biodiversity loss, and climate change. Many of these various global challenges originate from the life 
cycle of food, including how food is produced, distributed, and consumed (Zou et al., 2023).
The food system is therefore not considered an isolated entity, but as part of a network of interconnected systems 
such as health, economy, transport, and energy. This suggests that sustainability transitions within the food system 
requires a systemic understanding that embraces both complexity and interconnectedness (Thorsen et al., 2025; 
Parsons et al., 2019).
Transitions within the current food system could help mitigate these environmental, economic, and social global 
challenges. As 80% of the world’s food is expected to be eaten within cities by 2050, urban food systems can play an 
important role in driving sustainability transformations. Cities can therefore act as vital centres to connect actors to 
create systemic changes for sustainability transitions within urban food systems (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2019; 
Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2022).
1.2 Sustainable transitions and systemic barriers
Sustainability transitions aim to redirect the current development pathways to more fairly meet the needs and well-
being of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet theirs (Geels et. al, 2023). They 
refer to long-term, multi-dimensional, and non-linear transitions of existing practices, norms, structures and 
technologies (Markard et al., 2012; Markard et al., 2020). These elements are closely linked, causing potential lock-ins, 
stabilisation and resistance to change within the system (Geels, 2005a; Markand et al., 2012). Sustainability transitions 
can therefore be considered multi-actor processes, requiring interaction between system elements (Coenen et al., 
2012; Geels, 2011).
As a result, sustainability transitions are often at a high risk of failing, unfolding too slowly or resulting in unintended 
consequences and lock-ins due to the complexity and interconnectedness of the systems involved (Wilkerson & 
Trellevik, 2021). The same applies to the food system, which is characterised by embedded barriers, including political 
silos, fragmented supply chains, economic incentives and cultural norms. For example, suggestions to diet changes, 
or change in consumption patterns, may encounter resistance if they do not take into account habits, availability or 
price. Similarly, policy measures may be ineffective if they are not systemically based and supported by broad 
collaboration across actors and sectors (Köhler et al., 2019; Wilkerson & Trellevik, 2021).
1.3 Systemic Design as an approach to transformation
To meet these challenges, methods are required that can both analyse complex systems and generate concrete 
changes. Here, Systemic Design holds great potential. By combining Systems Thinking’s ability to uncover structures 
and connections with Design Thinking’s capacity for creative, user-oriented solutions, Systemic Design can contribute 
new ways to identify and activate points of change in complex systems (Dorst, 2019; Wilkerson & Trellevik, 2021).
Systemic Design therefore enables a holistic understanding of how social, environmental, and economic factors are 
interconnected in food systems. By promoting collaboration between actors and combining methods for analysis 
and action, Systemic Design holds the potential of contributing to sustainability transitions. In an urban context like 
Copenhagen, where sustainability goals are ambitious, but where food consumption still constitutes a significant part 
of the climate impact, a Systemic Design approach can form the basis for new ways to organise and create 
sustainability transitions in urban food systems.

1.4 Copenhagen as a case: A food system in transition
Copenhagen presents as a relevant case for examining sustainable transitions in urban food systems, considering 
the city is the capital of Denmark, a country with ambitious goals of reducing greenhouse gas emissions 70 percent 
below 1990 levels by 2030 (Klimaloven Ch.1 §1, 2021). However, Denmark has one of the world's highest ecological 
footprints per capita (Earth's Overshoot Day, 2025). In Denmark, food consumption is the second-largest source of 
emissions, accounting for 20% of the country’s total carbon footprint (Concito, 2023). In Copenhagen, the impact from 
food is equally significant with 19% per capita (Københavns Kommune, 2023b).
Within Copenhagen, the Copenhagen Municipality is trying to turn these national ambitions into municipal strategies, 
one of them being the Food Strategy (Københavns Kommune, 2019). While the city is embedded in national and 
global food systems, initiatives are trying to drive positive changes in the current food system. The city therefore has 
both a significant environmental impact and a great potential for promoting sustainable solutions. This makes 
Copenhagen an interesting case for exploring how a Systemic Design approach can facilitate sustainability 
transitions.
1.5 Purpose and structure of thesis
The purpose of this thesis is to investigate how a Systemic Design approach can support sustainability transitions in 
Copenhagen’s food system. The aim is to develop and test a design framework that enables actors to navigate 
complexity, identify strategic areas for intervention, and contribute to more coherent and sustainable transition efforts 
within urban food systems.
The research is guided by two main goals: first, to investigate the potential of Systemic Design for enabling 
sustainability transitions in Copenhagen’s food system; and second, to develop and test a design framework that 
supports actors engaged in sustainability transition projects within Copenhagen’s food system.
To fulfil these goals, the study addresses five research questions (RQ), which explore both the systemic challenges of 
Copenhagen’s food system and the practical application of Systemic Design methods to support transition efforts.
1.
What are the key challenges in urban food systems, and how can Systemic Design support management of 
sustainability transitions?
2.
What is the current state of Copenhagen’s food system from a sustainability transition perspective? What are the 
needs and challenges of key actors?
3.
How can known Systemic Design methods and tools be used to identify leverage points for sustainability 
transitions in Copenhagen’s food system?
4.
How can a framework support sustainability transition projects within Copenhagen’s food system based on 
Systemic Design methods?
5.
To what extent does the developed framework support actors in sustainability transition projects within 
Copenhagen’s food system (and elsewhere)?
To do so, the thesis is structured as follows: The present chapter serves as an introduction to the thesis, clarifying the 
context of the research and providing an overview of the motivation, purpose, and goals of this thesis. Chapter 2 
explains the methodology, methods, and data input used to answer the described Research Goals and RQs. Chapter 
3 presents the theoretical background to guide the analysis of Copenhagen’s food system and design of a support 
framework. Chapter 4 provides an empirical study of Copenhagen’s current food system, highlighting areas of 
opportunity for the framework. Chapter 5 conducts the task clarification, defining the solution space by identifying 
requirements and criteria for the support framework. Chapter 6 performs a systematic selection of Systemic Design 
tools, defining the basis for the support framework. Chapter 7 design and perform the conceptualisation of the 
support framework. Chapter 8 presents the testing and evaluation of the developed support framework. Chapter 9 
discusses, reflects and highlights limitations for both the developed support framework and the thesis process. 
Chapter 10 serves as the conclusion of the thesis by summarising key findings.

2 Methodology
This chapter presents the methodology used for this thesis to guide the research, development and evaluation of a 
support framework. The thesis follows the Design Research Methodology (DRM) (Chakrabarti & Blessing, 2009). The 
framework consists of four iterative stages; Research Clarification (RC), Descriptive Study I (DS I), Prescriptive Study 
(PS), and Descriptive Study II (DS II). Besides the DRM framework, the Systematic Engineering Design Approach (Pahl et 
al., 2007) has been used to support the development and evaluation of the support framework, as shown by Figure 2.1. 
The figure also shows additional methods and data inputs used for developing and evaluating the support 
framework.
This chapter starts by presenting the two methodologies applied across stages, DRM and the Systematic Engineering 
Design Approach. The remaining methods and data input are then presented using the relevant chapters shown on 
Figure 2.1. 
2.1 Design Research Methodology
As described, this thesis followed the Design Research Methodology (DRM) developed by Blessing & Chakrabarti 
(2009). The four DRM stages (RC, DS-I, PS, and DS-II) offer a structured approach for conducting design research to 
develop and validate design in practice. A research project will not necessarily include each stage or undertake each 
stage in equal depth (Chakrabarti & Blessing, 2009).
There are seven possible design research types, depending on the use of a comprehensive study or a review-based 
study for each of the four stages. A review-based study is only based on literature, where a comprehensive study 
includes both a literature review and either an empirical study, a development of a support, or evaluation of the 
developed support. This thesis applied research type 5, meaning that the RC stage was a review-based study based 
only on the review of literature. The stages DS-I and PS were both based on a comprehensive study.  Finally, the DS-II 
was an initial study, meaning that the preliminary results from the developed support were presented to easily be 
used in future research projects. The last three stages were closely connected and ran in close parallel, especially the 
stages PS and DS II. The feedback from the DS II stage served as input for adaptations of the developed support and 
vice versa, which created an iterative connection between the two stages where multiple iterations were conducted 
(Chakrabarti & Blessing, 2009).
Specific research questions, and main deliverables are tied to each of the four stages in the DRM framework seen in 
Figure 2.2. The work in this thesis was conducted iteratively and in parallel, indicated by the arrows between the 
stages (Chakrabarti & Blessing, 2009).

Research Clarification (RC)
In the RC stage, the research goal, scope and plan were devised by conducting a review-based study. For 
the research plan, tasks included defining research questions, learning objectives and project goals, and to 
make a plan for the deliverables to be achieved within the specified timeframe for the thesis. The RC stage 
also seeked to define the research context by reviewing existing literature on sustainability transitions within 
urban food systems and identify challenges related to Systemic Design within this context. The purpose of 
the RC stage was to build a foundation for the Descriptive Study I, supporting the formulation of a realistic 
and worthwhile research plan
Descriptive Study I (DS-I)
In the DS-I stage, the focus was on building a deeper understanding of the research context through 
empirical investigation and a systematic exploration of relevant design approaches. The empirical study 
examined the current state of Copenhagen’s food system through document analysis and stakeholder 
interviews, identifying key challenges and needs. In parallel, a systematic selection of Systemic Design 
methods was conducted to assess relevant Systemic Design tools for actors working with sustainability 
transitions. The combined insights from the empirical and theoretical studies were used to establish a set of 
requirements and to outline the solution space for the subsequent Prescriptive Study.
Prescriptive Study (PS)
In the PS stage, insights from the DS-I stage were used to develop a framework aimed at supporting actors 
within Copenhagen's food system to facilitate sustainability transition projects. The defined requirement 
and criteria list from the DS-I stage was used to conceptualise solution concepts, which led to the 
development of a support framework.
Descriptive Study II (DS II)
In the DS II stage, the developed support framework was tested and evaluated with both experts and 
potential users through an iterative process. The involvement of experts and potential users aimed at 
revealing usability and usefulness concerns when using the support framework in practice. Tasks involved 
evaluating the support by collecting data on its performance and reflecting on the application to other 
systemic projects.
Using the DRM framework ensured structure and allowed an iterative approach throughout the thesis. It 
supported a clear understanding of the problem field, guided the development of a context-sensitive 
support framework, and enabled systematic refinement through evaluation and stakeholder involvement 
(Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009). This approach strengthened the coherence between the research phases 
and enhanced the practical relevance of the final outcome.
2.2 Systematic Engineering Design Approach
To guide the development of the support framework, this thesis used the Systematic Engineering Design 
Approach developed by Pahl et al. (2007). The framework provides a structured methodology to solve 
complex engineering problems using a sequence of clearly defined phases. The framework thereby 
ensures a traceable and logical design process, by guiding engineers from identifying a problem to 
developing a solution (Pahl et al., 2007).
The framework divides the design process into the following four main phases: Task Clarification, 
Conceptual Design, Embodiment Design, and Detail Design. Due to the selected DRM research type and 
scope of this thesis, only the first three phases was applied, as shown in Figure 2.3:

The following description elaborates on the main objectives and deliverables for each of the three phases in this 
thesis: 
Task Clarification
In the Task Clarification phase, the foundation of the design process was described. The primary purpose of this 
stage was to define the design problem in detail. Using a co-creation session with Copenhagen Municipality, 
requirements and criteria for the support framework were gathered, analysed, and structured in a list. The list 
guided the following design phases.
Conceptual Design
In the Conceptual Design phase, the requirement and criteria list was used to develop multiple solution 
concepts to the problem at hand. In the Conceptual Design phase, creativity was used in the development of 
solution concepts, and a systematic approach was applied in the evaluation of each solution concept. To 
conceptualise solution concepts, morphology was used for each requirement on the requirement and criteria 
list. The solution concepts were then evaluated based on the criteria from the list. The evaluation resulted in the 
specification of one principle solution concept that was embodied.
Embodiment Design
In the Embodiment Design phase, the principle solution concept was translated into a design layout. This was 
done by specifying the overall layout in line with the requirements and criteria identified in Task Clarification. The 
embodiment phase ended with an evaluation of the design, which resulted in new knowledge that was used for 
the iterations of refinements for the support.
Using this step-by-step methodology for the development of the support framework ensured that it aligns with 
real-world needs. The Task Clarification took place in the DS-I stage, the Conceptual Design in the PS stage, and 
the Embodiment Design took place in both the PS and DS-II stage (Pahl et al., 2007).
2.3 Methods for DS-I
2.3.1 Chapter 4 - Empirical Study
The empirical study phase aimed to uncover needs and challenges experienced by key actors within 
Copenhagen's food system. This was achieved using the qualitative research methods of conducting interviews. 
The qualitative data obtained from the interviews was then analysed using an affinity diagram to uncover 
common challenges.
Interviews
To gain a deep understanding of the needs and challenges experienced by key actors in Copenhagen’s food 
system, semi-structured interviews were carried out with six different actors from companies and organisations 
working within Copenhagen’s food system (Thagaard, 2004). The main criteria for selecting the interviewees 
were that they worked within areas related to Copenhagen’s food system. Attention was paid to interviewing 
companies and organisations from different areas of Copenhagen’s food system to gather different insights on 
challenges and needs. The interviews were audio-recorded with consent and transcribed. Transcriptions of 
interviews can be seen in Appendices 4.a to 4.f.
To protect the privacy of interviewees, personal identifiers were anonymised in this thesis, were relevant. 
Participants’ names were replaced with pseudonyms, while organisation names were kept for contextual 
relevance. This partial anonymisation allowed for meaningful analysis related to organisational context without 
compromising individual privacy (Moore, 2012).
Affinity diagram
Following the conducted interviews, Affinity diagram (Beyer, 1998) method was used to organise and synthesise 
qualitative data in form of quotes from the interviews. The method allowed grouping related ideas and 
observations from interviewees to identify patterns and themes (Beyer, 1998). This approach resulted in a deeper 
understanding of needs and challenges experienced by key actors within Copenhagen’s food system.
2.3.2 Chapter 5 - Task Clarification
Following the investigation of main challenges and needs experienced by key actors in the Empirical Study, Task 
Clarification was performed to identify the design space and ensure that the developed support framework 
aligned with user's expectations. To support a participatory exploration of the design space, co-creation was 
used to uncover requirements and criteria.
Co-Creation
To validate and further specify the needs and requirements identified through the empirical study, a co-creation 
session was conducted with selected stakeholders from Copenhagen’s food system. The session aimed to 
create a space for dialogue, mutual learning, and the joint development of design criteria for the support 
framework (Sanders & Stappers, 2008).
The co-creation session was structured using Staging Negotiation Spaces (Pedersen, 2020), which frames co-
creation as a three-phase process: backstage, frontstage, and interpretation. In the backstage phase, the 
facilitation of the frontstage phase was planned by preparing the overall structure, visual materials, and 
prompts for discussion. During the frontstage phase, the actual co-creation session was carried out, where 
actors contributed their knowledge and perspectives. The final interpretation phase involved analysing and 
synthesising the input collected during the session.

Systematic Literature Review
To validate and extend the table of identified Systemic Design tools from Boll (2024), a Systematic Literature 
Review (SLR) was conducted. SLR is a structured way of finding and analysing the existing research in a specific 
research area. By providing a research protocol for the SLR, it is designed to be replicable by others (Biolchini et 
al., 2005; Page et al., 2021). The research protocol consists of the methodology steps and strategies used to 
identify the relevant literature. The methodology steps included formatting a research string, designing

reviewed papers, references and citations were screened to identify additional papers using snowball sampling

3 Theory
This chapter outlines the theoretical foundation used to investigate sustainability transitions within Copenhagen’s 
food system. It is based on an exploratory literature review of approaches that can inform the following chapters. First, 
transitions in socio-technical systems are defined, focusing on sustainability transitions. Following this explanation, the 
Multi-Level Perspective (MLP) is considered to get a descriptive understanding of sustainability transition dynamics 
across system levels. Moving from the descriptive to the prescriptive, Transition Management (TM) is used to 
investigate how sustainability transitions can be managed actively. Based on this understanding, facilitation of 
sustainability transitions is investigated, and characteristics of different Transition-facilitator types are uncovered. 
Lastly, Systemic Design is reviewed as a supporting approach for Transition-facilitators when working with 
sustainability transitions.
3.1 Transitions in Socio-technical systems
Socio-technical systems describe systems consisting of networks of elements, such as technologies, institutions, 
societal practices, and knowledge, that together fulfill critical societal functions. Energy-, food- and transport systems 
are examples of socio-technical systems, as these systems fulfill functions such as securing power supply, feeding the 
population, and ensuring mobility (Geels, 2005a; 2005b; Markard et al., 2012).
Transitions in socio-technical systems refer to long-term, multi-dimensional, and non-linear transition processes in 
which existing socio-technical systems shift from one system to another, typically evolving over decades (Geels, 
2005a; 2011; Loorbach et al., 2017; Markard et al., 2012). One historical example of a socio-technical transition is the shift 
from horse carriages to automobiles (Geels, 2005b). In the context of sustainability transitions, these shifts entail a 
gradual reconfiguration of existing systems towards more environmentally, socially and economically sustainable 
ways of organising practices, production, and consumption (Markard et al., 2012; Markard et al., 2020). This involves 
redirecting development pathways within socio-technical systems to advance the well-being of today, without 
compromising the well-being of future generations (Geels et al., 2023). Therefore, unlike many historical transitions 
often driven by economic gain, sustainability transitions are often shaped by holistic concerns about environmental, 
social, and economic aspects (Geels, 2011; Geels et al., 2023)
Transitions often include technological innovation as well as shifts in markets, user behavior, policy and institutions 
frameworks, and can therefore be considered multi-actor processes, involving interaction between system elements 
(Coenen et al., 2012; Geels, 2011). However, the system elements are closely
linked, creating lock-ins in the system and causing it to be stable and resistant to change (Geels, 2005a; Markand et 
al., 2012). Actors such as firms and industries, policy makers, civil society, and researchers maintain and reproduce the 
system elements (Geels, 2011). Despite the system elements being interconnected, fundamental change can be 
achieved by identifying leverage points. These define places within the system where a small change in one element 
can lead to positive change in the entire system (Meadows, 1999).Additionally, as sustainability transitions aim at 
addressing collective purposes and long-term goals, private actors have limited incentives to engage. Managing 
sustainability transitions therefore require active involvement from public authorities and civil society to avoid free-
rider problems and negative externalities, where people do not feel personally responsible and companies are not 
held accountable for their negative impacts (Geels, 2011). This can in itself be challenging, as what is considered 
“sustainable” is subject to change over time, adding complexity to the sustainability transition efforts (Markard et al., 
2012).
There are several approaches to study management of sustainability transitions, including Multi Level Perspective 
(MLP) (Geels, 2005a, 2005b, 2011, 2019), Strategic Niche Management (SNM) (Kemp et al., 1998; Schot & Geels, 2008), 
Transition Management (TM) (Loorbach, 2010), Technological Innovation systems and Mission-oriented Innovation 
systems (TIS/MIS) (Bergek et al., 2008; Bergek et al., 2015; Coenen et al., 2012; Markard, 2020). While some focus on 
sustainability transitions from a systemic perspective, others concentrate on innovation dynamics or the 
development of niches. This thesis will focus on the approaches MLP and TM. While MLP offers a descriptive approach 
to understand how sustainability transitions happen across system levels, TM is a prescriptive approach focusing on 
how to actively manage ongoing governance processes within society to facilitate sustainable change (Loorbach, 
2010).

3.2 A Multi-Level Perspective for describing transitions
The Multi Level Perspective (MLP) is used to understand and explain how sustainability transitions in socio-technical 
systems happen through the interplay between technology and society (Geels, 2005a; Geels, 2011). MLP considers 
sustainability transitions as non-linear and multi-phase processes resulting from interactions between three different 
levels: Niche-innovations (micro-level), Socio-technical regimes (meso-level), and Socio-technical landscapes 
(macro-level), as shown by Figure 3.1. Each level varies in how dominant, far-reaching, and embedded it is in society, 
where higher levels are more stable and structured than the lower levels (Geels, 2005b; Geels, 2011).
Socio-technical landscape
The Socio-technical landscape considers the external environment that influences interactions between the Socio-
technical regime and Niche-innovations (Geels, 2005a; 2005b; 2011). It includes external trends that affect the current 
system, such as globalisation, environmental problems, and cultural changes (Geels, 2005a). The landscape level is 
therefore out of scope for individual actors in the system, and changes to this level usually happen slowly (Geels, 
2011).
Socio-technical regime
The Socio-technical regime functions as the central layer within socio-technical transitions. It consists of established 
rules and practices that shape the actions of key actor groups and thus contribute to maintaining and reproducing 
the existing socio-technical system (Geels, 2005a; 2005b). The stabilising role of the Socio-technical regime stem 
from interaction between the factors culture, industry, markets and user preferences, politics, science and technology 
(Geels, 2005a; 2005b; 2019). As sustainability transitions are considered as a shift from one system to another, the 
regime level is of primary interest. From this view, the niche and landscape levels are conceptually derived as 
contrasts to the regime (Geels, 2011).
Niche-innovations
The Niche-innovations consist of practices or technologies trying to disrupt the existing regime (Geels, 2011). Niches 
act as “incubation hubs” for radical innovations, providing a space for learning without direct pressures from the 
established system (Geels, 2005a; 2005b). Niche-innovations are important for sustainability transitions as they 
provide momentum for changes in the current system (Geels, 2011).
The three levels can be viewed as a hierarchy, where regimes are rooted within landscapes and niches within 
regimes (Geels 2005a, 2005b). The general pattern is that transitions happen when niche activities align with the 
pressure on the regime from the landscape, creating a window of opportunity for the niches to disrupt the existing 
regime (Geels, 2011). This is also considered by the MLP in its view of transitions being multi-phase. MLP divides 
transitions into the following four phases.
Experimentation
Radical innovations are developed through trial and error across system dimensions due to misalignments between 
regime and landscape. The phase is characterised by uncertainty, error and low cultural acceptance (Geels, 2019).
Stabilisation
The phase is characterised by shared visions, improved performance and support from powerful actors, which 
strengthens niche innovations. At the same time, resistance from the established regime increases (Geels, 2019).
Diffusion/disruption
Radical innovations break through and landscape pressures intensify, leading to adjustments in the Socio-technical 
regime as well as cultural and political conflicts (Geels, 2019).
Institutionalisation/anchoring
The new regime becomes dominant and shapes existing practices and culture. Over time, it may itself become stable 
and resistant to change (Geels, 2019).
The MLP provides an approach to consider sustainability transitions across system levels and transition phases. As 
defined by MLP, sustainability transitions do not result from one particular cause or driver, but from the interaction and 
alignment of activities across all three system levels (Geels, 2005b, 2011). To gain deeper insights of how to actively 
manage sustainability transitions, Transition Management (TM) is considered.

3.3 A Transition Management approach
Moving from a descriptive to a prescriptive approach on sustainability transitions, this thesis focuses on Transition 
Management (TM) due to its emphasis on actively managing ongoing governance processes within society to 
facilitate sustainable change (Loorbach, 2010). TM is a process-oriented and reflexive governance approach that 
aims to proactively facilitate socio-technical sustainability transitions (Loorbach, 2010). Instead of using traditional 
top-down methods, TM emphasises influencing over controlling, aiming at creating conditions under which systemic 
change can happen through collaboration among a wide range of actors (Loorbach, 2010; Massuga et al., 2024).
TM is a cyclical and adaptive process, consisting of four interconnected phases (Loorbach, 2010): Strategic, Tactical, 
Operational and Reflexive, as shown by Figure 3.2. The four phases are described on the next page.
Strategical
The strategic phase of TM focuses on the development of long term visions and goals that could lead to changes in 
the structures and culture of a socio-technical system (Gaziulusoy & Ryan, 2017; Kemp et al., 2007). Strategic activities 
include establishment of the transition arena, facilitating strategic discussions, setting collective norms and goals, 
formulating long-term goals and defining long-term anticipations to promote sustainability transitions (Loorbach, 
2010).
Tactical
The tactical TM phase is about implementing the desired goal based on the selected transition agenda, i.e., 
translating the strategic vision into action (Gaziulusoy & Ryan, 2017). Tactical activities include developing networks, 
negotiating scenarios, and exploring potential pathways for a transition, in order to select and create detailed 
pathways for sustainability transitions (Kemp et al., 2007; Loorbach, 2010).
Operational
The operational TM phase is related to performing experiments and learning-by-doing at the niche level on a shorter 
time horizon (Gaziulusoy & Ryan, 2017). The operational activities include mobilising actors to help with the 
implementation of innovative projects and experiments, e.g., test of new technologies, polities, initiatives  or practices. 
The experiments and actions must fit the context of the developed pathway and are carried out to deepen, broaden 
and scale up existing and planned actions and initiatives (Loorbach, 2010).
Reflexive
The reflexive activities monitor, assess and evaluate the transition path and the activities from the operational level. 
The activities refer to monitoring whether the transition goals and visions are reachable, and evaluating the 
management of the transition (Loorbach, 2010). The activities also relate to assessing the developments on a broader 
societal scale, e.g., assessing public opinions of effectiveness of changes in politics and political agendas (Loorbach, 
2010). These reflexive activities are necessary to prevent the development of new lock-ins and explore new ideas and 
trajectories (Loorbach, 2010).
TM is a cyclical and adaptive process where each phase of the process reinforces the next. The strategic phase sets 
long-term goals, guiding tactical planning, which is tested through experiments in the operational phase. The reflexive 
phase monitors and evaluates progress, feeding back to update strategies. This cycle enables flexible and responsive 
management of sustainability transitions (Loorbach, 2010).
TM recognises that sustainability transitions result from the dynamic interplay between actors, including public 
institutions, civil society, industry, and academia, engaged in co-creating new societal configurations. The 
effectiveness of TM depends on the ability of governance processes to be participatory, experimental, and inclusive 
(Massuga et al., 2024), implying the importance of actors with the ability to facilitate sustainability transition efforts. 
Actors taking on the roles of enablers and facilitators align with the principles of TM, as it supports collaboration 
among different actors, and enables learning within and across institutional boundaries (Massuga et al., 2024).

Facilitating actors can be considered effective for managing sustainability transitions, which is also reflected in the 
increasing number of articles on this subject in recent years (Kivimaa et al., 2018). The literature refers to these types 
of actors using various terms, including “middle actors” (Parag & Janda, 2014), “frontrunners” (Loorbach, 2010), 
“intermediaries” (Kivimaa et al., 2018; Polzin et al., 2016), and “changemakers” (Design Council, 2021). In this thesis, the 
term Transition-facilitators is used as a unifying concept for actors who support collaboration across stakeholder 
groups and drive sustainability-oriented change.
Transition-facilitators play an increasingly important role in the literature on sustainable transitions, which is reflected 
in the growing number of publications addressing their function and significance (Kivimaa et al., 2018). These actors 
are perceived as key forces supporting sustainability transitions and can operate at different levels in the system 
depending on their role, context, and point of emergence (Kivimaa et al., 2018; 2019; Loorbach, 2010; Polzin et al., 2016). 
By initiating collaboration, promoting new ideas, and challenging existing structures, Transition-facilitators can help 
reshape socio-technical systems in a more sustainable direction (Kivimaa et al., 2018).
Transition-facilitators are characterised as actors at the forefront of sustainable development. They have been 
identified as key actors capable of accelerating the transition towards sustainability (Kivimaa et al., 2018; Loorbach, 
2010). These actors tie networks together that, collectively and over time, can influence broader agendas and policy 
(Loorbach, 2010). Rather than acting as top-down decision-makers, Transition-facilitators take on a process-oriented 
role. They work across governance levels and sectors to align interests and mediate between competing priorities 
(Kivimaa et al., 2019).
Types of Transition-facilitators
Being a Transition-facilitator requires a strategic and systemic mindset, as navigating the complexities of socio-
technical systems demands the ability to engage with diverse actors and facilitate collective learning processes (Mok 
& Gaziulusoy, 2018). In order to better understand the diverse contributions of these actors, it is useful to distinguish 
between different types of Transition-facilitators. As identified by Kivimaa et al. (2019), Transition-facilitators vary in 
their system positioning, methods, and scope of influence. However, they share a common purpose: to promote 
sustainability through the mobilisation of networks and resources that can support transformative change. Table 3.1

Systemic Transition-facilitators
Systemic transition facilitators promote broad, cross-sectoral change by acting as neutral bridge-builders who

and shared visions focused on transformation rather than optimisation. Through strategic disruption and the 
development of innovative frameworks, they support the reconfiguration of socio-technical systems and enable 
experimentation that can drive long-term sustainability transitions. By connecting efforts across scales and sectors, 
systemic facilitators help steer structural change and create the conditions necessary for transformative change

Regime-based transition facilitators operate within dominant institutional structures and use their access to resources

Process Transition facilitators focus on the practical implementation of sustainable initiatives in concrete contexts.

3.5 The role of Systemic Design
Following the idea that transitions can be supported, recent research for Design for Sustainability emphasises that 
design can play a positive role in transitions, especially in supporting Transition-facilitators in governance processes of 
sustainability transition (Gaziulusoy & Öztekin, 2019)
Over the past decades, the field of design has expanded from a focus on products to engaging with complex societal 
and environmental challenges. Gaziulusoy and Ryan (2017) describe this shift as a move towards using design as a 
strategic tool in long-term sustainability transitions. Rather than concentrating solely on physical outputs, design now 
plays a role in shaping systems, facilitating exchange of knowledge, and supporting collaborative processes. Barbero 
and Pereno (2020) similarly underline design’s ability to enable systemic innovation by tackling interconnected issues 
such as climate change, social inequality, and urban development. This shift reflects a broader understanding of 
design as a process that not only solves problems but also helps shape futures through interdisciplinary collaboration 
and co-creation.
Systemic Design is an interdisciplinary approach that combines Design Thinking and Systems Thinking (Barbero & 
Pereno, 2020; Ryan, 2014). While both approaches aim to address complex problems, they differ in their methods. 
Design Thinking is good at generating innovative, user centered solutions but often struggles to account for broader 
systemic dynamics (Jones, 2014; Gaziulusoy & Ryan, 2017). Conversely, Systems Thinking offers tools to understand 
complex interconnections within larger socio-technical systems and identify leverage points for change but can lack 
the ability to translate insights into actionable design processes (Wilkerson & Trellevik, 2021). Systemic Design can 
therefore support the understanding of problems rooted in systems and help build a strong foundation for developing 
new strategies and ideas to handle complex problems (Barbero & Pereno, 2020). 
Several different disciplines and variations of the definition of Systemic Design exist in literature. To avoid theoretical 
confusion about the term, this thesis will apply the definition from Barbero and Pereno (2020), describing Systemic 
Design as an umbrella term for six different design disciplines, as shown in Figure 3.3. Despite their variations, the 
design disciplines share common abilities to analyse, define, and visualise systems and their challenges, to imagine 
creative and desirable futures, and to build co-creation processes involving diverse actors (Barbero & Pereno, 2020).

The six Systemic Design disciplines differ in their methodological framework, where some focus on stakeholder

niche innovations. However, the categorisation of the six disciplines does not intend to divide them into separate

4.1 Current state of Copenhagen’s food system

heavily dependent on both national and global food chains. Therefore, an analysis of the city's food system also 
provides insight into how it relates to the larger systems. The next sections analyses Copenhagen’s food system

4.1.1 Socio-technical landscape
The Socio-technical landscape represents the broader context that influences the dynamics of Socio-technical

meals and takeaway, that require minimal or no preparation (Rambøll Management Consulting, 2021; Landbrug & 
fødevare, 2024). The increasing consumption of convenient food has likely contributed to a higher demand for 
packaging, which can pose challenges for sustainability efforts, especially in terms of waste management and 
resource use (Miljøministeriet, 2021).

Accelerating society
Technological advancements have increased productivity and efficiency in modern societies, impacting the society

4.1.2 Socio-technical regime

characterised by established practices, norms, and rules that shape the structure and functioning of Copenhagen's 
food system. To clarify the forces that maintain or transform Copenhagen’s current food system, the following 
analysis focuses on the categories culture, industry, markets and user preferences, politics, science and technology.
Food culture 
The Danish food culture has historically been centred around meat and dairy products, which still play a significant 
role in the daily diets of Copenhagen’s citizens. However, there are signs of a slow change in the consumption of 
meat. More citizens than before are now following the official dietary guidelines of lower meat consumption, but meat 
is still one of the most purchased items in the supermarket (Madkulturen 2023; Fødevarestyrelsen, 2024). This 
indicates a strong attachment to traditional habits, which can hinder progress toward more sustainable choices.
Food industry
Copenhagen's food system is highly dependent on imports, as only 0.5 km² of the municipality is covered by 
agricultural crops (Danmarks Statistik, 2025). Food is therefore imported from other parts of Denmark or from abroad 
via distribution networks of large retail chains. Nationally, the food industry is characterised by industrialisation and 
large-scale operations, dominated by actors such as Salling Group and Coop (Reske, 2023). This creates high entry 
barriers for smaller, local producers, but as the current food industry is profitable in the short term, many actors have 
economic incentives to maintain the current system (Geels, 2011). Additionally, overproduction of meat, large amounts 
of packaging and production waste significantly impact the environment (Klimarådet, 2020).
Food Politics 
Copenhagen Municipality established one of Europe’s most ambitious sustainable food policies in 2019 with its Food 
Strategy. The strategy demanded over 90% of food in public institutions to be organic, and a 25% reduction in 
greenhouse gas emissions from public food purchases. The 25% reduction target was reached in 2023 (Københavns 
Kommune, 2019). However, agricultural policy maintains support for conventional and intensive production at national 
and EU level, particularly through the EU’s Common Agricultural Policy, which primarily benefits large-scale production 
and livestock farming (Pe’er et al., 2020). This creates a structural lock-in, where political and economic interests 
uphold the existing system. Importantly, this continued support is not solely due to the necessity of food production, 
but also because agriculture represents a significant contributor to GDP (Gross domestic product). This economic 
reliance reinforces institutional dependence and makes systemic change more difficult. Although new initiatives such 
as a forthcoming CO₂ tax are being developed (Ministry of Foreign Affairs of Denmark, 2024), the system remains 
shaped by these entrenched institutional and economic dependencies.

Market and user preferences
The Socio-technical landscape stated that climate change affects the environmental awareness of consumers. This 
also affects the market and user preferences of Copenhagen citizens. In recent years there has been a growing 
interest in Copenhagen about organic and plant-based foods on a larger scale than experienced in the rest of 
Denmark. Yet studies show that taste, health and convenience are the three most important factors when 
Copenhagen citizens choose food products (Rambøll Management Consulting, 2021). This indicates a mismatch 
between sustainable intentions and actual habits. Despite growing awareness of climate and environmental issues, 
practical considerations continue to influence consumer choices, slowing the pace of market-based change.
Science 
Several Danish universities are actively researching sustainable food systems and products (University of 
Copenhagen, 2025; The Technical university of Denmark, 2025; Aarhus university, 2025). The research topics range 
from alternative protein sources, agroecology, sustainable diets and circularity to food waste. Despite the high 
amount of research activities, there is still a gap between academic knowledge and consumer behaviour in terms of 
what a sustainable diet is (Rambøll Management Consulting, 2021). The results of the research are rarely translated 
directly into practice or policy, which reduces the potential for change in the food system.
Technology 
In Denmark the agricultural  sector has adopted technological tools like drones, sensors, and satellites. These 
technologies help enhance the precision of farming practices (Food Nation, n.d.). An important technology in 
Copenhagen is its food waste management system, which sorts and processes organic waste to convert it into 
biogas. The residual product from biogas production is used as natural fertiliser in agriculture, which creates a closed 
loop and reduces the need for chemical fertilisers (Circular Copenhagen, n.d). While biogas contributes to both CO₂ 
reduction and resource utilisation, it is not yet broadly implemented, as biogas competes with other technological 
energy sources such as solar and wind energy. Increased dependence on biogas can also create a new structural 
lock-in (path dependence), where investments and infrastructure keep the system in one specific direction. Likewise, 
precision agriculture’s reliance on technology systems risks technological lock-in. In this way, technologies play an 
important role but also carry risks if not flexibly integrated in the system.
The Socio-technical regime in Copenhagen's food system is largely characterised by stable structures and practices 
that create resistance to change. Danish food culture is still strongly rooted in meat and dairy products, which poses 
a barrier to dietary shifts with lower environmental impacts. The food industry is dominated by large supermarket 
chains and imports, which creates high barriers of entry for local producers and maintains environmentally 
damaging practices. Politically, there is a misalignment between ambitious municipal initiatives, and national and EU 
support schemes that maintain conventional production. The market experiences increased interest in organic and 
plant-based food, but at the same time consumers' choices are primarily driven by taste, health and convenience. 
Research into sustainable food systems is extensive but has limited influence on practice and policy. Technologies 
play a role in enhancing farming practices and close resource cycles, but risk creating new structural lock-ins. In 
summary, the Socio-technical regime is characterised by a dynamic interplay of change and stability, which together 
shape its current resistance to more transformative shifts.
4.1.3 Niche-innovations 
Niche-innovations focus on the radical innovations that can disrupt the current food system. Several promising niche 
innovations have emerged within Copenhagen, challenging and complementing the dominant regime and offering 
alternatives aligned with sustainability transitions. 
Alternative Agricultural methods 
Urban and Vertical farming initiatives, such as rooftop farms and community farming, are gaining traction in reused 
urban spaces around Copenhagen. Initiatives such as ØsterGro (ØsterGro, 2025) and Øens have (Øens Have, 2023) 
offer local production, reducing transport emissions and improving freshness of the products.
Alternative food supplier
Many alternative food suppliers have gained popularity in recent years. Initiatives such as  Årstiderne (Årstiderne, 
2022) and HelloFresh (HelloFresh, 2025), where consumers can order meal-kits containing all the ingredients needed 
for the dishes, offers an alternative to the supermarkets and can be seen as a response to an accelerating society. 
Other alternatives to the supermarket are the farm-to-consumer initiatives like FællesGro (FælleGro, 2021) and Grønt 
Marked (Grønt Marked, 2024). These initiatives both aim to strengthen the connection between consumers and 
farmers.
Reusable packaging and take-back systems
Reusable packaging and take-back systems continue to become more widely implemented in restaurants, take-
away services, and public institutions, to reduce single-use plastic waste. Systems like NewLoop (NewLoop, 2025) 
enable circular solutions for products previously consumed as single-use.
Circular Economy models and Food waste handling
The number of food waste handling initiatives have increased in recent years, with initiatives like TooGooToGo 
(TooGoodToGo, 2022) and Wefood (Folkekirkens nødhjælp, 2025) keeping food products from being wasted.

4.1.4 Summary
In summary, Copenhagen’s food system is shaped by global and EU landscape pressures that are increasingly 
incompatible with the current Socio-technical regime. Niche-innovations also add pressure to the existing food

transitions remain partial. This suggests that Copenhagen is currently in the diffusion/disruption phase of a 
sustainability transition in the food system. To better understand how sustainability transitions can be accelerated in 
Copenhagen’s food system, it is essential to engage with the actors operating within and across these levels. The 
following section presents qualitative findings from interviews with key stakeholders to uncover the needs, challenges,

Uneven capabilities and resources across actors
A recurring challenge highlighted in the interviews was the unequal distribution of resources and capacities among 
actors in Copenhagen’s food system. According to AD, larger companies typically have more capacity to invest in 
sustainability, innovate and respond to new regulations, while smaller companies often struggle to keep up (AD, 
personal communication, 2025). This inequality is becoming increasingly relevant as Danish and EU regulations place 
increasing demands on companies to comply with sustainability standards (seen section 4.1).
Although the level of ambition is not necessarily lower among smaller actors, their ability to act is constrained by 
limited financial and organizational resources. Furthermore, the differences are not only about size, but also about 
priorities. As JM pointed out: “There is a big difference in the focus of the actors in the system. Food producers are very 
focused on economics” (JM, personal communication, 2025). This economic focus can limit the commitment to 
sustainability among some actors, especially when short-term profitability is prioritised over long-term transitions. As 
a result, the sustainability transition unfolds unevenly across the system, with some actors making rapid progress 
while others fall behind, not necessarily due to a lack of will, but due to structural and strategic constraints.
Misaligned interests
Misaligned interests within Copenhagen’s food system emerged as a significant barrier to transitioning from the 
current regime. Both MA and CJ noted that varying approaches to sustainability among actors complicate the 
establishment of a shared vision (MA, personal communication, 2025; JM, personal communication, 2025). JM from 
Meyers Madhus pointed out that “different actors are working with sustainability transitions in different ways,” while MA 
from Copenhagen Municipality noted the challenge of creating a unified approach both internally and externally (MA, 
personal communication, 2025). AD, who facilitates a food network of diverse stakeholders, also emphasised the 
difficulty in bringing together actors with different priorities and perspectives. AD emphasised that although some 
large players can be difficult to influence directly, the network is designed to foster collaboration by creating spaces 
for dialogue and shared learning. (AD, personal communication, 2025). This diversity in approaches and interests 
contributes to the slow progress in moving beyond the current regime.
Limited influence on market and user preferences
The challenge that most actors mentioned in the interviews were the limited ability to influence market and user 
preferences. As explained in Section 4.1.2, there is a mismatch between consumer values and behaviour, making it 
difficult for actors within the food system to influence the actual behaviour of Copenhagen citizens. This was also 
highlighted by LF from FællesGro, who noted that “consumers have visions about wanting to act sustainably, but they 
don’t” (LF, personal communication, 2025), emphasising the gap between intentions and actions. Additionally, 
changing habits and consumer behaviour was highlighted as a long process by JC from Circular Copenhagen (JC, 
personal communication, 2025), often taking up to 10 years before change is seen. JM from Meyers Madhus 
mentioned the importance of showing the consumers how to act instead of only informing them about it, which was 
shared with MA from Copenhagen Municipality (JC, personal communication, 2025; MA, personal communication, 
2025). JC and AM described it as difficult to influence private consumption, partly due to political constraints such as 
the kommunalfuldmagt (KL, 2024) that limits municipal involvement in private market activities without explicit legal 
basis. This limits their ability to actively support behavioural changes despite strong ambitions (JC, personal 
communication, 2025; MA, personal communication, 2025).
Structural and economic barriers to local and sustainable food
Another challenge emphasised by several actors is the structural and economic barriers facing locally produced and 
sustainable food. LF from FællesGro pointed out that Danish-produced food often comes at a higher price, which 
does not align well with price-sensitive consumers (LF, personal communication, 2025). This creates a difficult 
situation where it is challenging to set a fair price for food products across the system if consumers are unwilling to 
pay the necessary amount. Additionally, local, national, and EU policies can sometimes slow down transition initiatives, 
creating further obstacles for change (LF, personal communication, 2025). These barriers are reinforced by the 
dominant role of large supermarket chains and the conventional agricultural system, which remains focused on 
meat and dairy exports. This creates a gap between Denmark’s green ambitions and the current functioning of the 
food system. RD from DTU Food highlighted this mismatch, stating, “Denmark presents as a ‘green country’, but it isn’t 
in the food system. Denmark is still very conservative with a large meat industry” (RD, personal communication, 2025).
Difficulties translating knowledge into practice
The last challenge shared by several actors during the interviews concerns the difficulties in translating knowledge 
into practical action. RD from DTU Food highlighted that although many knowledge resources and support tools 
already exist, it is crucial for users to understand how to apply them in practice for them to be effective (RD, personal 
communication, 2025). JM from Meyers Madhus emphasised that real-world situations are often very practical, so 
solutions need to be tangible and hands-on to truly assist users (JM, personal communication, 2025). MA stated that 
Copenhagen Municipality has previously achieved success implementing sustainability initiatives within public 
kitchens, but as they noted, “We have a hard time transferring the good experiences from the public sector to the rest 
of the city and the food system” (MA personal communication, 2025)(Copenhagen Municipality, 2025). This highlights 
the challenge of scaling and adapting successful public sector practices to the broader food system in Copenhagen.

leverage points can themselves be seen as leverage points; not as static elements, but as actors that enable and 
activate systemic change. Without such a facilitating role, there is a risk that promising initiatives will remain isolated, 
and the overall transition losing direction and impact.
4.3 Mapping of Transition-facilitators 
Section 4.2 showed that one of the main challenges in accelerating the sustainable transition in Copenhagen’s food 
system is a lack of coordination and coherence between the many actors involved. The absence of a common 
direction and synergies between initiatives means that efforts are made in one area of intervention and often remain 
isolated. To meet this challenge, there is a need for actors who can work across silos, build bridges and promote 
collaboration across sectors and scales, also called Transition-facilitators.
Transition-facilitators play a key role in driving sustainability transitions and can operate at different levels in the 
system depending on their role, context and point of emergence (Kivimaa et al., 2018; 2019; Loorbach, 2010; Polzin et al., 
2016). Considering this, it is important to understand which actors within Copenhagen’s food system have the 
potential to act as Transition-facilitators to help address the system's most important challenges.

transitions rather than optimising the existing system. Systemic Transition-facilitators aim to reconfigure it, through

Regime-Based Transition Facilitators
Regime-based Transition-facilitators are actors from established institutions such as public authorities, professional 
communities, or business networks, who actively support sustainable transitions, either voluntarily or as part of their 
official responsibilities (Berkvist & Söderholm, 2011; Parag & Janda, 2014; Kivimaa et al., 2018). In Copenhagen’s food 
system, examples of Regime-based Transition-facilitators are Copenhagen Municipality, Dansk Erhvervs 
Fødevarenetværk (food network), and Dansk Industri Fødevarenetværk (food network) (Københavns Kommune, 2025; 
Dansk Erhverv, 2024; Dansk Industri, 2022). These institutions operate within established frameworks, but work 
purposefully to allocate resources, create collaboration across sectors, and develop sustainability strategies.  In some 
cases, their role is institutionally defined within national or regional green strategies (Hodson & Marvin, 2012; Kivimaa, 
2018). This is the case for Copenhagen Municipality, as both the Food Strategy (Københavns kommune, n.d.) and the 
national reduction targets (Klimarådet, 2024) creates opportunities for sustainable innovation to take place in 
practice. The work of the municipality shares similarities with the characteristics of a Regime based Transition-
facilitator. The work of Copenhagen Municipality includes developing supportive strategies, translating political visions 
into practical action and stabilising innovation efforts in a shifting political landscape (Moss, 2009).
While not typically radical change agents, Regime-based Transition-facilitators play a key role in creating the 
institutional and political space needed for new ways of thinking (Kivimaa et al., 2018). Dansk Erhverv Fødevarenetværk 
has great experience with creating a platform for food providers to meet and discuss their challenges and get 
inspiration for new ways of innovating their production (Dansk Erhverv, 2024). Regime-based Transition-facilitators 
can bridge the existing regime with emerging alternatives, supporting experiments, fostering partnerships, and 
enabling collaboration across actors  (Polzin et al., 2016). This is something that both Dansk Erhverv and Dansk Industri 
do with their food networks, where they invite a range of actors from small entrepreneurs to the largest global food 
companies in Denmark.
Together, these actors demonstrate how Regime-based Transition-facilitators support sustainability transitions by 
creating institutional frameworks for collaboration, promoting experimentation, and building bridges between 
established structures and new solutions.
Niche Transition-facilitators
Niche Transition-facilitators work with experimentation and innovation, which often take place outside the dominant 
structures. Niche Transition-facilitators play a crucial role in connecting local projects, sharing knowledge and 
experiences, and thereby strengthening the development of alternative practices (Kivimaa et al., 2018; Howells, 2006). 
Examples of Niche Transition-facilitators include OneThird, Andelsgaarde and Sporá. These Niche Transition-
facilitators are NGOs promoting practice change, research developing and testing of new products, and thus support 
the development of alternative food systems and consumption  patterns (OneThird, 2024; Andelsgaarde, 2024; Spora, 
2023).
Niche Transition-facilitators often work relationally by identifying common challenges and solutions among different 
local actors, contributing to scaling and broadening innovative solutions that eventually can help challenge the 
established system (Moss, 2009). This is also the case for One Third, Andelsgaarde and Sporá (OneThird, 2023; 
Andelsgaarde, 2024; Spora, 2023).

Process Transition-facilitators
Process Transition-facilitators focus on the practical implementation of sustainable initiatives in concrete contexts.

4.4 Case Study: Copenhagen Municipality
Copenhagen Municipality is facing an important transition, in its work with the city’s Food Strategy. Whereas previous 
efforts have primarily focused on the municipality’s own public kitchens, the ambitions now extend much more 
broadly to include the entire city’s food system and the eating habits of its citizens. This development reflects an 
increased focus on sustainability, climate, and social engagement, requiring new ways of collaborating both 
internally within the municipality and with external actors.
In developing a new Food Strategy for the period 2026 to 2035, the municipality wants to set new ambitious 
sustainability goals for Copenhagen’s food system. These goals include concrete municipal initiatives but primarily 
aims to drive broader systemic change. This strategic shift means that the municipality aims to take on a new role as 
a Transition-facilitator, positioning itself as a coordinating and enabling actor who can bring together different 
stakeholders and create the framework for innovation and change. This role is new for the Department for Food and 
Meals, which until now has mainly worked through direct management and internal targets.
The following section analyses both previous strategic experiences, and planned initiatives in the new strategy to 
identify how the municipality can best be supported in this new role and thereby contribute to a sustainable 
transformation of Copenhagen’s food system.
4.4.1 Previous strategies 
Copenhagen’s Municipality has been working strategically with Food and Meals for over 20 years, particularly through 
the Department for Food and Meals. The department is organisationally anchored in the Economic Administration but 
works across five other administrations in the municipality. The department is responsible for around 1,000 public 
kitchens and delivers over 115,000 meals daily to daycare centers, schools, and nursing homes in the social sector in 
Copenhagen (Københavns Kommune, n.d.). 
The municipality’s approach to Food and Meals is not only about nutrition, but also about social engagement, health 
and environmental sustainability. The strategy therefore takes responsibility for both people and the planet 
(Københavns Kommune, n.d).
The initial target, set in 2001, was for 75% of the food served in public kitchens to be organic. This target was later 
increased to 90%, demonstrating the municipality’s capacity to drive change within the city’s food system. In 2019, the 
City Council adopted the municipality’s first official Food Strategy for the period 2019–2025. The strategy is structured 
around five key themes (Københavns Kommune, 2019):
1) Health and nutrition, 2) Quality and food enjoyment, 3) Sustainability and climate concern, 4) Strengthening social 
communities and food culture, 5) Copenhagen as a healthy, green and vibrant food city.
The first four themes focus on the transformation of the municipality's own kitchens, while the fifth theme is about the 
citizens and the local food system (Københavns Kommune, n.d.). The strategy has set specific goals and initiatives 
under each theme. Within the third theme, Sustainability and Climate Concern, the goal was to reduce the climate 
footprint of meals in the municipality's own canteens by 25%, a goal that was already achieved in mid-2023 (Cool 
food, 2023; Københavns Kommune, n.d.).

In general, the municipality has had the most success with the goals set for the first four themes, where the

4.4.2 New strategies 
Copenhagen Municipality is currently in the development of a new Food Strategy for the period 2026–2030 (Appendix 
4.g; Københavns Kommune, 2019). Whereas previous strategies have primarily focused on improving municipal 
kitchens, the new strategy places greater emphasis on involving the entire city’s food system and changing eating 
habits among citizens.
The strategy aims to support Copenhagen’s broader sustainability and climate agenda, including the municipality’s 
goal of reducing CO₂ emissions from citizens’ consumption by 50% by 2030 compared to 2019 (Københavns 
Kommune, 2023a).
To achieve this goal, it is necessary to change citizens’ consumption habits, a task that requires more than 
information and campaigns. Integrating sustainable choices into everyday life is therefore essential, but this requires 
more than traditional information campaigns. In this context, the municipality’s adoption of a regime-based 
Transition-facilitator role is particularly relevant, as it enables collaboration across sectors and stakeholders. By 
fostering partnerships, encouraging co-creation, and supporting local experiments, the municipality can help create 
environments where climate-friendly choices become easier and more accessible for citizens, retailers, producers, 
and other actors alike. This facilitative role is crucial to mediate between policy goals and everyday practices, thus 
supporting a deeper and more systemic sustainability transition (Teknik- og Miljøforvaltningen, 2023).
This strategic change marks a shift in the municipality’s role, from being an actor with direct management tools to 
being a Transition-facilitator who influences through collaboration, inspiration and networking. This shift forms the 
background for the next section
4.4.3 Department for Food and Meals entering a new role as a facilitator 
The new strategy positions the municipality more clearly as a facilitator of change, fostering innovation and 
behavioural shifts via collaboration and local experimentation. (Københavns Kommune, 2023b). Figure 4.3 illustrates 
the different roles a municipality can take on to promote sustainable transitions. The model shows how the 
municipality’s degree of direct influence and the potential for systemic change varies depending on the chosen role 
(Københavns Kommune, 2023a).
When the municipality acts as a business, it holds considerable influence, though with limited potential for broader 
systemic change. In this role, the municipality can reduce its own consumption-based emissions through measures 
such as sustainable procurement policies, as demonstrated in the public kitchens.
As an authority, the municipality can exert influence through instruments like local planning and regulation. One such 
initiative has been to ensure the availability of plant-based meal options in all public institutions.
When acting as a company owner, the municipality can impact the direction of companies it owns or co-owns for 
instance, through participation in the governing boards of municipal canteens. However, the influence is more limited 
in this role, as these companies typically operate with a degree of independence.
According to the model, the role of Transition-facilitator has the greatest potential for creating change, even though 
the municipality has limited direct control in this role. As a Transition-facilitator, the municipality can support the 
sustainability transition by creating connections across sectors, promoting knowledge sharing and supporting 
experiments together with actors such as consumers, retailers, agriculture and industry. The goal is to break down 
silos and develop new knowledge and new solutions that can lead to long-term changes in Copenhagen’s food 
system (Københavns Kommune, 2023b).
The low level of control makes it difficult for the Department for Food and Meals, to take on the role, as they cannot set 
requirements or ensure results through traditional management tools. Furthermore, the Department has not 
previously worked systematically as a Transition-facilitator and therefore lacks both the experience and tools to 
effectively fulfil the role.
Despite this, the Municipality of Copenhagen has a special opportunity. As both a political actor and a practical 
implementing body, the municipality can establish connections between new solutions and existing structures. By 
integrating sustainability into both strategies and daily practices, the municipality can support a broad and systemic 
transformation of the city's food system (Polzin et al., 2016; Kivimaa et al., 2018).

4.4.4 Summary
The collaboration with Copenhagen Municipality represents a significant opportunity, as the municipality is currently 
developing a new Food Strategy for the city. This strategy expands the focus from individual canteens to include the 
entire city's food system. Such a transition requires new skills and implies that the Food and Meals Department must 
take on a new role as a Transition-facilitator, a function that has not previously been an integrated part of the 
department's practice (Appendix 4.g). The role requires not only competences, but also new tools that can support a 
more interdisciplinary, strategic and citizen-involved approach.
4.5 Summary of the Empirical study
This chapter analysed Copenhagen’s food system from a sustainability transition perspective using the Multi-Level 
Perspective (MLP). First, an overview was presented, showing how global and EU-driven pressures challenge the 
existing Socio-technical regime in the food system. Niche innovations such as urban farming and meal boxes offer 
alternative solutions, while the City of Copenhagen’s food strategy acts as an important driver for sustainable 
consumption at the institutional level. Despite signs of adaptation, the transition remains partial, and the system was 
defined as being in the MLP phase  diffusion/disruption.
Through interviews with key stakeholders, five central challenges were identified: unequal resources between large 
and small actors, diverging interests, limited influence on consumer behaviour, structural and economic barriers, and 
difficulties in translating knowledge into practice. These challenges pointed to a need for better coordination and 
collaboration within the system.
This highlighted a significant potential within the system: the need for a Transition Facilitator — an actor who can 
create coherence, build trust, and connect efforts across sectors.
The analysis of existing and potential Transition Facilitators showed that actors such as Madland and Meyers Madhus 
are already supporting the transition but lack the capacity and strategic direction to create systemic impact. In 
connection with the development of a new, city-wide Food Strategy, Copenhagen Municipality was identified as an 
actor with the potential of being a Transition-facilitator. This role requires new competencies and tools that can 
support an interdisciplinary, strategic and citizen-involved approach that has not previously been integrated into the 
municipality’s practice.

This chapter examines how Copenhagen Municipality can be supported in the development of the new Food Strategy 
from 2026 to 2030, requiring the municipality to enter a new role as a Transition-facilitator. Needs and expectations for 
a support framework were identified by conducting a co-creation session with the municipality, as the representants 
from the department for Food and Meals is considered the main user of the support framework. The session resulted

5.1 Investigating how Copenhagen Municipality can be 
supported
As described in Chapter 4.4, the municipality is in the process of redefining its role, from a traditional authority to a 
Transition-facilitator within Copenhagen's food system. As discovered in section 4.4, Copenhagen Municipality has 
limited previous experience in acting as a Transition-facilitator in the Food Strategy, suggesting a need for support.
To understand how Copenhagen Municipality can be supported, a Task Clarification process was initiated (Pahl et al., 
2007). Central to this process was creating a co-creation session with two key representatives from the Food and 
Meal Department, Anna K. Jönsson and Mathilde H. Rønnow. 
5.1.1 Preparing for the session
The purpose of the session was to actively involve the department in formulating the preliminary requirements and 
criteria for a solution that could support them in their new role as a Transition-facilitator (Interview seen in Appendix 
4.g). In preparation for the co-creation session, a physical version of a requirements and criteria matrix was prepared 
and printed (seen in Appendix 5.a). To ensure structured requirements and criteria, the session was built around five 
central themes. As the participants had no prior experience with the method and had different professional 
backgrounds (seen in Appendix 4.e), the themes were used as guiding points for the session.
5.1.2 During the session
The co-creation session was part of the second meeting with Copenhagen Municipality and helped create the 
solution space for the support. The full meeting can be seen in Appendix 4.g. The output from the session is 
elaborated for each theme A) Format and accessibility,  B) Time and resources, C) Flexibility, D) Support for the 
phases of the process, and E) Implementation
A. Format and accessibility
The focus of this theme was how the support should be designed and presented, for example, whether it should be 
digital, physical or a combination. This also included how the content should be displayed (as maps, guides, videos or 
templates) and how the navigation should be.
The two representatives emphasised that it was important that the solution is digital, but at the same time allowing 
for printing individual parts. Visual elements and step-by-step instructions were highlighted as particularly important 
to quickly create a common understanding. At the same time, concerns were expressed about the solution being 
either too theoretical or technically complex, and the tools within the support should therefore be accessible and 
easy to use.
"It needs to be digital—something that can be integrated into a system. It is also nice you can get it as a one-pager 
for print. There should be a figure that could communicate, a step-by-step guide. No search function – that would 
be too technical"  (M. H. Rønnow,  personal communication, 2025)(Seen in Appendix 4.g).
“Yes, and for me it's important not to get too theoretical (...) It has to be easy to understand and use right away” (M. H. 
Rønnow,  personal communication, 2025)(Seen in Appendix 4.g).
“something visual that provides common understanding. If it takes more than half an hour to read aloud, we lose 
concentration. Better small tools that build on each other than one large tool” (A. K. Jönsson, personal 
communication, 2025).
In summary, the two representatives emphasised that the solution must be digital, easy to use and enable printable 
parts, with visual and simple step-by-step instructions. It must not be too theoretical or technically complex.
B. Time and resources
The focus for this theme was about how the support fitted the time and resources of users. This includes whether the 
tools should be able to be used in both short and long timeframes, and whether it is important to show how long 
each tool approximately takes to use. Based on insights from the two representatives, the support was only 
considered useful if it fitted within their available time and resources. Therefore, a key requirement for the support was 
that it must be quick and easy to access. The representatives from the department work under tight deadlines and 
with limited resources, so it must be clear immediately how to use the support without needing to spend significant 
time understanding them.
"We don't have time to try everything, so it needs to be clear how to take on a facilitator role – without us having to 
spend endless time understanding it all theoretically” (A. K. Jönsson, personal communication, 2025)(Seen in 
Appendix 4.g).
“It has to be simple. We often work with short deadlines and simple workshops, so we need to have the energy to use 
the tools in practice”  (M. H. Rønnow,  personal communication, 2025)(Seen in Appendix 4.g).
In summary, time and resources were crucial as employees work under tight deadlines, so the tools needed to be 
quick and easy to access for the facilitators without spending a lot of time understanding them.

C. Flexibility
The focus of this theme was about whether the support should be adaptable to different target groups and cases,

showed broad agreement about the importance of flexibility. However, this should not require the user to invent

“Yes, and flexibility is important – but that shouldn’t mean we have to develop everything from scratch every time.

The focus for this theme was identifying the phases within the strategy process, where support was most critical and

The representatives emphasised the importance of the tool supporting the entire strategy process, rather than only 
isolated parts. The tool should help create structure and overview in a process that is often experienced as complex

support on how to effectively co-create and co-develop.
"We want to know: how to co-create? How to co-develop? We want to think of it as a long-term tool. So, what can we

In summary, the representatives wanted a support that could guide the entire strategy process and create an 
overview. Since the facilitator role is new, they asked for help with co-creation and practical workshop facilitation.
E. Implementation
The focus of this theme was on how the support could assist in translating strategic ideas into concrete action. This 
included tools such as checklists and project management aids. The overarching aim was on how to support 
decision-making and implementation by offering practical tools or instruments that support the move from strategic 
choices to operational delivery.
The interview revealed an important point was the desire for concrete usability. The tool should be modular, so that 
individual parts can be easily used in meetings or workshops without necessarily using the entire tool.
“We would like to be able to reuse methods in other contexts as well. A tool that supports our role as facilitator – also

In summary, usability was a key wish for the representatives from Copenhagen Municipality. The tool should be 
modular, so that parts can be used individually in meetings and workshops. This also required that the tools are not 
only focusing on usability, but also the usefulness as the content should provide relevance and meaningful learnings 
to accelerate the transition process.
5.1.3 Summary
The session provided valuable insights into how the Food and Meal Department can be supported in the shift from a 
traditional municipal role to a Transition-facilitating role in Copenhagen’s food system. The representatives from the 
municipality expressed clear preference for high usability, including a digital and printable solution, a simple visual 
design, and modular tools that are easy to use under time constraints. At the same time, the session revealed a 
deeper need for support in navigating complex, systemic change processes.
The department’s shift towards becoming a Transition-facilitator requires more than just accessible tools, it demands 
developing a new mindset and new ways of operating. The transition is also about creating rooms for co-creation 
processes and engaging diverse stakeholders. The participants expressed uncertainty about how to enter this new 
role, especially in terms of moving from vision to action, workshop facilitation and stakeholder involvement.
The dual focus on usability and usefulness positions the support as a concrete tool for daily strategy work,  supporting 
the municipality to operationalise its new identity as a Transition-facilitator.

5.3 Supporting Copenhagen Municipality with Transition 
Management
To support the Municipality of Copenhagen in its role as Transition-facilitator, Transition Management (TM) was 
adapted and translated into a more concrete and usable form. The municipality’s representatives expressed a clear 
need to maintain an overview of the entire process. They also requested a step-by-step guide to help structure and 
prioritise their efforts. As one representative put it: “It needs to be a form of structuring and prioritization – an overview 
of the entire process, from A to Z. It can be a linear step-by-step guide.” (A. K. Jönsson, personal communication, 
2025).
Against this background, the original cyclical and reflexive TM model (Loorbach, 2010) was translated into a linear and 
phased version to create greater clarity and establish a common direction to guide collaboration across different 
disciplines. This adaptation responded to the practical needs of Transition-facilitators who must navigate complex 
governance structures while engaging diverse stakeholders (Massuga et al., 2024; Loorbach, 2010). At the same time, it 
was pointed out that technically complex solutions such as search or filtering functions were not desirable, as they 
would appear too technical and inaccessible (M. H. Rønnow, personal communication, 2025). TM is, as described in 
section 3.3, a process-oriented and reflexive approach to managing complex and long-term sustainability transitions 
(Loorbach, 2010). The original model comprises four phases, strategic, tactical, operational, and reflective, which 
together form a comprehensive framework for managing transition processes, as illustrated in Figure 5.1.
To support Copenhagen Municipality the four phases were translated into a more linear and practical structure, 
where each phase was linked to two central action points. The purpose was to clarify which activities and focus areas 
are relevant in the individual phases and thus make the process easier to navigate for the actors involved. The linear 
model made it possible to progress through the strategy development step by step, while maintaining an overview

6.3 Supplementary Systematic Literature Review 
The detailed assessment of tools within the initially selected subgroups revealed critical gaps in coverage for several 
key areas identified as important by Copenhagen Municipality, specifically within subgroups G1.2, G1.3, G3.1, G4.3, and 
G5.1. To address these gaps, a supplementary Systematic Literature Review (SLR) was conducted, replicating the 
search strategy from Boll (2024) to ensure methodological consistency, while adapting the inclusion criteria to reflect 
the practical realities of municipal application.
The search used the same comprehensive search string:
(TITLE-ABS-KEY(transition* OR transform* OR shift*) AND TITLE-ABS-KEY(manage* OR navigate* OR steer* OR design* OR 
facilitate OR govern*) AND TITLE-ABS-KEY(tool* OR technique* OR method* OR guide* OR practice* OR approach* OR 
model*) AND TITLE-ABS-KEY(“socio-technical system*” OR “innovation system*” OR “socio-technical transition*”) AND 
TITLE-ABS-KEY(sustain* OR circular* OR mission* OR pathway*))
This search resulted in 853 papers, reflecting an expansion in the literature since the original review. Unlike Boll’s 
original SLR, the inclusion criteria were selectively relaxed or tightened to better align with the municipality’s context. 
Specifically, systematic methods and comprehensive toolkits were excluded due to their high preparation demands, 
while empirical studies and field-specific tools were accepted to capture practical and contextually relevant 
approaches (Figure 6.3).
A three-stage screening process was then applied: an initial review of titles (853 papers), followed by a closer 
inspection of introductions and conclusions (51 papers), and finally, full-text analysis (38 papers), supplemented by 
three additional papers identified through snowball sampling (Wohlin, 2014). This resulted in a selection of seven 
papers (Figure 6.3).
From this process, a set of additional tools relevant to the previously underrepresented subgroups were identified and 
assessed using the same adapted characteristics and qualifiers framework. This resulted in adding five new tools to 
the collection (Appendix 6.c).

Copenhagen Municipality  with tools to guide them through the entire Transition Process in sustainability transition 
projects (Seen in Figure 6.4), from initial strategic planning to tactical development, operational implementation, and

6.5 Summary
This chapter has systematically selected Systemic Design tools to support Copenhagen Municipality in the 
role of a Transition-facilitator in sustainability transition projects within the food system. The starting point 
was an initial selection of relevant subgroups based on knowledge gained from the Empirical Study, 
followed by a meeting with two representatives from Copenhagen Municipality. Here, the most relevant 
subgroups were presented, which created a common understanding and validation of the proposed 
starting point.
An assessment was performed using an adaptation of the characteristics and qualifiers from Matschewky 
et al. (2024). Assessing the 52 Systemic Design tools within the selected subgroups led to a selection of 13 
Systemic Design tools from seven different subgroups. The assessment revealed both a collection of 
Systemic Design tools well suited for Copenhagen Municipality and methodological gaps. To better match 
the municipality's specific needs and ensure coverage of all phases in the Transition Management process, 
the selected subgroups from Boll (2024) were matched with the phases of the Transition Path developed in 
Section 5.3. As certain selected subgroups and process phases still lacked suitable tools, a supplementary 
Systematic Literature Review was conducted, which resulted in adding five additional Systemic Design tools 
to the collection of selected tools.
To provide Copenhagen Municipality with tools to guide them through the entire Transition Management 
process, an overall assessment was made of the 19 selected Systemic Design tools. The tools within each 
phase matching user-needs and with the highest evaluation scores were incorporated into a potential 
Transition Pathway. This led to a pathway of ten Systemic Design tools.
This systematic selection forms the basis for the next chapter, where the tools are conceptualised in a user-friendly 
support framework for Copenhagen Municipality.

This chapter presents the Conceptual Design phase of the Systematic Engineering Design Approach (Pahl et al.,

aim is to develop a concept that supports Transition-facilitators in managing sustainability transitions through the 
application of Systemic Design tools. The Systemic Design tools selected in Chapter 6 are integrated into this support 
framework.
To follow the Systematic Engineering Design Approach, the Morphology chart method is used to conceptualise 
potential solutions for the support framework. These concepts are then evaluated against the established criteria 
described in Chapter 5. Finally, the highest-scoring solution concept is embodied and presented as the first version of 
the support framework for Copenhagen Municipality.
The conceptualisation process, for this thesis, involves three main steps: 1) setting up the Morphology chart, 2) 
developing solution concepts (Pahl et al., 2007), and 3) selecting the most promising concept for embodiment.

7.1 Concept development
To explore multiple combinations of elements that could form potential solutions to support Copenhagen 
Municipality’s efforts in transforming Copenhagen’s food system, the Morphology chart method by Cross (2021) was 
applied. This method structures potential sub-solutions around a set of design parameters, representing key aspects 
that any proposed solution must satisfy.
7.1.1 Development of the morphology chart
The morphology chart was structured as a matrix, with design parameters listed in the first column and 
corresponding rows presenting several possible sub-solutions for each parameter (Cross, 2021). The design 
parameters were derived from the requirements list (see Section 5.2). Sub-solutions were generated through a 
focused brainstorming session on each design parameter, ensuring a comprehensive and consistent range of ideas. 
The toolbox is intended as a structured and accessible platform, enabling relevant tools to be accessed and 
combined in ways that directly address user needs and objectives.
The next step involved developing solution concepts by systematically combining sub-solutions across the design 
parameters, allowing the morphology chart to facilitate the creation of diverse solution concepts.
7.1.2 Developing solution concepts
With sub-solutions defined for each design parameter, the next step involved combining these to develop feasible 
and coherent solution concepts. The combinations were selected based on the identification of compatible elements 
that together provide an effective and practical way to support Copenhagen Municipality’s needs. This process 
resulted in the development of two primary concepts: a digital playbook and a website toolbox, as illustrated in Figure

8.1 Overview of the evaluation plan 
The evaluation was performed in three iterations, each differing in the participants and method of testing (Figure 8.1). 
The aim was to test the usability and usefulness of the developed toolbox to assess its ability to support actors 
engaged in sustainability transition projects within Copenhagen’s food system. Complete interview transcripts are 
available in Appendices 8.a to 8.g.
8.2 Evaluation of the first iteration
The evaluation of the first iteration of the toolbox was conducted through two separate sessions. The first session 
involved MM from Madland, while the second session was held with the representatives from Copenhagen 
Municipality; Anna K. Jönsson and Mathilde H. Rønnow from the department for Food and Meals. Each session is 
presented in its own subsection below, highlighting the key themes and insights that emerged. Following these 
subsections, a summary outlines how the feedback contributed to the development of the second iteration.
The evaluation process consisted of three distinct parts. To begin, participants undertook a First Impressions test, 
where they explored the toolbox freely to capture their immediate reactions. The second part focused on testing the 
navigation: participants were assigned a task requiring them to locate a specific tool based on its intended purpose. 
The final part involved a Tool evaluation, where participants examined a particular tool to assess their understanding 
and identify any areas requiring clearer explanation.
The next section details the evaluation with MM from Madland.
8.2.1 Evaluation with Madland
The aim of evaluating the toolbox with MM from Madland was to test its performance from a different type of 
Transition-facilitator perspective and to assess whether the toolbox was relevant in a food-specific context or too 
general to address the particular challenges of the food system.
The summarised key themes are outlined below (see Appendix 8.a for the full interview transcript).
The toolbox's relationship with the food system 
It was emphasised that the tools themselves ”(…) do not have to be exclusively food-focused to be useful - as the 
food system is connected to other systems” (MM, personal communication, 2025). The inclusion of concrete 
examples played a key role in making the toolbox feel relevant. In particular, the food-related examples helped the 
user understand how the tools could be applied in a food system context. This indicated that the tools do not have to 
be 100 percent food-specific, as long as they are clearly applicable to food system challenges.
The importance of involving the right actors
It was emphasised that having clear strategies for who is invited to participate in change processes is important. 
Municipal projects often work more intuitively and sometimes miss a systematic approach, which can lead to certain 
important voices being excluded from the political arena. As MM noted, “We are so used to working in silos, everything
points to the systemic approach, we need to think about a whole-oriented approach, a whole-oriented scope, but 
also a whole-oriented group of actors who need to come in and meet” (personal communication, 2025). This 
underlined the importance for the toolbox to encourage reflection on how to establish transition arenas that ensure 
inclusive participation and diversity.

8.2.2 Evaluation with Copenhagen Municipality
The goal of evaluating the Toolbox with Mathilde H. Rønnow and Anna K. Jönsson from Copenhagen Municipality was 
to ensure that the toolbox fits their needs and their style of working, as they are considered the primary users of the 
toolbox. To summarise the meeting, the key themes are highlighted below (see Appendix 8.b for full interview):
Understanding and relevance of the toolbox
The overall concept and purpose of the toolbox resonated well with both participants. “We can see how the toolbox 
can help us” (M. H. Rønnow, personal communication, 2025). They emphasised its potential value in their daily work, 
especially since they often operate under significant time pressure. “We also have a lot of time pressure on us – so if 
the toolbox could help, that would be very good” (A. K. Jönsson, personal communication, 2025). However, they noted 
that the toolbox’s usefulness largely depends on how time-consuming and intuitive it is to use: “It’s about how much 
time it takes to sit down and use it, and how easy it would be” (M. H. Rønnow, personal communication, 2025).
This emphasised the need for a toolbox easy to navigate, so users do not waste time finding the right tools. Once 
identified, the tools must be perceived as useful.
Language and accessibility
A key concern was raised about the language used in the toolbox. “You really have to concentrate more when 
explanations are in English, as terms and words are not necessarily the same in both languages” (M. H. Rønnow, 
personal communication, 2025). The use of English combined with an academic tone was perceived as a significant 
barrier to accessibility. Participants emphasised that the highly specialised language could hinder understanding and 
practical use. They expressed a preference for a shift towards more accessible, informal language: “It’s okay that the 
language is everyday language, it makes it easier for us to use it” (A. K. Jönsson, personal communication, 2025).
This showed that both the language and academic tone was perceived as a large barrier for accessibility.
Navigation and filtering
The navigation in the toolbox was generally seen as intuitive and user-friendly: “I found it quite easy to navigate” (M. H. 
Rønnow personal communication, 2025). However, the number of tools presented in the Transition Pathway at once 
could feel overwhelming. It was suggested to implement a filtering feature that would allow users to only view tools 
relevant to a specific phase in the Transition Pathway: “There was something about the amount of tools – it would be 
nice if it was possible to filter, by clicking so that it only showed some of the tools. It could be filtered by the stages” 
(M. H. Rønnow personal communication, 2025)
This highlighted that they generally found it easy to navigate the toolbox, but there would be room for improvement, 
especially the places with many elements.

8.2.3 Changes to a second iteration
Based on the evaluation with Madland and the Copenhagen Municipality, a number of changes and improvements 
were made to create a second iteration of the toolbox. This section shows how their input influenced further 
development, both in terms of content, navigation and usefulness.
1. More accessible language and less text
The language was experienced as too academic, and the amount of text made it feel demanding and overwhelming 
for the user. Therefore, the texts were rewritten in a language using simpler terms, and long introductions were

9.1 Discussion of the developed support: the toolbox
This section discusses the developed toolbox, focusing on both strengths and limitations in design and use. The 
toolbox was developed as a platform to support Transition-facilitators in navigating transition processes, which are 
often complex and dependent on context (Kilde). It is therefore central to reflect on how the toolbox works in practice 
and which assumptions shaped its design. The discussion starts by considering the general usefulness and usability 
of the toolbox for the users. The usefulness of the toolbox is then further discussed by focusing on the generalisation 
of tools and suggestion of one Transition Pathway. By comparing the developed toolbox with other existing toolboxes, 
user considerations and potential biases in the design are discussed, including which user groups the toolbox is 
specifically aimed at, and which may risk being overlooked. Lastly, the insights gained from the discussion of the 
developed support are turned into suggestions for further work of improvement.
9.1.1 Usefulness and usability
Usefulness and usability are closely related but conceptually distinct qualities in system design. Usability refers to how 
easily a system can be used, while usefulness concerns how well the system supports users in achieving their goals. A 
successful tool, balancing high usability without meaningful functionality can result in superficial use, just as a highly 
capable tool with poor usability may discourage engagement (Nielsen, 1993; MacDonald & Atwood, 2014).
The Support framework developed in this thesis was evaluated through three iterations with both users and experts 
focusing on usefulness and usability of the toolbox. However, broader insights can be drawn about its general 
usefulness and usability.
The development of the toolbox was in collaboration with two representatives from Copenhagen Municipality. The 
development was highly influenced by the big emphasis on tight deadlines and limited time ressources which 
required the tools to be practical and easy to understand. This context significantly influenced the selection of tools.
 That might lead to prioritisation of usability over usefulness to ensure that the tools could actually be applied in a 
real-life setting in Copenhagen Municipality. Matschewsky et. al (2024)’s user-centered approach was applied; this 
resulted in choosing tools that were relatively easy to use and required limited time and preparation. While this 
improved usability in the short term, it may have come at the cost of reduced usefulness, especially in cases where 
deeper reflection and more complex analysis would be beneficial. However, a pragmatic focus on user-centering 
design around the user is also highlighted by Matschewsky (2024), stating that the use of design methods remains 
low in industry due to a lack of understanding for the user in academia when developing the tools.
In relation to the choice of focusing on usability, it is however relevant to consider whether Copenhagen Municipality 
was challenged enough on complexity. Although more complex tools may require more time for familiarisation for 
users, the potential for systemic insight and long-term impact might be greater, as the more complex tools could 
support  a deeper understanding of the system, making a more robust basis for decision-making.
The consideration of usability and usefulness can be translated into a general tension for the developer. From the 
perspective of usability, the tools must be usable for actors working under time pressure and with limited resources. 
From the perspective of usefulness, users must also be able to handle the complexity that characterises real 
sustainability transition processes, which also applies in urban food systems where many interests, elements and 
actors intersect. While representing distinct design goals, the two are also connected.
In the context of this thesis, the developed toolbox reflects these challenges. To address this inherent tension, efforts 
to enhance usability through improved framing and clearer communication of the toolbox become essential. Making 
a clear presentation of the target group, suggestions for use cases, and practical examples all help users choose the 
right tools and understand how to adapt them to individual needs, leading to improvement in usefulness (Hartson & 
Pyla, 2018). These considerations however depend on the user's ability to translate the tools to the context they are in, 
which might be impacted by the level of generalisation of tools.
9.1.2 Level of generalisation of tools
While the purpose of the toolbox is to support sustainability transition projects in Copenhagen’s food system, many of 
the tools in the toolbox are considered applicable for other contexts. The general nature of the tools was also 
highlighted during the meeting with Copenhagen Municipality in the final evaluation: “It’s [the tools] not just for food - 
it can easily be used in other contexts where changes need to be made” (A. K. Jönsson, personal communication, 
2025), pointing to the toolbox’s use of more generic Systemic Design tools that can be adapted to different contexts 
and target groups.
While the high level of generalisation of the tools makes it possible to use them in other contexts than urban food 
systems, it may seem conflicting to apply generic tools in sustainability transitions, considering their definition of 
being unique and context-dependent (Susur & Karakaya, 2021). Conversely, Systemic Design methods also contain

Tailoring the tools to only be focusing on food would therefore be at the risk of losing the broader systemic

The choice of including generic tools in the toolbox should create structure and overview so that key aspects of the

the tools support a more holistic approach, where complexity and systemic connections can be handled without 
being reduced. In addition to the generic Systemic Design tools, the toolbox also contains one suggested pathway of 
users to follow, leading to similar reflections.
9.1.3 The use of one suggested Transition Pathway
Similar to the generalisation of the tools, the choice of suggesting only one Transition Pathway  can be considered 
conflicting with the nature of sustainability transitions. While the choice might initially seem limiting, potentially 
implying a linear or “one-size-fits-all” approach, it is important to clarify that the pathway is solely intended as an 
example and source of inspiration to guide users through complex transition processes.
For Copenhagen Municipality, a linear and structured overview was highlighted as valuable during the Task 
Clarification: “(...) be a form of structuring and prioritisation – an overview of the entire process, from A to Z. It can 
be a linear step-by-step guide” (A. K. Jönsson, personal communication, 2025). In this sense, the pathway helps 
users navigate uncertainty and complexity by breaking the process down into manageable action points.
The tools included in the pathway were also selected with the specific aim of minimising overlap and creating logical 
progression. Ideally, the output from one tool can function as input for the next. This intentional sequencing was 
designed to support coherence and build momentum across different stages of a transition process. However, this 
integrated flow between tools was not systematically tested during the evaluation phase due to time constraints. As 
a result, it is still unclear how well the connections between the tools actually work in practice.
Moreover, it can be challenging for users to determine which tool fits their needs at a given moment in the process. 
This raises the question of whether there might be other, more intuitive ways to begin working with the tools beyond 
the suggested pathway. To address this, the action points in the pathway could be considered guiding points of 
entry. These are framed in broader terms to allow users some flexibility in interpretation. While the abstract nature of 
the action points can make it difficult to evaluate the relevance of a tool before choosing it, the user interface allows 
for easy navigation back to the main pathway.
The pathway itself was designed with Copenhagen Municipality in mind, particularly to be applied for developing a 
new Food Strategy. Simultaneously, the toolbox also includes a broader collection of tools, accessible outside the 
pathway. These tools have been selected based on similar qualifications and could support comparable transition
 projects in other municipalities or sectors. The inclusion of additional tools in the broader toolbox makes it relevant 
to compare the toolbox with other existing toolkits designed for sustainability transitions, in order to better 
understand its unique contributions, overlaps, and potential complementarities.
9.1.4 Comparison to other Toolkits
During the development of the toolbox, two existing toolkits were identified: the Transition Toolkit developed by 
FoodSHIFT 2030 (2020) and the Toolkit for Public Engagement developed under Food2030 (2024). Both are designed 
to support actors involved in sustainable food transitions in urban settings. A comparison with these can help 
understand the strengths of the developed toolbox, its current limitations, and its potential areas for improvement. 
The comparative discussion will start by focusing on descriptive parts of the developed toolbox and the two toolkits. 
The discussion will hereafter move to the prescriptive parts, focusing on what the design of the developed toolbox 
and two toolkits prioritises. In the following discussion, the considered solutions will be referred to as “the FoodSHIFT 
Toolkit”, “the Food2030 Toolkit”, and “the developed Toolbox”.
Descriptive discussion: Content, Framing, and User Targeting
The FoodSHIFT Toolkit provides a clear structure that categorises tools by transition themes (governance, citizen 
engagement, or value chains) and type of innovation (governance innovation, process innovation, product 
innovation, or social innovation). This framing helps users locate themselves in the broader context and select tools 
accordingly. In contrast, the developed Toolbox offers a more open-ended framing, with less emphasis on helping 
users identify the nature of their transition challenge. While this openness promotes flexibility, it also places more 
responsibility on the user to navigate and interpret their own position in the transition process, which might be difficult 
without prior experience. Similarly, the Food2030 Toolkit defines a clear user group: facilitators working with 
participatory approaches in arenas such as living labs or food councils. Its methods are designed for engagement 
and dialogue, and the roles and goals of users are clearly shown. The developed Toolbox does not to the same extent 
offer a precise user typology beyond the general term “Transition-facilitator”. This can make it less clear who exactly 
the developed Toolbox is for and under what conditions it is most effective. This may reduce its immediate usability, 
particularly for new users.
Prescriptive discussion: Structure and Navigation
One of the most important features of the developed Toolbox is its systemic structure and the inclusion of a 
suggested Transition Pathway. Allowing users to go directly from one tool to the next is intended to reduce cognitive 
load for users navigating complex processes (Yablonski, 2023). However, this advantage is also a potential limitation. 
Unlike the more modular and flexible design of the FoodSHIFT Toolkit and the Food2030 Toolkit, which allow users to 
enter at multiple points depending on their needs, the suggested linear pathway in the developed Toolbox may 
unintentionally signal a “correct” way to proceed. While the pathway is presented as optional and inspirational, its 
centrality in the design risks being interpreted as the only way to use the developed Toolbox, especially for users 
looking for immediate problem-solving tools.

structured support for sustainability transitions in the context of urban food systems. The discussion revealed a design 
tension between usability and usefulness. Rather than being a shortcoming, the tension reflects the complexity of 
real-world transitions and the needs of actors involved.
The toolbox demonstrated several clear strengths. It offers a structured Transition Pathway that can serve as an 
orientation tool for users facing complex and uncertain processes. The selection of relevant tools reflects a Systemic 
Design logic, with efforts made to minimise overlap and ensure coherence. Its visual clarity and user-friendly interface 
enhance usability, particularly for actors with limited time. Furthermore, although developed within a food policy 
context, the toolbox has a level of generality that makes it adaptable to other domains of sustainability transitions.
However, while the suggested Transition Pathway offers structure, it may also unintentionally convey a linear logic that 
does not reflect the iterative and non-linear nature of many transition processes. If not used to follow the suggested 
pathway, the toolbox places a relatively high demand on users’ prior knowledge and interpretative capacity, which 
may affect its broader uptake. Elements such as filter functions, real-world use cases, and clearer signals about tool 
applicability could help users navigate resources more intuitively, which was discussed by comparing the developed 
toolbox to other existing toolkits.
Besides the navigating element, the comparison with existing toolkits such as the FoodSHIFT Toolkit and the Food2030 
Toolkit also revealed other areas for improvement. The developed Toolbox does not explicitly communicate its user 
typology besides the term Transition-facilitator. This lack of clarity around user typology may limit its usability, 
especially for new users trying to assess whether it fits their specific context. In contrast, the FoodSHIFT Toolkit helps 
users locate themselves by distinguishing between different types of innovation (e.g. governance, product, social), 
while the Food2030 Toolkit defines its user group more narrowly.
In summary, discussing the strengths and limitations of the developed toolbox revealed the following areas for further 
work and improvement:
Clarify user roles and contexts: Define the intended users more clearly to help different user types understand how 
the toolbox applies to their work.
Test tool flow in practice: Conduct practical testing to evaluate how well tools in the Transition Pathway work together 
and identify any gaps or overlaps.
Support different Transition Pathways: Reduce the emphasis on a fixed sequence of steps and encourage more 
flexible, iterative use of tools.
Improve navigation and accessibility: Test if adding filters, categories, or interactive features will help users find 
relevant tools outside the Transition Pathway.
Include real-world examples: Provide case studies or usage scenarios from real-life to show how the tools have been 
applied in practice.
Balance simplicity with systemic depth: Explore ways to include more complex tools or layered guidance for users 
seeking deeper systemic insights.
Further development of the toolbox could focus on better supporting different types of users, clarifying its underlying 
assumptions, and testing how well the tools in the Transition Pathway work together in practice. By adding more 
interactive features and practical examples, the toolbox could evolve from simply guiding users to also serving as a 
learning platform that empowers a larger range of Transition-facilitators. These considerations lead into a broader 
reflection on the thesis process itself, which played a key role in shaping how the toolbox was framed and developed.
Moreover, while the selection of tools in the developed Toolbox was deliberately made to minimise overlap and create 
flow between them, this tool-to-tool interaction remains untested in practice. In comparison, the FoodSHIFT Toolkit 
points to a wide range of pilot experiences and transition experiments from participating cities, which help 
contextualise its content. However, the FoodSHIFT Toolkit itself is less accessible in use. The structure of the website is 
difficult to navigate, and the tools are not always clearly distinguishable or easy to apply. By contrast, the Food2030 
Toolkit offers a more intuitive user experience, with a clear filtering system that allows users to sort tools by purpose, 
user role, or process phase. This makes it easier to explore and select tools based on immediate needs, which is 
something the developed Toolbox could benefit from incorporating in future iterations.

9.2 Reflection of the process
The process of the thesis is reflected upon by going through each stage of the Design Research Methodology (DRM) 
framework. The DRM framework was applied for a structured yet flexible investigation of a facilitating approach to 
sustainability transitions in Copenhagen's food system. However, as for the iterative nature of the framework, the 
thesis process has not been linear. Changes in scope, redefined research questions, and theoretical challenges all led 
to iterations of the thesis content, which ultimately influenced how the toolbox was framed and developed.
9.2.1 Research Clarification (RC)
The scope of the thesis was very broad during the RC stage. The potential challenges within this scope was extensive, 
resulting in exploratory research that could have been avoided if the scope for the RC stage was narrowed down 
from the beginning. Conversely, conducting an exploratory RC stage led to new insights and the discovery of 
Transition-facilitators in the DS-I, which resulted in a change of the scope. Without the broad and exploratory RC 
stage, this thesis might have turned out significantly different.
The initial research questions and use of the table by Boll (2024) partly guided the RC stage, but the iterative process 
made it difficult to use all the initial RQs without refining them as the scope changed. Therefore, one of the initial RQs 
was changed as deeper insights were gained to Copenhagen's food system and discovery of Transition-facilitators, 
as well as understanding the table of Systemic Design tools by Boll (2024) in greater detail.
9.2.2 Descriptive Study I (DS-I)
Theory
Theory was added as a chapter to create a foundation for the Empirical Study, using different but also 
complementary theoretical fields within sustainability transitions. Additionally, the theoretical foundation was used to 
guide the understanding of the reader. The literature used in the theory section revealed disagreements in academia, 
both in terms of what characterise facilitating actors, Systemic Design, and sustainability transition processes.
In the research of facilitating actors, concepts such as “intermediaries” (Kivimaa et al., 2018; Polzin et al., 2016), “change 
makers” (Design Council, 2021), and “orchestrators” (Gupta et al., 2024) were found in literature, often used 
interchangeably but with small differences in their definitions depending on the disciplinary lens. The choice of using 
the term “Transition-facilitators” instead of other terms was largely based on its resonance with the empirical findings 
from interviews with actors within Copenhagen's food system, but this decision might have limited the theoretical 
generalisability.
The same applies to the use of the term “Systemic Design”, which was equally found to be defined differently 
depending on the discipline (Figure 9.1).
In exploring definitions of systemic design, the thesis initially considered different perspectives from the literature. 
Barbero and Pereno’s (2020) definition positioned systemic design as a tool for facilitating actors to enable 
sustainability transitions. This framing was particularly relevant to the thesis, as it helped define the role of Transition-
facilitators and shaped the overall direction of the research. While Ceschin and Gaziulusoy (2016) also discuss 
systemic design, their view is more technology-focused, which did not align with the thesis’ emphasis on actor 
involvement and facilitation. Instead, their broader category of Design for System Innovation and Transitions was 
more relevant. As the thesis only applied Barbero and Pereno’s (2020) definition, it is important to reflect that this 
choice influenced how systemic design was understood and applied throughout the work.
Lastly, the analysis of Copenhagen's food system was based primarily on the Multi-Level Perspective (MLP) (Geels, 
2005a; Geels, 2005b; Geels, 2011, Geels, 2019) and Transition Management (TM) (Loorbach, 2010). While MLP was used 
descriptively to investigate sustainability transition elements on landscape, regime, and niche levels in Copenhagen's 
food system, TM was used prescriptively to frame sustainability transitions process of Transition-facilitators. Although 
this theoretical decision provided structure to the analysis, it didn’t account for other alternative theories that could 
have offered different insights.

Empirical Study
In the Empirical Study, the thesis first applied a system-oriented view by analysing the current state of Copenhagen's 
food system. Following this analysis, the thesis shifted into an actor-oriented perspective by conducting interviews 
with key actors in the system. Initially, the aim was to understand key challenges and needs that the solution had to 
address, but in reality, the interviews helped change the scope of the thesis towards Transition-facilitators. While it 
was considered important to speak with actors from different areas of Copenhagen's food system, it is unsure 
whether the selected actors were representative of the broader food system in Copenhagen. As it is unknown if other 
actors would have led to the same findings, both in terms of the Transition-facilitators and identified challenges, it is 
important to acknowledge that these discoveries are based both on who was interviewed as well as what was asked 
during interviews.
Task Clarification
An important reflection for the Task Clarification phase is the decision of basing requirements and criterias for the 
solution on one actor, when the challenges identified stemmed from multiple actors. In that sense it is important to 
consider whether the perspectives of Copenhagen Municipality can adequately reflect or address the perspectives 
and needs across the broader food system.
The possibility of collaborating with Copenhagen Municipality came relatively late in the DS-I stage, but was a 
possibility worth exploring, considering the actor's relevance for the scope of the thesis. However, the collaboration 
was shaped by certain limitations: Copenhagen Municipality had limited time available and only a basic 
understanding of sustainability transitions and Systemic Design. To support their involvement and ensure progress, 
five themes were introduced as guiding points for defining requirements and criteria in the Task Clarification. While 
this helped provide structure and made it easier for Copenhagen Municipality to engage with the process, it may also 
have unintentionally influenced how requirements and criteria were expressed. Some inputs may have been shaped 
more by the framing of the themes than by a full reflection of the municipality’s own underlying needs. At the same 
time, without this guidance, it may have been difficult for them to know what they needed.
Additionally, there was a risk of over-tailoring the solution to only fit Copenhagen Municipality, which was especially 
apparent when the cyclic model of TM was transformed into a linear format to accommodate the process of 
Copenhagen Municipality. While this adaptation of transition processes was necessary for usability, it potentially 
compromises the model’s original intent. While the linear format uses the same stage names of the TM model by 
Loorbach (2010), each stage was influenced by a broader understanding of transition process theories obtained 
during the RC stage to create a generalised transition process for Copenhagen Municipality.
Systematic selection of tools
The systematic selection of tools relied on the table by Boll (2024). While this approach allowed the thesis to build 
upon a Systematic Literature Review (SLR), it may have constrained the scope of the tool identification. Although a 
supplementary SLR was conducted based on the original one, it is unknown whether valuable Systemic Design tools 
outside the original review’s search string were overlooked.
The assessment of relevant tools was qualitative and based on perceived fit with the identified requirements from 
Copenhagen Municipality. While this approach aligns with the user-centered abilities of Matschewsky et al. (2024), the 
outcome might be influenced by subjective judgment and contextual factors, which may affect its transferability to 
other settings.
Additionally, the assessment of relevant subgroups was based on a meeting with Copenhagen Municipality, where 
the relevant subgroups were presented to them and they agreed upon the selection. While this approach is also 
limited by biases in the selection, the alternative of making them understand the meaning behind all subgroups was 
not possible due to time constraints.
9.2.3 Prescriptive study (PS)
The development of the toolbox, consisting of 19 Systemic Design tools intended to support the identified phases of 
the transition process, was shaped by a trade-off between breadth and depth. By creating a broad solution, the aim 
was to create a solution that could support Copenhagen Municipality in the entire process of the new Food Strategy. 
However, creating a broad solution might have lowered the usefulness of individual tools. Some tools were selected 
despite being very general and loosely connected to food systems. But the thesis was limited by the table of Systemic 
Design tools which mostly consisted of very general tools. The adaptation of certain tools involved simplifications that 
may limit their potential for sustainability transitions. Usability was again prioritised over usefulness. This is evident in 
how the tools were framed for Copenhagen Municipality. The tools were designed to be easy to understand and 
apply, which might have been at the cost of ability to drive sustainability transitions.
The design of the toolbox was influenced by looking at other similar digital toolkits such as  the Transition Toolkit 
developed by FoodSHIFT 2030 (2020) and the Toolkit for Public Engagement developed under Food2030 (2024). While 
the conceptualisation of the toolbox was systemised from the use of the Systematic Engineering Design Approach 
and morphology, the design of the toolbox was influenced by intuition and experience. Despite there being a 
possibility that the solution was limited by preconceived ideas for the elements and layout, Pahl et al., 2007 states that 
any systematic approach involves measures of intuition.

9.2.4 Descriptive Study II (DS-II)
When planning the evaluation of the developed toolbox, time constraints of participants limited the possibilities of 
testing the toolbox and tools significantly. The testing was planned according to what was feasible in the time frame 
of one hour, and tailored to the specific participants. This constraint led to primarily surface-level feedback, which can 
be problematic given the breadth of the solution. As a result, only a few tools were assessed, why key usability or 
relevance issues may have been undetected.
There was also a misalignment between user and expert feedback. Where experts focused on simplifying the toolbox 
and tools for the user to be able to navigate and use it, the potential users already demonstrated a good 
understanding on how to interact with the solution. This disconnect suggests that assumptions about the capacity of 
the users made by the experts can be misleading when altering the solution for the next iterations.
Overall, applying the DRM framework has highlighted both upsides and downsides of structured design methodology 
in research about complex, socio-technical systems. The methodology supported the iterative nature of this thesis 
and the engagement of stakeholders, but also exposed weaknesses in the management of the scope, tool selection, 
and validity of the evaluation. However, although the process was not perfect or linear, it reflects the complex reality 
of sustainability transitions, where openness to change, iteration, and adaptation are necessary. This thesis therefore 
contributes to the ongoing process, and the reflections highlighted may serve as guidance for future research 
seeking to connect Systemic Design and sustainability transitions in urban food systems.
 
This Master thesis has explored how a Systemic Design approach can support sustainability transitions in 
Copenhagen’s urban food system. Using the municipality’s development of a new Food Strategy as a central case, 
the study explored how transition processes can be effectively facilitated in complex urban contexts. The research 
was guided by two main objectives: to investigate the potential of Systemic Design for enabling sustainability 
transitions, and to develop and test a design framework that supports actors engaged in sustainability transitions.
Grounded in the Multi-Level Perspective, Transition Management, Transition-facilitators and Systemic Design, the 
thesis offered an analytical lens for understanding current sustainability transition dynamics in Copenhagen. The 
empirical investigation revealed key challenges such as lack of coordination, uneven resource distribution, and 
difficulty translating knowledge into action. These insights underscored the need for an actor that could act as a 
Transition-facilitator, capable of connecting actors, building shared direction, and supporting systemic change.
To support this role in practice, a digital toolbox was developed in collaboration with Copenhagen Municipality. The 
design process integrated theory, user input, and structured design methods to ensure relevance and usability. The 
toolbox consists of six elements: 1) The frontpage, 2) The facilitation page, 3) The Workshop page, 4) Transition 
Pathway, 5) The individual tools in the pathway, and  6) An online workspace closely connected to the tools. 
Systematically selected Systemic Design tools were tested and adapted to the needs and capacities of municipal 
actors.
The evaluation process confirmed the toolbox’s practical value while identifying areas for further improvement, such 
as onboarding materials, flexible navigation, and clearer role definition. Rather than offering a linear solution, the 
toolbox serves as a starting point for reflection, coordination, and learning in complex transition processes.
In sum, this thesis contributes both conceptually and practically by demonstrating how Systemic Design can be 
translated into actionable support for transition facilitation. It offers a structured yet adaptable framework that 
enables actors to navigate complexity without oversimplifying it. While the toolbox was developed in the context of 
Copenhagen’s food system, its underlying approach could be relevant in other contexts where systemic change is 
needed.

10. Reflect on the role of Design and Innovation and Industrial Engineering and 
Management in enabling sustainability transitions.
The work of this thesis reflects and builds upon the combined strengths of our academic backgrounds in 
Industrial Engineering and Management (IEM) and Design and Innovation (D&I), both of which play important 
roles in enabling sustainability transitions.
From the IEM perspective, our understanding of production systems, decision-making processes, and 
sustainability management enabled us to frame transitions as deeply embedded in organizational structures 
and processes. Tools such as life cycle thinking and strategic assessment supported this framing.
Meanwhile, the D&I perspective provided essential capabilities for navigating complexity and stakeholder 
diversity. Applied design methods helped us reframe problems, integrate user perspectives, and develop tools 
that support iterative, context-sensitive innovation. The specialization in Design for Circularity and Sustainability, 
which focuses on design methods to advance the sustainable transition of society, was particularly relevant. It 
offered approaches to develop systemic design interventions, which became central to our toolbox.
By combining these perspectives, we were able to analyze existing challenges and opportunities within the food 
system and position our toolbox to support systemic change within municipal operations. Our thesis project thus 
resulted in a solution that was not only technical and strategic, but also grounded in stakeholder engagement, 
circularity, and systems thinking. The collaboration between engineering and design methodologies proved 
crucial in shaping an intervention that is both implementable and transformative—demonstrating the value of 
interdisciplinary competencies in advancing sustainability transitions.
