Abstract
The present study aims at assessing the biogeochemical processes involved in the degradation
of chlorinated ethenes by microscale zero valent iron added with organic donor (Provect-IR) in
combination with a bacterial culture (KB-1) in a directly injected treatment zone. To understand
the processes and signiﬁcant relationship among the parameters, PHREEQC model to simulate
abiotic and biotic degradation of chlorinated ethenes by mZVI, simultaneously with donor oxi-
dation, was developed step by step. COWI together with Capital Region of Denmark initiated
pilot scale experiment upon which the investigations in this study are based. The biogeochemical
processes were assessed by evaluating ﬁeld results from groundwater monitoring. The speciation
results showed that bicarbonates is among the major species in groundwater and the ﬂuctuations
in its concentration is found out to be governed by zero valent iron (ZVI) oxidation, which causes
increase in pH, and then fermentation of organic matter, which reduces the pH, leading to its vari-
ation. The wells measured after 6 months of injection show over all increase in amendment induced
parameters. From the study, for some wells showing stronger indications, it has been anticipated
that amendment might have travelled back to those wells around 6 months post injection. Most
wells have been aﬀected by the amendment and thus amendment induced changes have caused
similar trend in change in pH and mineral saturation, with overall increase by 6 months post injec-
tion. For deeper understanding of governing processes, the study proposes a rudimentary approach
in modeling chlorinated ethenes abiotic and biotic degradation simultaneously with donor oxida-
tion in a closed batch system. The model simulates the dechlorination of chlorinated ethenes and
the change in groundwater parameters were studied from ZVI and donor oxidation. The model
when qualitatively compared with laboratory and ﬁeld results, showed the expected changes in the
parameters and thus eﬀectively provides the reasoning for the processes and source of the prod-
ucts formed. The model holds the capability to experiment with diﬀerent organic substrates to
understand the fermentation reactions. The model can be useful in making numerous predictions,
for instance change in pH and consumption of substrates during dechlorination process. Apart
from future use, the model oﬀers various opportunities for further development, which can aid in
understanding diﬀerent reactive processes occurring in the treatment zone and their signiﬁcance.

Introduction
Chlorinated ethenes are among the most widely used chemical in cleaning and degreasing in-
dustries and are used in a wide variety of other purposes such as adhesives, chemical intermediates,
clothes, pharmaceuticals, pesticides, and textile processing (Doherty, 2000). Their extensive us-
age over the years have caused serious environmental problems, among which, mainly is soil and
groundwater contamination. Remediation of chlorinated ethenes with permeable reactive barrier
(PRB) constructed with zerovalent iron (ZVI) has been found to be eﬃcient and economical solu-
tion for complex groundwater contamination (Xin et al., 2018).
Direct injection can be a economically feasible option when PRB needs to be installed deeper
in the subsurface(Uyttebroek et al., 2010). In PRBs constructed with direct injection, the reaction
zone is closer to the injection zone which aids in the remediation process. In direct injection tech-
nique, the contaminant can be reached from diﬀerent locations and depths and have additional
beneﬁts in comparison to a traditional barrier. Hence, the reactions could be at a diﬀerent sequence
or pace than in a traditional PRB.
Microscale ZVI (1-100 µM) are found out to be suitable for direct injection technique, as they
oﬀer high surface area, mobility and reactivity. Nanoscale ZVI (<1 µM) has been used in several
studies earlier, however, it was found out that its surface area is not signiﬁcantly greater than mZVI
(microscale), and the stability was limited to few meters. These limitations can be overcome with
mZVI ﬁllings (Gu et al., 2017). ZVI has been found out to be successfully able to degrade and
transform chlorinated ethenes into less harmful products (Satapanajaru et al., 2008).
ZVI initiates abiotic degradation of chlorinated ethenes, which primarily occur through reduc-
tive β elimination, where trichloroethene (TCE) can degrade to chloroacetylene, then to acetylene,
which further degrades to ethene.
In biotic treatment, TCE can be degraded sequentially by
hydrogenolysis through , cis-dichloroethene (cis-DCE), vinyl chloride (VC) and ﬁnally ethene.
(Hunkeler et al., 2011). VC, however, is highly probable to get accumulated when its production
rate is higher than the degradation rate. This is a serious concern because VC is considered to
be more toxic than other chlorinated ethene products (Henry, 2010). Thus, the treatment process
needs to be enhanced for complete degradation of chlorinated ethenes.
The reductive dechlorination process is dependent on availability of electron donors, redox po-
tential or presence of speciﬁc bacteria for a certain type of degradation. Therefore, additional
injection of organic donor and bacterial culture has great capability for improving the eﬃciency of
remediation process. This generates biotic sequential reductive dechlorination, in which the reac-
tions are aﬀected by the microorganisms within the barrier (Upadhyay and Sinha, 2018). Molecular
hydrogen is typically the electron donor here, and fermentation process becomes vital here due to
its function to supply the hydrogen. The addition of donor leads to consumption of inorganic
electron acceptors and thus initiating sequential course of redox processes. Imbalance in the avail-
1
==PAGE:11==
ability of electron donors and electron acceptors can create undesirable redox conditions which
adversely aﬀects complete degradation (Henry, 2010).
The groundwater chemistry plays a vital role in the performance of the barrier, like pH, re-
dox potential, dissolved oxygen and trace aqueous species (Tang et al., 2017). The reactions in
the barrier aﬀect the oxidation reduction potential and pH of the aquifer, which inﬂuences the
redox sensitive elements downstream (Kumar et al., 2016). The aqueous species calculation using
geochemical modeling codes have been used in several researches, to interpret the interactions of
diﬀerent substances and its inﬂuence on a water sample (Deng et al., 2011). The speciations in
the water show the percentage distribution of major species present, and hence the changes in the
water parameters due to reactions in the barrier can be studied.
Modeling and simulation of processes is highly useful to assess the parameters and signiﬁcant
relations among them. Diﬀerent processes related to soil and water chemistry in the aquifer can
hinder complete remediation by creating unfavourable conditions, which can aﬀect the dechlorina-
tion process as well as activity of micro−organisms. Thus, to investigate possible outcomes, several
studies have been conducted to develop models to simulate reductive dechlorination of chlorinated
ethenes. (Yu and Semprini, 2004) developed two biokinetic models which applied Michaelis-Menten
equation for anaerobic reductive dechlorination of PCE and trichloroethylene TCE, and compared
the results with batch kinetic tests. (Malaguerra et al., 2011) developed biokinetic model also using
Michaelis-Menten equation to simulate sequential reductive dechlorination (SERD) in association
with donor fermentation, iron and sulphate reduction and methanogenesis and implemented the
model in geochemical software PHREEQC.
Modeling leads to greater understanding of individual processes and becomes a great tool for
designing future laboratory or ﬁeld experiments. PHREEQC is one such tool that can analyse the
biogeochemical reactions aﬀecting geological systems. PHREEQC is capable of calculating specia-
tion, saturation indices, ion exchange and adsorption to surfaces. It can also eﬀectively model 1D
reactive transport which takes the transport processes into account (Parkhurst et al., 1999). The
given capabilities of PHREEQC will aid in modelling a system which will integrate the reactive
processes occurring in the aquifer.
A development project has been initiated by COWI to understand the remediation technology
PRB to remediate soil and groundwater contaminated by chlorinated solvents at Hvedemarken ,
Farum, Denmark. For the development project, COWI uses Provect-IR (microscale ZVI) added
with a bacterial culture KB-1 for formation of the PRB, after conducting batch and column tests
for selection of suitable substrate. The amalgamation of mZVI and the bacterial culture will re-
sult in a treatment train of biotic-abiotic degradation of the chlorinated ethenes (Obiri-Nyarko
et al., 2015). COWI installed the PRB at the site in August 2019. They created a number of
wells upstream and downstream of the treatment zone. There have not been found a PHREEQC
model example, of simulation of reductive dechlorination with mZVI and donor added with KB−1

bacterial culture, with data based on pilot ﬁeld test. The monitoring results from the site will
form the base for the research and the overall model built will provide insights into the reductive
dechlorination, which will assist in analysing the performance of PRB.
The overall aim is to develop biogeochemical geochemical models in PHREEQC to simulate
conditions and processes in laboratory batch experiments and contaminated groundwater after
addition of a remedial agent (Provect-IR). The model should provide insight into the main pro-

2.2
Degradation pathway of chlorinated ethenes and Redox chemistry
Chlorinated ethenes are characterized as dense non-aqueous phase liquids because of their prop-
erties. They rapidly move downward into the soil, leaching through the soil pores and water, as
they are denser than water. They have very high solubility and when they migrate, they leave
behind tiny residual pockets in the soil pores, that can dissolve, contributing to its long term
persistence in groundwater (Xin et al., 2015). The mobility of the ethene in the subsurface is
controlled by factors such as gravitational force, capillary pressure and hydraulic pressure, and
also by other properties such as their density and viscosity. The structural and physical properties
of chlorinated ethenes can be seen in the Table1. The complex distribution is also controlled by
variations in site geology. The released ethene will sink below the water table, until it reaches
layers that can’t be penetrated further, for example clay.
Zero-Valent Iron for remediation of Chlorinated ethenes:
For the wide range of ground-
water contaminants, many kinds of reactive materials have been proposed and tested (Council),
2005). Zero-Valent Iron (ZVI) is one of them and it has been found out to be successfully able
to transform chlorinated ethenes, pesticides, herbicides, explosives, nitrates and metals into less
harmful products (Satapanajaru et al., 2008). ZVI have been usually installed with the technology
of permeable reactive barriers (PRBs).
By deﬁnition, PRBs are generally constructed as a barrier, perpendicular to the direction of
groundwater, such that the passive capturing of the contaminant plume occurs, to remove and
degrade the contaminant. For such site-speciﬁc cases, one of the technique for constructing PRB
is through direct injection into the soil which may not necessarily be in the shape of a barrier.
Therefore, in this report, the term PRB is used interchangeably with direct injection treatment
zone.
2.2
Degradation pathway of chlorinated ethenes and Redox chemistry
Degradation of chlorinated ethenes as a whole, is a series of oxidation-reduction reactions. The
ethenes exist in their oxidised state, which makes them susceptible to reduction under anaerobic
conditions. Degradation occurs through diﬀerent pathways: biotic or abiotic processes.The anaer-
obic degradation proceeds to non-chlorinated products such as ethene, ethane and methane. The
degradation of chlorinated ethenes occurs in the sequence of PCE; TCE; 1,2-DCE; and VC to
ethene and ethane (Technology and Group., 1999).
The key abiotic pathways for degradation of chlorinated ethenes are reductive elimination,
hydrogenolysis, dehydrohalogenation and hydrolysis. However, some of the reactions here may
also occur through both abiotic and biotic pathway (He et al., 2015). The degradation pathway
include: 1) hydrogenolysis, in which a chlorine atom is replaced by hydrogen atom by the addition
of two electrons; and 2) reductive β elimination, in which two chlorine atoms are released, along
with formation of carbon-carbon bond.

2.4
Fermentation of organic matter:
The conversion of organic matter in the groundwater happen in three steps. The organic matter
is broken down by microorganisms in the absence of oxygen. The ﬁrst step is hydrolysis, in which
the complex organic molecules are broken down into simple amino and fatty acids. Acidogenesis is
the next step in which the acidogenic bacteria convert the amino acids into carbon dioxide, hydro-
gen and organic acids. This conversion is termed fermentation process. The hydrogen released is
consumed in the sequential reductive dechlorination. The result of fermentation is often fatty acids
such as acetate and propionate. The addition of electron donor stimulates dechlorinating bacteria
and also the activity of other bacteria (Jørgensen et al., 2005). VFAs are intermediate substances
produced during the process of fermentation as discussed in above section. The common VFAs
identiﬁed is acetate, and also propionate and butyrate at some concentrations. The fermentation
products formed can be seen in the equations below. CH3COOH: acetic acid (acetate: CH3COO−
); CH3CH2OH : ethanol; CH3CH2COOH: propionic acid (propionate: CH3CH2COO−).
VFAs can be used as indicator of redox conditions because when electron acceptors become
low, VFA concentration is observed to increase (Vroblesky et al., 1997). Monitoring of produced
hydrogen can also be used to detect redox conditions, but due to close production and consumption
of hydrogen, VFA form a better monitoring parameter, since they last longer in the aquifer. The
information about VFA help identify plume stability (Christensen et al., 2000).
All the fermentation products such as acetate or propionate, are used in any of the redox pro-
cesses which is denitriﬁcation, manganese, iron or sulphate reduction. Further detailed discussion
on redox processes will be presented in the next section.

Inﬂuence of Geochemistry in degradation process:
Geochemical conditions are crucial for chlorinated ethene’s degradation. Important factors re-
garding soil are changes in pH and cation exchange capacity (CEC). CEC is the capacity of soil to
hold exchangeable cations. Organic matter and clay fractions are tend to have higher CEC. Thus,
the soil geology can provide knowledge about CEC. The clay and organic matters of soil have neg-
ative charges on their surface, and so it absorbs and hold positively charged ions. Most nutrients
in the soil solution are taken up in their ionic form by microbes. Clay due to its structure, can
retain more cations. With lower CEC, the soil pH can also decrease with time. Soil with low CEC
are likely to be deﬁcient in nutrients (Leticia S. Sonon and Saha, 2017). Soil nutrient availability
is important for biotic degradation. The mineral precipitates formed can aﬀect the CEC of the
soil along with aﬀecting the reactivity of ZVI. CEC can be used to determine how suitable the soil
conditions are to maintain a neutral pH during biostimulation (Henry, 2010).
Grain size distribution is among the most important characteristics of sediment. It can be used
for describing transport mechanisms because grain size is dominant factor in controlling sediment
geochemistry. Clay generally posses negative charge, so during chemical mineral weathering cations
have tendency to get adsorbed onto clay. Organic matter has both positive and negative charge.
During biochemical changes in the subsurface, mineral precipitation from the groundwater and
soil takes place, and precipitates form on the soil surface. The performance of ZVI PRBs depends

Materials and Methods
5.1
Modeling tool PHREEQC
PHREEQC is a tool that can analyse the biogeochemical reactions aﬀecting geological systems.
It is available for diﬀerent software environments.
It is a graphical user interface which oﬀers
building and running PHREEQC input ﬁles. PHREEQC Version 3 is a computer program written
in C++ programming language. A free source code editor Notepad++ is used for building and
running the ﬁles. The capabilities of PHREEQC Version 3 include:
(1) Equilibrium Calculations - speciation, saturation calculations, ion exchange, adsorption to
surfaces, solid solutions
(2) (Bio)(Geo)Chemical Modeling - homogeneous and heterogeneous reactions at equilibrium
or kinetically controlled, isotopes, inverse modeling
(3) 1 D Reactive Transport Modeling - all the above + advective dispersive transport including
multispecies diﬀusion, multicomponent ionic diﬀusion and dual porosity media
In this study, the groundwater process modelling can be used to forecast or predict simulations
in the future. The model will provide quantitative framework with in-cooperating ﬁeld information
and conceptualizing the biogeochemical processes. The given capabilities of PHREEQC will aid
in modelling a system which will integrate the reactive processes occurring in the subsurface in
relation with the soil and groundwater chemistry. Process modelling can be used to forecast or
predict simulations in the future. It will result in a model system which will explain the depen-
dency of the factors and processes ,mentioned in above sections, on each other and the eﬀect on the
groundwater quality. The outcome of the model results will help identify the important reactive
processes in the remediation technology and when further developed, can provide knowledge on
the working eﬃciency of PRB.
PHREEQC is an eﬃcient tool for obtaining species from any given water data. It shows the
calculated concentration and distribution of the aqueous species present in a water sample. The
distribution of total number of moles of a substance among diﬀerent phases can be calculated,
those phases which has been deﬁned in the input ﬁle. The desired phases must be included and
quantiﬁed in the codes.
The data required for speciation calculation are concentration of elements with their valence
states (if available), temperature and pH. The SOLUTION data block in PHREEQC is used to
deﬁne the composition of the water. The value pe is used for distribution of redox elements and
for the calculation of saturation indices. pe is calculated from a pair of redox couple in the input
data, in case the redox couple is not speciﬁed, the default will be the input pe.

5.2

In PHREEQC, results contains the input values along with other calculated values. The data
such as temperature, pH and pe were speciﬁed in input, where as the total carbon and total in-
organic carbon, ionic strength, percent error and electrical balance are calculated by PHREEQC.
The results show the molalities, activities and activity coeﬃcients of the species, separately for
their valence state. The saturation index (SI) is speciﬁed for all the minerals, along with ion activ-
ity product (log IAP) and the log of constant (log KT). It must be noted that only those minerals
are listed whose elements are included in the analytical data. For example, if the concentration of
magnesium (Mg) is not in included in input, the saturation indices of dolomite ( CaMg(CO3)2 )
will not be calculated.
In PHREEQC calculation, the saturation index of the minerals formed is always calculated.
But when the amount of phases, which are water, gas or solid phase needs to be calculated,
the EQUILIBRIUM PHASES data block is used to deﬁne the equilibrium saturation index of the
minerals. Each phase that will dissolve or precipitate to achieve the equilibrium value is calculated.
Some compounds or substances are not listed in the PHREEQC database ﬁles, when it is needed
to include these in the input data, two data blocks are generally used, SOLUTION MASTER
SPECIES and SOLUTION SPECIES. If the compound has redox states, the master species for
each valence state needs to be deﬁned. SOLUTION SPECIES contains the equations deﬁning
those species. When dealing with kinetic reactions for transport or batch simulations, the keyword
KINETICS is used to deﬁne the reaction and its parameters.
The rate of those reactions are
deﬁned in mathematical form, under the keyword RATES.

5.3.1
Donor oxidation model
For a reliable foundation of the model built in this report and to verify the accuracy of the
input codes, the results from (Hansen, 2005) were recreated. By producing the same results, the
codes in the input ﬁle is accurate. This makes further modiﬁcation in the codes and the results
obtained credible.
In (Hansen, 2005), the decreasing concentration of lactate over time was calculated by PHREEQC
and the evolution of redox processes by measuring concentrations of redox parameters was mod-
eled. The time of simulation was 27 days. The data block RATE deﬁnes the rate of reaction, for
which the equation was generated from batch measurements. At ﬁrst, the study used PHREEQC
to calculate the saturation indices for selected minerals and observed the changes over the period
of time in anaerobic conditions. From that the most common SI values observed were taken and
deﬁned in EQUILIBRIUM PHASES in PHREEQC to create the most suitable anaerobic envi-
ronment. During the processes, reduction of water occurs and the volume of gas increases in the
system, thus GAS PHASE has been included in the model. However, only change in amount of
lactate and redox parameters is referred in this report.
Recreating the model from (Hansen, 2005), provides the codes that can simulate lactate oxida-
tion. The model was then transitioned from lactate oxidation to propionate oxidation, by replacing
lactate with propionate. It makes the model useful for this study, as Provect-IR contains calcium
dipropionate as the organic donor.

A process is added one at a time and its output is analysed. After the output displays the
expected changes, another process is added then and the steps repeat. The Table 5 shows the step
by step processes that have been modeled, along with input parameters used at each step. Initially,
the model is built without including groundwater. In this way, the output is focused only on the
process and changes from the processes are easily analysed.
During the model building, it is assumed that dechlorination of TCE, cDCE and tDCE can
occur simultaneously with ZVI oxidation and/or propionate oxidation. Propionate is assumed to
ferment and produce acetate. Hydrogen is assumed to be the primary fermentation product, which
in the presence of bacteria, is oxidised in terminal respiration processes. In biodegradation of or-
ganic compounds, its process is assumed as hydrolysis, fermentation and respiration. However,
the existence of non dechlorinating, such as sulphate reducing, iron reducing or methanogens, or
dechlorinating bacteria or their growth is not included in the model. (Hansen, 2005) included
EQUILIBRIUM PHASES in the model to calculate the minerals precipitated and dissolved to
sustain the anaerobic conditions. However, the dechlorination model focuses primarily on under-
standing amendment induced changes, thus the saturation indices are not predeﬁned in the model.
No equilibrium values were assigned to the minerals, therefore their values in the model results is
due to the processes.

6.3.1
Sequential reductive dechlorination
Conceptual Model.
The model consists of hydrogenolysis and reductive β elimination pro-
cesses, which occurs from the addition of ZVI. This section consists of the ﬁrst three set-up in
Table 5.
Reductive β elimination
consists of abiotic degradation of TCE. The ZVI in Provect-IR cor-
rodes and it reduces water to hydrogen gas and the two chlorine atoms in TCE is replaced by the
hydrogen atom and carbon-carbon bond forms. The product is chloroacetylene. Then through
hydrogenolysis, chloroacetylene converts into acetylene. Afterwards, another mechanism, hydro-
genation, removes the C-C bond and addition of two hydrogen atom occurs. Thus, through hydro-
genation, ethene forms and further end product ethane forms. The model developed for Reductive
β elimination is presented in the Appendix 10.3.1. The formation of chloroacetylene and acetylene
can be seen in the Figure 44. ZVI oxidation, increase in pH and reduced pe can be noticed.
Hydrogenolysis.
In hydrogenolysis, sequential dechlorination of chlorinated ethenes occur. One
chlorine atom is replaced by one hydrogen atom and when the degradation of TCE occurs, products
are formed, which are cDCE, tDCE, 1,1-DCE, VC, ethylene and ethane. The iron corrodes and
releases hydrogen which aids in the dechlorination process. Some of the hydrogen here can also
be microbially mediated. The model developed separately for hydrogenolysis is presented in the
Appendix 10.3.4. The formation of the products and other parameters can be seen in Figure 45.

7.1
Biogeochemical conditions pre and post Provect injection
Groundwater parameters and their species distribution provides the knowledge about change
in major species over the period of time, occurrence of mineral precipitation, and redox chemistry
of the aquifer. The changes in groundwater parameters provides indications about the presence
of amendment. The amendment induced changes constitutes the biogeochemical processes in the
aquifer. In general, some signs of amendment can be witnessed in all the monitoring wells. While
some wells show obvious signs. (Christensen, 2020) studied the distribution of amendment and
found that the amendment is seen to be spread more in screen 1 of most wells. This observation
is evident from the groundwater parameters data from the monitoring events. There are several
factors to understand the presence of amendment, and to understand the change in conditions of
the groundwater.
7.1.1
Speciation distribution
Discussion for MB2-1 and MB13-1.
These two screens are among the screens which show
the strongest signs of amendment presence. (Christensen, 2020) also states immediate signs of
amendment delivery in MB2-1 and MB13-1. The signs of amendment in species distribution is
known by changes in Na species as Provect-IR contains sodium sulphite which dissociates in water
producing Na ions. Na increases for both screens after 2 weeks of injection, as the dissociation
occurs right away, Figure 14 and Figure 16.
The amendment induced changes is known from increase in H2, produced from water reduction
due to ZVI oxidation and also from fermentation of organic donor. H2 drastically increases in
MB2-1 after 3 months. The sequence can be understood that in 2 weeks, amendment occurs, and
ZVI corrodes, producing H2 in 3 months. The major species in general, Cl, Na and bicarbonates,
starts recovering after 2 weeks and reaches roughly to baseline conditions after 6 months in both
the wells.
Overall speciation distribution.
The wells overall are governed by the three major species,
with occasional changes in other parameters which either indicate amendment signs or amendment
induced changes. MB2-1, MB7-1, MB8-1, MB9-1, MB13-1 and MB15-1 show increase in Na and/or
increase in H2 by 3 months. MB8 and MB9 are surrounded by injection points, causing fastest
amendment delivery, thus increase in Na is not noticed after 2 weeks, because direct increase in
H2 is noticed after 2 weeks. This explains presence of great amount of ZVI and the occurrence of
corrosion process. Somehow only slight change in Na is noticed in MB7-1 and no H2 formation
has occurred. The amendment seems to be delivered mostly in MB8 and MB9 in transect B.

7.3
Reductive dechlorination model
The dechlorination model set-up is based on the batch experiment conducted by Geosyntec and
COWI. Thus, a batch set-up is made in PHREEQC and parameters are taken from the Geosyntec
laboratory batch experiment. The model has been developed primarily with an aim to simulate the
degradation of chlorinated ethenes. Thus, the model has been developed step by step, starting with
simple dechlorination processes, with addition of ZVI. Hydrogenlysis and reductive β elimination
has been simulated. The model is further built with addition of donor, simulating dechlorination
with only donor, then in further steps, ZVI and groundwater data were added. The modeling steps
can be seen in the Table 5.
The rate of degradation of chlorinated ethenes needs to be deﬁned in the model. Hence, the
dechlorination process of chlorinated ethenes is simulated with a ﬁxed rate, which stays the same
in each set-up, thus producing mostly same plots for both abiotic and biotic degradation. The
addition of ZVI and organic donor cause the amendment induced changes in the system. The
changes are also seen when groundwater parameters are added. The changes in the parameters
vary according to the processes added, and therefore their relations can be analysed. The changes
in the input parameters can also be understood with the respective equations used.
Hydrogen.
In the abiotic degradation of chlorinated ethenes with ZVI, the hydrogen produced is
in same amount as Fe2+, as both Fe2+ and H2 is produced when Fe0 is oxidised, Figure 25. Since
there is no organic matter present in the system, the hydrogen is not consumed and its concentra-
tion persists in the system. When dechlorination is modeled with addition of donor (propionate),
hydrogen is produced with acetate, and hydrogen is consumed for the degradation of chlorinated
ethenes. The donor oxidation also occurs simultaneously and methane is formed. The hydrogen
also could be consumed in methane formation, therefore, the hydrogen completely declined in the
system, Figure 26.
When dehlorination is simulated with both ZVI and donor, the hydrogen concentration declines
slightly in comparison to Fe2+, inspite of its consumption in dechlorination and methane formation,
Figure 27. It is because of large amount of ZVI present in the system, suﬃciently supplying the
hydrogen. When groundwater parameters are added to this system, the eﬀect of donor oxidation on

Future use and model development
The simulations performed in this report represent a simple model which includes dechlorina-
tion of chlorinated ethenes in addition with both ZVI and donor oxidation. It is a simple model,
as it focuses on the primary processes arising in the system from ZVI and donor oxidation. The
simulation takes into account the redox processes generated by oxidation of the donor along with
the degradation of chlorinated ethenes. The method of simulation used in this study also includes
geochemical equilibrium model which has been used to explore species distribution and mineral
saturation states as well.
The model is a batch system with a deﬁned rate of chlorinated ethenes in the system. The
changes in the system from parameters of ZVI and from propionate oxidation have eﬀects on
groundwater. The model provides the capability to understand the signiﬁcant relations between
changes in these parameters.
The model, being developed in series, provides an advantage of
adjustment of only a few parameters at a time, making it simpler to understand the governing
processes.
The model set-up developed in this study is capable of simulating any other donor type and an
alternative substance to ZVI along with dechlorination process. The model at each step in the se-
ries provide insights into a certain process at a time. For example, the source of carbon in methane
and the source H2 at ﬁeld could be predicted from the model. The mass balance calculated at each
step ensures the authenticity of the processes. Thus, from the results, the model look promising to
continue further with the development, with further research into processes and an aim to depict
the real aquifer scenario as close as possible.
The model in this study provides an advantage of a strong foundation for the future model
development and a clear understanding of the processes involved.The dechlorination plots in this
study is not directly comparable with the ﬁeld monitoring data. The model needs further de-
velopment for better comparison. The model built in series in this study, gives insight into the
relationship between the development of redox processes and the transformation of chlorinated
ethenes. The rate of reactions of chlorinated ethenes can be modiﬁed for deeper understanding of
consumption of added donor.
By taking into account the oxidation of propionate in the model, the redox processes are simu-
lated to be comparable with the ﬁeld groundwater data. The model can be enhanced by deﬁning
saturation indices of minerals in anaerobic conditions and pH can be ﬁxed to further simulate
the desired processes. The formation of methane can be curbed in the model, since Provect-IR is
antimethanogenic, to compare the results well with the groundwater data.
The dechlorination model in this study does not take into account the microbiological activity.
The dechlorination model is based on a simple batch set up which did not take into account all

the substrates present in Provect-IR and thus all the fermentation product, and respiration pro-
cess and dechlorinating or non dechlorinating bacteria culture. Therefore, the simple batch model
can be further developed by addition of all the products in Provect-IR and exploring their precise
degradation pathway and equations, by gaining knowledge on the important fermentation products.
The model can be further build by producing dechlorination process in combination with com-
plete fermentation of substrates in Provect-IR, iron reduction, sulphate reduction and methanogen-
esis by the set of microorganisms, which include denitrifying bacteria, iron and sulphate reducing
bacteria, methanogens and dechlorinating bacteria culture. The inhibition of chlorinated ethenes
on transformation of each other exists (Yu et al., 2005). TCE can have inhibitory eﬀects on cDCE
and VC formation and also the eﬀect is vice-versa, although at diﬀerent degree. Thus, the model
can further include competitive inhibition kinetics with inhibition constants for each chlorinated
ethenes. The growth of bacteria and biomass growth can be included and modeled by inhibition
model (Malaguerra et al., 2011).
The batch system provides the beneﬁt of understanding by focussing precisely on diﬀerent
processes occurring in the system. It creates a clear picture on the ongoing processes. The amal-
gamation of speciations formed of a parameter and minerals formed with the batch model, imparts
even deeper understanding of the processes. One of the most important factor in the simulation
of dechlorination model, is the rate of degradation of chlorinated ethenes and the precise circum-
stances that leads to faster degradation. Thus, the model can transform from a batch system to a
column set-up, that will take into account the aquifer properties. In the batch model, the rate of
degradation of all substances in the system is deﬁned and therefore ﬁxed through the simulation.
The rates can be adjusted only based on the expected results from comparison with laboratory or
ﬁeld results. The batch system allows to understand the processes, however, still does not replicate
the real aquifer system, because the groundwater does not remain standstill. The existing column
experiment set-up by Geosyntec for comparing treatment performances by diﬀerent ZVI products
can be utilized by inputing its parameters to build the PHREEQC column model. The results
again form a qualitative comparison with the Geosyntec column test results. Thus, the reactive
transport model of chlorinated ethenes takes the model closer to real aquifer situation.
The Geosyntec column test consists of continuous ﬂow and stop ﬂow. Stop ﬂow set-up was
created to check the rate of degradation of chlorinated ethenes with longer residence time. Dif-
ferent processes can occur in reactive transport modeling, such as sorption, which will show the
retardation of chlorinated ethenes, can be included in the model. The precipitates formed due
to biogeochemical processes in the PRB, may clog the pores of the soil reducing its permeabil-
ity. Thus, numerical models are build for understanding and quantifying the coupling between
hydraulic and biogeochemical processes within the PRBs. The mineral precipitation aﬀects the
hydraulic conductivity and it can be measured at the site by slug test and grain size analysis. The
information of hydrualic conductivity will provide insight into the rate of ﬂow of groundwater,
which will help generate the rate of degradation of chlorinated ethenes.

However, the model built in this study provides the set-up to obtain greater understanding to
diﬀerent amendment induced processes while simultaneously simulating dechlorination.

9
Conclusion
The present study aims at assessing the biogeochemical processes involved in the degradation
of chlorinated ethenes by mZVI product (Provect-IR), which contains organic donor, in combi-
nation with a bacterial culture (KB-1) in a directly injected treatment zone. To understand the
processes and signiﬁcant relationship among the parameters, PHREEQC model to simulate abiotic
and biotic degradation of chlorinated ethenes by mZVI, simultaneously with donor oxidation, was
developed step by step. COWI together with Capital Region of Denmark initiated a pilot scale
study upon which the investigations in this study are based upon. Biogeochemical processes have
been assessed to gain knowledge about geochemical conditions pre and post Provect-IR injection.
To understand biogeochemical processes, the change in species distribution and redox chemistry
was analysed by using PHREEQC. Groundwater was sampled for 5 diﬀerent monitoring events,
and parameters from the monitoring event have been used in PHREEQC to evaluate biogeochem-
istry and for building the model. The model is qualitatively compared with laboratory batch tests
and ﬁeld data. Based on the results, the future use and development of model has been assessed.
The conclusions made from this study are mentioned in this section.
The speciation distribution results showed that the groundwater is comprised of three major
species: Cl, Ca and bicarbonates. Among which bicarbonates constitute the highest percentage.
Observations over the period of 6 months showed that their concentration increase or decrease
throughout. In general, the bicarbonate concentration is higher in wells measured for 6 months in
comparison to data from 3 months post injection. The organic matter in the amendment, seems to
have gone through fermentation process sometime before 3 months, which caused overall decreased
in pH in most wells after 3 months. The fall in pH led to the formation of bicarbonates, and thus
the overall pH increases after 6 months post injection.
Anaerobic conditions prevail in all the wells immediately after injection.
Nitrate reduction
occurs instantly after injection. Thereafter, most of the wells with signs of amendment experience
iron and sulphate reducing conditions. The wells experiencing rise in NVOC after 6 months is
paired with rise in VFAs and Ca or Na, which are present in the amendment. The amendment
delivery and amendment induced changes, through speciation distribution, can be known by large
increase in Na and H2 respectively. Overall increase in Na is seen for wells after 6 months. It has
been predicted that the amendment might have travelled back to those wells sometime before 6
month monitoring event. Iron concentration has also been found to increase in general in the wells
by 6 months. The all time highest concentration of iron is found in MB2-1 after 6 months. This is
in alignment with the assumption about delivery of amendment occurring again. Ammoniﬁcation
was observed after 6 months, which indicates the fermentation of organic matter, which can be
sourced by the amendment.
The trend of change in pH is similar in most wells throughout. The pH in general increase in
most wells 2 weeks post injection. This increase is presumed to form from ZVI oxidation. The pH
69
==PAGE:79==
then falls after 3 months. The changes also aligns with change in mineral saturation. The minerals
tested, which are calcite, dolomite, siderite and gypsum, were found to be dissolving in the water
after 6 months, which explains the rise in pH.
(Hansen, 2005) lactate oxidation model in a batch set-up is created in this study, and the model
is modiﬁed to simulate propionate oxidation. The evolution of redox processes, nitrate reduction,
sulphate and iron reduction and methane formation, in the model can be well observed.
The
development of anaerobic conditions in the system can be clearly detected. Mineral saturation
indices represent the values in anaerobic conditions, which justiﬁes the development of anaerobic
environment.
The study proposes a rudimentary approach in modeling chlorinated ethenes abiotic and biotic
degradation simultaneously with donor oxidation in a closed batch system. The diﬀerence in rate
of degradation was found in the model when compared to laboratory batch test and ﬁeld test.
The relationships between signiﬁcant processes occurring by addition of ZVI and donor oxidation
could be thoroughly studied by building the batch model in series. The models proved beneﬁcial
in understanding the source of the parameters in the product. The abiotic degradation by ZVI
shows oxidation of ZVI and that pH drastically increases and low pe persists.
The biotic degradation degradation of chlorinated ethenes simulates the redox processes and pH
reduces, due to fermentation of propionate. The model simulating both biotic and abiotic degra-
dation shows the change in pH is governed solely by ZVI oxidation. The source of H2 in species
distribution at the ﬁeld could be known from the model upon comparison with ﬁeld results. It can
be known that ZVI oxidation contributes to most of the H2 present in a sample, and is the reason
behind increase in pH even in model with donor fermentation. The evolution of redox parameters
from donor addition can be seen in the model. The organic carbon is used in the biotic component
of the model. It is consumed in the model to form acetate from propionate and methane formation
from propionate oxidation. The source of carbon for methane formation could be known from
the model. Methane formation does not occur at the ﬁeld due to anti-methanogenic properties of
Provect-IR.
Processes originating from the amendment tends to generate geochemical processes in the
aquifer, such as mineral precipitation or dissolution, which can aﬀect the aquifer properties. Thus,
the eﬀect of diﬀerent processes on mineral saturation and pH can be predicted from the model.
The model, whether or not developed with more fermentation reactions, even in this case, can
be used to predict the amount of type of substrates that needs to be added to acquire stable pH
for existence of microorganisms that assist in degradation process. The model can predict the
change in pH and hence knowledge on pH can inform on the water quality. Apart from studying
amendment induced changes in the model, the rate of degradation of the contaminants can be
altered and adjusted to gain insight into the consumption of the substrates.
