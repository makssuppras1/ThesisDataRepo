Sammenfatning
Denne rapport er et casestudie, der undersøger indflydelsen af ændringen i lovgivningen vedrørende
de økonomiske rammer for spildevandsforsyningsselskabers klimatilpasningsprojekter, som blev
indført ved ændringsloven Lov nr. 2210 i 2021, på serviceniveauerne for Sjælør Boulevards sky-
brudsgren i København, Danmark.
Gennem litteraturstudie og interviews med interessenter, fra HOFOR og Københavns Kommune,
blev den skiftende lovgivning og dets konsekvenser analyseret. Avanceret hydrodynamisk mod-
ellering ved brug af MIKE+ software blev anvendt til at simulere nuværende og fremtidige kli-
matilpasningsscenarier, herunder en statusmodel og en masterplanmodel samt en scenariemodel,
der afspejler de sandsynlige projektimplementeringer under den nye lovgivning. Undersøgelsen
anvendte en trepunktsmetode (3PA) til at vurdere den hydrauliske ydeevne på tværs af tre domæner
(A: Hverdagsdomæne, B: Designdomæne og C: Ekstremdomæne), suppleret med yderligere simu-
leringer for at evaluere overholdelsen a funktionskravet til kloakken.
Modelresultaterne indikerer, at ændringen i reguleringen i 2021, som førte til annulleringen af to
projekter inden for studieområdet, bidrager til manglende overholdelse af serviceniveauet for både
kloakken og vand på terræn. Trepunktsmetoden viste sig effektiv til at fange effekten af reguler-
ingsændringer i et komplekst system, hvilket understreger fordelene ved at bruge denne metode
i analyser af klimatilpasningsprojekter. Denne undersøgelse fremhæver kompleksiteten i regu-
leringslandskabet for klimatilpasning og viser, at ændringer kan forstyrre masterplaners mål om
hydraulisk sammenhængende projektpakker. Derudover bekræftede interviews med interessenter
vanskelighederne ved at overholde både lovændringen fra 2021 og de fastsatte serviceniveauer
i Københavns Kommune, hovedsageligt på grund af de høje dokumentationskrav. Dette under-
streger vigtigheden af industristandarder, der sikrer ensartethed på tværs af forskellige sektorer,
virksomheder og hydraulikere.
På grund af den skiftende karakter, af både reguleringslandskabet for klimatilpasning og de nega-
tive virkninger af klimaforandringer, vil yderligere forskning i effekten af ændringer inden for den
organisatoriske struktur af klimatilpasningsplanlægning være gavnlig.

Abstract
This report is a case study investigating the impact of the regulation change in regards to the
framework of wastewater utility companies’ climate adaption projects, introduced by the (Dan-
ish) amending act Law no. 2210 in 2021, on the service levels of Sjælør Boulevard cloudburst
branch in Copenhagen, Denmark.
Through a literature study and stakeholder interviews, with HOFOR and Copenhagen Munici-
pality, the changing regulatory landscape and its implications were analysed. Advanced hydrody-
namic modelling using MIKE+ software simulated present and future climate adaptation scenarios,
including a status model, a masterplan model, and a Scenario Model reflecting the likely project
implementations under the new regulations. The study applied a three-point approach (3PA) to
assess hydraulic performance across three domains (A: Everyday domain, B: Design domain and
C: Extreme domain), supplemented by additional model runs to evaluate the service level of the
sewer system.
The model results indicate that the 2021 regulation change, leading to the cancellation of two
projects within the study area, contributes to non-compliance with service levels for both the of
the sewer system and water on terrain. The 3 point approach proved effective in capturing the
effect of regulation change in a complex system, highlighting the benefit of using this in analysis
of climate adaptation projects. This study underscores the complexity of the regulatory landscape
for climate adaptation, revealing that changes can disrupt the masterplans’ aim of hydraulically
coherent project packages. Additionally, interviews with stakeholders, HOFOR and Copenhagen
Municipality, confirmed the difficulty in complying with both the 2021 regulation change and
the decided service levels of Copenhagen Municipality, mainly due to the high documentation
requirements. This emphasises the importance of industry standards ensuring consistency across
different sectors, companies and hydraulic engineers.
Due to the fact of the changing nature of both the regulatory landscape of climate adaptation and
the adverse effects of climate change, further research into the impact of changes within the organ-
isational structure of climate adaptation planning, would be beneficial.

1.1
Regulation change in climate adaptation in Denmark
On the 1st of January, 2021, the amending act, Law no. 2210 [Lov nr. 2210] (Klima- Energi- og
Forsyningsministeriet, 2020a), came into force, making the necessary amendments to several reg-
ulations regarding climate adaptation projects by wastewater utility companies and, additionally,
added two new statutory orders:
Regulations Amended by Law no. 2210
• The (Danish) Water Sector Act (LBK1693) [Vandsektorloven]
(Klima- Energi- og Forsyningsministeriet, 2021b)
• The (Danish) Environmental Protection Act (LBK1317)[Miljøbeskyttelsesloven]
(Miljøministeriet, 2015)
• The (Danish) Payment Act (LBK1775) [Betalingsloven]
(Klima- Energi- og Forsyningsministeriet, 2021a)
• The (Danish) Economic Framework Statutory Order (BEK2291) [ØR-bekendtgørelsen]
(Klima- Energi- og Forsyningsministeriet, 2020c)
• The (Danish) Wastewater Statutory Order (BEK2292) [Spildevandsbekendtgørelsen]

New Regulations Enacted by Law no. 2210
• The (Danish) Service Level Statutory Order (BEK2276) [Serviceniveaubekendtgørelsen]
(Miljøministeriet, 2020a)
• The (Danish) Cost Statutory Order (BEK2275) [Omkostningsbekendtgørelsen]
(Klima- Energi- og Forsyningsministeriet, 2020b)
These regulations tightened the economic framework of wastewater utility companies’ climate
adaptation planning and increased the documentation requirements for their projects. It, addition-
ally, repealed the co-financing scheme, which made it possible for collaborative projects between
wastewater utility companies and municipalities/private stakeholders to get partly or fully funded
by the water tariffs. Furthermore, the Service Level Statutory Order sets new requirements for
determining the service level of water on terrain (Miljøministeriet, 2020a). It demands for projects
handling roof and surface water to be both socioeconomically viable and corporate economically

ceed its lifetime economic savings. Corporate economic cost-effectiveness being that socioeco-

the greatest economic savings. This statutory order sets the technical specifications for the so-

found in the (Danish) Water Pollution Committee’s Report 31 (Olsen et al., 2017), which is used

from earlier laws/regulations as the socioeconomic analysis is the deciding factor for the project.
This could potentially lead to contradictions within the industry standards and municipal plans of
Copenhagen. This is because Copenhagen Municipality has decided, that there should be a fixed
performance requirement of the sewer system to allow surcharging from manholes every 10 years
for combined sewer and every 5 years for separate sewer cf. the (Danish) Water Pollution Commit-

change in the approach and execution of climate adaptation projects in Denmark (Soja et al., 2021)
(Plesner, 2022).
These regulations carry significant implications for urban climate adaptation, complicating nav-
igation through the regulatory requirements and necessitating a departure from conventional ap-
proaches. While it is still possible for the municipality, state or other parties to pay the additional
costs to wastewater utility companies, if a higher service level is desired, this places a heavier finan-
cial burden on the municipalities. A consequence of this could be, that they have to decide whether
to put money in climate adaptation projects or in other municipal project, e.g. the construction of
new daycare centres. This could force the decision making process to be highly politically charged
and potentially limit the allocation of funds to climate adaptation projects, consequently keeping
future climate adaptation projects at the minimum required service level. Contrary, there is also
the possibility that the new regulations might lead to more political engagement and professional
capacity within the municipalities in regards to the development of climate adaptation.
Even with this regulation taking effect in 2021, the implementation is currently still in a transitional
period as the planning and execution of such infrastructural projects have long time spans. Addi-
tionally projects, that were ongoing during the regulation change, still fall under the previous co-
financing rules under certain circumstances (Miljøministeriet, 2020a). This transition may lead to
uncertainty in ongoing projects, especially regarding the service level requirements. Additionally,
this is an industry where the chosen government has a significant influence on the development.
This could lead to many changes and halts along the way. Moreover, this could result in urban

1.2
Case study and modelling work
Given the current change in climate adaptation planning in Denmark, resulting from the new regu-
lations, it is crucial to explore how these regulations influence planned projects and consequently
the outcome of climate adaptation masterplans. These plans have a long implementation horizon.
Thus if the execution of the plan overlaps with the changing regulations it could pose problems,
particularly as masterplans are often composed of multiple projects within a cloudburst branch.
Ensuring hydraulic coherence between these projects is essential to maintaining an optimal ser-
vice level, thus if some of these projects are affected by the new regulations, the masterplan may
not function as intended. Understanding this dynamic is essential to understand how changes in
masterplans affects the water balance of an area and ultimately determines the final service level
with multiple hydraulically interconnected climate adaptation projects.
This thesis will delve into the masterplan of the Sjælør Boulevard cloudburst branch in the south-

Furthermore it was determined that the municipalities should carry out a risk assessment of their
area and produce a municipal climate adaptation strategy by the end of 2014, resulting in a Cli-

in connection with this, a Cloudburst Plan in 2012 (Københavns Kommune, 2012). Copenhagen
has established many strategies in order to secure that the city is protected against and adapted
to future climate change impacts e.g. the Wastewater Plan (Købenavns Kommune, 2018). Since
2005 the performance requirement of the sewer system was set to the recommended requirement of
the (Danish) Water Pollution Committee’s Report 27 (Harremöes et al., 2005) (Købenavns Kom-
mune, 2018). In 2012 the Cloudburst Plan added a performance requirement for water on terrain
(Københavns Kommune, 2012). These requirements are present in all planning regarding climate
adaptation in Copenhagen and are defined as follows:
• Service level for sewer systems: Surcharging from manholes onto terrain every 10 years for
combined sewer and every 5 years for separate sewer as recommended in (Danish) Water
Pollution Committee’s Report 27 (Harremöes et al., 2005).
• Service level for water on terrain: Surcharging from manholes may surpass a 10 cm pre-
cipitation depth every 100 years. This is decided based on a socioeconomic analysis, which
proof that securing the city against a rain event with occurrence between 20-400 years results
in a net gain, with protection for a 100 year rain event resulting in the highest gain (Olsen
et al., 2017) (Københavns Kommune, 2012) (Københavns Kommune, n.d.) .
Note that there is a difference between the terms of ”performance requirement” and ”service level”
(See Appendix A.2). In general terms the performance requirement is, when the requirement to
a system is defined based on a certain level of functionality e.g. the minimum permissible return
period for surcharging manholes, and the service level is all performance requirements in an area
combined. However, the (Danish) Water Pollution Committee defined the two terms in their Report
31 (Olsen et al., 2017) as performance requirement being specifically in regard to the sewer system
and its compliance with their Report 27 and service level being as a combination of the performance
requirements of the sewer system from Report 27 and for surface water on terrain in compliance
with their Report 31 (Olsen et al., 2017). This distinction is now widely used in academia and
industry, however, ”performance requirement” and ”service level” can be used interchangeable if
context is provided e.g. service level of water on terrain. In regard to the service level, it is standard
practice to refer to what is commonly called the ”The 10 cm Note”, where the service level is
focused on areas with public access and therefore specifically set to 10 cm water on terrain for a
100 year rain event in the boundary between public and private areas. Furthermore, the Climate
Adaptation Plan specified the need for a projection of 100 years (due to an expected increase in

However, climate adaptation is a multifaceted challenge that involves a diverse array of actors and
regulatory frameworks. Within climate adaptation planning, there are many actors e.g. public,
private, national, municipal, local etc. and different forms of regulations e.g. acts, laws, plans etc.
To manage this, a plan hierarchy is put in place where no plan may conflict with a higher-level
plan (Munk-Hansen, 2024). The hierarchy specifies national first, then municipal and lastly local
plans. Only in few specific instances local plans can overrule higher level plans. Furthermore
some standards are so widely used in the industry that they become common practice, without
being mentioned in the law. This includes the several reports from the (Danish) Water Pollution
Committee, such as Report 27 (Harremöes et al., 2005), Report 30 (Gregersen et al., 2014), Report
31 (Olsen et al., 2017) and Report 32 (Arnbjerg-Nielsen et al., 2023). Additionally, the Danish
standard for drainage installations on private grounds (DS432) (Dansk Standard, 2020) is also
widely used.
One of the widely used reports from the (Danish) Water Pollution Committee is their Report 32
(Arnbjerg-Nielsen et al., 2023), which is both a report and an CDS generation Excel Tool. A
CDS (Chicago Design Storm) as a synthetic rainfall commonly used for urban drainage and flood
management. It represents a storm event with specific rainfall intensity and duration patterns,
characterised by a high peak intensity at the midpoint. Report 32 is a follow-up to Report 30
(Gregersen et al., 2014), where a number of assumptions are tested, and the regional model for
extreme rainfall is updated using the latest data and knowledge in the field. The new rain data
is collected in the period of 1979-2019 and therefore the rain intensities without a climate factor
corresponds to year 2000, which is incorporated into their new the Excel tool for computing CDS
rains. In the old tool from Report 30 (Gregersen et al., 2014), the rain data is based on the period of
1961-1990 and therefore must reflect the year 1976 approximately. In Report 30 the climate factor
to T=2, T=10 and T=100 is given for a 50 year and 100 year projection period. If data for other
projections was needed it could be found through interpolation. However, the new Excel Tool is
made more accessible as it contains a tab for calculating the suitable climate factor, clearly stating
that the projection horizon is starting from year 2000.

2.1
Regulation before 2021
One of the major changes in the economic framework for climate adaptation projects, introduced
by the amending act Law no. 2210, is the repeal of the co-financing scheme (Klima- Energi-
og Forsyningsministeriet, 2020a). In Denmark, the funding mechanisms for wastewater utility
company projects are typically governed by the Water Sector Act and managed through the (Dan-
ish) Utility Secretariat. The (Danish) Utility Secretariat oversees the allocation of funds collected
through water tariffs. These tariffs are paid by consumers and businesses and form the primary
source of funding for wastewater projects (Klima- Energi- og Forsyningsministeriet, 2013).
Traditionally only the wastewater utility company could get their climate adaptation projects funded

• The project must be located on/in roads, in streams or in recreational areas.
• The project owner must be municipal or private.
• The project must be cost-effective.
• There must be an agreement between the utility company and the project owner.
• Municipal risk mapping must indicate the need for mitigation.
By cost-effective it is meant that the cost of the project (an alternative solution to mitigate roof

applies to unforeseen costs, where this is only funded up to the costs of the conventional wastewater
engineering solution, unless the unforeseen costs would also have applied to the standard solution.
This approach also applies if the project is expanded. Any changes to the project, which influence
the main goal/character of the project, will need to be re-evaluated and approved by the Utility
Secretariat.

2.2.1
The Service Level Statutory Order
One of the new acts was the Service Level Statutory Order (Miljøministeriet, 2020a) which sets
the framework for participation between municipalities and water utility companies and states the
importance of socioeconomic analysis to ensure no investments with net loss. It came into force
the 1st of January 2021 (Miljøministeriet, 2020a)(DANVA, 2021). Under these regulations, there
are new requirements for socioeconomic viability e.g., that the costs of building the project do not
exceed the economic savings the project can provide over its lifetime (Miljøministeriet, 2020a).
A simplified socioeconomic method has been developed to determine the necessary service level.
It is furthermore determined that a project should be economically efficient, meaning that the so-
The effect of regulation change on the service level of climate adaptation
19
==PAGE:36==
cioeconomic savings for several alternative projects should be calculated as well, resulting in the
project with the greatest economic savings being chosen.
Once a project has been assessed as socioeconomically viable, it must be evaluated to determine if
it is economically efficient according to the Cost Statutory Order by calculating and comparing it
to at least one other alternative solution, which should be a different type of solution than the one
in question (Klima- Energi- og Forsyningsministeriet, 2020b).
The socioeconomic method consists of multiple different steps resulting in a net present value
(NVP) for the project, determining if it is worth pursuing. This method will be put to use if the
municipality considers raising the service level above the minimum requirements. Here a raise
in service level is only permissible if the socioeconomic method results in a net profit. For the
method, there should be a proper division of the catchments and sub-catchments determined by
hydrology and the sewer-network. Next step is producing a risk map by combining a flood map
and a value map. The flood map will consists of different rain scenarios for the chosen catchment
incl. a climate factor. The value map will provide economic values of the infrastructure both
directly e.g. buildings, cropland etc. and indirectly e.g. production loss, temporary housing etc. It
should be noted, that for the value map non-marked values and special economic gains e.g. nature,
ecology or reduction in CO2 emissions are not taken into account. Thereafter the net present value
is found by comparing calculations of the expected annual damage (EAD) for the status scenario
(no additional climate adaptation implementations) and for different scenarios of climate adaptation
projects implemented in the area. Dependent on the magnitude of the investment a certain number
of climate adaptation scenarios need to be compared. The economic gain is calculated as the net
present value, taking inflation into account.
It is possible for the municipality/state or other parties to pay the wastewater utility company for
additional costs if a higher service level is desired than that achieved by the socioeconomic method.
However, if the service level is set to the standard for a 5-year event for separate sewerage and
a 10-year event for combined sewerage (or if the aim of the project is not climate adaptation),
calculations of socioeconomic viability and company economic efficiency are not needed.

3.2
The 3 Point Approach
The Three Points Approach (3PA), initiated by Govert Geldof and later expanded upon, is a frame-
work designed for urban flood risk management (Geldof, 2008). It emphasises a holistic and inte-
grated method to address the complexities of urban water systems. It divides the climate adaptation
impact into 3 domains:
A. Everyday domain: Water resource utilisation.
B. Design domain: Urban drainage.
C. Extreme domain: Pluvial flood mitigation
It was developed by Govert Geldolf through his work as an engineer and his PhD study (Geldof,
2008). The thought behind this approach was that water management and societal infrastructure
are part of a complex adaptive system, where the infrastructure tries to adapt to the changing envi-
ronment. However, the impact thereof is difficult to predict as it acts non-linearly (Geldof, 2008)
(Lerer, 2019). The 3PA is graphically based on a pattern identified by Per Bak in 1996, where
there is a linear relationship between the log of their magnitude and the log of their frequency in
regard to crisis e.g. avalanches, floods etc.(Bak, 1996) (Lerer, 2019). (Figure 3.1 displays a newer
version of the graphical representation of the 3PA, showing the 3 domains in regard to return pe-
riod, magnitude of event and frequency, for the original see Appendix A.6) Geldof presented the 3
discussion arenas, later renamed the 3 domains, which needed to be addressed, in order to achieve
successful flood mitigation activities. Especially the everyday domain (originally point 3, later re-
named domain A) is necessary in order to secure long term sufficient water management (Geldof,
2008).
Sara Maria Lerer later quantified the 3 point approach through analysing Danish rainfall series,
producing the axis of the 3PA and furthermore renamed the numbered discussion arenas to domain
names A, B and C in order to ease communication (See Figure 3.1) (Lerer, 2019).

4.1.2
Protection level
The design basis for the masterplan was based the two performance requirements decided by
Copenhagen Municipality as surcharging from manholes every 10 years for a combined sewer-
system and a maximum of 10 cm water on terrain up to a 100 year rain event. This service level
for water on terrain applies for the borders between the public and the private space e.g. more than
10 cm water on terrain is allowed in public spaces, however, the precipitation depth at the bor-
ders should be under the 10 cm. The Centre for Climate Adaptation has determined that there are
no special infrastructure or vulnerable public institutions within the Sjælør Boulevard cloudburst
branch, which are in need of a higher service level.
4.1.3
Infiltration to groundwater
In the northern part of the cloudburst branch there is a wish to utilise space underground for every-
day rain, The groundwater has a higher salt concentration and therefore it was necessary to inves-
tigate whether this area would be suitable for infiltration of rainwater from the roads, especially
during the winter months when the roads are salted. In general, there is a high level of chloride in
the north-western part of the cloudburst branch, due to salting of roads. An investigation concluded
that the ground water renewal rate is not enough to sufficiently lower the chloride level enough
during the summer month to keep the level suitable for drinking water. Therefore the conclusion
is that it is not allowed to infiltrate 100% of the everyday rain the north of the cloudburst branch
as long as Copenhagen municipality wishes to keep the standards of the groundwater at drinking
water level. This can also not be infiltrated only through rain beds due to the limited size of the
rain beds. It should also be noted that there is a risk of water in basements at Kløverbladsgade if
a lot of water is infiltrated (due to the low infiltration power in that area). In conclusion only part
of the everyday rain can be infiltrated to limit the chloride in take in the ground water and due to
limited infiltration capacity of the rain beds. It may be possible to close the rain beds in the winter
time since the groundwater table normally is lower in the summer and then the rain beds can have
bigger dimensions and therefore higher infiltration power in the summer.
In the southern part of the cloudburst branch no solutions with infiltration to groundwater are
considered, since the groundwater table lies high in this area (Københavns Kommune & HOFOR,
2019).

5.2.2
Conversion from MIKE Urban to MIKE+
The given model was converted from MIKE Urban to MIKE+, which necessitated some alter-
ations. MIKE+ has the ability of importing a classic MIKE Urban model (.mdb-file), conducting
the conversion process itself. However, MIKE+ is error-tracking, indicating problematic inputs
and missing data, which sets more strict demands to the MIKE+ model compared with MIKE
Urban. Therefore part of the conversion process was to correct errors in MIKE+ and afterwards
comparing the network output for the same rain series in MIKE Urban and MIKE+ in order to
determine, if the water balance and control rules, in the converted model, acted accordingly to the
original model. This was done for a CDS with return period T=10yrs, duration of 4hrs and an op-
erational factor f=1, which was run on both models including a hotstart file generated from a 4 day
network fun of dry weather flow (DWF), included as hotstart at day 3 at time step 23:50:00. After-
wards a network run was computed for a CDS of T=100, duration of 4hrs and an operational factor
of f=1, to ensure a stable network run. As these simulations were only for comparison purposes,
there were not many requirements in the choosing of the CDS parameters.
Not all errors were corrected, since MIKE+ is still able to run with some of them as warnings.

The influence of CDS time step
Another factor which can impact the results in domain B is the time step of the CDS. In some
consultancies/Wastewater Utility Companies it is common practice to use a 10 min. time step
instead of the default 1 min. time step. In Figure 6.5 two CDSs are displayed with return period
T=10, operational factor f=1.3 and a duration of 4 hours, however their time step is on ts=1min
and ts=10min, respectively (See Appendix A.8 for the list of CDSs used in this study). It is clear
that longer time steps decreases the peak by a lot, as the intensity is averaged over a longer time
step. This is also the reasoning behind, why some choose a longer time step when computing a
CDS rain, when investigating surcharging manholes, as the MIKE software has shown sensitivities
towards the intense peaks and peak differences in between time steps. The smoothing of the peak
is not changed for this examination, however, also needs to be taken into account.

tion=4h with a 10 min. time step. When compared with Figure 6.4 of the same CDS but with a 1
min. time step, the biggest difference is shown in the northern area. Even the two nodes 322260
and 322259, that were the only surcharging nodes in the Scenario Model and not in the Masterplan
Model, are not surcharging, if the CDS with longer time steps is used. However, there is still a
very clear problem area containing a lot of surcharging manholes in the south of the cloudburst

Discussion
The following chapter will discuss the findings and results of this study in regards to the impact of
regulation change on Sjælør Boulevard cloudburst branch, modelling approach and future research.
7.1
The impact of regulation change on Sjælør Boulevard
cloudburst branch
When analysing the model results, it is evident, that the change in regulation in 2021 did in fact
influence the service level of both the sewer system and water on terrain in Sjælør Boulevard
cloudburst branch. Additionally, it displayed an increase of the amount of surcharging from man-
holes and flooding on terrain, both in general and above the critical level in boundaries between
public and private spaces. Note that the Masterplan Model itself is also non-compliant with both
service levels, thus the influence of the regulation change is seen in regards to changes of the ser-
vice levels in the areas of the two cancelled projects, KV67 and KV91. The non-compliance with
the performance requirement for the sewer system is seen in domain B within the KV91 project
area. In Figure 6.4 for the network run of the Scenario Model in 2100, it is displayed that the only
difference in surcharge between the Masterplan Model and the Scenario Model is two surcharging
manholes overflowing, due to the removal of project KV91. For domain C in the Scenario Model,
more flooding was present on the roads along with instances of surpassing the 10 cm precipitation
depth in boundaries between public and private spaces near project areas of KV67 and KV91. A
significant difference is seen near the project area of KV67, as the nearby roads east of the project
area experience a big increase in flooding on terrain after the removal of the project.
In general, the 3 models show great similarity in domain B, due to the fact that the implemented
projects in the Masterplan Model struggle to limit the amount of surcharging manholes, and the
Scenario Model is very similar to the Masterplan Model with only two projects differentiating them.
This complicates the service level investigation, as the Masterplan Model is not a clear example of
a service level compliant masterplan. When the compliance in the Scenario Model is investigated,
it is of importance to always compare with the Masterplan Model, as the Scenario Model being
non-compliant does not necessitate, that the annulment of the two projects is the cause of this. It
is clear from both the Masterplan Model and Scenario Model results, that additional or different
adaptation measures are needed in order for the area to reach the optimal service levels.
In general, the results show, that Sjælør Boulevard cloudburst branch has great difficulty com-
plying with the service levels, both today and in the future. It is clear that the regulation change
in 2021, did impact the levels of surcharge and flooding, and, additionally, adversely effected
compliance with the service levels. This reflects the challenging task of both complying with the
new regulation changes and the service levels decided by Copenhagen Municipality, showing a

need for the regulations of climate adaptation and the climate adaptation strategy of Copenhagen
Municipality to be streamlined.
When examining the regulatory landscape of the wastewater utility companies’ climate adaptation
projects, it is clear, that the influence of many regulating factors complicates the planning process
of the projects. It is evident from the regulation change in 2021, that the core driver was to tighten
the economic framework of the wastewater utility companies and their climate adaptation projects,
consequently causing changes in the masterplans such as hydraulic incoherence between project
This interference in the planning of climate adaptation projects was echoed in the interviews with
HOFOR and Copenhagen Municipality (See Appendix A.6 and Appendix A.7, respectively). Here
it was clear that the regulation change in 2021 did have adverse effects in climate adaptation plan-
ning, necessitating a new strategy from Copenhagen Municipality in 2024.
In the case of Sjælør Boulevard, the results might show a smaller impact from the regulation
change, however, as the implementation of multiple projects is currently up for debate at HOFOR,
this could potentially pose a bigger influence, than showed in this study. As Sjælør Boulevard
masterplan is highly focused on hydraulic coherence between the projects, with the concept of all
excess runoff being led to Karens Minde Aksen (KMA), few differences could break this entire
concept. The dimensions for the pump is currently on hold and any impact on this would greatly
change the function of KMA. Additionally, the annulment of projects KV67 and KV91 did show
non-compliance with the service levels of both the sewer system and water on terrain, sparking
the question, why the annulment of a project is only based on its timing within the transitional
arrangement and not additional parameters such as its contribution to service level compliance and
hydraulic factors in the area. The annulled projects KV67, 3 detention spaces, and KV91, the im-
plementation of two bumps in order to guide the water onto the sports field detention space, are
likely some of the cheaper climate adaptation projects in the masterplan. Therefore this begs the
question if the annulment of the project would actually be an economic saving. These projects
were cancelled due to the repeal of the co-financing scheme and because they were not accepted
under the transitional arrangement, but they could still proceed under the new regulation, if they
could be proven socio-economically viable and corporate economically cost-effective. Notably,
this puts the stakeholders in a bind, as the implementation of these projects is necessary in order
to comply with the service levels, however, if implemented, necessitates high documentation re-
quirements to prove compliance with the new regulation from 2021, which might not be of interest
for the given stakeholder. This challenge was also highlighted in the interview with the HOFOR
employee, who pointed out the heavy documentation burden, leaving stakeholders in a limbo, as
meeting both requirements demands significant resources. Ultimately, the decisive factor for pro-
ceeding with an adaptation project must be its socio-economic viability, excluding considerations
such as vulnerable infrastructure e.g. hospitals and evacuation routes, as the project is not feasible,
if the construction costs exceed the potential cost of flood damage.

Another perspective is the impact on the other masterplans of Copenhagen. With Sjælør Boulevard

The goal of masterplans is to create project packages, which are hydraulically coherent and which
interplay collectively in order to comply with the service levels. This was incorporated in the
municipal strategy to prevent non-coordinated climate adaptation planning, where loop-holes and
projects added later on might be needed in order to both raise the service level in the area and

regulatory environment. With the new climate adaptation strategy from Copenhagen Municipality
in 2024, yet another regulatory change will intervene the process of the masterplans. Additionally,
the approval and adoption of projects in the Wastewater Plan, by changing governments, introduce
another variability throughout the implementation period. However, notably changes in master-
plans are common due to their nature as non-binding project proposals.
The changing regulation also has impact on the focus of the climate adaptation planning. Over
time the focus of climate adaptation has gone from domain B towards domain C, however, with
the new strategy, from Copenhagen Municipality in 2024, it will go back to domain B. Seeing the
implementation of the 3PA, effectively capturing the complexity of the system with the impact
of all 3 domains, it could be questioned whether climate adaptation planning could benefit from
a similar approach. This reflects the complexity of climate adaptation planning as it is affected
by many changing factors such as regulation, government and industry standard, whilst adapting
to the changing and increasingly adverse effects of climate change, as echoed by Geldof in his
presentation of the 3PA (Geldof, 2008).
7.2
Modelling approach
In discussing various modelling approaches, it is crucial to consider, how the setup of a model
can influence its results and its capacity to accurately simulate real-world mechanisms. The given
model computes the water balance through applying the rain as a boundary condition on the catch-
ments, which are connected to the nodes and through that also the rest of the drainage system. In
a network model the nodes can surcharge through a fictive cone, where the volume of surcharge
is tracked. In a coupled model the nodes can surcharge onto the terrain model, allowing for flow
exchanges between the 1D and 2D model until simulation ends and consequently visualising the
impact of water movement on terrain. Due to this setup, the model struggles to display the impact
of the surface adaptation measures, which are implemented in the terrain layer, in the network
The effect of regulation change on the service level of climate adaptation
73
==PAGE:90==
model results of domain B.
A coupled model run, would be able to reflect the exchange flows between the 1D and the 2D model
such as increased surcharge due to runoff collecting in a certain area and entering the drainage
system. However, with this model setup, as rainfall is firstly added to the catchments and then the
nodes etc., the impact of projects implemented in the terrain layer might be skewed in the 1D model
as rainfall is not applied directly onto terrain, being captured in detention spaces, and therefore
offering strain relief on the sewer system. Instead it is applied to the nodes, and therefore also the
drainage system, allowing for surcharge in areas of limited capacity, where interaction with the
2D model would allow surcharge to flow to the projects implemented on terrain. Therefore, with
the model setup in this study, it is important to keep in mind, that surcharge in close proximity to
projects implemented in the terrain model, might not accurately reflect the mechanisms of the real
world. Consequently, this reflects that, with the model setup of this study, using network results
alone to display a masterplan like that of Sjælør Boulevard, with multiple terrain adjustments,
might not be the optimal modelling approach. However notably, the model setup does allow for
implementations in the model catchments or drainage system e.g. initial loss in catchments or
implementation of cloudburst pipes, which would be reflected in a network model run.
Another approach to obtain better results in domain B, could be to build a model entirely for this
purpose, by implementing all projects in the 1D model alone e.g. using basins to reflect detention
spaces. Another solution could be changing the model setup of the rainfall. Instead of applying
it as a boundary condition on the catchments, it can be applied directly onto the terrain model,
reflecting the effect of terrain movement on the flood placement.
The present model settings are a better fit for domain C. This is logical since the model is used
for the masterplan, which is heavily focused on cloudburst protection measures such as detention
spaces. It is evident, that different model setups support the representation of different domains.
For example the green roads, which were implemented through a higher initial loss in the catch-
ments, could be reflected in both the network and coupled model results, representing performance
in both domain B and C, due to the setup of the rainfall boundary condition. This might visualise
appropriate results for a cloudburst event of high intensity and short duration, as the porous areas
would be saturated quickly, leading to complete runoff. However, for an everyday rainfall of low
intensity and a long duration, representing domain A, the model would not show realistic results as
the initial loss implemented in the catchments would behave the same as in the cloudburst model
run, slowly filling up until all is runoff (100% impervious). This does not reflect real life mech-
anisms, where complete saturation of the porous areas would not be reached, instead there would
be constant infiltration throughout the long term rainfall event. This points out, that in regards to
the 3 point approach, the model setup is crucial, in order to fully reflect the implications on each
domain.

Conclusion
This study investigated, how the regulation change in 2021, for the economic framework of wastew-
ater utility companies’ climate adaptation projects, impacted the service level of Sjælør Boulevard
cloudburst branch. A literature study and interviews with stakeholders, HOFOR and Copenhagen
Municipality, was carried out in order to research the change in regulation and the impact thereof.
Advanced modelling techniques was employed using the MIKE+ software (DHI, 2024) to simu-
late both present and future scenarios of climate adaptation projects, using 3 models depicting the
status, the masterplan and lastly a Scenario Model representing the likely project implementation
due to the regulation change. The 3 point approach (3PA) was used in order to reflect the effect
on all 3 domains, and additionally the service level was examined through additional model runs.
The method was as follows:
• A literature study was performed, exploring relevant subtopics for this research through a
review of literature, legal databases and government publications.
• An interview guide was made and used in interviews with relevant stakeholders, HOFOR
and Copenhagen Municipality.
• Domain B of the 3PA was examined through a network run in MIKE+ of a CDS with a
T=10yrs return period on all 3 models.
• Domain C of the 3PA was examined through a coupled model run in MIKE+ of a CDS with
a T=100yrs return period on all 3 models.
• Domain A of the 3PA was examined qualitatively for all 3 models by analysing the effect of
the planned climate adaptation projects on the water balance.
• The actual service level of the sewer system was tested for the Scenario model through a
network run in MIKE+ of multiple CDSs with return periods T=1yr, T=5yrs and T=9yrs.
This study showed, through model results, that the regulation change in 2021 did cause non-
compliance with the performance requirement for the sewer system and the service level for water
on terrain in Sjælør Boulevard cloudburst branch. Additionally, it revealed non-compliance for the
Masterplan Model as well, indicating a challenging task of implementing sufficient climate adap-
tation projects in the study area, which are able to combat the adverse effects of climate change.
Due to this, the 3 models revealed highly similar results. However, in the area of the two cancelled
projects, KV67 and KV91, model results showed an increase in surcharge and flooding to the level
of non-compliance.
The analysis reflected the complexity of the regulatory landscape for wastewater utility companies’

climate adaptation projects, with numerous influencing factors complicating the planning process.
While masterplans aim to create hydraulically coherent project packages that collectively comply
with service levels, in order to avoid uncoordinated climate adaptation planning, the changing reg-
ulatory environment challenges this objective. In the case of Sjælør Boulevard cloudburst branch,
multiple projects are currently up for debate, possibly causing even more changes to the actual im-
plementation of the masterplan, which is very depended on the hydraulic coherence between the
projects in the area, especially Karen Minde Aksen (KMA). Additionally, the study area clearly
reflects the difficulty in complying with both the 2021 regulation change and decided service lev-
els of Copenhagen Municipality, as the two projects, KV67 and KV91, were cancelled instead of
continuing under the new regulation with a higher documentation requirement in order to prove
socio-economic viability and corporate economic cost-effectiveness. This was echoed in the inter-
views with HOFOR and Copenhagen Municipality, which stated that the regulation change highly
impacted climate adaptation planning and implementation, with the HOFOR employee pointing
out the high documentation burden of complying with the service levels, consequently putting
stakeholders in a limbo phase. The method, the 3 point approach, proved effective in capturing the
effect of regulation change in a complex system, highlighting the benefit of using this in analysis
of climate adaptation projects.
The model setup highly influences its capacity to accurately simulate both real-world mechanisms
and the different domains of the 3PA. Due to the model setup of this study, the results for domain B
did not sufficiently display the impact of the multiple cloudburst protection measures, which were
implemented in the terrain layer. However, this was accurately reflected in the results for domain
C, as the model setup showed a better fit for that domain. This displays the significant influence of
model setup on results and highlights the importance of an industry standard to ensure consistency
within hydraulic modelling across sectors, companies and hydraulic engineers.
To further expand the research in this case study, a quantitative analysis of domain A, a different
model setup for domain B and additional research of the actual service level and a sensitivity of
input factors in domain C, would be of interest. Any additional changes of the masterplan of
Sjælør Boulevard cloudburst branch, due to regulation change, would be of interest to examine,
along with other masterplans highly dependent on hydraulical coherence between projects or with
a higher interference from the regulation change. Although the climate adaptation strategy of
Copenhagen Municipality is changing this year in order to further streamline the regulation of
climate adaptation and strategy of the municipality, studies such as this, which analyses the impact
of regulation change on climate adaptation projects, will still be of interest due to the complexity
of the organisational structure of climate adaptation planning in combination with the increasing
and changing adverse effects of climate change.

A.2
Glossary
A glossary was made to cover the essential terms relevant to this thesis. This was created mainly
based on glossaries from the websites of Copenhagen Municipality (Københavns Kommune, 2018d)
and The Danish Environmental Protection Agency (Danish Ministry of Environment and Food,
2018) as well as the glossary from the annual climate adaptation report from Copenhagen Munic-
ipality (Københavns Kommune, 2022).
Act (Danish Consolidation Act) An administrative act in which the latest amendments to a law
are incorporated, publishing the updated law in its entirety.
Amending Act (Danish) A legislation that modifies, updates, or repeals specific provisions of an
existing law to improve, correct, or adapt it to new circumstances. It specifies the changes
and the sections of the original law being altered.
Basin Open/closed structure, where wastewater is stored in case of insufficient capacity down-
stream in the drainage system.
Bathing zone A harbour/beach area where bathing/swimming is allowed.
Blended finance The strategic use of public, private, and philanthropic funds to mobilise addi-
tional private capital for sustainable development projects. This approach aims to address
market failures and create opportunities for private investors to support social and environ-
mental goals.
Catchment An area where precipitation and runoff are collected through flow paths towards a
single point e.g. sewer, lake or other body of water.
Chicago design storm (CDS rain) A design storm used widely across the world by drainage en-
gineers. The (Danish) Water Pollution Committee published a spreadsheet ”SKVs Regional
Regnrækkeværktøj” (Miljøministeriet, 2013a) where a CDS rain can be calculated with a
few inputs.
Climate change adaptation It refers to physical measures or actions aimed at adapting to the
changing climate conditions. An example is flood protection measures to mitigate the im-
pacts of heavy rainfall events.
Climate factor A factor added on a design storm in order to incorporate the changing climate.
The factor depends on return period and the given project e.g. the lifespan of the project.
86

==PAGE:103==
The standard climate factors are given in the (Danish) Water Pollution Committee’s Report
30 [SVK 30] to be 1.2, 1.3 and 1.4 for return periods of 2, 10 and 100 years respectively.
Cloudburst The meteorological definition is a rain event where it rains more than 15 mm within
30 min. (HOFOR, n.d.). However it is also used in general to describe large rainfall events,
larger than what the sewer system is designed to cope with, where water will flow in surface
solutions or in cloudburst tunnels.
Cloudburst branch A defined area, which is hydrologically interconnected. The City of Copen-
hagen is divided into 60 cloudburst branches, each containing between 1 and 17 surface
projects. Projects within a cloudburst branch are interdependent, meaning that if one project
is unable to handle the required volume of water, this water must be managed by one or more
of the other projects within the same cloudburst branch.
Cloudburst catchment Same definition as for a catchment, but this term is used in regards to
cloudburst management. Copenhagen consists of 7 cloudburst catchments.
Cloudburst pipeline/tunnel A pipeline/tunnel dimensioned to be able to transfer big amounts of
runoff from cloudburst events.
Cloudburst protection Measures aimed at protecting against damaging flooding caused by heavy
rainfall (cloudburst).
Cloudburst system A coherent system of cloudburst protection measures, which operates inde-
pendently of the sewer network.
Co-financial project A cloudburst protection, which is partly or fully funded by the wastewater
tariff. The project must be a cloudburst protection measure implemented on terrain, where
the stormwater is managed either in/on the road, in an open water body, in a park, in a green
area, or in connection with streams or lakes. There are multiple additional requirements for
the project, planning and process in order to obtain the funding.
Combo project When a sewage disposal plant is built in coordination with a communal co-financial
project. Here the municipality and the utility company work together closely, and often the
co-financial project is dependent on the capacity of the sewerage disposal plant.
Corporate economic cost-effectiveness In the (Danish) Cost Statutory Order (Klima- Energi-
og Forsyningsministeriet, 2020b) it is demanded that the Wastewater Utility Companies’
climate adaptation projects regarding roof- and surface water must be corporate economic
cost-effective. To prove this the wastewater utility company must compare the costs of the
chosen solution with an estimate of the costs for achieving the same service level by at least
one other relevant type of solution. The process of this is described in the (Danish) Service
Level Statutory Order (Miljøministeriet, 2020a).

Decoupling Sometimes the roof water is disconnected from the sewer system and instead con-
nected elsewhere, e.g. to a cloudburst system or infiltration system, in order to ensure that
sewer capacity can keep up with the development of precipitation amounts resulting from
climate change.
Drainage system A system designed to remove excess surface water and groundwater to prevent
flooding and waterlogging in an area e.g. stormwater pipes.
Drainage water A common term for all water (wastewater, rainwater, drainage water etc.) dis-
charged from residential areas, commercial enterprises, other buildings and paved surfaces
and collected in the drainage system. This term is a bit broader than wastewater as it is
defined to be in drainage systems and not only sewer systems.
Efficiency requirements According to the (Danish) Water Supply Act, wastewater supply com-
panies must record and report information about the company’s environmental, service, op-
erational and economic conditions to the (Danish) Utility Secretariat. This is for the (Dan-
ish) Utility Secretariat to use in calculating the water companies’ efficiency through results-
oriented benchmarking.
Everyday rain Minor rain events that statistically occur quite often, e.g., 5 times per year (Lerer
& Sørup, 2016).
Flood map A map showing areas of flooding in a given area for a given rain event.
HOFOR The wastewater utility company of Greater Copenhagen. HOFOR is a multi-utility com-
pany, with responsibility for energy, water and wastewater in Greater Copenhagen (but not
the big treatment plants, which are in a separate wastewater utility company called BIOFOS).
Household/domestic wastewater/sewage Wastewater from households e.g. kitchen, bath and
toilet. No mix with runoff or other types of drainage.
Impermeable surface/reduced area An area or part of an area, which due to its use, for example,
roads, buildings, etc., is completely or partially impermeable to water. Often measured in
square meters or hectares.
Local drainage of rainwater Draining the water with local solutions to limit stress on the sewer
system e.g. soakaways and bio-retention units (rain gardens).
Masterplan A master plan is an extensive, long-term planning document that establishes a strate-
gic framework for the development, management, and sustainable growth of a specific area

or project, addressing land use, infrastructure, transportation, and environmental considera-
tions. It aims to guide decision-making and coordinate efforts among stakeholders to achieve
cohesive and well-integrated outcomes. In the Copenhagen municipality, master plans are
developed for each cloudburst branch. These plans are designed to implement the initia-
tives and goals outlined in Copenhagen Municipality’s Climate Adaptation, Cloudburst, and
Wastewater Plan.
Mixed wastewater/combined wastewater/combined sewage A combination of domestic, indus-
trial, and stormwater runoff that is conveyed through the drainage/sewer system.
Operational factor The overall factor that is multiplied with the design rain intensity. It can
include: a safety factor for runoff, scenario projection, and bias factor. Along with the
operational factor, it documents the differences between the current status and the planned
model, including how urban densification is described (Arnbjerg-Nielsen & Illeris, 2023).
It is formerly known as the safety factor.
Outlet Where water is being discharged to a discharge point e.g. from sewage system to water
body through CSO, rainwater to water body, WWTP to water body etc.
Overflow structure Structure, where wastewater spills to a recipient or other drainage system,
whenever the drainage system is overloaded.
Percentage of impermeable surfaces/imperviousness/runoff coefficient The ratio/fraction be-
tween the paved area and the total area of a property, catchment, etc. Often expressed in
percentage.
Performance requirements A performance requirement is when the requirements to a system is
defined based on a certain level of functionality e.g. in regards to drainage systems often
defined as a given critical elevation level and the minimum permissible return period for sur-
charging from manholes above this level. The municipality has an obligation to ensure that
drainage systems provide a certain minimum functionality, which is formulated in simple
performance requirements. An example can be found on the (Danish) Water Pollution Com-
mittee’s Report 27 [SVK 27] (Harremöes et al., 2005), where the performance requirement
is defined for municipal combined sewer systems (with focus on maintaining a given service
level in the area) as the critical elevation level being the terrain and the minimum permis-
sible return period for surcharging from manholes above this level being 10 years. Report
27 has been a standard in the industry for many years, thus when mentioning performance
requirements in regards to drainage systems and climate adaptation, often what is referenced
is the recommended minimum performance requirements for sewer sizing in Report 27.

PULS (Danish) National database for wastewater discharge from point sources.

Recipient Water body, which receives wastewater/stormwater.

goal of increasing the capacity in the sewer network, minimising negative effects such as
spillage from sewerage to natural water bodies and poor bathing water quality.

terms, all performance requirements in an area combined make up the service level. In the
(Danish) Water Pollution Committee’s Report 31 [SVK 31] (Olsen et al., 2017), the service
level of water on terrain is defined as a combination of the performance requirements for
sewer system and for surface water on terrain. If both performance requirements are fulfilled,
then the service level is considered met.
Sewage disposal plant Sewage disposal plants are typically underground pipes or basins, but
could also be open basins or channels, where the goal is to manage the wastewater including
stormwater from a rain event with a return period of up to 10 years. In Copenhagen, this is
within the framework of HOFOR.
Sewer (system) strategy A sewer strategy, defined by HOFOR, is a long-term strategy for the
development of the sewer system. The strategies provide solutions at the catchment level,
which are further elaborated in master plans (HOFOR, n.d.).
Sewer catchment area An area from which the domestic wastewater is led to the wastewater
treatment plant and where the municipal wastewater plan is in force.

Socioeconomic viability That the costs of building the project do not exceed the economic savings

Statutory Order (Danish) A general legal provision issued by the public administration with cit-

issuing the often technically detailed provisions for the implementation of a law.
Strain relief/Unloading Discharge of mixed wastewater from the sewer network’s overflow struc-
tures to the water body, which in turn offers strain relief in the drainage system.
Structure Collective term for certain structures in the drainage system e.g. overflow structure,

Tariff The price for 1 cubic meter of water, which is paid by citizens and industries to the wastew-
ater utility company. It covers the costs of both the water and wastewater/stormwater infras-
tructure and management.
The annual climate adaptation report An annual report updating the status of projects and new
planning in regard to climate change adaptation within a municipal area.
Wastewater A common term for all water (wastewater, rainwater, drainage water etc.) discharged
from residential areas, commercial enterprises, other buildings and paved surfaces and col-
lected in the sewer system. This term is less broad than drainage/mixed wastewater as it
refers to water in the sewer system specifically.
Wastewater collection system A wastewater system refers to the infrastructure responsible for
collecting, transporting, and sometimes storing wastewater from households, industries, and
other sources. It consists of open as well as closed pipes and other facilities that serve for
the discharge and/or treatment of wastewater in connection with discharge to recipients or
discharge to the ground. Often combined with a wastewater treatment plant.
Wastewater treatment plant (WWTP) Plant for purification and after-treatment of wastewater,
as well as the management of the residues.
Wastewater Utility Company A company which is responsible for the maintenance and func-
tionality of the sewers and WWTPs in a given area/municipality.
