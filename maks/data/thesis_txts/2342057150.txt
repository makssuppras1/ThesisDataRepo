ABSTRACT 
A rapid increase in energy use has been observed during the last decades, mainly 
due to population growth, while the latest trends for extensive glazing in commercial 
buildings, have especially increased the cooling demands during summertime. 
Enhancement of the buildings energy efficiency is considered a promising approach 
for the reduction of energy use, since according to IEA buildings account for over 
40% of the primary energy consumption. Phase change materials (PCM) have become 
a forerunner in the thermal energy storage field over the latest years, but the high 
production costs hinder from widespread utilization within the built environment. 
In the current project, an investigation to define the optimal thickness of a phase 
change material (PCM) for cooling applications in buildings was primarily carried out, 
while afterwards it was studied as a complete solution to be implemented on an office 
room ceiling. 
On a comparison with concrete slab, PCM was found able to store over the double 
amount of heat for a 6 hour period, when exposed to room drifting temperatures 
between 20 and 26°C. The effective thickness for a PCM was found to be less than 
20 mm.  
Five different paraffinic PCMs were tested through numerical simulations and 
PureTemp 23 was found to perform the best. For a PCM structure with active 
discharge though embedded pipes, the optimal pipe to pipe distance proved to be 
0.115 m.  
Since paraffins have relatively low thermal conductivity enhancement techniques 
were also examined. Aluminium and graphite foam were the most promising 
solutions which can reduce even by 50% the discharge time without altering 
significantly the thermal properties of PCM. 
As a radiant cooling solution for office rooms, it was proposed as optimal a 20 mm 
PCM panel, actively discharged overnight with a water flow of 120 kg/h and supply 
temperature of 18°C.

During the last decades a rapid increase in energy use has been observed, mainly 
due to population growth. The latest trends for extensive glazing in commercial 
buildings tend to result in high internal and solar heat gains leading to overheating 
during summertime, even in moderate and cold climates in Central and Northern 
Europe [1]. In fact, IEA states that in many member countries, the building sector is 
responsible for over 40% of the primary energy use [2]. 
Enhancement of the building energy efficiency, either through its envelope or by the 
cooling and heating systems performance, is considered to be a solution with high 
potential [3]. Over the last years several innovative technologies have been developed, 
with the field of energy storage drawing increasingly the interest of the research 
community. A large variety of research studies demonstrated that an application of 
thermal mass in well insulated structures could generate heating and cooling energy 
savings between 5 and 30% in residential buildings [4], [5], [6]. 
Sensible heat storage has always been the most commonly used method for thermal 
energy storage, latent heat storage though is gaining ground over the last few years. 
Concepts of latent heat and specific heat were initial discovered by the Scottish 
scientist Joseph Black in the mid-eighteenth century [7] 
One of the prospective techniques for thermal energy storage is that of phase change 
materials [8], due to their ability to provide high energy storage density at an almost 
constant temperature [9]. 
The first documented use of phase change material (PCM) was in 1948, by MIT 
researcher Dr. Maria Telkes [7] in a house located in Dover, Massachusetts, USA. 
Since then, PCMs have been tested as a thermal mass component in buildings and 
they can be described as emerging materials with the potential to drastically reduce 
cooling energy use and peak cooling demand in buildings, when applied properly [7]. 
Phase chance materials introduced in building envelopes can be used for many 
different purposes including space conditioning on a reduced energy use, shifting of 
thermal peak load, temperature control in building envelope components, or even 
improvement of the overall system durability [7]. 
This report mainly discusses several strategies for mitigation of thermal loads 
generated in office buildings, by the implementation of PCM in the suspended ceiling.

Objectives 
The starting point of this work was the observation for immediate need of new 
applications in the thermal energy storage sector. PCM technologies were specifically 
selected because of their promising properties and the broad possibilities for 
research. 
The literature review revealed several parameters that hinder PCM applications from 
becoming widespread employed. Therefore, the research objectives for the current 
project were: 
- 
To identify the effective thickness of a phase change material with respect to 
the temperature fluctuations and the heat gains that occur in an office room. 
The investigation is limited to numerical simulations and only comparison with 
existing applications was performed. 
- 
To investigate different mass enhancement methods, with respect the ease of 
production and asses their effect on the total thermal conductivity. 
 
- 
To optimize the proposed ceiling panel structure as an active radiant cooling 
system for an office room and examine its performance. 
 
Summarizing, the general aim of this master thesis has been to optimize radiant 
ceiling solution for cooling application in office buildings.

2.1 
Phase Change Materials 
It is predicted that by 2050, over 70% of the world population will live in urban 
environments. Therefore effective and sustainable thermal energy storage systems 
(TES) in buildings will be of great importance even more [10], since the modern trends 
in the building sector have turned to lightweight construction materials with low 
thermal mass. Incorporated addition of PCMs in the thermal mass of the building 
could increase its thermal storage capability and in that way help improving the 
overall building energy performance [7]. 
PCMs are substances which have the ability to absorb or release “latent” heat at an 
almost constant temperature, during a change in their physical state (solid, liquid or 
vapor), which allows them to store larger amounts of thermal energy. In fact they can 
have 5-14 times greater heat capacity per unit volume, comparing to sensible storage 
materials as water, masonry and rock [8]. 
2.1.1 General Classification 
Lane [11] reviewed nearly 20000 chemical materials, on order to identify those with 
a melting point in the region of 10 - 90°C. PCMs are classified based on the particular 
phase change that occurs in the operation temperature envelope, which can be: 
- 
Solid – solid 
- 
Solid – liquid 
- 
Solid – gas 
- 
Liquid – gas  
For extensive information regarding PCM classification, someone can refer to the 
papers of Lorsch et al. [12], Lane et al. [13] and Humphries and Griggs [14] who have 
reported a large number of possible candidate materials for latent heat storage, 
covering a wide range of temperatures. 
However, among them, PCMs with solid-liquid phase change, which will be further 
analyzed in the current report, are the most commonly used in building applications 
[7]. The reason is because during solid-solid transition, there is only a change in the 
crystalline structure, resulting to small latent heat capacities [8], while in solid-gas 
and liquid-gas phase change large volume changes have been observed during the 
transition [15].

2.1.2 Solid – Liquid PCM 
Solid-liquid phase change materials can be further categorized into 
- 
organic  
- 
inorganic and 
- 
eutectic 
Inorganic PCMs are mainly composed of salt-hydrates and metals, while organic 
include paraffins, fatty acids and sugar alcohols. Although in general inorganic 
compounds have greater volumetric latent heat storage capacity, organic PCMs are 
extensively used due to their capability to melt and freeze repeatedly without phase 
segregation and absence of super-cooling effect. Additionally, organic PCMs do not 
usually demonstrate corrosive behavior [7]. The most commercially promising 
organic PCM for building applications is paraffin [10]. 
Operation principle 
In a solid-liquid phase change process, large amounts of heat can be stored with a 
proper material selection, while melting is characterized by a rather small volume 
change, usually less than 10%. Therefore, if the pressure is not significantly changed, 
melting and solidification are able to take place at a constant temperature, also called 
“phase change temperature” (Figure 1) [16]. However, important is to point out that 
most of the times the phase transition is happening within a temperature range. 
 
Figure 1.  Heat and storage as latent heat for the case  
of solid-liquid phase change [16] 
Outside the phase transition range, the compound is simply performing as a sensible 
heat storage material. The heat supplied upon melting, is referred to, as “latent heat” 
and in case of small volumetric changes, the stored heat is assumed equal to the 
enthalpy difference. 
2.1.3 Candidate PCM materials 
While many PCMs have been reviewed over the years [17]–[19], for successful 
applications in buildings, there are several limiting factors to be considered when 
selecting a phase change material [10], [16]: 
Physical Properties 
- 
Appropriate phase change temperature according to the desired application. 
4 
 
==PAGE:15==
 
- 
High latent heat of fusion per unit mass, in order to achieve greater heat 
storage capacity. 
- 
High thermal conductivity, to absorb heat faster. 
- 
Reproducible phase change, “cycling stability”, to repeatedly store heat as 
many times is required. 
- 
Little or no supercooling1, so as to achieve phase transition in a narrow 
temperature range.  
Technical Requirements 
- 
Small volumetric change during phase change 
- 
Chemical stability, to ensure long lifetime 
- 
Compatibility with other materials 
- 
Absence of flammability 
- 
Absence of toxicity 
Economic factor is also an important parameter to be considered, if the desired 
product is intended to be widespread. Low cost and easy availability are two 
characteristics that should be taken in account in order for the product to be 
attractive compared to the other cooling systems in the market and recyclable to 
comply with the environmental restrictions. 
However, it is not easy to find a material which fulfils all the aforementioned 
parameters, so Zhou in 2012 highlighted three key properties to be met for any 
potential PCM [20]: 
- 
Suitable melting temperature 
- 
Desirable heat of fusion 
- 
Thermal conductivity as specified by the practical application 
Early studies on latent heat storage focused on low cost salt hydrates, as they 
generally have great latent heat capacity. Over the years though, it was discovered 
that hydrated salts demonstrate stratification during the transition, tendency of 
subcooling, while corrosion on metals has also been observed [21]. 
2.1.1 Enhancement Methods for Improved Thermal Conductivity 
During the operation of a PCM system, either passive or active, all forms of heat 
transfer, convection, conduction and radiation, take place. Effective conductivity and 
total heat transfer coefficient are the most commonly used parameters to express the 
general thermodynamic properties of a PCM system [22]. 
Unfortunately, one major issue to be addressed, is the low thermal conductivity of 
most PCMs [20] and hence, heat transfer enhancement techniques are required in 
order to be implemented in LHTS applications.  
1“Supercooling”, is the effect where solidification process begins only after the material has reached a 
relatively lower temperature compared to the phase transition temperature.

Several attempts have been carried out to enhance their heat transfer capabilities 
including the use of metal foams, aluminum powders, carbon fibers and expanded 
graphite structures [23], [24]. 
Introduction of metal fins and additives has been found to increase conductivity; the 
cost and incompatibility with certain PCM however, impedes progress. Expanded 
graphite has shown great potential for enhancement of paraffin-base systems. It 
demonstrates high thermal conductivity, high stability, good compatibility and has 
lower density than metal promoters. Zhang et al have been researching the 
development of a more efficient manner to produce expanded graphite, through 
microwave irradiation [25]. 
Microencapsulation and emulsion-based PCM slurries improve heat transfer by the 
utilization of convection to the maximum [23], [24]. The performance of emulsion 
slurries can vary significantly, due to surface interaction, increasing the undesirable 
effects of super-cooling [26]. 
Various other methods have been proposed to enhance the heat transfer in a latent 
heat thermal store. Metallic fillers, metal matrix structures, finned tubes and 
aluminum shavings were used to improve paraffin’s thermal conductivity [15]. The 
use of finned tubes in thermal storage systems with different configurations has been 
reported by Morcos [27], Sadasuke and Naokatsu [28], Costa et al. [29] and 
Padmanabhan and Murthy [30]. The use of thin aluminum plates filled with PCM 
was developed by Bauer and Wirtz [31]. Another method used is to embed the PCM 
in a metal matrix structure [32]. Py et al. [33] proposed the PCM to be embedded 
inside a graphite matrix to increase the thermal heat conductivity in the PCM without 
much reduction in energy storage.  
The economic impact, often quantified in payback time, should be optimized 
alongside with the environmental and technical performance [10]. Generally, the 
price of commercially available PCM varies in the range of 0.5 – 10 €/kg. However, 
to give a better insight, some of the results in [34], are presented below. [34], focusses 
on applications in US region, but the results could be possibly used as an index, also 
for the rest of the world. 
PCM prices are generally driven by market demand and supply relationships. The 
current market is not yet fully prepared for the introduction of PCM, resulting to a 
limited demand mainly responsible for their relatively high prices. However, as 
manufacturers base their prices on future market expectations, prices are likely to 
drop in the future. 
PCM product is primarily governed by two factors; the cost of the raw material and 
the encapsulation cost. Two main approaches of encapsulation are applied today; 
macro-encapsulation or packaging, and microencapsulation. The final PCM product 
cost varies significantly according to the encapsulation method. 
The material cost depends also significantly on the classification of the PCM. 
Commercial paraffinic PCMs are by-products of the oil refineries; therefore, they are 
6 
 
==PAGE:17==
 
abundant at a relatively low price, which generally increases with the purity. The 
current cost of paraffin wax is about 1.88-2 USD/kg [34]. A low-cost paraffin 
alternative available is Baker Petrolite’s POLYWAX, which costs 0,53 USD/L, while 
Entropy Solutions Inc. quotes 1.65-5.50 USD/kg for its commercial volume organic 
PCM products.  
The cost to macroencapsulate a PCM is approximately 20% of the total cost, while 
microencapsulation process can even exceed 50% of the final product cost. 
Currently, paraffins are the most popular choice for PCMs because they offer several 
attractive features. They are chemically inert, nontoxic, and easy to encapsulate [34]. 
Yet, bio-based PCMs are another renewable, cheap, and eco-friendly option to 
paraffins, which is possible to become commonplace in the near future. Bio-based 
PCMs, are obtained from animal fat and oils from plants and can be a renewable and 
green alternative for paraffinic PCMs in the future, as well as to compete with the 
existing energy efficient building materials and technologies. They are nontoxic and 
they don’t exhibit material degradation, even after thousands of cycles. They are 
chemically stable and can last for decades, while in the same time they can offer a 
greater degree of fire resistance, and reduced costs [34]. 
The effective enthalpy of the PCM-enhanced building envelope components plays an 
important role in determining the overall cost effectiveness and payback period. In 
most of the applications studied [34], the PCM price needs to be below 4.40 USD/kg 
in order to achieve a 10-year payback period. 
The most promising developments to reduce the cost of PCM products are the 
reduction of microencapsulation cost for organic PCMs, and the development of 
microencapsulation technology for inorganic PCMs.  
Another important characteristic affecting energy performance and cost effectiveness 
is peak-hour load shifting. This feature is especially important for locations with 
time-of-use pricing. Cost analysis presented in [34] demonstrated that it is possible 
to reduce the cooling energy costs by up to 30% with PCM applications of significant 
peak-hour load shifting capability and by taking advantage of the off-peak electricity 
tariff.  
Generally future PCM cost reductions can be achieved through: 
- 
Applying PCMs of higher enthalpies; the best-known PCM candidates are bio-
based PCMs 
- 
Reducing the cost of basic raw PCM; for example, by using inorganic PCMs 
- 
Reducing microencapsulation costs 
- 
Optimizing PCM loads in building envelope products 
2.1.2 Applications in the Built environment 
Several PCM applications for the building sector have been academically tested, but 
a growing number of industries are expressing lately interest in PCM solutions [10]. 
7 
 
==PAGE:18==
 
Initially building applications targeted on an increase of the thermal capacity in 
lightweight constructions. A list of capable PCM-enhanced building applications was 
outlined by Dincer and Rosen [35] 
Several indicative examples, illustrating the wide variety of potential applications 
where PCM can be utilized throughout the built environment are presented. 
Wallboard 
PCM-incorporated wallboards can provide TES throughout the natural framework of 
the building, increasing its conventionally low thermal capacity. Early studies by 
Peippo et al. [36] reported that simply by adding PCM to walls and using passive 
solar heating offered savings of 5-20% on heating costs.  
Concrete 
Through direct application of PCM to 19 mm floor panels, energy savings of 24% were 
found by static analysis under Denver building conditions. PCM-enhanced concrete 
boasted an overall heat capacity of 8000 kJ/m2K – 10 times that of PCM-enhanced 
wallboard [37].  
Ceiling and Floors 
Various designs of PCM-incorporated ceiling fabrications have been made [38],  [39], 
utilizing passive solar heating coupled with night cooling of the PCM [20]. Results 
showed that passive PCM ceiling installations tend to overheat; thus the diurnal 
phase change is not always achieved. 
Active PCM radiant ceiling panels charged with PCM were coupled with geothermal 
heat sinks and passive solar heating to maintain the effective PCM radiative heat 
management throughout the seasons [40]. 
PCM incorporation in underground floor systems can discharge heat to the earth 
during the day, shifting electricity consumption to night rates. Shape stabilized PCM 
incorporated into underground ductless heating systems have demonstrated good 
feasibility, although other underground systems have only marginal economic 
benefits [20]. 
Insulation 
From investigation has been found that PCM-incorporated and PU-foam insulators 
when fitted to attic, can reduce peak summer temperatures from 43°C to 32°C [37]. 
Shutters/window blinds 
Research into PCM- incorporated blinds reported 10 K reduction in blind 
temperature and a 3 h time delay. The utilization of PCM in blind utilizes a relatively 
small amount of PCM for considerable effect on room temperature in comparison to 
traditional incorporations [10].  
8 
 
==PAGE:19==
 
Cooled ceilings 
Wang et al [41] explored the incorporation of PCM slurries as heat transfer fluid. 
They suggested that cooled ceilings employing PCM could effectively offset the cooling 
load to low-cost night tariffs. [42]. 
Air-conditioning systems 
In air-conditioning units PCM slurries can operate in their dual function, that of heat 
storage and transfer. Cool-Phase, by Monodraught, has developed a system suitable 
for heating office spaces. Monodraught claims that by the use of the system can be 
achieved even 90% of energy savings, by efficiently recycling the waste heat in the 
exhaust air [43]. 
Further Applications 
Dinsen and Rose [35] list further alternative applications, including PCM applied in 
the refrigeration cooling system.

2.1.1 Barriers to PCM utilization 
Four areas hindering widespread use of PCM technologies are discussed below. 
Technical limitations 
Even though PCM boasts some impressive thermal properties, it  has reported that 
where diurnal phase change has not been achieved, the thermal performance 
significantly drops and the material simply operates under sensible heating patterns 
[10]. Cautious selection of PCM based on the optimum phase transition temperature 
has been reviewed by Zhou et al. [20],  [37] to minimize this occurrence. 
Modeling limitations 
Dutil et al. addressed two key areas for PCM modelling [44]. Nevertheless, they 
suggested further research on composite encapsulated PCM performance was 
needed, along with refinement of modelling systems. An effort is undertaken to 
populate a consistent database to aid the research on PCM materials, it is however 
not yet accessible to researchers [44].  
Industrial barriers 
The lack of real examples, hold back the widespread application of PCM technology 
in the construction and HVAC industries [44].  
Economic barriers 
High construction costs, long payback times and uncertain green benefits, hinder 
significant incorporation of green technology [45]. From a brief analysis, Mehling and 
Cabeza [16] concluded that under 2008 energy prices, the widespread incorporation 
of PCM was economically uncompetitive. 
 
2.2 
Implementation of PCM in Ceiling panels 
PCM cooling applications in ceilings can be either passive (Figure 2), or active, which 
are usually part of more complex, dynamic air-conditioned systems using over-night 
precooling (Figure 4) [7]. 
 
Figure 2.  Passive applications of PCM-enhanced ceiling systems [7] 
10 
 
==PAGE:21==
 
 
Figure 3.  Active applications of PCM-enhanced ceiling systems [7]  
 
Gutherz and Schiler (1991) developed one of the first passive solar heating systems 
that incorporated PCM, located in the ceiling. It was shown that the use of such a 
system has the potential to recover 17-36% of the heat lost over the initial gains [46]. 
Kondo and Ibamoto (2006) performed a study in an office building, aiming to reduce 
the cooling peak load of the air-conditioning system, using the PCM in the ceiling 
board. The melting point and latent heat of fusion of used PCM was 24.5°C and 174.4 
kJ/kg, respectively. It was found that the rise in the room temperature was only 
about 2°C as compared to the 6°C rise in the room temperature, if PCM was not used. 
and that the peak-hour cooling load was reduced by 14.8% [47]. 
A thermally activated ceiling panel with the PCM storage, was proposed in [64] for 
applying in lightweight and retrofitted buildings. Results demonstrated that a 5 cm 
layer of microencapsulated PCM (25% by weight) and gypsum is sufficient to 
maintain a comfortable room temperature in standard office buildings. [48] 
Turnpenny et al. [49] developed a latent heat storage unit incorporating heat pipes 
embedded in phase change material. Measurements, showed that a large 
temperature difference between air and PCM (15°C or higher) was necessary to melt 
and solidify the PCM in practical timescales (7-10 h). At more reasonable temperature 
differences and flow rates (e.g. 5°C, 0.18 m3 s -1 ) the heat transfer falls below 40 W, 
and the melt time increases significantly. 
Bruno [50] developed a system, which stored coolness in phase change material in 
off peak time and released this energy in peak time. It was found, that existence of 
air gap between PCM and the external structure layer, largely decreases the heat 
conduction coefficient. 
2.2.1 Phase change thermal storage for shifting the peak heat load 
Energy use varies during the day and night according to the demand by industrial, 
commercial and residential activities. The shift of energy usage from peak periods to 
off peak periods, as presented in Figure 4, can provide significant economic benefit. 
The development of an energy storage system may be one of the solutions to the 
problem when electricity supply and demand are out of phase. 
11 
 
==PAGE:22==
 
 
Figure 4.  Active applications of PCM-enhanced ceiling systems [51] 
The working principle of PCM based cooling4 for buildings, which follows the peak 
heat load shifting idea, consists of two  modes of operation, charging and discharging, 
described in [52]. In the design of a PCM storage unit for free cooling applications 
[31], melting point of PCM has a key role and it should be selected in a way so as the 
room air temperature during discharging process is within the range of deﬁned 
comfort levels, 23°C - 27°C, for summer season [23,24]. Therefore, as pointed out in 
[24], the melting temperature of the PCM for free cooling systems should be between 
19 and 24°C.  Several different criteria to choose the melting point of PCM are 
elaborated in [52]. 
2.2.2 PCM Containment 
PCM containers should generally meet the following basic requirements: 
- 
Strength, ﬂexibility, corrosion resistance and thermal stability 
- 
Suﬃcient surface area for heat transfer 
- 
Structural stability and easy handling. 
Below, are mentioned the most common methods currently used for containment of 
phase change materials and possible drawbacks that have been observed in practice. 
Impregnation of building materials 
Khudhair and Farid [18] described numerous different impregnation techniques. 
Reported materials used for impregnation are plaster [39], [53]–[60], concrete [61]–
[63], wood [64] and cement [65]. Unfortunately, in the current containment 
technique, particularly Xiao et al. [49] have remarked leakage. 
Micro-encapsulation 
By enclosing the PCM in a microscopic polymer capsule, it is possible to include it in 
the mixture of a building material. The size of the capsules is generally small enough 
in order to prevent from a disproportionate isolation of the solid crust of the PCM. 
No leakages have been reported with this method [65].

Shape-stabilized PCM are prepared from a liquid mixture of the PCM and a

2.2.4 Integrating PCM with Nocturnal Radiant Cooling 
The use of radiative cooling in building has been investigated over the years, with 
specific focus on the limit of the resource and the variations of cooling potential in 
different locations [5]. Nowadays, many building rooftops are covered with 
photovoltaic-thermal panels, for energy and hot water production. However, 
utilization of this system could also effectively provide cooling applications. The 
nocturnal sky can be used as a heat sink, due to the fact that comparing to the 
ambient temperatures, can have up to 30°C lower temperature. Therefore, mainly 
through long-wave radiation, heat can be emitted from a panel facing the sky [6]. 
Nocturnal radiant cooling can be coupled with PCM applications, and actively 
discharge the material during night-time. Within this system, the heat can be 
removed from the PCM by water circulating into pipes embedded in the PCM modules 
and dissipated by means of natural convection and night sky radiation [3]. Several 
studies have researched the integration of these two systems [3], [68], [69].

4.5 
Conclusions 
For a ceiling panel with “pure pcm”, complete melting is generally achieved within 8 
hours, for a panel height of 5 mm. However, in that case water pipes is not possible 
to be implemented. 
PureTemp 23 appears to have the best performance within the PCM tested and it 
also has transition temperature 23°C which has been proven as optimal for office 
applications. However, PCM with phase change at 22°C show promising cooling 
performance and should be furtherly investigated.  
Four pipes with an in-between distance of 115 mm, found to be the optimal number, 
for an effective over-night discharge of the pcm panel, even for double the heat load 
from a two person office. 
Metal fins does not show significant enhancement of the thermal conductivity within 
the PCM, unless a dense metal matrix is constructed. 
Introduction of metal foam in the panel enhances significantly the total thermal 
conductivity. Between aluminum and graphite foam there have not been observed 
notable differences.

As the cooling demands have increased over the last decades, utilization of storage 
technologies becomes ever more essential. Enhancement of the buildings energy 
efficiency is considered one of the most promising solutions, since, according to IEA 
buildings account for over 40% of the primary energy use. Phase change materials 
can have a leading role in the thermal energy storage field, but the currently high 
production costs hinder from widespread utilization. 
Key features of the PCMs that have space for improvement, were targeted from the 
currently existing literature and investigated. 
Specifically the effective thickness of a phase change material, with respect to the 
temperature fluctuations, was examined and compared to a concrete slab with 
different surface finishing. It was found that in general the penetration of room 
temperature fluctuations, decreases exponentially as a function of the structure 
depth. The effective thickness was found to be less than 20 mm for the PCM, while 
for concrete slab it can be up to 250 mm. Specifically, PCM was found able to store 
over the double amount of heat (130 MJ), compared to concrete slab (62 MJ), for a 6 
hour period, when exposed to room drifting temperatures between 20 and 26°C. 
Surface finishing was proved to decrease significantly the temperature penetration 
to the material and therefore prevent from applications like TABS. 
Five different paraffinic PCMs were tested through numerical simulations and 
PureTemp 23, which was used for most of the simulations, was found to perform the 
best.  
There were also investigated through numerical simulations, different mass 
enhancement methods, with respect the ease of production and asses their effect on 
the total thermal conductivity. For an active discharge of the PCM, introduction of 
embedded pipes were examined and a distance of 115 mm between them is proposed 
as safe solution, even though a greater gap could also discharge the pcm. For 
enhancement of the PCM thermal conductivity, introduction of solid mater in forms 
of metal fins and foam was considered. The results indicated foreseeable future as 
10% of metal solid mater can decrease to half the discharge period. Metal fins could 
only considered as enhancement method, only in the case of a dense metal matrix.

From the findings of transient simulations, where a complete solution of a PCM panel 
is implemented in an office room, can be concluded that a 20 mm PCM panel can 
regulate the indoor thermal environment and reduce the energy needs from the 
ventilation system.  
From the Findings in the Chapter 5, it can be concluded that a PCM ceiling panel 
together with the proposed systems could provide sufficient thermal environment. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
53 
 
 
==PAGE:64==
 
 
 
On Chapter 3 it was found from numerical analysis that the effective thickness of 
PCM PureTemp 23 is less than 2 cm. However, evaluation of its thermal properties 
and validation of the results through experimental testing, with for instance the hot 
plate method, is proposed. 
Additionally, the general drawback of paraffinic PCM that hold back their widespread 
use in building applications, is their low thermal conductivity. Therefore a more 
extensive research on enhancement of the component should be prioritized. 
Graphene, which was discovered over the last years, has exceptionally high thermal 
conductivity and it could enhance the PCM without significantly changing its 
physical and thermal properties.  
Investigations regarding the cost optimization of PCM are also necessary, in order to 
have commercially available solutions. Microencapsulation of PCM shows promising 
results, with respect to the thermal performance, but it is still rather expensive. 
Coupling PCM with existing building systems, like Nocturnal radiative cooling, could 
counterweight the high investment costs of the PCM by reducing the operational 
costs. It is so, a potential field for further research.   
Finally, PCM studies are mainly focused in mild (European) climatic conditions. 
Investigation on PCM applications for extreme weather conditions, like arctic or 
desert climate, are rather limited.
