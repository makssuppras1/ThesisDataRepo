Abstract  
Listeria monocytogenes is one of the most common pathogenic bacteria in ready-to-eat (RTE) foods 
and can cause one of the most lethal foodborne diseases, listeriosis. Several food products, including 
fresh vegetables, have been linked to outbreaks of listeriosis. Based on the outbreaks and most 
commonly used leafy vegetables in RTE salads, it has been decided to investigate the growth potential 
of L. monocytogenes in iceberg, rucola, spinach, parsley, kale, heart and romaine lettuce at 5 and 10 
℃ with special focus on differences between cut and intact leaves. The experimental part was divided 
into two parts. First, an IT experimental part was established where generation times of L. 
monocytogenes for different vegetables were estimated from a number of literature studies. Next, a 
challenge test part was accomplished where the growth of L. monocytogenes in different cut and intact 
leafy vegetables at 5 and 10 ℃ was investigated. From the scientific literature the lowest generation 
time was found in asparagus, peas and corn (13-15 h at 5 °C). The generation times for cut spinach, 
kale and iceberg were between 23-37 h at 5 °C, whilst one of the highest generation times was found 
for intact rucola and parsley (63-94 h at 5 °C). Selected vegetables were inoculated with L. 
monocytogenes cocktails with focus on estimating the growth potential (𝛿) and the growth rate (rmax) 
of L. monocytogenes. The results are presented in three sections, pretests, main tests and data 
analyses. Pretests showed that the needed time for incubation of BHI broths, to obtain cultures in the 
late exponential growth phase, was between 60-65 h at 10 °C. Main tests indicated that 𝛿>0.5 log-
increase was observed in all leafy vegetables at 5 and 10 ℃ which means all leaves are classified as 
able to support the growth of L. monocytogenes. The growth potential of L. monocytogenes was 
significantly different in cut and intact leaves since the p-value of a T-test was estimated to 0.024, 
where 𝛿 for intact leaves was significantly lower compared to cut leaves. An effect of the naturally 
associated psychrotrophic bacteria on growth of L. monocytogenes was seen in many of the leafy 
vegetables, where a clear effect was seen in cut and intact kale, since the growth behavior of 
psychrotrophic bacteria and L. monocytogenes was parallel, i.e. increase, decrease and entering into 
stationary phase occurred at the same times. From data analyses, rmax resulted in 14 of the 24 challenge 
tests showing statistically significant growth of L. monocytogenes, where 8 of the 14 challenge tests 
with cut leaves showed significant growth. For intact leaves 6 challenge test showed significant 
growth. The average generation time of L. monocytogenes in cut leaves ranged from 6.8 to 41 h at 10 
℃ and 16 h at 5 ℃ in the challenge test showing significant growth. The average generation time of 
L. monocytogenes for intact leafy vegetables ranged from 9-21 h at 10 ℃ and 11 h at 5 ℃. The 
estimated average generation time in this study was compared with the predicted generation time 
from the scientific literature were iceberg batches and spinach at 10 ℃ was very close to the predicted 
generation time.

Introduction  
Now-a-days, ready-to-eat (RTE) meals have become more popular around the world. Meal salads in 
particular, are a great solution for people living a fast-paced and healthy lifestyle, since they require 
no preparation and contains vegetables that provides important vitamins, minerals, and nutrients 
(Franz et al., 2010). The production and consumption of RTE foods has increased and the varieties 
have expanded significantly in recent years (Mishra et al., 2017). 
RTE foods include a wide variety of food products such as several kinds of mixed salads with meat 
or non-meat, deli salads, smoked/salted seafood, cheeses, sausages and pre-packaged cut fruits and 
vegetables. The safety of RTE foods can be a concern since it is consumed without additional cooking 
or reheating which is an important step that inactivate the pathogenic microorganisms contaminating 
the foods (Franz et al., 2010). Therefore, there is a risk of bacterial growth during the shelf-life of 
RTE foods, which can lead to recalls and/or foodborne diseases (Mishra et al., 2017). One of the most 
common foodborne pathogenic bacteria in refrigerated RTE foods is Listeria monocytogenes as it can 
cause listeriosis, which is one of the most lethal foodborne diseases (Hernandez-Milian et al., 2014). 
RTE foods are available in many canteens, institutions and retail stores and are generally stored at 
chilled temperatures, where L. monocytogenes can survive and grow (Sahu et al., 2017). L. 
monocytogenes is ubiquitous, which means it is found everywhere in the environment e.g. soil, 
surface water, animal feces, feed, wastewater and food processing plants. The European Commission 
has established a critical level for the concentration of L. monocytogenes in RTE foods, including 
RTE salads, that has to be below 100 cfu/g in the entire shelf-life to avoid people being affected (EC, 
2005; EURL-Lm, 2014) 
The increased consumer demand in healthy food, including RTE salads with leafy vegetables, has 
resulted in an increased consumption of fresh produce in Europe and United states (Franz et al., 2010; 
Little et al., 2007). Leafy green vegetables that are consumed raw, such as lettuce, spinach, and 
endive, are the most commonly implicated produce types associated with foodborne disease 
outbreaks, especially disease resulting from L. monocytogenes (Franz et al., 2010; Kyere et al., 2019). 
Thus, foodborne illness associated with fresh produce appears to be increasing in the United States, 
Europe and Australia (Franz et al., 2010; Kyere et al., 2019). For example in 2013-2014, Switzerland 
experienced a nationwide L. monocytogenes outbreak from RTE green salads distributed throughout 
the country via the same retail chain (Stephan et al., 2015). Based on these outbreaks, a deeper 
==PAGE:9==
8 
 
knowledge of the growth potential of L. monocytogenes in different leafy vegetables under different 
conditions such as, temperature and time could provide understanding of how to prevent recalls and 
disease outbreaks of RTE salads. 
The composition of a product has a large impact on the growth of L. monocytogenes for example, the 
normal microbiota of the plant, composition of the product and the availability of plant juice 
depending on the level of cut vs. intact leaves (Ziegler et al., 2019). Vegetables have a chemical and 
physical composition, such as water activity (0.92-0.95) and pH (4.0-9.3), which generally is quite 
favorable for L. monocytogenes growth. Despite this, L. monocytogenes fails to grow in some 
vegetables making it difficult to use water activity and pH as growth predictors (Lokerse et al., 2016) 
as otherwise prescribed in EU regulation as measures to distinguish between growth/no-growth of L. 
monocytogenes in RTE foods (EURL-Lm, 2014).  
Therefore, more information is needed to assess the growth of L. monocytogenes at chilled 
temperatures in terms of cut and intact leaves to predict the safety of the products (EC, 2005). 
Thesis objectives  
According to Lokerse et al. (2016) and Ziegler et al. (2019) have proven that it is not possible to 
assess the growth potential of L. monocytogenes based on pH and aw of vegetables. Therefore, the 
growth potential has to be determined for each kind of vegetable. 
 
The aim of this study is to investigate the growth potential of L. monocytogenes in leafy vegetables 
at chilled temperatures. Since it is expected that cutting of vegetables releases exudates nourishing 
the attached bacteria (Sant’Ana et al., 2012), the study will include both cut and intact leaves as a 
way of quantifying the effects of nutrient availability. Another factor, which will be in focus in this 
study, is the effect of the naturally associated microbiota as many studies have shown that microbiota 
can have an inhibiting effect on growth of L. monocytogenes in vegetables (Sant’Ana et al., 2012; 
Ziegler et al., 2019).

Characteristics of Listeria monocytogenes 
Classification 
The genus Listeria has 17 species and only two of these are pathogenic. Listeria monocytogenes is a 
human and animal pathogen, whereas Listeria ivanovii is an animal pathogen (Orsi et al., 2016). 
Strains of L. monocytogenes are always D-xylose negative and produce lecithinase, and is normally 
𝛽-haemolytic and L-rhamnose positive (EURL-Lm, 2014). The human pathogen L. monocytogenes 
are the causative agent of listeriosis and can be categorized into 13 serotypes where each group of 
serotype has different properties according to their lineages and serotypes. The 13 serotypes are 1/2a, 
1/2b, 1/2c, 3a, 3b, 3c, 4a, 4ab, 4b, 4c, 4d, 4e and 7 (Jamshidi et al., 2019), where the most common 
serotypes causing human disease are 1/2a, 1/2b, 1/2c and 4b (Gray et al., 2004; Vazquez-Boland et 
al., 2001). There are four lines for L. monocytogenes lineages I, II, III and IV. Lineage I include 
serotypes 1/2b, 3b, 3c and 4b and they are clinical isolates and have lower mutation rate. Lineage II 
includes serotypes 1/2a, 1/2c and 3a and they are food isolates and has higher mutability. Lineages 
III and IV contain serotypes 4a, 4b and 4c and they are rare animal sources (Rees et al., 2017). In 
conclusion, it can be said lineages I and II represent the majority of isolates involved in human clinical 
cases, whereas lineage III and IV strains have been found less frequently among humans and are more 
common among animals (Rees et al., 2017). 
 
Reservoir 
L. monocytogenes, also described as ubiquitous, is distributed in natural and man-made environment 
such as; soil, surface water, animal feces, feed, wastewater and food processing plants (Rees et al., 
2017). Approximately 2-6% of healthy people are asymptomatic fecal carriers of the organism 
together with wild and domestic animals, birds, and fish may also carry the organism in their 
gastrointestinal tract (Rees et al., 2017). From different experiments, it has been shown that flies and 
beetles can be contaminated with Listeria spp. and behave as a vector for transmission in farm and 
food production environments (Rees et al., 2017).

General characteristic 
L. monocytogenes are small gram-positive short rods (0.5 – 4 𝜇m in diameter and 0.5 – 2 𝜇m in 
length) (Wang et al., 2013), and has a typical genome size of 2.7-3 mega base (Jay et al., 2005). They 
are facultative anaerobic, non-spore forming, catalase-positive, and oxidase-negative organisms 
(Ajayeoba et al., 2016; EURL-Lm, 2014). Due to peritrichous flagella, Listeria has tumbling motility 
at 20 – 25 °C (Jamshidi et al., 2019) but many Listeria strains are incapable of moving at 37 ℃ due 
to lack of flagellin expression at this temperature (Dortet et al., 2019). 
L. monocytogenes has the capability to grow under many different conditions, including refrigerated 
temperatures, acidic foods, high content of salt in foods and within the host immune system (Gandhi 
et al., 2007). One of the main characteristics of L. monocytogenes is that it is able to grow in the 
temperature range from -1.5 to 45 ℃, where the optimum growth is between 30 ℃ and 37 ℃ (Table 
1). L. monocytogenes is relatively resistant to NaCl and can grow at 10% NaCl and survive at 20–
30% NaCl. The growth of L. monocytogenes will also occur over a wide range of pH conditions (4.6–
9.2) whereas it is relatively easily killed by pasteurization at 60 ℃ (Dortet et al., 2019). 
Listeria's ability to grow at refrigerated temperatures makes it difficult to control in refrigerated foods 
with extended shelf life. Therefore, it is necessary to understand the mechanisms behind its low 
temperature survival and growth as it can help to provide information for developing more effective 
control methods for the pathogen (Gandhi et al., 2007).

Human listeriosis 
L. monocytogenes causes the disease listeriosis in humans where it essentially is transmitted through 
consumption of contaminated food. The infection can also occur during contact with the animals or 
by infection during birth (Rees et al., 2017). Listeriosis is very severe and highly fatal opportunistic 
foodborne infection with a mean mortality rate in humans of 20-30% or higher and listeriosis can be 
treated with antibiotics (Vazquez-Boland et al., 2001). The uncertainty of dose response studies for 
human from animals is unknown but is known to be highly dependent on the host’s immune status 
(Rees et al., 2017). L. monocytogenes in food at a concentration of less than 100 cfu/g is considered 
as a very low probability of causing disease listeriosis, where for healthy adults the concentration of 
L. monocytogenes is considered of no concern if the limit is 1000 cfu/g or less (Rees et al., 2017). 
The risk groups for acquiring listeriosis are pregnant women, neonates, children, elderly people and 
immunocompromised people (Gandhi et al., 2007; Tasara et al., 2006; Vazquez-Boland et al., 2001). 
The symptoms of human listeriosis will usually be fever, muscle pain, headache, fatigue, neurological 
symptoms and gastrointestinal symptoms such as nausea or diarrhea (Tasara et al., 2006; Vazquez-
Boland et al., 2001). Most Listeria infections can go unnoticed, but in some cases, a Listeria infection 
can lead to life-threatening complications, such as meningitis, sepsis and encephalitis (Tasara et al., 
2006; Vazquez-Boland et al., 2001). Meningitis is blood infection or inflammation of the membranes 
and fluid surrounding the brain (Sandstrom, 2007). Sepsis is blood poisoning and can develop into a 
life-threatening condition whereas encephalitis is an infection of the brain tissue (Dou et al., 2020; 
Mello et al., 2016; Sarrazin et al., 2012). Listeriosis in non-pregnant adults can be associated with at 
least one of the following conditions as malignancies, chronic liver disease, kidney disease, diabetes 
or collagen disease (Vazquez-Boland et al., 2001). Some babies can be born with listeriosis if the 
mother ate contaminated food during the pregnancy and was infected (Rees et al., 2017). The mode 
of transmission of Listeria cells to the fetus is either trans-placental via the maternal bloodstream or 
ascending from a colonized genital tract. Infections during pregnancy can cause premature delivery, 
miscarriage, stillbirth, or serious health problems for the newborn (Kovačević et al., 2013; Rees et 
al., 2017; Vazquez-Boland et al., 2001).

Outbreaks 
The number of foodborne outbreaks of L. monocytogenes is low, but still of high concern due to the 
high case-fatality rate. For example, in 2009-2011 the annual incidence was 0.29 case per 100,000 
population in the United States, where the case-fatality rate for listeriosis was 21% (Rees et al., 2017). 
The same applies worldwide as relatively low annual incidences are being reported. According to 
European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) and European Centre for Disease Prevention and Control 
(ECDC) (2018) some outbreaks of L. monocytogenes have shown a significant increase of confirmed 
listeriosis cases in the EU/EEA during the period 2008–2017, where there has been reported 2,480 
invasive human cases of listeriosis, and 227 deaths are reported due to listeriosis in 2017 (EFSA, 
2018). In the European Union 2012, 1,642 cases of listeriosis were registered, where the overall EU 
notification rate was 0.41 cases per 100,000 population (Stephan et al., 2015). The likely source of 
an outbreak of L. monocytogenes that has been affecting Denmark and other countries since 2015 is

Outbreaks according to serovars 
Several food products such as meat, seafood, fruits and vegetables have been linked to outbreaks of 
listeriosis (Rees et al., 2017). Serotypes, such as 1/2c, are often found as food contaminants. Among 
the listeriosis-associated serovars, 4b strains cause over 50% of listeriosis cases worldwide (Rees et 
al., 2017), but strains of antigenic group 1/2 (1/2a, 1/2b, and 1/2c) predominate in food isolates 
(Vazquez-Boland et al., 2001). A survey showed that serovar 4b isolates were found to be responsible 
for major outbreaks of foodborne human listeriosis in California in 1985, Switzerland from 1983 to 
1987, Denmark from 1985 to 1987, and France in 1992 (Vazquez-Boland et al., 2001). The first cases 
of human listeriosis were reported in 1929 in Denmark (Vazquez-Boland et al., 2001). 
 
Outbreaks involving vegetables 
Data from EFSA (EFSA 2011, 2012, 2013, 2014, 2015) have shown significant increasing cases of 
listeriosis between 2009 and 2013 (Chaves et al., 2016). According to EFSA fruits and vegetables are 
considered vehicles for the transmission of these diseases. An increasing number of diseases due to 
transmission of L. monocytogenes by vegetables and RTE salads has been observed (Chaves et al., 
2016). Recent data revealed an increased association of RTE salads including leafy vegetables, lettuce 
and spinach, with foodborne disease outbreaks in Europe and the United States (Chaves et al., 2016). 
This may be related to the use of untreated irrigation water, improper use of organic fertilizers and 
pesticides, and to the possibility of contact of these vegetables with sources of contamination during 
==PAGE:15==
14 
 
harvesting, handling, processing, and packaging (Chaves et al., 2016). In the United States, analysis 
of data from 1998 to 2008 indicates that about 22% of foodborne illness were associated with 
consumption of contaminated leafy greens, where leafy greens were the second most frequent cause 
of hospitalizations (Matthews, 2014). Although, the majority of produce related outbreaks are 
associated with lettuce and spinach, foodborne outbreaks under the heading “leafy greens” encompass 
more than just lettuce and spinach (Matthews, 2014). Leafy green products include romaine lettuce, 
green leaf lettuce, red leaf lettuce, butter lettuce, baby leaf lettuce, escarole, endive, spring mix, 
spinach, cabbage, kale, arugula, and chard (Matthews, 2014). Romaine lettuce have caused several 
recalls of L. monocytogenes in United States in 2016 where a listeriosis outbreak was linked (Shenoy 
et al., 2017). Another study shows also an increase in outbreaks of L. monocytogenes associated with 
leafy vegetables between 1973-2012 (Mishra et al., 2017; Omac et al., 2018). An older study reported 
that there has been an increase in the number of outbreaks of food-borne disease with consumption 
of fresh produce, where 731 cases of human listeriosis have been demonstrated in the United States 
between 1933 and 1966 and some of the cases were associated with contamination of L. 
monocytogenes in iceberg (Beuchat et al., 2004; Beuchat, 1996). 
 
Occurrence in RTE vegetables 
A study has investigated the presence of L. monocytogenes in pre-packed RTE mixed vegetables 
salads, where 99.7% were acceptable for microbiological quality, and out of 99.7%, L. 
monocytogenes was present in two of samples (0.1%) which exceeded 100 cfu/g (Little et al., 2007) 
which means the product exceeded the food safety criteria in Regulation EC No. 2073/2005. The 
result was reported to the food authority, manufacturer and the UK Food Standards Agency were a 
full investigation were undertaken (Little et al., 2007). Many samples were collected from different 
places, where 77.8% were collected from supermarket and 22.2% samples were collected from 
department stores, sandwich shops and other stores (Little et al., 2007). The presence of L. 
monocytogenes in mixed salad from sandwich shops was 8.0%, which was more often compared to 
4.6% samples from supermarkets (Little et al., 2007). The presence of L. monocytogenes in samples 
collected from department stores was 4.2% and 4.6% in samples from other stores (Little et al., 2007). 
Among all the samples 93% of pre-packaged mixed salad were stored at or below 8 ℃, were the rest 
were stored at or below 5 ℃, and it has been observed that present of L. monocytogenes were more 
often in mixed salad samples stored above 8 ℃ (5.7%) (Little et al., 2007). Occurrence of L.

Growth of L. monocytogenes in RTE vegetables 
It has been observed from the study of Sant’Ana et al. (2012) that L. monocytogenes was able to grow 
in all RTE vegetables, except for carrots when partially or fully exposed to abuse temperature (15 °C) 
during shelf-life. The increase in the populations of L. monocytogenes at the end of shelf-life was ≥ 
2 log in collard green, arugula, green salad and watercress and an increase of L. monocytogenes 
population ≤ 2 log was observed in escarole, cabbage, spinach, yakisoba and watercress (Sant’Ana et 
al., 2012). Collard green and arugula has the highest growth potential, while the lowest growth 
potential was observed in cabbage (Sant’Ana et al., 2012). Another study had a purpose to determine 
to what extent different ingredients can contribute to growth of L. monocytogenes in RTE salads at 7 
°C where the results showed that growth inhibition or differences in relative increase in many cases 
could not be explained by aw, pH or presence of competitive microbiota (Lokerse et al., 2016). In 
addition, growth of L. monocytogenes was observed in products that did support the growth of L. 
monocytogenes and in products that did not support growth of L. monocytogenes at pH of 7 (Lokerse 
et al., 2016). The growth characteristics of L. monocytogenes on twelve RTE salads have also been 
investigated by Ziegler et al. (2019) where the experiments had focus on the food matrix, storage time 
and storage at either 5 or 8 °C  which are the factors that have an impact on the growth of L. 
monocytogenes. The results showed that the product group that supported growth of L. 
monocytogenes comprised iceberg lettuce, parsley, arugula, beetroot, garden radish, white cabbage 
and the mixed salads. The maximal growth potential was observed in parsley at 8 °C. There was 
==PAGE:17==
16 
 
difference in the growth of L. monocytogenes for the different vegetables, where four of the twelve 
RTE salads did not support growth of L. monocytogenes in a significant way under the tested 
conditions, while the other eight products supported growth of L. monocytogenes at varying levels 
(Ziegler et al., 2019). In addition, the results are likely affected by changes in the microbiota and 
composition of the salad matrix due to differences in soil composition, farming practices and seasonal 
fluctuations in temperature and humidity. Temperature had also a significance, where there was a 
considerable increase in growth at 8 °C compared to 5 °C. Therefore, the reduction of the storage 
temperature at retail level to 5 °C and product shelf life could help mitigate the risk of L. 
monocytogenes in RTE salads (Ziegler et al., 2019). The study of Omac et al. (2018) support this 
hypothesis that the growth of L. monocytogenes in spinach leaves is temperature dependent.

Some authors Brandl. (2008) and Ells et al. (2006) have reported that growth of L. monocytogenes 
are more prominent when vegetables are cut or shredded during minimal processing but according to

Control of pathogen bacteria in RTE vegetables 
All food business operators in the EU are obliged to implement a food safety management system 
((EF), 2010). According to Official Journal of the European Union (EU, 2016), a food safety 
management system consists of prerequisite programs (PRPs) and procedures based on the Hazard 
analysis, Critical Control Points (HACCP) principles, including the facilitation/flexibility of the 
implementation in certain food businesses. Safety management must be implemented primarily to 
ensure the safety of foodstuffs. The HACCP procedures are based on seven principles, which includes 
hazard analysis and identification of critical control point (CCP) to avoid hazards for the consumer 
(EU, 2016). Monitoring requirements has to be used to control the hazards and corrective action 
should be taken if critical limits are exceeded (EU, 2016). One of the ways to prevent hazards, or 
reduce the likelihood of occurrence to acceptable levels, is implementation of control measures, 
where many preventive control measures are part of PRPs to avoid contamination from the production 
environment (EU, 2016). Other control measures are used for reduction or elimination of hazards that 
are more specific to the particular production such as, pasteurization, fermentation and result in the 
establishment of CCPs or PRPs (EU, 2016). More than one control measure are usually required to 
control the identified hazards e.g. pasteurization controlled by time, temperature and flow rate of the 
fluid (EU, 2016). Food business operators that produce RTE foods which may contain L. 
monocytogenes shall sample the processing areas and equipment for L. monocytogenes as part of their 
sampling scheme (EC, 2005). Several control measures can be used in order to prevent and control 
occurrence and growth of L. monocytogenes. One of the methods to avoid cross-contamination of L. 
monocytogenes is to separate raw materials and RTE foods during the storage and handling 
(Fødevarstyrelsen, 2020). Cleaning is another important control measure to avoid transferring L. 
monocytogenes from equipment and other food contact surfaces to food (Fødevarstyrelsen, 2020). 
Furthermore, control measures to reduce the start concentration of L. monocytogenes, such as rinsing 
==PAGE:21==
20 
 
in electrolyzed water or chlorine, can be used (Stopforth et al., 2008). For controlling the growth of 
L. monocytogenes, factors like temperature, pH, aw and the natural microbiota can be manipulated to 
minimize or even stop growth. For some RTE foods, this is, however, not possible and companies 
use the safety management to investigate shelf life based on the growth of L. monocytogenes where, 
with a short shelf life, there is less risk of the bacterium growing to such a high level that consumers 
become ill from it (Fødevarstyrelsen, 2020). 
 
Temperature as control measure 
One of the most important environmental parameters affecting both food quality and food safety is 
temperature as it has a considerable effect on L. monocytogenes growth and survival dynamic (Marik 
et al., 2020). The control of temperature has been widely used throughout the food industry to limit 
microbial growth, improve quality and/or extend the shelf-life (Marik et al., 2020). It is recommended 
to maintain the fresh vegetables below approximately 5 °C to reduce the proliferation of spoilage 
organisms and human pathogens (Franz et al., 2010; Ziegler et al., 2019). Several studies have 
confirmed that temperature has an influence on the growth of L. monocytogenes in vegetables (Marik 
et al., 2020; Ziegler et al., 2019).  
 
pH and water activity as control measures 
The inhibition for growth of L. monocytogenes by acids is dependent on pH and acidulant type and 
concentration, where the growth of the organism has been discovered between pH 4.0 up to pH 9.3, 
with optimal growth taking place at neutral pH and 0.5% NaCl (Dortet et al., 2019). The lowest water 
activity that allows growth of L. monocytogenes is to some extent dependent on solute and other 
physical conditions where the lower limit is usually reported at or near water activity of 0.92-0.95 
(Rees et al., 2017). pH can be used as control measure by adding lactic acid bacteria or citric acid in 
vegetables to inhibit the growth of L. monocytogenes. Since vegetables are low in proteins and lack 
of buffering capacity it is difficult to control the pH change during the growth of other microorganism 
(Hansen et al., 2000; Jay et al., 2005). Some studies Carrasco et al. (2008) and Rees et al. (2017) have 
confirmed that neutral pH and high water activity have a major impact on RTE salad as it can enhance 
the growth of L. monocytogenes. Vandamm et al. (2013) have also reported that pH and other intrinsic 
factors of fresh cut produce can have a major impact in proliferation at abusive temperatures.  
==PAGE:22==
21 
 
Microbiota as control measure 
The microbiota of vegetables can have an important impact in relation to the growth of L. 
monocytogenes, as it can lead to inhibition of the growth of L. monocytogenes. A study of the 
interactions between L. monocytogenes and the background microbiota in endive leaves has tested 
the effect of disinfecting endive leaves on the fate of L. monocytogenes, and by challenging L. 
monocytogenes along with 10 strains of pseudomonads and Enterobacteriaceae isolated from endive 
leaves (Carlin et al., 1994). The presence of a high population of P. fluorescens inhibited the growth 
of L. monocytogenes, where a better growth of L. monocytogenes occurred if the background 
microbiota on endive leaves were reduced by a chemical disinfection (Carlin et al., 1994). Microbiota 
can be used as control measure by adding the bacteria that inhibit the growth of L. monocytogenes, 
without destroying the quality, appearance, texture and taste of the vegetables. This method is called 
bio-conservation and are used in meat, fish, bread, fruit and vegetables (Hansen et al., 2000; Nilsson 
et al., 2000).  
Regulatory aspects 
Microbiological criteria 
To ensure that the food is not hazardous for the consumer, the European Commission has developed 
the regulation no. 2073/2005 for the microbiological criteria, which describes that “Foodstuffs should 
not contain microorganisms or their toxins or metabolites in quantities that present an unacceptable 
risk for human health” (EC, 2005). Microbiological food safety criteria for microorganisms is set, 
including L. monocytogenes in RTE food products and the criteria must be fulfilled by all food 
business operators. 
The regulation of L. monocytogenes is categorized in two different criteria with three different critical 
limits; food products which are able to support growth and food products which are unable to support 
growth (EC, 2005) (Table 4). Food products, which are unable to support growth, are categorized as 
stabilized products where some requirements should be met to ensure that it is a stabilized product, 
as the product must have the following characteristics; pH ≤4.4 or aw ≤0.92 or a combination of pH 
≤ 5.0 and aw ≤0.94 or shelf life less than 5 days (EURL-Lm, 2014). It has to be documented that a 
concentration of 100 cfu/g is not exceeded at the end of the shelf life (EURL-Lm, 2014). Food 
==PAGE:23==
22 
 
products that are able to support growth are categorized as non-stabilized products and the criteria of 
<100 cfu/g at any time during the product shelf-life must be met (EURL-Lm, 2014).  
The company must apply the second criteria about absence in 25 g (Table 4) if the first prerequisite 
about <100 cfu/g is not met. The food products should be sampled before send to the markets to 
achieve the criteria that L. monocytogenes is not detected in 25 g of the RTE product (EURL-Lm, 
2014).

The methods to prove these criteria can be durability study, predictive modelling and challenge test 
(EC, 2005). Durability studies are based on the shelf life of the food regarding L. monocytogenes in 
a naturally contaminated food during its storage according to reasonably foreseeable conditions 
(EURL-Lm, 2014). According to (EURL-Lm, 2014) predictive modeling is based on pH, aw and 
temperature to obtain the growth probability of L. monocytogenes by using growth/ no growth 
boundary modules, where in this present thesis the predictive modeling are used to estimate max rate 
and generation time from challenge test. Challenge test methods are investigating the growth of L. 
monocytogenes in artificially contaminated food, where the durability studies are considered more 
realistic as the contamination of L. monocytogenes is naturally occurring in the food (EURL-Lm, 
2014). In this present thesis, a challenge test method has been applied.

Challenge tests 
The aim of challenge test is to provide information based on the behavior of L. monocytogenes, which 
have been inoculated into a food under given storage condition. Variability of the batches, food 
samples and strains are taken into account (EURL-Lm, 2014). It is required that the challenge test is 
performed by using conditions, which are most favorable for growth of L. monocytogenes within a 
possible range of each parameter such as pH, aw and temperature. As shown in Figure 2, there are two 
types of challenge test, which can be categorized: a challenge test assessing growth potential and a 
challenge test assessing maximum growth rate (EURL-Lm, 2014).

Predictive microbiology 
Predictive microbiology is important in quantitative food microbiology, as it can facilitate the 
assessment and management of microbial food safety (Dalgaard et al., 2019). The aim of predictive 
microbiology is to develop mathematical models, that quantitatively describe the combined effect of 
important environmental parameters, such as water activity and pH and extrinsic factors including 
temperature, relative humidity, and atmosphere, that can be used to predict microbial growth, survival 
and product shelf-life (Fakruddin et al., 2012). The process of developing such mathematical models 
is called predictive modelling.

Pre-tests 
According to the European Union Reference Laboratory for Listeria monocytogenes (EURL-Lm, 
2014), at least two strains of L. monocytogenes are needed to include differences between the strains 
and their possible variations in growth potential. Four strains of L. monocytogenes were chosen where 
two was from EURL’s List (EURL-Lm, 2014) and two were kindly provided by Associate Professor 
Marianne Halberg Larsen at KU: 
1. LM28A/ 6793, isolated from cut leaves (Larsen et al., 2002) 
2. LM49A/ 7378, isolated from green salad (Larsen et al., 2002) 
3. RefLM49, serotype ½ b, isolated from industrial environment (EURL-Lm, 2014) 
4. RefLM50, serotype 4 b, isolated from industrial environment (EURL-Lm, 2014) 
 
BHI broth were stored at 10 ℃ and optical density (OD) at 600 nm was measured twice in a day with 
6 hours interval for each strain and the growth of all four strains were followed by 10-fold serial 
dilution and spread-plating, as described later, in up to 10 days.  
 
Main test  
Preparation of L. monocytogenes cultures  
At the beginning of the experiment the stock cultures of the three strains were taken from the freezer 
at -80 °C and each strain was streaked on a palcam plate (Sarstedt) and incubated at 37 °C for at least 
2 days. The plate with L. monocytogenes culture were stored on the laboratory table and to maintain 
L. monocytogenes cultures during the whole period of experiment, the three strains (LM28A, LM49A 
and RefLM49) were spread-plated on new palcam plates every second months and incubated at 37 
°C at 2 days. 
 
The L. monocytogenes cultures were prepared in the same way for all the experiments (Figure 3). 
Approx. 5-10 colonies were transferred to 10 ml BHI (Brain heart infusion broth; OXOID, Ref: 
BO1230M) in 15-ml centrifuge tubes (Cellstar, Greiner bio-one, Cat.-No. 188 271). The three 
cultures (LM28A, LM49A, RefLM49) were incubated at 37 °C for 24 h. Afterwards, 10 µl of each 
culture was subcultured in a new 100-ml flask of BHI and incubated at 10 ± 1 °C for approx. 60-65 
hours where approx. 108 cfu/ml of late-exponential culture was expected. Two ml of each subculture 
was mixed into a cocktail in a 15-ml centrifuge tube and then appropriate dilutions were made in 0.9

Preparation of vegetable samples 
Seven different leafy vegetables, iceberg (Lactuca sativa var. capitata), parsley (petroselinum), 
spinach (spinacia), romaine (Lactuca sativa var. langifolia), heart (Lactuca sativa var. langifolia) and 
rucola (Eruca vesicaria) were analyzed as both cut and intact leaves (Klassifisering, n.d.) Mini-
romaine is considered as heart lettuce in this present thesis.  
All leafy vegetables were purchased in their intact form from local supermarkets. It was important to 
ensure the leafy vegetables were washed before starting the experiments and from the labels, it was 
known that parsley, spinach and rucola had already been washed and cleaned and were declared free 
of soil. For iceberg, kale, romaine and heart lettuce the outer leaves were removed, and the remaining 
leaves were carefully separated, washed under running tap water and air-dried on paper towels before 
cut to obtain intact pieces of leaves of 5 x 5 cm weighing 2.1 ± 0.5 g. 
These intact leaves were placed in separate sterile petri-dishes (Sarstedt) with a diameter of 9 cm. 
The cut leaves were cut into long thin pieces and 10 ± 0.5 g was transferred into sterile stomacher 
bags (105 x 155 mm, Seward).  
 
==PAGE:31==

Inoculation of vegetable samples 
The vegetables were inoculated with L. monocytogenes cocktail or saline. For the intact leaves in 
petri-dishes, 20-25 µl of the L. monocytogenes cocktail was added, corresponding to approx. 1 % of 
the samples weight. Using an electronic pipette, 20-25 µl of L. monocytogenes or saline were added 
in 4-5 drops of 5 µl, to have a sufficient distribution of the cocktail on the entire leave. Afterwards, 
they were dried for 1 h under a laminar flow hood. For the samples consisting of 10 g cut leaves, 100 
µl of the L. monocytogenes cocktail or saline were spread onto various spots of the cut leaves in 
stomacher bags and rested at room temperature for 1 h. 
 
Incubation 
Cut and intact samples from all seven vegetables were incubated at 10 °C for up to 15 days. Iceberg, 
romaine and heart lettuce samples were also incubated at 5 °C for up to 20 days. Air temperature of 
incubators was recorded with a temperature logger (Verdict, 2K: T) and an overview of the entire 
experiments can be seen in A-table 2. 
 
Microbiological analyses 
The intact leaves in petri-dishes were transferred with sterile tweezers to stomacher bags (105 x 155 
mm, Seward biomaster 80 cat.no:BA6040) and 0.9% saline was added in the ratio 1:9. For the cut 
leaves, which already were in stomacher bags, 90 mL 0.9% saline was added to achieve the same 
ratio of 1:9. All samples were homogenized in a stomacher apparatus (Laboratory Blender 
STOMACHER® 400, Seward) for two minutes at high speed. Thereafter, 100/200 µl of sample from 
the stomacher bag were surface spread-plated on the plates with an L-shaped drigalski spatula. For 
further dilution of the samples, serial 10-fold dilution rows were prepared by transferring 100 µl to 
900 µl 0.9% saline in 1.5 ml Eppendorf tubes. Mixing of dilution tubes was performed with a vortex 
mixer (VF2, JANKE & KUNKEL IKA®Labortechnik). Then appropriate dilutions of samples were 
surface spread-plated to the agar plates. An overview of the used agar plates for different experiments 
can be seen in A-table 3.  
 
Four different plates were used at different temperature and conditions to obtain different number of 
colonies. Palcam-plates were incubated at 30-37 °C for 2-3 days, where number of L. monocytogenes 
colonies was counted as black halo around colonies (Oxoid, 2020a). TSA-plates were incubated at 
22-23 °C for 2-3 days where naturally occurring psychrotrophic bacteria were counted. MRS-plates

were incubated anaerobically in glass jars with 4 AnaeroGen 2.5 L sachet (Thermo Scientific) at 30 
°C also for 2-3 days and number of lactic acid bacteria was counted. MacConkey-plates were 
incubated at 30-37 °C for 24 hours and E. coli bacteria was counted as red colonies (Oxoid, 2020b), 
then the plates were incubated again at 22-23 °C for further 2 days where all  different colonies were 
counted (Oxoid, 2020b).

Maximum log increase and T-test 
According to Regulation No. 2073/2005 indicates if 𝛿> 0.5 log cfu/g, the food is classified into 
“Ready-to-eat foods able to support the growth of L. monocytogenes” and when 𝛿< 0.5 log cfu/g,

monocytogenes”(EURL-Lm, 2014). Therefore, the max log-increase was calculated to represent the 
growth potential, 𝛿. The maximum log-increase of L. monocytogenes in different cut an intact leafy 
vegetable have been calculated by finding the maximum L. monocytogenes cfu/g where the inoculated 
level is subtracted.  
A T-test has been performed in Software Excel to assess whether there was a significant difference 
of growth of L. monocytogenes between cut and intact leaves. p-value was estimated by choosing the 
T-test function in Software Excel. The max log-increase values were inserted into the matrix and two-
tailed distribution was used, where two- sample equal variance (homoscedatic) was selected for type 
number.  
 
Estimation of growth rate 
Challenge test results for L. monocytogenes were plotted into the DMFit tool at www.ComBase.cc to 
estimate the max growth rate of L. monocytogenes and its standard error (SE). 
In some of the experiments, which did not start with time 0, the inoculation level was applied as a 
theoretical count at time 0 for DMFit to provide an estimate. To obtain the best fit of the curves some 
of the estimated point has been removed (A-table 9). In order to determine how well the DMFit

(Baranyi et al. 1993) growth curve model or a linear model depending on how bacterial counts were 
scattered, and how close the adjusted R squared value was to 1.0 and SE of the Fit to look at the 
scattering of the points from the regression line.

Results 
This section features the results for all the experiments conducted during the course of this project 
including the IT experimental part and challenge tests. At first, the IT experimental part was 
conducted to illustrate and compare growth in various vegetables at different temperature for L. 
monocytogenes and to choose different leafy vegetables for challenge tests. Challenge tests are used 
to investigate if there are any undesirable microorganism, which can grow and survive in food 
products, where the aim of the challenge tests in the present study were to investigate the difference 
between cut and intact leafy vegetables in relation to the growth of L. monocytogenes.

Challenge tests 
From supermarket, bagel stores and canteens it has been observed that leafy vegetables constitute a 
major part of the ingredients in RTE salads and sandwiches in Denmark. In particular, iceberg, rucola 
and spinach are commonly used. From Table 7, it can be seen that leafy vegetables are among those 
with lowest generation time at temperature abuse conditions of 8 ℃. As leafy vegetables, such as 
iceberg, spinach, rucola, kale and romaine lettuce, also previously have been implicated in a number 
==PAGE:39==
38 
 
of  outbreaks (Beuchat et al., 2004; Matthews, 2014; Shenoy et al., 2017), leafy vegetables, as a 
group, have been chosen for the present study. 
Seven different leafy vegetables were selected for further investigation of the growth of L. 
monocytogenes; iceberg, spinach, parsley, rucola, kale, heart and romaine lettuce.  
The challenge tests were divided into three sections; 1) pre-tests with single strains in BHI broth, 2) 
main tests with mixed strains in vegetables and 3) data analyses. The aim of pre-tests was to find the 
late exponential phase for further investigation in relation to growth of L. monocytogenes. The pre-
test included growth curves for the selected L. monocytogenes strains in BHI broth at 10 ℃. The 
purpose of main tests was to investigate the growth potential of L. monocytogenes and other 
microorganisms at 5 and 10 ℃ in relation to cut and intact leaves. Furthermore, it was evaluated 
whether the naturally occurring bacteria in the vegetables affected the growth of L. monocytogenes. 
Lastly, data analyses were conducted where the growth rate of L. monocytogenes was estimated by 
fitting the obtained growth curves of L. monocytogenes using the software DMFit (www.combase.cc) 
and suitable primary predictive growth models.

study and tested for growth in BHI broth at 10 ℃. Firstly, OD600 was measured for each strain to 
identify the late exponential growth phase. Thereafter, the growth of L. monocytogenes was identified 
by spread-plating on blood agar plates and estimating bacterial concentration. Figure 5 illustrates that 
RefLM50 differed from the other three strains as it had the lowest concentration and OD600 during 
the exponential growth phase. Based on the behavior of these four strains, LM28A, LM49A and 
RefLM49, were selected for application in the main tests.

The growth of psychrotrophic bacteria was investigated in order to see the effect of natural indigenous 
microbiota in relation to the growth of L. monocytogenes in cut and intact leaves for the different 
vegetables, i.e. kale, parsley, spinach, heart and romaine lettuce (Figure 12).

monocytogenes over time was almost parallel in both cut and intact kale, as increases, decreases and

a significant increase in the number of psychrotrophic bacteria and L. monocytogenes in the first 3 
days, where after the growth slowed down and appeared to enter the stationary growth phase after 
approx. 10 days. In intact kale leaves, the growth of psychrotrophic bacteria and L. monocytogenes 
appeared to enter stationary growth phase around day 2 to 3 (Figure 12B).

of approx. 9 days. The psychrotrophic bacteria in cut parsley appeared to grow in two laps, starting a 
second growth curves around the same time as L. monocytogenes entered the exponential growth 
phase. In intact parsley (Figure 12D), the growth of psychrotrophic bacteria and L. monocytogenes 
are relatively stable from day 4.

Discussion 
Different methods to define the growth/no-growth of L. monocytogenes 
Two different methods have been used to investigate the growth/no-growth of L. monocytogenes in 
the seven different leafy vegetables at 5 and 10 ℃. One of the methods followed the guidelines 
associated to the Regulation No. 2073/2005 (EURL-Lm, 2014), which indicate that if  the RTE food 
is classified into “Ready-to-eat foods able to support the growth of L. monocytogenes. The results 
from challenge tests of all seven leafy vegetables, both at 5 and 10 ℃, have indicated a growth 
potential of L. monocytogenes in cut and intact leaves since max log-increase was higher than 0.5 log 
cfu/g in all cases. At 10 ℃, the maximum log-increase of L. monocytogenes in all three cut iceberg 
batches was obtained between 1.4 to 2.2 which is consistent with literature. Francis et al. (2001) found 
growth potential of L. monocytogenes in iceberg lettuce at 8 ℃ of approx. 1.5 cfu/g during 0-7 days. 
The study from Carrasco et al. (2008) has shown growth of L. monocytogenes in shredded iceberg 
lettuce (MAP) where an increasing of 3 log cfu/g at 13 ℃ was obtained after 7 days and a total of 
4.85 log cfu/g after 14 days. Lokerse et al. (2016) observed maximum increase of 0.7 log cfu/g in cut 
iceberg lettuce at 7 ℃ which is inconsistent with the obtained results for iceberg batches in this present 
thesis. The possible reason for the lower increase in the study of Lokerse et al. (2016) compared to 
this study may be due to the temperature difference, since the temperature in this study was higher. 
Steinbruegge et al. (1988) reported 2 log cfu/g increasement of L. monocytogenes in cut iceberg 
lettuce after 7 days and 3 log cfu/g after 14 days at 12 ℃. The higher temperature in Steinbruegge et 
al. (1988) can be an explanation to a higher max increase of L. monocytogenes compared to this study. 
However, other studies  have observed no growth of L. monocytogenes in iceberg lettuce at 4 ℃ and 
10 ℃ (Kakiomenou et al., 1998;Oliveira et al., 2012). At 5 ℃, the maximum log-increase for cut 
iceberg was 1.2 log cfu/g after approx. 17 days of storage, which is consistent with (Steinbruegge et 
al., 1988) that has observed 1 log cfu/g growth of L. monocytogenes after 14 days at 5 ℃, and (Koseki 
et al., 2005) that has obtained 1.2 log-increase at 5 ℃ on RTE cut iceberg lettuce.  
The other method, which has been used in this study, to investigate growth/no-growth of L. 
monocytogenes, is the estimation of rmax from the growth curves obtained in the challenge tests and 
assessing whether the rmax is statistically significant from zero. Using this method, results indicated 
that 14 of the 24 challenge tests showed a significant growth.  
The two methods, maximum log-increase and significant rmax can be used in different aspect, e.g. the 
rmax is considered more robust as more information are included, while maximum log-increase 
appears easier to estimate. One of the main advantages of a challenge test, assessing growth potential, 
==PAGE:56==
55 
 
is that the lag phase are related to the temperature, where the stress or adaption could be applied to 
the cells and influence the lag phase (EURL-Lm, 2014). The disadvantage of challenge test, assessing 
growth potential, is the lack of flexibility in the explanation, as the result is only valid for the tested 
product under specific conditions which means a new test has to performed each time there is a change 
(EURL-Lm, 2014). In this present thesis the max log-increase has been estimated differently 
compared to the protocol (EURL-Lm, 2014), the growth potential (d) is the difference between the 
log cfu/g at the end of the test and the log cfu/g at the beginning of the test according to (EURL-Lm, 
2014), whilst the maximum log-increase (cfu/g) in this study is calculated by finding the maximum 
value of L. monocytogenes (cfu/g) and the inoculated level is subtracted, which extend to a higher 
value of maximum log-increase. 
The pros of challenge test, evaluating maximum growth rate, is the flexibility as it makes it possible 
to examine an rmax at one temperature to predict rmax values at other temperatures, which are less than 
25 ℃, in the same food (EURL-Lm, 2014). The disadvantages are that lag time and stationary phase 
are not taken into account when predicting growth potential during shelf life based on rmax (EURL-
Lm, 2014). The drawback of the estimation of rmax in the present study was, that only the beginning 
of the growth curve was taken into consideration to investigate the description L. monocytogenes with 
focus on the effect of vegetable itself and storage temperature. By using the rmax method, the growth 
potential of L. monocytogenes can exaggerate as the effect of microbiota is not taken into 
consideration and this can lead to a shorter shelf life if the microbiota has an affect on the growth of 
L. monocytogenes. Since the applied growth model provide only the description of a single 
microorganism, L. monocytogenes, it is recommended to use another growth model which include 
the effect of microbial interactions on the growth of L. monocytogenes. 
These two methods, which have been used in this study to investigate the growth potential of L. 
monocytogenes, have led to different results regarding the evaluation of growth/no-growth and 
classification of the particular RTE vegetables in accordance to Regulation no. 2073/2005.  
In the present thesis the estimation of rmax is considered to be more reliable compared to the results 
of maximum log-increase, since rmax involves more information compared to maximum log-increase.

The average generation time of L. monocytogenes in this study (Table 13) has indicated a generation 
time between 16 to 26 h in cut iceberg I and III which is close to the predicted generation time of L.

monocytogenes of 5 to 17 h at 10 ℃ by estimating the lowest and highest value at 10 ℃  in Table 7. 
The average generation time of L. monocytogenes obtained for cut spinach 8.3 h 10 ℃ in this study 
(Table 13) is consistent with other studies for cut spinach, i.e. 10 h at 10 ℃ (Table 7). Generation 
time in intact rucola are estimated to be 9 h at 10 ℃, which is very far from the generation time 43 h 
found for literature data (Table 7). This may be due to arugula in the study of Sant’Ana et al. (2012) 
being examined under modified atmosphere, since the increased CO2 in the modified atmosphere 
packaging (Francis 2001) has been found to inhibit microbial growth (Caillet et al., 2006; Carrasco 
et al., 2008). Heart lettuce at 5 ℃ in the present study has resulted in faster growth on intact leaves 
compared to cut leaves which was not as expected. A reason for this can be some laboratory errors 
as some of the agar plates used for investigating growth in cut leaves were outdated. Therefore, it is 
important to repeat the experiment for verification of the result. Overall, the average generation time 
in this study varied for different leafy vegetables and this can be due to the differences in nutritional 
content, in naturally occurring antimicrobial compounds or in pH. For example spinach had one of 
the lowest generation times in this study and this can be due to spinach (Frida food, 2020a) containing 
higher amount of proteins and amino acids compared to iceberg (Frida food, 2020b) and also has a 
neutral pH, since these factors can be quite favorable for the survival and growth of L. monocytogenes 
(Rees et al., 2017).  
Asparagus, corn and peas have the lowest predicted generation times (Table 7) among the collected 
literature data. Common for these vegetables are that they have received a heat treatment, such as 
pasteurization or blanched before packing or freezing. Pasteurization and blanching conditions vary 
with temperatures generally between 75 ℃ and 105 ℃  (Mazzotta, 2001) and it is known that removes 
microbiota, which can have an impact on the growth potential of L. monocytogenes (Beuchat, 1996). 
Normally, corn and peas are high in nutrients, especially corn contains 5.7 g/ 100 g sugar (“Frida 
food,” 2020c) and peas contain 6 g/100 g protein (“Frida food,” 2020d), which has a significant 
influence for survival and growth bacteria, including L. monocytogenes. The pH for corn is normally 
between 5.9-7.5 where Kataoka et al. (2017) obtained pH 7.2, which is favorable for the growth of L. 
monocytogenes and that can be an explanation of the low generation time. Overall, the predicted 
generation times of vegetables in general ranged from 6.0 to 45 h at 10 ℃ which is consistent with 
the average generation times in leafy vegetables from 6.8 to 41 h at 10 ℃ in the present study. 
This indicate that leafy vegetables cannot be considered a subgroup of vegetables with the same 
growth potential of L. monocytogenes. However, if the generation time, observed in the uncertain

challenge test for cut heart lettuce is excluded, the range of generation times is narrowed to 6.8 to 26 
h at 10 ℃ for the leafy vegetables. 
 
The growth of L. monocytogenes in cut and intact leaves 
All batches of iceberg (Figure 7) and rucola (Figure 9) indicated that cut leaves have a greater growth 
potential of L. monocytogenes compared to intact leaves. This can be due to L. monocytogenes is 
more likely to grow in cut vegetables, because cutting gives rise to more surfaces for attachment and 
possible penetration of bacteria resulting in better survival and growth on the vegetables, since cutting 
causes surface damage, cell disruption and nutrient leakage in vegetables (Gleeson et al., 2005). 
According to Gleeson et al., (2005) and Likotrafiti et al. (2013) the availability of nutrients and 
moisture is also important for bacterial growth potential on the injured surfaces of vegetables.  
More fluctuations of the bacterial counts were also seen for all intact leaves compared to cut leaves. 
This may be due to larger variation of intrinsic parameters among the pieces of intact leaves 
depending on where the pieces are cut from the intact leaves, since other studies (Likotrafiti et al., 
2013; Marik et al., 2020; Vandamm et al., 2013) describe that the nutrient availability on the surface 
of raw materials varies and can affect the population of L. monocytogenes. 
The maximum log-increases of L. monocytogenes were generally higher in cut leaves compared to 
intact leaves, this may be caused by a higher nutritional availability in the cut leaves as the nutrients 
are released by cutting, making them more uniform as a growth substrate compared to intact leaves 
(Ziegler et al., 2019). Nevertheless, the maximum log-increase for heart salad at 5 ℃ has resulted in 
higher maximum log-increase in intact compared to cut. This may, however, be due to a laboratory 
error as some of the agar plates used for investigating growth in cut leaves were outdated and in order 
to be able to comment on the result, the experiment should be repeated.  
The results of 95 % confidence intervals for rmax of L. monocytogenes in different leafy vegetables 
indicated nonsignificant difference between cut and intact leaves for individual batches of leafy 
vegetables (Table 12). In addition, L. monocytogenes grew more or less with the same rate across all 
cut and intact leaves, except for cut kale and cut and intact rucola I that, where rmax was significantly 
higher than cut heart lettuce and iceberg I (Figure 14). Furthermore, there appears to be a tendency 
that the darker green leaves had a higher growth rate compared to the lighter green leaves (Figure 
14). 
However, there might be no difference in the growth rate but perhaps growth in the intact leaves stops 
earlier than in cut leaves, because the availability to nutrients is less in intact leaves compared to cut. 
==PAGE:59==
58 
 
To evaluate this hypothesis the maximum log-increase for intact leaves should be significantly lower 
than for cut leaves. Using a T-test performed in excel, there was a significant difference in maximum 
log-increase of L. monocytogenes in cut and intact leaves. The p-value obtained from the T-test was 
0.024 which indicated that p <0.05 and there is a significant difference between cut and intact and 
intact is significantly lower. 
 
 
Effect of microbiota 
The microbiota of vegetables consists mainly of pseudomonads, Enterobacteriaceae and lactic acid 
bacteria (Francis et al., 2001), where in this present thesis lactic acid bacteria and Enterobacteriaceae 
have been examined to investigate the effect of background microbiota in relation to growth of L. 
monocytogenes. Several studies (Carlin et al., 1996; Francis et al., 1998; Omac et al., 2018) have 
indicated that natural microbiota may affect the growth of L. monocytogenes, therefore, it is very 
important when examining the growth of L. monocytogenes in raw foods that natural microbiota is 
taken into consideration. 
The results for MRS plates indicated that lactobacillus was not the dominant microbiota in the 
psychrotrophic bacteria since the colonies from MRS plate proved to be L. monocytogenes. The result 
for MacConkey plates in rucola II and iceberg III at 10  ℃ indicated that a major part of 
psychrotrophic bacteria belonged to Enterobacteriaceae (Figure 8E, F, Figure 10C and D).  
In the present study, some observations regarding the effect of the natural microbiota have been 
detected. For example, some results suggested a Jameson effect where the growth of L. 
monocytogenes stops when concentration of psychrotrophic bacteria reach their maximum growth 
(Møller et al., 2013; Omac et al., 2018). This applies for cut iceberg and parsley (Figure 8 and 12C) 
since the growth behavior of L. monocytogenes and psychrotrophic bacteria were parallel and their 
growth stopped at the same time. Modified Jameson effect was seen in cut kale (Figure 12A), where 
L. monocytogenes continued to grow, but with a different growth rate, when the growth of 
psychrotrophic bacteria stopped (Møller et al., 2013).  
In this present thesis, all vegetables were inoculated with L. monocytogenes cultures in the late 
exponential growth phase. This has been done to avoid lag phase in the challenge tests as that lag 
phase duration is dependent on many factors and is difficult to predict from real-life situations. 
Overall, results showed that this was achieved, with cut parsley as the only exception (Figure 12C). 
As there was no sign that the L. monocytogenes cocktail inoculated onto parsley was different, other 
==PAGE:60==
59 
 
reasons have to be sought. A reparation phase was obtained for intact parsley and spinach (Figure 
12D and F) and this can be due to the samples being dried before incubation. Drying can have an 
effect on L. monocytogenes as the bacterial cells can be damaged and unable to form colonies on 
palcam agar-plates. When spinach also contains the organic acid oxalic acid, drying becomes an 
additional stress for L. monocytogenes to overcome before it can multiply and form colonies on 
palcam agar-plates where there are some components which are selective and toxic for L. 
monocytogenes (Anang et al., 2015). An important factor, which can influence the growth of L. 
monocytogenes, is the pH. In the present thesis, this can be seen for rucola batches as the highest rmax 
(Table 12) and highest initial pH-value of 7 (Table 9) are obtained in rucola I (Figure 10). Other 
researchers have observed that a rise of pH during storage of RTE salads, enhanced the growth 
potential of L. monocytogenes (Janickova et al., 2019). In those products, the psychrotrophic bacteria 
appeared to grow in two laps, as also observed for cut parsley and romaine lettuce (Figure 12C and 
I) in the present study. As the second lap of growth of the psychrotrophic bacteria coincided with a 
change in the growth of L. monocytogenes, this could be due to some microorganism, such as 
pseudomonas, impacting the rise in the pH and leading to an increase of the growth of L. 
monocytogenes.

Conclusion 
The aim of this present thesis was to investigate the growth potential of L. monocytogenes in different 
cut and intact leafy vegetables at 5 and 10 ℃. Seven different leafy vegetables, iceberg, spinach, 
parsley, kale, rucola, heart and romaine lettuce were chosen based on most commonly used leafy 
vegetables in RTE salad and some outbreaks associated with L. monocytogenes. From the pretests, it 
can be concluded that the strain RefLM50 differed from other three strains. In addition, the needed 
time, for incubation of L. monocytogenes cultures in BHI broth at 10 ℃ to obtain late exponential 
cells, was between 60-65 h. The challenge tests suggested that all seven leaves were able to support 
the growth of L. monocytogenes, since 𝛿>0.5 for all cut and intact leaves. A significantly (p=0.0024) 
higher growth potential of L. monocytogenes was observed in cut leaves compared to intact leaves. 
This might be explained by a higher nutrient availability in cut leaves, as nutrients are released by 
cutting making them more uniformly available for the bacteria present on the leaves. The results from 
the estimated growth rates for L. monocytogenes in cut and intact leaves have indicated that growth 
rates more often were statistically significant from zero in cut leaves compared to intact leaves 
independent of storage at 5 or 10 ℃. The obtained average generation times for cut and intact leafy 
vegetables, with significant growth ranged from 6.8 to 41 h at 10 ℃ and from 11 to 16 h at 5 ℃. In 
addition, cut spinach at 10 ℃ showed the fastest growth of L. monocytogenes, where in intact leaves 
at 10 ℃, growth was fastest for rucola. In some of the leafy vegetables, including cut iceberg and 
parsley, a microbial interaction like the Jameson effect was detected, where modified Jameson effect 
was seen in cut kale.

Further consideration 
In this current study, the obtained results have indicated that there may be some kind of effect of the 
associated microbiota on the growth potential of L. monocytogenes. Therefore, it would be interesting 
to investigate to what extent the associated microbiota inhibits the growth of L. monocytogenes in 
different cut and intact leafy vegetables. This would be useful for the development of more accurate 
mathematical models for predicting growth of L. monocytogenes in RTE vegetables.    
 
In this present thesis, the pH for all cut and intact leafy vegetables was only measured at day 0, except 
for rucola II and iceberg III. In order to be able to know the effect of pH on the growth of L. 
monocytogenes in leafy vegetables, it would be relevant to measure the pH change during the 
incubation period and relate it the growth behavior of L. monocytogenes.
