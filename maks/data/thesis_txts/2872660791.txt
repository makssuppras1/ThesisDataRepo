Abstract
Despite e-mail’s ubiquity - 4.2 billion users sending 347.3 billion mes-
sages every day - its core functionality has changed little since the medium’s
inception, creating persistent productivity bottlenecks. This study investi-
gates how large-language-model (LLM)-driven, agentic workflows can recon-
ceptualise the e-mail client as an integrated workflow hub.
Across three
phases-exploration, implementation, and evaluation - we examined how
AI can reduce the 23 % of the workday that knowledge workers typically de-
vote to e-mail.
In an observational field study (n = 20) we found that complex mes-
sages (14 % of volume) accounted for 38 % of handling time. Agents
switched applications 8–12 times per case, and 75 % of participants manu-
ally copied information between disparate systems. Guided by these findings,
we built a prototype featuring (1) automatic summarisation, (2) retrieval-
augmented knowledge-base integration, (3) Model Context Protocol connec-
tors to enterprise systems, (4) context-aware response generation, and (5) a
chat-centric user interface.
A subsequent evaluation with customer-service professionals (n = 12)
indicated substantial time-saving potential by reducing context switching
and accelerating information retrieval. Participants reported moderate
trust in AI-generated drafts for routine enquiries but consistently
favoured human oversight - especially for emotionally nuanced or
high-stakes messages - highlighting the need for a tiered deploy-
ment model.
This work offers three contributions: (i) a concrete re-imagining of the
e-mail interface around intelligent assistance, (ii) a demonstration of how
open protocols can enable deep cross-system integration without compro-
mising existing IT boundaries, and (iii) empirical insights into trust and
adoption factors for AI-assisted professional communication. While limited
by data-privacy constraints, our findings suggest that LLM-based agentic
workflows can transform e-mail from a siloed communication tool into an
integrated workflow catalyst, addressing a long-standing productivity pain
point in modern business environments.

Introduction and Motivation
1.1
Context and background
Email has been a cornerstone of business communication for decades.
The first network email was sent in 1971 by Ray Tomlinson in a simple test
message [1]. Since then, email has grown exponentially to become a ubiqui-
tous tool: as of 2023 there are over 4.2 billion email users worldwide, and an
estimated 347 billion emails are sent and received each day [2]. This trajec-
tory solidified email’s role as the default medium for professional correspon-
dence, information exchange, and record-keeping. Even with the emergence
of new communication platforms, email remains indispensable - it constitutes
roughly 31% of workplace communication, slightly edging out instant mes-
saging platforms in share [3]. In short, email is deeply entrenched in how
organizations operate and collaborate on a daily basis.
However, while the volume and importance of email have grown, innova-
tion in email workflow and user experience has largely stagnated. The basic
model of email - drafting messages, manually sorting and reading an inbox,
and replying thread by thread - remains much the same today as it was in
the last century. Modern email clients like Outlook or Gmail have intro-
duced improvements (e.g. better search, filtering, mobile access), but these
are incremental enhancements on an old paradigm. The underlying format
and protocols (SMTP/IMAP) are essentially unchanged, and users still bear
the brunt of organizing and processing information. As one observer noted,
"email has not changed much conceptually" in decades [4]. This stagnation
has led to well-known pain points: professionals face overflowing inboxes,
long reply-all threads that are hard to follow, and important details easily
lost in the deluge. In effect, the burden of managing and making sense of
email falls largely on the individual, with relatively little machine assistance
beyond spam filters and rudimentary priority markers.
In parallel, the last few years have seen a revolution in AI text generation
and summarization technologies. Advances in natural language processing -
particularly with transformer-based models - now enable computers to gen-
erate human-like text and understand context at an unprecedented level. A
prominent example is OpenAI’s ChatGPT, which reached 100 million users
just two months after its launch in late 2022 [5], a testament to the main-
streaming of AI’s language capabilities. These AI systems can produce co-
herent paragraphs, answer questions, and even draft emails or reports with
minimal human input [5]. Equally relevant is the progress in automatic text

summarization. AI summarization tools can condense large volumes of text
into concise summaries that preserve key points [6]. For instance, given a
lengthy email thread or a dense document, modern algorithms can extract
or abstract the most important information into a short "TL;DR" version.
This capability is critical in handling information overload, as it allows users
to grasp the essence without reading every word. In short, the technology
now exists to parse long communications and generate new text (such as
summaries or replies) that closely mimic what a competent human assistant
might produce.
These parallel trends - email’s persistent importance and AI’s rapid mat-
uration - suggest an opportunity to radically improve the email experience.
By combining email with AI through a chat-based assistant interface, we can
transform how professionals interact with their inbox. Instead of a static list
of messages that must be individually read and managed, one can envision
the email client as an interactive assistant. A user might converse with this
assistant to triage emails ("Which messages are most important today?"),
get summaries ("Summarize the thread with subject ’Project Update’"), or
delegate drafting tasks ("Compose a courteous reply thanking the sender
and requesting more details"). Recent experiments support this vision: for
example, new AI-driven email platforms have introduced conversational as-
sistants that can read entire email threads and produce succinct bullet-point
summaries or draft responses autonomously [7]. By layering such an AI onto
email, the reading and writing process becomes far more efficient - routine
emails can be scanned or answered in seconds, and the user’s cognitive load
is lightened. In essence, the stagnant email interface is augmented with a
dynamic, intelligent co-pilot.
1.2
Relevance and Significance
The need for innovative agentic email workflow is underscored by the sheer
amount of time and effort professionals currently spend on email. Numerous
studies have quantified how email dominates the workday. On average, of-
fice workers receive on the order of 100-120 emails per day [8], and handling
this influx consumes a significant portion of their working time.
One re-
cent summary indicates that employees spend about 23% of the workday on
email-related tasks [2]. Over a full week, this amounts to roughly 11 hours
dedicated just to email management [9] - the equivalent of nearly 25 full
workdays per year spent reading and writing emails. Not only average fig-
ures are concerning; extreme cases are common as well. Surveys have found
that over a third of U.S. workers (35%) spend between two and five hours per

This persistent “email overload” has tangible effects on employees: 38% of
office workers report that email fatigue is severe enough that it could cause
them to consider quitting their jobs [9]. The communication burden has be-
come so high that it impacts job satisfaction and retention, especially among
younger professionals who often feel perpetually behind on correspondence.
The productivity cost of inefficient email practices is enormous. Every
minute spent sorting through irrelevant messages or mentally refocusing after
an interruption carries a business cost. Research shows that frequent email
interruptions not only eat up time during the interruption, but also incur
significant switching costs - one study noted employees spend over 90 min-

drain productivity. About 14.5 billion spam emails are sent per day, and the

in lost productivity [11]. In sum, the opportunity cost of current email habits
- measured in hours of lost productive time and direct monetary terms - is
staggeringly high. This presents a clear business case for any solution that
can streamline email handling: even a modest improvement could translate
into saving millions of work hours across an organization.
AI-powered automation offers a compelling path to recapture this lost
productivity. If routine email tasks can be accelerated or offloaded to an in-
telligent assistant, professionals can redirect their time to higher-value activi-
ties. For example, automatic summarization can compress a multi-paragraph
email or a long thread into a few key bullet points, potentially saving each
recipient several minutes. Multiplied by dozens of emails, an email summa-
rizer could save hours per week for a single employee. Similarly, an AI that
can draft an email reply (even if the human only uses it as a starting point)
might shorten the composition process from, say, 10 minutes to 2 minutes.
Early indications from AI deployments are promising - some AI email assis-
tant tools claim productivity boosts on the order of tens of percentage points
in email throughput [7].
While such figures need careful validation, they
hint at the scale of improvement possible. Beyond raw time savings, there
is also a quality and consistency benefit: an AI assistant can ensure that
important emails are not overlooked, that responses maintain a professional
tone and include all relevant information (pulled automatically from records
if needed), and that follow-ups happen promptly. In effect, AI can help in-

Crucially, this approach democratizes the personal assistant experience.
In traditional settings, only high-level executives or those with significant re-
sources could employ personal assistants to manage communications. For the
vast majority of professionals, however, such support has been out of reach –
they must handle all their emails on their own, no matter how overwhelming
the volume. AI changes this equation. An AI email assistant can act as

lowering the barrier to getting assistance, AI-driven email clients can help
a much broader segment of the workforce handle complex communications
effectively. In summary, the convergence of a pressing productivity problem
(email overload) and a nascent technological solution (AI text generation and
understanding) makes the case for LLM-based email assistants compelling.
The potential gains – in saved time, reduced stress, and improved commu-
nication efficiency – represent a strong motivation for bringing AI into the
email domain.
1.3
Objectives and Scope
The overarching goal of this project is to build an LLM-powered email
client. This research aims to explore how LLM-based agentic workflow can
transform email management by addressing common pain points and enhanc-
ing productivity through:
• Intelligent Email Processing: Leveraging AI capabilities to help
users manage their inbox more efficiently and extract key information
from large volumes of messages.
• Context-Aware Assistance: Exploring how AI can understand and
utilize relevant information to provide more meaningful email interac-
tions.
• Flexible System Architecture: Developing a foundation that can
accommodate various enhancements and extensions as AI capabilities
evolve.
Page 4 of 57
==PAGE:9==
• Natural Language Interaction: Creating intuitive interfaces that
allow users to accomplish email tasks through conversational prompts.
The scope of this project is limited to business settings where employees
handle large volumes of emails requiring complex, multi-step processing. This
focus allows for targeted optimization in environments where the return on
investment is likely to be highest. While the technology could potentially
benefit personal email management, the research will evaluate primarily in
professional contexts where email overload presents significant productivity
challenges.

The Importance of Email Productivity
Professionals today spend a significant portion of their workday dealing
with email.
Recent studies indicate that office workers commonly devote
around 5–10 hours per week to email correspondence, equating to roughly
1–2 hours per workday [13]. In fact, one survey found that 35% of employees

==PAGE:12==

spend between 2 and 5 hours every day in their inboxes.
[10]. This time includes reading, writing, and organizing messages – a
substantial commitment that eats into the work week. Email remains a dom-
inant communication tool in business, so these hours are often unavoidable.
However, the cumulative time sink of email can directly impact productivity
and focus. Beyond just the raw hours, there is a hidden productivity cost due
to constant context switching between email and other applications. Knowl-
edge workers frequently must jump from an email thread to internal systems
– for example, opening a CRM to look up client data, or checking a project
management app after reading a task request. Each such context switch car-
ries a cognitive penalty. A Harvard Business Review study quantified this
effect: employees toggled between applications nearly 1,200 times per day,
resulting in about 4 hours per week lost merely to reorienting themselves
after each switch [15]. Over a year, that amounts to five working weeks of
productivity drained just from tool switching overhead [15]. The constant
interruption of thought processes – reading an email, then searching for infor-
mation elsewhere, then returning to the email – fragments employee attention
and contributes to mental fatigue [15]. In summary, email is both essential
and time-consuming: it occupies a large chunk of the workday, and the in-
efficient workflow of switching between email and other enterprise systems
exacerbates productivity loss. This has driven interest in solutions that can
make email handling more efficient and reduce the need to leave the inbox
for routine lookups and updates.
2.2
AI in Communication Tools
In recent years, email and chat platforms have begun integrating AI-
powered features to boost user productivity. For example, Google’s Gmail
has introduced tools like Smart Compose and Help Me Write that use gener-
ative AI (large language models) to suggest phrases or even draft entire email
responses [18]. These features leverage patterns learned from vast datasets to
predict likely continuations of a sentence, allowing users to compose messages
faster. Gmail also offers Smart Reply, which provides one-click short replies
generated by AI based on email context. Likewise, in team communication
tools such as Slack, AI has been employed to manage information overload.
Slack’s built-in AI (launched in 2024) can automatically summarize lengthy
message threads and channel discussions, giving users a concise recap of what
was said and by whom [19]. It even allows users to ask questions in natural
language – for example, “What happened on the project while I was out?” –
and then generates an answer by pulling information from relevant messages
in Slack [19]. These capabilities demonstrate how modern AI can serve as an
Page 8 of 57
==PAGE:13==
2.2

intelligent assistant, parsing and generating text to save users time.
However, current AI integrations in email and chat clients have notable
limitations, especially regarding integration with broader enterprise systems.
Gmail’s and Slack’s AI features primarily operate on the data within their
own platforms (emails and chat messages respectively) and do not directly
connect to a company’s internal databases or business applications. As An-
thropic (the developer of the Claude LLM) observes, even the most advanced
AI models are often “constrained by their isolation from data – trapped be-
hind information silos and legacy systems” [22]. For instance, Gmail’s AI
might help draft a reply, but it cannot automatically look up the status of an
order in an ERP system or update a customer record in Salesforce. Slack’s
AI can summarize conversations and, in some cases, integrate with certain
linked apps (e.g. providing a summary of a Notion document when a link is
shared in a channel) [19]. Yet these are typically shallow integrations. They
don’t enable the AI to perform multi-step transactions in enterprise software
(such as querying a database, then using that result to take an action) –
such tasks still require users to manually switch to those systems or rely on
custom integrations.
Despite these limitations, the underlying large language model (LLM)
technology in tools like Gmail and Slack is quite powerful in its domain.
LLMs excel at text generation and understanding, enabling features like
composing fluent emails, suggesting contextually appropriate replies, and
extracting key points from discussions. Crucially, the functionality of LLMs
can be extended when they are connected to external data and tools. One
emerging approach to achieve this is via the Model Context Protocol (MCP),
which will be discussed in the next section. By interfacing an LLM with en-
terprise systems through a standardized protocol, the AI is no longer limited
to just the text it was trained on or the content of a single email or chat
thread. It can be empowered to retrieve up-to-date information or execute
actions in other applications on behalf of the user.
A related foundational technology here is retrieval-augmented generation
(RAG). RAG is a framework that combines LLMs with real-time information
retrieval, usually from a company’s knowledge bases or databases. Instead
of relying solely on the static knowledge within an AI model, a RAG system
will search for relevant data (documents, records, etc.) and feed that into the
model’s context when generating a response. This yields more accurate and
enterprise-specific outputs. In essence, “RAG augments a Large Language
Model with fresh, trusted data retrieved from authoritative internal knowl-

edge bases and enterprise systems,” reducing hallucinations and improving
relevance [20]. For example, if an employee asks an AI assistant (in email or
chat) a question about a company policy or product data, a RAG-enabled
assistant could pull the latest answer from the internal wiki or database and
include it in its response. Many modern enterprise AI assistants – includ-
ing those in Slack and Microsoft Teams – use this technique: Slack’s own
implementation notes that all needed context (like the messages to summa-
rize) are provided to the model at query time, ensuring the model’s output is
grounded in the company’s data [17]. Thus, RAG and similar strategies are
pivotal for bridging LLMs with external data sources. Still, to fully integrate
such capabilities into everyday email workflows, a more structured approach
like MCP is required, as it standardizes how an AI can securely interface
with a variety of enterprise systems.
2.3
Model Context Protocol Integration
Model Context Protocol (MCP) has emerged as a promising standard to
connect AI assistants with the internal tools and data sources of an enterprise.
Developed by Anthropic in 2024, MCP is described as “an open standard that
connects AI assistants to the systems where data actually lives — content
repositories, business tools, development environments, and more.” [26] In
other words, MCP provides a universal adapter between powerful language
models and the company-specific context those models often lack. The idea
is straightforward but powerful: rather than building a custom integration
for every single application (email, database, CRM, etc.) that an AI needs
to access, one can use MCP as a common interface. This protocol elegantly
handles the flow of data and commands between the AI (the client) and vari-
ous backend services, so developers don’t have to reinvent the wheel for each
new integration [31].
At a high level, MCP follows a client–server architecture [23]. On one
side, you have an AI assistant application acting as an MCP client (for ex-
ample, a chatbot in an email client or a desktop AI app like Claude). On the
other side, you have one or more MCP servers – lightweight connectors that
interface with specific data sources or services. Each MCP server exposes
a standardized set of actions (often called “tools” or APIs) that the AI can
use. For instance, one MCP server might connect to a customer database
and expose a lookupCustomer(name) function, while another connects to a
CRM and exposes functions like createTicket(details). The MCP client (the
AI assistant) can invoke these tools through the protocol in a uniform way,
regardless of the underlying system. This is why MCP is often compared to

app, the MCP client library, and the MCP servers (along with the actual data
sources those servers connect to) [23]. When the AI assistant needs something
– say, to retrieve information or perform an action – it sends a request via
the MCP client to the relevant server. Because MCP is a stateful, two-way
protocol, the assistant can have a multi-step back-and-forth with a server,
maintaining context across those steps [31]. Crucially, MCP was designed
with security and interoperability in mind. Connections are authenticated
and authorized; the protocol includes mechanisms to handle credentials and
usage policies so that an AI agent only accesses data it’s permitted to [31].
Responses from servers are returned in standardized formats (often JSON),
making it easier for the AI to interpret the data consistently.
One of MCP’s strengths is the growing ecosystem of pre-built servers
for common enterprise systems. Anthropic and the open-source community
have already provided connectors for popular services and databases. Upon
launch, there were MCP server implementations for Google Drive (document

Enterprise System Integration Challenges
Integrating AI assistants with enterprise systems through something like
MCP is powerful, but it is not without challenges. Enterprises have a het-
erogeneous mix of technologies – SQL databases, NoSQL data stores, legacy
on-premise systems, modern SaaS platforms, etc. Creating an MCP server for
each system requires understanding that system’s APIs or interfaces. While
many modern apps have REST APIs (which eases integration), legacy sys-
tems might lack easy programmatic access. In such cases, developers may
need to build custom wrappers or use RPA (robotic process automation) tech-
Page 12 of 57
==PAGE:17==
2.4
Enterprise System Integration Challenges
niques, which can be complex and fragile. There’s also the issue of schema
complexity: for an AI to query a database, it must know something about
the database schema (table names, fields, etc.), otherwise queries could fail or
produce errors. Toolkits to automatically teach the AI the schema (perhaps
by providing a schema overview via the MCP server) are needed, adding an-
other layer of complexity to integration design. Security and compliance are
arguably the most critical concerns when extending AI agents into enterprise
systems. By design, MCP opens up write access to internal tools (not just
read access), which means a misconfigured or overly-permissive setup could
allow an AI to, say, modify a database record incorrectly or disclose sensitive
information. Without proper safeguards, an attacker might even try prompt-
injection techniques to trick an AI into performing unauthorized actions. The
importance of robust security in this context is highlighted by the pitfalls of
earlier custom integrations – “inconsistent security implementations across
custom integrations create vulnerabilities that can become extremely dan-
gerous, as attackers can gain access to sensitive data and critical systems.”
[32] MCP attempts to mitigate this by centralizing and standardizing the
security model. For each server, developers can enforce authentication (e.g.
API keys, OAuth tokens) and define access control policies. Every request
the AI makes to an MCP server can be validated against these policies before
execution [25]. For example, an MCP server connected to a database might
be configured only to allow read queries on certain tables, or to redact certain
sensitive fields, regardless of what the AI asks for. The protocol’s standard-
ized nature makes it easier to implement auditing as well – logging every tool
invocation and its result. Even so, organizations must carefully decide which
data and actions to expose to an AI assistant. Many companies operate un-
der strict regulatory regimes (financial data regulations, healthcare privacy
laws, government standards, etc.). These rules may forbid certain data from
being processed in external cloud services or require rigorous logging of any
access. A notable approach to address compliance is to constrain where the
AI model runs and how data is handled. For instance, Slack’s engineering
team, in implementing Slack AI, decided that “customer data never leaves
Slack-controlled VPCs” [34]. They deployed the AI models within their own
AWS infrastructure rather than sending data to an outside API, ensuring
that no third-party could store or learn from their sensitive message data.
In an email context, a company might similarly prefer an on-premises or pri-
vate cloud LLM for handling content from MCP, to keep everything within a
trusted boundary. Additionally, Slack’s solution used the RAG approach in a
stateless manner – they feed relevant message content into the model for each
query (and do not allow the model to retain it thereafter) [34]. This limits
the risk of data leakage because the AI isn’t building a long-term memory

of sensitive info; each interaction is self-contained. Email assistants could
adopt the same pattern: assemble the necessary context (via MCP queries)
at query time, but not let the AI store that context beyond responding to the
user. Another challenge is maintaining data integrity and oversight. When
an AI can execute actions like creating a ticket or updating a record, those
actions should be traceable. MCP servers should ideally log all transactions

forms often have usage limits on their APIs; if an AI agent too aggressively
queries a CRM via MCP, it could hit rate limits or incur additional costs.
Therefore, smart throttling and caching strategies might be required in the
MCP layer to prevent abuse or unintended load. All these technical nuances
mean that adopting MCP in a large enterprise is not just a plug-and-play
install; it involves thoughtful planning of security, compliance, and reliability
aspects. In summary, while MCP provides the framework to connect AI with
enterprise systems, organizations must navigate challenges of integration ef-
fort, security control, and compliance. The protocol itself encourages good
security hygiene – for example, by enabling centralized policy enforcement
(the MCP server “validates [requests] against security and access policies”
before executing them [32]). But the onus is on implementers to define those
policies correctly. Companies will need to decide which data the email AI
assistant can retrieve (perhaps financial data is okay, but personal HR data is
not) and what it can do (maybe create draft entries but not final approvals,
etc.). When these challenges are addressed, the payoff is significant: em-
ployees can interact with multiple systems through a single natural language
interface (their AI assistant), without constantly switching apps, all while
maintaining corporate security standards.

Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG)
To address the challenge of information retrieval across fragmented knowl-
edge sources, we implemented a retrieval-augmented generation system with
the following components:
Our system processes various document formats (PDF, DOCX, PPTX,
TXT, MD) through specialized handlers. For PDFs, we employ a primary
PyPDF2 extractor with Mistral OCR as a fallback mechanism for complex
documents. For Word documents, we utilize the python-docx package to ex-
tract text content, tables, and metadata from DOCX files. PowerPoint pre-
sentations are processed using the python-pptx library, which extracts text
from slides, notes, and shapes while preserving the presentation structure.
For plain text documents (TXT) and Markdown (MD) files, we apply stan-
dard built-in text extraction methods that efficiently handle these simpler
formats. The extracted text from all document types undergoes a two-tier
chunking approach:
1. Header-based Primary Chunking: We use a
MarkdownHeaderTextSplitter to parse document structure along se-
mantic boundaries (H1, H2, H3 headers), preserving the hierarchical
context as metadata.
2. Size-based Secondary Chunking: For content exceeding optimal
embedding length, we apply a RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter with
configurable parameters:
• Secondary chunk size (default: 500 characters)
• Chunk overlap (default: 0-100 characters)
• Intelligent separator hierarchy ("\n\n", "\n", ".", " ")
The flow implements a two-stage filtering process that maximizes both
recall and precision when searching through knowledge bases, ensuring highly
relevant information is provided to users.
1. Query Generation: When a user submits a request through the chat
interface, the agent (Claude) intelligently determines when to lever-
age external knowledge and generates an appropriate query using the
search_knowledge_base tool.
2. Vector Search (First Pass):
The query is embedded using the
VOYAGE-3-Lite embedding model, translating natural language into

==PAGE:35==

the same high-dimensional vector space as our document chunks. Pinecone
then performs an efficient similarity search against our indexed vectors,
returning the top 50 most similar document chunks, optimizing for both
recall and computation speed.
3. Neural Reranking (Second Pass): To refine these results, we ap-
ply VOYAGE-Rerank-2, a specialized cross-attention reranking model
that performs deep semantic matching between the query and each
retrieved chunk. This reranker evaluates the actual relevance of each
candidate chunk rather than just vector similarity, selecting the top 5
most contextually relevant chunks.
4. Context Integration: The final selected chunks, along with their
source metadata for attribution, are formatted and injected into the
LLM’s context window, enabling Claude to generate responses grounded
in the retrieved knowledge.
Reranking Optimization Trade-off We specifically chose a two-stage
retrieval approach (vector search followed by reranking) rather than applying
the reranker to the entire document collection for several critical reasons:
1. Computational Efficiency: Neural rerankers employ intensive cross-
attention mechanisms between query and document pairs, making them
computationally expensive to apply across large document collections.
By first filtering to 50 candidates via vector search, we reduce reranking
computations by orders of magnitude.
2. Latency Requirements: Direct reranking of the entire vector database
would introduce unacceptable latency in response generation, severely
impacting user experience in interactive contexts.
This hybrid approach represents an optimal balance between retrieval
quality and system performance, delivering high-quality contextual informa-
tion to the agent while maintaining responsive user interactions.

Sample Size and Generalizability: The observational study was con-
ducted at a single, mid-sized company (Modstrøm) with 20 participants in
customer service roles. The prototype evaluation involved 12 professionals.
These sample sizes are relatively small and may not fully represent the di-
versity of email usage patterns across different industries, company sizes, or
job functions. The findings, particularly regarding specific time distributions
and workflow patterns, might not be directly generalizable without further
research in varied contexts.
Evaluation Methodology: The evaluation relied on demonstrations
and interviews assessing expected benefits and user trust based on short-
term interaction. It did not involve long-term deployment and measurement
of actual productivity gains or error rate reductions in a real-world setting.
System Actions: The current prototype, particularly its MCP integra-
tion, primarily focuses on read-only actions – retrieving information from
external systems. While it can technically perform write actions that modify
data in external systems (e.g., updating a CRM record, creating a support
ticket), it is not well-suited for these tasks as they would require more robust
human-in-the-loop infrastructure and error/revert handling.
This current
limitation restricts its ability to fully automate end-to-end workflows, mak-
ing it most effective for information gathering and communication drafting
assistance.
Data Privacy and Security Considerations: A significant limitation
of the current approach involves data privacy concerns inherent to cloud-
based LLMs and vector databases when handling sensitive email communi-
cations:
• LLM API Data Exposure: Utilizing third-party LLM APIs (Claude,
Llama 4) requires transmitting email content to external cloud services.
This creates potential privacy vulnerabilities as sensitive business com-

munications, customer information, and proprietary data may be ex-
posed to service providers. Even with data processing agreements, this
represents a fundamental privacy trade-off.
• Vector Database Security: The RAG implementation involves stor-

This creates additional attack surfaces for po-

• Internal Information Leakage: Even within an organization, cen-

would normally have segregated access controls.
• Audit Trail Limitations: The current prototype may lack compre-

frameworks when handling sensitive communications.

Potential limitations include:
• Latency: Real-time interaction depends on the LLM API response

• Accuracy and Hallucination: While RAG mitigates this, LLMs can

The reliability of summaries and drafted responses needs
continuous monitoring.
• Cost: Utilizing powerful LLMs via APIs incurs operational costs.
• Model Specificity: The capabilities are dependent on the specific
models chosen and their training data.

Future Work
Context Specificity of Implementation: The prototype was designed
based on findings from a specific business context (customer service at an elec-
tricity trading company). The interface, RAG configuration, and potential
MCP connections would likely require customization for different depart-
ments or industries with unique workflows and terminology.
These limitations highlight the need for further research, including larger-
scale deployments, longitudinal studies measuring real-world impact, devel-

across diverse organizational settings.
8.4
Future Work
Building upon the current prototype and addressing its limitations, sev-
eral promising directions for future work emerge:
Background Agent Processing:
• Concept: Enhance the AI agent to operate proactively in the back-
ground. Upon arrival, an email could trigger the agent to automati-
cally perform initial analysis, search relevant knowledge bases via RAG,
query connected systems via MCP for related data (e.g., customer his-
tory from CRM, order status from logistics systems), and potentially
even draft a preliminary response or summary.
• Benefit: This would significantly reduce the user’s initial workload.
Instead of starting from scratch, the user would find a pre-processed
email in the chat interface, complete with relevant context and a sug-
gested course of action or draft response, ready for review and refine-
ment. This could dramatically shorten handling times, especially for
recurring or information-intensive inquiries.
• Challenges: Requires robust triggering mechanisms, efficient resource
management for background tasks, state management to track ongoing
processing, and clear UI presentation of the agent’s background work.
Enhancing Privacy through On-Premises AI Infrastructure:
• Concept: Develop deployment architectures that leverage on-premises
or private cloud LLMs and vector databases to address the privacy and
regulatory compliance challenges identified in the limitations section.
This approach would keep sensitive email data and business informa-
tion within the organization’s own infrastructure while still enabling
advanced AI capabilities.

Future Work
• On-Premises LLM Integration: Investigate the feasibility of inte-
grating smaller, more efficient LLMs that can run on organizational
hardware. While these models might offer reduced capabilities com-
pared to their larger cloud-based counterparts, they eliminate the need
to transmit sensitive data to third-party services, providing a better
balance between performance and privacy.
• Private Vector Database Deployment: Implement the RAG pipeline
using self-hosted vector databases that remain behind the organiza-
tion’s firewall. This would allow for secure indexing of internal knowl-
edge bases, documentation, and email archives without exposing this
information to external services.
• Hybrid Architectures: Explore hybrid approaches where non-sensitive,
general tasks leverage cloud-based models for their superior capabili-
ties, while sensitive content processing is handled by on-premises mod-
els. This would require sophisticated data classification and routing
mechanisms to direct different types of content to the appropriate pro-
cessing pipeline.
• Benefits: Such implementations would significantly expand the po-
tential adoption of AI-powered email systems in privacy-sensitive and
highly regulated industries (healthcare, legal, finance, government) where
the current cloud-based approach presents prohibitive compliance bar-
riers. Organizations could maintain full control over their data while
still leveraging AI to enhance email workflows.
• Challenges: Key challenges include the computational resources re-
quired for on-premises AI infrastructure, potential performance trade-
offs with smaller models, maintenance overhead, and the need for spe-
cialized expertise to deploy and maintain these systems.
Research
would need to identify the minimum viable model sizes and config-
urations that could deliver acceptable performance within reasonable
organizational resource constraints.
This direction represents a critical path toward enterprise-wide adoption,
particularly for organizations that handle sensitive communications but still
wish to capture the significant productivity benefits of AI-augmented email
workflows.
Enabling Update Actions with Human-in-the-Loop (HITL):

Future Work
• Concept: Extend the MCP integration to support write/update ac-
tions in external systems (e.g., "Update customer address in CRM,"
"Create support ticket," "Add note to order"). Given the potential
risks associated with automated data modification, implementing a
Human-in-the-Loop (HITL) verification framework is crucial.
• HITL Framework: Before executing any action that modifies exter-

for explicit confirmation. The interface would clearly show what action
is proposed, what data will be changed, and in which system. The user
must approve the action before it proceeds.
• Benefit: This would enable true end-to-end workflow automation di-
rectly from the email interface, moving beyond information retrieval to
task execution, while maintaining user control and mitigating risks of
erroneous automated actions. It formalizes and potentially speeds up
processes currently involving manual data entry across systems.
• Challenges: Requires careful design of the approval interface, robust
error handling, transaction logging for auditing, potentially rollback
capabilities, and clear security protocols for granting write permissions
via MCP.
Enhanced Customization and Learning:
• Allowing teams or individuals to customize AI behavior, response tones,
preferred knowledge sources, and common workflows.
• Exploring adaptive learning where the system improves its suggestions
based on user edits and feedback over time.
Longitudinal Evaluation and ROI Analysis:
• Deploying the system in a real-world business environment for an ex-
tended period.
• Quantitatively measuring impact on key performance indicators (KPIs)
such as email handling time, first-contact resolution rates, error rates,
and customer satisfaction scores, alongside qualitative user feedback.
• Conducting a thorough Return on Investment (ROI) analysis based on
measured productivity gains.
By pursuing these future directions, the potential of AI-powered email
systems to fundamentally transform business communication and productiv-
ity can be more fully realized, moving towards truly intelligent and agentic
workflow hubs.

9
Conclusion
9.1
Research Overview and Key Findings
This research set out to address the central problem of how email could be
reimagined using LLM-based agentic workflows to handle complex multi-step
processing. Through a combination of observational study, system design,
and user evaluation, we have demonstrated a viable approach that trans-
forms the traditional email client into an integrated workflow hub capable of
reducing the significant productivity challenges associated with email man-
agement in business environments. The findings from the Modstrøm study
provided critical insights into real-world email practices, revealing that em-
ployees were already unofficially adopting AI tools for assistance, frequently
consulting multiple systems per email, and experiencing substantial ineffi-
ciencies from context switching.
9.2
Design Approach and Implementation
These observations directly informed our three-pronged design approach:
integrating contextual chat assistance, implementing knowledge retrieval from
business repositories, and creating a framework for custom system connec-
tions via the Model Context Protocol. The resulting prototype successfully
addressed these challenges by combining a chat-centric interface for more nat-
ural interaction, implementing Retrieval-Augmented Generation to ground
responses in company-specific knowledge, and leveraging MCP as a standard-
ized mechanism for connecting to external business systems. User evaluation
confirmed the potential benefits of this approach, with participants recog-
nizing significant time-saving potential through automated summarization,
integrated information retrieval, and reduced context switching.
9.3
Contributions and Implications
Our research makes several contributions to the field. First, it provides
a concrete implementation that moves beyond simply adding AI features to
traditional email and instead fundamentally reimagines the interface around
an intelligent assistant capable of orchestrating complex workflows. Second,
it demonstrates how standards like MCP can facilitate deeper cross-system
integration, addressing the fragmentation problems identified in our study.
Third, it offers insights into user trust and acceptance of AI assistance specif-
ically in professional email contexts, providing guidance for future implemen-
tations.

9.4
Limitations and Future Directions
9.4
Limitations and Future Directions
While acknowledging limitations related to the evaluation methodology,
and particularly the significant data privacy considerations inherent to cloud-
based AI systems, we have identified promising directions for future work.
These include implementing background agent processing to proactively han-
dle incoming emails, enabling Human-in-the-Loop update actions in external
systems, enhancing customization and learning capabilities, conducting lon-
gitudinal evaluation in real-world environments, and exploring on-premises
AI infrastructure to address privacy concerns. The research demonstrates
that through careful design guided by real-world observations, significant ad-
vancements in AI email assistance are possible beyond the current generation
of tools. By moving from siloed communication to integrated workflows, from
manual information gathering to automated retrieval, and from disconnected
systems to coordinated action, LLM-based agentic workflows can transform
how professionals handle email.
