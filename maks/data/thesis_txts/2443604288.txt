Abstract
A. fumigatus is a pathogenic fungi, from the Fumigati section, known
as the main cause of the illness aspergillosis.
In this study, 27
Fumigati genomes and six reference genomes have been explored
in efforts to gain a better understanding of the section. With an
average genome size of 31 Mb and 73 % of the predicted proteins
annotated in Interpro, the Fumigati species fall within the range
normally seen for Aspergillus species. Investigation of homologous
protein families revealed high diversity among the Fumigati species.
Frequently occurring proteins shared among all Fumigati species
include proteins related to production and export of toxins, iron
uptake, (conidial) pigment production and light signalling. These
are all believed to be factors of the pathogenicity of A. fumigatus.
The presence of these proteins in such a magnitude across the entire
section is striking. Examining secondary metabolite gene clusters
revealed that Fumigati species in general encode a low amount
of secondary metabolites compared to other Aspergillus sections.
Moreover, a high level of diversity was found among the species
with high variance both in terms of number and types of secondary
metabolites encoded. Annotated clusters mostly included toxins,
(pigmentation of) spores and iron uptake, correlating well with
the results found in the homologous protein analysis. In addition
to the genomic exploration, a co-culture experiment was done of
A. fumigatus with the other genome-analyzed Aspergillus species.
This revealed a number of interesting candidates for drug discovery,
displaying interesting interactions against A. fumigatus.

Dansk Resumé
A. fumigatus er en pathogen skimmelsvamp, som er hovedårsagen
bag sygdommen aspergillosis. A. fumigatus er en del af sektionen
Fumgiati. I dette studie er sekvensdata for 27 Fumigati arter og
seks reference arter blevet udforsket for at forstå denne sektion
bedre. Med en gennemsnitlig genomstørrelse på 31 Mb og med
73 % af de forudsagte proteiner annoteret i Interpro, falder Fumigati
arterne indenfor værdier man normalt ser for Aspergillus arter. Ved
udforskning af homologe protein familier, ses det at der er stor
diversitet mellem Fumigati arterne. De hyppigst forekommende
proteiner, som er delt blandt alle Fumigati arter, inkluderer proteiner
relateret til produktion af toxiner og transport af disse ud af cellen;
jernoptag, pigmentproduktion og lyssignaler. Disse er alle funk-
tioner som menes at være vigtige faktorer i patogeniciteten af A.
fumigatus. Det er derfor interessant at disse proteiner er tilstede
både i mange kopier og i alle sektionens arter. Ved undersøgelse af
genclustre for sekundære metabolitter, ses det også at der er stor
diversitet mellem arterne forhold til både antal og typer af clus-
tre. Generelt koder Fumigati arterne for et lille antal sekundære
metabolitter, sammenlignet andre Aspergillus sektioner. Funktionsan-
noterede clustre koder hovedsageligt for toxiner, (pigmentering af)
sporer og jernoptag. Således stemmer disse resultater ﬁnt overens
med analysen baseret på protein homologer. Udover udforskningen
af den genomiske data, blev et co-kultiveringseksperiment udført
med A. fumigatus voksende overfor hver af de andre Aspergillus arter
analyseret i dette studie. Dette eksperiment resulterede i et antal
af kandidater, der viste interessante reaktioner mod A. fumigatus.
Disse arter kan være interessante udgangspunkter i opdagelsen af
ny antibiotika.

Introduction
Aspergillus is a genus of ﬁlamentous fungi, with a great impact
on humans, as its species include food fermenters, producers of
medicine and industrially important enzymes and chemicals and
on the negative side also food contaminants and pathogens. Due to
this impact, it is of great importance, relevance and interest to keep
expanding our knowledge about the species.
With the continuously falling prices and quality development of
Next Generation Sequencing (NGS), comparative genomics is be-
coming more and more utilized. Additionally, databases, algorithms
and general work methods are constantly developed and improved
in order to optimize the information which can be extracted from
such genomic data. Sequencing and comparative genomics is thus
an obvious starting point for expanding the knowledge about As-
pergillus, as shown recently by both de Vries et al. [2017] and Vesth
et al. [2018].
An example of one of these species is Aspergillus fumigatus (A. fu-
migatus) which is notorious for being the most common source of
the illness aspergillosis. Many other species in the same section (the
Fumigati section) are also pathogenic and it is thus of great interest
to expand our understanding of this section and what its species are
capable of producing. Moreover, aspergillosis is commonly treated
with azoles-based medicin, but strains resistant to these are becom-
ing more and more common - most likely caused by the excessive
use of azoles in agriculture [Berger et al., 2017]. This means that dis-

Theoretical background
This chapter has been divided into three main sections: The ﬁrst
section brieﬂy outlines the relevant classes of secondary metabolites
and the biosynthesis of these. The second section goes through the
general background and phylogeny of the Aspergillus genus. The
third and last section zooms in on the Aspergillus section Fumigati,
which is the main focus of this project.
2.1
Secondary metabolites
Secondary metabolites (SMs) are a group of organic molecules pro-
duced by prokaryotes, fungi and plants, which are not directly
involved in primary metabolism such as growth and reproduction.
However, they are often part of the long-term survival strategy of
the producing species, with examples including toxins, pigments
and metal transporting agents. SMs are utilized widely by humans
as eg. ﬂavors, colors, biocides and various types of medicin. In
fact, the interest in SM research took off with penicillin in the 1940s
[Khaldi et al., 2010] and as explained previously the natural products
such as SMs make up an extensive part of pharmaceutical products
[Newman and Cragg, 2016].
The genes necessary for biosynthesis of SMs in fungi are grouped
together in clusters known as secondary metabolite gene clusters
(SMGCs). The polymeric basis of the compounds are produced
in the initial synthesis step which is catalayzed by so-called back-

bone enzymes. These compounds are subsequently tailored with
further modiﬁcations, which are catalyzed by another type of en-
zymes called tailoring enzymes [Osbourn, 2010] [Andersen et al.,
2013]. There are ﬁve major types of backbone enzymes: nonri-
bosomal peptide synthases (NRPSs), polyketide synthases (PKSs),
NRPS-PKS hybrids, prenyltransferases (DMATs) and terpene cy-

peptides, polyketides, nonribosomal peptide-polyketide hybrids,
indole alkaloids and terpenes [Khaldi et al., 2010]. For examples of

Species of the Aspergillus genus are known to produce great variety
of secondary metabolites [Sanchez et al., 2012]. Basic information
and examples of species from this genus are brieﬂy reviewed in the
following section.
2.2
The Aspergillus genus
Aspergillus is a ﬁlamentous fungi genus, consisting of around 350
species mainly found in soil, on dead organic matter or as pathogens
on animal and/or plant hosts [de Vries et al., 2017]. The species of
the genus are linked together by their characteristic conidiophores,
which shape resembles an aspergillum 1 as seen on Figure 2.1. This
resemblance was noted by the Italian priest and biologist Micheli,
who was the ﬁrst to describe the genus in 1729 [Edito, 2006].
The genus has been widely studied since then, and more recently
with a genomic approach e.g. by de Vries et al. [2017]. From this
study, it is noted that genomes of Aspergillus species have a length
between 29-36 Megabase (Mb), the Guanine-Cytosine (GC) content
is between 48-53 % and the number of predicted genes is between
9113-13553 [de Vries et al., 2017]. Moreover, it has become clear that
the genus displays a very wide range of ecological diversity and
thus survival strategies [de Vries et al., 2017] [Sanchez et al., 2012].
The diversity of the genus is reﬂected in the metabolite proﬁle of the

2.3
The Fumigati section
2.3.1
A. fumigatus and aspergillosis
A. fumigatus is a saprophytic fungus commonly encountered in both
outdoor and indoor environments and survives temperatures up to
50 ◦C. A. Fumigatus produces a lot of highly volatile spores which
are constantly inhaled by humans [Kousha et al., 2011] [Berger et al.,
2017].
For most people, this is not a problem, as the immune system is able
to ﬁght off the intruding fungus. However, when spores are inhaled
by immunocompromised patients (e.g. patients on chemotherapy or
organ transplant recipients) and critically ill patients, it can cause
pulmonary aspergillosis, with a possible risk of fatality. Pulmonary
aspergillosis causes various clinical manifestations which can lead to
several different diseases including invasive pulmonary aspergillosis
(IPA), chronic necrotizing aspergillosis (CNA), aspergilloma and al-
lergic bronchopulmonary aspergillosis (ABPA) [Kousha et al., 2011].
An overview of these four syndromes of pulmonary aspergillosis,
the patients who normally suffer from them and common treatment
methods is given on Figure 2.3 and brieﬂy described below.
Aspergilloma (fungus ball) is the most common form of pulmonary
aspergillosis and usually develops in lung cavity caused by an un-

CNA is a rare pulmonary aspergillosis syndrome, with little litera-

such as broad-spectred azoles [Kousha et al., 2011].
As outlined above, the main medical treatment of aspergillosis are
the antifungal azoles, but the emergence of azole-resistant isolates
of A. fumigatus is becoming more and more common. This trend
is highly suspected to be caused by the widespread use of azoles

patients are not responding to treatment, which have fatal con-
sequences [Berger et al., 2017].

which work effectively against A. fumigatus, including azole re-
sistant strains, will be necessary to secure proper treatment against
aspergillosis in the future.

but many other species in the Fumigati section are also pathogenic
and recent research has illuminated that misdiagnosis is a common
problem. The Fumigati section is further described in the following.
2.3.2
Other members of Fumigati
The section Fumigati consists of 63 different species described so
far, although some might be synonymous [Frisvad and Larsen,
2016] and many of these species are either reported or suspected
to be pathogenic based on their SM proﬁle [Frisvad and Larsen,
2016].
These species cannot all be clearly distinguished purely
upon morphology. Due to this fact misdiagnoses has become a
common problem in health care, as recent research has illuminated
[Serrano et al., 2011]. Thus, although A. fumigatus is still suspected
to be the main cause behind aspergillosis, the actual prevalence of
other Fumigati species might be a lot bigger than what was initially
believed.

Research Strategy
As mentioned in Section 1.1 Project Aim (page 2), the main goal of
this thesis is to support the AWGS project and gain understanding
of both theory and methods in sequencing work - all the way from
obtaining the biomass to exploring the genomic data, with a speciﬁc
focus on the Fumigati section.
In order to reach this goal, the work is divided into two main parts:
laboratory work and computational exploration of genomic data.
The work of these sections are outlined in the following.
3.1
Cultivation of fungi
Over the duration of this thesis, various Aspergillus fungi will be
cultivated and harvested for biomass. This includes inoculation of
freezestock on agar plate, inoculation from plate to ﬂask, harvest of
biomass from ﬂask, freeze drying of this biomass and simultaneously
examining for contaminations.
3.2
Extraction of RNA and DNA
DNA and RNA will be extracted from a number of fungi. This
includes preparation of the freeze dried biomass, the extraction
itself and ﬁnally testing the quality and concentration by Nanodrop,
Qubit and gel electrophoresis.

3.3. Exploration of genomic data
3.3
Exploration of genomic data
The genomic data of 26 Fumigati species (two A. fumigatus strains)
as well as 6 reference species (amounting to a total of 33 genomes)
will be analyzed, as initial steps in annotating the sequences and
to improve the understanding of this speciﬁc section. To do this,
genomic data taken from JGI database will be manipulated in R.
The code used can be found in Appendix C and the dataﬁles can be
found in Appendix E.
3.3.1
Descriptive Statistics of the Genomes
Several statistical measurements will be calculated for species to
get an overview of the genomes and the quality of the data. These
include length of the genomes, number of genes, mean gene length,
number of exons per gene, percentage of annotated proteins, number
of scaffolds, N50, L50 and GC content.
3.3.2
Nanopore Sequencing
Some of the already obtained genomes are assembled from high
numbers of scaffolds, leading to a lower quality and the possibilities
of missed sequences. Re-sequencing two species where this is the
case, will be done by Nanopore in order to improve the quality.
3.3.3
Homologuos Protein Families
The genomes will be analysed for protein homolog families and this
data will be used to improve the understanding of the diversity as
well as the overall production capabilities of the Fumigati.
3.3.4
Secondary Metabolites
The genomes will be analyzed for secondary metabolite gene cluster
families and this data will be used to better understand the sec-
ondary metabolite proﬁles and corresponding biosynthesis enzymes
of the Fumigati species.

4.3. Whole genome sequence data
4.3
Whole genome sequence data
The sequence data was obtained from JGI MycoCosm1 and consist
of fasta nucleic acid ﬁles, fasta amino acid ﬁles and ﬁles containing
the assembled scaffolds (in fasta nucleic acid format). Moreover,
it includes General Feature Format (gff) ﬁles which contains start
codons, stop codons, exons and coding sequence (CDS). Not all
genomes are publicly available.
Two separate pipelines were run on the data, following the same
method as by Vesth et al. [2018]. These two pipelines lead to "HFAM
data" and "SMURF data".
The "HFAM data" consists of an organism table, a HFAM-table
and an Interpro (IPR)-table. The organism table contains the list
of all organisms, and information about these, such as full name,
abbreviated name, organism ID and the genus and section they
belong to. The HFAM-table includes all found HFAMs and the
associated organism IDs, organism names and protein IDs.
The IPR-table includes the same information as the HFAM-table,
but in addition to this, the associated IPR ID and IPR description, if
existing, is also included from the Interpro database [Mitchell et al.,
2015]. Moreover, a Composition Vector (CV) tree was constructed
based on the fasta amino acid ﬁles, as part of the pipeline.
The "SMURF data" consists of a SMURF-table and MIBiG ﬁles. The
SMURF-table consists of all clusters found via Secondary Metabolite
Unique Regions Finder (SMURF) [Khaldi et al., 2010] and sorted
in cluster families. This table also contains various information on
the clusters, including the corresponding organism ID, organism
name, organism section, protein ID, IPR ID and IPR description.
If available, a short name for the type of backbone enzyme is also
included.
The MIBiG ﬁles include fasta ﬁles of all genes located by SMURF,
fasta ﬁles of all Aspergillus and Penicillium SMs registered in the
Minimum Information about a Biosynthetic Gene cluster (MIBiG)
database, the BLAST alignment of these ﬁles against each other and
a translate ﬁle that includes the MIBiG cluster number together with

4.4
Obtainment of fungal biomass
Prior to both RNA and DNA puriﬁcation, freeze dried fungal
biomass was obtained as follows.
Fungal strains were inoculated with sterilized toothpicks in three
points from cryostock to solid medium of either CYA, CYA18 or
MEAOx. On plates the fungi were grown at 30 ◦C for approximately
4-14 days or until the colonies showed enough growth.
Before
continuing to the next step, the colonies were examined by eye
for possible contamination. From contamination-free plates, fresh
spores were harvested with a drigalski spatula and inoculated to
liquid medium (type corresponding to the solid medium) in shake
ﬂasks.
In shake ﬂasks the fungi were grown at 30 ◦C for approximately
4-10 days or after a suitable amount of biomass was reached. The
biomass was isolated from the ﬂasks by ﬁltering through a coffee
ﬁlter after which it was frozen at −80 ◦C and subsequently freeze
dried. Freeze dried samples were stored at −80 ◦C until extraction
of RNA and/or DNA.

4.8
Genome stats
The length of the genomes was calculated by adding together the
lengths of all scaffolds per organism from the contig ﬁles.
The number of genes in each genome was found by counting the
number of different protein IDs per organism from the gene ﬁles.
The mean gene length was found by taking the mean of all gene

The number of scaffolds was found by counting the number of
unique scaffold names per organism in the contig ﬁles.
N50 is deﬁned as the number of scaffolds in a genome needed to
cover 50 % of the entire length, while L50 is the length of the N50
scaffold. It was calculated from the contig ﬁles.
The GC content is the percentage of all bases which are either
guanine or cytosine and was calculated from the contig ﬁles.
Interpro annotations are the percentage of the protein in a sequence
which are annotated in Interpro and were calculated from the HFAM-
IPR ﬁle.
For each of these categories, the number for each organism was
colored according to the size of the number relative to the values for
the other organisms (lighter color for lower numbers, darker color
for higher numbers).

6
Discussion
In this chapter different aspects of the results are evaluated, dis-
cussed and related to already established knowledge.
6.1
Quality of extracted RNA and DNA
In this section the possible reasons for degraded RNA samples and
for DNA with low A260/280 ratios are outlined. Morevoer, the impact
on sequence data by using low quality RNA or DNA are discussed.
6.1.1
Possible reasons for degraded RNA samples
For RNA, the measured A260/280 ratios were generally around 2 as
desired, but many of the bands were smeared, indicating degrada-
tion of the samples. The ﬂow bench and equipment was cleaned
with RNase Zap before working and gloves were worn at all times,
but degraded samples were a continuing tendency. It was noted
by laboratory technician Jannie Felskov Agersten that this tendency
started after changing the place, where the work was carried out
and it is speculated that this could be the reason. RNA molecules
are very fragile and maybe the new place was contaminated with
RNases. It is also possible that the gel electrophoresis chamber,
tubes or something else was contaminated. However - until further
tested - these are only speculations.
61
==PAGE:84==
6.1. Quality of extracted RNA and DNA
6.1.2
Possible reasons for low A260/280 ratio of DNA
samples
For the DNA extracted from A. assulatus and A. aureoluteus (in order
to re-sequence these), the A260/280 ratio was only approximately 1.50.
This is below the accepted range and indicates contaminants present
in the solution. Contaminants could for instance be proteins or phe-
nol, which has not been properly separated or washed from the DNA
during the extraction process. Especially in the step of separating
the aqueous phase after adding phenol:chloroform:isoamylalcohol,
one should be careful not to take material from the other phase(s).
6.1.3
Impact of lower quality RNA or DNA
The impact on sequence data, by using RNA or DNA of low quality
is discussed in the following.
6.1.3.1
RNA
The impact of degraded RNA on ﬁnal transcript quality has been
evaluated by Romero et al. [2014]. They reach the conclusion that
it can be compensated for to a certain extent and would still be
valuable data in situations where there are no other options (e.g.
for samples collected in ﬁeld work), but that high quality RNA is
preferred when possible. Based on this, the most suitable solution for
the AWGS project would most likely be to put efforts into localizing
and eliminating the cause of the degradation, and then repeating
the extraction experiments.
6.1.3.2
DNA
If the sequenced DNA is impure and contains proteins, RNA, buffer
salts, phenol or other contaminants it can inhibit the enzymes
catalyzing the sequencing reaction, and thereby lead to messy se-
quences [Genomics, 2012]. Sequencing of degraded DNA will natu-
rally contain many short fragments [Heiner et al., 2015], leading to
a high number of short scaffolds - ultimately affecting the quality of
the sequence data.

6.2. Genome statistics
6.2
Genome statistics
6.2.1
Validity of the results
The validity of the results behind most of the values was evaluated by
comparing them to values found at JGI. All values, except Interpro
annotation and GC content, are stated at JGI for both A. fumigatus
as well as the reference species. All results are the same except
for the mean gene length, which is larger in the JGI database. The
difference is consistent and it is assumed that it has to do with the
’deﬁnition’ of gene length. In JGI it might be deﬁned as going from
start to stop codon, which would explain the difference. Neither of
these deﬁnitions are wrong, but of course it is important to note the
difference, while evaluating, and especially comparing results.
The Interpro annotation is found in [Vesth et al., 2018] for A. fumi-
gatus and all reference species except N. crassa and A. terreus. All
results are the same as determined in this thesis. Based on these
comparisons the results seem valid.
6.2.2
Quality of the data
The quality of the data was evaluated mainly based on the GC
content, the number of scaffolds, N50 and L50. The number of
scaffolds and N50 is quite high for many of the Fumigati species and
the L50 is quite low. This can be an indication of low quality. Most
species have a number of scaffolds below 500 and it is difﬁcult to say
exactly when it becomes unacceptable. In the study by Vesth et al.
[2018] the highest number of scaffolds is 505, which is not mentioned
as being critical. However, with a high number of scaffolds and a
high N50, comes a higher risk of missing information and it should
be kept in mind when investigating results.

and section Nigri
Considering both genome length, the number of predicted genes
and the GC content, species of the Fumigati fall between what is
considered normal in the genus [de Vries et al., 2017]. The average
Fumigati genome length is in the lower end (31.1 for Fumigati and

The (relative) size of the core and unique genomes of the Fumigati

The core genome of the Fumigati species covers a smaller fraction

compared to 61 % compare to Nigri). In the study Vesth et al. [2018],
this number was compared to the same fraction (core genome of the
pan genome) in the Aspergillus genus and as the fraction is similar
(63 % for the genus) it is speculated that this could indicate a high
genetic diversity across the genus in general. The values found here
provide further data for this hypothesis which still holds true, when
expanding to also include the Fumigati section. However, it should
be noted here - as it is done by [Vesth et al., 2018] - that the tendency
could merely be the result of overpredicting genes.

NRPS and NRPS-like are generally the most common types in both
sections, but the NRPS-like makes up a bigger fraction in the Nigri
section than in the Fumigati section. On the other hand, DMATs
make up a much bigger fraction in the Fumigati section than in the
Nigri section.

MIBiG Clusters
As outlined in the review of the results, a lot of compounds are

6.5
Co-inoculation experiment
No records have been found covering similar experiments inoculat-
ing A. fumigatus against (these) fungal species and thus, the results
are not compared directly to other studies. However previous work
has shown a similar approach being used with success [Bertrand
et al., 2014] . The method seems valid, but a few things can be noted
and considered. Firstly, all plates were left for ﬁve days and no
longer. It is possible new insights would occur if the fungi were left
for longer. Replicating the experiment would lead to more conﬁ-
dence in the results. Additionally, it should be noted that only solid
CYA medium was used. CYA medium is SM inducing [Frisvad,
2012], but the proﬁles of expressed gene clusters can change quite
signiﬁcantly depending on the medium. This means that doing the
same experiment but on different types of medium could possibly
show different results. Moreover, the experiments were not carried
out in replicates. These examples are not meant to show that the
found results are invalid, but merely that by changing some param-
eters, there is a potential of other results. This means that the fungi

Conclusion
The scope of this thesis was to support the AWGS project and
concurrently gain an understanding of both theory and methods
of comparative genomics with a focus on the Fumigati section. The
main conclusions made throughout the work are outlined in the
following.
RNA and DNA was extracted with various results. Especially RNA
extraction showed problems with a continuing tendency of smeared
bands and RNase contamination is suspected to be the cause.
With an average genome size of 31 Mb, divided on 10,854 predicted
genes with a mean length of 1404 bases and 73 % of predicted
proteins annotated in Interpro, the Fumigati species fall within the
range normally seen for Aspergillus species.
The quality of the
genomes was overall considered as good, although a few species
showed less satisfying values in number of scaffolds and N50.
In efforts to improve the quality of the genomes of A. assulatus
and A. aureoluteus, these species were selected for in-house re-
sequencing. However, the hardware unfortunately failed and only a
small amount of sequencing data was obtained. This data was not
evaluated further.
The core genome covers a smaller fraction of the pan genome for
the Fumigati section than for the Nigri section, indicating a higher
level of diversity amongst Fumigati species. However, the fraction of
the pan genome covered by the unique genome is almost the same
69
==PAGE:92==
7. Conclusion
in the two sections, indicating a high genetic diversity across the
Aspergillus genus.
The ten most frequently occurring HFAMs found include families
consisting of proteins related to toxic compounds, iron uptake, an-
tibiotic and/or pigment synthesizing PKS, light signalling and gen-
eral intracellular signalling, transcription factors and to enzymatic
breakdown of ethanol and carbohydrates.
The number of SMGCs and the distribution of types of backbone
enzymes varies considerably in the Fumigati section, with a pattern
that generally follows the phylogeny. When compared to section
Nigri, Fumigati species generally has a low amount of SMGCs, when
compared to section Nigri. When comparing the distribution of
different clusters among the species, it becomes evident that there is
a high diversity in the Fumigati section, as many clusters are found
in only a few or a single species. Examination of annotated clusters
found in the genomes, shows that clusters which are common in
the Fumigati section are mostly related to toxins, (pigmentation of)
spores and iron uptake. This correlates well with what was found
when investigating the function of the most frequently occurring
HFAMs.
Moreover, the Fumigati species and the Aspergillus reference species
were explored for potential as candidates for further drug discov-
ery by co-culture with A. fumigatus. Interesting interactions were
observed, including distance inhibition, contact inhibition and zone
line.
The results presented in this thesis provides new knowledge about
the Fumigati section, further corroborating already stated hypotheses,
bringing new insights and providing a strong foundation for further
studies.
