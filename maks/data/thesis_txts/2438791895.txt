Summary 
Anaerobic digestion (AD) is bound to be more and more in use since it is a source of clean 
energy. The AD process is rather stable but it rests on biochemical balances and can be subject 
to inhibition and therefore loses its efficiency. Ammonia is the major factor that affects AD 
process today, causing great economic losses in biogas plants across the world. 
Bioaugmentation of ammonia tolerant methanogenic consortia, has proven to be effective in 
relieving the ammonia inhibition pressure in biogas reactors. The bioaugmentation strategy has 
been successfully implemented at a laboratory scale and to bring it at a larger scale some 
challenges must be taken: optimization of the cultivation of the ammonia tolerant methanogenic 
consortia used to perform the bioaugmentation, optimization of the concentration of the 
ammonia-tolerant methanogenic consortia and optimization of the preservation method of the 
ammonia tolerant methanogenic consortia.  
In this study, three main experiments were conducted to take these challenges: Experiment 
1. Implementation of a cultivation method with fed-batch reactors compared to the conventional 
batch cultivation method; Experiment 2. Test of a method to up-concentrate the cells in solution 
with forward osmosis; Experiment 3. Entrapment of the ammonia tolerant methanogenic 
consortia in a gel to preserve them. 
The results from the study have demonstrated: a production of high volume (2 L) of 
concentrated inoculum with the fed-batch cultivation method under mesophilic conditions 
(20% more concentrated than batch cultivation method); a reduction of the inoculum volume 
(at a rate of 24 mL.h-1) along with an increase of the cells concentration (increase of 10%TSS.h-
1 on average) with the up-concentration method involving forward osmosis; and a viable 
preservation method with agar gel (90% survival of the entrapped cells).

1 Introduction 
Western populations live comfortably, with a technology providing health and leisure. But 
many scientists have identified the high cost for the environment of this way of living. Its impact 
is environmental (depletion of resources, climate change) and social (inequalities in the living 
standards between developed countries and developing ones, destruction of ecosystems in 
poorer areas) (IPCC, 2014). Fossil fuels are great sources of energy, highly concentrated in long 
carbon chains they release high power when they are burned. During decades, this source of 
energy seemed to be almost magical, easy to find, easy to extract and enabling many 
applications. But eventually, its cost increased, at first because of political reasons, and more 
recently because of its environmental price due to the extraction and the harmful emissions 
released. Adding up the social, the environmental and the economic pressures, it has become 
more and more urgent to find another source of energy. Therefore, public institutions such as 
the European Union and the Danish government have set some goals to reduce greenhouse gas 
emissions and increase the share of renewable energy. The European commissions has set a 
reduction of the greenhouse gases emissions by 20% and an increase of energy consumption 
from renewable resources by 20% for the year 2020 (European Commission, 2010). And the 
Danish government has set a more ambitious goal of reducing greenhouse gas emissions by 
40% for the year 2020  (The Danish Government, 2013). In this context, anaerobic digestion 
seems to answer many societal issues. This technology enables to produce clean energy from 
organic wastes. The biogas production is part of the solutions to tackle at the same time the 
environmental as well as the societal challenges. Anaerobic digestion is an economic option for 
food waste and high moisture content organic wastes (Tanimu et al., 2015).

1.1 Anaerobic digestion 
Anaerobic digestion (AD) is a process occurring in places with very few light and without 
oxygen (anaerobic). The AD process is described as a degradation of organic material by three 
groups of bacteria: fermentative bacteria, hydrogen producing acetogenic bacteria and 
methanogenic bacteria and archaea (Angelidaki, 2004). More specifically, it is commonly 
divided in four main steps: hydrolysis, acidogenesis or fermentation, acetogenesis and 
methanogenesis (Angelidaki, 2004). These steps are interdependent even though each one of 
them involves specific groups of microorganisms. Fig.1 is summarizing the steps of AD.

1.1.1 Hydrolysis 
The first step of anaerobic digestion is the breakdown of complex organic polymers into 
soluble monomers (Angelidaki et al., 2011). The main organic polymers hydrolysed are 
carbohydrates, proteins and lipids. The main products are simple sugars, amino acids and long-
chain fatty acids (LCFA). The hydrolysis of these compounds is extracellular because microbes 
are not able to take up particles. To perform this process three groups of bacteria produce the 
extracellular enzymes: The proteolytic bacteria produce proteases to convert the proteins into 
amino acids, the cellulytic and xylanolytic bacteria produce cellulases and xylanases to convert 
carbohydrates (cellulose and xylan mostly) to glucose and xylose, the lypolytic bacteria produce 
lipases that catalyse the degradation of lipids to glycerol and long-chain fatty acids (Angelidaki 
and Batstone, 2011). This step is considered as the rate limiting step for degradation of organic 
particulate matter. Thus, pretreatment of the organic substrate is performed to speed up the 
process by augmenting the particulate surface area and facilitate the breakdown of organic 
matter to enhance the biogas production (Angelidaki and Batstone, 2011). 
1.1.2 Fermentation 
During this step of AD, sugars and amino acids, two products of the previous step, are 
converted to methanogenic substrates (hydrogen, acetate, carbon dioxide), alcohols and volatile 
fatty-acids (VFA) following two different pathways (Angelidaki, 2004). The first pathway is 
the fermentation of monosaccharides, it occurs through the Emben-Meyerhof-Parnas (EMP) or 
Entner Doudoroff (ED) and Acetyl-CoA pathways. The first pathway was only observed during 
overload conditions and leads to the production of lactate and propionate (C3 products). The 
second pathway leads to the production of the acetate, butyrate and alcohols (C2/C4/C6 
products). Amino acids are degraded following a reaction called Stickland reaction (Wildenauer 
and Winter, 1986; Angelidaki et al., 2011) where they react in pairs forming an 
oxidation/reduction couple. 
==PAGE:18==
4 
1.1.3 Acetogenesis  
The production of acetate is the result of the oxidation of the VFA (propionate and 
butyrate), LCFA and alcohol, as well as the reduction of carbon dioxide. Following the Acetyl-
CoA pathway previously mentioned, hydrogen utilizing acetogens reduce carbon dioxide to 
acetate. Alongside of this reduction, hydrogen producing acetogens oxidize the organic acids 
produced during the hydrolysis and fermentation steps to acetate and hydrogen. The acetogens 
are catalysing reactions thermodynamically favourable at low hydrogen concentrations. 
Therefore, in order to insure the metabolism of the cells, the hydrogen produced during this 
step must be kept low. The viability of this process is insured by the syntrophic relationship 
between the different hydrogen utilizing bacteria keeping the balance in the reactor. This 
syntrophic relationship also prevent the reactor to be inhibited by high VFA or high LCFA 
concentrations.  
1.1.4 Methanogenesis 
Methanogenesis is the last step of AD. It is also the most sensitive to changes in pH and 
temperature (Angelidaki et al., 2004). The acetate and the hydrogen produced during the 
previous steps are now converted to methane through three main pathways. The first is the 
aceticlastic methanogenesis producing methane and carbon dioxide from acetate (Eq. 1), it 
represents usually around 70% of the methane production. The second is the hydrogenotrophic 
methanogenesis, acetate is first converted to hydrogen and carbon dioxide by acetate-oxidizing 
bacteria, and then methane is formed by the hydrogenotrophic archaea (Eq. 2). The last pathway 
is the methylotrophic methanogenesis, referring to the conversion of C1 compounds, such as 
methanol, into methane (Eq. 3).

Methanogenesis is conducted by the methanogens which belong to the archaea domain and 
are found in diverse habitats such as: fresh water, marine sediments, digestive and intestinal 
tracks of animals and anaerobic waste digesters. More than the ability to thrive in different 
habitats, methanogens have also been found at various temperatures: extremely thermophilic, 
thermophilic and mesophilic conditions. Their status comes from the only mode of energy 
metabolism: methane generation. Methanogens are usually classified in six main genomic 
orders: Methanobacteriales, Methanococcales, Methanomicrobiales, Methanosarcinales, 
Methanopyrales and Methanocellales. The methanogens sorted in these genomic orders operate 
with different carbon sources (Acetate, CO2, formate, etc.), different pH conditions (from 4 to 
10) and different temperatures (from 1 to 88°C) (Dhaked, Singh and Singh, 2010; Angelidaki 
et al., 2011). 
1.2 Operational parameters influencing anaerobic digestion 
There are several parameters regulating the AD process; for example: the substrate 
composition, the temperature, the pH, the organic loading rate, the carbon to nitrogen ratio, the 
mixing and the retention time (Jain et al., 2015). 
1.2.1 Substrate composition 
For AD to occur, it is essential that the cells find the nutrients their need to grow. There are ten 
basic nutrients that should be found in a concentration close to 10-4 mol.L-1 to prevent 
insufficiency and inhibition because of oversupply (Wolin et al., 1963; Angelidaki et al., 1990). 
These nutrients are: hydrogen, nitrogen, oxygen and carbon (essential for organic materials 
constitution), sulphur (essential for amino acids, cysteine and methionine), phosphorus (nucleic 
acids and energy cells such as ATP), potassium, magnesium, calcium and iron (enzymes’ 
cofactors). In addition to these 10 macro-nutrients nickel and cobalt should be added to insure

substrate used in AD contains all the required nutrients.

1.2.3 Temperature  
Anaerobic Digestion occurs at a very wide range of temperatures: psychrophilic (<20°C), 
mesophilic (20-40°C), thermophilic (40-60°C), hyperthermophilic (>60°C). As seen in Fig.2, 
an increase in temperature induces and increase in the growth rate until reaching the tipping 
point where the survival of the microorganisms is threatened. Nonetheless, most of the biogas 
reactors are operating under mesophilic and thermophilic conditions (Angelidaki, 2004). More 
precisely, thermophilic conditions are usually favoured since the higher temperatures enhance 
the reactions rates (Mao et al., 2015). Therefore, at thermophilic temperatures, lower hydraulic 
retention times, smaller reactors’ sizes and better removal of pathogens can be achieved. 
Nevertheless, mesophilic conditions bring more stability in the process, which is less sensitive 
to pH change, organic loading rate variations and substrate compositions (Mao et al., 2015). 
==PAGE:22==

1.2.4 Organic loading rate 
The organic loading rate (OLR) is the amount of substrate fed to the reactor. Usually, the 
OLR is expressed in gVS.L-1.d-1 and thus refers to the quantity of organic matter provided by 
the reactor daily. An increase in the OLR is followed by an increase in biogas production, but 
this phenomenon occurs only until a maximum OLR which is the threshold before an inhibition 
induced by an overfeeding of the reactor (Smith and Mccarty, 1990). The hydrolysis and the 
acidogenesis are not inhibited by high concentrations of substrate but the methanogenesis is 
more sensitive to the concentrations in the reactors. The high availability of substrate would 
lead to a high production of volatile fatty acids and therefore create an irreversible acidification 
of the system which would inhibits the methanogenic activity (Angelidaki, 2004) 
1.2.5 Carbon to nitrogen ratio 
The carbon to nitrogen ratio (C/N) expresses the balance between the carbon concentration 
and the nitrogen concentration in the system. A too high carbon concentration compared to the 
nitrogen concentration expressed by a high C/N ratio, would lead to a deficiency in 
microorganisms’ growth because of the lack of nitrogen. On the opposite, a low C/N ratio would 
be an indicator of a high nitrogen content and a possible ammonia inhibition (Siles et al., 2010). 
Usually, this ratio is assessed with the total organic fractions: Total Organic Carbon (TOC) and 
Total Nitrogen (TN), with the assumption that both nutrients sources are fully biodegrable 
(Puyuelo et al., 2011). Up to date, the optimal C/N ratio has been found to be between 20 and 
30 for anaerobic digestion (Wu et al., 2010; Mao et al., 2015). 
1.2.6 Retention time and mixing 
In the context of anaerobic digestion, two retention times are commonly defined: the 
hydraulic retention time (HRT) and the solid retention time (SRT). The SRT is characterizing 
==PAGE:23==
9 
 
the time the sludge spends in the reactor and therefore the time the microorganisms spend in 
the reactor before being ejected. This parameter must be controlled carefully to let enough time 
to the microorganisms to grow and reproduce, thus preventing to be washed out. The HRT is 
the duration of contact between the substrate fed to the reactors and the microorganisms 
operating in it. This retention time is basically expressed as the ratio of the working volume on 
the influent flow rate. These two retention times depends on the microorganisms’ growth rate, 
which are themselves subject to change with temperature, organic loading rate and substrate 
composition of the feedstock (Angelidaki, 2004). 
The mixing in an anaerobic digester is mandatory in order to prevent it from clogging but 
also to insure a good contact between fresh substrate and the microbial populations in the 
digester. Nonetheless, this mixing must be kept at a reasonable pace to protect the syntrophic 
relationships between the microorganisms but also to prevent the cells from being destroyed 
(Angelidaki and Batstone, 2011). Several techniques exist for the mixing, each one of them 
adapted to the type of reactor used (Angelidaki, 2004). Finally, most of the calculations 
regarding an anaerobic digester assume a complete mixing inside the reactor. Therefore, this 
mixing must be performed as well as possible to be able to track changes in the reactor. 
1.3 Inhibition in anaerobic digestion 
Various substances have been reported to be inhibitory to AD. Inhibition affects the 
biomass growth as well as its diversity. In most cases, the inhibition does not shut down 
completely the process but brings it in a steady state where the biogas production is lower than 
the theoretical one (Chen et al., 2008). Speece (1983) divided inhibition in two categories: 
Toxicity, adverse effect on microbial organisms, and inhibition of bacterial function. This 
division has been investigated deeper by Batstone et al. (2002) by defining biocidal inhibition, 
biostatic inhibition and endproduct inhibition.

1.3.1 Biocidal inhibition 
Biocidal inhibition refers to reactive toxicity of the substance. The toxic compound would 
react with a functional component of the cells, making it unable to function normally. 
Detergents, cyanide, antibiotics, xenobiotics such as polyaromatic hydrocarbons and metals are 
biocidal inhibitors. For example, heavy metals accumulate in the reactors because they are not 
biodegradable. Moreover, they act on protein molecules and take natural metals roles in the 
biomass growth and contribute to the enzyme disruption (Chen et al., 2008). Light metal ions 
can also be inhibitors. At high concentration they increase the osmotic pressure at the cell 
membrane and therefore increase dehydration of the cells. These light metal ions such as 
potassium, sodium, magnesium and calcium are found in anaerobic digester because of natural 
organic matter degradation and also because of artificial addition for pH (Chen et al., 2008). 
Sulfide is also a biocidal inhibitor. Its toxicity lead to microbes’ early death. But its inhibitory 
effect also relies on the competition for substrate between sulphate reducing bacteria and 
microbial groups found in AD. Which makes it also a biostatic inhibitor. Biocidal inhibition is 
mainly irreversible (Angelidaki and Batstone, 2011). 
1.3.2 Biostatic inhibition  
Unlike the previous category of AD’s inhibition, biostatic inhibition is a nonreactive 
toxicity that is normally reversible. The compounds of this category disrupt the cellular stasis 
or simply change the energy production. They mainly disturb the fragile balance of redox 
conditions, pH and total salts, and therefore the microbes need to shift they energy consumption 
from anabolism to maintenance of stasis (Angelidaki and Batstone, 2011). Such inhibition can 
be caused by accumulation of products like hydrogen inside the reactor. The most sensitive 
microbial population to biostatic inhibition are the one having a particularly marginal yield. For 
example acetogenic, hydrogen utilizing and aceticlastic microbes. pH inhibition created by free 
acids and bases inhibitions is the main biostatic inhibition. As explained previously, when the 
==PAGE:25==
11 
 
pH gets out of the optimal range for the microbes, they are shifting they activity from anabolism 
toward maintenance. Others example of biostatic inhibitors are the organic compounds such as 
chlorophenols, halogenated aliphatics and long chain fatty acids (Jian Lin Chen et al., 2014). 
They have a very low solubility in water and a high tendency to attach to flocs’ surfaces. 
Accumulating inside the anaerobic digester these compounds would create a range of 
unbalances depending of the state of the reactor (age and concentration of the biomass, exposure 
time and temperature) (Chen et al., 2008). 
Ammonia 
Ammonia is a good example of biostatic inhibition. This compound is essential for biomass 
growth but at high concentrations (>3gNH4.L-1), it becomes an inhibitor for anaerobic 
digestion. Ammonia is mainly found in anaerobic digester because of the natural degradation 
of nitrogen matter provided by urea and proteins (Fotidis et al., 2014). Feedstock for biogas 
production with high ammonia content, such as slaughter houses’ wastes and poultry manure, 
have made ammonia inhibition a big challenge for AD. The total ammonia nitrogen (TAN) 
found in an anaerobic digester is the sum of two forms of ammonia: the ammonium ions (NH4+) 
and the free ammonia nitrogen (FAN, NH3). Free ammonia has been identified as the main 
cause for ammonia inhibition. Ammonia has been found to be inhibitory when its TAN 
concentration is above 3 gNH4.L-1 or the FAN concentration is above 0.15 gNH3.L-1, 
independently of the conditions (Chen et al., 2008). The inhibition of AD by ammonia is the 
result of three parameters combination: pH, temperature and ammonia concentration (FAN). 
Fig.3 (adapted from Fernandes et al. (2012)) highlights the dependency of FAN to pH and

conditions becomes easier (Rajagopal et al., 2013).

The mechanism proposed for ammonia inhibition is the diffusion of free ammonia through 
the cell membrane due to its permeability. The penetration of free ammonia inside the cell 
would induce a change in the intracellular pH and therefore disrupt the cell functioning (Chen 
et al., 2008). The microorganism involved in AD are not equal regarding ammonia inhibition, 
and the methanogens have been found to be the most sensitive to it (Calli et al., 2005). More 
precisely the aceticlastic methanogens were assessed to be more sensitive than the 
hydrogenotrophic ones (Fotidis et al., 2014). The inhibition of methanogenesis by ammonia 
disturbs the balance between the acetogens and the methanogens and therefore endangers the 
AD process. The VFA, main products of acidogenesis, are no longer converted by the 
methanogens and a drop in the pH is observed. As a consequence of this pH drop, the free 
ammonia concentration decreases and along with it the inhibition pressure, leading the reactor 
to operate in an inhibited steady state. This inhibited steady state has been described as a 
functioning involving lower but stable methane production. In fact, a consequent number of 
biogas plant in Denmark have reported to operate under this inhibited steady state caused by 
ammonia inhibition (Fotidis et al., 2014).

1.3.3 Endproduct inhibition 
An increase in products’ concentration can be a cause of process inhibition in an anaerobic 
digester. The increase of a product creates a drop in available free energy for catabolism and 
therefore an inhibition. For example, acetogens are inhibited by high concentrations of 
hydrogen and even by high concentrations of acetate. It is also referred as overloading.  
1.4 Bioaugmentation  
Generally speaking, bioaugmentation is the process of adding microbes with particular 
functions or properties into a biological system to enhance its performance (Stephenson and 
Stephenson, 1992). Several steps of AD have been improved thanks to this process: hydrolysis 
and acetogenesis/acidogenesis, by the maintenance of high dihydrogen concentrations and the 
overcoming of transient toxicity induced by dioxygen (Nzila, 2017). In the frame of the study, 
ammonia inhibition is overcome by the inoculation of ammonia tolerant methanogens that are 
able to conduct methanogenesis in an ammonia rich environment. A mechanism has been 
proposed by Fotidis et al. (2014): the bioaugmented ATMC (ammonia tolerant methanogenic 
consortia) would induce a decrease in H2 partial pressure inside the anaerobic digester and 
therefore create favourable thermodynamic conditions for acetate oxidizers to thrive. Then 
more robust methanogens in front of ammonia inhibition such as the hydrogenotrophic 
methanogens will be enabled to grow. This process has been referred as a “microbiological 
domino effect” by Fotidis et al. (2014). The bioaugmentation process in the ammonia inhibition 
context has been proven to help the reactors to exit their inhibited steady state to recover their 
full methane production potential, e.g. recovering 30% of methane production (Fotidis et al., 
2014). 
Bioaugmentation in the context of ammonia inhibition is possible thanks to the acclimation, 
or adaptation, of microorganisms to high ammonia concentration. This acclimation has already 
==PAGE:28==
14 
been studied and some optimal acclimation methods have already been investigated in the 
purpose of performing bioaugmentation (Tian et al., 2017). However, bioaugmentation has also 
been unsuccessful in some cases due to the failure in acclimation of the bioaugmented inoculum 
or because of its washout. These two causes of failures are the two main challenges identified 
so far to be addressed in order to perform a successful bioaugmentation (Fotidis et al., 2014).

Lag phase and delay 
There is an on-going discussion among the scientific community on the definition of the 
lag phase and its modelling. So far, the lag phase duration was defined by Pirt (1975) as the 
point of intersection between the parallel line to the time axis and the tangent to the growth 
exponential curve (exponential phase). But as highlighted by Vadasz and Vadasz (2010), a lag 
phase is commonly observed even when the inoculation takes place during the exponential 
growth phase of the microbial population. Therefore, a difference was made between the lag 
phase and the delay phase. The lag phase was defined as the period during which the 
microorganisms adapt to the new environment and start again their growth functioning (or 
switch from maintenance functioning to growth functioning). And the delay phase was defined 
as the time for the substrate to diffuse to the cells (Vadasz and Vadasz, 2010). Generally, the 
lag phase in microbial growth has been found to be dependent on the activity of the cells, their 
concentration, the pH, the temperature and the solute concentration (Robinson, 1998).

1.5.2 Cultivation methods  
Three methods of cultivation have been developed so far for microorganism’s cultivation: 
the pure batch cultivation method, the fed-batch or semi-batch cultivation method and the 
continuous cultivation method in a chemostat (Minihane and Brown, 1986). 
The continuous cultivation method has been used to cultivate targeted microorganisms to 
obtain specific products (Weuster-Botz et al., 1993). But this cultivation method requires daily 
maintenance and can be subject to issues such as wrong plugging, clogging of the tubes and 
even contamination (Usack et al., 2012).  
The pure batch cultivation method has proven itself to be reliable and to provide a very 
stable environment for the microorganisms to grow (Minihane and Brown, 1986). Protected 
from any change of their environment except the one they create, the microbial populations can 
grow at ease. Moreover, the closed environment with this method allows to feed a specific 
substrate and therefore to target easily the microbes to grow. 
Fed-Batch reactors are batch reactors with a continuous or sequential feeding method. The 
feeding substrate is progressively added to the reactors until it is filled up. And the removal of 
the product is then processed at the end of the experiment (Johnson, 1987). The fed-batch 
cultivation method is widely used in the industry as an optimization method for microbial 
growth (Johnson, 1987). The main challenge with this cultivation method is the estimation of 
kinetics parameters in order not to overload the system but neither starve the microorganisms. 
Therefore, having a method for growth control is highly recommended (Minihane and Brown, 
1986). This cultivation method has been found to be more efficient than the batch one in many 
studies (Minihane and Brown, 1986). This method allows to control more efficiently the 
concentration of nutrients in the feeding substrate and therefore is ideal for growth processes 
dependent on a limiting substrate. 
==PAGE:31==
17 
 
Furthermore, in microbial cultivation the microbial concentration is a factor to optimize 
and it is very difficult to achieve with the pure batch cultivation method, either because a high 
concentration of the substrate will lead to an inhibition or because of the limiting solubility of 
the feeding compound (carbon source) (Lee et al., 1999). On the opposite, the fed-batch 
cultivation method allows such achievements by using different cultivation strategies (Ding and 
Tan, 2006).  
Two main techniques of fed-batch cultivation are usually described: with and without feed-
back control (Lee et al., 1999). The method without feed-back control relies on a perfect 
knowledge of the growth mechanisms and a mathematical model supporting the feeding 
strategy. Even though this strategy was proven to work for microbial cultivation, it does not 
allow any optimization nor reaction to specific events. The method with feed-back control 
implies regular measurements or the implementation of a monitoring method involving a sensor 
able to follow quick responses. This control can be direct, such as the Teflon tubing ethanol 
sensor to control the ethanol content as a unique carbon source for the culturing of yeast 
described by Yamanè and Shimizu (1984), or indirect such as the measurement of methane 
content to follow methanogenic growth. This second control method allows to react to the 
behaviour of the microorganisms, but it also needs to be driven by a mathematical model that 
would frame the cultivation. The mathematical model is the root of the feeding strategy that 
can be intermittent, constant, exponential and optimized (Neubert et al., 1984). These different 
processes aim at controlling the growth of the microorganisms by optimizing as much as 
possible the feeding of the carbon source. With the knowledge on the growth of microorganisms 
and its description with the Monod equation (Eq.4), the exponential feeding strategy has been 
found to be the most adapted for a feed-batch cultivation method (Ding and Tan, 2006).

1.6 Up-concentration of ammonia tolerant methanogenic consortia 
In many microbiological processes, the biomass needed is quantified by its concentration 
in the final solution (Lee et al., 1999). Therefore, if a large volume is to be treated, a highly 
concentrated solution or a large volume of less concentrated solution need to be used. Since the 
use of large volume is not handy in terms of transportation and can disturb the functioning of 
the biogas reactor by diluting the indigenous microorganisms. The use of highly concentrated 
solutions appears to be a more favourable option. Therefore, the possibility of up-concentrating 
(increase the concentration) microorganisms and the activity of these microorganisms after the 
process must be investigated to take this step. 
Practically, centrifugation is used to extract a supernatant from an inoculum with a high 
cells concentration (Al-Faqheri et al., 2017). And in this study the up-concentration with 
forward osmosis has been investigated. 
1.6.1 Centrifugation 
Centrifugation is the process of separating substances of different densities from a mixture 
with the help of a centrifuge (Majekodunmi, 2015). Centrifugation force is the result of the

force from the centre which pushes the materials against the wall of the tube. The components 
of a mixture get separated because the denser particles are forced to the bottom while the lighter 
ones remain at the top. After the centrifugation, a supernatant can be extracted to obtain a 
solution with targeted high biomass concentration (Li et al., 2017).

1.7 Preservation of methanogens 
The preservation of methanogens is a challenge in the field of anaerobic digestion, and 
more specifically for bioaugmentation. Many immobilization of whole cells techniques have 
been investigated such as cross-linking, covalent binding, flocculation and gel entrapping 
(Kennedy and Cabral, 1983). The study ran by Kennedy and Cabral (1983) investigated the gel 
entrapping method to immobilize living cells. This immobilization technique has already been 
investigated with methanogens by Karube et al. (1980), who managed to successfully entrap 
methanogens and compare the entrapment in three different gels: agar gel, polyacrylamide gel 
and collagen membrane. The highest methanogenic activity found during this study for 
entrapped methanogens was with agar gel. Nonetheless, this method has not been widely used, 
probably because of its low mechanical strength as well as the low diffusion capacities of the 
gel (Kennedy and Cabral, 1983).

2 Aim and Objectives 
With an increasing use of nitrogen rich substrates, ammonia inhibition will become an even 
bigger challenge. Along with the use of other substrates less rich in nitrogen, the 
bioaugmentation technology is an efficient way to alleviate ammonia inhibition in AD process 
(Fotidis et al., 2014). However, this technology is only available at laboratory scale. 
Methanogenic populations have already been successfully acclimated to high ammonia 
concentrations under mesophilic (37°C) and thermophilic conditions (55°C) (Tian et al., 2017). 
And to bring this technology to a bigger scale it is necessary to work on the optimization of the 
microbial consortia production and preservation with: 
→ An efficient way to grow the ammonia tolerant methanogens. 
→ A method to achieve maximum biomass concentration. 
→ A viable preservation method to provide ready-to-use inoculum.

Therefore, three main comparative experiments were designed to investigate these 
challenges: 
• 
Experiment 1 was aiming to achieve the maximum biomass concentration in the best

• 
Experiment 2 was aiming to optimize the biomass concentration with a comparison

viability of microorganisms stored in cultivation medium in the form of liquid.  
Therefore, the overall objectives of the study were: 
• 
To identify the best cultivation method for ammonia tolerant methanogens between batch 
and fed-batch cultivation methods. 
• 
To find a proper method to further increase biomass concentration of ammonia tolerant 
methanogenic consortia by assessing the efficiency and the viability of the two up-
concentration methods. 
• 
To determine the quality of ammonia tolerant methanogens’ preservation in agar gel.

3.1.3 Experimental setup 
3.1.3.1 Batch reactors 
The batch reactors were bottles of 238 mL of total volume, with 60 mL of working volume. 
The working volume of each bottle was first composed of 42 mL of BA medium under

Yeast extract (0.2 g.L-1) and vitamin solution (0.1 g.L-1) were then added. The bottles were then

by adding sodium hydroxide solution (NaOH, 4 mol.L-1) and/or hydrochloric acid (HCl, 4 
mol.L-1). In total, 9 bottles were used for each experiment: 3 blanks, 3 test bottles and 3 
cultivation bottles to insure the supply of inoculum for further experiments.

3.3 Experiment 3-Gel preservation of methanogenic consortia 
3.3.1 Agar gel 
The gel used to preserve the methanogens was made with Agar powder (Agar-agar). The 
chosen concentration of 10 g.L-1 of agar to form the gel was based on Karube et al. (1980) study 
and preliminary tests performed before the main experiments. One gram of agar powder was 
then added into 100 mL of BA medium. Then the solution was heated to 90°C, using a 
microwave, to perform the dissolution of agar. When this solution containing the dissolved agar 
cooled down, the gel started to become solid around 40°C. Therefore, the inoculum to be 
preserved was added to the solution when its temperature was between 50°C and 60°C. The 
tests performed before hand showed that the gel could be formed when the proportion of the 
solution containing agar gel was 25% of the final solution (2.5 g.L-1 of agar gel in the final 
solution). Applying a safety factor of 1.2 the proportion used during the experiment were 30% 
of the solution containing the dissolved agar and 70% of the solution containing the active 
inoculum.

3.4 Analytical methods 
3.4.1 Methane content 
The methane content in the headspace of batch and fed-batch reactors was determined using 
gas chromatography (GC Thermo Fischer scientifc 1310). This machine functions with a flame 
ionization detector using hydrogen as a gas carrier. For each GC measurement, the calibration 
was conducted based on three methane concentrations of reference (5%, 40% and 60%) in 
triplicates. 
3.4.2 VFA analysis  
VFA samples were prepared as following: each sample was subject to a dilution of 67% 
with distilled water, then an acidification with ortho-phosphoric acid (33% v/v) was performed 
before a centrifugation of the sample for ten minutes at 13.400 rpm. Finally, 0.1 mL of 4-
methyl-valeric acid internal standard was mixed with 1 mL of the diluted sample and stored at 
-18°C until being uploaded to a gas chromatographer. The gas chromatographer (HP5890 series 
II) was equipped with a flame ionization detector and a FFAP fused silica capillary column 
using nitrogen as gas carrier. This GC was able to detect the following acids: acetic, iso-butyric, 
propionic, butyric, iso-valeric, valeric and hexanoic. 
==PAGE:50==

3.4.3 VSS/TSS 
Volatile suspended solids (VSS) and total suspended solids (TSS) were measured following 
the standardized method (APHA, 1999). The filters used to collect the suspended solids were 
placed in the crucibles and dried at 105°C during 30 minutes before weighting (Wini). Then the 
crucibles with the filters were place in an oven at 105°C for 24 hours to dry the samples. Then 
the crucibles with the dry filters were measured again to determine the TSS concentrations 
(Wdry). Then the crucibles with the dry filters were placed in another oven where the 
temperature was first progressively increased from 200°C to 550°C during an hour before 
letting the samples in this oven for two more hours at 550°C. The crucibles with the filters were 
then weighted a last time to determine the VSS concentration (Wash). The two following

4 Results and discussion 
4.1 Experiment 1-Optimal cultivation of ammonia tolerant methanogens 
4.1.1 Methane production and yield 
From the methane content measurements (Fig.13), it can be seen that the batch reactors have 
reached the theoretical maximum methane yield (373 mLCH4.gVS-1) under both mesophilic 
and thermophilic conditions. Conversely, the fed-batch reactors have only reached 80% of the 
theoretical maximum methane production (2500 mL.CH4 instead of 3000 mL.CH4) under 
mesophilic conditions and they have not finished their growth after 110 days under thermophilic 
conditions. Therefore, it was concluded that the batch reactors have achieved maximum growth 
when the fed-batch reactors have only achieved a growth approaching the maximum without 
reaching it under mesophilic conditions, and failed in providing a good growth environment 
under thermophilic conditions.

4.1.2 Performances comparison 
Table 8 summarizes the main criteria for the assessment of the cultivation methods: 
practical maximum methane production compared to the maximum theoretical methane 
production, time to reach the stationary phase of growth and final TSS concentration. This table 
underlines the efficiency of the batch cultivation method compared to the fed-batch cultivation 
method. The practical maximum methane production reached 99% of the theoretical one in the 
case of the batch reactors cultivation method and only 80% in the case of the fed-batch 
cultivation method under mesophilic conditions. Similarly, the time to reach the stationary 
phase for the batch reactors was 32 days, when the fed-batch reactors only reached this phase 
after 53 days under mesophilic conditions. In their study, Tian et al. (2017) found a production 
efficiency of 100% for the batch cultivation method but only reached 83% for the fed-batch 
cultivation method. However, the goal of their study was to acclimatize the ATMC to high 
concentration of ammonia, performing the feeding in sequences along the growth of microbial 
population inside the reactors. Therefore, their study provided with a more reliable growth but 
not optimized (64 days to reach this 83% instead of 53 in the present study for the fed-batch 
reactors).  
The TSS measurements have proven the fed-batch cultivation method to provide higher 
TSS concentration than the batch cultivation method, with a TSS concentration 20% higher for 
the fed-batches than for the batches under mesophilic conditions. This difference could come 
from the shift in functioning during the growth. The ATMC cultivated in fed-batch reactors 
have started to consume acetate for their maintenance (or survival) before converting the acetate 
to methane. This phenomenon would explain the lower practical maximum methane production 
and the higher cells concentration in the fed-batch reactors. This shift in functioning was usually 
observed in case of biostatic inhibition (Angelidaki and Batstone, 2011), and here the change 
==PAGE:59==

of environment could have create a change in pH and therefore a biostatic inhibition during the 
adaptation growth phase might have happened in the fed-batch reactors.  
Under thermophilic conditions, the batches reached the maximum theoretical maximum 
yield (100%) in 18 days, on the opposite, the fed-batches reached only 21% of the maximum 
theoretical methane production in 110 days without reaching the stationary phase of growth. 
These results were in accordance with previous experiments ran under thermophilic conditions 
where an inhibition of the methanogenic growth with the fed-batch cultivation method was also 
observed under thermophilic conditions (Jéglot, 2018).

Performance assessment 
The fed-batch cultivation method provided with a large volume (2 L) of inoculum under 
mesophilic conditions. The fed-batch reactors were inoculated with 90 mL of inoculum to reach 
a final volume of 2 L, whereas the batch reactors were inoculated with 12% of the working 
volume (7.2 mL). Therefore to reach 2 L of final volume, 240 mL of initial inoculum volume 
would be necessary with the batch cultivation method. A volume that would provide with 
enough inoculum to start three fed-batch reactors under mesophilic conditions (70 mL used as 
starting inoculum). Observing that the time to reach the stationary phase for the fed-batch 
reactors (53 days) was 1.6 times longer than the time for the batch reactors to reach the 
stationary phase (32 days). It seemed more efficient to use the fed-batch cultivation method in 
order to get faster a large volume of inoculum. Furthermore, the TSS concentration inside the 
fed-batch reactors (140 mg.TSS.L-1) has been found to be higher than in the batch reactors (113 
mg.TSS.L-1), providing with a concentration 20% higher and therefore highlighting the benefits 
of the method. 
4.1.3 Fed-batch cultivation method optimization 
The fed-batch cultivation method was found to be a better cultivation method than the batch 
cultivation method under mesophilic conditions but this method has not provided maximum 
growth (80% of maximum theoretical methane production) and a long adaptation phase of the 
microorganisms was observed (lag phase length of 32 days). Moreover, the success of the fed-
batch cultivation method under mesophilic conditions was not replicated under thermophilic 
conditions, showing a difference in behaviours between the ATMC at different temperatures.

Feeding strategy  
As a potential explanation for the failure of the fed-batch cultivation method’s failure under 
thermophilic conditions and the not optimum performances of the fed-batch cultivation method 
under mesophilic conditions, the feeding strategy must be discussed.

The feeding strategy for the fed-batch reactors has been initially based on assumptions of 
initial cells concentration and maximum specific growth rate of the microorganisms. But, later 
on, these parameters were based on the measurements from the previous experiments (TSS 
concentration for initial cells concentration in the inoculum and maximum specific growth rate 
based on the growth of the culture). Even though, the fitting of these parameters created more 
stable conditions, preventing the microorganisms from being inhibited by a carbon source 
overload, the impact was not significant enough to identify the estimation of the kinetic 
parameters as a cause for not reaching maximum growth for the ATMC with the fed-batch 
cultivation method. 
To overcome the long lag phase observed with the fed-batch cultivation method. The 
exponential feeding strategy chosen at the beginning was adapted as described in the materials 
and methods part. This choice was made for two main reasons: the first one was based on the 
hypothesis that the changes in volume, temperature and composition inside the fed-batch 
reactors had a significant impact on the methanogenic growth and especially on the duration of 
the lag phase. The second one was based on the carbon to nitrogen (C/N) ratio, which was very 
low at the beginning of the experiment (0.01 compare to 0.30 at the end). The new strategy 
provided with a more constant C/N ratio in the fed-batch reactor (0.15 at the beginning to 0.22 
at the end). 
The C/N ratio ensured by the new feeding strategy remained low compared to the optimal 
C/N ratio in AD (15 to 20), but the late growth of the methanogens in the fed-batch reactors has 
proven that this factor was not a main cause of inhibition in this case. 
Sensitivity of the fed-batch cultivation method 
In a first time, it could be assumed that the delay in growth for the methanogens in the fed-
batch reactors was a consequence of a more changing environment: change in composition 
==PAGE:64==
50 
(ammonia concentration, acetate concentration), temperature, and bigger headspace volume 
containing potentially some oxygen. Furthermore, the bigger volume of cultivation in the fed-
batch reactors prevents the substrate and the methanogens from a good natural mixing. 
Reducing the availability of the carbon source for the methanogens (Lindmark et al., 2014). 
The results under thermophilic conditions have been underlining the temperature sensitivity 
of the process. Unable to overcome the lag phase, the thermophilic ammonia tolerant 
methanogenic consortia did not show any growth in a time frame comparable to the 
thermophilic batch reactors (Fig.13, B & D). Moreover, VFA measurements were performed 
45 days after the start of the fed-batch cultivation method under thermophilic conditions, and 
an acetic acid concentration close to 3 g.L-1 was found, meaning that the ATMC had not been 
consuming acetate at all.  
As stated by Treu et al. (2018), the mesophilic and thermophilic methnaogenic population 
operating in AD were very similar but very different in abundance. Therefore, it seems that the 
aceticlastic methanogens working under thermophilic conditions were more sensitive than the 
ones working under mesophilic conditions. More precisely, it was found by Konstantopoulos 
(2017) that the most abundant species in the thermophilic ATMC were Methanosarcina 
thermophile spp. and Methanothermobacter thermautotrophicus spp.. When the most abundant 
one in the mesophilic ATMC was Methanomassiliicoccus spp.. Methanosarcina thermophile 
spp. is an aceticlastic methanogens that has been found to be inhibited by hydrogen (Ahring et 
al., 1991a). However, the hydrogen content in the bottle was very low (under the detection limit 
of the gas chromatograph). Moreover, it was found that a cation such as Na+ could inhibit 
Methanosarcina thermophile spp.’s growth depending on the ratio between sodium and 
magnesium concentrations (Ahring et al., 1991b). It was found that a concentration of 0.5 
mol.L-1 of sodium would inhibit the growth of the methanogens in absence of magnesium. The 
==PAGE:65==
51 
 
feeding substrate was prepared with sodium acetate (NaHAc), and the final concentration of 
sodium in the fed-batch reactor was 0.65 mol.L-1 (calculated concentration).  
Therefore in a second time it could be assumed that the mass of sodium introduced in the 
thermophilic fed-batch reactors had an inhibitory effect on the methanogenic growth of 
Methanosarcina thermophile spp.. The other most abundant genera, Methanothermobacter 
thermautotrophicus spp., is a thermophilic methanogen using hydrogen to reduce carbon 
dioxide to methane (Martin et al., 2013). Therefore, it seemed that the inhibition caused by 
sodium on Methanosarcina thermophile spp.’s growth prevented it to produce methane and 
CO2 and was consequently preventing Methanothermobacter thermautotrophicus spp. from 
converting CO2 to methane. 
The composition of the cultivation media (BA medium) could also be questioned according 
to Hendriks et al. (2018). The requirements in trace metals and vitamins could be higher in fed-
batch cultivation methods and the lack of the necessary nutrients for growth would lead to an 
inhibition. This hypothesis was supported by Hendriks et al. (2018) when they affirmed that 
the thermophilic methanogenic consortia needed more trace metals than the mesophilic ones.

4.3 Experiment 3-Preservation of ammonia tolerant methanogenic consortia 
4.3.1 Primary comparison 
The methane production measurements from the batches started at day 0 presented in

shown a maximum yield having a value of 93% of the maximum theoretical yield.  
In their study, Scherer et al. (1981) also observed that the entrapment of methanogenic

The batches from day 1 have all shown a similar trend in their growth independently of the

For the batches started at day 7, clear differences between the different series of batches

period regardless of the temperature of preservation.  
The batches started at day 14 showed different results, the methanogens kept at 24°C had a 
faster growth regardless of the way they were preserved. The methanogens kept at 4°C had a 
longer lag phase (over 50 days while the methanogens kept at 24°C had a lag phase around 12 
days). From this batch assay, the effect of temperature on the preservation of the ATMC was 
noticeable. The ATMC preserved at 4°C had a lag phase almost four times longer than the ones 
preserved at 24°C (Table 15, Appendix G). Moreover, the high standard deviations of the 
methane production measurements of the ATMC preserved at 4°C proved the heterogeneous 
growth of these populations and therefore the harmful effect of the cold temperature on the 
ATMC. As found by Nozhevnikova et al. (2007), the methanogenic activity is inhibited at low 
temperatures. In this study it was especially found that the hydrogenotrophic methanogens were 
the most inhibited at low temperature when the aceticlastic methanogens had a better survival 
rate. In Konstantopoulos' (2017) study, it was found that the archea in the ATMC were mostly 
hydrogenotrophic methanogens except for the order Methanosarcina. Which are methanogens 
able to mediate both aceticlastic and hydrogenotrophic pathways. Therefore, the longer 
adaptation phase for the ATMC preserved a 4°C could be explained by this inhibition. 
Similarly, the batches started at day 28 have expressed the same results. The methanogens 
kept at 24°C had a faster growth than the ones kept at 4°C. But a new parameter showed its 
==PAGE:78==

influence, the methanogens kept in the liquid phase at 24°C have started their growth earlier 
than the methanogens immobilized in a gel at 24°C. However, Fig.22-Day 28 also shows that 
both the ATMC preserved in gel and liquid at 24°C reached the stationary phase of their growth 
at the same time (after 55 days). This phenomenon shows the better adaptation of the ATMC 
preserved in liquid than the ones preserved in gel. But the shorter exponential growth phase of 
the ATMC preserved in gel showed that the preservation of the cells in agar gel provided with 
a good preservation of the cells. In Fig.23-Day 28, it can also be seen that the ATMC preserved 
at 4°C had a delayed growth supporting again the hypothesis of the effect of the temperature 
on the methanogenic populations. The ones preserved in liquid at 4°C have started their 
exponential growth phase but the ones preserved in gel at 4°C were still in the lag phase of their 
growth. 
During the experiment of Karube et al. (1980), a slower methane production of the 
methanogens preserved in agar gel was observed compare to the regular liquid inoculum, and 
it was hypothesized that this phenomenon would be caused by the low diffusibility of the 
nutrients and the carbon source in agar gel. This low diffusibility would affect the transport of 
the nutrients to reach the cells but also the mixing of the cells in the feeding substrate and hence 
the availability of the carbon source. Therefore, the adaptation phase would be increased by the 
presence of agar gel. Lebrun and Junter (1993) studied the effect of the agar gel concentration 
in sucrose diffusion and it was found that the higher the concentration, the lower the 
diffusibility. The concentration of agar gel used in this experiment might have influenced the 
diffusibility of the substrate and therefore increased the lag phase length. Diffusion of salts in 
agar gel was studied by Schantz & Lauffer (1962), and it was found that the diffusion through 
agar gel was sensitive to temperature (lower diffusion at cold temperature) providing a 
hypothesis regarding the slower growth of the ATMC preserved at 4°C.

4.3.3 Influence of preservation time 
Fig.24, presents the methanogenic growth of the different series of batches according to the 
way of preserving the methanogens (media and temperature). A general trend showing a 
decrease in the growth velocity with time was observed. For the methanogens preserved in 
liquid phase at 24°C this trend existed even though all the series kept in these conditions showed 
a fast growth. It was even clearer for the ATMC preserved in different conditions (in a gel at 
24°C, in a liquid at 4°C and in a gel at 4°C).  
These results were in accordance with a previous study regarding methanogenic growth on 
acetate conducted in the same research group (Jéglot, 2018). During this study, it has been 
shown that the inoculum quality defined by its activity and its growth phase affected the 
velocity of its growth. The microorganisms just getting to the stationary phase of their growth

==PAGE:80==

were more likely to have a short lag phase before entering in the exponential growth phase 
when re-inoculated. Therefore, the time spent by the microorganisms in the incubators during 
the preservation period have triggered the death phase of the microorganisms. As a 
consequence, the lag phase of the batches started later (at days 7, 14, 28) was longer than the 
previous ones. Scherer et al. (1981) in their trial to immobilize Methanosarcina barkeri sp. in 
an ionic gel have also found a decrease in conversion rate of substrate over the days. In their 
study it was found that the conversion rate of methanol to methane was inversely proportional 
to the time spent by the pure methanogenic population in a gel for preservation.  
There was a clear difference between the batch started at day 0 and the ones started later. 
Therefore, it can be stated that the growth of the ammonia tolerant methanogenic consortia was 
affected by the preservation time. The immediate methanogenic activity after feeding of the 
preserved cultures have been decreasing (increase of lag phase length, Table 15 in Appendix 
G). Similar results have been found by Bhattad (2012) when assessing the revival of 
methanogens after preservation with heat-drying and freeze drying methods. During his phd 
thesis, Bhattad (2012) observed a significant methanogenic activity loss during the first two 
months of storage, thereafter the methanogenic activity decrease was very low.  
The effect of the time on the revival of the ATMC was obvious for the ones preserved at 
4°C and for the ones preserved in gel at 24°C. However, it was less noticeable for the ones 
preserved in liquid at 24°C, for these ATMC, only the batches started at day 28  showed a clear 
difference in their growth (longer time to reach the stationary growth phase). This result showed 
the limit of the preservation method (liquid at 24°C), underlining the necessity to find a better 
preservation method for the ATMC.

The standard deviation of some measurements was really high. For example the standard 
deviation of the measurement of the methane yield production of the batches containing gel, 
preserved at 4°C and started at day 28 was of 119 mLCH4.gVS-1 (60% of the measured value). 
This high standard deviation was due to the delay of some batches compared to others. This 
results questioned the assumption of same initial cells concentration and same impact of the

performing better than the average and some were performing less as seen in a previous study

terms of regularity (Fig.24) and adaptation (lag phase lengths, Table 15 in Appendix G). This

adaptability of the ATMC preserved in agar gel (reduced the duration of the lag phase), the 
preservation in gel would be more interesting than the preservation in liquid.

5 Conclusion 
The results of Experiment.1 showed: 
→ The fed-batch cultivation method was more effective under mesophilic conditions in 
providing with maximum biomass concentration in the best time frame (four times higher 
increase in volume of a more concentrated solution, 20% higher TSS concentration with the 
fed-batch cultivation method in a period 1.6 times longer).  
→ The fed-batch cultivation method has failed under thermophilic conditions. Therefore, the 
batch cultivation method was assessed to be more adapted under thermophilic conditions. 
The results of Experiment.2 showed:  
→ The up-concentration method of ATMC with forward osmosis has successfully increased 
the cells concentration in the inoculum (10 mgTSS.L-1.h-1, 29% TSS increase per hour for 
the maximum), reducing the volume of the solution (at a rate of 25 mL.hour-1) and therefore 
providing with an optimization of the cells concentration.  
→ The viability of the up-concentration method with forward osmosis has not been proven (no 
growth of the up-concentrated inoculum with forward osmosis). The up-concentration with 
centrifugation remains the most reliable method. 
The results of Experiment.3 showed:  
→ The preservation of the ATMC in agar gel was successfully conducted (90% revival). 
→ the ATMC preserved in at 24°C (gel and liquid) were the fastest grower and the one 
preserved at 4°C were the slowest grower, underlining the effect of temperature. 
→ The lag phase length of the ATMC preserved in liquid was shorter than the one preserved 
in agar gel. But the maximum specific growth rates of the ATMC preserved in agar gel were 
higher. Therefore, to use agar gel as a preservation method the long adaptation period 
should be overcome.

During the three experiments conducted during this study, the importance of the adaptation 
of the ATMC (lag phase) has been highlighted. With the fed-batch reactors having a longer 
adaptation time than the batch reactors during Experiment.1. With the late growth of the injured 
up-concentrated cells during Experiment.2. And with the delayed growth of the batch assay 
started at day 28 preserved and at 4°C during Experiment.3.

6 Future perspectives 
Potential improvements and directions to take to improve the research have been identified:  
Experiment.1 
→ An even better optimized feeding strategy could be determined.  
→ Other feeding methods for the thermophilic ATMC and maybe a new feeding media 
should be tested. Among others solutions, using chemicals without sodium to prepare 
the feeding substrate under thermophilic conditions should be tested. 
Experiment.2 
→ The optimization of the pumping rate in order to reduce the shear stress on the cells 
while having a good turbulent flow at the membrane should be performed.  
→ Other types of forward osmosis membranes should be tested in order to find the optimal 
one.  
→ The development of a model to compare the practical results with the theoretical 
modelling (improvement of the model). 
Experiment.3 
→ The observation of the impact of the preservation in a longer time frame should be 
conducted. Some bottles had already been prepared to be started day 84 and day 168. 
→ The diffusion of the feeding substrate through the gel should be assessed in order to 
understand the lag-delay phase phenomenon.  
→ An optimal agar gel concentration should be investigated in order to allow the 
immobilization of the methanogens and to optimize the nutrients diffusion through the 
gel.

Appendix F – Modelling of the tubular membrane with Comsol 5.3a 
A basic model was developed with the software Comsol 5.3a to model the exchange of ions 
from the draw solution to the feed solution during the membrane experiment. 
A simple geometry has been chosen to model the tubular membrane: 
→ 
An inner compartment, the feed. 
→ 
An intermediary layer, the membrane. 
→ 
An external layer, the draw. 
The concentrations and data used to calculate the theoretical flux were provided to the software. 
The next three pictures show the first results from the simulation. In the first picture, it can be 
seen an evolution in the feed solution ionic concentration, going from dark blue (1x10-4mol.m-
3) to turquoise (5x10-4mol.m-3). No change in the draw solution ionic concentration was 
observed, accordingly to the high existing concentration (1.28x10-3mol.m-3). The second one is 
showing the evolution of the concentration along the radius of the tubular membrane, high 
concentration in the draw solution and low in the feed solution with a fast change in 
concentration in the membrane. It should be noted that a high concentration is observed in the 
feed solution at the contact of the membrane. This effect could be due to an aggregation of the 
ionic molecules on the feed side of the membrane. The third picture shows the distribution of 
==PAGE:102==
88 
the concentration at the entrance of the feed solution. A gradient of concentration can be seen 
between the feed solution and the draw solution. The fourth picture is showing in the same way 
the distribution of the concentration at the exit of the feed solution.  
The difference between the two pictures highlights the diffusion of the ionic molecules 
through the membrane.
