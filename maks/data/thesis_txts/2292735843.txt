Preface 
This thesis has been developed over a period of 
months and is written as a final paper for the 
Master in Fire Safety (MiB)education at DTU. It 
is written as a research paper and primarily fo-
cus on developing the level of knowledge on the 
subject among colleagues, with a certain profes-
sional level of knowledge concerning the sub-
ject.  The paper is written in English, in order to 
have as many interested people reading it as 
possible. Due to the fact that the paper is writ-
ten in association with a Danish educational sys-
tem the decimal, and thousand marks is done 
accordingly to the Danish /European system.  
 
The pages are divided and margined accord-
ingly to the system described by Peter Stray 
Jørgensen in his book “Skriveklodsen,” pub-
lished by Byggeteknisk Højskole 1995.

retical background part, and the study part.   
 
The First part introduces the theory and ques-
tions which is of interest for the reader, descrip-
tion of the compartment, the basic models and 
validation of these models. 
 
The second part is exploring the different sce-
narios concerning fires in a compartment using 
the validated model.

Abstract 
During the last 40 years the requirements to 
save energy has grown steadily. The old build-
ing, primarily build during the late fifties and 
into the late seventies-beginning of the eighties, 
have been a result of the transformation of soci-
ety from a rural- to an urban based society. To-
gether with a growing living standard and trans-
formation of the old city centers from dense 
populated, into less dense populated areas, 
caused an explosive development of housing 
buildings in the suburban areas of the major cit-
ies and towns. These buildings are, at the mo-
ment on the basis of present political agenda, in 
the process of being refurbished and thereby 
given a more up to date energy profile. This 
means that these essentially concrete buildings, 
are being insulated. This post insulation of the 
perimeter of the buildings is presumably chang-
ing the way the fire develops and is thereby af-
fecting the structural basis of the buildings. 
Herby primarily the concrete slabs and the sup-
porting walls.  
 
In order to develop a more precise image of the 
consequence of a post insulation of the perime-
ter of the old buildings, the object is to set up a 
numerical model, which can create an image of 
the potential consequence of a possible fire in a 
compartment. 
 
The ventilation of the compartment is depend-
ing on the fall-out of the window, which is why 
an intense search for the parameters for the 
windows has been made. The results show so 
far, that the fall-out parameter is related to the 
temperature of the compartment gas and 
thereby the temperature of the glass. The model 
is made even more complicated, by the fact that 
the modern high insulating windows consist of 
multiple layers of glass with a diversity of gas 
between the glass panes. Furthermore, the size 
of the fall-out of the glass pane is happening in 
stages depending on the number of panes, and

Résumé 
During the last 2 ½ year, the participation in the 
MiB-education at DTU have brought me into a 
more diverse part of civil engineering. My prac-
tical background as skilled carpenter gives me 
an approach to problems and issues which is 
different from the one needed for the education 
at a University. 
 
Based on a note during lectures from professor 
Kristian Hertz it came to my attention that the 
breaking and fall-out of windows in a compart-
ment, must have an influence on the develop-
ment of a fire, and thereby on the bearing capac-
ity of the structure of the building. 
 
Based on that assumption, the idea was to de-
fine a synopsis, which somehow could develop 
parameters for the behavior of windows in a fire 
compartment. Preliminary research showed 
that the literature, did have a diversity of re-
search on windows, however, a clear view of an 
overall theory of the breakage and fall-out of 
windows were not available and scientifically 
proven. 
 
The creation of models has been going on since 
the start of the period, and several versions 
have been created and runned. To eliminate the 
possibility of a very complex set of results, the 
different final models have been created in 
steps, with small and defined differences, which 
have given me the possibility to focus on the sig-
nificant differences in the results that the vari-
ety of models have created. 
 
The transformation of the models into managea-
ble spread sheet files have been a task , due to 
the fact that when turning a corner, new and 
other analytical possibilities have revealed it-
self. The final work does show a lot of possibili-
ties to do further studies and further sophistica-
tions and development in the method, and ap-
proach to the issue.

1. Introduction 
Ever since the 1973 oil-crises, the legislation 
and building code in Denmark has been focusing 
on the reduction of energy consumption in the 
building structures in both the existing and in 
the new erections, knowing, that 40% of the 
consumed energy is used in housing. 
 
The requirement to focus on energy conserva-
tions has caused big investments in the public 
residential structures. During the last couple of 
years there has been invested considerable 
amount of money, in total more than 1 billion 
kroner, in giving these residential prospects a 
social modernization, combined with the focus 
on optimizing the energy consumption. 
 
These structures are in Denmark mainly made 
of precast concrete elements as bearing walls 
and precast concrete extruded hollow slabs. Due 
to the focus on low cost at the time of the origi-
nal erections of the buildings, all the structural 
elements have almost no spare bearing capacity. 
 
Focusing on fire in those structures there is a 
presumably fundamental change in the fire sce-
nario. The increase in insulation in primarily the 
facades, do somehow change the energy balance 
and the fire scenario is changing.  
 
The façade is consisting of a closed façade with a 
window. These surface areas would, during nor-
mal fire scenarios open up because the glass will 
fall out. When the windows are changed into en-
ergy preserving multilayer glazing, the behavior 
in the fire scenario is changing. 
 
The thermal reaction of the glazing areas is very 
different from prior single or double-glazing 
windows. This will assumedly have an effect on 
the behavior of the fire, the thermal develop-
ment; and the energy balance in the compart-
ment. Again, it will impact on the structure, and 
possibly reduce the bearing capacity of the

1.1. Background 
The environmental and socio-economical need 
to reduce the consumption of fossil energy, have 
put a political focus on the energy consumption 
of buildings in Denmark since the 1973 oil cri-
ses. This has led to stricter energy efficiency re-
quirements, concerning both the construction of 
new buildings and the refurbishment of old 
buildings. Not entirely irrelevant, considering 
that structural heating is consuming 40 % of all 
consumed energy.  
  
Due to higher insulation and double/multiple-
glazing insulating windows, the thermal and 
ventilation properties of low-energy buildings 
significantly differ from those of the old tradi-
tional buildings. These properties are described 
to have a significant effect on the fire and 
thereby the structure in the building (Petterson 
1976). 
 
As a consequence of a refurbishment, aimed at 
lowering the energy consumption of a building, 
increases the duration and the peak tempera-
ture of a fire that may trigger the compartment 
and exposes the structural elements to a higher 
thermal load than, what was considered at the 
time of design and erection. 
The increment in the fire duration is due to a 
higher resistance of the low-energy windows 
(Joshi & Pagni 1994c) that determines a lower 
ventilation of the compartment during fire. The 
increment of the peak temperature is due to a 
reduction of the thermal inertia consequent to 
the installation of insulating linings in the inte-
rior of the building. 
Neither of the two effects is taken into account 
when the building is verified according to the 
resistance classes prescribed by the Eurocodes 
(Dansk Standard 2007) , which have been cali-
brated on the basis of parametric fire in tradi-
tional compartments with higher ventilation 
and thermal inertia.

1.2. Scope 
The changes in structural behavior poses a seri-
ous issue on the actual safety of new and refur-
bished low-energy building of in case of fire and 
raises the need of resorting to more advanced 
tools for the fire designing of such buildings. 
 
In this respect, the use of computational fire dy-
namics software is becoming more and more 
common among fire engineers, due to the  
promising capability of accounting for aspects 
that cannot be considered in a simplified design, 
such as the variation of the ventilation condi-
tions during the fire. Furthermore during the 
last years it has been defined in many cases that 
evaluating a structure based on the standard 
fire indicates a calculations on the safe side, and 
thereby a financially more expensive way to 
build. 
 
Usage of a parametric fire has shown that it is 
on the safe side. Usage of a CFD/FDS model 
could be a possible way to obtain usable and 
more realistic and maybe even cheaper results.  
However, the validity of results obtained by CFD 
/FDS investigations is dependent upon a careful 
calibration and validation of the model, which 
requires high expertise numerical modelling 
and knowledge of complex fire dynamics phe-
nomena and fire-structure interactions(Hertz 
1999)

properties of compartment enclosures on the

research can be used in a structural analysis.

2.1.1. Fire development 
According to (B. Karlsson 2000) a fire develops 
in three stages.  
 
Stage 1: Growing phase 
Stage 2: Flash-over, steady phase 
Stage 3: Decay 
 
The development characterized by the  
development in energy release measured in kW. 
The energy release rate or Heat Release Rate 
(HRR) is measured as a steady release rate, with 
no limit to the amount of combustible materials. 
 
The intent of this paper is not to look at the ef-
fect of a fire on a structural construction. The 
main issue is to establish a basis for a numerical 
model in order to make it possible to evaluate a 
fire based on a numerical basis.

2.1.3. Fire development stage 2 
The steady phase is characterized by the equi-
librium between the two major materials partic-
ipating in the fire; oxygen and the fuel material.  
The equilibrium occurs, when the amount of 
fuel and the amount of oxygen reaches a level, 
where further development of the fire is not 
possible. This stage goes on, until the fuel is 
used and the fire dies out. The major influence is 
the ventilation of the room, in other words, the 
possibility for the fire to be nourished by oxy-
gen. Controlling the access of oxygen can be 
done by controlling the ventilation of the com-
partment. This is not a reliable factor, due to the 
fact that each apartment is inhabited by single 
individuals with different preferences to furni-
ture, and other combustible inventory.

2.2. Window 
2.2.1. Window/glazing history 
Windows have two main components: frame 
and glass pane. The focus is primarily on the 
glass pane. By using a standard supplier of win-
dows, it has been possible to define the win-
dow-glazing.  The windows mounted in the old 
buildings from around 1960, were 1 or maybe 2 
glass pane.  
 
The double pane was mostly coupled double 
framed windows. Two frames ventilated space 
in order to establish an insulating space in-be-
tween. Later in the 70ies the thermal double 
pane windows were introduced. The windows 
did have glancing bars within the window 
frames in order to divide the glass pane into 
subdivisions, where some were able to open 
and others were permanently closed. The win-
dows consisted mostly of 3 mm glass, some 2 
mm. These windows have a heat transmission 
coefficient of around 1,8-2,4 W/m2K 
 
Modern windows have developed over the last 
10 years and today the heat transmission coeffi-
cient is down to 0,65 W/m2K  .  These windows 
are triple layer with a noble gas (argon) in the 
space between the glass panes. The glass is 4 
mm, and the space varies but as a standard is 
chosen to be 14 mm. 
 
The area of the window frames are in a fire sce-
nario of minor importance. The frames and glaz-
ing bars hold on to the glass pane and ensures 
that they stay in the frame. The moldings that 
are placed to hold onto the glass pane are fixed 
to the frame. The moldings can be made of 
wood, but in more modern windows moldings 
made of plastic or aluminum can be seen. The 
moldings create a shadow to the class pane by 
covering the edges.   
 
New windows have a tendency to be subdivided 
in order to ensure smaller openings.

2.2.2. Glazing theory 
The literature has a reasonable amount of infor-
mation about the behavior of single sheet glaz-
ing. The major issues concerning the behavior of 
glass exposed to fire/energy is made by A.A. 
Joshi and P. J. Pagni (Joshi & Pagni 1994a). They 
have developed the basic theory, when and why 
the windows break. The major issue is to pre-
vent the windows to break because it will cause 
possible danger for the people on the ground, 
and because it will change the ventilation of the 
fire and change the development of the fire.  
In this case the idea is to determine the time for 
the breakage of the glazing and to determine 
when the fire can be expected to exhaust the ac-
cumulated energy, and prevent it from entering 
the structural system and go into the atmos-
phere instead. Skelly-Roby  experimental re-
search (Skelly 1991)report is showing that glaz-
ing is breaking from the edge and inwards. Each 
crack is developing into breaking patterns.

and radiation. The radiation is the most signifi-
cant reason for temperature change in the glaz-
ing pane. Primarily the fire itself causes the ra-
diation. A fire placed away from the glazing will 
cause less direct radiation to the pane compared 
to a fire up against the pane or close by.  
The radiation is inversely proportional to the 
square of the distance from the source, just like 
light and sound. 
 
Babrauskas (Babrauskas 2011) do mention that 
a high radiation is required to ensure a break-
age of the pane. He is referring to bush fire situ-
ation in Australia, and other places where a ra-
diation of 17,7 kW/m2 . The test results on dif-
ferent sizes of panes show a great variety in ra-
diation before the panes brake. This emphasizes 
the importance of a more specific analyzes of 
the parameters by which the single and multiple 
pane windows brake.  The conclusion in 
(Babrauskas 2011), that the tests have pre-
sented a radiation of 9kW/m2 for single glazing, 
25 kW/m2 for double glazing, and 43 kW/m2 for 
triple glazing. 
The major problem is that these tests are from 
external exposing (bush fires). 
 
The major reference to breaking of glace panes 
is by far Pagni (Cuzzillo & Pagni 1998) and 
Skelly (Skelly 1991)

The small glass panes do have a tendency in the

a glass pane is most likely to occur in a certain 
distance from the corners. This behavior is due 
to the fact that along the shaded areas the ten-
sion is uniaxial parallel to the edge, In the 
heated area and close to the shaded area the 
uniaxial stress is compression. These different 
behaviors will along the edges create difference 
in tensions, whereas around the corners the 
compression is isotropic and reducing to zero 
around the physical corners of the glass pane.

area, the present glazing is most likely a double 
pane window or even triple pane 
 
The double pane windows are described in 
(Cuzzillo & Pagni 1998) by Cuzzillo and Pagni. 
An abbreviated interpretation is made by 
(Maeso 2009) Maseo. By several test different 
issues have to be considered. In a multiple pane 
glass, the hollow space in between will, until 
one pane breaks, initiate a stop for radiation of 
heat, and delay the heating of the next glass 
pane. Furthermore the gas temperature in the 
gap will not increase to a level that will initiate a 
higher gas pressure, and thereby a static load on 
the glass pane.  Maseo concludes that there have 
been made a number of attempts to develop 
codes, aiming at a clear description of breakage 
of glass panes in a fire compartment. All of these 
models are based on a number of assumptions 
and which simplify the problem critically. 
 
The remarks by Maseo is supported by the theo-
retical analysis and the tests made by Cuzzillo 
and Pagni (Cuzzillo & Pagni 1998).

2.3.1. Structure history 
During the 60’ies and 70’ies a large amount of 
public buildings were built in the suburbs and 
surroundings of the main cities and towns of 
Denmark, to receive the populations that were 
moved out of the downtown highly dense resi-
dential areas. 
These new structures were built by the big en-
trepreneurs at that time; Larsen & Nielsen, Mon-
berg & Thorsen, Højgaard & Schultz, etc. Each of 
these entrepreneurs had structural building sys-
tems and conducted their own precast concrete 
element factories all around the country.  
 
Over a period of years the structural basis sys-
tem developed a basic concrete structure, con-
sisting of concrete bearing core around stair-
wells and concrete walls going from façade to 
façade. During this period the actual walls in-
side the apartments were made of concrete as 
the drywall technique was not yet adequately 
developed. Some of these walls were made of 
different kinds of concrete, which means walls 
with different kinds of aggregate and different 
density depending on the required bearing ca-
pacity. In the early structures, the walls were of 
a heavy density concrete. Later on in the 80’ies 
the light aggregate concrete was implemented.

2.4.3. Hydrodynamic model 
The solutions of FDS are based on the Navier 
Stokes equations, based on a low-speed, ther-
mally driven flow of smoke and heat from the 
fire. Based on a core algorithm with an explicit 
predictor corrector scheme, and a second order 
accuracy in both time and space. In a fire the 
turbulence is handled LES based on Sma-
gorinsky. LES is a default method of determin-
ing turbulence, instead of the more computa-
tional requiring DNS method. 
2.4.4. Combustion model 
FDS can calculate combustion models on the ba-
sis of multiple chemical reactions. In order to 
maintain a reachable computational model, the 
present calculation is based on a 2-step chemi-
cal reaction. The first reaction is the mass frac-
tion of unburned fuel and the second is the frac-
tion of burned fuel. The equilibrium between 
these two is the basis of the calculation. The 
combustion is in this assignment secondary to 
the development of the energy in the combus-
tion. 
 
2.4.5. Radiation transport 
Using a FVM (finite volume method), the pro-
gram is using a gray gas transportation equation 
and solution. The FVM is using approximately 
100 discrete angels. Including in the computa-
tion is the absorption derived from the absorp-
tion coefficient of the soot from the gas mix-
tures, using a narrow band model. The usage of 
absorption in droplets is not used, because the 
compartment is not assumed to have installed a 
sprinkler extinguishing system.

small opening factor, and it is often used as a 
basic opening factor if others are not chosen. A 
validation of a defined opening compared to the 
FDS LEAK function is not applied. A lower or no 
opening factor is of no interest because the fire 
will self-extinguish. 
 
Because the opening is to be considered as a 
leakage opening to other parts of an apartment 
the opening is shaped as a narrow door, with a 
height of 2,1 meter. This is to ensure that the air 
emerging along the floor is contained in the sim-
ulation, as well as the air close by the ceiling. 
 
The test-models do all have a given constant 
opening factor. In the study-models besides con-
stant low ventilation and opening factor the 
opening factor varies depending on the amount 
of breakage and fall-out of windowpane. The 
change in the glass fall-out is changing the open-
ing factor, and thereby also the amount of fresh 
air/oxygen nourishing the fire, and at the same 
time a greater chance for the exhaust smoke to 
get out. This gives a variable ventilation and 
thereby a changing consequence for the fire de-
velopment.  
 
Whether the fall-out of the glass will provide a 
greater capability of fresh air and a greater ca-
pability of exhaust of smoke is not possible to 
decide with great accuracy, because it all de-
pends on at what horizontal level the breakage 
of the window occur. 
 
A breakage in the top provides the possibility 
for a greater exhaust which is most likely, where 
as a breakage in the bottom of the window 
leaves a possibility of an inlet of fresh air. The 
exhaust of smoke and inlet of fresh air will cre-
ate a change in neutral level in the compart-
ment.

3.1. Method: 
The analysis divides into 2 main sections. For 
this purpose, a number of models in CFD has 
been created.  
 
Study 1 is an investigation by the means of the 
validated model, of the compartment behavior 
to a fire before and after the energy refurbish-
ment of the compartment. The assumption is a 
investigations without a breakage / fall-out of 
the glass pane of the compartment 
 
Study 2 is an investigation of the compartment 
behavior with a variety of window fall-out both 
in size of window and of the size of the fall-out 
and at what time the fall out occurs.  
 
Because of the prior validations it has been nec-
essary to approach the problem differently than 
assumed at the start of the project. 
 
The data’s obtained by CFD is imported into a 
spreadsheet program by the means of a comma-
separated file.  
 
The data consist of temperature, smoke height, 
and HRR (Heat release rate). 
 
Temperature and smoke-height data is in each 
model by the CFD program registered in 1000 
measurements and is then subdivided into 100. 
The subdivision is done by taking the average of 
10 measurements. This gives a manageable 
number of data, and because it is an average it is 
considered to be scientifically valid. 
 
The development of each measuring column is 
then set up into a graph, showing the tempera-
ture development in each column. 
 
The smoke-height is set into a graph and the de-
velopment is shown over time.
