Abstract
Superluminous Supernovae (SLSNe) are a poorly understood group of supernovae (SNe)
that are known for its very bright explosions, although it is not known what causes them.
By studying their host galaxies we may be able to constrain the progenitors of the SLSNe.
In this thesis we study 71 host galaxy spectra of 59 SLSN host galaxies spectroscopically
by measuring emission line fluxes and computing metallicities and star formation rate
(SFR). We then compare the SLSN host galaxies by type and with GammaВӯray burst
(GRB) host galaxies, extreme emission line galaxies (EELGs), and large surveys such
as COSMOS and SDSS. We divide the study into three regions of redshift: z < 0.3,
0.3 < z < 0.8 and 0.8 < 2.05. We find that SLSN type I hosts are generally low mass
and low metallicity with a dwindling specific star formation rate (SSFR) as redshift grows,
coming to resemble common galaxies of their size, although at a high ionisation level.
SLSN type II hosts are generally more massive and of higher metallicity than their type I
counterpart. However, at high redshifts most emission lines are nonВӯdetections, leaving
very little data to study. Nothing can conclusively be said about high redshift SLSN host
galaxies without procuring more spectra with detections.

Introduction
Superluminous Supernova (SLSN)Вӯe are a relatively new class of transients discovered
during this century. Compared to traditional Supernova (SN), which have been known for
a long time, these are many times brighter. However, SLSNe are poorly understood and
there is no established model for how they work. Since they are predominantly found in
galaxies with specific properties, their environment must be a defining factor in how they
develop Вӯ otherwise, we would find most of them where we find most stars [1].
SLSNe are thought to be related to the death of very massive stars. By studying these
explosions we may be able to understand the massive stars and their environment even
better and how different galactic environments lead to different stellar evolutions. As these
explosions are very bright, we will be able to see them from very far away. Using this, we
can study the distant universe and even use SLSNe to find distant galaxies, that possess
the right qualities [2].
By understanding the host galaxies of the SLSNe, we may be able to understand what
causes them and drives their intense luminosities. Numerous studies have been conВӯ
ducted studying the host galaxy environments of SLSNe. One such study Leloudas et al.
(2014), henceforth referred to as SUSHIESВӯI [3]. In this study they measured emission
line fluxes from 23 SLSN host galaxies and computed metallicities and Star Formation
Rate (SFR) for them. They concluded, that many SLSN type I host galaxies occur in ExВӯ
treme Emission Line Galaxies (EELGs), a class of galaxies known for having very strong
line fluxes, high SFR and low metallicity. These conditions are even more extreme than
those galaxies which are known to harbor GammaВӯray burst (GRB). SLSN type II, on the
other hand, occur in more massive, more metalВӯrich galaxies.
This study is meant as a followВӯup study to SUSHIESВӯI. We have obtained 71 host galaxy
spectra from 59 different hosts and recreate the analysis from SUSHIESВӯI in order to study
if the same conclusions are valid as the data sample grows. As an addition to the analysis
in SUSHIESВӯI, we split the analysis in this study into three redshift bins to study how the
SLSN host galaxies develop with redshift: z < 0.3, 0.3 < z < 0.8, 0.8 < z < 2.05.
We start by presenting some theory in chapter 2 regarding SLSNe, their hosts, how to do
spectroscopy, what we measure with spectroscopy, and how we carry out our modelling.
Then in chapter 3 we present a short review of three papers whose work are relevant for
this study. In chapter 4 we then present the entirety of the data that we work with in this
analysis, where it comes from, and what it is. We then introduce in chapter 5 how we
apply our theory and go from data to results. Chapter 6 describes some main results,
which are then discussed in chapter 7.
Like for SUSHIESВӯI, we conclude that SLSN type I host galaxies are primarily lowВӯmass
and lowВӯmetallicity. When looking at their evolution in redshift, they maintain the lowВӯ
metallicity condition, but their Specific Star Formation Rate (SSFR) gradually comes to
resemble most other galaxies, including those of GRBs, although still highly ionised. This
indicates that metallicity is a stronger requirement than SSFR for the progenitors. HowВӯ
ever, it is difficult to say anything conclusively, as the higher redshift bins are mainly popВӯ
ulated by host galaxies with undetected emission lines.
We adopt a Planck 2018 cosmology throughout this study [4]. All errors reported are 1Пғ.

2.1
Superluminous Supernovae
Supernovae have been known for a long time. Chinese astronomers write about вҖҷguest
starsвҖҷ as far back as 185 AD [1], and later Tycho Brahe wrote extensively about the вҖҷnew
starвҖҷ of 1572 [5]. Around the turn of the millennium an established explanation of superВӯ
novae was in existence and supernovae were divided into two major types depending on
the presence (type II) or nonВӯpresence (type I) of hydrogen lines in their spectrum (see
figure 2.1 for the schematics of spectral classification). Type Ia occur when a white dwarf
in a binary system accretes sufficient mass from its companion that it surpasses the ChanВӯ
drasekhar limit and a runaway thermonuclear reaction happens [6]. Type II, type Ib, and
type Ic are believed to come from the death of stars above 8Mвҳү. These are commonly
called coreВӯcollapse supernovae as the violent death of the star comes from the nuclear
reactor in the core having turned all of its fuel into stable, and thus it can no longer exВӯ
tract energy to support itself against its gravitational forces leading it to collapse into a
neutron star or black hole and release its gravitational energy [6, 1]. Both type I and type
II supernovae release вүҲ1051 erg in kinetic energy and peak with a luminosity of up to
Lpeak = 109 Lвҳү[1].

nous supernovae, which have a preference for dwarf galaxies [3, 1], and were thus not
seen when focusing on the massive galaxies. Superluminous supernovae have a peak
luminosity of about Lpeak = 1010 Lвҳү, hence the name superluminous supernovae.

2.1.1
Powering the SLSN
Studies suggest that SLSNeВӯI are associated with the death of very massive stars and
their decay powered by either radioactive isotopes or compact objects formed after their
explosions, yet there is no single accepted explanation for what is powering a SLSN as
all models fails to thoroughly explain the observed properties of SLSN [2]. A short introВӯ
duction to various explanations of the SLSNe mechanisms follows here.
Figure 2.5: A table showing the different models for superluminous supernovae. Taken
from [2]
Central engine
The central engine explanation usually prescribe a magnetar as the origin of the large
amounts of energy released through a SLSN. Magnetars are strongly magnetized and
rapidly spinning neutron stars that release energy to the surrounding medium as it spins
down [2].

Out of all the main explanations this one requires the smallest mass of the progenitor star.
PairВӯinstability
For very massive stars well above 100 Mвҳүand low metallicity [7] it is possible that the
temperature in the core during oxygen burning becomes high enough that some photons
convert into electronВӯpositron pairs. This leads to a reduction in radiation pressure, and
thus a core contraction, resulting in an increase in temperature and density in the core
igniting the oxygen. The process is a runaway thermonuclear process and the star exВӯ
plodes as a pairВӯinstability supernova. In the explosion very large quantities of radioactive
56Ni is produced, which leads to the enormous luminosities observed for SLSNe [2].
Pulsational pairВӯinstability
In a variant of the pairВӯinstability supernova the production of electronВӯpositron pairs can
occur without a runaway process that terminates the entire star. Instead several incomВӯ
plete explosions occur as the star sheds its outer layers in pulses, hence the name. This
creates a circumstellar medium for the supernova to interact with to drive the high obВӯ
served luminosities [2].
2.1.2
SLSN hosts
No matter what the mechanisms behind the SLSNe are it must be linked to the host galaxy
in some wayНҫ SLSNe are rare events, but we do not see them where most of the stellar
mass is, indicating that the very nature of the host galaxies is intrinsically linked to the
SLSNe occuring [1].
SLSNe are usually found in dwarf galaxies, galaxies with mass between 107 вҲ’109 Mвҳү[9],
and especially in host galaxies with a high starВӯformation rate (see section 2.4) and low
metallicities [1, 3, 7]. A range of SLSN host galaxies can be seen in figure 2.6. It can be
seen that they are mostly irregular in shape and small galaxies. About 50 % of SLSN host
galaxies have a companion of similar size, which is expected to increase star formation
as they interact [10].
About 50 % of SLSNВӯI host galaxies occur in extreme emission line galaxies, which is a
type of lowВӯmass, metalВӯpoor, intensively starВӯforming galaxy with strong emission lines
and hard ionising radiation fields [3]. These galaxies are quite distinct from the regular
galaxy main sequence, so the odds of that connection being by chance is negligible [3].
The presence of strong ionising radiation fields may impact progenitor evolution [3, 1].
Only about 2 % of star formation occurs in galaxies with a metallicity < 0.5 Zвҳү, despite
SLSNeВӯI being found almost exclusively in galaxies adhering to that limit. This is a great
indication that the host galaxies are important to study in order to understand SLSNe [1,
11]. Host galaxy environments can provide strong constraints on the progenitors for the
events. Since pairВӯinstability SN should only be produced by very massive stars with a
low initial metallicity, we would expect host galaxies of PISN to be low metallicity and
preferably experiencing starbust in order to create satisfactory progenitor stars that we
can observe the death of [7]. The central engine model is similar to the one used to
explain gammaВӯray bursts, so its credibility as a model for SLSNe would increase if we
observe similarities between GRB host galaxies and SLSNe host galaxies [7].

2.2.2
Equivalent widths
Equivalent widths, as noted before, is a measure of line strength in comparison to the
underlying continuum. While a strong line in itself may not matter much if over a strong
continuum, a strong equivalent width indicates that a large fraction of energy is carried in
that specific emission line. This can be used to say something about the conditions of the
galaxy that exhibits a strong equivalent width of an emission line.
If the host galaxy spectrum is contaminated in some way, e.g. by a supernova, it may
be difficult to determine the underlying continuum. In those cases it will be difficult to fit
an equivalent width to an emission line. It is most likely a lower limit on the equivalent
width because the actual continuum will have a lower flux than a continuum with an added
contaminating source.
EELGs are named so because of the extreme emission lines they possess. These show
up as strong equivalent widths, with WО»5007 > 100 AA being one criteria to use [3]. These
galaxies are lowВӯmass, metal poor, and very starВӯforming galaxies with a hard ionising
radiation field [3]. The extremity of these properties is what causes their equivalent widths
to be so strong.

extended objects. In addition, any instrumental features are also subtracted when using
NODВӯmode [26].
Observations are handled independently in each arm and do not have to have the same
exposure times. It is fairly common procedure to have four 300 s observations in the NIR
for every 1200 s observation in the VIS and UVB. This is done because the sky in the
NIRВӯspectrum range is changing rapidly in the order of minutes and thus has to be cut
short in order to get a good sky background, while the UVB and VIS prefer to get as large
an integration time as possible [26].
For the reduction of XВӯshooter spectra through the ESO Reflex pipeline see 5.1.
ADC
Part of the XВӯshooter instrument is the atmospheric dispersion compensator (ADC) in the
UVB and VIS arms. These units compensate for atmospheric dispersion and keep the
trace centered. Unfortunately, they were disabled between 2012Вӯ08Вӯ01 and 2017Вӯ05Вӯ01
[27]. This means that observations made between these two dates can be affected.

wavelength bin in order to estimate the combined flux of the line. We sum using the

SUSHIESВӯI
SUSHIESВӯI [3], titled Spectroscopy of superluminous supernova host galaxies. A preferВӯ

closely associated with. It studies SLSN host galaxies and measures emission line fluxes

compared with galaxies from the Sloan Digital Sky Survey (SDSS).
Through the study they show that SLSNВӯR and SLSNВӯI do not have different host galaxy
properties, but combined they differ significantly from the other types of galaxies, except

then their origins are probably different than those of SLSNeВӯI. Furthermore, they propose
that HВӯpoor SLSNe result from the very first stars exploding following a period of high star
formation rate.

5.1
ESO Reflex pipeline
The journey of a host galaxy being used in this study starts with downloading the raw
spectra and associated raw calibration files. The raw spectra usually consists of 12 obВӯ
servations Вӯ 4 observations in NIR, UVB, and VIS done in NODВӯmode in positions ABBA.
The raw spectra are then filtered through a program, courtesy of Steve Schulze, that reВӯ
moves cosmic rays in the spectra. Figure 5.1 shows how the UVB spectrum of IPTF14DEK
looks at this point. It is in the form of arcs of light across the image. It is this type of image
that must be reduced down to a 1DВӯspectrum for us to work with.
This happens through the ESOREFLEX pipeline [38]. The pipeline is shown in figure 5.2.
From starting in the upper left and moving to the lower right the pipeline reads the raw
science images, finds the relevant auxiliary data, calibrates the measurements, divides
the target into object and background spectrum, and outputs a 2DВӯspectrum as well as a
lot of metadata. The 2DВӯspectrum is what we are interested in.
Figures A.2, A.3, and A.4 in section A.2 show some of the calibrations that the ESOREFLEX
pipeline goes through. Each of these steps are interactive and allow for adjusting caliВӯ
bration parameters, e.g. how many times it should iterate over the wavelength calibration
[26].
Having calibrated the images and created a model that translates between image coВӯ
ordinates to a rectangular spectrum it is time for the last interactive window where the
spectrum is divided into the object spectrum and the background sky spectrum as in figВӯ
ure 2.8. Figure 5.3 shows the window for NODВӯmode and figure 5.4 shows the window
for STAREВӯmode. After this step, the reduced 2DВӯspectra are saved.
The NIRВӯspectra and any accompanying starВӯspectra to help with telluric corrections are
reduced in NODВӯmode, while the UVBВӯ and VISВӯspectra are reduced in STAREВӯmode.
Reducing them in STAREВӯmode means we imagine each of the observations in positions
ABBA are independent observations and reduce them as such. By combining them afterВӯ
wards, we can improve the SNR by at least a factor
вҲҡ
2 and we also improve the spectrum
by better removing bad pixels, instrumental defects, and manually defining the trace and
sky background regions. The reason we only do this for the VISВӯ and UVBВӯspectra is
because there is no real improvement by doing it for the NIR Вӯ NODВӯmode already does
a great job of correction sky emission lines and sky variations in this band [26, 27].
As the NIRВӯspectra have been reduced in NODВӯmode, there is only one spectrum generВӯ
ated per observation run and it is ready to have a 1DВӯspectrum extracted from it. The UVBВӯ
and VISВӯspectra, however, have been split into a spectrum for each observation position
in NODВӯmode, so they each have 4 spectra each. These will have to be combined.
This combination is handled by a program called XSHComb.py written by Jonatan Selsing.
It takes in all 4 spectra in each band and converts them to a single spectrum per band.

5.2
Correcting Redshift
In order for the fitting algorithm to perform better it is important that it receives are reasonВӯ
able initial guess. This is especially important if it is a noisy spectrum, as it can be led to
believe a random fluctuation is an emission line without fitting the actual line. The most
important aspect of the initial guess is the position of the line Вӯ and that position depends
on the redshift of the line as the lines are fitted in observerframe (see section 5.4).
Many of the redshifts reported for SLSNe host galaxies are reported with a precision of
3 to 4 decimals. However, in order to hit the emission lines on target when it comes to
XВӯshooter spectra, it is often necessary to go to 5 decimals. This has another benefit Вӯ
with a better precision on the position of the emission line centre the direct integration
integrates more of the actual, physical line. This leads to more useful results for the direct
integration.
Thus there are two different useful improvements by using more precise redshifts for the
host galaxies. However, as these do not exist, we have to get them ourselves.
To do this we develop a simple code. When we consider a spectrum we can plot where the
emission lines would be considering a given redshift. Assuming we know the redshift to
a precision of вҲҶz = 0.001 we can then search for any higher flux value within вҲҶz = 0.001
to either side of where the line would be with the reported redshift. If there is one, we can
then compute what the corresponding redshift is if we believe that peak of flux to be the
emission line. Figure 5.9 shows an example of a redshift that has been corrected in this
way.
By doing this for all the emission lines used in this analysis we can average over the
computed redshifts for the different lines in order to determine a redshift with a higher
precision. The computed redshifts are shown in table 5.1. Only host galaxies where the
fitter had problems finding the lines or where the direct integration integrated over an area
that was clearly not centered on the emission line centre have had their redshifts updated.
One source Вӯ PS1Вӯ12BMY Вӯ had its redshift determined by looking for lines in the 2DВӯ
spectrum in DS9 and subsequently putting it into the above algorithm to get a more precise
redshift.
It should be stressed that the computed redshift values in table 5.1 are only useful for this
analysis. In order to be taken as accurate redshift values a much higher precision in the
position of the emission lines would be required.

5.4
Line Fitting Analysis
Having procured a spectrum for a host galaxy, updated its redshift, and found the total
extinction parameter the line fitting can commence.
For each host galaxy spectrum we are interested in the flux and error values for each
wavelength bin. If no error spectrum is associated with the flux spectrum, then the error
spectrum is assumed to be 10% of the flux spectrum.
5.4.1
DeВӯreddening the spectrum
If the spectrum has been reduced as in section 5.1, then its flux will already have been deВӯ
reddened for Milky Way extinction. For spectra that have not been reduced through this
study, such a correction would have to be applied to the spectra. We use a CardelliВӯlaw
with RV = 3.1 [23, 22] and deВӯredden the spectra in observerframe using the extinction
parameter found from the NED database.
5.4.2
The fitting regions
We then divide the spectrum into three regions, if applicable. For each of these regions
100 AA is added to either side of the outermost lines in order to get some continuum
around the lines:

length range of 4761 Вӯ 5107 AA. We dub this region вҖҷO3HBвҖҷ.

5.4.3
Fitting the regions
Before each region is fitted a mean value of the flux is computed for the region. The
spectrum is then normalized with this value. This is done because most flux values would
be in the range of 10вҲ’17 erg sвҲ’1 cmвҲ’2 AAвҲ’1. This could interfere with the fitter depending
on how sensitive it is to small values. By normalising the spectrum with the mean we fit
in a region with values of approximately 1. After the fits have been found all parameters
are then scaled back again with the same normalising constant. All fitting happens in the
observerframe.
O3HB
We start by fitting the O3HB region. This region is the first to be fitted because it is present
in each host galaxy spectrum and contains a strong line in the shape of [OIII]О»5007.
We construct a model based on a line (the continuum) and three Gaussians of equal
width as we assume the line spread function is equal over the entire region. The conВӯ
tinuum and the three emission lines are then fitted together maintaining the same width.
[OIII]О»5007 acts as the base line meaning that the positions of the other two line centres
are constrained to be a fixed interval from the centre of this line.
The fitter is quite sensitive to the initial guess. It is therefore important that the initial guess
for parameter values are reasonable. Using the redshift we provide a guess at where the
line positions would be and it is assumed that the highest peak in the region belongs to
[OIII]О»5007. [OIII]О»4959 is then assumed to be 1
3 of this peak and H ОІ 1
2 of this peak. All
emission lines are assumed to have a spread of 1 AA originally.
Every plot showing the fit to the lines will also show the initial guess. This is done to show
if the fit seems reasonable based on the information available before fitting.
N2HA
If the spectrum goes enough into the red end that these lines are present then they are
next to be fit. Like above, a model is constructed based on a continuum line with three
Gaussians of equal width on top of it. Here H Оұ makes up the base line. It is assumed that
the highest peak in the region belongs to H Оұ and that it is located where the redshift of
the host galaxy would put it. The [NII]Вӯlines are then constrained to be a fixed wavelength
interval from the H Оұ line.
From the fit of the emission lines in the O3HB region a first estimate of the line spread
in this region can be extracted. However, as this region exists in a different wavelength
range, the line spread is allowed to change compared to this value. It is still constrained
to be the same for all emission lines within a region.
The [NII]Вӯlines are generally weak, so by fitting them along with the generally strong H Оұ
line the constraints can help find reasonable fits for the lines because they have to be a
fixed distance from the H Оұ line and they have to have the same line spread function since
they are so close together.
O2
If the spectrum is blue enough this region is the next to be fit. The [OII]Вӯlines are a doublet
that only shows up if the resolution of the spectrograph is strong enough to resolve them.
This is the case for XВӯshooter spectra, so any spectrum that have been reduced in this

5.4.5
Limit detection and direct integration
Next step is then determining if the emission lines we have detected are actual detections.
We count them as being detections if they rise above a 2Пғ significance level based on the
integrated error spectrum underneath the emission line.
For each emission line we model the surrounding continuum as a line. We then subtract
the line from the actual spectrum, leaving us with a spectrum located on the xВӯaxis with
the emission lines rising above it just as strong as in the original spectrum.
From the fitting we have an estimate of the spread of the emission line. We use this
spread to determine the width of the emission line. In order to get as much flux from the
line as possible but without incorporating the noisy tails we integrate over the area defined
by 2 Г— Пғl to either side of the line centre, with Пғl being the spread of the Gaussian fit to
the line. This means we operate with 95.45 % of the flux of the line. Figure 5.12 shows
an example of such an area.
Within this same area we also integrate the associated error spectrum. In order to get
a 2Пғ limit, we multiply the value with 2. This gives us a limit on the flux that has to be
detected in order for an emission line to count as a detection. It can be helpful to consider
how the error spectrum looks in comparison to the continuumВӯsubtracted spectrum in the
region of a line. Such an example is shown in figure 5.13.
We can then compare the limit on the flux for a line with both the line flux computed from
the fitting and from directly integrating underneath the emission line. If the fitted value is
above the limiting value, then we have a detection. It can be helpful to look at the directly

7
Discussion
7.1
Line fitting comparison
When comparing the line fluxes and fitting we see that we get a generally good overlap,
indicating that the analysis used in this study is valid to compare the results with. There
were of course some cases where the lines fluxes were different, but it is not necessarily
indicative of any fitting being done wrong, just that they are not fit the same way. Seeing
the figures in section A.4 and A.5 clearly shows that even in the biggest discrepancies,
the lines are fit quite well to the spectrum.
One of the primary reasons the fits are different is that here only three models are fit Вӯ one
for each wavelength region. Compared to an approach where most lines are fit individВӯ
ually, the approach followed here is more constrained. Some of the biggest differences
between the fits reported here and those reported in Perley et al. (2016) [7] are from HОІ
and [OIII]О»4959, both of which in this analysis are constrained by the location and strength
of [OIII]О»5007. One could argue that this approach will provide results that gives more
meaningful metallicity results for our study, as we force the [OIII]Вӯlines to match case B
recombination ratios if they deviate to much from them.
7.2
Redshift bins
In this analysis we have decided to split the analysis into three different redshift bins,
z < 0.3, 0.3 < z < 0.8, and 0.8 < z < 2.05, and draw conclusions based on each redshift
bin. Due to the limited number of host galaxies within each bin in it not unreasonable to
think that the results may look slightly different if we compared e.g. z < 1 to z > 1 instead.
We opted for three bins in this study. The three bins would allow us to study how the SLSN
host galaxies evolve with redshift, while still maintaining a sufficient number of hosts in
each redshift bin. The borders of the redshift bins are determined by each being 1/3 of
the lookback time of the highestВӯredshift object in our sample. This means that in each
bin the highest and lowest redshift galaxies will have had the same amount of time pass
between them before the light reaches us. While this does not necessarily say anything
about the age of the host galaxy, it does set a limit on the maximum age difference, and
thus evolution, that can go into each redshift bin.
7.3
Multiple spectra for one host
We have 71 spectra distributed over 59 different SLSN host galaxies. This means that
we have 12 spectra Вӯ one sixth of the sample Вӯ that belong to a host galaxy that is already
part of the analysis. Assuming all spectra from the same host galaxy are good, we would
expect the same intrinsic properties to be measured again. There is therefore a slight
bias in our data, that could have been avoided by selectively choosing one spectrum per
host galaxy.
There may still be some use in having two different spectra from different locations in the
same host galaxy. If we have a spectrum taken at the site of the SLSN explosion, and it
is resolved, we can probe the very local environment that led to the SLSN. However, for
many host galaxies we can only study the entire thing at once, getting an average value.
By having a spectrum of a host galaxy at both the location of the explosion and a more
general location, both can support the knowledge of where they occur.

7.4
Analysis interpretation
Considering table 6.5, we can see why we may not just adopt the test results as the truth
right away Вӯ in many comparison, we are dealing with very few objects. SLSN type II and
EELGs are compared in metallicity once, and thatвҖҷs with 2 galaxies in one of the samples.
Looking at the figures, we can see that these two typeВӯII SLSN hosts are placed right in
the middle of the EELGs cluster. However, when we consider those that are limited, they
seem to be placed and for metallicity also pointing away from the cluster. So despite the
test saying they come from similar samples, the figures tell us that that is not necessarily
true.
An interesting thing to note in the AndersonВӯDarling tests is that SLSN type I host galaxies
seem to become similar to the GRB sample as we go towards higher redshifts, although
only in SSFR.
The AndersonВӯDarling test for M91 and log(q) suffers from two problems other than the
sample size: one is that the value in many cases is the average of the two branch values,
muddying the values a bit. Another is that, unlike for the SSFR, the host galaxy masses
are not considered when comparing them. As metallicity is expected to grow with the
mass of a galaxy, and our SLSN type I host galaxies are generally lower mass compared
to the others, there is an artificial gap in the metallicities causing them to appear more
different that would be at similar masses.
Looking at figure 6.3 we can visually inspect how the SLSN type I host galaxies change
over the range of redshifts. At higher redshift, they generally have lower SSFR, and they
move closer to the isoline going through the centre of the main galaxy cluster. Metallicity,
on the other hand, does not seem to develop in the same way, no matter the scale (see
figure 6.4 and figure 6.6). Figure 6.8 shows that as redshift grows, the metallicity becomes
comparatively smaller than what would be expected if it had been a galaxy on the main
branch and 6.7 shows that they are generally quite ionised. We report a similar trend to
[11] that almost all type I SLSN host galaxies have Z < 0.5Zвҳү.
Combined with the median values in table 6.3 from our analysis, it seems to point towards
metallicity being an important parameter in all redshift bins to have a type I SLSN explode Вӯ
metallicity remains consistently at a similar value, with a much smaller evolution compared
to the main branch. The specific star formation rate, though, falls off higher redshift,
indicating that it is probably not a main driver of what leads to the SLSN type I progenitors
at high redshift.
Since log(OIII/HОІ) > 0.5 for most of our SLSN type I host galaxies, it indicates that we
are dealing with young starВӯforming regions [31], and this seems to mostly stick through
the redshift bins.
Having strongly ionised galaxies that maintain that level of ionisation through the redshift
bins, but drop off in SSFR, could indicate that we are dealing with very large stars forming
at a higher ratio compared to smaller stars. There is evidence that for higher redshift, we
expect a topВӯheavier Initial Mass Function (IMF) [42]. This could perhaps explain what
we see Вӯ moving into higher redshift, we still create massive stars, even though the SFR
is lower. This points towards the SLSN progenitors being massive stars and the exact
relationship of how massive depending on the IMF.

SLSNe
GRBs are generally found to the right of our type I SLSN host galaxies when plotted vs.
mass. They appear to have weaker ionising fields, higher metallicity, and lower SSFR,
although especially the last one appears to change at higher redshifts.
SLSNВӯII hosts occur in different environments than HВӯpoor SLSNe: more massive,
more metalВӯrich and with softer radiation fields
By comparison with our figures, we see that we get similar results. Type II hosts are
generally heavier, have a higher metallicity (independent on the scale), and a lower log(q)
along with being placed further down on the BPTВӯdiagram.
7.4.2
Equivalent widths
One of the primary points of SUSHIESВӯI dealt with Equivalent Width (EW) widths of the
emission lines of the SLSN host galaxies. The EW translates into ionisation of the galaxy
and its age. Unfortunately, when fitting the EW, they returned values quite different to
those reported in SUSHIESВӯI. Considering they operate on the same spectra, they should
be fit similarly, and if they do not, they are not a meaningful comparison. The equivalent
widths are therefore not considered further in this study, but the values fitted for WО»5007
and WО»6563 can be seen in section D.
7.5
Further work
As could be seen in the analysis, we generally do not have many SLSN host galaxies in
the highest redshift bin. What makes it even worse is that many host galaxies emission
lines are limited in the higher redshift bins. This means it is rather difficult to say something
conclusively about the galaxy evolution with redshift.
Much of it stems from the [NII]О»6584 not being detected, even though it is fit together with
HОұ. This specific line is used in the N2 and O3N2 metallicity calibrations, which should
A spectroscopic analysis of superluminous supernova host galaxies
71
==PAGE:80==
be great to use at high redshifts, because they eliminate an uncertainty in the host galaxy
reddening, but it is also used to break the degeneracy of the R23 metallicities. Not having
this line detected means that it becomes very difficult to estimate the metallicity of the host
galaxy, unless another method is used. Some of this problem can be mitigated by allowing
a lower limit for a detection, but if conclusive spectroscopic studies of high redshift SLSN
host galaxies are desired, better spectra and / or spectrum reductions are needed. We
saw in section 5.1.1 that a different approach to reducing spectra can mean a difference
between observing an emission line or not.
This analysis specifically could benefit from an equivalent width analysis. This would
enable a much better comparison with the EELGs sample and would also look further
into whether the conclusions from SUSHIESВӯI are the same when we expand the sample
from 23 host galaxies to 59 host galaxies.

8
Conclusion
In this thesis we have presented a spectroscopic analysis of 59 SLSN host galaxies with
a combined 71 spectra covering a wavelengthВӯrange within the atmospheric transmission
window. Some of these spectra have been obtained from earlier spectroscopic samples Вӯ
being covered in papers like [3, 7] Вӯ and some spectra have been reduced as part of this
thesis.
The latter group of spectra have been obtained with the XВӯshooter instrument, which is a
mediumВӯresolution spectrograph. We have reduced them manually in order to improve the
SNR and shown how that can be the difference between being able to use the spectrum
or not.
We fit a model to the subspectra of each host galaxy spectrum near the major emission
lines used to determine metallicity. By fitting to a subspectrum rather than just a line we
constrain the position and width of multiple emission lines in order to more easily fit weak
lines such as the [NII]Вӯlines.
As XВӯshooter has a good resolution, we find that many of the reported redshifts for the
host galaxies are not at a sufficiently high precision, so we correct the redshift of about 60
% of our host galaxies. We can then precisely point out where an emission line is located
and check if it clears a detection limit.
All spectra have been deВӯreddened, both due to Milky Way reddening, and also Balmer
decrement, if applicable. We then compute metallicities, ionisation parameters, and SFR.
We first compare our fit results to the fit results obtained in SUSHIESВӯ1 [3] and Perley
et al. (2016) [7]. We get a generally good overlap with any deviations most likely due to
different approaches to fitting.
We then compare our host galaxies, split into type I and type II depending on the kind of
SLSN found within them, with galactic catalogues such as SDSS and COSMOS as well
as a sample of GRBs and EELGs. The comparison takes place in three different redshift
bins: z < 0.3, 0.3 < z < 0.8, and 0.8 < z < 2.05, each of which corresponds to a lookback
time of 3.5 billion years.
We conclude the following:
1. SLSN type I host galaxies are of lower mass and metallicity than SLSN type II host
galaxies and the general galactic population.
2. Almost all SLSN type I host galaxies have Z < Zвҳүacross all redshift bins.
3. SLSN type I host galaxies have high ionisation parameters and are located above
the main sequence in the BPT diagram with a lower metallicity than what would be
expected of them.
4. We observe metallicity and ionisation parameters to be generally consistent across
redshift bins for the SLSN type I host galaxies, but their SSFR go down as they seem
to move towards similar values as most galaxies in the catalogs. This indicates that
A spectroscopic analysis of superluminous supernova host galaxies
73
==PAGE:82==
metallicity and ionisation of the host galaxy plays a much bigger role than SSFR
does when it comes to creating SLSN type I progenitors.
However, at higher redshifts, the conclusions become more uncertain, as we have very
few valid values to compare with. This is due to many of the host galaxies in the high
redshift end not having emission lines that can overcome the detection limit. In order to
say anything conclusively about the redshift evolution of SLSN host galaxies, we need to
have more galaxies with detectable emission lines.

B.2
Limit investigation
In this analysis the limits are determined by evaluating the integrated error spectrum in
the same region as the line exists in. This follows from equation 2.7 and this method is
referred to as the вҖқIntegrated error spectrumвҖқ. Other methods were investigated as well.
One method investigates the standard deviation of the flux in the region of the line. The
idea is that the standard deviation of the flux within the region of the line is greater than
the peak, then it may be difficult to argue the line is detected.
Another method uses the mean of the errors in the region of the line. This is in some ways
a simpler version of the integrated error spectrum where it is assumed the mean value of
the error spectrum sets a lower limit of what counts as a detection.
Yet another method is inspired by [43]. Here the limiting flux of a line detection depends
on the detected flux alone and does not consider the error spectrum.
Let us call the intensity of the line I and have it lie on a background continuum with average
value b across w channels, so that the total background of the line is B = bw. The total
intensity is J, so the the line intensity can be found through:

A spectroscopic analysis of superluminous supernova host galaxies

==PAGE:142==
Superluminous Supernovae (SLSNe) are a poorly understood group of supernovae (SNe) that are
known for its very bright explosions, although it is not known what causes them. By studying their
host galaxies we may be able to constrain the progenitors of the SLSNe. In this thesis we study 71
host galaxy spectra of 59 SLSN host galaxies spectroscopically by measuring emission line fluxes
and computing metallicities and star formation rate (SFR). We then compare the SLSN host galaxies
by type and with GammaВӯray burst (GRB) host galaxies, extreme emission line galaxies (EELGs),
and large surveys such as COSMOS and SDSS. We divide the study into three regions of redshift:
z < 0.3, 0.3 < z < 0.8 and 0.8 < 2.05. We find that SLSN type I hosts are generally low mass and low
metallicity with a dwindling specific star formation rate (SSFR) as redshift grows, coming to resemble
common galaxies of their size, although at a high ionisation level. SLSN type II hosts are generally
more massive and of higher metallicity than their type I counterpart. However, at high redshifts most
emission lines are nonВӯdetections, leaving very little data to study. Nothing can conclusively be said
about high redshift SLSN host galaxies without procuring more spectra with detections.
