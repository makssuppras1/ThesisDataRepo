Abstract
The background for this master thesis is a wish from Alfa Laval to optimize the time in­
terval between the major service of dynamic contact seals, installed inside the conveyor
bearings of its decanters.
To achieve this, the aim is to create a numerical model, making use of the Finite Element
Method (FEM), capable of accurately predicting the wear and lifetime of dynamic con­
tact seals. Before this final step, experimental tests are conducted at the DTU CASMaT
laboratory, to fully characterize the seals performance when subject to sliding wear. The
seals for this project are O­rings, made of FKM rubber, and are tested using a block­on­
ring (BOR) test rig. Wear experiments are conducted with varying load (20 ­ 40 N) and test
time (1 ­ 8 hours), to obtain a mass loss trend as function of both the applied load, and the
running time, with the intent of understanding whether this material behaves according to
Archard wear theory or not. The experimental results are then used as a comparison with
the FEA ones to assess the simulations’ accuracy.
BOR experimental tests ended up suggesting that the O­ring’s wear behavior does not
follow a linear trend. The measured mass loss instead appears to follow a power­law
curve, dependent on the applied load. Higher loads (especially 40 N) are seen to cause
more significant and replication­dependent wear results. Such observed behavior does
not agree with Archard theory but is in accordance with some research papers. Tests
performed at a constant load but with various running times showed a linear dependency
between mass loss and elapsed time up until 4 hours, with the rate decreasing afterwards.
With respect to the FEA simulations, studies conducted to replicate the BOR experiments
were able to produce satisfactory wear results. Both a 2D model and a more accurate
3D model of the BOR setup were created, and surprisingly not only the 3D model yielded
accurate results but also the 2D one was able to successfully replicate the experimentally
measured wear volumes. Some limitations were however addressed on the possibility of
predicting the complete lifetime of seals.
Overall, it can be concluded that this project was able to achieve, to a satisfactory extent,
both the experimental material characterization, which allowed to understand how such
type of seal wears down with different loads and running times, as well as the creation of
a FEA predictive model that can compute wear of hyperelastic rubber materials.

1
Introduction
The background for this master thesis is a wish from Alfa Laval to optimize the time in­
terval between the major service of dynamic contact seals, installed inside the conveyor
bearings of its decanters.
Dynamic contact seals are a key component in machines with a stationary and a rotating
component, or with two rotating ones at different speeds, as their task is to isolate specific
sections of the machine from others. In the case of this project, the type of seals studied
are O­rings, in contact between two components having different rotational speeds and
aiming to confine the grease around the area where the bearing operates, in order to
keep it lubricated for the longest time possible. The sealing mechanism done by the O­
ring takes place by inserting it in a groove which has a tighter dimension than its diameter.
This compresses the O­ring, forcing it to deform and creating a pressure­tight seal that
effectively fills the empty space between the two moving parts of the decanter, preventing
leakage of grease.
The confined space in which the O­ring is placed, alongside with the fact that it is a com­
ponent in constant rotational sliding between two surfaces, implies that wear occurs at a
relatively quick pace. Seals are made of rubber, which is an hyperelastic material, and
when sliding they tend to experience visible elastic deformation and internal friction, be­
cause of the soft material properties (low modulus of elasticity) and damping [1]. The
seal life duration is therefore impacted by this phenomenon, and the performance of the
decanter itself is also affected for obvious reasons as maintenance repairs have to be
scheduled accordingly.
1.1
Project scope
Without the proper knowledge of the seal’s performance when subject to sliding, the only
reliable way to understand the wear rate and lifetime of the operating seals is by conduct­
ing periodic field test observations, which can be long and time consuming. Therefore,
the aim of this project is to contribute towards the implementation of an alternative so­
lution that can predict wear in a faster but still reliable way. The idea is to develop a
theoretical model using the Finite Element Method (FEM), able to calculate wear, modify
the contact surface of the seal according to the amount of worn material, and update the
contact pressure. Using FEM allows to make faster predictive simulations which can be
extremely useful if accurate, and makes it possible to easily test small modifications that
would require a more difficult implementation if conducted experimentally.
Before reaching the final goal of having a predictive FEM model however, laboratory­
controlled experiments have to be conducted in order to characterize the (material) be­
havior of the O­rings while sliding, and how various operational conditions (different loads
and running times) affect its wear rate. This step is extremely important because, with­
out proper knowledge of the seal’s wear rate, one would not have enough parameters to
compute wear through Finite Element Analysis (FEA). During this stage, the objective is
to understand whether the O­ring wears down according to the commonly known Archard
theoretical wear law, or if it follows other trends. As it will be discussed later, the litera­
ture with respect to wear on rubber is limited and sometimes inconclusive regarding this
matter, even more if the search is specifically restricted to wear on seals, especially in the
case of seals being made of FKM rubber such as those used in this project. Hence the

need of conducting extensive experimental tests to characterize how sliding wear affects
the rubber O­rings.
Moreover, the decision of conducting laboratory­controlled experiments and not testing
the seals in their real operational environment is to isolate the problem and exclude pos­
sible interfering phenomena, with the aim of hopefully obtaining more reliable results.
Given these considerations, two important research questions (RQ) can be posed:
• RQ1: By experimentally testing the rubber O­rings with different operational loads
and running times, is the witnessed wear trend in agreement with the principles
highlighted by Archard theory or does it deviate, resembling different behaviors?

Project structure
What follows is a structured description of the various steps this project is made of. In
order to provide a more detailed overview of the main key objectives but also of the com­
plementary aspects that still contribute in giving precious information on the matter.
• Initially, a comprehensive literature review is conducted in Chapter 2. The main as­
pects treated are: the initial contact between surfaces before wear takes place, the
wear process, and the mechanical behavior of rubber compared to linear materials.
The aim here is to try and highlight if experimental testing is consistent among differ­
ent test replications, and if FEA simulations can provide an accurate representation
of experimental and theoretical results.
• Alongside the literature review, the theoretical side of the topics treated in this project
is presented in Chapter 3.
• In Chapter 4, with the aim of discussing the reliability and accuracy of using a com­
mercial FEA software to model wear, relevant case examples found in the literature
are replicated and validated.
• Making use of a block­on­ring (BOR) test rig, laboratory­controlled wear experiments
are conducted on O­ring specimens, and presented in Chapter 5. Tests are con­
ducted with different load cases and different running times (thus different sliding
distances), in order to characterize the seal’s wear behavior in various operational
conditions.
• In Chapter 6, an equivalent FEM model that matches the functioning of the block­on­
ring setup and all of its boundary conditions is created. The FEA simulation results
are compared with the data obtained from the experimental tests to check whether
a satisfactory wear prediction can be made.
• Conclusive considerations on the obtained results are summarized in chapter 7,
and the two research questions are finally answered. Additionally, a series of pos­
sible implementations that might improve the future work on wear characterization
of contact seals are included.

Literature Review
In this chapter, a satisfactory review of preexisting literature is presented, aiming to provide
enough information on the main topics treated in this project. This step has the intention
of providing a clear perspective of the previous relevant work done, and consequentially
obtaining a better understanding on how to carry on future experiments and simulations.
The significant areas to be covered are:
• Normal contact interaction between surfaces of linear and nonlinear materials.
• Adhesive and abrasive wear due to sliding.
• Sliding wear of contact seals, and of rubber in general.
For all these areas, the theoretical, experimental and numerical sides will be discussed
to highlight correlations or deviations between them.
2.1
Normal Contact between surfaces
Surface contact between two (or more) elastic bodies has been a highly researched topic
for many years, due to its contribution into various mechanical phenomena such as fa­
tigue, indentation hardness, adhesion, and wear. As a consequence, great effort has
been dedicated to understanding the physics behind contact mechanics and be able to
simulate it using FEA. However, while at first sight the way two surfaces come into con­
tact may seem quite straightforward, in reality there are some factors that come into play
which complicate the theoretical foundations. Therefore, depending on the type of con­
tact, additional studies and considerations may have to be conducted.
The most valuable contact theory was published by Hertz in 1882 [2] and is still being
widely used nowadays, often to be compared to experimental and numerical results. It
has however some limitations, mostly because it is based on linear elasticity and relies on
assuming non­conforming, continuous and frictionless surfaces. Despite these assump­
tions, the Hertzian theory can still accurately predict contact pressures in most circum­
stances, while being a good point of reference in more complex studies. Finite element
simulations of contact problems have been performed increasingly over the years, as it
is of high interest to find predictive numerical models that can substitute time consum­
ing experimental tests. FEA simulations have been found to be in accordance with both
experimental results as well as, under specific conditions, with Hertzian theory.
Among the aforementioned limitations of Hertz theory, the fact that a non­conforming con­
tact is considered is surely the most impactful one. Non­conforming contacts are those
that take place along a point or line [3]. In real life conditions though, any surface is char­
acterized by having a certain surface roughness, which causes the contact between two
surfaces to be uneven and not continuous. Depending on the roughness magnitude, the
difference between theoretical, numerical and experimental results varies.
Great contribution on this topic was initially provided by Greenwood et al. [4] which, by
studying the contact between a smooth sphere and a rough flat surface, were able to
state that the influence of surface roughness in the contact pressure is governed by a
non­dimensional parameter. The non­dimensional parameter found is α = σR/a2

Analogous trends, interestingly obtained through the use of FEA, can be found in a study
presented by Sellgren et al. [5]. Also in this case normal contact between a smooth
sphere and a surface with various surface roughness values was considered, with the
surface roughness being directly implemented in the mesh topography. Once again, it
is found that the higher the surface roughness, the different the contact pressure distri­

implementing a surface roughness close to zero, the pressure distribution is in great ac­

contact pressure that is ∼80% the Hertzian one, while values of ∼1 µm result in pres­
sures only being ∼5% lower. It implies that good reliability can be laid upon the use of
the Finite Element Method when dealing with contact problems, provided that boundary
conditions and analysis settings are respected.
In many industrial applications, components in contact have a quite refined surface for
wear optimization and precise fitting purposes. This implies that, given the just men­
tioned considerations, the actual pressure distribution comes close to the theoretical one.
As a consequence, surface roughness is often not implemented when performing FEA
simulations, as it is time consuming and would yield not so different results. It is therefore
shown that, conducting FEA simulations of similar sphere on flat surface contacts without
taking the surface roughness into account [6][7], which essentially means that the con­
tact condition is considered to be non­conforming as in Hertz theory, the initial contact
pressure distribution observed in the FEM model almost perfectly matches the theoretical
solution. Note that a good match between numerical and theoretical results is also given
by the fact that in these experiments a highly fine mesh was used. Contact between sur­
faces is strongly dependent on mesh size and small element sizes are needed to ensure
preciseness.
Everything sensibly changes when contact between surfaces is analyzed after there has
been sliding (i.e. wear has developed). Indeed, as shown by Johansson [8], the more the
wear cycles are affecting the contacting surfaces, the different the pressure distribution
becomes compared to the initial one from Hertz. This is because Hertzian theory does
not take into account geometrical deformations due to the effect of wear. Therefore, af­
ter wear has taken place, the predicted pressure distribution will be incorrectly estimated
if Hertz theory is used. What happens is that the pressure distribution varies with time,
as the surface areas with high contact pressure will eventually wear down, and thus the
pressure distribution profile tends to become more uniform as a consequence, moving
away from the typical shape considered in Hertz theory.
A research with similar considerations, but more interesting for what concerns the test
setup, is the one presented by Kim et al. [9], since in this case the contact studied is
conforming. In this study, wear development of a metal/metal contact between the outer
surface of an oscillating ring and the inner one of a concave cylinder block is studied.
First, experimental tests are run, and through the obtained results a FE model is built.
This analysis setup means that, unlike the previous studies presented, even at zero load

∼40% of the radius of the spherical indenter (i.e. Hertz becomes imprecise for large de­

to predict sphere­on­plane contact pressure distributions even for hyperelastic materials.
Note that for each different hyperelastic material, the range of Hertz accuracy varies.
2.2
Sliding wear and the Archard model
Wear, due to its complex nature has, for many years, been mostly calculated experimen­
tally. In the late 20th century, with the advent of FEA software and more computing power,
great interest has been laid upon finding a way to accurately predict volume loss due to
wear over meaningful time intervals.
There are numerous phenomena that generate wear and, in this project, wear caused by
sliding is the one to be treated. The Archard wear equation [11], is the equation usually
used to estimate wear of components in contact and sliding with respect to each other. It
takes into account various factors such as the applied load, sliding distance, and material
properties.
The aim of using the Archard equation in the most accurate way possible, is to calculate
wear loss considering the constantly changing contact and pressure conditions due to
the deforming geometry under the effect of wear. As noted by Kim et al. [9] and Mukras
et al. [12], initial methods used to predict wear through the Archard model solely based
on the initial conditions results (i.e. initial geometry and contact pressure distribution)
without contemplating the effect of wear on the surface geometry during the operating
time considered. This can result in overpredicting [13][14] or underpredicting [15][16] the
wear rate. When predicting wear through the use of FEA, by considering the Archard
equation to be constantly (or periodically) active, this issue does not persist since the

variation in geometry and contact conditions is then taken into account.
First notable FEA studies to have contributed to the literature are those performed by
Põdra and Andersson [6][17], involving first the contact between a spherical pin on a
flat surface and subsequently two cones experiencing conical spinning contact. It was
observed that FEA can, in general, provide quite precise results compared to experimental
and analytical ones, and can therefore be used to estimate wear. Great care though
should be taken when dealing with model refinement ad time step size. Model refinement
has to be performed, but exaggerating can easily ramp up the computational time. With
respect to the time step, it should be smaller enough for the iterative process to not diverge
or oscillate.
Regarding saving computational time, Öqvist [18] performed a wear simulation using a
variable step size. The time step was allowed to increase by 50% between different wear
steps but, if convergence was not reached, it was decreased by 33%. This technique
allowed to reduce the computational time by nearly 50%, while still providing really precise
results when compared to the experimental ones based on the same conditions.
FEA on a conforming contact performed by Ho Kim et al. [9] using a block­on­ring test
rig, showed accordance with experimental results, even though not as precise as those
obtained by Põdra and Andersson [6], and Öqvist [18]. As a matter of fact, simulated
wear loss was estimated being up to 23% more than the one measured in the experiment.
It has to be noted though that in this study, the wear coefficient used in the FE analysis
was extrapolated from a separate pin­on­disc experiment and not from the block­on­ring
experiment. The block­on­ring experimental wear coefficient was found to be about 20%
lower than the one used in the FEA simulation, which gives partial explanation on the
discrepancies found between the results.
On the same page are the findings presented by Mukras et al. [12], which also studied
a conforming contact. In their FEA simulation, the same wear coefficient obtained by Ho
Kim et al. with the pin­on­disc was used. In this case, results were underpredicted com­
pared to real experimental ones, even though by a smaller margin.
These two studies shed light on the importance of the wear coefficient used when predict­
ing wear via the Archard equation making use of FEA. The Archard equation does depend
on a limited number of parameters and therefore an imprecise wear coefficient will have
a sensible impact in the simulation accuracy. Better results are going to be obtained if all
the considered parameters are as close as possible to the experiment counterpart ones.
It has to be clarified that wear in nature does not form in a precise and repetitive method
but it’s a rather probabilistic event, as it depends on many factors that cannot be guar­
anteed to be the same every time. The surface roughness of the materials in contact,
for example, is always determined based on the average height of the surface asperi­
ties, but the pattern and distribution of the asperities is randomized. The consequence
is that, conducting a specific wear test experiment multiple times will yield similar trends
but will never exactly match. In the pin­on­disc experiments of Põdra and Andersson [6],
this problem can be easily observed by considering the differences in wear depth values
obtained in two test replications conducted in the same conditions, or by looking at the
instability of the experimental wear coefficient obtained. Uncertainty in the evaluation of
the wear coefficient is mentioned by Kim et al. [9], which also evidence irregularities in
the wear depth profiles, the latter being also witnessed by McColl et al. [7].
It is clear that FEA cannot perfectly match experimental results given these observations,
but it has been discussed how it can produce from satisfactory to really accurate predic­
tive trends that resemble the experimental ones. To obtain successful simulation results
is therefore fundamental to extrapolate well calculated average wear coefficient values,

Wear computation in finite element analysis is notably an expensive iterative procedure.
The standard (and most accurate) common procedure is to calculate wear at each itera­
tion using the newly updated contact conditions. Additionally, geometry can be updated
as well at each iteration via remeshing procedures for improved accuracy. Various differ­
ent techniques have been adopted in an attempt to speed up the computation process.
A more efficient way is implementing wear computation and geometry update at a post
processing stage as it was done by Põdra and Andersson [6], McColl et al. [7], and oth­
ers [9][19][12]. Post processing wear computation allows to control and modify the wear
increment step size, and therefore to sensibly accelerate the simulation. To additionally
speed up the process, as shown by Kim et al. [9], wear can be computed only for an initial
fraction of the total process and then linearly extrapolated for the remaining part, as it is
assumed that wear has a linear progression after the running­in period has terminated.
This procedure is however successful only if the effect of friction does not constantly mod­
ify the contacting surface. Friction typically does not influence conforming contacts, while
non­conforming contacts are affected if the sliding is alternated and not continuous.
2.3
Sliding wear of rubber and contact seals
Wear tests performed on seals or rubber specimens, are typically executed in order to
obtain experimental results that can give an idea of the wear behavior to be witnessed
during the actual use in the field. Occasionally, these tests cannot be run in the same
exact operational conditions as in real working use because of experimental limitations,
but even in those cases the general trend of results is still useful in predicting wear.
In this section, previous wear tests performed in various situations are presented. The
aim is to analyze useful findings and determine whether the experiment results that will
be carried over in this project should resemble their trend, taking into account eventual
differences in the test methodologies. Wear studies on rubber compounds are not widely
treated in the literature, or at least not to the extent this project would require, and the
difficult predictive behavior of such material results in many articles presenting limited or
inconclusive outcomes. A few papers with some interesting findings are however found
and presented.
Fukahori et al. [20] experimentally tested rubber specimens to characterize abrasive slid­
ing wear. They were able to suggest a relation that links wear volume as function of the
applied normal load, for natural rubber. The proposed equation is not linear, in contradic­
tion with Archard theory, but is instead a power­law curve in the form of V = k·P α. Where
k is a constant and the exponent α is larger than unity in normal abrasive conditions (V
and P are the wear volume and applied load, respectively). α depends on both the rubber
compound and the surface roughness of the sliding surface.
Wu et al. [21] studied dry sliding wear on SBR rubber specimens in a particular block­on­
ring test and also observed a similar outcome. Rubber wear was observed to not increase
proportionally to the applied load, with a trend that resembles the one from Fukahori et
al. Similar trends were also found by Pal et al. [22]. On the contrary, Shen et al. [23]
conducted pin­on­disc tests studying two­body abrasion of NBR rubber samples and did
observe a linear dependency between wear volume and applied load. With respect to
to wear as function of the sliding distance, according to Muhr and Roberts [24] wear on
rubber is proportional to the traveled distance. Fukahori et al. [25] instead found out that
different rubber compounds can have wear rates that do not resemble each other. And
out of the three compounds presented, none had a consistent linear rate.
Mofidi and Prakash [26] analyzed friction and wear behavior of FKM rubber and other

Theoretical Background
The role of this chapter is to provide a theoretical background for the same topics treated
in the literature review.
3.1
Contact Mechanics
When components come into contact, elastic (or plastic) deformations occur, leading to
the transmission of force, and therefore energy. Contact can either be static, in the case
of bolted connections for example, or dynamic, when there is relative motion between the
two components, like in the case of ball bearings or gears. Additionally, when operating
forces become higher than the frictional ones, local or global sliding occurs, resulting in
energy losses and wear development.
Contacts are also distinguished between being conforming and non­conforming. A con­
tact is conforming when the surfaces of the contacting bodies are mating, and no addi­
tional deformation is needed to accomplish a surface contact. On the contrary, a contact
is non­conforming when the two contacting bodies do not share any surface area unless
in the presence of an applied load, meaning the contact ideally happens on a single point
or line [3]. Realistically, a non­conforming contact never takes place on a point or line
because of the existence of surface roughness, but for low roughness values the theoret­
ical assumptions come in good accordance with experimental results. In the case of this
project, a toroidal component with a circular cross­section (the O­ring) slides on a ring,
the contact is therefore ideally non­conforming.
In both cases, the contacting area is mathematically defined as the location where the gap
between the two surfaces in contact is equal to zero. The remaining part of the interface,
where the gap is larger than zero, is called separation region [30].
The simplest form of contact is the one with only normal forces involved, which allows to
study the interaction between bodies while neglecting the existence of friction. When in a
contact between two bodies tangential forces are present, friction needs to be considered
for obvious reasons.
The most relevant theory developed with respect to normal contacts is the one presented
by Hertz [2] in 1882, which is still being used nowadays for analytical calculations and
comparison purposes given its high reliability. All of his findings are based on few but
important assumptions:
• The bodies in contacts have isotropic properties.
• Strains and stresses have to be small and within the elastic limit of the materials in
use. Therefore, the characteristic length a of the contacting area is much smaller
than the radius of curvature R of the bodies in contact.
• The surfaces in contact are frictionless (i.e. it is purely a normal contact).
• The surfaces in contact are smooth. Meaning that inside the contacting area the
gap is zero everywhere, whereas outside of it the gap is greater.
• The contact is non­conforming and the bodies can be considered as an elastic half­
space.
General equations for the contact pressure distribution were developed based on these
assumptions. From these equations, more simple ones can be derived for specific contact

Contact in FEA
In FEA, contact between components can be modeled in several ways and is heavily de­
pendent on the generated mesh. Contacts can exist between a rigid and a deformable
component, or between two deformable components [33]. In this project only deformable­
deformable contacts will be treated. When two bodies are in contact, one is chosen as
Wear­ and Lifetime Analysis of Dynamic Contact Seals using the Finite Element Method – Theory and Experiment
11
==PAGE:24==
the ”contact” body and the other as the ”target” body. This differentiation is made to distin­
guish the role of the two components. If the contact behavior is chosen to be ”asymmetric”,
the output results are only computed for the ”contact” body. Whereas, if the behavior is
”symmetric”, results can be extracted for both ”contact” and ”target” bodies. The contact
condition is typically set to bonded, frictionless or frictional, although other less used op­
tions are available. If a frictional contact is chosen, a friction coefficient does need to be
defined as well.
Another important parameter to be specified is the contact detection method. The two
types of methods are the detection on Gauss integration points and the nodal detection
methods. The Gauss point integration method is the more accurate one, since the Gauss
points are more than the number of nodes in contact, and thus the contact detection is
more defined, with a resulting lower penetration between the bodies. The nodal detection
method directly uses the nodes of the meshed bodies to calculate the contact condition,
it allows more penetration but is necessary for some specific situations, such as wear
computation.
Contact penetration is the parameter to be monitored to understand how precise the sim­
ulation results are going to be. A contact with a lot of penetration may not be able to
yield a correct contact pressure, which can affect the overall results. Penetration can be
corrected by imposing a strict penetration tolerance or by increasing the stiffness of the
contact with the stiffness factor. Note however, that penetration cannot be avoided com­
pletely, and imposing a penetration tolerance too low or an excessive contact stiffness
may result in convergence difficulties. A good compromise between result accuracy and
convergence has to be made [33][34].
3.2
Wear
Wear is defined as the progressive loss of material from the surfaces of solids in contact
caused by mechanical interactions [35]. These mechanical interactions, lead to the de­
velopment of surface forces, called frictional forces, which stress the bodies and lead to
micro (or macro) fractures formations, and eventually to wear development. Moreover,
friction causes energy dissipation in the form of heat, which can modify the mechani­
cal properties of the materials in contact and influence the wear rate. Wear can also be
caused by chemical processes, such as corrosion.
A common mechanical process that generates wear is when there is sliding between the
contacting surfaces. Especially when the contact is normal, where the tangential frictional
forces caused by the sliding motion are the highest possible. Wear due to sliding can be
divided into two different phenomena: adhesive and abrasive wear [35].
Adhesive wear takes place when adhesion forces between the contacting surfaces are
high (i.e. at high loading conditions) resulting in micro­welding between the surfaces, this
leads to the transport of material from one surface to the other.
Abrasive wear mostly develops when the bodies in contact have a sensible difference in
hardness, with the asperities of the harder body deforming the surface of the softer body
eventually leading to plastic deformation.
In applications where the contacting pressures are not high and therefore the yield stress
is not locally exceeded, both adhesive and abrasive wear do not develop in one single
loading cycle. Prolonged sliding cycles, however, inevitably lead to fatigue and wear
formation.

developed throughout the years and one of the simple but quite precise one is the Archard
wear law. The equation was first introduced by Holm [36]:
V = K P

(3.8)
Which implies that the wear volume V [mm3] is a function of the load P [N], the sliding
distance s [mm], the hardness of the material H [MPa] and a dimensionless coefficient
K, commonly known as the wear coefficient.
Holm linked the constant K to the number of removed atoms per atomic encounter. Suc­
cessively, Archard highlighted the unpredictability of wear, since not every local contact
results in the formation of a wear particle. He therefore perfected the equation by defining
K as a probability factor of an asperity interaction to generate a wear particle [11][37]. For
this reason, eq. (3.8) is commonly known as the Archard equation.
The main conclusions stated by Archard after studying the reliability of eq. (3.8) are that
[11]:
• The wear rate has a linear dependency with the applied load.
• The apparent area of contact does not influence the wear rate.
• As long as the wear coefficient and applied load remain constant, the wear rate does
not depend on the speed of sliding.
These considerations are extremely important as they indicate that the use of eq. (3.8)
is independent of the sliding velocity, which means that experiments can be sped up to
decrease the required time or linearly extrapolated. This however has some limitations,
for example in applications where friction and alternated sliding play a relevant role (like in
fretting applications), the contact conditions change in a significant way during the wearing
process. Another limitation is that, if the speed is increased too much, the rising tempera­
ture at the contact surface can result in a modification of the mechanical properties of the
bodies in contact, changing the outcome of the results. In general though, where possi­
ble, linear extrapolation or increase in sliding velocities are commonly adopted measures
to accelerate the process.
The wear coefficient is a parameter extracted experimentally using standardized tests,
like the pin­on­disc or block­on­ring ones, for each different material. The extrapolated
coefficients are then used in industrial applications possessing similar contact conditions
to predict wear. In reality, each application has a unique wear coefficient that slightly
differs from the standardized ones, but in most cases the difference is not so significant
and for time efficiency the wear is predicted using the tabulated coefficients. The wear
coefficient is extracted using the inverted form of eq. (3.8), thanks to the volume loss of
the specimen that can be measured after the experiment run. If the hardness H of the
material (in MPa) is known beforehand, then the wear coefficient can be extrapolated in its
purest dimensionless form and is defined as K as already stated. In the eventuality that
H is not available, the wear coefficient is specified including the hardness and is simply
expressed as k with MPa­1 units and thus eq. (3.8) becomes V = k · P · s.
3.2.1
Wear in FEA
Wear is a phenomenon that can be computed making use of Finite Element Analysis.
Some software already come with a built­in function of the Archard wear law, others only
allow the user to create their own wear function from scratch. In this project, all the sim­
ulations presented are conducted making use of ANSYS Mechanical (version 2023 R1),
Wear­ and Lifetime Analysis of Dynamic Contact Seals using the Finite Element Method – Theory and Experiment
13
==PAGE:26==
which has an Archard built­in function, and the possibility of creating a user­defined wear
model if needed. In FEA, the volume loss caused by wear is approximated by moving the
contact nodes of the component at the contact surface. The magnitude of the node repo­
sitioning is based on the chosen wear model and on its parameters. This process takes
place during all the load steps where wear computation is activated. If the load steps are
divided into several substeps, which generally always happens, the node repositioning
takes place after every substep based on the calculated amount of volume loss [38].
In this project, the built­in Archard wear model will be used. In ANSYS, but generally in
FEA, the structure of the Archard equation is slightly different from the theoretical one, as
the contact pressure of the body subject to wear is used instead than the applied load.
The modified Archard equation in ANSYS calculates the rate of wear ˙w at a contact node:

(3.9)
Where:
• K is the wear coefficient
• H is the material hardness (in MPa)
• p is the contact pressure
• vrel is the relative sliding velocity between the two nodes in contact
• m is the pressure exponent
• n is the velocity exponent
K, H, m and n are user specified values, with K being dimensionless and H in MPa. If
the experimentally obtained wear coefficient to be used in ANSYS was not distinguished
from the material hardness and its unit of measurement is MPa­1, then the hardness has
to be set to 1 to compensate. Moreover, when frictional effects do not play a big role and
the wear coefficient is supposed to remain constant, the wear rate is independent of the
speed of sliding. The actual sliding between the components can be therefore avoided,
the wear coefficient is then scaled with the sliding velocity, and the coefficient n set to
zero. [33].
When a large amount of wear needs to be computed, the continuous repositioning of the
nodes will eventually cause the contact elements to become progressively worse, and
the analysis might not be able to reach the final prescribed substep. In this eventuality,
nonlinear adaptive remeshing can be included to remesh the model when desired. The
algorithm adjusts the nodes of the elements in contact and moves all the other nodes as
a consequence. The remeshing procedure is activated after the elements in contact have
endured a user specified percentage of linear wear [33].
Both the Archard wear model as well as the nonlinear adaptive remeshing have to be in­
cluded in ANSYS Mechanical by inserting two dedicated APDL command snippets, using
ANSYS APDL syntax. The Archard wear model is defined by the TB,WEAR command, with
the property option set to TBOPT = ARCD to specify the Archard model. The nonlinear adap­
tive command is instead defined by the NLAD command [39][40]. Snippets are provided in
appendix E and appendix F.

Nonlinear hyperelastic materials
Hyperelastic materials, specifically elastomers (or rubber) for the sake of this project, are
materials characterized by undergoing large deformations under the effect of moderate

stress. These large deformations are of nonlinear nature, but remain fully elastic, meaning
that once the load is removed the material gets back to its original shape. The nonlinearity
implies that a Young’s modulus cannot be directly determined apart from the initial region
of small deformation, which is usually too limited for most applications. Moreover, hypere­
lastic materials are independent of the deformation history, which means that a specimen
can be stressed multiple times with the same load and it will result in the same deformation
every time. Other than their exceptionally elastic properties, elastomers are isotropic, in­
compressible (thus with a Poisson ratio close to 0.5), and poorly thermally conductive. All
these characteristics make elastomers perfectly suitable for sealing applications [41][42].

This section analyzes a journal article from Põdra and Andersson [6] introduced in the
literature review. A spherical pin is worn down making use of a pin­on­disc test rig. In this
test, both the pin and the flat disc are made of steel. The pin is loaded in the normal di­

average wear coefficient k is determined. FEA is then performed employing the derived

erties. The mesh used in the current study can be seen in fig. 4.1b and is made using
quadrilateral elements, which are preferred over triangular elements from a computational
perspective. Most importantly, it was ensured that the mesh element height was large
enough to be able to compute wear without the necessity of remeshing as the authors did
not take such feature into account. At the axis of symmetry, the element height was set

4.2.2
Influence of mesh morphing in computational performance
Another useful feature when considering the impact of computational time in simulations is
the use of mesh morphing (also known as remeshing). Employing mesh morphing allows
the system to take control of the global and local mesh element sizes, changing them to
optimize the simulation.
In general, remeshing is performed to solely avoid mesh elements from becoming too
skewed under large deformations, or when high amounts of wear have to be simulated.
In certain problems and applications however, the algorithm is able to determine areas in
the model where it is possible to apply larger element mesh sizes compared to the initial
ones specified by the user. This procedure helps reducing the number of cumulative
iterations and therefore the total computational time.

Simulating fretting wear of a cylinder on a flat surface
McColl et al. [7], analyzed wear under fretting conditions both experimentally as well
as using FEA, highlighting the importance of including friction in FEA wear computation
when dealing with periodic oscillating sliding. The study first involves conducting a set of
experiments to evaluate the wear coefficient, which is then used in the FEA simulation
to compute wear. The experimental setup involves sliding a steel cylinder over a steel
flat surface at high frequency to simulate a fretting behavior. A simplified representation
of the setup is shown in fig. 4.9, where v is the oscillating velocity, w is the components
(and contact) depth, and FN is the normal load applied. The radius of the cylinder is
6 mm while w = 10 mm. The sliding velocity v has a frequency f = 20 Hz with an
oscillating stroke of 2δ = 50 µm. Three load cases are tested to see the difference in
wear development, FN = 185, 500, 1670 N. In total, 18000 wear cycles are performed,
which means that 18000 oscillating strokes are applied. For simplicity, in this section only
one load case is replicated, FN = 185 N, to analyze how the ANSYS built­in Archard
model performs under the effect of friction and sliding motion.
In fretting applications, the influence of friction becomes relevant to obtain accurate re­
sults. Ensuring that the correct contact surface width is in a sticking condition yields com­
pletely different results compared to a surface with no friction applied. In a situation like
this experiment, it is therefore not acceptable to avoid the inclusion of friction, compared
to what was done in section 4.1.

4.3.2
Wear results
To perform the wear simulation, first the 185 N load is applied in a first load step without
implementing any sliding, nor activating the Archard wear model. Subsequently, from the
second load step the fretting motion is applied to the cylinder with the aforementioned
frequency and amplitude, and the Archard wear model is activated. For this simulation,
the contact stiffness factor is set to 10. The friction coefficient applied to the contact region
between cylinder and flat plane is set to 0.8. This value comes from the average friction
coefficient calculated from the experimental tests performed at an earlier stage.
Moreover, when running the experiments the authors witnessed visible wear both on the
cylinder as well as on the flat surface. The resulting decision was to compute wear on both
surfaces to obtain comparable and accurate results. Two wear coefficient were therefore
determined from the experiments, k1 = 2 · 10−8 MPa­1 for the cylinder and k2 = 3 · 10−8
MPa­1 for the flat plane.
In the current study it is therefore chosen to also compute wear on both surfaces by im­
plementing a symmetric contact condition, in order to have a fair replication of the authors

data and the chosen parameters are C1 = 4.702 kPa and C2 = 0.47 kPa.

which allows to spare computational time. The indentation is imposed on the sphere
through a vertical displacement in one load step with a 250 ms duration, while the the

is chosen to be frictionless to have a fair comparison with Hertz theory. Given the high
imposed indentation and the expected large strain, the mesh is highly refined in the vicinity
of the sphere (0.2 µm), while being progressively more coarse going further away from it.

The limited accuracy of the Hertz equation is however expected as one of its assumptions

5.2
Experimental testing strategy
This section is dedicated to presenting and motivating the various experimental tests that
were decided to be run. Together with this, measurement limitations and variable and
fixed errors are discussed.
The FKM rubber material studied in these experiments, is hyperelastic. Hyperelastic non­
linear materials tend to behave differently compared to linear materials (such as steel) in
most of their mechanical applications, due to their particular stress­strain properties. Ar­
chard wear theory was studied on metallic materials, which are characterized by having
a high stress­strain ratio, meaning that material failure is achieved with small deforma­
tions (and high stresses). Hyperelastic materials are instead opposite, having low yield
stresses but being able to withstand a large amount of deformation before failing. As dis­
cussed in the literature review, research publications on rubber wear behavior do not all
come to the same conclusions, many results indicate evident nonlinearities in the wear
trends while others are in agreement with Archard theory.
It is not entirely clear whether for this specific rubber compound Archard theory can be a
valid approximation or not. Therefore, the aim of these BOR experiments is to understand
two of the key findings determined by Archard:
36
Wear­ and Lifetime Analysis of Dynamic Contact Seals using the Finite Element Method – Theory and Experiment
==PAGE:49==
1. Whether the wear rate is proportional to the applied load.
2. Given a constant load, if the wear rate is independent of the area of contact, to
discuss contact seals lifetime. Therefore, if the wear volume as function of the sliding
distance is linear.
Determination of the sliding conditions
It was determined to run the experiments in dry conditions. Performing them with lubri­
cated sliding would have been a much proper procedure, but implementing lubrication was
considered to be another added complication to an already quite difficult phenomenon to
study. Additionally, to be able to witness mass loss with lubrication the duration of each
experimental run would have needed to be much longer, requiring continuous 24/7 oper­
ation of the BOR test rig which, at its current version, it was not possible.
Moreover, the sliding speed and thus the speed of the rotating ring had to be decided.
Various different ring speeds were tested to understand what could have been a suitable
value. The main constraints regarding this matter were temperature and time. A high
number of rpm, together with the dry sliding and thus high friction, can sensibly raise
the O­ring specimen temperature at the contact surface, potentially leading to changing
the mechanical properties of the rubber. A too low number of rpm means having to run
longer experiments in order to witness meaningful wear, longer experiments implies less
available time to do test replications, which is not recommended considering wear unpre­
dictability.
After some attempts, a ring speed of 20 rpm was deemed to be an acceptable compro­
mise. 20 rpm equals to a sliding velocity at the contact surface of 102.6 mm/s. At 20 rpm,
the temperature on the contact surface of the O­ring specimen was measured to be in
the range of approximately 35 ­ 40 degrees Celsius. Note that the temperature was mea­
sured with an infrared thermometer and, given the poor thermal conductivity of rubber, it
is believed that the reported temperature reading might be slightly lower than the actual
one. Overall, this temperature magnitude is considered to be low enough to not chemi­
cally alter the mechanical properties of the O­ring specimen during the sliding process.
All the following experiments, unless otherwise specified, are conducted with a ring speed
of 20 rpm.
5.2.1
Wear as a function of the applied load
According to Archard theory, the worn volume of a sliding object should be proportional
to the applied load, if the sliding distance is kept constant.
In order to verify whether this applies also on the O­ring object of study, the initial step
was to determine an applicable load range to test in the experiments, taking into account
that the experiments would have been conducted in an unlubricated dry sliding environ­
ment. The idea was to find a load range where mild wear would occur, therefore avoiding
high loadings that would have influenced the removal of large particles. Wear is a prob­
abilistic phenomenon and an excessive load, combined with the high strain property of
the FKM rubber, can cause a lot of variance in mass loss results. Moreover, O­rings are
components that operate for long running hours and thus experience extremely low wear
rates, being in a lubricated environment. Running the experiments in dry conditions is
already far from its real application, and it is therefore not meaningful to study high wear
rate conditions.
Preliminary tests were run with loads ranging from 10 N to 50 N. It was observed that below
20 N the wear rate was too low to obtain meaningful results in a reasonable amount of
time, whereas experiments with 40 N already showed nonuniform wear formation and the
fracture of large chunks of rubber. The choice was therefore to run the real set of wear

5.2.2
Wear as function of the sliding distance
The other important statement in the Archard wear theory is that a material subject to a
constant load exhibits a constant wear rate, meaning that the wear coefficient K should
remain constant as the running time (or sliding distance) progresses.
The strategy to conduct these tests was slightly simpler than the one presented in sec­
tion 5.2.1, but much more time consuming as it required to run longer experiments. It was
decided to use 20 N as the constant load, mainly because it was one of the load cases al­
ready studied, meaning that mass loss data was already available in the correspondence
of 1 hour of test time length. Secondly because, among the various load cases studied in
the 20 ­ 40 N range, it is the load that generated the best looking wear scars, without too
many irregularities in the worn surface. This consideration was deemed important as this
type of test included longer runs that would have generated more wear. Regarding the
test duration, other than the one hour test results already available, replicated runs were
conducted from 2 hour tests up until 4 hour tests at an interval of 1 hour. Tests were also
conducted for 6 and 8 hours. Given the expensiveness of these tests in terms of required
time, runs were replicated for either 3 or 4 times.

In all of the replications executed for the tests presented in section 5.2.1 and section 5.2.2,

• To understand whether, given the same applied load, there is a correlation between

• To visualize if during long runs the friction force curve has a somewhat constant

5.2.4
Measurement limitations and experimental errors
There are some limitations in the measurements performed in these tests, as well as
several experimental errors that need to be addressed in order to anticipate the quality of
the results. Some have been already mentioned, such how the O­ring test pieces were
obtained, and will be further discussed here.
Measurement of the volume loss
In a standardized BOR experiment, the resulting volume loss due to wear is achieved
by measuring the scar width of the block. Extracting the wear volume by measuring the
block’s worn mass loss is not recommended as it can be influenced by mass transfer
from the ring to the block, generation of oxide films and lubrication interference (when
considered) [44]. The measurement of the wear scar width is however only accurate when
the wear scar has an uniform shape along the line of contact. In the tests conducted in this
project, the high friction forces together with the soft material properties of the FKM test
specimen, resulted in a wear scar that was nonuniform along the test piece length. The
wear scar on the test piece end facing the rotation was witnessed to be much wider than
average, which was compensated by a nonexistent wear scar on the test piece end facing
away from the rotation. The later presented Figure 5.6b helps to visualize the effect of
the friction, that with the help of the sliding velocity warps the contact surface generating
a nonuniform wear scar.
In other occasions, the linear wear scar depth is used to calculate the volume loss. This
especially happens in pin­on­disc experiments, as shown in section 4.1 [6]. Unfortunately
this is another parameter that necessitates of a wear scar with regular geometry, that the
O­ring test piece cannot show. This means that the displacement sensors of the BOR test
rig are not helpful for these experiments.
Given these considerations, the only direct way to extract the volume loss is by measuring
the test piece mass loss.
The positive aspect is that these experiments are between a rubber test piece and a
stainless steel ring, meaning that, compared to a steel­steel contact, mass transfer from
the ring to the block is improbable as well as generation of oxide films. The first one
Wear­ and Lifetime Analysis of Dynamic Contact Seals using the Finite Element Method – Theory and Experiment
39
==PAGE:52==
because the hardness of the rubber is incredibly smaller than the steel one, thus the steel
ring should not develop wear, the second because the ring is made of stainless steel
which does not oxidize. Therefore, the mass loss results should not be affected by these
phenomena.
Accuracy of the weight scale
It has been said that the volume loss has to be determined through weighting the mass
loss. A clarification has to be then made with respect to the weight scale available for these
experiments. The scale at the DTU laboratory is a precision balance scale with a great
sensitivity of 1 mg. Unfortunately, despite the high sensitivity of the scale, the witnessed
mass loss on the specimens was observed to be in the same order of magnitude, which is
not optimal. However, as discussed in section 5.2.1, the only way to obtain a higher mass
loss would have been to either increase the applied load, generating a type of wear that is
far from the real O­ring operational conditions, or to conduct longer tests which would have
been too time consuming. The decision of conducting a high number of test replications
in the experiments with variable load is due to not only counteract the probabilistic nature
of wear, but to also mitigate the weight scale limitation.
Accuracy in the O­ring test piece shape
The accuracy of how the O­ring specimens are cut is also a factor that can yield variance in
the results. However, the material properties of the FKM rubber made it really complicated
to obtain regular test pieces. Results linking test piece length versus measured mass loss
will however be presented, showing that apparently the error variation in test piece length
does not seem to have a direct correlation with the mass loss.
Other instrumentation errors
All the instrumentation on the BOR test rig is subject to having inevitable fixed errors. The
motor moving the load on the sled, the motor rotating the ring, the friction force transducer,
all have manufacturing fixed errors that can never be zero due to machining tolerances.
In an experiment where all the experimental errors at play are around the same level of
influence, these instrumentation errors can be taken into consideration in the total error
computation. For these experiments however, the sensitivity of the weight scale compared
to the measured mass loss is believed to be a much more relevant error, making the
instrumentation ones insignificant.

when sliding, due to the high elasticity and low yield stress of the FKM rubber, combined

resulted in the specimens wearing down in a non­uniform pattern. Specifically, refer­

specimen to wear down much more fast and unpredictably compared to the rest of the
contacting surface. As a natural consequence, at position 3 the specimen compensates
by having no contact pressure, with part of the surface being actually lifted from the ring.

Friction in lubricated conditions
The average and standard deviation COF of the three test replications with lubricated

still considerably high.

thinner one. This might be counter intuitive but grease is an extremely viscous fluid and
requires a notable amount of energy to be displaced. When the majority of the grease
has moved away from the area of contact, the grease film thickness becomes consistent
over time, resulting in a more regular COF value.

6
Predicting wear of hyperelastic
materials using the Finite Element
Method
In chapter 4 a lot of examples and discussions have been presented with respect to the
usefulness of performing FEA to predict wear, and the outcomes resulted to be quite
positive. The work done does not anticipate the certainty of accurately computing wear
for hyperelastic materials using a commercial software, as these are trickier materials to
study, but surely suggests that it can be possible. This chapter will therefore dig deep into
trying to obtain the most faithful replication of the BOR test setup, that will hopefully yield
good wear results that resemble the experimental ones.
The strategy for this part of the project, given the complexity of the subject, was to proceed
step by step, starting from the simplest possible model, thus introducing many approxima­
tions, and then slowly adding features to reach the final and most complete simulation.
The benefit of structuring the work in this way is also to provide a clear view on which
aspects have more influence in wear computation.
Among the various experiments conducted in chapter 5, it is decided to replicate only one
loading condition and running time, since the sole objective of this chapter is to understand
whether FEA can be used to accurately predict wear. Therefore, the 20 N load case with
a running time of 1 hour at 20 rpm is chosen.
6.1
Characterization of the hyperelastic material model
The accurate representation of the real life material behavior that characterizes the O­ring,
is of great importance to try having a simulation that yields good results. Characterizing
hyperelastic materials is not as straightforward as it is for linear materials. Therefore,
before presenting the various simulations performed, this section aims to provide the rea­
soning behind the hyperelastic model specification that was employed in the simulations.
First, let’s consider what the available information are, with respect to the actual FKM
material properties of the O­ring. The data­sheet provided by the manufacturer is available
in appendix A. This data­sheet provides a few useful parameters for this study:
• Hardness: 70 ± 5 Shore A
• Tensile strength: 14.5 MPa
• Ultimate elongation: 210%
• Specific gravity: 2.02 ± 0.03
However, to characterize an hyperelastic material according to a model such as those
presented in section 3.3, the parameters just listed do not provide much help. As a matter
of fact, the hyperelastic models necessitate of only one parameter which is the initial
Young’s modulus E0 or, alternatively, of the initial shear modulus G0, being one third of
E0. Unfortunately, the O­ring manufacturer does not provide this material property, which
means that a research has to be conducted to understand which initial Young’s modulus
this rubber compound is most likely to have.
Wear­ and Lifetime Analysis of Dynamic Contact Seals using the Finite Element Method – Theory and Experiment
53
==PAGE:66==
The data­sheets of three other FKM compounds having comparable hardness, tensile
strength and ultimate elongation, and also including information on the Young’s Modulus,
were found [47]. All three data­sheets are provided in appendix C. It can be observed
that, the Young’s modulus does not vary much, ranging from 5.5 to 6.63 MPa. This does
make sense considering that all the other mechanical properties are of similar magnitude.
Moreover, discussions have been brought up about the existence of a rough relationship
between material Young’s modulus and hardness of the FKM material, with a hardness
of 70 approximately corresponding to a modulus of 5.47 MPa [48].
Given both the Young’s moduli shown in appendix C, and the rough estimated relationship
between hardness and modulus [48], the Young’s modulus for the FEA simulations of this
project was chosen to be E0 = 6.5 MPa, corresponding to a shear modulus of G0 = 2.17
MPa.
The other parameter necessary to fully define a hyperelastic model is the Poisson ratio
and, for materials like rubber that are considered to be almost incompressible, a typical
Poisson ratio is ν = 0.499.
Choice of hyperelastic model
The two main hyperelastic models used when it comes to characterizing rubber materi­
als are the Neo­Hookean and the Mooney­Rivlin models. Both can give good represen­
tation of the real stress­strain curve of the material, but the Neo­Hookean definitely is
more straightforward to implement. As a matter of fact, looking at the strain energy func­
tions (eq. (3.11) and eq. (3.12)), while the Neo­Hookean function only depends on one
coefficient A10, the Mooney­Rivlin one depends on two coefficients A10 and A01. This
difference means that the only coefficient of the Neo­Hookean model can be easily de­
termined (A10 = G0/2), whereas the coefficients of the Mooney­Rivlin model can have
infinite combinations since A10 + A01 = G0/2.
In a case like this project, where the tensile experimental data for the hyperelastic mate­
rial is not known, making use of the Neo­Hookean model makes much more sense given
its simpler implementation. Therefore, the shear modulus is inserted in ANSYS and the
software directly calculates the derived stress­strain curve. The only other important pa­
rameter that ANSYS requests as an input is the incompressibility parameter, which is
linked to the Poisson ratio. In order to achieve a Poisson ratio of 0.499, the incompress­
ibility parameter is set to 1 · 10−9 Pa­1.
6.2
Simulation of a 2D simplified model
The first simulation that is performed is done in the simplest possible way, trying to remove
as many complex features as possible, but without overly affecting the model accuracy
with respect to reality. The main simplification is to convert a problem that is three di­
mensional into a 2D one, with the obvious goal of having a model that can be solved in a
much faster computational time. To achieve this, a 2D plane stress configuration is cho­
sen. This means that the O­ring is considered to be a cylinder instead than a section of a
torus. The model thickness is set to 24.1 mm, which is a good approximation of the real
length of contact between the O­ring specimen and the ring (see fig. 5.18 for the specimen
experimental average length values). The O­ring diameter corresponds to the physical
one, being 6.99 mm.

6.4
Overall considerations
Both the 2D and 3D simulations were able to yield a wear volume that is in good ac­
cordance with the experimentally observed one. This is definitely a positive result, as it
suggests that FEA can be a good tool to predict wear in an accurate way also in the case
of hyperelastic materials. The downside revealed by the experiments, is that the wear co­
efficient does not appear to have a constant trend. This means every time the loading or
running time conditions are changed, the correct wear coefficient has to be extrapolated
from the experimental observed trend before conducting FEA. Such extra step is not a big
deal breaker, but the versatile usability of Archard law gets lost. Moreover, it means that
performing FEA to predict the full lifetime of seals would not be ideal, as it would require
to experimentally know the wear coefficient at multiple time instants. A modified version
of the equation, that takes into account the varying experimental wear coefficient through
a time­dependent function, would make much more sense.
A possible explanation on why the 2D and 3D simulations yielded comparable wear vol­
umes may be answered by looking at how Archard law works in FEA. Wear is computed
based on the local pressure of each mesh element in contact, which means that elements
with a higher contact pressure will wear out more than others. However, the applied load
is always the same, implying that the global contact pressure should not change signif­
icantly within the different simulations and, consequently, the same applies to the total
wear volume. Therefore, while the peak pressure varies depending on the type of simu­
lation, also the number of elements subject to that contact pressure change as well. For
example, in the 3D rotated ring simulation the peak contact pressure is really high (fig. 6.8)
but the amount of elements subject to that pressure are not many, and there is a numer­
ous amount of elements with a low contact pressure. On the other hand, in the frictionless
3D simulation (and similarly in the 2D simulation), the maximum contact pressure is much
lower, but the amount of elements experiencing it is higher (fig. 6.5).

7
Conclusions and future improvements
The objectives of this master thesis project were essentially two:
1. Through experimental testing on a block­on­ring test rig, characterize the impact
of sliding wear on O­ring contact seal specimens, at various loading conditions as
well as different running times. Investigate whether hyperelastic materials exhibit
wear tends consistent with Archard theory, or demonstrate alternative behaviors as
suggested in existing research articles.
2. Resembling the setup of the experiments, create a FEA model able to accurately
predict wear of hyperelastic rubber seals. Use the experimental results to validate
the simulations and assess possible limitations of this procedure.
Block­on­Ring experiments performed with multiple load cases, indicated that the O­ring
specimens wear behavior does not appear to follow a linear rend. The mass loss as
function of the applied load seems instead to have a power­law relationship. Such trend
agrees with some research articles that studied sliding wear on similar rubbers ([20][21]),
and it takes distance from the proportional relationship that Archard theory addressed. A
large amount of test replications was ensured, to counteract the evident scattering of the
results with respect of the mass loss. Thanks to this decision, the curve fit and its 95%
confidence bounds passed through the majority of the results data points, suggesting that
the fit could be a good approximation of the real trend.
When tests were conducted at different running hours (and thus sliding distances), mass
loss results suggested that the wear rate as function of time might be linear until 4 hours,
after which the rate is seen to decrease. Thus, the trend until 4 hours is in accordance
with the linearity considerations made by Muhr and Roberts [24]. The following wear
rate decrease was potentially addressed to the wear scar surface becoming too large
compared to the magnitude of the applied load. Consequence being, that the local contact
stresses become too low for some of the the asperities in contact to be removed, resulting
in a lower wear rate. It has to be stated though, that the relatively low number of test
replications, implies that the confidence in the observed trends cannot be considered
substantial. Such results however, definitely provide an useful starting point for future
research.
Moreover, the trend of the individual friction coefficient curves corresponding to the var­
ious conducted tests was revealed to be extremely unpredictable, which could suggest
the substantial scattering in the measured mass loss results. Averaging the COF curves
among the test replications luckily resulted in more clear and consistent curves, and indi­
cated that the COF does not seem to reach a steady state condition, not even for longer
running times. The total average measured COF among the different load cases was sur­
prisingly quite consistent. Additionally, lubricated runs showed not only a lower average
COF (as expected), but also a much smoother curve trend, which suggests that future
lubricated experiments might yield more consistent results.
With respect to the FEA part of this project, initial replication of meaningful journal articles
confirmed the reliability of using the built­in Archard equation in ANSYS to predict wear
of linear materials such as steel. Studies on hyperelastic indentations instead showed
the different behavior such materials have when compared to the known Hertz contact
theory.

The Finite Element Analyses conducted to replicate the BOR experiments were able to
produce a satisfactory outcome. Surprisingly the 2D model, despite being a quite sim­
plified version of the real setup geometrically speaking, ended up predicting the correct
expected amount of wear volume. The three more real life representative 3D models stud­
ied, all produced great results, but considering the accuracy of the 2D model, they were
deemed to be rather unnecessary, as their computation expensiveness is much higher.
The 3D model with the implementation of friction and rotation remains useful to visualize
the correct pressure distribution pattern in operational conditions. Overall, the obtained
results highlight the possibility of correctly predicting wear via FEA using Archard equa­
tion even in the case of hyperelastic materials, which is in agreement with the studies
made by Angerhausen et al. [29]. However, the experimental tests have shown that the
relationship between wear loss and applied load or running time is not linear, meaning
that the wear coefficient is not constant. This implies that each time, before performing
FEA, the correct wear coefficient has to be extrapolated from the experimental observed
trend. Moreover, if the wear rate as function of the running time is not linear, using the
Archard law in FEA would not be ideal to predict lifetime and a modified version of such
equation might be of more interest.
Ultimately, the two research questions posed at the beginning of the project can be an­
swered:
• RQ1: By experimentally testing the rubber O­rings with different operational loads
and running times, is the witnessed wear trend in agreement with the principles
highlighted by Archard theory or does it deviate, resembling different trends?
The results revealed that the O­ring wear rate does not seem to respect Archard
theory when the load is varied. It is not entirely clear if the same applies when the
running time is changed, although the current experiments suggest that it might be
the case. Such behavior is in agreement with some research papers.
• RQ2: By creating a FEA model that resembles the experimental setup conditions,
is it possible to predict wear of hyperelastic rubber seals and obtain comparatively
accurate results?
The FEA model revealed that wear can be accurately calculated, closely matching
experimental results. However, due to the wear coefficient not being constant, the
Archard equation is not well suited for lifetime predictions.
It can be concluded that this project was able to achieve, to a satisfactory extent, both the
objectives presented. Naturally, some aspects are still uncertain, such as the wear rate
and COF trend at much longer running hours, or if data scattering in the mass loss results
can be reduced to improve accuracy. Overall however, the presented results definitely set
a great starting point to fully characterize wear on hyperelastic contact seals (and more
specifically, FKM rubber seals).
7.1
Future improvements
While conducting the experimental tests, but also after having considered the obtained
results and performed the FEA simulations, various potential aspects that could be imple­
mented in the future have been considered. These can be summarized in the following:
• The O­ring specimens used in the experiments have been manually obtained from
an entire O­ring. This has resulted in having specimens that slightly differ from each
other in weight and length. This variance could be avoided by finding a supplier that
can provide previously cut standardized specimens of the same material.
64
Wear­ and Lifetime Analysis of Dynamic Contact Seals using the Finite Element Method – Theory and Experiment
==PAGE:77==
While results presented in section 5.3.4 indicated that there was no potential corre­
lation between the different specimens’ weight and length with respect to the mea­
sured mass loss, utilizing uniform test pieces could provide much more consistent
and stable results.
• Test runs longer than 8 hours should be performed, to better understand the O­ring
mass loss rate and friction coefficient under continuous sliding.
• Extensive experimentation should be conducted in lubricated conditions, to under­
stand whether adding lubrication only affects the wear rate or if it also has an impact
on the mass loss trend with respect to the applied load or running time. Unfortu­
nately, this is an extremely time consuming process as lubrication decreases the
wear rate significantly.
• According to the experimental results, the mass loss is not proportional to the ap­
plied load or running time, and thus the wear coefficient is not a constant value but
instead varies. This means that to use the Archard equation in FEA simulations with
different boundary conditions, the wear coefficient has to be specified beforehand
for each simulation. One alternative could be to build a user­defined wear model
where the wear coefficient follows a prescribed load­ or time­dependent equation.
Such equation would be the average fit that can be determined though experimental
tests.
• Other types of rubber O­rings could be tested to see how their wear behavior is
compared to the FKM ones studied in this project. If the resulting wear coefficients
have a trend and magnitude that does not differ much, it might suggest that only
one wear coefficient curve might be needed to perform FEA on multiple types of O­
rings, without compromising accuracy too much. This is usually done when testing
wear on steel for example, wear coefficient values are taken from preexisting data to
avoid conducting time consuming experiments, at the expense of the final accuracy
in the FEA simulation, which is impacted but not to an unacceptable extent [12][19].

G
General FEA procedure for wear
computation
This section of the appendix aims to provide an indicative procedure for wear simulation
in FEA, but more specifically in ANSYS Workbench Mechanical.
For simplicity and coherence with the project, the procedures that will be presented will
be referred to the BOR setup but they can be adapted to studying wear on any type of
model.
Specific information for the following steps can be found in:
• Mechanical User Guide [34].
• Mechanical APDL Contact Technology Guide [33].
• Mechanical APDL Command Reference [39].
• Mechanical APDL Theory Reference [38].
• Mechanical APDL Nonlinear Adaptivity Analysis Guide [40].
Step 1: Build the geometry
Build the geometry of the model. The geometry can be built in whichever preferred CAD
software, but using one of the software from ANSYS gives some advantages in terms of
feature management and communication smoothness with Workbench Mechanical.
Once the geometry is set, specify the desired material for each component.
Step 2: Create the proper contact connections
Generate the desired connections between components. The connections can be of mul­
tiple nature: frictionless, frictional, bonded based on preferences. Set the con­
tact behavior to Asymmetric for computational stability purposes. An asymmetric contact
means that the output parameters are computed only for one of the two contact surfaces.
In the eventuality that wear on both the surfaces in contact is to be computed, set the
behavior to Symmetric.
In the Advanced section, multiple options can be changed. The most important one is the
Detection Method, remember to set this one on one of the nodal detection methods (the
most used is Nodal-Normal To Target). Apart from this one, the Normal Stiffness and
Penetration Tolerance can be varied to ensure better accuracy or easier convergence.
Step 3: The wear command snippet
Add an APDL command snippet unde the desired contact connections where wear is to be
computed. The snipped should resemble the structure of the one shared in appendix E.
The snippet is commented with all the necessary instructions, in addition section 3.2.1
includes additional precious information regarding this matter.
Step 4: Mesh the model
When meshing, a general rule is always to maximize the number of quadrilateral (or hexa­
hedral in 3D simulations) elements at the expense of triangular (or tetrahedral) elements.
This is also valid when wear has to be computed.
The mesh around the contact area has to be very fine to correctly capture the deformations
and contact pressures. Far from the contact area the mesh can be coarse.

==PAGE:101==
Step 5: Apply loads and boundary conditions
Apply the desired loads and boundary conditions. The more you replicate the experimen­
tal conditions, the higher the chances are that the final FEA results will be close to the
experimental ones.
Step 6: The NLAD command snippet
Add an ADPL command snippet in the analysis settings to activate the nonlinear adaptive
remeshing in case too much wear has to be computed. The snippet should resemble the
structure of the one shared in appendix F.
Step 7: Modify the Analysis Settings
In the Analysis Settings modify the desired number of load steps and substeps. When
working with hyperelastic materials make sure to have a sufficiently high number of sub­
steps to reach convergence. In rare difficult cases, it may be useful to switch the Newton­
Raphson method to Unsymmetric. Another important parameter when dealing with hy­
perelastic materials or a lot of wear is to activate the Large Deflection option.
Step 8: Run the simulation
Run the simulation and visualize the results through some contact trackers that can be
activated in the Solution option.
