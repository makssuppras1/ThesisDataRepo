Abstract
Offshore operations play a crucial role in the life time of an offshore wind farm. Ac-
tivities performed offshore come with a degree of uncertainty due to its sensitivity to
meteorological and oceanic (MetOcean) conditions. Offshore operations during both
the construction and operational phase introduce uncertainties to the duration and
cost of the works, ultimately impacting the overall business case of the projects. Be-
cause of this, it has then become vital to assess the weather impact on the different
construction and operations activities during the construction and life time of offshore
wind power projects. The scope of this methodology aims to provide a representative
weather downtime estimation of an installation campaign.
In the first part of this report, two different sources of MetOcean data sets have
been analyzed in order to evaluate its suitability for the weather downtime analy-
sis. The two types of data sets evaluated are measurements and re-analysis ERA5
data from the European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF).
The data analysis was performed at two offshore sites, identifying deviations between
measured and re-analysis data in terms of wind speed, significant wave height and
peak wave period. Limitations have been found in the ERA5 data set such as extreme
value underestimation, difficulties regarding long fetched swells with low wave heights
and reduced time and spatial resolution. These limitations should be re-assessed if
ERA5 data are to be used for other applications than the one presented. Concluding
this section, the ERA5 data looks promising for the purpose of providing a powerful
catalogue of data for weather downtime analysis around the globe, despite the devia-
tions between measurements and re-analysis data – however to be further investigated
in the following section of this report.
In the second part of the report, the estimated duration of a number of specific
installation campaigns is analyzed using both MetOcean data sets in order to com-
pare the results. The analysis is done using a discrete event simulator. The impact
on the estimated campaign duration is performed in two different foundation installa-
tion methodologies and for one turbine erection methodology. The difference on total
campaign duration between the two MetOcean data sets is on average less than 10%.
A simple yet illustrative example is presented assessing the performance of the two
foundation installation setups at the Forschungsplattformen in Nord- und Ostsee Nr.1
(FINO1) offshore measurement station, located around 40km off the German coast

performance of an installation campaign, a sensitivity analysis has been undertaken

Thirdly, the duration of the monopile installation campaign at Veja Mate offshore

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
This report aims to provide a tool to compare the uncertainty of weather downtime
estimates during offshore wind farm construction activities. The weather downtime is
derived from the estimated installation duration, obtained by modelling the logistics
involved in the installation process. The comparison of weather downtime estimates
is undertaken across sites and different installation setups.
1.1
Content
The first chapter ”Introduction” presents an overview of the content of this Master
Thesis report. The second chapter ”Background”, presents the background for this
Master Thesis project. The third chapter ”Methodology”, describes the theoretical
basis and exposes the proposed methodology introducing each component of it. It
also includes an analysis of the MetOcean input data prior to the methodology exe-
cution. The fourth chapter,”Results”, presents and discusses the results obtained by
implementing the methodology to several installation cases. Additionally, this chap-
ter contains a sensitivity analysis on vessel operational limits, a comparison on results
when using measurement and re-analysis data as well as an indexing method to eas-
ily compare installation campaigns at different sites. The fifth chapter ”Conclusion”
presents the final thoughts and most relevant information one should extract from
the work carried out along this Master Thesis. The sixth chapter, ”Outlook”, points
out the limitations of this project and describes several paths to continue research
with in order to improve the presented methodology. An appendix is attached, con-
taining further information on the offshore site locations considered, useful scripts to
retrieve re-analysis data remotely and full description on the installation campaigns
used. Finally, a list of references used along this project is included.
1.2
Problem formulation and project objective
Offshore wind has experienced an increasing development around the globe, with new
projects and markets coming up constantly. Offshore wind projects have a significant
amount of uncertainty, translating into greater risks that result in lower business case
value. The installation phase represents a significant share of the total costs of an
offshore wind farm project - 20% [1] - and is a process involving a notable amount of

uncertainty, including weather downtime estimation affecting the offshore activities
in the construction process.
This Master Thesis focuses on the installation campaigns, presenting a methodol-
ogy that aims to reduce the uncertainty in the estimation of installation durations,
ultimately translating in increased business case value. Additionally, using the pub-

provides the tools to identify the main drivers that influence an installation strategy

CHAPTER 2
Background
2.1
Offshore wind industry state, trends and needs
Renewable energy sources, therein offshore wind, align with the goals of the inter-
national community of cutting down greenhouse gas emissions to the atmosphere to
tackle climate change. Particularly in Denmark, the state has an objective of being
fossil fuel free by 2050 1 as well as having a share of renewable energy sources in the
total enegy consumption of 50% by 2030 2 - currently at slightly over 40% 3.
Offshore wind energy is expanding at a great rate, all over the world. It’s Lev-
elized Cost of Energy (LCOE) - an indicator that represents the costs per unit of
energy produced for a specific project - is higher than other renewable technologies
such as onshore wind or solar photo voltaic (PV) [1]. Albeit, offshore wind enables a
great installed capacity with increased social acceptance compared to onshore wind.
Through the past 20 years, the costs of offshore wind have been increasing mainly due
to the limited competition from wind turbine manufacturers, the increasing distance
to shore and deeper waters that makes the foundations as well as the installation
process more costly [2]. In addition, until recent years - around 2012 4 - the existing
installation vessels were either not purpose built or its design was not sufficient to
handle optimally the different components of the construction phase which finally
translated into inefficient and costly installation campaigns [3].
Nowadays the market has matured: turbine suppliers have increased in number
and have managed to cope with the increasing demand supplying more powerful
machines that translate into lower costs per installed capacity, vessels specifically
designed for offshore wind plants have been built in greater number - although still
considered to be under-dimensioned for the market needs - , industry experience has
grown and market policies and regulations have been further implemented lowering
investment risks. All this have translated into surprisingly low bids for new installed

2.2
Installation Campaigns
The life cycle of an offshore wind farm project can theoretically be split into the
following phases: development, engineering and design, construction and operation.
Today the construction phase is typically 1 to 2 years and the operational phase is
typically planned to be 20 to 25 years. Depending on each type of wind farm and its
location, the installation campaign may vary. However, most wind farms constructed
nowadays contain the following offshore activities:
2.2.1
Seabed surveys
Seabed surveys are conducted in order to provide information to the teams in charge of
the foundation design, installation and cable laying. This information include, among
other, soil conditions to optimize the jack-up of the installation vessel, verification
of soil conditions used to design the foundations and inspection of non-approachable
areas (e.g. unexploded ordnances, already existing cabling...).
2.2.2
Offshore substation (OSS)
The substation gathers the power generated by the turbines and increases the voltage,
minimizing power losses in the cabling from the wind farm site to the connection to
the onshore transmission system. Today the typical OSS capacity is 400 MW for
alternating current (AC) systems. In some cases, the power from multiple AC OSS is
connected to a collector substation connected to the onshore grid via direct current.
Substations are generally of great size and weight, and are supported by large jacket
structures commonly used in the offshore oil and gas industry. The installation of
the OSS and its topside is performed by a floating heavy lift crane vessel, that can be

2.2.3
Foundation
The installation of foundations depend very much on the type of foundation to be
used. Until now, the most commonly used structures have been the monopile, jacket
and gravity based, the first being the most popular and reaching lengths of up to 80m
7 and weights of about 1300 tonnes 8. Monopiles are fixed to the bottom by piling
the structure into the seabed while jackets are fixed with pin-piles installed before or
after the jacket is placed on the sea bed. In case of installing the pin piles after the
jacket is laid on the seabed, the jacket is used as the piling frame for the pin piles.
Additionally, jackets can also be fixed to the sea bed with suction buckets, although
this technique is more experimental at this point of time. Jackets are more suitable
for rougher conditions and deeper waters - they have been used in water depths of up
to 50 m [4]. Jackets and monopiles are commonly installed by jack-up vessels, either
self-propelled or barges, that elevate themselves to gain stability and perform the
required deck activities followed by the piling using a hydraulic hammer. Vessels can
also achieve stability by the so called dynamic positioning system, in which stability
is reached not by jacking-up but rather by compensating the environmental forces on
the vessel.

2.2.3.1
Scour protection
Scour refers to the phenomenon caused by sea currents removing seabed material. For
offshore foundations, and especially for monopiles, this event translates into structural
instability around the foundation’s base on the seabed. There are several methods to
cope with this issue that can be performed by more regular vessels, such as laying a
pile of rocks around the foundation’s base which prevents scouring to happen.
2.2.4
Cable installation
The cabling between turbines inside the wind farm (inter array cabling) and from the
wind farm to land depends mainly on the type of cable laying required. The type of
cable laying will depends of the site conditions: seabed surface characteristics, sea cur-
rents, safety and impact on the environment. Several types of vessels have been used
depending on the specific project requirements: Remotely Operated Vessels (ROV),
barges with towing spreads or self propelled vessels. Normally, cables are pulled in
from one end, then the cables are laid on top of the sea bed. Afterwards, the cables
are pulled in from the other end and jetted down in the sea bed using a ROV.
2.2.5
Turbine erection
Turbine erection can start once the transition piece has been mounted on the founda-
tion. It usually begins after the cabling campaign is finished. The vessels used are the
same jack-up vessels than for the foundations (either self propelled or barges). How-
ever, no dynamic positioning vessels have been used yet due to safety reasons. This is
linked to the notable sensitivity of the turbine components to the climatic conditions.
The different parts of the turbine are on the vessel’s deck, with a certain degree of
pre-assembly already performed onshore (harbor or factory). A pre-assembly strat-
egy can be bringing the turbine to site in a full rotor (three blades and hub), nacelle
and a full tower, requiring only three lifts offshore. Another more common example
is erecting the turbine in 5 lifts: one full tower, pre-assembled hub and nacelle and
three single blades. This strategy requires more lifts to be performed offshore but on
the other hand optimizes vessel’s deck usage, enabling more turbines to be carried
per trip which can be beneficial for sites located far from shore [5].

2.2.6
Commissioning
The commissioning is the last phase of the construction of an offshore wind farm. It
refers to all activities performed after all components are installed and before com-
mercial operation can begin. During this period, it is made sure the turbines are set
up as required in the contract and compliant with the grid code in order to start
generating power into the grid. The activities to be performed during this period
include mechanical and electrical test on the turbine components as well as trial pe-
riods during which the turbine must demonstrate reliability - typically 240 hours.

2.2 Installation Campaigns
9
The commissioning is performed by technicians which need to be transferred into
each and every turbine. The two most common means used to access the turbines
during the commissioning phase are: firstly, the Crew Transfer Vessel (CTV) approach
in which these relatively small vessels (compared to the installation vessels) carry
teams of technicians from the harbour to the site. Secondly the Service Operation
Vessel (SOV) approach in which a larger vessel that can host many different teams
of technicians is stationed by the wind farm site, without the need of going back and
forth from the harbour to the offshore site. The use of one strategy or the other
one depends on many factors such as distance from site to harbour, vessels rates or
environmental restrictions (e.g. limited marine traffic through an area).

2.3
Existing tools and literature review on offshore wind
farm installation campaigns
Due to its significant share in the CAPEX of an offshore wind farm, the installa-
tion phase has taken the attention of both the professional and academic community.
Studies have been carried out regarding the optimization of the wind turbine pre-
assembly strategies. [5] analyzes among others, the distance from site to harbour
and turbine pre-assembly degree by analyzing vessel’s deck space and site distance.
It finally concludes that for wind farms located further from shore, a lower degree
of pre-assembly outperforms the number of lifts to be carried out offshore for the
turbine erection. [7] presents an assessment on the impact of vessel load and offshore
lifts into the overall duration of an offshore wind farm. This is done by assessing, in
sequence, if the weather requirements (three types) for the activities to be performed
are met considering probabilities out of a two year period weather time series.
Another line of investigation has been the usage of linear programming in order
to optimize different aspects of an offshore wind farm construction. Linear programs
are composed by an objective function to be optimized, either minimized (e.g. total
installation costs) or maximized (e.g. total revenue), and some constraints (that can
be known or uncertain) that bound the domain of the objective function. [8] and [9]
propose a similar approach to [7], in which by means of linear programming the ag-
gregate construction plan costs are minimized, evaluating the weather requirements
of the activities involved using 50 years of past weather data. [10] follows [7] and
[9], including an optimization problem to determine the optimal period in which the
installation must be performed. This linear program considers different weather sce-
narios, which lead to uncertain boundary conditions, constructed out of a two year
weather time series. Weather scenarios increase the number of constraints the linear
program is subjected to, extending the computational time needed to solve the master
problem. In order to cope with this, decomposition techniques (Benders decomposi-
tion) are applied in this study. It concludes that installation campaigns should lean
towards the period with most favourable weather conditions to avoid huge costs dur-
ing periods with severe weather. [11] introduces an optimization approach for the
whole construction phase of a wind farm, by simulating the total time of construction
considering weather scenarios based on synthetic time series which derive from a real
weather time series, as presented in [12].
[13] tackles the offshore wind farm construction schedules and costs directly, feed-
ing all the constraints and deterministic time series into the linear problem. In order
to solve the problem in a reasonable amount of time, the different steps on the con-
struction phase are simplified. It finally delivers an analysis on optimal port selection
and vessel fleet as well as an analysis on weather window impact.

2.4 Motivation

Discrete event simulation (DES) is another tool that has been widely used to
better understand the drivers of uncertain processes, including the construction of
offshore wind farms. This approach simulates a process as the sum of discrete events
that happen through time. [14] presents a tool which introduces the sequence of ac-
tivities to be performed and their weather requirements and window duration. The
evaluation of a workable weather window is based on probabilities of having the de-
termined weather requirements for the time specified obtained from a weather time
series of approximately 10 years. [15] evaluates workable weather windows by compar-
ing operation requirements to a set of synthetic weather time series generated out of
real measurements, following [12]. The analysis provides valuable insights, including
a sensitivity analysis on the number of vessels and their operational limits and its
impact on the total duration of a jacket and turbine installation campaigns. It finally
concludes that loading operations are the ones most responsible of the total delay of
the project for that specific case considered. [16] also applies the synthetic weather
scenarios approach and includes an analysis on the number of vessels used and its
impact on the total costs of the campaign. [3] makes an analysis of the different in-
stallation vessels used across the past UK round 1 and 2 offshore wind farm projects.
The different types of vessels are represented by their operational limits, and imple-
mented in scenarios representing the two rounds: first round projects were closer to
shore and with milder weather conditions whereas second round projects were deeper
and further from shore meaning also rougher weather conditions. By implementing
a DES considering different synthetic weather scenarios (following [12]), the authors
make a thorough evaluation of the different vessels used for the two different kind of
projects. They conclude that the spreads used in the first round were less capable
although the total uncertainty of the duration of the campaigns was low, due to the
project’s shallow waters and distance to shore. Second round spreads were purpose
built - however installation duration contained more uncertainty due to the rougher
conditions and further distances to shore compared to the first round projects.

Motivation
This project comes out of a collaboration between DTU and Lautec ApS. The latter
has extensive experience in offshore wind farm planning and provides, among others,

Is it worth it to hire a more powerful vessel that operates in rougher conditions or
a less capable vessel delivers fine in the site under study? The comparison is not
straight forward at first sight, as every project is unique.

3.1.1
MetOcean time series
MetOcean time series are recorded values of meteorological and oceanic variables
with a defined resolution that represent the weather state for a period considered -
e.g. a two year record of wave height in a specific location, with a logging frequency
of 1s gives an idea of the ocean conditions over that particular period in that location.
The main purpose of using MetOcean time series in this methodology is to be able
to reproduce the past weather characteristics of the site object of evaluation. Thus,
it is important to use MetOcean time series that are representative of the specific site
in order for the final evaluation of the installation campaign to be of any value. The
MetOcean variables relevant for this project are :
• Significant wave height:
the mean value of the highest third wave heights
recorded during the time period considered.
• Peak wave period: period containing the highest energy content of the wave
motion. It is obtained by doing first a frequency analysis of the wave motion,
determining the frequency with the highest energy content. Afterwards, this
frequency f is translated into the period T by:
T = 1
f
(3.1)
• Wind speed: the mean value of the horizontal wind speed recorded through the
time period considered.
The source of this data can be very varied, from measured values taken with mea-
surement devices to synthetic data generated with statistical properties. Taking into
consideration the motivation of this project - tool to compare installation campaigns
in different locations - , it becomes essential to be able to have access to these MetO-
cean time series in a wide range of geographical locations.
MetOcean time series
coming from measurements are the most representative of a location since the data
logged are real values on-site (although containing measurement uncertainties). How-
ever, the access to this information is constrained: limited amount of measurement
devices deployed, data rights and ownership, limited amount of time period recorded
- to just mention a few. All in all, MetOcean time series coming from measurements
are the most representative of that location, but on the other hand they pose an
accessibility issue which interferes with this project’s site comparison goal. To cope
with this, another MetOcean data source was found, re-analysis data. This type of

3.1.1.2
Re-analysis data
Re-analysis data is the result of two steps. In the first one, a weather model uses
equations that are solved along a grid of points or nodes expanding along the globe
with a defined spatial resolution which results in a prediction of past weather be-
haviour. Additionally, the model uses past measurements at available locations as
initial conditions to run the calculations. The second step comprises the calibration
and adaptation of the weather model output with real historical measurements. Re-
analysis data is then able to represent past weather events in places where there were
no measurements with a degree of accuracy that will depend on the re-analysis model,
spatial resolution and measurement availability in the area of interest.
There are many re-analysis products and models. The one chosen for this project
has been the one developed by the European Centre for Medium-Range Weather
Forecasts (ECMWF), the ECMWF Re-Analysis 5 (ERA5). This product is one of
the newest developed by the organization to this date (June 2018) and features a
spatial resolution of some 30km (depending on the data chosen) for a state of the
art weather model that combines a coupled atmospheric and ocean model. The mea-
surements used for the model adaptation are recorded in the ECMWF website6. The
biggest share come from satellites although some land-based stations are also used.
In addition, ECMWF expects to cover a time period from 1950 until three months
of real time with ERA5, to be ready for use early 2019 7. As of now, a time interval
from 2008 until three months of real time is ready and available for download at
their website 8. The codes used to retrieve the data sets (wave and atmospheric) are
contained in Listing B.1 and Listing B.2.

Table 3.1 shows a weather window column which refers to the minimum time period
during which the weather requirements, in this case wind speed at 100m and signifi-
cant wave height Hs, must be met in order for the activity to be performed. In real
life, the installation vessel will receive weather forecasts periodically (approximately
every 6 hours) and based on these, the contractor will evaluate if there is a work-
able weather window based on the minimum weather window duration with specific
requirements for the given activity. In case of having a minimum weather window
duration shorter than the activity duration, this means that the activity can be halted
in between. This can be observed in ”Load 3 turbines” activity in Table 3.1, where
the short minimum weather window is representing the minimum weather window
duration to load one turbine, so the activity of loading three turbines can start if one
turbine can be loaded.
The minimum weather window duration is established in real projects with the al-
pha factor α defined in ”Section 4: Operational requirements” of Det Norske Veritas’
(DNV) standard[18]. By taking into account the minimum duration of the activity
to be performed and its kind (its degree of complexity), the quality of the forecasts
received, the existence of a monitoring system or meteorologist on board and the
maximum acceptable value of weather variables, the alpha factor assesses the risk of
the activity. The risks are accounted for in the contingency time, that is added on top
of the minimum duration to perform the activity. The sum of the contingency and
minimum time to perform the activity yield in the minimum weather window dura-
tion. The more risk involved, the greater the contingency time resulting in increased
minimum weather window duration. An example could be : offshore transfer of a
jacket foundation from a barge to a jack-up vessel, with poor quality weather forecasts
and no meteorologist nor monitoring system on board. Due to this circumstances,
the minimum weather window for this activity would be significantly greater than
the minimum duration to perform the activity in order to reduce risk and guarantee
an acceptable degree of safety. Additionally, the Marine Warranty Surveyor is the
neutral figure between contractor and employer, that bears witness of all operations
making sure minimum weather window durations and operational limits are respected.
Furthermore, there are other recent approaches on how to calculate these minimum
weather window durations, based on the probability of failure for a specific task and
equipment - [19] and [20]. However, DNV’s [18] approach remains industry standard
for offshore operations.

The tool can in fact perform two types of simulations:
• Continuous simulations perform the list of activities provided in the installation
campaign setup, normally just a generic cycle representative of the campaign
as in Table 3.1 and loops them over time. The tool starts the discrete event
simulation as described until it finishes the sequence of activities. After the
sequence is finished it executes the same sequence of activities again until the
end of the MetOcean time series.
Doing such, the tool can obtain, for this
cycle representative of the campaign, the downtime for the period considered
in the time series. It is also possible to compute the downtime by period of the
year (seasonal,monthly, weekly...) or by the constraint responsible, e.g. wave
height or wave period. Having several years of data enables the user to compute
statistics, which are more representative the greater the period considered.
• Non-continuous simulations do not perform a sequence in a loop. They perform
a project specific sequence, defined by all the activities to be performed in the
campaign and a start date.
– Non-continuous simulation example: 50 position foundation campaign with
a start date on March 22nd. The sequence of activities have to include all
actions needed to finish the campaign. Assuming the user has 10 years of
MetOcean data, the tool starts the campaign every 22nd of March. Dif-
ferent campaign duration estimates are obtained each year, depending on
the MetOcean conditions of the specific year. In this example, the ten du-
rations obtained are used to capture different statistical results that help
better understand the characteristics of the site and campaign.
The tool can also account for inter-dependencies between activities and vessels, mean-
ing for example that it can take into account the installation of a foundation campaign
comprised by five vessels: two barges with foundations and another two with tran-
sition pieces feeding and installation vessel. In this case, the foundation installation
cannot start until the foundation feeder barge has arrived at the site, and the lat-
ter cannot leave until the foundation is installed by the installation vessel. For the
sake of simplicity, campaigns including such inter-dependencies have not been con-
sidered in this report. However, it is left out to the user to apply this methodology
to whichever campaign they consider of interest - there contained more complex ones.

Furthermore, the software can also take into account the frequent breakdowns
that happen during the installation process, during which the operations are stopped
and no progress in the installation is made. This breakdowns comprehend the dif-
ferent events such as technical breakdowns (e.g. fault on the hydraulic system that
prevents the vessel to jack up), maintenance and any other unpredictable event that
the user might want to account for. They are executed for the duration and frequency
specified by the user after an activity is performed.
It must also be mentioned that some assumptions have been made. The first one
is regarding activity locations and weather time series. The sequence of activities are
performed in different locations (offshore site, harbour, coast line...), albeit there is
a single time series representative of a single location. Thus it is not strictly correct
to evaluate a weather window for an activity in port by assessing if the weather
requirements are met, looking at the time series representative of the offshore site.
However this assumption is made for the sake of simplicity but it must be taken into
account, specially in cases where offshore sites are significantly far away from port
resulting in big differences in climate conditions. The second assumption is regarding
the usage of past weather data. In reality, described in Section 3.1.2, a workable
weather window is assessed by usage of weather forecasts obtained every 6 hours. By
implementing the described tool, past weather data is employed for weather window
evaluation, rather than updated forecasts every 6 hours for the sake of simplicity. The
third and last assumption is regarding the breakdown modelling. It is assumed that
breakdown duration is constant, as well as it frequency. In addition, it is considered
that it cannot happen in between an activity but rather before the start of a new one.
This assumption hold the best the more activities an installation campaign is divided
into e.g. divide the blade installation activity into 10 sub activities, so breakdown
can happen in between the blade installation activity. Finally, activity durations are
assumed to be constant. From this end, a learning curve can be applied to the first
activities in the campaign. However in general in real life, the duration of the same
activity will vary along the campaign which is something this model doesn’t take
into account but it could be implemented in future works - see Chapter 6 for further
details.

4.3
Veja Mate offshore wind farm case comparison
This section aims to provide a comparison between a real life installation duration
and the estimates obtained by the methodology using non-continuous DES simula-
tion. The project to be compared against has been the monopile installation on the
Veja Mate offshore wind farm 1 commissioned in 2017. The reason why this project
has been chosen is that the installation activities were recorded using Lautec’s Daily
Progress Report (DPR) system 2 , and analyzed afterwards, obtaining the mean du-
rations of all activities involved in the installation process - which is crucial for the
input file of the methodology. The reason to choose the monopile installation has
been the simplicity for the modelling - only one vessel installing, whereas for the tur-
bine erection there were multiple vessels involved, starting at different times 3. The
type of simulation used has been the non-continuous simulation, explained previously
in Section 3.2, which is targeted to project specific cases, like this one. The start date
for the project is April 1st as in the real case 3. The input file concerning all ac-
tivities involved for the installation of the 67 monopiles has been made taken into
consideration the durations obtained from Lautec’s DPR system - list of activities
and durations not depicted to avoid confidentiality issues.
It must be noted that the values used to create the input file are average duration
values of an activity, meaning that a specific activity (e.g. piling the 44th monopile)
could deviate from the average, potentially making an impact on the total duration
of the project. An example could be that the jacking-up of the installation vessel 3
was slower than the average during the first months (start date beginning of April),
which coincide with the best installation season, pushing the project towards autumn
when the jacking up duration would be shorter than the mean. However, the project
would suffer greater downtime as all the activities left to be performed are pushed
into harsher weather conditions.
Additionally, during the installation process, a certain amount of time was spent
repairing components, doing vessel maintenance and such - referred as breakdown. In
general, it is downtime not due to weather or logistics constraints. This breakdown
was also registered in Lautec’s DPR system and accounted for in this analysis. It was
modelled as the average duration of the breakdown recorded with a frequency of once
a week - modelling described in Section 3.2. It must also be taken into consideration,
as for the average duration of activities, that considering an average breakdown fre-
quency and duration involves certain assumptions and limitations.

re-analysis data set described all along this report, implementing Listing B.1 and

Figure 4.19 show two distinct trends of the average monthly weather downtime.
The first trend, with increased downtime values is related to the OWF4 and OWF5

weather conditions. The second trend, with more contained downtime values, relates
with the OWF1, OWF2 and OWF3 sites located in the Kattegat Straight and Baltic
Sea - sheltered areas where weather conditions are milder. The downtime differences
between the two trends increase during the winter months, reaching its maximum in
December with a difference of about 30%. Overall, the monthly downtime values for
different sites contained in the same trend do not differ significantly. Additionally,
downtime uncertainty increases during the winter months. Considering Figure 4.19
and assuming all other site characteristics are identical between each other, the best
place to locate an offshore wind farm would be either at OWF1 or OWF3 locations.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
In this report, a preliminary evaluation on the re-analysis ERA5 input data against
measurements has been performed in two locations. The comparison has shown that
ERA5 reproduces observed values with different degrees of accuracy. In respect to
wind speed, ERA5 data adapts remarkably well with a bias of less than 0.3%. In
regards to significant wave height (Hs) values, ERA5 data still shows a good per-
formance, however it is less accurate than for the wind speed values.
This lower
performance is due to a wider scatter, with an overall average bias of 2 and 8% for
the NOAA 44097 and FINO1 locations, respectively. Additionally, ERA5 Hs values
underestimate extreme values observed. In respect to peak wave period, ERA5 values
have a lower performance compared to the Hs and wind speed variables. This cannot
be deducted from the average bias values of 4 and 6% for the two locations but rather
on the spread scatter plots against measurements. After a further analysis, it has
been concluded that these large differences in peak wave period (Tp) values occur at
low Hs values from long fetched directions - pointing out the difficulties of ERA5 to
represent Tp values of long swells coming from long distances. It can thus be men-
tioned that considering a campaign subjected to Tp constrained activities will imply
greater uncertainties on estimated downtime when using ERA5 MetOcean input data.
An analysis was also performed on the methodology’s estimated downtime when
using the two sources of MetOcean data (measurements and ERA5 re-analysis) at
FINO1 location for two foundation installation and one turbine erection campaigns.
In order to perform this comparison, the time resolution of the measurement MetO-
cean input data has been downsized to hourly values. It has been shown that the
campaign’s monthly estimated downtime when using ERA5 is on average around 5%
(with a maximum recorded value of 10%) underestimated compared to what the mea-
surements yield. Additionally, the maximum monthly weather downtime difference is
recorded in the foundation installation campaign performed by a dynamic positioning
vessel subjected to Tp constraints. This aligns with what was expected in the Tp time
series analysis - greater uncertainty for Tp constrained activities.
Overall, it can be concluded that the use of ERA5 as MetOcean input data for
the methodology brings a vast amount of locations available and, for the specific loca-
tion considered of FINO1, yields slightly underestimated downtime values compared
to those obtained by using measurements as MetOcean input data. The degree of
==PAGE:74==
62
5 Conclusion
accuracy on estimated downtime using ERA5 data as input data depends on the
variables the activities in the campaign are subjected to - Tp constrained activities
convey more uncertainty. It is worth mentioning that ERA5’s limitations such as it’s
one-hour time resolution, difficulties to reproduce long swells and extreme values do
not have a significant relevance for the scope of this project. However, this should be
re-assessed when using this data set in a different application, including component
design or wind resource assessment.
Additionally, an illustrative example is presented in which two fictional installa-
tion strategies are presented to be carried out at the FINO1 location, represented by
the usage of two different vessels for a foundation installation campaign: a jack-up
vessel and a dynamic-positioning vessel, assumed to be faster and cheaper. ERA5
and measurement data has been used to represent the MetOcean conditions at site
and a brief cost analysis is presented, establishing a starting point for the planning
stage of the installation phase of an offshore wind farm. It must also be noted that
this methodology has focused mainly on foundation and turbine erection campaigns
which play a major role in the total costs of the construction phase of an offshore wind
farm, yet it can be implemented on any installation campaign as long as weather re-
quirements and activities are known in order to create an operational input file like
in Table 3.1. Moreover, a sensitivity analysis has been performed on the operational
limits of a vessel carrying out a wind turbine erection campaign at the FINO1 lo-
cation, which can also serve as a starting point for the planning of the installation
phase of an offshore wind farm. The analysis has shown that for the case considered,
the minimum weather window duration doesn’t have a significant impact on the cam-
paign’s performance but rather the mean wind speed at 100 meters and significant
wave height constraint - especially the latter. The analysis also shows that within
a campaign, certain months are more sensitive to a change in operational limits de-
pending on the climatic characteristics of that specific month.
The methodology described has been implemented to estimate the weather down-
time in a real installation case using ERA5 data as MetOcean input data.
The
case considered has been the installation of 67 monopiles on the Veja Mate offshore
wind farm. The results, compared to recorded values, show that with appropriate as-
sumptions on the campaign sequence input data, regarding durations and operational
limits, the methodology delivers representative estimates of the installation duration.
Additionally, it is also concluded that by increasing the data period considered, more
results can be obtained and a more representative risk assessment can be undertaken.
Furthermore, no apparent relationship has been observed between weather variables
(wind speed and significant wave height) annual inter-variability and campaign dura-
tion.

the purpose of visual campaign benchmarking across sites on a regional scale. Even
though it hasn’t been exposed in this report, this benchmarking can also be imple-

been benchmarked in Denmark, providing decision support for the forthcoming pro-

shows that, only taking into account the total duration of the installation process,
the most suitable area to develop the next offshore wind power projects would be in
the Baltic Sea and Kattegat Straight area.

CHAPTER 6
Outlook
In order to gain a more complete perspective of ERA5’s MetOcean input data per-
formance, the comparison against measurements presented in this project should be
extended ideally to other locations including as well an impact analysis on weather
downtime estimation. It would also be recommended to carry out an analysis of ERA5
wind speed data performance at a location where comparison against measurements
can be performed at lower heights than 100 meters. Additionally, further research on
why ERA5 struggles to reproduce long swells with low wave heights would be interest-
ing in order to try to cap uncertainties on wave peak period constrained campaigns.
Concerning the MetOcean input data time resolution, an analysis could be carried
out to determine the influence of higher time resolution data on the methodology’s
downtime estimation. This can be executed by directly using measurements, which
have greater time resolution than one hour, into the methodology.
In regards to the discrete event simulation approach used in the methodology,
more sophisticated modelling could be performed including probabilistic values of
activity durations, breakdown duration and breakdown frequency rather than the
deterministic values used. The probabilistic values could be based on Lautec’s own
daily progress reports (DPR) of activities and breakdown durations and frequencies.
For example, the modelling of a blade installation could be done with a mean value
and established standard deviation coming from DPR data. By implementing such
approach, the sensitivity of the campaign to variable and uncertain parameters could
be tested.
The time period available of ERA5 data is at present time limited to 10 years, but
will increase up to 50 years by 2019. Greater time period of input data translates into
greater amount of results, which could be treated with a more reliable probabilistic
approach. Probability functions of estimated downtime could be obtained, enabling
a risk-based performance of an installation campaign by introducing percentiles. Ad-
ditionally, a study could be conducted with the purpose of establishing the minimum
number of years of data needed to obtain reliable simulation results. Further studies
could analyze the impact of considering different weather scenarios, by making use
of the ERA5 ensemble data set containing 10 different realizations instead of the
deterministic time series currently used.
==PAGE:78==
66
6 Outlook
The expected increase in the time period covered by ERA5 data will help improve
the methodology’s estimates of installation durations. Following, the methodology
could be extended, if valid vessel day rates were known, estimating the optimal deci-
sion by undertaking a risk assessment based on estimated installation costs.
Ultimately, a color graded map based on the indexing described in the methodol-
ogy could be created, enabling a quick comparison between different sites at a regional
or global level. This tool could give valuable insights at an early stage of an offshore
wind farm site selection.
