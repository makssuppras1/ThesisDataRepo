Abstract
Ontology matching is an important tool for master data management (MDM)
systems. In MDM systems, the synchronization of data is paramount and
ontology matching is an important tool in ensuring that.
Ontologies are
represented in the Web Ontology Language (OWL) based on description
logic.
In this project, the notion of simple ontologies from [1] is expanded from
being a simple tree structure of concepts to also include the attributes in
the tree nodes. These attributes in the simple ontologies are used to find
initial matches between concepts in the ontologies. One problem of ontology
matching is where to start; this project aims to efficiently discover initial
matches by comparing the attributes of concepts in the ontologies using cus-
tom conditions for specialization.
To compare simple ontologies to OWL ontologies, and to test the proposed
conditions for initial matches on actual ontologies, some OWL ontologies
from the Ontology Alignment Evaluation Initiative (OAEI) is turned into
simple ontologies.
The initial matches found with the specialization conditions are unfortu-
nately not satisfactory. The conditions are too strict and seemingly obvious
matches are missed.
To evaluate the specialization conditions, the satisfiability modulo theory
solver Z3 is used. While Z3 is an efficient solver, another evaluation method
based on maximum flow algorithms is more than 10 times faster than Z3.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Retailers often work with numerous suppliers. A supermarket has a supplier
for bananas, another for coffee, a third for peanut butter, and so on. There
might even be multiple suppliers for the same product category, and one
supplier can supply products to multiple categories. In Denmark, the two
largest retail groups, Salling Group A/S and Coop Danmark A/S, have more
than 5700 [2] and 2000 [3] suppliers respectively.
Having this many suppliers has it upsides: the consumers have access to
a large variety of goods and the products are consistently available. When
multiple suppliers deliver the same product, the price might even go down as
they compete. But there are also downsides, the most prevalent for this thesis
being representing the products from the thousands of different suppliers in
one place.
In a perfect world, there would exist a standard for how suppliers must
represent their products in a database. But the real world is messy, meaning
that the databases of the suppliers are likely to be vastly different to each
other. When a retailer, who has their own database for all the products they
sell, then wants to work with a supplier, the data from the suppliers database
must be mapped to the retailer’s database.
1.1
Ontologies and Matching
The problem of mapping data is in this thesis reduced to the ontoloy matching
problem. In simple terms, an ontology is a tree of concepts and subconcepts
where a concept can be thought of as a category. A simple ontology is shown
in Figure 1.1 where a fruit can be either an apple or a banana, and the apple
and banana have subcategories (in this case: sorts) themselves.
The problem of ontology matching is then: given two ontologies, is it
1

Figure 1.1: Simple fruit ontology (without attributes).
possible to match concepts between the two ontologies? In Figure 1.2, two
ontologies are shown. The right ontology shows a retailer that sells books
and vinyl records while the left ontology is that of a book supplier. In the
two ontologies, it is clear that some concepts are the same:
• a Volume in the left ontology is a Book in the right ontology;
• Nonfiction in the left ontology is Non-fiction in the right ontology; etc.
In Figure 1.2, it is also shown that ontologies can contain data, i.e. ontology
concepts have attributes. In ontologies, the attributes of a concept are in-
herited to subconcepts so a Book in the right ontology inherits the attribute
name from Product because a Book is a Product. Attributes can also be
aligned, e.g. the title of a Volume in the left ontology is the name of a Book
in the right ontology.
While seeing these connections between concepts and attributes is very
intuitive for people, the point of ontology matching is to automate the pro-
cess. A computer does not have the same intuition and it is therefor forced
to look at many different combinations of matchings. Hence, the complexity
of the problem quickly becomes huge with many concepts and attributes in
each ontology.
1.2
Master Data Management (MDM)
Ontology alignment is important to Stibo Systems as they focus on master
data management (MDM). MDM is about combining all master data (sup-
plier data, product data, employee data, etc.) that a company has into a
“central repository for building and sharing a single version of truth” [5]. To

Stibo Systems’ MDM ontology matching problem is used as the inspira-

none of their data has been used.
1.3
Related Work
In the academic sphere, a big driver in the ontology matching sphere is the
Ontology Alignment Evaluation Initiative (OAEI). Each year, OAEI hosts
a campaign with the aim of “evaluating ontology matching technologies”
3
==PAGE:10==
[6].
Here, a consistently high ranking matcher is LogMap [7].
LogMap,
as the name implies, focuses on using logic to match ontologies: finding
logically consistent matches by use of lexical comparison and respecting the
logical structure of the ontology. Other solutions, and OAEI competitors, like
BERTMap [8], moreover uses machine learning (ML) techniques to assist in
the matching. The use of artificial intelligence (AI) or ML makes intuitive
sense, especially considering the strides made in recent years.
OAEI uses the standard Web Ontology Language (OWL) to represent
and work with the ontologies. OWL is built upon description logic (DL) and
is thus very expressive. The ontologies considered are typically more complex
than those in MDM systems.
The ontology editor Prot´eg´e [9] allows for creating ontologies in OWL
and apply reasoners, like HermiT [10], to the ontologies to check for contra-
dictions.
In [1], Michael R. Hansen (supervisor for this thesis) employs standard
product ontologies, ECLASS and UNSCPC, as use cases for ontology match-
ing.
The ontologies used here are however not in the OWL format.
A
notion of simple ontologies is introduced which makes use of a simple tree
data structure for the ontologies. Hansen’s work is also motivated by Stibo’s
MDM systems.
1.4
Goals
Using ideas from the related work, to automatically align two ontologies like
the ones in Figure 1.2, the following goals are posited:
Goal 1. Expand the notion of simple ontologies in [1] with attributes.
Goal 2. Construct a reduction from OWL to simple ontologies and develop
a piece of software that does it automatically.
Goal 3. Develop rules for aligning attributes and sets of attributes such
that initial matches can be found. A similar idea is present in LogMap;
where LogMap focuses purely on lexical matches for “anchor mappings”
[7], this thesis uses attributes in the search for initial matches.
Goal 4. Test if the SMT solver Z3 is an efficient tool for finding initial
matches and evaluating consistency of ontologies. Z3 has the tagline
“An efficient SMT Solver” [11] – it is tested if Z3 is “efficient” in this
domain.
4
==PAGE:11==
Goal 5. Devise an algorithmic alternative to Z3 such that the performance
of Z3 is comparable.
Goal 6. Read OWL ontologies from OAEI, turn them into simple ontologies
and find initial matches.
The idea to use attributes to find initial matches is rooted in MDM: the
important aspect of MDM is the single, agreed-upon truth, i.e. that no data
is duplicated. Attributes are the fields containing the data, hence aligning
the attributes is paramount.
1.5
Thesis Structure
First, in Chapter 2, the notion of ontologies is made clear. Simple ontologies
are explained as well as ontologies in the Web Ontology Language (OWL)
format. Chapter 3 then shows how OWL ontologies can be converted to
simple ontologies.
In Chapter 4, the rules for alignments in ontology matching are presented.
These are the rules for consistent alignments of concepts and attributes.
Some custom rules are furthermore introduced in an attempt to find initial
matches between two ontologies.
Chapters 5 and 6 tackle the same problem: finding initial matches using
rules from Chapter 4. In Chapter 5, the SMT solver Z3 is used to find the
initial matches in the hope that it is as efficient as its tagline [11].
The
problem is solved algorithmically in Chapter 6 where the problem is reduced
to a maximum flow problem.
In Chapter 7 all the methods discussed in the previous chapters are used
on ontologies from OAEI Conference track. Two OWL ontologies are con-
verted to simple ontologies and then initial matches are found.
It is ensured that the solution works in Chapter 8. Not only that, the per-
formance of each approach from Chapters 5 and 6 is measured and compared.
The performance of the software in general is discussed.
Finally, further work is discussed in Chapter 9. Everything is wrapped
up in the conclusion, Chapter 10.

The database table analogy is not perfect though: storing multiple values

the domain is atomic, i.e. not a collection of elements.
For other data storage options, this is not a problem. For instance, the
JSON format is more suitable for having collections as values.
2.3
Wellformedness
There are some conditions for a simple ontology to be wellformed concerning
both concepts and attributes. The conditions are:
1. Each concept in a simple ontology has a unique IRI.
2. The concept hierarchy is a tree and thus, it does not contain any cycles.
3. Each attribute in a simple ontology has a unique IRI.
The first condition is that all concept IRIs must be distinct, i.e.
no
two concepts must share IRI. Because a concept’s IRI simply consists of the
base IRI of the ontology and the name of the concept, this wellformedness
condition also dictates that no concept in a simple ontology can share name
with another.

2.4.1

A literal is either an atomic proposition p ∈B (a positive literal) or a negated
atomic proposition ¬p (a negative literal). A Horn formula is a conjunction
of so-called Horn clauses where a clause is a disjunction of literals with at

The reason for using Horn logic is that it allows for fast satisfiability check.

2.4.3
Interpreting Concept Horn Clauses
When representing simple ontologies as Horn formulas, two types of Horn
clauses from Table 2.3 are used: definitive and pure negative. The meaning
behind them is interesting to interpret.
The first rule, that a concept c implies its ancestor ca, can be interpreted
as “c is an ca.” ([1] uses the notion of an is-a relation where c is-a ca.)
This makes intuitive sense as c is a subconcept of ca, i.e. a special case (or a
specialization) of ca. It is only an implication as ca is not necessarily a c.
The second rule, c ∧c′
=⇒false, can be understood as “c and c′ are
mutually disjoint.” That is, it is not possible to find a third concept c′′ such
that c′′ is a c and c′′ is a c′.
To understand why the rules work in a logical setting, it makes sense to
think of each concept as ‘existing’ or that ‘each concept in the ontology must
exist on its own’. Checking for ontology consistency is checking that each
concept in the ontology can exist. In practice, this means the following:
1. Pick one concept c as the anchor concept;
2. assume that c exists, i.e. c ≡true; and
14
==PAGE:21==
3. check satisfiability for the Horn formula.
If there exists a satisfiable assignment for each c as the anchor concept in the
ontology then the ontology is consistent. The idea here is not that multiple
concepts all have to “coexist”, the important thing is that each concept can
exist on its own. If all concepts can exist on their own, then the ontology is
consistent.
Example 2.7. Invariant 1 in Section 2.3 can be explained via consistency.
In Figure 2.2a, the Horn formula for the ontology is:
(Apple =⇒Fruit) ∧(Other fruit =⇒Fruit) ∧(Banana =⇒Fruit)
∧(Apple ∧Banana =⇒false) ∧(Other fruit ∧Banana =⇒false)
∧(Apple =⇒Other fruit) ∧(Other fruit ∧Apple =⇒false).
Here, Banana can exist. The formula is valid when Banana and Fruit are
true while Apple and Other fruit are false. Thus, Banana can exist on its
own.
The ontology is incosistent as Apple cannot exist on its own: Apple ≡true
leads to a contradiction. Because Apple =⇒Other fruit, Other fruit must
also be true. This then leads to a contradiction as Other fruit ∧Apple =⇒
false is unsatisfiable.
2.4.4
Attribute Relations as Horn Clauses
In the context of Stibo and master data management, it is also valuable to
align attributes – the goal is to synchronize the data in the ontologies after
all. To accomplish that, the attribute relations (to concepts) must also be
formulated as Horn clauses. The rules are analogous to those for concepts:
1. An attribute a of a concept c is
a =⇒c
2. No two attributes a and a′ for the same are the same:
a ∧a′ =⇒false
Example 2.8. For a product in Figure 2.1, the rules for the attributes are:
(price =⇒Product) ∧(name =⇒Product)
∧(id =⇒Product) ∧(created by =⇒Product)
∧(price ∧name =⇒false) ∧(price ∧id =⇒false)
∧(price ∧created by =⇒false) ∧(name ∧id =⇒false)
∧(name ∧created by =⇒false) ∧(id ∧created by =⇒false)

Restrictions are also translated to attributes. Attributes don’t have cardi-

applied to class and applies to a given property for a certain range. The in-

flags are, as range has already been covered in Section 3.2.2.
A simple restriction pattern is the mandatory value pattern. The only
restriction that satisfies this is the exact cardinality restriction with n = 1.
That is one, mandatory value, hence the attribute flags:
IsOptional = false
IsCollection = false

A optional value is the maximum cardinality with n = 1 – i.e. at most
one value. This is reflected in attribute flags like so:
IsOptional = true

Overrides = false
Another restriction pattern is a mandatory collection. A mandatory col-

IsOptional = false

has become less meaningful as the cardinality is no longer present.

3.2.5
Restriction Pairs
Restrictions can also come in pairs. A restriction pair is two (object) re-
strictions for the same class with the same property – not necessarily range.
Three pairs have been identified:
• OnlySome,
• SomeExactly1Thing, and
• Min0Max1Thing.
The names are cryptic, so the idea behind each will be explained.
The first restriction pair, OnlySome, is a pair of the existential restriction
and the universal restriction with the same range.
Example 3.6. The two restrictions a Book can be
hasAuthor some Author
hasAuthor only Author
This means that an author of a book can only be an author and a book must
have at least one author.
In short the OnlySome restriction pair says: for this property, have at
least one mapping to this range and only this range. This is much like the
mandatory collection pattern from Section 3.2.4 but slightly different: the
universal restriction overrides the range of the attribute, so the flags are:
IsOptional = false
IsCollection = true
Overrides = true
Another restriction pair is SomeExactly1Thing. This is a pair of an ex-
istential restriction and the exact cardinality restriction with n = 1 and
owl:Thing as the range.
Example 3.7. A Product in a shop can have the following restrictions
fromSupplier some supplier
fromSupplier exactly 1 owl:Thing
A product is from a supplier and seemingly there can be more with the ex-
istential restriction, but the exact cardinality limits the cardinality to maxi-
mum one thing. Thus a product must have one and only one supplier.
29
==PAGE:36==
The SomeExactly1Thing restriction can be summarized as: for this prop-
erty, the class can only map to one thing and that one thing must be within
the specified range. Notice that without the universal restriction, the range
has been overridden:
IsOptional = false
IsCollection = false
Overrides = true
The final restriction pair is Min0Max1Thing. The restrictions in this pair
are minimum cardinality restriction with n = 0 and maximum cardinality
restriction with n = 1. The range for both restrictions is owl:Thing.
Example 3.8. In an ontology for books, a hasPublisher property has the
range Publisher. The class Book has the following restrictions.
hasPublisher min 0 owl:Thing
hasPublisher max 1 owl:Thing
Again, the cardinality restrictions apply to owl:Thing so the range of hasProperty
is not overridden: a Book can either have a publisher or not (be indepen-
dent).
In stead of limiting the range of a property, the Min0Max1Thing restric-
tion pair limits the cardinality. This is not 100% supported in the current
program but an approximation is to set the attribute flags as
IsOptional = false

Overrides = true
and setting the attribute’s range as the inherited attribute’s range.

3.2.8
Why Java?
Initially it was attempted to read OWL files using dotNetRDF for .NET.
This made sense as simple ontologies initially are implemented in F#. While
primarily a RDF library, the documentation hints at some OWL compatibil-
ity5. The library works fine for uncomplicated ontologies but quickly becomes
cumbersome. For instance, it can’t handle union domain elegantly. It is pos-
sible to extract the information: dotNetRDF works with graphs, so to get
the classes in a union meant recursively iterating through the graph. This in
itself is not awful but it did not bode well for the library.
In stead, it was attempted to use Python and the Owlready2 [22] library.
The library seemed great: It handles class unions with easy. Unfortunately,
the library does not handle OWL class restrictions to a satisfying degree.
The library casts the OWL datatypes to Python types and it is impossible
to backtrack what datatype the restriction has as range.
This is weirdly
enough not a problem for property ranges in general.
In stead, the program is implemented in Java using the industry standard
OWLAPI library. It worked great and had all the necessary features.

A new term is introduced: specialization. The subconcept relationship
is one type of specialization but an entity in one ontology can specialize an

entity in another ontology. If an entity e1 ∈O1 specializes an entity e2 ∈O2,
this means that e1 is a special case of e2. That is, if they were in the same
ontology, e1 would be a subconcept of e2. e1 specializes e2 is written as
e1 ≤e2.
(4.1)
Specialization for concepts is transitive meaning that if an entity e1 spe-

then e1 specializes e3 (e1 ≤e3), i.e.
(e1 ≤e2 ∧e2 ≤e3) =⇒e1 ≤e3.
(4.2)
This makes intuitive sense if thinking of specialization as a subconcept rela-
tion.
The equality alignment can be interpreted as special case of the special-
ization alignment such that
e1 = e2 =⇒e1 ≤e2 ∧e2 ≤e1.
(4.3)
Note the implication. It is tempting to use bi-implication but that is not
correct. But because specialization is transitive, Equation (4.2), it is possible
for two entities to mutually specialize each other without being equal. This
is easiest visualized in an example, see Example 4.3
Example 4.3. Literature and Fiction might not be the exact same cate-

4.4
Rules for Initial Matches
A difficult aspect of matching two ontologies is knowing where to start. An
obvious approach could be looking for concepts in both ontologies that share
37
==PAGE:44==
the same label. A more simple approach could be to ask the user for initial
alignments.
However, in this thesis, a different approach will be tested:
finding initial alignments of concepts by aligning their respective attributes.
This approach takes advantage of the notion of attributes in simple on-
tologies. Where properties in OWL are more abstract mappings, attributes
in simple ontologies are clearly defined fields/columns for each concept. That
is, because attributes are simple structures bound to a concept, they can be
used to aid in matching concepts.
4.4.1
Attribute Specialization
As an aid in finding initial matches, not as general rule, the concept of
attribute specialization (≤) is introduced. An attribute a1 specializes another
attribute a2, a1 ≤a2, if and only if:
• a1 and a2 has the same datatype;
• if a2 has a unit then a1 has the same unit;
• if a1 is optional then a2 is optional; and
• if a1 is a collection then a2 is a collection.
The most simple rule, that a1 and a2 share the same datatype makes
intuitive sense: if the attributes are supposed to represent the same thing,
the datatype must be the same. However, this rule might be a bit too harsh
as datatypes are not necessarily disjoint: an integer can be stored in a string
for instance. This is discussed more in Chapter 9.
The idea of calling a1 ≤a2 a specialization is that a1 is a more special
(or perhaps strict) version of a2. For the second condition, the idea is that
having a unit is more specialized (or restricted) than not having a unit. The
reasoning being that without a unit, an attribute can represent anything. But
if an attribute has a unit like meter, it can’t represent a weight attribute.
The third condition in effect says that an attribute being mandatory is a
specialization of being optional. The reasoning being that an optional value
can be the be there or not, while an mandatory value has to be there. Thus,
being mandatory is more restrictive.
Finally, having one value is more restrictive than having multiple values
and thus, a1 being a value is a specialization of a2 being a collection.
Example 4.5. In Figure 2.1, the name of a product and the title of a volume
are represented as
38
==PAGE:45==
• name: String and
• title: String+
respectively, where String+ means that a volume has at least one title.
The two attributes have the same type (String), unit (None), and they
are both mandatory. However, name is a value while title is a collection.
This means that name ≤title but the opposite is not true: a collection is
not a specialization of a value.
4.4.2
Attribute Set-specialization for Concepts
In this project, it is a goal to attempt to find initial matches using techniques
based in logic and formal methods. To this end, attribute set specialization
(≤s) will be defined. The goal is to find initial alignments between concepts,
and because concepts have a set of attributes, it might prove a worthwhile
endeavour to see if one concept’s attribute is somehow related to another
concept’s attributes.
The idea behind attribute set-specialization is as follows: for a set of at-
tributes A to specialize another set of attributes B, each attribute in A must
be able to specialize a distinct attribute in B. If all elements in A can spe-
cialize a distinct attribute in B, but B has leftover attributes, i.e. attributes
that are not specialized, all the leftover attributes must be optional.
To define the rule more formally, an auxiliary function is defined for
representing the distinct mappings from A to B. The injective function is a
distinct specialization map u≤: A →B such that for all mappings u≤(a) = b,
where a ∈A and b ∈B, it must be the case that a ≤b, i.e.
∀a ∈A . ∀b ∈B . u≤(a) = b =⇒a ≤b.
(4.5)
Furthermore, the function is injective:
∀a1, a2 ∈A . u≤(a1) = u≤(a2) ⇐⇒a1 = a2.
(4.6)
The function is not surjective, i.e. it does not necessarily map to all the
elements in the domain.
An example of the distinct specialization map is in Example 4.6.
With the concept of distinct specializations, it is possible to define set
specialization (≤s).For a set of attributes A to specialize another set of at-
tributes B, A ≤s B, the following conditions must be met.
1. Each attribute in A can specialize a unique attribute in B,
∀a ∈A . ∃b ∈B . u≤(a) = b.
(4.7)
39
==PAGE:46==
2. All attributes in B, that are not uniquely specialized to, must be op-
tional,
∀b ∈B . optional(b) ∨(∃a ∈A . u≤(a) = b).
(4.8)
As one formula:
A ≤s B ≡(∀a ∈A . ∃b ∈B . u≤(a) = b)
∧(∀b ∈B . optional(b) ∨(∃a ∈A . u≤(a) = b)).
Equation (4.7) implicitly contains the following implications.
• All elements in A must specialize an attribute in B,
A ≤s B =⇒∀a ∈A . ∃b ∈B . a ≤b.
(4.9)
• The number of attributes in B, |B|, must be at least the size of A, |A|:
A ≤s B =⇒|A| ≤|B|
(4.10)
Example 4.6. It is seen if Volume and Book in Figure 2.1 can possible set
align. (The two concepts are also shown in Figure 4.1.) Between the two
concepts, there are the following attribute specializations:
V olume/wholesale price ≤Book/price
V olume/author ≤Book/created by
Book/id ≤V olume/isbn
Book/name ≤V olume/title
Book/price ≤V olume/wholesale price
Book/price ≤V olume/pages

It is immediately clear that it is not possible for the attributes of Volume
to specialize the attributes of Book: only two attributes specialize, thus not
satisfying Equation (4.7).
However, Book ≤s V olume is possible. The attributes Book/id, Book/name,
and Book/created by can only specialize one attribute each in V olume so
they already have distinct specialization mappings. Book/price can special-
ize two attributes: V olume/wholesale price and V olume/pages. To satisfy
Equation (4.8), it is paramount that u≤(Book/price) = V olume/wholesale price
as V olume/pages is optional.
Using attribute set-specialization, initial matches can be discovered be-
tween two ontologies: iterate through all the possible matches (pairs of con-
cepts) in the ontologies and see if the attributes of the concepts set-specialize.
If they do, add that pair of concepts to the initial matches.

Chapter 5
Computation of Alignments
Using Z3
The rules for initial matches in Section 4.4 are implemented in the satisfia-
bility modulo theory (SMT) solver Z3 [11]. A theory in this case is a system
(disjunction) of logical formulas. Satisfiability modulo theory then means:
“is there way in which the theory, i.e. all the rules in the system, can be
satisfied?”
The point of using Z3 to check for alignments is that it can answer ques-
tions. It is possible to set up a complicated system and query Z3 for answers.
This makes it possible to get all the possible attributes that specializes a cer-
tain attribute, i.e. answer the question “given this attribute, what are the
possible attributes that can specialize it?” Here, Z3 can give possible inputs
that satisfies the output.
Most of the literature on how to use Z3, e.g. Nikolaj Bjørner’s tutorial
“Programming Z3” [23], uses the Python API. Hence, there is surprisingly
few resources on how to use Z3 for other frameworks/programming languages
like .NET.1
In this project, the rules of initial matches have been implemented in two
slightly different ways with equivalent output: using quantifiers in Z3 and
defining the relationship on a case-by-case basis. But first, the notion of a
simple ontologies along with all its entities must be represented in Z3.

In Z3, types are called sorts. There are basic sorts like integer sort, boolean
sort, etc. The word “sort” has its root in logic, specifically many-sorted logic
[24] which is exactly what Z3 deals with: Z3 can handle theories incorporating
multiple different sorts.
The opposite of many-sorted logic would be an
axiomatic system with only one sort.
That Z3 uses the word sorts for types has a nice benefit: when something
is referred to as a sort, it is in a Z3 context.

enum sorts, and more. Using these complex sorts, it is possible to construct
custom sorts to represent simple ontology entities.

Another approach is to find distinct mappings as the Bipartite Matching

Definition 6.1 (Bipartite Graph [26, §1.2]). A bipartite graph G = (V, E),
where V is the vertices and E the edges, is a graph where the set of vertices
52
==PAGE:59==
can be partitioned into two subsets V = X ∪Y that are disjoint, X ∩Y = ∅,
such that every edge e ∈E has one end in X and the other end in Y .
■
An example of a bipartite graph is shown in Example 6.3. The example
uses attribute set specialization as the motivation. It is first proven that that
is even possible.
Theorem 6.2. Let A1 and A2 be attribute sets. A1 ≤s A2 can be modelled
as a bipartite graph G = (V, E) where V = A1 ∪A2 and
E = {(a1, a2) | a1 ∈A1 ∧a2 ∈A2 ∧a1 ≤a2} .
Proof. Set specialization checks if two attribute sets A1 and A2 from two dif-
ferent ontologies are related. Because A1 and A2 are from different ontologies
A1 ∩A2 = ∅. This is due to the fact that the two ontologies have different
base IRIs, so an attribute in A1 can never share IRI with an attribute in A2.
Furthermore, each edge in E is constructed as a A1 × A2 pair. Because
A1 and A2 are disjoint, this means that all edges E goes from one partition
to the other. Hence, A1 ≤s A2 can be modelled as a bipartite graph.

Note that the edges in a bipartite graph are undirected, so an edge (x, y)
is equivalent to the edge (y, x). This is not ideal as attribute specialization
(≤) is directional: in Theorem 6.2 it would perfect sense if the edges were
simply A1 × A2 pairs and in that direction alone.
If all the edges were
directional in the direction from A1 to A2 then each edge e = (a1, a2) could
read “a1 specializes a2”. With undirected graph, the other, equivalent edge
e = (a2, a1) can be interpreted as “a2 is specialized by a1”.
Fortunately, the lack of edge directions for bipartite graphs is not a prob-
lem as the algorithm used to solve the problem later enforces directions in
the edges. The problem being solved is of course the Bipartite Matching
Problem, see Definition 6.4.
Definition 6.4 (Bipartite Matching Problem [26, §7.5]). Given a bipartite
graph G = (V, E), find the largest set of matches M ⊆E where each node
v ∈V appears in at most one edge in M.
■
The fact that “each node v ∈V appears in at most one edge in M” is
important: that is the distinct mappings.
An interesting property of the Bipartite Matching problem in Defini-
tion 6.4 is that there are possibly multiple, equally likely solutions, see Fig-
ure 6.3.
Theorem 6.5. A solution to the Bipartite Matching Problem gives the max-
imum number of possible distinct mappings u(·) for set specialization (≤s).
Proof. Applying Theorem 6.2, A1 ≤s A2 can be modelled as a bipartite graph
G = (V, E) where V = A1 ∪A2. The Bipartite Matching Problem can be
applied to G to find the largest set of matches M ⊆E. Each edge m ∈M is
on the form m = (a1, a2) = (a2, a1) where a1 ∈A1 and a2 ∈A2. Because each
vertex v ∈V appears at most in one edge in M, each m contains a unique
mapping from A1 to A2 where m = (a1, a2) = (a2, a1) can be interpreted as
u(a1) = a2.

6.3
Dijkstra’s Algorithm: Prioritized Search
In Section 6.2 it is argued that Edmonds-Karp is insufficient for the problem
at hand.
This is because of Edmonds-Karp’s BFS that fails to prioritize
certain nodes. To ensure all possible augmenting paths with prioritized nodes
are checked, Dijkstra’s algorithm [26], [27] is used to find augmenting paths.
The idea behind Dijkstra’s algorithm is to find the shortest length between
two nodes given each edge has a length. To use the algorithm, the edges must
then be assigned lengths (or weights). To this end, the notion of prioritized
edges is introduced.
55
==PAGE:62==
Given a graph G = (V, E) and a set of prioritized nodes V + ⊆V , the
prioritized edges E+ ⊆E are
• forward edges that contain an endpoint in V +,
• backward edges that contain a starting point in V +, and
• all forward edges with endpoint t.
See example of prioritized edges in Figure 6.4.
Figure 6.4: Residual graph showing prioritized edges – green, thicker lines –
in prioritized search. Prioritized nodes marked with asterisk (*).
To model the residual graph with weights,
• a prioritized edge e+ ∈E+ has weight we+ = 0 and
• a non-prioritized edge e−∈E−has weight we−= 1.
Because Dijkstra’s algorithm wants to minimize the weight between the two
nodes, and it is a greedy algorithm, all the discovered prioritized edges will
be searched before the rest.
Using Dijkstra’s algorithm to find augmenting paths in a flow network
F = (V, E) is then a matter of finding a path from the source node s ∈V to
the sink node t ∈V such that the weight is minimized.
The use of Dijkstra’s algorithm fixes the problem with BFS. In Figure 6.5
it is shown how Dijkstra’s algorithm finds the longer, correct augmenting path
that BFS fails to find in Figure 6.3c. The final assignment in Figure 6.5d
matches the desired result in Figure 6.3d.
Because Dijkstra’s algorithm is not simple BFS, finding maximum flow
with the algorithm is not an Edmonds-Karp algorithm.

algorithm.

6.4
Dial’s Algorithm and Analyzing Runtime
For a (flow) graph F = (V, E), Dijkstra’s algorithm has the running time of
O(|E| log |V |) using a priority queue [26]. This is of course worse than BFS
which runs in O(|E| + |V |) using a queue [26].
Luckily, the running time of Dijsktra’s algorithm can be improved by use
of an alternative implementation: Dial’s algorithm [28].
Dial’s algorithm
works well when the range of weight values is small. For this use case, the
range of weight values is small – {0, 1}.
Dial’s algorithm works by keeping a “bucket queue” [29]. Here, a bucket
queue is a collection of multiple queues of vertices, one queue for each weight
value. When an edge is explored add the edge’s target node to the queue
corresponding to the edge’s weight [30]. When picking the next vertex to
57
==PAGE:64==
expand, dequeue from the queue with the lowest index that is non-empty.
Example 6.6. For the problem of distinct mappings, there are two weight
values 0 and 1. Hence there are two queues, q0 and q1, in the bucket queue.
In Figure 6.5a, the current vertex is a2. It has two unvisited neighbors, b2
and b3. Because the edge a2 →b2 is a prioritized edge, and the weight of
such edges is 0, b2 is enqueued in q0. The edge a2 →b3 is not a prioritized
edge, so the weight is 1 and b3 is enqueued in q1.
Now q0 = [b2] and q1 = [b3]. q0 is a non-empty queue with the lowest
corresponding weight, so b2 is expanded next.
Because Dial’s algorithm uses queues with constant-time en-/dequeue, the
algorithm with n queues in the bucket queue has the running time O(|E| +
n|V |) [30], [31]. In this project, n = 2, meaning
O(|E| + 2|V |) = O(|E| + |V |).
(6.1)
Thus, finding an augmenting path while ensuring optimal inclusion of prior-
itized nodes can be done in linear time – relative to the graph’s number of
vertices and edges.
The graph F = (V, E) is constructed from two sets of attributes, A and
B be two sets of attributes, where the edges are A ≤s B. The amount of
nodes |V | in the network is simply the sum of the attributes in A and B
along with the source node s and sink node t, i.e.
|V | = |A| + |B| + 2.
(6.2)
The amount of edges |E| in the flow graph must be the sum of:
• the edges from the source s to each attribute in A,
• the edges from A to B, and
• the edges from B to the sink t.
The amount of edges from s and to t is |A| and |B| respectively as it simply
connects to all the nodes in each set. For the edges |A| and |B|, the worst
case is that all elements in A can specialize all elements in B. The amount
of edges |E| is then given by
|E| ≤|A| + |A| · |B| + |B|.
(6.3)
The runtime complexity of Ford-Fulkerson is
O(|f ∗| · |E|)
(6.4)
58
==PAGE:65==
where |f ∗| is the maximum flow [26]. This is assuming a search algorithm
like DFS, which itself has the runtime complexity O(|V | + |E|). Because
Dial’s algorithm has the same runtime, see Equation (6.1), the runtime of
Ford-Fulkerson is unchanged.
For a flow graph based on two attribute sets A and B, the maximum flow
is |f ∗| = |A|: the only forward edges from the source node are to nodes in A
and all those edges have capacity 1. With |E| given by Equation (6.3), the
runtime complexity is
O(|A| · (|A| + |A| · |B| + |B|)) ≈O(|A|2 + |A|2 · |B| + |A| · |B|)
≈O(|A|2 · |B|).
(6.5)
After the max flow has been found, the distinct matches has to be ex-
tracted. This means finding the forward edges between A and B vertices
that have flow 1. This can be done by traversing all the forward edges in the
graph, hence O(|A| · |B|).

But an ontology with a loop cannot be tested in the same way. If an OWL
ontology has a loop, one of two things happens:
• The root concept owl:Thing has no subconcepts as subconcepts are
primarily found by finding classes that are not subclass of any other

crashes. The program notices one of the classes in the loop as a repeated

9.3
Alignment Confidence
In stead of having a binary (true, false) understanding of alignments and
specializations, it could in stead be a confidence interval.
Assigning confidence values, the flow evaluator will still work.
If the
interval is in discrete steps, like {0, 0.25, 0.50, 0.75, 1}, Dial’s algorithm can
be used with a few more queues (or “buckets”) and the runtime of the distinct
mappings finder will remain the same. If, however, all floating points values
between 0 and 1 are allowed, Dijkstra’s algorithm must be used to the (slight)
detriment of the runtime complexity.
Using confidence values in stead of help improve the harsh condition for
attribute alignment that the datatypes must be equal. A solution here is
to allow more granular results: an integer can be represented as a string, a
boolean can be an integer, etc. This can be done using confidence values.
9.4
Expand OWL Support, Match On Data,
and Match Multiple Ontologies
The current implementation of OWL to simple ontologies does not cover the
entirety of OWL. Neither is it supposed to: the point of simple ontologies
is that they are simple compared to OWL. However, there are still multiple
aspects of OWL that can enrich simple ontologies while keeping them simple:
• equivalent-to relations and
• value restrictions.
The equivalent-to relations are discussed in Chapter 3: some of the OAEI
Conference track ontologies are edited such that equivalent-to-relations are in
stead subclass relations. The simple edits could be made programmatically.
In OWL, it is possible to have value restrictions. These can take many
forms but an interesting one is this: another OAEI track, Material Sciences
and Engineering (MSE), has ontologies with units.

Chapter 10
Conclusion
In this thesis, the concept of simple ontologies from [1] has been expanded
with the notion of attributes. These attributes, with their datatype, optional
flag, and unit, have been the foundation on which initial matches are found
between two ontologies: certain rules for when attributes can specialize each
other, and when sets of attributes can specialize each other, has been used
to find initial matches between ontologies.
These rules have been implemented in two different ways: using the Z3
satisfiability modulo theory solver and reducing the problem to a maximum
flow problem. In Z3, three different approaches were taken, ending up with
the third being more than a 100 times faster than first. The moral being that
quantifiers in Z3 are slow, and it is much more efficient to simply enumerate
the rules programmatically.
Despite a fast Z3 evaluation for initial matches – relative to other Z3
attempts in this thesis – the evaluator utilizing the maximum flow prob-
lem reduction was 10 times faster than the best Z3 solution – 1000 times
faster than the worst. For large ontologies, with up to a hundred concepts,
each with possibly hundreds of attributes, the flow evaluator can find initial
matches between two ontologies in a few seconds.
In an attempt to apply the initial-matches-finder to some real world on-
tologies, it was a goal to use ontologies from the Ontology Alignment Eval-
uation Initiative (OAEI). To achieve this, another program was developed
in another language, this time Java. This program reads an ontology in the
Web Ontology Language (OWL) format and outputs a JSON representation
of a simple ontology. Here the attributes in the simple ontology are based
on properties and restrictions from the OWL ontology. While the entirety
of the description-logic-based OWL is not supported, more than half of the
ontologies in the OAEI Conference track are supported to varying degrees.
Applying the initial-matches-finder – using the flow evaluator – to the
82
==PAGE:89==
simple ontology representation of some of the OAEI Conference track on-
tologies yielded unsatisfactory results. None of the relevant matches were
found. The problem is that the attribute specialization rules are too strict.
As this thesis has dealt with efficiently finding initial matches, the possi-
bilities for expanding on and improving this foundation furthermore are vast:
incorporating lexical analysis of labels, include ontology data in the matching
process, and .... But most importantly: incorporate a matcher with a search
space.
In short, the foundation is laid with an efficient solution for finding initial
matches between concepts between two ontologies based on the concepts’
attributes. This solution is not an efficient Z3 encoding but rather a use
of maximum flow algorithms. The conditions for finding initial matches are
unfortunately too strict.

Appendix B
Flow Network Theory
It has now been shown that the problem of unique matches in set special-
ization be reduced to the Bipartite Matching Problem which itself can be
reduced to a problem with a known solution: Maximum Flow Problem [26],
see Definition B.3. First, however, flow networks are defined.
Definition B.1 (Flow Network [26, §7.1]). A flow network is a graph G =
(V, E) where there is a flow from a source node s ∈V to a sink node t ∈V .
The graph is directed such that the flow is always in the direction from s to
t. Each edge e ∈E in the flow network has a capacity ce ≥0 which is a
maximum amount of flow that can parse through that edge.
■
Known Result B.2. A bipartite graph G = (V, E), where V can be parti-
tioned into V = X ∪Y , can be transformed to a flow network F = (V ′, E′)
in the following steps [26, §7.5].
1. Construct a source node s and connect s to each x ∈X with a directed
edge such that (s, x) ∈E′.
2. Connect each pair of x ∈X and y ∈Y , that has an undirected edge
e = (x, y) = (y, x) in E, with the directed edge (x, y) ∈E′.
3. Construct a sink node t and connect each y ∈Y to t with a directed
edge (y, t) ∈E′.
4. Set the capacity of all edges e′ ∈E′ to ce′ = 1.
Definition B.3 (Maximum Flow Problem). Given a flow graph G = (E, V ),
find the maximum flow, from source node s ∈V to sink node t ∈V , while
never exceeding the capacity ce for each edge e ∈E.
■

Known Result B.4. Solving the Maximum Flow Problem for a bipartite
graph converted to a flow network is equivalent to solving the Bipartite Match-
ing Problem.
Definition B.5 (Residual Graph [26, §7.1]). A residual graph Fr = (V, Er)
is an auxiliary data structure for a flow graph F = (V, E) where E ⊂Er.
Let each e = (x, y) ∈E be referred to as a forward edge and f(e) ≤ce be
the flow through e. For each forward edge e = (x, y) ∈E, Fr also has the
corresponding backward edge e′ = (y, x) ∈Er where ce′ = f(e).
■
In Definition B.5, it must be the case that |E′| = 2|E| as.
Definition B.6 (Augmenting Path [26, §7.1]). An augmenting path is a path
in a residual graph Fr = (V, Er) from the source node s ∈V to the target
node t ∈V such that no node is visited more than once. An augmenting
path carries a flow fa > 0 from s to t via its path. Let e→= (x, y) be a
forward edge and e←= (x, y) a backward edge in Er. If the augmenting path
passes through e→, fa is added to f(e); if e←is on the path, fa is subtracted
from f(e).
■
Because an augmenting path p carries a flow fa(p), an augmenting path
can only use forward edges e→that has leftover flow, ce→−f(e→) > 0, or
backwards edges e←that has a capacity ce←> 0, i.e. when there is a flow
through the corresponding forward edge.
When an augmenting path p is found/applied, its flow fa(p) is added (or
subtracted) to the relevant edges in the flow graph.

more can be found/added. Let P denote the augmenting paths found in Gr
