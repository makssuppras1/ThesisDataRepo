Abstract 
Shared mobility systems provide a means of transport that is less polluting and that lowers 
the number of cars in cities, thus improving traffic flow and parking availability. The most 
widely spread of these systems is the Free-floating type. Car-sharing falls into this 
category. It is so due to the flexibility it provides, by allowing users to instantly rent 
vehicles in an area and then leave them in another one. This system is prone to 
unbalancing by design, as most users share the same travel patterns (travelling to work 
in the morning and returning in the afternoon). Currently there are two ways of balancing 
this system: changing the price of low demand vehicles (user-based) and manually moving 
them from low to high demand areas (vehicle-based).  
Reinforcement learning algorithms attempt to train agents that can learn from the 
environment and take actions based on what they perceive. This thesis proposes the use 
of reinforcement learning algorithms to decide more optimally on the vehicle-based 
method by considering supply and demand. More specifically, offline reinforcement 
learning is used to prevent interaction with the environment, as letting an agent learn by 
trial and error would become awfully expensive.

1.Introduction 
Shared mobility is the concept of a transportation system where different people share the 
same means of transport. This could either be at the same time, such as car-pooling, or 
over time, such as car-sharing. The main benefits from systems like these are mainly the 
decreased pollution that originates from both constructing more cars than needed and 
then having them ride at the same time. By reducing the number of cars, these structures 
free up space in the cities thus not only making it easier to find parking spaces but also 
improving traffic flow. 
The concept of car sharing has been around for more than 70 years, but only started 
developing recently as the technology to support viable systems became available. 
Although there are several approaches to this concept, nowadays the “Free floating” type 
is by far the most popular. This style of car sharing allows the users to start and end the 
trip in separate locations. However, it is frequent that trip end in locations with little 
demand, leaving it inactive, and thus defeating the point of the entire system. Moreover, 
most users have similar travel patterns, meaning that after rush hour most cars will be 
located away from residential areas, which is where new customers will appear as the rest 
are working. 
This is where the system needs to be rebalanced. A well-balanced system will benefit from 
a lower inactive time, which can be seen as less cars needed in the fleet for the same 
number of users or more users for the same number of cars.  
The goal of this project is to create a model that can suggest rebalancing moves (ie. where 
to moves cars) based on supply and demand in a Free-floating system. The system used 
in this thesis is the fleet for the car-sharing company ShareNow in Copenhagen. The model 
should learn a policy from historical data which shows when and where cars were rented, 
what the demand was at that time and when cars were moved to balance the system. 
With a well-trained policy, the whole ecosystem will benefit from both and increase in 
revenue (since this is the directly maximised metric) and a decrease in idle time (as cars 
are indirectly encouraged to be in rented state), minimising the number of needed cars to 
satisfy a certain demand. 
In the current system used for this thesis the rebalancing process is twofold: a dynamic 
pricing system1 and manual relocation2. 
1.1. Dynamic pricing 
This system, which is already in place, lowers the cost of renting a car that is supposed to 
have low demand and increases that which has higher demand. There are precedents to 
this problem, such as Hierarchical Reinforcement Pricing Algorithm [1].  
By doing this it is expected that the customers will move the cars to more desirable 
positions, thus stabilising the shared mobility system.  
Currently this dynamic pricing system is outsourced by the car sharing company, and there 
is no clear information on how it works. However, its behaviour is implicitly embedded in 
the historical data, because of the many characteristics known to each rental and the 
obtained revenue.

2.Theory and literature 
Reinforcement learning has been an area of research for many decades already, with the 
first discussions of it in the late 1980s, when the fields of dynamic programming, learning 
by trial and error and temporal-difference methods merged together [2].  
Nowadays, the idea behind it is that of an agent that learns the consequences of actions 
by interacting with the environment. The agent iteratively collects information about the 
environment by exploring it, to later take actions the updated policy and exploiting it. The 
consequence is often quantified as a numerical reward. 
This is not always possible, as in some use cases it can prove too expensive or dangerous 
to let an untrained algorithm perform actions [3]. This is where offline reinforcement 
learning takes the stage, as it would in theory allow for a policy to be learned without 
needing to interact with the environment by using pre-recorded data from another agent. 
In a real-life scenario, this data would be the actions taken by a human being. Figure 1 
shows the basic differences between these approaches. Here 𝜋 represents a policy, the 
globe is the environment, 𝑠 the state of the environment, 𝑎 the action taken by the policy, 
𝑟 the reward obtained by the policy for performing action 𝑎 in state 𝑠, and 𝑠′ the next state. 
𝐷 represents the dataset containing the tuples (𝑠, 𝑎, 𝑠′, 𝑟). 
In the on-policy type (a), the policy performs an action and the updates itself based on 
the collected data. The on-policy and off-policy (b) reinforcement learning differ in the 
sense that the off-policy algorithm saves the data in a buffer, allowing for it to be used 
again in the future. This means that the data from an old policy can be reused. Taking this 
a step forward, the policy originally interacting with the environment in (c) is separate 
from the one learning. 𝜋𝛽 is possibly an unknown policy, for which data has been collected 
into the buffer. Then, using all the collected data a new policy 𝜋 is trained to then be 
deployed. The first time this new policy is in contact with the environment is in deployment.

However, as this last offline learnt policy cannot explore the environment on its own, it 
relies on learning from the “mistakes” of the original policy. That is why including random 
actions in the dataset can drastically improve both learning speed and performance of the 
offline algorithm, sometimes even overtaking the online reinforcement learning equivalent 
[4]. 
2.1. Online reinforcement learning 
As explained previously, this algorithm learns by directly interacting with the environment. 
Initially, when the policy is new and untrained, the algorithm takes random actions untied 
to the deterministic policy, thus allowing for the exploration of the environment. Gradually, 
as the training progresses, the control is given to the policy to exploit what it has learnt.

This is the epsilon-greedy algorithm, where the probability of performing a random action 
during training starts at 1 or very close to it and decreases as training progresses until it 
is almost 0, or 0. Without this, the policy would not deviate considerably from the original 
path it was in and not learn other alternatives.  
Historically, reinforcement learning algorithms have suffered from several challenges. To 
begin with, the obtained rewards can be delayed in time and noisy which makes it hard to 
discern the quality of an action. Aside from this, there are two common assumptions in 
ML4 which do not hold true: the data samples are independent, and the underlying 
distribution is static. It is evident that the first assumption is not valid in reinforcement 
learning, as actions have direct consequences in the subsequent samples. The second 
assumption falls apart when the algorithm learns new behaviours [5]. 
The policy gauges how good the action taken is based on the reward function. Since the 
idea is that the algorithm takes the action the yields the highest reward in the long run, 
the reward function takes into account the discounted future rewards. Using an off-policy 
approach and allowing for the samples to be “replayed” and then feeding them in random 
order to the algorithm helps relieve problems related to the other two assumptions. 
In Q-learning, the algorithm calculates this reward for all possible actions, to then take 
the action with the highest score. This translates into the Bellman equation:

2.2. Offline reinforcement learning 
As opposed to online reinforcement learning, the offline approach cannot interact with the 
environment. This poses the problem that the policy is not able to verify that new, out-of-
distribution, actions are good or viable, as exploration is not possible. Including a wide 
variety of actions, especially bad ones, can considerably improve the performance [4]. 
However, this is rarely possible, as generally the origin of the data is direct interactions 
with the physical world. Because of this, the process of obtaining such actions would incur 
in the aforementioned risks and costs. 
In principle, the implementation of such an offline reinforcement learning algorithm should 
be similar to the off-policy online reinforcement method. However, just removing the 
interaction with the environment and learning from the recorded dataset does not yield 
satisfactory results, as there can be a distributional shift between the dataset and the 
learnt policy which leads to an overestimation of Q-values. This occurs because, during 
training, the target values are taken from the learnt policy, but the actions are from the 
original dataset. This can cause that actions not present in the dataset are expected to 
perform much better than they do, and the algorithm has no way of testing their actual 
performance. As the premise of reinforcement learning is to maximise rewards, the policy 
becomes biased towards these actions [8].

2.3. Reinforcement learning precedents on shared mobility 
Precursors on shared mobility have approached the problem of rebalancing the fleet 
through two separate ways: dynamically changing the price so that the customers move 
the units or moving the vehicles manually. 
The first one, also called the user-based approach, is the more common process. In 
contrast to the second one, the vehicle-based approach, it does not require jockeys to 
actively rebalance the fleet, resulting in lower costs. Furthermore, a good predictor of the 
demand is essential for the vehicle-based approach, as the inflexibility of the process can 
make it hard to adapt in real time [1]. 
A reinforcement learning example of the user-based approach is the Hierarchical 
Reinforcement Pricing Algorithm [1]. This method uses the geographical distribution of 
supply and demand to decide the price of using the units. Most interestingly, the metric 
that is optimised is the long-term service level, which is represented as the total number 
of satisfied user requests. 
This is done by, when looking at a particular user that has no available units in the area it 
is in, lowering the price of vehicles in adjacent zones to make it more attractive to the 
user to walk to them. It is assumed that the user values the cost of walking to the vehicle

long the wait might be, and proposes three ways to approach the optimisation of the

The first one resorts to mathematically finding the optimal solution to this problem which 
results in a Mixed Integer Nonlinear Programming problem. Just like in this thesis, they 
assign a cost to the rebalancing moves that is proportional to the distance between the 
origin and destination and that the travel time is deterministic. As opposed to this thesis, 
they constrain all of the trips and rebalancing moves to be completed in the same time 
period when they originated. 
Since the first method is not viable for large systems, as these problems can become very 
computationally expensive, the second method reduces the size of the analysis to a local 
scale. It does so by allowing each area to “pull” or “push” vehicles from/to adjacent areas. 
The decision of whether to do it and where to is done based on the relative demand of the 
current and adjacent areas. 
The last approach goes back to looking at the complete system and deciding on the 
rebalancing moves with a DQN. This DQN is only allowed to move cars to adjacent areas, 
like the second method, but the reward is a measure of idle time of the vehicles. 
Most interestingly, when looking at the results, the second method outperforms the first 
and third ones while also having an almost equal computational time compared to the 
DQN. This is surprising because the method does not have a holistic view of the system 
by design. 
However, the few assumptions made in this paper are not valid for this use case: 
• 
The request frequency is known and imbedded in the historical data 
• 
As it will be shown in the next section, users dropping requests is quite common 
• 
In the current system it is not possible for two users to share the same trip 
• 
Although the area of analysis can be discretised in a grid, this would lead to an 
inefficient system. Not to mention that the vehicles have designated parking areas 
and cannot be parked in any location 
• 
Last but not least, the quantity of rebalancing moves is constrained 
Finally, all of these approaches work on their own with a system that is prone to 
unbalancing. This is not the case in this thesis, as the system is mostly balanced already 
by the user-based approach as explained in Section 1. Moreover, the systems developed 
here are supposed to work on top of the existing ones, learning their underlying rules, 
instead of together at the same time.

3.Data 
The raw data consist of two datasets: one for app openings and one for rentals for the 
ShareNow fleet. The app openings dataset has 9,868,814 openings and the rentals dataset 
1,594,902 rentals. 
3.1. App openings 
This dataset includes information on when and where (GPS coordinates) the mobile 
application was opened as well as information about the platform, such as having 
notifications enabled, default language and whether it is an Android or iOS-based device. 
The dataset ranges from the February 1st, 2020, until the May 3rd, 2021. 
The only dimensions deemed relevant were datetime and location. 
3.2. Rentals 
The rentals dataset is comprised of much more data compared to the previous one. For 
each rental there is information about the customer, rented vehicle, date and location at 
the start of the rental and at the end alongside other characteristics of the rental, and a 
whether it was a service drive or not.  
This dataset ranges from the January 30th, 2020, until the July 31st, 2021. 
3.2.1. Customer information 
This only includes customer number, state (active, dormant, etc.), age and profile type 
(private or business). This data was ignored, as it does not provide much context to the 
rental. 
3.2.2. Vehicle information 
Each vehicle has a unique identifier (in this case license plate number). Aside from this, 
the engine type is specified (electric or fuel), model. From this set of information, only the 
engine type was not used. 
3.2.3. Characteristics of the rental 
This is the biggest section of this dataset. Within it can be found date, time and location 
of the rented car at the beginning and at the end of the rental, the revenue it generated, 
whether or not the car was fuelled (or charged in case of electric vehicles), the fuel level 
at the end of the trip, operation states before and after the trip, the actual distance driven 
(not the distance between the origin and end GPS coordinates), whether the rental was 
prebooked or part of a package, and other miscellaneous classifications or values that 
could be derived from other fields. 
From this part of the data the net revenue was used alongside the date, time and GPS 
coordinates at the start and end of the rental.  
Other dimensions within this set that could give insight onto the trips are the state of the 
car and if the car had been reserved. They were not used because: 
• 
State of the car: lack of information on how it works or what it means 
• 
Reservation status: useless without previously knowing the booked reservations. 
This data was not available.

3.2.4. Service drive 
This single column indicates if the rental originated from a customer or the company 
providing the car sharing service. 
There can be several reasons that originate these service drives, such as rebalancing 
moves to another areas, need for repairs or general service, refuelling, etc. There is no 
direct way of telling the underlying reason for each drive. 
3.3. Usage 
The app openings dataset represents the demand, as it can be assumed that the vast 
majority of the times the app is opened, there is the intention of renting a car, either 
immediately or within a brief period. However, there are a few known exceptions:  
• 
To reserve a car. In this case, the actual demand could be at a much later time 
than the app opening. About 42% of the rentals are reserved. 
• 
To reserve a package (for example: 24-hour package). Like in the previous 
case, the demand might occur much later. On top of this, the characteristics of 
these rental might be substantially different from the rest. Only about 7% of 
the rentals are part of packages. 
• 
To just check general availability of cars, possibly for later use.  
• 
Other unknown factors. 
Since the app openings dataset does not include information on what the app was used 
upon opening, these exceptions are unknown until the rental happens. 
The rentals dataset directly represents satisfied demand. Furthermore, the total supply 
can be deduced from it by tracking the movement of cars at each point in time. Aside from 
this, the rebalancing moves can be derived by using the service drive column, albeit with 
an unknown amount of noise that originates from other non-rebalancing service drives.

3.5. Processing 
It was chosen to only filter the dataset based on the overlapping dates: from 1st February 
2020 to 3rd of May 2021 and discard all app openings that were too outside of the area of 
analysis. Filtering based on the exceptions mentioned in Section 3.3 means that we reduce 
the “effects”, and this would require to reduce the associated “causes” to them. However, 
the direct cause-effect relationship is unknown. Therefore, these exceptions have been 
ignored and no further filtering of the data was done. 
Many of the rentals contain GPS coordinates without any assigned parking areas. This 
could be due to the car being parked outside of a designated parking area or errors during 
the registration. Moreover, 68% of the rentals are assigned to a single area (Copenhagen 
Business Area), which is why there is such a big spike in Figure 11 in the first bar. 
Based on this, it is only reasonable to generate new virtual areas such that when the 
rentals are assigned to them, based purely on GPS coordinates, they will be more uniformly 
distributed. These new areas could be generated in diverse ways: randomly, using expert 
knowledge, grid-like system, using an unsupervised machine learning algorithm on the 
historical data, or selecting from the original areas to name a few. For this problem, the 
latter was the choice, as it required the least manual labour and generates the most 
uniform distribution by design compared to the other choices. 
3.5.1. Area generation 
The algorithm of choice was K-Means, as it will only place areas where it is needed and 
avoid when it is not, thus reducing the number of areas and simplifying the problem. This 
method also does not require expert knowledge and the manual labour is minimal, 
especially when several iterations might be needed to find the optimal number of areas.

new areas to improve performance during the generation. The other choice when using

4.Methodology 
Several models were developed, both with different approaches and parameters. The 
different approaches were a FFNN9 for behavioural cloning, a DQN for online reinforcement 
learning and a CQL for offline reinforcement learning. 
To simplify the decision making of the models, it was assumed that a perfect predictor for 
the demand was available. By doing this, the model can focus on only deciding where to 
move cars and not in forecasting the demand. This was done by providing the actual 
demand in the next period, instead of past demand. This would not be possible when using 
these models in deployment, as the future demand is unknown. A natural enhancement 
to this model would be to create and include this predictor.  
Moreover, the agents that will be performing the actions are each of the individual areas. 
The other natural choice would have been to allow each car to decide if it should be moved 
and where to, but this solution was not working satisfactorily due to the following reasons: 
firstly, the data was being split in much smaller chunks because the number of cars is 
much greater than the number of areas, thus reducing the potential learning of patterns; 
and secondly, because cars were unaware of other cars’ actions. This makes it so that if 
an area needs one car to be moved, several cars in that area might choose to move 
themselves, since they do not know what their neighbours will do. 
Hence, by making the areas the agents, the total amount of agents is reduced, improving 
performance, both computational and operational, as it was found during development. 
In order to establish a baseline and allow for benchmarking of the models, a simulator was 
built. This was needed because when a new policy needs to be tested, an “alternative” 
history occurs. 
4.1. Simulator 
The simulator is a heuristic algorithm that, given some historical data and an analysis 
period, generates rentals, executes them, and calculates the obtained revenue. It requires 
historical data so that it can preserve the characteristics of supply and demand throughout 
the test period. 
Algorithm 1 shows how a single step in the simulator operates. The algorithm tries to 
satisfy a fraction of all app openings, because the historical data shows that for one rental 
there are approximately 8 app openings. The app openings that are selected are randomly 
sampled on each step. This selection are the effective rental requests. 
Then, for each request, the closest area is selected and, if the distance does not surpass 
a certain threshold, a trip is generated. This trip originates from the closes area to the 
request and has an unknown destination. To determine the destination and keep the same 
stochasticity, the destination is obtained by sampling from the subset of historical data 
where the rentals originate in the same origin, on the same day of the week and at the 
same hour of the day. This means that if, historically there has been twice as many trips 
from area A to B than from A to C on Mondays at 8 am., then the simulator will output 
twice as many trips to B as opposed to C, when the trip originates from A on a Monday at 
8am. 
If there are no precedents, ie. no trips originating from A at the specified datetime, the 
probability distribution is uniform, which translates into picking the destination randomly. 
There is one last check before executing the rental: whether there is enough space to park 
the car in the destination.

==PAGE:21==

Once the rental is cleared to take place, the car is instantly transferred from the origin to 
the destination area, but it is only made available after a certain period. This is to simulate 
the travel time and is a function of the distance between the two points. The revenue is 
also calculated as a function of the distance. In an attempt to simplify the algorithm, these 
functions were chosen to be linear. 
As a side note, even though more complex functions could be used, they would severely 
punish the performance, which as it will be shown later, is a bottleneck during training of 
the models. 
The simulator allows for rebalancing moves to be made. When these happen, the car takes 
an ordinary trip, but the obtained revenue is a negative multiple of what would be for a 
regular rental. This is to simulate the associated costs of moving the car, such as fuel and 
salary of the jockeys. 
To summarise, the simulator runs through a specified period of time all the app openings, 
randomly selects a fraction of them, fetches statistical data to determine where the trip is 
headed and executes the trip.

4.3. Behavioural cloning 
The purpose of this models is to directly learn the current policy for moving the cars by 
looking the previous observations of the system and the actions that were taken for each 
of them. Since it is only the behaviour that is being looked at, the obtained reward is 
meaningless when training this model. This is often referred to as imitation learning. 
The idea behind imitation learning is to train a model that learns the MDP10 behind the 
system, so that the model will output the optimal action to a given state based on training 
data, usually recorded historical data from human behaviour. However, in most 
applications this approach fails because there is an assumption from supervised learning 
that is broken: the state-action pairs are independent and identically distributed. This is 
false in a MDP, where the current state is a consequence of the previous state and action. 
This can cause that due to small errors in, the learned policy starts deviating from the 
original distribution and faces state which it has not observed before, giving into the 
possibility of failure [12].

For this task NNs were chosen, as they can normally generalise the policy better and 
perform more satisfactory in out-of-distribution situations, compared to other ML 
algorithms. 
Although this could be achieved with a single network, the results were considerably better 
when the decision was split in two steps: choose whether to move a car and, if the answer 
is yes, where to. This is also quicker to train because the action space for the first network 
is much smaller, and the amount of data for the second network is much smaller (as shown 
in Section 3.4.1).  
The first NN learns from sequential data and is therefore eligible for LSTM11 networks. In 
practice the LSTM did not perform better than an FFNN, and the FFNN was chosen based

every time step, which is the FFNN architecture was elected.

4.6. Metrics 
During training (and testing) of the imitation learning FFNN networks, some metrics were 
defined to evaluate the performance of each of them individually.  
The first network serves as simple binary classifier, for which there are many proven 
metrics developed. The chosen metric was the F1-score, given its wide use in ML and easy 
understandability. 
 
For the second network an ad-hoc metric was developed. In this case the network works 
as a multiclass classifier, and for this case there are also many metrics available.  
However, there could be cases in which these metrics would unfairly punish the 
performance. One of such would be when, hypothetically, there are 2 areas very close to 
each other and where there would be no difference in picking one or the other. This would 
be shown historically when, in very similar conditions, both areas are picked randomly. 
The network would learn this behaviour, but since it is random it would get a low score. 
This could also happen even if historically the same area was always chosen, as there is 
not much difference between the two neighbouring areas. 
As a consequence of this, the developed metric, called Distance to area and shown in 
Equation 17, measures how “sure” the network was about the correct area. In this metric, 
a score of 1 means that the network is far from choosing the correct area, while a score 
of 0 means that it picks the true area with absolute certainty.

5.2. Testing 
The testing was made by running all the policies through the simulator, alongside with the 
historical moves. Since the metrics are not standard among the different models, the test 
was run in both the train and test periods. 
Each of the individual cases was run at least three times, since the simulator contains 
stochastic elements, which is especially important when dealing with reinforcement 
learning as a small difference in the beginning can snowball into one much larger. An 
analysis of the variability of the simulator described in the Appendix.  
Aside from running all the policies, a baseline policy that moves cars to a constant area 
every fixed number of steps was run. Eight of these policies were run, where the number 
of steps between moves ranges from 1 to 128. It is expected that the scores range from 
very bad to the equivalent of not moving any cars. These were dubbed Constant moves. 
Moreover, all the policies were run for the same cost factors as the DQNs were trained on, 
ie. 0, 0.001, 0.01, 0.1, 1 and 10. It is expected that each model outperforms the others 
when it is tested in with the same cost factor as it was trained. Another expectation is that 
the higher the training cost factor, the fewer number of moves the policy chooses to 
perform. 
Given the observations named in Section 3.4.1, it can be seen in Figure 19 that the 
historical moves regularly obtain a score close to not performing any action at all and is 
thus close to the highest score of Constant moves. 
The training period scores also display that DQN policies trained with low cost factors seem 
to train better and learn meaningful ways to move the cars and there seems to be a 
breakpoint between cost factor 1 and 10. Most notably, the policy trained with cost factor 
0.1 seems to perform remarkably well in most cases, sometimes outperforming other 
policies in their own tests. Another important note is that the policies trained with higher 
cost factors are frequently close to the Constant moves, just as the historical actions, 
suggesting that they do not perform many moves. 
The same trends hold true in the test period, where the policies in the middle seem to 
perform best in most situations, suggesting that that a fine tuning of the cost factor can 
have a substantial impact on the final performance of the model. 
However, the CQL is for the most part unable to match the performance of the policy it is 
trying to imitate, either while training or testing.

This is a clear display that the decisions are influenced by the time aspects, but also by 
the availability of cars and the demand, which are the other inputs to the models.

6.Conclusions 
The original objective of this thesis was to create a model that can look at past states and 
actions of the system and suggest what the optimal rebalancing moves are. This can be a 
challenging task, as the current advancements in this field can still struggle to achieve 
successful results even in simple cases. 
From the results obtained with the imitation learning model, it can be concluded that the 
historical moves are unrelated to the information being input to the model, as the results 
do not change based on the version used and the model does not find patterns. This can 
be expected, as it is known that a subset of the historical moves originated from 
breakdowns, no fuel, etc. and thus are not rebalancing moves. 
The online reinforcement algorithm seems to work well for the most part. There are cases 
in which this method makes questionable decisions, as seen when picturing the moves 
carried out. This can either be a display of either not enough training, incorrect 
hyperparameters in the model, wrong choice of the input data or just not sufficient 
historical data. 
Aside from this, as the scores and number of moves show that the cost factor is an 
essential parameter in the quality of the policy. A very low cost factor makes an excessive 
number of moves and doesn’t know how to prioritise them, while a very high one learns 
to do nothing at all. 
On the other hand, the offline reinforcement algorithm struggled to achieve acceptable 
results in most cases. This is surprising especially when trained from historical data, as 
the presence of “random” moves (those not for rebalancing) should in theory improve its 
performance [4]. 
However, it would quickly pay off to develop an algorithm that can be trained from offline 
data, mainly because the prohibitive cost an online one would entail. Furthermore, the 
volume of data available is much larger than that the online operation could collect. 
Some changes to the presented work that might improve the performance are: 
• 
Include more accurate historical data, where a direct link between app openings 
and rentals can be found 
• 
Include input data regarding weather, concerts, sport events, etc. These events 
have an evident influence on the dynamics of the system and could add an 
important insight to the model 
• 
Clip rewards to [-1,1]. This has in some cases resulted in the algorithm interpreting 
the rewards better [6] 
• 
In CQL, a uniform distribution was used a prior when calculating the KL- 
divergence. For high-dimensional action spaces using a previous policy (like the 
target network) can result in better performance [8] 
• 
Instead of maximising the total reward, the idle time could be minimised. Although 
not exactly the same, minimising idle time will increase the revenue and improve 
on the environmental aspect of shared mobility 
• 
The cost factor should be thoroughly tuned, given what the results show 
• 
Finally, having a simulator that, given historical data, can accurately represent the 
real-world environment could be pivotal. An online algorithm could be trained from 
it, effectively bypassing all the challenges with offline reinforcement learning

Since the simulator is not deterministic, an analysis was done on how variable the results 
can be. As each of the tests was run at least three times, the standard deviation can be 
calculated from them. To illustrate the scale of the standard deviation it was divided by 
the mean of the runs, as the scores vary so much based on the cost factor. This is called 
the coefficient of variation. 
The next figure shows the values for each policy and test case. As it can be observed, the 
policies which are not from reinforcement learning have a very low coefficient of variation. 
The CQL and DQN cases do not always follow this trend. This is clear evidence that the 
variation observed in the episode rewards does not originate from the stochasticity of the 
simulator but from the “snowball effect” mentioned in Section 5.2. 
Moreover, the higher coefficients of variation are observed in the cases in which a policy 
was trained with a low cost but tested with a high cost. This is due to the fact that the 
policies learned to take actions, but the results are not what is expecting. The opposite 
case, ie. when the policies are trained with high cost but tested with low cost, does not 
have much variation, as the number of actions is so low that it approximates the static 
“no moves” case.
