Abstract
Two-dimensional materials are associated with novel exotic properties, and their low di-
mensionality offers unprecedented access to external manipulation and property engineer-
ing, as the entirety of the material is directly exposed.
In particular, the 2D carbon
allotrope, graphene, has received intense attention due to its unique properties, which in-
clude an extraordinary high intrinsic mobility associated with ballistic motion of the charge
carriers. The remarkable properties of graphene may be further engineered by lithographic
structuring. An interesting phenomenon occurs when graphene is subject to both a perpen-
dicular B-field and a lithographically defined periodic electric super-potential, a so-called
superlattice. It has been shown that the interplay of the two external perturbations influ-
ences the trajectories of the charge carriers in an interesting way, leading to pinned orbits
that are commensurate with the period of the imposed superlattice. As a result, magne-
toresistance measurements show modified features, known as commensurability or Weiss
oscillations, with respect to the traditional Landau fan, where only a B-field is imposed.
In this work, greyscale nanopatterning is performed using thermal scanning probe lithog-
raphy in combination with reactive ion etching, to create smooth topographic surface
profiles in hBN/graphene/hBN van der Waals heterostructures. The target patterns con-
stitute simple unidirectional sinusoidal profiles with a single period of 300 nm, and ∼30
nm peak-to-peak vertical modulation. AFM characterization of the pattern topography
shows good agreement with the target profile, albeit the modulation is lower than expected
(∼15 nm). The stack is converted into three Hall bar devices using standard nanolithogra-
phy techniques, with local metal top gates deposited over the sinusoidal patterns, forming
topographically modulated top gates, along and perpendicular to the modulation direc-
tion. Electrostatics simulations are performed to gain insight into the expected potential
modulation at the graphene layer, both for the actual device geometry and alternative
geometries. Electrical characterization is in progress to probe the possibility of Weiss os-
cillations in the devices, which will depend on whether the induced potential modulation
is suﬀiciently strong and the length of the ballistic mean free path. If successful, this work
constitutes a first proof of concept towards more complex freeform general superlattices.

1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Traditional silicon (Si) based integrated circuits (ICs) face fundamental scaling issues due
to inherent limitations associated with Si, when the sub-10 nm regime is approached [1].
Performance advancements have for decades been obtained through dimensional downscal-
ing, increasing the transistor density in ICs, leading to the empirical obervation known
as Moore’s law, where the number of transistors in ICs are doubled around every second
year [2]. When the lateral transistor dimensions are continuously decreased, scaling rules
require the thickness of the conductive channel to decrease as well, to suppress unwanted
short-channel effects (SCEs)1, that lead to lowered electrostatic control of the current
flow and reduced carrier mobility [1]. However, when the channel thickness in Si-based
devices is decreased below 4 nm, the carrier mobility suffers a fatal drop, as illustrated
in Fig. 1.1. Therefore, the prospects of continuing the progression predicted by Moore’s
law have become unsustainable within the traditional semiconductor industry, motivating
alternative materials research.
Figure 1.1: Overview of relation between mobility and channel thickness for Si, Ge and
selected 2D materals. Reproduced from [1].
Two-dimensional materials are promising candidates for next-generation atomically thin
electronic devices, both because their unique nature is perfectly compatible with the con-
tinuous dimensional downscaling, and because the continuously growing family of 2D
materials embodies a wide range of exotic materials properties. In contrast to Si-based
devices, where the mobility degrades severely as a function of decreasing channel thickness,
the carrier mobility of 2D materials is sustained even at a sub-nm channel thickness, see

channel thickness

Figure. 1.1. In particular, graphene has received intense attention due to its unique elec-
tronic properties counting an intrinsic high carrier mobility and a wide range of accessible
carrier densities in both the electron and hole regime.
Successful 2D electronics rely on precise control and manipulation of the current flows.
The low dimensionality of 2D materials allows for improved external control of the ma-
terials properties compared to 3D bulk materials, as the entirety of the 2D material is
directly exposed [3]. Moreover, 2D materials may respond differently to external manipu-
lation, hinting at a large unexplored potential of novel physical behaviour and phenomena,
and motivating property engineering of 2D materials [4]. Hence, the remarkable intrinsic
properties of graphene may be further manipulated and explored, e.g. by engineering the
environment around graphene through lithographic structuring. A particularly interesting
example is achieved when subjecting the graphene carriers to a combination of a litho-
graphically defined electrostatic periodic potential and a magnetic field, leading to electron
orbits that are commensurate with the periodicity of the imposed super-potential [5]. The
fascinating interplay leads to observable magnetotransport features, known as commensu-
rability oscillations or Weiss oscillations, and has been observed for both conventional 2D
electron gasses and graphene. The peculiar phenomenon may be used to study and gain
insight into the effect of various periodic ”superlattices” on graphene charge carriers, and
future prospects for enhanced next generation 2D graphene based devices.
In literature, a ”superlattice” is used as a term to describe an external spatial periodic
electrostatic potential imposed onto the charge carriers of a system, in addition to the
intrinsic crystal structure of the material. Consequently, an artificial lattice is effectively
felt by the charge carriers, given by the contributions from both the original crystal lattice
and the imposed superlattice [6, 7]. Graphene superlattices have been realized by three
main techniques: utilizing heterostructure Moiré superlattices [8], direct patterning of
graphene [3], or indirect patterning, e.g. by patterned gate arrays [9] or patterning the
dielectric in the vicinity of graphene [6]. The former method exploits the isostructural
lattices of graphene and hBN, leading to a laterally modulated and periodic superlattice,
known as a Moiré lattice, in hBN/graphene heterostructures [8]. The wavelength of the
Moiré lattice is tunable by varying the in-plane rotation angle between the two lattices,
and has the advantage that no invasive lithographic techniques are neeeded. However, the
Moiré lattice is still fundamentally limited by the hBN and graphene crystal structures.
The second technique is realisable e.g. by lithographically etching arrays of holes into
graphene. While improved fabrication techniques show high-quality patterns, that don’t
comprise on the carrier mobility, e.g. due to rough edges from the etch process [3], the
method is limited by the binary nature of the patterning approach. This induces a strong
modulation of the graphene carrier density, forfeiting the possibility of studying graphene
carriers in smooth and soft potential landscapes. Binary patterned gate arrays or binary
dielectric patterning may be used to induce softer potential modulations [9, 6], however,
the possibility of creating smooth arbitrary, deterministic, and complex modulations, e.g.
with a profile resembling mathematical functions, is yet untested. Therefore, this thesis
seeks to investigate the question: Can the limitations of binary patterning be
overcome to achieve mathematically precise potential landscapes in graphene
heterostructures?
1.2
Thesis scope
In this work, the above question is explored using thermal-scanning probe lithography
in combination with reactive ion etching to perform smooth greyscale dielectric pattern-
ing.
Thermal scanning probe lithography (tSPL) is a high-resolution nanolithography
2

==PAGE:11==
technique, that enables greyscale depth control, allowing the creation of smooth pattern
profiles with nanoscale precision [10, 11]. This work sets out to create simple sinusoidal
topographic profiles in the top layer of a stack comprising monolayer graphene encapsu-
lated by hBN on top of a Si/SiO2 chip. It has been shown in literature that hBN can
be structured precisely with tSPL, however, it has not yet been experimentally proven
that this will have an effect in the electronic device properties [11]. By evaporating a thin
gold (Au) layer on the top patterned hBN surface, the expectation is that a local top
gate can be created, conforming to the underlying pattern topography. By applying a top
gate potential, it is hypothesized that a modulated periodic super-potential is imposed on
the graphene plane, with a profile reflecting the pattern topography, and a modulation
strength accurately controlled by tuning the applied top gate potential. A target period
of Λ = 300 nm is chosen for the sinusoidal superlattice, which is expected to be feasible
to achieve with thermal scanning probe lithography [12]. The chosen period is similar to
the superlattice period in the original works on Weiss oscillations for 2DEGs [5, 13], and
is on the same order of magnitude as studies on Weiss oscillations in graphene [9].
Hence, this work comprises the following four interconnected goals:
• Carry out electrostatics simulations using the finite element method solver COM-
SOL, to predict the effect of the structured topography on the underlying potential
landscape as the geometrical parameters are varied.
• Perform greyscale patterning of the upper hBN layer in an hBN/graphene/hBN
stack with the goal of creating deterministic topographic sine patterns with a target
wavelength of Λ = 300 nm. This includes determining the proper etch rate for the
process, so that the sine patterns are transferred successfully into the upper hBN
layer, without imposing directly on the graphene layer, and with low etch-induced
roughness.
• Incorporate the patterned hBN/graphene/hBN stack into contacted Hall bar devices
with local Au top gates residing on top of the sine patterns.
• Perform electrical characterization of the devices to probe the magnetoresistance
properties and the possibility of Weiss oscillations.
The focus of this work is creating simple unidirectional sinusoidal patterns with a single
period. If Weiss oscillations are successfully demonstrated in the fabricated devices, these
simple patterns will constitute a first proof of concept for achieving Weiss oscillations
by greyscale dielectric patterning, and furthermore, a first proof of concept for electronic
control using smooth topographically modulated top gates in general. Hence, the aim
of this work is to be the first stepping stone towards general freeform superlattices, i.e.
multiwaveform patterns, employing several periods and/or rotate several sinusoidals with
respect to each other in a Moiré-like fashion [11].

Thesis outline
The thesis comprises the following chapters:
Chapter 2 presents the theoretical foundation relevant to this work, briefly introducing
two-dimensional materials, followed by an overview of the graphene and hBN lattices.

2
Theoretical background
2.1
Two-dimensional materials
Two-dimensional (2D) materials denote the family of atomically thin materials, where the
atoms are arranged in layered crystalline sheets that are only a single or a few atoms
thick. The research field was ignited in 2004, when Geim and Novoselov succesfully iso-
lated a single sheet of carbon atoms, graphene, from graphite [14]. Quickly, the family
was extended to include single-atom thin graphene-like members e.g. hexagonal boron-
nitride (hBN), few-atoms thick transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDCs) e.g. MoS2 and
WTe2, and 2D oxides like bismuth strontium calcium copper oxide (BSCCO). The reduced
dimensionality leads to strong confinement in the out-of-plane–direction, resulting in dif-
ferent electronic, optical, and mechanical properties than their associated 3D compounds
[15]. Moreover, the 2D geometry enables unprecedented direct access to manipulating and
probing the entirety of atoms that the crystal is comprised of [3]. Another interesting fea-
ture is the ability to assemble different 2D layers into layered designer structures, so-called
van der Waals (vdW) heterostructures. This allows for ”building” customized novel ”3D-
like” structures, associated with new properties and phenomena, with single-atom-layer
precision, in a fashion resembling atomic scale lego-brick stacking [16].
This work focuses on graphene and hBN, and the following sections describe their distinct
properties and the advantages associated with combining the two materials into vdW
heterostructures.
2.2
Graphene and hBN
The real-space lattices of graphene and hBN are depicted in Fig. 2.1. As mentioned in the
previous section, graphene is an atomically thin carbon allotrope derived from graphite.
However, the properties of graphene differ notably from those of its mother compound,
as graphene is optically transparent and flexible1 in contrast to graphite that is non-
elastic and opague with metallic luster [19]. Moreover, graphene is the strongest material
currently known, with an unprecedented tensile strength2 of ∼130 GPa [17]3, and an
extraordinary high mobility, which is described in more detail in the following section 2.3.
Graphene consists of a single layer of carbon atoms arranged in a honeycomb lattice,
comprised of two intertwined triangular sublattices A and B. As carbon is the sixth element
in the period table, each carbon atom has four valence electrons filling 2s, 2px, 2py, and
2pz orbitals. In graphene, the 2s orbital and the two in-plane p-orbitals, 2px and 2py,
hybridize to form three sp2 orbitals in the graphene plane oriented 120 degrees to each
other [21]. The sp2 orbitals from neighboring carbon atoms overlap, forming strong in-
plane covalent σ-bonds, as shown in Fig. 2.1b. The remaining unhybridized 2pz-orbital is
oriented out-of-plane and forms π-bonds to neighboring unhybridized 2pz-orbitals through
lateral tunneling between the protruding lobes, see Fig. 2.1b. These π-bonds hybridize to
form delocalized π-bands, which are responsible for the remarkable electronic properties
of graphene [22], discussed further in section 2.3.

The ballistic transport of the charge carriers implies a high intrinsic carrier mobility. In

the mobility is often limited by device imperfections. First, the graphene sheet itself may
contain defects in the form of cracks, ripples or folds, leading to short range scattering.
In addition, it may be affected by charged impurities residing close to the sheet, causing
long-range Coulomb scattering, or the charge carriers may interact with surface phonons
from the underlying substrate [22].

exhibits a mobility far inferior to that of pristine graphene, partly due to the SiO2 surface
roughness [28].

graphene, due to its atomically smooth surface, that is almost free of dangling bonds and
charged impurities, and its close lattice match with graphene [8]. Furthermore, hBN is
a wide-bandgap semiconductor, with a band gap of ∼5.9 eV, providing good electrical
insulation.
Moreover, by fully encapsulating graphene by hBN, the smooth dielectric
serves as a protective cover against contamination adsorbing on the graphene surface. For
graphene6 fully encapsulated by hBN, literature has shown a low temperature mobility of
∼3 million cm2/(V·s), corresponding to a mean free path exceeding 28 µm, and a mean
free path longer than 1 µm at 200 K [29].
2.4

Effect
When a 2D electronic system is subject to an external uniform perpendicular magnetic
field (B-field), the carrier density condenses into discrete energy levels. The phenomenon
is known as Landau level quantization, and the resulting energy levels are called Landau
levels (LLs). As a results, the charge carriers undergo in-plane cyclotron motion, moving
in orbits dictated by the allowed LLs [30].

The previous subsection 2.4.2 describes how a B-field applied perpendicular to a graphene

is the focus of this section.

3
Electrostatics simulations
In this work, COMSOL is used to perform electrostatics simulations to gain insight into
the expected strength of the potential modulation at the graphene layer, imposed by the
sinusoidally patterned top gate via electrostatic gating. Recalling section 2.5.1, literature
distinguishes between weak and strong potential modulations, e.g. using the amplitude
of the modulation as a measure of the modulation strength. The expected modulation
amplitude can be probed using electrostatics simulations, which is the focus of this section.
The simulations take as a starting point the actual device geometry, where the thickness
of the two hBN layers are revealed by AFM measurements (see Fig. A.5). The pattern
topography parameters are extracted by fitting a 2D sine function to AFM data of the
patterns in section 5.5.2. The electrostatics simulations are used to map out the expected
modulation strength in relevant parameter spaces, exploring also alternative geometric
values to those of the actual device.
3.1
COMSOL
COMSOL Multiphysics 5.6 is a finite element method (FEM) solver, that numerically
solves partial differential equations (PDEs) subject to a set of boundary conditions. While
space- or time-dependent PDEs provide the mathematical description of a wide range of
physical and engineering problems such as mechanical stress or strain, fluid flow, diffusion,
heat transfer, and, particularly relevant for this work, electrostatics, they are often very
diﬀicult or even impossible to solve analytically, especially for complex geometries. FEM
provides an alternative solution route by discretization of the originally continuous PDE
problem.
Here, the computational domain is subdivided into smaller solution regions,
a mesh of finite elements1. An approximate solution is calculated numerically for each
element, and by interpolating the values at the element corners, the so-called nodes, an
approximate solution for the entire system is obtained.
The COMSOL simulation platform allows for drawing or importing geometries, defining
materials properties, adding the relevant physics and boundary conditions, and based on
these inputs carries out FEM computations.

respectively.
The terminal simulates the local top gate of the device and is set to a
constant electric potential of 10 V, while the ground electrode is set to 0 V. The resulting
distribution of the electric potential throughout the device is computed in a stationary
study, and a line cut is made at the line representing the monolayer graphene, to extract
the expected potential profile at the graphene layer, see Fig. 3.1a. The profile reflects the
sine pattern in the upper hBN flake, phase shifted by π, i.e. the potential profile has its
maximum at the topographic minimum. This is because the vertical distance from the
top gate to the graphene layer is smallest here, hence, inducing the largest field. Twice
the profile amplitude (top of Fig. 3.1a) is used as a measure of the potential modulation
in the following parametric studies. Fig. 3.1b displays the resulting magnitude of the
electric displacement field, D. The field is largest around the center of the upper surface
of the top hBN flake, where the potential changes most rapidly, in good agreement with
Eqs. 3.2-3.6.
3.1.2
Mesh convergence
For the simulations, an automatically generated mesh is used, a so-called physics controlled
mesh. The mesh element size should be small enough, that the computational results are
unaffected, or the changes are negligible, when changing the element size. However, the
computation time generally increases for finer meshing. Hence, decreasing the element size,
beyond the point where mesh convergence is obtained, can prove time-wise ineffective.
To test for mesh convergence, a sweep of the mesh element size is performed. For an
illustrative comparison, Fig. 3.2 displays the geometry subdivided into a selection of mesh
types, that can automatically be produced by COMSOL. The modulation at MLG does
not appear very sensitive to the mesh resolution (see Fig. A.1 in the appendix). For the
present electrostatics simulations, the computation time does not increase significantly,
and so the ”extremely fine” mesh is chosen.

Experimental methods
4.1
Mechanical exfoliation
Figure 4.1: Illustration of the mechanical exfoliation process. a) A small piece of bulk
crystal is put on a piece of tape and is copied into arrays of flakes by repeatedly and
precisely folding the tape onto itself as shown. The tape piece is cut in half, resulting in
two stamp tape pieces. b) A stamp tape piece and a new tape piece are rubbed together
(1) and pulled apart quickly (2) to create a copy of the flake arrays on the new piece.
This process is repeated, both for the original stamp piece and the copy piece, creating
more additional copies, until all flakes appear approximately equally thin. c) To transfer
the flakes onto a chip, a soft Q-tip is used to gently rub the tape backside, before slowly
peeling off the tape maintaining a low angle. d) Top view sketch of a chip with exfoliated
flakes.
The foundation for a high quality device is laid with the initial building blocks, high quality
2D flakes. In this work, mechanical exfoliation is used to produce monolayer graphene and
multilayer hBN flakes. The method exploits the layered nature of the materials, as the
weak vdW forces holding together individual layers allow for mechanically peeling off 2D
flakes from bulk ”mother” crystals. This fabrication technique produces very high-quality
2D crystals, and hence, it is widely used in research applications where the crystalline
quality is essential.
On the downside, the method is not easy scalable and produces
Smooth greyscale dielectric nanopatterning of graphene
25
==PAGE:34==
smaller flakes than alternative techniques, such as CVD synthesis [41]. Moreover, in the
case where 2D crystals of non-standard chemical compositions are desired, such as alloyed
2D materials, a limitation is the availability and possibility of growing high-quality bulk
crystals with the specific chemical compositions.
Fig. 4.1 illustrates the general principle behind mechanical exfoliation. A more detailed
description is provided in section 5.1. In brief, a small piece of bulk crystal is placed on
a piece of tape and replicated into flake arrays by folding the tape onto itself (Fig. 4.1a).
Once the produced flake arrays obtain a proper size, the tape is cut in half, yielding two
stamp tape pieces. The flake arrays are copied onto new tape pieces, by rubbing together
a stamp piece and a pure tape piece, before pulling them apart quickly (Fig. 4.1b). This
copy process gradually thins down the flakes, and it is repeated until the exfoliated flakes
appear (equally) thin. The transfer of flakes from the tape pieces onto chips are achieved
by gently rubbing a Q-tip on the tape backside, promoting adhesion to the chip surface,
and slowly (a couple of minutes) peeling off the tape (Fig. 4.1c). This leaves behind
a chip with exfoliated flakes (Fig. 4.1d), some of them monolayer and/or few-layer, as
the adhesion forces between the flake and chip surface overcome the vdW forces between
crystal layers.
4.2
Stacking
Stacking is a top-down assembly technique for joining 2D flakes vertically into stacks,
using polymer stamps.
The technique allows for assembling different 2D crystals into
tailor-made structures with new and untested properties.
The distinct layers are held
together by attractive vdW forces, hence, the stacks are also called vdW heterostructures
[16].
The method consists of three overall steps: 1) Pickup of first flake, 2) Pickup of subsequent
flakes, and 3) Final drop-down of stack onto a target substrate. In the first step, successful
pickup implies that the vdW forces between the polymer stamp and the flake exceed the
vdW forces between the flake and the original substrate that it resides on. This is achieved
by controlling the temperature, since the polymer stickiness increases with temperature, as
the viscosity decreases. Subsequent flake pickups rely entirely on the vdW forces between
the flakes. In the final drop-down step, increasing the temperature above the polymer
glass transition temperature1 enables detachment of the stack from the stamp onto a
target substrate.
In general, higher stacking temperatures minimizes interlayer contamination due to the
increased mobility of the contaminants. This increases the probability of contaminants
being squeezed out and escaping sideways between layers during the stacking process [43].
However, too high of a temperature decreases the polymer viscosity to a degree where
slow and controlled pickups become more diﬀicult and/or problems arise with retracting
the stamp from the surface, due to the polymer sticking too well to the substrate surface.
A detailed description of the stacking process, in this work used to encapsulate monolayer
graphene flakes by few-/multi-layer hBN flakes, is provided in section 5.2, together with
an illustrative flow diagram (Fig. 5.3) quoting relevant stacking parameters.
4.3
Thermal scanning probe lithography
tSPL is a high-resolution nanolithography technique, enabling greyscale patterning with
a minimum lateral feature size comparable to what can be achieved by electron beam

distance between adjacent features, and the pattern slope is limited by the tip shape and
size. The tip diameter at the apex is ≈10 nm, with a variance on the order of a few nm

interactions with the resist and tip contamination. During patterning, part of the resist
is sublimated, leaving as volatile products, and part of the resist is re-deposited on the
tip as contamination. Material is redeposited and broken off of the tip in a cyclic process
while patterning, continuously changing the effective tip size and shape, which ultimately
compromises pattern quality, and requires changing of the tip. However, the read-write

It should also be noted that the patterning conditions change, whenever the environment
below the polymer resist changes, e.g. a substrate with exfoliated flakes or stacks. Firstly,
such environmental variances affect the heat flow from the tip into the resist, and sec-

polymer is often not the final target. In the case of pattern transfer via etching, additional
factors may affect the final pattern quality, such as etch induced roughness and pattern
amplification/dampening caused by differences in etch rate selectivity between the resist
and flake/substrate.
4.4
Electron beam lithography
An alternative high-resolution lithography technique is electron beam lithography (EBL).
Here, patterning of a resist is obtained in a two-step process: exposure followed by de-
velopment. In the first step, a tightly focused electron beam is scanned across an e-beam
sensitive resist, exposing the desired pattern and locally changing the solubility.
For
a positive resist, the exposed areas become more soluble2, while for a negative resist,
the exposed areas become crosslinked, improving its resistance against a developer. In
the second step, a solvent is used to remove either the exposed (positive resist) or non-
exposed areas (negative resist) [44]. While the resolution of conventional photolithography
is diffraction-limited by the wavelength of UV-light to a few hundred nanometers, EBL
exploits the small de Broglie electron wavelength, λ < 1 Å [44]. However, this limit is
solely theoretical, and in reality, the resolution is limited by the quality of the electromag-
netic lenses that form and focus the beam, as well as electron backscattering in the resist,
also known as the proximity effect [44]. Minimum feature sizes of sub-10 nm using EBL
has been demonstrated [44], hence, the resolution is comparable with tSPL.
EBL is ideal for binary patterning, since a resist area is either exposed or not exposed.
Smooth greyscale patterning can be achieved e.g. by dose-modulated exposure in combina-
tion with thermal annealing post-treatment [45]. However, precise height control is more
diﬀicult to achieve than with tSPL, and mathematical functions are not readily adopted
and converted into physical patterns in the resist, at least not on the scale relevant to the
present work [46]. Moreover, EBL is not sensitive to the surface topography in the same
way as tSPL. This makes tSPL the preferable choice for some cases where the pattern
quality is dependent on the surface structure. For the greyscale patterning in this work, it
is critical to avoid surface inhomogeneities, e.g. impurities or bubbles in the resist, where
some may be too small to detect with an optical microscope.

chamber is grounded. Switching on the RF source generates an oscillating electric field on
the sample electrode, which attracts and repels the electrons in the gas in each field cycle.

4.6
Atomic force microscopy
Atomic force microscopy (AFM) is a surface topography characterization technique, allow-
ing the quantification of surface structures with nanometer precision. Like tSPL (section
4.3), AFM belongs to the family of scanning probe microscopes. The technique relies
on raster-scanning a fine tip, attached to a cantilever, across a sample surface, either in
contact or tapping mode, and recording the resulting bending of the cantilever from its
equilibrium position. A focused laser beam is reflected off of the cantilever backside into
a photodetector, which records the cantilever deflection, converting it into an electrical
signal. As this signal is proportional to the tip displacement, it provides direct informa-
tion on the surface topography as a function of lateral position, allowing for topographical
surface imaging [50, 51]. Section 5.5 provides more details of the specific AFM instrument
and settings, used in this work for the topographical characterization of the produced
sinusoidal patterns.

Device fabrication
5.1
Exfoliation of hBN and graphene flakes
The mechanical exfoliation of graphene and hBN flakes is carried out at an exfoliation
”station” under local exhaust.
The first step of the exfoliation process is the preparation of chips, onto which the 2D
crystal flakes will be exfoliated. This work utilizes new and unmarked silicon (Si) wafers
with a silicon dioxide (SiO2) film thickness of ∼300 nm. While gold (Au) index marks
make it easier to later note down the positions of potential flakes, there is a risk of unin-
tentional doping of the flakes. For some applications this is less critical, however, as the
goal is to make electrical devices from the exfoliated flakes, unmarked wafers are used.
Using a diamond tip, the unmarked Si/SiO2 wafers are cut into chips with approximately
1.5 cm - 2 cm side lengths. A nitrogen (N2) gun is used to clear each chip of residual
crystal grains and dust, inevitable by-products from cutting the wafer. Since new wafers
are used, no further cleaning is performed.
The exfoliation process follows the workflow presented in Fig. 4.1. The bulk ”mother”
crystals of graphite and hBN originate from the manufacturer HQ Graphene. Scotch tape
and blue tape1 are used for the exfoliation of graphene and hBN respectively. Scotch tape
generally results in a larger number of monolayer flakes, while blue tape leaves fewer tape
residues on the chip, hence the respective choices [53]. The small piece of bulk ”mother”
crystal is handled with a set of tweezers and put on a tape piece, where the edges are
folded for easier handling. Gently tapping the tip of the tweezers on the piece of crystal
and then gently peeling it off makes part of the crystal stick to the stamp tape. The crystal
piece remaining on the stamp tape is then replicated into arrays of exfoliated flakes on
the stamp tape as shown in Fig. 4.1a, which is then used to create copy tape pieces (Fig.

5.2
Stacking
The hBN/graphene/hBN stacks are produced following the vdW stacking procedure, de-
scribed in section 4.2. This work uses a two-layer polymer stamp for flake pickup, con-
sisting of a polycarbonate (PC) film on a poly-dimethylsiloxane (PDMS) cushion, and a
mechanical stacking platform for assembling the flakes into vdW heterostructures.
5.2.1
Stamp preparation
The first step of the stacking process is the preparation of the PDMS/PC stamp. PC has
a relatively high glass transition temperature of 145 ◦C [42], which enables high stacking
temperatures and improves the conditions for clean inter-layer surfaces as discussed in
section 4.2.
Fig. 5.1 illustrates the stamp preparation process. A N2 gun is used to clean a set of glass
slides, before a pipette is used to place 7-8 drops of a solution of PC in chloroform, with
a 10wt% (weight percent) concentration, onto one of the glass slides (Fig. 5.1a), inside a
fumehood. The second glass slide is put on top (rotated 180 degrees with respect to the
first glass slide), and the liquid pool spreads out between the two glass slides for a few
seconds, before quickly sliding them apart (Fig. 5.1b), leaving smooth uniform films on
both glass slides (Fig. 5.1c). The PC films are left to dry inside the fumehood, while the
PDMS cushion is prepared. A razor blade is used to cut a small (1-2 mm) PDMS square,
which is placed with a set of tweezers on a glass slide (Fig. 5.1d) (prior cleaned by a N2
gun) while ensuring that the protective film on top of the PDMS square is removed. A
small square hole, slightly larger than the PDMS square, is cut and poked out from a long
piece of scotch tape (with edges folded for easier handling), leaving behind a tape ”window
frame” (Fig. 5.1e). Then, a square, slightly larger than the ”window frame”, is cut from
the dried PC films (Fig. 5.1f). The surrounding residual PC film is removed by carefully
scraping the razorblade sideways across the glass slide away from all four sides of the PC
square. Alternatively, scotch tape can be used to clear the PC square of the surrounding
film. The tape ”window frame” is now positioned on top of the PC square (Fig. 5.1g),
picking it up, effectively filling the tape ”window frame” with a PC ”window” (Fig. 5.1h).
The small PDMS square is covered by the PC ”window” (Fig. 5.1i), and adhesion to the
glass slide is promoted by rubbing a wide tweezers tip/spatula across the tape backside,
carefully avoiding the PC area in the tape hole. A razor blade is used to cut away any
tape pieces falling outside of the glass slide, and the PDMS/PC stamp is ready to be used
for stacking. A top view and a side view of the final stamp is illustrated in Figs. 5.1j-k.

5.2.2
Stacking process
A mechanical stacking system, HQ2D MAN from HQ graphene, is used to stack the ex-
foliated graphene and hBN flakes. Fig. 5.2 displays an overview of the stacker, which
consists of a sample stage and a stamp stage, both equipped with three-axis microma-
nipulators, allowing precise position adjustments. The z-micromanipulator of the sample
stage is furthermore connected to a piezo scanner, enabling movements with nanometer
precision and tunable speed, controlled from an external computer. Moreover, the sample
stage is connected to a heating source to adjust the stacking temperature, and a vac-
uum chuck secures the sample chip in place. The stamp stage is equipped with a set of
clamps for securing the stamp, and is free to rotate in the horizontal plane, which makes
it easy to create enough operational room to change sample chips during stacking. A long
working distance optical microscope, connected to a computer screen, allows for monitor-
ing the flake positions during the stacking process. The whole setup is mounted on an
anti-vibration platform.

A workflow diagram for the stacking process is displayed in Fig. 5.3. For the first pickup,
the stage temperature is set to 80 ◦C. The chip containing the thick hBN flake (which will
constitute the top flake of the stack) is placed on the sample stage, and the flake position
is located in the OM. The glass slide with the PC/PDMS stamp is inserted in the stamp
stage, and rotated into the OM column, where its position is locked. The condition of the
stamp is investigated by studying the uniformity of the PC film with the OM. In case of a
non-uniform film, the stamp is replaced with a new uniform stamp. The stamp is guided
to an empty region on the chip, and the two are slowly brought into contact, to locate the

point where the stamp first touches the substrate. The stamp is retracted, and the flake
is positioned a bit away from the point-of-first-contact to ensure a smooth pickup, before
slowly bringing the stamp and flake into contact, the PC front spreading smoothly across
the flake. After approximately 3 minutes, the stamp is retracted, and the OM is used to
check if the flake is transferred successfully to the stamp. In the case of an unsuccessful
pickup, the stacking temperature is increased to 90-100 ◦C.
For the subsequent pickups, the sample chip is replaced with first the chip containing the
graphene flake, and afterwards the chip with the thin hBN flake (which will conclude the
stack as the bottom layer). The graphene flake is located in the OM, and the top hBN (on
the stamp) is carefully and continuously aligned to it, by repeatedly alternating the OM
focus between the two flakes and making small x- and y- adjustments, as they are slowly
brought into contact. For the graphene pickup, the temperature of the sample stage is
increased to 100 ◦C, and contact is maintained for around 5 minutes, before retracting
the stamp, and checking in the OM if the flake is successfully picked up. The process is
repeated for picking up the bottom hBN flake, only with a lower pickup temperature of
80 ◦C, except in the case of a failed pickup, where the temperature is increased in the
re-attempts.
In the final drop-down, a Si/SiO2 (300 nm) chip with electrical contact pads is the target
substrate on the sample stage. The temperature is increased to around 140 ◦C and the
stack and target substrate are brought into contact. Then, the temperature is increased
to 180 ◦C for around 1 min, increasing the contact area between the stamp and the
substrate. Since this temperature is above the glass transition temperature of PC, the PC
film melts/detaches from the PDMS cushion as the stamp is retracted, leaving behind the
PC-covered stack on the target substrate.

The tSPL step (Fig. 5.6.3) is performed using a NanoFrazor Scholar instrument, with
a working principle similar to the description and figure provided in section 4.3. Pat-

forcing the NanoFrazor tip as far down as possible in the PPA layer, which is achieved by

maximum value allowed by the program), to write out a test pattern with the read-write
feedback loop disabled (section 4.3). The test pattern is not the actual design pattern, but
rather some simple pattern, to probe the resist thickness. Due to accelerated tip cooling
close to the substrate surface, the NanoFrazor tip is not able to decompose the last ∼10

Before the design pattern is written over the region of interest, three important pre-steps
are performed:
1. First, the design pattern is imported to the NanoFrazor software as a bitmap file
(section 4.3), after which a pixel size value is assigned, and the depth range of the
pattern is defined. The difference between max and min are chosen based on the
final target pattern in the flake/substrate, considering the expected etch selectivity
between the PPA and the particular underlying layer. However, the vertical position
of the pattern in the PPA layer also requires some thought. Preferably, the PPA
”cushion” between the bottom of the pattern and the substrate surface should be
as thin as possible to give the best pattern transfer results during etching. How-
ever, the thermal flow from the tip into the resist is different in the vicinity of the
substrate surface and may affect the pattern quality. Hence, typically a cushion of
30-50 nm is the best trade-off. Moreover, the top of the pattern should be recessed
within the PPA layer at least 10 nm deeper than the top surface, to avoid etching
the surrounding area in case of a slight overetch.
2. Second, the PPA thickness is checked locally at the area of interest, since local vari-
ations in the resist thickness occur. In particular, the PPA film thickness may be
different on protruding flakes or stacks, compared to the surrounding flat substrate.
3. Third, a few test runs of the design pattern are performed away from the area of
interest, letting the read-write feedback loop calibrate the writing parameters6. It is
especially critical to pre-calibrate the pattern if the area of interest is small, e.g. a
small flake or stack, leaving small room for re-attempts. However, tip contamination
generally increases with writing time, hence, as soon as the measured profile matches
the target profile (Fig. 4.2f), the calibration pattern should be aborted and the real
pattern initiated.

5.5.1
Matlab analysis
The AFM data is analyzed and fitted using the code written for the AFM data processing
in [10]. Fig. 5.11 presents a flow diagram that illustrates how the code works, using as
example data the acquired AFM data for P3 after the O2 clean.
The raw AFM data (.spm) is opened in the open source software Gwyddion, from which it
is saved as an ASCII data matrix (.txt), a format which can be read by MATLAB. After
importing the data to MATLAB and plotting the raw data (Fig. 5.11a), an initial pre-
processing levelling is performed to remove measurement artefacts, centering each data
row by subtracting its median value (Fig. 5.11b). A second levelling is performed (Fig.
5.11c), employing a flat median filtering step. This means that the patterned area is not
included, when calculating the row-wise median. The alignment is based on the row-wise
median, rather than the mean, as it less affected by a few extreme data points, that are
not representative of the general topography. The data is cropped in the vertical direction,
46

==PAGE:55==
including only the area of interest, i.e. the pattern area. The subsequent analysis steps
are based on this cropped data. A 2D plane is fitted to the unpatterned stripes beside the
pattern (Fig. 5.11d-e), and is subtracted from the data (Fig. 5.11f-h). To quantify the

5.6.1
E-beam resist spin coat
Polymethyl methacrylate (PMMA) is used as a positive (see section 4.4) mask layer for
the e-beam exposures. PMMA with a concentration of 4wt% (weight percent) in anisole
is used. A pipette is used to drop PMMA onto the chip, until ∼2/3 of the chip surface is
covered. Spin coating is performed at a spin speed of 3000 RPM, with an acceleration of
500 RMS/s for 60 s. The backside of the chip is cleaned carefully with an acetone wipe,
removing residual PMMA that has leaked under the chip during the spinning. This is
done to prevent misalignment during EBL. Finally, the chip is baked on a hotplate set at
150 ◦C for 2 min.
5.6.2
E-beam exposure
An Elionix ELS-F125 EBL system is used to perform the e-beam exposures. The design
patterns are exposed using a beam current of 1 nA and a dose of 1000 µC/cm2. The EBL
exposures and designs are made with PostDoc. Tuan Khanh Chau.
EBL layout
The EBL design, used for the EBL exposures, is made in the hierarchical layout editor
program L-edit. As the surface topography cannot be inspected before EBL patterning,
in contrast to tSPL, the design of the EBL patterns rely on OM images of the patterned
hBN/Gr/hBN stack. OM images (20x and 100x magnification) are converted to bitmap
files and imported to L-edit, where they are rescaled and aligned to the chip layout. The
20x OM is required to align to the alignment marks in the chip layout, while the 100x OM
provides the level of detail required to create precise EBL pattern designs.
A combined EBL design file is made containing the layouts for all three EBL patterning
steps: top gate deposition, etching, and electrode deposition. The entire device design is
made beforehand to ensure that the features in the separate EBL patterns align to each
other. However, the layouts for the three EBL steps are assigned to their own individual
layer in L-edit, so that each layout can be viewed, edited and exposed separately.
The first layer comprises the top gates and their contacting wires. In the following EBL
step, the graphene edge is exposed via etching, so it is critical that the electrical contacts
for the top gates are defined in the first EBL exposure, to avoid electrically shorting the
devices. One top gate is defined along the modulation direction of P1, one perpendicular
to the modulation direction of P3, and one outside of P2, to constitute an unpatterned
control device.
The second layer defines the areas that will be etched, leaving behind Hall bars carved out
from the stack material. While a Hall bar is typically depicted with only four side legs
(and two end contacts for source and drain), more are preferred in reality, as some of the
metal contacts may not result in high-quality ohmic connections. However, the number of
legs is limited by geometric aspects. In this work, the lateral extent of the sine patterns
and the top gates imposes a lower limit on feasible leg widths and spacings. Moreover, the
number of Hall bar legs is limited by the number and distribution of contact pads on the
chip. In this work, the original chip contains 20 contact pads and it is feasible to fit six
additional contact pads in the remaining space. This enables eight legs on each of the two
patterned Hall bars and seven legs on the control Hall bar, where two legs are reserved for
source and drain electrodes on each device. Each leg is 200 nm wide with an approximate
500 nm lateral distance between adjacent legs. To account for the possibility of a small
misalignment during exposure, the etched areas between the Hall bar side legs are defined
to extend slightly into the main channel, to ensure that all of the main channel is uniform
and covered by the metal top gate, without ungated strips along the edges. Moreover, the
layout includes etching around the wires (which are defined in the following layer three),
Smooth greyscale dielectric nanopatterning of graphene
55
==PAGE:64==
to prevent the metal leads from coming into contact with unencapsulated graphene or
graphite flakes. This includes the monolayer graphene flake in the stack, that in certain
areas extends out from the cover of the top hBN flake, and surrounding graphite flakes
picked up in the stacking process. The etched channels are centered around the wires,
with a width that is twice that of the wires, to provide electrical insulation. In addition,
etched channels are defined between the three Hall bars, ensuring that they are electrically
insulated from each other.
The edge contacts to the Hall bar legs and the network of wires connecting them to the
surrounding contact pads are defined in the third layer. The width of the wires range from
0.5 µm to 4 µm, the wires closest to the Hall bar devices being the most narrow due to the
limited space. If the distance between wires is too small, problems can arise during lift-
off. As the wires extend away from the Hall bars towards the contact pads, the distance
between adjacent wires increases, allowing for a transition to gradually thicker wires. The
additional contact pads are also defined in this final EBL layer.
Once all three layers are complete, the L-edit design file is converted to GDSII format,
which is the format read by the EBL exposer.
5.6.3
Development
The PMMA regions exposed by EBL, have become more soluble (see section 4.4), allowing
them to be chemically dissolved in a development step. A mixture of methyl isobutyl ke-
tone (MIBK) and IPA is used as developer. The development is performed by submerging
the chip, using a set of tweezers, in MIBK:IPA 1:3 for 20 s, followed by 20 s submerged
in pure IPA. Lastly, a N2 gun is used to gently blow-dry the chip at a 90 degree angle, to
prevent that the chip is blown away.
5.6.4
Metal evaporation
Metal evaporation is used in two different steps of the fabrication process: deposition
of the top gates and the electrical contacts to the Hall bars (See Figs. 5.16 and 5.18,
respectively). For the first application, a thin metal layer is evaporated onto the exposed
areas of the sine patterns, creating local top gates that, ideally, conform perfectly to the
pattern topography. In the same step, the electrical contacts to the top gates are made.
For the second application of metal evaporation, electrical pathways are formed between
the Hall bar devices and the surrounding contact pads on the chip, via edge contacts.
The metal evaporation comprises a two step deposition process, where first a thin Cr layer
is deposited, followed by a thicker Au layer. The Cr layer serves as an adhesive layer,
improving the deposition of the noble Au atoms, which adhere poorly on their own due
to their low reactivity [58]. A MiniLab ST90A deposition system from Moorfield, located
inside a Moorfield glovebox, is used for the metal deposition. The working principle is
similar to the description in section 4.7. Prior to the deposition of edge contacts, an O2
plasma cleaning is employed (similar to Tab. 5.1), to remove contaminants at the exposed
graphene edge. The sample holder is retrieved from the deposition system, and the chip
is fastened to it with Kapton tape, ideal for usage in high vacuum systems due to its low
outgassing properties. A set of tweezers is used to rub on the tape backside, promoting
adhesion to the chip and sample holder. It is critical that this step is performed inside the
glove box in the second deposition step, where the edge contacts to graphene are created,
as the graphene edges have just been cleaned with O2 plasma. Hence, to ensure high
quality edge contacts, the chip should not be exposed to air between the O2 clean and the
metal deposition. However, it is more tricky to fasten the chip to the sample holder inside
the glovebox, and for the first deposition step (top gates), it is done outside the glovebox.

5.6.6
Lift-off
A lift-off step is used to remove residual metal (and the PMMA underneath) after metal
evaporation or PMMA remaining after etching. The chip is put in a beaker filled 2/3
with acetone and placed on a hotplate set at 60◦C. The beaker is covered with alu-foil to
minimize evaporation of acetone. After 1-2 hrs, the Au film appears loose and crumbled.
While the chip is still submerged in acetone, a pipette is used to ”pulse” acetone over
the chip surface, removing the loosened Au flakes. The chip is transferred directly to an
IPA petri dish. Due to the fast evaporation of acetone8, this step should be performed
as quickly as possible, to prevent that dissolved species re-adsorbs on the chip surface.
The petri dish is carried carefully to the OM, where the status of the lift-off is checked at
low magnification. If the lift-off appears successful, the chip is rinsed with an IPA spray
bottle, and blow-dried gently with a N2 gun at a 90 degree angle.

Wire-bonding
To enable electrical characterization of the devices, they must be electrically connected
to an external chip carrier. This is obtained with wire-bonding. First, a set of tweezers
or a diamond tip is used to scratch the center of the chip carrier and the Si on the chip
backside, before the chip is fixed to the center with a sticky silver paste. This will enable
the Si to act as a global backgate. Then, a F&S BondTec 5630 wire-bonder is used to
perform wire-bonding, using thin platinum wires, to connect the contact pads on the chip
to the electrical pads on a chip carrier. Since each contact pad is uniquely connected to a
Hall bar contact, the wire-bonding allows independent access and probing of each device
contact. Moreover, one of the contact pads on the chip carrier is connected to the center
area of the chip carrier (electrically connected to the chip backside with the silver paste)
creating a backgate connection. Fig. 5.20 displays an overview of the wire-bonded chip
with the enumerated connections.

6
Discussion
This work set out to perform smooth greyscale nanopatterning of hBN, targeting sim-
ple unidirectional single period sinusoidal profiles. The goal was to create electrical de-
vices comprising hBN/graphene/hBN stacks with topographically modulated top gates,
with the objective of characterising the resulting magnetotransport properties and probing
Weiss oscillations in smooth, deterministic superlattices. Three devices were successfully
fabricated from the same hBN/graphene/hBN stack: one with a top gate deposited per-
pendicular to the modulation direction of the topographic sine pattern, one deposited
along the sine tops, and one deposited on flat hBN, constituting a control device. Some
different fabrication aspects are discussed below, and comparisons are made with models
(COMSOL simulations) and relevant literature.
6.1
Device fabrication
Overall, the greyscale patterning process appears successful. NanoFrazor topography data
shows that the three initial PPA patterns in general match very well with the target sine
profile (Λ = 300 nm and 2A = 15 nm). The patterns are slightly vertically upshifted and
the peaks (valleys) are slightly sharper (rounder) than the target profile, see subsection
5.4.2. Since this was not an issue during the calibration run, which was performed above
an area of plain SiO2 (see Fig.
A.4), the observed discrepancies are probably due to
the change in dielectric and thermal environment, when moving on to patterning the
area on top of the hBN/graphene/hBN stack (subsection 5.4.2). The precise effect on the
patterning process is diﬀicult to predict, and due to the limited area available on the stack,
no preliminary test runs were performed here. While it was observed during patterning,
that the stack appeared to interfere slightly undesirably with the patterning process, the
discrepancies were so small, that no adjustments were made. Based on the data, improved
pattern quality might perhaps be achieved by employing a slightly thicker PPA cushion,
than what test runs on flat SiO2 deem necessary. However, more studies are needed to
confirm this.
The patterns are transferred from PPA into the underlying hBN layer with RIE, see sub-
section 5.4.3. Generally, the etch transfer appears successful with only minor discrepancies
between the target and final results. Most importantly, AFM data reveal that the pattern
did not etch all the way to the graphene layer, which was the most critical concern, as
depositing gold directly on top of graphene would cause electrical shorting of the devices.
In addition, AFM data for the unpatterned regions display smooth surfaces, indicating
that these regions were protected from etching as planned.
AFM data show that the
patterns are slightly underetched compared to the expected target, see subsection 5.5.2,
which is most likely explained by the unexpected plasma strike time discussed in subsec-
tion 5.4.3. Similar incidents may be avoided by performing a test-run of the etch recipe
without the sample, to attain an approximate idea of the strike time. This is employed for
the subsequent RIE, where the stack is shaped into Hall bar devices. Despite the slight
underetch, fitting a 2D sine function to AFM data of the patterns reveals that the fitted
parameters in general match well with the target sine parameters, e.g. Λ ≈300 nm, with
a low RMSE between the fit function and the data. Furthermore, FFTs, especially the
ones acquired after the final O2 plasma cleaning step, reveal peaks in the Fourier spectrum
that correspond to a nearly pure sinusoidal topography in real space. Hence, the slight
underetch appears insignificant, considering the low error on the sine fits where the FFTs

show minimal unwanted additional spatial frequencies. Comparing data before and after
the final O2 clean indicates improved smoothness of both the patterned and unpatterned
regions after cleaning, and a reduced fit RMSE for the patterned areas. Specifically, a
sub-nm roughness is achived for the unpatterned areas and a fit RMSE below 2 nm is
observed after the O2 cleaning. Hence, implementing an O2 plasma cleaning step after
the hotplate sublimation step appears to improve surface smoothness and enhance pattern
quality.
6.2
Electrostatics simulations
The COMSOL simulations in chapter 3, can be used to infer three important results.
First, the potential modulation at the graphene layer is quenched as the period of the to-
pographically defined sine pattern becomes small, and complete cut-off is achieved when
the period equals the cushion value (the vertical distance from the bottom of the topo-
graphic pattern to the graphene layer). Aside from imposing constraints on the device
geometry, the result has another practical implication. The cutoff behaviour effectively
implies a low pass filter-like property, where any etch-induced roughness in the topographic
pattern profile is filtered out as long as the feature sizes associated with the roughness are
below the cushion value. In this work, the pattern roughness in the AFM data appears
well below the cushion value of 50 nm, hence, the small etch induced pattern roughness
is not expected to be reflected in the super-potential imposed on the charge carriers by
electrostatic gating.
Second, the simulations provide useful maps for navigating alternative device geometries.
For instance, the expected modulation strength can be additionally increased by decreasing
the cushion value, increasing the amplitude of the topographic pattern, and consider the
possibility if utilizing chips with thinner oxide layers. Moreover, the simulations indicate
that a larger superlattice period results in a stronger modulation. However, the larger the
period of the superlattice, the larger the ballistic mean free path needs to be, to resolve
the same number of commensurate orbits.
Third, the simulations imply that for the specific parameters of the device fabricated in
this work, twice the modulation amplitude at the graphene layer is expected to be on the
order of 100 mV, when 10 V is applied to the top gate, corresponding to a modulation
that is around 1 % of the initial input potential. Hence, the simulations indicate that
the potential modulation at the graphene layer appears to belong to the regime of a weak
potential modulation. This result aligns with the initial analytical prediction (see section
2.4.1), and opens up for the possibility of comparing with several interesting studies also
utilizing weak modulations [9], including the very first paper on Weiss oscillations [5].
6.3
Expectations for magnetotransport when comparing to
literature
The time span of this work was not quite long enough to obtain magnetotransport data
and probe the interesting possibility of Weiss oscillations in the device.
Mainly, this
is because it took more work to estimate the etch rate of SF6 in PPA than estimated,
due to various unforeseeable instrument issues. However, the device fabrication appears
successful, and it is still insightful to discuss possible expectations and considerations in
relation to literature. Particularly two criteria need to be fulfilled for Weiss oscillations to
be sustained in the fabricated devices: 1) The quality of the device must be suﬀiciently
good, e.g. the ballistic mean free path must be preserved over several superlattice periods.
2) The topographic modulation must be large enough to cause a potential modulation at
the graphene layer, that is significant enough to affect the charge carrier motion. In [9],
64
Smooth greyscale dielectric nanopatterning of graphene
==PAGE:73==
Weiss oscillations up to third order, λ = 3, are resolved, corresponding to a cyclotron
orbit circumference of 2πrc = 1.7 µm. In this work, to resolve a similar number of Weiss
oscillations, a ballistic mean free path of 2πrc = π(λ −1/4)·0.3 µm ≈2.6 µm is needed
due to the larger superlattice period (recall Eq. 2.18). Moreover, the target topographic
modulation of hBN was ∼30 nm (see subsection 5.4.2), however, AFM results indicate that
the actual modulation is closer to half of that (see section 5.5.2). Whether the mean free
path and the topographic modulation are suﬀicient, may be uncovered by the progressing
magnetoresistance measurements.
Moreover, as mentioned in section 2.5.2, the graphene carrier density is expected to be
laterally spatially modulated by the imposed periodic superlattice.
Recalling that the
cyclotron radius, in the case of graphene, has a square-root dependency on the carrier
density (Eq. 2.23), the commensurate cyclotron orbits are not expected to be perfectly
circular orbits, but perhaps be accompanied by a wavy component (supp. info. in [39]).
This may imply that a higher ballistic mean free path is needed to resolve the same
number of Weiss oscillations, than in the simple picture of perfectly circular trajectories.
Furthermore, it is not trivial how edge effects, such as charge accumulation, will affect
the transport in the device.
Some studies [9, 62] report increased low-field resistance
peaks ascribed to diffusive scattering at the device boundary. However, this appears to
contradict [39], where magnetotransport data indicate the presence of specularly reflected
electrons in a skipping orbit motion along the device edges persisting over several skipping
periods.

7
Conclusion and Outlook
In this work, three Hall bar devices have successfully been made from hBN/graphene/hBN
stacks, with smooth simple unidirectional sinusoidal surface patterns. The relevant find-
ings in terms of simulations and topographic characterization are summarized below.
Electrostatics simulations were carried out with the FEM solver COMSOL, mapping out
the expected modulation strength at the graphene layer in different relevant parameter
spaces. The results imply that the potential modulation approaches a clear cutoff region
when the topographic sinusoidal period becomes small, with the cushion value as the de-
termining threshold for complete modulation quenching. Moreover, when using the exact
parameters of the device fabricated in this work, simulations suggest that the expected
potential modulation is ∼100 mV, for a top gate potential of 10 V. This implies a modu-
lation of ∼1 % of the applied top gate potential, hence, belonging to the weak modulation
regime.
Fundamental experimental work has been performed to improve the fabrication process.
This includes estimating a reliable etch rate of SF6 in PPA, as ∼1.20 nm/s, given the
parameters quoted in this work. Moreover, for the tSPL process, this work suggests the
presence of an etch-induced contamination layer after the SF6 etch, that is not removed
along with the remaining PPA layer in the sublimation step. However, OMs and AFM
data indicate that this thin unknown layer is successfully removed by O2 plasma cleaning.
Therefore, this final cleaning step is suggested as a general implementation to achieve
enhanced post-etch pattern quality and surface smoothness.
Greyscale nanopatterning was employed using tSPL in combination with RIE. A sinusoidal
topographic pattern with a wavelength of Λ ≈300 nm was successfully patterned in poly-
mer resist and transferred into the upper hBN layer in a hBN/graphene/hBN stack. The
patterns appeared slightly underetched with a topographic modulation ∼15 nm, hence,
only around half of the target modulation. However, the underetch appears insignificant
based on 2D sine fitting of the AFM data, with a low RMSE fit, accompanied by FFTs
that reveal an almost pure sinusoidal real-space topography. A plausible reason for the
slight underetch is identified and a suggestion for future success is provided. The pat-
terned stacks were successfully fabricated into electrical devices using EBL and selective
RIE. Top gates were successfully defined and contacted on top of the pattern areas in a
direction perpendicular and longitudinal to the pattern modulation direction.
Magnetotransport measurements are not yet completed, with characterization at zero B-
field being performed at the time of writing. The presence of WOs is dependent on the
modulation strength.
WOs will only manifest, if the topographic modulation is large
enough to affect the motion of the charge carriers, once electrostatic gating is employed.
The number of resolved Weiss oscillations is expected to be limited by the ballistic mean
free path. Exotic electron trajectories are expected, due to the smooth periodic lateral
modulation of the carrier density imposed by the modulated top gate.
In case measurements don’t indicate WOs, alternative device geometries may be explored,
e.g.
increasing the topographic modulation, decreasing the cushion, i.e.
the vertical
distance between the graphene layer and the bottom of the topographic pattern, or utilizing
a thinner SiO2 layer. Alternative device fabrication techniques may also be explored, such
as greyscale EBL, or skip the etch step, keeping the patterned polymer layer in the stack,
and deposit the metal top gate directly on the polymer layer. However, it is uncertain
Smooth greyscale dielectric nanopatterning of graphene
67
==PAGE:76==
how well the pattern topography may be controlled with EBL, and the stability of the
polymer may become an issue for the low-temperature transport measurements.
However, if the ongoing magnetoresistance measurements reveal WOs, the simple pat-
tern in this work (unidirectional, single period sinusoidal) act as a first proof of concept
towards general superlattices (GSL), obtained by smooth greyscale dielectric patterning.
This could be more complex multiwaveform patterns, where several periods and/or rota-
tional degrees of freedom are explored. While freeform topographic patterns are realized
in literature, [11], the effects on the electronic properties, including the presence and
behaviour of WOs, is untested.
