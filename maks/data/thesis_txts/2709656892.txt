Summary 
This study summarizes reviews on modelling tools for Building integrated 
Photovoltaics (BIPV). The uptake of BIPV projects has been slow due to the high 
complexity and cross-industrial requirements. Hence, there is an urgent need for a 
valid and fast multi-simulation program that tightly integrates Photovoltaic 
performance calculations with other Building Information Modelling (BIM) tools. 
This study validates the Photovoltaic (PV) components, Import CEC photovoltaics 
module, Photovoltaics surface, Sunpath shading, and DC to AC derate factor in 
Ladybug Lagacy [2], a plugin for Rhinooseros3D/Grasshopper. The sub models are 
validated against measured data from a roof- mounted PV-array at DTU Risø with 
input weather data measured on site. The three validated sub models implemented 
in this PV performance model are the Transposition model calculating the plane of 
array(POA) irradiance using Perez 1990 [3], the NOCT Cell temperature Model [4], 
and the DC power model using CEC Module Model [4]. To avoid carrying errors from 
one sub model to another, the models are validated individually. Python scripts to 
each of the respective sub models are edited using measured data as direct input. 
The complete PV simulation model includes the component Sunpath shading, that 
uses concepts from Site Evaluation Forms to calculate annual weighted shading loss. 
The PV simulation is validated and compared against an Hourly shading model with 
an additional Sky View Factor implementation, which was developed in this study.  
It was found that the original implemented Ladybug PV performance model 
including Annual shading, estimates the DC power output with 1% inaccuracy 
respect the analyzed period of around 2.5 months in the summer. Since the shading 
model is based on an annual weighted shading factor, the precision decreases for 
BIPV in complex urban settings and cannot be used for optimizations through hourly 
performance metrices. The Hourly shading model has a 1.85% inaccuracy within the 
analysis period but takes hourly shading into account and is therefore better for 
BIPV implementation. The validation of the sub models showed that the increased 
inaccuracy was caused by a 6.6% overestimation in the transposition model 
calculating POA irradiance and a 10% overestimation of the Cell temperature while 
only 1% overestimation in the DC power model. The results showed that residuals 
occur due to partial shading, hence the models don’t include the specific setup of 
bypass diodes, strings, power electronics, inverters and therefore not accounting for 
the mismatch losses that happened in the 10 serially connected PV modules. The 
performance models account for the effects of shading losses linearly with the shaded 
area, hence it is only suitable for panels with individual micro inverters or DC/DC 
power optimizers but does not estimate mismatch losses due to partial shading in 
strings or arrays. This is a compromise to its relative fast computation time that 
enables instant results in the 3D CAD environment of Rhino. It can be concluded 
that there’s a need for new BIPV performance models that optimize the POA 
irradiance for example with raytracing and cell temperature models for different 
façade implemented PV modules.

1 Introduction 
The world stands against a humanitarian crisis caused by carbon emissions from 
fossil fuels, deforestation etc. resulting in global warming [5].  The global energy 
demand is growing. However, introduction of clean energy sources cannot keep pace 
with this demand [6]. Buildings stand for nearly 40% of the annual global CO2 
emission, with building operation accounting for 28% [7]. To reach the 1.5˚C climate 
target, all CO2 emissions from the built environment must be eliminated by 2040 
[7]. A way to reach this goal is by electrification [8] and a transition to renewable 
energy sources such as wind and solar power. But this transition is not without 
challenges. The large fields of land needed for solar energy could cause problems for 
food production and biodiversity by limiting the space for agriculture and natural 
habitats [9]. Additionally, large scale projects have often met resistance from 
neighbors concerned about aesthetics and environmental impact. Large scale power 
plants in the countryside have also been challenging power grids [10]. For example, 
there are some parts of the Danish power grid connecting rural and urban areas 
which don’t have the sufficient capacity to transport big amounts of electricity. This 
has forced the rural areas to limit their production of electricity [11], [12].  
These problems can easily be resolved by integrating photovoltaics in existing 
buildings’ envelope. On one hand, the electricity is produced where is needed. On the 
other hand, this solution leads to a lower usage of construction materials by 
substituting a conventional building material with a PV [13].  
However, the uptake of Photovoltaic (PV) units in urban environments has been 
controversial due to poor placement of numerous smaller PV systems which 
negatively impacts architectural aesthetics [14], [15]. Nevertheless, new optimized 
technologies have been developed with improved efficiency and aesthetics, which 
make solar cells less visible by modifying the color and structure of the surface. This 
combined with an 80% reduction of the PV module price since 2009 [16], makes it 
more relevant to use Building integrated photovoltaics (BIPV) now, than ever before.  
A big challenge now is to ensure that architects and their computer design tools are 
capable of designing BIPV systems. Studies [17] have shown that architects make 
80% of the design decisions which influence a building’s energy performance in the 
early design phase. Designing energy efficient buildings is a complex process, and 
adding BIPV to the facades makes the process even more complex and 
multidisciplinary [18]. This has been recognized as the main factor of the slow 
uptake of BIPV, together with a lack of the right computing tools to address all the 
parameters influencing design decisions [19]. 
This study provides a review of the current BIPV simulation tools, design workflows 
and their challenges, and it validates PV performance simulation tools from 
Ladybug. Ladybug is a plugin for the multidisciplinary BIM/CAD design tool 
Rhinoseros3D/Grasshopper. The PV performance tool is validated against measured 
performance data from a roof mounted PV system at DTU Risø. The integrated sub 
models are the Transposition model, the Cell temperature model, and the DC

1.1 BIPV, a competitor to conventional building materials 
A study from BIPV Boost in 2020 investigated the competitiveness of BIPV compared 
to conventional building materials. The result has shown that it is highly dependent 
on the location of the project, both in terms of incoming irradiance, but also according 
to the local electricity prices and the resale prices of the electricity [13]. In Figure 1, 
prices of various facade solutions are compared with BIPV facades. 
 
Figure 1 Comparison of end-user cost various ventilated (rainscreen) facade solution [13]. 
As expected, BIPV facades are more expensive than the conventional materials. 
However, the graph does not take in to account the energy production that the active 
BIPV facades produce.  
The study also included the electricity production together with other important 
parameters for different locations, and the results show that some BIPV solutions 
are competitive with conventional materials. But this is highly dependent on the 
specific scenario. A sensitivity analysis of different parameters on the 
competitiveness has been made and the results show that the Module Efficiency [%] 
and the Yield [kWh/kWp] have the greatest influence on the relative 
competitiveness, since these parameters have a direct influence on electricity 
production [13]. Non-optimal placements are much likely to happen in urban areas. 
Therefore, it is important to be able to accurately estimate the production, and from 
this, the competitiveness, making it easier for the decision makers.

2 BIPV Design tool review 
This chapter aims to investigate the challenges that architects, developers and 
stakeholders meet in the early phases of the BIPV systems design process. Some of 
the most significant features and workflows are recognized and an insight of the 
BIPV performance modelling tools and their implemented methods are reviewed, 
ending up in a brief introduction to the proposed multidisciplinary BIPV design tool. 
This review builds upon some major studies [20][21] from 2018, where almost 200 
solar design tools have been compared, and their integration in 50 different 3D 
CAD/BIM software packages is evaluated. A more recent study [21] from 2019 
reviews 40 state-of-the-art BIPV tools and methods from the perspective of BIPV 
integration in design and multi-performance modelling and planning. The conclusion 
from these studies is that there’s an increase in demand for a software tool for 
stakeholders along with the supply chain and planning process that can be used for 
design, analysis, and troubleshooting [21]. And none of the evaluated tools can cater 
for all the factors related to BIPV project design and management. 
Architects and stakeholders target different information along with the design 
phases in a BIPV design process. In the early design phase, it is important to be able 
to create fast simulations for different design solutions making it possible to optimize 
the design for the current urban environment and criteria from stakeholders, such 
as payback time or aesthetics. Later in the process, more detailed information is 
needed regarding structure, fire safety etc.  
BIPV systems differs from conventional solar power plants in many ways. Since 
BIPV often is placed in urban areas, it is more likely that the modules are in non-
optimal places and higher spatial-temporal granularity is necessary to provide 
reliable results [21].  
IEA PVPS task 15 (2021) is a review of workflows and methods used in solar 
irradiation modelling and simulation. It is also a review of building performance 
modelling workflows, and design and finance outcome workflows [19]. It is concluded 
that further research is required on the methods that are more suitable for BIPV 
design and analysis. Especially on methods and models used to calculate GPOA, 
output power, embodied emission, heat island effect, thermal, daylight structural 
and fire requirements of BIPV projects [19]. 
The current available software that integrate PV performance in CAD/BIM software 
are analyzed in [21] and is identified as Solarius-PV, Skelion for Google Sketchup, 
INSIGHT for Revit, Ladybug Tools for Rhino and DDC-CAD PV Polysun. A 
comparison of these tools and their functions regarding PV performance simulations 
are wrapped up in the next table. It is seen that Ladybug is the tool with most 
implemented functions.

2.1 BIPV Performance modelling 
In [21], a classification on simple and complex BIPV performance modelling has been 
made. Since many simulation tools are developed for Ground mounted PV systems, 
the study presents the possible use in BIPV field with their limitations and indicate 
improper use. Different demands of BIPV compared to PV are also identified.  
It is emphasized how the many different mounting systems create various 
ventilations, hence the temperature of the BIPV varies strongly. It is strongly 
recommended by [21], that a careful analysis of temperature losses with regard to 
PV performance is made for every BIPV project. 
2.1.1 Loss mechanisms 
PV system DC loss mechanisms are, according to [22], comprise of module nameplate 
DC rating, DC wiring, diodes and connections, mismatch, and MPP tracker 
efficiency. Especially two loss mechanisms for BIPV should be emphasized: shading 
and temperature-induced losses. Shading and partial shading is a major challenge 
for the design of BIPV systems due to the complex geometry of the surroundings [22]. 
Partial shading may cause electrical mismatch losses [21], and therefore, the spatial 
resolution of the shading simulation should preferable be on a cell level (or higher) 
to account for these losses. It is stated by [21] that only Ladybug together with an 
external MATLAB script has been able to perform such BIPV performance 
simulations. The study [23] found that by detailed “planning of module string 
configuration, PV cell orientation, and location of bypass diodes reduces electrical 
mismatch losses induced by partial shading and can result in more than 50% higher 
energy yield compared to uninformed design strategies” [23].

2.1.2 Irradiance in plane of array 
The most common approach to access the Global irradiance in plane of array (GPOA) 
in the solar industry is by an analytical method to calculate the sum of the separate 
irradiance components [21], the Direct/beam in plane of array (POA), the sky diffuse 
irradiance in POA, and the ground reflected in POA [25]. 
Direct/Beam in POA represents the irradiation contribution from the sun. The 
measured Direct normal irradiance is corrected to the plane of array by the Angle of 
incidence (AOI) modifier [26]. 
Diffuse irradiance from the sky dome to POA is usually described by several models. 
The models are ordered by level of complexity: Isotropic, Simple Sandia, Hay and 
Davies, Reindl and Perez 1990. Here Perez is the most complex model that is based 
on empirical coefficients for each of the sub sky models, isotropic, circumsolar, and 
horizon components [27]. A study [28], tested 12 different models estimating hourly 
diffuse irradiance in POA. The tests were drawn out in Iran with a south facing 
surface and a west facing surface. The results showed that the residuals were higher 
for all the models when estimating the west facing surface compared to the south 
one. The best performing for the south facing surface was the Hay, Reindl and Perez 
1990, and for the west facing, Perez 1990 performed the best [28]. 
Another study [29] has compared the models Liu and Jordan, Klucher, Hay and 
Davies, Reindl and Perez. They were tested in all orientations and with different tilt 
angles, up to 90˚. A comparison of modelled and measured POA irradiance from 
different models showed that the models performed better if the measured input data 
and the validation data was measured by sensors of the same type. Furthermore, it 
was concluded that the deviations from the anisotropic models increased with 
increasing deviation from south orientation. It was also seen that the influence of 
Albedo on the POA irradiance increased as the tilt increased [29]. 
The study concludes that Perez 1990 model provides the best estimates of global 
irradiance in POA when measured albedo for the location is used. Perez is more 
accurate when estimating clear sky conditions whereas Hay and Davies model is 
recommended for regions with mostly cloudy conditions [29].  
Ground reflected irradiance in POA is based on the reflectivity of the ground surface 
(Albedo), the Global horizontal irradiance (GHI), and the angle of the surface. This 
method assumes the reflections to be isotropic. This method is described as 
reasonable for open spaces without shading, as in the case of PV plants, but can be 
highly inaccurate in complex urban settings[21].

It is described in [21], that 3D packages using advanced light transport models that 
can model an urban environment, should outperform PV tools in terms of accuracy. 
POA with shading 
The review [21] of methods used for calculation of POA shading explaines various 
shading approximations with low temporal resolution. They can be divided into Far-
field shading caused by terrain, trees, surrounding buildings. Horizon shading 
masks were used to account for shading losses in PV module software. And to 
increase the accuracy by including losses caused by near-field shading objects such 
as chimneys. This modelling software uses a specific procedure such as the near-field 
shading factor that is reliant on many approximations [21]. The methods are not 
physically based and cannot model specular near field objects. This is suitable for a 
roof mounted PV system. However, for complex BIPV systems in urban areas, it 
might lead to significant biased results. As a result, these methods are outdated, and 
advanced irradiance models such as ray-tracing approach should be used instead 
[21]. 
Sky view factor 
The sky view factor (SVF) is a measure that affects the isotropic sky diffuse and the 
ground reflected irradiance in several ways depending on the POA diffuse 
calculation method used. SVF quantifies obstructions from “self-shadowing” by the 
slope itself, and from adjacent terrain or obstructing elements called shadowing 
[[30]. It only considers the geometric aspects of the available sky radiation 
represented by the solid angle of the visible celestial hemisphere normalized by the 
solid angle of the total celestial hemisphere. Tregenza [31] proposes a way to 
calculate SVF by subdividing the sky hemisphere into smaller segments or sky 
zones. There are multiple ways of calculating SVF, and they can be divided into four 
main categories: analytical, photographic, GPS and raytracing methods [32].  
Different methods for predicting solar access in urban areas are described in [33]. 
Ray tracing algorithms are used to cast rays from the subdivided sky dome to a point 
of interest in the 3D model, then the SVF is calculated from the ratio between the 
number of rays cast and the number of rays that reaches the analysis point. SVF is 
time independent unless significant changes occur in the surroundings, which might 
happen in an urban environment [33]. 
It is described in [21] that the simple models calculating SVF are often implemented 
in PV simulation programs. And they are sufficient for roof mounted or integrated 
PV systems, but not for BIPV facades. 
 
Complex 3D simulation 
Raytracing is a rendering technique that calculates light transport of a large number 
of rays in a scene by means of recursive point-sampling. The accuracy of this 
numerical solution depends on the level of model description in terms of sky, 
materials and light transport characterized by the number of samples and bounces 
[21].

Radiance is a powerful industry-standard simulation tool for evaluating the light 
distribution in indoor and outdoor spaces. It is based on the physical behavior of light 
in a volumetric 3D model. It is extensively used in applications related to solar 
potential for electricity generation and daylighting analysis [21]. 
Daysim is a validated Radiance-based daylighting analysis program that generates 
an annual illuminance profile at each point in and around the digital model of the 
buildings in a scene. It takes into consideration contextual shading and reflections 
[21]. 
 
PVSITES-BIMSolar was specifically developed for BIPV, which also supports 
BAPV workflows, 3D model import, weather data and financial analyses, among 
others [21].   
There are hybrid approaches of ray tracing with radiosity and with rasterization to 
handle complex illumination and specular materials efficiently, such as INSIGHT 
(Solar analysis tool) for Revit. It is claimed to be up to 10 times faster than Radiance 
[21]. However, indirect illumination is based on Instant Radiosity, a biased method, 
which may be a drawback for complex BIPV. 
Frostbite is another approach that combines ray tracing with rasterization. Despite 
being originally developed for the gaming industry, it demonstrated great accuracy 
[34]. 
The complexity of the objects or structures that the ray tracing can manage is limited 
by the power of the Central Processing Unit (CPU) of a computer. But with the 
increasing effectivity of modern GPUs, special-purpose circuits designed for carrying 
out graphic functions, new GPU-based tools like 3DPV are seen to dramatically 
reduce calculation times of very complex shading scenes. Moreover, they can also be 
implemented using the programming language WebGL, which allows to carry out 
the simulations from a user-friendly Web browser, or to use online 3D objects 
libraries. 
2.1.3 Cell temperature 
The PV performance models calculating DC power output depends on the cell 
temperature. Different PV technologies have different temperature dependencies 
and therefore different temperature coefficient for voltage, current and power 
output. The temperature of a cell depends on the absorption of the POA irradiance, 
and the panels’ ability to emit heat energy in terms of radiation, convection, and 
transmission [35]. These factors are depending on the specific mounting type, the 
wind speed on the front and on the backside of the panels, the size of the panels, its 
tilt, the ambient air temperature and the distance to other objects and their 
temperatures. There are several simplified cell temperature models based on 
empirical data that are created to only depend on the mounting type, the irradiation, 
windspeed and the ambient air temperature [36], [35]. Some Nominal Operating Cell 
Temperature (NOCT)-based Cell temperature models are compared in [37]. SAM 
implements two different models [4]. One model is based on Duffie and Beckman 
2013 [38], that uses NOCT temperature where wind speed is adjusted for height 
==PAGE:16==
 
 
16 
 
about ground, and different adjustment factors for different mounting standoff. 
Another model described is the Sandia Cell Temperature Model [39], with 
coefficients derived from empirical measurements from different Cell types and 
mounting configurations. It is described in [21], that it is especially important for 
BIPV projects where the ventilation often is poor, to assess temperature carefully. 
Not only because of the decrease in power production, but also because of the 
stability and material strain. 
Additional concerns regarding the excessive heat from the BIPV modules has been 
stated in [21]. Here it is stated that there’s a need of a tool that can simulate the 
effects of the excessive heat that the BIPV facades will provide, since it has a higher 
absorptance of radiation than conventional building materials. And from this make 
a cost/benefit analysis associated with the PV system design and the building’s 
cooling or heating load. The same concerns are stated about the Urban heat island 
effect that BIPV might contribute to. 
 
2.1.4 DC power output  
DC power models can be divided into two groups, empirical models, and equivalent 
circuit models. The output is dependent on irradiance, temperature, the 
interconnection of cells, modules, stings, and the electric model that is applied to fit 
the actual cell technology that is used and the actual production [21]. The incoming 
irradiance varies significantly, especially for BIPV systems where non optimal 
placements are likely to occur. Therefore, the electrical model should be capable of 
predicting the DC yield from a broad range of irradiance values between 100 and 
1500 W/m2. Studies [40] and [41] investigate the cell performance in low light 
conditions. It is described how the “cumulative impact on PV module performance 
can decrease by 30% in module efficiency from 1000 to 200 W/m2, whereas in some 
latitudes this drop can be as high as 57% [42]. For this behavior, basic photovoltaic 
cell characteristics at low irradiance are mainly responsible, while the angle of 
incidence and spectral changes only account for up to 15%” [21]. 
SAM’s Sandia Module Model is an empirical model based on [36]. It uses the module 
parameters and module input variables that can be collected from Sandia module 
database maintained by Sandia National Laboratories [4]. Another method 
implemented in SAM is the CEC Module Model [4]. This method is an 
implementation of the single-diode equivalent circuit model using 6-parameter to 
describe the IV curve of photovoltaic modules in different conditions. Here a CEC 
library provides the 6 parameters needed for the calculations of the DC power at 
maximum power point (MPP). Those parameters are derived from algorithms [43] 
using only the PV manufactures datasheets. These two models do not only describe 
the power, but represent the shape of the IV curve, hence it allows a good description 
of the various operating conditions. And methods for calculating mismatch losses can 
be added [4].  
A simple method [44] that only contains three parameters which are derived from 
empirical parameters that can be collected from datasheets if the output is given for

2.2 Parametric design and workflow 
The first stages of the BIPV design process involves numerous design options [19]. 
An iterative process for several solutions is made either through parametric or 
traditional workflows. In a multi-performance simulation tool, it is for example 
possible to make an environmental analysis. And through parametric design, the 
simulation result with a set of criteria can be the input for the next iteration. This 
process enables the discovery of many solutions in a short time. These solutions can 
then be evaluated according to a set of criteria to optimize the design solution.  This 
method is used in a study [45] for parametric modeling and surface-specific 
sensitivity analysis of PV module layout on building skin using BIM. Here Dynamo 
is used to discriminate between different types of surfaces in Revit. This study 
proposes a flow to identify the surfaces of interest and to analyze them. The study 
utilizes a cumulative radiation potential to evaluate the potential for BIPV on the 
different surfaces, and later performs an analysis for different scenarios. The

3.1 Solar resource in plane of array - Transposition model  
 
Solar irradiance 
Solar irradiance is a measure of the instantaneous power from the sun that reaches 
per unit area of surface in [W/m2]. If this is measured on a horizontal plane it is 
called Global horizontal irradiance (GHI). This irradiance can be divided into a 
number of other solar irradiance components.  An illustration of the sky components 
is seen in Figure 12. 
• 
Direct normal irradiance (DNI) also called beam irradiance; this is the portion 
of the solar irradiance that reaches the collector without scattering and 
absorption events.  
• 
Diffuse horizontal irradiance (DHI) is the diffuse solar irradiance on a 
horizontal plane from the sky excluding the solar disk. This component can 
be further divided into  
o Isotropic diffuse (Di) that is received uniformly around the sky dome,  
o Circumsolar diffuse (Dc) is from forward scattering of light around the 
sun on clear sky days, and  
o Horizon brightening (Dh).  
• 
Reflected irradiance, (Ground reflected) is the irradiance that is reflected 
from the ground, where Albedo is the reflectivity of the ground surface, where 
0 is a black body, and 1 is a perfect reflector.

3.2 PV module performance model 
The performance model which is used for the validation is based on the CEC 
performance model that is an implementation of the six-parameter, single-diode 
equivalent circuit model of photovoltaic modules as described in[61]. This is an 
extension of the five-parameter model[61] with an additional temperature correction 
coefficient [4]. The CEC model calculates the IV curve for a given time step, and thus 
detailed information about current and voltages is available, which in a detailed way 
allows to determine specific operating points and mismatch quantification. 
The DC power output of PV panels depends on the cell temperature, higher cell 
temperatures reduce the performance. Various methods exist to calculate cell 
temperatures, the one used in Ladybug is based on the NOCT cell temperature 
algorithm[38] and is described in the next sub chapter.

3.2.2 Cell Temperature model 
It is necessary to estimate the cell temperature to calculate the temperature 
dependent parameters, light current 𝐼𝐿, the diode reverse saturation current 𝐼𝑜, and 
the modified ideality factor 𝑎.  
The inputs needed for calculating the cell temperature with the NOCT cell 
temperature algorithm[38] is the dry bulb temperature of the ambient air that is 
measured by a thermometer exposed to the air but shielded from the sun and the 
rain. Furthermore, the wind speed is needed. Those two components are provided by 
the EPW file. 
The incident irradiance that is not reflected or converted to electrical energy is 
converted to heat in the PV module. The PV module then heats up and the efficiency 
of the PV module decreases.  The temperature of the PV module depends then on the 
amount of heat emitted from the panel to the surroundings through convection, 
radiance and small amounts of conduction through the mounting system. The 
amount of convection transmitted from the panel depends on the air movements 
around it and therefore the wind speed and the size of the airgap between the panels 
and the roof.  NOCT adjustment factors based on empirical data is used to correct 
for different mounting systems[35].  The NOCT Cell Temperature Model is described

4.1.3 Weather file as simulation input 
Energy Plus Weather file format (EPW file) is originally developed for the major 
simulation programs EnergyPlus and ESP-r, but has since been adopted to other 
building simulation tools. It is created of the same data as the TMY2 weather format, 
but it is reorganized and two other columns with data values are added. One is fields-
minute data, allowing minute data to be used and another infrared sky field which 
makes it possible to calculate the effective sky temperature for re-radiation during 
nighttime [67].  
The EPW file can be read and edited as a normal text format and the header is eight 
lines, defining needed basic location information such as longitude, latitude, time 
zone, elevation etc. This is followed by 8760 lines of data representing each hour in 
a year. Each line can contain up to 32 different data columns separated with 
commas. If some data is not available the data column can be replaced with a so 
called, “missing number” value specified in [67]. 
Ladybug is using around 30 data fields, while the 
Photovoltaic modules are using only five of those,

4.2.4.2 Sky View Factor 
The Sky View Factor (SVF) is the percentage of the sky that is visible from the PV-
panels. It is calculated with the Ladybug component LB View Percent and is based 
on same CAD environments ray intersection method as mentioned before. The sky 
view factor weights the portion of the sky according to their projection into the plane 
of PV panels. This means that the sky view factor for a horizontal panel would be of 
more importance to the sky patches that are overhead vs. those near the horizon[74]. 
4.3 Validation method 
In this chapter the estimated model results will be compared with the measured 
performance of the PV array. A standard model validation approach developed by 
Sandia National Laboratories with help from industry partners has been followed 
[75]. This standardized approach makes it possible to compare results from different 
validation studies, by applying a unified mathematical/statistical approach. 
Furthermore, it suggests methods to identify opportunities for model improvements 
where possible.  
This validation chapter is therefore divided into two main parts. One validating the 
DC Performance model, followed by a second part that validates the implemented 
sub models and identifying possible improvements.  
To validate these models’ performance, indicators based on statistical performance 
metrics were used. These indicators are described here:

errors occur, the residual analyses are used. This analysis examines the distribution 
and sensitivity of the model residuals i.e. the differences between measured and 
modelled data.

The relationship between a “perfectly valid” model and measured data should be 
“statistical” rather than deterministic[75]. This is because the model is created to 
predict mean behavior and not point by point behavior. Because of this a completely 
valid model should results in residuals randomly distributed, with respect to all 
variables in the analysis. The randomness of the residuals is evaluated by plotting 
them as a histogram and a probability plot on top of a normal distribution curve. 
The residuals are plotted with time to see if any trends arise that will indicate errors 
and outliers. These could be caused by a number of physical reasons and are not 
meant to be in the model prediction. Therefore, it is justified to filter out these 
outliers and data outside of the normal operating conditions.

6 Discussion 
The discussion will be divided into four main parts, one for each of the three sub- 
models, and a final part to discuss the relevance of Ladybug as a multi-performance 
tool with the knowledge that we have gained from these validation process.  
6.1 Transposition and shading model 
The measured weather data DNI and DHI has been transposed into POA Beam 
irradiance and POA diffuse irradiance that is further derived from POA diffuse sky 
and POA ground reflected. Here the method transposing the beam irradiance is a 
simple geometrical cosine function of the AOI, while the ground reflected is 
depending on the tilt angle of the PV module and the reflectivity, albedo, of the 
ground. The diffuse sky model is based on Perez that is dividing the sky diffuse into 
three components, Isotropic sky diffuse, Circumsolar diffuse and the Horizon 
brightening component. That is calculated from DHI and complex empirically fitted 
functions. The estimated components are then summed up and compared to the 
measured GPOA.

6.2 Cell temperature model 
The variable parameters that the cell temperature depends on, according to the used 
NOCT cell temperature model, is the POA irradiance, the windspeed and the 
ambient temperature.  
The accuracy of the input parameters (causes of error) 
The uncertainty of the wind speed measurements, that the Smart weather sensor 
measures in a non-optimal located as shown in Figure 21, and also the fact that it is 
measured 50 meters away from the PV modules and in another height that the model 
does not account for since the panels are located higher than the threshold of the 
correction of 3 meters, as seen in eq. (3.19). This questions the accuracy of the wind 
speed measurements and the actual impact on the measured module temperature. 
The monthly mean wind speed measured at the weather station is compared with 
DMI measurements for Roskilde. And the mean value for the period is between 3,4 
m/s and 3,7 m/s and is resendable compared to the mean of the measured at Risø. 
The accuracy of the temperature measured on the back side of the PV module can be 
questioned, measurements from the back side of 6 other panels in other PV systems, 
on the same roof has been evaluated in [79], results showed large differences in the 
measurements. On top of this the measured temperature are the module 
temperature, and thus not the cell temperature that is compared in this study. 
Therefore, the Sandia cell temperature model that was utilized for correcting the 
measured module temperature to cell temperature, adds another uncertainty to the 
results.

6.3 DC power model 
The DC power output is calculated with the CEC performance model that is an 
implementation of the 5-parameter model with an additional temperature 
adjustment parameter. The specific PV module parameters are collected from the 
CEC library as a CSV file, then the irradiance components in plane of array, and the 
cell temperature and at last the DC power output is calculated for maximum power 
point (MPP).  
Cause of error 
Since the model always finds the MPP the afore mentioned gap between the 
measured GPOA and measured DC output is a source of error. The model is not 
taking mismatch losses due to partial shading into account. The installed PV 
modules are positioned in portrait with the short length towards the top of the roof. 
The PV modules does have three bypass diodes each, and their purpose is to bypass 
the current in case of shading. But the shading from the staggered roof hits the 
panels perpendicular to the bypass diode circuits making them ineffective.   
It has been noticed by checking the implementation of the 6-parameter model in 
Ladybug compared to the standard formulation[43], that the sixth parameter adjust, 
is only used in the temperature and irradiance adjusted light current, but it is not 
included in the intended temperature dependence for the open circuit voltage. This 
is most likely due to simplification and the selected method for identifying the MPP. 
But the implementation follows the same implementation method as SAM and 
therefore the method of validation is validated[4],[77]. 
Since it is seen that the residuals are occurring in the morning and in the evening 
the reflection losses due to the low angle of incidence could cause errors. But since 
this bias occurs in the same period as the shading starts to appear resulting in 
residuals then it is hard to distinguish between the causes of error.  
Validation 
From the clear sky day comparison of the three simulations, it is seen that the 
Annual shading analysis seems not to overestimate in the same manner as the 
Hourly simulations in the middle of the day. This is due to the derate factor that 
takes the annual weighted shading losses into account. On the other hand, it 
overestimates the DC power output at 16:00 where the shading occurs. This is all a 
question of the used input data that is the cell temperature and the GPOA. So, what

6.4 Ladybug as a multi-performance modelling tool 
The current analysis is based on a roof mounted BAPV system, where there’s not 
much shading, and the tilt of the PV modules is only 15°. Therefore, the performance 
and the conclusions might change when analyzing and comparing wall mounted 
BIPV systems.  
The fact that the current implementation of the PV performance model in Ladybug 
does not calculate hourly shading, makes the simulation unreliable when using the 
simulation for sizing the system to the actual hourly energy consumption which is 
analyzed as an important feature in [21]. The current Ladybug simulation with the 
annual weighted shading loss showed quite small residuals during no shading hours, 
but the residuals increased when shading occurred. This observation supports the 
need for an optimized hourly shading model when analyzing BIPV systems.  
The validation of the sub models showed that the implementation of the 
Transposition model overestimates the irradiance, and the literature study showed 
that the precision is very dependent on the orientation of the modules. The further 
away the orientation is from south the larger the error. 
The sub model calculating cell temperature is very sensitive to wind speed changes, 
and the discrepancy of the cell temperature measured in the night and the air 
temperature in the night causes a gap between measured and modelled cell 
temperature half of the clear sky day. The literature summarized in [21], highly 
recommends new models that are created to fit BIPV systems.  
==PAGE:80==
 
 
80 
 
The DC power model calculates the DC power at MPP but excludes mismatch losses 
due to partial shading. Studies [24] have shown that a knowledge-based PV module 
setup could improve the power performance for BIPV facades by 50% compared to 
uninformed design strategies. This supports the recommendation on a desired 
feature that the simulation program optimizes the stringing, panel orientation, with 
regards of optimizing the DC power output but also decreasing the cable lengths and 
complexity. An alternative to this is depending on the specific BIPV design and 
module size, but power optimizers could solve the problem. This is also a future 
security for optimal performance since the urban area could change whit new 
buildings casting shadows on places that a knowledge-based design can’t predict.  
The hourly shading model that is created to optimize the original Ladybug 
simulation, on an hourly basis appears to contain a number of implementation errors 
for diffuse irradiance. However, the main influence caused by the beam irradiance 
seems to estimate the performance correctly. The problem with this simulation is 
caused by the inaccurate transposition model that overestimates the irradiance. This 
could be because of the current measured weather data, but to conclude on this, the 
model should be reevaluated with a longer analyses period. The shading model is 
based on the CAD environments ray intersection method which requires large 
computational time if complex models are analyzed. Other methods for calculating 
the GPOA could therefore be investigated.  
Here Ray tracing with radiosity can be used to identify the GPOA irradiation,[80] 
where ray tracing is tracing all the rays from the analysis point to the light source, 
and radiosity is the diffuse propagation of light starting at the light source. 
Another method is the Ray tracing with Rasterization, here the quality of the 
visualization from the Ray tracing is kept but with the speed of rasterization.  These 
methods are used in INSIGHT for Autodesk Revit.  
To validate a performance model by validating the sub models is a very precise 
method, but it is also very time consuming especially if the simulation tools doesn’t 
allow for feeding the sub models with measured input data, hence the program need 
to be reprogrammed. An alternative to this is proposed by [75] where the parameters 
that has the biggest influence on the residuals is found by Stepwise regression, this 
method has also been drawn out in [81].

7 Future work 
From this review and validation process it is clear that there is an extensive potential 
for further improvements to the existing multi-performance tools that allow for fast 
but precise estimation of the total incident irradiation, cell temperatures and power 
outputs for complex BIPV systems in urban areas. There’s a need for a tool that 
integrates architectural workflows with BIM and the complex multi-disciplinary 
BIPV process. A tool that can be used in all stages of the BIPV process, from the 
initial design phase to commissioning and all steps in between is needed.  
Further developments and analysis for the first design phases could consist of: 
Validation of existing BIM tools that allows BIPV simulations with high accuracy 
using variable spatial and temporal resolution, might be methods like ray tracing or 
if further developments are achieved in validating Raytracing with rasterization, 
then that would be preferred since it is a faster process.  
Rhino/grasshopper with plugins like Ladybug could be the platform for hosting these 
features in its already multi-functional open-source environment. But this would 
require a much cleaner and more intuitive workflow, than the repeatedly component 
workflow that Ladybugs Renewables toolbox offer today.

8 Conclusion 
It can be concluded that Ladybugs PV performance model with CEC module model 
and Annual shading is quite accurate when analyzing DC power output for the whole 
test period of 2.5 months. Results show a small underestimation of only 1% error, 
hence the model can be used for BAPV with a small temporal resolution. Since the 
shading model is based on an annual weighted shading factor the precision decreases 
for BIPV in complex urban settings and cannot be used for optimizations through 
hourly performance metrices. This is reflected in the relatively high RMSE of 44.7 
W and MBE of -19 W.  
The Hourly shading model with an integrated SVF was shown to be slightly more 
inaccurate with an overproduction of 1.85% error within the analysis period. This 
model takes hourly shading and corrects the hourly irradiance components in POA 
and is therefore better for predicting the shading. But it is seen from the Validation 
simulation of the GPOA that it is estimating with high input irradiance DNI and 
DHI. This is reflected in the RMSE of 36 W and MBE of 9.5 W.  
The Validation of the sub models showed that the increased inaccuracy was caused 
by an 6.6% overestimation in the transposition model calculating POA irradiance 
and an 10% overestimation of the Cell temperature while only 1% overestimation in 
the DC power model.  
Deviations between measured and modeled data occur due to partial shading, hence 
the models do not include the specific setup of bypass diodes, strings, and power 
electronics. The CEC performance model utilizes the 6-parameter one-diode 
equivalent model to calculate the IV curve and find the MPP. Therefore, it is possible 
to optimize the model and account for mismatch losses for the actual setup of the PV 
modules and cells. The current performance models account for the effects of shading 
losses linearly with the shaded area, hence it is only suitable for panels with 
individual micro inverters or DC/DC power optimizers. Consequently, there is a 
requirement for new BIPV performance models that optimizes the POA irradiance, 
for example with raytracing and cell temperature models for different BIPV systems.
