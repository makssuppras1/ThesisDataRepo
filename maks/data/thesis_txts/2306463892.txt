SUMMARY
With the recent push to move wind energy further and further oﬀshore, large-scale
Vertical Axis Wind Turbines (VAWTs) are becoming an area of interest for ﬂoating
applications. Their potential to decrease the cost of energy, due to their low center
of gravity and simpler control systems, makes them an attractive option for ﬂoating
applications in deep water. Several projects have been initiated in recent years on
this topic, including the DeepWind Project completed in conjuction with DTU Risø,
and a ﬂoating VAWT project at Sandia National Laboratories.
During the course of these projects, several instabilities have been observed, includ-
ing blade edgewise vibrations and excessive yaw rotations, speciﬁcally in the original
DeepWind work. Further design changes to the rotor have limited the blade insta-
bilities in an onshore turbine, but the eﬀects when the turbine is put on an oﬀshore
spar ﬂoater for both the blade and yaw behavior have not yet been explored.
This thesis aims to study these previously-seen instabilities, and other potential in-
stabilities, in the current ﬂoating model of the two-bladed 5MW Darrius DeepWind
turbine with spar ﬂoater. First, the ﬂoater and mooring system will be rescaled
from the original DeepWind rotor to ﬁt the updated rotor. Second, the simulations
with instabilities from the most recent rotor design will be rerun for the ﬂoating case
to determine the extent of blade instabilities in the current ﬂoating design. Next,
a parametrization analysis will be conducted to see the eﬀect of various design pa-
rameters on the stability of the system.
The results indicate that the instabilities originally seen in the DeepWind ﬂoating
model are not present in the current model. Blade edgewise instabilities are not
seen during normal operation, but are observed when the turbine is parked at cer-
tain rotor angles. The yaw rotation instabilities in the original model are also not
observed in this model.
The conclusion of the work is that the main instability of concern for large Darrius
turbines is the edgewise vibration, which for the current model only occurs when
the turbine is parked. Further work should focus on increasing the accuracy of the
simulation results, through i.e. higher ﬁdelity airfoil data inputs, as well as on the
speciﬁc behavior of the airfoils during stall. This will help determine the extent
of the edgewise vibration issue, since the behavior seen in these results is highly
dependent upon the inputs and simulations tools.

CHAPTER 1
INTRODUCTION
1.1
Background
Wind power has been used for many centuries, dating back to the ancient Egyptians
when Hero of Alexandria powered an organ with a windmill. The Babylonian em-
peror, Hammurabi, used windmills as early as the 17th century BC for irrigation.
The Persians also used vertical axis windmills in the 7th century AD for irrigation,
as well as for milling. The ﬁrst windmills in Europe appeared much later, but by the
end of the 13th century, the familiar ‘European windmill’, with horizontal axis, had
been developed. This was the typical model until around the 18th century, when
more improvements were made. By the end of the 19th century, Europe had more
than 30,000 windmills, which were used for milling grain or pumping water [1].
1.1.1
Vertical Axis Wind Turbines
A vertical axis wind turbine (VAWT) is characterized by rotation about a vertically-
oriented axis, generally the tower [2]. Typically, VAWTs are stall-controlled ma-
chines and are simpler than HAWT equivalents due to their lack of pitch and yaw
control systems, amongst other simpliﬁcations. Several VAWT rotor conﬁgurations
are shown in Figure 1.1. The H Type, Savonius, and Darrieus rotors are explored
in more detail below.
H Type
The H Type rotor, Figure 1.1b, was developed in the UK in the 1970s and 80s [3],
with the largest rotor, a 500kW machine, being built in 1989 by a research team led
by Peter Musgrove [4]. They are generally easy to manufacture due to the straight
and uniform-shaped blades, but they may have higher bending moments than other
VAWTs. They have a better self-starting ability than Darrieus turbines and are
generally eﬃcient with a Cp of around 0.40 - 0.45 among existing models, with the
possibility to reach the same values currently seen by HAWTs [4].

Savonius
The Savonius rotor, Figure 1.2, was invented in 1929 by Finnish engineer S.J. Savo-
nius. It is a drag force turbine, with two cups facing opposite directions. It is a low
eﬃciency machine, with a Cp of around 0.25. This type of rotor can be useful for
low-power applications and is often used in wind speed instruments [3].
Figure 1.2: Savonius Rotor, reproduced from [5]
Darrieus
The Darrieus rotor design was invented by Georges Jean Marie Darrieus in France
in 1925, and in the U.S. in 1931 [2]. It is considered more eﬃcient than the Savonius
rotor, with Cp reaching 0.45 [4]. A typical Darrieus rotor is shown in Figure 1.3.
Sandia research suggests that struts are necessary for structural stability of the
blades, even though they reduce the aerodynamic performance of the turbine [2].
The Darrieus rotor is not self-starting due to inadequate starting torque, but for a
grid-connected turbine, this is generally not a major issue [3].

1.1.2
The Sandia 34m Test Bed
The Sandia 34m Test Bed project from the 1980’s formed the basis for the oﬀshore
ﬂoating VAWT research today at DTU and Sandia. The Sandia 34-m Test Bed
Turbine, Figure 1.4, was a variable speed machine, with a height to diameter ratio
of 1.25, reaching 500 kW at 37.5 rpm and 12.5 m/s wind speed. The test bed was an
onshore Darrieus VAWT with three aluminum blades. The turbine was supported
by 3 sets of guy wires, a common reinforcement method where cables are connected
from the top of the tower to the ground, which can be seen at the top in Figure 1.4
[2]. However, this type of support is challenging to incorporate oﬀshore.
The test bed led to the FloWind commercial concept, a 3 bladed design with a
composite rotor and struts, shown in Figure 1.5. The results of this design showed
that a third blade provides stability and more than compensates for the cost of
an extra blade due to the decrease in costs for the tower, bearings, guy wires,
and foundation. This is because there is less torque variability with three blades
compared to two [2].

It was thought that VAWTs were particularly prone to fatigue, but this was merely
due to the choice of aluminium for the blades. After the test bed was disassembled

slowed until recently when its application for ﬂoating deepwater generation was
considered [2].

1.1.3
Floating Application of VAWTs
In the past decade, VAWTs have been considered for ﬂoating applications. Some of
the advantages over HAWTs include:
• Heavy power generation equipment is located at the base of the rotor or lower,
decreasing the center of gravity and providing easier maintenance.
• No yaw system is required, which simpliﬁes the design, leading to decreased
maintenance and costs. This is especially important for multi-megawatt and
oﬀshore turbines.
• Blades are connected to the tower at both ends so they can have less weight
per length than HAWT blades.
4
==PAGE:27==
1.1. BACKGROUND
• Blades generally have a constant chord and no twist, leading to easier manu-
facturing.
Some potential disadvantages also exist, including:
• Darrieus VAWTs may not self-start so they need a starting system. However,
this can actually be considered as an advantage for the safety system. Plus,
when the turbine is connected to the grid, a starting system is not an issue.
• Struts may be required for structural stability of the blades, which decreases
the aerodynamic eﬃciency.
• According to the Sandia research for onshore VAWTs, guy wires may be re-
quired for the stability of the tower and overall structure. However, guy wires
are challenging to incorporate oﬀshore.
Figure 1.6 summarizes the beneﬁts of VAWTs over HAWTs for ﬂoating applications.
Sandia estimates that ﬂoating VAWTs could have a 20% decrease in cost of energy
over a HAWT of the same capacity [6]. These savings come from a decrease in
component costs, such as the ﬂoating platform due to a lower center of gravity, and
simpler control systems which decrease both capital and operation and maintenance
costs.

The DeepWind Project
DeepWind was a three-year project by various European partners, including the
Risø National Laboratory of the Technical University of Denmark, funded by the
European Unions Seventh Framework Programme for research, technological devel-
opment and demonstration. The project developed a design for a 5MW ﬂoating
VAWT with spar ﬂoater, along with the relevant and necessary design tools [7]. The
concept is shown in Figure 1.7.
Figure 1.7: DeepWind Concept, reproduced from [8]
Basic parameters of the original DeepWind design are listed in Table 1.2. It is a two-
bladed Darrieus design, which allows the rotor to be ﬂoated out to the installation
site. The airfoil used for the DeepWind design is a symmetrical NACA airfoil, with
18% thickness for most of the blade and 25% thickness close to the rotor axis. This
is a common airfoil, chosen for ease of comparison. The blade is made of glass ﬁber-
reinforced plastic, and it is intended that the blade will be manufactured in parts
by pultrusion and joined later [9]. The turbine is supported by a spar ﬂoater, with
buoyancy chambers in the top and stabilization from the heavier, ballasted section
at the bottom. The mooring system is a three-chain catenary system. The design
was tested using a 1 kW model in the Roskilde Fjord [10].

1.3. INSTABILITIES
The updated DeepWind rotor model will be used for this project, and these insta-
bilities will be the basis for the analysis.
1.3
Instabilities
Instabilities in the turbine design “can lead to rapid destructive failure or limit-cycle
oscillations” which cause vibration and fatigue, and therefore must be discovered and
avoided [13]. An instability is self-excited, whereas resonance requires an external
excitation and the frequency of the external excitation corresponds to one of the
natural frequencies of the system. Additionally, resonance occurs at discrete-value
frequencies while instabilities occur over discrete ranges of conditions [13].
There are several ways to eliminate instabilities, including damping, altering the
design, using controls, or ‘zoom-through operation’ where a certain operational range
is avoided [13]. In this study, the primary way to alter the instabilities will be to
alter parameters of the design.
Aeroelastic instabilities are of particular concern for lift-generating structures with
aerodynamic loads. This has been shown to be a potential issue for large HAWT
blades, and it will likely be an issue for VAWT blades as well. For an equivalent
power rating, a Darrieus VAWT blade is much longer and generally more ﬂexible
than a HAWT. Instabilities in VAWT rotors have been investigated by Sandia Na-
tional Laboratories. In a 2014 study, they found that the glass composite Darrieus
designs, such as the DeepWind design to be explored in this work, had the low-
est “aeroelastic stability margins” and they suggested further exploration into the
eﬀects of a ﬂoating platform on the aeroelastic response of VAWTs [14].
1.4
Motivation
Multiple factors are driving wind farm construction further and further oﬀshore;
lack of remaining onshore sites, stronger and steadier wind resource, and less visual
impact among them. There is limited available area in shallow waters for commercial
development, and in the United States, for example, the ocean coasts have much
deeper waters than countries like Denmark which are surrounded by shallow seas.
In waters deeper than 30-50m, ﬂoating platforms are more economical than bottom-
ﬁxed turbines [15]. Vertical axis turbines are believed to be at a possible advantage
over the traditional horizontal axis turbines for ﬂoating applications, due to their
lower center of gravity which decreases the loads on the platform and therefore also
the required size.
Instabilities have been observed in constructed onshore VAWTs and in simulations
of oﬀshore VAWTs in the past which could have an impact on the feasibility of their
wide-scale commercial deployment. One example is the blade edgewise instability,
9
==PAGE:32==

which is still seen in the DeepWind model after increasing the blade stiﬀness [12].
It is unknown if and how the stability of the rotor will change once placed on an
oﬀshore ﬂoating platform.
It is important for engineers to understand the potential instabilities of ﬂoating
VAWTs and ways to mitigate these instabilities, in order for this technology to
be viable. The joint interest of the Wind Energy Departments at the Technical
University of Denmark in Roskilde, Denmark and Sandia National Laboratories in
Albuquerque, New Mexico, USA in the viability of ﬂoating VAWT technology was
the motivation for conducting this study. The results will be of use in the next
phases of their respective ﬂoating VAWT projects.
1.5
Objectives
The thesis aims to achieve the following technical objectives:
1. Convert the current DeepWind onshore rotor model into a ﬂoating model.
This involves resizing the ﬂoating platform and mooring for the updated rotor
design.
2. Repeat the instability analysis from the previous work to see the eﬀect of the
ﬂoating platform. This will be the basis for further analysis.
3. Identify instability behaviors in the model under various conditions or poten-
tial instability behaviors and the design parameters which could aﬀect these
instabilities.
4. Conduct sensitivity analyses by changing these design parameters and observ-
ing their eﬀects on the turbine stability.

2.1
Aeroelastic Code: HAWC2
For the aeroelastic modeling of the turbine, the HAWC2 code, developed by Risø Na-
tional Laboratory, was used. For structural modeling, the code uses a multibody
formulation, where each body consists of Timoshenko beam elements. The intersec-
tions of these bodies are modeled with constraints such as ﬁxed or rotating bearing.
The aerodynamic modeling uses the beam element momentum theory, with addi-
tions for dynamic stall; dynamic, skew, and shear inﬂow; and the eﬀect of large
deﬂections [16].
As part of the original DeepWind project, the Actuator Cylinder (AC) induction
model was incorporated into HAWC2 to more accurately model a vertical axis tur-
bine. The AC model was developed from 1979 to 1982 as a PhD study [17].
Based on the ﬁndings in [12], the Stig Øye dynamic stall method was used for the
majority of these simulations. However, it was deactivated near the blade roots
because lift coeﬃcients above 3 were observed near the roots for high wind speeds,
which is unrealistic. In several simulations, an updated version of the Beddoes-
Leishman dynamic stall model is used instead, which has been updated in HAWC2
speciﬁcally for vertical axis turbines.

2.3
Floater Modiﬁcation
The original DeepWind design included a spar ﬂoater and mooring system, which
were designed for the original rotor design and mass. However, when the rotor was
updated, the mass of the rotor changed and therefore also the overturning moment
on the ﬂoater. Consequently, the ﬂoater must be modiﬁed to compensate for this.
The mass of the blade increased from 48 tons to 52.4 tons and the mass of the tower
increased from 355 tons to 511 tons [12].
The ﬂoater concept was shown in Figure 2.4. From the top, it consists of a 15m
section with diameter D1, followed by a conical section of 10 meters varying from D1
to D2. Then, a middle section of diameter D2 followed by a heavy ballast section at
the bottom with the same diameter. The generator is also attached to the bottom
of the ﬂoater. The diameter values are listed in Table 2.3.
In the design process, the draft was designed to be 108m (including the generator),
with 10m of the ﬂoater above the mean water level [15]. However, in the HAWC2
model, 16m of the ﬂoater is above water level once it reaches equilibrium. Since all
analysis of the original design used this ﬂoater and corresponding water level, this
was kept the same for the redesign of the ﬂoater.
It was initially decided to maintain the ﬂoater geometry, but to change the ballast
of the ﬂoater to maintain the same center of buoyancy. This meant decreasing the
mass of the ballast by the same amount that the mass of the rotor increases. In
order to change the mass of the ﬂoater, the lengths of the ballast and buoyancy
portions were altered. The ballast has a higher density, so decreasing the length of
this section decreases the overall weight of the structure.
However, because the mass of the rotor increased by over 35 percent, the hydro-
static stability was reduced, resulting in unstable platform performance at high
wind speeds. Therefore, a more complete redesign of the ﬂoater was needed in order
to achieve stability of the turbine. This involved increasing the draft and length of
both sections with diameter D2, though the cone section and above did not change.
The diameters were not changed in order to maintain a more similar power loss to
the original model, since the spar ﬂoater rotates in the water under normal opera-
tion. The power loss increases only linearly with length, but by a power of 4 with
the diameter.
In order to redesign the ﬂoater, ﬁrst the maximum pitch displacement is set at 10
degrees, which was the same limit considered in the original ﬂoater design. The same

Implementation in HAWC2
The ﬁnal HAWC2 model used in this analysis is a combination of the initial ﬂoating
DeepWind model and the updated stiﬀer rotor. To create the model, the main ﬁle
and structure from the ﬂoating model was used, but with the updated tower and
blade structures and properties from the new rotor design. Additionally, the ﬂoater
was updated, which required updating the physical and hydrodynamic properties of
the ﬂoater body in the htc ﬁle, as well as the structural data ﬁle. The power loss
from the ﬂoater is also included using a skin friction coeﬃcient, Cf, of 0.0063.
The mooring lines are implemented in HAWC2 through an external dll. The line
length and end points were updated for the new mooring system, and the stiﬀness is
also speciﬁed. The slack line positions are an input, but this ﬁle must be written the
ﬁrst time a new model is used. Before running simulations on the ﬁnal model, the
position of the mooring lines is recalculated for the new ﬂoater draft. The generator
control is also implemented as an external dll.

Hub Height
The selection of a ‘hub height’ for a VAWT is not straight-forward, as the concept
of a ‘hub’ only exists for HAWTs. However, the ‘hub height’ is a necessary input
for aeroelastic simulations, since it is the point for which the average wind speed
is deﬁned. For a HAWT, the hub height is both the point of maximum horizontal
radius and the midpoint of rotor swept area. It is also generally close to the center
of gravity.
For comparison, the hub height is kept the same as from the rotor redesign work.
In this report, the hub height is deﬁned as the point where the rotor swept area
is cut into two equal portions. This is 9m higher than the hub height used in the
original DeepWind report, which was at the ‘equator height’ where the rotor radius
is maximum. This cuts the rotor swept area into two unequal portions, with 40%
of the rotor area being below this point [12]. This also means that the calculation
of the overturning moment on the ﬂoater is higher.

Dynamic Stall Model
For the previous simulations, the Stig Øye (SØ) dynamic stall model was used. To
compare with this model, the same simulations were run for two other cases: with
an updated version of the Beddos-Leishman dynamic stall model and without a
dynamic stall model.
43
==PAGE:66==
CHAPTER 3. PARKED SIMULATIONS
The Beddoes-Leishman (BL) dynamic stall model was originally implemented in
HAWC2 speciﬁcally for horizontal axis wind turbines, which neglected some added
mass terms and was previously validated for small angles of attack. However, vertical
axis wind turbines see large variations in the angle of attack under normal operation
and in addition have large lift and drag contributions due to the torsion rate or
acceleration of the airfoil. In particular, the original model has large lags in the
angle of attack at high wind speeds for VAWTs, which has particular inﬂuence in
the parked case. Therefore, an updated dynamic stall model for VAWT applications
which includes these factors has been added to HAWC2 [27]. When the BL model
was used during the simulations, it was activated along the full length of the blade,
since it was not possible to turn oﬀdynamic stall in speciﬁc portions of the blade
with this version of HAWC2.

Kyaw = Myaw/φyaw
(4.1)

results for the three torque arm lengths investigated are shown in Table 4.7 for yaw

The current design has a new mooring line length, so these values are not the current
yaw stiﬀnesses, however, a similar pattern of increasing yaw stiﬀness with increasing
torque arm length should be expected.

CHAPTER 5
DISCUSSION OF RESULTS
5.1
Blade Edgewise Instability
In the ﬁrst iteration of the DeepWind design, a blade edgewise aeroelastic instability
was observed under many normal operating conditions [11]. After a redesign of the
rotor which increased both the stiﬀness and mass, this was no longer observed.
The IEC standards dictate that a parked turbine must show that it can withstand
50 year winds from any direction [25].
Using the current simulations tools and
blade data, it cannot be proven that the turbine can be subjected to loads from any
direction without edgewise blade vibrations. However, this does not mean that the
turbine cannot be stable in this condition, but it should not be manufactured and
put up before the issue is addressed.
This analysis explored changing several parameters of the design to see their eﬀect.
A summary of these parameters and how they aﬀected the blade edgewise stability
is shown in Table 5.1. A minus sign (−) indicates the parameter made the prob-
lem worse and a plus sign (+) indicates the parameter made the problem better.
Parameters that did not have an eﬀect have an ‘x’ in the insigniﬁcant column.
Overwhelmingly, changing physical parameters of the turbine did not have an eﬀect
on the edgewise stability. The only parameter that had any eﬀect was the torque
arms, but it was still not very signiﬁcant. Additionally, it is probably not the most
eﬃcient way to mitigate this instability.
Generally, changing aspects of the modeling methods are what changed the result
the most. Speciﬁcally, the dynamic stall model and the airfoil data, however, these
things cannot be changed directly in reality. Only the airfoil itself can be changed,
but higher ﬁdelity dynamic stall models and airfoil data by further comparisons to
measurement data could help increase the accuracy of the results of these simula-
tions.

5.2
Other Instabilities
Previous to this work, the instabilities observed were edgewise blade motion and
ﬂoater yaw motion. The edgewise blade instabilities are still observed in model,
as discussed above, however, the ﬂoater yaw motion does not seem to be an issue.
Decreasing the torque arm length does increase the yaw motion, however it does not
produce an instability.
These ﬁndings seems to validate the previous hypotheses about the origins of both
instabilities. The ﬁrst observed instabilities in the blade were observed during nor-
mal operation, and were ﬁxed in the onshore model by making the blades stiﬀer.
This work has shown that this design change works equally well oﬀshore. The more
recent blade instabilities were seen in the onshore model while parked, and were
not mitigated by changing the physical blade properties. This instability still ex-
ists in the oﬀshore model, though the eﬀect is less severe, and is still not aﬀected
by changing most turbine properties. Minor changes occur when the yaw stiﬀness
changes.
In terms of the yaw motion, this was thought to potentially be caused by the con-

5.3. LIMITATIONS OF THE ANALYSIS
troller implementation. This work did not use the original DeepWind controller,
and the yaw instability was not observed. Using the smaller torque arm length of
9m did produce some large yaw motions at higher wind speeds, but this was only
seen with a calm sea state. Additionally, the time series of this motion does not
show the same pattern of response that was seen in the original model. Therefore it
seems likely that the original controller was one of the causes, if not the main cause,
of the yaw instability.
Through the other simulations, no additional instabilities were observed, even when
changing the turbine properties. Moving forward, the blade edgewise instabilities
should be the main focus.
5.3
Limitations of the Analysis
One of the largest limitations of this work is that the current design is not optimized,
but merely suﬃcient.
With the increase in stiﬀness in the new rotor, the mass
increased over 35%.
However, with other methods, such as using stiﬀeners, the
stiﬀness could increase with less of an increase in mass.
Additionally, the ﬂoater design process was very simpliﬁed for this new rotor, and
therefore it may be possible to design a better ﬂoater, both in terms of stability and
cost/mass, with more robust methods. Moreover, since the ﬂoater design depends on
the mass of the rotor, the optimization of the rotor would have a signiﬁcant impact
on the ﬂoater design. This also holds true for the mooring system, since only the
length was changed in the redesign. Other parameters should be considered for a
ﬁnal design.
There are also some limitations in the tools and inputs used. One very signiﬁcant
one is the airfoil data. The speciﬁc lift and drag coeﬃcients of the blade can change
over the length due to changes in the Reynolds number. In this model, the data is
constant for a constant thickness ratio, for a Reynolds number of 8e6 to 10e6. A
more accurate model would consider the Reynolds number variation over the length
of the blade. It is also evident that diﬀerent lift and drag coeﬃcients can be found
for the same airfoil, depending on the methods used to calculate them, so this has
an impact as well. The accuracy of this data is important for the reliability of the
results.
Additionally, HAWC2 is a very comprehensive aerodynamic simulation tool, but it
does have some limitations when used for VAWTs, since its original intended use
was for HAWTs. Changes have been and continue to be made to use it for VAWTs,
where certain aspects such as the dynamic stall model can have a large impact on
the results, particularly in the edgewise blade instabilities.

CHAPTER 6
CONCLUSIONS AND
RECOMMENDATIONS FOR FURTHER
RESEARCH
In this project, the stability of the DeepWind ﬂoating vertical axis turbine was
analyzed.
First, the ﬂoater and mooring systems were resized for the updated,
stiﬀer, and heavier rotor design. Then, the turbine was simulated under the parked
condition with an extreme wind speed of 42.5 m/s, where instabilities were observed
in the onshore model. These instabilities were also observed in the ﬂoating turbine,
though the blade loads were lower than for the onshore. This was determined to
be caused by the airfoil properties which cause negative damping in the blade at
certain angles of vibration and angles of attack and is exacerbated by the changing
lift and drag forces entering stall.
Multiple parameters were altered in order to see their eﬀect on this behavior, in-
cluding the dynamic stall model, the blade stiﬀness, the blade thickness, the airfoil
data, and the ﬂoater draft. One of the most inﬂuential properties altered was the
airfoil data, which signiﬁcantly brought down the blade root moments, but did not
eliminate the edgewise instability all together. The eﬀect of waves and wind speed
were also considered. Although all of these parameters produced some eﬀect on the
edgewise instability, none could signiﬁcantly mitigate the issue.
Next, a parametrization study was conducted on the turbine for various design pa-
rameters, including the stiﬀnesses of the blade and tower and the torque arm lengths.
These changes did not produce any additional instabilities, though decreasing the
torque arm length decreases the yaw stiﬀness and can signiﬁcantly increase the yaw
motions in the generator and mooring. However, they also generally did not signiﬁ-
cantly mitigate the edgewise instability already observed. Increasing the torque arm
length also had some eﬀect on the instability, due to its eﬀect on the yaw motion of
the ﬂoater which is also a contributing factor to the blade angle of attack.
77
==PAGE:100==
CHAPTER 6. CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS FOR FURTHER
RESEARCH
The main ﬁndings of this work include:
• Placing the VAWT on a ﬂoating spar decreases the loads and could therefore
mean even lighter rotors are possible oﬀshore versus onshore (Section 3.4).
• Blade edgewise instabilities occur in the oﬀshore model only while parked, and
the loads are lower than in the onshore model. These aeroelastic instabilities
are most signiﬁcantly aﬀected by the dynamic stall model and airfoil data used
in the simulations (Section 3.12).
• Yaw motion instabilities do not occur in the present model, but excessive yaw
motion could be observed when the torque arms were shortened to 9m during
simulations with wind and no waves. However, when waves are included, the
yaw motion is more similar among the three lengths (Section 4.5).
• The yaw stiﬀness and yaw motion can have an impact on the blade edgewise
stability (Section 4.5).
• Most of the physical parameters analyzed did not have a signiﬁcant impact on
the turbine stability.
• No other instabilities were found besides the blade edgewise vibrations.
Further exploration of the blade instability is necessary in order to determine whether
this is a real physical issue and the actual extent of the issue. This could involve
using 3D CFD simulations, but this would require a highly complex CFD analysis
that includes a moving structure, which may currently be infeasible. However, in a
2D model, the full dynamics cannot be adequately modeled, since it is possible that
the ﬂuid ﬂow and blade response varies over the length of the blade, which could
decrease the overall loads.
Various methods could be explored in future work in order to mitigate or dampen
the edgewise vibrations seen in the parked condition. One such possibility is sinking
the ﬂoater past the blade root, so that the water dampens the motion of the blade.
Sinking of a spar ﬂoater as part of a safety system was explored in [31], where it
was a potential method of braking. There would be a cylinder in the spar that
could be ﬁlled with water in order to sink the ﬂoater to the necessary level. This
type of system could possibly be applied to the parked case, where the ﬂoater would
be sunk when the turbine is parked in high wind speeds. Further simulations are
necessary in order to deduce if this method would suﬃciently mitigate the edgewise
instability.
Further work on the input airfoil data and VAWT simulation tools could also help
with this issue. The vibrations were shown to be quite sensitive to the input data,
so accuracy in this data is important in order to determine the actual magnitude of
this vibration issue. Additionally, simulation tools which allow for multiple sets of
airfoil data with varying Reynolds numbers to be used for diﬀerent portions of the
blade could give a better representation of the real physical response of the VAWT
blades. The results are also sensitive to the type of stall model used and therefore
78
==PAGE:101==
accuracy with this modelling is important. Another possibility is to explore other
airfoils which produce more favorable damping values.
Finally, other platform types and rotor models should be analyzed. Only the spar
ﬂoater was simulated here, which has a much diﬀerent yaw stiﬀness than, for ex-
ample, a semi-submersible platform. This could have a signiﬁcant impact on the
stability. Additionally, newer rotor designs may use even lighter materials such as
carbon ﬁber composites. This could signiﬁcantly decrease the rotor mass and there-
fore the ﬂoater size for the same power output. This may also have a signiﬁcant
impact on the turbine stability.
