ABSTRACT 
 
Offshore wind turbines are today being developed to increase productivity which implies need for 
greater steel foundations. In design of steel foundations, the fatigue performance is the 
determinant factor when the structural integrity of the welded joint is considered.  
Recommended practice states that welding residual stresses should be assumed to be up to yield 
strength. However, this assumption is being debated and it is therefore of interest to investigate the 
residual stresses in order to optimize the fatigue performance of the offshore steel foundation. 
In order to determine the welding residual stresses, computational simulation by means of the finite 
element software Abaqus/CAE is conducted, where a 3D validation model of a submerged arc 
welding is simulated. By use of a plug-in in the software environment, a sequentially coupled 
thermo-mechanical analysis is conducted where the heat input of the weld is simulated by a 
prescribed temperature on the surface approach.  
Experimental measurements is necessary firstly in order to validate the numerical thermal model 
and secondly to define the material properties at elevated temperatures in the mechanical analysis. 
Thermal measurements of a submerged arc welding and a laser hybrid welding are therefore 
performed. Mechanical properties of a S355ML steel specimen is determined by a static and cyclic 
loading at elevated temperatures which is used as input data in the numerical material model.  
An isotropic plasticity model applied in the performed analysis revealed that the tensile residual 
stresses exceeded the yield stress magnitude, determined experimentally. Compared with results 
of a similar analysis with a S235 steel material, the greater yield stress applied in the material 
properties of the S355ML model contributed to a narrower plastic deformation zone close to the 
weld zone.

CHAPTER 1
INTRODUCTION 
 
1.1 
Background 
Today, the worldwide renewable energy demand is increasing rapidly due to the climate problem 
and also because of the limited resources of fossil fuels. Wind energy produces green energy and 
is one of the greater areas of development when it comes to renewable energy. Land based wind 
turbines offer a relatively easy and accessible way to construct and maintain the wind turbines. As 
the development of wind turbines has been enhanced, the possibility of exploiting the better wind 
conditions offshore has been harnessed and also due to the space required for installation is 
becoming less onshore [1].  
To further increase the production rate of renewable energy by use of wind energy and at the same 
time reduce wind turbine owners production price per megawatt hour (MWh), offshore wind 
turbines is gaining in size, which introduce comprehensive design considerations. To install a wind 
turbine offshore, several methods to design the foundation is current, depending on the 
geotechnical soil conditions, water depth, wind, tides, waves, ice conditions, sand waves and bed 
changes current at the installation site. As seen in Figure 1.1, three types of foundations are 
illustrated; steel jacket-, concrete gravity base-, and steel monopile foundation. The latter type of 
foundation is investigated in this thesis and the challenges in design of larger foundations will be 
elaborated in the forthcoming. 
 
Figure 1.1 Offshore wind turbine foundations types, steel jacket foundation (left and second to left), concrete gravity 
base foundation (right and third to the right) and steel monopile foundation (third to the left and second to the right) [2] 
An offshore wind turbine with a steel monopile foundation consists mainly of three parts, the steel 
foundation, a transition piece and the wind tower and turbine (Figure 1.2). Considering the 
production of the steel foundation at Bladt Industries A/S, large steel plates are initially welded 
together in three steps, first with a tack welding by gas metal arc welding (GMAW), followed by a 
V-groove weld from the inside of the monopile and finally a U-groove welding from the inside of the 
monopile, both performed with submerged arc welding (SAW). These welded steel plates would 
subsequently be rolled into a cylindrical can and welded together in the vertical direction of the 
monopile with the same welding procedure as latter described, to finally form one can section of 
the monopile. As the final step of the monopile production, these cans would now be lifted and 
welded together. The welding is performed by a double-sided circumferential butt weld.

The material utilized to produce the monopile corresponds to a high strength steel of quality 
S355ML. In general, use of higher strength steel allows the design of lighter, slenderer and simpler 
structures. However, the increase of yield strength of the steels does not correspond to 
proportional increase of fatigue resistance, which is a major concern of the design [4]. 
During the welding of structures, welding residual stresses (WRS) are introduced to the material, 
caused by the transient heat source of welding, in which plastic deformation arise as the heat 
affected zone (HAZ) expands and subsequently contracts as the material cools down. Residual 
stresses could also be imposed by the preprocessing of the material to be welded.  
Fatigue damage of materials is a very complex process, which is today not fully understood [5]. As 
a result, recommendations given by Eurocode 3, Det Norske Veritas (DNV) and International 
Institute of Welding (IIW) stipulate that residual stresses up to yield strength should be assumed for 
welded structures.  
Offshore steel monopiles are subjected to cyclic loads in terms of rotating wind blades, currents, 
wind and wave loads, which in turn cause large bending moment and lateral loads. These will be 
imposed to the monopile by means of the stress range which can be either compressive, tensile or 
even a combination of both, where the tensile stresses will reduce the fatigue strength of the 
monopile significantly. Due to the assumption of residual stress magnitude up to yield strength, the 
stress ranges would be equally damaging on the monopile structure as the cyclic stress is 
superimposed on the tensile residual stresses. 
This assumption is however being debated as the stress and numbers of cycles (S-N curves) 
proposed in the design curves do not show dependency of the material, which implies significant 
safety margins [4]. Analyses of the residual stress influence on the mean stress dependence have 
in addition shown to be overrated and that the assumption of residual stresses up to yield strength 
is being too conservative [6].

It is therefore of interest to investigate the magnitude of the tensile residual stresses in the welded 
joints of the offshore steel monopiles, with regard to the fatigue performance, as the trend of 
development shows a steel foundation design which is gaining in size.  
By use of computational models and increasing computational power, numerical simulation by the 
finite element method (FEM) is becoming a well implemented tool to predict the WRS. However, 
this method is very complex because it requires experimental data of both the weld procedure and 
material properties in order to precisely predict the stress magnitude.  
Presently, experimental data is needed, firstly in order to calibrate and validate the thermal 
modelling and subsequently to describe the material properties applied in the mechanical analysis 
of the stress-strain response of the material. This need for material parameters is the serious 
limitation today in numerical models and simulations [7][8].  
Constitutive plasticity theory is commonly applied to the numerical analysis to predict the cyclic 
hardening/softening behavior of the material by use of an isotropic hardening model, kinematic 
hardening model or a mixed isotropic-kinematic hardening model. Conflicting arguments regarding 
the choice upon material model and its effect on the numerical accuracy is current. 
Recommendations according to [9] states that a material model with mixed isotropic-kinematic 
hardening rule should be used in analyses of residual stresses in multi-pass welds due to the 
cyclic-thermo-mechanical loading during welding. In addition, it is stated that a cyclic stress-strain 
curve of the material should by applied as a monotonic stress-strain curve may provide non-
conservative fatigue life estimates, especially for high strength steels.  
However, [10] concluded that the isotropic hardening model gave the best overall agreement when 
the aim of study was to investigate which material model which gave the best agreement with 
experimentally measured WRS distribution.

-

CHAPTER 2
FATIGUE MECHANICS AND TESTING 
 
Fatigue is produced by cyclic application of stresses by mechanical or thermal loading. The metal 
subjected to fluctuating stress will fail at stresses much lower than those required to cause fracture 
in a single application of load. The key parameters are the range of stress variation and the 
number of its occurrences. Low-cycle fatigue, usually induced by mechanical and thermal loads, is 
distinguished from high-cycle fatigue, mainly associated with vibration or high number of small 
thermal fluctuations [11]. 
In a specimen subjected to cyclic loads, such as a monopile, crack nucleus preferably in the fragile 
zones of the welded joint due to stress distributions, would initiate the crack growth. The fatigue 
cracks are in a microscopically small scale in this period of fatigue life and would further grow into 
macroscopically cracks and finally to specimen failure in the last cycle of fatigue life. Several 
conditions such as the material surface quality, environmental influence and finally the residual 
stresses, which is elaborated in the forthcoming chapter, affects the fatigue life and fatigue crack 
growth [12]. 
  
2.1 
Phases of fatigue life 
The fatigue life is divided into two separate periods; a crack nucleation period and a crack growth 
period (Figure 2.1). During the crack initiation period the cracks are in a microscopically scale. It 
was investigations in the beginning of the 20th century that first showed that fatigue crack nuclei 
start as invisible micro cracks in slip bands [13]. Further studies and investigations of this 
phenomenon revealed that the formation of these micro cracks appear early in the fatigue life as 
the cyclic stress above the fatigue limit is applied. The movement of the stress cycles cause fine 
slip movements in the order of 1nm and result into the slip bands. It is this back and forth 
movement that eventually would lead to a formation of intrusions and extrusions, which causes the 
creation of a micro crack. Stress cycles will be treated in a separate paragraph later, but for now it 
could be said that the fatigue limit is the cyclic stress level below which a fatigue failure does not 
occur [12]. Once the fatigue damage in the crystalline structure has consolidated into macro 
cracks, the remaining fatigue life is relatively short compared to the total life [5].

In service life, when a metallic member is subjected to cyclic loads, either from mechanical or 
thermal loads, the fatigue cracks usually grow in direction which is macroscopically perpendicular 
to the main principle stress. In this mode, which corresponds to Mode I in Figure 2.2, it is the 
uniaxial tension stress that opens the crack. Oppositely a compressional stress range would not 
affect the crack growth negatively as a tension stress range would. This is discussed further in 
section 2.3. Mode II and III are crack openings nucleated under pure shear loading, in-plane and 
out-of-plane respectively. These modes would however exhibit a transition into a fatigue growth 
governed by the tensile mode [12].

2.2 
Stress concentrations in general and in relation to welds 
As initially stated in this chapter, crack nucleus would preferably occur in the fragile zones of the 
welded joint. Any discontinuity in a member subjected to stress would resolve in a stress 
concentration, namely a local region in the member where the stress level is raised above the 
average. Stress concentrations could be caused by discontinuities, notches or holes, anything that 
would cause the stress flow redistribute. This could be related to a pipe with flowing fluid inside. A 
transition to a smaller pipe dimension would cause higher flow and even higher if the transition is 
rough, which is the same principle in a member induced by stress [16]. The stress concentrations 
is the answer to why fatigue cracks would initiate at all when the applied stress is lower than the 
ultimate strength of the member. As seen in Figure 2.3, the flow of stress is disrupted by change in 
the geometry, which forces the stress lines to gather locally, hence causing increased local stress 
concentrations.

Hitherto, stress concentrations have only been described in general terms of occurrence. In 
relation to welds, the weld shape and discontinuities could cause increased stress concentration 
and higher risk of fatigue failure. Figure 2.4 illustrates the change of shape in a welded plate, and 
hence the concentration of stress. The concentration of stress occurs at the toe weld, which in turn 
is where typical welding defects would also arise, resulting in a highly critical area with regards to 
fatigue failure [16]. Discontinuities such as undercutting in the weld toe give rise to an abrupt 
convex weld profile which then increases the local stress concentration.

2.2.1 
Weld irregularities and misalignments 
Apart from the mentioned weld toe discontinuities, weld flaws and imperfections such as slag 
inclusions, porosity, lack of fusion and incomplete weld root penetration. To control and approve a 
weldment with regard to imperfections, non-destructive control by visual assessment, radiography 
control, ultrasound test or magnetic particle inspection could be implemented. Destructive controls 
exists and relates to testing the weld until failure by tension, bending or as hardness or impact test 
[18].  
Another type of imperfection is misalignments, either axially or by angle as seen in Figure 2.5. 
Misalignments are derived from the production stage where in this instance of the huge tubular 
members are to be fused together. When a joint is misaligned, the eccentricity between the plates 
cause increased stress when it is loaded, as a result of local secondary bending stress. Hence, this 
would reduce the fatigue life [16].

2.3 
Stress cycles 
To cause fatigue failure, the member needs to be affected by three factors; a maximum tensile 
stress of sufficiently high value and a sufficiently large number of cycles of the applied stress with 
large enough variations or fluctuations in the applied stress [14]. A mechanical or thermal load 
cycle could be expressed as in Figure 2.6. One cycle is defined as the distance between two peaks 
in the cycle and is used to determine the fatigue endurance. The peak of the stress cycle is 
determined as σmax, the smallest stress in the cycle is notated as σmin, while the mean stress, σm, is 
the average level of a constant amplitude cyclic loading, and the stress amplitude, σa, is the 
variation about this mean. The overall stress range, Δσ, is twice the stress amplitude and could 
range from compressive to tensile stresses or entirely in one of the ranges. Which range the 
applied stress is in affects the stress ratio R, which is equal to σmax/σmin. When R is <0 the stress 
range is affected by compressive loads and when R is positive or zero, the stress cycle is tensile.

In a weldment, the tensile residual stresses are assumed to be in range up to the yield strength of 
the material [20][21]. Thereby, when the stress cycle of external load is applied, the material will 
experience stresses above the yield strength, in the plastic region at the peak of the cyclic curve. 
When decreasing the stress level, the member would follow an elastically linear line due to work 
hardening, before reaching the minimum point in the stress cycle. The phenomenon of work 
hardening will be treated in the consecutive section.  As the stress range in fatigue loading is 
usually much less than the material yield strength, the stresses experienced in the weld would 
therefore be in the tensile region even if the applied stress cycle is partly or wholly compressive 
[22].  
The stress range, Δσ, has been shown to be the main parameter influencing the fatigue life of 
welded details. This is illustrated in Figure 2.7 where the applied stress is seen to be partly in 
compression and tension. Before these stresses are applied, the residual stresses are initially 
considered to be at the level of yield strength, and thereby, as cyclic stresses are applied, further 
tensile stresses are developed, even though the stress range is in compression [23]. Hence, the 
behavior of a fatigue crack is influenced by the summation of the applied and the residual stress 
range as the residual stresses are assumed to be in the range of yield limit in the 
recommendations given by Eurocode 3, DNV and IIW. Improvement of the residual stress field can 
however be obtained by introducing compressive residual stresses which would be treated in 
section 3.6.

2.3.1 
Low- and high-cycle fatigue 
Usually, when a specimen is undergoing a fatigue limit test, either a low- or high cycle fatigue test 
is conducted. In cyclic strain-controlled fatigue, the strain is held constant during cycling. Since 
plastic deformation is not completely reversible, the stress-strain response during cycling can 
change, largely depending on the initial condition of the material [14]. This change in stress-strain 
condition is caused by either cyclic hardening or softening. However, the material can also remain 
stable. The effect is explained further in the next section, and is applied in the numerical material 
models by either a mixed - kinematic or by an isotropic - hardening model, further elaborated in 
section 5.3.  
High-cycle fatigue (HCF) test comprise a large number of applied cycles, N > 105 and an elastically 
applied stress. The amount of cycles for a HCF-test is 107 cycles and sometimes 5 X 108 cycles. In 
general, low cycle fatigue is related to conditions characterized by high amplitude load (stress, 
strain or strain rate) and shorter lifetimes. The amount of cycles, N, for a low cycle fatigue limit test 
are approximately 10 000. An important distinction between the high- and low-cycle fatigue test is 
that in a high cycle test, most of the lifetime is spent in the crack initiation period, while for a low-
cycle test most of the lifetime is spent in the crack propagation period [11]. 
  
2.4 
The Bauschinger effect 
During a simple cyclic tension and compression test, the development of true stress is plotted 
against the natural strains. True stress, defined as the load divided by the current cross-sectional 
area of the specimen, could substantially deviate from the engineering or nominal stress, based on 
the initial cross-section of the specimen without taking into the effects of necking of the specimen. 
More about measuring of stresses and strains is elaborated subsequently in the part of the 
performed static and cyclic loading tests (Chapter 6).  
Plotted against the strain, either the true or engineering stress will exhibit an elastic part in the 
regime of a steel stress-strain curve. Releasing the stress back to the initial zero state will also 
remove the deformation from the applied force in this regime. Further tensioning will initiate the 
plastic deformation, also called the elastic limit or yield strength, σs (see Figure 2.8), represented 
by a short straight line where the dislocations in the metal start to move freely until they reach the 
grain boundary. 
From the elastic limit, the slope of the stress-strain curve would steadily decrease with increasing 
stress. The material is said to be in the plastic regime due to the permanent deformations of the 
material if the loading is to be released. If released in the plastic zone, the strains and deformation 
would decrease and the curve would follow the same linear elastic line down to the initial load 
state, but with permanent deformed shape. Loaded once again in the same direction, the material 
would exhibit the force, but without further deformation. This phenomenon is also known as work 
hardening and is a topic which would also be elaborated further in the report in relation to material 
modeling (section 5.3).

Consider the specimen now being in a stress free state, and then again applied a force but in the 
reversed direction, a new yield point, σ´s, significantly smaller than the one explained in the latter 
paragraph would be present. This effect was found and named after Bauschinger in 1886 and is an 
important knowledge of the mechanisms materials exhibits under cyclic loading.  
 
Figure 2.8 Bauschinger effect illustrated in a stress-strain diagram, where, σs, is the yield strength at loading in tension 
and, σ´s, is the yield strength at reversed loading [24]. 
The Bauschinger effect is important both in terms of thermal cyclic loads due to welding, but also in 
service life when the monopile is affected by thermal and mechanical cyclic alternating loads with 
high amplitude of the load [25]. The origins of the Bauschinger effects are related to the changes in 
dislocation substructure by the mentioned reversed cyclic loadings and the corresponding changes 
in the internal stress systems [26]. When the metal is work hardened, i.e. when the stress at which 
plastic deformation occurs is increased, the density of dislocation is high, thereby allowing for a 
stable rearrangement of dislocations at cyclic strain. If the density of dislocation initially was low, 
cyclically loading the material would cause an increase in the dislocation density and thereby 
increasing the elastic stress and strain. Various metals exhibit different effects of softening and 
hardening. In general, high-strength metals tend to cyclically soften, and low-strength metals tend 
to cyclically harden. After a while during a cyclic loading, the hysteresis loop tends to stabilize,

3.1 
Definition and classification of residual stresses 
Stresses arising during the welding process are referred to as internal or locked-in stresses [27]. 
No external forces are applied and thereby the internal stresses are in equilibrium with itself.  
Classifying of the welding stresses could be determined based on their characteristics such as; 
lifetime, direction and origin of. Welding stresses could be either temporary or residual, when 
considering the period of appearance. As will be elaborated further in this chapter, the stresses 
could be distinguished directionally in longitudinal and transverse residual stresses. A distinction 
between the internal stresses could be made based on their origins [25];  
- 
Thermal stress (caused by non-uniform temperature distribution) 
- 
Stresses caused by the plastic deformation of the metal 
- 
Stresses caused by phase transformations 
The transient heat source from the welding causes a thermal stress field which dissolve after 
cooling and temperature is stabilized. When the filler material and the HAZ are heated it will 
expand. Thereby if reaching a sufficient gradient of temperature, plastic deformations would occur 
and thereby cause stresses. Phase transformations due to the elevated temperature evoke volume 
change of the material without significant change in the stress development process. 
Internal stresses are also subdivided into different orders depending on their size and range [28]. 
As seen in Figure 3.1 the crystallite structure of a material is modelled in an x-y axis system, were 
the stresses are acting in the y-direction. First order residual stresses (<1mm in size), σI, extend 
over macroscopic areas and is the averaged stress over a volume with several material grains. 
The second order residual stresses (1 – 0.01mm), σII, are averaged within each grain or crystallites 
and acts between adjacent grains. Third order residual stresses, σIII, are the smallest in terms of

size (10-2 - 10-6 mm) and act between the atomic areas, and is a deviation from the averaged σII, 
caused by different impurities of the atomic lattice.

3.2 
Causes of welding residual stresses and deformations 
During a process of welding the weld area is heated up significantly compared to the surrounding 
area and fused locally. This causes an unbalanced temperature distribution in the specimen, by 
which the heat affected material expands volumetrically. The cooled material restrains the heated 
material to expand further, and during cooling the restraints by means of the fused material 
produces residual stresses and deformations [28]. 
Residual stresses and deformations do not occur if a material is heated uniformly and could be 
cooled down without any restraints. Consider a simple rectangular two plated system with same 
lengths and fixed restrains in the short ends (Figure 3.2). A narrower plate is placed between them 
which would represent the weld filler material. The middle plate is then heated to a temperature 
Tmax, while the neighboring plates are kept at a constant temperature, T0. The middle plate would 
then try to expand while the limiting plates would not allow for this expansion, causing compressive 
stresses in it. If the material was only compressed elastically, the stress and deformation level 
would decrease to its initial state after cooling. On the other hand, if the expansion caused by the 
heated filler material produces plastically compressive stresses, then a plastic compressive strain 
is introduced in the central plate. Thus, during cooling the plates on the sides (representing the 
area not affected by the welding heat) would endeavor to keep its original length while the central 
part (representing the heat affected area during welding) would contract. Consequently, tensile 
stresses would occur in the central area, leaving compressive stresses in the side areas to 
establish equilibrium.

3.5 
Deformations 
The welding process as described in the latter sections, initiate the rise of residual stresses as the 
restraining of weld increase the stress. Highly restraint welds would yield an increased stress and 
oppositely a weld where the deformation is unrestrained, a low stress field is current. A 
compromise between the deformation and stress is needed to preserve the intended geometry 
while still preventing increase of residual stresses.  
As the increased temperature field moves in the direction of the welding arc and the subsequent 
cooling phase introduce tensional stresses in the HAZ, the welded specimen would deform as the 
stress level increase. This deformation takes form depending on geometrical factors and restrained 
or boundary condition. The deformation is caused by plastic shrinkage that is induced as the 
thermal expansion and shrinkage develops in the welded zone during the thermal processes [34].

heating. This contraction also leads to longitudinal shrinkage if the ends are unrestrained. In a thin 
butt welded plate, angular shrinkage results as the sides are unrestrained. In the case of plate with 
restrained sides, the shrinkage would initiate a bending distortion. The latter mentioned 
geometrical factors are prevailing for thinner plates and distortion may take form as buckling or 
rotational distortion [35].

3.6 
Stress relief 
As mentioned in section 2.2 stress concentrations are causing build-up of tensile residual stresses, 
especially in the region of the weld toe. In the current section, these residual stresses have been 
further elaborated upon, and it could be seen that compressive residual stresses would act as 
beneficial on the structural integrity. Thereby, two kinds of measures are introduced in order to 
either demote stress concentrations in the weld toe or promote compressive residual stresses by 
modifying the stress distribution. These are rendered in the following list: 
- 
Weld toe grinding 
- 
Weld toe remelting 
- 
Shot-, hammer-, needle or ultrasonic- peening 
- 
Stress relief annealing; low temperature stress relief 
By weld toe grinding the weld joint is improved by reducing joint stress concentrations and 
secondly weld toe intrusions are removed. Recommendations according to [37] and [21] differ in 
detail about the extent of improvement by grinding, but a rough estimate is to assume that the 
allowable stress range could be increased by approximately 30% [22]. However, as the monopile 
in the current thesis is exposed to a corrosive environment during service life, surface attack and 
pitting would counteract the beneficial effects by an increased stress concentration.  
Weld toe remelting intends to create small localized remelted regions around the weld toe and 
thereby remove weld toe intrusions and reduce stress concentrations. Usually tungsten inert gas 
welding (TIG) and plasma welding are used for this purpose with no filler material added and with 
a low heat input to reduce plastic stress by the thermal gradient field.  
Local plastic deformation in the vicinity of the weld toe can be introduced in order to modify the 
residual stresses such that compressive- instead of tensile stresses are present. In a fatigue point 
of view, crack growth is only present under tensile stress. Consequently, compressive stresses 
would be less damaging and hence a fatigue improvement is current. This topic is further 
elaborated in the subsequent section. The techniques of introducing local plastic deformation are 
applied with a hammer-, shot-, ultrasonic- or needle peening. With hammer peening a tip with 
radius of 3-9mm is used to pneumatically hammer the weld toe with a peening depth of 0,5mm. 
The variations between the techniques are generally the tip radius and speed of peening, which 
result in different preciseness and effect.  
Another way of relief the residual stresses subsequently after welding is by post-weld heat 
treatment (PWHT). In this approach the material is heated to a temperature of 550-625°C for 
approximately an hour per inch of thickness [22]. PWHT would in addition to relief the residual 
stress range, minimize the potential for hydrogen induced cracking as the metallurgical and 
mechanical properties lost during the thermal cycles of weld heat is restored [38]. According to 
recommended practice codes, a stress relief of 20-40% of the yield strength is a good estimate 
when PWHT is induced. However, it should be noted that for thin structures PWHT cannot be 
conducted due to permanently deformation would occur and for larger structures, such as the 
monopile, geometrical considerations is limiting as there would not be ovens large enough to fit the 
structure.

Many studies have investigated the benefits of stress relief, with intentions to improve the design of 
structure and it has been found that complete stress relief can rarely be achieved. In addition, 
influence of the service loading has been found to be highly variable, depending on the cyclic 
stress curve of the material, the magnitude of the applied loads and the number of load cycles 
experienced [39][40]. 
  
3.7 
Influence of welding residual stresses on fatigue strength 
As latter described in section 2.3, the thermal and mechanical load cycle induced on a structure is 
specified by stress ranges and mean stresses. Due to plasticity induced crack closure, 
compressional residual stresses could be beneficial in terms of fatigue life. When the zone of 
compressive residual stresses is induced by cyclic loading, the material is undergoing elastic and 
plastic deformation during unloading, while during the next cycle of loading the crack tip would not 
open unless the applied load is enough to overcome the residual compressive stress. 
On the other hand, tensile residual stresses have a very significant effect on fatigue [40]. As the 
tensile residual stresses in design of a structure is assumed to be up to yield strength [20][21][37], 
applying cyclic stresses will superimpose the residual stresses and the effective stresses acting on 
the welded joint will fluctuate from yield stress and downward as seen in Figure 3.9.  This means 
the effect of mean tensile stress is equally damaging for each type of loading, either compressive 
or tensile.

However, the influence of residual stresses on the fatigue life of a material is currently debated as 
studies have shown residual stresses lower than yield strength due to re-distribution and/or 
relaxation of the stresses during service life [40][41].

To investigate the magnitude of internal stresses and deformational responses due to the process 
of welding, a decoupling and analysis of the procedure is of highly importance. As the materials are 
fused together in the welding process, transient heat initiated by the heat source causes an 
increased stress field internally in the material as described in the latter chapter. The accuracy of 
the prediction by computational welding mechanics (CWM) thereby relies on the precision of 
thermal, physical, mechanical and metallurgical properties used by the models. Numerical 
modelling and analysis has been the main link between theoretical mathematics and the desire to 
solve scientific and technological inquiries for several years, whereas the FEM is amongst the 
methods. This method will be applied in the current models through the software Abaqus/CAE.  
This chapter intends to explain the CWM procedure and subsequently the chosen sequentially 
coupled thermal-mechanical analysis that takes place by solving the numerical problem and to 
present the material parameters utilized in the numerical model. Methods of solving and model the 
numerical model are also presented and their influence and use is discussed. 
 
4.1 
Computational weld mechanics 
Simulation of the welding process demands understanding of the influence of the different stages 
in the multi-physical problem and the interactions between the couplings. Welding is a physical 
phenomenon that could be described by different coupled field equations that overlap or have 
common boundary (Figure 4.1). As seen, the welding process involves many phenomena that are 
strongly coupled e.g. non-linear temperature dependent material behavior, 3D nature of welding 
process, microstructural evolution, fluid dynamics etc.  
The objectives of a computational weld model are numerous, ranging from strength of weld, 
defects, fatigue and corrosion properties or the purpose can be development of welding processes 
or procedures. Overall, several dependencies in the multi-physical problem could be accounted for 
when modeling a weldment, but depending on the objective of the analysis, assumptions and 
simplifications should be made. This is also due to the limiting factor of computational cost which 
plays a high role when it comes to numerical modeling. 
Many authors have reviewed these coupled phenomenon [42][35], with the effort in optimization of 
mathematical solutions to scientific issues in the areas of heat and fluid flow in welds, heat source-
metal interaction, solidification microstructure and phase transformation.  
A weld could be investigated on a macroscopic or a microscopic level depending on the desired 
output. At the macroscopic level a weld can be considered to be a thermo-mechanical problem of

4.2 
Sequentially coupled thermal-mechanical analysis 
The chosen approach to the numerical problem is defined by a thermal analysis followed by a 
mechanical analysis. In this approach, the thermal model is run first as a pure heat transfer 
analysis. The thermal model typically takes into account the latent heat due to metallurgical phase 
transformation especially the latent heat of melting [43]. The accuracy of the predicted residual 
stresses and distortions in the mechanical analysis is highly dependent on the accuracy of the 
thermal analysis. The main aim of the thermal analysis is to obtain the transient temperature 
distribution due to the localized moving heat source. The heat source could be modelled by several 
methods which will be further described in section 4.5. Nevertheless, the main parameters for 
modeling of the heat input are; thermal conductivity, heat capacity and density (Figure 4.3). These 
will be elaborated and defined in section 4.4.1.

implicit or explicit integration schemes and the chosen approach is discussed in the following. 
4.3.1 
Explicit or implicit analysis 
In Abaqus/CAE, distinction should be made on Abaqus/Standard and Abaqus/Explicit, which 
solves the numerical problem implicit and explicit respectively. In Abaqus/Standard one can 
choose between full and reduced integration for quadrilateral and hexahedral (brick) elements. In 
Abaqus/Explicit one can choose between full and reduced integration for hexahedral (brick) only. 
However, reduced integration is available for quadrilateral first-order elements. Both the 
formulation and type of integration can have a significant effect on the accuracy of solid elements 
and reference is made to section 4.6 and 4.7 for the discussion about the chosen element and 
formulation [46]. 
Choosing between the implicit and explicit analysis approach should be based on the expected 
nature of the problem, as in this case would be a smooth nonlinear problem. Thus, an implicit 
analysis would be the preferred choice as an explicit analysis is the clear choice for a wave 
propagation analysis. However, there are certain static or quasi-static problems that could be 
solved with either analysis. Solving these problems with an implicit analysis would converge 
perfectly but difficulties could be experienced by solving it for an explicit analysis due to contact or 
material complexities resulting in a large number of iterations, which would cause convergence 
issues [46]. Furthermore, when utilizing implicit analysis, the discrete time steps are employed to 
calculate the solution iteratively.

4.4.1.1

The specific heat of a substance is defined as the amount of heat required to increase the 
temperature of a unit mass by one degree [J/kg/°C] or in other words how well the substance 
stores heat.  
When a material changes phase, the change in internal energy can be significant. The amount of 
energy liberated or absorbed can be defined by specifying a latent heat [J/kg] for each phase 
change a material undergoes. Latent heat arises from the work required to overcome the forces 
that hold together atoms or molecules in a material i.e. when the material change from solid to 
liquid during the melting phase, it loses energy without change in temperature. This effect is 
captured as seen in Figure 4.4 in the range of melting temperature (650-850°C) where an

4.4.2.3 
Modulus of elasticity, yield strength and plastic stress-strain curves 
When a material undergo non-reversible changes of shape and structure due to the response of 
loading, the properties describing the evolution of phases in this event could be defined as 
modulus of elasticity, yield strength and plasticity, respectively from start to end. Before the 
material reach the yield limit, it could be defined to behave elastically, i.e. the material could be 
deformed and then relaxed to a zero stress state, described according to the elasticity modulus. 
The elasticity modulus is the range of rise at which this slope of stress-strain curve behaves, where 
the stress is proportional to the strain according to Hooke’s law. After the material pass the elastic 
region, a stress level describing this interception is defined as the yield strength or yield point. 
Once the material is loaded beyond the yield point, it could be defined as behaving plastically. 
Several models of describing the plastic behavior of materials are current to approximate the 
solution with regards to the phenomenon of work hardening. This was initially elaborated in section 
2.4 where the Bauschinger effect was described and in section 5.3, further analyses and 
formulation of the material models considered in this thesis is described. 
Since the behavior of steel is highly dependent on the temperature gradient during loading, tests at 
elevated temperatures in different ranges is necessary to describe the softening of the material as 
the temperature increase. Fairly little literature describing the material properties of steel grade 
S355ML at elevated temperatures is present, and one of the main objectives in this thesis is 
therefore to define these properties by means of experimental investigations. This experimental 
testing is described in Chapter 6, where also the results and definitions of elastic modulus, yield 
strength and plastic stress-strain curves are found.  
 
4.5 
Heat source modeling 
As discussed in section 4.2, the concept of modeling a weld procedure concerns with simplification 
of the thermal and mechanical material behavior and also modelling of addition of filler material. 
Due to the heat from the heat source, the increased temperature field melts the base metal and 
thereby creating a weld pool. As mentioned, addition of filler material from an electrode is led into 
the weld pool. This liquefied weld pool moves along the weld joint and after the transient heat has 
decreased it leaves a weld pass of solidified material. It is this transient temperature field that has 
to be computed by useful accuracy in order to predict and analyze the behavior of material, which 
as latter described causes phase transformations, thermal strain and stress, distortion and residual 
stress. Primarily, the transient temperature field outside of the weld pool depends on the 
distribution of energy from the heat source, as well as the conduction of heat away from the weld 
pool by conduction in the solid [36]. 
Due to the often complex physics of a weld heat source, thermal models of weld heat sources have 
been established to handle the complexity by reducing the models into more manageable levels. 
Hence, these reduced models ignore most of the physics of the welding process, while still being 
solved remarkably accurate if the input data are precise. Then the heat and energy equation is 
solved according to an approximate solution [35]. 
Modeling of a heat source could be done on basis of experimentally obtained data of the welding 
process which is used to validate and calibrate the numerical model. Experimentally, currents,

measured by use of video cameras, some with laser illumination to visualize the droplet transfer

4.5.2.1 
Inactive element approach 
The first approach is very similar to the way the thermal analysis is conducted. All beads are 
removed initially and then activated as the weldment is laid. As the beads are inserted, the torch is 
activated on the laid down bead and moved forward in the same speed. The approach requires 
restricting of data each time the model is extended by inserting a bead.  
4.5.2.2 
Quiet element approach 
In the second approach for modelling a multipass welding, the method is to include the welds or 
beads that have not been laid in the computational model. These elements are made passive by 
giving them material properties so that they do not affect the rest of the model in the deformation 
analysis. They are assigned a low stiffness and heat conductivity by having nodal temperatures 
near the melting temperature as specified by the initial conditions [8].  
The elements and respective nodes are given soft material properties at the start of the weld pass 
were the weld chunk is being deposited. As the torch and additional filler material being laid moves 
forward, the consecutive bead is being removed with soft material properties and then reinserted 
with normal properties, which allows for the beads to move with the deformation in the weld zone 
while not affecting the overall response until they are “active” or deposited [51]. 
4.5.2.3 
Comparison of element approaches 
The two different approaches, inactive and quiet element approach for modelling the addition of 
filler material give very similar results [50]. Even though, for the quiet element approach, an 
appropriate choice of material properties for the passive elements has to be found by trial and 
error.  This has to be conducted in order to tune the stiffness small enough so that this region does 
not perform any loading on the surrounding material, but large enough so that the resulting global 
stiffness matrix is well conditioned. This issue is not relevant for the inactive element approach. 
However, in the inactive element approach, re-computation of the matrix profile when adding filler 
material takes additional computational time. This is especially inconvenient in a three-dimensional 
model where the additional filler material is added in every time step [36]. Nevertheless, the quiet 
element approach will be chosen in the numerical modeling in this thesis.

In FEA several types of elements used to divide the geometry into discrete parts to be analyzed is 
present. The division of geometry is defined as meshing, and the refinement and division of the 
mesh will result in different accuracy levels. To verify that the results from the analyses are 
sufficiently accurate, a mesh sensitivity study is necessary. A convergence study is conducted in 
order to make sure the results are adequately similar regardless of the element size. This is 
implemented by rerunning the analysis with different mesh refinement in the weld toe and nearby 
area (HAZ), which is the part of main interest [9]. 
Recommendations according to [9][20][52] outline a uniform recommended practice for selection of 
element type depending on the desired analysis approach. However, a standard practice is still not 
current, and therefore, the chosen element type will be based upon the mentioned recommended 
practices together with experienced conclusions found from literature investigating the same 
objectives as this thesis [50][53][54][55]. 
In Figure 4.10 element types utilized in Abaqus/CAE is shown in its simplest form of one-
dimensional to three-dimensional elements. As described in section 3.4, tensile residual stresses 
are almost always uniform in the middle part of a long welded joint except near both ends. 
Thereby, a two-dimensional analysis could be of interest if only the residual stresses at the 
transverse section in the middle part are of concern [34]. Since this is not the case, a three-
dimensional analysis were hexahedral, also known as brick elements are preferred because they 
tend to be more accurate and easier to use and interpret compared to tetrahedral elements [35]. A 
more profound discussion about the chosen element type will be elaborated in the following 
section.

the weld is important [8]. A more accurate result will be found by a finer mesh, but the finer the 
mesh, the more time demanding will the model be. Thereby, the mesh density needs to be 
implemented with a compromise with regards to the computational time.

4.6.1 
Shell and solid elements 
In light of the latter section, a continuation upon the choice of element type in terms of shell or/and 
solid elements (Figure 4.11) will be found in the consecutive part.  
The shell element is a two-dimensional element generated to model the in-plane to curved 
surfaces. Temperature and stress gradients in the through thickness direction is not included while 
a linear distribution is assumed, and the shell elements has thereby half the number of degrees of 
freedom, which reduces the computational costs. The heat or stress pattern in shell element flows 
in the tangent plane of the element. By this, larger elements could be modelled compared to three-
dimensional models with continuum, also known as solid elements [35]. 
Solid elements could be used to correctly model the geometry and will thereby introduce a large 
number of nodes and elements in the numerical model. This will subsequently increase the 
computational time. Furthermore, since most solid element types only have three degrees of 
freedom at each node, they tend to be stiffer. Hence a denser mesh could then be needed 
compared to what shell elements require [57]. 
Both shell and solid elements could be used to model plates of constant thickness, and shell 
elements are even suitable to model thick plates and still obtain a good representation [57]. 
However, in the weld and the HAZ shell elements turn out to be insufficient since the through 
thickness heat and stress gradients are steep in these areas [35]. Additionally, when the weld 
needs to be included in the model, use of three-dimensional elements yield a more reliable result. 
8-noded elements are recommended in the regions of high heat and stress gradients, with use of 
at least four elements in the thickness directions [21]. 
As mentioned previously, the choice of element type should be based on a compromise between 
accuracy and computational cost. In order to reduce the computational time, two approaches are 
present. A study were a combination of 8-noded solid elements near the weld and 4-noded shell 
elements applied in the rest of the model showed reasonably equal results compared to a model 
consisting entirely of solid elements [58]. The second approach is to reduce the amount of element 
and nodes in the model by applying at least four elements in the thickness direction in the weld and 
HAZ as recommended, and reduce amount of elements in the thickness direction elsewhere. The 
latter approach will be utilized in this thesis as preliminary design of the model took reasonably 
computational time. The 8-noded element has only nodes in its corners and uses linear 
interpolation in each direction and is thereby called linear elements or first-order elements.

4.7 
Reduced or fully integration formulation 
The before mentioned elements can be integrated by use of full or reduced integration. Selection of 
either one of the two integration formulations has crucial influence on the numerical accuracy as 
the material behavior will be altered depending on the choice and will be described in the 
subsequent paragraph. Abaqus uses numerical methods to integrate diverse amounts over the 
volume of each element. The finite element (FE) software calculates the material response at each 
integration point in each element [46]. 
When defining the formulation as fully, the term refers to the number of Gauss points required to 
integrate the polynomial terms in the elements stiffness matrix at the integration step were the 
element has a regular shape. At each node, the fully integrated linear element uses two integration 
points in each direction.  
Reduced integration element has the advantage that it use one less integration point in each 
direction compared to the fully integration. Thereby, the computational time needed to analyze the 
model will be significantly reduced.  
Each formulation has its own weakness to numerical phenomenon that could depreciate the 
accuracy of the numerical results. For example, linear reduced integration elements tend to be too 
flexible which causes a numerical problem called hourglassing. Linear elements using full 
integration are prone to the numerical problem named shear locking and should not be used for 
simulations involving very large mesh distortions [46][9]. 
From these descriptions and recommendations a first-order (linear) reduced integration element 
will be utilized in the numerical analysis. To prevent the phenomenon hourglassing, a fine mesh is 
applied in the regions where the distortion levels are high, i.e. in the weld and HAZ. Abaqus has 
also the ability to monitor this effect by an hourglass control. This hourglass control and the 
hourglassing and shear locking phenomena are further described in the following section.

5.3 
Material modelling 
One of the major problems in welding simulation is the material modelling which raises 
uncertainties of the material properties at elevated temperatures. The net heat input is also a 
source to a problem, but the thermal analysis is more straightforward and easier to calibrate and 
verify compared to the mechanical modelling and analysis. Exception is the large latent heat during 
the solid-liquid transition, also known as the phase changes which is implemented and taken into 
account in the thermal properties (section 4.4.1) [35][59]. According to [60] selecting the 
appropriate material model is the most crucial part of finite FE modeling in order to accomplish 
valid results, since implementation of inaccurate material data or wrong material model would 
counteract the validation of results.  
The effects of work hardening and softening has a profound influence on multi-pass welds, since 
the material in the HAZ undergo multiple thermo-mechanical loading cycles during the welding 
process [61]. Several models have been made in order to accurately predict the effects of the 
thermo-elastic-plastic behavior of steel during static or cyclic loading. The most commonly 
employed constitutive plasticity theories used in CWM are isotropic plasticity theory, kinematic 
plasticity theory and mixed (isotropic-kinematic) plasticity theory [62]. These theories will be treated 
in the following as well as the rate dependent model and annealing temperature function in 
Abaqus/CAE.  
Recommendations according to [9] states that a material model with mixed isotropic-kinematic 
hardening rule should be used in analyses of residual stresses in multi-pass welds due to the 
cyclic-thermo-mechanical loading during welding. Also it is required that the cyclic stress-strain 
curve of the material is applied because a use of monotonic stress-strain curve may provide non-
conservative fatigue life estimates, especially for high strength steels. This is supported by 
[61][62][63]  
However, [10] conducted a study to determine which material hardening model gave the best 
agreement with experimentally measured weld residual stress distribution and concluded that the 
isotropic hardening model gave the best overall agreement. The choice of hardening model 
influences the residual stress greatly in the weld material, i.e. in the HAZ, but further away each 
model has been found to yield similar stress gradients [48].

5.3.1 
Microstructural behavior 
Several factors can affect the microstructure of a metal, ranging from the initial metallurgy of the 
weld and base material and its influence on each other, the cooling rate of the material, to the 
thermal cycle of the heat input of welding, which is the dominant factor. The cooling rate is 
dependent on factors including plate thickness, welding conditions, preheat, length of weld and 
joint geometry. The thermal cycles cause grain growth microstructural changes in the HAZ, where 
the HAZ ranges from the solid-liquid transition zone on its inner surface to the unaffected base 
metal on its outer surface. Peak temperatures and the heating and cooling rate govern the phase 
changes and microstructures [48]. 
The microstructure of the material changes during heating from ferrite and pearlite to austenite and 
during cooling from austenite to ferrite, pearlite, bainite and/or martensite depending on the heat 
input and cooling rate. The microstructural changes exert a strong influence on the final material 
properties such as the toughness, wear resistance and hardness. The hardness could be improved 
by adding alloying elements to steels such that the time required for decomposition into ferrite and 
pearlite is delayed, thereby causing slower cooling rates in which would promote a martensitic 
structure. Hence, the chemical composition of the material influences the material properties 
through the microstructure formations. As well as the latter stated connection, the integrity of the 
weld and the behavior of the material on the macroscopic scale are largely dependent on the 
microstructure [47]. 
In case of large austenite grain size present in the weld, as well as transformation products such 
as martensite and bainite, the base metal would be responsive to brittle fracture. These hard and 
brittle products can also lead to increased stress and corrosion in the HAZ and make the weld 
susceptible to cold and hot cracking. Measures to prevent these phenomena can be initiated by 
production processes such as cooling- and heating- control and pre- and post- heating [35]. 
In this thesis, the microstructure of the weld is not of main concern and is therefore omitted. 
However, the volume change due to phase changes will be included in the property of thermal 
expansion, as described in section 4.4.2, as well as in the annealing function which would be 
elaborated further in section 5.3.6. Concerning the volume changes for the solid-liquid phase 
changes, accurate models usually include this, but as far as this objective and scope goes, they 
could be ignored [48].

5.3.5 
Limitations of the hardening models  
The nonlinear mixed hardening model can provide accurate results but has still some limitations 
regarding cyclic behavior. Experimental observations show that the isotropic hardening component 
varies depending on the strain range magnitude [66][67]. Additionally, if the specimen is cycled at 
different strain rates, deformation in the first cycle would affect the isotropic hardening in the 
second cycle. Thus, the model would only be a coarse approximation of the actual cyclic behavior 
[11]. Non-proportional and proportional load cycles would be predicted with the same cyclic 
hardening behavior even though physical observations show that the behavior could deviate. 
Despite for these limitations, the model would be based upon the strain range recommended by 
[66] which is elaborated further in the section of experimental testing (Chapter 6). 
A mixed hardening model should also be calibrated for a specific strain range because different 
strain ranges in cyclic tests result in change of the material parameters. In a multipass weld it is 
unlikely that the strain rate would remain constant during welding of weld pass, and this strain 
history has not been well established. When conducting the cyclic tests to find the material 
properties to be assessed in the mixed hardening model, it is not clear at which strain rate the 
parameters should be based upon [10].

5.3.6 
Rate independent modelling and annealing temperature 
Plasticity models used for calculation in Abaqus are defined as rate independent models. Thus, the 
latter discussed influence of strain rates would be omitted which is also sufficient according to 
recommendations. A rate independent model is not valid in the liquid region of the weld and could 
not simulate the response of the material near the melting point where viscous effects are present 
[35]. According to the accuracy classification in section 4.5, hot cracking, creep and the 
microstructural effects does not need to be considered in the basic accuracy category due to the 
scope of this thesis were residual stresses are of main interest.  
Due to the lack of high temperature data and the numerical problems that may be caused by a too 
low stiffness in the high temperature region a cutoff temperature or annealing temperature is 
implemented in the CWM. By this procedure the relaxation of stresses and plastic strains that 
occurs as the metal is heated to the specified temperature are simulated. When the temperature of 
the material exceeds this anneal temperature, Abaqus assumes that the material loses its 
hardening memory. All prior effect of work hardening is removed by setting the equivalent plastic 
strain to zero. For the combined hardening model implemented in the numerical model in this 
thesis, the back stress tensor is also set to zero.

6.1.1 
Experimental procedure - Static test 
Two types of static test methods are commonly used to determine the stress-strain relationship in a 
specimen inflicted within an elevated temperature range, namely the steady-state test and the 
transient state test. Both methods are described in [66].  
In the steady-state test, the steel specimen is heated up to a constant temperature during a 
soaking time, ts, equal to 10 minutes to stabilize the temperature of the test piece prior to the 
mechanical loading. The specimen is then applied a constant rise of load until failure, where the 
stress and strain values are measured during loading.  
The transient state test is conducted by applying a constant mechanical load on the test specimen 
under a constant rate of temperature growth. Temperature and strain are recorded throughout the 
test and the thermal elongation is subtracted from the total strain before the results could be 
converted into stress-strain curves. The transient-state test would give a more realistic result if the 
material´s behavior under fire conditions were to be predicted, since the specimen would then be 
applied a constant rise of temperature, but since this is not the case, a steady-state test method 
would be performed. 
Due to the absence of an original test setup machine for static and cyclic tests at elevated 
temperatures at the test location, an external oven is fixed to the test machine. The oven is open in

6.1.3 
Experimental procedure - Cyclic test 
In the cyclic tests, the same steel of grade S355ML is applied a cyclic load at a specific strain rate. 
The applied cyclic condition corresponds to a low-cycle fatigue test, where the objective is to 
propose a stabilized stress-strain curve after a certain amount of cycles. Therefore, a new 
mechanism able to fix the specimen during compression is applied. A slip resistant bolt connection, 
where the specimen is fixed with two bolts in each end is utilized as fixing mechanism. 
As seen in Figure 6.4, a 40mm x 40mm quadratic steel rod is connected to the specimen by the 
mentioned slip resistant bolted connection, stretching out from the oven and in to the gripping 
elements of the MTS testing machine. The same mechanism used to fix the extensometer and 
displacement sensors as in the static test is also applied in the cyclic test, only with a shorter

6.2.1 
Static test 
During the static tests, as the specimen was heated and test procedure executed, cross-sectional 
reduction of the specimen evidently caused the extensometer and displacement sensor 
mechanism fixed to the specimen to fell off at some tests. Having in mind the theory of Poisson’s 
ratio, this phenomenon could be explained by the contraction in the directions transverse to the 
direction of tensioning.  At the time the mechanism fell off, the specimen had been tensioned past 
the elastic region in each case this occurred, and as would be elaborated further, results could still 
be obtained from the plastic region by the displacement measured by the MTS test machine.  
A strain rate loading controlled by the extensometer, which were initially applied in the MTS 
machine with the same strain rates defined in the experimental procedure, showed fluctuating 
measurements of loads as the data was processed initially during the first specimens. The 
fluctuations appeared in the elastic and yield limit region which corresponds to strain range 1. As 
the load increased along with the given strain rate, a comparison of the strain control rate in the 
MTS test machine and the measured strains by the extensometer revealed that the MTS machine 
could not follow the strain control rate, which it detected and thereby increased load to compensate 
for the lower rate. Hence, the loads rattled from high to low since the strain control rate could not 
be fitted. Due to this event, it was decided to change the strain rate control from the extensometer 
to a displacement controlled rate in the MTS machine as described in the section of test procedure. 
Since a few tests were already conducted, the correct strain rate could be found by from the initial

6.2.2 
Cyclic test 
As mentioned in the section of test performance and setup, the applied slip resistant bolted 
connection did not perform as desired at temperatures above 300°C. As a consequence, it was 
decided to MIG weld the specimen to the gripping arms in addition to the applied bolts. This 
connection could introduce stress concentrations to the specimen, but the effect is considered to 
be negligible on the results as the welding was performed at the part of specimen around the holes 
for the bolts and would hence not influence the area which strains and elongation is measured.  
Due to the test setup of the cyclic test which was considerably more challenging in terms of 
initializing and implementation, only one specimen test at each temperature range was able to be 
performed. Anyhow, since the specimen is loaded during several cycles its behavior is seen to 
stabilize which is concluded as the cohere trend of specimen material behavior. A discussion could 
instead be dealt with the accuracy of measuring equipment which was seen to be inflicted by some 
noise at temperatures above 600°C.  
The main objective of the cyclic experiment was to achieve material data applied in the numerical 
modelling, and analysis of the fatigue life performance is omitted. A shifted stabilized stress-strain 
curve is required to be applied in Abaqus/CAE and the following procedure is conducted in order to 
achieve the stress-strain curve. Firstly, a stabilized hysteresis curve has to be established, where 
the stress level is seemed to reach a steady-state. This process is purely based on the author’s 
stipulation as there is no analytical approach to determine when this state is reached. However, as 
is seen from the results, slight deviations is current as the specimen is loaded throughout the 
cycles, except for temperature range at 600°C and 850°C, and the selection would thereby have 
small impact on the results. At the mentioned temperature ranges of 600°C and 850°C it is seen 
that the unsymmetrical stress cycle does not close or stabilize and that the measured strain value 
creeps or ratchets to the left per cycle, while the stress range is kept at a stable magnitude.

Having the material parameters needed as input for the numerical analysis of the monopile, next 
step is to obtain the thermal distribution associated with the specific welding procedure applied to 
produce the monopile. Following section treat this in detail where the objective of the weld 
analysis, in terms of thermal measurements, is to have a realistic insight in how the transient heat 
develops during and after welding of a multipass weld of a S355ML base material. As stated in the 
introduction, LHW is becoming more utilized by the industry and could be used in the future as a 
weld technique for the offshore steel monopiles. Hence, LHW test is therefore chosen as a welding 
method to be investigated in this thesis, as well as the current weld method at Bladt Industries; 
SAW. The welding by use of LHW is performed at LORC located in Lindø, Denmark, where a joint 
venture between LORC and FORCE Technology called Lindoe Welding Technology offered the 
possibility of testing LHW, while the SAW is performed at FORCE Technology in Brøndby, 
Denmark. The results will be used as a basis for the calibration of heat analysis in the subsequent 
numerical analysis. 
 
7.1 
Test setup and performance 
To be able to compare and investigate the considered welding methods in terms of residual 
stresses, the same geometrical parameters of the plates to be welded is applied for both methods, 
except for the cutting of the longitudinal edges to be welded together. This is due to the different 
methods of welding, where the LHW requires the cover pass to be welded with the edges closely 
positioned together and SAW requires a groove angle cut which is then filled with filler material 
during the welding. In addition, the geometry of the plates to be welded and the WPS were chosen 
to be similar to the experiment conducted by [45] due to the possibility of comparing it to his results 
for the SAW. The same results of thermal gradients from the SAW in [45] were also used as a 
basis for the thesis of Ammitzbøll and Sommerlund in [79] and their results are also exploited as a 
comparison in this thesis such that the results of the residual stresses induced by the same SAW 
procedure could be compared with regard to the steel quality as [79] investigated steel material of 
quality S235.   
Furthermore, the specific WPS are specified for the accompanying welding method in the 
subsequent sections. Three tests in total are made for each weld procedure to make sure results 
are gained from a perfectly performed test and to compare and validate the tests. No preheat was 
applied to the material before the first and second weld pass. The plates are externally supported 
==PAGE:96==

in the vertical downward direction by an underlying bench and no external supports are applied in 
the transverse and longitudinal direction of the weld. 
7.1.1 
Initial manufacturing process and geometry of steel plates 
The plates to be welded origin from a large hot rolled plate where the welding plates are cut out in 
the same matter as the static and cyclic test specimens; namely a water jet cutter. This procedure 
was chosen due to the minimal production of residual stresses compared to a flame cutting 
procedure. The initial plate where the plates to be welded together are cut out from is seen in 
Appendix B: Verification of specimen dimensions. Subsequently, the groove angle had to be cut 
and was done by a milling cutter in order to have a precise and clean finish of the angle for both 
the LHW and SAW. The groove angle depends as latter stated on the specific weld procedure and 
is seen in Figure 7.2 and Figure 7.3 together with the rest of the cross-section of the plates to be 
welded together for SAW and LHW, respectively. As seen in Figure 7.1, the geometry of the plates 
is seen. These dimensions are the same for both welding procedures. The weld length was chosen 
according to the statements in section 3.4.3 and a numerical investigation of the influence of the 
weld length upon the residual stresses showed that a 450mm long weld would be sufficient to 
stabilize the peak longitudinal residual stresses. The distribution of stresses had the same trend as 
seen in Figure 3.5 where a flat part marked the peak stresses until the weld ends where the tensile 
stresses were kept in equilibrium.

7.1.2 
Positioning of thermocouples 
In the analysis of the thermal gradients from the welding procedure it is desirable to have 
measurements as close as possible to the weld pool. This is due to the buildup of residual stresses 
will be highly influenced by the peak temperatures caused by the transient heat source. The 
distance between the thermocouples are therefore most dense nearest the weld. It is also of 
interest to know the temperature distribution in the middle of the plate with regard to the thickness. 
Hence, holes are drilled 5mm into the plates were the thermocouples would be positioned. The 
positioning as seen in Figure 7.2 for the SAW and in Figure 7.3 for the LHW is chosen due to 
several reasons.  If the holes were, in contrary with the chosen positioning, positioned on a straight 
line perpendicular to the weld, the holes and thermocouples could influence the measurement at a 
distance further away from the weld. Due to this reason the holes are shifted along the longitudinal 
direction of the plate so that the conduction of heat would affect the measurements directly. To 
==PAGE:97==
 
HEAT INPUT CALIBRATION BY EXPERIMENTAL MEASUREMENTS 
 
85 
 
have knowledge about the alignment of the welding machine and to see if it is actually centered at 
the geometrical centerline, measurement points are arranged on both sides of the weld. By this, 
any effect on the specific plate can also be analyzed. In Figure 7.3 it is also seen that holes are 
drilled from the bottom side. This is done in order to fix the thermocouples during the first pass of 
metal active gas (MAG) welding, since in the procedure of LHW the plate is positioned upside 
down.  
The thermocouples utilized are of brand RS Components, type “K” MI with a probe-diameter of 
1.5mm and -length of 150mm. Hence, in order to have a tight fit at the setup, the holes were drilled 
with a 1.5mm drill diameter. The thermocouples are connected to a datalogger of brand Hewlett

During welding of the first test, it was seen that the thermocouple closest to the weld centerline

In the second pass, it is clearly seen that the SAW machine has been misaligned with regards to 
the geometrical centerline and the thermocouple measured from this line. The SAW machine was

on a straight line when accuracy down to millimeters was needed to be obtained. The semi manual 
procedure of the weldment required the weld operator to manually start the machine by tightening 
a fixture that the engine that drove the machine at the desired welding speed was connected to. 
During this operation, movement on the machine probably occurred and could be the reason to 
why the temperature peaks at measurements further away from the centerline show higher peak 
gradients compared to measurements closer to the centerline.  
Another reason to the misalignment could also be due to the manual fitting of the welding machine 
with regards to centering, which had to be fitted by eye.

CHAPTER 8
VALIDATION MODEL 
 
In the validation model, investigations in terms of a numerical analysis as described in Chapter 4 
and Chapter 5 is conducted. It is desired to gain an approach to solve the transient heat 
distribution and calibrate this by the thermal distribution determined experimentally in the latter 
chapter. Material properties revealed by the static and cyclic experimental tests would be utilized to 
solve the mechanical weld analysis and achieve the residual stress pattern and distortional effects. 
By use of the experimental data, an isotropic plasticity hardening model is built upon the static 
tests and applied in the numerical modeling. In addition, neutron diffraction measurements and 
numerical results of a S235 base material with a similar weld procedure as the modelled SAW will 
be used as a basis in order to see the influence of the steel grade on the residual stress and elastic 
strains pattern, as neutron diffraction measurements of the SAW presented in the latter chapter is 
currently not available.  
The validation model will aim at recreating the SAW, described in Chapter 7. Left out from the 
analysis is the effects from the initial processing in terms of water let cutting nor the tack welding of 
the side plates, as these effects are considered to have a minimum impact on the WRS sought. 
 
8.1 
Joint model geometry 
When analyzing the weld in Abaqus/CAE by use of the plug in AWI, the sequential coupled 
thermal-mechanical analysis (as described in section 4.2) will generate two models. First, one heat 
input model simulated by a prescribed temperature applied on the considered deposited chunks. 
And finally, one mechanical model where the beads behave according to the deformation until their 
accompanying torch step, where it is removed in a short step and then reinserted strain free.  
These chunks have to be defined initially, where a swept mesh is applied to define the weld 
passes. As seen in Figure 8.1 - Figure 8.3, a macrograph is made out from the weld of the SAW, 
test 2. A piece of the joint is cut out, polished and then chemically etched so that the weld, the HAZ 
and base material become apparent. A sketch where the initial base material geometry and the 
chosen weld geometry to be modelled numerical overlay the macrograph is also seen in the 
figures.  As the numerical model utilize a prescribed temperature in the analysis, the thermal 
response depends on the amount of area the torch would be applied on. Hence, the HAZ is 
exploited to model the weld bead geometry to fit the experimental obtained measurements.

8.2 
Boundary conditions 
In Abaqus/CAE, boundary conditions have to be applied in terms of the thermal and mechanical 
analysis. As would be described in the following, a symmetry condition is introduced when 
modelling the plates to be welded.  
8.2.1 
Thermal boundary conditions 
Two kinds of thermal boundaries parameters are applied through the AWI plug-in on pre-defined 
surfaces in the main geometrical part of modelling and is elaborated in the following section. By 
use of the surface definitions, a symmetry plane is established in the centre of the weld, which 
allows for modelling of only half of the entire geometry and thereby reduce nodes and elements to 
be analyzed. Hence, a remarkably computational time saving is made. The defined surfaces are 
also exploited to restrain heat transfer by convection and radiation on the area the flux powder 
covers, which was observed and measured during the experimental procedure of SAW to cover an 
area of 40mm from the weld center line. During most SAW processes in the industry, the 
redundant flux powder gets removed by a vacuum cleaner right after the weld is deposited and 
since the flux powder has an insulation effect on the surface underneath, this would also affect the 
temperature distribution. Hence, in order to numerical model the experimental procedure most 
similar, the flux powder was not removed until right before the subsequent pass would be laid 
during the experiments. All surface definitions are seen in the figures in Appendix I: Surface 
definitions - numerical modeling. 
The end plates attached by a tack welding to the main plates are modelled as a solid part in 
connection with the main plates. Convective heat transfer is therefore allowed through the surface 
between the minor plates and main plate. This difference is assumed to influence the thermal 
response insignificantly as the thermal heat distribution is measured at points in the centre of the 
plate with regard to the longitudinal direction of the weld.  
The effect of temperature on the emissivity coefficient of carbon steel has been studied in [83] and 
it was seen that the coefficient varied between 0.2 and 0.9 as the coefficient was clearly 
temperature dependent. Defining the emissivity coefficient to be temperature dependent in the 
numerical modelling would be highly computational time demanding and instead, various 
coefficients between 0.6 – 0.9 is applied, firstly in order to calibrate the heat input correctly but also 
to investigate the influence of the magnitude on the corresponding residual stresses.  
The film coefficient could also be seen as a function of temperature but due to the increase in 
computational time by implementing this coefficient as temperature dependent, a choice of 
selecting a constant coefficient was made. This is also due to by implementing more varying 
parameters in the thermal analysis would enhance the difficulty of interpreting the sensitivity effects 
of the variables.

The global three-dimensional system is defined on every figure related to the geometry of the

As mentioned, the plate is modelled with a symmetry condition applied at the centre of weld, which

8.6 
Mechanical analysis 
In the following section, results from the numerical stress analysis are presented. An isotropic 
material model is defined in the material properties, where the established material properties from 
the static test conducted is applied. An annealing function is applied to the material model which 
removes the hardening memory above the temperature set to the melting temperature of the 
material equal to 1500°C, hence all phase change effects are included in the mechanical analysis 
(section 5.3.6). The strains and stresses results will be shown as a function of yield strength of the 
material and also as a function of the emissivity coefficient applied. Neutron diffraction 
measurements from [45] and numerical results by [79] are utilized to compare the results with 
regard to the steel grade of base material, as a S235 base material were applied in their analyzes. 
Model 10 refer to the simulation with an emissivity coefficient equal to 0.9 while model 11 is 
identical, only by a lower emissivity coefficient, equal to 0.6. 
The main analysis of the strains and stresses are extracted from a plot perpendicular to the weld 
direction in order to investigate the peaks of the elastic strains and residual stresses. These are 
expected to occur in the longitudinal direction of the plate and hence most attention is paid to 
strains and stresses in this direction.  
It should be noted that since a symmetry condition was applied, the results from the numerical 
investigations in the graphs are mirrored at the centre of the weld in order to visualize the final 
distribution of strains and stresses.

All measurements of the longitudinal residual stresses shown in Figure 8.16 are extracted from a

Before comparison of the numerical obtained residual stresses is made with the experimental 
measurements of the S235 steel, it should be emphasized that the preprocessing of the base 
material was done by flame-cutting of the S235 steel, while in this validation model, a water-jet 
cutter was utilized. Hence, initial residual stresses and elastic strains caused by the initial 
fabrication are not taken into account in neither of the numerical analyses. However, the production 
of residual stresses caused by a water-jet cuter compared to a flame-cutter could be concluded to 
be lower as the heat applied by the flame-cutter cause a high transient heat distribution in the 
material and thereby plastic deformations.  
Having the latter conclusion in mind, a lower stress magnitude is seen in the outer region for the 
results of S355ML steel numerical model compared with the neutron diffraction measurements in 
Figure 8.16. Furthermore, as a greater yield stress was applied in material properties of the 
S355ML model, as found from the experimental static loading test, contributing to a narrower 
plastic deformation zone close to the weld zone is conspicuous in the model. A tensile region is 
seen to extend approximately 42mm from the centerline for the S355ML steel, while for the S235 
steel a distance of approximately 56mm from the weld centerline is in the tensile region. 
The higher peak magnitude of the longitudinal residual stress in the S355ML numerical model can 
be related to the input material curve of the stress-strain relationship at elevated temperatures 
(Figure 6.15), where a profound representation of the stress and strain levels are determined at 
several temperature ranges, compared to the curve utilized in [79]. Furthermore, and more 
importantly with regard to the peak magnitude, is the slope of stress applied in the material 
properties in this validation model, which increase rapidly to large stress levels, consistently for 
every temperature range.  
The influence of the weld heat input is in addition of significant importance in relation to the buildup 
of plastic stresses. As was seen in the comparison of the two experimental SAW tests in Figure 
7.5, the heat distribution of the SAW utilized in [45] and [79] showed higher peaks and a shorter 
interpass between the laid weld passes, and should be taken into consideration when comparing 
the results in Figure 8.16 as a higher heat input will result in a wider plastic deformation zone. Also, 
in relation to the peak magnitude, the black dashed line illustrates the ultimate stress at 20°C 
defined in the material properties of the S355ML model, equal to 574.7MPa and is not exceeded.  
The numerical validation model has been simulated with an emissivity coefficient of 0.6 and 0.9, 
and the difference between them would not be paid further attention to in the consecutive analysis. 
It could be concluded that only a minor effect is seen on the accompanying residual stresses and 
elastic strains and the emissivity coefficient has as expected more influence on the thermal 
analysis. This does not exclude the effect on the mechanical analysis results, rather stating it is 
indirectly affecting the stress and strain pattern as the thermal input is changed.

With regard to the transverse and through thickness residual stresses, a plot showing the

for this deviation is considered to be caused by the approach to calculate the stresses from the 
neutron diffraction measurements. In an experimental neutron diffraction measurement it is only

of the measured elastic strains components in all three directions, Poisson’s ratio and elastic 
modulus. As a consequence, if one strain contribution in one direction is overestimated, this will 
affect the stress distribution by an accumulated error. Additionally, are the Poisson’s ration and 
elastic modulus applied in the calculation manually, and deviations from the actual magnitude 
could be an reason to noise in the estimation of residual stresses.

DISCUSSION AND FUTURE WORK 
 
9.1 
Experimental testing 
9.1.1 
Static test 
In the static tensile loading experiment, mechanical properties of a S355ML steel type were 
established. In the tests, stable temperature ranges were managed to be achieved before and 
during loading of the specimens. Even though the extensometer mechanism revealed to fell off at a 
certain strain magnitude due to the contraction of cross-sectional area, measurements from the 
machine displacement was utilized to provide results in the plastic region of the stress-strain curve. 
As it was seen during the tests, a strain magnitude above the elastic limit was reached before the 
mechanism fell off. Hence, it was assumed that the specimen alone would float and measurements 
by the MTS machine displacement measurements could therefore provide results for the plastic 
region. 
Except for temperature ranges at 450°C and 1000°C, measurements from two or more specimens 
at every temperature range were utilized in order to establish the mechanical properties in terms of 
elastic modulus, yield strength and a stress-strain curve fitted by the Ramberg-Osgood approach, 
and finally applied in the numerical modeling.  By this, a statistical basis was provided as the trend 
of stress-strain curve supported one another.  
 
From the results it was found that a decreasing trend of yield stress was apparent as the 
temperature increased, except for specimens loaded at 200°C and 300°C, which exhibited higher 
yield stress than the specimens at 100°C but lower than the one at 20°C. This is considered to be 
due to a metallurgical effect which generates a hardening of the material as it is heated to the 
specific temperature and could be a topic for future studies.   
9.1.2 
Cyclic test 
Since the specimens were loaded both in compression and tension, a new gripping arm 
mechanism was applied in the cyclic test. This caused the test procedure to be more arduous and 
time consuming in terms of replacing the specimen after ended test. Hence, only one specimen 
was achieved to be tested at the inflicted temperature range, which deteriorated the statistical 
foundation.  
In addition, a steady temperature was harder to maintain compared to the static test, as the new 
gripping arms revealed to exhibit extensive heat loss. This is considered to be due to the excluded 
bolted connections which were applied in the static test, which showed that the heat transmission 
was interrupted when included.  
==PAGE:132==

It is also unknown in what degree the performed MIG welding of the specimens to the gripping arm 
at temperatures at and above 600°C affected the specimens in terms of stresses, anyhow this 
effect is assumed to be negligible as the welding was performed in the bolt holes area of the 
specimen. As a final point, tests from 20°C and to 400°C showed reasonable results were a stable 
stress-strain hysteresis were a cyclic softening effect was distinct. 
By the experience from the cyclic tests performed, a future study could be to repeat the experiment 
with an original furnace provided by the hydraulic test machine manufacturer. This enables 
gripping of the specimen directly in the machine and the need of the gripping arms applied in this 
project could be omitted. Hence, a more convenient test procedure could be performed in order to 
obtain a better statistical background.  
 
9.2 
Numerical modelling 
9.2.1 
Heat input calibration 
To numerically model the heat input source, the prescribed temperature approach was utilized in 
the Abaqus plug-in AWI. This restricts the user to iteratively configure the transient heat distribution 
by limited parameters, which was seen to be a time consuming task as the weld geometry was 
seen to be the most comprehensive parameter to fit. 
In reality the WPS prescribes the heat input in terms of [KJ/mm] compared to AWI in which use a 
prescribed temperature on the surface for the heat input. By this, experimental thermal 
measurements are needed in order to validate the thermal analysis numerically, which also applies 
for every method of model the heat input numerically.  
This also indicates that when various welding procedures are intended to be simulated numerically, 
an investigation of the heat input in terms of the prescribed temperature is necessary as various 
welding methods, yield a different temperature applied on the material surface, e.g. LHW.  
Since thermal measurements of a LHW are made in this project, an investigation could be dealt 
with the WRS by the LHW as the thermal validation basis is now laid. 
9.2.2 
Isotropic plasticity model – residual stresses 
In the mechanical analysis of the weld in terms of WRS, contributions from initial processing of the 
base material was not considered and assumed to have a negligible effect on the total stress 
pattern.  
An isotropic plasticity hardening model was applied in the material data, where the experimental 
material properties obtained from the static tests was utilized. Results showed that the longitudinal 
residual stresses exceed the yield stress by 156.6MPa. However, a question arises as the 
redistribution of stresses during service load is unknown and could be a topic of interest in future 
studies.

It was not accomplished to have the numerical model with mixed-hardening material model applied 
in time for submission, as the steel specimens and plates needed for the experimental work were 
prone to delivery delays. It would therefore be suggested as a topic of future work to implement 
cyclic material data in the mixed-hardening material model and perform a mechanical analysis in 
order to predict the WRS.  
In addition, it is suggested to conduct a WRS measurement of the experimentally performed SAW 
and LHW, preferably by neutron diffraction, to have realistic basis in which the numerical results 
obtained in this project could be validated against.

CHAPTER 10
CONCLUSION 
 
As fatigue performance is the determining factor in design of offshore steel monopiles and 
recommended practice from Eurocode 3, DNV and IIW which states that residual stresses up to 
yield strength should be assumed for welded structures, a CWM investigation of the WRS was 
conducted in this thesis.  
A Ramberg-Osgood fitted stress-strain curve was established by measurements from a static 
loaded experiment in addition to the elastic modulus and yield strength of a S355ML steel 
specimen. This data was implemented in the numerical model where an isotropic material model 
was defined. 
A sequentially coupled thermo-mechanical approach was utilized in the CWM to predict the WRS 
of a two pass SAW validation case. In the thermal analysis, experimental thermal measurements 
made from the SAW were exploited to calibrate the transient thermal distribution in the numerical 
model, which was applied by a prescribed temperature on the surface approach. After an iterative 
process where the applied chunk length, weld geometry and emissivity coefficient was 
investigated, a good resemblance between the numerical results and experimental measurements 
was achieved. An average deviation of 12.5% and 10.8% was apparent between the numerical 
results and experimental measurements for the first and second weld pass temperature peak, 
respectively. However, as a similar experimental SAW procedure was conducted by [45] and 
furthermore numerically investigated by [79], a comparison revealed that the two outer measuring 
points made experimentally in this project could have underestimated the temperature gradients, 
and hence influenced the percentage of deviations remarkably. Hence, it was decided to exclude 
the two points since the rest of the measuring points deviated 3.4%, which finally resulted in an 
average deviation of 4.0% and 2.7% for the first and second peak, respectively. 
In the mechanical analysis, a model defined with an isotropic material model of the S355ML steel 
resulted into a narrower plastic deformation zone close to the weld zone compared to an identical 
model with S235 steel. Regarding the longitudinal residual stress peaks, the S355ML model 
experienced a magnitude of 567.7MPa measured on the bottom surface of the weld, which was the 
surface seen to be inflicted by the highest stress gradient. The yield stress at 20°C determined 
experimentally in the static loading test in this project resulted to be 392.8MPa and was therefore 
exceeded.  
Influence of the increased thickness in the offshore steel foundation and hence several weld 
passes needed would consecutively affect the stress distribution. Further research involving the 
thickness as a parameter is therefore essential so the data could be extrapolated or interpolated to 
form a stress pattern to predict the WRS.

Hewlett Packard model 34970A, measuring the signals as voltage (VDC) which is then 
transformed into distance (mm) as described in point 1-2. 
 
Figure A.0.1 Setup of displacement sensor calibration 
1. First the displacement sensor is moved from its initial position to 3mm, back and forth four 
times. The signals are recorded continuously with the data logger and in both positions the 
sensor is kept stationary for a sufficient time to be able to read out the data point at these 
positions as seen in Figure A.0.2. 
2. These points are then logged in a graph with the displacement initial (0 mm) and 3 mm in 
the y-axis and the corresponding signal in the x-axis (Figure A.0.3). The signals (VDC) at 
the two positions (0 and 3mm) are averaged before plotted. Furthermore, a trendline is 
applied to find the corresponding interception of the curve. Hence, the coefficient to be 
multiplied with the signal output is found, which result in output in terms of distance (mm). 
3. To verify the coefficient, the displacement sensor is moved in steps of 0,5mm from its initial 
position to 4mm. Step 1 and 2 is repeated for the verification with the only adjustment of 
multiplying the coefficient found from the measurements in the first graph (Figure A.0.4) 
and plot the result in the x-axis with the corresponding displacement position (mm) in the y-
axis in the second graph (Figure A.0.5). Furthermore, the relative length error of the 
displacement system is checked, according to the following formula, for each position and

Extensometer 
The procedure of calibrating the extensometers is performed in a slightly different matter then the 
displacement sensor and is described punctually underneath. The reference caliber used was of 
type Mitutoyo external digital micrometer, measuring from 0-50mm.  
 
Figure A.0.6 Extensometer calibration setup 
1. Initially, the readout from the MTS station manager is adjusted so that the zero point on the 
caliber corresponds to 0% strain in the station manager by adjusting the fine zero button in 
the station manager.  
2. The extensometer is then controlled up to the maximum test displacement of 3mm, which 
corresponds to 6% for the static test extensometer and 2mm and 8% for the cyclic test 
extensometer. If the station manager displayed some deviation, this deviation was adjusted 
by controlling the post-amp button until the strain in the station manager showed 6% or 8%, 
for the static and cyclic extensometer respectively. 
3. The zero point was adjusted again before embedded hysteresis was diminished by 
extending the extensometer back and forth three times to 3mm for the static test 
extensometer and 2mm for the cyclic test extensometer.  
4. Step one and two is repeated. 
5. The calibration is then evaluated by measuring fixed extension positions on the caliber, 
were the corresponding read-out from the station manager is noted. It should be noted that 
the strain noted was the running max and min value in the station manager, which is then 
averaged.  
6. By these measurements, the relative bias error, qrb, could be calculated according to 
equation 6.2, and the results are rendered in Table A.0.1 and Table A.0.2, for the static and 
cyclic test extensometer respectively. 
7. To validate the calibration, the readout from the machine is plotted in the x-axis against the 
calculated strain in y-axis, where a trendline function is displayed to find the R-squared 
deviation value as seen in Figure A.0.7 and Figure A.0.8, static and cyclic test 
extensometer respectively.

Appendix E: Cyclic test results 
Stress - strain response and stabilized curves 
As seen in the figures below, first the true stress and strain response of the considered specimen 
at the temperature range is shown with the chosen stabilized hysteresis loop rendered. For every 
specimen, the corresponding true stress is plotted as function of time where also an inset of the 
initial phase is seen, in order to investigate the material behavior in terms of softening or 
hardening. 
For every specimen up until 300°C a softening response could be seen as a cause of the cyclic 
applied load. This is apparent as a decrease in applied stress in the initial phase of cyclic loading 
before the behavior stabilize and a steady mean stress magnitude is apparent. For the specimen at 
400°C a cyclic hardening behavior could be seen as the stress magnitude increase. Anyhow, this is 
not to be concluded with as the response of the other specimens show the opposite, and the 
specimen could have been inflicted by some noise of either the test specimen itself or the 
measuring equipment.  
The stress response as a function of time of test specimen at 600°C and 850°C show a decreasing 
trend for each load cycle applied. Nevertheless, this behavior could not be interpreted as softening 
of the material if one consider the corresponding figure of the stress and strain relationship 
response, it is clear that there is some noise in the measuring equipment that cause the strain 
response to ratchet or creep to the left as the cycles is applied.

Reaction forces 
Boundary conditions are applied virtually to the plate in the element nodes where it is fixed against 
displacement in the y- and z-direction, in addition to provide a symmetry condition by fixing the 
model against displacement in x-direction and rotation in the y- and z-direction on the weld center 
line surface as seen in Figure 8.4.  
In reality, the plate would be supported by underlying bench and self-weight of the plate would 
contribute to the boundary conditions. Hence, since the gravitational effect is not included in the 
simulation, it necessary to investigate if the applied boundary conditions will contribute to reaction 
forces which could influence the precision of results. In Figure A.0.129 and Figure A.0.130 the 
reaction forces from the applied boundary conditions in the element end nodes are seen for the y-
and z- direction, respectively. From the contour plot it is seen that the entire plate exhibits zero 
reaction forces except in the nodal points of boundary conditions which is scaled up to visualize 
better the reactions. Anyhow, the reactions are seen to be diminishing small and the residual 
stresses can be considered to be uninfluenced by the reaction forces.
