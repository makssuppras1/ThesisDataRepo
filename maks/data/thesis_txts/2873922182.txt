Abstract
Increasing energy demands, strict performance requirements and accelerating climate
change have greatly increased the risk of indoor overheating in modern buildings. The
Danish Building Regulations (BR18) address indoor environment in living spaces through
a static, fixed­threshold temperature limit, a regulatory philosophy that may be ill­suited
for future challenges. This thesis investigates the efficacy of this approach, arguing that it
creates a policy paradox by creating a choice between passive thermal failure or unsus­
tainable energy consumption for active cooling.
Using dynamic thermal simulation software (IDA ICE), this study models a state­of­the­
art, DGNB­certified academic building to undergo simulations under different control con­
ditions. The building’s performance is analyzed under past, recent, and future Danish
climate scenarios. Two distinct operation modes are contrasted: a ’Passive’ approach
relying solely on the building’s high­performance fabric, and an ’As­Designed’ approach
utilizing active systems, including automated shading and mechanical cooling.
The results demonstrate that while the ’Passive’ design fails to meet the BR18 overheating
criteria, particularly in future climates, the ’As­Designed’ model achieves this regulation, at
the cost of consuming energy at a level that conflicts with Denmark and the EU’s national
energy framework.
This outcome proves that BR18’s static metric is an inadequate tool for guiding resilient,
low­energy design and creates a direct conflict between thermal comfort and energy ef­
ficiency regulations. The thesis concludes that the fixed­threshold model is obsolete and
recommends the adoption of an adaptive thermal comfort model (e.g., EN 16798­1) to
create a more coherent, climate­resilient, and energy­conscious regulatory framework for
Denmark’s future building stock.

Introduction
For generations, particularly in northern climates like Denmark’s, the primary challenge
has been the cold. Our building science, our regulations, and our design instincts have
been honed to create highly insulated, airtight envelopes designed to keep the warmth in
and the winter out. This approach has been remarkably successful. But what happens
when the climate from which we are sheltering begins to change? What is the conse­
quence when our well­intentioned fortresses, designed to trap heat, become too effec­
tive?
As global temperatures rise and extreme weather events, particularly heatwaves, become
more frequent and intense, a trend confirmed for Denmark by the national Klimaatlas
(DMI, 2022) the resilience of our building environment is being tested like never before.
Buildings, which account for approximately 40% of total energy consumption in the Eu­
ropean Union (45% in Denmark), are at the core of this challenge, among other things.
At its most fundamental level, they must not only become radically more energy efficient
but also adapt to provide comfortable indoor environments in a world that is growing pro­
gressively warmer. Studies already indicate that around a fifth of homes in well­insulated
northern European building stocks experience overheating even in today’s climate (CCC,
2022), a figure evidently set to rise.
1.1
Establishing this project
A cascade of difficult questions remains. What is the true cost of a building that is per­
fectly energy efficient on paper but becomes uninhabitable during a heatwave? How do
we reconcile the national imperative to reduce energy consumption with the fundamental
human need for thermal comfort? Are we inadvertently creating a new set of problems
for occupant health and, more importantly, occupant productivity? It seems, now that we
have solved keeping buildings warm, we must progressively figure out how to keep them
cool.
This project was born from these wonderments. It stems from an adamant refusal to ac­
cept that comfort and sustainability be mutually exclusive. It questions the very foundation
of how we measure and regulate the indoor environments we spend the vast majority of
our time in. Are the rules we use, the static lines we draw in the sand, still relevant for a
world in constant flux? Or are we trying to solve a dynamic, 21st­century problem with a
rigid, 20th­century mindset?
1.2
Intent and Objective
The intent of this thesis is to identify a potential problem. However, considering the
complex interdisciplinary nature of the problem, it should come as no surprise that this
requires the exploration of a paradox.The central objective is to critically investigate
and challenge the philosophy behind Denmark’s current approach to regulating
overheating through static means, among other things. This study seeks to demon­
strate that a simple, fixed temperature limit is an insufficient, and perhaps obsolete tool for
the task at hand. By simulating a building’s performance under past, present, and future
climate scenarios, this thesis aims to reveal the tension between passive design ideals,
occupant comfort, and the hard limits of energy policy.
By questioning the validity of our current metrics, this research aims to build the case for a
more intelligent, resilient, and human­centered approach, one that embraces adaptation

Statement of Significance
The push for sustainable, low­energy buildings is one of the defining challenges of our
time. In Denmark, this effort has been a clear success, driving down the energy needed
to operate our buildings.
Yet, this success has created a problem, one that is often
overlooked­ our highly­insulated, airtight homes and offices are becoming increasingly
prone to overheating. This thesis matters because it steps directly into that conflict be­
tween energy efficiency and thermal comfort, a problem with consequences that reach
far beyond just building science.
2.1
Interdisciplinary Context
The significance of this research is amplified by its position at the intersection of several
key disciplines. The problem of overheating is not merely an architectural challenge; it
is a complex issue that encompasses not just several engineering fields, but holds deep
roots in public health, policy­making, and urban science.
2.1.1
Public Health and Productivity
At its core, this is a public health issue. The term ”thermal comfort” extends beyond sub­
jective preference to encompass physiological well­being. Chronic overheating is linked
to reduced sleep quality, decreased cognitive function, lower productivity and mood/emo­
tional instability. By critiquing a standard that may permit unhealthy indoor temperatures,
this thesis directly engages with the role of architecture as a determinant of public health.
2.1.2
Energy Policy & Economics
The ”policy paradox” identified in this thesis is a classic problem in energy policy. The work
analyzes how two well­intentioned regulations— one for energy efficiency (Energiramme)
and one for comfort (BR18) operate exclusively and can create conflicting outcomes. This
research explores the economic trade­offs between the upfront investment in resilient
passive design versus the long­term operational cost and carbon footprint of relying on
energy­intensive active cooling. This may also be of interest, particularly to the work
industry, where occupant comfort and productivity could cost companies much more every
year than they aim to save on things such as air conditioning, working schedules, etc.
2.1.3
Urban Planning & Climatology
Thhis thesis connects the building­scale simulation to the city­scale reality. It recognizes
that buildings do not exist in a vacuum; they exist within urban microclimates that are
demonstrably warmer than their rural surroundings. This links the research to urban plan­
ning strategies such as green space allocation and material choices.
2.1.4
Law & Public Policy
This thesis is fundamentally a work of policy analysis. It examines a specific piece of
legislation (BR18) and questions its continued relevance and effectiveness, a concept
known as regulatory obsolescence. It delves into a core debate in administrative law: the
strengths and weaknesses of a rigid, performance­based standard versus a more flexible,
adaptive approach to achieving a desired public good. Ultimately, this thesis aims to shift
the conversation. It moves past simply identifying overheating as an issue and instead
questions the very philosophy of the tools we use to prevent it. The goal is to advocate
for a smarter, more resilient way to think about and create the buildings of the future.

2.2
Originality and Contribution
This work brings a fresh perspective to the field through its specific method and focus. Its
original contributions fall into three main categories:
A Novel Dataset: At its heart, this study generates a new and specific set of simulation
data. By testing a detailed model of a state­of­the­art, DGNB­certified building against a
range of past and future climate scenarios, it produces unique evidence on the perfor­
mance limits of even our best architecture.
A New Line of Inquiry: Instead of asking if a building fails a standard, this work flips
the question on its head: is the building failed by the standard? This shift, from a simple
compliance check to a critical policy analysis, represents a different way to use building
simulation—not just as a design tool, but as a method for investigation of our regulations.
A Deeper Understanding of a Known Problem: The primary contribution here is the
clear framing of a ”policy paradox.” While the tension between energy use and cooling is
understood, this thesis argues it is a direct result of an outdated regulatory philosophy. By
showing how a rigid comfort rule forces a choice between two bad outcomes—passive
failure or unsustainable energy use—this study offers a new and powerful understanding
of a core flaw in our current system, making a data­driven case for the move to adaptive
comfort models. Essentially, it combines much known theoretical concepts and points at
an unrecognized source of error in the way we approach building design.

Theoretical Framework
This chapter unpacks the science behind how we feel comfortable, the tools we use to
predict that feeling, and the specific rules that govern our buildings in Denmark. This isn’t
just a review of existing knowledge; it’s a critical look at the very assumptions our
regulations are built on, designed to reveal the cracks in the foundation.
3.1
The Competing Philosophies of Comfort
What does it actually mean to be comfortable? The official definition by ASHRAE Standard
55 calls it ”that condition of mind that expresses satisfaction with the thermal environment.”
The phrase, ”condition of mind”, is subjective, psychological, and deeply personal. To deal
with this complexity, building science has split into two very different schools of thought.
The Static, Heat­Balance Model
The first approach, created out of highly controlled laboratory experiments, is the static
or PMV (Predicted Mean Vote) model. It treats the human body like an engine, a system
that needs to maintain a perfect heat balance with its surroundings. It is based on a
heat­balance equation with six inputs: air temperature, radiant temperature, air speed,
humidity, clothing, and activity level. This model has been incredibly influential because
it gives engineers a clear, quantifiable target. The fixed temperature limits you see in
regulations all over the world, including Denmark’s (BR18), come directly from this way
of thinking. The problem, of course, is that people aren’t engines, and buildings aren’t
laboratories. The static model’s greatest weakness is that it completely ignores our
remarkable ability to adapt.
The Adaptive Model
The second school of thought is the adaptive model, which is built on decades of field
studies in real buildings with real people. This model starts with a simple, observable
truth: our expectations change. For instance, A room at 26°C feels completely different
on a hot July day than it does on a cool day in February. The adaptive model, which is the
basis of modern standards like EN 16798­1, makes this official. It argues that the range
of temperatures we find comfortable is not fixed, but floats in relation to the recent outdoor
weather. This model recognizes that we are not passive victims of our environment.
We are active participants, constantly making small adjustments, like opening a window,
taking off a sweater, moving out of the sun to the shade. This approach doesn’t just align
better with human experience; it has profound implications for energy use. By allowing
for a wider, more flexible range of acceptable temperatures, it dramatically reduces the
need for energy­hungry air conditioning. This model accounts for three types of human
adaptation:
Behavioral: Conscious actions such as changing clothing, opening windows, using fans,
or moving to a cooler location.
Physiological: The body’s natural acclimatization to prolonged thermal conditions.
Psychological: A shift in expectations based on context. A warmer indoor temperature is
more acceptable and expected during a hot summer than during a mild spring.
Subjective Perception
A key psycho­physical principle that underpins the adaptive model is alliesthesia. In sim­
ple terms, alliesthesia describes how the pleasantness of a sensation is not fixed, but
depends entirely on a person’s internal state. For example, the feeling of cool air from an

open window is perceived as highly pleasant when you feel too warm, but deeply unpleas­
ant if you are already feeling cold. The physical stimulus—the cool air—is identical, but
the subjective experience, the ”condition of mind,” is completely different. This concept
exposes a fundamental weakness in the static PMV model, which cannot account for this
dynamic shift in perception. The adaptive model, in contrast, recognizes that our interac­
tions with our environment, like opening a window, are not random; they are goal­oriented
behaviors aimed at seeking pleasant sensations to restore our internal thermal balance.
It’s a more realistic and human­centric view of how we actually experience comfort.
3.2
Simulating Reality
To test these ideas, this thesis relies on Building Performance Simulation (BPS). Using
the software IDA ICE, we can build a detailed virtual model of a building and subject it to
a full year of weather, hour by hour. The simulations are driven by standardized .EPW
(EnergyPlus Weather) files, including the official Danish Design Reference Year (DRY)
for baseline compliance checks, as well as historical and future climate files to analyze
performance under changing climatic conditions. This allows us to see how the building
breathes, how it heats up and cools down, and how it responds to the climate. But it’s
crucial to be honest about what a simulation is. There is a well­known phenomenon in
building science called the ”performance gap”—the difference between how a building
is predicted to perform and how it actually performs once it’s built and occupied. This
gap exists for all sorts of reasons, from construction quality, complexity and variability
of simulation algorithms to the unpredictability of human behavior. While this study is
a theoretical exploration, acknowledging the performance gap adds a necessary layer
of critical realism. The results of a simulation are not a perfect representation of a
control situation, but they are a powerful tool for comparing the relative performance of
different design choices and regulatory philosophies.
3.3
Denmark’s Regulatory Conflict
A central element of this thesis, a recurring paradox also comes from two powerful but dis­
connected parts of the Danish Building Regulations (BR18). They operate in disconnect,
creating a direct conflict for designers and for the country’s climate goals.
The Energy verdict: For new commercial buildings, the Energiramme sets a very strict
energy budget. The rule in BR18 §260 limits primary energy use calculated by the formula:

3.4
The Urban Heat Island Effect
Finally, we have to consider where our buildings actually exist. The standard weather files
used for simulations are typically based on data from airports, which are open, grassy, and
rural. A city like Copenhagen, however, creates its own microclimate. The vast surfaces
of asphalt and dark roofing absorb and radiate heat, while the lack of vegetation reduces
cooling from evaporation. This is the Urban Heat Island (UHI) effect, and it means that a
city can be several degrees warmer than its surroundings, especially on calm, clear nights.
For Copenhagen, studies have noted this temperature difference can be significant (Büh­
ler et al., 2010), amplifying the thermal stress on buildings beyond what standard weather
data would suggest. This makes the static, one­size­fits­all approach of BR18 even more
questionable, as it fails to account for the amplified heat of the very places where most
people live and work.

Methodology
To properly fulfill the purpose of this thesis, a multi­faceted approach was necessary.
This research isn’t based on a single method, but rather a combination of quantitative
simulation and qualitative analysis. This leverages complementary strengths of various
methods, prioritizing adaptability over specialization. The core of the work is a detailed
building performance simulation, which provides the hard data. This data is then con­

highlighted by a pilot public survey. This chapter details each of these components.

4.1.2
Model Preparation, Simplification and Troubleshooting
Transitioning an architectural model into a valid model for thermal simulation is a critical
and often laborious process. The initial architectural data provided by DTU Campus ser­
vice was in the form of specialised Revit building models. An Industry Foundation Classes
(IFC) format file was generated to be exported to IDA ICE, required significant clean­up
and simplification to be suitable for analysis in IDA ICE. This preparatory work involved
removing geometrically complex but thermally insignificant elements, such as furniture
and detailed architectural finishes, that would drastically increase calculation time without
contributing to the accuracy of the heat balance. This process of refinement, however,
revealed several critical errors and unrealistic default settings within the initial model that
had to be systematically identified and corrected. Dozens of iterative simulations and
scouring through results yielded errors after errors. These troubleshooting steps were
fundamental to achieving a valid simulation through some of the most impactful errors:
Boundary Conditions: Initial simulations produced errors related to ”leaks in uncon­
nected internal walls”.
Phantom Cooling: The most significant challenge was identifying and eliminating several
sources of ”phantom” cooling that were masking the building’s true overheating potential.
This involved a series of iterative tests to disable the AHU’s heating coil during summer
nights (and generate a more real night cooling), change the supply air temperature control
from a fixed setpoint to one that tracks the outdoor air (eliminating a huge free­energy
heating error), and correcting an imbalance where the return fan was running when the
supply fan was off, creating an unrealistic vacuum effect.
Realistic Occupancy: The default window opening schedules were found to be overly
optimistic, allowing for perfect nighttime cooling seven days a week. This was corrected
by limiting window operation to occupied hours on weekdays.
Ground Connection: Early results showed unusually high heat loss through the floor.
Investigation revealed the model was using a simplified ground model with a fixed tem­
perature. This was corrected by implementing the dynamic ISO 13370 ground model,
which provides a more physically realistic calculation of ground heat transfer.
This iterative process of simplification, error correction, and assumption refinement was
a necessary and substantial part of the methodology, ensuring the final model was both
computationally stable and a credible representation of the building’s intended perfor­
mance.
4.1.3
Simulation Scenarios & Parameters
The specific input values for the building’s construction, internal loads, and systems, were
based on a combination of the Danish Building Regulations (BR18), associated standards
(DS 418, DS 447), and international comfort standards (ASHRAE 55, ISO 7730). Some
values such as the AHU model’s power requirements, fan and generator efficiencies, min­
imum and maximum return volumes, and others were left at IDA defaults, if not available
by standards. This is to ensure a neutral comparison (neither optimistic, nor critical) where
no alternative was available.

5.1
Survey Results
While not statistically exhaustive, the results offer a powerful glimpse into how people
actually feel in their indoor environments. Appendix A1 provides a detailed breakdown
question­by­question, while some important details are highlighted below:
Overheating is a Widespread Problem: A significant majority, 73.3% of respondents,
reported feeling uncomfortably warm in their home or office during the summer. This
wasn’t a rare occurrence; 53.3% said they experience this discomfort monthly, and an­
other 30% experience it almost daily/weekly. The distribution of temperature in the workspace
also seems to be evidently heterogenous.
The Impact is Real: Almost 100% of people said it negatively affects their concentra­
tion at work, with over 50%, reporting significant effects­ spanning changes in quality,
efficiency as well as pace of their work.
It’s More Than Just Temperature: When asked to describe the feeling of discomfort,
the responses went beyond simple heat. Among the top factors, humidity, an indirect and
related effect of temperature played a role for the most people (70%). Lack of light and
Noise stood right behind (63%), showing opening windows may not be a straightforward
answer to our problems. This strongly suggests that people’s perception of comfort is
holistic, and that a regulation focused solely on a temperature number is missing key
parts of the picture. A combined effects of indoor factors may offer insights.
People Adapt by Opening Windows: The most common way people deal with overheat­
ing is overwhelmingly clear. 83.3% of respondents said they open their windows to find
relief. This finding directly validates the core premise of the ”Passive” simulation scenario,
confirming that natural ventilation is the primary and most intuitive occupant response.
Physical Health Symptoms: Beyond general discomfort, 53% of respondents reported
experiencing physical health symptoms like headaches or fatigue as a direct result of the
warm indoor climate.
A Clear Preference for Adaptive Environments: The survey strongly suggests that
people’s expectations of comfort are not static. When asked to choose between a con­
stant/stable temperature all year round, 73% said slight seasonal variations are accept­
able and even pleasant.
The Need for Passive Solutions: The survey also sheds light on occupant behavior.
When asked 53.3% of people would prefer more energy being spent on maintaining a
comfortable environment with only a 30% willing to tolerate temperatures over 28 C a few
days a week to save energy.
Future concerns: This isn’t a static issue. Close to 75% of people feel that the problem
of overheating is worsening over years, with 50% saying the change is significant.

6
Discussion
The results from the simulations and the survey don’t just suggest a problem; they paint
a detailed picture of a regulatory framework with the realities of a changing climate, and
with the lived experiences of the people inside these buildings. The data shows that
Denmark’s current rules for thermal comfort, when projected into the near future, are not
only inadequate but create a paradox that forces a choice between two bad outcomes.
This chapter pulls together the different threads of this research to analyze that paradox
and build the case for a new direction.
6.1
The Policy Paradox
The central finding of this thesis is the clear, quantitative evidence of a policy paradox.
The simulation of the ”As­Designed / Active” model shows that it is technically possible
to keep a modern building comfortable, even in the extreme 2090 climate. However, this
comfort comes at a staggering.
According to the simulation, maintaining comfort in the 2090 scenario requires 57.4 kWh/m²
per year for thermal comfort alone. The legal limit for this building, as defined in BR18
§260, is 48.6 kWh/m² per year. The two regulations are in direct conflict.
This isn’t just a future problem. The simulation for the recent years (especially the very
hot 2016 year), already pushing the absolute legal limit. This finding, combined with the
survey result that 75% of people feel the problem of overheating has gotten worse in
recent years, suggests that the policy paradox isn’t a distant threat, it’s already here.
This conflict is set to become even more severe. The current energy framework is just a
waypoint. The voluntary ”Building Class 2020” from BR10 aimed for an energy use of just
25 kWh/m² per year. As these more ambitious NZEB and ZEB targets become mandatory,
the energy required for active cooling will become an even larger and more problematic
portion of a building’s total energy budget, making the paradox an unavoidable crisis.
6.2
The Obsolete Standard
The second key finding is that the static 100/25 hour rule is an obsolete metric. The ”Pas­
sive” simulation shows that while the building already underperforms under the historical
DRY climate file, it begins to fail as the climate warms, culminating in a hundreds of hours
above 27°C in scenarios such as 2016. A standard based on past climate is not a reliable
tool for designing buildings that will be resilient in the future, a point made clear by De
Wilde & Coley (2012) who call for a fundamental rethinking of building design in the face
of climate change.
This aligns with the foundational research on adaptive comfort (de Dear & Brager, 1998),
which argues that a static model is a poor representation of real human comfort. Our own
survey data powerfully supports this: 73% of respondents explicitly preferred a variable
indoor climate over a constant one. This suggests a public that is psychologically primed
for an adaptive approach.
6.3
A Reduced View of Comfort and Productivity
The current regulation’s narrow focus on a single temperature metric ignores the holistic
nature of true comfort and its impact on the modern ”knowledge worker” (Duffy, 1997). Our
Is the concept of allowing 100 hours above 27°C still relevant for office buildings in Denmark
19
==PAGE:28==
survey data shows that people’s discomfort is strongly linked to feelings of lathargy and
dizziness, and ”humidity,” not just heat. This is critically important because, as the land­
mark ”CogFx” study by Allen et al. (2016) proved, it is often these non­thermal factors like
poor air quality (high CO￿) that have the most significant negative impact on higher­order
cognitive function. A building that is ”compliant” on temperature but has poor ventilation
is failing its occupants in a way the law cannot see.
Furthermore, the economic implications are significant. The meta­analysis by Seppänen
et al. (2006) demonstrated a clear link between elevated temperatures and a decrease
in office task performance.
6.4
The Path Forward: An Adaptive Future
The conflicts and failures identified in this research all point to a single, clear solution: Den­
mark must move away from its static comfort standard and embrace an adaptive thermal
comfort model, as defined in the European standard EN 16798­1. The groundwork for
this has already been laid by researchers like McCartney & Nicol (2002).
An adaptive model would resolve the policy paradox by providing a more flexible and
realistic definition of comfort. By allowing for a wider range of acceptable temperatures
in the summer, it would dramatically reduce the need for energy­intensive active cooling,
bringing the goals of comfort and energy efficiency back into alignment. It would also
better reflect the preferences of the population, as shown in our survey.
This does not mean abandoning technology. As Brager (2006) argues, the future lies
in intelligent ”mixed­mode” buildings that prioritize passive solutions like the nighttime
ventilation analyzed by Artmann et al. (2007), but have mechanical systems available as
a backup, not a default. The challenge, as noted by Leaman & Bordass (2007), will be to
engage occupants in this process, as their tolerance and behavior are key components
of a successful low­energy strategy.
Ultimately, this thesis demonstrates that the current Danish comfort standard is a relic of
a stable climate that no longer exists. If the weather over an year changes, and so does
our perception, why shouldn’t the requirements? It urges Denmark to once again lead
the way through an Industry­leading effort and pave way for not just a comfortable, but a
productive tomorrow.

7
Limitations and Future Research
No single study can provide all the answers. The purpose of this chapter is to be trans­
parent about the boundaries of this research and to use those limitations as a basis to
propose a clear pathway for future work.
7.1
Limitations of this Study
While this thesis provides a robust critique of the current regulatory framework, it’s impor­
tant to acknowledge its limitations in that approach.
Single Case Study: The simulation is based on a single building. While using a ”best­in­
class” model strengthens the critique of the regulations, the specific quantitative results
may not be generalizable to all building typologies, particularly the existing building stock.
Hoever, with the growing advent of more apartment complexes built similar to an office
typology, it makes good case against BR18.
Partial Model: For reasons of relevance and feasibility, this study modeled a selection of
common rooms rather than the entire complex building. The specialized thermal behav­
ior of the laboratories was therefore not included. The study also heavily underlines the
fact that the intent here is not to model or express the exact results pertaining to Building
112, but to show how different control mechanisms and climate trends affect a building’s
functionality. Thus, the study does not make any claims in reflecting a perfect represen­
tation of the building’s actual real­time functionality, but simply uses it as a basecamp for
experiments.
The Simulation­Reality Gap: This research is based on a building performance simula­
tion. While every effort was made to use realistic inputs, a ”performance gap” between
simulated and real­world operational performance is a well­documented phenomenon in
building science. The results should be seen as a strong indicator of performance, not a
perfect prediction.
Exploratory Pilot Survey: The public survey provided invaluable human­centered con­
text, but with a sample size of 30, it is not statistically representative. Its findings are
used here to frame the problem and suggest trends, not to draw definitive quantitative
conclusions.
Benchmark Application of BR18: The thesis deliberately uses the BR18 overheating
criteria, a standard for residential buildings—as a benchmark to test the regulatory phi­
losophy against a non­residential building. This is a methodological choice to critique the
underlying static model, not a direct legal compliance check. It thus raises questions not
limited to a single code, but instead challenges our philosophy about building expecta­
tions.
7.2
A Roadmap for Future Research
The findings and limitations of this thesis open up a plethora of opportunities for future
research. This isn’t just limited to filling gaps but also about building a comprehensive,
multi­disciplinary understanding of how to create buildings that are truly fit for the future.
The Human Experiments: The most important next step is to conduct the human subject
experiments that were part of this project’s original scope. A controlled study in a climate
Is the concept of allowing 100 hours above 27°C still relevant for office buildings in Denmark
21
==PAGE:30==
chamber is needed to empirically test the combined effects of temperature, humidity, and
air quality on both subjective comfort and objective cognitive performance using special­
ized tests, under control conditions, and both desirable and undesirable conditions, with­
out informing participants to prevent bias. This may also be tied to studies to understand
how the human factor and mental perception may override physical perception. This
would provide the hard, physiological data to move the debate beyond simulation and
prove the need for a more, truly holistic comfort standard.
The Human Factor in IEQ: Future research must dig deeper into the ”human factor.” Field
studies in real offices, using advanced environmental sensors and wearable technology
to monitor occupant biometrics, could help us understand the complex interplay between
different IEQ factors. How much does a rise in CO2 concentration affect an occupant’s
tolerance for a higher temperature? Answering these questions is key to creating truly
intelligent building control systems.
From Pilot to Policy: The pilot survey revealed clear trends. The next step is to val­
idate these findings with a large­scale, statistically significant survey across Denmark.
Quantifying the prevalence of overheating, its impact on national productivity, and the
public’s clear preference for adaptive environments would create a powerful, evidence­
based mandate for regulatory change that policymakers could not ignore.
Refining the Adaptive Model: This thesis recommends adopting the EN 16798­1 adap­
tive model, but this should be a starting point. Future work should compare different
adaptive models (including the one from ASHRAE 55) and could even aim to develop
a new model specifically calibrated for the Danish climate, building stock, and cultural
expectations. A key area of investigation should be how to apply adaptive principles to
mixed­mode multi­purpose buildings, which are a likely future for Danish architecture.
Comfort vs. Productivity­ Are They the Same?: We assume that a comfortable worker
is a productive worker, but is that always true? Future studies could investigate the po­
tential trade­offs between comfort and productivity. For example, is a perfectly stable,
comfortable environment optimal for cognitive tasks, or does a slight, pleasant variability
in temperature or airflow actually increase alertness? This research could lead to more
nuanced control strategies that optimize for performance, not just satisfaction.
The Business Case: To drive change in the real world, we need a clear business case.
The next step is to partner with Danish companies to conduct detailed economic studies.
This would involve measuring the real energy costs of cooling against the measured fi­
nancial cost of lost productivity from overheating. Is there a break­even point? How much
should a company invest in a better indoor environment to see a return in employee per­
formance? Answering this question would transform the conversation from an expense
to an investment.
Identifying Barriers to Implementation: Finally, if adaptive models are better, why aren’t
they already the standard? Research is needed to identify the current barriers to imple­
mentation. Are they technical (engineers aren’t trained in the new models), regulatory
(the current code is too entrenched), or cultural (a preference for mechanical control)?
This would involve interviews and surveys with architects, engineers, and policymakers
to create a clear strategy for overcoming these hurdles.
