Abstract
Wildfire events have become an issue in the modern world, since they are becom-
ing more frequent and more destructive. This rising in frequency and devastating
power has been attributed to the human-induced climate change by several studies.
Furthermore, some regions in the world, such as the US, the Amazon or Australia,
have particularly experienced these extreme episodes more than other regions in the
globe.
The early detection of fire is feasible nowadays by means of state-of-art Remote
Sensing measurements from satellites. This way, humanity counts with diverse tech-
niques to track these episodes and be able to rapidly respond and mitigate them so
the damages caused are the lowest possible.
Nowadays, counting with more than 50 years of satellite data, considerably large
time series can be constructed so researchers are able to use it to predict the climate
in the past, to run models, study the action of climate change, etc. Concerning
wildfire hazards, several studies have used these time series of climate, vegetation,
atmospheric or any kind of variables to model and forecast wildfire events.
By using the Climate Change Initiative (CCI) Burned Area (BA) product provided
by ESA the assessment of wildfire events in two of those regions suffering more
extreme episodes (California and Australia) was performed in this study. The driving
processes of fire hazards were analyzed and inferred for each region by using different
variables such as: surface soil moisture (SM), drought indices, vegetation indices,
land cover type, land surface temperature (LST) and wind speed (WS). Monthly
anomalies were calculated for soil moisture (globally), wind speed and land surface
temperature products (regionally).
A Random Forest regressor was implemented in order to help in the retrieval of
the driving factors of those wildfire events. The most important variables inferred
by the model in California were, in that order: SPEI 1 (accumulated Standardised
Precipitation-Evapotranspiration Index over the one month), SPEI 6 (accumulated
SPEI over the 6 months), NDVI (NormmalizeD Vegetation Index), SM anomaly 18
(corresponding to 18 months before an incident), WS anomaly, LST anomomaly
and SM anomaly 30 (corresponding to 30 months before an incident). SPEI1 and
SPEI 6, as dought indices they are, play a major role in fire forecasting. On the
other hand, NDVI, being a widely used vegetation index, also is one of the most
important variables since it is one of the main fuel resources for a wildfire event. As
guessed before implementing the model, past episodes of high moisture (between 18
and 30 months before) have a substantial impact on fire occurrence. Lastly, both

1
Introduction
1.1
Context
In recent years, human-induced climate change has originated widespread adverse
impacts resulting in losses and damages to nature, including more intense and re-
current intense extreme events (such as droughts or floods).
This increasing in
climate and weather extreme episodes has driven into some irreversible impacts as
both natural ecosystems and humanity are forced to adapt [1]. The consequences
of a warming climate are wide-ranging, affecting fresh water resources, global food
production and sea levels.
Several studies [2, 3, 4] claim wildfires are an example of these more frequent and
intense extreme event, strongly affected by temperature increase and precipitation
decline. These factors would be the major driver of fire regimes under future climates
as evapotranspiration (the total water loss to the atmosphere from a land surface)
increases and soil moisture decreases [5, 6].
Even though wildfire is an essential and natural phenomenon of forest, woodland
and grassland ecosystems, e.g., killing pests, releasing plant seeds sprout, thinning
out small trees, and serving other functions essential for ecosystem health; the rise
in wildfire episodes can kill people, cause breathing illnesses from the smoke, destroy
homes and damage ecosystems. Furthermore, the rise of fire events contributes to
climate as a positive feedback loop, i.e., it increases the effect of the climate change
and produces instability in different ways.
Firstly, emissions from wildfires and
biomass burning are a significant source of green house gases (GHGs) such as car-
bon dioxide (CO2), methane (CH4) and nitrogen dioxide (N2O) or carbonaceous
aerosols. Moreover, a fire episode alters vegetation and soil properties, both in the
short- and the long-term, with consequences for carbon dioxide stock changes (ham-
pering the ability to be carbon sink), albedo, fire-atmosphere-vegetation feedbacks
and the biological capacity of the land [7]. Nevertheless, wildfire activity is also
driven by a range of other factors including land management and ignition sources,
and on the global-scale most data sets indicate a reduction in burned area in recent
years, primarily due to the increasing of land use for agriculture [8].
In some regions in the world, wildfires are burning wider areas than in the past,
becoming very large incidents and leading to substantial impacts on human popu-
lation. Some examples are Portugal 2003 and 2005; Greece, Italy, and the US 2007;

Australia 2009; the US 2013; Canada and Chile 2016; Portugal and the US 2017;
and the United States, the Amazon and Australia 2020 [9].
Hence, to maintain a sustainable environment and to mitigate the direct effects of
wild fires on environment the early detection of these incidents is of prime impor-
tance. Satellites have continuously observed the Earth over the last fifty years to the
present day and all this information helps to monitor the evolution for the key com-
ponents of the climate, better understand Earth system processes and predict future
change. CCI (Climate Change Initiative) programme employs the full potential of
the long-term global EO (Earh Observation) archives that ESA has established over
the past 30 years by constantly processing of the EO data sets applying the most
state of the art algorithms, plus development of improved algorithms for the Essen-
tial Climate Variable (ECV) production. This way, CCI programme provides a fire
data set in which burning date and burned area products are available (open source)
which will be used in the present work.
This ability to early detect fire should be followed by a prediction of the expanse
or severity of a fire that erupted to ensure quick and eﬀicient caring and help in
decision making. Thereupon, due to this increase of devastating wildfire catastro-
phes, numerous researchers have attempted to model and predict fire occurrences
in some regions by means of remote sensing observations and/or data from in situ
data. Creating fire forecasting models can help to estimate the long term effects of
climate change on the forest distribution in the region and to prevent the long term
damage of the ecosystems and societies induced by wildfires.

2.1
Remote sensing background
In order to clearly understand the data sets used in the present work, a brief sum-
mary about the principles of remote sensing and how the observations are taken
will be performed. Remote sensing is the measurement of object properties on the
Earth’s surface (and other planets) using data acquired from aircraft and satellites.
In this way, it is a powerful tool to measure something at a distance rather than
in situ by means of propagated signals.
There is a wide range of applications,
some of the most important ones are: environmental assessment and monitoring
(urban growth); global change detection and monitoring (global warming, deforesta-
tion); agriculture (crop condition, soil erosion), meteorology (atmosphere dynamics,
weather prediction); or mapping (topography) [19].
Remote sensing measurements can be divided into two main types depending on
the source of the signal they use to retrieve information from an object: active and
passive.
2.1.1
Active remote sensing
Active RS instruments count with their own source of emission by which they emit
its signal to an object and they check the response. There are different kinds of
active RS instruments based on what they want to determine (e.g., height, distance,
atmospheric conditions, etc.) and what they transmit (e.g., visible light, microwaves,
acoustic waves, etc.). An example of an active RS instrument is the scatterometer.
Scatterometer
A more in-depth explanation about the scatterometry systems (a specific type of
radars) will be performed since, as it will be explained in section 2.3, a soil moisture
product measured by scatterometry has been used in the present work.
Scatterometers’ physical principle is the following. If an interface is separating two
media with different electric or magnetic properties it will affect an incident EM
wave; e.g., following Fig. 2.1, a plane EM wave is incident to an interface sepa-
rating ”vacuum” (Earth’s atmosphere is almost invisible to the frequencies used by
spaceborne radars) and a dielectric half-space with dielectric constant ϵ. Hereafter,
the EM wave will interact with the atoms of the dielectric and some energy will be
radiated in all directions, which can be measured by the scatterometer’s antenna.

Passive RS
Passive RS systems, unlike active ones, do not emit radiation towards the object
they want to observe. They depend on the natural light (sunlight) which can be
either reflected by the object or absorbed by it and then re-emitted. Passive RS
engines count with multispectral sensors which can capture the acquired quantity
with multiple band combinations. MODIS is an example of passive RS intrument.

olutions (250 m, 500 m and 1000 m). MODIS counts with two different spacecrafts

whole Earth’s surface every one to two days.

From a satellite’s point of view, the “surface” is whatever it sees when it looks
through the atmosphere to the ground. It could be snow and ice, or the roof of a
building.

The Standardized Precipitation Evapotranspiration Index (SPEI) [56] is a climatic

the long-term balance. Hence, pixels with positive values will correspond to pixels
where the precipitation was greater than the evapotranspiration, presenting the same
sign as the SM anomaly, where positive values mean pixels possessing a moisture
greater than the climatology.

WS anomaly influences, not only the fire activity, but the capacity of the land to
evapotranspirate, as repeatedly stated along the project. When strong winds blow,
particles of vapor move away which leads to a decrease in the amount of water vapor
in the atmosphere and thus an increase in evaporation. Hence, it also affects the
water content on the soil and, eventually, to the vegetation growing in that area.
So, WS has an effect on some of the other climate and vegetation variables which
run fire episodes. Furthermore, wind can contribute to spread a wildfire when it
acquires high values.
Finally, LST has been classified as the sixth most important variable. LST is an
appealing variable since it can be used as a predictor (as it reflects the temperature
of the soil, vegetation, or any surface covering the land) but it can be used as well
as an indicator of pasts wildfires. When a wildfire occurs, the temperature in land
immediately increases and, in turn, LST observations. Moreover, LST gets affected
with the change of vegetation cover over a region because this surface covering the
land changes. Therefore, if a fire occurs, damaging the vegetation of a region, it will
affect the LST too.

4
Conclusions & Outlook
To sum up, this thesis have focused in studying the driving factors and behaviour
of the wildfire events in two AOI (in California and Australia). The data used to
analyze these fire events was taken from the CCI programme (mainly measured
by MODIS), taking into account the period between 2000 and 2020.
By means
of climate and vegetation variables, a Random Forest regressor was implemented
in order to forecast the burned area caused by these wildfires.
Those variables
were pre-processed in order to achieve the same temporal and spatial resolution
to use as input in the model.
Furthermore, monthly anomalies of SM, LST an
WS products were performed which were useful to retrieve the first pre-modeling
results comparing them (along with the other variables) with the BA product were
discussed and analyzed. Results after modeling were also presented, inferring the
most significant variables when forecasting BA, which were, in that order: SPEI
1, SPEI 6, NDVI, SM anomaly 18, WS anomaly, LST and SM anomaly 30. The
prediction results were not satisfactory as expected, since the MSE was not able to
improve from a ”dummy model”. Nonetheless, the discussion of the problems faced
when implementing the model and possible issue explanation was preformed.
This project has shed some light in the process of forecasting wildfire events. For
further research, multiple factors can be taken into account in order to improve
the performance of the developed forecasting model. In first place, the AOI could
have been chosen to have similar climate all over the studied region, e.g., take into
account regions around the Mediterranean Sea which, even thought they can be very
separated in distance, the same climate conditions could help the model to count
with more observations of different places. Following, the short spatial scale used
was possibly too small. Instead of using single pixel observation, an average all over
those regions abovementioned could have been used in order to infer a more general
and wider insight of the problem. Moreover, the lack of human-related data such
as population in the region or the distance to the closest road could have been a
potential wildfire driver factor which has not been exploited.
Lastly, those pre-processing methods applied to the ”raw” data can be extrapolated
to other data sets and/or furthermore, use them in other projects where they could
be potentially used.
