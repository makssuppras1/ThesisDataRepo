Summary
Power over Ethernet allows power to be supplied along with data in the Ethernet cables
which are commonly used to connect devices to the internet. The power supplies used for
this are commonly designed with multiple components on a PCB and switching frequen-
cies below 500 kHz. While higher switching frequencies allows for smaller components,
these have generally been avoided as they introduce additional losses.
Due to advances in integrated technologies that allow higher on-chip voltages, it
is possible to design power supplies with a much higher degree of integration. Higher
switching frequencies are possible due to the possibility of integration, resulting in addi-
tional decrease in component sizes. By doing this the total size of the power supply can
be reduced hence increasing power density.
In this thesis a partially integrated 10 MHz half-bridge buck converter is designed,
including: power stage, gate drivers, level shifters, and bootstrap circuit. A literature
review is conducted for each part of the circuit, before a design topology is decided
upon. An initial design is then created and simulated across process variations. The
initial design is modified to accommodate for process variation and ensure correct op-
eration at all times. Finally the results are documented before design of the next part
is designed. After all blocks have been designed the whole system is simulated and the
results documented.
Efficiency above 90 % is reached in simulation, and the design is shown to be fully
operational across process and temperature variations. The efficiency comparable to
previous lower frequency designs, and demonstrates the potential that integrated power
supplies for Power over Ethernet applications have in comparison to previous work.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Over the last couple of decades there have been a general tendency towards making
electrical devices more battery powered.
While this development has increased the
mobility of many different applications most electrical devices still require a constant
connection to the power grid. This means that a power chord must be available at
the location of the device. For some devices which require reliable internet access an
Ethernet cable is also required.
To accommodate this reliance on physical connection to the grid, most buildings are
designed with a large number of wires and cables running inside the walls and in cable
trays. For devices which are placed far from power outlets, or in hard to reach places,
such as security cameras, the reliance on multiple cables may be an issue.
Power over Ethernet, often shortened to PoE, uses the Ethernet cables that provide
internet access to supply power to the device as well as transmit data. This means that
just a single cable is needed to supply the device with both power and access to the
internet [29]. This is especially useful in places where cables are costly due to cluttering
or where Ethernet outputs are readily available. PoE is commonly used in a number
of low power applications which require constant internet access. These applications
include: routers, security cameras, RFID scanners, clocks, amongst others [31].
The voltage supplied by the Ethernet cables is not a constant 48 V, but varies from
37 to 57 volts. A large variation, which makes it unsuited for supplying most devices
directly. To correct this a switch-mode power supply (SMPS) can be implemented to
transform the variable voltage from the input to a given constant voltage at the output.
Figure 1.1: PoE functionality illustrated.

As the voltage has been too high to integrate into a single chip, this power supply
is traditionally designed with multiple components on a printed circuit board (PCB).
However, as new developments within integrated chip design allows for > 70 volts on an
integrated chip, this allows the design of a power stage as well as all supporting circuitry,
such as gate drivers and control circuitry, to be fitted into a single chip.
Integrating the power supply into a single chip reduces parasitic capacitance and
inductance which enables higher switching speeds which in turn allows for smaller com-
ponents in filters and decoupling, which still needs to be placed outside of the chip. This
reduces the size of the circuit as a whole to increase the power density. Increased power
density allows for smaller devices, which is generally advantageous.
In this master thesis a partially integrated (SMPS) will be designed, to minimise the
area of the power supply which connects the PoE device to the Ethernet cable. The scope
of the thesis is focused around the integrated part of the circuit, and the functionality
and performance of this. The design process will be described in detail to allow the
reader an insight into the design procedure of such a device.
1.1
Specifications
There are a number of different Power over Ethernet standards, which can supply differ-
ent amount of power and have different voltage swings [31]. To properly design a SMPS
for PoE applications, specifications for the project must be defined.
Specifications for the project were made to reflect the IEEE PoE 802.3af standard
[13]. The 802.3af standard defines the input voltage range that SMPS must be able
to handle, as well as the maximum input power which can be supplied through the
Ethernet cables. An output voltage of 12 was chosen, as this results in a reasonable
voltage conversion ratio of 1/4 at the nominal input voltage, as well as being a common
supply voltage for many applications. A switching voltage of between 8 MHz and 15
MHz was chosen as the design window, to allow a trade-off between increase in switching
losses, faster system response, and smaller devices. The allowed frequency range is made
wide to allow a degree of freedom in the design. The entire specified frequency range
is higher than commonly seen in SMPS’s, designed for similar power, which is part of
what enables the integration of the SMPS. Finally a maximum operation temperature is
defined, ensuring that the device should be possible to handle without protective gloves.
The resulting specifications are:
• Input voltage: 37 V - 57 V
• Output voltage: 12 V
• Maximum input power: 12.95 W
• Switching frequency: 8 MHz - 15 MHz

1.2
Project Outline
The SMPS topology chosen for this thesis is a half-bridge buck converter, a choice which
will be explained in Chapter 2. The half-bridge buck converter design will consist of the
following five parts:
1. Power stage
2. Gate drivers
3. Level shifters
4. Bootstrap circuit
5. Performance
The power stage will be designed first, and includes the design of an output filter as
well as power MOSFETs. While the output filter is not part of the design in this project,
a simple filter must be in place to design the power stage correctly, as the output filter
determines the peak currents of the power stage. After the design of the power stage, the
gate drivers will be designed to ensure proper switching of the power MOSFETs. This is
followed by the design of the level shifter circuitry designed to supply the input signals to
the gate drivers. The Level shifter design will cosist of both a high-voltage level shifter
and a low voltage level shifter, as well as a delay block which follows the low-voltage
level shifter. Finally a bootstrap circuit will be designed to supply the high-side gate
driver and level shifter.
Figure 1.2: Illustration of the general design procedure which will be followed in this
thesis.
For each part of the design, a discussion of the design topology will be made in the
form of a literature review. After this review an initial design will be created, which is
then simulated to test its performance. If the initial design experience performance issues

CHAPTER 2
Power Stage
The first part of the system to be designed is the power stage, which is the last in-
tegrated circuit block before the external power filter. The design of the power stage
starts with a review of relevant available typologies to select the most suitable topol-
ogy for the application. The topology choice is followed by characterisation of available
MOSFET architectures, as well as optimising of MOSFET sizes to lower power losses.
Dimensioning of the output filter will also be discussed in this chapter as the output
filter influences the functionality of the power stage itself by determining the current
ripple, as well as the peak current. Before any of the design steps can commence, theory
on losses in power stages and its implications is examined.
2.1
Losses in a Power Stage
Losses in the power stage is expected to be the dominant source of losses in the SMPS,
as it handles almost all of the current flowing through the system. For this reason it
is important to understand the losses in the power stage before continuing the design
procedure. Since none of the different typologies that are discussed later include diodes
in their design, diode losses are not included as they are not relevant for the design
procedure in this thesis.
The losses in the power stage itself can generally be divided up into four parts, which
describe different aspects of losses in the switching devices of the power stage [27]. All
loses are calculated for the individual switching device, as a typology has not yet been
decided upon:
• Conduction losses - caused by the output current running through the on-resistance
RDS_on, referring to the equivalent ohmic resistance of a fully open MOSFET.
• Switching losses - caused by the discharging of the discharging of the MOSFET
drain-source capacitance (CDS) as it turns on.
• MOSFET gate-charge losses - caused by the charging and discharging the MOS-
FET gate in order to turn it on or off.
• Dead-time losses - caused by current being forced through the body-diode of a
MOSFET while it is turned off.

2.2
Topology Selection
The first step in the design of the power stage is to choose a suitable topology. As the
input voltage of the converter is between 37 V and 57 V, and the output voltage is 12
V, the topology only needs step-down capabilities. Since the goal is to integrate the
power stage, all topologies with capacitors, inductors or transformers included in the
power stage is discarded, as these cannot be fully integrated at 10 MHz. This includes
topologies such as the flyback converter [10], the Series Capacitor Buck Converter [28]
and the Ćuk-2 converter [7], which were all considered for the design.
Two different typologies which live up to the criteria set above have been selected
for further consideration:
• A classical synchronous-rectified half-bridge buck [10], Figure 2.2.
• A 4-switch buck converter [1], Figure 2.3.
Both of these converters use the same first-order output filter, which makes the main
difference between them the amount of MOSFETs and how the losses are distributed
among these.
The classical half-bridge buck converter (HBB converter) as described in [10] is the
simplest topology available. Implementing this topology will reduce die area, as only
two power MOSFETs and gate drivers are required, along with only a single high-voltage
level shifter. This simplicity indicates that the circuit will take up less overall chip area
and pull less power gate drivers and level shifters than typologies with a larger number
of MOSFETs. The drawback is that both of the power MOSFETs in the HBB converter
experience the full VDD_In across them before switching, resulting in high switching
losses in both MOSFETs in every switching period. As described in (2.2) the losses
are proportional to the voltage squared, making switching losses a serious issue in this
simple topology. The conduction losses on the other hand are relatively minor, as only
two MOSFETs contribute to these losses in the HBB-converter.
The 4-switch buck converter presented in [1] has the same functionality as the HBB-
converter, but uses two equally sized input capacitors to allow for a cascading of MOS-
FETs. The cascading of MOSFETs means that the voltage across each in any given
switching period will be VDD_In/2. As the switching losses increase quadratically with

The MOSFETs should be re-sized to find a new optimum for the 4-switch buck

shifter circuitry, as well as an expectation of a smaller overall area were primary consid-

2.3
MOSFET Characterisation
There are a number of different MOSFET device models available for the power stage,
including both P- and N-type MOSFETs.
Those of the available MOSFETs with a
maximum blocking voltage high enough to handle the maximum VDD_In = 57 V are
characterised in this section, in order to select the best suited MOSFETs for the power
stage. MOSFETs with voltage ratings just below the maximum VDD_In are also charac-
terised for comparison. While NMOS modes are generally better suited for the integrated
application due to a larger µCox, the PMOS transistors are also characterised for refer-
ence. The use of a PMOS as a high-side MOSFET would allow the converter to operate
without a bootstrap circuit, which is otherwise needed to supply the gate voltage of the
high-side NMOS.
To properly compare the best different MOSFET device models it is important to
make it clear which parameters are the most important. As the MOSFETs are used as
power MOSFETs the most relevant parameter is the RDS_on of a unit sized device. A
lower unit RDS_on will result in a smaller overall device, which reduces total chip area
as well parasitic capacitance. Since increased parasitic capacitance results in increased
switching losses in the power stage, this RDS_on of a unit sized device is the most relevant
parameter to compare in this case. Capacitance is not characterised for a unit size device,
as MOSFET capacitance does not scale linearly in the same way that resistance does,
as well as being dependant on how the MOSFET pins are connected.
All transistors have been characterised with a unit width of 10 µm, and at minimum
length. This length is not always the same, as the process does not allow change in the
length of all MOSFET models. Since the power devices in question will always be sized

it is preferable to have a smaller MOSFET to minimize the parasitic capacitance, and
thus the switching losses. This means that a larger RDS_on will be present, as the the
point where losses contribute equally is reached with a smaller MOSFET size, and thus
a larger RDS_on.

this application the maximum allowable voltage drop across the low-side MOSFET, is the

is too large, the body-diode of the device will be conducting some of the current passing
through, even when the MOSFET is turned on. This introduces additional diode losses
and should be avoided.

dead-time of tDT = 2 ns was chosen for the simulation. This results in a total switching

CHAPTER 3
Gate Driver
The function of the gate driver is to supply the current needed to switch the MOSFETs
in the power stage. Two gate drivers are needed for the power stage designed in Chapter
2, one for each power MOSFET. There are three main concerns when designing a gate
driver. The first two are the rise- and fall-time of the output, and the propagation delay
through the gate driver. The rise- and fall-time determines the switching times of the
power MOSFETs, while the propagation delay is the time it takes the switching signal
to get from the input of the gate driver to the power MOSFETs. A trade-off is usually
present, where a shorter propagation delay and rise time can be achieved by a larger
and more power consuming circuit. The last concern is the power consumption which
should be minimised as much as possible, within the chosen specifications for the gate
driver.
It is important to ensure that the propagation delay of the high-side gate driver is
the same as that of the low-side gate driver, to avoid the need for additional dead-time
in power stage. If these two propagation delays are unequal, additional dead-time will
have to be added to ensure that the switching in the power stage does not overlap due
to skewed propagation delays. Increased dead-time results in greater losses, as current
is forced through the low-side body-diode. During the dead-time the body-diode of the
low-side MOSFET will be conducting as the inductor forces current through it while
both of the switches are off. This increases losses which makes it is preferable to have
as little dead-time as possible. If equal propagation delay is achieved, the propagation
delay is only a concern in controller design as very large delays may inhibit the controller
speed.
In the design of the gate driver a number of design specifications are chosen to guide
the design process: Rise- and fall-times of tr < 1 ns and tf < 1 ns should be achieved
across all corners. This will allow a maximum dead-time of tDT = 6 ns (3 ns for each
transition) and still ensure that the switching time of the converter is less that 10 % of
the switching period Ts. While a tDT < 6 ns is preferable, switching times faster than 1
ns is not required, as faster switching times will lead to higher gate driver losses while
not improving efficiency in the power stage significantly. All gate driver designs are
initially simulated with a capacitive load equal to that of the power MOSFETs. This
gate capacitance has been found to be CG = 105.8 pF.
The gate drivers are designed for a 4.5 V supply voltage, as this is the low-side supply
voltage (VDDL). 4.5 V was chosen as the low-side supply voltage, as a 5 V supply results
in voltages of VGS > 5.5 V in the last of the gate driver inverters, which is above the

Improved Design
As the inverter stages increase in size by a factor of 9.2 between each stage, the current
draw increases by the same factor, as described in Shichman-Hodges equation (2.16).
This means that the last stage (I5) will pull 9.2 times as much current as the preceding
stage I4, which pulls 9.2 times more than I3, and so on. This also means that cross-
conduction at the switching moment becomes an issue in the final inverter stage, as the
current running through each MOSFET is significant. A way to avoid this shoot-through
current is to implement a dead-time circuit for the gate driver. A way to design this
dead-time circuit is to implement a break-before-make (BBM) circuit as suggested in [9],
using an AND and an OR gate, connected as shown in Figure 3.6.

since it is a very robust design which ensures correct operation over all corners at all
times. Both of these things are an advantage when the gate driver is placed in a floating
well. The drawback of the chosen design is an increased propagation delay, as the signal

CHAPTER 4
Level Shifters
A floating high-side gate driver requires a circuit which can generate a floating input
signal which floats together with the gate driver.
The duty cycle and frequency is
generated by a modulator which is generally not designed to be floating. This means
that a circuit is needed to shift this input signal from the low voltage modulator up to
the floating voltage of up to 61.5 V. This is the purpose of the high voltage level shifter
(HVLS).
The HVLS needs to be able to shift the modulator signal from 4.5 V up to a maximum
of 61.5 V which is the maximum input VDD_In + 4.5 V. This should be done using as little
power as possible and without introducing a propagation delay which is prohibitively
large for any later control circuit design.
Figure 4.1: Block diagram of high- and low-side level shifter circuit, input signals are
between 0 V and 4.5 V.
To ensure that the propagation delay of the low-side matches this increased high-
side propagation delay, a low voltage level shifter (LVLS) along with a delay block is
implemented on the low-side. Figure 4.1 shows a block diagram showing what will be
designed in this chapter. The HVLS will be designed first, and the LVLS will then be
designed along with a delay block to ensure equal propagation delays.
4.1
High Voltage Level Shifter
A traditional HVLS consisting of two branches (Q1 - Q6) is shown in Figure 4.2. There
are three cascaded MOSFETs in each branch which discharge and charge their respective
==PAGE:58==
42
4 Level Shifters
branch depending on the input signal. The two inverters are there to ensure a binary
output. The traditional HVLS has a number of issues in its design. A propagation delay
of more than 200 ns is to be expected, which is slow considering the 100 ns switching
period of the converter. This slow rise time is caused by the fact that the MOSFETs
are not operating in the active region through the entire transition. The slow operation
also makes the power consumption higher as the level shifter consumes power when it
switches the signal from low to high voltage. All of this results in a generally slow and
power consuming level shifter [23]. The traditional HVLS does not latch the output
either which makes it prone to output fluctuations when the VDDH changes.
Figure 4.2: Simple high voltage level shifters.
As a traditional HVLS is not suitable for this project a different HVLS design must be
implemented. This design will be performed by first conducting a literature search, then
performing design calculations and finally testing the resulting design across corners.
4.1.1
Literature Search
A literature search has been conducted to find a suitable design for the HVLS which
should be designed to have an output capable of handling large changes in its VDDH
without fluctuations, as well be designed to prevent voltage spikes which might break
the MOSFETs in the level shifter. It should also be fully on-chip integrateable and be
power efficient.
All designs with constant power consumption has been discarded, as power consump-
tion is a primary design consideration. Designs which feature internal capacitors were
also discarded as on-chip capacitors takes up a large amount of chip area. Examples of
==PAGE:59==
4.1 High Voltage Level Shifter
43
such level shifters can be seen in [4], [16], [17] and [25]. This left two pulse-triggered
designs with very low power consumption described in [18] and [19], as well as an im-
proved versions of a regular HVLS presented in [23] to be considered. Pulse triggered
designs are generally more power efficient but has a tendency to be more sensitive to
changes in supply voltage than other level shifters. For this reason, both then pulse
triggered HVLS’s and the improved HVLS should be considered for implementation in
this project. All three designs consist of two high voltage branches made up of a number
of cascaded MOSFETs. These two branches react to the low voltage input signal and
the charges one branch to VDDH, while discharging the other branch from VDDH to gnd.
All additional circuitry as well as the nature of the input signal depends on typology.
The design presented in [18] is illustrated in Figure 4.3. This design uses an input
pulse to to pull down the voltage of one of the two branches to increase the current pull of
the top MOSFETs Q5 and Q6. This current is then mirrored by a current mirror which
changes the state of an output latch that is connected to the floating high-side supply.
The issue with this design method is that the change in output is directly triggered
by a short pulldown at VSet or VReset caused by an input pulse, this makes the design
highly prone to output instability when the VDDH floats up or down with the switch node
voltage VSW. Issues may also occur with the voltages across the top PMOS transistors
(Q3 and Q4), as the voltage across these can be pulled below their maximum VGS rating
by the pulse. This is especially true for applications where the supply voltage changes
such as this, as it may increase current pull causing larger fluctuations than otherwise.
For these reasons the design from [18] was not chosen.

The design which was finally decided upon, is the design presented in [23] and shown
in Figure 4.5. This design is not pulse triggered, and thus retain the PMOS transistors
Q3 and Q4 which shield the top MOSFETs (Q5, Q6, Q9, and Q10) from having their
drain voltages pulled lower than VSSH. This way it is ensured that Q5, Q6, Q9 and Q10
cannot exceed their voltage rating. This version of HVLS also uses active switching of
Q5 and Q6, by implementing buffers on each of the branches consisting of I2-I5, which
actively switch the gates of the top MOSFETs instead of using a cross coupling like the
one seen in Figure 4.2. The MOSFETs Q7 and Q8 primarily function as diodes, as their
body diode prevents the voltage of their drain from dropping more that one diode drop
below the source voltage. Finally two additional MOSFETs (Q9 and Q10) have been
added in parallel to the top MOSFETs Q5 and Q6, in order to supply more current in
the switching moment, resulting in smaller propagation delays. A latch ensures that
the output remains constant even when the VDDH and VSSH changes.

The next part to design is the latch and the addition of two inverters to the output,

Replacing the cross-coupling with a buffer results in a significant increase in perfor-
mance [23] by significantly increasing the amount of current the top PMOS transistor
Q5 and Q6 can pull before the transition is complete. By making the buffer sensitive to
the initial drop in the VS, which happens right after a change in the input, both buffers
will go low. These buffers then drive the PMOS transistors with a VGS of 5 V, instead of
the lower gate voltage delivered by the cross coupling of Figure 4.2. The fact that both
PMOS are pulling current at this point is not an issue, as the rest of the transistors have
a current pull large enough to discharge VS sufficiently until a steady state is reached.
The latch is made by combining two NAND gates which are designed as described
in [3]. Designing the latch this way ensures that the output does not change unless both

4.2
Low Voltage Level Shifter
A low voltage level shifter has been designed to ensure that the input signal of the
low-side gate driver matches its supply voltage. It is also important that a delay block
is implemented on the low-side to ensure equal propagation delay from the modulator
to the gate of both high- and low-side power MOSFET. A delay block is required for
the LVLS as the HVLS is inherently slower than the LVLS, due to the higher voltage
that the output has to be shifted to. In the initial design the LVLS changes the voltage
from 5 V to 5 V which requires no complicated circuitry. The LVLS circuit is mostly
implemented to make it easier for later iterations of the SMPS design, to change the
modulator output voltage as it ensures that the correct voltage reaches the gate driver.

complimentary input signals. Figure 4.15 shows this basic design. The design is made
completely symmetrical, including the output buffers to ensure matching capacitance.
This is in spite of the fact that only one of these output buffers will be used as the

CHAPTER 5
Bootstrap Circuit
To ensure that the floating high-side supply of the high-side gate driver as well as the
HVLS, a bootstrap circuit capable of supplying sufficient current is required. The boot-
strap circuit in this project is designed as can be seen in Figure 5.1, with a diode (D1)
and a capacitor (Cboot).
Figure 5.1: Bootstrap circuit.
The capacitor is charged through the diode while the low-side power MOSFET is
conducting and the low-side supply VDDL is higher than the switch-node voltage VSW.
While the high-side power MOSFET conducts, VSW will be equal to the VDD_In and the
diode will be reverse biased, allowing the capacitor to supply the high-side gate driver
and HVLS with a voltage equal to VDDH = VSW + VDDL.
The diode D1 will be integrated into the chip, and as there is only a single available
diode model available in the process capable of handling more than the 57 V required,
that model is chosen. The diode is capable of blocking a maximum of 70 V, and in this
design it will be designed to have a voltage drop close to that of the power MOSFET
body-diode of around 650 mV. A forward voltage of 674.4 mV is achieved with an area
of 50 µm · 50 µm = 250 µm2, and will be used for the bootstrap circuit. Matching the
forward voltage of the bootstrap diode is important, since a lower forward voltage may
cause a larger high-side supply voltage, resulting in potential overvoltages.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
An integrated half-bridge power stage with gate drivers, level shifter circuitry, and boot-
strap circuit, have been successfully designed, and simulated in all corners and with
temperature variations. The SMPS has been designed to work with a switching fre-
quency of 10 MHz, to meet the specification given in the introduction.
A power stage has been designed first, after reviewing different compatible typologies,
and choosing the topology which was deemed most suitable. During the design process,
a characterisation and selection of available MOSFET models and architectures has
been done, as well as a dimensioning of the chosen MOSFETs to attempt to reach the
lowest possible losses in the power stage. The power stage was tested across corners,
temperatures and input voltages ranging from 37 V to 57 V, and correct functionality
has been confirmed in all cases. Losses in the power stage were also estimated to be
used for later comparison and showed promising results.
Gate driver circuits has been designed for the power stage, and dimensioned for power
efficiency. Different methods for estimating the optimal number of inverter stages have
been presented, before a 5-stage gate driver was chosen. The gate drivers were designed
with BBM functionality to improve power efficiency. Simulation across corners and with
floating supply reach rise- and fall-times of less than 1 ns in all cases, on both high- and
low-side.
To generate the input signals of the gate drivers, a level shifter circuitry was designed
for both high- and low-side gate drivers.
The HVLS implemented for the high-side
was chosen for its robustness and speed amongst several different available designs. It
was initially designed symmetrically, but modified to an asymmetric version to improve
performance when operated asymmetrically, as this will be the primary operation of the
HVLS in the SMPS. A LVLS as well as a delay block was designed for the low-side gate
driver, to mirror the functionality and propagation delay of the high-side. Both high-
and low-side circuitry was simulated to ensure functionality across corners, temperatures
and different input voltages. Correct functionality and low skew was demonstrated in
all cases.
Finally a bootstrap was designed to supply all high-side circuitry, and dimensioned
after the initial estimates of power consumption from each design.
The entire design including all blocks has been simulated across corners and with
different temperatures, as well as with input voltages ranging from 37 V to 57 V and
exhibit correct functionality in all cases. A final efficiency above 90 % is reached, which
==PAGE:94==

is comparable to other PoE power converter designs and higher efficiency than other 10
MHz converters in the same power range. The converter achieves all specifications, and
reaches high efficiency compared to other high-frequency power converters in the same
power range. This demonstrates great promise in the design of high frequency power
converters using high-voltage CMOS processes, to improve power density in areas such
as PoE.

Future Work
During the course of the project, several ways to improve the existing design, in the
form of additional work, has been found. These are the next steps in the design and
improvement of the SMPS. None of the suggestions are part of the original scope of the
thesis, and as such they fall into the future works category.
Level Shifter Enable Circuit
The enable signal for the high voltage level shifter should be generated by a start-up
circuit, which needs a separate high-voltage level shifter with a latch to ensure correct
start-up.
Figure 8.1: Initial design proposal for the start-up circuit generating the EN-signal to
the HVLS.
This start-up HVLS must be designed and tested across corners like the rest of the
circuit to ensure that it works correctly when connected to the rest of the circuit. An
initial design has been tested in the typical corner, and shows promising initial results.
The initial design is a modified version of the HVLS from Chapter 4, which has been
designed for this thesis. Since transition times are not a concern, most of the circuitry
==PAGE:96==
80
8 Future Work
has been stripped from the design to save power while keeping the latch to ensure a stable
output. The gate of Q3 and Q4 has been connected directly to the output to turn them
permanently off as soon as the output goes high, thus reducing power consumption. To
prevent the output from charging by accident, a small NMOS (Q7) connects the output
to VSSH, to discharge it over time.
The design has been simulated in the typical corner and shows promising results,
as the output is kept stable with a continuous power consumption of
3 mW. For this
reason the main cost of implementing this circuit is the extra area. Calibration of the
MOSFET sizes is likely to reduce power and/or area additionally.
The design was not included in the main part of the report as it is part of the start-up
circuit, and since it has not been tested across corners or optimised for power and size.
The completion of the enable circuit should be conducted before the chip is ready for
layout.
Controller design
Implementation of a control loop should be implemented to ensure that the output
remains at a constant 12 V, as the input varies. Redesigning of the output filter might
be part of the controller design if a different filter response is needed for the hypothetical
controller specifications. This should not constitute a problem for the power stage as
long as the output inductor is not reduced in size.
While the control could both be designed externally, with only a single input into the
chip itself, it is also possible to design the control loop on-chip. Designing the controller
as a part of the integrated chip would allow much of the control circuitry to be produced
on the same chip as the SMPS, thus reducing area. The limitations to an integrated
control circuit is the fact that large resistors and capacitors take up a lot of space, and
would likely have to be placed externally in all cases.
The implementation of the controller could be as simple as implementing a PID-
controller to ensure a stable output, as described in [10]. While this is the standard
approach to designing controllers for SMPS’s, more advanced control-solutions, such as
state based controllers, are also a possibility. This is especially the case if the controller
is implemented either completely or partially on-chip.
Adaptive Dead-time
The biggest future improvement that could be implemented to the SMPS is likely to be
an adaptive dead-time circuit. While the implementation of such a circuit is not straight-
forward, correct implementation might further improve the efficiency of the SMPS as
a whole. For an adaptive dead-time circuit to be successfully implemented it would
have to modify the dead-time by time-steps significantly smaller than the < 1 ns. The
implementation of such a circuit is likely an entire project in and of itself.
==PAGE:97==
8 Future Work
81
The adaptive dead-time circuit would decrease dead-time losses in the power stage.
On top of this, the power stage would approach zero-voltage switching with a correctly
implemented adaptive dead-time circuit.
By getting closer to zero-voltage switching
the switching losses are reduced as the capacitor CDS is discharged before the switching
moment, resulting in lower losses.

Introduction
The Ethernet cables used for providing internet operates at a voltage of 48V. While it is common
to think of these cables as useful only for transferring data, they are capable of delivering energy
up to up to 12.95 Watts along with the data. This is useful for a lot of diﬀerent applications
where a separate power supply might be an issue. This could be routers, cameras, data receivers
or other devices which might be placed in areas without a lot of readily available power sources
or where installing power cables is impractical or expensive.
Devices with some kind of computational power will generally include a microprocessor
along with other electronic parts, none of which generally runs on a 48V supply voltage. This
means that the application in question will need some type of internal power converter capable
of transforming the 48V of the Ethernet down to a voltage which is usable for the application.
This project focuses on designing an integrated power supply for 12V application of the
kind mentioned above. This means that the voltage level needs to be reduced from 48V down
to 12V. This is complicated by the fact that the Ethernet supply voltage is not at a constant
48V, but is instead rated to 37V - 57V with an average of 48V. The output needs to be constant
at all input voltages, and at varying loads.
This project plan contains an overview over how this project is supposed to be conducted.
A detailed description of the project as well as the learning objectives relevant for this project
will be presented. Additionally, a schedule has been created to to show the milestones in the
project as well as highlight the diﬀerent parts of the project that needs to be designed and the
order in which the will be made. Finally the resources available in this project has been listed.

Project Description
Main objective
Designing and simulating a functioning 48v to 12V DC/DC converter for use in Power over
Ethernet (PoE) applications. The design is made according to the following speciﬁcations:
• Input voltage: 35V to 60V
• Output voltage: 12V
• Power: 12.95W
• Frequency: min. 8 Mhz, max. 15 MHz
What will be designed
The DC/DC converter will consist of a power stage where the dimensions of the power MOS-
FETs needs to be determined as well as the other components. Furthermore, both the gate
driver and voltage level-shifter connected to this driver should be designed for the DC/DC
converter. Control of the converter is not in the scope of this project.
How should it be done
The power stage will ﬁrst be researched and after a ﬁtting topology is chosen for the power
stage the design process will begin. the design will be created in cadence where VerilogA will
be used as a stand-in for the control circuit and gate driver while the design of the power stage
is ongoing. After the power stage is functional the design of the gate driver will commence with
the same process as for the power stage.

E.1
Introduction
This project evaluation is included to provide insight into how the time spend on my
M.Sc. thesis has been spent. The deviations from the original time schedule are dis-
cussed, as well as how design issues where handled along the way. A short evaluation of
my own performance over the course of the thesis is given at the end.
E.2
Deviations from Schedule
The original schedule has been followed for most of the 5-month period, with only the
level-shifter design resulting in significant delays. The final time usage can be found in
Figure E.1.
The delays in the level-shifter design where primarily caused due to issues in selecting
the correct topology. Initially a pulse triggered design was chosen, and simulations with
a constant voltage supply was completed. This resulted in a large number of issues when
simulation with floating voltage started, as the topology was unfit for the application.
The result was that I had to go back and choose a new topology, to begin the design
process over from the beginning.
The buffer time was primarily used to ensure that simulations across corners had
correct functionality, and with simulations times of above 1 hour, this can take up a
lot of time if any issues arise. The main issue in the corner simulations was that the
efficiency measurements exhibited strange behaviour, which was identified at coming
from the resonance frequency of the output filter. This resonance frequency changes
with load, and at some loads it requires significantly longer settling times than the initial
simulation times. After the problem was identified the problem was quickly solved, and
the writing of the thesis could commence.
E.3
Self Evaluation
The project has taken up most of the available time of the day across the entire 5-month
period. However, by treating each milestone as a deadline I managed to complete most
of the milestones within the original time schedule. I also had large success with writing
up a draft for each chapter as soon as the work described in it was completed, as this
made it easier to document the process later on. Long hours and no holidays or weekends
for long periods of time has been quite taxing in periods, especially as days got shorter
and some days were completed without seeing direct daylight. My efficiency has been
high overall, and the final product has been completed on-time.
