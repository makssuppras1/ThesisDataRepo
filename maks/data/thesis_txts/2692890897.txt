Abstract 
Techniques for the replication of organically-shaped structures using the AM-assisted 
investment casting (IC) process were explored with 3D scanning methods. Four different 
objects were selected to test different parts of the process. For example, one object needed 
to be extracted from a pattern and core box intended for sand casting, pushing the 
boundaries of the conventional use of 3D scanners. To examine where error was introduced 
into the process, different stages including 3D printing and casting were compared to the 
original structure using 3D scanning and color mapping. Both structured light scanning (SLS) 
and photogrammetry were used, with some models being scanned using both methods to 
make a comparison. Procedures and equipment for all steps in the AM-assisted IC process 
were developed, with numerous practical activities and experiments being performed at 
each stage.  
Three different types of gating systems were attempted: bottom, top, and tree gating. It was 
found that top gating was the easiest and most straightforward approach, while bottom-
gating and tree-gating suffered from structural issues and shell cracking, respectively. At 
least two shells for each object were produced and cast in aluminum using a top gating 
layout. One received an experimental dewaxing treatment while the other was subjected to 
burnout without dewaxing. Most castings were found to suffer from various degrees of 
misrun and other defects. However, unaffected areas were shown to have minimal deviation, 
rivaling industrial standards for light-alloy investment castings. The dewaxing treatment did 
not appear to produce any notable differences in the casting. Additional casting trials were 
performed at an increased temperature in an attempt to eliminate defects. This appeared to 
be partially successful, though it did not completely resolve all issues. SLS and 
photogrammetry were also compared. When properly scaled to one another, these scans 
showed small deviations that were mainly a result of photogrammetry producing a slightly 
lower resolution scan. Various improvements to the generated procedures are 
recommended for practical/safety reasons, though it is clear that the casting step requires 
the most attention for an increase in quality.

1. Introduction 
Investment casting, also known as lost wax casting, is one of the most precise casting 
processes available [1] [2]. In this process, a pattern of the desired part is typically produced 
by injecting wax in a semisolid or liquid state into metallic dies [3]. This pattern is then 
attached to a gating system which controls the flow of the melt during casting. Afterwards, 
the pattern is invested, or given a shell, by repeatedly dipping it into a slurry. The wax can 
then be removed by application of heat, leaving a hollow shell into which molten metal is 
poured. Finally, the shell is destroyed, and the casting extracted. This usually results in a part 
that requires little to no finishing afterwards [3]. 
Investment casting is particularly useful for producing parts of complex shape. In other 
casting processes, such as traditional sand casting, the pattern must be extracted by splitting 
the mould open across a parting line. This fact requires a pattern of a certain shape; 
undercuts relative to the parting line must be avoided, lest the number of moving parts be 
increased. Solutions such as adding a core or gluing multiple sand moulds together increase 
tooling costs, labor, and the chance for something go wrong. They are also highly impractical 
for smaller parts. Investment casting techniques offer a good alternative because the parting 
line is often simpler or can avoided all together.   
Up until recently, however, the investment casting process was only economical for large 
production volumes due to high tooling costs associated with creating the wax pattern [4]. 
This situation has changed with the advent of additive manufacturing (AM). Fused 
deposition modeling (FDM) 3D printers can now be used to print complex parts in wax or 
other compatible materials. Using such techniques, unique, organically-shaped wax patterns 
can be made without the need for costly metallic dies or specialized injection machines. 
Indeed, the 3D printing process can be used to create complex patterns with a shorter cycle 
and lead time, as well as a lower cost [5]. 
The resulting decrease in cost has enabled investment casting to be performed economically 
at much smaller scales. This report seeks to explore AM-assisted investment casting in a 
facility that is otherwise not well equipped for large scale production. To evaluate the 
versatility and precision of the process, a number of organically-shaped objects are 
replicated in aluminum using the technique and 3D scanned at different stages of the 
process. These stages are then compared to determine sources of error and identify where 
improvements can be made.

2. Methods & Project Work 
2.1. Summary of Process 
2.1.1. Project Summary 
This report describes the process in which a reliable setup for producing small investment 
castings using additive manufacturing techniques was made and evaluated. Each step in the 
process was explored to determine a practical approach, as well as produce clear procedures 
and document remaining issues. After each stage was made functional, the entire process 
was implemented to produce several castings, with results being measured at various stages 
using 3D scanning techniques for comparative analysis.  
Initial work began with setting up FDM 3D-printer hardware to see if high-quality prints 
could be made reliably. Despite calibration attempts and numerous hardware upgrades, the 
original, provided setup was found to be highly impractical. Unfixable issues stemming from 
machine design as well as a difficult to use filament led to poor results. This issue was 
eventually resolved by replacing the entire system with a more reliable setup.  
Once repeatable, high quality prints could be made, different 3D scanning techniques were 
explored. This included structured light scanning (SLS) and photogrammetry. Four different 
objects were scanned using either method, with two objects being scanned using both 
methods for comparative purposes. Each of the models presented its own challenge – one 
required that it be extracted from scans of a pattern and core box intended for sand-casting. 
These scans produced 3D models that required further refinement in order to be compatible 
with 3D printing. For this purpose, a cleanup procedure was established and then performed.  
With models able to be 3D printed and scanned, work was then done to make them possible 
to cast using the IC process. Initially, a bottom gating system was designed using principles 
from the work of John Campbell. This system was then manufactured, but structural issues 
prevented its use in practice. As alternatives, top and tree gating systems were explored. Top 
gating was found to be the easiest and most straightforward approach, while tree-gating 
suffered issues in shell-making steps. Procedures and equipment for shell-making, burnout, 
casting, and knockout were also developed, with numerous experiments being performed at 
each stage. For example, experiments were performed on different types of filament to try 
to determine an ideal temperature for dewaxing.   
With all the different procedures established, three of the four models were put through the 
entire process. At least two castings were attempted for each model, with one undergoing an 
experimental dewaxing treatment. The original models, 3D prints, and resulting castings 
were then compared using color mapping techniques, as shown in Section 3. A basic 
flowchart of work performed in this project is shown in Figure 2.1.1-1 below. A step-by-step 
breakdown of the full process is described below, with each stage being described in detail 
in a subsequent section.

2.1.2. Full Procedure 
The project work resulted in the following procedure for replicating organically-shaped 
objects using the AM-assisted IC technique: 
1. The objects are selected. 
2. Each object is 3D scanned via SLS and/or photogrammetry.  
3. The models are digitally manipulated and prepared for 3D printing.  
4. A gating system is designed and prepared for manufacture. The models are printed, 
scanned, and attached to a gating system, though not necessarily in that order.  
5. The pattern is dipped in a ceramic slurry and layered with stucco, building up a 
ceramic shell. 
6. The shell is de-waxed and/or fired at a high temperature, hollowing out the shell. 
7. The shell is pre-heated and molten metal (e.g., aluminum) is poured in and allowed 
to cool. 
8. The shell is removed using force (the knockout process). 
9. The casting is scanned and compared to other scans using color mapping techniques. 
All steps are described in detail in the sections that follow, with some sections deferring to 
the appendix in the interest of readability. Numerous practical activities were necessary to 
carry out the full procedure, including: the setup and calibration of two 3D printers; the 
design, simulation, and manufacture of a universal gating system, as well as a ‘dip plate’ to 
streamline the shell-making process; manufacturing a new ventilation duct for burnout; the 
setup of a casting area; and the production of a glovebox for knockout. The experimental 
procedure is summarized in Figure 2.1.2-1 below.

2.3. 3D Scanning of Models 
Being of organic shape with few well-defined features, the selected geometry in Section 2.2 
cannot easily be recreated in traditional CAD software. Instead, the structures must be 
digitized via 3D scanning. Two options for 3D scanning are explored and compared in this 
report: SLS and photogrammetry. The two methods are shown side-by-side in Figure 2.3-1 
below, with scanning procedures and general remarks outlined in following sections. The 
result of the scanning process is an .STL or .OBJ file in which the part has been polygonised 
into upwards of a million triangles. In both methods, the files undergo a decimation process 
that reduces model detail to 500,000 vertices. This makes subsequent cleanup operations 
less computationally intensive. Though the decimation process reduces model quality 
slightly, the effect appears to be largely negligible (see Section A2.3 in the appendix).  
If feasible, models were scanned using both methods for comparison later on (See Section 
2.11). SLS scans were done of the Bird, Gecko, and Elephant (pattern and core box). 
Photogrammetry scans were performed on the Bird, Gecko, and Bust. 
 
Figure 2.3-1: A view of the two 3D scanning methods in action, with the Bird as an 
example. a) SLS using the ATOS Triple Scan, and b) photogrammetry using a Nikon D7000 
camera.  
2.3.1. Structured Light Scanning 
In structured light scanning (SLS), narrow bands of light are projected onto the subject. The 
bands are distorted as they pass over the object being scanned, and these distortions are 
converted to 3D coordinates. With the exception of the Bust, all of the objects described in 
Section 2.2 were scanned using SLS. Scanning equipment consisted of the ATOS Triple Scan 
fit with the MV100 lens, with processing done using ATOS Professional 2018 software. This 
setup measures about 8,100,000 points per scan in a volume of approximately 10x75x70 
mm3. The scanner utilizes blue light to filter out ambient light [7].  
To avoid glare, all objects utilizing this scanner were first sprayed with a layer of ReflexEx 
Medium (titanium dioxide) over a piece of cardboard. Layers of this material produce a matte 
texture that should be easy to wipe off after scanning is complete. As shown in Figure 2.3.1-

such that at least three dots were visible from all directions. These dots are needed for the 
software to locate the part as it rotated; at least three are needed in each view. It should be 
noted that placing the dots on curved surfaces produces a mark; the dots are digitally 
removed by the software and replaced with planes. Such marks can be digitally removed by

Prior to every scanning session, the scanner was allowed to warm up for 15 minutes and 
calibrated. The process involves placing a calibration artifact – a plate covered with precisely 
measure dots – at various angles and distances from the scanner.  
Scanning consists of holding the part in a vise or attaching it securely to an external piece 
using poster tack. The object is manually rotated and scanned from many different angles, 
with the objective of leaving as few holes in the point cloud as possible. Multiple scan sets 
are needed to produce both sides of the model due to the presence of a fixture, which is 
digitally removed once scanning is complete. Scans were done using the ‘Fast scan’ and ‘More 
points’ options for practical purposes; other settings greatly increase the time of scans from 
a few hours to full days. 
After the completion of the scanning process, the digital model was carefully inspected in the 
ATOS software to remove as many artifacts as possible. It is generally easier to do it in this 
software rather than in the Blender as described in subsequent steps (See Section 2.4). Scans 
are decimated to 500,000 vertices prior to being exported as .STL files.  
After initial object scans were performed, it was discovered that smaller 0.4 mm reference 
dots were also available, which produce smaller marks on the scans. These were utilized 
when scanning cast and 3D printed parts, which are produced in subsequent steps. It should 
be noted that the supposedly easy to remove spray is in fact quite difficult to remove from 
3D prints without introducing water, which is not good for the burnout process. Attempts to 
remove it included brushing with a toothbrush as well as a wire brush, but neither solution 
completely removed the spray. However, the presence of a thin layer of titanium dioxide 
appears to have had little, if any effect on the final results.

2.3.2. Photogrammetry 
Digital models can also be created via photogrammetry. This method involves using software 
to extract 3D features from overlapping photographs. This reconstructs the object as a 3D 
model. Because photos can be taken using an ordinary camera, the method is significantly 
more accessible than SLS, which requires expensive equipment and a controlled 
environment that may be limited in size. The ability of photogrammetry to function outdoors 
makes it an ideal solution for objects such as the Bust, which are immobile and/or cannot be 
fit with reference dots.  
Photogrammetry scans were taken of the Bird, Gecko, and Bust using a Nikon D7000 DSLR 
camera with a zoomable 18-200 mm lens, steadied by a heavy tripod. The Elephant was not 
included in the photogrammetry scanning process due to the difficulty in the reconstruction 
process from the pattern and core box. In each of the remaining three cases, a feature of 
known length was identified and measured using a ruler. This was used to scale the object in 
subsequent steps (see Sections A2.4.0.1 and A2.4.3 in the appendix). Below, important

Photogrammetry scans were performed under carefully chosen diffuse lighting conditions. 
The Bird and Gecko were captured in a white room with incandescent lighting (see Figure 
2.3-1 b above and Figure A2.3.2.1-1 in the appendix). The Bust was more difficult to capture

highly overcast day (see Figure 2.3.2-1 below). Scans consisted of taking a series of pictures 
in concentric circles around the object at roughly 5-10˚ intervals. Each successive circle had 
a different elevation. The height of the camera was changed from the highest to lowest 
feasible allowed by the tripod in increments of approximately 6 cm. At least 175 images were 
taken for all objects scanned using photogrammetry.  
After scanning, the pictures were then examined for issues. Blurry or poorly exposed images 
were removed from the dataset. The remaining images were then imported and processed 
in Meshroom 2021.1.0, a free, open-source photogrammetry software that uses a node-
based pipeline (see Figure 2.3.2-2 below). The software performs two 3D-feature extraction 
steps in sequence: Sparse reconstruction (SfM) and dense reconstruction (depth mapping). 
This produces data that can then be meshed and textured, producing a colorful 3D model 
with the .OBJ filetype. While the texturing step is technically optional, the colors applied can 
be useful in distinguishing noise from the model during cleanup step (see Figure A2.4.0.2-2 
in the appendix). For this reason, texture data was retained whenever possible.  
For the Bird and Gecko, default settings were used with the exception of an extra ‘Mesh 
Decimate’ node that decimates the model to 500,000 vertices (located after the ‘Meshing’ 
node). It should be noted that the processing of the Bust was subject to problems in the depth 
mapping step (see Section A2.3.2.2 in the appendix). Ultimately, these issues were partially 
mitigated by meshing the results of the SfM step directly without refinement from depth 
mapping. This produced a passable untextured model (see Figure A2.3.2.2-1 in the 
appendix).

2.4. Digital Manipulation of Models 
After scanning, models were imported into Blender v2.92 (a free 3D graphics software) and 
digitally manipulated in order to prepare them for 3D printing. Two rough steps can be 
identified in the digital manipulation process: coarse adjustment and fine-tuning.  
In coarse adjustment, the model is separated from the majority of the noise in the scan, 
followed by rotation, scaling, and flattening of the base. With the print mostly ready, the 
model is then subject to fine-tuning, in which it is made manifold (i.e., a closed shape) by 
fixing any remaining issues, such as holes. Digital sculpting tools can also be used to smooth 
out any obvious defects, though this step was only performed on the Bird scans as a proof of 
concept. All models were made at a 1:1 scale with the physical object with the exception of 
the Bust, which was made at an approximately 1:10 scale due to size constraints. A 
comparison of the Bird before and after digital manipulation is shown in Figure 2.4-1 below.

The scaled digital models were then imported into the slicer Ultimaker Cura. This free

setup, a BQ Witbox using a blue ‘Print2Cast’ filament, was replaced after the discovery of 
numerous unfixable hardware issues. The replacement and current setup is an Ender3 Pro 
utilizing a white ‘Polycast’ filament. Both are shown in Figure 2.5-1 below. 
The current setup was then used to print out the models that had been adjusted for

setups are recorded below.  
2.5.1. Original 3D Printer Setup 
Prints were initially performed on a BQ Witbox modified to have a heated bed, utilizing a 
blue “Print2Cast” wax filament. The printer itself was initially poorly calibrated, and the 
original firmware was set up in such a way that calibration values could not be saved to 
memory (EEPROM). Thus, a new firmware, Marlin 2.0.8.1. was installed using the Autobuild 
==PAGE:22==
21 
 
Marlin extension for Visual Studio. The configuration files also needed to be edited to allow 
for an unusually low print temperature of 140 °C required by the blue Print2Cast filament. 
Issues with this initial setup can be summarized as a combination of hardware-related 
problems coupled with a difficult to print filament. The most significant problems with the 
hardware were Z-banding (see Figure 2.5-2 below), which originated from issues with the 
printer’s lead screw, as well as a slightly warped bed that could not consistently reach 
temperatures above 70 °C.  
The Print2Cast filament was found to be quite difficult to work with, with defects such as 
stringing appearing repeatedly despite calibration attempts. The filament also functioned 
poorly in situations when the hotend remained in one place for an extended period, with 
convection melting previous layers and producing holes. This was partially mitigated by the 
use of a sacrificial print tower as recommended by the manufacturer [8], but it was found to 
be insufficient whenever a lot of fine, clustered features would be printed in one place. 
Significant difficulty was added by a need for very precise bed-leveling and high bed 
temperatures greater than 80 °C, which conflicted with hardware issues present in the 
printer. If not precisely leveled, the filament would form a wax-like droplet; this would often 
cool and clog the extruder, requiring a tedious cleanup procedure. This occurred frequently 
to the great annoyance of the author. Additionally, when bed temperatures were too low, the 
print would often warp significantly. After several unsuccessful weeks of calibration and 
maintenance work, the original printer and filament were replaced with a more robust setup. 
2.5.2. Improved 3D Printer Setup 
The improved setup consists of an Ender3 Pro printer modified to use a Micro Swiss direct 
drive extruder, as well as a white ‘Polycast’ filament that produces low amounts of ash 
relative to ordinary PLA filament. This is helpful for burnout, as described in Section 2.8. 
Before printing, the system was subject to the calibration procedure described in Section 
A.2.5.0.1 of the appendix. Final settings are listed in Section A2.5.0.2, also in the appendix. 
This setup was found to be much easier to use than its predecessor; no hardware issues with 
z-banding, bed leveling, or unreliable bed temperatures were encountered. Additionally, the 
filament can be described as similar to PLA; it prints accurately at overhangs with angles up 
to 70° and requires no special conditions such as a sacrificial tower. Some defects such as 
stringing were observed, but these were significantly less than in the original setup and also 
easier to remove due to decreased toughness compared to the Print2Cast material. It should 
be noted that this decrease in toughness can make prints somewhat fragile; prints can 
shatter from a drop of desk-height. Additionally, Polycast filament is fairly expensive, costing 
about 3-4 times as much as ordinary PLA. It is also somewhat moisture sensitive, ideally 
being stored in controlled conditions (such as in a plastic box with desiccant) when not in 
use. To justify the increased cost, burnout tests comparing Polycast to ordinary PLA were 
performed (See Section 2.8.3).

2.6. Gating System Design & Manufacture 
After scanning, the Polycast patterns were attached to a gating system. Three gating systems 
were attempted: bottom gating, top gating, and tree gating. It is well-known that bottom-
gating systems minimize turbulence and produce the highest quality castings in general [10]. 
For this reason, a bottom gating system was generated in Solidworks using 
recommendations from Campbell’s ‘10 rules of casting’ [10] and simulated in Magmasoft 
5.4.3.1. Size constraints required that the gating system be split into sections, though these 
proved to be difficult to join due to the type of wax used. Ultimately structural issues related 
to these weak connections prevented use. Top gating and a basic tree gating system were 
then investigated as possible alternatives. Most castings were top gated. 
2.6.1. General Bottom Gating Theory & Layout 
A bottom gating layout was chosen in an attempt to produce the highest quality castings that 
can be produced using gravity-pour techniques. According to the work of Campbell, most 
casting defects arise from turbulence and the entrainment of bifilms [10], which are enfolded 
oxides from the surface. It has been suggested that these bifilms serve as nucleation points 
for a variety of defects, such as cracks and porosity [10], though it is somewhat unclear if 
these defects affect bulk geometry and surface quality, which is what is captured by 3D 
scanning.   
For most metals, the velocity of the melt should be equal to approximately 0.5 m/s or lower 
to avoid entrainment defects. This speed balances the forces of gravity and surface tension 
[10]. It should be noted that gravity-assisted pouring almost always results in much greater 
speeds; a drop of roughly 10 mm is sufficient to exceed the critical value [10]. Because such 
drops can occur in the part itself, it clear that the melt must enter at the lowest point of the 
casting. Theoretically, top-gated castings are too uncontrolled and turbulent to reliably 
achieve good results. 
To pressurize the melt’s upward climb, gravity-fed systems require an inlet/pouring basin 
that is above the casting itself. This is at odds with the low-velocity requirement. In initial 
stages of pouring, the large drop creates high velocities that tend to cause the melt to jet into 
the cavity in an uncontrolled fashion, introducing defects. To prevent this surge of melt, a 
number of features can be added to the gating setup, such as a surge control system.  A gating 
system was generated using suggestions from Campbell’s work, with some features being 
determined by practical issues, such as size constraints. Design decisions are listed below 
with supporting calculations in Section A2.6.1 in the appendix. These choices were 
implemented in Solidworks 2020, with the result shown below in Figure 2.6.1-1 below. 
− All gating systems used models that had been scaled up by 1.3% to adjust for 
patternmaker’s shrinkage, i.e., shrinkage after solidification. This value is typical for 
aluminum castings [10]. 
− The casting volume was assumed to be 110 cm3. This is the approximate volume of 
the Elephant, the largest model. 
− The system is naturally pressurized, utilizing a 1:1:n gating ratio. 
==PAGE:25==
24 
 
− A pouring basin is used as opposed to a typical funnel, constraining the speed of the 
melt before it enters the sprue. 
o Anti-splash features are present for safety during pouring. 
o The basin is given a 1.5° draft for manufacturing purposes. 
o The basin also has an added circular feature on the top, which is used to 
place an inlet during simulations in Magmasoft. The feature is otherwise 
entirely cosmetic. 
− The sprue is tapered downwards as is typical to prevent the entrainment of air. 
− The sprue/runner interface is a round 90° bend with an inner radius of 
approximately twice the length of the bottom of the sprue.  
− A swirl-trap (a cylinder) is placed at the end of the runner to create an energy-
wasting vortex; this prevents the initial surge and prevents a shockwave. 
− The entire system is designed to be universal, with the possibility of adding various 
extensions to the gate, as necessary. 
− Vents are added by placing wax filament at strategic locations to allow air to escape. 
− The system was designed to fit within an area of L= 20 cm x W = 15.5 cm x H= 20 cm 
with any one of the models attached to ensure that it would fit within the furnace 
available at DTU’s foundry. 
− The entire system is designed to be flush on one side to assist manufacturing steps. 
− Features are split into multiple bodies in Solidworks to allow them to be selected 
individually in Magmasoft.

2.6.4. Manufacture of Wax Pattern & Practical Issues 
With Magmasoft simulations in Magmasoft suggesting optimal flow in multiple cases, work 
was put into the physical manufacture of the setup. 3D printing the gating system in the 
Polycast filament was quickly shown be non-viable for larger-scale testing; the method is 
time consuming and also uses large amounts of expensive filament (which also burns 
extremely strongly; see Section 2.8). Previous investment casting experiments at DTU [11] 
suggested injection-moulding parts using silicon moulds and a blue wax heated to 80 °C. In 
these experiments, parts were then joined through the application of heat. Efforts to 
reproduce this process were partially successful, though practical issues were encountered 
that ultimately prevented the gating system’s reliable use. The specifics of the manufacturing 
process are outlined in a step-by-step manner in Sections A2.6.4.1 to A2.6.4.4 in the 
appendix. 
The first issue encountered was that available equipment was only intended to produce 
silicone moulds for small, flat parts approximately 15 x 110 x 45 mm3 in size; this is much 
smaller than most parts of the designed gating system. The issue was resolved by cutting the 
gating system into sections to be joined later (see Figure 2.6.4-1 below), followed by creating 
new frames to contain the silicone using laser cut scrap acrylic. After assembling these 
acrylic frames using hot glue, 3D printed parts were inserted alongside a reverse-engineered 
‘wax entrance’ part. This extra part was used to create an entrance for the nozzle of the

As in the other previous experiments at DTU, parts were then injection-moulded using the 
blue wax heated to 80 °C. Few difficulties were encountered except for larger parts such as 
the pouring basin. It was found that such parts could be created by filling their moulds with 
wax and continuously rotating them for about 30 minutes. The long rotation time prior to 
extraction is needed to coat all sides equally. In cases where less rotation was used, curved 
sections were often found to be thinner than average, leading to holes. 
After the wax parts were cool and cleared of defects, they were cleaned using Ranclene 2 
pattern cleaner. The manufacturer claims that this etches the wax pattern and promotes 
adhesion to the slurry used in subsequent steps (see Section 2.7). However, it is not entirely 
clear if this pattern cleaner step is necessary, as its main purpose appears to be to remove 
mould release, which is not used. Parts are easy to remove due to the flexibility of the silicone 
and/or built-in drafts.  
Soldering the joints as performed in previous experiments was found to produce a weak, 
highly warped connection. To solve this issue, numerous attempts were made to try to 
strengthen the system as whole. Initial attempts focused on trying out different types of glue 
==PAGE:30==

to strengthen the connection. This included hot glue, low-temp hot glue, Loctite in both liquid 
and gel forms, Dana universal glue, and Panokitt quick glue. Only the hot glues were able to

accurate connection. Adding hot glue strengthens the joint considerably compared to using

extreme amounts of melting were found to produce any connection at all; the wax does not

2.7. Shell-making 
After careful construction of the gating system, patterns were prepared for the shell-making 
process. The shell is created by repeatedly dipping the pattern into a ceramic slurry that 
contains a ceramic flour and a binder. Larger grains of stucco are then sprinkled on, building 
up the shell. This process is continued until the shell is sufficiently thick to withstand the 
stresses of burnout and casting. Ceramic shells usually consist of layers of a prime coat, 
backup coat, and a seal. These respectively preserve details, provide strength, and prevent 
stucco from being removed by abrasion/handling [12]. A slurry and stucco based on fused 
silica (FS) was selected because castings would be done in non-ferrous materials. 
Specifically, FS SuspendaSlurry was used in conjunction with a fine FS Ranco-sil A for the 
prime coat and a coarse FS Ranco-sil B for the backup coat.  
2.7.1. Shell-making Setup and Procedure 
Before these materials could be put to use, however, precautions needed to be established 
for safety reasons. Crystalline silica dust is a well-known carcinogen and can cause a 
disabling condition called silicosis if it enters one’s airways. The safest setup that could be 
arranged under the circumstances is shown in Figure 2.7.1-1 below, consisting of a paper-
lined fume hood containing a steel wire suspended from two adhesive hooks. The wire 
serves as a drying rack and is fit with small sections of tape to prevent shells from sliding 
around during use. The paper helps to prevent droplets of slurry from sticking to various 
surfaces; it is very difficult to remove once it has dried. Full PPE is worn during the process, 
consisting of a lab-coat, nylon gloves, chemical goggles, and most importantly, an FFP3-grade 
respirator. It should be noted that the SuspendaSlurry is situated outside the fume hood due 
to lack of space.  
As described in greater detail in Section A2.7.1 in the appendix, the slurry’s viscosity was 
first tested by a #4 Zahn cup and adjusted with de-ionized water in accordance with 
manufacturer specifications [13]. Afterwards, patterns were dipped in the slurry and 
transferred to the fume hood for the application of stucco. Stucco was stored in two labeled, 
sealable buckets within the fume hood, and carefully applied to the wet pattern by sprinkling 
it on manually using some 3D printed shovels [14] (the ‘cat box’ method). Afterwards, the 
patterns were hung on a steel wire within the fume hood to dry for approximately one hour 
prior to the application of the following coat or transfer to the furnace area for 
dewaxing/burnout (see Section 2.8).  
It should be noted for future experiments that some form of sieve is needed in the long term. 
The stucco appears to become contaminated with drops from the slurry over time. A 3D sieve 
shovel would suffice.

2.7.3. Shell-making Issues for Elephant 
The Elephant shells in particular had some specific issues that were not encountered 
elsewhere. Most significantly, the shells did not appear to dry properly on the internal 
surface of the mould. This is shown in Figure 2.7.3-1 below, which shows slurry leaking from 
an Elephant shell prior to burnout (see Section 2.8). It seems likely that airflow in the internal 
cavity of the elephant was insufficient, preventing proper drying. Despite these issues, 
burnout proceeded as normal. 
A secondary issue involved the hanging/dipping mechanism. Like the other top-gated 
castings, the Elephant was dipped by a gripping a small nail and/or piece of steel wire located 
at the base of the model. This proved to be exhausting for the fingers in the case of the 
Elephant, which resisted movement both before and after dipping; it was buoyant on the way 
in due to its hollow center, and heavy afterwards due to being filled with slurry. The fully 
coated dry Elephant weighed about 283 g, which is quite a lot to be holding with two fingers 
for an extended period. It is therefore recommended for larger models that additional, longer 
screws be attached for an easier time during the shell-making process.

2.8. Dewaxing and Burnout 
The final step in the production of the shell is the dewaxing/burnout process, which removes 
the pattern through the application heat. Typically, a ceramic shell is first dewaxed at a lower 
temperature; the wax flows out and is collected for reuse. Afterwards, the shell is subjected 
to a high heat, which simultaneously sinters the shell and increases its strength, and also 
removes any remaining traces of wax. An optional step is to then rinse out the shell with 
water after cooling, ensuring that it is free from ash. 
Before any such steps could be performed, several practical issues needed to be solved. Most 
significantly, the foundry at DTU was found to be lacking in equipment for the burnout 
process: namely a functional ventilation system and some sort of temperature resistant 
support structure to hold the shell up while inside a furnace. The design and manufacture of 
these components is described in Sections 2.8.1 and 2.8.2 below, respectively. 
Additionally, the specific temperatures and times for the dewaxing/burnout process were 
unknown, with the manufacturers being somewhat vague on the specifics. The clearest 
information supplied was in regard to SuspendaSlurry, which suggested that shells be 
treated at temperatures ranging from approximately 880-980 °C for one hour [15]. In 
contrast, the white Polycast filament lists no specific dewaxing or burnout procedures, 
though is intended to remain within the shell until burnout. In practice, however, doing so 
was found to be problematic for larger prints or during the simultaneous burnout of multiple 
smaller shells. Straightforwardly proceeding with burnout at 880 °C causes the filament to 
auto-ignite and burn so fiercely as to extend outside the furnace. Such an event is shown 
below in Figure 2.8-1.  To address these problems, a number of experiments were performed 
to try to find an optimal temperature to either dewax or otherwise ‘pre-burn’ the filament to 
reduce the intensity of such fires. These experiments are discussed in Section 2.8.3 below.

2.8.1. Ventilation 
The burnout process releases noxious fumes that should be extracted for safety reasons. At 
the beginning of this project, the DTU foundry was under construction and no ventilation 
==PAGE:37==
36 
 
systems were available except for a ceiling mounted structure with weak suction that was 
flush with a plaster wall (see Figure A2.8.1-1 in the appendix). This was clearly not an ideal 
place for burnout procedures, as smoke would have heavily stained the wall possibly risking 
a fire. Under the instruction of the project supervisor, a basic ventilation cover was designed 
(see Figure A2.8.1-2 in the appendix) and sent to DTU’s manufacturing department. The 
department took care of the rest, improving and installing the design. The cover serves its 
purpose, providing protection to the wall and neighboring equipment, while also guiding in 
fumes.  
While the ventilation cover seems to achieve its basic purpose, the suction power of the 
ventilation system above it was found to be insufficient for situations where flames extended 
outside the furnace (see Figure 2.8-1 above). In one instance, a nearby fire alarm was set off. 
For this reason, the entire burnout setup was moved below a larger ventilation system, 
which had been re-installed after the construction at DTU’s foundry had been completed. 
This was used for a small time until it was realized that DTU’s automatic ventilation control 
system would automatically shut it off outside of business hours, making it impractical or 
even dangerous for time-consuming burnout steps. A third, final system was then used. 
Though it was highly impractical due to its position and size, it could be restarted for use 
after-hours. The three ventilation systems used are shown side by side in Figure 2.8.1-1 
below.  
The ventilation system behavior was found to be one of the largest safety issues in the AM-
assisted IC process. There is currently no documentation of shutoff behavior available to 
students, which caused a number of unsafe events in which the foundry was filled with 
plastic fumes that were irritating to the throat and lungs.  
 
2.8.2. Burnout Rack 
In industrial settings, the dewaxing process is carried out in specialized autoclaves that are 
fit with a grille, have an integrated wax collection system, and utilize pressure to prevent 
shells from cracking. The DTU foundry’s electrical furnaces do not have such luxuries; most 
are entirely incompatible with dewaxing due their size, heating mechanism (such as the 
induction furnace), or access to ventilation. Only one furnace, shown in Figure 2.8.1-1 b), fit 
these three criteria, but even this was too small for a simple grille-based solution. To get 
around severe size constraints, a rack was designed in which shells could be hung using steel 
wire above a cylindrical collection container. The rack was made of steel and designed to 
provide the maximum amount of space for shells as possible while still being sufficiently 
stable under high temperatures of burnout (880 °C, as discussed in Section 2.8.4). An 
engineering drawing is shown in Figure 2.8.2-1 below.

2.8.3. Burnout Temperature Experiments 
A small burnout experiment was performed to determine if Polycast could be dewaxed or 
pre-burnt to reduce the possibility of flames. Polycast was compared to ordinary PLA to see 
if the burnout behavior is different. Small cylinders with a radius and height of 10 mm were 
printed out in Polycast and light grey Primavalue PLA. This was done on the current 3D 
printing setup using 20% infill and a low print quality of 0.28 mm for extra speed. These 
cylinders were then placed in ceramic crucibles and weighed using the KERN EMB 1200-I 
scale. Each cylinder was then subject to a temperature between 200 and 375 ˚C for one hour, 
as recorded in Table A2.8.3-1. The treatment temperature range was defined by the printing 
temperature (200 ˚C) and the approximate upper limit before autoignition in the Polycast 
material (which occurs at 400 ˚C). Note that 260 ˚C is the decomposition temperature for 
Polycast. 
After melting, the resulting sludge was allowed to cool, weighed, and its general appearance 
recorded. Finally, the melt was returned to the furnace at 880 ˚C for another hour to mimic 
the shell burnout process recommended for SuspendaSlurry [15]. The mass of the crucible 
with remaining ash and appearance were then recorded. Tests were performed one at a time 
to prevent ash cross-contamination. Repeat trials were not performed due to time 
limitations.  
In processing the data, it was found that the scale used for mass measurements was not 
sensitive enough to measure the minute quantities of ash produced, which appear to be 
below the scale’s lowest significant digit (0.1 g). Given the small weight of the samples 
(~1.5g) combined with the low sensitivity of the scale, the quality of the measurements is 
likely low when the percent change in mass is close to zero or one. However, changes in 
sample mass between these two extremes (such as when the change in mass is ≤ 0.2 g) 
would still be possible to detect to some degree. Such changes would occur during the ‘pre-
burning’ state that is being sought. 
The results of this experiment are visualized in Figure 2.8.3-1 below and recorded in Table 
A2.8.3-1. In Polycast, pre-burning appears to occur between the temperatures of 350 and 
375 °C, while PLA appears to undergo the process around 300 °C.   
Visual records indicate that Polycast has a greatly reduced ash content compared to PLA, at 
the cost of having a molasses-like viscosity at all temperatures, preventing effective 
dewaxing unless a very simple top-gating approach is used. In contrast, PLA became a liquid 
==PAGE:40==
39 
 
with a water-like viscosity at 300 ˚C, suggesting that dewaxing might be possible at this 
temperature. In both cases, ash content visually appeared to be maximized at 350 ˚C, which 
might be confirmed by using a more accurate scale. As a result of these experiments, a 
possible pre-burn/dewaxing temperature of 350 °C was chosen. 375 °C was avoided as it 
may cause auto-ignition if the internal temperature is raised by thermal decomposition.

2.8.4. Finalized Dewaxing and Burnout Procedure 
The dewaxing and burnout procedure was based on the results of previous experimentation 
coupled with recommendations from the slurry manufacturer. While it was known from 
preliminary testing that shells could survive the burnout process, it was unclear if shells 
could survive the dewaxing process due to stresses from thermal expansion of the filament 
or wax. To test this, two sets of shells were made for each model being cast. The first set of 
shells were fired at 880 °C for one hour one at a time to reduce the possibility of the furnace 
catching fire again. The second set of shells underwent dewaxing at 350 °C for one hour, 
followed by burnout at 880 °C for another hour. Melted wax/filament remained in the 
collection plate to be combusted away. 
Parts using blue injection wax as part of a gating system were dewaxed at 230 °C. This value 
was attempted because it was known to melt the blue wax quickly without thermal 
decomposition – time constraints prevented more rigorous analysis of the behavior of this 
wax. In all cases, shells were hung right-side up from the burnout rack using steel wire. After 
==PAGE:41==
40 
 
burnout, all shells were flushed out with water to remove any remaining ash. Finally, they 
were placed in the furnace at ~100 °C for approximately one hour to dry. An example of a 
model going through the dewaxing and burnout procedure is shown in Figure 2.8.4-1 below.

2.9. Casting 
2.9.1. Casting Setup and Procedure 
The ceramic shells produced in previous steps were then prepared for gravity casting in 
aluminum, which is typically performed at 700 °C. As in the shell-making and burnout 
processes, deficiencies in equipment required a specialized setup. Ideally, the shell is 
embedded in sand and heated to upwards of 450 °C [2] to boost surface quality and prevent 
defects such as misrun. Misrun is when the melt freezes prior to filling the entire cavity. 
However, the majority of the furnaces available at the foundry were too small to contain the 
sandbox apparatus needed for such a setup. Additionally, a lack of larger steel containers 
required that shells be embedded in an awkward setup consisting of a large diameter steel 
pipe placed on top of a larger box of sand. This is shown in Figure 2.9.1-1 below. While there 
was a furnace capable of holding this sandbox setup, it was immobile and located at the 
opposite end of the foundry. The heft of the sand and flimsy nature of the steel box at the 
base of the sandbox made it too difficult to safely transport across the foundry when also 
considering the high temperature.  
In an attempt to resolve this issue, an experimental workaround was tried. A crucible 
containing an aluminum casting alloy was first heated to 700 °C near the sandbox area and 
measured using a type K thermocouple. The sandbox area itself remained at room 
temperature with the shell embedded inside. An interesting technique for placing shells in 
sand using was found and is described in Section A2.9.1 in the appendix. Once the shell was 
secured, a smaller container of heated sand was transported across the foundry and quickly 
poured into the larger sandbox around the shell. Because a pouring action was needed, 
requiring increased dexterity, the sand was only heated to 350 °C for safety reasons. In all 
cases, the temperature of the sand was then quickly measured with the thermocouple to 
ensure that it was at within 50 °C of the original temperature. Finally, the aluminum melt 
was removed from the nearby furnace, removed of dross using a skimmer, and poured into 
the shell. After pouring, the sandbox apparatus was tapped a few times with some pliers, 
shaking the system in an attempt to dislodge any bubbles remaining in the melt. Each of the 
two top-gated shells made for the three of four models tested were subjected to this 
treatment. This included the cracked Elephant shell shown in Figure 2.8.5-1 a). The tree-
gated Bust, however, was not attempted due to the destroyed pouring basin. 
2.9.2. Additional Castings & Issues 
The results of this process (see Section 3) found defects resembling misrun. A few remaining 
Bird shells from early on in the experiment (that were unfortunately not scanned in the 
printing phase) were then utilized to test an alternative setup in which the sand could be 
heated to higher temperatures. This sandbox only needed to support the smaller Bird shells, 
meaning its area could be decreased in size. This removed the need for the flimsy steel box 
and allowed the entire system to be transported across the foundry, meaning that it was 
possible to heat the shell at the same time as the sand. Two final castings were then 
performed: one raised the temperature of the sand and shell to 450 °C, while the other raised 
the temperature of the melt to 750 °C and the sand to 550 °C. The second of these castings 
==PAGE:44==

used melt recycled from a faulty Elephant casting because supplies were running low. A list 
of all of castings performed and their various treatments is listed in Section 3.2.

2.10. Knockout 
In knockout, the shell is removed. In industry, this process is somewhat violent, typically 
being removed by jackhammer-like tools or by waterjets. However, such equipment is not 
available at the DTU foundry. Instead, shells were broken using simple tools such as a 
hammer (and supporting piece of steel), as well as a small flathead screwdriver. These 
operations were carried out within a specially constructed knockout chamber. 
2.10.1. Knockout Chamber 
The knockout process releases dangerous silica dust. Indeed, knockout is the most likely part 
in the process where operators will be exposed [16]. To combat the dust issue and prevent 
a mess of larger shell fragments, a simple glove box was constructed using a large plastic bin, 
a pair of 44 cm Workman rubber gloves, and 3D printed arm holes. This is shown in Figure 
2.10.1-1 below.  The box was sealed using a combination of duct tape, weather sealing strip, 
hot glue, and zip ties. After casting and full cooled, shells were transported to this chamber 
and carefully broken with the tools inside. Afterwards, fragments and dust were removed 
using a vacuum cleaner. In Section A2.10 in the appendix, an engineering drawing of the 3D 
printed armhole can be found alongside a demonstration of the knockout chamber’s use.

2.10.2. Issues in the Knockout Process 
The knockout process as currently designed suffers from several practical issues. For 
example, from a product quality perspective, the shell can sometimes stick to crevices, 
making shell removal very difficult without accidentally scratching the casting. An example 
of this is shown in Figure 2.10.2-1 below. Numerous issues were also observed in the 
Elephant castings. For example, the shell within the hollow cavity of the Elephant could not 
be cracked or easily have leverage applied to it. This made shell removal impossible using 
the tools available (see Figure 2.10.2-2). It was also found that melt had invaded the internal 
cavity, possibly due to an internal crack caused by the small steam explosion noted in Section 
2.9.2 or incomplete drying observed in Section 2.7.3. Scanning could not be performed on 
this Elephant shell because of the possibility of introducing dust and shell fragments into the 
scanning room. The casting done with the cracked Elephant shell became the mess observed 
in Figure 2.9.2-1. It was not worth taking the time to properly clean it. 
The chamber also has flaws from an operator safety perspective. For example, the chamber 
is not airtight. While working with the chamber, gases escape from the cast shell and are 
difficult to avoid due to the proximity of the operator. It is unknown if these gases are actually 
dangerous, although it is probably best to avoid them altogether by using the chamber in 
conjunction with a fume hood. A secondary safety issue is that the rubber gloves are sensitive 
to being cut by the sharp edges of castings.

castings in particular were scanned in such a way that no reference dots were present on the

Nominal and actual models were aligned using the pre-alignment function, followed by main 
alignment using local best fitting. An example of this is shown in Figure 2.11-1 below.

deviation histogram was generated. The results are shown in Section 3 with whichever view 
angle conveyed the most information.

3. Results 
Many different operations were performed in previous sections while exploring the AM-
assisted IC process. Only some could be considered successful. Section 3.1 below summarizes 
the status of unsuccessful approaches throughout the project and their causes. Each issue is 
discussed in greater detail within Section 2 in their respective sub-section. Section 3.2 lists 
castings that made it through the entire process in some form or another. These particular 
castings are examined in greater detail, with larger defects being discussed in Section 3.3 
using photographs. More precise details and comparisons are shown in Sections 3.4 and 3.5, 
examining 3D prints and castings, respectively. Section 3.6 compares the two 3D scanning 
techniques, namely photogrammetry and SLS. Finally, Section 3.7 shows an estimate of the 
total time needed to carry out each stage of the entire AM-assisted IC process. 
3.1. Summary of Unsuccessful Approaches and Issues 
• Photogrammetry processing: Depth mapping steps failed for the Bust but were 
mitigated by meshing SfM results directly. 
• Bottom gating: Structural issues related to weak connections prevented use. 3D 
printing of a ‘gate extension’ piece also proved to be too difficult for the Gecko. 
• Tree gating: Dewaxing treatment and mishandling led to an unusable, cracked shell. 
Polycast filament also appeared to be too viscous to be used as a vent, possibly being 
responsible for cracking. 
• Gecko model: Casting not attempted due to time constraints. 
• Elephant model: Elephant shells did not appear to dry properly in the internal cavity. 
Dewaxing led to a cracked shell. Casting of the cracked shell was attempted but led to 
poor results. For the casting of the non-cracked shell, the shell could not be extracted 
from the internal cavity during knockout, preventing scanning due to dust concerns. 
There may also have been internal cracking in this case. 
• Dewaxing/Burnout: Unreliable ventilation caused safety issues. The burnout rack 
was warped by the end of the project. The drying process was too short and may have 
caused steam explosion in the larger Elephant shells. 
• Casting: Casting setup was complicated due to ventilation quirks and lack of properly 
sized furnaces. 
• Knockout: Some practical/safety issues related to design. 
• Color mapping: Photogrammetry models were slightly out of scale with the SLS 
scans and needed to be adjusted.

3.3. Photographs of Castings & Defects 
All of the castings produced showed some form of obvious, visible defect. Defects were the 
most obvious in the Bird castings, particularly those preheated to lower temperatures as in 
Figure 3.3-1. The castings had entire sections that were missing or were of reduced size, 
typically featuring a smooth, rounded edge where the feature should be. This effect was 
particularly evident in thin areas, such as the legs, tail, and beak, as well as the sharp edges 
of the box-like pouring cup. This defect appears to be misrun, in which the melt freezes 
prematurely during filling. As a result, sections of the shell are left empty, leaving a rounded 
melt front behind [17]. It can also be seen that the extent and location of the issue varies from 
casting to casting. For example, Bird 1 and 2 were cast near-simultaneously, but both legs 
formed in the first case, while the latter appears to have just barely filled one. This created a 
feature so thin that the casting broke in half during knockout.  
Misrun appears to be mostly a result of the shell temperature being too low. When the 
temperature of the shell and surrounding sand was increased, the extent of the issue was 
consistently reduced. This can be seen in Figure 3.3-2, which shows the castings performed 
with an increased temperature. These castings were filled more completely and consistently, 
with only a small section of one leg missing in each case. The fact that raising the temperature 
did not entirely mitigate the issue suggests that the simple top gating system may be 
unsuitable for filling this geometry. 
Some additional, lesser defects can also be observed. Smooth irregular features on the upper 
part of the wing can be seen in the Bird 1 casting in Figure 3.3-1 b). The cause of this defect 
==PAGE:51==
50 
 
is somewhat unclear, but it appears be related to gas formation originating from the shell or 
turbulent pouring [17]. Both of these processes entrap gases within the casting, which then 
rise to the surface and leave this type of defect. Gas also appears to be responsible for the 
large defects observed in the Bird 5 casting, which has an auxiliary close-up view that can be 
found in Figure A3.3-1 in appendix. Larger pits as well as many smaller holes can be observed 
over the entire surface. This porosity is likely due to the presence of hydrogen in the melt, 
which is known to dissolve in the liquid aluminum but has poor solubility in the solid [18]. 
Higher melt temperatures would lead to increased hydrogen content in the melt, leading to 
increased gas precipitation during cooling. Such events lead to issues similar to those seen 
in the Bird 5 casting. The recycling process uniquely performed for this casting may also have 
contributed, as hydrogen may have been released by the Elephant shell as it sat in the 
crucible during re-melting. 
Defects of a different nature can be found on Bust castings, shown in Figure 3.3-3. A close-up 
view of the facial area can be found in Figure A3.3-2 in the appendix. Like the Birds, small 
amounts of misrun are again present, particularly at thin or sharp areas such as nose, edges 
of the helmet, and corners of the box-like pouring cup. However, there is also evidence of 
possible issues in the shell-making process. What should be thin headset microphones near 
the corners of the mouth are instead blob-like masses. This appears to be partially as a result 
of misrun, but there are also areas with excess material that might be as a result of small air 
bubbles that are trapped in the primary slurry layer [17]. In other words, the slurry may not 
have surrounded the headset microphones properly, leading to these areas being filled in. 
Another defect includes metal penetration, which can also be seen in the Bust 1 casting at 
the left shoulder. This likely occurred due to a weak spot in the shell, creating a leak. It may 
be necessary to increase the shell thickness. 
These defects aside, the surface quality appears to be quite high, with lines from the 3D 
printing phase clearly visible. The Bust castings appear to be higher in quality than the Birds, 
but this is simply because the extent of misrun is much smaller. Aside from this, most of the 
Bird and Bust castings appear quite similar in quality.

3.4. 3D Print Color Maps 
Color mapping suggests that 3D prints are repeatable as well as faithful to their original 
models. In general, deviations are low, on the order of 0.1-0.2 mm, with the exception of 
pockets of defects that can produce larger deviations up to 1.5 mm in size. Two types of 
defects appear to be present: those caused by support structures, and those by stringing. 
This is best seen in Figure 3.4-1 below, which shows a Bust print compared to its original 
model. The vast majority of the surface is in green, but areas where supports were present 
show reduced precision. For example, the edge on the upper part of the Bust’s helmet has 
slightly reduced material and is thus highlighted blue. Larger defects in red can be found in 
a rough line up the side of the print. This is likely stringing or oozing that occurred at the end 
of a layer change during the print. The location of these defects appears to be somewhat 
consistent from print to print, but the size is not. This can be seen in Figure 3.4-2 and Figure 
3.4-3, which compare prints to each other. Deviations that are associated with stringing 
appear in the same locations, but the magnitude is different between prints. This is likely a 
result of inconsistent sanding operations.  
It appears that these defects do indeed carry over into the casting process. A comparison of 
defects between the print and casting, as shown in Figure 3.4-4, show only a little deviation 
on the order of 0.3 to 0.4 mm in areas where these defects are present. Because these 
deviations are negative, they appear in the casting to a lesser extent than in the print. It can 
also be seen from this color map that the 1.3% scaling factor is fairly accurate; areas not

3.5. Casting Color Maps 
The color maps comparing the castings show that areas not affected by defects such as 
misrun or bubbles in the primary shell coating are faithful to the original model and 
consistent across castings. Indeed, such areas may be on par with, or even exceed industrial 
standards. In Figures 3.5-1 and 3.5-2, it can be observed that deviations in areas unaffected 
by defects are on the order of 0.1 to 0.4 mm. This can be compared to ISO 8062-3:2007, a 
document that describes standard tolerances for castings. For investment castings in light 
metal alloys such as aluminum, linear tolerances are stated to be DCTG grade 3-5, 
corresponding to 0.18 to 0.36 mm for the smallest features (<10 mm) and slightly looser for 
larger nominal dimensions [19]. This magnitude is similar to the deviations observed in 
areas without defects. Thus, it can be observed that if defects such as misrun can be 
eliminated, the process would rival industrial standards.  
Dewaxing as performed in this report appears to have no appreciable effect on quality; there 
is little difference between the quality of the dewaxed and non-dewaxed Bust castings, as 
shown in Figure 3.5-3.  The castings are consistent with each other, on the order of 0.1-0.4 
mm except at the location of defects as previously noted. In agreement with trends of the 
Bird, misrun appears to have occurred to roughly the same extent in the two castings, albeit 
at slightly different locations.

3.6. Comparison of Photogrammetry and SLS 
When models produced by both scanning methods are properly scaled to one another, 
photogrammetry appears to rival SLS. In Figure 3.6-1, which compares the Bird scans, it can 
be seen that the initial scaling attempt was slightly off. Unscaled, deviations are higher, on 
the order of 0.5 to 1 mm. With proper re-scaling, however, they can be cut roughly in half to 
0.2 to 0.4 mm. Deviations in both scales are most present on the head and beak, which is 
unsurprising given that major parts of these areas were reconstructed by eye using digital 
sculpting techniques. In fact, it is somewhat surprising that it came out as well as it did, given 
the amateur nature of the author’s skills in Blender. There are a few small areas with larger 
deviations, and they appear to be in areas that are difficult to photograph, such as the rough 
patches underneath the bird as well as the thin edge of the tail. It should be noted that some 
of these deviations originate from the reference dots used in SLS. These can clearly be seen 
on the side of the left leg. Deviations from reference dots appear to be on the order of 0.2 to 
0.3 mm. 
These dot-like defects are even more prominent on the Gecko, shown in Figure 3.6-2. In fact, 
they appear to be the main source of deviation on detailed areas such as the back. Deviations 
after proper scaling are on the order of 0.05 to 0.1 mm. Some details, such as the scales, are 
at slightly lower resolution than the SLS scan, as suggested by the yellow streaks between 
them. Overall, however, photogrammetry appears to be the better option for finely textured 
models, as reference dot artifacts would not need to be removed. Texture data is also applied 
in this method, preserving details such as color. 
The scaling issue is perhaps a side-effect of selecting organically-shaped structures to test 
the AM-assisted IC process. The objects are simply difficult to measure due to having poorly 
defined features, and this error carries over into scaling. Thus, scaling may be more 
successful if objects have well-defined features.

3.7. Time Consumption During the Entire Process 
As a final result, the time needed to complete each step of the AM-assisted IC process was 
estimated and plotted in Figure 3.7-1. It should be noted that this is an underestimate of the 
real process, as it does not account for many small intermediate steps such as traveling time 
between equipment. It can be seen that the entire process, with 3D scanning and 
comparative analysis included, takes a little more than a day at approximately 30 hours. The 
3D printing and shell-making step take up the majority of the time during the manufacturing 
phase. Time improvements to these steps can be done, albeit at a cost. 3D printing time is 
known be closely tied to the layer height, as is print quality. One could increase the layer 
height leading to a much faster print, though quality would suffer as a result of coarser layers. 
The shell making process can be improved by increasing airflow [15], drying the shells out 
more quickly. However, this may increase the risk of silica dust in the air. Of course, all steps 
the process could be made faster with more robust equipment and trained personnel.

4.  Discussion 
With deviations between the original model and castings being on par with industrial 
standards, the results look quite promising. Clearly, the process needs the most 
improvement at the casting stage, particularly with regard to misrun. Indeed, the largest 
deviations appear to originate from the unusual casting methods necessitated by a lack of 
equipment. As suggested by the additional casting attempts, this misrun can be addressed to 
==PAGE:64==
63 
 
an extent by raising mould temperatures, though increasing the temperature too high 
appears to result in gas defects. An alternative approach to eliminate misrun may be to 
improve the gating system. Top-gating systems appear to be the most straightforward and 
useful for small-scale castings, but they may be insufficient for filling some types of geometry.  
Other approaches, such as the bottom and tree gating were not able to be made to work in 
this project, but with additional time and equipment, they may prove to be superior 
alternatives. It is recommended that future attempts for small, bottom-gated castings use 
thicker gating dimensions than suggested by the work of Campbell for increased stability 
and strength during manufacture. Because of time constraints and a sample size of one, it is 
unclear if tree-gated approaches using filament vents made of Polycast can be made to work. 
It may be possible to avoid cracks using this method by using a thicker shell. 
It is clear that the AM-assisted IC process is not ideal for all geometries. For example, parts 
with hollow sections, such as the Elephant, need those sections to be wide enough to remove 
the shell afterwards. Additional airflow may also be required for accurate internal features.  
As a whole, the work performed in this project is quite broad, with many different 
approaches being attempted and tests done along the way. Without a doubt, more elegant 
solutions and better results can be achieved at all stages of the process with more 
sophisticated equipment, methods, and additional time. Basic solutions such as those used 
in this report, however, appear to be sufficient to rival industrial standards provided that 
more attention is paid to addressing defects.  
Some aspects of the process as described in this report could use some practical/safety 
improvements before being attempted again. From a quality perspective, larger furnaces and 
a more practical casting setup should be used. Ventilation systems should also be prioritized. 
Many of the steps, such as burnout, produce fumes or dust that can be dangerous to one’s 
health. Care should be taken to make it clear if automatic shutoff procedures are in use. It 
may also be advantageous to have a separate room for shell-making procedures due to the 
mess (and possible safety issues) that this working with slurry and fused silica can create. 
The burnout rack designed for this process is also a good candidate for improvement. It is an 
acceptable short-term solution, but longer-term solutions made out of robust high-
temperature materials should be looked into. The knockout box as designed could be also 
improved by adding some sort of ventilation system to prevent operators from inhaling 
gasses released by shells after casting. However, the use of a fume hood in conjunction with 
knockout is a possible workaround. The gloves could also be made cut-proof for a longer-
lasting solution. 
As a whole, the AM-assisted IC process appears to be quite accessible, making additional 
research at other locations quite feasible. This report describes the implementation of the 
process in a basic sand-casting foundry under construction; AM-assisted IC may be much 
easier in more dedicated areas. With the exception of comparative analysis, the entire 
scanning/digital manipulation process can be carried out using only free software and a 
camera. Equipment such as 3D printers are readily available. For those that have access to a 
==PAGE:65==
64 
 
furnace and fume hood, the process may be highly useful. For example, the methods used in 
this report could be used by sculptors wishing to convert small clay sculptures into metal 
castings without tampering with the original. Industrially, the process could be used to 
replicate unique metallic objects with minimal lead time provided that the setup is ready for 
it. For instance, duplicate parts of a vintage car could be scanned and produced in the event 
of damage. Realistically, this might be able to be done in only a few days.  
5. Conclusion 
In this report, procedures and basic equipment were developed to replicate organically-
shaped structures in aluminum using AM-assisted investment casting techniques. Both 
structured light scanning and photogrammetry were used to translate different physical 
objects into digital models, allowing them to be 3D printed, given a ceramic shell, and finally 
cast. These objects included the Bird, Gecko, Bust, and Elephant, and each were taken 
through as much of the process as possible. Not all models led to successful results in every 
stage, but all were able to reveal something different about the process.  
The Bird showed that the casting process is by far the leading cause of error using the 
procedures generated in this report. Small, thin sections are likely to lead to misrun in the 
casting step. However, there is evidence that this can be partially mitigated by increasing the 
shell temperature prior to casting. 
The Gecko, although not cast, was useful in comparing photogrammetry to structured light 
scanning. If properly scaled, deviations between the two types of scanning can be on the 
order of 0.1 mm. Photogrammetry appears to be slightly lower resolution than structured 
light scanning, but it is much more accessible due to only requiring a normal camera and free 
software. It may be the superior option for models with a fine surface texture. 
The Elephant revealed that is indeed possible to convert a pattern and core box back into a 
3D model that can be printed. It also exposed issues in the shell-making process, namely that 
internal cavities that are too small may suffer from incomplete drying or difficulty in removal 
during the knockout step. 
Finally, the Bust showed that if misrun can be controlled, metal replications of organically-
shaped structures can rival industrial standards if made using the AM-assisted investment 
casting process using the procedures shown within this report. Theoretically, replications 
can be done in just over a day. 
The procedures in this report appear to be quite accessible and useful in a number of 
situations, assuming access to basic foundry/chemical safety equipment, such as a furnace 
and a fume hood. Undoubtedly, many improvements can be made in this process, and it is 
the hope of the author that this work be used as baseline for future improvement. Additional 
testing should focus on the elimination of defects in the casting stage, as well as improve the 
practicality and safety of some manufacturing processes. A greater understanding and 
control of each part of the process may also be able to increase quality even further.

A2.3.2.1. Camera Settings & Photogrammetry Location Considerations 
Photographs taken for the purpose of photogrammetry must be as high quality and sharp as 
possible. Ideally, photographs are taken with a high depth of field such that both the object 
and background are in focus. This enables the software to better recognize features and 
decreases noise in the mesh.   
The depth of field DOF a function of object distance u, f-number N, circle of confusion c, and 
the focal length of the lens f. Mathematically, 
𝐷𝑂𝐹≈2𝑢2𝑁𝑐
𝑓2
. 
 
(A2.3.2.1.1) 
Based on this equation, the depth of field was boosted whenever possible by minimizing the 
focal length of the lens f. In other words, the camera was as zoomed out as possible. However, 
the Bust and Bird required significant zoom due to physical obstructions and small size, 
respectively. To further boost the DOF and sharpen the image, the f-number N is increased, 
lowering the aperture size. However, this reduces the amount of incoming light, 
necessitating lower shutter speeds, which hold the aperture open for a longer period. Lower 
shutter speeds, in turn, have the consequence of increased motion-blur, necessitating the use 
of a heavy tripod and a 2-second capture delay to avoid camera shake from pressing the 
capture button. 
The ISO is an additional setting that can be used to increase the quality of an image. Lower 
ISO values (e.g., 100) decrease the sensitivity of the camera to light, which reduces noise. 
However, this decreases brightness further, requiring bright lighting (such as diffuse 
sunlight on an overcast day) or very low shutter speeds (as used indoors).  
A good photogrammetry scan can be difficult to perform outdoors due to changing lighting 
conditions. The ideal weather condition for photogrammetry is an overcast sky, as this 
minimizes the presence and movement of shadows, which interfere with digital feature 
recognition. However, scans can take hours, so a break in cloud cover can occur, changing 
lighting conditions enormously. Additionally, forces such as wind can cause movement in the 
subject if poorly secured (Gecko) or lightweight (Bird). For this reason, most scans were 
performed indoors despite a 10-fold decrease in shutter speed, slowing everything down. 
The Bust’s location required that photogrammetry take place outdoors.

A.2.5.0.1. Calibration Procedures and Remarks 
A number of tests were performed to calibrate the printer, which are briefly described below 
alongside important results. Many of the calibration procedures can be found in greater 
detail on the Teaching Tech website by Michael Laws [20], which proved to be particularly 
helpful in understanding and solving several of the issues present in the initial setup. Since 
calibration was only critical for the initial setup, most of the discussion is limited to this 
system. 
A2.5.0.1.1. Interfacing with the Printer 
Calibration requires interfacing with the printer and running G-code commands. The free 
Pronterface software was used within this report to run commands, observe temperature 
trends, as well as visualize precompiled g-code. An example of the interface is shown below 
in Figure A2.5.0.1.1-1. Connecting to the initial setup’s printer (the Witbox) required the 
installation of USB2.0 serial drivers to connect with the Arduino. 
 
Figure A2.5.0.1.1-1: The Pronterface software, visualizing the g-code of a calibration cube 
and sacrificial tower. 
A2.5.0.1.2. PID Tuning and Adjustment the Heated Bed 
A 3D printer’s PID constants (Kp, Ki, and Kd) are used to control the current going to the 
hotend and bed. When incorrect, the system can overshoot during heating or take a long 
time. These constants can manually be altered in the configuration file, where firmware 
settings are located. This was only performed for the initial setup due to the hardware issues 
present in this system.  
Prior to calibration, the heated bed of the initial setup was found to heat extremely slowly, 
capping out at the suboptimal temperature of 70 °C. This occurred when using both the older 
firmware and using the default settings of the newer Marlin firmware. PID autotuning using 
the M303 command was performed to rule out the possibility of this being a firmware issue.  
It was found that the default PID constants were not suitable, as the integration constant (Ki) 
was small, leading to very wait long times to get to the desired temperature. Marlin’s thermal 
protection periods were also edited in the advanced configuration file to prevent false alarms 
==PAGE:82==
81 
 
due to slower-than-average heating of the bed. The PID constants for the hotend were also 
slightly adjusted based on empirical testing.  
In addition to these configuration changes, the power supply was found to have an 
insufficient voltage, contributing to long heating times and the maximum temperature of 70 
°C. The power supply voltage was changed from 12V to 14V. This allowed for faster heating 
and a maximum temperature of 80 °C at the cost of slightly over-volting some of the 
components. Unfortunately, it was later determined that the power supply appears to be too 
weak to sustain this temperature over a prolonged period; the temperature dips after 
approximately 30 minutes of printing, leading to a thermal safety shutoff.  
A2.5.0.1.3. Bed Leveling & Interaction with Filament Material 
One of the most basic adjustments to improve print quality is bed-leveling, in which the bed 
is made parallel to the extruder at the correct distance from the nozzle. This is performed by 
adjusting several large knobs on the bottom of the printer. This was performed on both 
setups when necessary. However, this process is extremely important for the Print2Cast 
material used in the initial setup for reasons described below. 
The Print2Cast wax filament used in the experiment appears to be particularly sensitive to 
this; the material does not readily leave the hotend nozzle unless dragged away by the build 
surface. This leads to droplet-like blobs when too far away from the build plate. When 
distances are too low, the material does not extrude at all, instead collecting and solidifying 
within the hotend, requiring time-consuming maintenance. 
On the Witbox printer, bed leveling entails manually screwing three knobs. Leveling was 
performed by starting a large, flat print and adjusting the relevant knob until the entire print 
was uniform. In practice, this procedure was not entirely possible, as the build plate was 
found to be warped, with the center higher than the corners. Unfortunately, the plate could 
not be replaced due to limited resources, and alternative leveling methods such as patches 
of tape did not work due to poor filament adhesion. Ultimately, the bed was leveled as best 
as possible, and the raised center avoided during prints.  
A2.5.0.1.4. Extruder E-step Calibration 
3D print quality can also be improved by ensuring that a precise amount of filament is 
deposited. E-steps refers to the number of steps that the extruder stepper-motor takes to 
extrude one millimeter of filament [21]. Poorly calibrated E-steps results in under- or over-
extrusion leading to defects such as gaps or blobs, respectively.  
The calibration of E-steps can be performed by marking the filament 120 mm from the 
entrance to printer followed by extruding 100 mm at a slow speed. This can be performed 
using the command ‘G91’ to place the printer in relative mode, followed by ‘G1 E100 F50’ to 
extrude the filament. The amount of filament remaining is measured, and the E-steps are 
adjusted accordingly. This is continued until a distance of 20 mm is measured from the mark 
to the entrance of the printer. This calibration procedure was performed on both setups, 
with the results recorded in Section A2.5.0.2. 
==PAGE:83==
82 
 
A2.5.0.1.4. Retraction Calibration 
Retraction is a reversal of the extruder’s direction after an extrusion operation. This can be 
used to prevent stringing, a cobweb-like structure that occurs when filament continues to 
ooze out after an extrusion operation. To test retraction settings on the current setup, a G-
code tower was generated using the Teaching Tech website [20]. Using this tower, retraction 
settings are automatically changed at certain intervals during the print, and the setting with 
the least stringing is chosen. If necessary, the range of retraction settings can be made 
smaller for increased fine tuning.  
This procedure was performed on the current setup successfully, tuning to 2.5 mm at a speed 
of 40 mm/s. However, no setting was found to satisfactorily reduce stringing in the initial 
setup. Automated attempts using the tower always resulted in melting and collapse during 
printing. An alternative model [22] consisting of spikes of random height was chosen, and 
numerous individual tests performed, but this too was unsuccessful. Failure to rectify 
stringing in the initial setup was one of the major reasons for switching to a different 
filament. Examples of retraction tests are shown in Figure A2.5.0.1.4-1 below.

A2.6.4.2. Procedure for Producing Silicon Molds 
This procedure may be used to produce silicone moulds, which are useful to mass-produce 
the gating system without the need to 3D print it (which can be cumbersome or expensive). 
The process is summarized in Figure A2.6.4.2-1 below. 
1. 3D print the desired piece along with a ‘wax entrance’ part (see Figure A2.6.4.4-2). 
2. Attach the wax entrance to the piece using a small amount of hot glue. 
− A small circular slot ~3.5 mm in diameter can be added to the part to hold the 
wax entrance in place, as done in this report. 
3. Laser cut a frame with an open top out of acrylic (See Section A2.6.4.1). 
− It is also sufficient to use pre-existing pieces of suitable size if they can be 
obtained. 
4. Use a hot glue gun to assemble the frame, sealing the edges to make it watertight. 
− If laser engravings are flipped where the silicone will be held, lines will be 
imprinted in the silicone. This can be used to mark the parting line. 
− The watertightness can be easily tested by pouring water into the frame. Add 
extra glue to areas that show leaks.  
5. Apply a small amount of glue to the flat end of the wax entrance and glue the piece to 
the bottom of the frame.  
− It is recommended to keep at least 5 mm of clearance from the sides of the 
frame. 
6. Combine the necessary amount of silicone and catalyst and stir thoroughly. 
− For RTV 250 silicone, A and B are mixed at a 100A:10B ratio (by weight). 
− One way of estimating this amount is to fill the frame with water and then 
quickly pour this into a cup and marking off the level with a permanent 
marker. Water can permeate most 3D printed parts, so it is best to do this 
quickly to prevent it from being saturated with water. 
7. Pour the mixture into the frame. 
− The frame and part may need to be sprayed with mould release to prevent the 
part from sticking.  
− A vacuum chamber can be used to remove air bubbles, increasing the quality 
of the mould as well as its transparency, making cut out steps easier. 
− Neither of these two optional steps were performed in the context of this 
report due to not having the necessary equipment. 
8. Leave the mixture overnight to solidify. 
− Using a box cutter or scalpel, carefully cut the silicone along the intended 
parting line and extract the 3D printed part. 
− It may be helpful to draw this line with a marker or to use laser engraving 
before cutting. 
9. The final result should be a silicone mould with a cone-like entrance for subsequent 
wax injection.

A2.6.4.3. Procedure for Wax Injection 
This procedure is used for mass-producing the wax parts of a gating system using a wax 
injection machine. The process is summarized in Figure A2.6.4.3-1 below. 
1. Put on PPE: Eye protection, a light face mask, lab-coat, and nylon gloves. 
− This gear is intended to prevent burns from hot liquid wax, which can 
unpredictably spray out of the silicone moulds during filling. 
2. Heat the wax injection machine to approximately ~80 °C (82 °C in this experiment) 
− It can take around 30-60 minutes for the wax to sufficiently melt. 
− The extent of melting can be assessed by color. The blue wax used within this 
report is light blue when solid and dark blue (almost black) when liquid. 
3. Use the hand pump to increase the pressure to approximately 1 bar (15 psi) 
− If the pressure does not rise, ensure that the white pressure release valve is 
closed and that the O-ring is present on the inside of the device. 
4. Ensure that the silicone mould is clean. 
− Small particles such as sand tend to stick onto the rubbery surface of the 
silicone and are difficult to remove unless washed with water. 
− Wax from previous injection sessions should be removed. 
5. Close the silicone mould and compress between two pieces of acrylic secured by thick 
rubber bands. 
− This solution was found to be more reliable that using clamps, which can be 
unwieldy, as well as apply too much pressure and distort the mould. 
− Larger moulds may be held closed by rubber bands alone. 
6. Press the conical entrance of the silicone mould against the wax injector’s nozzle and 
fill. 
− If degassing has not been performed, bubbles present in the silicone make the 
mould largely opaque. Thus, one can only be certain that it is full when the 
mould begins to flash wax out the sides. 
− Hollow parts can be made by partially filling the mould and continuously 
rotating the mould until sufficiently solid (see Figure A2.6.4.3-2). This is 
particularly useful for larger parts. 
7. Wait several minutes for the wax to finish cooling and remove the part. 
− Wax cooling is dependent on the volume of the part; thicker parts need longer 
to cool.  
− Removing parts too early can result in distortion or the piece ripping at thin 
areas. This is especially true for larger parts such as the pouring basin. 
8. Remove flash and other defects from the part. 
− Most defects are easily removed by a simple rub with the fingers. More 
significant issues can be removed via boxcutter.

A2.6.4.4. Procedure for Wax Cleaning and Assembly of the Pattern: 
This procedure may be used to prepare larger wax patterns for shell-making. Wax parts are 
dipped in pattern cleaner (Ranclene 2) to promote good adhesion with slurry. They are then 
joined using hot glue and a soldering iron. See Figure A2.6.4.4-1 below for a summary of the 
process. 
1. Before first cleaning session: Transfer Ranclene 2 to a smaller HDPE container. 
− The original container is large and heavy, making pouring difficult and 
increasing the likelihood of splashes significantly.  
2. Ensure that all wax pieces are reasonably clean and ready for assembly. 
− Avoid using pieces that are distorted or contain holes. 
− Holes can be patched using low-temperature hot-glue. High temperature hot 
glue can cause melting, worsening holes. 
2. Gather materials: Ranclene 2, a large HDPE funnel, an HDPE or glass container, a pair 
of tongs, and two clean, sealable containers (ideally HDPE) filled with de-ionized 
water, labeled ‘first rinse’ and ‘second rinse’. 
− PP buckets appear to degrade when exposed to Ranclene in only a few days 
even when heavily diluted with water. HDPE does not appear to degrade. 
3. Pour the Ranclene into the glass container using the HDPE funnel. 
− The use of the funnel prevents splashing. 
4. Using tongs, dip the part into the Ranclene pattern cleaner and agitate for 30 seconds 
[23]. 
5. Dip the part in the first bucket of de-ionized water and agitate for 15 seconds. 
6. Dip the part into the second bucket of de-ionized water and agitate for 15 seconds. 
7. Using the funnel, pour the Ranclene back into the HDPE container. 
8. Allow the parts to dry for at least one hour.

9. Drill any holes that need to be made in the wax parts. 
− Example: The screw holes in the top of the pouring basin 
− It is significantly easier to do this prior to assembly, as the thin connections 
tend to break if not treated with care. 
10. Create the pattern by joining parts together: apply low-temp hot glue and compress. 
− For the gating system in this report, it is highly recommended to lay the part 
out on its flush, flat side. When doing so, the parts are supported by the surface 
below and do not need to be held in place. 
− It is recommended to avoid touching the wax with the nozzle of the hot glue 
applicator, as the wax will liquify and make a poor connection with the glue. 
11. Under a fume hood, smooth out the seams where the hot glue is using a soldering iron. 
− The aim is to remove cracks that may be filled with slurry in subsequent steps, 
as well as strengthen the bond. 
− Only a light pass is needed. Avoid over-soldering, as this can heavily distort the 
wax and ruin the connection. 
− Avoid using flames for the smoothing process, as this appears to be largely 
ineffective for this purpose.  
i. Flames should under no circumstances be brought into contact with 3D 
printed materials, as they ignite easily and burn fiercely. 
− It is recommended that vents only be superglued, as the use of the soldering 
iron tends to break the connection.

− Vents made using wax filament should be taped or hot glued to auxiliary holes 
on the dip plate.

A2.7.1. Procedure for Shell-making: 
This procedure can be used in the shell-making process. Before proceeding, it is important 
to understand that the shell-making materials contain respirable crystalline silica which can 
cause silicosis. Wear PPE. This procedure uses SuspendaSlurry based on fused silica (FS) and 
applies for non-ferrous metals only. See Figure A2.7.1-1 for a visual overview. 
1. Ensure that the pattern is ready to be dipped. Attach the pattern to the dip plate or 
insert a screw into the pattern and wrap with steel wire (see Figure A2.7.1-2 below 
and Figure A2.6.4.4-1 above). 
2. Put on PPE: Respirator (ideally FFP3 or equivalent), lab coat, nylon gloves, and 
chemical goggles. 
==PAGE:100==
99 
 
3. Gather supplies and place in or near a fume hood: SuspendaSlurry, as well as two 
labeled buckets. Each should contain a 3D-printed shovel and Ranco-Sil A or B.  
4. Remix the slurry by hand until a creamy texture is obtained. 
5. Perform slurry tests in accordance with manufacturer guidelines [13]: 
− Start of each shift: Slurry viscosity using Zahn Cup: 
i. Submerge the #4 Zahn Cup into the slurry fully. Simultaneously pull the 
cup straight out while starting a timer. The slurry will begin to drain 
through the hole in the cup. Stop the timer when a hole is visible in the 
bottom of the cup once again. 
1. If draining time is greater than 21 seconds: Add de-ionized 
water. 
2. If draining time less than 19 seconds: Allow water to evaporate. 
ii. It is important to clean the Zahn cup before the slurry dries, as it could 
permanently clog the cup’s drain hole. A combination of water and 
paper towels were used for this purpose in this report. 
iii. The #4 Zahn cup tends to clog, and multiple viscosity tests are generally 
required before a valid test is achieved. Paper towel was general used 
to clean it out. 
− Weekly: Total solids 
i. Measure the mass of a disposable container 𝑚𝑐𝑜𝑛, such as an aluminum 
pan. 
1. It is highly recommended to use disposable containers. Dried 
SuspendaSlurry can be difficult to impossible to remove without 
force.  
ii. Place a small amount of wet slurry on the aluminum pan and measure 
the total mass 𝑚𝑤𝑒𝑡. 
iii. Place the slurry and pan in an oven at 175 °C for one hour. 
iv. Measure the total weight of the dry slurry and the pan 𝑚𝑑𝑟𝑦. 
v. Calculate the total solids TS: 𝑇𝑆=
𝑚𝑑𝑟𝑦−𝑚𝑐𝑜𝑛
𝑚𝑤𝑒𝑡−𝑚𝑐𝑜𝑛∗100% 
vi. If 𝑇𝑆> 72%, add de-ionized water. 
vii. If TS < 70%, remix thoroughly and/or allow water to evaporate. 
− Note: Evaporation of water can take a significant amount of time, so it is best   
to add small amounts of water. Evaporation can be sped up by increasing 
airflow, for example by directing a fan at the slurry [15]. 
6. Submerge the pattern into the SuspendaSlurry material and rotate several times to 
produce an even coat. 
7. Remove the pattern and allow to drain until dripping is minimal. The pattern is now 
ready for the application of stucco sand. 
8. Within the fume hood, apply stucco sand to build up the coat. Allow it to dry 
sufficiently that it can be touched with a gloved hand without smearing. 
− Testing found that this takes about one hour for small models. 
==PAGE:101==
100 
 
− SuspendaSlurry contains an indicator that changes color as it dries. The shell 
is sufficiently dry for another coat when the color changes from a greenish-
yellow to orange (see Figure A2.7-1 d and e). 
− Stucco can be applied manually using the cat box method: 
i. Place the pattern in the bucket with the appropriate stucco. 
ii. Gently scoop the stucco and pour on the wet pattern. 
iii. Repeat until an even coat of stucco is present on the pattern.  
9. Hang the pattern to dry on steel wire.  
− Two methods are presented in Figure A2.7.1-2 below, showing a way to hang

inserting the shell. This allows the sand to shift, ensuring that the sand does not become
