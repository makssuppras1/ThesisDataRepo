Abstract 
 
Quantification of pollution impacts on surface water quality and ecological status is lim-
ited by conventional characterization techniques. Dissolved organic matter (DOM) and 
suspended solids play a critical role in pollutant source identification and contaminant 
transport within river ecosystems. Nonetheless, traditional sampling fails to capture high 
temporal resolution DOM composition and dynamics, resulting in contaminant sources 
being overlooked and pollution impacts being underestimated. Prolonged high-fre-
quency in situ protein-like (Peak T) fluorescence monitoring was implemented in a peri-
urban stream (North Sjælland, DK) where hydro- meteorological and urban discharge 
data were available. Signal processing was carried out to assess the fluorometer response 
to different wastewater treatment plant (WWTP) discharge types during different 
weather and flow conditions. A strong correlation was found between the normally 
treated WWTP discharge and Peak T fluorescence, indicating a robust in-stream sensor 
response to wastewater inputs. The indirect effects of precipitation on the fluorescence 
signal of the stream were investigated and unknown wet weather urban discharges with 
protein-like DOM signatures were identified as a consequence of the in situ sensor spa-
tio-temporal resolution capabilities. A tributary discharge lacking in Peak T DOM was 
found to not affect the protein-like fluorescence signal of the studied stream and high-
lighted the need for monitoring additional fluorophores in order to more completely 
characterize DOM composition and dynamics. This research demonstrates the potential 
of in situ fluorescence spectroscopy as a valuable approach for identifying urban pollutant 
sources and tracking wastewater impacts on surface water bodies. 
 
In addition to a precise understanding of DOM dynamics, a detailed characterization of 
particle behavior is critical for quantifying contaminant impacts on surface water bodies 
as well as improving pollutant fate and transport modelling. Suspended solids are a sig-
nificant source and important transport mechanism for many priority pollutants. The 
particle settling velocity distribution (PSVD) has been determined to be a critical metric 
for characterizing the behavior of solids within an urban drainage context, but conven-
tionally has not been applied to stream environments. The present study validated the 
ViCAs sedimentation protocol for a peri-urban stream while simultaneously evaluating 
three distinct sampling procedures. Different PSVD results were found for specific 
stretches, where wastewater discharges possibly influence the particle characteristics, and 
have implication for future work with modelling the fate and transport of solid-bound 
contaminants.

Introduction 
Urban runoff and waterways contain considerable quantities of pollutants such as heavy 
metals, chlorinated compounds, and hydrocarbons (Smith et al., 1987; Hvitved-Jacobsen 
et al., 1994; Sansalone et al., 1996; Sansalone and Buchberger, 1997b; Stackelberg, 1997; 
Characklis and Wiesner, 1997; Munn and Gruber, 1997; Furumai et al., 2002; Lin et al., 
2003; Vaze and Chiew, 2004; Tuccillo, 2006; Vallet et al., 2014). These compounds are 
emitted from point sources such as wastewater treatment plant (WWTP) effluents, com-
bined sewer overflows (CSOs), separate sewer system outlets (SSSO), and contaminated 
sites, as well as from diffuse sources such as surface runoff, contaminated sediments, and 
geogenic sources. Pollutant sources can discharge continuously, intermittently or in 
pulses, as is the case for WWTP effluents, CSOs and runoff events respectively. Peri-
urban catchments blend these urban characteristics with agricultural activity that can act 
as a significant non-point source of nutrients, pesticides and organic matter (Kronvang 
et al., 2003; Stedmon and Markager, 2005; Mendoza et al., 2012; Coble et al., 2014; 
Blaen et al., 2016; Soni et al., 2020). Pollution pathways and transport mechanisms in 
these complex environments are difficult to characterize through conventional sampling, 
however novel techniques are available which can lead to improvements in quantifying 
the impacts of contaminants on surface water quality and ecological status. 
The fluorescence spectra of dissolved organic matter (DOM) constituents (i.e. protein-, 
humic-, fulvic-like and lignin-derived components) have been used to identify source-
specific contamination inputs to surface waters (Coble, 1996; Stedmon and Markager, 
2005; Williams et al., 2010; Chen et al., 2017; Khamis et al., 2020). DOM composition 
and dynamics are highly dependent on human activities, land use and water type, and 
therefore provide information on natural and anthropogenic inputs to stream ecosystems 
(Stedmon and Markager, 2005; Williams et al., 2010; Khamis et al., 2020). Understand-
ing of DOM fluorescence in peri-urban streams thus has the potential to provide valuable 
insights into unknown pollution pathways and variable contaminant sources. 
Sediments provide a mechanism by which a range of contaminants can persist and be 
transported in surface waters. Heavy metals, hydrophobic organic compounds and nu-
trients are all preferentially found in the solid-bound phase (Hvitved-Jacobsen et al., 
1994; Pitt et al., 1995; Sansalone et al., 1996; Larson et al., 1997; Sansalone and 
Buchberger, 1997b; Stackelberg, 1997; Gromaire et al., 2001; Furumai et al., 2002; Vaze 
and Chiew, 2004; Wang et al., 2004; Brown and Peake, 2006; Tuccillo, 2006). More-
over, the exposure to and toxicity of contaminants in aquatic environments is strongly 
depend on particle characteristics (Stackelberg, 1997; Munn and Gruber, 1997; Lin et 
al., 2003; Tuccillo, 2006; J. J. Rasmussen et al., 2015). As such, a detailed evaluation of 
particle composition is necessary for accurately describing pollutant fate and transport in 
surface waters. 
Conventional discrete DOM sampling techniques fail to adequately characterize diurnal 
biogeochemical cycles, changes in hydrological regimes and event based dynamics 
(Nimick et al., 2011; Halliday et al., 2015; Bieroza and Heathwaite, 2016; Blaen et al., 
1

2016; Khamis et al., 2020). As a result, pulsed discharges, short duration storm events 
and diel fluctuations in continuous discharges may be overlooked and total pollutant 
loads underestimated. Incomplete understanding of DOM dynamics consequently entails 
a deficient characterization of pollutant sources, making high frequency monitoring of 
DOM a critical issue for improving the quantification of impacts on surface water and 
ecological quality from highly variable pollution events. An increased spatio-temporal 
monitoring resolution through the use of in situ fluorometer technology can improve 
the understanding of DOM dynamics (Khamis et al., 2020) and field deployable fluores-
cence sensors are a viable means for characterizing known discharges and identifying 
unknown pollutant pathways. 
 
Models allow to predict the concentrations of contaminants beyond the range of moni-
toring stations, carry out scenario elaboration for decision making, and design pollution 
control and treatment methods. Contaminant fate and transport in peri-urban streams is 
strongly dependent particle composition, however suspended solids are conventionally 
modelled with a single particle settling velocity. This simplified approach fails to take 
into consideration particle heterogeneity and consequently any associated contaminant 
transport mechanisms, which can lead to erroneous estimations of pollutant sources, sinks 
and fluxes. A particle settling velocity distribution (PSVD) based framework addresses 
this limitation and has been shown to improve modeling of contaminant loads (Aiguier 
et al., 1996; Jacopin et al., 1999; Furumai et al., 2002; Torres et al., 2007; Chebbo and 
Gromaire, 2009; Bachis et al., 2015; Plana et al., 2019; Vanrolleghem et al., 2019). The-
oretical PSVD calculations are challenging and pose serious limitations (Chebbo and 
Gromaire, 2009; Torfs et al., 2016), making direct experimental determination of the 
PSVD is a worthwhile endeavor. 
 
1.1. Objectives 
 
The current study applied novel techniques for detecting DOM composition and dy-
namics through in situ fluorescence monitoring and experimentally characterizing the 
PSVD of a Danish peri-urban stream. The general objective was to validate the applica-
bility of a field deployable fluorometer for in-stream pollutant source tracking through 
direct measurements of the receiving water body’s characteristics. Additionally, an exist-
ing sedimentation protocol was applied for the first time within the context of a stream 
environment to determine whether PSVD characterization was practicable. Specific ob-
jectives included: 
 
1) Implement long term, high frequency in situ fluorescence monitoring up- and down-
stream of two wastewater discharge locations. 
2) Evaluate the pollutant source tracking capabilities of the in situ fluorometer with 
respect to WWTP effluents, tributaries, land uses and background stream conditions; 
integrate fluorescence and hydro-meteorological data for the identification of un-
known pollution pathways. 
3) Develop the necessary hardware for the sedimentation column in-house and establish 
procedures for the current and future studies. 
4) Evaluate the suitability of PSVD characterization in a riverine environment.

Background 
2.1. DOM and contaminants in peri-urban catchments 
Dissolved organic matter plays a major role in stream biogeochemical processes, acting 
as an energy source for microbial metabolism, altering bacterial composition and func-
tion, controlling levels of dissolved oxygen and nutrient availability, and driving changes 
in stream conditions (Leenheer and Croué, 2003; Evans et al., 2005; Judd et al., 2006; 
Battin et al., 2008). Furthermore, the ability of DOM to bind with pollutants is consid-
ered to control the speciation, concentration and bioavailability of metals (Leenheer and 
Croué, 2003; Evans et al., 2005; Sarathy and Allen, 2005; Fu et al., 2007) as well as the 
transport and bioavailability of persistent organic pollutants (Gourlay et al., 2005; Katsoy-
iannis and Samara, 2007). Different land uses and human activities have been found to 
influence DOM composition and dynamics, and peri-urban catchments incorporate a 
high spatial density of distinct DOM sources with closely interlinked anthropogenic and 
natural features (Stedmon and Markager, 2005; Williams et al., 2010; Khamis et al., 
2020). These sources include paved surfaces, buildings, sewage systems, agricultural lands 
and vegetated areas, and each can display a specific DOM composition (Khamis et al., 
2020). DOM constituents include biologically labile protein-like components, refractory 
humic-like, fulvic-like and lignin-derived components, and hydrocarbon components, 
among others (Reynolds, 2002; Coble et al., 2014). 
Vehicular activity on urban roadways is a significant source of heavy metals (Al, Cd, Cr, 
Cu, Fe, Pb, Mn, Ni, Zn) that are mobilized in surface runoff as dissolved and particulate-
bound constituents, and transported directly into receiving water bodies, combined 
sewer systems, and separate sewer systems (Hvitved-Jacobsen et al., 1994; Sansalone and 
Buchberger, 1997b; Characklis and Wiesner, 1997; Tuccillo, 2006). Additional sources 
include industry, buildings and metal structures, accumulated sediments in sewer systems, 
as well as fuel and oils (Makepeace et al., 1995; Gromaire et al., 2001; Brown and Peake, 
2006; Barbosa et al., 2012; Sakson et al., 2018). Heavy metals exert short-term toxicity 
as a function of speciation and environmental concentrations, as well as long term im-
pacts due to accumulation in bed sediments (Makepeace et al., 1995; Sansalone et al., 
1996; Lin et al., 2003; Tuccillo, 2006). Removal by WWTPs is incomplete (Sakson et 
al., 2018) and metal pollutant loads per unit area are of a similar order of magnitude for 
combined sewer systems, urban runoff, and separate sewer systems, all of which can result 
in hazardous impacts to aquatic life and downstream users (Hvitved-Jacobsen, 1990; 
Hvitved-Jacobsen et al., 1994; Makepeace et al., 1995; Characklis and Wiesner, 1997; 
Sakson et al., 2018; Vanrolleghem et al., 2019). 
Legacy compounds such as organochlorine pesticides and polychlorinated biphenyls 
(PCBs), despite no longer being in use or outright banned, are still present at environ-
mental concentrations that cause adverse effects (Larson et al., 1997; Stackelberg, 1997; 
Munn and Gruber, 1997; Kronvang et al., 2003; J. J. Rasmussen et al., 2015). These 
compounds are characterized by their resistance to degradation and strong partitioning

to organic matter, resulting in accumulation in bed sediments that in turn act as long-
term contaminant sources (Smith et al., 1987; Larson et al., 1997; Stackelberg, 1997; 
Munn and Gruber, 1997). Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) have similar char-
acteristics (prevalence, toxicity, persistence), however current sources include incom-
plete combustion of fossil fuels, vehicle emissions and wear, factory discharges, hydro-
carbon spills, and commercial incinerators (Makepeace et al., 1995; Wang et al., 2004; 
Brown and Peake, 2006; Barbosa et al., 2012). Current and historic land use is related to 
the prevalence of these pollutants, where organochlorine pesticides are associated with 
past agricultural activity, and to a lesser extent urban use, while PCBs and PAHs are 
associated with urban and industrial uses (Smith et al., 1987; Larson et al., 1997; Munn 
and Gruber, 1997; J. J. Rasmussen et al., 2015). 
 
2.2. DOM characterization 
 
Different sources of organic matter display a characteristic DOM signature consisting of 
specific components. Humic substances derived from the remineralization of organic 
matter and amino acids from biological activity are the two major constituents of DOM 
in uncontaminated natural waters; fluorescence spectroscopy has been used extensively 
in order to characterize these humic-like and protein-like components (Birdwell and 
Engel, 2010; Coble et al., 2014). Excitation-emission matrix spectroscopy (EEMS) col-
lates DOM emission spectra corresponding to a range of excitation wavelengths into a 
three dimensional data matrix. Fluorescence maxima corresponding to specific excita-
tion-emission wavelength pairs (λEX/λEM) in these arrays are indicative of constituent 
components and have been used to identify DOM contributions to surface waters from 
forested catchments, agricultural lands, urban areas, treated wastewater effluents, and ur-
ban drainage and runoff (Coble, 1996; Stedmon and Markager, 2005; Williams et al., 
2010; Chen et al., 2017; Khamis et al., 2020). Mixtures of fluorescing DOM result in an 
addition of their respective spectra, allowing for the identification of specific contaminant 
source types by decomposing the bulk signal into individual characteristic emission peaks 
(Galapate et al., 1998). 
 
Humic-like fluorescence varies across environments with the three most commonly de-
scribed peaks being: Peak A at λEX/λEM = 240-260nm/400-460nm, Peak C at 320-
360nm/420-460nm, and Peak M at 290-310nm/370-410nm (see Figure 2-1) (Birdwell 
and Engel, 2010; Coble et al., 2014). A subset of the longer wavelengths of Peak A and 
Peak C are always observed together and can be found in soils, freshwaters, and the deep 
ocean, albeit in varying proportions (Coble et al., 2014). Peak M is indicative of recent 
autochthonous microbial transformation of humic substances and is also always associated 
with a second peak, namely the subset of shorter wavelengths of Peak A (Coble et al., 
2014).  
 
Protein-like Peak B fluorescence occurs at λEX/λEM of 275-280nm/305nm and Peak T 
is observed at two excitation-emission wavelength pairs of 220-230nm/340-350nm and 
275-280nm/340-350nm (see Figure 2-1) (Reynolds and Ahmad, 1997; Coble et al., 
1998, 2014; Baker and Inverarity, 2004). These maxima are considerably less variable 
than the humic-like components, with Peak B and Peak T resembling the fluorescence

emission spectra possibly arising from a multitude of different free or combined amino 
acids (Coble, 1996; Baker and Inverarity, 2004; Coble et al., 2014). 
 
Peak T fluorescence is generally indicative of treated and non-treated wastewater, as well 
as anthropogenic impacts on surface water bodies (Reynolds and Ahmad, 1997; Reyn-
olds, 2002; Baker and Inverarity, 2004; Hudson et al., 2008). Peak T fluorescence as a 
proxy for anthropogenic impacts on surface water quality will be the focus of the present 
investigation, together with the characterization of particle settling velocities.

2.3. Monitoring fluorescence 
 
Ex situ EEMS analysis presents a series of logistical limitations which effectively limit the 
number of samples that can be processed: airtight containers must be kept in the dark, 
storage temperatures need to be maintained at ~4ºC, storage time should be minimized 
to prevent sample degradation, and care must be taken to avoid contamination (Carstea 
et al., 2020). Moreover, monitoring frequency attainable through discrete sample col-
lection may be insufficient to capture changes in stream DOM composition (Nimick et 
al., 2011; Halliday et al., 2015; Bieroza and Heathwaite, 2016; Blaen et al., 2016; Khamis 
et al., 2020). In situ fluorescence measurements can address these limitations through 
high spatial and temporal resolution monitoring, as well as direct measurement of the 
actual environmental conditions (Carstea et al., 2020; Khamis et al., 2020). Field deploy-
able sensors, however, are limited to specific excitation-emission wavelength pairs, such 
as either protein-like or humic-like fluorescence.

2.4. Solid-bound contaminant transport 
 
Significant concentrations of suspended solids in aquatic environments are due to parti-
cles spanning in size from sand (>60µm), to silt (2-60µm), and clays (<2µm), and accu-
mulate in bed sediment according to their settling characteristics (Reible, 2014). The 
larger fraction has a tendency to settle quickly, whereas fine-grained material remains 
largely suspended in high energy environments but is deposited and accumulated in low 
energy zones. A low energy environment is characterized by a continuous low flow, or 
in dynamic systems, temporary relative quiescence interrupted by high flow events 
(Reible, 2014). Fine-grained sediments are also typically enriched in organic matter and 
may contain 1-5% organic carbon (Characklis and Wiesner, 1997; Andral et al., 1999; 
Tuccillo, 2006; Rügner et al., 2013; Reible, 2014). 
 
Hydrophobic organic contaminants of concern, including legacy pesticides, PAHs and 
PCBs, preferentially sorb to the organic carbon fraction of particulates; thus the solid and 
aqueous phase partitioning can be defined as (Reible, 2014):

Heavy metals interact with solids through irreversible (precipitation) and reversible (ad-
sorption and cation exchange) pathways. Direct adsorption of metals onto particulates is 
due to the extremely large specific surface area and resulting intermolecular forces of 
fine-grained materials (Förstner and Wittmann, 1981; Lee et al., 1981; Alkhatib and 
Castor, 2000; Lin et al., 2003; Reible, 2014). Cation exchange occurs when positively 
charged metal ions are electrostatically attracted to negatively charged surface sites of 
particulates such as clay minerals, freshly precipitated iron hydroxides, amorphous silicic 
acids, and organic substance (Förstner and Wittmann, 1981; Reible, 2014). The attrac-
tive force between metal cations and particulate matter can be weak, resulting in the

metals being easily replaceable; likewise, metal ions are sorbed and released by cation 
exchange with other species or by the acidification of the matrix. Metal precipitates, on 
the other hand, are effectively insoluble: metal sulfides are the most important precipi-
tates in natural environments and are stable under the reducing conditions found a few 
centimeters below the surface of most sediments (Reible, 2014). Exposure of precipitates 
to oxic conditions, however, can result in the release of these metals (Hong et al., 2011). 
 
In urban wet weather flows, fine suspended solids (<100µm) account for most of the 
TSS, with the majority of particles corresponding to the silt and clay size fractions 
(Chebbo and Bachoc, 1992; Andral et al., 1999; Furumai et al., 2002). A greater pro-
portion of particles larger than 100µm, as well as an overall decreased particle density is 
exhibited in combined sewer networks as compared to separate systems (Chebbo and 
Bachoc, 1992) and has direct implications on particle-bound contaminants (i.e. particle 
size dependent pollutant loads, see below) and organic carbon content (see above for 
contaminant sorption to fOC). Solids transport is dependent on settling velocity and is 
determined by particle size, density and shape, which in turn vary by rain event, as well 
as location due to site specific characteristics and grain heterogeneity (Chebbo and 
Bachoc, 1992; Aiguier et al., 1996). 
 
Many contaminants of concern in urban environments are predominantly associated with 
suspended solids and sediments: greater pollutant loads have been found in the particle- 
bound phase than in the dissolved form for heavy metals (Hvitved-Jacobsen et al., 1994; 
Pitt et al., 1995; Sansalone et al., 1996; Sansalone and Buchberger, 1997b; Gromaire et 
al., 2001; Furumai et al., 2002; Brown and Peake, 2006; Tuccillo, 2006), hydrophobic 
organic compounds (Pitt et al., 1995; Larson et al., 1997; Stackelberg, 1997; Wang et 
al., 2004; Brown and Peake, 2006), and nutrients (Hvitved-Jacobsen et al., 1994; Vaze 
and Chiew, 2004). Organochlorine pesticides can be present in suspended and bed sed-
iments at concentrations orders of magnitude larger than in the water column (Larson et 
al., 1997; Stackelberg, 1997). PAH loading in sewer systems has been found to increase 
due to mobilization of particulates during storm events, and solid-bound concentrations 
of both PCBs and PAHs constitute the primary stocks of these pollutants in the environ-
ment (Makepeace et al., 1995; Pitt et al., 1995; Stackelberg, 1997; Wang et al., 2004; 
Brown and Peake, 2006). 
 
Ecotoxicological experiments have shown similar concentration-response relationships 
for freely dissolved versus solid-bound contaminants (J. J. Rasmussen et al., 2016). In 
fact, rather than becoming immobilized in the solid phase, particles can be an important 
transport and exposure mechanism for pollutants (Stackelberg, 1997; Munn and Gruber, 
1997; Lin et al., 2003; Tuccillo, 2006; J. J. Rasmussen et al., 2015). Suspended solids can 
be a primary transport mechanism as well as an influencing factor in metal toxicity and 
bioavailability (Makepeace et al., 1995; Lin et al., 2003; Tuccillo, 2006) and fine sedi-
ments can transport legacy pollutants decades after their initial input to the environment, 
as well as provide an important exposure route for stream biota (Stackelberg, 1997; 
Munn and Gruber, 1997; J. J. Rasmussen et al., 2015). Moreover, smaller particle sizes 
have been associated with higher pollutant loads (Sansalone and Buchberger, 1997a;

Particle characteristics are consequently a key parameter for determining the transport of 
contaminants and the effect of urban flows on the environment. Moreover, according 
to Aiguier et al. (1996) and Vanrolleghem et al. (2019), settling velocity is the most 
important process to consider when describing solid-bound pollution fate and transport, 
as it encompasses other particle parameters such as density, size and organic matter con-
tent. Thus, there is a need to accurately determine the settling velocity of suspended 
solids in urban water systems.

the specific gravity of the particle (kg/m3) , 𝜌w is the specific gravity of water (kg/m3),

However, individual particle properties vary by site and rain event (Chebbo and Bachoc, 
1992; Andral et al., 1999) making it especially difficult to accurately relate the particle 
size and density distributions (Chebbo and Gromaire, 2009; Torfs et al., 2016). Likewise, 
variability in measured particle densities (Zanders, 2005), effects on particle diameter 
characterization of different methods (Plana et al., 2018), and organic matter content of 
the solids (Chebbo and Bachoc, 1992) can lead to variations in the calculated settling 
velocity by a factor of more than 25. Moreover, the shapes of particles in combined 
sewer systems differ considerably from the spherical assumption, further impairing the 
characterization. 
 
Several experimental protocols have been developed by multiple research teams in order 
to overcome the above mentioned limitations. These methods are based on the direct 
measurement of particle settling velocity classes (i.e. cumulative fraction of TSS mass 
with a settling velocity less than or equal to a predefined value) in order to quantify the 
distribution of settling velocities in a representative sample. Existing protocols can be 
classified by the following conditions: (a) static flow, such as in quiescent sedimentation 
columns; and (b) dynamic flow, such as in elutriation devices. 
 
Some recognized static settling methods include the Aston procedure (Tyack et al., 
1993), Umwelt- und Fluid-Technik (UFT) column (Michelbach and Wöhrle, 1993), 
the procedure developed at the Centre d’Enseignement et de Recherche pour la Gestion

des Ressources Naturelles et de l’Environnement (CERGRENE) (Chebbo, 1992), and 
the ViCAs (Vitesse de Chute en Assainissement, French for settling velocity in sanitation) 
protocol (Chebbo and Gromaire, 2009). Sedimentation column heights vary from 0.2 
to 1.8m, sample volume ranges between 1 and 40L, and sample pretreatment is required 
for some procedures. Dynamic settling protocols include mechanically generating tur-
bulence in a sedimentation column (M. R. Rasmussen and Larsen, 1996) and multi-
chamber elutriation systems (Krishnappan et al., 2004). These dynamic experimental set-
ups are designed to more closely represent natural conditions by incorporating fluid mo-
tion in the design, but are mechanically complex, require constant pumping, and neces-
sitate sample volumes of up to 65L. Comparative studies have shown that different static 
methods result in significantly different PSVD curves due to differences in procedures 
and devices (Aiguier et al., 1996), whereas the static ViCAs protocol and dynamic elu-
triation systems provide equivalent results (Plana et al., 2019). The ViCAs protocol stands 
out for its simplicity, no need for pretreatment, relatively small sample volume require-
ments, and reproducibility.

3.2. Sampling, monitoring and analytical protocol 
 
The current study relied on excitation-emission matrix spectroscopy results from samples 
previously collected during the Riverscapes2 project. The EEMS data can be used to 
characterize land uses, specific contaminants, and different anthropogenic impacts within 
the catchment, and in this case would guide the implementation of an in situ fluorescence 
sensor for the continuous monitoring of a specific pollution pathway, namely, the dry 
and wet weather effluents of WWTPs. Deployment of the fluorometer was accompanied 
by a general water quality parameter multimeter as well as DOM grab sampling for full 
EEMS analysis. Finally, suspended and streambed solids were collected to evaluate the 
applicability of the ViCAs protocol to the current study area.

3.2.4. ViCAs experimental protocol 
 
The ViCAs experimental protocol was originally developed by Chebbo and Gromaire 
(2009) in order to better characterize particle settling velocity distribution (PSVD) in 
stormwater, and over the following decade was repeatedly proven reliable and repro-
ducible within an urban water management context (Vallet et al., 2014; Maruéjouls et 
al., 2014; Bachis et al., 2015; Plana et al., 2019; Vanrolleghem et al., 2019). This oper-
ating procedure requires comparatively small sample volumes (~5L), utilizes relatively 
inexpensive and simple to manufacture hardware, can be carried out by a single operator, 
and is less time intensive than other methods. Equally important is the open availability 
of technical drawings for the experimental setup, detailed descriptions of the sampling 
procedure, and data processing software. Thus, the ViCAs protocol proves to be useful 
not only as an experimental method, but also as an excellent tool for defining particle 
settling velocity classes as inputs for water quality models (Vallet et al., 2014; Bachis et 
al., 2015).

Results and discussion 
4.1. Preliminary EEMS evaluation 
Excitation-emission matrix spectroscopy analysis of DOM fluorescence has provided in-
sight into pollution pathways and contaminant inputs to surface waters from a variety of 
distinct sources. As part of the Riverscapes project field campaigns, EEMs had been pre-
viously recorded for discrete samples along the Usserød stream in varying flow conditions 
and seasons from Oct-2018 through Oct-2019. This wealth of data included samples 
taken up- and down-stream of two distinct WWTPs discharge locations and provided 
an opportunity to investigate whether the treated wastewater effluent imparted a dis-
cernible fluorescence signal to the receiving water body.

was followed by a pulse in the sensor response at the iuDÅ location; consequently, the

4.3. ViCAs PSVD 
 
Three distinct sampling techniques (see sections 3.2.5 and 3.2.6) were investigated with 
respect to the experimental ViCAs protocol under dry weather flow conditions. Of 
these, 80% of the kick samples conformed to the quality control checks (QCCs), whereas 
neither the water column nor the sediment samples passed the QCCs. These failed tech-
niques provided valuable insights into the limitations of the experimental setup as well 
as the practical limits of sampling a peri-urban stream. 
 
No minimum effective suspended solids concentration has been reported for the ViCAs 
protocol, however published work has focused on samples with TSS ranging from ap-
proximately 60 – 360 mg/L (Bachis et al., 2015; Plana et al., 2019). Given that ViCAs

PSVD studies have been carried out in the context of stormwater and sewer systems, the 
comparatively low TSS environment (~10mg/L) of the Usserød stream would test the 
limits of the experimental protocol. In order to maximize the chances of good quality 
results, preliminary water column sampling was carried out at the usOUT location as it 
was expected to have the highest TSS concentrations based on a visual inspection of the 
study area (see Figure 7-20, Appendix I). Additionally, given the knowledge that low 
TSS samples contain a larger fraction of slower settling particles (Bachis et al., 2015; Plana 
et al., 2019) the maximum recommended duration for the settling experiment of 22hrs 
was selected in an effort to conform to the QCCs. 
 
The evaluated water column sample contained a TSS concentration of 7.3mg/L and the 
mass balance check was less than ±10%. However the final mass of particles remaining 
in the settling column was approximately 70% of the initial concentration of solids in the 
column; this remaining fraction of the solids was much greater than the recommended 
maximum of 30%, and consequently the measurement failed the QCCs. Moreover, 80% 
of the masses of the collected particles were on the order of 0.4mg, which is comparable 
to the scale error, and would require adjusting the predefined time intervals to improve 
the quality of the measurements.  
 
Despite the ViCAs settling results for the water column sample not being quantitively 
significant, important characteristics of the Usserød stream could be inferred. A majority 
of the suspended solids in the stream were characterized by a settling velocity of less than 
0.03m/hr. This extremely slow settling fraction is expected to be readily transported and 
unlikely to ever reach the streambed under non-stagnant flow conditions; as such these 
suspended particles would be expected to have quantitively different chemical and bio-
logical characteristics to those found in the streambed sediments (Alber, 2000). The dis-
tinction between a non-settleable and settleable solids could thus have implications on 
the fractionation of contaminants in the context of quantifying solid-bound pollutant 
transport. However, these particle characteristics are specific to dry weather and low TSS 
concentrations and are not representative of all streamflow conditions. 
 
Water column sample results confirmed the need for high TSS concentrations in order 
to carry out a successful ViCAs characterization within the context of a peri-urban stream 
and served to guide additional sampling in the study area. A straightforward method that 
was attempted in order to increase the suspended solids load involved collecting sediment 
core samples that would be used to prepare a solution with an appropriate concentration 
of TSS. This approach was expected to conform to the ViCAs QCCs as well as allowing 
for representative sampling of streambed sediment and flexibility in preparing different 
TSS concentration mixtures; however, limitations regarding all three aspects were en-
countered. 
 
The preparation of the suspended solids sample solution turned out to be much less 
flexible than expected. Weighing out a portion of the pooled and homogenized core 
samples with the intent of achieving an approximate TSS concentration was counter-
productive since a large fraction of the solids were retained when washing the sample 
through a 2mm mesh, as dictated by the protocol (see Figure 7-21, Appendix I). 
 
40 
==PAGE:47==
 
Consequently, purposefully selecting the final solution TSS concentration was essentially 
not possible and had to be gauged primarily by the sample appearance. This limitation 
could be addressed by additional preparation steps of the core samples but would run 
counter to one of the primary tenets of the ViCAs methodology: simplicity. Thus an 
alternative sampling procedure (i.e. kick sampling) would prove to be a more appropriate 
approach.  
 
With respect to the representativeness of the cores, the rocky structure of the streambed 
limited the locations where useful samples could be obtained. Zones with high flow 
exhibited a predominantly gravel bottom and resulted in cores that were not suitable for 
the sedimentation experiment due to a lack of fine particles. This resulted in areas of 
relatively quiescent flow being preferentially sampled in order to obtain useable results 
(see Figure 7-22, Appendix I for a range of collected sediment cores). An extreme ex-
ample of this limitation was the dsCSO location where core samples could not be taken 
because the stream section had been lined in concrete. Again, this limitation would be 
addressed with the kick sample procedure. 
 
Both sediment core samples failed the mass balance QCCs for the ViCAs sedimentation 
experiment because of the characteristics of the collected solids. It must be noted that 
the geometry of the ViCAs setup limits the maximum particle settling velocity that can 
be evaluated, requiring a larger settling column (on the order of 2m (Plana et al., 2019)) 
for samples with fast settling particles. Core samples, however, preferentially collected 
fast settling sand-like particles that could not be adequately quantified by the current 
experimental setup. This could be seen when preparing the suspended solids sample so-
lution (see Figure 7-21, Appendix I), as the particles remaining after wet sieving were 
extremely fast settling. Moreover, even with vigorous mixing, a homogeneous sample 
was difficult to achieve due to the fast settling velocity of the solids; this would prove a 
major shortcoming as the ViCAs protocol hinges on a homogeneous starting solution. 
As mentioned previously, adequate characterization of these fast settling particles would 
require modifying the experimental apparatus; an appropriate alternative would be a dif-
ferent sampling procedure such as the kick sample procedure which will be discussed 
next. 
 
The kick sample procedure was devised with the aim of mimicking the increased load 
of SS resulting from sediment resuspension during high flow events. Additionally, this 
sampling methods addressed the limitations discussed above by simultaneously being sim-
ple to implement, providing a high TSS load, being representative of the stream section, 
and consistently passing the QCCs. 
 
In contrast to core sampling, kick sampling could be easily applied to any stream section, 
even in the concrete-lined dsCSO area. Agitating the streambed resulted in a plume of 
suspended sediments regardless of how rocky or compacted the area. Additionally, kick 
sampling could be quickly and effectively applied to different zones of the sampled stream 
section (i.e. high flow, low flow, soft bottom, hard bottom, etc.) providing suspended 
particles representative of all the local stream conditions. Moreover, as mentioned pre-
viously, the suspended particles collected with the kick samples were more likely to

stream section and can be influenced by anthropogenic inputs such as WWTP discharges. 
Consequently, the concept of PSVD should be introduced to a contaminant fate and 
transport model in order to evaluate the impacts of non-unique settling velocities on 
solid-bound contaminant transport in a peri-urban streams context. Two main aspects 
should be taken into consideration when assessing the importance of PSVD on contam-
inant transport: (a) number and types of velocity classes to be considered; and (b) frac-
tionation of contaminants into velocity classes. The number of settling classes has been 
investigated in the context of primary settling tanks, with five constant settling velocities 
proving to be a good balance between model accuracy and computational requirements 
(Bachis et al., 2015). Moreover, the type of velocity classes (i.e. representative of the 
slower or faster regions of the PSVD curve) have been shown to have timescale depend-
ent effects on modelling outcomes (Vallet et al., 2014). Consequently, the same evalua-
tions should be carried out in the context of peri-urban streams in order to determine 
whether a significant effect on contaminant transport exists. Additionally, an equivalent

Conclusions 
Understanding of DOM dynamics and particle behavior are both essential for improving 
the characterization of pollutant fate and transport. The present study implemented novel 
techniques regarding in situ fluorescence monitoring and PSVD measurements in an 
attempt to address these knowledge gaps and demonstrated that in-stream pollutant 
source tracking is viable through the use of a field deployable fluorometer. Additionally, 
a PSVD characterization was successfully carried out for a peri-urban stream. 
An autonomous Peak T fluorometer was deployed between Winter and Spring 2020 
(19-Feb to 20-Jun) in order to capture the impacts of wastewater discharges on the bulk 
characteristics of a peri-urban stream. The in situ fluorometer proved to be a robust tool 
with minimal maintenance requirements that was well suited for high frequency moni-
toring of wastewater and other urban discharges, and demonstrated the potential for fu-
ture work with a network of in situ sensors for characterizing the complex DOM dy-
namics of peri-urban catchments. 
A cross-correlation analysis showed that the Peak T sensor was sensitive to diel and intra-
daily variations in WWTP effluent for two distinct discharge locations across a range of 
flow conditions. Dry weather and low flow conditions resulted in the strongest correla-
tion between the sensor response and the treated wastewater discharge due to the 
WWTP effluent accounting for up to 60% of the total streamflow. Precipitation events 
were observed in conjunction with a decreased correlation between the sensor response 
and the wastewater discharge because of additional contributions to the stream fluores-
cence signal through various pathways. Monitoring of Peak T fluorescence during rain 
events upstream from identified WWTP outlets led to the identification of protein-like 
DOM contributions from an overflow reservoir and a seasonally eutrophic lake. Land 
use impacts on the sensor response were evaluated by monitoring a tributary with an 
agricultural headwaters. The tributary showed a distinct DOM composition to the 
wastewater impacted stream with a comparatively weak protein-like signal that was 
found to not appreciably affect the Peak T dynamics of the studied stream. This simul-
taneously highlighted the robustness of the sensor response to a specific pollutant source 
type and the need for monitoring additional excitation-emission wavelength pairs in or-
der to carry out a more complete characterization of DOM dynamics.  
ViCAs experimental hardware and protocols were established and are now available for 
future PSVD studies at DTU Environment. Three sampling techniques were tested for 
the development of a reproducible sedimentation procedure; these included water col-
umn, sediment core, and kick sampling methods. Water column and sediment core sam-
ples failed QCCs due to the physical limitations of the experimental setup, whereas kick 
samples demonstrated consistent results. The particle characterization method could be 
further improved by evaluating different experimental setup geometries and additional 
sampling methods.

To the author’s knowledge, quantification of the PSVD for a peri-urban stream was 
successfully demonstrated using the adapted ViCAs methodology for the first time. Dis-
tinct particle characteristics were observed for specific stream sections and may have im-
plications for the modelling of solid-bound contaminant fate and transport. Results sug-
gest that wastewater inputs have an effect on particle settling velocity characteristics, even 
for intermittent discharges, and are spatially consistent with anthropogenic impacts cap-
tured by in situ fluorescence data. The ViCAs methodology has been shown to be an 
applicable method for characterizing suspended solids in a peri-urban environment, and 
further work is necessary for identifying the range of PSVDs and quantifying the impacts 
of settling velocity classes on contaminant transport. 
 
The insights gained from the present study shed light on often unquantified traits of peri-
urban stream environments. An in situ technique for tracking unknown and variable 
pollutant sources was validated and should prove invaluable for improving the charac-
terization of dynamic contaminant loads on aquatic environments. Moreover, a new 
paradigm for describing particulate composition in stream environments has been intro-
duced that will shape future studies on solid bound contaminant fate and transport. The 
findings herein represent the increased level of detail that can be achieved for surface 
water quality characterization through the use of novel techniques. 
 
5.1. Future outlook 
 
A wealth of opportunities exist for expanding upon in situ fluorescence monitoring and 
experimental PSVD characterization in stream environments, and include: 
 
In situ fluorometric quantification of contaminants 
A combination of site specific empirical correction factors and field calibrations are nec-
essary in order to accurately quantify pollutant concentrations based on fluorescence 
spectra. This work would allow for high temporal resolution quantification of discharge 
impacts through direct monitoring of bulk stream characteristics. 
 
Multiple excitation-emission wavelength pair monitoring 
Monitoring of additional fluorescence peaks increases the ability to distinguish sources 
of DOM and additionally provides insights into the effects of biochemical transformation 
of organic matter on the fluorescence signature of the water body. 
 
Serial PSVD characterization and impact on contaminant transport 
Spatio-temporal variation in stream PSVDs should be quantified in order to determine 
the possible range of particle characteristics present in peri-urban streams; different ex-
perimental setups and sampling techniques can be implemented for slow and fast settling 
velocity extremes . Water quality modelling can subsequently be applied to evaluating 
the impact of PSVD ranges on contaminant fate and transport. 
 
Integration of characterization technique 
Ultimately, the integration of high frequency pollution inputs derived from in situ fluo-
rometry and PSVD data should be pursued in an effort to improve surface water quality 
characterization.

Appendix D – ViCAs experimental operating procedure 
 
Based on Experimental methods in wastewater treatment (Torfs et al., 2016) and modified for the hard-
ware used in the present study. 
 
MATERIALS: 
- 
ViCAs experimental setup (settling column, collection cups, cantilever support, sample trough, 
vacuum pump and flasks) 
- 
500mL glassware 
- 
Stopwatch 
- 
TSS analysis materials 
 
PROCEDURE: 
The sample to be analyzed is poured into the sample trough and the open base of the settling column 
is submerged within the trough. A sample is quickly aspirated into the column where the desired water 
level is held under vacuum pressure for the duration of the procedure. Settled solids are recovered in 
a series of collection cups from the bottom of the column during predefined time intervals; the collection 
cups are first filled with distilled water and then immersed in the sample trough and placed under the 
settling column. The contents of the collection cup are filtered and the mass of recovered solids is 
measured. 
 
Sample preparation 
- 
Pour composite sample through a 2mm sieve into sample trough and homogenize with vigorous 
mixing; for sediment core samples, weigh out the desired mass of solids and wash through a 
2mm sieve with sample water into the sample trough and homogenize with vigorous mixing. 
- 
Collect a 500mL from the homogenized sample trough to determine the initial particle concen-
tration. 
 
Filling the column 
- 
Make sure sample trough is thoroughly homogenized. 
- 
Place the sedimentation column in the sample trough. With the valve closed, pull a vacuum on 
the vacuum flasks. Open and close the valve to fill the column (in 2 to 5 seconds). 
 
Sedimentation 
- 
Immediately after closing the valve, place the first collection cup under the sedimentation col-
umn and start the timer. 
- 
Mark the water column height. 
- 
Disconnect the pumping equipment. 
 
Changing the collection cups 
- 
Ten seconds before the predefined time interval, introduce the water filled collection cup in the 
sample trough. 
- 
Simultaneously remove the previous cup and place the new cup under the sedimentation col-
umn. 
- 
Predefined time intervals were selected at 1, 2, 4, 6, 10, 16, 32min, 1, 2, 4, and 22hrs. Devia-
tions from the defined time intervals must be noted. 
 
Final particle concentration 
- 
After the final collection cup is removed, plug the sedimentation column and remove it from the 
sample trough. 
- 
Homogenize the contents and collect a 500mL sample to determine the final particle concen-
tration. 
 
Solids analysis procedure 
Initial particle concentration, final particle concentration, and collection cup contents are analyzed ac-
cording to Standard Method 2050 D (Clesceri et al., 1998) as modified for ViCAs sedimentation exper-
iment (Appendix E).

Appendix E – TSS analysis procedure 
 
Based on Standard Method 2050 D (Clesceri et al., 1998) and modified for ViCAs sedimentation exper-
iment. 
 
MATERIALS: 
- 
Whatman glass microfiber GF/F filters 
- 
Filtration apparatus 
- 
Aluminum weighing dish 
- 
Pre-weighed Erlenmeyer flask 
- 
Drying oven 
- 
Desiccator 
- 
Precision scale 
 
PROCEDURE: 
Preparation of glass-fiber filter disks 
Insert filter into filtration apparatus with wrinkled side up. Apply vacuum and wash the filter with three 
20mL volumes of distilled water; continue suction to remove all traces of water. Transfer the filter to an 
aluminum weighing dish and dry in an oven at 105ºC for 1hr. Cool in desiccator and weigh; repeat 
drying, cooling, desiccating and weighing until a constant weight is recorded, or weight change is less 
than 4% or less than 0.5mg. 
 
Sample analysis – initial and final particle concentrations in ViCAs sedimentation column 
Insert filter into filtration apparatus, apply vacuum and wet the filter with a small volume of distilled water. 
Homogenize sample and fill filter funnel with approximately 150mL. Collect filtrate in pre-weighed Er-
lenmeyer flask; determine sample volume from the filtrate mass. Transfer the filter to an aluminum 
weighing dish and dry in an oven at 105ºC for 1hr. Cool in desiccator and weigh; repeat drying, cooling, 
desiccating and weighing until a constant weight is recorded, or weight change is less than 4% or less 
than 0.5mg. 
 
Sample analysis – ViCAs collection cups 
Insert filter into filtration apparatus, apply vacuum and wet the filter with a small volume of distilled water. 
Carefully pour the collection cup contents into the filter funnel and thoroughly rinse the collection cup 
and funnel with distilled water. Transfer the filter to an aluminum weighing dish and dry in an oven at 
105ºC for 1hr. Cool in desiccator and weigh; repeat drying, cooling, desiccating and weighing until a 
constant weight is recorded, or weight change is less than 4% or less than 0.5mg. Duplicates could not 
be carried out due to the lack of sample volume.
