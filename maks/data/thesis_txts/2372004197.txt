Abstract  
This Master Thesis provides a methodology to model future wholesale electricity prices which can 
characterise uncertainty with energy systems. The Balmorel model has been used for this purpose. 
The applied approach has consisted of three main steps.  
First, a critical review of several methodologies to inspect the diverse ways of forecasting prices in 
Denmark, focus of this work, has been performed.  Secondly, the main drivers of electricity prices have 
been identified by using a novelty method based on Morris Screening, i.e. Global Sensitivity Analysis 
methodology, which has analysed many parameters and provided as result a measure of the relative 
impact of each parameter on prices and the interrelations between the inputs. And third, to assess 
the robustness of results with respect to the uncertainty on input parameters, an Uncertainty Analysis 
has been performed following a Monte Carlo approach.  
The findings from the Global Sensitivity Analysis showed that the main drivers of electricity prices are 
highly dependent on the composition of the energy system and the relative values of the input 
parameters. For the year 2020, electricity demands were found to be the most influencing factors, 
and for the year 2030, CO2 prices. 
The results from the Uncertainty Analysis showed that, despite having a higher share of renewable 
energies in the system, electricity prices in Denmark are likely to increase slightly in 2020, and 
drastically in 2030, with respect to 2016’s level, which was 28 €2016/MWh. The forecasted average 
prices are 30 €2016/MWh for 2020 and 64 €2016/MWh for 2030. The main reasons behind these 
results are the assumed increase in fossil fuel and CO2 prices. The great difference in forecasted price 
between 2020 and 2030 was due to the huge expected rise in CO2 price by 2030. However, the 
uncertainty range of the average electricity price in 2030 was higher than in 2020 because of the 
greater uncertainty of the main drivers of prices in that year. 
Additionally, the expected CO2 emissions from the energy sector and its correlation with electricity 
prices were calculated to relate this work to the “Paris Agreement” of achieving a sustainable society 
by 2050. The results showed that the relation between electricity prices and CO2 emissions was 
dependent on the main drivers of electricity prices of each year. On the other hand, the CO2 emissions 
are expected to fall around 63% by 2030 with respect to 2014 levels due to increasing CO2 prices and 
higher renewable energy penetration. However, its uncertainty range was also high.

1. Introduction 
 
1.1. Objectives 
 
This work aims at providing a methodology that can help answer the following research questions: 
1. How can one do long-term electricity price predictions? 
2. What are the main drivers affecting electricity prices? 
3. What is the effect of uncertainty on electricity price forecast? 
Based on these research questions, the objectives of this Master Thesis are as follow: 
- 
To perform a critical review of the main methods used to forecast electricity prices in 
Denmark. 
- 
To create a framework for characterising uncertainty with energy models. 
- 
To identify which parameters are the key drivers of electricity prices.  
- 
To assess the robustness of results with respect to the uncertainty on input parameters.  
It is anticipated that the results of this work are intended to be particularly valuable for plant investors 
due to their private economic perspective.  
1.2. Overview of the energy trend  
 
The world is experiencing the transition to a sustainable energy system based on renewable energies. 
After the “Kyoto protocol” in 1997, many countries decided to start taking real action against climate 
change. The “Paris Agreement” of the COP21 of 2015, established the frame to fight climate change 
from 2020 onwards, reflecting this worldwide compromise. These agreements are consequently 
affecting the energy sector. As an example, Deutsche Bank has announced this year that they will stop 
financing coal projects [1].

1.3. Parameters influencing wholesale electricity prices  
 
By modifying the composition of the energy system, the transition towards renewable energies is 
affecting Electricity Prices (EP). To understand the reasons behind this fact, the way prices are 
calculated in the current system will be explained, and for that, the focus will be on the electricity 
market in which Denmark participates, i.e. NordPool.  
NordPool is the organization which covers the Day-Ahead and Intraday power markets of the Nordic 
countries, Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania, Germany and the UK [4].  
The Day-Ahead market will be the focus of this Thesis, as it is the one where the hourly wholesale 
price is cleared. The Intraday market and balancing markets are used to help balance the system close 
to real time and that are deeply affected by intermittent renewable energies. However, they do not 
change the wholesale price established in the Day-Ahead market, which is why they will not be 
analysed deeply in this report. The balancing markets are normally operated by the TSO of each 
bidding area (Energinet in Denmark). 
In the Day-Ahead market, each producer offers an amount of energy at a given price for a given hour 
of the next day, and a buyer assesses how much energy it will need at that hour and how much it is 
willing to pay for it. Then, for each hour, the system finds the equilibrium point where supply and 
demand meet, clearing the hourly prices. The producers’ offers are normally based on their short-
term marginal costs, although economic strategies can also play an important role on the actual offers. 
 
Figure 2. Illustration of the formation of the wholesale electricity price formation 
The wholesale market is based on the merit order. This means that the producer with highest offer 
entering the market will be the one fixing the price at a given hour. Figure 2 illustrates this fact. 
However, it is important to note that in the Danish system some combined heat and power producers 
are prioritized in the power market due to heat contracts that forces them to be producing at certain 
hours. This means that they bid with a zero price in the power market even though their actual costs 
are much higher, which disturbs the price. Nevertheless, if they do not have a big share of the market 
this does not change the pricing significantly.  
The energy trend towards renewable energies is modifying the demand and supply curves. Particularly 
on the supply side, it is pulling renewable energies into the game and pushing polluting ones out by 
means of political decisions. Clean energies have been supported with special schemes, e.g. Feed in 
Tariff, Premium Tariff or Green Certificates, to incentivize investments on them. Polluting plants have 
been penalised with CO2 prices, which has increased their short-term marginal costs, making them

For all these issues and more, electricity price forecasting is a tough issue. It is also important to 
understand who has the most interest in knowing how the wholesale price is going to evolve. Apart 
from the consumer, who pays the final bill, this information is very important for plant investors. They 
decide on the size of a new power plant or assess how much a plant is worth. The value of a power 
plant is based on its capability to make money and this is directly affected by the hourly electricity 
prices. If wholesale prices keep falling, some investors will reconsider putting their money in new 
power plants.   
 
With the current system, this could lead to a situation where there may not be enough capacity in the 
system to provide security of supply, likely implying extreme prices in some hours, as there would be 
less suppliers in a scenario with increasing demand. For this reason, a discussion regarding the 
introduction of new markets to deal with this problem, like capacity markets, is taking place. 
Moreover, taxes and subsidies may become even more important in the future, especially in Denmark 
with its high energy taxing system.   
 
Because of these facts, having a robust methodology to forecast electricity prices, both yearly average 
and the probability of each price during the year, can make an enormous difference in the decision of 
a plant investor, and that, is the purpose of this Master Thesis. 
 
This study is structured as follows. Section 2 is divided in two parts. The first makes an overview of 
recent models applied to predict electricity prices in Denmark and the second contains the 
methodology used in this work to model them. In section 3 the results obtained are shown and in 
section 4 a discussion regarding the methodology applied. Finally, the main conclusions obtained in 
this work can be found in section 5.

2. Modelling of long-term electricity prices 
 
2.1. Overview of models applied for Long Term Electricity Price Forecast in Denmark 
 
In the literature, several modelling approaches have been used to estimate electricity prices. [8] 
classifies the different methods in 5 types:  
- 
Multi-agent models: they calculate the price by simulating the operation of the different 
agents of the system, i.e. producers and consumer.   
- 
Fundamental methods: they calculate the electricity prices by modelling the physical and 
economic factors. 
- 
Reduced-form models: they do not aim at providing hourly price forecast, but to replicate the 
main characteristics of daily prices, such as price dynamics or correlations between 
commodity prices. They are useful for risk management and derivatives valuation. 
- 
Statistical models: they make use of historical data to forecast future prices by applying 
different types of statistical methodologies, such as regression models. 
- 
Computational Intelligence models: they are capable of learning from previous mistakes in 
complex dynamic systems and improve their forecasts. Neuronal networks are an example of 
this type of models.  
The methods can also be combined to become hybrid. Some of them are normally useful for the short 
term (days) and mid-term (weeks) whereas others are used in the long term (years).  
This Thesis will focus on Fundamental Methods, which are practical to perform long-term analyses, 
the most valuable for plant investors. The idea of these methods is to give input parameters to a 
mathematical model and then perform an optimization based on a specific objective function, having 
previously defined the structure of the energy system of every geographical unit considered 
(geographical distribution, interconnections, etc.). With this, one obtains information such as energy 
generation, emissions, prices, etc. Figure 11 shows a generic representation of this type of models.  
 
Figure 11. Illustration of the structure of the Fundamental Method 
The main drawbacks of these methods are that results are very sensitive to changes in the assumptions 
and the data availability to model accurately the structure of the system [8].   
In Denmark, several organizations have performed long-term electricity price forecast by using tools 
that are based on this method. From the reports that have been published, 3 have been selected and 
analyzed in this Thesis. The criteria applied for the selection was based on the status of the

Even with a perfect model of the energy system, any forecast will be wrong because of mistaken 
assumptions. Performing a reliable electricity forecast is quite difficult because there are simply too 
many variables affecting these prices and one should be careful when making assumptions. 
Nevertheless, are all variables equally important, or are there any that should be paid more attention 
to? Moreover, what is the effect of uncertainty on the forecasts? To perform a reliable forecast, it 
seems crucial to understand the effect that each factor can have on electricity prices, and under which 
circumstances.  
This Thesis will try to provide answers to these questions to help making modelling of future electricity 
prices more reliable and therefore, obtain a more accurate forecast. The proposed methodology to 
achieve this purpose will be explained in the following section. 
2.2. Modelling of long-term electricity prices with Balmorel 
 
The methodology proposed in this Thesis to model long-term electricity prices, which was based on 
[15], is shown in Figure 13. 
 
Figure 13. Methodology proposed for modelling of long term wholesale electricity prices in Balmorel. Inspired from [16] 
The first step was to create a model of the energy system to be able to calculate electricity prices.  For 
this purpose, two things were needed: a formulation model and data for the inputs parameters.  
Balmorel was chosen as starting point for the formulation model due to the availability of the tool and 
for its key features, i.e. long-term scope, fair representation of the energy system, investment decision 
capability and complementing add-ons. Additionally, some improvements were added to the model.  
==PAGE:30==
16 
 
The dataset and time slices chosen were built based on the Flex4RES database [17]. Moreover, the 
energy system was validated against historical data to test its adequacy.  
The following step was to model the uncertainty of the energy system. In an energy model, there are 
two types of uncertainties, i.e. parametric and structural. The first, focus of this Thesis, is related to 
the uncertainty of the input parameters and the latter is originated by the limitations of the 
mathematical formulation of the model to represent reality. This last uncertainty type was slightly 
studied in the model additions section.  
The next step, and by using preselected input parameters, was to perform a Global Sensitivity Analysis 
(GSA) to assess the impact of each of them on the electricity prices and the possible interrelations 
between the different inputs. A Morris screening methodology was applied for this purpose. The main 
result of this analysis were the key drivers of electricity prices. 
The last step was to perform an Uncertainty Analysis (UA) of the future electricity prices by means of 
Monte Carlo simulation with Latin Hypercube sampling to deal with the fact that there are many input 
parameters which cannot be predicted nor controlled and that can have a considerable influence on 
the results. For this purpose, only the main drivers of electricity prices obtained with the GSA were 
used to reduce the computational effort required. The results of this part were the expected prices, 
their uncertainty range and associated probability. Additionally, an analysis on the emitted CO2 
emissions, and its relationship with electricity prices, was also be performed. 
2.2.1. Energy System Model  
 
2.2.1.1. Balmorel 
 
The Balmorel project is a deterministic linear optimization model based on partial equilibrium, which 
covers the electricity and district heat sectors. It was originally developed for the Baltic Sea Region but 
it has been substantially internationally extended. The model has a bottom-up approach and assumes 
perfect competition. Balmorel is implemented in the GAMS modelling language [13]. 
 
The objective function in Balmorel is to minimize the system’s cost, seen from a socio-economic 
perspective, to fulfill the energy demand of a selected time horizon in the chosen countries. To do so, 
the model optimizes long-term investments and short-term energy dispatch [18]. The general 
structure of Balmorel is as the one showed in Figure 11. However, a more detailed structure is 
represented in Figure 14. 
 
To run a Balmorel model, one must specify a dataset of exogenous parameters regarding energy 
demand, fuel prices, wind and solar production profiles, CO2 prices, installed and planned 
transmission capacity and generation plants, technology costs, technical restrictions, etc. Then, by 
using the GAMS interface where the equations are defined, one can perform the optimization and 
obtain results.  
 
On the other hand, the available time slices in Balmorel are years, seasons (e.g. weeks, months, 
seasons, etc.) and hours. It is the user who decides which time slices are going to be used. It is also 
possible to assign different weight to the time slices so that the model can prioritize certain periods.

In Balmorel, one can obtain negative prices if the system finds more profitable to produce one more 
unit in a certain hour. This fact is something that also occurs in reality due to slow ramping rates, start-
up costs and the obligation to provide system services such as the provision of reserve capacity [20]. 
Special attention should be paid to those Balmorel models that allow for investments when calculating 
electricity prices. If there were not enough capacity in the system in a certain hour, the shadow price 
obtained in Balmorel would represent the long-term marginal cost of the producer, instead of the 
short-term marginal one, which is the one that theoretically companies use to bid on the market. For 
this reason, enough back-up capacity in the system was ensured in the model used in this Thesis by 
assigning 100 GW of a fuel oil technology with a relatively high operational cost in each region. 
So far, Balmorel has 4 model variations in the core model: BalBase1 (BB1), BB2, BB3 and BB4. BB1 is 
normally used to solve in detail the economic dispatch with a yearly foresight and does not allow for 
investments. BB2 is commonly used to perform investment optimization in a more aggregated time 
step. BB3 is like BB1 but instead of yearly foresight, it assumes a weekly one, which is relevant when 
optimizing for example hydro reservoirs.

BB4 is a relatively recent version, still at developing stage, which is also used to perform investments. 
BB4 differs from BB2 in the investment approach. In general, whereas BB2 calculates endogenous 
investments by individually optimizing each year with a myopic approach, i.e. with no knowledge 
about the future, BB4 weights future years relative to the first year by means of the discount rate in 
the objective function and performs the investment optimization considering perfect foresight for 
each time segment [21]. The model does so by applying a rolling horizon optimization, whose 
functioning will be illustrated with three simple examples shown in Table 4. 
First, the user decides the years that will be included in the model, which do not have to be 
consecutive. Then, one decides the number of elements (years) that will be part of each BB4 
submodel. Finally, the user defines the separation between these elements: the first number indicates 
the starting year (0 means first year), and the rest will be the distance with respect to the following 
element. Different specifications lead to different number of submodels. 
Once all the BB4 submodels are defined, Balmorel optimizes each of them individually starting from 
the first one. In every submodel, Balmorel has perfect foresight of the years included in that submodel, 
so it optimizes investments considering how the system is going to evolve. After solving the first 
submodel, the model saves the investments of the first year of the submodel (red figure in the table), 
and perform the next optimization including the endogenous optimization results from the previous 
optimization as exogenous input parameters. That is why is called rolling horizon. For more technical 
and further details, see [19]. 
In the case that the future is very different to the present, BB4 is better than BB2, as it could avoid 
suboptimal investments during the transition towards challenging environmental limitations.  
In this Thesis, BB4 was used to optimize the system and calculate electricity prices. As it was in 
developing stage, some issues were detected in the code and managed to solve in time.  The appendix 
contains the list of the issues found.

2.2.1.3. Model additions 
 
• 
Ramping limits 
Without using the Unit Commitment add-on, the model lacked of ramping formulation that limited 
the variation in production from hour to hour. Therefore, the power plants could change their 
production as pleased. This did not represent the fact that the ramping of a power plant is limited nor 
that some thermal power plants do not vary much their production between hour to hour as they 
strategically bid on the market in blocks to produce during several hours because of their low ramping 
limits and high start-up costs. To study the effect of applying ramping limits into the system’s results 
it was decided to include ramping constraints. 
Addressing this problem was not an easy task. Real ramping constraints are minute-wise, whereas the 
minimum time step in Balmorel is the hour, meaning that the variation between fuel consumption 
would have to be hourly-wise. Transforming minute-wise ramping limits to hourly-wise ramping limit 
was not considered as a good option as theoretically, in one hour the generation of most thermal 
power plants can vary much and this cannot be model in Balmorel as the production is a constant 
value in a certain hour. For this reason, another approach was followed.  
The selected approach consisted of dividing technologies in high-flexible, low-flexible and non-flexible 
technologies, and then assigning different hourly ramping rates according to the type of technology. 
The purpose of this division was to somehow divide technologies in two types: the ones used for the 
base load and the ones used for matching demand. If the ramping rates were too high, then this 
limitation would make no effect on results, as the hourly variation of electricity demand minus the 
variation of the production of non-dispatchable technologies, such as wind or solar pv, is limited.  
Therefore, the ramping rates should be low enough to make an impact on results, but at the same 
time, relatively different between each other to account for the three technology types considered. 
As the used dataset contained around 1200 different technologies, the technologies were classified in 
the three flexibility categories according to the fuel use. All burning gas thermal plant were considered 
as high-flexible ones. Nuclear was considered as non-flexible and the remaining fuels used in thermal 
power plants, e.g. coal, straw, wood pellets, etc., were considered as low-flexible.

Loop code 
To solve a vast numerous of scenarios in Balmorel in a more efficient way, a GAMS code containing a 
loop which changed automatically the input parameters according to each scenario was used in this 
Thesis. The code was previously used in [16] for BB2 and it has been adapted to run in BB4 to be able 
to use it in this Thesis. 
The code consisted of saving the original data, modifying the corresponding parameters for each 
scenario, updating the bounds of some equations, solving the model, saving results and then start 
over with the following scenario. Because of using a loop, the model takes advantage of the previous 
solution and as long as the parameters have not changed much, finds the solution much faster.   
2.2.1.4. Data and time slice selection 
 
Selecting carefully the data and time slices was a crucial issue. The objective was to use the minimum 
number of countries and time slices but at the same, obtain coherent and representative results. 
Computation limitation was the reason behind this fact. The model could not be too big, otherwise 
too much time would be required to solve the model and no analysis could be performed due to lack 
of time.  
The years selected for the study were 2020 and 2030, with equal weight on the objective function. 
Among the countries available in the database, it was decided to include those countries which were 
considered to have a bigger impact on electricity prices in Denmark in the future. The selection was 
based on country size, data availability and interconnection development. The chosen countries were 
Denmark, Norway, Germany and Sweden. 
It was decided to use full weeks, as it was intended to represent the hourly profile variations as close 
as reality as possible. The chosen ones were not arbitrary though, they were selected with an 
algorithm developed for this Thesis that combined Excel and VBA. 
The method consisted on the following steps: selection of time series profiles, calculation of the yearly 
accumulated probability curves for each of them and optimization of the selection of a user-specified 
number of weeks. Equal weight was given to the weeks in the optimization. The optimization was 
based on minimizing the error between the accumulated probability of the original time series profiles 
and the resulting ones obtained by using only the specified number of weeks. The errors were 
calculated with the Least Squares method to penalize big differences.  
Because of including several countries in the model, there were many time series profiles that could 
be included in the optimization. However, to optimize the selection of the weeks only the wind 
generation and electrical demand profiles of Denmark, focus of this study, were used. The reason 
behind using these profiles came from the high variability of these profiles with respect to others and 
due to its direct effect on the electricity balance equation.

historical data could be validated, but the past years’ data were the base for the future development 
assumptions, which will be used in the rest of the Thesis.

Full Load Hours of renewable energies 
The annual FLH of PV, Wind and Hydro technologies is a function of two variables: technology 
performance and weather patterns.  
Technology performance is a coefficient which represents the ratio between the output generation 
and the total available energy of the input. These coefficients are limited by physics: Betz limit for 
wind, cell solar cell efficiency for PV and turbine efficiency for hydro. The theoretical maximum values 
are never achieved due to losses in the system. However, losses are being reduced with time due to 
improvements in the technologies.

2.2.3. Global Sensitivity Analysis methodology 
 
Morris screening is a methodology used to perform sensitivity analysis which is capable to determine 
the relation of each input parameter (x) with respect to the output (y). These relations can be (a) 
negligible, (b) a non-zero constant over all values of x, (c) nonlinear, or (d) involved in interaction with 
other inputs [32]. The methodology is considered a Global Sensitivity Analysis because it performs 
local sensitivity analysis in a global context, which makes it less computationally demanding [16]. 
The total number of scenarios created is a function of the number of repetitions (r) and the number 
of input parameters (N). In each r, the method creates a sample of N+1 scenarios, assigning randomly 
a value to each parameter among the determined possible ones. The value that each parameter can 
have lays within its corresponding uncertainty range and is a function of the p-level, which is the 
number of possible values that each input may have for each scenario. This number should be even. 
For instance, given a parameter whose uncertainty range is {0,10}, if p is chosen as 2, its final scenario 
value will be either 0 or 10, whereas a p value of 4 will allow the parameter to have any of the following 
values: 0, 3.33, 6.66 or 10. Morris screening follows a one at a time (OAT) approach, meaning that 
each scenario of inputs differs from the next and previous one in one value [32]. 
The higher the number of repetitions the higher the quality of results. However, the adequate number 
of repetitions is a trade-off between computational time and quality of results.  Typical values are 
found between 10 and 50 [33]. 
The resulting samples are assumed to follow a uniform distribution with values within {0,1}. The 
samples are then converted from the unit hypercube to their corresponding scaled uncertainty 
distributions, which can be uniform, normal or any other [16]. 
Once the input parameters scenarios of each repetition are created, the model is solved as many times 
as total number of scenarios there are (r*(N+1)) and results are saved. 
To calculate in each repetition the effect of every input parameter, the method calculates the 
Elementary Effects (EE) of each input, based on Equation 7, being 𝛥 a perturbation factor calculated

2.2.4. Uncertainty Analysis methodology 
 
In order to deal with the uncertainty of the results obtained from the model originated by the 
uncertainty in the input parameters, the Monte Carlo simulation procedure presented by [34] and also 
used in [16] is applied, which is accepted as computationally-effective and reliable [35]. 
The main drawback of this method is the numerous number of scenarios that are needed to obtain an 
adequate accuracy on the results. For this reason, only the parameters with non-negligible impact 
obtained in the GSA will be studied.  
The Monte Carlo method is based on random sampling within the uncertainty range of the input 
parameters. However, to keep randomness, but at the same time to ensure some stratification of the 
data and a proper coverage of the uncertainty space, and therefore, avoid having some points 
clustered closely, some specific sampling methodologies are used; e.g. Latin Hypercube Sampling 
(LHS), Sobol, etc.  
Because of the presumably considerable number of parameters that will be obtained as non-negligible 
from the GSA, the LHS methodology is preferred over the rest, and for this reason, it will be applied 
on this Thesis. By using the LHS sampling methodology, random stratified values between {0,1} are 
assigned in the scenarios. As done with the Morris screening, these values are then converted from 
the unit hypercube to their corresponding scaled uncertainty distributions. After this, the scenarios 
are solved in the model and output saved.  
Finally, a statistical evaluation of the model results is applied to characterise their uncertainty with 
respect to the uncertainty in the input parameters [16].

3.3. Key drivers of electricity prices  
 
By applying Morris Screening methodology in the pre-selected 52 parameters the main drivers of 
electricity prices were calculated. The number of repetitions, r, was set to 15 and the number of levels, 
p, to 6. This first was chosen because of computational limitation and the second to have more 
possible values within the uncertainty ranges. These selections resulted in 795 scenarios.  
The frequency of the values that each parameter took in the scenarios was dependent on the type of 
distribution. For the parameters that followed a normal distribution, i.e. FLH, the values closer to the 
mean should have appeared more times than the ones far from it, and for the inputs following a 
uniform distribution, all values should have had the same frequency. However, as the number of 
repetitions used was not sufficiently high, the shapes of the frequency curves were just an 
approximation of these types of distributions.  
As an example, Figure 22 shows the histogram for 2 of the 52 parameters, i.e. Full Load Hours of Wind 
Onshore in Denmark and CO2 prices in 2020. For the first parameter, the sum of the frequencies of 
the values closer to the mean, i.e. 2043, was higher than the sum of the frequencies of the values 
further than it, whereas for the second one, the frequencies of the different values tended to be the 
same.  
 
Figure 22. Morris Sampling histogram for 2 of the 52 parameters 
After solving the scenarios, the Elementary Effects (EE) of the inputs parameters were computed using 
Equation 7. The frequencies of the EEs obtained for one of the input parameters in 2020 are depicted 
in Figure 23 as an example. These frequencies varied much depending on the input, showing that not 
all the parameters affected electricity prices in the same way. The shape of this frequency curve does 
not seem to follow any type of distribution. Probably, using a higher number of repetitions would have 
ended up showing some type of distribution, but because of computational limitation this number 
was not set higher.

then a positive variation of the input likely leads to a positive variation in prices and so on, and if the

to have a negligible impact on results, however, having performed only 15 repetitions limited the use 
of this statement. 
First, the year 2020 will be analysed. Results showed that the main drivers of electricity prices in this 
year were the electricity demands of Denmark, Sweden and Norway (parameters 41, 45 and 43 
respectively), although they presented the highest standard deviations, meaning that its relative 
impact was quite sensitive to the rest of the parameters. The electricity demand in Germany 
(parameter 47) seemed to have also an effect on prices, although lower than the rest of the countries. 
District heating demand (parameter 49) also reflected a considerable effect on prices in this year, and 
with a negative mean. The cause of this fact was investigated by comparing results of two scenarios 
with different district heating demands. Results showed that increasing heat demand led to increasing 
electricity production of CHP plants in Denmark to provide for this extra heating demand. However, 
this increase in demand was not proportionally distributed among fuels. Both the share and level of 
coal use for electricity production increased, whereas the natural gas’ ones decreased. This explains 
obtaining lower electricity prices as the marginal cost of natural gas technologies was higher than coal 
ones. On the other hand, increasing district heating demand led to an increment in heat prices.

4. Discussion 
 
4.1. Limitations of the Energy System Model  
 
As for any deterministic model, the simplification of the system leads to uncertainty in the results. 
Assessing the robustness of the methodology is a long task that should be performed. For this purpose, 
it seems important to analyse the consequences and limitations of the elements used in the 
methodology.  
As previously stated, the way Balmorel estimates hourly electricity prices is just an approximation of 
reality based on the short-term marginal costs of the producers. This way of determining prices has 
been proved in this Thesis to be an adequate way to estimate the yearly average electricity prices. 
However, by not considering the strategic factor of the producer nor flexible demand, the hourly 
profiles could not be totally matched. 
 
In principle, the methodology applied is valid to any country. However, one should pay special 
attention to the hydro parameters in those countries where this factor has a considerable role in the 
system, as it is the case of Norway. As the model did not consider the strategy factor into account, the 
electricity production in Norway was based on hydro and municipal waste, without any use of natural 
gas since there was already a source for flexibility, i.e. hydro. Historically, the energy mix of Norway is 
around 95% hydro and then a mix of wind, natural gas and waste [37]. Therefore, the prices obtained 
in this country were lower than historical data.  
Regarding the data used and uncertainty ranges considered, even though the Flex4RES database 
seemed reasonably fair, it lacked of precise references that allowed to double check with other 
sources and establish improved ranges. Moreover, the scenario considered modifications in 
transmission lines, power plants capacity, costs, etc., and much of these assumptions may occur or 
not. However, guessing what is likely to happen 13 years in advance seems reasonably adequate based 
on the current energy trend of Europe, although one should never forget that economic crisis can 
occur and can change completely the panorama.  
 
The methodology applied to optimize the weeks selected seemed to be suitable for the purpose of 
this Thesis. However, this optimization was based in Denmark´s time series, but been Europe highly 
connected, it may have been better to include time series from other countries, and probably also 
more than 2 weeks. Moreover, limiting the annual investments of some technologies to 1 GW in each 
year analysed may have been insufficient for some technologies such as PV, as the model invested 
always in the maximum capacity regardless of the scenario. Related to this, it is important to remark 
that Balmorel does not consider the balancing cost that increasing intermittent renewable energies 
would generate in the investment optimization, and this can have a major impact in the future. 
 
Relatively small number of scenarios were calculated in the GSA and Uncertainty Analysis sections due 
to reduced computational limitation and time, limiting the accuracy of the conclusions. However, they 
seemed to be enough to obtain coherent results. 
Moreover, because of lack of previous experience with the dataset and limited one with the Balmorel 
model, together with the fact that they were both in developing stage, it was incurred much effort on 
detecting and solving issues instead of going in depth with other aspects of the Thesis. Nevertheless, 
the work performed in this Thesis will most likely be very useful for further use of the model and 
dataset.

4.2. Comparison of results  
 
In [20] it was investigated which factors had influenced most on the decline of electricity spot price in 
Germany during 2011 and 2015. In the report, they analysed the impact of CO2, coal and gas prices, 
demand, and solar and wind penetration, which were considered as the main drivers of electricity 
prices.  These drivers are in line with the GSA results obtained in this Thesis. 
On the other hand, the electricity price forecast obtained in this work is in line with the studied reports 
[9] [10] [11], most likely because similar assumptions were made. Apart from this fact, in [38] it was 
also found that increasing wind penetration leads to higher volatility on electricity prices. 
4.3. Further work  
 
There is current debate about future electricity markets that could invalidate the results obtained in 
this Thesis, such as capacity markets or flexibility markets. About the latter, the market seems to be 
seeking for demand flexibility, and this aspect has not been studied in this work. For this reason, it 
seems interesting to consider including this aspects in the model.  
 
On the other hand, the 2012 Flex4RES dataset may be outdated, as the energy system has changed 
substantially in the recent past years. For example, in Denmark several thermal power plants have 
closed since 2012. Therefore, updating the database seems like a good practice to improve the quality 
of results. At the same time, calibrating the total production and the shares of each fuel in each 
country should be performed. 
 
Further studies could also be performed to assess the impact of modifying the number of weeks and 
countries implemented. The methodology for selecting the time slices can be completely changed. For 
example, instead of selecting the weeks that better approximate the entire year, one could create a 
combination of one or several fictitious weeks that represented the full year. 
 
Moreover, because of the uncertainty in the future development of biogas, this fuel was barely 
touched in the Thesis. As it is a potential source of flexibility for the electricity system, likely 
substituting natural gas in the long-term, deeper research on this factor should be done. 
 
Other ideas that could be analysed from the GSA and that have not been covered in this Thesis is that 
the proposed methodology can be used to point out on which parameters one should focus if the 
wholesale electricity prices is to be reduced. However, in Denmark the wholesale price accounts for a 
low share of the total bill, for instance, in 2014 it was responsible for just 15% of the total cost for an 
average household consumer [39]. Therefore, the total impact of reducing the wholesale price is 
limited.  
Based on the impacts obtained in the GSA analysis, research about policies which aim at reducing 
electricity demand should be promoted to avoid drastic increase of electricity prices. FLH of operation 
of wind turbine also showed a considerable impact on electricity prices; therefore, research that can 
further improve them or new wind rotor sizes might highly impact the energy system. Water 
availability reflected a strong influence too, therefore further research related to the impact that 
climate change might have in the Nordic hydrological cycles might be very important to establish 
optimal energy strategies. Transmission capacity was also found very important, there is the need to 
ensure adequate policies that avoid distribution bottlenecks to optimize the future European market. 
On the other hand, even though the main purpose of CO2 prices is to reduce CO2 emissions, as shown 
in this work, its impact on electricity prices can be considerably high. Therefore, deeper study on the 
appropriate levels of these prices should be performed to reach a sustainable energy system and at 
the minimum cost for society.

About the Uncertainty Analysis section, instead of using the applied methodology for creating the 
scenarios, a Correlated Sampling variance reduction technique in the Monte Carlo method could have 
been a more efficient and better practice as some parameters show in reality similar trends, as it is 
typically the case for oil and natural gas prices.  
Finally, there is an interesting fact that has not been studied in this Thesis. As previously stated, 
decreasing electricity prices can lead to lack of system capacity. This was not allowed in the model to 
avoid long-term electricity prices when permitting investments in the model. But in reality, there is no 
infinite back-up capacity and it would be quite interesting to study what would happen to the prices 
in an scenario where traditional power plants closed down and intermittent renewable energies have 
taken their place, if suddenly there is no wind or sun production and there is not enough flexibility in 
the system. This could be quite relevant for the year 2050.

5. Conclusion 
 
In this Master Thesis, a methodology to model future wholesale electricity prices has been performed. 
The proposed methodology can characterise uncertainty in the energy system and calculate its impact 
on results. 
A critical review of different methodologies to model electricity prices has been performed and among 
the analysed tools, Balmorel was chosen to model the energy system of this Thesis because of its 
characteristics and the fact that it is open-source. The model was improved by adding ramping 
constraints and by solving several errors which were found because it was in developing stage. 
Together with the dataset, which was based on the Flex4RES project data, the energy system was 
completed and ready to simulate electricity prices. 
The main drivers of electricity prices in the studied years were identified by using a Global Sensitivity 
Analysis, which was based on the Morris Screening methodology and whose output gave information 
about the relative impact of each parameters on electricity prices and the interaction with other 
inputs. For this purpose, a set of 52 parameters were selected and the uncertainty range of their future 
development was characterised. The findings from the Global Sensitivity Analysis showed that the 
main drivers of electricity prices were highly dependent on the composition of the energy system and 
the relative values of the input parameters. For the year 2020, electricity demand was found to be the 
most influencing factor, whereas for the year 2030, CO2 prices were found to be by far the main driver 
of electricity prices. 
The uncertainty of the input parameters on electricity price forecast has been modelled with the 
combination of the main drivers of electricity prices obtained in the GSA and the Uncertainty Analysis 
to assess the robustness of the results. The Uncertainty Analysis has been based on the Monte Carlo 
method to build up the scenarios. The results from the Uncertainty Analysis showed that, despite 
having a higher share of renewable energies in the system, electricity prices are likely to increase 
slightly in 2020, and drastically in 2030, with respect to 2016’s level. The main reasons behind these 
results were the assumed increase in fossil fuel and CO2 prices. The great difference in forecasted 
price between 2020 and 2030 was due to the huge expected rise in CO2 price by 2030. The fact that 
the electricity demand was assumed lower in 2020 than in 2016 softened the price increase in 2020, 
although its expected increase towards 2030 with respect to 2020 contributed to raise prices in 2030. 
However, the uncertainty range of the average electricity price in 2030 was higher than in 2020 
because of the greater uncertainty of the main drivers of prices in that year. 
Additionally, the expected CO2 emissions from the energy sector and its correlation with electricity 
prices were calculated to relate this work to the Paris objectives of achieving a sustainable society by 
2050. The results showed that the relationship between electricity prices and CO2 emissions was 
dependent on the main drivers of electricity prices of each year. On the other hand, the CO2 emissions 
are expected to fall around 63% by 2030 with respect to 2014 levels due to increasing CO2 prices and 
higher renewable energy penetration. However, its uncertainty range is also high.
