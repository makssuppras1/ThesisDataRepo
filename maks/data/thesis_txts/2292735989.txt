Summary (English)
The goal of the thesis is to investigate the possibility of applying multiple curves
pricing framework in the interest rate modeling area with the presence of nega-
tive interest rate observed in the current European market.
We choose model OIS rate and OIS Libor spread separately and derive a close
formula for pricing Libor-linked Caplet with the presence of negative rate. The
close formula provides an eﬃcient way to calibrate the multiple curve model to
observed market data.
Then we start to revisit the performance of diﬀerent popular interest rate models
to see whether these models can ﬁt the volatility smile in this multiple curve
frameworks.
It is found that SABR model in a multiple curve framework can ﬁt the volatility
smile quite well, but the numerical problem of the SABR model make the price
of implementation and calculation very expensive. Based on that, we argue that
the choice of the interest rate model depends on the application and motivation.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Interest rate derivative is one of the most important asset classes in ﬁnancial
modeling area that has been studied for a decades. After ﬁnancial crisis, the
presence of basis spread and negative interest rate start to let academic and
market participant revisit the basic assumption of these interest rate models. In
this thesis, we are looking into this issue and revisit the classical interest rate
model in a new multiple curve pricing setting.
In particular, the study focused on one question: "whether a multiple curve
pricing model can still ﬁt volatility smile in today’s market?". To answer this
question, ﬁrstly, we will revisit two important challenges in today’s interest rate
modeling area which is the presence of basis spread and the negative interest
rate. The ﬁrst challenge will explain why we need a multiple curve pricing model
and the second challenge bring additional issues which would be addressed in
this thesis.
The following part of this chapter is organized as follows: ﬁrstly we will discuss
the basis spread issue in a more detail level, then we start the analyze the
negative interest rate issue and its challenge to interest rate model, and then
the main problem of this thesis will be formally presented.

1.1
The presence of basis spread dynamics
Up to 2008, the classical approach to price Libor-related derivative assumed that
the counter-party credit risk implied in market quoted Libor can be neglected.
As a consequence of this assumption, the standard pricing approach use a unique
interest rate curve for each currency, the pricing methodology has been well
discussed in classical ﬁnancial book like Hull [2009] or Brigo and Mercurio [2007],
this approach that construct one interest curve for both discounting and forecast
forward rate is also reefed as the "single curve approach".
After recent ﬁnancial crisis in 2008, various basis spread in the interest rate
markets reach a signiﬁcant level and can not be neglected any more in the
pricing model. Further more, a high volatility of these basis spreads has been
observed in the market.
Figure 1.1 compared the Libor rate with overnight
index swap rate(OIS) in three month tenor in last ten years, the two rates start
to diverge with each other since 2007.
After the recent ﬁnancial crisis in 2008, various basis spread in the interest rate
markets reach a signiﬁcant level and can not be neglected anymore in the pricing
model. Furthermore, a high volatility of these basis spreads has been observed
in the market. Figure 1.1 compared the Libor rate with the overnight index

The presence of the OIS-Libor spread can be explained by the increasing worry
of bank’s credibility after the ﬁnancial crisis (Thornton [2009]). Following the
presence of this basis spread, the one curve pricing approach should be replaced
by a multiple curves approach in order to price and hedge ﬁnancial products

1.1.2
Model stochastic basis spread
Looking at the historical time series of OIS Libor spread suggested by Figure
1.1, the OIS spread was not constant over the time horizon, and it seems more
suitable to model it as the stochastic process. In this section, we will analyze
several possible model choice.
There is rich literature in recent years that try to develop the interest rate
model with stochastic basis spread. And an active research area is extending
Libor Market Model (LMM) to this multi-curve setting. As pointed by Mercurio
[2010], there are three diﬀerent model choices:
• Modeling the joint evolution of rates Lk(t) and Fk(t), k = 1, · · · , M.
• Modeling the joint evolution of rates Lk(t) and spreads Sk(t), k = 1, · · · , M.
• Modeling the joint evolution of rates Fk(t) and spreads Sk(t), k = 1, · · · , M.
Mercurio [2009] studied the ﬁrst choice, they choose model FRA rate Lk(t) and
OIS forward rate Fk(t) directly. The advantage of this approach is this setup
"bring some convenient in term of model tractability and calibration to the mar-
ket data", and the close form of pricing caplet and swaption is straightforward
to obtain. However, the main problem is the implied basis spread might be
negative, which should always be positive in theory.
To my knowledge, there are not much attention have been paid to the second
choice, model Libor rates Lk(t) and spread Sk(t). The main drawback of the
second approach summarized by Mercurio [2010] is the implied OIS rate Fk(t)
maybe negative, but that is what we can observed at today’s European market.
So more research should be performed in this approach.
However, another
problem for this approach is not consistent with the current market practice of
curve construction.

Many proposed models use the third approach mainly because this approach
is consistent with the current market practice of building a Libor curve at a
spread over the OIS curve.
Mercurio [2010] consider a Libor market model
framework and derive the close form of pricing caplet and swaption. Another
similar approach was used by Fujii et al. [2009] by modeling the basis spread
and OIS rate separately.
One challenge on third approach is how to calibrate the parameter of OIS spread
model and OIS model. Since there is no liquid vanilla option whose underlying
is OIS-Libor spread and the liquidly of OIS underlying option is problematic,
we should try to calibrate these models to the market quoted price of the Libor-
linked product. That means an exact or close formula of the vanilla Libor-linked
product should be derived in this multi-curve settings.
In the later part of the thesis (Chapter 2), we will present such a close formula
for Libor-linked Caplet under this multi-curve framework. Under this frame-
work, the formulation of OIS and spread model are not speciﬁed as long as the
probability density function of the underlying has support.
However, before we go to that, we will ﬁrstly address another issue in the cur-
rent market which has a signiﬁcant impact on the model landscape, the recent
presence of negative interest rate in the European market.

1.2
The presence of negative interest rate
Starting from 2011, banks and other ﬁnancial participants have observed that
the increasing emergence of negative interest rate in European ﬁnancial market.
The ﬁrst negative rate observed in the market is regard to the Swiss franc
as a consequence of the action taken by Swiss National Bank (SNB) to ﬁx
the EUR/CHF exchange rate at CHF 1.20. Then the eﬀect spread to several
markets including DKK, SEK, and even the EUR market.
From a theoretical point of view, there are several explanations for the negative
interest rate.
For example, the negative bond yields for some countries like
Switzerland, Denmark and Germany could be interpreted as the price investor
wish to pay for the protection against the Eurozone break up (Wigglesworth
[2012]). The motivation of central bank to set the negative rate on reserves is
to encourage bank issue more long-term loan to business instead of putting the
money in the reserve account (och Heidi Elmér [2009]). In all these situations,
the negative rate is a long-term economic phenomenon with sound economic
explanation rather than just short-term price ﬂuctuation. The argument can
also be veriﬁed by the empirical perspective, for example, the short tenor of
CHF MM rate has been negative for more than three years.

1.2.5
Literature review of negative interest rate
There is limited literature on dealing with the negative interest rate in the
interest rate model. However, it is interesting to note that before 2008, the
presence of negative interest rate in pricing model was treated as an undesirable
feature of interest pricing model. For example, one major criticism of the Vasicek
model (Vasicek [1977]) is that the negative interest rate is possible. This reason
usually is used as a main motivation for go to more advanced model in the
classical ﬁnancial textbooks like Brigo and Mercurio [2007] and Hull [2009].
However, this issue has attracted more attention in recent years, Fries et al.
[2013] reviewed a displaced diﬀusion model and found that this model can handle
the close to zero or negative interest rate quite well. A Technical note by Höchtl
[2013] argues that in the current market environment, the classical "lognormal"
model should be replaced by "normal" or "shifted lognormal" model. As we

Introduction to displaced diﬀusion model
Displaced Diﬀusion is one of the most popular approaches which has been con-

rate issue.
However, the history of displaced diﬀusion model can date back
to Rubinstein [1983]. A displaced-diﬀusion process assumes that the quantity
St + θ follow the geometric Brownian process:
d(S(t) + θ) = (S(t) + θ)σdz(t)
(3.1)
Since in this model, the quantity S(t) + θ rather than S(t) follow a log-normal
distribution, so some literature also refer this model as "Shifted log-normal
model" (Brigo and Mercurio [2002],Haahtela [2006] and Brigo and Mercurio
[2001]).
The attractiveness of this model is the only the quantity S(t) + θ is guaranteed
to be positive. So if θ is a positive number, then S(t) can assume values between

model was mainly used to model other underlying rather interest rate.
For
example, it is a popular choice for inﬂation model when assuming disinﬂation is

4.1
Introduction
Constant-Elasticity-of-Variance model(CEV) model , introduced by Cox [1975],
is another popular diﬀusion model used in ﬁnance. A general CEV model has
below formulation
d(F(t)) = σF(t)βdBt
(4.1)
Where β is the key parameter that control the relationship between volatility and
the level of the rate and is one of the central feature of this model. By setting
diﬀerent value of β, the general CEV model can be transformed to diﬀerent
classical ﬁnancial models:
• When β = 1, black model.
==PAGE:50==
40Constant-Elasticity-of-Variance model(CEV) in multiple curve framework
• When β = 0, normal distribution model.
• When β = 1
2, Cox–Ingersoll–Ross (CIR) model.
Various empirical research has shown that the negative relationship between the
level of stock price and volatility (Glosten et al. [1993] and Brandt and Kang
[2004]), so when using CEV model to price stock price, usually β will be set less
than one.
In contrast, for the commodity market, several research shown that the level
of price and volatility has positive correlation, so in that market, β is usually
chosen as a value larger than one (Emanuel and MacBeth [1982]).
Andersen and Andreasen [2000] ﬁrstly introduce CEV model into interest rate
area with the motivation of modeling the skew of implied caplet-volatility struc-
ture. They consider the equation (4.1) with the condition that 0 < β < 1.

absorbing boundary, in that case, the model reads:

6.1
Conclusion
The presence of basis spread between OIS rate and Libor rate raise a big problem
to the classical one curve pricing framework. Although many researchers have
paid attention to discount curve construction, all this methodology is assuming
OIS spread should be deterministic. However based on the historical volatility of
OIS spread time series in the developed market, OIS spread should be modeled
as a stochastic model.
In this thesis, we examine a multiple curve pricing framework that can model
OIS spread and OIS rate using two diﬀerent form of models. If assuming the
spread and OIS rate are independent of each other, we found that it is possible
to write the analytic pricing formula of the Libor-linked option. Furthermore,
we extend the formula to negative rate case.
Also, it is found that the assumption of independence makes moving the dis-
count factor outside of the expectation possible.
We have proofed that the
discount factor can be moving outside of the expectation both in OIS and Libor
discounting under this assumption.
Then we tried three popular interest models (displaced log-normal, CEV and
==PAGE:80==
70
Conclusion and Future work
SABR) in the interest rate model area to test the multiple curve frameworks. It
is found that the multiple curve pricing formula works well in all three models
we tested. Furthermore, SABR model can generate the desired volatility smile
that can be observed in the today’s market. The advantages and disadvantages
of the three models are summarized in Table 6.1.
From the analysis, we can see that the choice of the speciﬁc model is dependent
on the application. For example, if the multiple curve interest rate model is
used in a risk management environment and doesn’t need ﬁt the volatility smile,
the displaced diﬀusion model is a good choice because of the eﬃcient simulation
scheme and analytic feature. On the other side, when this multiple curve interest
rate model is used in front oﬃce pricing that ﬁtting volatility smile is desired,
then multiple curve SABR model should be chosen.
So if we back to the question we asked at the beginning of the thesis: " whether
we can ﬁnd a multiple curve interest rate models that can ﬁt the observed
volatility smile ?". It is found that a Hybrid SABR-Black model that model
OIS rate as SABR process and model spread as a Black model could ﬁt the
current observed volatility smile well.

Conclusion and Future work
6.2
Future work
There are lots of interesting future works to explore further in this area, ﬁrstly,
in this thesis, we only consider modeling OIS spread as a General Brownian
Motion (GBM) type process that lack any empirical evidence, and as we shown
in Chapter 5, the skew of the volatility smile is signiﬁcantly aﬀected by the
level of the OIS spread volatility, so an interesting direction would be test other
model formulation like CEV or SABR for OIS spread as well. For example,
Mercurio [2010] consider modeling OIS spread and OIS rate by SABR model.
Another topic would be trying a diﬀerent modeling approach, for example, the
short rate model for the spread and (or) OIS rate.
In this thesis, we only
consider the Market Model which model the forward rate dynamic directly,
however, based the natural of OIS rate , it could be argued that short rate
model might also be an ideal option. Moreover, trying a combination between
short rate model and market model would be interesting as well.
There are many other basis spread now existed in the market, for example,
the basis spread coming from the cross currency swap and tenor swap. In this
thesis we only consider the Libor-OIS basis spread in a single currency, a global
multiple curve framework might be necessary as suggested by Fujii et al. [2010].
In this thesis, we only consider the OIS rate and spread are in the same terminal
measure so that we do not have to worry about the transforming measure.
However when applying the multiple curve model into practical, a multiple
dimensional model that handle diﬀerent tenor simultaneously is desired, then
transforming the measure would be an important topic to have a careful look.

.3
Another way of handling negative rate in SABR
model
Although we focus on shrift approach in this thesis to handle negative rate
issue , it is natural to ask whether there are other extensions, in this section,
a recently published approach rather than shifted approach has been reviewed
and analysed.
Antonov et al. [2015] describe one possible extension of the SABR model and
gave an exact formula for the caplet price in the case of zero correlation between
the rate and its volatility.
The advantage of this approach is that we don’t have to choose a priori selection
of the shifted amount which we did not know in advance. This shifted amount is
very hard to estimate especially when pricing some long tenor product, and we
may end up in a situation that we have to change the shifted amount because
interest rate move to a more negative level than expected, as a consequence of
this change, the PV and risk of the portfolio have to be recomputed.
In this section we will review this extension by pricing a series of caplets using
the exact formula and simulation technique at the same time.
