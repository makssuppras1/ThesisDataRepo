Abstract
Ocean alkalinity enhancement (OAE) is a promising tool with respect to climate mitigation that aims
to enhance the capacity of the ocean to take up, and store carbon dioxide from the atmosphere, by
spreading large amounts of suitable minerals to the ocean. Yet, the consequences of OAE on plankton
communities remains rather unexplored, particularly for mesozooplankton (mesoZP) that connects pri-
mary producers to higher trophic levels. To study the influence of OAE on the mesoZP community and
the abundances of key taxonomic groups (copepod nauplii, small/medium copepods, large Calanus sp.,
appendicularians and hydromedusae), a large scale in-situ mesocosm experiment was conducted over 53
days in Raunefjord, Norway. Alkalinity was altered according to a five level gradient (∆TA = 0, 150,
300, 450 and 600 µmol L−1), with two different mineral based OAE treatments (calcium- and silicate-
based). Fish larvae were introduced to all mesocosms to add a higher trophic level. Regardless of the
OAE treatment (n = 10), we found the temporal dynamics of the mesoZP community composition to
follow many of the same trends, particularly under post-bloom conditions (Phase I and II). In the last
part of the experiment (Phase III), both the blooms and the mesoZP community composition diverged
across the mesocosms as one would expect. Considering the entire experiment, the mesoZP community
seems relatively unaffected by OAE. Even when significant effects were detected (∆TA, the mineral or
the interaction) they never persisted for longer periods of time. Furthermore, the impact of general
top-down and bottom-up effects seems to be much more dominant than any influence of OAE, tested
in the range between ∆TA = 0-600 µmol L−1. Negative impacts of higher ∆TA levels on mesoZP can
not be excluded and should be tested in future experiments. It should clearly be emphasized that OAE
is still a relatively new technology. Hence these results can give a better understanding of the response
of mesoZP communities in temperate water, but there is still a need a lot more research to widespread
investigation to understand how OAE impacts plankton communities in general.

Introduction
1.1
Climate change and Ocean alkalinity enhancement (OAE)
In 2021, the global atmospheric carbon dioxide (CO2) levels reached 414.7 parts per million (ppm)
(Lindsey, 2022), which is higher than it has been at any point during the last 2,000,000 years (IPCC,
2021). As a result of the increasing concentrations of anthropogenic CO2 and other greenhouse gases
(GHGs), more heat is trapped in the atmosphere, thus causing global warming. The earth is currently
warming at a rate that exceed any previous temperature changes during last 2000 years. Compared
to pre-industrial times, the global mean surface temperature has already increased by 1.1 °C. The
Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) also projects that an increase will persist until
the the middle of the 21st century at the minimum, independent of which emission trajectory that is
considered (IPCC, 2021). This can have serious impacts on various ecosystems and human-kind, which
in worst case scenario can be difficult to reverse, if certain tipping point are reached (IPCC, 2018).
To avoid the most severe consequences of climate change, the Paris Agreement1 was adopted in 2015.
Hence, countries are legally bound to pursue efforts to not let global temperature rise exceed 2 °C by
2100 compared to pre-industrial levels, but ideally limiting it to 1.5 °C. It also entails that the global
GHG emissions should be reaching a peak as soon as possible, with a net zero around 2050 (IPCC,
2018).

of this century (IPCC, 2022). Accordingly, immediate and large scale action is required as quickly as
possible, which demands fast decarbonization, transitions and large-scale efforts in all sectors (IPCC,
2018). Unfortunately, this might not be realistic in many countries or for certain industries within the
nearest future. In order to compensate for such emissions, the use of carbon dioxide removal (CDR) and
negative emission technologies (NETs) is likely to play a key role towards reaching net zero emissions
(Minx et al., 2018). CDR is defined as activities where humans intentionally remove CO2 from the
atmosphere, by storing it in other geological reservoirs or products over longer time-scales.
These
technologies can be used to balance out residual emissions that otherwise will make it hard to achieve
a net-zero, or even reduce the global surface temperatures in scenarios where human-induced removal
exceeds anthropogenic emissions (IPCC, 2021).
Currently, NETs are mainly confined to land-based techniques, whereas ocean-based solutions have
received less attention (Richardson et al., 2018).
One promising ocean-based approach with a high
potential to capture and store CO2 over longer time scales is Ocean Alkalinity Enhancement (OAE)
(Kheshgi, 1995; Gattuso et al., 2021). OAE aims to accelerate natural rock weathering processes that
shift the carbonate equilibrium of seawater (Eq. 3) from CO2 to bicarbonate/carbonate (i.e. increasing
the alkalinity), hence increasing the atmospheric CO2 storage capacity of the ocean. A greater increase
could theoretically lead to a larger draw-down of carbon dioxide from the atmosphere, if the CO2 deficit
in the seawater is compensated by in-gassing of atmospheric CO2 (equilibration). Until this happens,
marine ecosystems systems could be prone to CO2 limitation and phases with elevated pH levels, de-
pending on how much the alkalinity is altered among others (Bach et al., 2019). Both unequilibrated and
equilibrated OAE has been found to have the potential to increase the inorganic carbon sink of seawater
by 21 %, in a case where alkalinity was raised by 500 ± 3.2 µmol kg−1 (Ferderer et al., 2022). Suitable
minerals for OAE typically include limestone and olivine, i.e. calcium- and silicate-based minerals, that
need to be provided in up to billions of tons for OAE to be a meaningful way to capture and store
carbon (Caserini et al., 2022). In order to obtain higher dissolution rates, hence increasing the alkalinity
flux, the minerals must be grinded into pulverized material, before being spread onto the sea surface in
coastal and offshore areas (Fuss et al., 2018).
Total alkalinity (TA) itself is a chemical concept, that has been defined as the excess of proton acceptors
(bases) over proton donors (acids), in relation to a zero level of protons (Dickson, 1981). Consequently,
enhancing the alkalinity of the sea surface would also increase the buffering capacity of the ocean towards
acidification. By shifting the carbonate equilibrium of seawater from CO2 to bicarbonate/carbonate,
the alkalinity of seawater increase. The total alkalinity (TA) can be expressed as:

1.2
Ecological impact of OAE
There is currently very little knowledge of how OAE can influence the marine biota in pelagic food
webs (Bach et al., 2019; Richardson et al., 2018; Gattuso et al., 2021). A particularly important group
of marine organisms to consider is mesozooplankton (mesoZP), which act as a link between primary
producers and organisms of higher trophic levels. Copepods are among the most abundant and important
primary consumers in the oceans, serving many important ecosystem services such as food for important
commercial fish species (Kiørboe, 2011). Traditionally, these small crustaceans have been regarded as the

most important link between larger primary producers and planktivorous fish (Sommer et al., 2002), as
well as having a crucial contribution to carbon sequestration via sedimentation of fecal pellet production
(e.g. Urban-Rich (1999)). The role of gelatinous mesozooplankton in the pelagic food web on the other
hand, still remains poorly understood. They have been considered as a trophic dead-end (Verity and
Smetacek, 1996) until quite recently, thus often neglected from food web interactions, despite new
evidence challenging this misconception (Jaspers et al., 2015). The filter feeding appendicularians can
for instance short cut the microbial loop and transfer energy directly from picoplankton (0.2-2 µm),
which is smaller than what copepods consume, to higher trophic levels. The pelagic tunicates can also
contribute to increased carbon sequestration by discarding their external feeding structure, also called
house (Gorsky and Fenaux, 1998). Hydrozoans can both compete with fish (larvae) as predators of ZP
(Purcell and Arai, 2001), but also make up their prey (Runge et al., 1987). Hence, structural changes in
the mesoZP community could have cascading effects up the food web (e.g. Murphy et al. (2020)), which
makes it important to understand how these functional groups responds to OAE in a pelagic ecosystem.
To do this, the direct and indirect impacts can be broken down into potential effects arising from 1)
changes in the carbonate chemistry and 2) introduction of mineral dissolution products.
1.3
Implications of increasing the alkalinity of seawater
With respect to the carbonate chemistry, large perturbations can occur for non-equilibrated OAE until
the seawater equilibrates with the atmosphere.
Accordingly, marine ecosystems can be exposed to
phases with high pH values and low pCO2 concentrations, with increasing magnitude related to the
amount of alkalinity added per volume. The perturbations in the carbonate chemistry will also depend
on how fast the volume mixes with nearby water masses, and how long it takes for the seawater to
equilibrate (i.e. air-sea gas exchange) (Bach et al., 2019). Seasonal fluctuations in the pH and the pCO2
are not uncommon for surface waters in coastal areas (e.g. Melzner et al. (2013)), although average
global pH and ambient pCO2 concentrations are approx. 8.1 EPA (2022) and 414 ppm Lindsey (2022).
At the end of the century however, we can expect the atmospheric CO2 concentrations to potentially
double, resulting in a lowering of both the pH and the the calcium carbonate saturation state (ocean
acidification). Hence, OAE could potentially counteract this by increasing the pH and the buffer capacity
of the seawater, which could give calcifiers (e.g. coccolithophores) a competitive advantage compared to
non-calcifiers (Bach et al., 2019). On the other hand, the primary producers might be at risk of facing
carbon limitation (Riebesell et al., 1993), which could limit the growth rate of e.g. larger diatoms if
pCO2 drops below approx. 100 µatm ((Goldman, 1999)). Alternatively, a shift from larger to smaller
primary producers with a lower nutrient affinity could take place before equilibration, which could favour
filter feeding appendicularians, as they graze on smaller particles than copepods (Gorsky and Fenaux,
1998).
Increasing the pH of the seawater could also have a direct physiological impact on mesoZP. Appendicu-
larians have been found to respond positively to higher ocean acidity as a result of increased fecundity
at reduced pH (Troedsson et al., 2012; Bouquet et al., 2018). In more alkaline environments, appen-
dicularians would loose this benefit, suggesting that OAE could have a negative impact for the pelagic
tunicates until equilibration of the system. In contrast, Hansen et al. (2017) found the tolerance of

1.4
Implications of mineral dissolution products
The impact of OAE on the marine biota is also linked to the type of mineral chosen, as limiting nutrients,
micronutrients or toxic metals can be introduced via mineral dissolution and hence alter the biology
of the ocean. Molecules or element that can be produced as the minerals dissolve on the sea surface
depend on the mineral, but can be categorized into: Bicarbonate and carbonate ions (i.e. alkalinity),
silicic acid (i.e. silicate), alkaline earth metals (e.g. calcium and magnesium), alkali metals (e.g. sodium
and potassium) and trace metals (e.g. iron and nickel) (Bach et al., 2019). Introduction of silicate will
mainly be the focus of this thesis, although implications of toxic trace metals and other micronutrients
should be considered in other studies. In contrast to carbonate based OAE (e.g. limestone), Si-based
OAE schemes could increase the pool of dissolved silicate in the ocean by many orders of magnitude
compared to natural Si-sources (Bach et al., 2019). This could have important implications for silicifiers
like diatoms, that are often constrained by a deficiency of DSi in subsurface waters, in comparison to
other macronutrients (Sarmiento et al., 2004). DSi is essential for their shell formation and the organisms
are responsible for approximately 1/4 of the global primary production, making diatoms probably the
most important silicifiers in the oceans (Tréguer and De La Rocha, 2013). Consequently, diatoms could
out-compete calcifiers like coccolithophores in Si-based OAE schemes, despite the energetic advantage
calcifers gain from increasing the alkalinity (Bach et al., 2019). For mesoZP, increased DSi concentrations
could hence benefit copepods and other crustaceans via bottom-up control, which could result in a highly
efficient pelagic food web (Sommer et al., 2002) if nitrogen and phosphorus is not limited.
1.5
Aims and objectives
As much of the complexity of OAE is related to the interaction between chemical, biogeochemical
and biological processes, mesocosm studies provides an excellent way to reduce these knowledge gaps.
Therefore, a large scale in-situ mesocosm experiment was conducted over 53 days in Raunefjord, Norway,
where alkalinity was added by means of a five level gradient for calcium-and silicium-based minerals.
Despite the many parameters monitored during the experiment, I was responsible for assessing the
impact of OAE on mesoZP, which represented the link between primary producers and introduced fish
larvae in the mesocosms. Accordingly, two main objectives of the thesis were set:
1) To assess the mesoZP community composition after alkalinity addition

To do this, zooplankton was sampled throughout the experiment (55 µm mesh size) and identified in
terms of taxa-specific abundances from the EtOH preserved samples. Certain taxa were then selected and
categorized into five main groups, based on their functional roles, size and life-stage: Copepod nauplii,
copepods excluding large Calanus sp., large Calanus sp., appendicularia and hydrozoa. Furthermore,
the experiment was divided into three different phases, depending on the response time to the changes
in the carbonate chemistry (short-, intermediate and long-term) and the chlorophyll a concentrations
(low and high). The temporal abundances and the community composition could then be assessed in
relation to each of the experimental phases.
2) To assess the impact of OAE on mesoZP abundances by analyzing the response of the
five functional groups to the effect of alkalinity, the mineral and/or an interaction
The effect of ∆TA (alkalinity gradient) was thought of as a combination of indirect and direct effects
arising from changes in the carbonate chemistry, that could alter the mesoZP abundances and community
composition. As many of these changes depend on how long the system takes to equilibrate and species
acclimation/adaptation, I considered the short, intermediate and long-term responses of the mesoZP to
OAE. The mineral effect was considered as the difference between the DSi concentrations between the
Ca- and Si-based OAE treatment, as no toxic trace metals or essential micronutrients were introduced
to any of the mesocosms via the OAE-based treatments. Hence, I assumed that addition of Si alone
should not alter the abundance of mesoZP directly, but indirectly trough bottom-up control.
For the five groups, I hypothesized the following:
• The five mesoZP groups will respond differently to the effect of the mineral and ∆TA over the
course of the experiment
• The effect of ∆TA will be most pronounced in the initial parts of the experiment, as the changes
in the carbonate chemistry are more extreme
• The mineral effect will not be significant in the phases where nitrogen and phosphorous were
limiting primary producers (i.e. no bottom up control)

Materials and method
A general description of the in-situ mesocosm study will be given first, where the most important part of
the experimental design will be described. Despite the complex interaction between multiple parameters
in the mesocosm, the main focus of this thesis was on the impact of OAE on the mesozooplankton
(mesoZP) community and its composition and abundance development over time. Mesozooplankton in
this context includes all size fractions >55 µm, and includes early life stages of e.g. copepods (nauplii),
which normally belong to the microzoopankton. I was part of the zooplankton team, that quantiifed
e.g. fatty acid, stable isotopes and ran respiration and egg production experiments. However, I was
responsible for collecting the zooplankton (ZP) samples, which I later preserved, size fractionated,
identified and quantitatively analysed to generate taxa or species specific abundance data.
This is
further explained in the second part, followed by the analysis of the impact of OAE on the zooplankton
community.
2.1
In-situ mesocosm study
2.1.1
Study site
The in-situ mesocosm study was located in Raunefjord, which is a strait situated on the southwest coast
of Norway, in between Bergen municipality and the island Sotra (Figure 3). The north–south oriented
fjord is 15 km long and has a maximum depth of 240 m. It is connected to the Norwegian coastline
by means of Korsfjord in the south, and by Byfjord and Hjeltefjord in the north. As there are no large
freshwater inputs to the fjord, the water masses are strongly influenced by the saline Atlantic deep water
through rapid exchange events (Golmen et al., 2003).

The ten mesocosms were deployed in three rows by RV ALKOR at 60°15.9’ N, 5°12.1’ E, on the 10th
of May 2022, about 1.1 km from Espegrend Marine Research Field Station of Bergen University. The
water column depth at the site of deployment was approx. 70-85 m.
2.1.2
The “KOSMOS” Facility
Kiel Off-Shore Mesocosms for Ocean Simulations (KOSMOS) are free-floating mobile mesocosms, each
consisting of a floatation frame, and a 21.5 m long bag with a diameter of 2 m (Fig. 3). Additionally, a
2 meter long sediment trap is connected to the bottom part of the bags, whereas a hood is attached to
the top of the floating frames to prevent rain from entering the mesocosms. The material of the bags
is made of flexible thermoplastic polyurethane foil. Once the bags are deployed, unfolded at a 20 m
water columm depth and sealed with the sediment trap, the enclosed volume of the water in each unit
is approx. 60 m−3. For more detail, see Riebesell et al. (2013).
2.1.3
Experimental design
The in-situ mesocosm study lasted for 53 days, starting in May and ending in July 2022. Three days
after deployment, the mesocosms were closed on the 13th of May, which represented the start of the
experimental period (i.e. Day 0). Before the closure, nets with a 3 mm mesh size were pulled in all the
mesocosms to fish out larger or inhomogeneously distributed zooplankton, such as jellyfish or fish.
Alkalinity addition
The pre-treatment phase ended once the OAE treatments were introduced on Day 6 (19th of May)
according to a 5-level gradient (0, 150, 300, 450 and 600 µmol L−1) with two different mineral types
(Ca-based and Si-based).

Introduction of Atlantic herring
On the same day as alkalinity was adjusted (Day 6), Atlantic herring Clupea harengus was introduced
to all the mesocosms in abundances of 95 individuals per unit. This was done in order to add a higher
trophic level to the systems. Before the introduction, the mean size of the herring larvae was approx.
15 mm. C. harengus does occur all along the Norwegian coast (Tiedemann et al., 2021), which made it
a suitable higher trophic level organisms to use for estimating the potential impact of OAE on higher
trophic levels. One day after the final sampling day (Day 54), the entire mesocosms were concentrated
and the total number of remaining herring and their sizes assessed. Unfortunately, six other fish species
were found in addition to Atlantic herring, which had accidentally been enclosed in the mesocosms and
were not removed by the initial filtration of the entire water column by 3 mm. The final wet-mass was
dominated by cod-like species, whereas flatfish, goby and mackerel made up smaller contributions to
the total wet mass (Fig. 24, A.11)
Nutrient addition
On the 8th of June (Day 26), inorganic nutrients were added to all mesocosms to force the systems
from post-bloom to bloom-conditions. The target concentrations were 4 µmol L−1 of nitrate, 0.25 µmol
L−1 of phosphate and 1 µmol L−1 of silicate. On Day 27 however, the measured nitrate concentrations
showed higher values in M9 and M10, compared to the rest of the mesocosms. Moreover, the target
concentration of 4 µmol L−1 of NO−
3 was not reached. Hence a second nutrient addition was made on
Day 28 to adjust between the mesocosms. For development of nutrient conditions during the mesocosm
study, see Fig. 6).
Other activities
In order to assess trophic transfer efficiencies, 13C was added on Day 12 and 35. Besides, the walls of
the mesocosms were cleaned on the outside and on the inside multiple times to avoid materials from
accumulating on the surfaces, as this would impact the light penetration. All the above activities are
illustrated in Figure 5.
2.1.4
Sampling schedule
Multiple physical, biological, and biogeochemical parameters were monitored regularly in the mesocosms
(and the fjord) during the entire mesocosm study. For instance, CTD casts and integrated water samples
were taken every other day, together with the collection of particles from the sediment traps. The only
exception was on Day 15, where a storm event occurred. The CTD casts included measurements of
temperature, salinity, pH, and photosynthetically active radiation (PAR), where the mean salinity of
all the mesocosms after enclosure was 32.7 ± 0.34. The in-situ temperature in the mesocosms were
generally increasing from the start of the experiment until the end, as expected due to seasonality
effects of surface warming, with temperatures ranging from 8.72 to 13 °C (Fig. 14 in Appendix A.6).
For a further description of these parameters, the responsible person who measured the parameter should
be contacted.

2.2
Mesozooplankton abundances
2.2.1
MesoZP sampling
The mesoZP samples were collected in all the mesocosms on day 1, 5, 7, 9, 11, 17, 19, 23, 29, 33, 37,
41, 45, 49 and 53 of the experiment. Only the nets marked with a yellow box (Day 7, 11, 19, 23, 33, 41,
49 and 53) in Figure 5 were fully analyzed due to time restrictions, leaving the rest as backup samples.
Due to time constrains, no samples from the fjord were analysed.
The mesoZP samples were taken in order, starting with M1, in the time frame between 10:00 am to 1:00
pm on the sampling days. An Apstein net with a mesh size of 55 µm was used, which had a cone-shaped
opening of 16.7 cm (diameter) and a length of 120 cm. To collect the ZP, the net was lowered down
vertically in the mesocosms to a depth of 21 m, and hauled up to the surface. For every net haul, a
volume of 0.469 m3 was sampled (Appendix A.2). Once the net was brought up to the surface, the
mesoZP sample was washed into a jar of 500 mL with filtered seawater. Moreover, the samples were
stored in a cooling box with cool packs to maintain the water temperature at the sampling site. Back
in the laboratory, the samples were divided into three different size fractions: 55-200 µm, 200-500 µm
and >500 µm, by using sieves with a mesh size of 500 µm, 200 µm and 55 µm in the respective order.
Immediately after, the size-fractionated samples were fixed in 70% ethanol (EtOH).

Originally, samples from Day 1 of the experiment were supposed to be analyzed to get a baseline in
case the plankton communities differed vastly. They were however rinsed with EtOH instead of filtered
seawater during the concentration phase, which caused much larger aggregates than the other samples.
As these samples were before OAE treatment, we did not consider them for the analyses and instead
used samples from Day 7 as starting community composition.
The three different size classes (55-200 µm, 200-500 µm and >500 µm) belonging to a single sample were
either fully counted or sub-sampled. Since the largest size fractions (>500 µm) were inhomogeneous and
contained many large aggregates, the entire sample was analyzed. To untangle all the organisms, the
lumps were separated with two needles. It was decided not to count the copepod nauplii and Oithona
sp. in the largest size fraction of the samples, due to time restrictions. It was expected that most small
sized copepods and nauplii would be captured in the smaller size classes. Based on the initial counts
from Day 7, their contribution in the >500 µm size fraction to the total abundance represented from
2.4-7.7% (see Appendix A.3).
In the two smaller size fractions (55-200 µm and 200-500 µm), the organisms were uniformly distributed,
allowing for subsamples to be taken. The concentrated samples were first poured into a jar and diluted
until reaching a known volume, dependent on how dense the samples were. From this, subsamples were
taken with a calibrate pipette in multiple steps after stirring the sample properly. The subsamples
represented from 15 to 25% of the total sample. All the samples belonging to the same size fraction
that were taken on the same sampling day, were of equal sizes (% of total sample).
For the most
abundant taxa, a threshold of 200 individuals was set. Hence, after reaching this limit, the volume of
the subsample counted was noted down.
For the >500 µm size fraction, the mesoZP abundances (individuals m−3) could then be estimated by
dividing the number of individual counted for a specific taxonomic group by the total volume sampled
by the net (0.469 m3). As for the 55-200 µm and 200-500 µm size fractions, where subsamples were
taken, the abundances were calculated by using the following formula:

2.4
Data treatment and analysis
The temporal development of the copepod nauplii, the copepods excluding large Calanus sp., the large
Calanus sp., the appendicularians and the hydrozoans were plotted by using R, according to Table
3. To illustrate the temporal trends in the community composition, the abundance of the taxonomic
groups were divided by the total mesozooplankton abundance (>55 µm) sampled in each mesocosm,
and multiplied by 100. Both the raw data and the modified data can be found in Appendix A.2, A.3
and A.4.
2.4.1
Linear regression
First, the mean abundance of the taxonomic groups described in Table 3 was estimated for the three
phases (Table 4), for all the mesocosms. A single linear regression model with a categorical variable was
then fitted to investigate how the mesoZP abundance of the taxonomic groups responded to the effect
of OAE in the Ca- and Si-based OAE treatments. This was done by using the lm() function within the
R-package called "stats" (version 3.6.2) in RStudio. The full linear model contained a response variable
(phase average of mesoZP abundance for all 10 mesocosms), a continuous predictor (∆TA = 0, 150, 300,
450 and 600 µmol L−1), a categorical variable (mineral used) and an interaction term (∆TA:Mineral).
ANOVA (analyse of variance) Type II was performed in R to test whether the main effects or the
interaction terms were significant, with a significance level of α = 0.05. In case the interaction term was
insignificant, the model was further reduced so that the response of mesoZP abundance was predicted
only according to the main effects, ∆TA and Mineral. In terms of the visual representation of the
regression lines, the data was fitted for each mineral treatment for illustrative purposes, whereas the
annotated statistic was based on the outcome of the full/reduced model described above.
Before fitting the model, the four assumptions associated with a linear regression model were tested:
1) linearity of the data, homogeneity of residuals variance, independence of residuals error terms and
normality of residuals. This was done by visual inspection of diagnostic plots in R. If the assumptions
were violated, the raw data was log-transformed for the full and reduced model to see if the assumptions
were met. Otherwise, it was indicated in the summary statistics shown in Appendix A.5.

it was originally planned to do the same analysis for different groups of primary producers. The data was
however not ready in time, so it was excluded from the analysis. Hence, total chlorophyll a concentrations
were used as a proxy, where the same type of regression analysis was made as for the mesoZP. As

3.1
Population dynamics
3.1.1
Temporal development of mesoZP abundances
To illustrate the temporal dynamics of the functional mesoZP groups after alkalinity addition, the
abundances (ind. m−3) of copepods, appendicularians and hydrozoans are shown in Figure 7 for each
mesocosm. Phase I, II and III are labelled on the plots to indicate the short-, intermediate- and long-term

4
Discussion
Ocean Alkalinity Enhancement has a high potential to mitigate climate change by enhancing the uptake
of carbon dioxide by the ocean, although many questions still need to be answered before large-scale
implementation would be feasible (Bach et al., 2019). Little is for instance known about the conse-
quences of changing the seawater chemistry and introducing mineral dissolution products to the ocean
for the succession and structuring of marine pelagic food webs. Contributing to reducing these knowl-
edge gaps, this study focuses on the impact of OAE on key mesozooplankton groups, particularly on
the abundances of copepod nauplii, small/medium sized copepods (copepods excluding large Calanus
sp.), large copepods (Calanus sp. with prosome length > 1.5 mm), appendicularians and hydrozoans.
Whereas controlled experimental studies are necessary to assess direct physiological changes of OAE on
a certain taxa or species, mesocosms gives us the opportunity to study both direct and indirect effects
by including ecological interactions. This is especially important for mesozooplankton, which function
as a link between lower trophic levels (algae) and higher trophic levels (fish). Hence, their population
development and species composition can be impacted by both bottom up and top down effects, as well
as directly impacted by OAE.
4.1
Environmental conditions and chlorophyll a
OAE is likely to have various different outcomes, depending on the system and environmental conditions
that you introduce it to. The temperate investigation area in SW Norway, Raunefjord, is for instance
characterized by inflow or upwelling events that supply nutrients to the fjord. Hence, the area has a
high seasonal and annual variability in terms of phytoplankton biomass (chlorophyll a) and community
composition. In temperate waters, diatoms often initiate the spring bloom in periods with a high supply
of macronutrients (N and P), as well as dissolved silicate (DSi) (Sommer et al., 2002). This is also the
case in Raunefjord, with diatoms dominating the spring blooms typically in March, where the chlorophyll
a concentrations typically reach 4 to 10 µg L−1 (Paulino et al., 2018). When nutrients become limiting,
diatoms are often replaced by smaller primary producers, with higher surface:volume ratios (Raven,
1986). This post-bloom situation describe the time when the mesocosms were enclosed (May 2022),
i.e. in low nutrient- and chlorophyll a conditions. Until after nutrients were supplied on Day 26, the
chlorophyll a concentrations in Phase I and II ranged between 0.31 and 1.01 µg L−1 across all mesocosms.
These low chl a levels could have resulted in food limitation for abundant primary consumers, such as
copepods (especially larger sized calanoid copepods), that can not feed on the smallest phytoplankton
(Sommer et al., 2002), that often dominate in post-bloom conditions.
As illustrated in Figure 6C, the peak cholorophyll a concentrations across all mesocosms occurred 13 to
21 days after the first nutrient addition on Day 26, ranging from 2.09 (M6 0) to 5.10 (M10 150) µg L−1.
Although the values are similar to summer blooms reported by Paulino et al. (2018) (3 to 16 µg L−1),
the large range could be a result of e.g. variations in the phytoplankton community composition and
differences in the grazing pressure by the mesoZP. We can see that the uptake of nitrate varied across the
OAE treatments in Figure 6B, although nitrate was depleted in all mesocosms on day 49. On the other

hand, the abundances of the primary consumers (i.e. copepods and appendicularians) were relatively
high at the time of nutrient addition, likely due to the recent spring bloom before enclosure. Hence, it

been a result of heavy grazing pressure exerted by mesoZP on the primary producers. Direct food web
interactions and cascading effects are difficult to decipher in mesocosm settings. However, mesocosms
do give a good representation of the overall community response to e.g. environmental stressors such
as pH or warming (Riebesell et al., 2013) in a more realistic way, compared to single species bottle
incubation. Therefore, a combination of both approaches is needed in order to arrive at a mechanistic
understanding of the underlying interactions and how these translate into ecosystem changes.
4.2
Mesozooplankton population dynamics
The total mesoZP was largely dominated by copepods in the two first phases (Fig. 4), which were
characterized by low chlorophyll a concentrations (i.e. food limiting). The contribution of the copepod
nauplii to the total mesozooplankton community declined across all mesocosms in most of Phase II
(Fig.
8B), in contrast to the small/medium sized copepods.
This could imply lower reproduction
rates of the female copepods, as a result of the low phytoplankton biomass, suggesting that bottom-up
effects controlled the copepod standing stocks initially in the experiment. A correlation between the
chlorophyll a concentrations and the fecundity rates of female copepods has been found by others (e.g.
Kimmerer et al. (2005)), although Bunker and Hirst (2004) only found a relationship between the two
for broadcasters, in contrast to sac spawners. Besides, food is also a matter of not only quantity, but
also quality. When chlorophyll a is less than approx. 5 µg L−1 (i.e. in most of the experiment) in
natural waters, the picoplankton (<2 µm in size) contribution to the total phytoplankton biomass can
vary substantially, and reach very high levels in low chlorophyll a conditions (Bunker and Hirst, 2004).
This is important to consider as copepods are selecitve feeders (Hansen et al., 1994), normally grazing on
larger phytoplankton that would accordingly be less dominant in environments with low phytoplankton
biomass and nutrient limitation. Furthermore, we can see that the copepod nauplii contribution to the
total mesoZP community picked up in all the mesocosms in the last part of the experiment, following
the blooms. This was despite having copepod abundances that were almost an order of magnitude lower
than at the start, further strengthening our assumptions of a bottom-up control in the early part of the
experiment.
From Day 19 however, we saw decreasing abundances across all mesocosms for the copepod nauplii, the
small/medium sized copepods and the large Calanus sp. In most cases, copepod mortality is rather a
result of predation than starvation, with the exception of certain life stages (Hirst et al., 2007). Thus,
reductions in the copepod standing stocks implies that the mortality of the copepods exceeded the
reproduction rate of the female copepods from this point, which could also contribute to a further
reduction in the number of copepod nauplii, along with food limitations. Mortality on the copepod
community was likely imposed by i) herring larvae, ii) hydromedusae, iii) accidentally included fish eggs
and larvae during mesocosm enclosure that were missed by filtering the out at the start and that reached
relatively large sizes in Phase III of the experiment. As mentioned in Section 2.1.3, accidentally enclosed
cod-like species made up most of the total wet mass of the fish caught at the end of the experiment.

The introduced Atlantic herring (Clupea harengus) had a very high mortality (> 95%), also presumably
due to predation by bigger fish, which could suggest fast growth rates of the accidentally enclosed fish.
At the time of introduction, the C. harengus larvae were approx. 15 mm, suggesting that their main
prey would be copepod nauplii (e.g. Temora longicornis and Oithona similis) or mid-sized copepodites
of e.g. Pseudocalanus sp. (Wilson et al., 2018). We did not find any correlation between the abundance
of the taxonomic mesozooplankton groups on Day 53 and the total wetmass of fish caught on the final
fishing, which could be a result of the various species that were enclosed.
Predatory hydromedusae can also exert a profound top-down control on plankton communities, espe-
cially when present in high abundances (Alldredge, 1984).
In western Norwegian fjord close to the
investigation area, Hosia and Båmstedt (2007) found peak abundances of medusae during the spring of
less than 17 ind. m−3. In contrast, the mesocosms had substantially higher densities during most of the
experiment (4 to 204 hydromedusae m−3), presumably due to the removal of their natural predators
before enclosure. Runge et al. (1987) have for instance found Atlantic mackerel (Scomber scombrus) to
feed on A. digitale, which can be found in along the Norwegian coast (Olafsdottir et al., 2019). Whereas
Hosia and Båmstedt (2007) found a seasonal peak abundance of A. digitale reaching 8 ind. m−3, the
abundances in the mesococms in the two first phases ranged from 2-38 ind. m−3. Many hydromedusa
have a polyp stage that need a surface to attach to (e.g. Jaspers et al. (2021)), in contrast to A. digitale,
which is holoplanktonic (Runge et al., 1987). Increasing the surface areas by introducing mesocosms into
pelagic systems could therefore also have contributed to higher abundances of the hydromedusae, than
compared to the surrounding fjords. Hydromedusa have a diverse diet, where A. digitale for instance
commonly prey on smaller/medium sized copepods and cladocerans, but also occasionally Oikopleura
sp. (Pagés et al., 1996). Thus, the hydrozoans and fish larvae have overlapping diets, which probably
further increased the top-down control on particularly copepods, but also on other mesozooplankton.
Without any information on changes in the biovolume of the sampled hydromedusae, it is however chal-
lenging to conclude about their potential impact. However, the abundance of hydromedusae increased
during the last sampling days, which was likely driven by reproduction events of the hydromedusae, as
their abundance increased in the smaller size fraction and decreased in the largest size fraction (Fig. 16,
Appendix A.9).
In contrast to hydrozoans, appendicularians can have extremely short generation times (e.g. 6.2 days at
12.0±0.7 °C found by Bouquet et al. (2018)), meaning that responses in the standing stock to changes
in the environment/food could be observed much faster.
Compared to copepods, O. dioica is also
considered to be less prone to food limitations (López-Urrutia et al., 2003), in addition to their ability to
feed on picoplankton (Gorsky and Fenaux, 1998). Accordingly, the pelagic tunicates could theoretically
have had an advantage in low chlorophyll a conditions (Phase I and II), despite being outnumbered by
copepods across all the mesocosms. Therefore, it is common in marine environments, that copepods
outnumber appendicularia abundances (e.g. Vargas et al. (2002)). However, it needs to be considered,
that calanoid copepods can also exert a top town control on appendicularians via predation of their eggs
(Sommer and Stibor, 2002). Around the time of the appendicularian blooms in Phase III, the mesoZP
community composition changed quite drastically, where appendicularians suddenly contributed to up

4.3
The impact of OAE on mesoZP
According to Figure 6, we can see that the copepod nauplii, small/medium sized copepods, large Calanus
sp., appendicularians and hydrozoans responded differently to the effect of OAE in the three phases of
the experiment, as hypothesized. A significant effect of ∆TA, the mineral and/or the interaction was
only found for the abundance of certain taxonomic groups in one of two phases, except from for copepod
nauplii. None of the identified effects did however translate from one phase to the next phase, or showed
any consistency over the course of the experiment. In these types of experiments where many interactions
are considered on a community level, it is however challenging to find exact explanations to the reason
for the observed findings. We do however suspect that trophic interactions were a strong driver of many
of the temporal trends seen for the key mesoZP groups as discussed, irrespective of the OAE treatment.
Besides, nutrient limitation (N and P) in the two first phases were likely controlling the community
structure of the primary producers (favouring smaller ones), suggesting that the indirect effect of OAE
on mesoZP could be very different and much more pronounced after nutrients were supplied. With
respect to some of the key mesoZP groups with longer generation times, changes in their standing stock
as a result of the blooms were likely not captured before the end of the experiment. Moreover, the
strong-top down control in Phase III, has probably introduced a lot of noise to the mesoZP abundances,
as the accidentally enclosed fish and fish (eggs) cannot be accounted for (i.e. high variabiliy). Even
though we do not find a strong and consistent effect of OAE on any of the investigated groups over
the entire lifetime of the experiments, the results presented here should be interpreted with caution.
Further research is needed in order to determine the interaction of ∆TA, pH and mineral effects on the

4.3.1
Increasing alkalinity
The effect of increasing the alkalinity was for the key mesoZP considered to be a combination of direct
and indirect effects, that originated from changes in the carbonate systems. It was hypothesized that a
significant effect of alkalinity (∆TA) would be most pronounced on the mesoZP in the initial parts of
the experiment, as the carbonate chemistry perturbations would become less extreme as the mesocosms
equilibrated with the atmosphere. This can not be confirmed for two reasons, 1) there was no effect of
∆TA on most of the key mesoZP groups considered in any of the three phases and 2) the mesocosms
did not really equilibrate a lot over the course of the experiment. The latter can be seen in Figure 4,
where the pH and the pCO2 (Fig. 4) after alkalinity addition remained at similar levels throughout the
entire experiment (53 days), indicating that the air-sea gas exchange was rather slow. This also relates
to the importance of the oceanographic settings to which OAE is applied to. In systems where there
is a fast air-sea gas exchange, the perturbations in the carbonate chemistry (low pCO2 and high pH)
would become air-equilibrated quicker, as the CO2 deficit in the seawater would be compensated for via
in-gassing of atmospheric CO2 faster (Bach et al., 2019). In such systems, marine organisms would thus
be exposed to potentially extreme carbonate chemistry perturbations for a shorter time, compared to
areas where equilibration between the sea surface and atmosphere takes longer (Bach et al., 2019).
Despite the little equilibration taking place during the experiment, the mesocosms did change from am-
bient conditions to pCO2 and pH values ranging from 66.1-192 µatm and 8.33-8.73 (Day 7), respectively.
The most extreme perturbations were found in the mesocosms with the largest ∆TA. Sudden changes in
the environment could result in physiological stress for certain taxa, which can vary a lot depending on
the species, the magnitude of change and how long it persist. Although the carbonate perturbations in
the mesocosms were expected to be mitigated as a result of in-gassing of atmospheric CO2, this did not
really happen over the course of the experiment as seen in Figure 4. Hence, the pCO2 and pH values
at each level of ∆TA were rather similar, implying potential long-term stress for the mesoZP (Melzner
et al., 2013). The standing stock of copepod nauplii, large Calanus sp., appendicularians or hydrozoans
did not show any response to ∆TA in Phase I or Phase II, which could indicate that the effect of ∆TA
had a little overall impact on these mesoZP groups in periods with low chlorophyll a concentrations.
With that said, the perturbations in this experiment should also also compared to what can naturally
occur in the field. In the mesocosms where alkalinity was modified, the increase in pH was larger than
what Omar et al. (2016) found in western Norwegian fjords with the highest monthly mean of 8.19
in April (2005-2009). In contrast, pH levels as high as 9 to 9.7 have been found in eutrophic waters
(Mariager Fjord, Denmark) during the summer in periods with high primary production, compared to
approx. 8 in October month (Hansen, 2002). Hence, perturbations in the mesocosms were much less
severe than what could occur in surface waters at all levels of ∆TA. Severe impacts of higher ∆TA
values can not be excluded, and must therefore be investigated further.

should also be carefully interpreted, due to the presence of accidentally enclosed fish, which are likely
to have exerted a high predation pressure on the mesozooplankton community.

4.3.2
Mineral effect
The mineral effect was considered as the difference in the silicate concentration between the Ca- and Si-
based OAE treatment after mineral dissolution, since no toxic heavy metals were added to the mesocosms
via the OAE-treatments. DSi concentrations should not directly affect the key mesoZP groups considered
here, as neither are silificying organisms. Accordingly, any potential mineral effects would rather be
indirectly mediated to the mesoZP, as a response to mineral dissolution at the base of the food web.
The measured silicate concentrations in the investigation area (Raunefjord, Norway) typically range
from 0 to 6 µmol L−1 (Paulino et al. (2018), Fig. S2), meaning that most of the Si-treatments had
more than 10 times higher concentrations than those commonly measured. Since diatoms are among
the most important silicifiers in the ocean (Tréguer and De La Rocha, 2013), and also are known to
dominate (spring) blooms in the investigation area (Paulino et al., 2018), any potential response in
the mesoZP abundances were therefore naturally linked to the presence or absence of diatoms. During
post-bloom conditions however, no mineral effect on the mesoZP community was expected, as both the
nitrogen and phosphorous were low, and therefore excluding primary producers with a high nutrient
uptake like diatoms (Sommer et al., 2002). Diatoms are also usually found absent in Raunefjord outside
bloom conditions (Paulino et al., 2018). It is therefore reasonable to assume that a negligible presence
of diatoms in the initial parts of the experiment, although this can be confirmed at a later point when
more data is available. The temporal plot of silicate in the post-bloom phases also show very little
uptake until after nutrient addition (Day 26 and 28), where the concentration across all the Si-based
treatments remained at similar levels (65.9-70.1 µmol Si L−1).
No mineral effect was found for the mean abundances of the total copepod nauplii, large Calanus sp.
and hydrozoans in either Phase I or II, when nitrate and phosphate were limiting. In contrary, the
abundances of small/medium sized copepods were significantly higher in the Si-treatments in the initial
shock-phase (Phase I), compared to the Ca-based OAE treatments (Fig. 9A). The same was found in
terms of the chlorophyll a concentrations (Fig 6C), although the phase average phytoplankton biomass
was lower than 1 µg L−1 across all the mesocosms.

of picoplankton, which are generally not grazed upon by copepods. Moreover, it seems unlikely that

amounts of DSi should be carefully handled as diatoms can out-compete other primary producers at

in the Si-treatments during the bloom, which can be assessed once data is available, could have huge
implications for the structuring of the entire food web.
It could give copepods a benefit, which in
the classical representation of a pelagic ecosystem leads to a more efficient food web (i.e. higher fish
production) (Sommer et al., 2002). Moreover, enhanced diatoms blooms could also increase marine
export production, which overall could have large implications for the biological pump (Köhler et al.,
2010). It is therefore necessary to also conduct similar OAE experiments where macronutrients are not
limiting, to see the full potential impact of increasing the DSi pool, which is otherwise limiting.

Conclusion
This study investigated the potential impacts of unequilibrated ocean alkalinity enhancement (Ca- and
Si-based) on a coastal plankton community, with a main focus on five key mesozooplankton groups:
copepod nauplii, small/medium sized copepods, large Calanus sp., appendicularians and hydrozoans.
No effects of increasing the alkalinity of seawater (∆TA) or mineral dissolution products (i.e. differences
in the silicate concentrations) were found for most of the key taxa in the range of up to ∆TA of +600
µmol L−1.
A few exceptions occurred in some of the parts of the experiment, illustrating different
responses of the mesoZP groups as the conditions changes, which will need to be further investigated as
more data will become available. As the these were only confined to a certain part of the experiment and
often seem to be related to other parameters, it strengths the overall impression that bottom-up/top-
down control generally were controlling the mesoZP community to a larger extent than any impact
of OAE, irrespective of the treatment.
Although the carbonate chemistry perturbations, according
to each level of ∆TA, did not change much over the experiment, even the most severe perturbations
are much lower than what can naturally occur in e.g. eutrophic waters. Careful consideration should
however be taken considering the oceanographic settings and seasonal variations, which can result in a
quite different situation and potentially be more harmful for mesoZP. The impact of introducing large
amounts of silicate to temperate areas should however be more carefully considered, as nutrient were
added too late into the experiment to see a full effect on the mesoZP community composition. Thus also
indirect effects from large changes in the phytoplankton community composition to OAE, also remain
rather unknown in term of how this can impact mesoZP communities and their composition.

Future perspectives and considerations for using OAE to miti-
gate Climate Change
Ocean alkalinity enhancement has the potential to sequester large amounts of carbon, hence counter-
acting both climate change and also ocean acidification by increasing the alkalinity of seawater (Köhler
et al., 2010; Bach et al., 2019; Ferderer et al., 2022). It seems as if the mesoZP were relatively unaffected
by the ∆TA (0-600 µmol L−1) in this experiment, compared to other food web interactions. This does
not however imply that there could be severe effects if alkalinity is enhanced even further, which makes it
urgent to test how plankton communities respond to OAE at higher levels of ∆TA, under unequilibrated
conditions. The use of mineral is also crucial, as toxic metals or other ions could potentially have large
implications for the food web, when supplied in large amounts. Currently, there is no legal framework
to practice OAE or other CDR technologies (Oschlies and Klepper, 2017), making in-situ mesocosm
studies like this, an important step towards increasing our knowledge of the possibility of large-scale
implementation. As clearly illustrated in this study, the interactions between lower and higher trophic
levels must not be neglected when assessing the impact of ocean alkalinity enhancement on a natural
pelagic system. Especially the introduction of otherwise limiting nutrients like silicate through mineral
dissolution could have the potential to alter the plankton community substantially through bottom-up
effects, compared to the normal succession. In contrast to oligotrophic seas, temperate areas have much
more seasonal and interannual variations, where nutrients can be sporadically introduced to the surface
waters and change the plankton community composition substantially. This could therefore result very
different mesoZP community structures under a Ca-based and Si-based OAE scheme. Such variations
mediated via primary producers to primary consumers were not captured to its fullest in this mesocosm
study, as nitrate and phosphate were depleted immediately and only supplied once during the experi-
ment. The timing was also unfortunately too late to see a full response in some of the mesoZP groups.
This study has however contributed with valuable insight to the influence of OAE on the abundances
of key mesozooplankton in post-bloom conditions, although more data exploration with respect to pri-
mary producers is needed to understand potential bottom-up driven responses to OAE. It should also be
considered that treatment differences in terms of mesoZP biomass were not assessed here, which could
have given quite different results. A similar type of mesocosm should be conducted with Ca-based and
Si-based OAE treatments, where conditions in the fjord are mimicked by adding pulses of nutrients into
the mesosoms from the very beginning. The potential benefit of calcifiers to increased alkalinity should
also be assessed, particularly in e.g. Arctic areas where pteropods (holoplanktonic marine gastropods)
dominate (Comeau et al., 2010).

Potential effects of Ocean Alkalinity Enhancement on Appendicularia
Amrita Bhaumik1,2, Synne Spjelkavik3, Maarten Boersma1, 2, Cornelia Jaspers3
1Alfred-Wegener-Institut Helmholtz-Zentrum für Polar- und Meeresforschung, Biologische Anstalt Helgoland, Kurpromenade, 27483, Helgoland, Germany
2Fachbereich 2 Biologie/Chemie, Universität Bremen, Leobener Straße, 28334, Bremen, Germany
3Centre for Gelatinous Plankton Ecology and Evolution, DTU Aqua - Technical University of Denmark, 2800 Kgs. Lyngby, Denmark
Background
Ocean
alkalinity
enhancement
(OAE)
could
mitigate
climate
change
by
reducing
atmospheric CO2 levels and countering ocean acidification (OA), thereby, increasing the
buffering capacity of the carbonate system (Fig. 1). Two general pathways for OAE exist,
either using olivine or quick lime (Fig. 2). Olivine could benefit silicifiers and N2-fixers
(cyanobacteria), subsequently increasing ocean productivity and turning the blue ocean
into a green(er) ocean. Alternatively, quick lime would profit calcifiers and may turn the blue
ocean into a white(r) ocean.
Appendicularians, pelagic tunicates, are capable of directly feeding on the microbial food
web through their external feeding structures, called houses (Fig. 3). They participate in
exporting and sequestering carbon to deeper waters. They show enhanced fecundity at low
pH levels. Thus, they might play an important role in the future ocean to shortcut the food
web and maintain carbon export. However, it is unknown to date if or how OAE might
impact a key functional zooplankton group like appendicularians, focusing on their house
production and clearance rate.

Objectives
•
To understand the potential impacts of OAE (using olivine and quick lime) on
the abundance of gelatinous zooplankton with a focus on Appendicularia.
•
To determine any change in the physiological function (i.e. house production
rate and clearance rate) of Oikopleura dioica due to OAE.
Experimental setup
Natural coastal plankton was used as a seeding community for 10 mesocosms which
represented a gradient in OAE levels from ∆0 to ∆600 µEql-1. Calcium and silica-based
minerals were added to reach the respective alkalinity levels to investigate the effects of
OAE (increased alkalinity and pH) on cosmopolitan Appendicularian species Oikopleura
dioica.
Discussion
A change was detected in the gelatinous zooplankton/overall mesozooplankton ratio in the mesocosms. However, no specific trend was observed based on the minerals or alkalinity
gradients.
No significant difference was found regarding clearance rate and house renewal rates (p>0.05), suggesting that OAE in the tested alkalinity gradients does not impact their feeding
as well as house production and thereby carbon sequestration potential.
Conclusion
Appendicularians are relatively robust against altered pH and alkalinity levels. The results highlight the importance of multi-trophic analysis including neglected groups like
gelatinous zooplankton to understand the impacts of global climate change and its mitigation strategies on ecosystem structure and function.
