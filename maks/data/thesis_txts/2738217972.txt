Abstract
This study investigates the integration of Reverse Water Gas Shift (RWGS) and Hydro-
formylation (HyFo) processes for conversion of 1-Butene into aldehydes by using supported
liquid phase (SLP) and supported ionic liquid phase (SILP) catalyst systems.
The first part of the study is pursuing a RWGS catalyst that has a sufficient conversion
of carbon dioxide (CO2) to carbon monoxide (CO) at low temperatures to increase the
compatibility with the HyFo reaction. A simulation conducted on the RWGS reaction re-
vealed that the equilibrium CO2 conversion is 3.41 % at 150 °C, and 2.66 % at 130 °C. The
results obtained in the RWGS autoclave experiments show that the ionic liquid (IL) 1-
Ethyl-3-methylimidazolium acetate (EMIM Ac) plays an important role in capturing and
activating CO2, but also that the CO2 conversion decreases remarkably when decreasing
the temperature from 150 °C to 130 °C. Different optimizations were implemented in or-
der to compensate for the low temperature, including a metal content screening, which
revealed that the optimal Ru loading is 0.055 wt.%, yielding a CO2 conversion of 6.3 %
at 150 °C. The conversion obtained at 150 °C is higher than the equilibrium conversion
revealed by the simulation. This could possibly be due to trapping of CO2 in the IL, or
due to an alternative reaction path, involving the initial formation of formic acid, and the
subsequent decomposition of the formic acid to CO and H2O. Calculations of trapping in
IL hints that a small amount of CO2 is trapped, and this explanation therefore seems less
significant. Finally, the optimized system did yield a conversion of 0.295% at 130 °C.
The second part of the study is seeking a catalyst for the HyFo reaction that promotes
high conversion of 1-Butene and high selectivity towards the linear aldehyde, near condi-
tions at which CO has been generated during the RWGS reaction. Since phosphite ligands
are known to be water sensitive, two more water resistant phosphine ligands (DPPE and
Xantphos) were tested. Results of the HyFo experiments revealed a trade-off, since the ex-
periment based on the DPPE ligand yielded low conversion, and high selectivity towards
the linear aldehyde, while the experiment based on Xantphos resulted in the opposite.
BiPhePhos was therefore chosen as the ligand for the combined experiment, despite being
a phosphite. This HyFo catalyst was tested with the optimized RWGS catalyst (10 wt.%
IL, and 0.055 wt.% Ru). A small amount of the linear aldehyde was produced, hinting at
a proof of concept. However, further studies are needed to improve the system.

1. Introduction
In light of global warming, where the chemical industry is accountable for approximately
5 % of the carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions globally, the desire for greener and more sus-
tainable productions to reduce the carbon footprint is yet bigger than ever before [1] [2].
A recent study has shown that the production of around 90 % of commercially available
chemicals relies on the application of catalysts [3]. This emphasizes the power of cataly-
sis, and the importance of research within this field to improve and innovate conventional
processes.
This project examines the integration of Reverse Water Gas Shift (RWGS) and Hydro-
formylation (HyFo) processes for the conversion of 1-Butene into aldehydes by using
supported liquid phase (SLP) and supported ionic liquid phase (SILP) catalysts.
CO2 is converted to carbon monoxide (CO) in the RWGS reaction. The produced CO
will then be used in the HyFo reaction to produce aldehydes. The two reactions will be
conducted in a single reactor where CO will be consumed by the HyFo reaction, which
shifts the equilibrium towards the desired direction, i.e. CO production in the RWGS
reaction.
The strategy investigated in this project has the potential to reduce the carbon footprint,
while increasing process safety by eliminating the need of handling dangerous CO.
The main challenge is to design two compatible catalyst systems, while the two reactions
are known to favor different reaction conditions. The conversion of CO2 to CO is consid-
ered the main bottleneck, and the big question investigated in the final part of the project
is whether the generated amount of CO is enough to shift the equilibrium, and drive the
proposed CO2 utilization strategy to success.

2. Theory
2.1 Introduction to Catalysis and Sustainable Chemistry
The term ”catalyst” originates from the Greek language, where the word ”katal´uˆo” de-
notes the action of ”untying”. This is exactly what a catalyst does - It unties the con-
straints of a reaction. In order for a chemical reaction to proceed, reactant molecules
must collide with sufficient energy, i.e. higher than the energy barrier of the reaction. As
it can be seen on Figure 1, catalysts function by lowering this energy barrier [4]:
Figure 1: Activation energies for a reaction with and without catalyst. Redrawn and modified
from [4]. Created with Biorender.com [5].
When catalysts lower the energy barrier, they decrease the activation energy of the reac-
tion, by providing an alternative reaction path, which increases the amount of successful
reactant collisions, i.e. collisions which entail product formation. A catalyst is thus de-
fined as a substance that increases the reaction rate, while not being consumed during
the reaction and while not changing the equilibrium point of the reaction. In addition to
accelerating reactions, catalysts can also be used to perform reactions at milder condi-
tions, including at lower temperatures and pressures, where it is normally more difficult
for the reactants to collide successfully [4] [6].
By promoting more efficient reactions in terms of both speed and energy, catalysis has
become an irreplaceable brick in a broad range of industries, including petroleum refin-
ery, chemical engineering, polymers, and pharmaceuticals. In fact, it has recently been
estimated that the production of more than 90 % of commercially available chemicals
relies on the application of catalysts [3]. Research within catalysis is therefore key for
optimizing processes in the chemical industry.

In light of global warming, the desire for more sustainable and environment-friendly pro-
ductions that reduce the carbon footprint are yet bigger than ever, since CO2 gas emissions
are one of the major global warming contributors, and since the chemical industry is ac-
countable for around 5 % of the CO2 emissions globally [1] [2] [7].
Several strategies have therefore been developed for carbon capture and storage (CCS)
and for carbon capture and utilization (CCU) [2][7][8]. This project focuses on a strategy
for carbon utilization, where CO2 is converted to CO through the Reverse Water Gas
Shift (RWGS) reaction, after which the generated CO is used in the Hydroformylation
(HyFo) reaction of 1-Butene to aldehydes. The following two subsections will therefore
give an introduction to each of these reactions.
2.2 Reverse Water Gas Shift (RWGS) Reaction
The RWGS reaction is a reversible reaction, in which CO2 and H2 react to form CO and
H2O, as shown in the following [7]:
CO2 + H2 ⇀
↽CO + H2O, ∆H°(298K) = 41 kJ/mol
(1)
The produced CO is very attractive, as it together with H2 constitutes the synthesis gas
(syngas) that is used as feedstock in many industrial reactions, including both in the
production of fuels through the Fischer-Tropsch (F-T) synthesis and in the production of
aldehydes through the HyFo reaction [7] [9] [10] [11].
While ∆H° for the RWGS reaction is greater than 0, the reaction is said to be endother-
mic. In practice, this means that high temperatures are usually required in order to obtain
high conversions of CO2 [12] [13]. Obviously there is a desire to conduct the RWGS re-
action at lower temperatures to increase safety, and to decrease process costs, but also
to enhance the compatibility and integration with syngas consuming reactions that are
exothermic and conducted at lower temperatures [14].
The role of catalysts in the proposed carbon utilization strategy is fundamental, while
catalysts are assigned as the main driving force for the conversion of CO2 to chemicals,
and for the execution of endothermic reactions at lower temperatures [1]. This report will
therefore investigate different catalyst types, and potential low temperature catalysts for
the conversion of CO2 to CO.

This project will also examine the equilibrium behaviour of the RWGS reaction, in par-
ticular the equilibrium conversion of CO2 to CO and how this is affected by temperature,
through simulation. Results of the simulation will be compared to the experimental re-
sults of different catalysts and reaction conditions to evaluate the different systems.
The aim is to produce a sufficient amount of CO during the RWGS reaction. A CO
consuming reaction, i.e. the HyFo reaction, will be performed in the same reactor. This
should shift the reaction towards the desired direction and drive the proposed carbon
utilization strategy to success. The next section will therefore delve further into the HyFo
reaction.
2.6 Hydroformylation (HyFo) Reaction
The aim of the HyFo reaction in this study is to use the CO that is produced during the
RWGS reaction to obtain desired products. The HyFo term consists of ”Hy”, representing
hydrogen, and of ”Fo”, representing the formyl group. Both of these groups are added
to the double bound of an alkene, when the olefin is reacted with CO and H2. For most
olefins the product mixtures constitute of branched- and linear aldehydes. However, the
product mixtures can also include cis- and trans alkenes from isomerisation reactions,
linear alifatic products from hydrogenation reactions, and aldols from aldol condensation
reactions. Selectivity is therefore a very important parameter in the HyFo reaction [9].
This project will investigate the HyFo reaction of 1-Butene to n-Pentanal and 2-Methyl
butyraldehyde. n-Pentanal is in significantly higher demand than 2-Methyl butyralde-
hyde, as it can be further converted into acids, alcohols, and esters, which are being used
to produce plasticizers, perfumes, surfactants, and solvents [15]. The regioselectivity, i.e.
the ratio between the two aldehydes is therefore another very important parameter in the
HyFo reaction.

Co- and Rh-complexes are among the most used catalysts for the HyFo reaction [9]. Es-

The catalyst used by Madani et al. is set as starting point for the carbon utilization
strategy in this project. However, a well known problem with phosphite ligands is their
water sensitivity. The RWGS reaction is producing water, and a phosphite ligands might
therefore not be the best ligand choice in this project, while the aim is to conduct the
two reactions within the same reactor. For this reason, we instead look at phosphine
ligands, which are not sensitive to water. In the literature it has been reported that the
highest linear aldehyde selectivities are achieved when using bisequatorial coordinating
ligands, and that diphosphine ligands with a bite angle of around 110 °C promote the
best coordination of the ligand to the transition metal [19]. Bis(diphenylphosphino)-9,9-
dimethylxanthine (Xantphos) is a bisequatorial coordinating phosphine ligand with a wide
bite angle making it a good ligand candidate for this project [20].

2.3 Different Types of Catalysts
The current catalyst market features a broad range of different catalysts that vary in
terms of size, shape, structure, composition, form, and phase in which they can be em-
ployed. Optimization of these parameters is very important in order to obtain a catalyst
with the desired abilities. More about this will be provided later in the section on cata-
lyst design. Instead we now look at the differences that separate the catalysts. Based on
structure and surrounding of employment catalysts can be divided into four different cat-
egories (homogeneous-, heterogeneous-, heterogenized homogeneous-, and bio catalysts)
[25]. Figure 4 summarizes pros and cons of the first three mentioned types [26]:
Figure 4: Advantages and disadvantages of homogeneous, heterogeneous, and heterogenized ho-
mogeneous catalysts. The figure has been redrawn and modified from [26]. Created with Bioren-
der.com [5].
In homogeneous catalysis, the catalyst is in the same phase as the reactants [25]. Advan-
tages of homogeneous catalyst include high activity, high selectivity, and the possibility of
conducting the reaction at mild conditions [27]. The biggest disadvantage of homogeneous
catalysis is the difficult separation of products from the reaction mixture [28].
In heterogeneous catalysis, the catalyst is in a different phase than the reactants [25].
Advantages of heterogeneous catalysts include complete recovery, and easy separation of
products from the reaction mixture, while low activity and low selectivity are the cons [29].

Many strategies have therefore been tested in order to combine the advantages of homo-
geneous- and heterogeneous catalysts. One of these is the heterogenization of homoge-

2.4 Supported Liquid Phase (SLP) Catalysts
Supported liquid phase catalysts are systems in which the catalytic compound, e.g. a
metal precursor is dissolved in a liquid phase and distributed on the surface of a porous
solid support. It can be said that such systems are both homogeneous and heterogeneous,
depending on the point of view, as shown on Figure 5. In this way, it becomes possible to
recover the catalyst completely and separate the catalyst from the products easily. Last
but not least, all of these advantages are obtained without compromising on activity and
selectivity [16][31]. Figure 5 shows the structure and concept of SLP catalysts [32][33][34]:
Figure 5: Structure and concept of a supported liquid phase (SLP) catalysts. The figure was
redrawn from [32], [33], and [34]. Created with Biorender.com [5].
Different liquid phases are possible for SLP systems. Madani et al. had success catalyzing
the HyFo reaction of 1-Butene at 120 °C through the use of a SLP catalyst featuring a
liquid phase based on Sebacate, as described earlier [16]. However, a good catalyst system
for the RWGS needs to be able to catch and activate CO2. It is encouraging to see that
Yasuda et al. have had success performing the RWGS reaction at 170 °C through the use
of a supported ionic liquid phase (SILP) catalyst [32]. The next sections will therefore
look further into SILP catalysis.

2.5 Supported Ionic Liquid Phase (SILP) Catalysts
SILP catalysts are SLP catalysts with the liquid phase being constituted by ILs. Most
often, ILs are defined as salts that melt below 100 °C, while the electrostatic forces be-
tween the anions and the cations are weak [35] [36].
Properties of ILs were originally described as air and water stable, non-flammable, and
nonvolatile. However due to the recent discovery of thousands of cations and anions that
satisfies the broad definition of ILs, the family now also includes compounds that are
flammable, volatile and unstable [37]. With that being said, the diversity of the current
IL family also opens up for a broad range of applications [37].
Traditionally, the non-flammable and nonvolatile ILs were used as green solvents instead
of conventional organic solvents. One of the newer applications of ILs include CO2 cap-
ture [38]. The mechanism of the CO2 capture depends on the type - and the structure
of the IL. Imidazolium based ILs have high CO2 solubility making them suitable for CO2
capture [39].
ILs based on imidazolium absorb CO2 mainly through their unique structure and through
intermolecular interactions such as van der Waals forces and hydrogen bonding [35]. In
a study conducted by Cadena et al. it was found both experimentally and through sim-
ulations that the anion has the biggest impact on the CO2 solubility. The simulations
revealed that the CO2 molecules surround the anions in a manner that maximizes fa-
vorable interactions. Experimentally it was found that for the same anion and different
cations, the CO2 solubilities are pretty similar, which emphasizes the importance of the
anion and proposes that the interaction between anion and CO2 is key for the CO2 cap-
ture [39].

IL seems promising for capturing CO2, despite the fact that different mechanisms have

RWGS reaction at temperatures below 200 °C [32].
Especially, transition metal complexes with pincer ligands have gained a lot of attention
recently, as these have shown great performance with regards to selectivity, long-term
stability, and as these can function well under mild conditions with small amounts of
catalyst used [42].
A pincer complex is a compound that can be described by the formula [2, 6−(ECH2)2C6H3]−
(ECE). E is a neutral two electron donor, while C represents an anionic aryl carbon of
the 2,6-disubstituted phenyl ring. The center is constituted by a transition metal, and
its properties are controlled through chelation, i.e. the binding of ligand to the metal
through two or more bonds. The metal center of pincer complexes does retain their great
properties in this way, while the pincer ligand makes the complex very stable [43].
Starting with the ECE mode, where E=N,P, the field of pincer ligands have gone through
a fast development, and today several other coordinations exists, including ENE where
E=C,S,Se,P, PCN and YNX, where Y=C,N,O, X=O,S [44][45]. Especially PNP is receiv-
ing a lot of attention these days, while it contains an amino group that together with the
metal center operates on the activation of H2 [41] .
Examples of PNP-pincer complexes are Ru-MACHO and Ru-MACHO-BH. The combi-
nation of a stable type of ligand and the H2 activating ability are the qualities that make

2.7 Catalyst Design
Catalyst design is a long and dynamic process. It usually starts with a need from the
industry to optimize an already existing catalyst or to develop a new catalyst. The design
process then proceeds with the discovery and selection of components for the catalyst,
such as the support material and the metal precursor. The support is characterized to
make sure that it possesses the desired properties. The catalyst is then synthesized, and
activity tested. The results from the characterization and activity testing phases are then
evaluated in order to optimize the previous steps, i.e. the component selection and the
synthesis. In this way, catalyst design relies strongly on the interaction between synthesis,
characterization, and activity testing, which can be seen on Figure 9:

2.7.1 Synthesis
The easiest and most popular way of synthesizing supported catalysts is through impreg-
nation, in which a solution containing the precursor is added to the support, after which
the following three steps are performed: aging, drying, and calcination [49].
Two impregnation modes exists. One is incipient wetness impregnation, where the vol-
ume of solution does not exceed the pore volume of the support material. Another is wet
impregnation, where the volume of the solution exceeds the pore volume of the support
material [49].
The support type, and the posttreatment conditions including atmosphere, rate of heating,
time, and final temperature are important parameters that can be tailored in order to
achieve the desired catalyst features [49].
2.7.2 Characterization
Especially supports are very important to characterize when developing SLP / SILP cat-
alysts, since a porous support is required, and since it is important to know the pore
volume when designing experiments, i.e. in our case when determining how much liquid
phase to use. This is important in order to avoid overfilling the pores and leaching.
There is not a single characterization method that can characterize the entire range of
pores sizes found in supports. Mercury intrusion porosimetry (MIP) is therefore used for
pores between 3 nm and 360 µm. This technique covers areas of the meso pore region
(2-50 nm) and the macro pore region (>50 nm). N2-Physiosorption is on the other hand
used for pores between 0.35 nm and 300 nm. This technique covers areas of the micro
pore region (< 2 nm), the meso pore region, and the macro pore region [50] [51]. In the
following, we will elaborate each of these characterization tools. We will also take a look
at scanning electron microscopy with energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (SEM-EDX),
which is used to analyze the morphology of a sample.
2.7.2.1 N2-Physisorption
Adsorption of nitrogen at 77 K (-196.15 °C) was first performed by Brunauer and Emmett.
It is a popular method for determining the surface area and pore size distribution of porous
materials. The physiosorption experiments are conducted at constant temperature and
includes finding an adsorption isotherm [52]:

As mention earlier, MIP also reveals information on pore sizes. MIP is a techniqe where

2.7.2.3 Scanning Electron Microscopy with Energy Dispersive X-ray Spec-
troscopy (SEM-EDX)
SEM is a technique that is broadly used in various scientific fields to analyze and observe
surfaces of organic- and inorganic materials. SEM uses an electron beam to irradiate the
sample surface. When the beam hits the sample surface, several signals are generated.
Some of these are backscatter electrons, secondary electrons, X-rays, Auger electrons and
photons that have different energies. The first two mentioned signals are very important,
while they differ depending on the surface topographies, which enables high-resolution im-
ages to be generated. When the beam interacts with the sample, X-rays are also emitted.
With an energy-dispersive X-ray (EDX) detector, it is possible to analyze characteristics
of the X-rays to get compositional information on the sample [54][55].

is higher than the bite angle of DPPE.

5. Conclusions and Future Perspectives
The integration of RWGS and HyFo processes for 1-Butene conversion into aldehydes was
investigated in this project through the use of SLP/SILP catalyst systems based on Ru
and Rh metals.
The conducted simulation study on the RWGS reaction revealed an equilibrium CO2 con-
version of 3.41 % at 150 °C, and of 2.66 % at 130 °C.
Introductory RWGS experiments with SLP catalysts containing different metal precur-
sors (Ru2Cl4(CO)6, Ru-MACHO, and Ru-MACHO-BH) and a Sebacate liquid phase did
not produce CO, while experiments with IL (EMIM Ac) and the metal precursors Ru-
MACHO and Ru-MACHO-BH did. It was found that the conversion drops significantly
when decreasing the temperature from 150 °C to 130 °C. An optimization study was
therefore performed in order to compensate for the low temperature, and to increase the
compatibility with the HyFo reaction. It was found that the CO2 conversion increased
when increasing the wt.% of IL from 5 to 10, when using Ru-MACHO-BH instead of
Ru-MACHO, when increasing the SILP loading to the autoclave reactor from 1.5 g to 3
g, and when changing the CO2:H2 ratio from 1:3 to 1:1. Lastly, the metal content screen-
ing revealed an optimal wt.% of Ru around 0.055. Combining all these features resulted
in a CO2 conversion of 6.3 % at 150 °C. In addition, the optimized system did yield a
conversion of 0.295 % at 130 °C. The conversion obtained at 150 °C is higher than the
equilibrium CO2 conversion, and could potentially be attributed to trapping of CO2 in
the IL and an alternative reaction path, involving the initial formation of formic acid, and
the subsequent decomposition of the formic acid to CO and H2O [41][48]. Calculations
of trapping in IL shows that a small amount of CO2 is trapped, and this explanation
therefore seems less significant.
HyFo experiments with SLP catalysts based on the the Rh(acac)(CO)2 metal precursor,
a Sebacate liquid phase, and different ligands (DPPE and Xantphos) revealed a trade-
off between conversion and selectivity near conditions of the RWGS reaction. The SLP
catalyst based on DPPE showed low conversion but high selectivity towards the linear
aldehyde, n-pentanal, while the SLP catalyst based on Xantphos showed improved con-
version, but decreased selectivity towards n-pentanal, and an increased promotion of the
isomerization reaction, near conditions of the RWGS reaction. It is important to note

that industrial feeds do contain more z-Butene than 1-Butene, and it would therefore be
interesting to test the investigated catalysts with such mixtures.
It was decided to proceed with a phosphite ligand for the combined RWGS and HyFo
experiment, despite the fact that these ligands are known to be sensitive towards water.
A high conversion was obtained in the combined experiment, but the selectivity towards
aldehydes was very low, while the isomerisation selectivity was high but similar to normal
system, and the hydrogenation selectivity was much higher than expected.
Nonetheless, we did manage to produce a little bit of the linear aldehyde, and this could
therefore be a proof of concept. However, further studies must be done in order to confirm
this, as the conversion could be due to the reaction itself or residues in the pipes. Even
though we might have a proof of concept, n-Butane/n-Pentanal ratios above 500 indicate
that the integration of the two reactions in a continuous flow reactor is far from success.
A two step strategy (production of CO in one reactor, and downstream use in a second
reactor) therefore seems more viable.
The findings of the combined experiment also indicate that we did not form enough CO.
The time study performed for the RWGS reaction revealed that the conversion scales
almost linearly with time. Most of the RWGS experiments were conducted with a resi-
dence time of 24 h, while the residence time in the continuous flow experiments was much
lower. In the future, it could therefore be interesting to investigate the execution of the
two reactions in a batch reactor for 24 h with the aim of producing more CO for the
HyFo reaction. As a last experiment, this was tried in this project, but did not yield very
promising results. However this was only a first attempt, and there might therefore still
be something to come after.
