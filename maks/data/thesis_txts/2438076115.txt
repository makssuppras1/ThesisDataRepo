Abstract
The aim of this study is to investigate a non-ideal mass-fractional uptake response
measured in a PVDF polymer. The study is based on experimental data published by
Von Solms in 2005 [5]. Various polymeric behaviours have been simulated in Comsol and
collated with the experimental data. The results vary according to which models have
been applied; a concentration dependent diﬀusion, a concentration dependent skin eﬀect,
a variable surface concentration and a variable surface concentration combined with a
concentration independent skin eﬀect.
A phenomenological approach was used in deriving the models, and some of the models
are more physically viable than others. The results show that the most promising models
are the variable surface concentration or the variable surface concentration combined
with a concentration independent skin eﬀect. This study concludes that these two models
are most likely to explain the non-ideal behaviour of the experimental data.
Keywords: Fick’s Diffusion, non-ideal, skin effect, surface concentration, modelling, Simulation

1
Why go Flexible?
This section will explain the features of the ﬂexible oil pipe and how it aﬀects the
installation of the subsurface ﬂow system. Flexible pipes are larger, heavier and much
more expensive compared to the rigid steel pipes. But the features of the ﬂexible pipe
do, in some applications, outweigh the additional expense.
1.1
Subsurface Flow System
The subsurface ﬂow system is essential to the production of hydrocarbons. It connects
the wellheads, and facilitates the extraction and injection of well ﬂuids. The system as a
whole is designed with many features in mind:
 Safe containment of well ﬂuids
 Facilitate the production over the lifetime of the well
 Flow assurance (Prevent wax- and hydrate plugs)
 Facilitate completion and workover of the well. [1]
The system is split up into two systems, the seabed infrastructure and the riser section.
The seabed infrastructure ties the production wells together with an optional subsurface
production system and the riser system. It can be necessary to pre-process the well ﬂuids
before raising them to the surface. This is done in order to prevent the formation of
hydrate and wax plugs in the system. Furthermore it can be economically beneﬁcial to
only transport the most valuable ﬂuids to the surface.
The formation of wax- and hydrate plugs can be problematic in a ﬂow system as they
prevent the ﬂow. Wax plugs form from small amounts of wax paraﬃns in the oil. They
precipitate from the oil under certain high pressure and low temperature conditions.
Hydrate plugs are ice-like solids that consist of water and natural gas, which similarly
combine under high pressure and low temperature conditions. A common way to prevent
these plugs from forming, is to keep the high pressure sections of the ﬂow system at
an elevated temperature.
Another preventive measure is to add wax- and hydrate
inhibitors. Flexible piping has an inherent insulating eﬀect in the form of many layers
in its composition. For some pipes, an optional layer of insulation may be added. The
insulation helps to maintain the temperature and ensure the ﬂow.

1.2
Riser System
In the subsurface ﬂow system the seabed infrastructure is immobilized and only exposed
to drag by the ocean current. In contrast the riser system connects to the surface and
is subject to a lot of external forces from waves, currents and vessel displacement. The
riser system conﬁguration can be categorized into four groups:
 Free Standing Riser (FSR)
 Top Tensioned Riser (TTR)
 Catenary Riser
 Hybrid Riser
The FSR and TTR are both built with rigid steel piping, and are subject to the material
behaviour of the steel. The FSR is under compression over its entire length and the TTR
is under tension over the entire length. These risers can to some extent compensate for
lateral movements by absorbing tension. Extra systems have to be installed in order
to compensate for the vertical perturbations from waves, which can take up valuable
topside space.
The Catenary Riser covers a group of risers that are suspended in a catenary arc shape
from the topside to the sea ﬂoor (see ﬁgure 30 in appendix C). The shape absorbs vertical
motions and only transfers a minimum of deformation to the take-oﬀpoint at the sea
ﬂoor. The catenary riser can be constructed in both steel tubing (SCR) and ﬂexible
tubing. Steel tubing can be used when the depth allows a large enough catenary radii.
SCR is the most widely used riser conﬁguration, however it is prone to fatigue and joint
failure.
A hybrid conﬁguration is a combination of rigid and ﬂexible tubing (see ﬁgure 31 in
appendix C). The majority of the riser’s length is installed as a TTR. A buoy below,
but close to, the surface pulls the steel riser taught. Flexible tubing, known as a jumper,
is installed from the TTR to the surface. This system utilizes cheaper steel tubing for
the majority of the riser’s length and a short section of ﬂexible tubing in the form of a
jumper, enabling it to compensate for wave motion and lateral displacement.
1.3
Flexible versus Rigid
The previous subsection has given an insight into the behaviour of rigid tubing and
ﬂexible tubing in a riser system. The ﬂexible pipe boast other beneﬁts compared to the
rigid steel tubing besides its ﬂexibility. The high quality of materials and its ease of
installation yields secondary economic beneﬁts which will be described below.
1.3.1
Corrosive Resistance
The ﬂexible pipes are made from stainless steel. Stainless steel is resistant to corrosive
ﬂuids and gasses that are present in the well ﬂuids. This gives a longer lifespan compared

to regular steel tubing.
The rigid steel tubing is built in a lower quality steel, which is more susceptible to
corrosion. The lifespan of both types of tubing is dependent on the amount of corrosive
chemicals that they have been exposed to.
1.3.2
Installation
Installing ﬂexible pipes is faster and simpler than its rigid counterpart. A specialized ship
(see ﬁgure 32 in appendix C) can unspool a prefabricated length of pipe at the location.
The pipe is tested before delivery, and no joints need to be welded oﬀ-shore.
In a rigid installation, sections of pipe need to be welded oﬀ-shore which requires longer
valuable oﬀ-shore time to weld and quality check.
1.3.3
Reusable
When a steel tube bends it will pivot around the centreline. This sets one side of the
tube under compression and the other half under tension creating metal fatigue that
will reduce the lifespan of the tubing. It also leads to one of the disadvantages for rigid
steel tubing – it is not reusable. Due to the metal fatigue, the rigid riser has to be
decommissioned after use.
In contrast the ﬂexible pipe bends more freely by rearranging the steel ﬁbres in its mantle.
Bending will not deform the steel hence it is not to the same degree prone to metal
fatigue. This property allows for the pipes to be coiled up and reused at another site.

1.3.5
Global Outlook for Flexible Solutions
The world’s energy consumption is predicted to rise. From 2015 to 2040 estimates predict
a 28% increase in energy consumption, from 575 quadrillion BTU1 to 736 quadrillion
BTU in 2040[3]. The crude oil and lease condensate production is predicted to increase
from 73 million b/d (2012) to 100 million b/d (2040) ([3] ﬁg 2-10).
It is a general conception that the easiest accessible oilﬁelds have been discovered. The
future of oil is found in enhanced recovery2 of present oil ﬁelds, and from development of
more complicated and less accessible ﬁelds.
With increased demand it also becomes more economically feasible to produce from
smaller ﬁelds. Smaller ﬁelds have shorter lifespans and re-usability of the ﬂow system
thus becomes an important factor.
The high re-usability of ﬂexible piping may better accommodate needs of future ﬂow
systems. It is predicted that the compounded annual growth rate of the ﬂexible market
is 3% up to 2020 [4].
1.4
Flexible Conduit Requirements
For the remainder of the report the focus will lie on the ﬂexible pipe, the environment it
is installed in, and the requirements of the pipe during the ﬂow of oil.
The development of deeper oilﬁelds in the future entails higher pressure and temperature
conditions3 under which the ﬂexible tubing will have to operate. The mechanical proper-
ties of the pipe should be able to withstand internal pressure above 1000 atmospheres,
and temperatures as high as 430 Kelvin. It should also be able to withstand the external
pressure of the sea in the scale of 300 atmospheres4. The ﬂexible pipe relies on an combi-
nation of steel ﬁbres supporting the polymer liners in order to fulﬁl these requirements.
The composition of the pipe is further explained in section 1.4.1.
From a chemical point of view the pipe has to eﬀectively contain the oil. The chemical
composition of oil consists of a distribution of oil fractions as well as a fraction of car-
bon dioxide (CO2) and sometimes a fraction of hydrogen sulphide (H2S). The lightest
fractions of the oil known as petroleum gasses (C1-C4), together with H2S and CO2,
are small molecules. These small molecules are highly mobile and diﬃcult to contain.
The ﬂexible pipe relies on its inner liner as a barrier to contain the oil and all of its
components. Careful material engineering has gone into producing the perfect polymer
for this barrier, which will be further explained in section 1.5.

1.4.1
Flexible Pipe - Layer Composition
To meet the requirements described above the ﬂexible pipe is composed of both liner and
steel layers, each with a speciﬁc contribution to the overall performance. Every layer has
to be ﬂexible, which is why polymers are used as liners even though they are not ideal
under HPHT conditions. The steel layers are designed as interlocking strands so they
can rearrange themselves freely during ﬂexing. The layers are explained and illustrated
on the next page.

The layers are listed from the inside out5.
 The Carcass prevents the pipe from collapsing due to outer pressure at seabed
conditions. It is created by folding the metal into a continuously interlocking
S-shapes. The layer is ﬂexible and the structure is similar to the chromate steel
tubes often used for shower-tubes in homes.
 The Inner Liner is an advanced polymer that is melted onto the carcass. It acts as
a barrier that contains the oil. The oil exerts pressure on the inside of the inner
liner.
 The Pressure Armour has the opposite function of the Carcass.
It serves to
withstand the inner pressure by supporting the inner liner.
It is constructed
with two layers of steel strands, with a ∁-shape, facing each other and thereby
interlocking. Together with the inner liner and carcass, the pipe can withstand
internal and external pressure, but it is unable to support its own weight lengthwise.
 The Tensile Armour carries the weight of the pipe itself. For the biggest pipes,
the weight reaches ∼500 kg/m. The tensile armour is a double layer made of
rectangular steel strands, that are preformed into a helix shape, and wound around
the pipe. The layers are wound in opposite directions so the unwinding forces
cancel each other out.
 The Insulation is an optional layer that can be installed for ﬂow assurance. The
pipe is operational from −40 ◦C to 130 ◦C dependent on the insulation. It consists
of polymer bands infused with hollow glass pearls, that are wound around the pipe.
 The Outer Liner is the ﬁnal layer. It is a polymer that is moulded onto the pipe
and its purpose is to prevent seawater from entering the pipe and corroding the
outer layers.
 Intermediate layers of kevlar tape between the tensile armour are installed in order
to prevent birdcaging. The pipe is not designed to be under lengthwise compression.
Should it happen, the Carcass and pressure armour will compress, and the tensile
armour will separate and bulge out like a lantern or ”birdcage”. Kevlar reinforced
tape is used as an attempt to prevent this from happening.

1.5
Inner Liner
The inner liner, as previously mentioned , is required to contain the components of the
oil. Whereas the denser fractions of the oil are easily contained, the petroleum gasses
H2S and CO2 are very volatile and can to a higher degree penetrate the polymer. In
addition, CO2 is often in its supercritical stage under common production conditions.
CO2 in its supercritical stage is a very good solvent, but diﬃcult to contain.
1.5.1
Polymer
Polymers are, among other things, used in the gas separation industry which relies on gas
diﬀusing through a polymer membrane. Gasses are thus able to diﬀuse through polymers,
even though the polymers appear solid. Why then, is it advantageous to utilise polymers
as a barrier in a pipe that transports gas? The answer is that the liner needs to be ﬂexible,
and a polymer is the most promising material for this application so far. However, it is
essential that one understands what a polymer is, and how its barrier properties can be
improved, in order to lengthen the lifespan of the pipes. To understand why the gas can
diﬀuse through the polymer, one has to understand the molecular geometry of a polymer.
A polymer molecule is a long strand of monomers. It will be diﬀerent monomers de-
pendent on the type of polymer produced. The common conception is that these long
polymer strands act like a bowl of spaghetti. The polymer has its solid properties from
the intertwining of the strands, but it also has a large void volume. The polymer strands
have some mobility and can be rearranged giving the polymer ﬂexibility. This concept is
further explained in section 4.2
In perspective, the molecular scale of a metal such as pure iron is arranged as a crystal
lattice of iron atoms. They sit tight in their lattice and have little void volume with
almost no mobility. This is not to say that nothing can diﬀuse through iron. Carburizing6
relies on carbon gas diﬀusing into mild steel.
1.6
Issues Arising from Diﬀusion
For the engineer, the issue with the inner liner is that the volatile gasses diﬀuse through
the liner and occupy the void space in the pressure- and tensile armour. CO2 is an acidic
gas that corrode the steel and H2S is a particular degradative gas due to the eﬀect of
hydrogen embrittlement.
The lifespan of the tensile- and pressure armour is then dependent on the exposure to
these gasses. It is imperative to know how high the exposure is in order to correct the
lifespan expectancy. The hazard is that the pipes will break long before expected and
cause an oil spill. The life expectancy is also used to predict when to issue a replacement.
The pre-emptive replacement ensures the ﬂow, and keeps the down-time to a minimum.

2
Scope of the Thesis
Through the layer composition of the ﬂexible pipe it is seen how the barrier properties
of the inner liner is correlated with the life expectancy. Ideally the degradative gasses
should be contained in the core of the pipe but they are present in the intermediate
layers.
Present diﬀusion theory has been applied to the issue in order to predict the ﬂow of gas
through the inner liner. Full sized experiments have been conducted but the results of
those experiments do not agree with the diﬀusion theory. In reality the ﬂow is measured
in full scale to be signiﬁcantly higher than theoretically predicted.
Laboratory experiments [5] have been conducted on small discs cut out of the inner liner.
The data from these experiments deviates from the theory and suggests that the theory
is not comprehensive enough. Using simulations in Comsol, this thesis will investigate
diﬀerent diﬀusion eﬀects to uncover if they are responsible for the adverse behaviour.
This study will focus not on the discrepancy between the full-scale experiment and the
theoretical ﬂow, but on the non linearity measured in the lab by von Solms [5]. The
investigation of this non-linearity might yield knowledge of the polymer, that can help
adjust the theory to comprehend the full scale measurements. The investigation can also
assist in the design of a new polymer with improved barrier properties.

Litterature Review
Diﬀusion in polymers are important to investigate as it dictates how we should use
polymers for food packaging, gas-separation, coatings, membranes or barriers.
Early experiments on diﬀusion in polymers are published by Richard Barrer in 1939 [6].
He points out that prior investigations in membrane diﬀusion are mainly for technical or
biological purposes. The use of variables is inconsistent and sometimes even mixed up.
Barrer’s study attempts a more scientiﬁc approach to systematize and create a complete
set of data on polymer diﬀusion. He measures the mass ﬂow through various organic
polymer membranes with respect to a pressure diﬀerence over the membrane. Ficks ﬁrst
law[7] was used as the correlation between the permeability and pressure diﬀerence.
Some of his results are plottedas a pressure increase over time [[6], ﬁg. 2]. Barrer assesses
the data by introducing the ”time lag” approach where only the steady state section of the
data is evaluated. Through a mathematical deduction by Daynes [8], Barrer evaluates an
average diﬀusion coeﬃcient with this technique. The time lag Lt is the time of where the
steady state pressure incline is extrapolated to zero (point A in Figure 1). With the thick-
ness of the membrane, l, the average diﬀusion coeﬃcient is evaluated from equation (1).

The crystalline structures were assumed to impede the ﬂow, hence they reduce the
diﬀusivity. Michael & Bixler use the time-lag method, as earlier described, to determine
the diﬀusion coeﬃcient.
It is seen that the time-lag method is widely used as the means of measure. Daynes,
Barrer, Michael & Bixler, and Flaconneh (2001)[15] use the time-lag method. There is
however also an analytical solution derived by Crank (1979) [16] and used by Von Solms
(2004)[17] as another method of determining the diﬀusivity.

4
Theory
The classical theory of Fick’s diﬀusion is used to explain the transport processes in a
disc geometry. The modelling programme; Comsol, obeys Fick’s diﬀusion.
In addition to Fick’s theory this section also explains the diﬀerent properties of a polymer,
and how these properties relate to the transport processes. Understanding the polymer
is essential when the diﬀusion models are modiﬁed. The diﬀerent models in section 1.4.1,
rely on alterations of the transport properties in the polymer. These alterations should
be physically feasible based on the gained knowledge on polymeric behaviour. If the
alterations can be justiﬁed it supports and strengthens the model.
4.1
Diﬀusion Theory
How come when you mix two types of gasses that the heaviest gas of the two does not
arrange itself as a bottom layer – but instead turns into a homogeneous mixture? The
diﬀusivity explains the mixing.

4.2
Polymer
This section will assess the polymer properties that aﬀect the diﬀusion coeﬃcient. As
a general conception, a polymer is understood as a bowl of spaghetti. The ”spaghetti”
can be entangled, ordered or glued together, each eﬀect inherent to the properties of the
polymer.
4.2.1
Glassy Polymers
The physical state of the polymer is an essential property to its implication. It changes
with respect to the temperature being solid at low temperatures, rubbery at medium
temperatures and even liquid at high temperatures.
Figure 9 displays the tensile modulus, and how it changes with temperature. It is seen
that at a speciﬁc threshold temperature, the polymer transitions from a state of high
modulus to a state of low modulus. The polymer’s high modulus state, where it appears
solid, is called the glassy state. It is not a solid state such as a metallic crystal lattice,
but rather a chilled pot of spaghetti. The threshold temperature is referred to as Tg.
Figure 9: Fig. II-8 from [[23], p.31]. Tensile modulus, E, of a polymer with respect to the temperature.
As the temperature surpasses Tg the polymer strands can move and the polymer is
in a rubbery state. Table 2 displays Tg of some of the common polymers.

4.2.2
Free Volume Theory
The free volume approach is a useful concept to describe and understand the diﬀusion of
small molecules through polymers. The concept is that a molecule can only diﬀuse from
one place to another if there is a space of suﬃcient size in the polymer. These spaces
together make up the free volume.
The free volume of a polymer is deﬁned as the volume not occupied by the polymer
macromolecules including their Van Der Waals volume.
The behaviour of the free
volume undergoes a transition at Tg. In the polymer’s glassy state the free volume is
approximately constant, and in the rubbery state the free volume increases (see Figure
10). The rate of which the free volume increases is proportional to the thermal expansion
coeﬃcient of the polymer[[23], p.33].

The rate of which the penetrant diﬀuses through the polymer is dependent on the free

the polymer is greater. On the other hand, the same polymer can seem inaccessible to
the penetrant, if the penetrant is of large molecular size. In general the higher the free
volume is, the higher the mobility, thus the higher the diﬀusion coeﬃcient will be. In an

4.2.3
Segmental Mobility and Plasticizers
”Polymers with low glass transition temperature possess greater segmental mobility and
will have higher diﬀusivity” [24]
As a penetrant diﬀuses through the polymer it can, on a molecular scale, push the
polymer segments aside to ease its path. This maluability of the polymer strands is
referred to as the segment mobility. Polymers can have diﬀerent types of pendant groups
on the backbone which can reduce the mobility of the chain and make it more intertwined.
The pendant groups can in some cases form bonds to other pendant groups, and thereby
link multiple segments together. This eﬀect is referred to as crosslinking.
It is seen how the segment mobility is dependent on temperature, but the saturation
of the polymer also modiﬁes the mobility. Auerbach (1958) [25] has reported a decrease
in diﬀusivity as the polymer becomes saturated. As stated: ”This is because unsaturation
in polymer chains provides for segmental mobility, whereas saturation tends to restrict
it.” [[24] p.987]

The arrangement of the polymer strands is not always random. The strands can be
partially aligned and ordered in a superstructure referred to as a polymer crystallite. How
crystalline regions appear in a polymer is visualized in Figure 11. The polymer displayed
has amorphous and crystalline regions rendering the polymer as a semi-crystalline

Crystallites have considerable inﬂuence on the polymer’s mechanical and transport
properties. The degree of crystallity hardens the polymer and turns it towards its glassy
state. Figure 12 displays how the crystallity prolongs the tensile strength beyond Tg.
If the crystallites are considered somewhat impermeable, the degree of crystallization
proves resistant to the ﬂow, thus decreasing the diﬀusion coeﬃcient [[23], ﬁg. V-28].
Polymers can be crystallized in various ways. During a melt, the polymer can crystallize
by Van der Waals interactions. If the polymer is dissolved in a solution, then it can
crystallize upon evaporation of the solvent. Stretching will also crystallize the polymer,
the strain aligning the strands parallel to the direction of the strain.
4.2.5
Swelling
As a polymer becomes saturated it tends to swell in size. An unconstrained polymer
can freely vary in size dependent on the saturation. If the polymer is constrained it
creates internal stress that manipulates the diﬀusivity. The polymer used in this study is
unconstrained and the concept of swelling is thus not considered further.

4.3
Penetrant
The substance that diﬀuses through- or into a medium is referred to as the penetrant.
The penetrant’s properties are as essential to the diﬀusion coeﬃcient as the medium
itself. As mentioned earlier, the molecular size of the penetrant aﬀects the diﬀusion
coeﬃcient. The smaller the molecules, the easier they propagate, and the higher the
diﬀusion coeﬃcient becomes. If the medium and the penetrant are compatible, the
penetrant can react and bind to the medium which reduces the diﬀusivity.
4.3.1
CO2
In this study CO2 is used as the penetrant. CO2 is unique in many ways. It is solid
when frozen to −80 ◦C at atmospheric pressure – this state is commonly known as dry
ice. In the other end of the scale CO2 is easily brought to its supercritical state (sCO2)
at above 31 ◦C and 72 atm [28] (see ﬁgure 33 in the appendix). These conditions mean
that CO2 is almost exclusively in its supercritical state in the oil production industry.
A substance in its supercritical state is at the same time a ﬂuid and a gas. It ﬁlls a
container like gas, but with the density of a ﬂuid. sCO2 is an excellent solvent with high
diﬀusivity and high mass transport due to its supercritical state.

5
Experimental Data
This section will give a brief overview of how the experimental data was gathered in the
article by von Solms [5]. Further it will enlighten what conditions to apply in the Comsol
simulation. A detailed description of the experimental setup can be found in Von Solms
(2004) [17], whereas the experimental conditions are extracted from Von Solms (2005) [5].
5.1
Experimental Setup
The experiment is enclosed in a chamber able to sustain vacuum or high pressure. Inside
the chamber there is a weighing cell that can measure a sample during the experiment.
The sample is located in a void surrounded by a bath of glycerine. The glycerine bath is
used to control the temperature of the sample by heating and circulating the glycerine.
A gas cylinder is connected to the chamber as well as a vacuum pump. Two probes are
present, one to measure the temperature and one to measure the pressure.
5.2
Experimental Steps
After placing a polymer sample in the cell, the chamber is sealed and submerged in the
glycerine. A vacuum is then applied in order to extract as much atmosphere as possible
from the polymer. The chamber is then rapidly pressurized with the gas of interest
and the sampling begins. Initially the time, weight and temperature is logged every 2
seconds, and after 5 minutes it is then logged every 20 seconds. The experiment is then
left for 30-48 hours to reach equilibrium. The second half of the experiment is a rapid
de-pressurization of the chamber where the desorption of the polymer is logged similarly
to the sorption in the ﬁrst half of the experiment.

Modelling
The modelling is based upon von Solms’ experiment explained in section 5. The simulation

If there is a compliance between Comsol and Matlab it is then assumed that the Comsol

6.1.4
Boundary Conditions and Initial Values
The geometry of the polymer disc is now deﬁned in the model. It is also necessary to
determine the transport properties of the model. This means deﬁning the diﬀusion in
the geometry, and setting up the boundary conditions
Recalling that the polymer disc is suspended in a vacuum before the experiment, it is
assumed that there is no gas in the disc, hence the initial concentration in the domain is
set to zero.
Gas is then quickly introduced to the chamber from a neighbouring cannister and the
initial stage in the experiment is set: the chamber is full of gas at a known concentration,
but no gas has yet diﬀused into the polymer disc. This is modelled by applying a
boundary condition equivalent to the initial concentration of the gas. This boundary
condition is assumed constant during the whole simulation since, as earlier described, the
chamber is much larger than the polymer disc. This boundary condition is manipulated
when other eﬀects are added to the model.
A ﬁnal boundary condition is that there is no outward ﬂux on the surface. This boundary
condition is deﬁned through the observation that the concentration in the chamber always
is higher than the concentration within the disc (during sorption). It is thus impossible for
the gas to diﬀuse out of the disc as it would then contradict the concentration gradient.
6.1.5
Meshing
The meshing of a model is very important and entire studies are developed around
meshing. If you produce an extremely ﬁne mesh, your simulation might be very accurate
and you will have an excellent spatial resolution. But it comes at a cost – the calculation
time for a high resolution mesh is long. Further treatment of the results of the simulation
is even longer for costly operations like integration. So costly, that any standard desktop
will run out of memory in its attempt to crunch the numbers.
There is a need to optimize the mesh and navigate around the high resolution meshes,
but at the same time get accurate results. A simple solution is to reduce the resolution
and get a mesh with fewer cells. One could also chose mesh-types that diﬀerentiate the
resolution of the mesh. That type of meshing requires a good physical understanding of
the model. For example, when modelling pipe-ﬂow it would be more accurate to create a
high resolution in the mesh around corners and other places where the velocity gradient
is large. Another example is the boundary layer mesh that provides a high resolution
on the surface of the model, but a rougher mesh everywhere else. This is ideal when
modelling airﬂows and other ﬂows where you have boundary layers.
The aim of these advanced meshes is to stay accurate whilst reducing the number of cells
in the domain.
The question is how low the resolution of the mesh can be, before the results become
unacceptable. To determine the threshold, a mesh convergence study may be conducted.
In this study, a mesh convergence study is carried out by ﬁtting data that is simulated
with various mesh sizes. The ﬁtted data provides diﬀusion coeﬃcients that change and
converge with respect to the number of cells in the mesh. The result of this process is

6.1.6
Time Frame
When the setup of the model is complete, the next step is to choose what type of study
Comsol should perform. For the simulations in this study, it is interesting to see how the
mass uptake in the polymer behaves over time, so a Time Dependent Study is chosen. In
the time dependent study the duration of the simulation can be chosen, as well as the
size of the time increments.
The time resolution of the simulation is dependent on the mesh size. For a rough mesh
you can get a high time resolution before exceeding an acceptable calculation time and
vice versa. An optimized time resolution can be made so that you diﬀerentiate the time
steps. You can take small steps in the early stage of the simulation and then increase
the time-steps. This yields a lower number of time-frames, yet a high resolution in some
stages of the simulation.
Some of the simulations are compared to experimental data. It then makes it easier to
make the model simulate in the same time steps as that of the experimental data, which
is done for some of the simulations.

6.4.1
Concentration Dependent Diﬀusion
The concentration dependent diﬀusion (CDD) model is based on the observation that
CO2 acts as a plasticizer. CO2 softens the polymer and makes it easier for the penetrant
to manipulate the polymer strands which ultimately increases the diﬀusion.
The hypothesis is that this is the eﬀect responsible for the sudden increase in the slope
of the mass uptake curve in its early stage (see Figure 3).
The CDD is implemented in the Comsol model by rewriting the diﬀusion coeﬃcient in
transport properties into a term that includes the parameter for concentration, c. The new
governing diﬀusion coeﬃcient is described from a phenomenological approach. Equation
(26) is used since it provides the alleged diﬀusion behaviour. For low concentrations
the coeﬃcient equals D0/n, whereas for higher concentrations, approaching the surface
concentration, the diﬀusion becomes D0.

6.4.2
Skin Eﬀect
Another hypothesis is that the responsible phenomena is a kind of skin eﬀect. The skin
theory is backed by Crank & Park [18] and by Crank [16, sec. 9.6]. The approach taken
by Crank & Park is to apply a skin with a diﬀusion coeﬃcient that is greater than that
of the core. This Dskin then decreases as a function of time.
In this case the skin transport eﬀect is applied diﬀerently. The skin will instead inhibit
the diﬀusion in the beginning, and then ”loosen up” and allow the penetrant to ﬂow into
the core of the sample. It is not dependent on the time, but instead governed by the
concentration. This phenomena is modelled with two domains of governing transports.
The external geometry of the sample is kept intact, but internally the sample is divided
into a skinlayer segment and a core segment. The skinlayer is deﬁned as a percentage
depth of the total height of the sample, and it is enclosing the core.
The transport property of the skin layer is similar to equation (26) from section 6.4.1,
but with a much higher n factor. The skin concentration dependent diﬀusion model is
referred to as sCDD.
6.4.3

data. Crank & Park note that prior investigations on the matter all rely on a constant
surface concentration. Crank & Park suggest that the surface concentration can be time-

(27). β is a phenomenological factor that controls how fast the surface concentration
converges towards the ﬁnite concentration; C0.

Likewise, the CDD model does not obtain a good ﬁt due to its linear behaviour. The
lower χ2 value of the CDD model seemingly indicate that the CDD model is preferable
to the disc solution. This would be a misleading conclusion to draw. It is true that
the CDD model ﬁts the experimental data better on average, however as the disc solu-
tion and CDD do not display non-ideal behaviour, it makes little sense to include the
early non-ideal data in the ﬁtting process. Thus a more physically suitable χ2 value
for CDD is 0.638 (The model with n = 1.5), which is roughly the same as the disc solution.
The sCDD model displays the sought after non-ideal response. It results in a reason-
ably good ﬁt, better than the disc- and CDD models. The best ﬁt is achieved with a
2% skin and an inhibiting factor of n = 500. The model is seemingly very insensitive
to the n-factor as Figure 24 shows, and very sensitive to the thickness of the skin as
Figure 23 shows. The notion that the skin is 1/500 of the core seems physically unlikely.
In my opinion the sCDD rather displays a pseudo-VSC. The skin gradually ”opens up”
exposing the core to an increasing concentration, very similar to the VSC. In this case,
the eﬀect is imitated in the few percentage of skin instead.
The literature by Crank & Park [18] does support the concept of a skin eﬀect. The article
investigates a skin with high diﬀusivity, but not a skin with low diﬀusivity as implemented
in this model. I have not come across other articles that mention an inhibiting skin layer
in polymer diﬀusion.
The VSC model provides a good ﬁt with a low χ2 value. It is a simple model with
only one parameter. As stated in section 7.5 by Crank & Park, this model is reasonable.
The sorption of penetrant in the surface rearranges the polymer molecules, thus the
surface concentration increases. Crank & Park refer to the molecules within the surface,
which it could be interpreted as a skin eﬀect. It can then be argued whether sCDD and
VSC models are distinguishable.
If the two eﬀects are indistinguishable it might be better to model the non-linearity as a
VSC due to the simplicity of the model and the goodness of ﬁt.
The combination of sCDD and VSC was an attempt to provoke a dampening inﬂexion
point at ˆt = 0.2. This achieves a reasonable goodness of ﬁt, but considering the complexity
of the model, I ﬁnd the arguments for using this model to be relatively weak. Using the
VSC is supported by the litterature ([18]), however combining these two eﬀects seems to
not have been investigated prior to this study.
The combined eﬀect of VSC and sCID is the model that provides the best χ2 results
compared to the other models. The model does however, have four parameters that can
be tweaked, yielding it a fairly complex model. Unlike sCDD, sCID is similar to the model
of Crank & Park. It has a constant concentration independent diﬀusion that is high in
the skin. The response from sCID alone is assessed in [[18], section 3]. The combination
of the two eﬀects are not investigated by Crank & Park. The reason primarily being that
they wanted to investigate diﬀerent behaviours and keep it as simple as possible.

8.1
Conclusion
If a conclusion were to be drawn it would be that; The eﬀect responsible for the non-ideal
behaviour is due to a variable surface concentration, or a variable surface concentration in
combination with a skin eﬀect. The skin having a diﬀusivity independent of concentration.
The two models, VSC and VSC+sCID, both display features that make them reasonable.
They are both supported by the literature and display a high goodness of ﬁt. VSC is less
complex than VSC+sCID, which is often preferable. However, VSC+sCID displays a
better goodness of ﬁt and the included eﬀects are reasonable.

Improvements and Further Research
In evaluating the methodology of this study some improvements to the modelling and
programming processes will be suggested, which could provide for a more eﬃcient
experimental process.
The Comsol model could be improved through a more extensive mesh study. The mesh
which was used was a pre-setting with a laminar boundary layer and triangular cells.
Alternative meshes could have been investigated. For example, it is possible to mesh
with squared cells or other shapes. These shapes could possibly produce an accurate
result at a lower cell number and improve the eﬀectiveness of the simulation.
The basis for this thesis is the classical diﬀusion theory of Fick. Thus the analytical
solutions and simulations are derived from Fick’s laws. Another perspective would be to
use the Maxwell-Stefan diﬀusion theory [30] to describe the problem. Maxwell-Stefan
diﬀusion is more complex and it is often used in multicomponent diﬀusion. A comparison
between Fick’s diﬀusion and Maxwell-Stefan diﬀusion might have yielded some results or
even supported each other in the proposal of a surface/skin eﬀect in the experimental
data.
Another matter that would be interesting to investigate would be to change the governing
equations regarding the CDD- and VSC model. The rate of which the diﬀusion coeﬃcient
changes in the CDD model is governed by equation (26), however the conﬁguration of the
equation can be changed and still fulﬁl its boundary conditions. For example, another
version could be a CDD governed by a squared concentration dependency as in equation

F
Investigation of Summation Length
The analytical solution based on equation (20) is dependent on a summation. The exact
result is achieved by an inﬁnite sum which is impossible. This investigation show that
the sum does not have to be inﬁnite but can be assumed correct with far fewer iterations.
To determine how few iterations is acceptable a criteria is deﬁned: n has to be of a value
that yields a simulation with 99.9% of the correct value.
The correct value is known at one time, which is that it has to be zero. The value of
the analytical solution ranges from 1 to zero, thus a n is chosen so that the value of the
Matlab solution yields 0.001 at t = 0.
Through manual testing with diﬀerent n, the value of n is then found to be around 200.
