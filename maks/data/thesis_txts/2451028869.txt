Abstract
Load Analysis in Waked Turbine during Active Wake Steering
Diﬀerent studies are shown that fatigue loads under wake operation present an impor-
tant increase that can not be ignored. The aim of this project is investigate how the
incoming wakes aﬀects the fatigue loads in a wind turbine. The relationship is investi-
gated using models with diﬀerent ﬁdelity, allowing the quantiﬁcation of the accuracy of
the lower-ﬁdelity models compared to the high-ﬁdelity models. Aeroelatic simulations
are carried out on HAWC2 for the NREL 5 MW reference wind turbine. To recreate a
wake condition, diﬀerent turbulence boxes are created that contain the main parameters
to describe the speciﬁc wake condition: wind deﬁcit at diﬀerent positions and the stan-
dard deviation across the time series. Two models with low and medium ﬁdelity were
develop. The results of the aeroelastic simulations were compared against the fatigue
loads from a high-ﬁdelity model which consist of turbulence boxes based on LES simu-
lations for the NREL 5MW reference wind turbine. All the results were analyzed with
the help of diﬀerent wake metrics developed throughout the project that facilitate the
analysis of the diﬀerent sensors. In general, the three models present good agreement
for certain sensors, as was the case for the blade root moment fatigue loads, estimating
the trends and the values for the fatigue loads. For others, as the tower top tilt and yaw
moments and the tower bottom fore-aft and side-side moments, the medium and low
ﬁdelity models do not capture the behavior of the fatigue loads and underestimate the
values, especially under partial or full wake, due to the absence of the wake meandering
phenomenon on these low ﬁdelity models. Finally, an increase in the great majority
of the sensors and cases can be seen when the wind turbine is operating under waked
conditions.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Over the last decades, renewable energies have had a high development, driven by the
necessity to meet long-term climate and other sustainability goals. However, it is re-
quired that this development is accelerated to meet the goals requires for a sustainable
future [EIA (2018)].
Wind energy is the renewable energy source with the highest global installed capacity
worldwide, reaching 597 GW, where 50,1 GW was just added during 2018 [WWEA
(2019)]. In order to make wind energy more competitive and attractive, reduce the cost
of wind-generated electricity is extremely important. For this, one of the main strategies
is to install wind turbines in groups, since this will allow sharing infrastructure.
A wind turbine is designed to extract energy from the wind and from this energy produce
usable energy (e.g electrical, mechanical).
In broad terms, a wind turbine extracts
kinetic energy from the wind, leaving less amount of kinetic energy downstream due to
the conservation of energy. The energy extraction generates changes in the structure of
the wind ﬂow by reducing the wind velocity and creating small turbulence structures
that will increase turbulence intensity downstream [R´ethor´e (2009)].
This phenomenon is known as wind turbine wake, which is one of the most important
aspects of wind power meteorology because they decrease power production and increase
the loading of downstream wind turbines [Kim et al. (2018)].
As was already mentioned, the downstream wind turbines observe a wind inﬂow mod-
iﬁed both in terms of mean velocity and turbulence. These wind ﬂuctuations increase
the structural vibrations of the wind turbine, which cause increased fatigue loads. The
fatigue loads reduce the wind turbine lifespan and can potentially increase the mainte-
nance cost [R´ethor´e (2009)].

Wakes eﬀects can reduce power production on a wind farm by 10% to 20% [Barthelmie
et al. (2007)] and produce an increase in lifetime damage between 2.5% to 104% due to
the presence of the neighboring wind turbines depending on the components [Karlina-
Barber et al. (2016)]. Other studies suggest that the increase in turbulence intensity can
increase fatigue loads by up to 15% in comparison with the freestream case [Thomsen
and Sørensen (1999)]. However, aerodynamic processes and loading eﬀects are still up
to a degree uncertain [Damiani et al. (2017)].
In order to maximize power while minimizing loads on a wind turbine, diﬀerent wind
farm control strategies have been studied, such as wake steering, where the upstream
turbine are purposely misaligned in order to deﬂect the wake from the downstream
turbine.
This can be accomplished by yaw misalignment and tilt angle adjustment,
which was studied through simulations in SOWFA in [Fleming (2014)].
From these techniques, an improvement in the net power production due to the higher
wind speed for the downstream turbine can be obtained.
However, the downstream
turbine will also likely to operate under partial wake conditions, which could lead to an
increase of fatigue for certain components. This leads to the conundrum of increasing
power production while taking the risk of accelerated machine damage and associated
repair cost [Damiani et al. (2017)].
Other control technique is individual pitch control for load reduction, studied in [Bossanyi
(2003)], where it was shown that a very signiﬁcant reduction in operational loading can
be achieved through individual pitch action.
In this project, a load analysis in waked turbine during active wake steering is carried
out, where the eﬀects on the fatigue loads under wake eﬀects are investigated using
models with diﬀerent ﬁdelity.
This is carried out through simulations in the aeroelastic code HAWC2 [HAWC2 (2019)]
where the diﬀerent wake scenarios are recreated trough turbulence boxes based with dif-
ferent ﬁdelity models. The high ﬁdelity model comes from turbulence boxes in HAWC2
format based on LES simulations. From these turbulence boxes, the data for the cre-
ation of the turbulence boxes with low and medium ﬁdelity are extracted, from where
the mean wind speed and the standard deviation across the time series are obtained,
given as a results two planes (or 2D matrices), one for the wind speed and other for the
standard deviation.

where at each time step the average wind speed plane is added together with the con-

Rotor equivalent wind speed (REWS)
The rotor equivalent wind speed, from now on referred to as REWS, is a method to
calculate the averaging weighted wind speed over the rotor area. If the wind speed is
4

uniform over the rotor area, then it is easy to calculate the power output of a turbine.
However, to the extent that wind turbines increase in rotor diameter, the horizontal
wind speed also loses representativeness of the wind speed to calculate the power output
or thrust coeﬃcient for the turbine. Additionally, the wind speed in most of the cases is
not uniform, on the contrary, it has diﬀerences either by the vertical wind shear or by
the presence of full or partial wakes.
Given the above, the REWS delivers a more accurate estimate of the kinetic energy ﬂux
passing thought the rotor than considering only the wind speed measured at hub height

area since it will be less dependent on factors as the shear.

3.1
Simulations with HAWC2
To estimate loads and fatigue of a wind turbine under certain conditions of operation,
the aeroelastic code HAWC2 (Horizontal Axis Wind turbine simulation Code 2nd gen-
eration) will be used. This code calculates the wind turbine response in time domain,
and it was developed within the years 2003-2006 at the aeroelastic design research pro-
gramme at Risø, National laboratory Denmark [HAWC2 (2019)], but is continuously
updated and improved.
In order to run a simulation, the code requires a model for the speciﬁc wind turbine,
which consists of diﬀerent ﬁles, such as:
• Proﬁle coeﬃcients ﬁle, or pc ﬁle.
• The aerodynamic blade layout, which is contained in the ae ﬁle.
• Structural data ﬁle, st ﬁle.
• control system ﬁles, .dll format.
• and .htc ﬁle, which is the main ﬁle, and it has all the information required for
the simulations as the structural model, wind model, aerodynamic model, control,
characteristics of response, among others.
HAWC2 has been veriﬁed through measurements and comparisons with other codes, and
it has been used to simulate more than 100 diﬀerent wind turbines [HAWC2 (2019)].
The wind conditions in HAWC2 can be divided into deterministic and stochastic wind.
The ﬁrst part includes mean wind speed, sudden acceleration, linear trend, special gust
11
==PAGE:24==
Data and simulations
12
events, and special shears. The second part corresponds to the turbulence. HAWC2
can work with wind and turbulence boxes generated by external sources, which allow
to simulate speciﬁc conditions.
The turbulence data can have two diﬀerent formats
depending on the coordinate system. One is the Mann turbulence, which works with
Cartesian coordinates, and the second one is the FLEX5 format which operates in polar
coordinates. Lastly, the tower shadow eﬀect is part of the wind module in HAWC2,
since this can change the wind conditions near to the tower [HAWC2 (2019)].

3.5
PyConTurb:
an open-source constrained turbulence
generator
PyConTurb, which is the short name for ”Python Constrained Turbulence” is an open-
source tool implemented on Python, to generate turbulence boxes constrained by mea-
sured data or by a speciﬁc condition. This tool used the Kaimal spectrum with Exponen-
tial Coherence method (KSEC) and it enforces spatial coherence relationships between
all measured and simulated points [Rinker (2018)]. Notice that PyConTurb is based
on the same technique used in the KSEC model in wind turbine design standard IEC
61400-1 Ed.3.
PyConTurb can eﬀectively simulate turbulence boxes that are constrained by measured
data, as was demonstrated on [Rinker (2018)], being a very powerful tool in validation
studies that requires this type of analysis. Also, it presents certain advantages compared
with the Mann turbulence model: Mann model is more computationally expensive than
the KSEC method and in some cases, the generated ﬁeld has unrealistic speeds variations
for two close positions.
However, the KSEC model also has drawbacks: Firstly, it does not follow the conserva-
tion of mass, in contrast with the Mann model, and secondly does not feature coherence
in the lateral and vertical turbulence components and its lack of cross-component co-
herence could aﬀect resulting load calculations [Rinker (2018)].
PyConTurb is a very versatile and ﬂexible tool, and not just allow to create turbulence
boxes from measured data, but also permit the user to implement customized functions
for the wind speed or/and the standard deviation, which will allow the creation of tur-
bulence boxes with speciﬁc characteristics. This last distinctive was very useful for this
project since allowed the design of turbulence boxes with speciﬁc mean values through
the time series for the wind speed and for the standard deviation, as was required on
this study. More about the customized creation of the turbulence boxes for this project
will be discussed in section 4.
PyConTurb is available at the git repository: https://gitlab.windenergy.dtu.dk/
rink/pyconturb.

Chapter 5
Wake Metrics
The operation of a wind turbine under full or partial wakes is a very common situation,
which produces undesirable eﬀects as is the reduction of the power output and the
increase on the fatigue loads.
For this reason, have methods that allow us to quantify and describe the wake char-
acterization is a desirable thing. This topic has been the center of many studies and
many diﬀerent tools and models exist to do this. However, it is not part of this study
to analyze them, just to deﬁne suitable wake-metrics that represents a wake-situation
and how this wake-metrics correlated with the load variations observed in the low and
medium ﬁdelity approaches.
For this, the ﬁrst step was to calculate the rotor equivalent wind speed 2.2, refereed
as REWS, where the mean wind speed and mean standard deviation through the time
series from the LES data contain in the planes in appendix A will be the input values
for this evaluation. It is important to have this in consideration because this could lead
to some bias in our results at the moment to compare them with the results of the high
ﬁdelity approach.

Results and analysis
In order to analyze the diﬀerences between the three approaches, it is important to have
a clear idea of all the parameters that could lead to diﬀerences outside the accuracy
of the method in-self. The ﬁrst consideration for the diﬀerences will come from the
sensitivity of the grid of the turbulence boxes.
The high ﬁdelity model is based on
turbulence boxes with a grid spacing of 2.5 m, having 53 grid points in each direction of
the plane YZ. However, the turbulence boxes for the medium and low ﬁdelity approach
have 9 grid points in each direction with a spacing between each point of 15.75 m, due
to what was discussed in section 4.2. From here, it was shown that diﬀerences around
30% could be expected for the short term DELs sensor tower bottom side-side. The
rest of the sensor present less sensitivity to the grid selection. Since this could have an
impact on the diﬀerences between medium and low ﬁdelity approach compare against
the high ﬁdelity, it has to be considered, especially in the loads that prove to have more
sensitivity to the grid selection.
The second consideration will come from the selection of the seeds for the creation of
the turbulence boxes, which will add a diﬀerence in the standard deviation of the wind
speed in the spatial position, aﬀecting the turbulence intensity and wind speed in the
points of interest, as it will discuss in section 6.3.
Another important consideration is the impact that the wake meandering eﬀect will
have on the simulations. The wake meandering from LES it is including in average on
the low and medium ﬁdelity, since these turbulence boxes are based on the mean wind
speed planes and standard deviation planes A, which are the mean at each grid point of
the turbulence box for the whole time series (28.000 points) 4.3, having as a result an
average values on a plane in the YZ direction. These planes will smooth out the wake
edges since the movement of the wake can not be seen on the plane, just the average
behavior from the original turbulence box. It is expected that the wake is actually more

6.5
Fatigue loads
To analyze the fatigue loads, the short term damage equivalent loads (DELs) were
calculated as was explained in section 2.5.
All the short term DELs were normalized by the value of the respective DEL for the high
ﬁdelity model at the upstream positions m2D at the center of the YZ plane, selecting
their respective value according to the turbulence intensity case (Low or High). For
the medium and low ﬁdelity, the average between the six DELs for each sensor was
considered as the ﬁnal value for this analysis.
In general, it can be seen in all the cases the increase in the short term DELs is sig-
niﬁcantly higher for the case with Low TI in comparison to High TI case. This can be
attributed to the diﬀerence in the increase of turbulence intensity for both cases, as was
mentioned on 5.2, since the increase for the Low TI case goes from 5.64% to 15.85% from
freestream to 4D (increase of 181.0%), but High TI case just goes from 9.46% to 16.15%
(increase of 70.7%. See ﬁgure 6.2 and table 5.1), and since the DELs are normalized
by their respective free stream case, a higher increase is also expected to be visualized
on the low TI case. Consequently, it is expected that loads with higher sensitivity to
turbulence intensity will have a greater increase for the Low TI case.

Discussion and perspectives
In general, a higher increase for the loads in the low TI case is seen in all the sensors.
This could be attributed to these following factors:
• The increase of turbulence intensity is higher for this case since it goes for the
downwind distance 4D from 5.64% at the freestream case to around 15.85%, which
represents an increase of 181.0% for the turbulence intensity. In contrast, the high
TI case for the same case goes from 9.46% to 16.15%, which represents an increase
of 70.7%, which is considerably lower than for the low TI case. In general, this
leads to a higher increase on the loads for the low TI cases on those sensors that
are sensitive to the turbulence intensity.
• The wind speed at the low TI case at the freestream present a more ﬂat behavior
across the diﬀerent positions on the plane 6.1, which does not create problems
when the DELs are normalized by the freestream center. However, for the high
TI case, this is not the case, and an important variation from the center to the
edges, especially at + 200 m can be observed 6.1, with an approximate variation
of 0.4 m/s. When the loads are normalized by a higher value, the increase in the
fatigue loads will appear lower than actually is. This also occurs for the turbulence
intensity, as can be seen in ﬁgure 6.2.
• In some cases, higher wind speed are seen at the low TI case for certain positions,
which will lead to higher operational loads.
From the above, is important to notice that in some cases, the operational loads be-
come more signiﬁcant in the impact of the fatigue loads than the increase in turbulence
intensity, and this is especially the case for some sensors for the high TI case, as the
tower top tilt moment, where the increase in turbulence intensity is not strong enough
64

to compensate the decrease from the operational loads, therefore, fatigue loads lower
than for the freestream case are presented for this case.
To improve the results, the ﬁrst suggestion will be to recreate the same seven selected
positions for the wake cases also at the freestream cases, and normalized each case for
the freestream case at the respective position. This will take out biases created by this
situation, which increase or decrease the fatigue loads, especially for the high TI case.
Regarding the creation of the turbulence boxes for the low and medium ﬁdelity models,
even though these boxes were created imposing certain speciﬁc values (like the mean for
the standard deviation for the time series for each individual grid point, for the medium
ﬁdelity or the average value across the whole plane, for the low ﬁdelity), the results were
not exactly the same as the input conditions, and this is mainly for two reasons: The
turbulence generator always created variations among the diﬀerent turbulence boxes
which can be attributed to the diﬀerences introduced by the selection of the seeds, as
was shown in 6.3, but also to that the KSEC model with PyConTurb does not conserve
the standard deviation Rinker (2018).
This is important to consider, since the sensors that had a higher sensitivity to the
turbulence intensity, will be impacted by these situations.
The grid sensitivity for the DELs showed interesting results, where the tower bottom
side-side moment had the highest sensitivity to the grid selection. Deﬁnitely this is an
important consideration when it comes to analyze ﬁnal results. It would be interesting to
make a similar analysis, following the same procedure, but this time using a high ﬁdelity
turbulence box as the reference one under the most representative wake conditions, and
from here start to extract columns and rows, in a similar manner as was done in 4.2.
This would help to take out bias that are aﬀecting the ﬁnal results, especially on the
cases where a considerable under or over estimation can be found. However, it is always
important to consider the time factor. Create turbulence boxes with a higher mesh will
increase the time for the creation exponentially, so a balanced decision needs to be made.
For this project, a total of 528 turbulence boxes were generated with PyConTurb. The
cost of time for the generation of the turbulence boxes was a decisive factor when the
decision was made.
Wake metrics are an important tool to have a better understanding of the wake situation
over the rotor area, bringing a better estimation of the power production with the rotor
equivalent wind speed (REWS), but also with the rotor equivalent turbulence intensity
(RETI), since allows to have a better interpretation of the situation.
However, the
interesting part was the diﬀerence among sectors, since it gives a very clear indication of
when the rotor is facing partial-wakes and how much of the rotor swept area is aﬀected

by it. Additionally, the diﬀerence among sectors become a very good tool to analyzed
those sensors where the partial wake scenarios present a higher increase in the fatigue
loads.
When analyzing short term DELs under wake situation, it is important to look at the
wake metrics together with the operational conditions for the turbine, which will lead
to had a better understanding of the situation that is being seen, helping to identify
from where the diﬀerences between the models could come, and this allows to improve
future analysis, but also to know when a low or medium ﬁdelity approach could become
a useful and reliable tool for the estimation of fatigue loads on a wind turbine, since
for some sensors, a very good agreement was found, as was the case for the blade root
moments.
The reason why the blade root moments have a very good agreement for the three models,
can be attributed to the rotation of the blades, which means that the movement of the
component is what makes that the absence of the meander of the wake does not have a
greater impact on the estimation for the fatigue loads on these three sensors (ﬂapwise,
edgewise and torsion) for the low and medium ﬁdelity. When the power output and
rotational speed were compared together with the REWS for the three models, it was
possible to see that when the wind turbine is coming out from the wake, the prediction
becomes less accurate, with the higher diﬀerences are at the edges, at positions Y: -126
and Y: +126 m 6.3. However, this was not the case for the DELs, where the highest
diﬀerences among the three models normally were found at the center for sensors as:
tower top tilt 6.12, tower top roll 6.11, tower top yaw 6.13 and shaft torsion 6.10. In
some cases, the medium ﬁdelity was able to predict the peaks and trends of the high
ﬁdelity model, in contrast with the low ﬁdelity that did not predict the behavior at
the speciﬁc wake scenario, as was the case for the tower top roll, were the low ﬁdelity
just predict a ﬂat behavior around the center 6.11. This is mainly attributed to the
wake meander. When diﬀerences around the center can be appreciated, especially when
the low and medium ﬁdelity models underpredict or neglect completely some peaks
situations around the central positions, deﬁnitely these results can be explained by the
absence of wake meandering of the low, medium ﬁdelity models.
For the blade root edgewise moment, it was interesting to see partial wake can produce
a decrease on loads or an increase depending on the sector where the wake is hitting the
rotor. If the partial wake hits the rotor from the sector where the blade goes from the
upper to the bottom part (0° to 180° angular position), the fatigue loads will decrease
due to the partial relieving of gravity acting on the blade on this direction.
Comparing the results among the medium and low ﬁdelity models, the results match
considerably, having the same trends and values, except in DELs as the tower top roll

(MyTT) and shaft torsion (MzMB) were the medium ﬁdelity was able to follow in a
better manner the trends shown by the high ﬁdelity simulations, which is an indication
that for these sensors, the distribution of the turbulence intensity around the rotor area
have an important impact.
These can also be seen from the behavior of the DELs.
The higher fatigue loads occur at the half-wake situation, where the higher turbulence
intensity is in one side of the rotor area 6.11. However, when the distribution of the
turbulence intensity is even across the rotor, the loads decrease considerable, but this
eﬀect is not well captured by the medium-ﬁdelity model, and the low ﬁdelity neglected
completely.
High ﬁdelity models are complex and computationally expensive, in contrast with the
method tested in this study, which are easy to implement. However, where the diﬀerences
become higher for some sensors, is mainly by the absence of wake meandering.
Wakes can be considered as steady or unsteady phenomena. Normally, analyzing wake
behavior as a steady phenomenon will lead to an overestimation of the velocity deﬁcit,
which is why in many of our sensors, the low and medium ﬁdelity underestimates fatigue
loads. A complete analysis is to consider wakes as non-stationary ﬂow structures, where
the key phenomenon is meandering, which contributes to fatigue loading [Muller (2015)]
as was shown in sensors as tower bottom side-side, fore-aft, and tower top tilt and yaw
moment.
To improve the results, an important solution will be to include some model for the
wake meandering phenomenon in the low and medium ﬁdelity model, and see how this
change the estimation for the fatigue loads in some sensors. It is expected that a better
agreement could be found.
Diﬀerent types of turbulence models used in CFD exist for modeling wind turbine wakes.
However, the most advanced is the LES method. LES models are particularly adapted
to wake modeling as the wake can be seen to have two distinct ways to dissipate. One
part interacts mainly with the large eddies of the atmosphere, which create the wake
meandering phenomenon. While the other part, the small-scale of turbulence, is mainly
self-generated through the local velocity shear [R´ethor´e (2009)].
Another important observation to make is about the wake steering. When it comes to
wake deﬂection, diﬀerent control methods can be used. In [Vollmer et al. (2016)] it was
noticed that the shape of the wake under yawed conditions is curled rather than circular,
which has an impact on the estimation of the wake center. Also, it is explained that
these changes in shape are mainly due to counter-rotating vortices that appear in the
ﬂow behind a yawed turbine, generating this distortion. This is important to consider,
since if a more accurate analysis for the fatigue loads under wake conditions coming
==PAGE:80==
Discussion and perspectives
68
from yawed conditions is needed, then the LES simulations need to be made considering
the yaw misalignment, which is not the case for the LES data used on this project.
Finally, the turbulence boxes created on this project are based on the KSEC model.
However, it will be also interesting to see how the low and medium ﬁdelity model behave
when the turbulence boxes are created with the Mann turbulence box model.

Conclusion
An increase in the fatigue loads was sawed in the great majority of the cases for almost all
the sensors, where the only exception is the short term DEL blade root torsion moment,
where when the rotor is more in the presence of a wake condition, higher is the decrease
of the fatigue loads, following the same trend of the wind speed. The behavior of DELs
depends on the speciﬁc sensor and the speciﬁc wake situation that the rotor is facing
since the sensors present diﬀerent sensitivities to diﬀerent factors.
The increase in the fatigue loads was always higher for the low TI case, due to in this
case the increase of the turbulence intensity was considerably higher with regard to the
freestream case, in comparison with the high TI case. For this reason, in many cases,
while a considerable increase in the fatigue loads can be appreciated for the low TI case,
the fatigue loads for the high TI case present a decrease. This is mainly because of the
reduction in operational loads since under wake conditions, the wind speed is reduced,
therefore the power output and rotational speed will decrease accordingly. From this, is
possible to conclude that even though the majority of the sensors are very sensitive to
the increase of the turbulence intensity, a certain increase of the turbulence intensity is
necessary to become more relevant than the alleviation of the operational loads on the
wind turbine due to the velocity deﬁcit in the wake.
Regarding the agreement between the low, medium and high ﬁdelity models, this will
depend on the sensor.
For the blade root moment, very good agreement was found
among the three models, with a very good estimation of the values and trends. This is
mainly due to the rotational movement of the blade, which make the absence of wake
meandering in the low ﬁdelity models do not create a great impact since it is constantly
moving. This is important since the wake meandering phenomenon is responsible for
the diﬀerences across the models in the sensors that are sensitive to this phenomenon.
69
==PAGE:82==
Conclusion
70
The wake meandering eﬀect can be found on the sensors tower top tilt and yaw moment,
and tower bottom fore-aft and side-side moment, where at the central positions the low
and medium ﬁdelity are not able to predict the trend or neither the values for the fatigue
loads with a good agreement. These sensors are the ones with the lowest agreement
among the three methods, and it is mainly related to the absence of the wake meander.
In order to improve the low and medium ﬁdelity models, the implementation of wake
meandering models is required to improve the accuracy of these models.
For the shaft torsion and top roll moment, the agreement among the three models is
also good, however, at the central position, under full wake, the low and medium ﬁdelity
models are not able to predict the drop on the fatigue load under this condition.
Another important observation is that the models tend to have a better agreement when
the rotor is under full or almost full wake conditions. When the rotor is coming out
from a wake, the estimation of the rotational speed, power output, and forces become
less accurate for the low and medium ﬁdelity models.
Finally, wake metrics were a useful and powerful tool when the analysis of the short
term DELs was made, due to allow better visualization of the wake situations and the
direct relation of the behavior of the fatigue load for a speciﬁc sensor compare with this
wake metrics, making the detection of trends more easy and clear. The REWS show an
important contribution for those sensors more sensitive to the wind speed as the blade
root edgewise moment. The RETI was very useful when the analysis for the tower top
tilt, yaw moment and tower bottom fore-aft and side-side moment was made. Finally,
the diﬀerence among sectors was interesting and bring a very good understanding when
it comes to the sensors that present a higher increase under half-wake cases, such as
blade root ﬂapwise, shaft torsion and top roll moment.
