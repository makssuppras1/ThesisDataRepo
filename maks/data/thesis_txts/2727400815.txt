Abstract  
In recent years, many new findings have greatly advanced the field of gene therapy. Discovery of the 
genome editing system “Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats-CRISPR Associated 
Protein 9” (CRISPR-Cas9) has expanded the applications of genome engineering and a variety of CRISPR-
Cas9-based therapeutics are currently being investigated in clinical trials. CRISPR-Cas9 is an RNA-guided 
endonuclease enabling double-stranded breaks in a target DNA sequence. The editing system relies on 
either of two DNA repair pathways; nonhomologous end-joining (NHEJ) or homologous recombination 
(HR), which facilitate gene knock-out, gene insertion, or gene correction. Understanding of key steps of the 
structural dynamics of the allosteric Cas9 protein are yet to be elucidated. Gaining such knowledge is highly 
warranted and could pave the way for improved CRISPR systems in terms of specificity and efficiency.  
In this project, initial efforts to reveal dynamic Cas9-guide RNA (gRNA) interactions utilizing fluorescent 
probes are outlined. The synthetic work presented herein was directed towards designing novel fluorescent 
nucleoside analogues for application in optical studies including steady-state fluorescence spectroscopy, 
UV-VIS spectroscopy, fluorescence polarization, and fluorescence anisotropy to asses for the sensitivity of 
the nucleosides to amino acid microenvironment. A pyridine substituent was successfully incorporated at 
the 5-position of the nucleobase uracil in 2'-deoxyuridine via a Stille coupling reaction. Pilot screening of 
fluorescent nucleoside analogues derived from 2’-deoxyuridine containing thiophene, furan, methyl-
imidazole, chloro-pyridine, and methyl-indole modifications revealed that a combination of furan-dU and 
chloro-pyridine-dU will give a clear distinction for the acidic vs. basic environment. Additionally, 
fluorescence polarization and anisotropy studies were conducted for furan-dU and imidazole-dU and the 
results showed that these tools are excellent for detecting protein binding. In order to search for optimal 
positions of fluorophores in gRNA sequence to detect dynamics of Cas9 protein during interaction with 
target dsDNA, four 39-mer oligoribonucleotide sequences bearing various 2-aminopurine (2AP) 
modifications have been synthesized.  
It was planned to incorporate the fluorescent nucleoside analogue synthesized in this project into RNA 
oligonucleotides utilizing solid-phase oligonucleotide synthesis and a phosphoramidite strategy for further 
examination of the Cas9-gRNA complex. Unfortunately, this was not finalized due to time constraints.

Resumé 
I de senere år er der blevet gjort store fremskridt på genterapiområdet grundet mange nye fund. 
Opdagelsen af genredigeringssystemet "Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats-CRISPR 
Associated Protein 9" (CRISPR-Cas9) har udvidet anvendelserne af genteknologi, og en række CRISPR-Cas9-
baserede terapier er på nuværende tidspunkt ved at blive undersøgt i kliniske forsøg. CRISPR-Cas9 er en 
RNA-guidet endonuklease, der forårsager dobbeltstrengede brud i DNA-sekvensen, der ønskes redigeret. 
Redigeringssystemet afhænger af en af to DNA-reparationsveje; ikke-homolog rekombination (NHEJ) eller 
homolog rekombination (HR), som faciliterer gen-knock-out, genindsættelse eller genkorrektion. Forståelse 
af nøgletrin i den strukturelle dynamik af det allosteriske Cas9-protein er endnu ikke belyst. Opnåelse af en 
sådan viden er yderst vigtig og kan bane vejen for forbedrede CRISPR-systemer hvad angår specificitet og 
effektivitet. 
Dette projekt skitserer de indledende bestræbelser på at afdække dynamiske Cas9-guide RNA (gRNA) 
interaktioner ved hjælp af fluorescerende prober. Det eksperimentelle arbejde præsenteret her var rettet 
mod at designe nye fluorescerende nukleosidanaloger til anvendelse i optiske undersøgelser, herunder 
steady-state fluorescensspektroskopi, UV-VIS-spektroskopi, fluorescens polarisering og fluorescens 
anisotropi for at vurdere nukleosidernes følsomhed over for aminosyremikromiljø. En pyridinsubstituent 
blev succesfuldt inkorporeret i 5-positionen af uracil-nukleobasen i 2'-deoxyuridin via en Stille-
koblingsreaktion. Indledende screeninger af fluorescerende nukleosidanaloger afledt af 2'-deoxyuridin 
indeholdende thiophen, furan, methyl-imidazol, chlor-pyridin og methyl-indol modifikationer viste, at en 
kombination af furan-dU og chlor-pyridin-dU vil udtrykke en klar forskel mellem det sure vs. basiske miljø. 
Derudover blev fluorescens polarisering og anisotropiundersøgelser udført for furan-dU og imidazol-dU, og 
resultaterne viste, at disse værktøjer er gode til at påvise proteinbinding. For at finde optimale placeringer 
af fluoroforer i gRNA-sekvenser til undersøgelse af dynamikken af Cas9-protein under interaktion med 
dsDNA er fire 39-mer oligoribonukleotidsekvenser, der bærer forskellige 2-aminopurin (2AP) 
modifikationer, blevet syntetiseret. 
Det var planlagt at inkorporere den fluorescerende nukleosidanalog syntetiseret i dette projekt i RNA-
oligonukleotider via anvendelse af oligonukleotidsyntese og en phosphoramidit-strategi til yderligere 
undersøgelse af Cas9-gRNA-komplekset. Desværre blev dette ikke afsluttet på grund af tidsbegrænsning.

1 Introduction  
In the last century, significant advances in drug discovery and the medical field have had a tremendous 
impact on the quality of human health. The ability to control and treat infectious and nutritional diseases is 
increasing but comes with the realization that genetic disorders are a major cause of disability and death 
[1]. Attempts to accommodate the need for controlling and treating genetic diseases began in the 1970s 
and continued throughout the following decades, where it has witnessed both early success and tragic 
failures in clinical settings. Utilizing virus vectors for gene transfer of exogenous DNA was the main 
treatment strategy. Unfortunately, some clinical trials had fatal outcomes [2]. Traditional gene therapy has 
raised some concerns as its reliance on viral vector delivery of therapeutic transgenes can cause insertional 
oncogenesis and immunogenic toxicity. Based on these previous experiences, lessons have been learned 
and research are aiming at the development of selective, efficient, and safe gene therapies.  
In the past 20 years, great progress in genome editing techniques has been made. With the discovery that 
“Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats-CRISPR Associated Protein 9” (CRISPR-Cas9) 
system can be used in mammalian cells for gene editing tool, the field of gene therapy has greatly emerged 
and is raising hopes towards viable treatment for genetic diseases [3]. At the present time, the CRISPR-Cas 
system is the most popular gene editing platform due to its high efficacy and specificity and it finds 
applications in various sectors such as agriculture, nutritional research, and medicine [4]. The technology 
relies on two components; Cas9 nuclease and a guide RNA (gRNA). The Cas9 nuclease relies on the 
guidance of the gRNA, which is composed of a scaffold sequence anchoring it to the Cas9 protein and a 
customized 20 nucleotide spacer sequence complementary to the target DNA. The target gene is positioned 
next to a protospacer adjacent motif (PAM), that is required for binding of the Cas9 protein. Subsequent 
unwinding of double-stranded DNA and hybridization of the spacer to the target gene initiate cleavage of 
target DNA introducing a double-strand break (DSB). The DSB mediated by CRISR-Cas9 is repaired by either 
of two DNA repair pathways; non-homologous end joining (NHEJ) or homologous recombination (HR). The 
NHEJ pathway can disrupt or inactivate the target gene, whereas the HR pathway can modify or correct a 
nucleotide sequence [5]. Thus, the CRISPR-Cas9 system offers selective targeting and more specific gene 
editing, hereby providing a safer treatment compared with traditional gene therapy.  
Although 14 nucleic acid-based drugs are FDA approved and have entered the market by 2021, still no 
therapies exploiting the CRISPR-Cas9 technology have been approved yet [6]. However, a variety of CRISPR-
based treatments hold great potential for treating human genetic disease and are currently being 
investigated in clinical trials to gain a potential FDA approval [7].

1.1 Nucleic acids: Structure and interactions  
Nucleic acid oligomers are key molecules for instructing and maintaining the functioning of living cells. They 
come in the form of deoxyribonucleic acids (DNA) and ribonucleic acids (RNA). DNA contains all the genetic 
information of an organism and is stored in the nucleus folded up into chromosomes. Another nucleic acid 
class is RNA which performs several functions within the cell, such as regulating gene expression (small 
interfering RNA (siRNA) and microRNA (miRNA)), but the most important role is serving as a template for 
the synthesis of proteins. There are three main types of RNA molecules; messenger RNA (mRNA), transfer 
RNA (tRNA), and ribosomal RNA (rRNA), which are all crucial for the process of protein synthesis [8]. 
1.1.1 DNA 
The central dogma of molecular biology describes the flow of genetic information from DNA to RNA to 
protein in cells. It includes critical processes of DNA replication, transcription, and translation. Replication 
of DNA takes place during cell division, and is followed by transcription, which is the first of two major steps 
in gene expression. During transcription, information in DNA is transcribed into a single-stranded RNA copy 
of a certain gene. The second step is translation, where RNA serving as template is translated into protein 
by ribosomes [9]. The central molecular dogma is depicted in figure 1. 
 
 
Figure 1: Depiction of the central dogma of molecular biology. 
From the chemical stand point, DNA and RNA are composed of nucleotide monomers comprised of a five-
carbon ribose moiety coupled to a heterocyclic nitrogenous base at the 1’-position and a phosphate group 
at the 5’-position. In 1953 James Watson and Francis Crick elucidated the structure of DNA as a 
complementary right-handed double helix held together by hydrogen bonds [9]. The sugar backbone in 
DNA consists of 2-deoxy-D-ribose, while the single-stranded RNA contains D-ribose units (figure 2). RNA can 
have regions of helical secondary structure. 
 
 
Figure 2: Sugar units in DNA nucleotide (2-deoxy-D-ribose) and RNA nucleotide (D-ribose). 
==PAGE:12==
 
3 
 
In DNA, there are four different nucleobases; adenine (A), cytosine (C), guanine (G), and thymine (T). In 
RNA, thymine has been replaced with uracil (U), leading to the four following nucleobases for RNA; A, C, G, 
and U. Structures of nucleobases are seen in figure 3. The nucleobases can further be divided into purines 
(A and G) and pyrimidines (C, T, and U). The purines contain two fused nitrogen rings and are linked to the 
C1’-position of ribose via their N9 position. The pyrimidines contain only a single nitrogen ring and they are 
linked at their N1-position to the C1’-position of ribose. Each base has its own set of functional groups.  
 
 
Figure 3: Nucleobases in DNA (A, C, G, T) and RNA (A, C, G, U). 
The nucleotide building blocks are joined together by covalent phosphodiester bonds between the 3’-
hydroxyl group of one nucleotide and the 5’-phosphate group of the next nucleotide. Thus, the 
polynucleotide has an alternating ribose-phosphate backbone. The two strands in DNA run in opposite 
directions, hence they are anti-parallel. The double helix is formed through hydrogen bonds between the 
nucleobases of the two opposite nucleotide strands (figure 4). As a result of the hydrogen bonding, the 
nitrogenous bases are oriented into the interior of the core of the helix. By this means, they contribute to 
the overall stabilization of the helical structure via van der Waals forces and π-stacking [10].  
The hybridization between two complementary single-stranded DNA molecules does not occur in a random 
fashion. In fact, they are brought together in a very specific manner according to Chargaff’s rule that state 
that there in DNA exists an equal number of purines and pyrimidines [11]. This rule provides the foundation 
for the Watson-Crick base pairing rules, where A pairs with T in DNA and U in RNA through two hydrogen 
bonds, whereas C pairs with G through three hydrogen bonds. Besides the hydrogen bonding between 
nucleobases, nucleic acids interact with other molecules through stacking interactions, van der Waals 
forces, and hydrophobic forces.  
==PAGE:13==
 
4 
 
 
Figure 4: Nucleobase hydrogen bonding (A:T, C:G) in accordance with Watson-Crick base pairing. 
Different positions of the nucleobases can be used as potential modification sites for design of fluorescent 
nucleobase analogues without affecting the ability to obey the rules of Watson-Crick base pairing. The 
pyrimidines are commonly modified on positions 5 and 6, while the purines are often modified on positions 
2, 5, and 8 of adenine and positions 6, 7, and 8 of guanine. Position 5 in pyrimidines and position 8 in 
purines are in particular subjected to modifications as they are very electrophilic [12]. Modified nucleobase 
derivatives are described in more detail further on. 
1.1.2 RNA: Dynamic, regulatory nucleic acid biopolymer 
Chemically, the main difference between DNA nucleotides and RNA nucleotides is the presence of the 2’-
hydroxyl group in RNA, which makes the phosphodiester bonds in RNA much more prone to base-
promoted hydrolysis resulting in cleavage of RNA. In fact, the stability of RNA is decreased with a 100-fold 
compared to DNA [9]. The hydrolytic cleavage of the RNA backbone is initiated by deprotonation of the 2’-
hydroxyl group of the RNA nucleotide. This hydroxyl group has a lower pKa value compared with typical 
alcohols making it much more acidic than many alcohols [13]. Once deprotonated, it makes an 
intramolecular nucleophilic attack on the phosphate group to generate a five-membered ring cyclic 
phosphate diester. This cyclic phosphate is strained and thus, attack hereon occurs rapidly, leading to 
departure of a 5’-oxyanion and formation of a 2’,3’-cyclic phosphate in addition to RNA strand break. 
Ultimately, the 2’,3’-cyclic phosphate leads to a mixture of 2’- and 3’-phosphates, as illustrated in figure 5 
[14]. Ribonucleases (RNases) are enzymes exploiting this mechanism as they catalyze the degradation of 
RNA. 
==PAGE:14==
 
5 
 
 
Figure 5: Base-catalyzed hydrolysis of RNA. 
Due to the enhanced reactivity of RNA, it is more difficult to manufacture and store compared with DNA. 
For gene therapy applications the aim is to increase resistance to endo- and exonucleases to confer drug-
like properties and exhibit moderate stability. A frequently used strategy to increase the half-life of RNA 
therapeutics is to introduce chemical modifications such as 2’-O-methyl or 2’-fluoro, use locked nucleic 
acids (LNA), or replace the phosphodiester linkage with phosphorothioate as depicted in figure 6 below 
[13], [15].  
 
Figure 6: Modified RNA structures. 
Another approach to explore in order to circumvent the problems faced by the labile single-stranded RNA is 
utilizing double-stranded RNA as it has proven to be less likely to undergo spontaneous cleavage [14]. In 
order to exert its functions and fold correctly, RNA helices undergo dynamic rearrangements. While RNA is 
typically single-stranded, it can adopt diverse structural forms and fold up into structures with double-
stranded regions. These secondary structures are often hairpins (also called stem loops). They are 
composed of a hybridized stem and a single-stranded loop. Hairpins are made by pairing between 
complementary sequences in a nucleotide strand and held together through hydrogen bonds. Besides 
Watson-Crick base pairing, RNA frequently makes use of Hoogsteen base pairing [16]. The four-nucleotide 
==PAGE:15==
 
6 
 
sequence “UNCG”, depicted in figure 7, is a particularly good loop for stabilizing hairpins [9]. RNA has larger 
structural diversity than DNA linked to the many different functions, that RNA carries out in the cell, and 
mismatches between bases are more often permitted in RNA double-helical regions than DNA:DNA or 
DNA:RNA hybridizations [17]. Consequently, many potential hairpin structures can be adopted by RNA. The 
hairpin structure can be denatured by heating as it will unfold and reveal its linear, single-stranded form.

1.2 CRISPR-Cas9 
Interactions between nucleic acids and proteins accompany each step of the aforementioned central 
molecular dogma. Moreover, nucleic acid processing with nucleases also involves interactions of 
nucleotides with enzymes. One exciting new enzymatic DNA editing system is CRISPR Cas9. 
CRISPR-Cas9 is an acronym for “Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats-CRISPR 
Associated Protein 9” and is a gene editing tool. The system has evolved in prokaryotes as a natural part of 
their immune system as a defense mechanism to fight invading mobile genetic elements (MGEs) such as 
viruses [18]. The CRISPR-Cas system integrates short sequences from the MGEs into the prokaryote’s 
genome, allowing sequence-specific cleavage of targeted DNA via delivery of the RNA-guided Cas9 nuclease 
for eradicating the genome of the virus upon subsequent encounter. Thus, the system provides prokaryotes 
with adaptive hereditable immunity [19]. 
1.2.1 Natural defense mechanism  
Cas proteins integrate fragments of invading DNA into the host genome at the CRISPR locus (figure 8) that 
are composed of two parts; a CRISPR-array, where information from previous infections is stored, and a 
group of genes encoding Cas-proteins. The array is composed of variable spacers, which are segments 
originating from foreign DNA, sandwiched in between palindromic repeats [20]. 
==PAGE:16==
 
7 
 
 
Figure 8: Generalized CRISPR locus for Cas9. 
The spacer regions serve as transcription templates for non-coding precursor CRISPR-RNA (pre-crRNA). 
Upon secondary encounter with a virus, from which a particular spacer sequence originated, the 
transcription is initiated followed by enzymatic cleavage of pre-crRNA to yield short crRNAs. This is followed 
by assembly of an effector complex composed of three moieties; Cas9-protein, crRNA, and trans-activating 
CRISPR-RNA (tracrRNA). Hybridization of the two RNA strands forms a crRNA:tracrRNA duplex called dual-
guide RNA (dgRNA). The Cas-protein has nuclease function and is responsible for cleaving target DNA. 
crRNA fits into the Cas-protein, recognizes and binds foreign DNA through Watson-Crick base pairing, and 
thus dictating where Cas9 should cut. tracrRNA is complementary to the palindromic repeats on the crRNA 
and anneals to them, thus holding crRNA in place inside the Cas-protein and serving as a handle for Cas9.  
Inside a cell, the Cas9-complex randomly associates and dissociates with DNA searching for its target. The 
foreign DNA sequence targeted by the spacer, called the protospacer, is immediately followed by a 
protospacer adjacent motif (PAM) [20]. A PAM is a short stretch of DNA, typically 2 to 4 nucleotides, that is 
required for Cas9 to bind targets, and found directly downstream of the target sequence on the non-target 
strand. The PAM is abundant throughout the genome. A given CRISPR-Cas system recognizes a specific 
PAM, which is crucial in order to distinguish self from non-self and prevent binding of effector complexes to 
the CRISPR-array from where spacers originated. The widely used Cas9 derived from Streptococcus 
pyogenes (SpyCas9) recognizes the PAM sequence “NGG”.  
Following recognition of and binding to PAM, Cas9 employs its helicase function, unwinding the DNA 
double helix of the target DNA sequence [21]. If the target DNA sequence perfectly matches the spacer, the 
crRNA will base pair with the complementary DNA strand (the target strand) forming an R-loop, see figure 
9. When no PAM is present, or DNA and crRNA does not perfectly match, the Cas9-complex will move on an 
continue its search elsewhere [22]. 
==PAGE:17==
 
8 
 
 
Figure 9: Structure and mechanism of the CRISPR-Cas9 system. 
Cas9 is composed of two different lobes; the nuclease lobe (NUC) and the recognition lobe (REC). Both 
lobes are made up of several domains. The main role of the REC lobe, comprising REC I and REC II domains, 
is recognition of and binding RNA, although the role of the REC II domain is not yet well understood [23]. 
The arginine-rich bridge helix (BH) domain assists in the RNA recognition process and is particularly 
important for initiating cleavage activity. The PAM interacting domain (PID) is responsible for identifying 
the PAM and initiate DNA-binding and found within the NUC lobe. The NUC lobe is in charge of Cas9 
cleavage of the target DNA, and this function is performed by two endonuclease domains; the His-Asn-His 
(HNH) domain and the RuvC domain, which are the blades of the “molecular scissors” and activated upon 
binding to PAM and a matching sequence. Binding of DNA to RNA inside the enzyme triggers Cas9 to 
undergo a conformational change of its three-dimensional structure to expose its nuclease domain. The 
relatively large and mobile HNH domain moves from one side of the enzyme to another and cleaves the 
target DNA strand as depicted in figure 10. The structural change of the HNH domain induces activation of 
the RuvC domain, which is less dynamic, leading to cleavage of the non-target DNA strand [20]. The 
presence of Mg2+ favors the formation of the catalytically active state of HNH [24], [25]. 
==PAGE:18==
 
9 
 
 
Figure 10: Cas9 protein is comprised of six domains: Rec I, Rec II, Bridge Helix, RuvC, HNH, and PID [21]. 
Cas9 cleaves the phosphodiester bond between two nucleotides within the chain via hydrolysis [26]. 
Cutting of DNA occurs three nucleotides away from the PAM [22]. Usually, the CRISPR-Cas system leaves 
behind blunt DNA ends, but Cas9 often “chew” back on the non-target strand, resulting in small overhangs. 
In prokaryotes, enzymes are thought to fully break down the cleaved DNA as part of the natural phage 
defense. Hence, the virus is neutralized as the gene can no longer be transcribed properly and the infection 
has been overcome.  
1.2.2 Gene-editing tool  
In general, gene therapy is the transfer of genetic material to a patient to treat or prevent a disease. Gene 
editing is related to gene therapy and offers the possibility to insert, delete or correct specific sequences in 
the genome. In the last decades, gene therapy and gene editing have been established as widely used 
techniques within the medical field owing to their capability of treating many genetic diseases, which are 
targeted on a transcriptional level [27].   
CRISPR-Cas9 has rapidly extended its applications from basic research to the development of therapeutics. 
In 2012, the CRISPR-Cas9 technology was repurposed to a gene-editing tool in eukaryotic species by making 
Cas9 a programmable DNA binding and cutting enzyme. As a therapeutic system, the CRISPR-Cas9 allows 
applications such as controlling transcription, modify genetic sequences, and carrying out targeted DNA 
insertions [28].   
As opposed to CRISPR-Cas9 as a natural phage defense in prokaryotes, the effector complex of CRISPR, 
when used therapeutically, only consists of two moieties; Cas9-protein and single-guide RNA (sgRNA). The 
dgRNA composed of two separate RNA molecules has been replaced with an engineered single RNA 
chimera made up of fused crRNA and tracrRNA molecules, which makes CRISPR-Cas9 a simple, economical, 
and efficient system to apply in gene therapy [18]. 
==PAGE:19==

The mode of action of CRISPR-Cas9 as a genome altering technology is very similar to that described above 
in prokaryotes. sgRNA adopts a T-shape form composed of one tetraloop and two or three stem loops in 
the 3’-end, which are crucial for the assembly of the Cas9:sgRNA complex (see figure 11). sgRNA enables 
anchoring to Cas9 and comprises a 20-base pair spacer sequence complementary to the target gene. A 
PAM motif is required for binding of Cas9 to the target. A study conducted by Mekler et al. displayed the 
importance of stem loops [18]. They found that sgRNAs truncated for the 3’-terminal stem loops 
demonstrated reduced activity during genomic editing due to decreased ability to withstand competition 
from intracellular RNA molecules for binding to Cas9. Furthermore, it was suggested that stem loops 2 and 
3 support the Cas9:sgRNA complex formation, thereby enhancing sgRNA stability along with improving its 
in vivo activity. The 5’-end of the sgRNA is complementary to the target DNA sequence and directs Cas9 to a 
specific location in the genome.  
 
Figure 11: T-shaped sgRNA with one tetraloop and three stem loops in the 3’-end. 
Cas9 induces double strand breaks (DSB) in DNA, which are followed by correction, addition, or deletion of 
the specific gene(s). Natural DNA repair mechanisms are activated upon DSBs, and these are repaired by 
either non-homologous end joining (NHEJ) or homologous recombination (HR) [28]. NHEJ is the major 
repair pathway for DSBs caused by the CRISPR-system. The broken ends are directly ligated without the 
requirement for a homologous template as repair guide, hence the term “non-homologous”. As there is no 
template, the repair machinery does not return DNA to its original sequence. Repair by NHEJ causes indels 
(insertions or deletions) in the sequence, leading to mutations as frame shifts occur [29]. This is an effective 
way to turn off specific genes in a target genome. In HR, a homologous template is used as a DNA template 
is added along with the CRISPR-complex. This DNA string is flanked by sequences identical to the sequences 
upstream and downstream of the target gene, so that the homologous template will stick to the sides of 
the cut and be inserted into the original sequence during the repair. By this process, it is possible to 
generate gene knock-outs and gene knock-ins [30]. Repair systems are depicted below in figure 12.

Despite CRISPR-Cas9 being an invaluable genome editing tool, the specificity of gene therapy utilizing 
oligonucleotides faces some challenges. In order to design and carry out gene therapy treatment, several 
criteria must be met: (1) identification of the gene(s) responsible for the disorder; (2) guide-RNA 
recognition of the gene(s) in question; (3) ensure production or delivery of Cas9 endonuclease to the cell; 
(4) delivery of gRNA to the cell. Requirement of a PAM motif limits the number of targetable DNA 
sequences. Generally, cleavage by CRISPR-Cas9 systems only occur when all nucleotides of gRNA base-pair 
with target DNA, but sometimes partial matches between gRNA and DNA are accepted for cutting as Cas9 
ignores a few mismatches, leading to off-target effects. Off-target limitations can be addressed employing 
engineered Cas9 proteins with enhanced specificity. Optimized high-fidelity variants can improve the 
accuracy of the system [31]. 
Targeted in vivo delivery of the CRISPR-system into the cell remains challenging. Several strategies have 
been implemented including viral, non-viral and physical methods. Utilizing viral vectors such as adenoviral 
vectors (AdVs) and lentiviral vectors (LVs) hold limitations in terms of carcinogenesis, constrained 
encapsulating capacity, and immunogenicity. Furthermore, integration of Cas9 into the target genome 
using LVs have proved to be difficult and is not yet completely resolved. However, regarding gene knock-in, 
viral vectors are superior compared with other approaches [32]. In contrast, non-viral vector systems offer 
a safer delivery method and these includes cationic polymeric materials, liposomes, and cell-penetrating 
peptides (CPPs). Their capability to deliver large size components, their reduced toxicity, easy preparation, 
==PAGE:21==
 
12 
 
and fewer off-targets effects compared with viral systems are beneficial. Based hereon, these types of 
delivery systems are chosen over viral systems. Due to the anionic nature of RNA and Cas9, the 
encapsulation efficiency into the cationic polymers is higher. Zuris et al. demonstrated successful delivery 
of gRNA and Cas9 ribonucleoprotein (RNP) complexes to mouse hair cells by cationic liposomes with 
achievement of 80% gene modification [33]. Cas9 nuclease can also be delivered into cells via physical 
methods such as microinjection and electroporation. This approach is advantageous owing to its target 
specificity, high reproducibility, and simplicity. One way to achieve efficient delivery and subsequent DNA 
cleavage in the future would be to prepare donor template and gRNA into viral vectors for constant 
expression, while the Cas9-protein should be incorporated into non-viral vectors [32]. Altogether, it is 
suggested that administration of both non-viral and viral vectors will be the most favorable approach for 
gene therapy based on the CRISPR-Cas system.  
Different classes and types of CRISPR-Cas systems exist, but the class II type II-A CRISPR system is the most 
streamlined version and frequently used as it only requires a single effector protein to exert its nuclease 
function, namely the Cas9-protein, which is a large 160kDa protein composed of 1368 amino acids [34]. 
This enzyme, found in Streptococcus pyogenes (SpyCas9), is the most extensively researched and 
characterized Cas-protein.  
Cas12 protein belongs to class II type V CRISPR systems and displays more dynamic applications such as 
epigenome editing. Cas12 can process the precursor crRNA by itself without requirement for tracrRNA, 
which makes chemical synthesis of its gRNA more cost effective. Unlike Cas9, Cas12 is capable of cutting 
both dsDNA and ssDNA. Despite its advantages and unique features including fewer off-target effects and 
an alternative PAM sequence, the CRISPR-Cas12 system has not been well utilized. The system faces several 
flaws like not having complete nickase function as it generates staggered DSBs and it only promotes HR 
rather than both NHEJ and HR repair mechanisms [35].  
Cas14 protein is composed of 400-700 amino acids and is thus much smaller than other characterized Cas 
proteins. Similar to Cas9, Cas14 requires both crRNA and tracrRNA. However, Cas14 is capable of editing 
dsDNA, ssDNA, and RNA. A PAM is not required for cleavage of ssDNA as it only utilizes T-rich sequences 
which allow editing of more targeted genomic sequences than Cas9. The less limiting PAM requirements 
also result in a more specific cleavage efficiency of Cas14 compared with Cas9 and Cas12. Additionally, the 
small size of the protein allows easier delivery than other Cas proteins for targeting any tissue. Cas14 shows 
promising results as a diagnostic system for high-fidelity genome editing of ssDNA and it could provide an 
advanced platform in the future for viral screening. Though holding various advantages, extensive studies 
of Cas14 must be conducted in the future to demonstrate and ensure its applicability [34].

1.3 Spectroscopic methods to study interactions of nucleic acids  
Being able to monitor and study nucleic acids and their interactions with other biomolecules such as 
peptides and proteins is of outmost importance in the field of gene therapy. Many important biological 
processes and the functions of living cells are mostly executed and regulated by proteins and nucleic acid-
protein interactions [38]. The quality and strength of nucleic acid-protein interactions are determined by 
interactions between amino acids and nucleotides by means of physical forces including electrostatic and 
hydrophobic interactions, hydrogen bonds, and van der Waals forces [39]. Spectroscopy is the study of 
==PAGE:23==
 
14 
 
absorption and emission of light by a molecule and optical properties of a compound are used to examine 
how a sample is changing under different conditions. Spectral intensity or emission/absorption maxima can 
change, when a sample is subjected to variations in temperature, concentration, interactions with other 
molecules, pH, or polarity. Hence, optical properties of a fluorophore allow a comprehensive study to 
investigate nucleic acid interactions with proteins and provide a fluorescent read out of nucleic acid 
environment [40]. Optical methods are non-invasive, provide high-resolution and real-time information, 
and are cost effective.  
1.3.1 Steady-state fluorescence spectroscopy  
Fluorescence is a type of photoluminescence and describes the ability of a compound to emit a photon 
upon absorption of light and is caused by photons exciting a molecule. A substance absorbs a photon in the 
singlet ground state S0, which is the lowest energy state of a molecule. Absorption induces promotion of an 
electron in the substance to the first singlet excited state S1 and upon excitation, the substance returns to 
the ground state under emission of a photon (see Jablonski, figure 13). Emission only happens from the 
lowest excited state. Internal conversion between excited states is non-radiative. The emitted photon is of 
lower energy and observed at a higher wavelength than the photon that was absorbed [41]. Absorption of 
light increases the energy of a molecule, whereas emission of light decreases its energy. The electron 
structure of a compound determines it fluorescent properties. The specific parts of molecules having the 
ability to undergo detectable electron transitions are referred to as chromophores. Chromophores are 
characterized by the presence of π electrons, heteroatoms, and symmetry. 
Phosphorescence is another type of photoluminescence related to fluorescence. It is emission of a photon 
during a transition between states with different spin, e.g., T1 → S0. 
 
Figure 13: Jablonski diagram. 
==PAGE:24==
 
15 
 
Fluorescence spectroscopy is a technique based on fluorescence properties of the sample to be analyzed, 
and is commonly used for identification of or changes in molecules. Steady-state fluorescence spectra are 
obtained when a fluorophore is excited by a constant photon flow followed by emission of fluorescence, 
and the fluorescent intensity is detected as a function of wavelength [40]. To record an emission spectrum, 
the excitation wavelength is fixed at a wavelength known to excite the sample and the emission is scanned 
through the different wavelengths. Likewise, an excitation spectrum can be recorded. The excitation 
spectrum shows what wavelengths the sample uses to produce its fluorescence. The emission wavelength 
is fixed at a wavelength where the sample is known to emit radiation and excitation is scanned through the 
different wavelengths. The emission and excitation spectra of a chromophore are mirror images of each 
other, and the excitation spectrum occurs at lower wavelengths than the emission spectrum. Thus, 
excitation spectra are specific to a given species, whereas an absorption spectrum measures all the 
absorbing species in a sample. The excitation spectrum has similar peaks as the absorbance spectrum, but 
the absorbance spectrum may have more peaks [42].  
One of the major advantages of fluorescence spectroscopy is the high sensitivity of fluorescence detection 
that can be achieved even with minute amounts of material. Compared with another major structural 
investigation technique such as X-ray crystallography that only provides a snapshot of a given system, 
fluorescent probes are particularly valuable for real-time imaging of nucleic acids and allow the study of 
progress in a system [43]. Other dynamic imaging methods are transmission electron microscopy (TEM), 
transmission electron cryomicroscopy (cryoTEM), and nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR). 
TEM is a high-resolution imaging technique in which a beam of electrons is transmitted through an ultrathin 
sample (<100 nm) to produce an image, which is formed based on the interactions between the electrons 
and the sample [44]. TEM is capable of capturing fine details and providing a high-resolution image on the 
sub-nanometer scale [45]. TEM finds application in drug discovery, cancer research, and cell biology. 
cryoTEM is a specialized form of TEM where samples are examined at cryogenic temperatures followed by 
image processing. Samples are frozen in a process called vitrification, that prevents formation of ice crystals 
in the biological molecule as ice crystals can damage the structure [46]. This method offers several 
advantages over traditional TEM and other imaging techniques like X-ray crystallography or NMR. cryoTEM 
allows the study of specimens in a near-native, hydrated state and to examine large, complex structures 
that may not be amenable to crystallization or NMR studies. cryoTEM is an essential tool in structural 
biology for determining high-resolution structures of proteins and macromolecular complexes [47].  
NMR is a versatile analytical tool applied in various fields such as chemistry, physics, and biology. The 
technique is based on the principle of magnetic resonance, where interactions of atomic nuclei with an 
==PAGE:25==
 
16 
 
external magnetic field are examined when exposed to radiofrequency (RF) pulses. Commonly applied 
nuclei are 1H or 13C. The corresponding NMR spectrum holds information about the chemical structure and 
dynamics of molecules in the sample [48]. Compared with X-ray crystallography and cryoTEM, NMR is 
advantageous by means of its capability to examine biomolecules in their native, solution environment and 
can be applied to systems that may not form crystals or be suitable for cryoTEM. However, NMR has 
limitations in resolving the structures of very large macromolecules and complexes due to the complexity 
of the spectra and the requirement for isotopic labeling [49].  
1.3.1.1 
Fluorescence quenching  
Fluorescence quenching describes any process that decreases the fluorescence intensity of a molecule. 
Hence, a fluorescence quencher is a substance that absorbs light but does not emit light. Consequently, the 
efficiency of the quencher is distance dependent. It suppresses the emission from an excited fluorophore 
by absorbing its emitted energy, only when they are in proximity. When far away from each other, the 
fluorophore will fluoresce. A great variety of quenchers exist, that have different quenching ranges to cover 
the majority of the visible spectrum [50]. Dual-labeled DNA or RNA probes with fluorophore and quencher 
can be used to monitor specific events, when the reporter and quencher are placed at strategical sites in 
the oligonucleotide so that a change in their distance will yield a change in fluorescence and the specific 
event can be monitored. 
1.3.1.2 
Fluorescence resonance energy transfer 
Fluorescence resonance energy transfer (FRET) is an imaging technology for investigation of intermolecular 
interactions such as nucleic acid-protein, protein-protein or ligand-receptor interactions. The technique is 
very sensitive and relies on photon delocalization from a donor fluorophore to an acceptor fluorophore 
[51]. Hence, the molecules must be tagged with different fluorophores to record FRET, they should be in 
close proximity, and spectral overlap between the fluorescence emission spectrum of the donor and the 
excitation spectrum of the acceptor is required [52]. When the donor receives energy from a light source, it 
is excited and this energy is then transferred to the acceptor, which emits light at a longer wavelength. 
Thus, the donor’s fluorescence intensity is reduced, while the emission intensity of the acceptor is 
increased. 
Single-molecule FRET (smFRET) is a variant of FRET to study nucleic acid-protein interactions. An 
oligonucleotide is immobilized to a surface and dual-labeled with two different dyes to measure changes in 
FRET occurring due to nucleic acid conformational changes when a protein is bound. Low FRET is observed 
when no protein is bound, while high FRET is observed when a protein is bound to the oligonucleotide since 
the binding induces a conformational change in the oligonucleotide resulting in the dyes being in close 
proximity as depicted in figure 14 [53].

1.3.1.3 
Molecular beacons 
Molecular beacons are composed of single-stranded fluorescently labeled oligonucleotide probes with a 
stem-loop structure that recognize and report the presence of specific nucleic acids in a homogenous 
solution [54]. A probe sequence complementary to a target sequence is situated in the loop and is flanked 
by the stem portion. A fluorophore is covalently linked to one end of the stem, while a quencher is 
covalently linked to the other end. Hence, molecular beacons do not fluoresce when the target is absent as 
the closed stem structure eliminates the ability of the fluorophore to fluoresce. When the target sequence 
is present, a probe-target hybrid is formed through hybridization of the beacon to the target sequence. This 
hybrid is more stable than the stem hybrid and forces the beacon to undergo a conformational change in 
which the stem dissociates leading to separation of the fluorophore and quencher. By this means, the 
fluorescent ability of the fluorophore is restored and it will emit fluorescence (see figure 15).  
Thus, molecular beacons are highly sensitive and sequence specific and widely applied for sequence 
detection in in vitro studies [55].

1.3.2 UV-VIS spectroscopy  
The ultraviolet (UV) part of the electromagnetic spectrum ranges from 190 nm to 400 nm, whereas the 
visible (VIS) part ranges from 400 to 780 nm. UV-VIS spectroscopy provides an absorption spectrum of a 
compound. Species analyzed by UV-VIS spectroscopy should be chromophores, i.e., they absorb 
electromagnetic radiation in the ultraviolet or visible region of the spectrum and include inorganic ions of 
transition metals and organic molecules with conjugated π-systems such as aromatics. When energy from 
UV or visible light is absorbed by the chromophore, one of its electrons jump from the ground state of 
lower energy to the first singlet excited state of higher energy [56]. UV light has shorter wavelengths and 
thereby higher energy radiation than visible light. When a fluorescently tagged nucleic acid adopts a new 
conformational structure, the resulting change in environment around the fluorescent chromophore can 
lead to its UV-VIS spectrum being altered [57]. 
A typical UV-VIS spectrophotometer consists of a light source, a monochromator, and a detector. Two 
different light sources are often used. A deuterium lamp emits electromagnetic radiation in the UV region

19 
 
of the electromagnetic spectrum, while a tungsten lamp is applied for the visible part of the spectrum. The 
monochromator spreads the beam of light into its component wavelengths, and focuses the desired 
wavelength on the sample cell. In a single beam instrument (depicted in figure 17), one wavelength is 
analyzed at a time. In a double beam instrument, the monochromator is followed by a splitter and a series 
of mirrors to get the beam to the analyte sample and a reference sample, which yields more accurate 
readings. As the light passes through the sample cell, it reaches the detector, where the intensity of 
transmitted light is recorded [58].

1.3.3 Fluorescent polarization and anisotropy 
Fluorescence polarization (FP) is a powerful tool to study molecular interactions by monitoring changes in 
the apparent size of a fluorescently-labeled molecule, referred to as tracer or ligand. It is used to analyze 
molecular binding events in solution such as receptor-ligand, nucleic acid-protein, or peptide-protein 
interactions, and provides a direct, nearly instantaneous measure of the ratio between the bound state and 
==PAGE:29==
 
20 
 
the free state of the tracer. It is a very sensitive technique that offers measurements into the low picomolar 
range of labeled tracers. Samples are analyzed to assess the effect on binding by changes such as pH, 
temperature, and polarity. Because FP is a time-resolved technique, it is often applied in kinetic analysis of 
association and dissociation experiments [59].  
FP measurements are based on how fast a fluorescent molecule rotates in solution during its fluorescence 
lifetime (the period between excitation and emission of a photon), when excited with plane-polarized light. 
The fluorescence intensities are measured with a polarizer oriented parallel and perpendicular relative to 
the polarization of the excitation light as the light emitted by a fluorophore has unequal intensities along 
different axes of polarization. If a fluorophore is excited with vertically polarized light, the emitted light will 
retain more or less of that polarization depending on the speed of the fluorophore’s rotation. Small 
molecules tumble rapidly in solution and the emitted light is largely depolarized. Conversely, if the tracer is 
bound by a large molecule, the effective molecular volume is increased resulting in slow rotation. This 
means that the emitted light is in the same plane as the excitation energy and more of the emitted light 
retains the polarization (see figure 18). The free state of the fluorescent molecule has a low intrinsic 
polarization value, whereas the bound state has a high intrinsic polarization value. The measured 
polarization is a weighted average of the two values and gives a direct measure of the fraction of bound 
tracer [60].

1.4 Chemically modified guide RNA: Synthesis approaches and properties  
All currently applied gene therapeutics contain modified nucleotides. This is to extend life time and to 
enhance target binding affinity and specificity of the nucleic acids. Similarly, guide RNA has been previously 
chemically modified by introducing 2’-O-Methyl, 2’-F and phosphorothioate modifications to the 
oligonucleotide resulting in increased editing efficiency, higher resistance to nucleases, and enhanced 
stability [63]. Furthermore, gRNA has been modified by introducing a fluorescent tag. Nucleobases 
modified with advanced fluorescent dyes that respond to their local environment by the changing of their 
fluorescence parameters are considered valuable reporters for experimental, clinical, and diagnostic 
applications [64]. Fluorescently tagged RNA can enable identification and subsequent enrichment of gene-
edited cells as well as detecting nucleic acid-based biomarkers associated with viral, bacterial, and non-
infectious diseases through diagnostics tests. By the same techniques, cleavage activity of CRISPR-Cas9 can 
be studied [65]. This approach is highly specific as the specificity arises from complementary base paring 
between DNA-RNA or RNA-RNA [66]. 
Investigating in depth Cas9–guide RNA interactions is an exciting topic that could provide with new valuable 
clues for gene editing research and therapy and advance our understanding of CRISPR’s molecular 
mechanisms. Fluorescence is a highly sensitive technique that can provide with diverse read out for specific 
tracking of nucleic acid interactions, for example with shifts of emission maxima, changes in emission 
intensity and with fluorescence polarization. Due to the weak intrinsic fluorescence of natural nucleobases, 
their use as emissive reporters in studies of dynamic structure and functions of nucleic acids is limited. 
Hence, significant modifications are required to turn them into sensitive probes for chemical and biological 
applications. Fluorescent nucleobase analogues have emerged as a powerful tool for studying nucleic acids 
and their interactions with other biomolecules. Fluorescently labelled base analogues can easily be 
incorporated into oligonucleotides and are of great importance for detection of nucleic acid location and 
environment in biophysical assays [67]. 
Fluorescent nucleoside analogues (FNAs) are chemically modified DNA or RNA with improved fluorescence 
properties. Over several decades, FNAs have served as diagnostic and research tools for tracking complex 
nucleic acid interactions in a dynamic fashion. The modifications can be made to preserve Watson-Crick 
base-pairing, called canonical pairing, or more drastic modifications to the nucleobase can be introduced, 
yielding a non-canonical design [12]. Canonical nucleobase analogues retain the natural scaffold and allow 
hydrogen-bonding with complementary bases by retention of at least two Watson-Crick hydrogen-bonding 
==PAGE:32==
 
23 
 
groups after implementing a modification. Hence, these analogues are constrained by the natural structure 
of the purine or pyrimidine, but they have potential to act as an enzyme substrate. Conversely, non-
canonical nucleobase analogues are not restricted by the natural molecular skeleton of purines or 
pyrimidines, but this can limit their recognition by proteins and enzymes. As these analogues do not have 
to obey Watson-Crick base-pairing, a broad range of fluorescent alterations can be explored.  
In general, an ideal FNA should mimic the natural nucleosides in order to minimize disturbance in the 
labelled oligonucleotide sequence. Therefore, retaining size and hydrogen-bonding pattern is preferred. 
Based on their structural relationship with the natural nucleoside, the FNAs are classified into four different 
categories; isomorphic, expanded, extended, and chromophoric, see figure 19. Isomorphic nucleosides 
have the highest resemblance with the natural nucleosides. They differ from their natural counterpart by 
the presence of small substituents or by the number and position of heteroatoms in the heterocyclic purine 
or pyrimidine core. Expanded nucleosides contain an additional aromatic ring on their nucleobase core. 
Extended nucleosides have a fluorophore attached to the nucleobase via a chemical linker. Lastly, in 
chromophoric nucleosides the nucleobase is replaced with an aromatic chromophore, that displays large 
structural differences compared with the natural base [68].

Incorporation of fluorescent nucleobases into oligonucleotides can be achieved following different 
approaches. They are commonly divided into three categories: (1) direct oligonucleotide synthesis, where a 
fluorescently labelled nucleoside is incorporated into the sequence via phosphoramidite chemistry (figure 
20); (2) post-synthetic or post-transcriptional modification achieved by a two-step process, where 
introduction of a functional group acting as a reactive handle is followed by fluorescent labelling of the 
==PAGE:33==
 
24 
 
oligonucleotide via conjugation strategies such as click chemistry, maleimides, or thiols (figure 20); and (3) 
enzymatic incorporation using primers containing fluorescent nucleotide [12]. All categories present 
advantages and disadvantages. The major advantages of the direct synthesis approach are its convenience 
and efficiency, which enable site-specific introduction of practically any structure at any position into the 
oligonucleotide strand. The drawbacks of this method are its high cost in combination with the fact that the 
fluorescent probe should be stable to survive oligonucleotide deprotection conditions, which limits the 
structural diversity of applicable probes [69]. Additionally, this method is restricted to produce modified 
DNA with length of <100 nt and RNA with length of <50 nt [12]. The post-synthetic implementation offers 
site-specific labelling of oligonucleotides at virtually any position. A great weakness of this method is the 
necessity of applying mild reaction conditions compatible with nucleic acids, which limits the number of 
suitable linking moieties. Finally, the enzymatic strategy allows inexpensive labelling of oligonucleotides 
and the length of the nucleic acid is not limited by this approach. However, the incorporation of 
fluorophores can be challenging due to large modifications, that are generally not tolerated as substrates of 
the enzyme [70]. 
 
Figure 20: Implementations of fluorescent probes in nucleic acids: A) Direct oligonucleotide synthesis. B) Post-synthetic modification. 
An ideal fluorophore displays several features with the main ones being high fluorescence quantum yield, 
high photostability, environmentally sensitive, and minimal autofluorescence. High autofluorescence can 
lead to high background signal resulting in high signal-to-noise ratios in the lower wavelength region of the 
visible spectrum. Doing optical investigations utilizing FNAs, one must ensure that the autofluorescence of 
the biomolecule in question does not interfere with the fluorescence of the FNAs. In this case, the 
spectrum will be mixed up and the fluorescent read-outs will be difficult to differentiate from one another. 
A way to avoid autofluorescence from the surrounding environment is to use probes with emission in the 
==PAGE:34==
 
25 
 
far-red or near infrared (NIR) region of the visible spectrum, i.e., 600-750 nm. These types of probes are 
emerging as they are highly sought-after due to their minimal autofluorescence and good resolution [43]. 
Moreover, it is crucial to have and in-depth knowledge of the photophysics of the FNA in order to properly 
interpret any spectroscopic variations in terms of structural or environmental changes [68]. 
At the present time, 2-aminopurine (2AP), a purine analogue of adenine and guanine, is the most 
commonly used fluorescent probe to study nucleic acid dynamics [68]. 2AP is advantageous due to its 
structural similarity with natural nucleobases, its sensitivity towards interbase interactions, and its 
commercial availability [71]. However, some of its photophysical properties leave room for improvement. 
Low fluorescence intensity in combination with short fluorescence lifetime (<100 ps) and hence, low 
quantum yields often require ultrafast lasers and long data-acquisition times. 
Unfortunately, a great amount of fluorescent nucleobase analogues is quenched by the DNA duplex upon 
incorporation into dsDNA [67]. Even though many attempts to create an ideal fluorophore have been done, 
many of them still prove insufficient and therefore, there is a pressing need for development of new 
fluorescent base analogues with enhanced photophysical properties in order to effectively study nucleic 
acid structure and interactions.  
This project will emphasize synthesis, characterization, and applications of isomorphic fluorescent 
nucleobases for the study of RNA-protein interactions in the CRISPR-Cas9 complex.  
1.5 Preparing modified nucleobases with Stille coupling reaction  
In the late 1970s, John K. Stille and co-workers discovered the Stille reaction and since then, transition 
metal catalysis has emerged into a rapidly expanding field. Today, the Stille reaction is established as one of 
the most general and selective palladium-catalyzed cross-coupling reactions. It provides a wide range of 
applications as it is tolerant towards most functional groups and displays high selectivity [72]. Thus, it 
provides an easy an effective way for synthesizing complex molecules. Furthermore, many substrates are 
commercially available and substrates can be easily made in the lab via a one-step synthesis procedure if a 
specific structure is required. The reaction involves the coupling of two organic compounds under the 
formation of a new C-C bond, which is the driving force of the reaction as C-C bonds are much stronger 
than C-Pd bonds. The Stille reaction is widely used for joining sp2 carbon atoms. The coupling occurs 
between an organostannane and an organic electrophile, usually aryl or vinyl halides, but allyl or alkynyl 
halides can also be employed. Especially vinyl iodides and bromides are applied with vinyl iodides normally 
preferred as they typically react faster and under milder conditions than bromides. The organostannane is a 
tin atom bonded to three alkyl groups in addition to an aryl, allyl, or alkenyl group.  
==PAGE:35==
 
26 
 
 
Figure 21: Catalytic cycle of the Stille coupling reaction. 
The reaction includes several steps; oxidative addition, transmetalation, and reductive elimination. A 
general mechanism of the catalytic cycle is depicted in figure 21. The catalyst is a Pd(0) complex, where 
palladium is bonded to phosphine ligands (Pd(PPh3)4), referred to as “L”. Generally, palladium chemistry 
involves two oxidation states, where the lower Pd(0) is the active state, as these complexes are highly 
reactive compared with Pd(II) complexes, which are usually more stable. The change in oxidation state is a 
result of gaining two new ligands with a formal negative charge. Tetrakis(triphenylphosphine)palladium(0) 
is commonly used as the catalyst in C-C coupling reactions. 
The first step in the catalytic cycle is activation of the electrophile by oxidative addition to a palladium 
catalyst. Hence, the metal is inserted into a single bond to yield a species which is very reactive towards C-C 
π bonds. The subsequent step is transmetalation of the organopalladium intermediate with the 
organostannane forming another organopalladium intermediate. The aryl is transferred from the tin 
compound to the palladium, while the halogen is released from palladium, thus leaving with the organotin 
compound. Finally, the catalytic cycle is completed with a reductive elimination removing the metal, 
releasing the product with a newly formed C-C single bond, while regenerating the reactive catalyst Pd(0). 
For the elimination to occur, the ligands being eliminated must occupy cis coordination [72].  
One main advantage of the versatile Stille reaction is its high selectivity compared with other cross-coupling 
reactions. This is due to tin compounds being less reactive than other organometallic compounds like boron 
compounds that are used in the Suzuki coupling. Hence, the Stille coupling can be performed under milder 
reaction conditions, which can lead to increased reaction yields. The Stille reaction is not commonly applied 
in the pharmaceutical industry, since many organostannanes are toxic. Thus, there is a risk of getting 
residual tin in pharmaceutical products, which much be avoided. For research purposes that do not involve 
cell studies and allow traces of Sn, the Stille coupling is a great reaction. As an alternative, the Suzuki 
==PAGE:36==
 
27 
 
coupling reaction can be used. The Suzuki reaction is another palladium-catalyzed cross-coupling reaction 
utilizing an organoboron species such as boronic acid or boronic ester and an organohalide to form a 
carbon-carbon single bond. The reaction is conducted under basic conditions to activate the boron atom. 
The Suzuki reaction offers several advantages including commercially available common borane substrates, 
mild reaction conditions, and decreased toxicity as boronic acids are less toxic and safer than organotin 
[73]. However, the Suzuki coupling faces some limitations as the boronic acid is not compatible with all 
functional groups. Additionally, commercially available borane substrates do not encompass all desired 
functional groups or structures, some alkyl borates do not show reactivity and if the base is omitted many 
side products are formed [74]. 
1.6 Solid-phase oligonucleotide synthesis of RNA 
In the 1960s, Bruce Merrifield invented the solid-phase chemical synthesis. This technique finds 
applications in various areas of chemistry including peptide synthesis, oligonucleotide synthesis, and 
oligosaccharide synthesis. It offers great advantages due to its quick and automated processes [75]. Thus, it 
is possible to efficiently make synthetic DNA and RNA that can be used in gene and gene-editing therapies. 
For oligonucleotide synthesis, synthetic oligonucleotide sequences are built from solid supports, nucleotide 
by nucleotide. The purpose of the solid support is to ensure fast exchange of reagents between the 
synthesis steps and allowing for use of high excess of reagents to secure high yields at each coupling 
without purification between each step. Additionally, the solid support protects the nucleosides from the 
reagents used. The synthesis proceeds in the 3’- to 5’-direction as opposed to enzymatic DNA biosynthesis 
that proceeds from 5’- to 3’-direction. The automated oligonucleotide synthesis utilizes a phosphoramidite 
strategy in which every nucleotide has a build-in phosphoramidite on the 3’-end, while the 5’-end is 
protected by a 4,4’-dimethoxytrityl (DMT) group to prevent polymerization during each step (see figure 22). 
The method is carried out on solid support held between filters, where the reactions occur in small columns 
that enable all reagents and solvents to pass through freely [75]. 
 
Figure 22: Synthesis of RNA phosphoramidite building block for solid-phase oligonucleotide synthesis. 2’-OH is protected with tert-
butyldimethylsilyl (TBDMS) protecting group. 
==PAGE:37==
 
28 
 
The 2’-hydroxyl group in RNA is protected with tert-butyldimethylsilyl (TBDMS). This is essential to avoid 
unwanted side reactions. The protecting group is kept throughout the synthesis, but removed in the end. 
Additionally, it is important that no migration of the protecting group from 2’-OH to 3’-OH takes place 
during the solid-phase synthesis. Solid-phase synthesis of RNA is more difficult and requires more time 
compared with DNA synthesis. This is due to steric hindrance caused by the introduction of the large, bulky 
protecting group to the RNA nucleoside, which is not necessary in DNA synthesis.  
The RNA synthesis cycle consists of a series of four steps; detritylation, coupling, capping, and oxidation. 
For every cycle, one additional nucleotide is added to the existing oligo sequence immobilized on the resin. 
Different types of solid supports exist, but controlled pore glass (CPG) and cross-linked polystyrene beads 
are often applied as they have proved to be the most useful. The pore size and loading of the solid support 
can limit the length of the synthesized oligo. This is because the growing oligonucleotide blocks the pores 
and reduces the diffusion of the reagents through the matrix [75]. Moreover, the size of the solid support is 
also important for the length of the oligonucleotide. Consequently, the technique is limited to sequences in 
a specific size range. Generally, the method is restricted to synthesizing DNA with length of <100 nt and 
RNA with length of <70 nt. If longer sequences are desired, small fragments can be assembled by ligation 
after synthesis. 
Structures of RNA phosphoramidites applied in this project are nucleobase protected natural RNA 
phosphoramidites and modified 2’-OMe RNA phosphoramidites, which are commercially available. Primary 
amino groups on A, C and G are nucleophilic and must be protected during oligonucleotide synthesis. All 
phosphoramidites are depicted in figure 23. 
 
Figure 23: Structures of RNA phosphoramidites applied in this project. 
The linker used in this project is an ester-functionalized CUTAG CPG linker, which comprises the universal 
linker “UnyLinker”. The linker must be stable towards all reagents, yet cleavable under certain conditions. 
The linker bears a DMT protecting group that is deprotected initializing the first cycle. Once deprotected, a 
==PAGE:38==
 
29 
 
free hydroxyl group is unmasked and ready for coupling with the first nucleoside phosphoramidite via the 
nucleoside’s 3’-end.  
A complete overview of the automated solid-phase oligonucleotide synthesis of RNA is depicted below in 
figure 24. 
 
Figure 24: Automated solid-phase oligonucleotide synthesis cycle of RNA. 
Oligonucleotide synthesis is initiated by detritylation in order to free the 5’-OH for subsequent 
phosphitylation. Detritylation is an acid catalyzed SN1 reaction using reagents such as trichloroacetic acid 
(TCA). The released carbocation is orange. Next, the support-bound nucleoside is subjected to a 
phosphitylation, where a phosphoramidite in shape of the next nucleoside reacts with the 5’-alcohol via a 
SN2 reaction. The nucleoside phosphoramidite monomer displays high reactivity towards nucleophiles, and 
is additionally activated by tetrazole prior to formation of a new phosphorus-oxygen bond [9]. The reaction 
is energetically favorable and generates a support-bound phosphite triester [76]. The cycle proceeds with a 
==PAGE:39==
 
30 
 
capping step as there are few unreacted support-bound oligonucleotides with free 5’-hydroxyl groups 
present. In order to prevent these from taking part in the next cycle, they are blocked by an acetylation 
reaction. If unreacted 5’-hydroxyl groups are left unchecked, deletion mutations in the target 
oligonucleotide can occur resulting in failure sequences lacking one base. To enhance the stability of the 
acid labile phosphite triester (P(III)), this species is oxidized to phosphate (P(V)). Before entering a new 
cycle, the DMT protecting group on the newly added monomer must be removed. The oligonucleotide 
synthesis cycle is then repeated to produce the required RNA oligonucleotide (supporting mechanisms and 
structures regarding solid-phase oligonucleotide synthesis can be viewed in appendix D). Very high 
stepwise coupling yields are necessary to obtain a good overall yield. The overall yield of oligonucleotide 
synthesis is dependent on the length of the oligo sequence and the coupling efficiency and expressed as 
𝑐𝑜𝑢𝑝𝑙𝑖𝑛𝑔 𝑒𝑓𝑓𝑖𝑐𝑖𝑒𝑛𝑐𝑦𝑛−1, where n is the length of the oligo.  
Upon completing the desired sequence, the oligonucleotide must be cleaved off the solid support. The 
cleavage proceeds via two steps; ester hydrolysis followed by a self-cleavage mechanism where an 
intramolecular cyclization occurs. Cleavage is carried out with base and despite facilitating cleavage, the 
base also promotes cyanoethyl removal and nucleobase deprotection. The tert-butyldimethylsilyl (TBDMS) 
protecting group on the 2’-OH group is an orthogonal protecting group and is resistant to the basic 
conditions. Instead, utilizing a fluoride ion source can selectively remove the silyl ether over acid labile 
groups. It removes the silyl ether because F-Si is a much stronger bond than Si-O.  
1.6.1 Purification of oligonucleotides  
Purification of newly synthesized oligonucleotide sequences is important to remove impurities and failure 
sequences. Different kinds of high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) are the preferred methods, 
where the crude oligonucleotides are separated according to polarity or charge [77]. In this project, reverse 
phase-HPLC (RP-HPLC) has been applied with divinylbenzene polymer as the stationary phase, which is 
specifically designed to oligomers. If apolar hydrocarbons like C18 was to be used as stationary phase, RNA 
would elute immediately as RNA is too polar to interact with the C18.  
Matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization time-of-flight mass spectrometry (MALDI-TOF MS) is a powerful 
tool to analyze masses of larger molecules such as oligonucleotides as well as identification and detection 
of molecules within a large mass range. A sample is added to a drop of matrix to form crystals, which are 
heated up by a laser to evaporate and ionize molecules which are then subjected to analysis in a TOF mass 
analyzer. During their flight into the mass analyzer, ions with different mass to charge (m/z) ratios are 
distributed in time, which separates ions based on differences in their m/z ratios [78].

1.7 Project aim 
With the purpose of providing and ensuring an efficient and safe gene therapy treatment utilizing the 
CRSIPR-Cas9 technology, in-depth examinations of the system are crucial to acquire an extended 
knowledge of the mechanisms involved in target recognition and DNA cleavage. However, the relationship 
between DNA unwinding, formation of the RNA/DNA hybrid, and coordination with nuclease domain 
movements is highly complex, and current understanding is not yet complete [79]. Thus, investigations of 
Cas9-RNA interaction and dynamics are sought to be explored herein with specific emphasis on seeding 
dsDNA, R-loop formation, and cutting dsDNA.  
This project is built on a hypothesis that rationally designed fluorescent nucleic acid monomers can reveal 
minor interactions of Cas9 nuclease with guide RNA while CRISPR-Cas9 performs its cutting function on 
dsDNA target. The hypothesis is based on accumulated scientific knowledge outlined above. Gaining such 
knowledge would provide critical steps towards understanding the molecular mechanism of RNA-guided 
DNA targeting by Cas9 and could potentially open up a path to improved performance in terms of efficacy 
and target specificity. Thus, this project outlines the initial efforts to design and conduct a mechanistic 
investigation of CRISPR-Cas9 to enhance its function. 
The project includes organic synthesis of a novel fluorescent nucleoside analogue as well as oligonucleotide 
synthesis, optical studies, and anisotropy measurements to assess the sensitivity of nucleosides to amino 
acid microenvironment. The fluorescent nucleoside is prepared by a Stille coupling and it has an extended 
π-system compared with a natural nucleobase in order to obtain a better fluorophore that could act as a 
spy dye. A pilot screening of different FNAs has been carried out to test the structures in various conditions 
that resemble different potential interactions established between gRNA and amino acids in Cas9, so we 
can search for an optimal FNA to act as a spy dye. The screening included absorption, emission, and 
excitation measurements. Related hereto, anisotropy measurements have been conducted to investigate 
the fluorophore’s ability to detect proteins. Fluorophores performing well in screenings will be 
incorporated into a guideRNA oligonucleotide via a phosphoramidite strategy. Ultimately, the kinetics of 
the Cas9-gRNA complex formation and dynamics will be studied in vitro in the presence of a Cas9 protein 
and target DNA, as illustrated in figure 25.

high resemblance with the natural nucleoside in order to retain hydrogen bonding ability. Following

2.1.3 Stille coupling with 2-(tributylstannyl)pyridine (strategy 3) 
Since strategy 1 and 2 for synthesizing a pyridine-containing fluorophore were not successful, a final

reaction times and an ether precipitation were utilized.  
For the synthesis of LB_2.4 via strategy 3, a controlled addition of Sn reagent in small portions was explored 
as opposed to using large excesses in the beginning of the synthesis like in strategy 1 and 2. The reaction 
showed incomplete conversion of starting material to product after 24 hours and hence, additional stannyl

was desired. Thus, extra stannyl reagent and Pd catalyst were added to the reaction. After further 48 hours, 
complete conversion was observed by TLC and the crude was purified by FCC.

yield of 30%.

opposite to each other as intensity for furan-dU will increase, while the intensity for Cl-Pyr-dU greatly 
decreases. Thus, we can obtain a clear read out in which the fluorophores confirm the response from each 
other. Furan-dU is preferred for detecting acidic environments, while Cl-Pyr-dU is preferred for basic 
environments.

The design principle of gRNAs was based on work conducted by Li et al. and redesigned by Master students 
Marie-Louise Wibom and Anna Jakobsen so they make a duplex with Cas9 [79]. It was decided to use a dual 
guide RNA. The first part of the strand is a 20 nt spacer that binds to a complementary DNA target, while 
the last part binds to tracrRNA. The last part constitutes the palindromic repeat region which ensures that 
the guide sequence is held in place inside the Cas9 protein. The sites for modifications have been

strategically selected. Lig1 is modified at position 4, hence, in the 5’-end, where there is an open structure 
and the RNA might not bind the target DNA sequence. Lig2 is modified at position 13 in the middle of the 
spacer sequence and is assumed to bind the DNA target sequence. Finally, the modification on Lig4 is 
placed in the end of the spacer sequence towards the repeat region, where an open structure is expected, 
as illustrated in figure 33. As 2AP is an analogue of adenine, 2AP is replacing adenine in the modified 
sequences. The 2’-OMe modification is installed to increase lifetime of the oligo by preventing degradation 
by ribonucleases (RNases). 2’-OMe modifications are completely non-toxic.

from the sequences of the RNA strands, all 2AP modification sites are right next to an uracil nucleotide, 
where the pyridine analogue should be positioned. Hence, when incorporating fluorescently labeled uracil 
analogues the rationale behind carefully selected modification sites for 2AP also applies for uracil

3 Future perspectives  
First and foremost, the synthesis of LB_2.4 should be repeated to obtain higher yield and purity. It would 
have been preferable to screen a larger selection of FNAs, but due to time limitations, an extended 
selection of FNAs were not synthesized nor tested. Hence, a further expansion of the FNA pilot screening is 
planned including novel FNAs and an aromatic additive such as PhOH. We plan to explore the potential of 
the following compounds; Pyr-dU, NH2-Pyr-dU, OH-Pyr-dU, CN-Pyr-dU, and thiazole-dU. These will be 
tested according to the screening protocol outlined herein. Additionally, the FNA screening of this project 
should be repeated to verify the reliability of the assay. If given more time, the synthesized RNA sequences 
==PAGE:59==
 
50 
 
would be properly purified using preparative RP-HPLC and subjected to screening with Cas9 to test for the 
most favorable positions of fluorophores. 
It would be interesting to investigate whether the synthesized fluorophore, LB_2.4, fulfills the aim of this 
project of revealing minor interactions of Cas9 nuclease with guide RNA. The efficiency of LB_2.4 as a 
fluorescence reporter would be verified by subjecting it to the FNA pilot screening. If great performance is 
achieved, phosphoramidites of this FNA will be made and they will be incorporated into gRNA sequences 
via solid-phase oligonucleotide synthesis. LB_2.4 and other selected reporters will then, after incorporation 
into Lig1-4, take part in an extended study with the goal of building a dynamic model that could elucidate 
Cas9-gRNA dynamics and explore the full potential of the spy dye approach. The screenings will be carried 
out with increasing level of full complex, i.e., first screening with buffer and guide followed by addition of 
Cas9 to form the ribonucleoprotein complex. Next, Mg2+ should be added to form the cutting complex as 
Mg2+ is required to obtain the active state. Finally, target DNA will be added to study cleavage mechanisms. 
Control experiments should be conducted with dead Cas9 (dCas9).  
4 Discussion  
Throughout the attempts on Stille couplings, it was a general trend that purity and yields were low. Due to 
the high polarity of sugars, their purification can be challenging. Hence, DMT-protected ribose moieties 
could be explored for the Stille coupling to aid with an easier purification. As an alternative, it could be 
considered to use a Suzuki coupling reaction instead. Furthermore, Wicke and Engels successfully 
demonstrated post-synthetic on column RNA labelling via a Stille cross-coupling reaction [85]. This 
approach could also be an option to explore that offers several advantages such as high conversion, high 
yields, and only economical amounts of reagents.  
It should be highlighted that FNA screenings conducted herein do not provide a clear picture of the 
fluorophores’ behavior in an amino acid environment. The studies are limited by use of small molecules 
which only resemble amino acids as they cannot be directly correlated to protein. However, small 
molecules can indicate the type of response expected from subsequent test with amino acids.  
In the synthesis of fluorescently modified RNA, it would have been preferable to include a positive control 
with a modification at a site known to bind Cas9 as reported from literature. Such a modification could be 
placed in the repeat region of the crRNA as it binds to tracrRNA in the dgRNA. It was planned to include the 
following structure as positive control:  
5’-r(mGmUmGAUAAGUGGAAUGCCAUGGUUUAAGAGCU-2AP-UGCUmGmGmA)-3’. 
Due to occupation of the Expedite instrument, this synthesis was postponed.  
==PAGE:60==
 
51 
 
When a dynamic model of Cas9 with fluorescently modified gRNA has been built and in vitro screenings of 
the full complex with target DNA have been completed, it would be interesting to compare the findings 
with already reported findings from the literature. Doudna et al. utilized cryogenic-electron microscopy 
(cryo-EM) to find that target capture of dsDNA requires Cas9 to unwind dsDNA at candidate sequences 
using an unknown ATP-independent mechanism. They showed that Cas9 sharply bends and undertwists 
dsDNA on PAM binding, thereby flipping DNA nucleotides out of the duplex and toward the gRNA for 
sequence interrogation [86]. Another study conducted by Nishimasu et al. revealed that the negatively 
charged sgRNA:target DNA heteroduplex is accommodated in a positively-charged groove at the interface 
between the REC and NUC lobes [87].  
Finally, before utilizing an advanced CRISPR-Cas9 system for therapeutic applications, the risk of 
immunogenicity must be considered. In vitro studies conducted by Roy showed that antibodies and T cells 
can recognize Cas9 antigens and elicit a host immune response [88]. Hence, this is an important parameter 
to assess when moving into clinical settings.  
5 Conclusion 
The aim of this thesis was to synthesize novel fluorescent nucleoside analogues and study their optical and 
fluorescent polarization properties to asses for their sensitivities towards amino acid microenvironment. 
The overall goal was to make them into phosphoramidites for subsequent incorporation into gRNA 
sequences to build a dynamic model for investigation of molecular dynamics in the CRISPR-Cas9 system 
that can be studied over time by utilizing steady-state fluorescence spectroscopy. In order to build such a 
model, FNAs should be properly positioned in gRNA to provide a clear read out. Therefore, another goal of 
this project was to synthesize different oligoribonucleotides with fluorescence modifications in various 
positions.  
A pyridine substituted FNA (LB_2.4) was successfully synthesized via a Stille coupling reaction with a yield 
of 30%. Optical measurements utilizing fluorescence and UV-VIS spectroscopy were performed on 
thiophene-dU, furan-dU, imidazole-dU, Cl-pyr-dU, and indole-dU. These studies revealed that a 
combination of furan-dU and Cl-pyr-dU will provide a clear read out for distinction of acidic vs. basic 
environment. Fluorescence polarization and fluorescence anisotropy studies of furan-dU and imidazole-dU 
showed that these fluorophores were particularly good at sensing protein.   
Four different 39-mer oligoribonucleotides (KAS348, KAS349, KAS350, and KAS356) were successfully 
synthesized using solid-phase oligonucleotide synthesis. KAS349, KAS350, and KAS356 were modified with 
2-aminopurine in positions 13, 4, and 18, respectively. KAS348 was a non-modified control. All RNA 
==PAGE:61==
 
52 
 
sequences remain to be purified. However, initial analyses with RP-HPLC and MALDI were carried out and 
confirmed the presence of all target strands.  
Due to time constraints, LB_2.4 has not taken part in any optical screenings nor have screenings of 
synthesized oligonucleotides taken place. As a result, no FNAs have been made into phosphoramidites. 
Consequentially, it has not been possible to complete the novel fluorescently labeled gRNA structures. 
Thus, no fluorophores are ready to enter dynamic studies with Cas9 and plans have been put forward to 
finish this part in near future.

6.1 General methods 
Starting materials, reagents and solvents were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich, Merck, ABCR and Glen 
Research and were not purified before use. All solvents were of HPLC quality. Anhydrous THF, DCM, MeCN,

All synthesized RNA strands were subjected to the same work-up procedure including cleavage from solid 
support, deprotection, and TBDMS removal. 
Once completing an RNA synthesis, the column was dried and emptied into an RNase and DNase free PCR-
clean vial together with filters. Cleavage from solid support and phosphate and nucleobase deprotection 
were achieved by incubating the sample with 1 mL methylamine (33% in EtOH) at 65°C for 2 hours followed 
by cooling down at rt for 15 min prior to cooling in fridge for 10 min. The mixtures were filtrated into 2 mL 
PCR-clean Eppendorf tubes before washing solid supports and filters with 0.5 mL of 50:50 96% 
ethanol/MilliQ water. The oligoribonucleotides were spin down and evaporated to dryness on a speed-vac 
overnight at 45°C.  
The TBDMS protecting groups in the 2’ positions of the oligonucleotides were removed by adding 100 µL 
desilylation reagent (375 µL N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone, 187.5 µL triethylamine, and 250 µL triethylamine 
trihydrofluorid) to the dry pellet. The mixture was put on thermomixer at 67°C for 3-4 hours.  
==PAGE:69==

After desilylation, 10 µL quenching reagent (1000 µL 3M sodiumacetate and 1 µL diethyl pyrocarbonate) 
was added, which made the mixture cloudy. Then 15 µL 5M NaClO4 was added to the mixture followed by 
addition of 1 mL cold acetone to precipitate the oligonucleotide. The mixture was left in the freezer for 30 
min.  
The samples were centrifuged at 1330 rpm for 10 min at 21°C. The supernatant was removed from the 
pellet and 1 mL cold acetone was added followed by vortexing, centrifugation, and removal of supernatant. 
Washing was repeated 3 times in total and after the last wash, acetone was removed from the pellet and 
its was dried on a speed-vac until the oligonucleotide pellet was completely dry. Finally, the oligonucleotide 
was dissolved in 1 mL MQ water and vortexed before measuring concentration and verifying identification 
and purity of the crude product using MALDI analysis and running RP-HPLC, respectively.  
RP-HPLC analytical runs were performed using two buffers; Buffer A (triethylammonium acetate (TEAA) in 
MQ water, 0.05 M, pH 7.4) and Buffer B (MeCN 75% in TEEA 0.0125 M). RNA strands were run at following 
gradient: 5-20% of buffer B depending on sample polarity in a total run time of 30 min at 75°C as melting 
temperature of the synthesized oligonucleotides are 63°C. Flow rate was 0.5 mL/min and UV for collection 
was 260 nm. Applied injection volume for analytical method was 10 µL.
