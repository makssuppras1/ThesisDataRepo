Abstract
The McEliece cryptosystem is a public key encryption method that uses error­
correcting codes to provide strong security, especially against quantum attacks.
We start with an introduction to the basic algebra and finite fields needed to un­
derstand coding theory. Then we cover cyclic codes and BCH codes to build
up to Goppa codes and show how to decode them based on Euclid’s Extended
Algorithm.
Thereby, we introduce the McEliece cryptosystem and its variant, the Niederre­
iter cryptosystem, which are both based on the binary Goppa codes. Finally, we
describe Stern’s attack and prove its theoretical feasibility to attack the McEliece
cryptosystem.
As interest in post­quantum cryptography continues to grow, this work provides a
foundation for further research into improving the efficiency and security of code­
based encryption schemes.

Introduction
In this chapter, we make a brief introduction to some fundamental concepts that
will be useful for the thesis. We will give out the definition for channel system and
codewords. After that, we will cover algebra and linear codes, which give a solid
mathematical foundation. Finally, we give a short overview of public­key systems,
which will help motivate the McEliece cryptosystem later.
1.1
Channel System and Codeword
Communicating information from one person to another is an essential activity
for mankind. But because the medium cannot always be perfect, the receiver
sometimes cannot get the same information as was sent originally. People have
come up with several methods to solve this problem and have formed the theory
of information and communication systems.
The medium over which information is sent, together with its characteristics, is
called the Channel. The characteristics here consist of an input alphabet X, an
output alphabet Y , and a transition probability function P.
We will give its mathematical definition as follows:
Definition 1.1.1 (Channel). A channel (X, Y ; P) consists of an alphabet X of input
symbols, an alphabet Y of output symbols, and for each x in X and y in Y , there
is a conditional probability p(y|x) that symbol y is received, where symbol x was
transmitted (this probability is independent for each transmission).
Clearly ∑
y∈Y p(y|x) = 1 for all x in X.
When transmitting the original information directly, the accuracy of each x is p(x|x) ≤
1. However, some operations could be used to increase the accuracy.

and P given by p(0|1) = p(1|0) = p and p(0|0) = p(1|1) = 1 −p, we define an

Definition 1.1.3 (communication System). An encoder is an algorithm that trans­
forms the original sequence of symbols A to a longer sequence but still only uses
symbols from the alphabet X, in such a way that redundancy is added.
An decoder is an algorithm that transforms the received sequence of symbols of
the output alphabet Y to the original message A. Sometimes the algorithm could
return ”fail”.
The channel, encoder, decoder, and the sender and receiver form a communi­
cation system.
There are also differences between encoders, such as whether the encoder has
memories for previous information. We will only focus on the situation in which
each encoding activity is independent.
Definition 1.1.4 (Codeword). If the encoding algorithm transforms each k­length
sequence of symbols to an n­length sequence from the input alphabet X inde­
pendently (without influence from any previous transformations), the resulting set
of |A|k elements that have length n is called a block code.
The elements of a block code are called codeword.
Let n be fixed and let the input and output symbols of the channel belong to an
alphabet Q that |Q| = q. We denote the set of n­length sequences as Qn. A
codeword C is a subset of Qn.
Definition 1.1.5 (Hamming Distance). The hamming distance d(x , y) between
x = (x1, x2, . . . , xn) and y = (y1, y2, . . . , yn) in Qn is given by:
d(x , y) = |{1 ≤i ≤n | xi ̸= yi}|
Hamming distance counts the number of coordinates where x and y differ. We can
easily prove that d(x , y) is a distance metric. As d(x , x) = 0 and d(x , y) = d(y , x)
holds for each x and y in Qn.
The triangle inequality also holds: d(x , z) ≤d(x , y)+d(y , z) for any x, y, z in Qn.
We can compare each coordinate for x, y and z. If xi ̸= zi, then either xi ̸= yi or
xi ̸= zi must holds. If xi = zi, the situation is trivial.

Algebraic Background and Finite Field Theory
The theory of finite fields plays a crucial role in algebraic coding theory. Therefore,
before introducing linear codes, we briefly review and summarize several useful
results from finite field theory in this section.
We assume that the reader is already familiar with these results, so most proofs
are omitted. For detailed explanations and proofs, one may consult standard al­
gebra texts such as Algebra by Artin [1].
Theorem 1.2.1. Let (Z/nZ)∗= {a ∈Z | gcd(a, n) = 1}. This set forms a finite
multiplicative group modulo n. For any q in (Z/nZ)∗, there exists a smallest posi­
tive integer m such that qm ≡1 (mod n). This m is called the multiplicative order
of q modulo n.
Theorem 1.2.2. Let m be a positive integer. The ring (Zm, +, ×) is a finite field
with m elements if and only if m is prime. Moreover, for p as a prime number, the
only finite field with p elements is (Zp, +, ×). It can be written as Fp.
Theorem 1.2.3 (Polynomial Ring). Let F be a field. Then the set of polynomials
F[x] with coefficients in F, under polynomial addition and multiplication, forms a
commutative ring with unity (i.e., the multiplicative identity).
Theorem 1.2.4 (Extended finite field). Let Fp be a finite field with p elements, and
let f(x) be a polynomial of degree n over Fp. Then the quotient ring Fp[x]/(f(x))
is a finite field with pn elements if and only if f(x) is irreducible over Fp.
Theorem 1.2.5. Let p be a prime number and q = pm, m ≥1. Then a finite field
of order q exists.
Definition 1.2.6 (Primitive element). An element α in a finite field Fq is called a
primitive n­th root of unity if its order is n. If α is a primitive (q −1)­st root of unity,
it is called a primitive element of Fq.
Corollary 1.2.7. For each α ∈Fq, it holds:
αqn = α.
Theorem 1.2.8 (Subfield of a finite field). Let Fq be a finite field of characteristic
p. Then Fq contains a subfield isomorphic to Fp = Zp.
Theorem 1.2.9. Let Fq be a finite field of characteristic p. Then q = pm, for some
integer m ≥1.

1.4
Public Key Encryption
Finally, we briefly introduce the concept of public­key encryption—the foundational
principle underlying the McEliece cryptosystem. Readers interested in more de­
tailed treatments of cryptography and public­key encryption are referred to acces­
sible texts such as The Joy of Cryptography [2].
The concept of public­key cryptography was introduced by Diffie and Hellman in
1976, revolutionizing the field of secure communication. And it has become a
fundamental concept in modern secure communication.
Public­key (sometimes also called asymmetric) encryption schemes use different
keys for encryption and decryption. The idea is that the encryption key can be
made public, so that anyone can send an encrypted message to the owner of the
cryptosystem without having to share any secret key in advance. However, the
decryption key remains private, to guarantee that only the designated owner can
decrypt.
A public key encryption consists of the following three algorithms:
KeyGen: The output is a pair (pk, sk) where pk is a public key and sk is a pri­
vate/secret key.
Enc: Takes a message m and pk as input, and outputs a ciphertext c.
Dec: Takes a ciphertext c and sk as input, and outputs the message m.
6
McEliece Cryptosystem
==PAGE:13==
To ensure correctness, the algorithms must satisfy the following:
For any m in the set of message M, and all (pk, sk) generated by KeyGen, we
have Dec(sk, Enc(pk, m) = m.
At the same time, it should be computationally infeasible to recover m from the
ciphertext c without access to the secret key sk.
Most public­key cryptosystems are based on mathematical problems that are easy
to compute in one direction but hard to reverse without secret information. Exam­
ples include integer factorization (RSA), discrete logarithms (Diffie­Hellman), and
decoding random linear codes (McEliece).
In this thesis, we focus on the McEliece cryptosystem, which is based on the hard­
ness of decoding a general linear code — a problem rooted in algebraic coding
theory.

In this section, we introduce cyclic codes. Cyclic code has an interesting con­
nection to polynomials. This property enables us to compute in polynomial form.
Therefore, some excellent algorithms for polynomials, like Euclid’s Extended Al­

This chapter will take a step into the McEliece Cryptosystem and one of its vari­

3.1
McEliece Cryptosystem
McEliece first introduced this cryptosystem in 1978 [5]. It is a public­key cryptosys­
tem based on Goppa codes. We will gradually set up the McEliece Cryptosystem
as follows.
Let Γ(L, G) be a binary Goppa code. The set L = {α1, α2, . . . , αn} is a set of n
distinct elements in some extended finite filed F2m. The polynomial G(x) is an
irreducible polynomial in Fqm[x] with deg G(x) = s. We require deg G(x) = s to be
2 ≤s ≤(n −1)/m.
We have already proved in Theorem 2.4.4 that the parameters for the Goppa code
are [n, ≥n −ms, ≥2s + 1]. As for the McEliece cryptosystem, the dimension for
the Goppa code we used should be strictly equal to n −ms.
Given L and G(x), we can easily compute a parity check matrix H for the Goppa
Code like in Example 2.4.3. And a generator matrix G can be computed accord­
ingly. (One could use matrix row operations to get a parity matrix with the form of
(−AT | In−k), a generator matrix can be (Ik | A).)
The McEliece secret key consists of an n×n permutation matrix P, a non­singular
k×k matrix P, and a generator matrix G for the Goppa Code Γ(L, G). Note that all
the elements in L, the irreducible polynomial G(x), P, S, and the generator matrix
G are randomly generated.
The McEliece public key is the k × n matrix SGP.
McEliece Encryption: Given a message m of length k, compute mSGP and add a
random error vector e of weight s and length n. So the final encrypted ciphertext
has the form c = mSGP + e.
McEliece Decryption: When receiving a ciphertext c, firstly, compute cP −1 =
mSG + eP −1. As P is a permutation matrix, the permuted error eP −1 still has
weight s. Meanwhile, mSG is a codeword in Γ(L, G), as S is a non­singular ma­
trix. We can decode cP −1 by applying the decoding algorithm for binary Goppa
codes to get t = mS. Finally, we compute tS−1 to get the original message m.

3.2
Niederreiter Cryptosystem
Niederreiter Cryptosystem [6] is a famous variant of McEliece Cryptosystem. Nieder­
reiter first used the generalized Reed­Solomon codes instead of the Goppa code
for his cryptosystem. However, the generalized Reed­Solomon codes were bro­
ken by Sidelnikov and Shestakov in 1992 [7].
But if we used the Goppa codes for the cryptosystem, it can provide the same
security as the McEliece Cryptosystem [8].
The Goppa code Γ(L, G) is defined the same as for the McEliece cryptosystem
with parameters [n, n−ms, ≥2s+1]. We will define the Niederreiter cryptosystem
as follows.
The Niederreiter secret key consists of an n × n permutation matrix P, a nonsin­
gular (n −k) × (n −k) matrix S, and a parity check matrix of Γ(L, G).
The Niederreiter public key is the (n −k) × n matrix SHP.
Niederreiter Encryption: Let m be a message of length n and weight s, the en­
crypted ciphertext c is computed by c = SHPmT.
Niederreiter Decryption: When receiving a ciphertext c, firstly, we can find a vec­
tor z such that HzT = S−1c by linear algebra. Note that z −mP T is a codeword
in Goppa code Γ(L, G), and mP T is an error vector of weight s. So we can de­
code z using the decoding algorithm of binary Goppa code to get the codeword
x. Correspondingly, the error vector mP T = z −x. Therefore, we can retrieve the
message vector m.

Stern’s Attack
This chapter will introduce a possible attack against the McEliece Cryptosystem.
Stern’s attack [9] is an algorithm aiming to find a low­weight codeword, which
can be used to break the McEliece Cryptosystem. We will prove its theoretical
feasibility to attack the McEliece cryptosystem. However, the attack on the original
McEliece parameters is computationally infeasible with current technology.

4.3.1
Practical Limitations
We have demonstrated the feasibility of using Stern’s attack to break the McEliece
cryptosystem in the above discussion. However, it is not realistic to apply Stern’s
attack against practical instances of McEliece.
Information Set Decoding(ISD), which is the basis of Stern’s Attack, has been for­
mally analyzed by many researchers. In particular, Coffey and Goodman (1990)
showed that, for virtually all linear codes, the complexity of ISD grows sub­exponentially,
with the number of operations approximately ∼o(2n/ log n) [10]. This level of com­
plexity becomes infeasible for the recommended McEliece parameters.
Given the classic parameters of the McEliece cryptosystem such that n = 1024,
s = 50, k = 524, breaking the system via Stern’s attack requires on the order of
260.6 operations [11]. And the cost continuously grows rapidly with larger parame­
ter sets, making Stern’s attack computationally infeasible with current technology.

5
Conclusion
Starting from linear codes and Goppa codes, this thesis explores the McEliece
cryptosystem, a code­based public­key encryption scheme renowned for its ef­
ficiency and strong resistance to attacks. We introduce Stern’s attack, which is
theoretically feasible but computationally infeasible with current technology.
Classic McEliece, a code­based KEM (Key Encapsulation Mechanism) that uses
binary Goppa code in the Niederreiter cryptosystem, combined with standard tech­
niques to achieve IND­CCA2 security, is still considered a secure post­quantum
cryptosystem by NIST [12]. However, NIST also points out that due to its large
public­key size, it could be an unattractive choice for most common applications.
Recent research continues to evaluate the resilience of code­based cryptosys­
tems under new algorithmic strategies. Notably, the paper by Beunardeau, Gau­
thier, and Schrek [13] introduces a novel algebraic approach that significantly im­
proves the efficiency of information­set decoding attacks. While their method cur­
rently applies to specific parameter regimes and structures, it highlights the im­
portance of carefully selecting parameters for McEliece­based systems to ensure
long­term security.
Future work on the McEliece cryptosystem will focus on continued scrutiny in the
post­quantum era and efforts to standardize the classic McEliece scheme for po­
tential widespread adoption.
