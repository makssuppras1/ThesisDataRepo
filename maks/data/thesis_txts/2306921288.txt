Preface 
This thesis project belongs to the research group NanoBio Science with collaboration to the 
center ‘Intelligent Drug Delivery and Sensing Using Microcontainers and Nanomechanics 
(IDUN) at the Department of Micro- and Nanotechnology, Technical University of Denmark 
(DTU Nanotech).  The experiments described in this thesis used chitosan from two different 
suppliers. Heppe Medical Chitosan GmbH (HMC) was the supplier of pharmaceutical grade 
material as this company has a collaboration with the NanoBio Science group.   
The experiments and studies throughout this project were developed chronologically according 
to the availability of materials. At first, chitosan from Sigma Aldrich and HMC (85/2500, 
85/3000 and 90/2500) were used to set the operational parameters of the electrospraying 
process to produce microparticles. Viscosity is an important parameter for the electrospraying 
technique. Unfortunately, the amount of polymer available was limited. Therefore, qualitative 
studies related to viscosity were achieved, whereas quantitative studies were unable to be 
performed. Then, another two types of chitosan (HMC 70/5 and 85/5) were supplied and the 
corresponding experiments were performed.  
Finally, three additional types of chitosan were provided by HMC (70/10, 80/10 and 90/10); 
the experiments with these materials were conducted in a limited time, compared with the 
other studies. However, the experience gained through the processing and studying of the first 
microparticles paved the way to develop microparticles with the three new types of chitosan.

Abstract 
Purpose: To use electrospray for encapsulating ovalbumin (antigen) and Quil-A (adjuvant) in 
the polymer chitosan to produce microparticles with a size of 5-10µm for development of a  
potential oral vaccine delivery system. 
Methods:  Ten different chitosan-types with different molecular weights and deacetylation 
percentages were tested for particle formation. Chitosan-types with particle formation 
potential (HMC 70, 85, 90, 701 and 801) were loaded with ovalbumin (OVA) and Quil-A.  
Two types of formulations were electrosprayed, depending on the molecular weight of the 
chitosan:  
a) 3% (w/v) chitosan in 1:2 acetic acid (10% v/v solution) and ethanol with 30-50% (w/w) OVA 
loaded and 10% Quil-A (w/w) in relation to OVA weight. This was electrosprayed with a 16 
G needle, using a distance of 10 cm from the tip of the needle to the collector, using 24 kV 
and a flow-rate of  10 µl/min.  
b) 3% (w/v) chitosan in 1:2 acetic acid (30% v/v solution) and ethanol with 30-50% (w/w) 
OVA loaded and 10% Quil-A (w/w) in relation to OVA weight. The solution was 
electrosprayed with a 16 G needle, a distance of 15 cm, using 24 kV and a flow-rate of  7 
µl/min.  
The morphology or the electrosprayed particles was checked using scanning electron 
microscopy (SEM). The size and zeta potential were measured using dynamic light scattering 
(DLS) and microelectrophoresis, respectively. OVA’s stability was analyzed after being 
electrosprayed with circular dichroism (CD) spectroscopy. The encapsulation efficiency and 
release of OVA were studied in phosphate buffer (PBS) at pH 6.8 using fluorescein-labelled 
OVA (FITC-OVA). Quil-A release from the particles was studied using a photonic crystal 
slab (PCS) sensor. Particles’ mucoadhesion was tested with a periodic acid Schiff assay (PAS) 
for mucin detection. Furthermore, in vitro production of IL-6 from dendritic cells (DC) was 
measured during 18 hours by using an Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assay (ELISA) kit. 
Results: Chitosan-types with low molecular weights performed better in the particle formation. 
The usage of ethanol as co-solvent enhanced the formation of particles and stabilized the 
electrospraying process.  The viscosity played an important role in the electrospraying and was 
==PAGE:6==
 
4
tuned by modifying the formulation concentrations and the operational parameters in order to 
obtain microparticles. Changing the electrospray distance had an impact on the size of the 
particles; decreasing the distance, decreased the average particle size. When loading OVA and 
Quil-A, the microparticles shifted from having a porous to a smooth surface.  The OVA did 
not suffer structural modification after being electrosprayed.  
The microparticles made from HMC 70, loaded with 50% OVA and 10% Quil-A had the 
largest IL-6 production compared to the other evaluated formulations. The size of these 
microparticles was 4.66±0.77 µm with a PDI of 0.45±0.49 and the zeta potential was 
45.94±2.42 mV. The particles did not show great mucoadhesive properties. The encapsulation 
efficiency was closed to 100% and after 72 hours a release around 40% of OVA was reached in 
PBS. 
Conclusion: Electrospray is an encapsulation method ensuring high drug encapsulation 
efficiency and can produce microparticles with different features by modifying its operational 
parameters. Chitosan particles loaded with OVA and Qui-A showed potential to be oral 
vaccine delivery systems, specially with HMC 70 as was the one with the greatest in vitro 
activity.

1 Introduction 
A vaccine is a biological preparation mimicking a disease-causing microorganism and 
creating a reaction of immunity to a particular disease.  The immune system recognizes the 
foreign agent, destroys it and creates antibodies that protect the organism in case of another 
attack (WHO, 2016d). A vaccine formulation is composed of: 1) an antigens that triggers the 
immune system for being “foreign” to the organism; 2) adjuvants, that are compounds 
stimulating the production of antibodies; and in some cases 3) preservatives, stabilizers and 
antibiotics protecting the formulation from bacterial contamination or from losing its 
effectiveness during storage (WHO, 2016a). 
Vaccination is considered the most cost-effective and successful medical intervention ever 
introduced, for the reduction of human mortality and morbidity due to infectious diseases. 
After World War II, vaccinations became wide-spread available and have improved the public 
health, eradicating diseases such as smallpox (Donaldson et al., 2015) or partially eradicating 
poliomyelitis, diphtheria, measles or hepatitis B, to mention some examples (WHO, 2016c).  
Nowadays, some vaccines are unaffordable and inaccessible for people in certain regions of the 
globe. In 2014, only 85% of children under 2 years old, in the world, received a dose of measles-
containing vaccine. Among the rest who did not have access to the vaccine, 20.1 million died 
(WHO & UNICEF, 2015). Most of the world’s population lacks a public health infrastructure 
capable of fulfilling the vaccines demand. Consequently, there is an interest in research and 
development of vaccines and delivery technologies to find ways to make vaccines more 
accessible and effective (Kaslow & Okwo-Bele, 2016). 
 
There are many published approaches about development of delivery systems targeting 
different sites of the human body or different diseases. These systems are looking into deliver 
drugs through different routes of administration, such as lungs, sublingual, buccal, oral and 
genitalia, rather than the invasive subcutaneous and intravenous injections (Donaldson et al., 
2015). Oral administration of vaccines comes with great challenges, one of them, the toughness 
of the gastric environment. Antigens cannot survive the conditions in the stomach due to the 
low pH and the enzymes that inactive them; therefore, they do not generate immune response 
when reaching the gut. Polymeric particles have been suggested as a way to overcome these 
==PAGE:11==
 
9
obstacles; being capable of protecting the antigens from the acidic conditions in the intestinal 
tract until reaching its target (Binghe, Longqin, & Teruna J., 2016). 
Under this context, the aim of this project was to develop microparticles of chitosan loaded 
with OVA (antigen) and Quil-A (adjuvant) using electrospraying as the encapsulation 
technique. The microparticles should deliver the antigen and adjuvant to microfold (M) cells 
in the small intestine, release the antigen and the adjuvant in a controlled manner and initiate 
an immune response.  Furthermore, the particles should be in vitro characterised in terms of 
size, charge (zeta potential), mucoadhesiveness, encapsulation efficiency, release profile, 
antigen stability and in vitro immune response.

2 Types of vaccines 
Traditional vaccines consist of live attenuated pathogens, inactivated organisms or 
inactivated bacterial toxins. The idea behind attenuated vaccines is to mimic the natural 
infection, producing a strong cellular and antibody response. Long-protective immunity is one 
of the main advantages. A disadvantage is the mutation or change that living systems can have 
over time, and the effects can be sometimes severe or even fatal (Donaldson et al., 2015). Thus, 
the drawback of this type of formulations is the possibility that the attenuated microbe reverts 
to a virulent form and cause the disease. Lastly, these vaccines must be refrigerated to stay 
potent, thus handling properly can become an issue in developing countries (NIAID & DHH, 
2013).  
To increase vaccinations’ safety, other approaches to living systems have been developed, 
such as inactivated or subunit vaccines. Inactivated vaccines are more stable than life ones, 
because the microbes cannot mutate or go back to their disease-causing state. They do not 
require refrigeration making them more accessible. Their performance regarding immune 
system response, is weaker than attenuated vaccines, hence, higher doses are in general required 
(NIAID & DHH, 2013). Contrary to the attenuated vaccines, the inactivated vaccines are 
formulated with killed or non-activated pathogens. Inactivated vaccines are less efficient due 
to faster clearance in the body. Nonetheless, they have a greater inflammatory immune response 
compared to the subunit vaccines, because most of them maintain the preserved components 
of the pathogen (Donaldson et al., 2015).  
Subunit vaccines are formed by one or more essential components of a pathogen, which 
antibodies or T-cells recognize and thereby can bind to. Due to the reduced pathogen content, 
the subunit vaccines have lower risks of adverse reactions.  There are two main types of subunit 
vaccines, toxoids and conjugates. Subunit vaccines are against bacteria producing toxins, so the 
main component is an inactivated toxin making the immune system generating anti-toxoid 
antibodies. Toxoids and conjugates are formed by an antigen or toxoid, which is a bacterial 
polysaccharide, attached to a carrier protein (Donaldson et al., 2015; NIAID & DHH, 2013). 
Toxoids are a safe type of vaccine because there is no possibility of reversion to virulence. They 
do not trigger a strong immune response, so addition of adjuvants is required (WHO, 2016b).  
==PAGE:13==
 
11 
DNA vaccines, still in development, are an attractive new generation of vaccines - among their 
advantages simplicity is the main characteristic. They induce the immune system by implanting 
a host cell with plasmid DNA encoding antigen, so the cell produces a protein (antigen) and 
immunity against this protein is subsequently induced (Donaldson et al., 2015).   
Lastly, dendritic cell-based vaccines are also emerging as a potential approach to vaccinations. 
They are dendritic cells (DC) loaded in vitro with antigens that are induced to patients. 
Dendritic cells present antigens to T-cells and provide stimulatory signals (Donaldson et al., 
2015). 
2.1 Administration of vaccines 
The administration of the vaccines to specific target sites in the human body represents a 
challenge for the vaccine delivery.  So far, the most common routes of administration have been 
intramuscular and subcutaneous injection. Among alternatives to use minimal invasive or not 
invasive administration routes, other approaches involve oral, nasal, pulmonary or sublingual 
delivery. Besides being more patient convenient, these routes can increase the willingness of 
the public to be vaccinated and improve the vaccination coverage in the world  (Donaldson et 
al., 2015).

3 Oral Delivery of Vaccines 
As mentioned previously, oral vaccines represent an alternative for non-invasive drug 
administration. Oral administration involves immunization via a mucosal route. Mucosal 
surfaces, according to the location of the tissue, are exposed to different environments which 
represent an individual challenge for the development of mucosal vaccines (Gebril, Alsaadi, 
Acevedo, Mullen, & Ferro, 2012).  Especially peptides and proteins are poorly stable in the 
gastric environment, have low solubility and bioavailability, and the mucus  barrier  can prevent 
their penetration and adsorption (Ensign, Cone, & Hanes, 2013). 
3.1 Intestinal tract 
The gut-associated lymphoid tissue (GALT) is lined with a single lympho-epithelial cell 
layer formed by epithelial cells, goblet cells and M-cells. Under this mucus layer dendritic cells 
(DC) are in close contact to B and T-cells (Gebril et al., 2012). The epithelium has M-Cells 
and the cellular spaces are sealed with tight junctions. Immunoglobulin A (IgA) is also found 
on the surface of the epithelium and prevents adhesion to cell-surface receptors and molecules. 
GALT (Figure 1)  contains different lymphoid nodules  covered with M-Cells such as Peyer’s 
Patches (PP), lymphoid follicles, cryptopatches and lymphoglandular complexes (Gebril et al., 
2012).  
Mucus is composed of proteins (mainly mucins), carbohydrates, lipids, salts, antibodies, 
bacteria and cellular debris. Mucus is being regenerated continuously (Ensign et al., 2013),  and 
the turnover time has been reported to be no longer than 2 hours (L. H. Nielsen, 2013) The 
thickness of the mucus layer varies throughout the gastro intestinal (GI) tract, to keep a balance 
between protectiveness and nutrient absorption rate. In the stomach, the mucus layer is 40-450 
µm in thickness, in the small intestine it varies according to the diet and specie (in rats it is 
around 29 µm), while in the colon the mucus layer has a thickness of 110-160 µm. Mucus traps 
pathogens and foreign particles via hydrophobic interactions, due to is composition of plus 20% 
of lipids content. Moreover, the entanglement of mucus creates sieves that can sterically block 
large macromolecules or particles (Ensign et al., 2013).

The pH also varies along the GI tract. In the stomach the pH is 1-2.5, in the small intestine it 
has an average pH of 6.6, at the terminal ileum the average pH is 7.5,  after which the pH 
drops to 6.4 in the caecum and from the right to the left colon the pH increases to a mean of 
7.0 (Evans et al., 1988). 
3.2 M-cells on Peyer’s patches 
PPs are aggregated lymphoid follicles located in the ileum lined with follicle-associated 
epithelium, such as M-cells (Jung, Hugot, & Barreau, 2010). The size of PPs varies between 
species; human PPs’ diameter is around one centimeter, while the PPs of mice are only one 
eighth of centimeter. In humans, the highest density of PPs are located at the ileum (10-1000 
individual follicles). As mentioned previously, PPs are covered with M-cells, that compared to 
other cells in the epithelium is lacking of an organized brush border. M-cells have the ability 
to internalize material continuously from the lumen to the lymphoid tissue (Brayden, Jepson, 
& Baird, 2005; Niedergang & Kraehenbuhl, 2000). It is estimated that M-cells represent 5% 
of epithelial cells in the intestinal tract, approximately 1 out of 10 million, while in mice it is 
10% (Azizi, Kumar, Diaz-Mitoma, & Mestecky, 2010). M-cells are highly transcytotic and 
can transfer nanoparticles of a wide range of materials, though the molecular mechanism of the 
transport is not completely known (Gebril et al., 2012). M-cells  present particles, proteins and 
antigens to the lamina propria (Figure 1), which contains lymphocytes, macrophages and 
dendritic cells. It is believed that the antigen transport of M-cells to antigen presenting cells 
(APC)  triggers different outcomes like tolerance as well as systematic and mucosal immunity 
(Brayden et al., 2005).

3.3 Immune system 
The immune system is a complex network of cells, tissues and organs working in synergy 
to protect the body from infections (NIH, 2016) and  is inducible, specific and with memory. 
Inducible implies that the immune system is deactivated but can be invoked quickly and 
effectively upon foreign particulates. Specificity refers to an exact response to a pathogen, which 
ensures an ideal defense towards a particular infection. And lastly, the immune system is 
activated faster when it is re-exposed to an infection (Donaldson et al., 2015). 
There are two types of mucosal immune systems (MIS); inductive and effector sites 
(Figure 2). The first one, is related to mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue (MALT) and lymph 
nodes; while the second one is created by lamina propria and the epithelial surface. 
 
  
Figure 2 Schematic representation of the mucosal immune system, formed by inductive and effector sites. 
The immune system is based on the migration of immune cells from the inductive to 
the effector tissue via the lymphatic system (Mcghee & Fujihashi, n.d.). MALT is covered 
with M-cells and epithelial cells  but not goblet cells. M-cells transport the antigen from the 
lumen to the PP by endocytosis or phagocytosis, known as transcytosis (Figure 3) (Charles A 
Janeway, Travers, Walport, & Shlomchik, 2001), where DCs, macrophages B-cells and 
follicular DCs are located (Brandtzaeg, 2009).  
==PAGE:17==
 
15 
 
Figure 3 Schematic representation of an antigen transported through M-Cells and took up by APCs . 
Naïve lymphocytes (T- and B-cells) coming from lymphoid organs via the blood stream 
pass through the lymphoid tissue and DCs present the antigen to them (Charles A Janeway et 
al., 2001). DCs excrete retinoic acid that makes T-cells migrate through the efferent lymphatic 
vessel, the thoracic duct  (Brandtzaeg, 2009), the blood stream and then to the GI lamina 
propria.  At the lamina propria  different immune cells are present (B cells, Ig-producing cells 
and CD4 T-Cells) (Mcghee & Fujihashi, n.d.). Polymeric secretory IgA and secretory IgM, 
antibodies, are synthesized  by plasma cells and are transported into the gut through immature 
epithelial cells that have a immunoglobulin receptor. The IgA and IgM  bind to the mucus 
layer where they can  neutralize pathogens (Charles A Janeway et al., 2001). IgA is secreted 
mostly in dimeric and tetrameric form, and it has been shown that in these forms viruses are 
neutralized more effectively than the monomeric IgA(McGhee et al., 1992). 
3.3.1 Cytokines 
Cytokines are small proteins produced by different cells, mostly leukocytes, that 
regulate immunity, inflammation and hematopoiesis. They produce multiple effects on the 
same target cell and can induce or inhibit the synthesis and effects of other cytokines (Khan, 
2008). 
The synthesis of secreted IgA is dependent, according to in vitro studies, on T-cell help 
(Th). Distinction between Th cells is not absolute, but generally Th1 cells, promote cell 
mediated immunity and produces mainly IL-2 and interferon gamma (IFN- "); and Th2, 
promotes antibody responses, producing IL-4, IL-5, IL-6 and IL-10 cytokines (Kramer, 
Sutherland, Bao, & Husband, 1995).  The cytokines IL-5 and IL-6, have shown to be most 
effective for induction of IgA synthesis (McGhee et al., 1992). Also, studies have shown that 
IL-6 is more potent inducer of IgA production than IL-5, and could act in synergy with IL-5 
to enhance even a higher synthesis or secretion of IgA (Kramer et al., 1995).

4 Oral delivery systems 
A good particle based oral vaccine delivery system must a) deliver a proper dosage of 
antigen in a controlled release, increasing the time of exposure of the antigen to immune cells, 
b) protect the antigen against degradation and c) should have a proper size as this defines the 
surface area and therefore, the dissolution rate and bioavailability (Donaldson et al., 2015). The 
release of the antigen can be done in different mechanisms: diffusion-controlled, degradation 
or environment trigger (pH, pressure or ionic strength)(Pal, Paulson, & Rousseau, 2013). In 
order to have a high immune response, the release of antigen and adjuvant should be 
simultaneous (Meraz, Segura-Ibarra, & Leonard, 2013; Schijns & O’Hagan, 2005). 
Liposomes, dendrimers, multiple emulsions, immune stimulating complexes and 
biodegradable polymers are some of the possible oral vaccine vehicles which have been 
identified to potentially being capable of carrying antigens and adjuvants. Previous studies have 
shown that polymers like chitosan may increase the interaction between the intestinal mucus 
and the antigen. The efficiency of this polymeric particles have been demonstrated in several 
animal experiments, but clinical trials in humans have been limited to assure this statement 
(Azizi et al., 2010).  
 
4.1 Biopolymers 
Biopolymers are synthesized by organism or plants, with a chemical structure that is more 
complex compared to the synthetic polymers. Generally, they are superior to synthetic polymers 
in terms of biocompatibility, but a disadvantage is the susceptibility to microbial 
contamination. Some of biopolymers used for drug delivery are: collagen, gelatin, chitin, 
chitosan, alginate, cellulose derivatives, starch, carrageenan, hyaluronic acid, pectin and dextran 
(Pal et al., 2013). Polymers that have been synthesized using natural monomers are also 
considered biopolymers and have similar advantages, examples are: poly(lactic-acid) (PLA), 
poly(glycolic acid) (PGA) (Pal et al., 2013), Poly-ɛ-caprolactone (PCL), poly(alkyl-
cyanoacrylates) (PAC), Poly(lactic acid co-glycolic acid) (PLGA) (Kumari, Yadav, & Yadav, 
2010), among others.

4.1.1 Chitosan  
  
Chitin is the second most important natural polymer and is extracted mainly from 
marine crustaceans, shrimps and crabs. When chitin reaches a deacetylation of 50%, it becomes 
soluble in acidic solutions and is then called chitosan (Figure 4). The solubilization occurs when 
the –NH2 on the C-2 position in each monomer is protonated, turning it into a polyelectrolyte 
(Rinaudo, 2006).  Chitosan is a hydrophilic, pH sensitive, pseudonatural cationic polymer, 
which properties depend not only on the degree of acetylation (DA) average, but also on the 
distribution of the acetyl groups along the chain and the molecular weight (MW). Due to its 
biocompatibility, it is used to prepare hydrogels, films, fibers or sponges for the biomedical 
field (Rinaudo, 2006). This polymer is also classified as mucoadhesive, nontoxic, biodegradable 
by deacetylases, antimicrobial, antiviral and with adjuvant properties. Nonetheless, in mucosal 
applications of antigens, chitosan has a poor immune response and therefore requires and 
adjuvant to incite a response (Carmona-ribeiro, 2014).

In literature, there are many studies reporting modifications to the chitosan structure by 
making blends with other biopolymers with the purpose of improving interaction, improving 
the antibacterial activity or the biodegradability. Some examples are chitosan-polyamide, 
chitosan-cellulose, chitosan-polyvinyl alcohol, chitosan, polyethylene glycol, among others 
(Rinaudo, 2006).  
Encapsulation of antigens for oral delivery can be achieved with a wide number of

4.2 Ovalbumin 
OVA (Figure 5) is a phosphorylated glycoprotein with a molecular weight around 45 
kDa and it represents the 60-65% of the proteins in egg white. It is composed by 386 amino 
acids (Huntington & Stein, 2001). The reported zeta potential in demineralized water at 
neutral pH is -30 mV (I. van der Lubben, 2001). OVA is widely used as a model protein to 
study antigen-specific immune responses in mice as it is non-toxic and inert (TACONIC, 
n.d.). In the studies reported in this thesis, OVA has also been utilized as a drug model.

4.3 Adjuvants 
Adjuvants are used to potentiate the immune response using different mechanisms; 
increasing the antigen uptake and present it to APCs, activation and maturation of APCs and 
migration to the draining lymph nodes, activation of the inflammasomes and regulation of 
cytokines and chemokines (Donaldson et al., 2015). Aluminum salts, such as aluminum 
oxyhydroxide (AlO(OH)), aluminum hydroxyphosphate (Al(OH)x(PO4)y) and  alum 
==PAGE:21==
 
19 
(KAl(SO4)2) are extensively used in veterinary and human vaccines.  However, these salts are 
limited in their adjuvant effect; therefore, there is a scientific boost on bringing to the market 
novel adjuvants that can have higher potency, more balanced immune response and less side 
effects. Some of the adjuvants currently being studied are:  oligonucleotides, emulsions, 
iscomatrix, liposomes, polymeric particles, saponins, virus-like particles, toxins, carrier 
proteins, lipo/polysaccharides, cytokines, lipids, surfactant-like compounds, RNA-like 
compounds, peptidoglycans, bacteria and spores. Moreover, studies have shown that adjuvant 
combinations have a higher effect, for example, aluminum salts with CpG 
oligodeoxynucleotides (Wang & Singh, 2011). 
 
4.4.1 Quil-A 
Quil-A(Figure 6) is a saponin isolated form the bark of Quillaja sanponaira Molina and 
in a more purified form it is known as QS21.  Both Quil-A and QS21 have shown to be very 
effective in enhancing immunogenicity of various vaccines, some examples are: foot-and -
mouth disease virus (FMDV) antigen in mice, vaccinia virus proteins in monkeys or parasitic 
antigens in sheep. Critical drawbacks of Quil-A and QS21 are the side effects when being 
injected: pain, headache, fever and nausea. Potential toxicities  is a major concern,  thus 
structural modifications of saponins  and alternative sources are being studied (Wang & Singh, 
2011). 
Different delivery systems have been prepared with Quil-A and they have concluded that Quil-
A improved the immune response (Aguila et al., 2006; Malik, Gupta, Rath, & Goyal, 2014).

4.4 Characteristics of particles used as drug delivery carriers 
Particles used for oral drug delivery of vaccines need to have specific properties and 
characteristics to be able to reach their target, protect the antigen and adjuvant, and release 
both in a controlled manner. Particle size is a critical determinant for the uptake of intestinal 
cells (des Rieux, Fievez, Garinot, Schneider, & Préat, 2006). Particles smaller than 10 μm are 
absorbed by M-cells and translocated to the APCs under the PPs. If the particles are >5 μm, 
the particles remain on the PPs releasing the antigen. If the particles are <5 μm,  they are 
endocytosed by membrane attack complex (MAC) 1+ cells through efferent lymphatics to 
systematic lymphoid tissue to stimulate a serum antibody response (Lavelle, Sharif, Thomas, 
Holland, & Davis, 1995). 
Since the cell membranes have a negative charge, the particles should be positively 
charged in order to interact with the cells and thus enable particle uptake by the cells. Another 
parameter affecting the uptake is the hydrophobicity of the polymer. It is  reported  that 
hydrophobic microspheres  of 1-10 μm were taken up by PPs more efficiently than less 
hydrophobic polymers(Ensign et al., 2013).  
Another parameter that affect the uptake of antigen is the integrity of encapsulation. The 
antigen should keep stable through the encapsulation process and the encapsulation efficiency 
should be high to have a good availability and pharmacological efficiency (des Rieux et al., 
2006). Lastly, mucoadhesion is also an important parameter to be considered in the design of 
delivery systems. PPs are slightly protected by mucus, thus particles aiming to the M-cells being 
highly mucoadhesive have  the risk of adhering to the GI mucus and not reach the PPs (Ensign 
et al., 2013).

5 Microparticles for drug delivery 
5.1 Techniques for particles production 
Different techniques have been reported for developing nanoparticles and microspheres 
such as  iontropic gelation, complex coacervation, emulsion and microemulsion techniques 
(Carmona-ribeiro, 2014), spray-drying (Harris, Lecumberri, Mengíbar, & Heras, 2011), 
reverse micellar method (Mitra & Dey, 2011) and electrospraying (Arya, Chakraborty, Dube, 
& Katti, 2009).  In this project, electrospraying was used to develop microparticles for oral 
delivery of vaccines. 
5.1.1 Electrospraying 
Electrospraying or electrohydrodynamic spraying is the production of simultaneous 
droplets by the use of an electric field. The process consists of pumping a liquid or low viscous 
solution through a capillary nozzle which is in contact with a power supply (Figure 7). The 
droplet will be deformed due to the electric field, creating an elongated meniscus or jet-cone 
from which the liquid atomization will be produce (Jaworek, 2007). The atomization takes 
place when the electric stress overcome the surface tension of the solution (Xie, Jiang, Davoodi, 
Srinivasan, & Wang, 2015). The jet is affected by the flow rate and voltage applied to the 
liquid, which defines the quality and properties of the liquid atomization and thus the particles. 
The fine liquid drop moves towards a collector, during which the solvent evaporates and solid 
particles are formed (Jaworek, 2007).

6.2 Dynamic light scattering and zeta potential measurements 
DLS is utilized to obtain information about the size distribution and homogeneity of a 
population of particles. The principle behind this technique, is that particles after being hit by 
light, scatter light, becoming a secondary source of light emission (Figure 9). The intensity, 
angular distribution and polarization of light provides information as size, shape and electrical 
character of the sample (Yin, n.d.). The polydispersity index (PDI), is a dimensionless measure 
that shows the broadness of the size distribution, if values greater than 0.7 indicate a very broad 
size distribution (Malvern Instruments Worldwide, 2011). 
 
Figure 9 Scheme of  DLS principle 
 
 
 
 Particles interacting with a liquid medium have an electrical double layer on their 
surface: stern or inner layer, where ions are strongly bound and an outer or diffuse layer, where 
ions are less associated. Within the diffuse layer, there is a region were the ions are stable 
(Figure 10). Therefore, when a particle moves, the ions at this region move with it and the rest 
stay with the bulk dispersant. The potential in this region is known as zeta potential and can 
be measured by microelectrophoresis. This process consists in applying voltage across a pair of 
electrodes at opposite ends of a cell that contains a dispersion. Particles will be attracted to the 
opposite charged electrode and the velocity or electrophoretic mobility is measured (Kaszuba, 
Corbett, Watson, & Jones, 2010). For physically stable  dispersions, the zeta potential should 
be minimum ±30mV (S. Honary, Jahanshahi, Golbayani, Ebrahimi, & Ghajar, 2010).

6.5 Enzyme-linked Immunosorbent Assay 
ELISA is a specific method for quantification of cytokines and other analytes. The 
sensitivity of this assay depends on the affinity of the antibodies used (MABTECH, 2014).  
Some advantages of this detection technique are its high sensitivity, its reproducible, the 
reading is objective, it has a relative low cost per test and the reagents have a long shelf-life. 
On the other hand,  the antigen preparation can be complicated (Bidwell et al., 1976). There 
are different types of ELISA: direct, indirect, sandwich and competitive. The sandwich ELISA 
has been reported to be 2-5 times more sensitive than the others (Aydin, 2015) and was utilized 
in this report. 
The sandwich ELISA works as follows (Figure 13): 1) on a high-protein binding 
microtiter plate a monoclonal antibody (mAb) is immobilized, so the mAb captures the protein 
of interest from the sample. 2) A specific biotinylated detection antibody is added to enable 
the detection of the captured protein. 3) A streptavidin-conjugated enzyme is bound to the 
biotinylated antibody. And 4) a substrate is added, which will generate a color reaction 
proportional to the bound protein (MABTECH, 2014).

The release tests of the adjuvant, were performed by Kristian T. Sørensen from DTU 
Nanotech using an all-polymer PCS sensor which measured the resonance shift of the Quil-A 
in 20 mM PBS pH 6.8. This technique was proposed because the molecular structure of Quil-
A has no functional groups that could make it totally distinctive from the OVA when using 
light absorbance techniques.    
For generating a standard curve of Quil-A, the process consisted firstly in acquiring a 
reference spectrum from water (30 µl) and washed the channels of the PCS (using 0.1 M 
hydrochloric acid with 0.1% TritonX-100 and water, alternated) until the signal stagnated, 
meaning they were cleaned. A sample of Quil-A in 20 mM PBS pH 6.8 was added and 
spectrum was recorded until stable; then, washing out was performed again and again until 
more diluted measurements were recorded.  
For data processing, relative resonance was considered as the resonance wavelength minus the 
previous reference resonance measurement, in order to compensate any drift in the system. The 
negative control was the 20 mM PBS pH 6.8. 
The evaluated microparticles were dispersed in 20 mM PBS pH 6.8 and they were loaded to 
the channels as explained before and their resonances were registered.

8 Results and discussion 
8.1 Optimizing the Electrospraying Process 
This section is divided into four parts. The first part concerns the development of 
electrosprayed microparticles using high MW chitosan, focusing on how electrospraying 
parameters and formulation components affect size and morphology of the particles. In the 
second section, it was aimed to study the use of low MW chitosan on the particle formation. 
In section three, the effect on the particle morphology when loading OVA and Quil-A to 
chitosan (size of the microparticles is discussed in the characterization section (8.2)). And the 
last part, is a sum-up of the electrospraying results.  
8.1.1 Setting up operational parameters 
The first electrospraying approach was similar to the method reported by Zhang and 
Kawakami, where chitosan with 80% DA and a MW of 120 kDa was used. The authors studied 
among others the impact viscosity and conductivity have on the process. According to their 
results, optimal particles were obtained using 3% (w/v) chitosan in 30% AA solution. For the 
operational parameters, they  applied 25 kV to a 19 G needle, a flow-rate of 8.3 µl/min and 
distance from the tip to the collector of 15 cm (Zhang & Kawakami, 2010). These parameters 
were the starting point in this project.  
Particles made from 3% (w/v) HMC 80/3000 chitosan in 30% AA solution (the most 
viscous polymer among the ones studied)  can be seen in Figure 19. However, as the figure 
shows, this setting resulted in fibrous structures rather than particles. The flow-rate was 3.8 
µl/min, the voltage 24 kV, a distance of 15 cm and using a 20 G needle. Under these 
operational conditions, the jet-cone was formed and the process was kept stable. The sample 
seemed like a gel and due to its viscosity it was difficult to load to the syringe.

different sizes of needles, 19 G and 22G.

8.2.1 Size  
The size of the particles was measured in a hydrated state, because this simulates the 
conditions they will have when they reach to the intestine cells. Table 4, summarizes the 
average size and the PDI of the samples. The statistical method ANOVA (one-way and two-
way with replicates) was used to analyse the results.  
Firstly, the two-way ANOVA revealed that there was a difference in sizes among 
HMC 70 electrosprayed at 10 or 15 cm and the three OVA loadings (p<0.05) mainly caused 
by the distance (p<0.05), rather than by the OVA concentration (p>0.05). This showed that 
electrospraying at 15 cm formed larger particles in comparison to 10 cm and that there was not 
enough variance among the different OVA loadings to conclude that the OVA concentration 
was the cause of the size variation.  
Regarding the loading of Quil-A, a one-way ANOVA was performed on the HMC 70 
to study if the different concentrations affected the size of the microparticles. The result 
showed that there was also no significant difference among the size values of the three different 
microparticles to assume that size depended on the loadings of OVA together with Quil-A 
(p>0.05).  
The size of both HMC 70 and its similar HMC 701 showed that with more viscous 
chitosan (701), the formation of particles was smaller in relation to the 70, when formulation 
includes OVA and Quil-A in the same ratios. One of the reasons of this difference could be 
the distance, because 7013010 was electrosprayed at 15 cm and 703010 at 10 cm.  
Interestingly, the two-way ANOVA concluded that the concentration of OVA 
together with the electrospraying distance caused a difference in the size values of the 
microparticles (p<0.05). Also the electrospraying distance (p<0.05) and the OVA loadings 
(p<0.05) affected the size of the microparticles. This conclusion was the same for the size of 
microparticles with OVA and Quil-A. The single factor ANOVA showed that the size 
variation was dependent on the different concentrations of OVA and Quil-A (p<0.05). The 
results showed (Table 4) that HMC 85 loaded with 30% and 40% formed larger particles when 
they were electrosprayed at 15cm. In contrast, when the loading was 50%, the size was larger

8.2.2 Protein stability  
It has been reported that proteins , as BSA, did not suffer any alteration in its structure 
after being encapsulated by electrospraying and being release from PLGA in PBS (Xie et al., 
2008; Xie & Wang, 2007).  Nevertheless, as explained previously, it is important that the 
antigen reaches the target without structural modifications, so it was decided to compare the 
CD spectrum of the OVA before electrosprayed and after 5.5 hours of release from chitosan

8.2.3 Encapsulation Efficiency 
One of the characteristics that make electrospraying a promising encapsulation technique is its 
simplicity, the high encapsulation efficiency and the monodisperse particles it can produce 
(Fukui et al., 2010). High drug encapsulations, above 90% have been reported using 
electrospray (Fukui et al., 2010; Lee, Mei, Bai, Zhao, & Chen, 2010; Nath, Son, Sadiasa, Min, 
& Lee, 2013). In some publications, the encapsulation efficiency was reported lower than 90%, 
because they collected the particles in solution, dried them and later they calculate the EE, 
causing a release with this collection step (Maruyama, Fukui, Tsuchiya, & Fujii, n.d.; Xu & 
Hanna, 2007).

8.2.4 Zeta potential 
The zeta potential is a parameter of high relevance in oral delivery systems because will 
determine the interaction that microparticles will have during the uptake by mucus in the 
intestine. Studies have shown that positive-charged particles could be beneficial for the uptake 
in the gut, because cell membranes have a negative charge (Soheyla Honary & Zahir, 2013). 
As a general rule, particles with a zeta potential about 20 mV provide a short term stability, but 
when it is higher than 30 mV have a good stability, meaning no agglomerations between them. 
Above 60 mV they are considered to have an excellent stability (Soheyla Honary & Zahir, 
2013). Using this scale, the results obtained were color-coded in Table 5. The strong green 
>60mV, >30mV green, >20 mV yellow and lower than 20 red.   
As it can be seen in Table 5, the addition of OVA to chitosan increased the zeta 
potential. There was not a correlation between the size of particles and zeta potential. Also, the 
addition of Quil-A did not seem to decrease the zeta potential of the particles and there is no 
clear trend among the samples. Regarding the DA of chitosan in empty particles, it seems that 
increasing the DA percentage increased the zeta potential, but when the drug is added, it stops 
making a difference. The sample 905010 seemed to be the most stable formulation.

8.2.5 Release profile 
The study of encapsulation was a bottle neck during this project. The first option was 
to use a BCA Protein Assay, but the chitosan seemed to have absorbance at 562 nm. Then, a 
µDiss dissolution profiler  was used, but chitosan had absorbance at 280 nm as OVA (Figure 
36). The third option, Bradford Assay, also had interference of chitosan in the absorption 
reading, it was decided to use FITC-OVA to study release, inspired by Gordon et al. (Gordon, 
Saupe, McBurney, Rades, & Hook, 2008).

8.2.6  Release of Quil-A  
To measure the release, as it was explained previously, the resonance shift of Quil-A 
was measured by a PCS during five hours.  The Plot A in Figure 38, showed that the resonance 
of Quil-A behaves linearly when the concentration was between 10 to 100 µg/ml, therefore 
the release experiment was approximated within this range. Plot B, showed the release of only 
three samples, that were selected for their different DA and high OVA content. It is important 
to notice that the curves in Plot B showed the resonance of the particles with Quil-A and the 
resonance spectrum of the the chitosan particles was not subtracted in order to obtain an 
absolute value.   
Interestingly, it can be seen that in this first approach, the Quil-A was released really 
fast also during the first 30 min and then the release slowed down, as with release of OVA. In 
the case of 705010 and 904010, it seemed to reach a plateau after the fourth hour, while 855010 
keeps a positive slope. It has been proved that the simultaneous release of the antigen and the 
adjuvant improves the potency of the immune response of the particle-antigen formulation 
(Meraz et al., 2013; Schijns & O’Hagan, 2005).  Hence, these results are not conclusive 
regarding a simultaneous release, but showed that during the first 30 minutes there is a burst 
of both OVA and Quil-A. The release of Quil-A should be tested for longer time, because as 
seen in the release of OVA, after the fifth hour there was a small burst of release that lead to 
more release in some samples and a plateau in others.

8.2.7 Mucoadhesion 
Chitosan is reported to be mucoadhesive due to the presence on –NH2 and -OH groups 
that are considered to be responsible for the binding to the mucus via hydrogen bonds and 
ionic interactions as well as Van del Waal’s interactions (Kongsong et al., 2014). When a 
polymer is mucoadhesive it prolongs the residence time of contact with the absorbing mucus, 
which results in a concentration gradient that stimulates the drug absorption (Dhawan, Singla, 
& Sinha, 2004).  
 
Figure 39 Unbounded mucin  from microparticles loaded with OVA and microcontainers as control sample.

because studies showed that the microcontainers have good interaction with mucus, which 
showed a higher oral bioavailabilty of the drug they contained (L. Nielsen et al., 2015). Figure 
39 shows the percentage of unbounded mucin, thus all the samples loaded with OVA  showed 
to be less mucoadhesive than the microcontainers. The DA had a clear effect in the 
mucoadhesion of the particles, the higher the DA the more mucoadhesive. The more 
decateylated the chitosan was, the more –NH2 groups available to interact with the mucus.

made the particles less mucoadhesive, the effect is clearer with HMC 85. The peak in 70E

9 Conclusion 
Analysis of electrospraying operational parameters showed that the molecular weight, and 
therefore, the viscosity played a major role in the formation of particles. Highly viscous chitosan 
(HMC 85/2500, 85/3000 and 90/2500) compared to the rest of the chitosan-types evaluated, 
did not formed particles that could work for the aim of this project. However, chitosan proved 
to be useful for the production of fibers. Both, Sigma chitosan and low viscous HMC chitosan 
molecules create particles, because interactions between them (due to their molecular weight) 
allows them to freely divide forming individual particles. The increment of acetic acid 
concentration improves the formation of particles, but also increase agglomerations. The use 
of ethanol as a co-solvent evidenced its important role as stabilizer of the process and in the 
particle formation.  
When only OVA and later together with Quil-A were loaded, the viscosity of the 
formulations changed and it was required to optimize the electrospray parameters. The distance 
of electrospraying showed an impact in the size of particles as well as the increment of OVA 
loaded. However, when the Quil-A was integrated to the formulation, according to the 
ANOVA performed, the concentration of OVA and Quil-A was not correlated with the size 
difference among the samples. On the other hand, Quil-A made the particles to shift from 
porous to a smooth surface. The deacetylation percentage among the chitosan particles, did 
not showed an influence in the particle size. 
Operational parameters employed during this research to produce particles, resulted in a 
wide variety of sizes in distribution samples. To avoid these variations, adjusting the initial 
parameters is suggested, as it has been done in other publications throughout literature.  The 
stability of OVA after being electrosprayed was evaluated using CD spectroscopy and, as 
reported in literature, the electrospray technique does not have an impact on any structural 
changes.  
Almost all the formulations, with exception of 7013010, resulted with a zeta potential of 
>30 mV, indicating that they are stable in solution and are not prone to cluster. The addition 
of OVA to chitosan increased the zeta potential, but there was not clear correlation with the 
deacetylation percentage or the particles size. As Honary and Zahir suggested, the positive zeta 
==PAGE:72==
 
70 
 
 
 
potential could be an indicator that the location of both Quil-A and OVA is in the center of 
the particles rather on the surface. 
The release of OVA occurred rapidly during the first 30 min, as well as the Quil-A. 
Further optimizing of the PCS sensors and more studies need to be done to conclude that the 
release of both is simultaneous.  The release of OVA varied among the samples with no clear 
correlation. The samples with 30% OVA seemed to perform the best with the higher release 
rates. It is important to highlight that the release of all the microparticles followed a control 
released and reach values up to 70%. 
The mucoadhesion test showed that the microparticles are mucoadhesive to some extent, 
but are less mucoadhesive than the containers used as a reference in this thesis. The particles 
that only had OVA, showed an increased rate of mucoadhesion when the deacetylation 
percentage increased.  When Quil-A was integrated, the trend disappeared and there was no 
clear correlation among the DA, size or loading of Quil-A.   
The dendritic cells test, showed that further research should be done considering other 
cytokines, as published in literature. Evaluating the immunogenic properties of this 
microparticles may explain more thoroughly its role in creating more efficient vaccinations. 
After contrasting all the results of the characterization tests, the particles that seemed as a 
potential to be an oral vaccine delivery system is 705010, because was a highly loaded 
formulation, which produced an immune response. The size of these microparticles was 
4.66±0.77 µm with a PDI of 0.45±0.49. The zeta potential was 45.94±2.42 mV and turned out 
not being mucoadhesive. The encapsulation efficiency was closed to 100% and after 72 hours 
it reached a release around 40% of OVA. The second best candidate was 855010 which also 
provoked production of IL-6 and the values of size, zeta potential, EE and release are also 
similar to 705010. In case of mucoadhesiveness, 805010 seemed to be more mucoadhesive than 
705010. 
Due to the morphology and other parameters, HMC 90 was thoroughly studied, but it 
did not end up showing positive results as HMC 70 or 85. Therefore, 704010 and 703010, 
should be considered also as promising, because they showed to offer higher OVA release than 
705010, the correct particle size and zeta potential, good EE and in case of 703010, higher 
mucoadhesive properties.

10 Future perspectives 
There are different things that could be optimised in order to reach more conclusive 
results, but due to the lack of time were not achieved. If I have had more time, I would optimize 
the electrospray process parameters to obtain a narrow monodisperse microparticles, as this 
would have assured the homogenous release and uptake of M-cells. Additionally, it would be 
beneficial to study the location of antigen and adjuvant in the particles to have a better 
understanding interactions between them and with the GI medium. Quil-A release studies 
using PCS sensors must continue, as it is important to confirm that the release of the OVA 
and Quil-A is performed simultaneously. 
Regarding the immune response studies, the ELISA protocol could be adjusted, so the 
the microparticles do not stand too long in solution before adding them to the cells. The 
importance of this adjustment is to recognize if the results obtained in this thesis were caused 
by an overexposure of the cells to high concentrations of released antigen and adjuvant.  
Alternative studies could be done for evaluating the production of different cytokines, rather 
than only IL-6. Some publications in literature suggest promising results with IL-2 and IL-
10, but this could only be quantified and studied with a Multi-Analyte ELISArray Kit. 
Finally, further research should be done using the 705010 sample loaded in the 
microcontainers developed by IDUN, to study the release profile of OVA and Quil-A.

12 Appendix A- Preparation of FITC-OVA 
Background: 
Preparation of FITC-OVA by using an ultrafiltration stirring cell. The FITC-OVA will be 
used for identification of OVA in microparticles. 
 
Chemicals: 
FITC    
Ovalbumin      
Na2CO3       
NaHCO3      
MilliQ Water                      
 
Method:  
100 mg FITC    
500 mg OVA      
50 mL carbonate buffer (220 mmol, pH 9.5)  
Na2CO3               2.33 g   
NaHCO3              1.848 g  
MilliQ Water     200 mL  
 
Day 1: mixing and stirring 
- 
dissolve FITC (100 mg) in carbonate buffer (50 mL), add OVA (500 mg) to solution  
- 
stir mixture gently in the dark (in a beaker covered with tin foil) at 4°C (cold room) for 18 
hours.  
 
Day 2 onwards: wash through an ultrafiltration cell to remove unbound FITC (takes 
approximately 2-3 days) 
- 
Assemble the ultrafiltration set: 
o Insert cellulose membrane (molecular weight cut-off 10 000, Millipore, MA) shiny side 
up into the filtering disc. Make sure membrane is in good condition (no holes – 
otherwise the FITC-OVA will be washed away).  
o Place red rubber ring on top of the membrane. 
o Sit this filtering disc on top of the base – screw the cylinder into the base tightly.  
o Insert the stirrer into the cylinder – don’t push the stirrer too far down into the cylinder, 
otherwise it will scrape along the membrane when stirring. 
o Insert the second rubber ring into the lid of the ultrafiltration unit. Lubricate the rubber 
ring with some ice, then push the lid securely onto the cylinder.  
o Sit ultrafiltration unit inside black frame; move black lever on the lid of the unit to the 
upright position. 
- 
Put assembled ultrafiltration unit into a basin filled with ice (replace with fresh ice when 
necessary). Place basin on top of a stirring plate and stir gently. Tube at the base of the unit 
goes into a waste beaker, tube on the lid of the unit attaches to the nitrogen cylinder 
(opposite direction). Cover the whole unit with a towel to protect it from light. 
==PAGE:84==
 
82 
 
 
 
- 
Flush some MilliQ water through the unit (see N2 operation below) – this wets the 
membrane, and also allows you to make sure the ultrafiltration set isn’t leaking.  
- 
Pour FITC-OVA solution into the ultrafiltration unit (wash beaker a few times to ensure 
you get it all) and top up with MilliQ water. Make sure the stirrer is stirring gently, then 
turn on the nitrogen pressure to 200 kPa.  Liquid should drip slowly into the waste beaker.  
- 
N2 operation: When cylinder is closed, make sure both blue and little black valves are loose. 
Turn cylinder to open position. Turn blue valve clockwise to increase pressure to 200 kPa 
(pressure from cylinder to this valve). Turn little black valve in direction of plus sign until 
the pressure reads 2 (x 100 kPa – pressure from this valve to the ultrafiltration set). Set will 
‘kick’ a little once this is done. When topping up with MilliQ or detaching for the night, 
perform in the opposite direction – undo little black valve first, then loosen blue valve. If 
disconnecting from the cylinder entirely, also turn the main valve to the closed position – 
don’t need to do this if just topping up to wash.  
- 
Ideally, fill the set to 100 mL with MilliQ water, then check on the water level every 30 
minutes. Keep topping up the water level, try not to let it fall below 50 mL - NEVER let 
the ultrafiltration set run completely dry. 
- 
Overnight: detach the cell from the nitrogen cylinder and leave it in the cold room stirring 
gently on a stirring plate. Cover the cell to exclude light. Next day: reattach the unit to the 
nitrogen cylinder as previous, resume washing. 
- 
Repeat washing steps until the waste runs clear (all unbound FITC has been removed).  
- 
(Determine F/P ratio – see Sigma protocol) 
- 
Once all unbound FITC has been removed, transfer the FITC-OVA solution to Falcon 
tubes and freeze at -20°C. Place frozen tubes into a freeze-drying vessel (with a filter in the 
opening) and freeze-dry for approximately two days until completely dry.  
 
Transfer freeze-dried FITC-OVA to a plastic screw cap jar and store at 4°C.

Mucoadhesion of particles. 
All experiments are performed in triplicate. 
Controls: 
Positive: 0.025% mucin solution in 0.1 M phosphate buffer pH 6.5. 
Negetive: in 0.1 M phosphate buffer pH 6.5. 
1. Prepare a 0.025% w/v mucin solution in 0.1 M phosphate buffer pH 6.5. 
2. Disperse the particles in 1 ml mucin solution to a final concentration of 5 mg/ml.  
3. Vortex and then shaken at 37C for 2 h. 
4. Remove the particles with bound mucin by centrifugation: 12,000 rpm for 2 min. 
5. The unbound mucin is in the supernatant. 
6. The free mucin concentration is determined using the PAS assay. 
a. Add 25 µl sample to each well (triplicate) 
b. Make a 0.06% (w/v) periodic acid solution in 7% acetic acid 
c. Add 120 µl of the freshly prepared 0.06% periodic acid in 7% acetic acid 
solution. Mix with pipette up and down. 
d. Cover the microtiter plate with plastic seal and incubate at 37 deg. For 1.5 h. 
e. Let the mixture cool to room temp. (RT)
