Abstract
Data transparency has always been associated with a degree of uncertainty, when it comes
to diﬀerent brands and industries, showcasing their sustainable practices. The customers
or even employees working in such brands or enterprises does not necessarily believe the
"green claims".
This study uncovers and idealizes to bridge the gap between such claims made by enter-
prises, by envisioning a proof of concept. This bridging of gap could turn the user’s pains
into gains by reﬂecting on their own spending-based sustainability insights (carbon and
water footprint). The idea behind showcasing the insights based on footprint comes from
the Global issue of sustainability, so that data-driven actions can be taken to lower the
impact on the environment. Majority of the industries are trying to cope with the Paris
agreement by being aligned with the Sustainable Development Goals drawn by the UN
and to gain a competitive advantage in the market.
This study comprises in-detailed research to understand the current standards of available
carbon calculators. The study objective as well, tries to educate about the knowledge gap
and proposes a way to alleviate the gap between the above-mentioned uncertainties, by
being aligned to the Global cause.
Such uncertainties are addressed in the respective research, where, a transparent method
is suggested for the enterprises to calculate their own carbon & water footprint. In order
to materialize the proof of concept, data analysis and visualization techniques have been
practiced. A solution is developed in the form of dashboard, based on the Business Intelli-
gence platform, where spend-based data is analyzed and integrated with calculated carbon
emissions. Existing Open API has been leveraged to automate the process and helped
illustrate the carbon and water footprint insights for the target audience to take measures.

Introduction
1.1
Background
Sustainability has become more popular across the globe. The most often referred deﬁni-
tion according to the United Nations World Commission on Environment and Develop-
ment is, "sustainable development is the development that satisﬁes the demands of the
present without jeopardizing future generations’ ability to meet their own needs" [14]. The
framework of sustainability revolves around three components: economic, environmental,
and social aspects, where the economic and social are the subsystems of the Environment
[15]. Among these subsystems, climate change and stratospheric ozone layer depletion are
the two of the most signiﬁcant environmental sustainability issues across the world [15].
These issues were caused due to heavy emissions of Greenhouse gases in the atmosphere.
Emissions of greenhouse gases into the atmosphere are considered as one of the main
causes of temperature rise [15]. Resulting in, gases such as carbon dioxide (CO2), Methane
(CH4), and Nitrous oxide (N2O) in the lower atmosphere absorbing outgoing infrared ra-
diation, emitted by the Earth’s surface, which causes a ‘greenhouse eﬀect’ [15]. One of
the greatest diﬃculties today is to neutralize the eﬀects of greenhouse gases in the atmo-
sphere to avoid the catastrophic impacts of global warming [14]. Carbon dioxide is the
primary gas, responsible for human-caused greenhouse gas emissions [16]. Since CO2 is
naturally present in the atmosphere as part of the earth’s carbon cycle, human activities
are disrupting the carbon cycle by adding additional CO2 to the environment [16]. There
is a demand for action in order to keep global warming below 2 ◦C [17]. In fact, 7 out
of 10 Europeans believe that climate change is a serious problem [18]. Additionally, 86%
of consumers worldwide expect businesses to do more to minimize environmental impact
[19]. It has become important that not only individuals but also the organizations need
to take actions together to battle the global issue of global warming.
Recent years have seen an increase in the importance of mitigation of emissions and re-
lated activities among businesses. Since Governments in many countries have shared a
variety of strategies to reduce emissions including taxation, setting reduction targets, and
charging for GHG emissions [20]. Huge eﬀorts have been focused on business activities
to reduce GHG emissions and compensation for their contribution to climate change [20].
Nevertheless, the sustainability agenda for an organization reﬂects sustainable develop-
ment based on the triple bottom line [21].
Assessing the sustainability of corporate can be beneﬁcial as it oﬀers several advantages,
from the respective perspectives like; cost-saving, loans, governance, reputation, and po-
sition in the industry. Enterprises that address environmental issues must be able to get a
competitive edge [17]. The impact of the competitive edge has aﬀected the change in the
last couple of decades, where corporate responses to climate change have shifted dramati-
cally [22]. From small-scale to large-scale business, are also giving extra attention to their
social responsibility due to their business operations. While, consideration of sustainable
development has become a prime focus for the policy-makers from the industries [23].

Local and global organizations are slowly adapting sustainable actions and publishing
commitments through actions like reducing waste, circular economy, recycling, endorsing
sustainable products, investing in renewable energy, etc [23]. Organizations are aiming
and complying with the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), such as zero waste,
reduction in carbon dioxide emissions, and circular economy [17]. While corporate are
making strategies to mitigate the emissions that involve investigation of the environmen-
tal impact caused by them. The investigation leads to sub-questions like; what are the
target emissions, how to measure this impact, etc [20].
Several accounting methods exist to mitigate the greenhouse gases, that are used to
showcase the sustainability insights (i.e., by measuring carbon footprint) [20].
These
methods are used to estimate the GHG emissions of an organization or an individual,
based on their activities. The traditional method to be considered by GHG protocol for
carbon emission accounting is distributed in three major scopes [20];
(i) Scope 1 Carbon emissions from industries or production.
(ii) Scope 2 based on the purchase of the electricity, heat or steam
(iii)Scope 3 reﬂects the indirect emission caused due to supply chain, purchase of goods
or waste disposal [20].
To reduce the carbon footprint, the initial step is to understand how the carbon footprint
is measured. Identifying carbon emissions should reﬂect on the impact, an individual or an
organization is making on the environment, followed by taking action to reduce emissions.
In recent time several carbon emission calculators are available to calculate GHG emis-
sions based on the guidance of GHG protocol [24]. These carbon calculators are commonly
used for estimating emissions for the activities within all three scopes. Carbon footprint
estimation can be viewed in the form of insights that addresses user to understand their
environmental impact and help them to make better sustainable choices. A carbon cal-
culator can be viewed as a feasible alternative that could be used to measure carbon
emissions based on the activities of an organization or an individual [24]. GHG protocol
provides a carbon calculator, that helps to measure emissions by entring details about
activities [25]. Such estimations by calculator play a crucial role to educate people that
blends activities and lifestyle choices [24]. Provided calculator by GHG protocol estimates
in terms of carbon equivalent CO2(e) (carbon footprint) [26].
A study by Andreas Birnik [24], provides an in-detail assessment of an online-based cal-
culator. The research addresses multiple calculators which estimate emission by using
GHG guidelines. Few of the major ﬁndings of the literature are mentioned; (i) none of
the calculators uses consumption-based data, (ii) poor documentation provided on the cal-
culation of sampling makes it diﬃcult to address which greenhouse gas are included in the
footprint calculation [24]. The current study addresses and tries to overcome limitations
as mentioned in the above literature by oﬀering an estimation method based on consump-
tion data.
In an another recent study based on consumption-based calculators mentions that these
consumption-based calculators are typically constructed on ﬁnancial transaction data [27].

In other words, the estimation of emissions is based on the transaction amount for a par-
ticular expenditure. Typically the use of ﬁnancial transaction data for measuring the
carbon footprint of a user represents a new approach that has not been described [27].
In most cases, the user transmits the information of their daily transactions, activities,
lifestyle and receives the emissions as an outcome of their impact. This transaction data
is entitled to an end-consumer or of an organization. For example; making a transaction
for food: a lunch brought by an individual or an organization buying lunch for employees.
In the respective example, the transaction category is food and the category may vary
based on the type of transaction.
The existing carbon emission calculators give information on GHG emissions depending on
categories such as food, electricity and transport, water use, purchasing pharmaceuticals,
electronics, and leisure, etc [27]. The outcome of emissions based on the transaction is
typically calculated by using Environmentally extended input-output (EEIO) data. This
EEIO data provides an emission factor based on all the emission of all the scope 3 up-
stream (Value chain) activities for that particular transaction [5]. The existing calculator
based on transactions draws several limitations. These limitations consider the lack of
transparency, as these calculators do not provide the logic behind the carbon footprint

teria for reducing greenhouse gas emissions [53]. Governments from all around the globe
have recognized the need for a blueprint or guideline to assist them in taking action in a

the overall basis for accounting for and reporting GHG emissions [26].
Greenhouse Gas Protocol (GHGP) is an internationally recognized standard for account-
ing, measuring, and managing greenhouse gas emissions, as well as a training and calculat-
ing tool for commercial, private, and government activities, and for taking mitigation steps
[25]. World Resources Institute (WRI) and the World Business Council for Sustainable
Development (WBCSD) collaborated on the development of the GHGP [25]. These GHG
emissions have been classiﬁed into Scopes 1, 2, and 3 based on the source of their emis-
sions [25]. This is presently the most extensively used accounting technique for tracking
GHG emissions, notably carbon emissions, based on the activities of the emission source.

Scope 1 refers to Direct emission, which can be attributed to manufacturing, factories, or
combustion emission activities owned or managed by an enterprise that is directly emitted
into the atmosphere.
Scope 2 refers to Energy Indirect Emissions. These are the emissions caused by the
purchase of electricity, steam, heating, and cooling by the reporting ﬁrm, government, or
non-governmental organization. These are the outcomes of actions that occur at sources
that are not owned by a certain institution.
Scope 3 refers to Indirect Emissions, which are not part of the scope 2 emissions, indi-

be liable for a signiﬁcant part of emissions caused by activities in scope 3 or 2. Meanwhile,
Scope 3 assessment provides a comprehensive understanding of the complete inﬂuence on
a climate. This also enhances understanding of its value chain and assists decision-makers

In this literature review, the following material will describe how carbon emissions are
calculated using conventional methods. To measure emissions, two types of data are major
requirements, i.e: Activity data and Emission Factor [5]. The most often used approach
for computing GHG emissions is to apply standard recorded emission factors (EF) to
an individual’s, organization’s, or institution’s available activity data [26], (such as kg
CO2 released per liter of gasoline consumed for 100km of automobile travel [5]) The
measurement unit can be in metric tons, KG, Grams, or Ounces depending upon the
choice of user and all GHG emissions are converted to CO2e (carbon emissions equivalent)
using the 100-year GWP factors (Global Warming Potential) as published in the Fourth
Assessment Report of the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) in 2007.
[26]. This allows all GHG emissions to be aggregated into a single metric, the carbon
footprint [26]. CO2-equivalent estimate can be represented as [55]:
GHG emission (CO2e) = Activity Data x Emission Factor (EF)
Activity data is a form of information that is being used to calculate GHG emissions from
combustion and other indirect processes, such as liters of gasoline consumed by an orga-
nization’s automobiles, or some business trips (by train or ﬂight), purchase of electronics,
and good, production and so on, although it also depends on distance and vehicle type
(electric or diesel). Often such data is easy to obtain, reasonably accurate and may be
found on bills, invoices, and receipts. Conversion factors are frequently clearly stated, but
in other circumstances, they must be determined accurately (for example, using average
values when there are slight diﬀerences between the sources, such as purchasing food,
gadgets, train travel, a bus trip, or a ﬂight) [55].

Mixed Methods
Mix method refers to a combination of research methods that advance the systematic
integration or use of qualitative and quantitative within a standalone research project
or associated program [73]. This kind of method is beneﬁcial for the study that has a
broader scope and needs more exposure, where the limitations of other methods can be
overcome by helping in magnifying the study to answer the research question [73]. A
group of researchers deﬁned a possible number of outcomes when it comes to combining
qualitative and quantitative methods, while this combination has results based on the
following four possibilities [74]:
Corroboration: Both methods estimate the same results.
Elaboration: Analyses of qualitative data show how the quantitative ﬁndings apply to
speciﬁc cases.
Complementarity: Both methods give diﬀerent results but together they develop in-
sights.
Contradiction: Results from qualitative and quantitative identify conﬂicts.
The research is henceforth using the mixed method based on the Elaboration combination.
The purpose of using mix method in the following research is to increase the credibility of
the study results, whereas the convergence of using qualitative and quantitative methods
in this study will help to enhance and validate the results. Moreover, this will add more

4.2.4
Data Modelling
A strategy for deﬁning and organizing the business processes is data modeling. Data
models are technically complex but are as well intended to be straightforward and visible.
Everyone in a business can better understand and work with similar accountability of
knowledge. Data modeling can be boiled down to represent with the terms dimensions
and measures [93]. Dimensions are nouns such as a person, thing, or a place, whereas,
measures are actions of those dimensions aggregated on sum, count, average, etc. Data
visuals are highly dependent on the foundation of data modeling [93]. Data modeling in
practice is performed on top of three diﬀerent schema, classiﬁed in Conceptual, Logical,
and Physical [101].
Conceptual data model is used to deﬁne on the high level of abstract information, where
the concept revolves around the details about the entities, for example, attributes, data
type, or identifying keys for the further steps. The respective layer of the model is op-
tional and is mainly utilized to discover conceptual strategies with the stakeholders [101].
Logical data model is a more detailed version of the conceptual model, and stands tool-
independent, considering that any certain tool can be leveraged to perform a logical data
model. The model identiﬁes all characteristics of the key records (by identifying unique
keys) and their cardinality with other existing ﬁelds [101]. Physical data model is respon-
sible for showcasing the very informative layer of the model. The model has its entities
represented as tables, its attributes represented as a table column, and the cardinality
amongst each table is connected with unique/foreign key concept [101].
Now that all the diﬀerent levels of the data model are explained, it has a certain way

Data Security and Integrity
As more companies commit their data to database systems, data protection from illegal
access is becoming increasingly important [110]. Preventing the incorrect distribution of
information is what maintaining secrecy implies, whereas, protecting data from illegal or
incorrect sources or deletions is what integrity denotes, however, the importance assigned
to the above-mentioned features are subject to the considered environment [111]. In gen-
eral, publicly governed institutions comply and practice both secrecy and integrity within
their ecosystems. Some of the private institutions that practice secrecy and integrity are
airline companies, ﬁnancial institutions which as well embed fraud detection mechanisms
and follow data correctness. However, data security and protection from unauthorized
access remain important goals of any data management system [111].
Some of the relevant research direction is related to the protection of data stored in data
warehouse systems. Datawarehouse is a database management system that is responsi-
ble to store data statically or dynamically from several other databases or collections of
databases. The idea of Datawarehouse revolves around handling Bigdata or a very large
amount of data. Diﬀerent access control models are built on the idea of data autho-
rization. For an unusual case where transaction data is being collected from numerous
diﬀerent ecosystems, live connecting datasets to a common data warehouse (cloud DBMS)
with huge storage Industry service providers like Amazon Web Services (leader amongst
leaders), Microsoft, and Google are a few of the best, recommended by industry stan-
dards, subject to the utility and practiced ecosystem [112].

Key Finding
The key ﬁnding of this study is the developed approach, that can measure carbon foot-
print based on transaction data. Literature review helped to explore that conventional
methods deﬁned by GHG protocol are eﬀective. However, it requires extra eﬀort to cal-
culate emissions if an organization wants to measure the emissions on its own. As it
requires, extensive activity data collection, appropriate selection of calculation methods,
and identiﬁcation of emission category to determine relevant emission factors.
The continued study discovers the existence of a carbon calculator. The existing calcu-
lators are based on the conventional method of estimating emissions and do not include
consumption data. With the enhancement of methods in recent years, the development
of consumption-based estimation of emissions has gained traction. It becomes more con-
venient to measure and analyze emissions on a continuous basis, with the updates in
methodologies. From the use cases in the literature, it was found that these calculators
are limited to determining emissions at the individual level. It has also been discovered
that the calculators do not oﬀer transparent calculation, which makes it diﬃcult to as-
sess the method used to estimate emissions. In this study, the methods and calculations
required for evaluating emissions based on transaction data have been understood. This
research has beneﬁted in providing more transparency in calculation techniques.
Another ﬁnding from this study is that it is possible to measure the emissions of an orga-
nization by analyzing ﬁnancial (transaction) data. This objective has been attained with
the help of the combination of two research methods, which are qualitative and quan-
titative. Findings from qualitative methods are the reﬂection of conducted interviews
and surveys. Interviews helped to grasp the perspective of the people, who are currently
working in the industry, which varied from interns to the decision-makers in the organi-
zation. It has revealed the challenges amongst individuals across corporate in regards to
identifying environmental impacts (in terms of emissions).
The challenges mainly revolve around the availability of information on the initiatives of
the organization, awareness about their own & company’s impact, transparency of claims,
and information. From interviews and surveys, it has also come to light about the desired
solution. The collected responses shared interest amongst individuals that they would
appreciate fact-based information in the form of info-graphics or insights that can help to
reduce emissions. Outcomes of the qualitative study have helped to continue the research
in the desired direction. Thus, a data analysis approach has been incorporated to develop
actionable insights that can show measured emissions based on analyzed transaction data.
Further, a proof of concept has been envisioned that shows insights about emissions, based
on available data. In addition, actionable insights/measures depict emissions in compar-
ison to ecological activities. The collection of visuals and KPIs have been cumulatively
acquired and presented in the form of a report/dashboard. It has been important to
validate the literature-based KPIs with the individuals from the organization. In other

Limitations:
Estimating sustainable insights based on transaction data also comes with certain limita-
tions, which have been discovered while pursuing the research. The corresponding section
will be arguing about the overall limitations i.e. concerning the thesis idea and scoping
the proof of concept (product). Based on the duration of the research, the study must
be limited to certain thoughts and therefore, a line has to be drawn to identify, what is
something this research keeps undiscovered or something which could be taken care of as
the future scope of the idea.
(i) It has been demonstrated during the study that, one of the key ﬁelds while calculat-
ing the emissions is the Merchant category code (MCC). MCC’s are used to identify the
emission factor based on EEIO (Environmental extended input-output) data. EEIO data
is linked and acquired from the government agencies to develop the calculation for MCC.
It as well provides an emission factor (EF) based on the category of emissions. EEIO
data indirectly implies an assumption about cost and resource, in other words, if there is
no granular information on a particular type of merchant, it autonomously aggregates the
emissions by assuming an equivalent (similar) merchant from a broader category. In prac-
tice, the prices vary from product to product or service to service, depending on the brand.
For example, a transaction where 5 kg of coﬀee is bought from a local vendor will diﬀer
from the emissions caused by a similar purchase of coﬀee exported from Africa. Since
both transactions fall in the category of goods but have a diﬀerent price for the same type
of product as it entails diﬀerent logistics and production that causes diﬀerent emissions.
In such a case, if the local merchant information is not available within the EEIO data,
the emission will be considered based on the average emissions for consumption of coﬀee
(goods). However, it has shown in the chapter literature 2.6, an example of the calculation
is that it does not vary much concerning the emission.In such cases, the business needs
to put extra attention while handing the data and to try not to miss precise information.
As mentioned in the introduction to this subsection, it is important to scope the utility
of the desired solution. It is critical to conﬁne the utility of the solution as it is essential
to draw a line on the implementation of the same. Thus, a few limitations to the current

Conclusion
Climate change is becoming a growing concern in society due to human activities. The
biggest challenge today is to lower the impact on the environment. The impact caused by
the emission of greenhouse gases into the environment causes global warming. In order to
mitigate the emissions, individuals, and industries are taking action and changing strate-
gies to lower the impact. At the same time, an increase in the popularity of sustainability
has inﬂuenced the decisions people make when they take a certain action. This has not
only impacted the demand for sustainable products but also made the industries take
necessary action to stay upfront in the market. However, the current change in strategies
of the organizations cannot be considered as eﬃcient to make an impact and requires even
smarter techniques to make the agenda actionable and popular.
Educating and creating awareness amongst individuals is as important as industries taking
action. In order to mitigate the acclaimed emissions, one should ﬁrst be able to measure
the emissions. In recent years, there has been a signiﬁcant shift in the way emissions are
being measured. Measuring carbon footprint has not only helped individuals and organi-
zations to measure their impact on the environment, but also helped them to learn and
make a better decision that causes a lower impact.
This research has been able to touch the base on understanding how transaction data
can be analyzed to gain sustainable insights. This kind of approach to estimate foot-
prints (carbon and water) by using spend-based data (transaction data) has enabled the
measurement of emissions in real-time. The idea of using transaction data has been in
demand and has been developed by diﬀerent organizations that give live insights. From
the study, it can be concluded that this approach has a lot of potentials that will not only
help the general population but also all kinds of industries and individual organizations
based on the depicted sustainability framework.
The study also discusses the strength to drive climate action (SDG 13), with the help
of data and actionable insights. The proposed solution, in the form of dashboard could
be a potential way of assisting organizations to measure, mitigate and manage emissions
from business activities to ease the way of taking actions. The corresponding solution also
shall help the organization to manage and communicate progress eﬀectively by making
real-time data-driven decisions and compiling yearly sustainability reports. However, re-
porting and reducing the carbon footprint for an organization is a time-intensive process
that requires special expertise and attention.
In addition, the study determines to empower service, with an ability to demonstrate
emissions against the organization’s goals. This will enable transparency between stake-
holders, end consumers, and working employees of the organization and enroll themselves
to take part in the Global initiative.
