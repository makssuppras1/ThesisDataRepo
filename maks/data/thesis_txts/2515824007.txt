ABSTRACT 
1 
This study examined the potential of peptides, which are predicted to be embedded in potato, 
2 
seaweed and single cell proteins, to be emulsifiers and antioxidants in 5% fish oi-in-water emulsions. 
3 
The physical (zeta potential, droplet size and creaming) and oxidative stability (peroxide value, 
4 
tocopherol consumption and volatile secondary oxidation product formation) were determined during 
5 
storage at room temperature at respective time points. The volatile compounds that were specifically 
6 
investigated were t,t-2,4-heptadienal, t,t-2,4-decadienal, 2-ethyl furan, 1-penten-3-ol, and t,t,-3,5- 
7 
octadiene-2-one. In total, the physical stability analyses confirmed that 82-s-a, 84-s-b, 86-s-b, 97-u-b, 
8 
104-p-g and g1 can produce stable emulsions. All emulsions exceeded the cut-off peroxide value (PV) 
9 
for oils (< 5 meq O2/ kg oil), and the PV mainly was at ≥ 10 meq O2/ kg oil, which denotes rancidity 
10 
and thereby rejected by taste panels. Despite this, 97-u-b outperformed all the samples in its ability to 
11 
hinder lipid oxidation. This was evident as this sample had the lowest PV, tocopherol consumption and 
12 
concentrations of volatile compounds, especially for t,t-2,4-heptadienal and 2-ethyl furan (0 ng/ g 
13 
sample). Non-enzymatic browning is elicited as a consequence of lipid oxidation and this is indicative 
14 
of advanced stages of oxidation. In this case, 97-u-b and cas clearly had *L, *a, *b values that did not 
15 
correspond to the presence of yellow-brown pigments or brown colored oxypolymers, which suggests 
16 
the incidence of non-enzymatic browning. There are a number of factors affecting the physical and 
17 
oxidative stability of emulsions and it must be kept in mind that it is the collective action of these factors 
18 
that ultimately dictate the behavior of the matrix. In entirety, 97-u-b proved to be effective in its 
19 
technological function as an emulsifier and biological function as an antioxidant.

1. INTRODUCTION 
21 
Processed foodstuffs typically exist in the form of complex, multiphase, multicomponent 
22 
colloidal systems. Emulsifiers are the key molecules that facilitate the formation and stabilization of 
23 
these structures during processing (Dickinson, 1993). These consumer products, which are usually 
24 
structured using oil-in-water (O/W) emulsions, are thermodynamically unstable systems, hence, oil 
25 
droplets must be stabilized physically to avoid the spontaneous separation between the oil and aqueous 
26 
phases. Emulsifiers adsorb at the oil-water interface during homogenization process, which decreases 
27 
surface tension between oil and water, thus the total free energy of the system and preventing 
28 
flocculation and coalescence of oil droplets via steric or electrostatic repulsions (Berton-Carabin et al., 
29 
2014). 
30 
Although many of the most effective emulsifiers currently used in food products are synthetic, 
31 
such as sorbitan esters, fatty alcohol ethoxylates, and sucrose esters (Hasenheuttl & Hartel, 2008; 
32 
Kralova & Sjöblom, 2009; Stauffer, 1999), there is a growing demand for the use of natural and 
33 
sustainable ingredients in food products due to increasing awareness of importance of healthy eating 
34 
and environmental sustainability (Can Karaca et al., 2015; Lam & Nickerson, 2013).  In this pursuit, 
35 
there has been a recent interest to identify and characterize bioactive peptides of plant and animal origin. 
36 
Bioactive peptides are protein hydrolysates, in which the intact proteins are subjected to 
37 
chemical or enzymatic hydrolysis, with a reagent that has selectivity for particular sequence sites within 
38 
the protein. Hydrolysis can add value to these starting materials such as improved solubility, antioxidant 
39 
properties, metal chelation or development of specific biological activities (Dexter & Middelberg, 
40 
2008). Their structural properties can induce beneficial biological activities such as antihypertensive, 
41 
antioxidative, immunomodulatory, anticancer, antimicrobial, etc. (Gómez-Guillén et al. 2011; 
42 
Hartmann & Meisel, 2007). Moreover, the study of antioxidant peptides from foods, plants, and animals 
43 
has garnered much attention in several industries (e.g. pharmaceutical, alimentary health, 
44 
processing/preservation) (Sarmadi & Ismail, 2010; Wang et al., 2013). In food systems, these food-
45 
derived antioxidants are deemed to be safer and free from side effects associated with synthetic 
46 
antioxidants (Wang et al., 2013). Bioactive peptides have also been scrutinized by a number of studies 
47 
==PAGE:5==
4 
 
for its technological function (i.e. emulsifying capacity) (Brinch & Lægsgaard, 2019; García-Moreno, 
48 
Jacobsen, et al., 2020; Smulders, 2000).   
49 
Isolating emulsifying peptides from plant proteins, as well as side streams from the food 
50 
industry is a practical alternative to synthetic surfactants (García-Moreno, Jacobsen, et al., 2020).  In 
51 
this study, bioactive peptides embedded within parent proteins from (1) potato, (2) seaweed and (3) 
52 
single cell has been particularly investigated, serving as economical and readily available sources of 
53 
protein.  
54 
Potato juice, which is one of the main streams of organic by-products of the potato starch 
55 
processing, is comprised of about 1-2% of potato proteins. The amount of potato juice per ton of tuber 
56 
ranges from 0.7-7 cubic meters per ton, depending on the processing technology (Løkra & Strætkvern, 
57 
2009). Hence, the proteins in potato juice represent a large pool of untapped and non-exploited protein 
58 
ingredients that have nutritional and functional properties (e.g. emulsifier, antioxidant).  
59 
According to Fleurence (1999), the annual global aquaculture production of fresh marine algae 
60 
is 6.5 x 106 tons. Although seaweeds are mainly exploited for human and animal foods for their mineral 
61 
contents and functional properties of their polysaccharides, they are rarely promoted for the nutritional 
62 
value of their proteins. In general, the protein fraction of green or red seaweeds ranges from 10- 47% 
63 
of the dry weight (Fleurence, 1999). However, in the recent years, people have been looking into using 
64 
seaweed as an economical alternative source for protein concentrates. Suresh Kumar et al. (2014) 
65 
studied the functional properties of protein concentrates from red seaweed, in which they highlighted 
66 
that these protein concentrates formed remarkably stable emulsions. 
67 
 Single cell protein or SCP is the manufacture of cell mass using microorganisms by culturing 
68 
on abundantly available wastes (Paraskevopoulou et al., 2003), with algae, fungi and bacteria being the 
69 
chief sources of microbial protein that can be utilized as SCP (Anupama & Ravindra, 2000). In general, 
70 
high production control and protein yields, as well as ease of production control, makes SCP more 
71 
attractive as a protein source compared with conventional plant and animal sources (Paraskevopoulou 
72 
et al., 2003).  
73 
==PAGE:6==
5 
 
Altogether, the untapped proteins obtained from these sources can be used for the extraction 
74 
and application of bioactive peptides as an emulsifier and antioxidant in food products. In principle, 
75 
peptides could be produced with a specific optimum sequence of hydrophilic and nonpolar groups, 
76 
thereby combining the favorable features of proteins with those of the most effective small-molecule 
77 
surfactants (Dickinson, 1993). 
78 
In order to form stable emulsions, the protein/peptide surfactant must adsorb at the oil-water 
79 
interface and thereby reduce the interfacial tension. As a result, the emulsifying capacity of peptides 
80 
depend on a balance of hydrophilic and lipophilic properties (Goldberg & Williams, 1991). Through 
81 
different structural conformations upon anchoring at the interface, peptides can exhibit emulsifying 
82 
properties (García-Moreno, Gregersen, et al., 2020). Moreover, based on their secondary structural 
83 
elements, peptides can be categorized as the following: (1) alpha, which are predicted to have 3,6-α 
84 
helix structure, hence, have facial amphiphilicity, (2) beta, which are predicted to have beta-strands and 
85 
these strands are the constituents of β-sheets, hence, facial amphiphilicity (Enser et al., 1990; Saito et 
86 
al., 1995) (3) gamma, which are predicted to have perpendicular amphiphilicity (Dexter & Middelberg, 
87 
2008). 
88 
Bioinformatics is a time and cost effective tool that can be used to locate active peptides 
89 
embedded within the highly abundant proteins from the aforementioned sources (García-Moreno, 
90 
Gregersen, et al., 2020). In conjunction with bioinformatics prediction, bioactive peptides can be 
91 
exploited for its dual-functional role in food systems as a potential emulsifier and antioxidant, hence 
92 
aim of this study was (1) to produce 5% fish oil-in-water emulsions stabilized with sodium caseinate as 
93 
control and 11 different synthetic peptides that are embedded within proteins from potato, seaweed and 
94 
single cell, (2) and to determine the physical and oxidative stability of these emulsions in 10 days of 
95 
storage as a consequence of the functionality of the peptides. This study was based on the hypothesis 
96 
that the selected peptides perform well as an emulsifier based on previous screening tests by Brinch and 
97 
Lægsgaard (2019) and Garcia-Moreno et al. (2020). In conjunction with the hypothesis, it can be 
98 
postulated that these peptides would also have the innate ability to hamper lipid oxidation.

2.2. Production of 5% fish oil-in-water emulsions 
118 
 
First, the aqueous phases were prepared by dissolving (0.2 wt%) 0.44 g of the peptides and cas 
119 
in 208.56 g of 10 mM sodium acetate-10 mM imidazole buffer. All the samples were subjected to a 
120 
water bath of 50 °C for 2 hours and afterwards left for overnight stirring at room temperature. Cloudy 
121 
aqueous phases (80-s-a, 83-s-a, 104-p-g) were sonicated (Cole-Parmer 8893, Illinois, USA) to improve 
122 
solubility and not cause blockage during the homogenization. 84-s-b, 86-s-b, 103-u-g were also 
123 
sonicated to ensure total solubility of the peptides even though the solutions were clear. The pH of the 
124 
samples was measured and adjusted to pH 7. Pre-homogenization was done using Ultra-turrax (Ystral 
125 
GmbH, Dottingen, Germany) at 16,000 rpm for 3 minutes, with oil being added at the first minute. 
126 
Homogenization was carried out using Microfluidizer (Gea Niro Soavi SpA, Parma, Italy) at 9 kpsi for 
127 
3 passes. The emulsion yield was too low for 80-s-a and 83-s-b as there was blockage in the instrument, 
128 
hence the small amount of obtained emulsions were thrown out. A 10% sodium azide preservative 
129 
solution was added in 0.05 wt%. Iron (II) sulfate pro-oxidant solution, which was prepared at the same 
130 
day of emulsion production, was added in 113 µl per 100 g emulsion.  The final emulsions were put 
131 
into Pyrex media bottles and the pH was measured for a second time. Before storage at room 
132 
temperature under darkness, the emulsions were divided as follows: 20 g aliquots for peroxide value 
133 
determination and 15 g aliquots for volatile compounds determination, both of which were placed in 
134 
separate amber bottles. At each sampling points, the emulsions were taken and stored at -40 °C until 
135 
analysis. The emulsions left after portioning were kept for the physical stability determinations and left 
136 
to stand at room temperature in darkness.

Triplicate data was performed.

the analysis. Emulsions were diluted a (1:1000) with buffer then zeta potential range was set at -100 
153 
mV to 500 mV. Samples were analyzed in 100 runs. Triplicate data was performed.

3. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION 
225 
3.1. Physical stability of emulsions 
226 
3.1.1. Zeta potential, droplet size and creaming 
227 
Proteins and peptides are essentially effective stabilizers against aggregation and eventual 
228 
coalescence. They hamper the formation of a thin film due to the strong steric repulsion caused by a 
229 
thick layer of adsorbed chains and due to the electrostatic repulsion by charged groups (Smulders, 
230 
2000). Table 2 exhibits the zeta potential, volume weighted mean droplet size and creaming of oil-in-
231 
water emulsions made with different peptides as a function of time. It was evident that the results from 
232 
the analyses exhibited significant (p<0.05) differences among the samples in different time points. To 
233 
briefly mention, the final pH of the emulsions ranged from 6.99 to 7.16 (7.08±0.05). Their pH was far 
234 
from the isoelectric points of the respective peptides (Table 1) and generally, proteins and peptides have 
235 
poor emulsion properties when near its isoelectric point (Smulders, 2000). Most of the peptides were 
236 
negatively charged (Table 1), which led to highly negative (<-30 mV) zeta potentials in the emulsions. 
237 
Samples 97-u-b and 103-u-g still had negative zeta potential values despite of their positive net charges, 
238 
which simply indicates that negatively charged parts are dominant at the surface. Moreover, zeta 
239 
potential measurements that denote an electrical differential greater than 30mV, whether positively or 
240 
negatively charged, typically suggest longer colloidal stability and reduced proclivity towards particle 
241 
==PAGE:13==
12 
 
aggregation (Particulate Systems, 2019). According to Garcia-Moreno, Gregersen et al. (2020), 
242 
variations in zeta potential of the emulsions essentially stem from the net charge of the peptides. In this 
243 
case, due to the presence of charged residues (R, K, D, E), 86-s-b, 104-p-g and g1 emulsions 
244 
significantly had the highest negative zeta potential. These samples were among those that had the 
245 
smallest droplet sizes after a 10-day storage: 2.68 µm (86-s-b), 3.41 µm (104-p-g) and 0.27 µm (g1). 
246 
Although 105-p-g also manifested a highly negative surface charge (-73.90 mV), it should be noted that 
247 
this emulsion had already creamed at the day of analysis (day 24). It was clear that this was the only 
248 
sample that manifested creaming instability, specifically “O”. Measurement of this sample was still 
249 
done in order to obtain a complete set of data; hence, this high value is not a reliable number. This 
250 
sample having a dubious zeta potential can be confirmed with its immense droplet size of 26.03 µm 
251 
(already a day after production) until day 10 (31.26 µm) and its creaming instability, hence, a highly 
252 
unstable emulsion.

3.1.2.  Interfacial tension  
288 
In measuring the interfacial tension, this study made use of a different approach to simulate the 
289 
actual situation of O-W emulsions, wherein the oil droplets are dispersed in a continuous aqueous phase. 
290 
A set-up of having the ambient phase and drop phase as the peptide aqueous solution and the MCT oil, 
291 
respectively, was executed. In this method, the IFT values achieved were constantly at around 2 mN/m 
292 
for most of the samples (Fig. S1 in Supplementary Materials). This was an unreliable number as the 
293 
IFT values for MCT oil and water were also similar with the peptide aqueous samples. Appendix C also 
294 
shows the various ways that this matter was tackled. Although the values obtained were deemed 
295 
dubious, these results altogether can be seen as an optimization to the non-standard drop tensiometer 
296 
set-up and prove to be useful for future studies.  
297 
In this case, Brinch and Lægsgaard’s (2019) report was used to gauge the performance of the 
298 
peptides in terms of lowering the interfacial tension (Table S1 in Supplementary Materials). The 
299 
interfacial tension (IFT) of the emulsions ranged from 9.6 mN/m to 18.2 mN/m. 86-s-b, 104-p-g, 105-
300 
p-g and cas emulsions had the lowest IFT values, whereas 82-s-a and 103-u-g had the highest IFT 
301 
values. This implies that the former peptides had greater abilities in reducing the IFT during 
302 
emulsification. This also equates to the peptides enabling the formation of smaller droplets, which 
303 
lowers the proclivity of droplet aggregation or coalescence (Smulders, 2000). In relation to the droplet 
304 
size, it was discussed in Sec. 3.1.1 that 86-s-b and 104-p-g were among the samples that had the smallest 
305 
oil droplets. Although this outcome concurs with the mode of action of emulsifiers, 105-p-g emulsion 
306 
demonstrated that the peptide was not able to reduce the interfacial tension to an extent where smaller 
307 
droplets are attained. Moreover, the IFT of latter peptides (82-s-a: 16.0 mN/m and 103-u-g:18.2 mN/m) 
308 
did not coincide with their foresaw ability to lessen the incidence of droplet aggregation; 82-s-a 
309 
emulsion had a low droplet size that was stable throughout the sampling period while 103-u-g produced 
310 
an unstable emulsion. One sample also worth discussing is 97-u-b, as this sample was clearly not among 
311 
those that had the smallest IFT values. This result was not in accordance with the expected capability 
312 
==PAGE:16==
15 
 
of this peptide to reduce the IFT and promote droplet disruption during homogenization, as was 
313 
manifested in the low droplet sizes of 97-u-b emulsion. Although g1 is not included in the reference 
314 
study’s results, Garcia-Moreno, Jacobsen et al., (2020) reported that g1 showed lower initial and 
315 
equilibrium interfacial tension when compared to sodium caseinate. The larger hydrophobic regions of 
316 
g1 may have favored the interaction of the peptide with the oil at the interface, hence better physical 
317 
stability and lower IFT.

3.1.3. Structural characteristics of peptides 
319 
The emulsifying performance of peptides can also be correlated to their structural 
320 
characteristics (i.e. structural conformation, amino acid sequence, molecular weight). The surfactants 
321 
(i.e. peptides) adsorbed at the interface commonly change their conformation and adopt a different 
322 
secondary structure to expose their hydrophobic core to the oil phase (Enser et al., 1990). According to 
323 
Dexter and Middelberg (2008), facial amphiphilicity allows the peptides to orientate parallel to the 
324 
interface rather than perpendicularly as typical of conventional surfactants, bringing about the 
325 
maximum number of interpeptide contacts to form, therefore, strengthening the interfacial layer and 
326 
yielding to optimal functionality. This configuration is readily achieved using either of the main peptide 
327 
secondary structures, namely alpha-helix and beta-sheet. In alpha-helix, there is a periodicity of 3,6 
328 
residues per turn, meaning the hydrophobic residues placed alternately in three or four residues apart 
329 
will form a single hydrophobic face in the folded peptide and will interact with a hydrophobic interface 
330 
(i.e. oil-water interface). The hydrophilic residues in the opposite face of the helix will latch into the 
331 
bulk aqueous phase, while flanking residues will participate in intermolecular bonding at the interface. 
332 
On the other hand, for beta- sheets, alternating hydrophobic and hydrophilic residues are adequate to 
333 
form distinct molecular faces and interpeptide interactions occurs as hydrogen bonds between the 
334 
adjacent peptide strands, supplemented by electrostatic and/or hydrophobic interactions depending on 
335 
the peptide design. Another structure, gamma, which have hydrophobic and hydrophilic halves, could 
336 
orientate perpendicularly to the interface. In relation to the structural properties of the peptides, only 
337 
the droplet size would be tackled since the surface charges of the samples are sufficiently negatively 
338 
high to prompt electrostatic and steric forces. Furthermore, good physical stability was seen in 
339 
==PAGE:17==
16 
 
emulsions that were predicted to have conformations in alpha-helical (82-s-a), beta-sheet (84-s-b, 86-s-
340 
b, 97-u-b) and gamma (104-p-g and g1) and they were all comparable with each other. However, the 
341 
actual peptide configuration at the interface remains to be confirmed by circular dichroism. Garcia-
342 
Moreno, Jacobsen et al. (2020) emphasized that alpha peptides should have at least 18 AA residues, 
343 
beta peptides with at least 13 AA residues and lastly, gamma peptides with at least 18 AA residues to 
344 
increase the potential of the peptides to adopt a helical conformation at the interface. This finding clearly 
345 
concurred with the results as 82-s-a, 84-s-b, 86-s-b, 97-u-b, 104-p-g and g1 were all above the threshold 
346 
limit, which in turn, emulsions demonstrated good physical stability. Amphipathic alpha-helix is a 
347 
structural feature which has been previously proposed to have enhanced emulsifying activity and high 
348 
solubility (Dexter & Middelberg, 2007; Shimizu & Saito, 1996), as well as contributing to surface 
349 
activity of protein (Krebs & Phillips, 1984; Le Visage et al., 1997). Saito and his colleagues (1995) 
350 
observed that amphiphilic beta-sheet peptides are also likely to exert good emulsifying capacity. It was, 
351 
however, noted that the low solubility of this peptide would be a barrier for use as an emulsifier. Axial 
352 
amphiphilicity of gamma peptides may lead to high packing density when compared to highly facial 
353 
amphiphilic peptides (Dexter & Middelberg, 2008), resulting in enhanced interfacial properties. It must 
354 
be taken into account that this likely demands a higher amount of peptide to cover the interface due to 
355 
the direction (García-Moreno, Jacobsen, et al., 2020). Additionally, 105-p-g only had 17 AA residues, 
356 
and this may have lowered its potential to a helical configuration at the interface. Although 103-u-g had 
357 
30 AA residues, which was above the threshold, this sample had significantly high D4,3 mean value at 
358 
day 10, which signaled droplet aggregation. Poon et al. (2001) argued out that molecules that have 
359 
helices with high amphipathicity and hydrophobicity are not the only structural features important for 
360 
emulsification. Krebs and Phillips (1984) also emphasized that the presence of alpha helix is not 
361 
essential for a protein to be surface active and it is known that other aspects of protein structure may 
362 
contribute.  
363 
Synthetic peptides are designed to possess molecular properties which were thought to be of 
364 
importance in their functionality (Walstra, 1993). It is generally believed that peptides need to have a 
365 
certain minimum molar mass to be able to stabilize an emulsion effectively (Chobert et al., 1988; Lee 
366 
==PAGE:18==
17 
 
et al., 1987). Several groups have researched that digest peptides smaller than 2 kDa have poor foam 
367 
and emulsion stabilizing properties, while other cite as high as 7 kDa (Caessens et al., 1999; Rahali et 
368 
al., 2000; Van der Ven et al., 2001). Basically, the smaller the molecular weight, the faster the diffusion 
369 
on the interface (Lam & Nickerson, 2013; McClements & Gumus, 2016; Tavernier, et al., 2016). Rapid 
370 
adsorption of the emulsifier on the droplet surface is always advantageous because this enables rapid 
371 
reduction of the interfacial tension, therefore preventing aggregation or coalescence (Ozturk & 
372 
McClements, 2016). Upon brief comparison of the molecular weights of the peptides, it can be seen 
373 
that 86-s-b, 97-u-b and 105-p-g were the smallest peptides, whereas, 103-u-g and g1 were the largest 
374 
peptides. This signifies that 86-s-b, 97-u-b and 105-p-g would be expected to perform well as an 
375 
emulsifier as adsorption takes place faster for smaller peptides. With respect to the results, however, 
376 
105-p-g did not achieve emulsion stability as its droplets had a significantly high rate of aggregation or 
377 
coalescence. In this sense, the adsorption kinetics of the peptides cannot be singled out as the principal 
378 
determinant of emulsion stability.

3.1.4. Aspects that brought about experimental deviations 
380 
Altogether, when the physical stability results are looked in a consolidated manner, 82-s-a, 84-
381 
s-b, 86-s-b, 97-u-b,104-p-g and g1 were the favorable peptides, whereas 103-u-g and 105-p-g were the 
382 
poorest performing peptides. Upon comparison with Garcia-Moreno, Gregersen et al. (2020), a similar 
383 
trend was seen for the physical stability of emulsions for g1 and cas. It is also noteworthy that their cas 
384 
emulsion demonstrated a significant increase in D4,3 from day 0 to day 2 (50 °C storage). Brinch and 
385 
Lægsgaard’s (2019) research had 84-s-b, 86-s-b, 104-p-g and 105-p-g as the top performers and were 
386 
even better than cas, as well as stating that these peptides were similar with each other using principal 
387 
component analysis (PCA). Apart from the discussed factors that influence emulsion stability, further 
388 
disparities between the reference study and this study can be possibly explained by the difference in the 
389 
emulsion preparation method. In some cases, the emulsion properties are affected by the type of 
390 
emulsifying machine used (Smulders, 2000). In Brinch and Lægsgaard’s (2019) emulsion preparation 
391 
method, the pre-homogenizing ultra-turrax parameters that they employed were different from this 
392 
study, as well the homogenizing instrument used, which was a sonifier. According to Vlachvei (2018), 
393 
==PAGE:19==
18 
 
the amount of pressure given off by different homogenizers influences droplet size. Increased pressure 
394 
equates to decreased mean droplet diameter; however, this correlation is still dependent on the 
395 
emulsifier type and amount. Also, issues with the microfluidizer during emulsion preparation were 
396 
encountered and the interaction chamber had to be disassembled and assembled several times in 
397 
attempts to improve the flow in the homogenizer. This circumstance should also be considered in 
398 
accounting the possible reasons for result deviations.

3.2. Oxidative stability of emulsions 
400 
Lipid autoxidation, a free radical chain reaction, leads to the formation of fatty acid 
401 
hydroperoxides, which then decompose to a wide range of secondary oxidation products.  Many of 
402 
these are low molecular weight compounds with strong flavor attributes, imparting disagreeable 
403 
characteristics and sensation of rancid fish oil. Since polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFAs) are highly 
404 
susceptible to oxidation due to their degree of unsaturation (i.e. presence of double bonds), lipid 
405 
oxidation is the most critical parameter affecting shelf life of fish oils and food products where marine 
406 
lipids have been incorporated (Kulås, 2000). 
407 
3.2.1. Peroxide value 
408 
Figure 1A shows the peroxide values of the emulsions prepared with different peptides and 
409 
sodium caseinate as control. Table S2 in the Supplementary Materials demonstrates where the 
410 
significant (p≤0.05) differences lie between the samples and time points. It can be seen that at day 0, 
411 
104-p-g and g1 already significantly had high PV at 25.23 meq O2/ kg oil and 25.16 meq O2/ kg oil, 
412 
whereas the PV of the rest of the samples were not significantly different with each other. It was reported 
413 
that the homogenization process can potentially be prooxidative due to high shear stress and 
414 
incorporation of oxygen during emulsification (Horn et al., 2012). Lag phase was noticed for 82-s-a, 
415 
97-u-b and cas from day 0 to day 1, which simply equates to slow increase in PV, whereas the rest of 
416 
the samples did not exhibit lag phase, indicating the rapid accumulation of hydroperoxides. From day 
417 
7 to day 10, hydroperoxide accumulation was significantly curtailed for 84-s-b, 104-p-g, 105-p-g and 
418 
g1, which may signal the onset of the development of volatile compounds. In total, 97-u-b outperformed 
419 
==PAGE:20==
19 
 
the rest of the peptides, as it always garnered the lowest PV throughout storage and was also comparable 
420 
to cas from day 0 to day 7. On the other end of the spectrum, 84-s-b accrued the highest PV (101.67 
421 
meq O2/ kg oil). Although this value was at day 7, it must be noted that peak values were observed at 
422 
this point for the majority of samples. Furthermore, all samples exceeded the cut-off value for the 
423 
peroxide value of oils (<5 meq O2/ kg oil). Moreover, oils with 10 meq O2/ kg oil are considered rancid 
424

425

426 
2010).  
427 
The remarkable antioxidant potency of 97-u-b can be plausibly explained by the presence of 
428 
several K (Lys) residues, as observed in its AA sequence KVKINETVEIKGKFHV. The free amino 
429 
group in K possess a pair of free electrons and hence, were able to chelate pro-oxidative Fe (II) ions via 
430 
electrostatic coordination (Cheng et al., 2014). It is also believed that the antioxidant properties of 
431 
peptides mainly stem from the presence of K, M, H, W and Y residues (Peńa-Ramos & Xiong, 2001). 
432 
The presence of hydrophobic AAs (e.g. A, L, V, G, P, F) is also important to promote peptide interaction 
433 
with lipids resulting in a augmented radical scavenging ability (Aluko, 2015; Egusa Saiga & Nishimura, 
434 
2013). Duan et al. (2014) found out amino acid residue H in combination with hydrophobic (L, P, G, 
435 
V) or aromatic (F) amino acids could also contribute to high lipid peroxidation inhibition property. Its 
436 
antioxidant potency can also be possibly related to its good oxidative stability, conferring good 
437 
interfacial characteristics. Peptide size also affects the potency of the peptides to retard lipid oxidation; 
438 
shorter peptides have higher scavenging and lipid peroxidation inhibition activities when compared to 
439 
long-chain peptides (Shahidi, 2015) and 97-u-b had the second shortest sequence among the peptides 
440

441

442

443 
antioxidant activity of peptides with a MW of 500-1500 Da was far more superior that those having a 
444

445 
good antioxidant properties can be expected from 86-s-b as this was the shortest peptide (14 AAs) and 
446 
==PAGE:21==

it only had a MW of 1599.85 Da. Its sequence ELQVSARVTLEIEL contains multiple L residues, which

519

520

4. CONCLUSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
543 
In summary, the dual functional role of a peptide to be an emulsifier and antioxidant in an 
544 
emulsion system can positively be exploited. Although synthetic peptides were used in this study, the 
545 
predicted peptides embedded within each protein source (potato, seaweed, single cell) would prove to 
546 
be a clean and natural alternative to synthetic surfactants. The favorable peptides in terms of conferring 
547 
good emulsion stability were 82-s-a, 84-s-b, 86-s-b, 97-u-b,104-p-g and g1 based on their highly 
548 
negative zeta potential, creaming stability and small oil droplets throughout storage. Their predicted 
549 
configuration (e.g. axial amphiphilicity or facial amphiphilicity) at the interface also allows improved 
550 
==PAGE:27==
26 
 
interfacial properties, thus enhanced stability. However, placing too much emphasis on the predicted 
551 
configuration of the peptides is not necessary as this does not dictate the surface activity of an emulsifier 
552 
alone and there are other factors that come into play. Another aspect that was looked into was the 
553 
oxidative stability of the peptide-treated emulsions. In this context, 97-u-b exhibited superior 
554 
antioxidant properties, due to it having the lowest PV, tocopherol consumption, concentration of volatile 
555 
products and incidence of non-enzymatic browning. The peptide’s mode of action in retarding lipid 
556 
oxidation is attributed to its size, composition, structure and hydrophobicity; 97-u-b is a short chain 
557 
peptide that not only has hydrophobic AAs that enhances radical scavenging activity but also multiple 
558 
K residues that chelates Fe (II) ions. Moreover, the total antioxidant capacity is a collective consequence 
559 
of the aforementioned factors rather than the individual actions of the peptide. Altogether, 97-u-b was 
560 
the best performing peptide in both the physical and oxidative stability analyses. For future perspectives, 
561 
further optimization experiments with IFT using the non-conventional method can be done to see if 
562 
simulating the actual scenario in an emulsion system would affect results significantly. Also, adjusting 
563 
the peptide concentration present in the emulsion can also be scrutinized, as different concentrations 
564 
may affect the peptide’s potency as an emulsifier and antioxidant.

Appendix A. Bligh and Dryer method- oil content 
 
Two 5 g aliquots were prepared in centrifuge tubes for each emulsion and 10 g of distilled water was added. 
Afterwards, addition of 30 ml methanol and 15 ml chloroform was done. The solution was subjected to Ultra-
turrax mixing at 6500 rpm for 15 seconds. Another 15 ml chloroform was added, followed by mixing at the same 
parameters. Lastly, 15 ml of distilled was added and the tube was gently shaken. It was ensured that the weights 
of the pair centrifuge tubes had within 1 g difference and if necessary, the weights were adjusted with distilled 
water. Centrifugation using Sigma 4-16KS centrifuge (Sigma Zentrifugen, Osterode am Harz, Germany) at 2800 
rpm and 18 °C for 10 minutes was then employed. Afterwards, the methanol/ water phase was siphoned using a 
vacuum suction. The remaining chloroform phase was filtered into a 50 ml Pyrex bottle. Approximately 8-9 g of 
the extract was weighed in a tared and previously weighed beaker. The beakers with the extracts were then put in 
the fume hood for overnight evaporation of the chloroform. The following day, the beaker was oven dried at 105 
°C for 1-2 hours then cooled in a desiccator for 20 minutes and weighed. The oil content was calculated as follows:

Appendix B. Peroxide value 
 
The peroxide value was determined using the colorimetric ferric-thiocyanate method. For Bligh and Dyer 
extracts, 2 aliquots per extract was prepared in test tubes. The extract was evaporated to dryness under nitrogen 
while being covered and protected from sunlight. Afterwards, the remaining oil was dissolved in 10 ml 
chloroform/methanol (70:30) and overlaid with nitrogen. Two blanks (10 ml of chloroform/methanol) and two 
standards (10 ml Fe (III) standard) were prepared as well.  Thiocyanate solution (50 µl) was added to all tubes, 
followed by nitrogen overlay. Fe (II) chloride solution (50 µl) was then added only to the samples and blank, 
followed by nitrogen overlay. It was important to flush all the tubes with nitrogen every after addition of reagents. 
All tubes were incubated at room temperature in darkness. Lastly, spectrophotometric analysis at 500 nm was 
done. The peroxide value was calculated as follows:

Appendix C. Different approaches employed for the measurement of interfacial tension 
In the initial measurements of the interfacial tension, results attained were at a default value of around 2 
mN/m or at time 40 mN/m. This did not make sense as even the interfacial tension for oil and mQ water versus 
oil and peptide solution were at the same value. At first, it was assumed that the incomprehensible values arose 
from having a high peptide concentration (0.2 wt%) in relation to the size of the pendant drop, thus lowering the 
IFT at a very fast rate. As a solution, it has been decided that 10-2 dilutions of the peptides were to be prepared 
and measured instead, however, this still gave off the same default results. Looking from another angle, one more 
solution was to determine how much surface area was created in the (1) emulsion and in the (2) oil droplet 
generated during IFT measurements, given that the peptide concentrations were known per matrix. The following 
data should be attained: the specific surface area of the emulsions available from the D3,2 data from droplet size 
distribution; the surface area of the oil droplet available from the drop tensiometer; the peptide concentration 
already known at 0.2% wt. From this information, the coverage of the oil droplet by the surfactant can be then 
determined, hence the peptide concentration in the droplet from the IFT measurements.  Following these premises, 
it can be postulated that the right dilution factor can be calculated to properly mimic the O/W emulsion system.
