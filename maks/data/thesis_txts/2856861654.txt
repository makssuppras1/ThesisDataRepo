Abstract
The growing urgency to combat climate change requires innovative solutions for decarboniz-
ing energy systems and mitigating CO2 emissions. This thesis investigates the integration of
carbon capture and storage (CCS) with Power-to-Methanol (PtM) production at the Avedøre
Combined Heat and Power (CHP) plant, a leading renewable energy facility in Denmark. By
implementing a dual-pathway system, the plant aims to utilize captured CO2 for either long-
term storage or methanol production, depending on real-time electricity prices and market
dynamics.
The study employs Aspen Plus V14 to simulate the physical and chemical processes involved
in CCS and methanol synthesis, including carbon capture, liquefaction, hydrogen production
via water electrolysis, and methanol synthesis. Additionally, an operational model is devel-
oped to optimize the utilization of each pathway under varying conditions, such as electricity
price fluctuations, carbon credit values, and methanol market prices.
The results demonstrate the economic and operational feasibility of the proposed system,
with methanol production emerging as a cost-effective option during periods of low electricity
prices. The frequent transitions between both model pathways pose challenges to equipment
performance and degradation. Two different proposed solutions reduce the single-hour op-
erations, thus reducing method transitions but impacting profitability.
Sensitivity analyses reveal that electricity costs, methanol selling prices, and carbon credit
values significantly influence the system’s profitability and performance. Furthermore, the
flexibility provided by this dual-pathway system allows the Avedøre CHP plant to maximize
its environmental and economic contributions, aligning with Denmark’s ambitious climate
targets and supporting the European Union’s Green Deal objectives.
This research highlights the potential of combining CCS and PtM technologies to achieve sus-
tainable and economically viable carbon management, paving the way for future integration
of renewable energy systems and advanced carbon utilization processes.

Introduction
The growing urgency of climate change is driving a global shift toward decarbonization,
especially in high-emission sectors like energy and industry. In Europe, the 2020 EU Cli-
mate Target Plan emphasizes ambitious climate goals for 2030, aiming for a 55% reduction
in greenhouse gas emissions compared to 1990 levels. This target underscores the need for
rapid innovation in carbon capture, utilization, and storage (CCUS) technologies, renewable
energy adoption, and energy system decarbonization. Initiatives like the European Green
Deal and the Next Generation EU recovery plan, which includes over e1.8 trillion in fund-
ing, are crucial in advancing this green and digital transition, ensuring that the continent
moves toward climate neutrality by 2050. These actions not only combat climate change
but also promote economic resilience by integrating climate goals into recovery strategies
post-COVID-19 [17].
Building on these European objectives, Denmark is committed to even more ambitious cli-
mate goals, targeting a reduction in greenhouse gas emissions by at least 70% compared to
1990 levels by 2030. The country has outlined a strategy that emphasizes renewable energy
and energy efficiency, with plans to achieve carbon neutrality by 2050. Key initiatives include
enhancing carbon capture and storage (CCS) technologies and expanding the production of
green hydrogen and methanol. These efforts are integral to transitioning to a sustainable
energy system and meeting the EU’s climate objectives [16].
As part of Denmark’s national decarbonization commitment, the Avedøre Combined Heat
and Power (CHP) Plant, located south of Copenhagen, is leading efforts to reduce CO2
emissions through the adoption of CCS technologies. Known as one of the world’s most
efficient power stations, Avedøre primarily relies on biomass to produce both electricity and
district heating [31]. In alignment with Denmark’s climate objectives, the plant plans to
install a carbon capture and liquefaction facility within its straw-fired boiler, enabling the
capture of 150,000 tonnes of CO2 annually [9]. The captured CO2 will then be transported
to Northern Lights’ terminal in Øygarden, Norway, for permanent storage in a deep saline
aquifer 100 km offshore. Northern Lights, a pioneering European initiative, offers an open-
source infrastructure for CO2 transport and storage, representing a scalable and safe solution
for long-term carbon sequestration, crucial for achieving European climate neutrality targets
[50].
1.1
Problem Statement
While carbon storage represents a long-term solution for managing CO2 emissions, it is not
the only viable pathway. Emerging technologies like Power-to-X (PtX) present an alterna-
tive strategy, allowing the transformation of captured CO2 into valuable synthetic fuels and
chemicals, such as methanol, which are widely utilized in the energy, transportation, and
chemical sectors. This dual approach, capturing CO2 either for storage or methanol pro-
duction, raises an operational challenge: under what conditions should the plant prioritize
1

methanol production over CO2 storage?.
The volatility of energy prices, particularly as renewable energy sources like wind and solar
increasingly dominate Denmark’s power grid, adds complexity to this decision.
During
periods of excess renewable generation, electricity prices can drop significantly, making PtX
and methanol production more economically favorable. On the other hand, during times of
high energy demand, carbon storage might present a more cost-effective solution. Optimizing
the plant’s operational strategy to balance these alternatives under varying market conditions
becomes essential to determine whether the PtM plant investment is profitable.
1.2
Objective
This thesis proposes the installation of a parallel operational pathway at the Avedøre CHP
plant to convert captured CO2 into methanol via PtX, in addition to the planned carbon
dioxide liquefaction facility. The aim of this study is to develop an operational model capa-
ble of determining the optimal utilization of each pathway, either CO2 storage or methanol
production—based on real-time fluctuations in power prices and plant performance.
To
achieve this, the physical and chemical processes for both CCS and methanol production
will be simulated using Aspen Plus V14. Additionally, an operational model will be created
in Excel to analyze how power price dynamics and other economic variables influence the
plant’s decision-making regarding CO2 utilization.
Ultimately, this thesis aims to provide a strategic framework for the Avedøre CHP plant to
maximize both its economical and environmental contributions, aiding Denmark’s carbon
neutrality ambitions and enhancing its role within the Northern Lights project and other
future initiatives.

Theoretical Background
This chapter provides the theoretical foundation essential for understanding the integration
of renewable energy in sustainable methanol production, with a particular focus on carbon
capture and PtX technologies. It begins with an overview of carbon capture and storage
methods, which are critical for supplying the CO2 required for e-methanol synthesis. This is
followed by an introduction to PtX technologies, detailing electrolysis methods and empha-
sizing methanol synthesis, which together enable the conversion of renewable electricity and

2.1
Carbon Capture and Storage
CCS has emerged as one of the most effective technological solutions to reduce greenhouse
gas emissions from large industrial sources, helping to achieve climate goals while maintain-
ing energy production.
Several methods for CO2 capture have been developed, including post-combustion, pre-
combustion, and oxy-fuel combustion techniques. Post-combustion, the most mature tech-
nology, involves capturing CO2 from flue gases after combustion using chemical solvents like
amines. This method is versatile and can be retrofitted to existing plants, but it suffers from
a significant energy penalty, reducing plant efficiency and requiring extensive solvent man-
agement. Pre-combustion capture, on the other hand, involves converting fuel into hydrogen
and CO2 before combustion, allowing CO2 to be captured more easily at high pressure.
While more efficient in certain applications, this method is complex and is better suited to
new plant designs due to the need for gasification technology. Lastly, oxyfuel combustion
burns fuel in pure oxygen, producing a flue gas that consists primarily of CO2 and water
vapor, simplifying the capture process. Despite its high efficiency in CO2 separation, the
method incurs high energy costs for oxygen production and presents technical challenges due
to the high combustion temperatures. Each of these technologies presents trade-offs in terms
of efficiency, cost, and technical feasibility, which currently limit widespread deployment [39].
The captured CO2 undergoes liquefaction to facilitate its transport and storage. This pro-
cess involves cooling and compressing the CO2 to convert it into liquid form, making it
easier to store and transport via pipelines or ships. Once liquefied, CO2 is transported to
storage sites, typically deep underground in geological formations such as depleted oil and
gas fields or saline aquifers. These storage sites are chosen for their ability to securely trap
CO2 for long periods, minimizing the risk of leaks. Although CO2 liquefaction and storage
are effective methods to prevent emissions, they require significant energy inputs and infras-

tructure without generating value-added products. Additionally, concerns persist regarding
the long-term stability and monitoring of storage sites [39].
2.2
Power-to-X Technologies
Power-to-X refers to a broad array of innovative technologies that utilize renewable electricity
to convert energy into various forms, such as synthetic fuels, chemicals, or heat. By harness-
ing surplus renewable power during periods of low demand, PtX facilitates the production of
value-added products. At the core of this process is electrolysis, where water molecules are
split into hydrogen and oxygen using renewable energy [2]. The resulting hydrogen can be
employed directly or combined with captured CO2 or nitrogen to produce synthetic fuels like
methanol, methane, or ammonia. This approach not only integrates carbon capture with re-
newable energy systems but also addresses the intermittency of renewable power generation
by transforming CO2 into valuable resources.
Methanol production via PtX has gained significant interest due to its versatility. Methanol
is not only a valuable fuel in sectors like shipping and transportation but also serves as a
key building block in the chemical industry. Additionally, methanol can be easily stored and
transported, providing flexibility for integration with existing energy systems [2]. Converting
CO2 to methanol offers a dual advantage: it reduces emissions by capturing carbon while
creating a commercial product, making the process potentially more economically viable
compared to simple carbon storage.
2.2.1
Electrolysis Technologies
There are three primary electrolysis technologies used to produce hydrogen from water: pro-
ton exchange membrane electrolysis (PEM), alkaline electrolysis, and solid oxide electrolysis
(SOEC). Each technology differs in terms of efficiency, cost, and suitability for various ap-
plications.
Alkaline Electrolyzer
Alkaline electrolysis is the most established method with a long history of industrial use.
It operates at temperatures between 70-90°C, using an alkaline solution as the electrolyte
(typically 25-40%wt aqueous solution of KOH or NaOH). Water is fed to the cathode side,
where OH- ions pass through the diaphragm, resulting in the production of H2 at the cathode
and O2 at the anode. This technology is suitable for large-scale production and allows the
use of inexpensive non-precious metal catalysts.
However, it has notable disadvantages,
including higher operational costs due to lower current densities, and safety concerns arising
from gas crossover and stringent mass transport balancing [2].
PEM Electrolyzer
PEM electrolysis is gaining attention for PtX applications due to its high efficiency and rapid
response to fluctuations in electricity supply, making it particularly suited to the intermittent
4
==PAGE:16==
MSc Thesis
December 30, 2024
nature of renewable energy. It uses a solid polymer electrolyte membrane to conduct protons
from the anode to the cathode, producing hydrogen in the cathode and releasing oxygen at
the anode [2]. It operates at temperatures between 40-80°C and pressures of 2-30 bar [6,
35]. Key advantages include high proton conductivity, which enables high current densities,
and a compact system design facilitated by the thin solid membrane. PEM systems can
operate at elevated pressures, simplifying hydrogen storage without the need for additional
compression. These attributes make PEM ideal for coupling with intermittent renewable
energy sources like wind and solar, due to their fast cold start-up and high flexibility [40].
Compared to alkaline systems, PEM electrolyzers achieve higher efficiency by operating at
lower cell voltages and higher current densities.
They can also handle higher tempera-
tures and pressures, further improving system performance [40]. However, the reliance on
expensive materials, such as iridium and platinum for catalysts, polymer films for the elec-
trolyte, and titanium-based porous transport layers, significantly increases capital costs [2].
This trade-off between performance, durability, and cost has limited broader commercial
adoption, but its superior flexibility and efficiency make PEM electrolysis a top choice for
hydrogen production.
SOEC Electrolyzer
Solid oxide electrolysis uses a solid ceramic electrolyte to conduct oxygen ions. Operating
at pressures of 1-30 bar and high temperatures (600–900°C), it splits steam into hydrogen
and oxygen, achieving higher efficiencies by utilizing thermal energy for part of the process
[35]. SOEC’s ability to operate in reverse as a fuel cell enables grid-balancing, allowing it
to convert hydrogen back into electricity when needed. However, its durability is limited by
the material stress due to the high operating temperatures, which has slowed its commercial
deployment despite its efficiency and dual functionality [2].

2.2.2
Methanol Synthesis
The combination of hydrogen produced through electrolysis and captured CO2 is a key
process in the methanol synthesis through catalytic reactions. Typically, the reactor operates
5

at temperatures of 250–300°C and pressures between 50 and 80 bar. The catalysts used are
primarily made of Cu and Zn, often enhanced with additives like Al, Zr, Cr, Si, B, and Ga
to enhance performance [6, 40, 48]. Three primary reactions facilitate methanol production:
CO2 hydrogenation (1), CO hydrogenation (2), and reverse water-gas shift (RWGS) reaction
(3):
CO2 + 3H2 →CH3OH + H2O
(1)
CO + 2H2 →CH3OH
(2)
CO2 + H2 →CO + H2O
(3)
The first reaction describes the exothermic reaction where one molecule of CO2 reacts with
three molecules of hydrogen, yielding methanol and water. The heat released can be har-
nessed to enhance the overall energy efficiency of the system. The second reaction involves
one mole of CO reacting with two moles of hydrogen to produce methanol, also exothermic.
Finally, the reverse water-gas shift reaction (3) is endothermic, converting CO2 and hydrogen
into carbon monoxide and water, highlighting the complex interplay of reactions that can
be leveraged for optimized methanol production.
The efficiency of methanol synthesis is largely dependent on the purity of both the CO2 and
hydrogen feeds. High-purity CO2 improves catalyst performance and conversion efficiency,
which underscores the importance of integrating high-quality CO2 capture technologies. In
addition, the continuous recycling of unreacted gases back into the reactor can significantly
enhance methanol yields [6, 40].
Despite the technical viability, significant economic challenges remain. The large energy
requirements for hydrogen production via electrolysis, alongside fluctuating electricity prices,
challenge the commercial feasibility of large-scale methanol production. However, with the
right integration into renewable energy systems, such as solar or wind, PtX applications for
methanol synthesis can help stabilize energy grids while reducing CO2 emissions [34]. This
makes methanol a promising candidate for use in industries like shipping and aviation, which
are actively seeking sustainable alternatives to fossil fuels.

Denmark is internationally recognized as a leader in renewable energy, with a well-established
focus on wind power as the cornerstone of its electricity generation. As of 2023, approx-
imately 67% of Denmark’s electricity is sourced from renewables, reflecting the nation’s
commitment to achieving carbon neutrality by 2050 [45]. Wind power alone accounts for
more than 50% of the total electricity generated, positioning Denmark as one of the most
wind-reliant countries in the world. This significant reliance on wind energy is supported
6
==PAGE:18==

by continuous investments in both onshore and offshore wind farms, leveraging Denmark’s
favorable geographic conditions for wind energy production.
However, Denmark’s renewable energy mix is not solely reliant on wind power. Biomass-
fired power plants contribute between 15-20%, primarily through the use of wood pellets and
other biogenic sources [45]. The country’s transition to biomass has facilitated the phase-
out of coal in many thermal power plants, ensuring greater stability in the renewable energy
supply. Solar energy also contributes to the overall energy mix, though its role is smaller,
yet growing steadily with technological advancements and cost reductions. This diverse re-
newable energy mix highlights Denmark’s strategic approach to ensuring grid stability while
minimizing carbon emissions.
Denmark’s energy market operates within the Nord Pool power exchange, utilizing a day-
ahead pricing system to determine electricity prices for the following day [28]. This system
reflects the balance between supply and demand, with wind power availability having a
significant impact on price fluctuations. During periods of high wind energy production,
electricity prices can drop dramatically, and in some instances, they even become negative.
Conversely, on days with low wind output, prices rise sharply due to increased reliance on
electricity imports from neighboring countries.
According to recent data from the ENTSO-E transparency platform [14], day-ahead elec-
tricity prices in Denmark have exhibited significant volatility. In 2024, hourly prices have
fluctuated between -60 e/MWh and 526 e/MWh, with lower prices occurring during periods
of high wind energy generation and higher prices observed during low wind output. This
volatility creates both challenges and opportunities, particularly for industries like Power-
to-Methanol that can adjust their energy consumption in response to lower prices. During
periods of low electricity costs, such industries could optimize production processes, partic-
ularly in methanol synthesis, to achieve greater cost efficiency.
Denmark’s energy strategy continues to emphasize large-scale renewable projects, such as
the development of offshore wind farms and energy islands. Projects like the Thor Offshore
Wind Farm and the proposed energy islands aim to further increase Denmark’s renewable
capacity while enhancing grid stability and storage capabilities. As these initiatives are im-
plemented, they are expected to influence market dynamics further, reducing the frequency
of price spikes but also potentially increasing volatility as the share of intermittent renew-
ables, such as wind and solar, continues to grow [13].
In this context, the fluctuating day-ahead electricity prices present a unique landscape for in-
dustries dependent on stable and affordable electricity. For instance, the Power-to-Methanol
sector, which relies heavily on renewable electricity for hydrogen production through electrol-
ysis, stands to benefit from periods of lower prices. At the same time, it must also contend
with price spikes during periods of low wind output, which may necessitate energy storage
solutions or flexible operational strategies to maintain cost-effectiveness.

2.4
E-Methanol Market
The e-methanol market is expanding rapidly as industries seek sustainable alternatives to
fossil-based fuels, particularly in sectors like shipping, chemicals, and energy. E-methanol,
produced through the combination of renewable hydrogen and captured CO2, offers signif-
icant environmental benefits, including a reduction in greenhouse gas emissions and align-
ment with global decarbonization targets. Despite these advantages, the current market for
e-methanol remains in its early stages, primarily challenged by its high production costs
compared to traditional fossil fuel methanol.
According to the International Renewable Energy Agency (IRENA), e-methanol production
costs are still 2-3 times higher than fossil methanol due to the high energy requirements of
producing renewable hydrogen and capturing CO2. While producing fossil-based methanol
costs approximately 100–300 USD/ton, e-methanol costs can range between 800 USD/ton
to 2,400 USD/ton, depending on the scale and technology used [19]. However, as renewable
energy sources become more cost-efficient and advancements in technologies such as electrol-
ysis and carbon capture progress, the price disparity is expected to decrease over time [19].
Several companies are taking the lead in developing and utilizing e-methanol. For example,
Mærsk, the Danish global shipping company, recently launched its first large container ves-
sel powered by green methanol and plans to deploy this vessel along the Asia-Europe trade
lane by 2024 [25]. This initiative aligns with Mærsk’s broader ambition to achieve net-zero
emissions by 2040, marking a significant milestone in the adoption of e-methanol for marine
transport.
Other companies, such as Proman, are investing heavily in sustainable methanol production.
Proman has partnered with Methanex, the world’s largest methanol supplier, to explore re-
newable methanol production technologies and scale up operations [33]. Proman’s recent
sustainability report highlights its commitment to developing green methanol as a sustain-
able fuel for shipping and other industrial applications.
Methanex itself is also leading
initiatives to promote methanol as a marine fuel, working with companies to demonstrate
its viability as a lower-emission alternative to conventional marine fuels [26].
Despite these advancements, the high cost of production remains a barrier to widespread
adoption. Nonetheless, growing demand, stricter emissions regulations, and investments in
renewable technologies are expected to make e-methanol more cost-competitive in the near
future. As more companies like Mærsk and Proman continue to invest in this technology,
e-methanol is expected to play a critical role in decarbonizing hard-to-electrify industries
such as shipping and heavy manufacturing.

Process Description
This chapter outlines the process flow for integrating carbon capture, storage, and utiliza-
tion. In this system, wheat straw is utilized as a biomass feedstock at the Avedøre CHP
plant, where it undergoes combustion to generate both electricity and heat. This combus-
tion process produces flue gas, which contains carbon dioxide as a significant byproduct. To

for transportation and permanent underground storage or utilized for methanol production.
In the latter case, the captured CO2 is combined with hydrogen, produced through water
electrolysis, to synthesize methanol, a versatile chemical that can function both as a renew-
able fuel and as a feedstock for various industrial applications.
This integrated process not only incorporates carbon capture technology but also facilitates
sustainable fuel production, demonstrating a circular approach to managing CO2 emissions.

The carbon capture unit will be retrofitted to the wheat straw boiler at the Avedøre CHP

flue gas emissions.
The carbon capture technology follows a standard amine absorption-desorption cycle. As
illustrated in Figure 3, flue gas containing CO2 enters the system via a direct contact cooler
(DCC), followed by a fan to overcome the pressure of the absorber column. In this column,
the gas comes into contact with a solution that selectively absorbs the CO2. Due to confi-
dentiality reasons, the process assumes the use of a 30% aqueous monoethanolamine (MEA)
solution to capture the CO2, forming a CO2-rich amine solution. The remaining flue gas,
now significantly lower in CO2, is released from the top of the absorber to the atmosphere.
The CO2-rich amine solution from the bottom of the absorber is then directed to a heat
exchanger, where it is preheated before entering the stripper column. In the stripper, heat

Following the carbon capture process, the captured CO2 exits the unit in a gaseous state. To
prepare it for long-term storage, the gas first passes through a direct contact cooler, where
water is used to lower its temperature. The cooled gas is then compressed to significantly
increase its pressure. This step is crucial for enabling the transformation of the gas into a
liquid state in the later stages of the process.
Following compression, the CO2 is further cooled and dried to achieve the optimal condi-
tions required for liquefaction. The gas first enters a cooler where the temperature is reduced
producing water condensation, and followed by a dryer where any remaining moisture is re-
moved. These steps are vital to ensure the purity of the CO2.
Once adequately conditioned, the gas enters the liquefaction and subcooling section. In this
stage, the CO2 is liquefied by exchanging heat with ammonia, which serves as the refriger-

ant. The ammonia absorbs heat from the CO2 gas, resulting in its condensation into a liquid

After liquefaction, the CO2 will be transported from the Avedøre Power Station in Greater
Copenhagen to the Asnæs Power Station in Kalundborg. Initially, this transportation will
occur via truck until a pipeline across Zealand is established. At Asnæs, the facility will not
only serve as a hub for shipping the captured CO2 but may also accommodate emissions
from other nearby facilities, thus forming part of the Ørsted Kalundborg CO2 Hub [9].
From Kalundborg, the CO2 will be transferred to the Northern Lights project and trans-
ported via ship to an onshore terminal in Øygarden, Norway. From there, the CO2 will be
pumped through a subsea pipeline to the Aurora storage complex, located approximately
100 km offshore. This complex consists of a saline aquifer, around 2.6 km deep, with two
primary storage units (sand reservoirs) and an overlaying sealing layer (cap rock) that en-
sures containment. The liquefied CO2 will displace the brine in the aquifer’s porous rock
framework, where it will be permanently trapped through various mechanisms, including
mineralization and dissolution [9].
By leveraging the Northern Lights initiative for the safe and permanent storage of CO2, this
integrated approach not only mitigates climate change by preventing CO2 from re-entering
the atmosphere but also establishes a comprehensive infrastructure for carbon management.

high temperatures, are more expensive and have limited market availability due to a lack of
widespread manufacturing infrastructure [8].
The electrolyzer requires deionized water to prevent impurities from damaging the membrane
and catalysts. With its low conductivity, deionized water helps maintain control over the
operation, preventing cations contamination and rapid increase cell voltage, and ensuring
efficient ion flow while protecting against corrosion. Additionally, it maintains the purity of
the hydrogen and oxygen gases produced, which is essential for high-quality outputs [51].
The water will be sourced from the desalination plant at the CHP facility, ensuring optimal
quality, minimizing additional infrastructure, and enhancing system efficiency.
The methanol synthesis process begins by compressing and heating the CO2 and H2 mixture
to the target conditions for the catalytic reactor. The reactor employs a CuO/ZnO/Al2O3
catalyst, which is widely recognized as the industry standard for methanol synthesis. As an
exothermic reaction, methanol synthesis generally favors higher yields at lower temperatures
and elevated pressures [34]. However, while lower temperatures improve yield, they also slow
down reaction kinetics, leading to less efficient conversion rates.
To address these trade-offs, an operating temperature of 250°C is chosen, providing adequate
conversion rates without necessitating a significant increase in reactor volume. Similarly, a
pressure of 80 bar is selected to optimize both the performance and cost-efficiency of the
system. This pressure is particularly suited to the Cu-based catalyst, which operates most
effectively within 50-100 bar to achieve high methanol selectivity. At lower pressures, the
reverse water gas shift reaction becomes more dominant, leading to an increase in CO forma-
tion and reducing methanol selectivity. While higher pressures could theoretically enhance
yield, such increases would result in substantial rises in both capital and operational ex-
penditures (CAPEX and OPEX), making them economically impractical. Therefore, the
selection of 80 bar represents a practical compromise between maximizing performance and
reducing costs [5, 40].
By carefully balancing temperature, pressure, and catalyst selection, this process achieves
an effective and economically feasible pathway for converting CO2 and H2 into methanol a
renewable fuel and versatile industrial feedstock.

Model Description
This section outlines the development of an Aspen Plus V14 process simulation, designed to
generate process inputs for an operational model that assesses the optimal choice between
carbon storage and Power-to-Methanol. First, the basis and methodology of the Aspen Plus
simulation are presented, detailing the assumptions and conditions applied to obtain accurate
process data. Finally, the Excel-based operational model, which evaluates the economic and
operational feasibility of the two pathways under varying conditions, is outlined.
4.1
Aspen Model
In developing the Aspen Plus simulations, the decision was made not to model the CHP
plant, as its inclusion would add complexity without significantly contributing to the final
analysis. Instead, three distinct simulations were created: one for the carbon capture unit,
another for the CO2 liquefaction process, and a third for the PtM plant.
For the carbon capture and liquefaction simulations, the model was based on the defined
process parameters of the already selected technologies applied at Avedøre. However, certain
modifications were made to the process flow diagrams to simplify the simulation and maintain
confidentiality, while ensuring the accuracy and reliability of the resulting outputs.
4.1.1
Properties Package
In the Aspen Plus simulations, selecting an appropriate property package is crucial for ac-
curately representing the thermodynamic behavior of all system components, particularly in
complex, multiphase processes. The decision on which property package to use directly im-
pacts the reliability of the model, as it determines the specific thermodynamic equations used
to calculate properties such as phase equilibria, activity coefficients, and enthalpy. Given the
diversity of compounds and conditions within a process, carefully choosing the right prop-
erty package ensures that interactions and behaviors are modeled realistically, ultimately
supporting accurate predictions and valid simulation outcomes.
In this thesis, the Electrolyte NRTL (ELECNRTL) property package was chosen to model
the carbon capture unit since it is the most versatile electrolyte model available [4]. This
method is specifically recommended for rigorous modeling of electrolyte systems, as it can
accurately handle a wide range of ionic concentrations and is suitable for both aqueous and
mixed solvent systems. This adaptability is essential for the carbon capture unit, where
ionic compounds arise primarily from the aqueous monoethanolamine solution, allowing for
precise simulation of the electrolyte interactions within this system .
For the liquefaction and PtM simulations, the Peng-Robinson (PENG-ROB) equation of
state was selected, as it is well-suited for handling non-polar to mildly polar mixtures and the
high-pressure conditions typical of these process stages [4]. Additionally, Peng-Robinson is
an effective choice due to the predominance of organic, light compounds in the gaseous phase,

P2X model includes an electrolyzer, an electrolyte property package is not applied in this

and validity of the overall simulation results.

4.2
Operational Model
An operational model was developed in Excel to evaluate the optimal operating mode, either
PtM or storage, on an hourly basis. This model serves as a decision-making tool to identify
the most advantageous approach under varying conditions and market dynamics. Three
distinct cases were analyzed: (1) utilizing all captured carbon for storage, (2) directing all
captured carbon to methanol production, and (3) a Mix scenario where hourly decisions are
made depending on power prices, so that all captured carbon is directed to either storage or
methanol production.
4.2.1
Electricity prices
The electricity prices used in this model were sourced from ENTSO-E’s transparency plat-
form for the DK2 market area, corresponding to the location of the Avedøre Power Station
[14]. Prices were taken from September 2023 to August 2024, ensuring that recent values
were incorporated. This period was chosen strategically as renewable energy penetration
in 2024 was higher than in previous years, resulting in lower electricity prices. This trend
provides a clearer perspective on how the future is assumed to unfold, with lower electricity
prices creating favorable conditions for introducing a PtM plant by enhancing the cost-
effectiveness of energy intensive PtX processes. However, the electricity market could evolve
in the opposite direction, potentially disfavoring the introduction of a PtM plant.
To evaluate feasibility under favorable future conditions, a realistic beneficial scenario was
assumed. Additionally, a sensitivity analysis is performed by varying the spread of electricity
prices to account for potential market uncertainties, results are presented in Section 5.4.4.

The storage model focuses on the scenario where all captured carbon dioxide is liquefied
and stored underground. The associated costs primarily include the energy consumption
and utility requirements for operating the carbon capture unit and the liquefaction process.
Revenues, on the other hand, are generated through the sale of carbon credits, which are
determined based on the amount of avoided CO2 emissions. This approach leverages the eco-
nomic incentives of carbon credit markets to offset operational costs, making carbon storage
a potentially viable and sustainable option.

such as a natural gas (NG) combined heat and power (CHP) plant, would be required to

additional tax for direct CO2 emissions, set at 0.442 DKK per Nm3 of NG [38].

only applicable to the portion of fuel used for district heating purposes, which is estimated

Mix operational mode
The Mix mode integrates storage and PtM methods to maximize revenue while maintaining
operational flexibility. Each hour, the operation mode is determined by selecting the option
with the higher profit. On one hand, the PtM profit calculated with Equation 34, incor-
porates all revenues and costs, including capital expenditures (CAPEX) for the PtM plant.
On the other hand, the profit from the storage method, determined by Equation 28, must
account for the PtM CAPEX (Equation 31) as this fixed cost applies every hour, regardless
of whether the PtM plant is active or not.
It is important to note that the method selection is independent of past or future operations,
relying solely on the parameters of each hour. This approach may result in frequent opera-
tional transitions and start-stop cycles, potentially posing significant challenges to equipment
durability and efficiency. These challenges will be analyzed in detail in Section 5.3.
This approach ensures that the analysis accounts for the financial impact of integrating the
methanol plant in parallel with the liquefaction pathway. By incorporating CAPEX, the
model evaluates whether adding the PtM plant is economically viable under varying market
and energy cost conditions.
This hybrid strategy leverages the benefits of both carbon
storage and methanol production, dynamically optimizing financial performance.

High water concentrations can severely impact catalyst performance during CO2 hydrogena-
tion to methanol. In the presence of water, Cu/ZnO-based catalysts undergo structural and
chemical degradation, including the sintering of copper particles, formation of zinc hydroxide
from ZnO, and disruption of the Cu-ZnO interaction. These changes reduce the active sur-
face area and catalytic efficiency, while prolonged exposure exacerbates deactivation effects,
as highlighted by Etim et al. (2020) [15].

operates effectively with the water content provided by Option 2 making the lower moisture
levels of Option 3 unnecessary to prevent significant catalyst deactivation.

Mix operational model
The Mix strategy dynamically combines the strengths of the storage and PtM methods by
selecting the more profitable approach on an hourly basis. This flexibility enables the sys-
tem to adapt to fluctuating electricity prices and optimize overall profitability. However,
the inclusion of the PtM plant’s CAPEX introduces a challenge since fixed investment costs
must be accounted for even during storage mode operation.
When operating in storage mode, the high carbon credit revenue compensates and exceeds
the CAPEX burden of the PtM plant. This penalty due to PtM CAPEX during storage
hours is rewarded by high revenue when PtM is active, as the methanol hourly selling price
is twice-fold. As a result, the Mix strategy achieves higher annual profits than either the
storage or PtM strategies alone, underscoring the value of dynamic operations despite inter-
mittent losses.

5.3
Operational Model - Base case feasibility analysis
Challenges in Dynamic Operation
The dynamic operation of PEM electrolyzers and methanol reactors, including frequent
on-off cycles and cold or warm starts, poses significant challenges to both efficiency and
durability. These transitions, driven by volatile electricity prices and fluctuating renewable
energy inputs, place considerable strain on system components. In addition to technical
challenges, these operational patterns lead to economic complications, though their impact
can be mitigated through careful design and management.
Frequent operational changes introduce stress to electrolyzer components, particularly through
fluctuations in current density. These variations promote the formation and detachment of
gas bubbles, which increase overvoltage losses and can damage protective coatings, accelerat-
ing corrosion and component degradation. Irreversible damage, such as membrane thinning
and catalyst dissolution, shorten the lifespan of the electrolyzers. However, research indi-
cates that intermittent operation with sufficiently long cycling intervals, such as six-hour
cycles, can allow reversible degradation processes to recover, partially offsetting these ad-
verse effects [8, 36].
Frequent on-off cycles accelerate degradation in both PEM electrolyzers and methanol reac-
tors. In electrolyzers, shutdown periods promote catalyst dissolution and hydrogen crossover,
reducing catalytic efficiency and accelerating wear. Although maintaining a low standby
current can mitigate these effects, it increases energy consumption, presenting a trade-off
between durability and efficiency [36]. Similarly, in methanol reactors, frequent start-stop cy-
cles impose thermal and mechanical stresses on reactor components, reducing their lifespan.
These cycles also degrade catalysts by repeatedly exposing them to suboptimal operating
conditions, ultimately diminishing reactor efficiency and methanol yield [10, 27].
48
==PAGE:60==
MSc Thesis
December 30, 2024
Cold and warm starts further influence operational dynamics, each with distinct challenges.
Cold starts, initiated from ambient conditions after extended downtimes, require significant
energy for heating and introduce high cell voltages at low current densities in PEM electrolyz-
ers, increasing component stress. This heating process can take 5 to 15 minutes, during which
electrolyzer efficiency declines. In contrast, warm starts—where the electrolyzer remains in
a ready state—are considerably faster, typically requiring less than 10 seconds. Warm starts
are also more energy-efficient, making them better suited for frequent cycling in dynamic
operations [8, 21].
Methanol reactor similarly benefit from warm starts. By maintaining temperatures closer
to the operational range during downtime, warm starts reduce thermal stresses associated
with cold starts, thereby minimizing material fatigue and preserving reactor integrity. This
approach not only enhances the reactor’s response time but also improves overall efficiency
under dynamic conditions [27].
Despite these challenges, PEM electrolyzers and methanol reactors offer significant advan-
tages in dynamic operations. PEM electrolyzers excel in their ability to respond rapidly to
renewable energy variability, achieving ramping rates of up to 90% per second, which makes
them ideal for integration into renewable systems [21]. Methanol reactors complement this
flexibility through their capacity to retain thermal energy, enabling them to adapt effectively
to variable operating conditions and fluctuating energy prices [27].
Ultimately, while dynamic operation imposes stress and potential degradation on PEM elec-
trolyzers and methanol reactors, their inherent flexibility and rapid response capabilities
position them as indispensable components of renewable energy systems. Strategic manage-
ment practices, such as optimizing cycling intervals and utilizing standby modes, are critical
to balancing efficiency, durability, and operational costs.
To address these operational challenges, a feasibility analysis was conducted based on the
base case operational model. The analysis involved two approaches: the average electricity
price model (1st Solution), designed to reduce short-term price volatility, and the consecutive
operation model (2nd Solution) - built on top of the first one - which enforced a minimum
two-hour operational duration to enhance stability. These adjustments aimed to optimize
scheduling, improve reliability, and prolong the lifespan of critical components in dynamic
electricity markets.
Feasibility Models

operational transitions caused by short-term price fluctuations. By calculating the average
electricity price based on the current, previous, and next hour, the model identified broader
trends and avoided overreacting to isolated spikes or dips. While real-time prices continued
to be used for profitability calculations, operational decisions relied on this averaged price
to improve scheduling stability.

Limitations and Future Recommendations
The present study offered valuable insights into the parallel operation of carbon capture,
storage, and utilization systems. However, it is essential to acknowledge the limitations and
identify opportunities for future improvements. These include addressing assumptions made
during the analysis, refining methodologies, and exploring potential modifications or addi-
tions to the system to enhance its accuracy, efficiency, and applicability.
The Aspen Plus simulation relied on several assumptions that influenced the outcomes. For
example, the reactor conditions, such as fixed pressure and temperature for the methanol
reactor and electrolyzer, and the selection of monoethanolamine (MEA) as the solvent for
the carbon capture process, were pre-defined. These choices, while practical for the scope of
this study, could lead to variations in power consumption and process efficiency if alternative
parameters or solvents were selected. Additionally, certain subsystems were simplified, such
as excluding the closed oil loop for compressors, and assuming 100% conversion of water to
hydrogen and oxygen with perfect separation. These simplifications may not fully capture
the complexity of real-world operations and highlight the need for a more detailed investiga-
tion. Future work should include a sensitivity analysis of key process parameters to quantify
their effects on efficiency and energy consumption, as well as refine the model to incorporate
more comprehensive subsystem modeling.
The process configuration used in this study represents only one possible design, and there
are opportunities to explore alternative configurations. For example, recirculating the top
product of the first distillation column back to the reactor mixer could enhance carbon
utilization efficiency, while performing an energy integration analysis for the entire system
could identify opportunities to reduce utility demands. Furthermore, the dynamic behav-
ior of the plant, including the variability in flue gas flow rates from the combined heat and
power (CHP) plant, could be incorporated in the analysis. In reality, such variations directly
affect carbon capture rates and economic performance. Expanding the analysis to include
dynamic plant operations could provide a more accurate representation of system behavior
and identify strategies for optimizing performance under variable conditions.
The composition of the flue gas stream exiting the carbon capture system was simplified
in this study, assuming it consisted primarily of CO2 with minor amounts of H2O, N2, and
O2. However, in practice, flue gases often contain additional impurities that can impact the
downstream processes and overall system performance. Addressing this limitation by incor-
porating a more detailed representation of flue gas composition would improve the accuracy
of future analyses.
The choice of PEM electrolyzer technology in this study influenced both the simulation’s
efficiency and the capital expenditure (CAPEX) estimates.
While the exclusion of solid
oxide electrolyzer cell (SOEC) technology was justified based on its higher costs and lower
availability, future studies could benefit from analyzing its potential as costs decrease and

availability improves. Additionally, advancements in electrolyzer technologies are likely to
enhance operational efficiency and adaptability, particularly in response to dynamic opera-
tional conditions.
Economic and market assumptions also introduced uncertainties into the analysis.
The
yearly profit was estimated by evaluating hourly plant operations, assuming all year around
operation. In reality, operational patterns may deviate from this assumption, between 6,000
to 7,000 operational hours per year, leading to discrepancies in revenue projections. The
electricity price used in the study was based on recent data but does not necessarily re-
flect future trends. Although a sensitivity analysis was performed to evaluate the impact
of changing electricity spreads, a more comprehensive approach to modeling price dynamics
could provide deeper insights. Additionally, the limited availability of public information on
carbon credit values and methanol market prices necessitated assumptions based on com-
petitor taxes and process costs. These approximations could be refined by using real market
data to provide a more accurate economic evaluation. Similar limitations apply to utility
cost estimates, which were based on literature values for water and steam usage that may
not fully reflect operational realities. Incorporating real utility cost data would enhance the
robustness of future analyses.
An additional revenue opportunity not included in this study is the sale of oxygen produced
as a byproduct of the electrolyzer. Identifying and quantifying potential markets for oxygen
could improve the economic feasibility of the PtM pathway and warrant further exploration.
Finally, alternative operating scenarios were not fully examined in this study. For instance,
incorporating a CO2 storage unit could allow the PtM plant and liquefaction system to op-
erate flexibly, targeting periods of favorable electricity prices. Similarly, splitting the CO2
flow between storage and utilization, rather than committing entirely to one pathway per
hour, could reduce the required capacity of the PtM plant, thereby lowering CAPEX. These
strategies could enhance operational flexibility and reduce costs, offering valuable directions
for future work.
Addressing these limitations and pursuing the recommended avenues for further research
would significantly enhance the understanding of PtM system integration with carbon cap-
ture and liquefaction.
These efforts could lead to improved process efficiency, economic
performance, and adaptability, advancing the feasibility of this technology under real-world
conditions.

Conclusion
This thesis has addressed the critical challenge of balancing carbon storage and Power-
to-Methanol production at the Avedøre Combined Heat and Power plant, aligning with
Denmark’s ambitious climate neutrality goals. Through a comprehensive simulation-based
analysis using Aspen Plus V14 and an operational model, the study evaluated the techni-
cal, economic, and environmental implications of implementing a dual-pathway system. The
research contributes to the broader effort to decarbonize the energy sector by exploring inno-
vative strategies to maximize the value of captured CO2, either through permanent storage
or conversion into renewable methanol.
The findings highlight the feasibility and benefits of introducing a flexible Mix operational
model capable of adapting to real-time electricity price fluctuations. Methanol production
via PtX emerges as a particularly attractive option during periods of low electricity prices,
which are increasingly common due to Denmark’s high penetration of wind energy. By uti-
lizing surplus renewable energy for hydrogen electrolysis, the plant can produce methanol as
a valuable product for the energy, chemical, and transportation sectors, effectively turning
CO2 emissions into an economic asset. Conversely, the CO2 storage pathway proves to be
essential during periods of high electricity demand and prices, ensuring the plant can main-
tain operations while adhering to environmental regulations.
Technologically, the integration of carbon capture, liquefaction, and methanol synthesis was
successfully modeled, showcasing the practicality of scaling up these processes at the Avedøre
CHP plant and achieving a 2.78% increase in profitability compared to the current CCS
scenario. However, frequent transitions between the two pathways were identified as a chal-
lenge to equipment performance and lifespan. To address this issue, two approaches were
proposed—both involving the calculation of electricity price averages across current, previ-
ous, and subsequent hours. While these methods effectively reduced single-hour operations,
they did so at the expense of profitability.
The sensitivity analyses performed in this study provide critical insights into the key factors
influencing the economic and operational viability of the dual-pathway system. Variations
in methanol and carbon credit prices were shown to significantly impact profitability, with
higher methanol prices offering greater incentives for PtM operations. The analysis also
showed that when increasing both variables, the resulting profit would see the maximum
increase from all studied scenarios - augmenting by a 73.80%.
Furthermore, the study revealed the influence of fluctuating electricity prices on the system’s
dynamics, demonstrating that low-cost renewable energy is critical for the competitiveness
of methanol production. In addition, the cost of capital expenditures (CAPEX) for PtM
infrastructure and advancements in carbon capture technology were identified as pivotal el-
ements in determining the system’s long-term sustainability and economic efficiency. The
study’s results emphasize the importance of precise operational modeling to optimize plant

December 30, 2024
performance and ensure a seamless transition between CO2 storage and methanol production
based on prevailing market and environmental conditions.
The broader implications of this work extend beyond the Avedøre CHP plant. The dual-
pathway approach offers a scalable framework that can be applied to other power and in-
dustrial facilities seeking to reduce carbon emissions while generating economic value. By
aligning with Denmark’s commitment to achieving a 70% reduction in greenhouse gas emis-
sions by 2030, the proposed system demonstrates how innovative combinations of CCS and
PtM technologies can contribute to both national and international climate targets. Addi-
tionally, the findings reinforce the importance of robust infrastructure and policy support,
including subsidies for renewable energy and carbon credits, to incentivize the adoption of
similar systems.
Despite its strengths, the study acknowledges several limitations. The analysis was based
on simulated data and assumed ideal operating conditions, which may differ from real-world
scenarios due to factors like equipment reliability, market volatility, and unanticipated reg-
ulatory changes. Additionally, the scope of this research focused on the techno-economic
aspects of the system, leaving room for future studies to explore broader considerations,
such as lifecycle assessments, supply chain optimization for hydrogen, and potential syner-
gies with other renewable technologies.
In conclusion, this thesis provides a valuable contribution to the field of sustainable energy
and carbon management by demonstrating the viability and benefits of a dual-pathway sys-
tem at the Avedøre CHP plant. The integration of carbon storage and PtM offers a versatile
and forward-thinking solution to the dual challenges of climate change mitigation and eco-
nomic sustainability. As renewable energy capacity continues to expand and technology costs
decline, systems like the one proposed here have the potential to play a transformative role
in the global transition to a low-carbon economy.
