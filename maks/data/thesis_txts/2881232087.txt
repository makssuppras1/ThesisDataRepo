Abstract 
Over 90% of the world’s trade is carried out by maritime transportation and it constitutes the 
most cost-effective way to move goods around the world. The port of Rotterdam is the largest 
European port with a well-structured transportation network and connections across Europe. 
However, in order to develop an efficient and competitive structure, the need of designing an 
orchestrated network arises. This project focuses on the European Gateway Services (EGS) 
network, which provides connections among the port of Rotterdam and several destinations in 
Europe. The ultimate goal of this project is to upgrade an intermodal network to a dynamic, 
real-time synchromodal network. More specifically, the problem of the distribution of freight 
is tackled, both at a strategic and an operational level, including the aspect of Synchromodal 
Planning. First, at a strategic level, the possibility of adding a consolidation center in the 
network is considered. The problem is formulated as a multi-commodity flow problem, 
including the fixed capacities, based on the services provided by the tri-modal EGS network, 
topology, demand, and availability of modes. The consolidation decision is based on the 
critical factors: efficiency, economies of scale, emissions, and overall costs. Next, the 
problem is tackled at an operational level, introducing the Synchromodal Planning. At this 
level, the problem is extended in the time dimension. The arrival time of cargo vessels and the 
scheduled departures of each mode are included. This information is used for a real-time 
switch among modes. The problem is now formulated for the single container, which is 
handled as a unit ruled by specific characteristics. The network includes the terminal 
locations, the gates of EGS and the Cross – Dock location. The model incorporates the 
topology, network services, demand, and “urgency” for each container. The objective is to 
create a model that could take online decisions and adjust the distribution patterns of goods, 
based on real-time data provided by an online exchange information platform. Finally, the 
results of the two aforementioned models are assessed and further analysed, providing 
interesting suggestions for a more efficient distribution plan with consolidated services.

1. Introduction 
In this chapter, a general introduction of the problem and the case study will be given. The 
main characteristics of the Port of Rotterdam network are introduced, along with the specific 
case of EGS network and its characteristics. The research problem and questions are stated, 
as well as the scope of research. 
 General Introduction to the Port of Rotterdam case 
The majority of world’s trade is carried out by maritime transportation, and it constitutes the 
most cost-effective way to move en masse goods around the world. The Port of Rotterdam 
(PoR) is the largest European port, with an annual throughput around 450 million tonnes. 
Rotterdam port acquires a well-structured transportation network, with connections around 
Europe and hence it is an attractive transshipment port. Its extended network offers services to 
Germany, Austria, Switzerland, Scandinavia, the UK, Eastern Europe and the Baltic countries 
(Figure 1.1). The area of the port is almost 12,500 ha (land and water) and it is extended for 
over 40 km. Every year the port is visited from 30,000 seagoing vessels and 110,000 inland 
vessels (Port of Rotterdam 2012).  
 
Figure 1.1 Hinterland transportation of goods in intermodal network (European Gateway Services 2016) 
The high position that PoR holds is due to several reasons, such as the good connectivity with 
the European countries, the geographical position for inland transport, the rail and road 
infrastructure. Starting from the overall structure of the port network, there are routes 
providing accessibility to many European and non-European destinations within 24 hours. 
==PAGE:18==
 
2 
 
The goal of Port Authority is to achieve an optimal distribution plan of cargo, and thus it 
provides expert advice and guidance to accomplish modal shift. Accessibility of all rail, road 
and cargo is of utmost importance. This concept includes services as InlandLinks (Inland 
Links 2015), Rail Scout (Rail Scout 2009) and Blue Road (Blue Road 2016), which create a 
more efficient network. PoR obtains a tri-modal transport network, meaning that there is the 
possibility of choosing between inland barge, rail and road.  
To begin with, inland shipping is a reliable and sustainable, both economically and 
environmentally, mode of transport. The physical location of the port is an asset, since it is 
situated at the mouth of the Rhine and the Meuse, leading to a direct transportation in the 
Netherlands, Germany, Belgium, France, Switzerland and Austria. These services are covered 
by a wide range of barges and frequent connections. The timetables vary among the 
destinations, providing services that range between one day to close destinations 
(Netherlands, Belgium, and Germany) and four to farther destinations (Switzerland).  
Inland Water Transport (IWT) is robust in the long distance transportation, and it could be 
combined efficiently with road transport for short distances. Inland shipping is responsible for 
a substantial percentage of the supply and transport of goods between the PoR and 
destinations in Europe.  
Furthermore, rail services in the network of the port are reliable and fast, providing short 
transit time to Western Europe destinations, ranging from some hours to a day. The 
combination of transporting large quantities of cargo, within a small time period designates 
railway to an efficient mode of transport. However, there is still room for improvement, since 
rail cargo transport possesses the lowest share among the three modes. There are more than 
250 weekly intermodal rail links beginning and ending in Rotterdam and the terminals in the 
port area are equipped with their own rail freight handling facilities. On top of this, the 
Rotterdam Rail Service Center (RSC) is an organization responsible of handling shuttle trains 
and combined transport. This railway network also provides direct freight railway to Germany 
via Betuweroute, which is a 160 kilometer railway line that is specifically designed for rail 
freight. This line connects the Maasvlakte and the port of Rotterdam directly with the German 
railway network, and in 2014 the service has risen to 550 trains per week (SpoorPro.nl 2014).  
Road transport holds the highest market share in freight transport within European countries 
among the inland modes. In 2013, road transport accounted for 74.9% of the total inland 
freight transport (Eurostat 2015). This is justified by the fact that road transport is a fast 
==PAGE:19==
 
3 
 
option for short distances, making the distribution just-in-time regardless of location or time. 
Especially in the port of Rotterdam, 40% of goods transported from the port by road remains 
in the region of Rotterdam. A 50% is destined towards the Dutch market and only a 10% 
crosses the border. Longer distance destination are mainly transported by barge and rail. 
Therefore, it should be stressed that road transport is a vital part in an intermodal logistic 
chain. Several are the cases where truck is the only option, especially in the beginning and 
final leg of a sequenced transport. Apart from the physical boundaries leading to the choice of 
truck, it demands incomparable low infrastructure costs, in contrary to rail. Also it is 
extremely flexible and it is always a backup solution. In the area of the port the main artery is 
A15, connecting the port with the rest of the country and Europe. Bridges and tunnels ensure 
a good flow of traffic in this densely populated area.  
The modal split of freight transport indicates the share of each mode of transport in the total 
inland transported goods, and it is expressed in tonne-kilometres (tkm). According to a recent 
research conducted in 2013, the modal split in the Netherlands was 38.9% for inland barge, 
4.9% for rail and 56.2% for road (Eurostat 2013).  However, the goal of the Port Authorities 
is to create a global leading European hub and gateway within the next years and built an 
integrated network, that would be the frontrunner in sustainable and efficient supply chains. 
Hence, several projects investigate the opportunities of creating a wide platform that engages 
the cooperation of different companies to achieve an integrated synchromodal network.  
The definition synchromodal originates from intermodal transportation. Intermodal 
transportation is a specific type of multimodal transportation, where the load is transported at 
the same unit without handling of the goods themselves (Crainic & Kim 2007). Synchromodal 
transportation follows as the next step of intermodal, and engages a structured, efficient and 
synchronized combination of two or more transportation modes. A synchromodal 
transportation involves the transportation through an intermodal network, along with a 
dynamic adaptation of the planning depending on real time data and disturbances that may 
occur. Therefore, it gives the possibility to carriers for real time switching to the best possible 
mode at any time (Verweij 2011).  
 Description of the European Gateway Services network 
In order to improve accessibility in the PoR and to strengthen its position as the biggest cargo 
hub in Europe, the Port Authorities are collaborating with several partners in an effort to 
==PAGE:20==
 
4 
 
formulate a uniform platform. Hence, both authorities and partners strive to create the best 
possible connection by land and water, having as a core an efficient and durable 
infrastructure. Hereby, an introduction to the specific case of Rotterdam network will be 
given, along with the partners participating in it.  
To begin with, European Container Terminals (ECT) is one of the main container terminal 
operators in Europe, it handles most of the containers passing through the PoR and possesses 
several deep-sea terminals in the port. ECT is inextricably linked to the European Gateway 
Services (EGS). EGS is a subsidiary company of ECT, and is the connection of the terminal 
locations and the network. ECT provides high frequent rail and barge connections between 
Rotterdam port and Europe. Moreover, EGS provides connections between other ports and 
terminals in West Europe. This network includes several inland terminals in strategic 
positions spread around Europe, all of them having frequent connections with the deep-sea 
terminals of ECT (Figure 1.2). At the moment EGS acquires 23 terminals in total, where the 9 
are located within the Netherlands and the rest in Germany, Belgium, France, Switzerland and 
Austria (European Gateway Services 2016). These terminals provide auxiliary services, such 
as transitory storage, pre and post truck services, empty depot services. All the subsidiary 
services provide customers with flexibility and promote a more efficient transport network. 
The concept beneath the EGS is also to operate as extended gates, where the customers can 
skip paperwork while travelling under ECT. This allows companies to achieve additional time 
savings and increase efficiency.  
 
Figure 1.2 European Gateway Services network (European Gateway Services 2016)

In addition, it should be stressed that operating in an orchestrated network, EGS and ECT, 
achieve a significant reduction in CO2 emissions. In the grounds of efficiency, the goal of 
these operators and the PoR is to achieve a synchromodal organization of hinterland transport, 
where the mode of each trip will be based upon the most efficient and sustainable solution.  
Real time switches will be allowed, providing the best possible solution for customers and 
operators, maintaining a balanced network. Especially the last years a port data base is created 
for information exchange and transparency in transportation. Portbase is a platform for cargo 
planning and the exchange of information in the port of Rotterdam. This Port Community 
System provides intelligent services and a 24/7 exchange of information, both among 
customers and the government, within the Netherlands and Europe. The advantages of this 
centralized service are several. First of all, it provides greater efficiency and lower costs, since 
it offers over 40 different services for all links in the logistic chain. Data indicated that in 
annual basis there was a € 186 million added value for businesses, 30 million less calls and 
100 million less emails in total, and 30 million vehicle kilometers avoided. In addition, it is an 
open platform, and hence other parties that offer services of general logistics interest can 
bring added value to the port community. Finally, the users of the Port Community System 
are central to Portbase, promoting a more centralized idea of cooperation between the parties 
via a user-friendly service platform (Portbase). 
Apart from the ECT terminals in Maasvlakte, there is an extension of the port called 
Maasvlakte 2, where two new terminals started operations in 2015. Rotterdam World 
Gateway (RWG) and APM Terminals Maasvlakte II revealed a totally new era of container 
handling. Both are completely automated, offering new dimensions in speed, sustainability 
and safety. In Maasvlakte 2, information always predates the containers, since both terminals 
request carriers, forwarders and shippers to provide all relevant container information in 
advance via Portbase. This leads to an improvement for each link in the logistical chain. 
Finally, the current strategy to be applied from the 1st of March 2016, is that containers from 
both APM Terminals Rotterdam and APM Terminals Maasvlakte II will have the opportunity 
to use EGS network for transportation. This new update in the network will create dense 
services and an increase of the demand that has to be covered. The only prerequisite is that 
this cargo should be pre-notified. 
All in all, the distribution of goods in the Rotterdam port starts to have a more centralized 
character towards a more synchromodal approach. The scope of this research is to elaborate

on the idea of synchromodality, aiming to build a model that represents an intermodal 
network planning, with a real time switch of mode decision. 
 Scope of the research 
This research is focused on the EGS network, including the two deep – sea terminal locations, 
Rotterdam Delta and Euromax, in the Maasvlakte area, and 19 extended gates and hinterland 
terminals (Amsterdam, Antwerp, Basel, Dortmund, Duisburg, Enns, Liege, Moerdijk, 
Munich, Neuss, Nuremberg, Oss, Strasbourg, Stuttgart, Terneuzen, Venlo, Vienna, 
Vlissingen, Willebroek).   
The scope of the research is the tri-modal network connecting the terminal locations in the 
Maasvlakte port with the Netherlands and Europe. The objective is to identify whether it is 
profitable and more efficient to use more centralized transportation, with flows accumulated 
at a strategically located Cross – Dock location (CD). Both problems’ objective is to 
minimize the overall costs incurred by variable and fixed costs. The variable costs included in 
the problem formulation incorporate the transportation and the emissions costs for conveying 
a load unit from an origin to a destination, and they are directly affected by the distance, the 
mode used and the average speed of the mode. Furthermore, the fixed costs that are taken into 
consideration include the fixed charge of using a service and a standard fee for a 
transshipment. 
 Problem statement 
In this part the research problem is outlined. The study is mainly focused on two interrelated 
directions. The first direction of the study introduces the decision of constructing a 
consolidation center at a strategic planning level. Thereupon, connected to the first problem 
the question of creating a CD is tackled at an operational level, and the dimension of time is 
introduced. The result given as an output at the strategic level is reevaluated and compared to 
the output provided by the operational, more detailed model. Following, the problems will be 
further analyzed, in order to depict the inner reasons of addressing them and their main 
characteristics. 
1.4.1 Distribution center design at a Strategic level  
The idea of creating a distribution center springs from the need to accommodate the upcoming 
demand of the extended port and create a more efficient manner of distribution. Since the 
==PAGE:23==
 
7 
 
network is not expanding, the additional demand from APM terminals transported via EGS 
network should be effectively distributed. At the strategic level the network topology is 
considered. Specifically, the locations of the already existing terminals, including port and 
hinterland terminals, with rail and barge connections are included. The problem is formulated 
based on a rough estimation of the yearly throughput of EGS network. This demand is 
distributed in the connections within the Netherlands and the destinations in Europe based on 
the results of the demand scenarios introduced later on. 
The establishment of a distribution center constitutes a crucial input in the network, since 
carriers and operators will have the opportunity to dispatch the container with cargo heading 
to the same direction, or shift between modes. Even though the terminals give the opportunity 
of temporary storage and most of them provide services with more than one modes, it is not 
easy to make a transit along the way. The advantage of a consolidation center, however, is 
that all cargo arriving within a time window could be aggregated and transported to their final 
destination, with the best possible mode. Hence, the driver of the decision of mode will be a 
matter of efficiency, economies of scale, emissions, and total service cost.  
Due to the fact that the focus of the research is on an established network, where the stations 
and the routes are already defined, the candidate positions are limited. The potential locations 
are narrowed down by the need of having all mode services in the position. Moreover, the 
importance of the station in terms of route connection and location is also added as a 
parameter of the decision.  
1.4.2 Synchromodal planning at an Operational level 
As a next step of the consolidation decision, a synchromodal network planning will be carried 
out. The components described at a strategic level also apply in this problem. However, the 
dimension of time is introduced, and the information of the real time arrivals of the cargo 
vessels in Maasvlakte port is included. This information along with the scheduled departures 
of each mode, is used for the synchromodal planning. 
The network includes the terminal locations, the distribution center and the gates of EGS. The 
considered network is a directed graph, because the focus is mainly on the distribution of 
goods from the port terminals towards the customers. At this level, it is assumed that each 
cargo has an importance, also translated as a due date of delivery in the destination. This 
parameter is added and three “urgency” categories are considered.  
==PAGE:24==
 
8 
 
The overall objective is to create a model that could take on-line decisions and adapt the 
distribution patterns of goods, based on real time data that are provided by the online 
exchange information platform. Ultimately, to upgrade an intermodal network to a dynamic, 
real-time synchromodal network. Furthermore, it is of utmost interest to identify whether a 
CD decision could be taken into a strategic level, or if the real time level would lead to a 
different solution. Therefore, the factors that affect such a decision would be clearly pointed 
out in the end of this research.  
The results provided by this project could be further modified to apply in every network 
design problem. Moreover, goal of this thesis is to identify the factors that affect the 
consolidation decision and in what extend each factor. 
 Research questions 
The main research questions that this project aims to answer, when it comes to the 
synchromodal design of EGS network, are: 
Would it be more efficient to organize the distribution of goods through a Cross – Dock in the 
network? Which would be the best location at a strategic level? Does this decision differ at a 
Strategic and an Operational level? 
The core steps followed to answer this question include the search of the candidate locations 
at a strategic level, and then the validation of the results at an operational level. The output 
will be validated maintaining the core values of minimization of costs and emissions, and the 
optimal utilization of the available resources.  
 Report Structure  
First, a literature review is carried out in Chapter 2, addressing previous research conducted 
on the field and the respective methods used on similar cases. Thereupon, the network design 
at the strategic level is described in Chapter 3, followed by the data explanation imported in 
the model (Chapter 4), and the results given by solving the mathematical model (Chapter 5). 
Then, the operational model is introduced in Chapter 6 and the aforementioned structure is 
kept. Finally, some general conclusions are extracted, as well as recommendations for further 
research on the topic.

2. Literature Review 
In order to develop the methods to answer the research questions of Section 1.6, a literature 
review is conducted. Network design and intermodal transportation are widely used topics, 
since several decades, in Operations Research (OR). Following some of the existing OR 
models, applicable in the tested case, will be presented. In Section 2.1 an overview of relevant 
literature on distribution center location and design is presented, both at strategic and 
operational level. Moreover, the literature included in Section 2.1.2 is focused on the case of 
the port of Rotterdam. In Section 2.2 there is more recent literature regarding the design of 
hinterland synchromodal transportation networks. 
 Distribution Center design 
The creation of distribution centers is one of the main strategies in warehousing industry. 
Facing a continuous growth in freight transportation over the last decades cross-docking has 
emerged as an important tool handling the increasing volumes of goods in transport networks. 
Distribution centers are defined as the consolidation centers of goods from incoming 
shipments, so they can be sorted for outgoing shipments. Therefore, the inventory levels are 
eliminated, giving though the possibility of consolidation. The incoming shipments are 
batched with differently originated shipments or dispatched properly towards the outgoing 
routes without being stored (Apte & Viswanathan 2000). 
2.1.1 Consolidation decision at a Strategic & an Operational level 
Cross-docking research is mainly conducted towards physical layout and location of a cross-
dock (Chen et al. 2006). In the transportation sector few are the papers solving distribution 
problems with cross-docking, however such a method would enhance intermodality and 
efficiency in an extended network. The approaches used in those cases are developing mainly 
the types of networks presented below.  
In the first type of network the distribution flow starts from and ends at a single customer 
through a cross-dock. Research in this area includes Sung and Song (2003) and Chen et al. 
(2006). Sung and Song (2003) explored the problem of deciding whether to open a cross-dock 
or not, while assigning vehicles for transportation from a supplier to a single destination via 
one of the open cross-docks. There is a path-flow formulation used and since the potential 
==PAGE:26==
 
10 
 
paths are finite, the paths are explicitly generated with respect to the time restrictions. The 
objective of the problem is to minimize the cost of locating cross-docking centers and of the 
vehicles allocated for established services. They used a tabu search algorithm, with a short-
term memory updated at every iteration, to address the problem. The results indicated that the 
proposed tabu search algorithm solve the problems effectively, and it was stressed that it does 
not seem to be dependent on the network structure. Three different instances were tested of 
V=30, 50 and 80 nodes. The average duality gaps for these instances were 15, 13 and 11%, 
respectively (Sung & Song 2003).  
Chen et al. (2006), inserted the parameter of time windows and an inventory cost at the cross-
dock. The objective in the cross-docking problem is to find a minimum cost distribution plan 
involving cross-docks based on anticipated supply and demand. The method proposed 
combined simulated annealing and a tabu search. The hybrid metaheuristic proposed, includes 
a tabu list concept with the simulated annealing framework. In this manner, the tabu list was 
maintained, while simulated annealing performed neighborhood search. There is a fixed time 
horizon at 24 hours in test sets, as this is typically the longest time shipments transit at a 
cross-dock. The results show that the heuristics outperform CPLEX, providing solutions in 
realistic timescales (Chen et al. 2006). 
In the second type of network, the problem is handled as a pick-up and delivery transportation 
problem, where each vehicle originated at the cross-dock leaves to pick up or deliver goods. 
The vehicles return at the cross-dock after completing every delivery. Research on this 
method has been conducted by Lee et al. (2006), Wen et al. (2009) and Liao et al. (2010). The 
former problem is formulated as a network with multiple suppliers and customers, with a 
single cross-dock. There is a time window associated with each delivery and all vehicles are 
assumed to arrive simultaneously at the cross-dock from the pickup arcs. The objective is to 
minimize transportation costs, while considering a planning horizon for the whole process. 
The problem is solved as a Mixed Integer Programming (MIP) formulation and a tabu search 
algorithm is implemented. This algorithm is suitable in this case because it allows to escape 
from a local solution by the creation of various neighborhoods, prevents from returning to the 
local solution, and has a memory of the search processes. Several instances were generated 
with 10, 30 and 50 vehicles, respectively. The results were tested by producing an 
enumeration method which finds the optimal solution. A maximum of 4% error was obtained 
between the tabu search and the enumeration (Lee et al. 2006).  
==PAGE:27==
 
11 
 
Wen et al. (2009) considered the problem as a Vehicle Routing Problem with Cross-Docking 
(VRPCD) and is similar to the aforementioned approach, without considering simultaneous 
arrivals. A fleet of homogeneous vehicles is considered, consolidated at the cross-dock, 
without intermediate storage. This problem involves not only vehicle routing decisions, but 
also a solidification selection of the cross-dock. It is also formulated as a MIP problem, 
followed by a tabu search heuristic within an adaptive memory procedure. The objective is to 
minimize the total distance travelled and the two type of constraints involve vehicle routing 
and consolidation decisions at the cross-dock. It is stressed that without the consolidation at 
the cross-dock constraints, the model is essentially the problem of solving two independent 
Vehicle Routing Problems with Time Windows (VRPTW), which provide a lower bound for 
the VRPCD. A crucial result of this research is that this problem could be strongly affected by 
the pickup and delivery parts, the time windows, and the number of supplier-customer pairs. 
Moreover, the pickup and delivery routes are interrelated, since same vehicles need to first 
pick up and then deliver goods within a time window. Hence, an optimal solution will 
incorporate the shortest route for a complete operation, along with the potential 
loading/unloading at the cross-dock within the time windows. These two aspects usually are 
conflicting. Near optimal solutions are produced for small instances (less than 1% from 
optimum) within less than 5 seconds of computational time. For large instances (30 to 200 
customers), the gap between the average solution value and the lower bound (2 separated 
VRPTW’s) is consistently below 5% (Wen et al. 2009). 
Liao et al. (2010) considered the same problem as Lee et al. (2006). The objective of this 
problem is to determine the number of vehicles and a set of schedules within a minimum 
operational and transportation cost. The main difference is that this research starts with an 
initial solution scheme and a defined number of vehicles. It is composed then by assigning 
suppliers to vehicles and retailers to available vehicles. Therefore, the tabu search algorithm 
in this research demonstrates some major differences compared to the previous paper. First of 
all, there is a different definition of the neighborhood because they assign a single node to 
another vehicle one at a time, while Lee et al. exchange nodes between two vehicles. 
Furthermore, it is allowed to remove an empty vehicle. Due to this adaptation this algorithm 
gives a percentage improvement and lower computational time compared to Lee et al. In the 
instances of 10, 30 and 50 vehicles, there is an average improvement of 16.7%, 31.4% and 
14.7%, respectively. The main reason for this improved performance compared to the 
previous case is that in Liao’s et al. research the number of vehicles is fixed, while in Lee’s et 
==PAGE:28==
 
12 
 
al. research the algorithm tries continuously to reduce the number of required vehicles (Liao 
et al. 2010).  
2.1.2 The port of Rotterdam case 
Apart from the aforementioned research conducted on the consolidation of a distribution 
center in a transportation network, there is an exploration on the topic in the port of 
Rotterdam. The development of intermodal networks provided a different extend in hinterland 
transportation, stressing the importance of quality of hinterland transport for the 
competitiveness of a port. Therefore, several studies are focused on improving the handling of 
barges and addressing hinterland accessibility as a strategic matter. More specifically, 
research conducted by Konings (2007) considers the possibilities to improve container barge 
handling in the port of Rotterdam, from a network perspective. This paper engages the idea of 
a revised network, where the existing services are split into a trunk line operation in the 
hinterland and collection/distribution operations in the seaport. The reorganization of the 
network suggested, aims in the reduction of waiting times by improving the quality of 
information between actors, introducing fixed time windows to handle the barge at seaport 
and better route planning. A marginal cost model is used to determine potential benefits of 
these revised services, testing the economic feasibility of these network operations. The 
results indicate that the split services can improve the competitiveness of barge hinterland 
transport. However, it is underlined that the efficiency of the network is also highly depended 
on other conditions, such as the design and organization of distribution and the characteristics 
of the trunk line in the hinterland (Konings 2007). 
A continuation of the previous study conducted by Konings, Kreutzberger, and Maraš (2013) 
concerned the development of a barge distribution center in the area of the port of Rotterdam. 
The aim of this paper is to examine how handling of container barges in the port of Rotterdam 
could be improved, in order to solidify and expand barge transportation. Barge handling could 
be a reliable alternative to trucks, due to the rising costs and congestion faced in this section. 
Therefore, seaport accessibility is included in the agenda of port authorities to advance 
sustainability. The idea is to formulate a hub-and-spoke network in order to reduce terminal 
congestion, excess routing and long waiting time for barge. Hence, the problem is addressed 
as a hinterland organization problem, instead of a terminal issue. Several factors are taken into 
account for the cost performance of the hub-and-spoke network (location, service model in 
seaport/hinterland, transport means, transhipment) and the impact is illustrated with an 
==PAGE:29==
 
13 
 
empirical analysis. The results indicate that a reformulation of the network could deliver a 
better cost performance, given that handling costs in hub are kept within limits. This is an 
outcome of reduced sailing time, terminal handling and waiting time, which induce more 
substantial costs compared to the handling costs in the hub. However, it is stressed that 
additional costs of the proposed network cannot be easily calculated, since barge hub 
terminals do not yet exist. Therefore the research results were focused on the marginal costs 
for handling, to derive a break-even cost performance of both current and hub-and-spoke 
services. In general the cost performance for the proposed services is calculated to be better 
when the hub is located at a greater distance from the seaport, creating economies of scale in 
the hub-seaport link (Konings et al. 2013). 
 Design of a hinterland Synchromodal transportation network 
Intermodal transportation is defined as the transportation of goods by a sequence of at least 
two different modes of transportation, where the load is transported from origin to destination 
in one and the same intermodal transportation unit (e.g. TEU) without handling of the goods 
themselves when changing modes (Crainic & Kim 2007). The next step of intermodal 
transportation is synchromodal transportation. Synchromodal transportation involves a 
structured, efficient and integrated network, making use of all transportation options in the 
most flexible way. This type of network gives the possibility of choosing the best mode 
combination, based on the operational circumstances and/or customer requirements (Verweij 
2011). 
One of the first research found in the literature is conducted by Macharis and Bontekoning 
(2004), who performed a scientific review about the opportunities for operations research in 
intermodal transport. The research focuses on the different stakeholders participating in these 
operations and the importance of the collaboration between them to create an intermodal 
network. The major conclusions of this research are that intermodal problems are studied 
from several perspectives and techniques, but research is still limited. Moreover, the 
complexity of problems led to the development of the existing OR techniques and the 
emergence of new heuristics. Also they pointed out the lack of research in problems such as 
the number of terminals in a network, location decisions for hub-terminals, consolidation 
strategies, and allocation of capacity and scheduling of operations in terminals (Macharis & 
Bontekoning 2004). Similar research has been carried out by Steadieseifi, Dellaert, Nuijten, 
Van Woensel, Raoufi (2014) for multimodal freight transportation. Also in this research the

underlined, which should be compromised in order to achieve efficient operations. The most 
common techniques used, as indicated by the literature, are decomposition and relaxation 
techniques, branch-and-cut algorithms and tabu search metaheuristic algorithms. The

Riessen, Negenborn, Dekker, Lodewijks (2013) conducted a research about the impact and 
relevance of disturbances in an intermodal container network at the planning process. The 
problem was focused on the connection between the sea terminal in the port of Rotterdam and 
North West Europe through the European Gateway Services (EGS). They formulated a linear 
programming model Linear Container Allocation model with Time-restrictions (LCAT) in 
order to create an adequate network design able to adapt to occurring disturbances. Three 
main types of transit disturbances are determined on the network performance: early 
departure, late departure, and cancellation of inland services. The influence of a disturbance is 
calculated as impact and relevance measures. These two measures indicate the additional cost 
due to a disturbance and the difference in cost between an optimal and a local planning 
update, respectively. The model combines the allocation of containers to paths with capacity 
constraints in all corridors, it allows overdue delivery at a penalty cost and it combines self-
operated services with subcontracted transport. The feasible paths are predetermined based on 
a space-time graph of the service schedule. The results of the study indicated that the use of 
full updates does not result in large cost reductions compared to local updates and the 
development and implementation of real-time planning methods for full updates is costly. 
Specifically, 6-16% more impact can be prevented if full updates are used and it was noticed 
that during full updates large amounts of containers may be rescheduled. While in contrary, 
local planning method could be improved to give planning results even closer to the optimal 
full update and to eliminate the necessity of full updates (B. Riessen et al. 2013). 
Another research conducted by Riessen, Negenborn, Dekker, Lodewijks (2013) dealing with a 
similar problem located in the same area, proposes a centralized planning of the container 
transportation by applying a model combination of a path-based formulation and a minimum 
flow network formulation. There are overdue penalized deliveries and a combination of self-
operated and subcontracted services. The service network design considers the network

the EGS network. This research focuses on the transportation from the seaport to a hinterland 
terminal and vice versa.  It is formed as a mixed integer linear problem, where the paths are 
predetermined, and defined by using the k-shortest path method based on the geographical 
length of the network arcs. Moreover, more than two intermediate transfers are not considered 
and paths with more than 10% detour are discarded, where detour is measured as the distance 
difference to the destination from both ends of a leg. In the EGS case study, this method of 
path selection between all network terminals resulted in a set of 13977 paths. There were four 
different scenarios investigated, and the current EGS network was assumed as the basic

due times. In the third scenario due times are ignored. Finally, a scenario without the 
possibility of selecting subcontracts is assessed. The results indicate that a combined business 
model for the network could be further investigated, as the transit costs can be reduced with 
lower transfer costs, which possess a large percentage of the transportation costs. Starting 
from the base scenario, a 22% of transportation costs arise due to on time delivery 
requirement. Moreover, the 10.6% costs are penalty costs for late delivery, indicating the 
importance of overdue flexibility in delivery introduced in the model. In the second case the 
number of transfers increase by 4.1% and the transit costs are reduced by 7.3%. Also the 
number of containers with an intermediate increase from 1.2% to 5.4%. In the third scenario 
there is 22% reduction of transit costs. Finally, in the last case the value of combing the self-
operated and subcontracted transports is underlined in order to reduce transportation costs (B.

A recent study from Behdani, Fan, Wiegmans, Zuidwijk (2014) introduces a multimodal 
schedule design for synchromodal transport systems. A mathematical model for integrated 
service schedules is proposed and an analysis on the benefits of synchromodal transport 
services is performed for the hinterland network of the Port of Rotterdam. The research aims 
to evaluate the possible trade-offs between the service and cost aspects of multiple modalities 
in a transport system. The network is addressed as a combination of Stationary Resources 
(transport infrastructure) and Moving Resources (modes), which provides services between 
specific origins and destinations. They formulated a mathematical model for “Operational 
Resource Scheduling” to apply the parameters of design and operation of a synchromodal 
freight network. In the problem all three modes are considered, there is only one route for 
each transport mode, the origin and destination are the same for all modes, and hence, there is

practitioners. Extending the presented model for cases with intermediate transit terminals is 
another possible direction for future work (Behdani et al. 2014). 
As the need of real-time decision and action has arisen, in order to achieve a more efficient 
network, Riessen, Negenborn, and Dekker (2014) on their research proposed the formulation 
of an online Decision Support System. The aim is to define suitable rules for the allocation of 
containers by involving the solution structure of an integrated method used offline. In this 
paper the reasons that prevent the formulation of a centralized approach are stressed, as well 
as the importance of an online approach. This online system will provide an improved 
planning, by allocating directly a container in the available inland service. Moreover, it does 
not require extreme automation and allows a centralized planning approach without entirely 
centralized information. The methodology used to address the problem, starts from the 
historical demand data, uses LCAT model of offline optimization (B. Van Riessen et al.

method for obtaining the real-time decisions was based on Decision Trees (DT). The DT are

solution for each demand set. Then the result solutions are used for the growth of a single DT 
and ultimately the selected DT is used in a planning heuristic. The planning heuristic is 
formulated by allocating a container in the first available service in the classification table, or 
alternatively if there is no capacity in the service, it is assigned according to a greedy 
approach. Two different scenarios were tested assuming different weights of TEU (Twenty –

A more resent paper on the topic of synchromodality composed by Riessen, Negenborn, 
Dekker (2015) includes an overview of the current research in Rotterdam case from practice. 
The majority of papers analyzed are already used in the current literature review. Starting 
from a definition of synchromodality, the creation of the transportation plan for a network of 
inland services is indicated as planning in this paper. Moreover, the importance of flexible 
planning is underlined, in order to achieve a more reliable network able to deal with 
disturbances. A synchromodal network would also provide lower costs and emissions, 
establishing more efficient distribution and on-time performance. Starting in 2007 ECT 
introduced the EGS to create a synchromodal network and introduced regular rail services on 
the corridor between Rotterdam and the inland Venlo terminal. EGS is now extended to 
around 20 hinterland terminals, with more than a hundred barge and rail services between 
deep sea ports of Rotterdam and Antwerp and inland terminals. However, due to several 
reasons the transportation is still considered per corridor and not as a whole network. First of 
all, there is a lack of suitable methods to create an integrated network planning. Moreover, the 
real-time changes and switches are still made manually centered on individual corridors, 
facing them as a disturbance and not holistic in the network. Finally, customers play a 
significant role, due to the predefined transportation contracts, which restrict operators’ 
flexibility. All three aspects are introduced in this paper as areas of improvement to achieve 
synchromodal transportation in EGS case and the respective literature in each of these fields 
is described (Riessen et al. 2015). 
Li, Negenborn, and De Schutter (2015) conducted a research on intermodal freight planning 
problems among deep-sea terminals and inland terminals. They firstly formulated an 
Intermodal Freight Transport Network (IFTN) model to incorporate the characteristics of an 
intermodal network. Then the problem was restructured as an optimal intermodal container 
flow control problem, by elaborating on the proposed IFTN model, and a receding horizon

transport demands. The base model (IFTN) incorporated node and link behaviour constraints,

In this chapter the model and research approach to determine the optimal flow of the EGS 
network is presented. The factors affecting the distribution of goods are introduced and

 Strategic Network design methodology 
In this section of the project, the network is modelled at a strategic level. The design of a CD 
location is considered, and this possibility is examined in terms of profitability. Furthermore, 
the location of the consolidation center is a substantial aspect, which is also under the scope 
of this study. Since this is a complicated decision, only some discrete locations are tested. The 
main factors affecting this decision are incorporated in the model as a cost of transporting one 
unit load, including the fixed cost of service and the variable transportation cost, an 
environmental cost of emissions and a transshipment cost. All these parameters are translated 
to monetary values and are included in the objective function. Ultimately, the final solution 
will be determined by the minimization of the overall costs with respect to some constraints. 
The constraints included in the formulation incorporate the demand towards the final 
destinations, the availability of services, the vehicles’ capacities and flow conservation in the 
network. Following the parameters of the problem formulations will be further explained.

3.1.5 Costs considered in the mathematical model 
There are four types of costs included in the mathematical model of the strategic level 
network design: 
 Fixed Cost – a settled cost depending on the number and type of vehicles used 
 Transportation Cost – the variable cost of transporting a unit between a pair of cities 
with a type of vehicle 
 Transshipment Cost – the cost of switching from a mode to another at a deep – sea or 
an extended gate/hinterland terminal 
 Emissions Cost – the variable cost of transporting a unit between a pair of cities with a 
type of vehicle 
At this point, it should be pointed out that in the considered network the transportation of 
goods always includes both the fixed cost of a service and the variable transportation cost of a 
load unit. More specifically, there is no distinction between committed transport, where 
containers are booked on an EGS service, and uncommitted transport, where containers are 
booked on existing services. 
 Fixed Cost 
This type of cost includes a fee on a service basis. In other words, this cost indicates the 
number of vehicles used in the network on a weekly basis. The number of vehicles is 
==PAGE:41==
 
25 
 
subsequently converted to an incurred cost per service. This cost is paid once for each vehicle, 
and thus the transport of a single container does not incur any extra costs, given that there is 
sufficient capacity. 
 Transportation Cost 
This type of cost introduces a cost per load unit transported between a pair of cities. More 
specifically, it is the variable cost which is paid on an arc basis, for every load unit. It depends 
on the distance of the leg and the type of vehicle used. 
 Transshipment Cost 
This type of cost arises every time a unit is unloaded from a mode of transport and then 
loaded to another. This switch can appear at any intermodal node, where in our case are all the 
nodes of the network, except for the deep-sea terminals in Rotterdam port. An intermodal 
node is a station that provides the possibility of switching between two different vehicles. In 
this formulation a container could make several transshipments in the network. 
 Emissions Cost  
The cost of emissions is included in the model to incorporate the minimization of the CO2 
footprint. In the problem formulation this cost is converted into a cost per container, 
depending on the type of vehicle and the distance covered. The emissions cost represents the 
internalized external costs that are translated into social welfare by using market-based 
instruments. The impact pathway approach (damage cost approach) is a widely acknowledged 
method and is used to incorporate air pollution costs. The internalization of external costs 
through the use of market-based instruments is generally regarded as an efficient way to limit 
the negative side effects of transport. Environmental costs are calculated by taking into 
consideration all damages of environmental nuisances (health costs, material damages, 
biosphere damages, long term risks). These costs include differences between transport 
modes, where measures differ depending on legislation and the level of environmental 
taxation or liability (Korzhenevych et al. 2014). The most important components affecting the 
cost of emissions introduce the impact of air pollution at human health, building/material 
damages, nature, noise, and climate change.

network. Finally, 𝑐𝑑 stands for the cross-dock location under evaluation. The set of directed 
arcs 𝐴 includes all feasible connections between each pair of nodes.  
Furthermore, a tri-modal network is considered including barge, rail and truck, where the 
different modes of transport are noted as 𝑚∈𝑀. Each type of vehicle 𝑚∈𝑀, has a physical 
capacity 𝑣𝑐𝑎𝑝𝑚. There is a fixed demand 𝑑𝑖𝑗 for each OD pair, with 𝑖∈𝑂 and 𝑗∈𝐷, which 
can be covered from one or more different modes of transport. In order to calculate the 
capacity on the arcs the following steps are used. First, the number of fixed services in the 
EGS network, conducted by rail or barge, is multiplied by the capacity of the respective 
vehicle type 𝑣𝑐𝑎𝑝𝑚, where 𝑚∈𝑀. This is the overall capacity between each OD pair. 
Afterwards, the OD pair capacity is converted into arc, by calculating the cumulative capacity 
of each arc, based on the services operating on that link. Therefore, the arc capacity with a 
vehicle type, and is noted as 𝑐𝑎𝑝𝑟𝑠𝑚, where 𝑟, 𝑠∈𝑁 and 𝑚∈𝑀. 
The costs included in the objective function of the mathematical formulation as described 
before are the fixed, transportation, transshipment and emissions costs. The fixed cost or the 
standard fee paid for each vehicle 𝑚∈𝑀 used in the network is noted as 𝑓𝑐𝑚. Moreover, the 
transportation cost  𝑐𝑟𝑠𝑚 introduces the variable cost of a load unit between a pair of 
consecutive cities 𝑟, 𝑠∈𝑁 on mode 𝑚∈𝑀. The transshipment cost is noted as 𝑡𝑐𝑟𝑚 and is 
associated with the switch between two different types of vehicles 𝑚∈𝑀 at an intermodal 
node. Finally, the emissions cost 𝑒𝑐𝑟𝑠𝑚 is introduced as the social cost that should be paid for 
the emissions occurring for transporting a load unit between a pair of cities 𝑟, 𝑠∈𝑁 on 
mode 𝑚∈𝑀.

The objective function (1) minimizes all the costs related to the fixed costs of the services, the 
transportation costs, the transshipment and emissions costs. Constraints (2) & (3) ensure that 
the demand in an OD pair will originate from the right terminal and will end up in the desired

destination.  Furthermore, constraint (4) ensures the flow conservation in every intermediate 
node in the network. In this problem, flow can be created/originated only in deep – sea 
terminal locations. Following, constraint (5) is introduced in order to count the number of 
load units that were unloaded and then loaded in a different type of vehicle at an intermodal 
station. This number 𝑦𝑠𝑚 is then used to calculate the transshipment costs. Constraint (6) 
provides us with the information about the number of vehicles used in the network. In fact 𝑧𝑚 
is the highest number of a vehicle of type 𝑚∈𝑀 observed in the network and is therefore 
used for the calculation of the fixed costs. Ultimately, constraint (7) ensures that the capacity 
of a vehicle type 𝑚∈𝑀 will not be surpassed on an arc 𝑟, 𝑠∈𝑁. This capacity is calculated 
based on the available services running on the network. The last three constraints define the 
type of the decision variables. 
At this point, it should be pointed out that 𝑧𝑚 is not the exact number of a vehicles of type 
𝑚∈𝑀 used in the network, but in contrary an estimation of them. In particular, this 
formulation of the problem does not give the exact number of vehicles used on each arc, and 
therefore the exact fixed costs, which would be more realistic. Instead of having this detailed 
information, the maximum number of vehicles observed in the busiest node is calculated and 
therefore added in the objective function as fixed cost. Ultimately, 𝑧𝑚 is an over estimation of 
the vehicles needed.

assumed regardless the location. Adding a constant cost in the objective function would not 
incur any changes in the final solution.

Design at a Strategic Level 
In this section, the formulation of the data used in the mathematical model is analyzed. This 
section summarizes all the sources used, in order to acquire the data, all the assumptions 
made and the methodologies used. This is the final part explaining the establishment of the 
data before applying the model.  
 Layout of the network 
As mentioned above, the network is represented as directed graph, including all possible 
connections among all origin – destination pairs. The distances are calculated in kilometers 
and the length assumed is the shortest path given by the map. The Table A. 1 includes all 
possible connections of the initial network assumed in the model. These distances are used to 
calculate the transportation (𝑐𝑟𝑠𝑚) and emissions (𝑒𝑐𝑟𝑠𝑚) costs between a pair of cities 𝑟, 𝑠∈
𝑁 on mode 𝑚∈𝑀. 
 Capacity of the network 
The capacities for each of vehicle (𝑣𝑐𝑎𝑝𝑚) are defined in TEUs, and are assumed to be 160 
for barge, 80 for rail, and 1 for truck. Furthermore, the capacity of the network is expressed as 
arc capacity given by the multiplication of the vehicles’ capacities and the services of the 
network (𝑐𝑎𝑝𝑟𝑠𝑚). This applies in the case of barge and rail. Regarding trucks it is assumed 
that there is unlimited capacity, therefore unlimited availability of vehicles. The weekly 
services of EGS network originating from Rotterdam terminals, as well as the capacities on 
each OD pair are presented in Table A. 2. Those capacities are used in order to calculate the 
accumulated capacity on every arc for each type of vehicle (European Gateway Services 
2016). 
 Estimation of the Demand 
The weekly throughput as introduced in the previous section is now distributed in the final 
destinations. The weekly flow is 5770 TEU’s. As a part of the pre – processing the validity of 
the three aforementioned demand scenarios is tested.

Ultimately, the costs of emissions (𝑒𝑐𝑟𝑠𝑚) are based on the Handbook on external costs of

under diesel particulate filter (DPF), in the category of bunker/tanker vessels. The considered 
capacity is 650 – 1000t. and the corresponding value is 5.6 (€/1000tkm).  
 Cross – dock location decision 
Making the decision of the candidate cross-dock location was a demanding task, requiring 
several parameters to be taken into account. The main aspects that influenced this decision 
where the network topology, the demand to each country, the connectivity of the cross-dock 
location compared to the other destinations, and the availability of modes. To begin with, it 
was calculated, based on the previous assumptions, that almost 54% of the demand is incurred 
within the Netherlands and Belgium. The rest half of the demand is destined to Germany, 
Switzerland, France and Austria. Therefore, it seemed natural to test two locations, the first 
close to the port terminals, located within the Netherlands and the second in a more remote 
location, in Germany, serving almost the one third of the demand. Another important criterion 
of the location is to serve both barge and rail, meaning that it should be on the sea side and 
close to an already existing rail station/terminal. Based on this criterion, the waterways 
connecting the Netherlands with Germany, France, Switzerland and Austria were explored. 
The waterways are represented in the following map (Figure 4.2). A location close to the 
borders of Germany and the Netherlands, before the separation of the main waterways 
towards different directions seemed to be a suitable location.

Following a synchromodal planning of the network will be assessed at an Operational level. 
Some components of the previous model have remained the same. However, there are major 
differences compared to the previous model. This model is now extended in time dimension,

and test whether the consolidation decision will change, when different parameters are taken

6. Synchromodal Planning at an Operational Level 
In this chapter the model and research approach to formulate an optimal plan for the flow of 
the EGS network at an operational level is presented. First the components that impact on the 
distribution of goods are introduced and quantified. In addition, the formulation of the 
problem is introduced and explained in Section 6.2. 
 Description of the problem at an Operational level 
This section introduces the Synchromodal Planning in the network of EGS, at an operational 
level. Synchromodal planning, by its definition, refers to the real time planning that gives the 
opportunity of dynamic changes of the plan to the operator. Therefore, the motivation of 
tackling this problem is to explore the dynamics of a network with consolidated services, 
combined with the flexibility of uncontracted services.  
The distribution plan is based on the different costs incurred by the transportation of one load 
unit, and the availability of services, or in other words, the capacity of the network, as before. 
However, there are extra parameters added at the operational level that will affect the final 
distribution plan. In particular, each container is associated with an arrival time at the deep – 
sea terminal, and an urgency that defines the required arrival time in the final destination. 
Specific services are scheduled in each terminal location. Furthermore, there is an overdue 
cost related to the potential delay of a container after the due date. It is therefore obvious that, 
in this level, time has a significant role, since the travel time needed to traverse an arc with a 
vehicle is included as a parameter, or even the time required to complete a transshipment. All 
the aforementioned parameters are translated into monetary values and are included in the 
objective function. Ultimately, the final solution will be determined by the minimization of 
the overall costs with respect to some constraints. The constraints included in the formulation 
incorporate the demand towards the final destinations, the availability of services, the 
vehicles’ capacities and flow conservation in the network. Following the parameters of the 
problem formulations will be further explained. 
6.1.1 Characteristics of the network  
To begin with, the layout of the network is maintained as in the previous model (Section 
3.1.1), therefore the previous explanation applies also in this case. The flow of the network is 
==PAGE:70==
 
54 
 
also defined as before (Section 3.1.2). The capacity is used slightly different in this model. In 
particular, in the operational model, the capacity is defined on a service basis based on the 
vehicle capacity. The capacity of a service exists if only there is a scheduled service on an arc 
at a specific time period.   
The demand is kept as before in terms of quantity (Section 4.3), however in the operational 
model the demand of an OD pair is not handled as a batch quantity, but in contrary the 
containers are handled as entities. This differentiation in the formulation is made in order to 
provide the operator of the network with the potential of unrestricted decision in terms of 
distribution. The main advantage is that each container can be differently shipped based on its 
destination and its due date, and not all containers heading to the same destination should 
follow the same path. On the other hand, this change creates a more complex formulation in 
terms of computational time needed to solve the problem. Ultimately, the distribution of 
arrivals of containers, with the vessels arriving at Maasvlakte port, is assumed to be random. 
6.1.2 Costs considered in the mathematical model 
The costs are also the same as before, except for the fixed costs that are neglected in this 
formulation (Section 3.1.5). In the former formulation, the available services exist as a 
capacity in the network and it is a part of the decision whether to use an extra vehicle or not. 
In contrary, the present model includes the scheduled services from each node and each type 
of vehicle. Since the goal of this model is to provide the decision of choosing any service, the 
intention was to not restrict the solution. The availability of services is the same in any case, 
thus the fixed costs are not included in the objective function. Moreover, a cost of overdue 
deliveries is added in the objective function as penalty, which is paid for every excessive hour 
after the scheduled. A high value is set in this cost, since the goal is to avoid extreme delays.  
6.1.3 Time considered in the mathematical formulation  
Regarding the time dimension, there are five categories of time considered in the operational 
model. First, the arrival time of the vessels in the deep – sea terminals, which are gathered by 
Marine Traffic website, for the Maasvlakte area (Marine Traffic 2007). Moreover, the 
departure time from each node is taken into consideration. This time constitutes a crucial 
parameter of the model, since it is based on the earliest scheduled departure, with a vehicle 
type, from the every station. Hence, the departure time from each node is the determinant of 
==PAGE:71==
 
55 
 
the distribution plan. Following, the travel time required to traverse an arc, using a vehicle 
type is included. This time is calculated based on the transit time provided from EGS 
(European Gateway Services 2016) for barge and rail, and by assuming an average speed for 
trucks. It is assumed that the transit time given incorporates the load/unload time to a vehicle. 
Moreover, it is presumed that a container could leave a station as soon as it arrives, and 
therefore no time in advance or release time is required. The transshipment time is assumed to 
be constant regardless the topology at the stations and the type of modes of the switch. 
Finally, the required time of arrival is based on the transit time from EGS network. This time 
is calculated based on highest travel time required to traverse a pair of cities (barge or rail), 
and is further elaborated in Section 7.4.  
6.1.4 Urgency categories  
Another parameter introduced in the Operational level model are the urgency categories of the 
containers. Specifically, it is assumed that there are three categories, defined as high, medium 
and low urgency. The required arrival time at the destination of these categories is calculated 
based on the transit time provided by EGS network (European Gateway Services 2016). In 
particular, the assumptions made will be further elaborated in the subsequent chapter about 
the data explanation.

The objective function (1) minimizes all the costs related to the transportation of a container, 
the transshipment, the emissions, and the overdue containers. In this problem, flow can be 
created/originated only in deep – sea terminal locations. Constraints (2) & (3) ensure that the 
single container will originate from the right terminal and arrive at desired destination.  
Furthermore, constraint (4) ensures the flow conservation in every intermediate node in the 
network. Each container traverses an arc once, using only one of the available vehicles, and 
this is represented by constraint (5). Following, constraint (6) is introduced in order to define 
whether there is a transshipment in an intermediate node or not. This variable 𝑦𝑠𝑚 is then used 
to calculate the transshipment costs. Constraint (7) ensures that each container is served by 
utmost one service, where service represents the arc (𝑟, 𝑠), the vehicle and the departure time. 
Moreover, constraint (8) is the linking constraint between the container and a service. In 
particular, these two variables are related to each other, since a service can only be used if 
there is a container traversing a specific pair of cities with a specific mode at the exact time. 
Constraint (9) is tailored to the origin nodes of a container, and it involves the earliest 
possible departure of a container arriving in one of the deep – sea terminals. The earliest 
departure time should be the first scheduled departure with a mode from the deep – sea 
terminal after the arrival. Depending on the urgency of a container there is the possibility of 
transporting it with a later service. Constraint (10) ensures that the capacity of a service, of a 
vehicle type, is not surpassed. The following constraint (11) calculates the departure time 
from each node. In particular, this time is calculated by using the information of the departure 
from the previous node, the travel time needed to traverse the pair of cities under 
consideration with a specific vehicle type, and the potential transshipment time. Using this 
constraint it is reassured that the earliest departure from the next node is the first scheduled 
service. Furthermore, constraint (12) is introduced in order to calculate the delay of a 
container at the destination node. As before, the departure time from the previous node is 
==PAGE:75==
 
59 
 
summed up with the travel time needed for traversing the arc, and is then compared to the 
required time of arrival. The residual value is the tardiness of the container. Constraints (13) – 
(16) define the type of the decision variables.

7. Data of Synchromodal Planning at an Operational 
level 
In this chapter the data as formulated to be used in the mathematical model are analyzed. 
Only the data that differentiate from the strategic model will be explained. This is the final 
part explaining the establishment of the data before applying the model. Following all the 
components are explained.  
 Characteristics of the network  
The layout of the network, the possible connections among all OD pairs, and the distances are 
as introduced in Section 4.1. The same applies for vehicles capacities, which are presented in 
Section 4.2. In this model, each service has the capacity of the vehicle type.  
The main assumption made in terms of the flow of cargo in the network is that there are some 
fix paths assigned to the OD pairs, in order to have consistency in the scheduled departures 
from each node. This applies in the cases of Oss, Liege, and Duisburg, which can be reached 
with more than one paths. In both cases, the shortest path was picked as the itinerary serving 
the path. Ultimately, Oss is served via Moerdijk, Liege via Antwerp, and Duisburg directly 
from Rotterdam: 
Rotterdam – Duisburg  
Rotterdam – Moerdijk – Oss 
Rotterdam – Moerdijk – Antwerp – Liege    
The number of services constitutes the network’s capacity and is given by EGS weekly 
services (European Gateway Services 2016). The exact scheduled time of the services was not 
provided, the departure of the services are randomly distributed within the day. This applies in 
the case of barge and rail. Regarding trucks, it is assumed that there is unlimited capacity, 
therefore unlimited availability of vehicles, and there are services departing from each node 
every six hours. The weekly throughput is maintained as before, and the weekly flow is 5770 
TEU’s. However, the number of containers used in the model is 2248, since only the cargo 
arriving within the three first days in the PoR is considered. Moreover, the containers arriving 
to their final destination after the one week time horizon, which is considered in terms of 
services, are neglected. Due to this modification, the overall flow towards Vienna is out of the 
==PAGE:78==

scope of this research, since the transit time from Rotterdam Delta and Euromax is 112 hours 
by barge and it occurred, based on the arrival time at the port, that none of the containers will 
arrive within the week. 
 Costs considered in the mathematical model 
The transportation, emissions, and transshipment costs are maintained as before. The overdue 
cost that is introduced in the objective function, is paid for every excessive hour after the 
required time of arrival. The penalty that should be paid for every overdue hour is 20 €. This 
is a relatively high price, compared to the scale of the others costs, since delayed deliveries 
are not desired. 
 Time considered in the mathematical formulation   
The arrival time is gathered by Marine traffic, in Maasvlakte port, including only container 
vessels for a 1-week period. Assuming that every week has the same characteristics in terms 
of vessel arrivals, the typical week of gathered data is for 22nd – 28th of March. The total travel 
time is taken directly from the EGS official page (European Gateway Services 2016), called 
transit time. The overall transit time for each OD pair is following distributed to the legs 
constituting the OD pair. These data are provided in Appendix A, Table A. 8, and apply for 
rail and barge. As regards trucks, an assumption is made about an average speed of 80 km/h. 
The scheduled departures from Rotterdam deep – sea terminals, along with the previous 
calculation of the transport time required to traverse an arc, define the departure times from 
each station with a vehicle type (barge/rail). The transshipment time is considered uniform, 
regardless type of vehicle and topology, and with a value of one hour. The required time of 
arrival of a container at its final destination is based on the following urgency categories.

9. Conclusions  
In this project, the real – case scenario of European Gateway Services (EGS) hinterland 
network is considered. The case study focuses on Maasvlakte Port, in Rotterdam (PoR), 
where the EGS network constitutes the gateway between the Netherlands and Europe. More 
specifically, this research tackles, both at a strategic and an operational level, the problem of 
the distribution of goods within this network. First, at a strategic level, the Network design 
problem is introduced, combined with decision of locating a Cross-Dock (CD) in the network. 
The incentive of addressing the first model is, therefore, to explore the result of a distribution 
plan through a CD location. At the next step, the Synchromodal Planning is considered, at an 
Operational level, by extending the problem in the time dimension. The objective of this 
problem is to reevaluate the solution of the strategic level, in an operational level. The 
motivation of addressing these problems, is to underline the superiority of a Synchromodal 
planning, in a network operating effectively under consolidated services.  
The main conclusions suggested by the Strategic Network Design problem, is that a CD 
location would not add much in the distribution plan. The distribution without time limitation, 
and with sufficient capacity will be carried out by the cheapest available mode, without 
making unreasonable transshipments. In addition, the CD in Rotterdam incurs incomparably 
high costs, therefore this candidate location is discarded. Another conclusion proposed is that 
a consolidation of services should be introduced in a more remote area, instead of the area 
near by the terminal locations. Ultimately, in an already intermodal network, the solution of 
the problem will consist of the shortest path.  
The main conclusion drawn by the Synchromodal Planning is that the consolidation of 
services suggests a better solution, providing in parallel distribution of goods, less delays, and 
reduced emissions. The benefits of having consolidated services are that the excess capacity 
of a vehicle is capitalized, and the plan is developed by incorporating the information for the 
upcoming itinerary. By including the time dimension and the overdue cost for delay, there is a 
switch into faster modes. Moreover, the decrease of overdue costs introduces a different 
solution, due to the tradeoff between the costs of transshipment and a delayed delivery. 
Furthermore, the trend followed by the costs at the strategic level is also confirmed at the 
operational level. Ultimately, the decision of consolidation should be taken at an operational 
level, since the synchromodal planning can only apply and make a difference in real-time.  
==PAGE:90==
 
74 
 
There are some limitations in this research, which should be improved in future research. First 
of all, the demand is assumed to be deterministic. However, there is a stochasticity in these 
events that should be introduced. In addition, the fixed cost of the services could be more 
representative at the strategic level, and also imported at an operational level. Moreover, a 
minimum load factor of the vehicles should be introduced in both level models. Furthermore, 
inexact methods could be applied in order to solve larger instances. 
The suggestion of this research is to upgrade an intermodal network into a synchromodal 
orchestrated network with, free of contract, services that could operate in parallel. The shared 
value created by such a network formation could be capitalized both by the operators of the 
network and the customers.

Appendix C: Cost estimation 
In order to analyze the transportation in EGS network, general cost formulas are estimated. 
The general cost structures are based on the available costs in the EGS network, provided by 
the research of Riessen (Riessen 2013). There is a cost function introduced for each mode. 
Following both transportation and fixed cost functions are presented, illustrating the cost per 
TEU and per service, respectively. 
Cost of Barge: 
The transportation cost of a load unit (TEU) for barge is given by the following function: 
𝑐𝑖𝑗= −5.11 + 0.14 ∙𝑑𝑖𝑗 
Where 𝑑𝑖𝑗 the shortest distance between a pair of nodes as is presented before. Since a 
negative start-up cost 𝑎 is unrealistic, the constant part of the equation is neglected.  
The fixed cost per service is calculated based on the weekly fee of a typical Benelux barge, 
based on Orca vessel. The weekly rent is 7083 €/wk. Therefore, an assumption is made, that 
each fixed barge service costs a daily rent, in other words 1011€. 
Cost of Rail: 
The transportation cost of a load unit (TEU) for rail is given by the following function: 
𝑐𝑖𝑗= 1.53 + 0.16 ∙𝑑𝑖𝑗 
The fixed cost per rail service is based on an electric train with capacity of 80 TEUs, and the 
value is 2008€ per trip. 
Cost of Truck: 
The transportation cost of a load unit (TEU) for truck is given by the following function: 
𝑐𝑖𝑗= 76.4 + 1.04 ∙𝑑𝑖𝑗 
In this case there are no fixed costs for trucks, since the infrastructure is free and an unlimited 
availability of trucks is assumed.
