Abstract
The spectrum of services provided by modern mobile networks to the customers is being
broadened by several novelties, such as Virtual Reality gaming, autonomous car control
or remote surgery. At the same time, the global number of users enjoying high-quality
video streaming and multimedia conferences everywhere from their portable devices is
skyrocketing. The requirements imposed to the mobile infrastructure as a consequence of
such processes are so demanding that telecommunication bodies have decided to design
and standardise a new, fifth generation of mobile networks. The vision of 5G assumes,
inter alia, much higher user throughput and ultra-high reliability. Realising substantial
improvements in such areas requires both architectural changes and paradigm shifts,
and better utilisation of the resources already available, for instance multiple interfaces
in smartphones or PCs. However, the most popular connection-oriented transport pro-
tocol, TCP, lacks the support of multi-path communication. At the same time, it is
desirable that the applications that use the legacy TCP need not be modified. The focus
of the thesis was to find and evaluate a transport-layer solution that would move 5G
architecture towards the throughput and reliability goals. MPTCP protocol attracted
the biggest attention, as it provides advanced multi-streamed data transmission fea-
tures and can still operate flawlessly with TCP-based devices. The study on MPTCP
consisted of theoretical analysis, conceptual model derivation and computer model im-
plementation. The goals of the modelling process were to discover protocol potential in
improving throughput and transmission recovery capabilities when an active path breaks
down. The design, programmed in OPNET Modeller, was simulated for FTP file trans-
fer between two double-addressed hosts. The results show that the modelling goals have
been partially fulfilled - a noticeable benefit in user throughput for FTP service could
be observed. However, model shortcomings did not allow to evaluate the behaviour of
the multi-path protocol in the resilience scenario. Nevertheless, it was suggested that
MPTCP can be applied in Dual Connectivity, a highly plausible member technology of
5G, offering bit-rate benefits by utilising simultaneous data transfer to the UE from 2
eNBs.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Humanity experiences now a true communication revolution, the pace and intensity of
which can be compared to industrial revolution. The way people perceive connectivity
is dynamically changing due to the presence of nearly-ubiquitous radio and satellite cov-
erage as well as the availability of fast Internet for more than 3 billion of people[22].
In fact, a possibility of establishing conference calls, sending e-mails or browsing mul-
timedia content has become perceived as a standard, which constitutes an important
part of everyday life.
The phenomenon of ever-decreasing cost of PCs, laptops and
various mobile devices (phones, tablets, etc.), together with more and more common
availability of such equipment in the global market, especially in developing and popu-
lous countries, cause the number of potential users of the Internet growing dramatically
for recent years. It is expected that the new generation of mobile networks, the first
commercial release of which is planned for the year 2020, will be able to serve more than
20 billion pieces of equipment, being used directly by humans, and at least 1 trillion
of IoT devices (machines that exchange data via Internet on a pre-programmed man-
ner, e.g. sensors sending measured values periodically) [32]. The hardware chosen to
enjoy the Internet is rather portable than stationary, which means that contemporary
members of the society desire to connect at any time and anywhere, using wireless tech-
nologies[21]. As natural consequence of that, the aspect of user mobility in a modern
telecommunication infrastructure is becoming a real challenge. Furthermore, the scope
of IP-based services, oriented so far mostly on web browsing, audio/video calls and VoD,
has broaden significantly - it is enough to mention the advent of VR/AR technology, the
first successful experiments with self-driving cars and strong interest in Internet of Ev-
erything. One has to mention that such a plethora of services imposes a set of extremely
strict, yet diverse requirements: starting from extremely high throughput and ultra low
latency, through very high reliability and excellent coverage, ending at improved energy
efficiency and environmental friendliness. Last, but not least, network traffic becomes
even more unpredictable and bursty, making the design of the mobile infrastructure even
more difficult. One may even state that it is unfeasible, if not impossible, to deploy a
single stationary solution, which would deal with all the variety of services, represented
by requirements, which are sometimes contradictory to one another. Therefore, one of
the key features of the mobile network of the future is flexibility. In order to make the
new, fifth generation of mobile network (5G) highly adjustable to instantaneous traffic
conditions, a lot of effort on developing software defined modules of the infrastructure
(be it traffic engineering, or cognitive radio), as well as on hardware virtualisation and
re-programmability, is being put[29]. Another important concept, ensuring appropriate
reliability level is multi-RAT communication - since 5G takes advantage of the legacy

technologies from previous generations, a UE will have several interfaces available at the
same time: LTE, Wi-Fi, a new 5G interface, and possibly others. The mobile terminal
would then use one or more of them, so that some data may be offloaded by a more con-
venient option (e.g. WiFi offloading LTE), and one interface takes over the transmission
should another one fail (e.g. mmWave-to-LTE handover). From IP network perspective,
such a new kind of UEs can be called multi-homed network devices, represented by
multiple IP addresses. However, the currently dominating transport-layer protocol pro-
viding reliable data communication, namely TCP[39], does not support multi-addressing
and therefore the whole burden of managing multi-path powered improvements, such as
the aforementioned traffic offload, is entirely left on the shoulders of applications. This
is not acceptable, as adapting all the applications would be incredibly expensive and
time-consuming. That is why the focus of this work has been directed to multi-path
TCP alternatives: SCTP[42] and MPTCP[17]. The latter one can operate seamlessly
with classical TCP and requires no changes to the applications, and for these reasons it
has been investigated more thoroughly. The protocol has been modelled conceptually
and then implemented and simulated in OPNET Modeller environment. The results
of the experiments have been juxtaposed with the performance of the legacy TCP. The
purpose of the modelling process was to gain an insight on whether MPTCP can become
a valuable member of 5G network, with a specific look at Dual Connectivity concept[48].
1.1
Organisation of the report
The thesis is organised as follows: chapter 2 explains briefly the evolution of mobile
networks and introduces 5G requirements, vision, use cases and architecture design con-
siderations. An overview of reliable transport-layer protocols for multi-path transmission
is provided in chapter 3. The fourth part of the paper documents the modelling pro-
cess of Multipath TCP, including modelling goals, conceptual design, implementation in
OPNET and model verification. Chapter 5 covers the considered simulation scenarios
and the results of the simulations, together with the analysis and discussion. The whole
work is concluded in chapter 6.

CHAPTER 2
Evolution of mobile
networks
In this chapter, the history of mobile networks is provided. Each generation is described
and the relations between them are highlighted. Then, the key points of 5G concept are
revealed. Final section presents a brief study on how the problem of user mobility is
handled in different network generations.
2.1
1G
First considerations concerning the development of wireless phone appeared during the
First World War, though due to a strong resistance from the wire-line telecommunica-
tion corporations the idea has been abandoned at that time[47]. Also, global conflicts
during the first half of the twentieth century kept public telephony almost entirely wired
(excluding military and governmental uses). The first mobile telephony deployment took
place in US in 1946 and from that time on, for next 20 years the analogue mobile infras-
tructures, using mostly vacuum tubes and car-mounted transmitters, became deployed in
several countries; however scarcity of spectrum and its poor reuse were major issues[16].
The advent of transistor and microprocessor allowed for significant equipment evolution
that led to the first big commercial cellular network deployment in Japan (done by NTT
company) and in Northern Europe (NMT). The network handled voice-only analogue
calls on a circuit-switched basis. The multiplexing system in use was FDMA - each of
the users could communicate on one of the available frequency bands, separated from
one another. The mobile network infrastructure was built on PSTN. Such technology is
now called the first generation mobile networks (1G).
2.2
2G
In 1982 a new standardisation group, named Groupe Spécial Mobile (GSM), was estab-
lished. The purpose of the body was to come out with a standard of a new generation
of mobile networks, that would enable better voice quality and coverage, encompassing
more users. The first commercial deployment happened in Finland in 1991 and then the
so called 2G technology spread through European countries, also in rural areas[11].
Contrary to previous solutions, the second generation of mobile networks is entirely
digital and uses both FDMA and TDMA - per each 200kHz channel there are maximum

8 timeslots available. Apart from voice calls, one is able to send short text messages
(SMS) - this service can be realised by means of additional SMS Gateway in core network.
Figure 2.1: GSM architecture
The above figure is a simplified illustration of 2G architecture. The user have their cell
phone (MS), equipped with a SIM card, containing essential identification information.
Mobile Stations belong to a specific cell, governed by a BTS, which receives and transmits
further the data captured from the phones. BCS handles the radio communication over
a group of BTS and is a middle-man between the BSS and the core network. MSC is the
most important unit of the NSS and is responsible for handling user calls, authentication
and registration within its area of operation. In order to fulfil these tasks, the module
accesses the content of database units within the core network. HLR entity provides
associations between network operator’s subscribers and their locations - thanks to such
data it is possible to properly establish a call. AuC consists of secret information used for
authentication of all of the subscribers. VLR is an unit that contains similar data as HLR,
but only for the users that are present within a region of the particular VLR/MSC. EIR
carries the identification information of all mobile devices within the network. During
user’s registration, thanks to EIR it is possible to manage the access to the network
for each individual phone. OSS constantly monitors network performance and traffic
conditions and, based on these, performs maintenance tasks[31].
It has to be mentioned that GSM has become a world-wide standard for mobile voice
communications. However, due to the circuit-switching nature and low throughput (14.4

2.3
3G
The third generation of mobile networks has been designed to enhance the performance
of GSM, but be backward-compatible at the same time to reduce costs of deployment.
Thus, voice calls are still handled by the circuit-switched part, whereäs the other sorts
of data flow through the packet-switched realm. In fact, the elements and operating
principles of the core network of UMTS (the technology 3G refers to) are similar to NSS
in GSM, and the novel part is the Radio Network Subsystem, namely UTRAN, that
provides the essential improvements to the network. RNS consists of:
• Radio Network Controller (RNC), managing the resources and controlling the
power within the RNS, performing data encryption/decryption and supervising
the nearest base stations - Node Bs;
• Node B - communicates directly with UEs, and is responsible for modulation and
coding, as well as soft handovers;
• User Equipment (UE) - equivalent to MS in 2G.
3G architecture is presented in figure 2.2.
An important feature of UTRAN is the support for both FDD and TDD, meaning
that uplink and downlink transmission may be separated in time or in frequency. 3G
networks utilise CDMA technique, in which all the users use the same frequency band
(spread spectrum technology). Each user is assigned a spreading code in order to stay
separated from one another.
Since the receiver must know the appropriate code to
retrieve the data, CDMA provides enhanced security. As the ”common” frequency band
in use is very wide (5MHz in 3G, thus named ”Wideband-CDMA”, or WCDMA), the
robustness against interference or jamming is improved. CDMA outperforms multiple
access techniques used in GSM also in terms of capacity - in 3G up to 384kbps speed
is achievable. However, synchronisation and power control requirements are strict, also
the complexity of communicating devices increases. In UMTS more than one type of
UE handover is supported. For example, the RNC may decide to handover a terminal

Standardisation efforts under the umbrella of 3GPP organisation (which started its ac-

As a result, so called HSPA approach has been proposed and is based on UMTS archi-

Evolution” (LTE), which leads directly to 4G concepts.
2.4
4G
As stated in the previous section, Long-Term Evolution is a natural continuation of 3G
development, and its goal is to provide a very fast and very low-latency infrastructure for
mobile Internet. However, starting from 3GPP Release 8 (first LTE version), backward
compatibility with UMTS has been stopped. The circuit-switched part for voice calls
has been dropped and the whole network architecture changed considerably, turning into
all-IP communications [6].

A new multiple access technique, Orthogonal FDMA, has been applied for LTE. In
OFDMA there are many narrow and densely spaced sub-carriers that are orthogonal to
each other, meaning that their placement does not disturb one another. Only 15kHz gap
is applied. As a result, the throughput is highly adjustable, as each of the sub-carriers
may be modulated independently, and the number of carriers may vary (peak data
rate may reach 300Mbps at maximum, whereäs the maximal bandwidth of the system
is 20MHz). The resources, represented by Physical resource Blocks (PRBs) are being
scheduled in time and frequency. LTE uses AMC, which means that, depending on radio
conditions, the current MCS may be switched between QPSK, 16 QAM and 64QAM. Air
interface uses HARQ for reliable data transmission. Also, another interesting feature is
Multi Input Multi Output, where both the transmitter and the receiver may use multiple
antennae to increase the bit rate of the communication.

Since LTE uses OFDM, the whole network operates with the same frequency, which may

LTE Release 8 provides significant improvements and clearly outperforms UMTS, but it

4G technology. In fact, the true 4G system is the one implementing the newest releases
of LTE: 10-12, called LTE-Advanced. LTE-A introduces the following features:
• carrier aggregation, increasing the bandwidth up to 100MHz,
• enhanced MIMO (up to 8x8 in DL and 4x4 in UL),
• Co-ordinated Multi Point (CoMP), enabling simultaneous transmission to UE from
multiple eNodeBs - require extremely low latency,
• Relay nodes, extending coverage in cell border scenarios.
All in all, it has become possible to achieve 1Gbps in downlink and 500Mbps in uplink,
with LTE-A.

Vision and requirements
In the previous sections all four generations of mobile networks, that have been deployed

1. Autonomous vehicle control. Cars of the future will offload the burden of driving

between the vehicle and a plethora of road elements (sensors, people, other vehi-

factor is an excellent reliability of transmission.
2. Emergency communication. The network needs to flawlessly serve people in case

are availability and possibility of establishing calls even when the infrastructure
is partially destroyed. A concept of Device-to-Device (D2D), enabling endpoint-
backed handovers, could be helpful to achieve that goal and therefore is considered
in 5G architecture. Another goal to fulfil is minimisation of power consumption of

3. Factory cell automation. Until now, automated industrial manufacturing, where

4. High-speed train. 5G technology is expected to enable mobile broadband services
for highly-mobile users, i.e. people moving with the speed of up to 500 km/h. The
challenge is to preserve high throughput and low end-to-end delays at the same

5. Large outdoor event. Occasional mass-events cause huge numbers of people gath-

7. Media on demand.
As nowadays people expect high quality multimedia to be

8. Remote surgery and examination. In order to enable remote operations on humans,

2.5.3
Network architecture
The requirements formulated in 5G vision and use cases are more strict than ever before
and constitute a tremendous challenge on architecture design. The network should be
flexible and exhibit high degree of dynamic adaptation, in order to be capable of serving
the predefined scenarios. Two important concepts, enabling such elasticity of 5G, are
considered:
• Network Function Virtualisation (NFV). This approach, introduced in [23], utilises
a specific type of hardware, being a high-capacity server, that can run one or more
virtual machines responsible for performing network functions like switching, QoS
control etc. As a result, a network can consist of such devices and thus become
quickly and cheaply adjustable, as replacing VMs in the NFV-node corresponds to
physical equipment replacement in ”classical” infrastructure.
• Software-Defined Networking (SDN). This concept separates control and data
planes in such a way that switches and routers are substituted by ”forwarding
devices”, which have only forwarding tasks and they utilise special forwarding
tables, which are configured by a centralised unit, responsible for all traffic en-
gineering. Therefore, re-configuring the network becomes substantially simplified
and can occur much faster.
RAN design remains a subject of intensive research, such as the one presented in [25].
The authors underline that the new access network should support multiple radio tech-
nologies, both the new air interface for 5G and legacy RATs. This approach enables both
extremely high data rates and ubiquitous coverage. For proper utilisation of multiple
radio access solutions, multi-connectivity of UE should be considered, and in fact the
approach has already been proposed in release 12 of LTE. Another interesting problem
is the optimal choice of functional split of network functions among RAN and core net-
work [29], and inter-working scenarios between LTE-A and the novel air interface for
user plane[25].

even a step further in distributing mobility management responsibility almost totally

The previous chapter introduced all generations of mobile networks and took a general
look at how they addressed user mobility issues. One of the mobility aspects, multi-
connectivity, mentioned already in section 2.6.1, is of special importance, as its im-
plementation in 5G architecture would greatly contribute to the achievements of ultra
resiliency and high throughput everywhere. In this work, multi-connectivity has been
studied from the point of view of transport-layer, as suggested in [36]. This chapter
focuses on multi-path reliable transport protocols that might be included in 5G stack
that would suit the extreme requirements, presented in Figure 2.4.
3.1
Current dominant reliable transport technology: TCP
In fact, modern all-IP infrastructures utilise TCP protocol at the transport layer, wher-
ever reliability and in-order delivery of data is needed (excluding some applications,
where unreliable UDP is used, and the application layer is responsible for reliability).
However, while TCP communication has been designed with wired infrastructure and
single-addressed endpoints in mind, a new reality of wireless, more erroneous interfaces
and communication among multi-interface hosts reveals that this protocol in its original
version ([39]) can no longer be the most convenient transport-layer option. The next
sections, highlighting how the protocol handles data transmission, elaborate more on
this statement.
3.1.1
Data transmission in TCP
A TCP connection between two hosts may be established, provided that both of them has
got an IP address and a port number - these four together (IP address and port number of
source and destination) must be unique for each TCP session[39]. An application, willing
to send or receive data, can successfully establish one or more connections, depending
on the number of available interfaces and ports. By activating more sessions, better
transmission performance can be achieved but at the same time more resources are
reserved by the application. The data are transferred reliably, so each segment needs
to be acknowledged before the next one can be sent (particularly, sequence numbers
are used and exchanged to indicate the number of bytes acknowledged so far). It has
to be mentioned that one TCP session is able to carry multiple streams from many
==PAGE:28==
3.1 Current dominant reliable transport technology: TCP
15
applications, should the pool of available ports or addresses be constrained, for instance
by the network middle-boxes, such as NATs.
3.1.2
TCP limitations
The design of classical TCP turns out to be inefficient in certain cases. From the TCP
point of view, each of the created connections is treated separately, meaning that, for
example, the application may not retransmit a segment lost on a certain connection
using the different one. As far as multiplexing multiple applications’ data in one TCP
connection is concerned, it may happen that such bottleneck TCP session suffers from
head-of-line blocking, when retransmissions of data from one of the multiplexed streams
are effectively preventing the rest of the segments from being sent. Consequently, the end-
user experiences underutilised bandwidth and increased application end-to-end delay,
when using TCP for modern applications.
The aforementioned considerations are exemplified in figure 3.1. Host A (UE), equipped
with two different network interfaces (e.g. 4G and WiFi) wants to download a large
file from Host B (a server) via IP infrastructure. However, the application running on
A is designed to establish a single connection with the destination, so the host chooses
one of the available own-peer interface pairs: A:If0-B:If0 (Figure 3.1 a)).
Assuming
that If0 at A’s side is a WiFi interface the quality of wireless transmission may worsen
significantly, and errors in received segments, as well as possible signal fading may lead
A to utilise another interface If1 (e.g. 4G) to continue downloading (Figure 3.1 b) ). The
new connectivity setting between A and B becomes as follows: A:If1-B:If0. Albeit, the
nature of TCP enforces the old, corrupted connection to be aborted first and only then
the new one using 4G can be initiated (Figure 3.1 c) ). One may see that not only is
the throughput potential of the network is underutilised, as A has multiple connection
opportunities, from which only one at a time is used, but also there is a time when the
UE does not download the file at all, since there is no flow-based resiliency implemented
in TCP - each connection is independent of one another.

compares two solutions: MPTCP and SCTP.

3.2
MPTCP
Multipath TCP (MPTCP) is an extended version of legacy TCP, enabling multi-homing,
i.e. interconnecting entities being represented by multiple locations (IP addresses) [17].
Multipath operation means that at a time more than one flow of data between the multi-
homed endpoints is established, so that the information may either be split through
alternative routes, duplicated, or it can use only one of the paths, keeping the other(s)
as backup option(s). The concept of the protocol is depicted below.
Figure 3.2: MPTCP concept
The design assumes that at least one of the endpoints is represented by multiple addresses.
Once a binding between two hosts is created (MPTCP connection), it may consist of one
or more TCP connections (MPTCP subflows), established between one of the available
locations of the source and the destination. The number of subflows may change at
any time (a host can advertise a new address or remove one), as well as the function of
each of them can do (they might be set as either backup or transmission paths). The
order of data delivery is preserved at the MPTCP connection level, but also within
the realm of a TCP subflow. The MPTCP session can be initiated and terminated in
similar ways as TCP (three-way and four-way handshakes). For non-MPTCP-capable
network elements the communication appears as a regular TCP connection, which makes
it feasible to deploy the suggested protocol broadly, without the need of dealing with
backward-compatibility problems.
Figure 3.3: MPTCP protocol stack [17]
As stated before, MPTCP is not a new standalone transport technology, but it is based
on legacy TCP. The signalling is conducted by means of a set of new TCP options, being
sent at TCP connection initiation, or data acknowledging. Therefore, a MPTCP-capable
terminal is in fact an ordinary TCP-capable device, additionally understanding the new
==PAGE:31==
3.2 MPTCP
18
options. As a consequence, there is a possibility of seamless fallback to classic TCP,
should the endpoint, or a piece of middleware (NAT, Firewall, traffic balancer) appear
as non-MPTCP-capable or modify the content of the TCP options unexpectedly. As
far as the application layer is concerned, there is no difference whether the programme
operates on top of MPTCP or TCP.
3.2.1
Protocol operation
Initiating a connection
MPTCP connection initiation signalling is depicted in figure 3.4 One may observe that
in order to create a new session three-way handshake of TCP is used, with the only
difference being that each PDU contains a new option - MP_CAPABLE. The purpose of
this option is twofold:
1. It confirms that the sending unit understands MPTCP and would like to run a
session;
2. It sends an 64-bit authentication key, identifying the connection. The binding can
be then referred to by a 32-bit hash of the key, so called ”token”.
It has to be mentioned that exchanging MP_CAPABLE TCP option is relevant for estab-
lishing a new MPTCP connection and, at the same time, the first subflow (TCP session).
Next subflows can be added and associated to running MPTCP session in a different
way, described later on.
Figure 3.4: MPTCP connection establishment
Adding a new subflow
Once a new MPTCP session is activated, the protocol provides a simple way of associat-
ing new subflows with the MPTCP connection. Similarly as previously, three-way hand-
shake (with a following ACK) has to be conducted and the additional option, MP_JOIN,
is exchanged between the communicating entities. During the handshake the hosts do
not send their IP addresses, but address IDs instead, in order to bypass a possible NAT

traversal problem. For authentication purposes, the sender includes the receiver’s to-
ken, acquired during MPTCP initiation and a random number - nonce. The receiver
responds with a new nonce and HMAC of the sender’s authentication data. Last, but
not least, the sender sends his HMAC to be acknowledged by the receiver. Besides, flags
are exchanged among which one of the bits is currently in use and it indicates whether
the subflow should work as a backup path or not.
Figure 3.5: Adding a new subflow to MPTCP connection
Adding or removing host addresses
At any time the MPTCP session is active, either of the hosts may modify the pool of
available IP addresses associated to it. In order to set up a new subflow with a new
address, the host simply needs to initiate the MP_JOIN handshake, indicating the new
localisation, so that the second entity can implicitly deduce that the peer wants to have
a new association under that localisation. Similarly, by terminating such subflow the
host highlights that one of the addresses can no longer be used for communication.
Another, explicit way of notifying the peer about IP address pool changes is to use
ADD_ADDR and REMOVE_ADDR options, as shown in figure 3.6. This method is particularly
useful when NAT does not allow initiating a new subflow in a certain direction.

Reliability in MPTCP
MPTCP involves another option, called DATA_SEQUENCE_SIGNAL, which enables reliable
transmission and in-order data arrival. The message contains a mapping of a subflow
sequence number (SSN, being in fact a TCP sequence number) onto MPTCP connec-

(Data ACK). Finally, checksum is included.
What is important, DSS option carries
also Data-level Length field, which specifies the scope of the whole sequence mapping
- this means that if the mapping is valid for N packets, then the MPTCP-level Data
ACK is exchanged in the N-th segment. As it may be impossible to include the whole
DATA_SEQUENCE_SIGNAL in one TCP segment, the option can be split and flag setting
informs which contents are present in a particular MPTCP message.

Terminating a connection
The rules of terminating a subflow in MPTCP are the same as in TCP - both endpoints
exchange FIN to be ACKed, to ensure that all the data to be transmitted, have been
received correctly. Once there is nothing more to be sent within the whole MPTCP ses-
sion, a DSS option, having DATA_FIN bit set high, is originated from one of the hosts.
A proper connection closing occurs when both devices send their own DSS/DATA_FIN
and acknowledge the message from each other by DSS/DATA_ACK. Otherwise, a time-
out value of acknowledging DATA_FIN is set, after which the MPTCP session is closed
anyway. When MPTCP connection is being closed, all the subflows are terminated in a
TCP fashion[17].
3.2.2
MPTCP vs TCP
One has to notice that, since embedded in TCP, MPTCP design principles are very
similar, regarding both the session initiation/termination and reliable transmission. As
in TCP, the concept of congestion windows remains valid in MPTCP, but since each
subflow is a de facto TCP session with its own window parameters, there can be a
problem in bottlenecks of the network, namely a multi-subflow MPTCP connection
might potentially exhaust more resources than the equivalent TCP session. Since this is
considered unfair, and thus not allowed, coupled congestion control is recommended [9].
If at any time during protocol operation there is a problem with missing MPTCP option
or corrupted MPTCP content, the host is obliged to terminate with TCP RST message
all the subflows, excluding the one that has been established first.
Thus, once the
MPTCP appears unsuccessful, the communication continues as a regular single session
of TCP. The main design goal behind this technique is that an application should not
be suffering from potential MPTCP-to-TCP transitions.
3.2.3
MPTCP for wireless networks
Once revealed to the public, Multipath TCP has quickly attracted attention of telecom-
munication research groups and companies. The protocol has been recommended as
the best transport-layer solution candidate for LTE Dual Connectivity [3].
Protocol
behaviour has been studied and evaluated thoroughly, thus proving MPTCP capability
of serving as an efficient and robust TCP successor for wireless-wireless and wireless-
wired applications [13] [36]. The experiments on real-life equipment, done in [30], has
shown that including Multipath TCP in mobile network protocol stack would facilitate
multi-RAT convergence (3G + Wifi) for various IP services, thanks to 3 different types
of protocol handovers, proposed by the authors.
The biggest advantage of MPTCP
communication over multiple wireless interfaces is, that from the application-level per-
spective there is no perceived goodput outage at WiFi loss, provided that at least one of
the established subflows has been set up for 3G transmission (cf. figure 1 in [30]). More-
over, intelligent subflow management may help in achieving better energy efficiency (by
==PAGE:35==
3.3 SCTP
22
offloading energy-consuming 3G(LTE) interface by WiFi) and more convenient billing
strategy for the user (again, WiFi preferred over costly 3G(LTE)). However, as connec-
tion establishment and termination requires more data to be sent, MPTCP may actually
perform worse than legacy TCP in certain situations, notably when files to be transferred
are very small or when sessions between the hosts last very shortly [13]. Another problem
of Multipath TCP operation is discrepancy in capacities among the paths. Considering
a case when a chunk of data is sent over two paths simultaneously, where the first one is
much faster than the second one, the order of packets can no longer be preserved, as a
new part of information from the quicker path would arrive sooner than the data from
the slower path, that need be acknowledged earlier. As a solution, a packet reordering
mechanism, aware of subflow bandwidth diversity, may be applied [2].
3.3
SCTP
Stream Control Transmission Protocol is a TCP alternative that has been created orig-
inally for more convenient transport of PSTN signalling over connectionless networks.
[42] formulates a list of primary services that the design provides:
1. reliable data transmission between hosts with multiple addresses,
2. higher-layer payload fragmentation, so that it never exceeds discovered MTU,
3. adjustable data ordering (it can be strictly in-order, unordered or partially or-
dered),
4. sending multiple packets bundled into one SCTP PDU,
5. network-level fault tolerance through supporting of multi-homing at either or both
ends of an association.
Data transport service in SCTP, similarly as in the case of TCP and MPTCP, is
connection-oriented - so called association between two hosts can be created and re-
mains valid for all the addresses that either of the endpoints advertises during the session
initiation.
3.3.1
SCTP signalling
Association establishment
In order to start multi-homed SCTP association between two endpoints, four-way hand-
shake should take place. The messages that need to be exchanged are presented in figure

Client A wants to start an association with server B. INIT message should therefore start
the establishment process, and it contains a verification tag of A and an initial sequence
number, chosen by A. By sending such data chunk A enters COOKIE-WAIT state. B
is obliged to answer at once with INIT-ACK and the PDU should include the following
fields: identification of both the endpoints (verification tags Ta and Tb), initial sequence
number K and cookie C. It is crucial to remember that at this point the connection is
still closed for the server, thus no resources have been allocated yet, but both hosts have
already advertised the number of inbound and outbound streams needed for the con-
nection. When A retrieves the initial acknowledgement from B, it echoes the cookie by
means of COOKIE-ECHO PDU, which may be coupled with some initial user data. Then A
goes to COOKIE-ECHOED state. Finally, B needs to respond with COOKIE-ACK, adding
data-acknowledgement for the potential data sent before, and putting his own data to
be transferred. At this point (and when A receives the cookie acknowledgement), both
endpoints are in ESTABLISHED state and the association is fully active. However, if
at any stage of the handshake, due to various reasons, a new connection cannot be set
up, the host should cancel the whole process by means of ABORT chunk, containing the
cause of failure.
One may observe that the cookie mechanism provides system robustness against resource
attacks, possible in TCP (SYN-floods). Moreover, even though the session needs 4-way
exchange to be fully established, the first portions of data might be successfully sent
already on the halfway (after 2 messages sent).
==PAGE:37==
3.3 SCTP
24
Data transmission
SCTP provides reliable transmission of data, but the atomic entity is a message (con-
trary to a byte in TCP). The protocol establishes inter-host associations that consist
of one or more streams that are ”sequences of user messages that are to be delivered
to upper-layer protocol in order” [42]. Consequently, each SCTP chunk is assigned a
sequence number of a relevant stream (SSN). The streams are not blocking each other,
which means that while one stream awaits the next ordered message, the data from the
other streams can be transmitted. Besides, the protocol introduces another type of se-
quence number (TSN), set independently, so that acknowledging TSNs corresponds to
reliable delivery, whilst SSNs are serving for ordered data delivery.
Multiple upper-layer payload portions can be bundled within the same SCTP chunk, as
well as multiple SCTP PDUs. The bundling function is controlled by the application.
Moreover, there is a possibility of fragmentation of the payload, should the given chunk
of data exceed the MTU. SCTP employs congestion control methods similar to those of
classical TCP.
Association termination
Once either of the communicating endpoints wants or needs to exit the session, asso-
ciation termination takes place. Unlike in TCP, half-open connections, i.e. the ones
when one of the hosts still sends data, while the other one is already closed, are not
possible in SCTP. Thus, at termination process no new packets are transmitted. One
may distinguish two types of SCTP association tear-down:
1. Abort.
An endpoint originates ABORT PDU, and removes the connection data
immediately.
The same action is expected from the receiver of ABORT, so that
no other PDUs are exchanged.
This type of termination might happen under
erroneous circumstances of the handshake, or due to a failure in the host. It is not
desirable to finish an ordinary SCTP session in such a way, as any unacknowledged
data would not be delivered upon ABORT.
2. Shutdown.
A graceful version of association tear-down in which all the unac-
knowledged data can be reliably delivered to the destination. The mechanism of
shutdown is illustrated in figure 3.9.
Once the upper layer triggers connection
termination, host A waits for all pending data sent to the host B to be acknowl-
edged (SHUTDOWN-PENDING state). Then, SCTP SHUTDOWN message is origi-
nated by A, which enters SHUTDOWN-SENT state. Upon receipt of SHUTDOWN,
B goes to SHUTDOWN-RECEIVED state, and afterwards it ensures that all the
data has been ACKed; then it originates SHUTDOWN-ACK message towards A and
finally reaches SHUTDOWN-ACK-SENT state.
The last stage of SCTP shut-
down happens when host A gets SHUTDOWN-ACK and immediately responds with
SHUTDOWN-COMPLETE, removing all the information about SCTP association be-

Path management
The design of SCTP, described in RFC4960 utilises multiple locations of endpoints in
such a way that all the paths established between them are disjoint. Moreover, only
one of the paths is used for regular data transmission (primary path), while the other(s)
serve for backup purposes. Concurrent Multipath Transfer SCTP, proposed by Iyengar
et al [33], and MPSCTP [41] are SCTP variants that enable sending data over multiple
paths simultaneously.
3.3.2
SCTP in wireless networks
Authors of [24] proved that SCTP, precisely mSCTP (a protocol with ADDIP exten-
sion, allowing the endpoint for adding or removing its available IP addresses via SCTP
PDUs) may be successfully used for soft handovers in wireless networks.
As a mat-
ter of fact, the possibility of informing the peer about changes in the available address
pool resembles MPTCP explicit address advertisement mechanism. In erroneous con-
ditions of wireless communication, multi-streamed SCTP provides better goodput than
single-streamed TCP[4]. Unlike (MP)TCP, SCTP is message-oriented and the order of
messages can be preserved or not, depending on the configuration. Despite that SCTP
provides secure multi-streaming and multi-homing and seems to be a good candidate
for modern mobile communication transport-layer technology it has not been deployed

been a dominating, well-known, well-standardised technology, whilst SCTP even today
lacks appropriate promotion and recognition in public, number of implementations and

strong and conservative ”TCP mindset” of network and software vendors does not help

The previous chapter introduced two multipath transport protocols: MPTCP and SCTP.
Both of them have been evaluated and the former one has been selected for further
examination. The motivation behind MPTCP investigation is based on the fact that the
protocol concept is relatively new, yet quite extensive research on it has already been
conducted. Therefore, the prospective results of the study may be compared with the
related work, and, possibly, extend the scope of previously undertaken research. One
may choose from 2 alternative methods of protocol examination:
• Experiments on real equipment with MPTCP implemented - this approach pro-
vides results affected by real-life network phenomena; on the other hand some
experimental setups may be expensive and difficult to realise.
• Computer model simulation - collecting data from customised simulation scenar-
ios of protocol design allows for cheap and detailed analysis of the system under
various circumstances; however, the limitations of the design and the modelling
environment have to be taken into account.
Having this in mind, computer modelling has been chosen as the most convenient method
of protocol evaluation, as it is assumed that a clever model design and a wise choice of
modelling software will make it possible to perform a detailed MPTCP analysis with
much lower cost and in shorter time, by avoiding delays related to, for example, the
shipment of the devices.
4.1
Modelling process
Modelling a network protocol is an iterative and multi-stage process, the workflow of
which is depicted in figure 4.1. First, the specification of the protocol (real system) is
studied and a simplified conceptual design, tailored to a set of specific goals, is created
(conceptual model). The design should be the simplest possible, yet it must have all the
features that are necessary to observe the desired operation aspects. The concept is then
implemented and becomes included in a protocol stack of network devices, modelled
in the simulation software. Thus network model (computer model), containing hosts,
switches/routers and links is given simulation parameters (forming a simulation scenario)

and then simulations of such system take place. The meaning of the collected data can
be twofold. At the early stage of modelling they show whether the implemented design
truly behaves according to the specification - if it is the case, then the model is said to
be verified. Later on, the model needs to be validated, which happens when the results
of the simulations concerning the modelling goals are reliable and prove that the design
is able to show the expected behaviour.

One may observe that both the goals presented above are very broad - the first one
corresponds to an extensive verification of the protocol design, and the second one allows
for conducting case studies, such as the ones presented in [30]. In this section the goals
will be analysed and the corresponding model design will be suggested. The modelling
process has been divided into a set of milestones, referring to goals’ aspects. Each goal
aspect defines the features of MPTCP to be implemented and simulations to be run
for the defined functionality. By achieving the milestones (aspects), one may gradually

Goal 1
Goal 1 covers the overall operation of MPTCP, which can be further subdivided into
several more detailed aspects:
a ) Connection establishment. Investigating the initial stage of MPTCP communi-
cation makes it possible to check whether the MPTCP host correctly signals its
capabilities and initiates the subflows according to the predefined strategy. This
aspect allows for simulating connection establishment time, an important criterion
for comparison between MPTCP and legacy TCP. In order to verify connection es-
tablishment the model must support the following MPTCP messages: MP_CAPABLE,
MP_JOIN and DATA_SEQUENCE_SIGNAL. Adding new subflows may be done assuming
that only one side establishes new subflows connecting to the same location of the
other side, thus the addresses do not need be advertised. However, more advanced
version of the model should support also explicit address advertisement messages:
ADD_ADDR and REMOVE_ADDR. Authentication shall not be taken into account. It is
required that the model continues connection initiation as regular TCP communi-
cation, once MPTCP messages are malformed or lost. In that case, also the model
state shall be properly transited from MPTCP-related state to TCP-related one,
so that no MPTCP variables or data structures remain allocated unnecessarily.
b ) Reliable and ordered data transfer. Once the subflows are established, the streams
of user data encompassed within one MPTCP connection are to be mapped onto
Data Sequence Numbers and, based on that, reconstructed at the receiving side
so that the initial order is preserved.
At this milestone the MPTCP sequence
numbers mapping and in-order delivery may be evaluated, while capacities of one
(or more) of the links vary. This goal aspect enables simulating the throughput gain
with respect to a single TCP flow (which corresponds to the second verification
scenario from section 2.6.2). The design verified against aspect a) may be used,
with the sequence mapping and ordering mechanisms added ”on top”. Thus, no
more MPTCP messages are needed. However, the mechanism of TCP fallback
should be extended to ”established” state, when upon an error the connection
should first close all the subflows except the first one, which in turn should continue
transmitting as if it was a regular TCP flow. Checksum modelling is not considered.
c ) Connection termination from ”established” state. At the MPTCP connection
level the only messaging needed is an exchange of a regular DATA_SEQUENCE_SIGNAL
with DATA_FIN flag raised. Afterwards, all the subflows have to be finalised by
a regular TCP handshake and only then the MPTCP connection may be prop-
erly terminated. This behaviour needs to be verified here. Besides, implementing
this aspect provides a possibility of comparing efficiency of TCP versus MPTCP

4.3.1
Choosing the modeller
Contemporary networks are extremely complex and therefore in order for modelling a
network protocol to be feasible, a piece of software that facilitates the process and serves
with ready-to-use models of network elements and protocol stacks is required. Such an
environment provides an API for network-specific actions (for example, packet process-
ing) and makes it possible to run simulations collecting customised results. Two most
popular modellers used in academia are ns-3 and OPNET. Both of them employ event-
driven simulations, meaning that network behaviour is represented by a set of discrete
events, upon which the operations such as handling packets and/or collecting results
occur.
ns-3[45] is an open-source environment, written in C++, while OPNET is a
commercial software, currently maintained by Riverbed Steelcentral[28], which supports
C++, but its Kernel and standard models are based on C. Both projects provide a wide
variety of built-in models of wired and wireless protocols and technologies, as well as
the possibility of real payload generation, thanks to which one may emulate a part of
the network and connect it with actual pieces of hardware. However, due to simulation
kernel differences and dissimilarities in network phenomena implementations, projects
written in OPNET are not compatible with ns-3 modules, and the process of adopting
a model from one of the modellers to another is complex and time-consuming[46].
OPNET has been chosen as the modelling environment for MPTCP design, as it was be-
lieved that the experience with this modeller, gained beforehand, as well as the presence
of GUI facilities and interactive debugger would accelerate model development process.

4.3.2
Hierarchical modelling
In order to investigate some network aspects in OPNET one needs to define a scenario,
where network topology is designed. Each network element (e.g. a host, a switch/router,
a link) is represented as a separate model and, in the case of nodes, comprises of one
or more process models, corresponding to processors, queues or transmitters/receivers.
It has to be mentioned that a separate process model needs to be created for each
protocol in the stack the node implements. Furthermore, the process may create child
process instances at simulation run-time - this ability is particularly useful when mod-
elling connection-oriented transport technologies, such as TCP, where the main module
spawns and controls multiple connection instances acting as TCP sessions. The actual
network events are performed by processes, designed as state machines, which, upon an
interrupt (timer timeout, packet arrival or explicit invocation from another state ma-
chine), perform pre-programmed executives and transit to a new state, and then does
not change the state until a new interrupt happens. The results of the simulations are
the values of statistics that the processes define and collect at event processing, for exam-
ple the number of received/sent packets or connection handshake time. The approach
of creating models in OPNET is called hierarchical modelling, as the entities form a hi-
erarchy (network→node→process→child process) that determines the way the processes
may be addressed, created and controlled, so that a parent process has full control over
its children, but not vice-versa. OPNET hierarchy is illustrated in the figure below.

4.4.1
Architecture
On the way towards protocol conceptual model development, a fundamental task is to
suggest an architecture that may enable the whole desired functionality to be operable in
an efficient and, what is important, scalable way. A network device that utilises MPTCP-
==PAGE:47==
4.4 Model design process
34
based transport layer runs many applications represented by wide variety of transport
service expectations. Thus, MPTCP module must be able to seamlessly interface with
the application layer and handle the demands of every application separately. At the
same time, serving for one application in MPTCP way means that a dedicated MPTCP
session manages multiple TCP pipes in order to provide throughput and/or resiliency
benefits. As the number of active applications, as well the number of TCP subflows
within a MPTCP connection are not fixed in time, the most convenient architectural
decision is to decouple MPTCP and TCP realms from the main protocol module, lead-
ing to a hierarchical concept, depicted in figure 4.3. Each MPTCP-aware network node
runs a single instance of Manager sub-layer, responsible for communication with higher
layers and management of MPTCP connections, represented by Metasocket sub-layer.
A manager can instantiate multiple metasockets (each instance serves a particular appli-
cation), realising reliability and in-order transmission within a MPTCP session. Every
metasocket controls and distributes user data to multiple subflows that wrap the pay-
load into TCP segments with MPTCP options and send them towards remote endpoints.
MPTCP Subflows are at the same time fully-functional TCP connections, and in case of
any errors with MPTCP signalling, the metasocket terminates all its subflows but the
first one, which continues data transfer on legacy TCP basis. Since the idea of MPTCP
concept assumes that the majority of applications will remain non-MPTCP-aware for
a long time, the first subflow is in fact the only TCP session visible to higher layers,
interfacing by the same technique as in legacy TCP transport.

Connection establishment: Goal 1a
This section describes how the system associates two hosts by a MPTCP connection. The
model scope in this field has been narrowed down to two subflows per connection (M=2

subflow; the primary socket is active in the client node, and listens passively in the server

each of the segments includes MP_CAPABLE option. Once the first subflow of B becomes

metasocket of B instantiates the second, passive subflow, awaiting a new handshake from

the standard requires so), and only then both subflows are considered established.

the initiating party starts a new handshake with MP_JOIN once MP_CAPABLE exchange is

the secondary address of the responding node is known to the initiator in advance. An
approach in which the client connects with two different interfaces to one and the same
server interface has also been rejected, since the existence of non-disjoint paths degrades
connection resiliency. In another technique the second TCP pipe establishment occurs
by the initiative of the responder (which is permitted by the standard). However, due
to the presence of NATs in the global network and potential problems with starting con-
nections in reverse direction, such an idea does not seem optimal. The suggested variant
resembles a situation, where a multi-homed portable device connects to a remote applica-

Reliable and ordered data transfer: Goal 1b
The fact that an application recognises only the primary subflow of a MPTCP connection
implies that no guidelines regarding path management does the higher layer formulate
and pass to the transport module. A strategy of disposing TCP API commands from
the application and to handle incoming and outgoing payload must be implemented
in MPTCP metasocket, as it has to be also tailored to the number of subflows that a
particular MPTCP session has succeeded to establish.
At the sending side the metasocket should be able to perform segmentation on the
incoming application data, in case a single chunk appears to be too large for subflow
sending buffer of one subflow or the internal buffers of the scheduler (or rather to prepare
more optimal share of user data for scheduler queues). The latter module controls the
way the payload is passed to the subflows. The following approaches of user data de-
multiplexing have been considered:
1. Round-robin scheduler - all the subflows are involved in data transmission and are
given fair share of application payload in circular order;
2. 1:1 resiliency scheme - the primary subflow transfers all the data, while the sec-
ondary one is suspended in established state and can be used for communication
as soon as problems with packets delivery on the first subflow are encountered.
As far as the receiving side is concerned, an important difference in MPTCP transmission
with respect to TCP communication is, that not only does the data in-order presence
need to be verified at the TCP session level, but also at the MPTCP realm, for the reason
that multiple TCP connections (subflows) are logically bounded within one MPTCP
connection. As a natural consequence of this, a packet captured and evaluated by the
relevant subflow cannot be forwarded to the application layer yet, as it was the case in
legacy TCP, but instead the packet is passed to the metasocket, which ought to ensure
that:
• Subflow sequence numbers are mapped properly to data sequence numbers, and
the packet has arrived in order with respect to the remaining subflows - in the case
of data split the segment may have come too early, so it needs to be stored in the
receive buffer until the missing data are captured;
• User payload is sent to the application in the same way as it was retrieved at the
sending side, i.e. if any segmentation has happened at the source node, the receiver
must reassemble the data based on sequence numbers mapping;
• Application segments are forwarded to the destination layer as if they were directed
from the first subflow, as higher layers do not know about other subflows;

• If the mapping is wrong, the subflow that has experienced a malfunction shall be
terminated.

OPNET implementation
The MPTCP design uses the standard TCP OPNET model as a base, on top of which
MPTCP-related features have been added in such a way, that the functionality of the
legacy TCP model is preserved. The design, written in C language with OPNET-specific
libraries, encompasses a packet format and 3 process models, and in the simulation sce-
narios it is included in OPNET models of multi-homed client and server.

4.5.1
Packet format
Data transport service in the MPTCP model can be realised by means of mptcp_seg_v2
packet format, being an extended version of a standard TCP format. The structure
of the segment is depicted in figure 4.7. Some of the fields in the packet have been
taken from TCP OPNET model: fields, MSS Option, SACK-Permitted Option, Window
Scaling Option, SACK Option, Timestamp Option and user payload. They have been
left untouched, as their presence is indispensable for proper TCP operation. MPTCP-
related options that have been added to the format and are described below.
Figure 4.7: MPTCP segment format. For the sake of readability the proportions of field
sizes are not preserved
• MP_CAPABLE option is simplified to only one 1-bit assignable flag, showing that the
checksum is required (always set to 0). Since other fields are related to authenti-
cation process (sender’s and receiver’s keys, cryptography algorithm negotiation),
which is not modelled at all, these fields are dropped and their sizes are set as
bulk data, in order to preserve realistic overhead volume. On the other hand, even
though the value of the only assignable bit is not used in the implementation, the
option has been put is such a way, because it is easier to verify if it has been cor-
rectly exchanged between the endpoints (standard TCP debugging printouts list
all the options assigned to a packet).
• MP_DSS (Data Sequence Signal) option consists of 5 assignable fields: flags, Data
ACK, Data Sequence Number, Subflow Sequence Number and Data-Level Length.
However, all other fields, including checksum, are dropped, and Data ACK and
==PAGE:53==
4.5 OPNET implementation
40
Data Sequence Number have always the shorter length of 4 octets. Thus, 42 bits
of bulk data needs to be set to compensate for the design simplifications.
• ADD_ADDR option contains 3 assignable fields: Address ID, Address (IPv4 only) and
Port. The dropped fields are represented by 24-bit bulk data.
• MP_JOIN option includes 2 assignable fields: 1-bit backup flag (B) and Address ID.
The rest of the fields are bulked, however, the size of bulk changes as the RFC
defines MP_JOIN contents differently, depending on the stage of the handshake,
wherein MP_JOIN is included. As a result, the bulk size for MP_JOIN in SYN is
86-bit, for MP_JOIN in SYN/ACK is 118-bit and for MP_JOIN in the third ACK
is 183-bit.
The fields that are omitted originally correspond to authentication
process, not considered in the model.

4.5.3
Process level decomposition
MPTCP module is represented in OPNET processor and can be decomposed to 3 process
models, corresponding to 3 sub-layers in the conceptual design:
1. mptcp_manager_v3.
This process is responsible for handling TPAL commands
and, consequently, for creating and maintaining MPTCP connections. It generates
transmission statistics at transport-layer level, i.e. accumulating the information of
all connections. There is only one instance of the manager in a MPTCP-aware net-
work device. Figure 4.9 shows the State Transition Diagram of the manager process
(OPNET-specific diagram is presented in figure 7.1 in Appendix A). The design
of the state machine is based on legacy TCP manager process, tcp_manager_v3,
available in OPNET. Interfacing with TPAL has been left unmodified, but the way
the manager identifies and communicates with lower sub-layers has been adapted
to MPTCP architecture. Namely, while in TCP version the manager had a list
of TCBs of TCP connections and references to connection processes, the MPTCP
variant keeps in the list the TCBs of the subflows and references to the metasock-
ets instead. Thanks to such an approach the manager no longer invokes subflows
directly, but the calls go through the metasocket, performing its executives before
invoking the destination entity.
After state variables initialisation in INIT state the machine enters ACTIVE state,
where it listens for commands from the application side, notifications from active
connections, or segment arrivals.
Every time one of these occurs, the machine
performs relevant operations and then comes back to ACTIVE state. A status
indication from one of the connections is passed towards the application, while the
command originated from an application may result in one of the following actions:
• OPEN command for opening a new transport session results in instantiating
a new mptcp_metasocket;
• SEND command for sending a given portion of payload is dispatched to the
appropriate metasocket;
• RECEIVE command for receiving a certain number of packets is forwarder
to the relevant metasocket;
• CLOSE command is dispatched to the metasocket, which triggers 4-way clos-
ing handshake on all subflows within a connection;
• ABORT command, passed to the metasocket, leads to TCP RST message
exchange on all subflows within a connection;
When a new segment arrives (SEG_RCV), the manager looks for the relevant
metasocket instance based on the information from packet header. If a connection
has been found, the manager invokes it, passing the received packet. Otherwise,
TCP RST is sent back, and the packet is silently destroyed. In a special case of
second subflow establishment at the client side the manager of the client reads the
==PAGE:55==
4.5 OPNET implementation
42
port number from ADD_ADDR option included in the captured packet, and then the
relevant metasocket is interrupted to start a new active subflow on that port.
Figure 4.9: MPTCP manager state transition diagram
2. mptcp_metasocket. This process plays a role of MPTCP connection - it is fully
responsible for path management and it controls the lifetimes of the subflows.
The information about subflow states and their processes’ references are stored in
static variables, and IP addresses of the remote node’s interfaces are hardcoded.
Metasockets calculate a scalar statistic of connection establishment time as the
amount of time needed to exchange the messages depicted in figure 4.4.
The module implements a round robin scheduler, however it does neither segmenta-
tion nor reassembly of the application payload. Moreover, a receive buffer is not in-
cluded. mptcp_metasocket process analyses and creates or discards TCP API com-
mands and indications (if necessary), but it does not manipulate MPTCP segments,
forwarding them to the subflows instead. The state machine of mptcp_metasocket
(see figure 7.2) is very simple - once the process is created by the manager, the
input TCP parameters are taken and immediately passed to a newly instantiated
mptcp_subflow_v3 instance. The reference to the first subflow is stored and the
machine automatically enters active state. There are three events that the process
is responsive to:
• An interrupt from the primary subflow that has just sent a segment with
ADD_ADDR option - in such a case, corresponding to the behaviour of device B
from figure 4.4, a new passive TCP session (mptcp_subflow_v3) is created,
and it listens on the port which has been retrieved from the interrupt code.
• An interrupt from the subflow with a new TCP API indication - OPEN or
CLOSED indications are forwarded to the manager only when both subflows
has notified so; other kinds of notifications from the secondary subflow are not
passed further, and they results in an update of metasockets’ state variables.
• An interrupt from the manager side, which has received a segment with
ADD_ADDR option (device A in figure 4.4) - as in previous case the port is
taken from the interrupt code, but now an active TCP connection is instan-
tiated, so that the handshake with MP_JOIN may start.
• An invocation from the manager - the accompanying user data are passed
to one of the active subflows, that is immediately invoked by the metasocket
process.

4.6
Model limitations
The implementation explained above is derived from the conceptual model, yet not all
the designed system features has been realised. First of all, the IP addresses of the
endpoints are not actually being learned by studying ADD_ADDR option contents and
instead they are hardcoded and only port numbers are being effectively read from the
MPTCP address advertisement message. Consequently, the concept of associating host
addresses with Address IDs has been dropped, as in the circumstances of fixed values
this idea becomes useless. This technique has been chosen, as data containers (lists)
provided by OPNET library did not work reliably and the stored values of addresses were
unexpectedly overwritten at state transition. For the same reason the variables related
to the managed subflows in the metasocket process are allocated statically for 2 subflows
only, instead of being stored in a dynamically extendable list. Lack of internal buffers
==PAGE:59==
4.7 Verification of the model
46
and segmentation/reassembly modules in mptcp_metasocket imposes some additional
constraints onto data transmission. At the sending side the scheduler distributes whole
application data chunks to the subflows, which segment them internally into TCP MTU-
sized portions, while at the receiving node there is no MPTCP-specific mechanism that
ensures in-order delivery, if the packets arrive out of order. The implication of the former
constraint is, the size of user payload to be transferred during the simulation ought to
be an integer number of application segment size, in order for RR scheduling to work
as expected. The latter simplification enforces one link speed configuration: both paths
must have the same capacity, so that one may ensure the segments to arrive in the
scheduled order. On the other hand, this allows for a new model simplification: if there
is no receive-related policy, then it is enough to count and utilise only data sequence
numbers in MP_DSS, whilst DATA_ACK processing has no significance.

TCP fallback
Four simulation runs have been conducted to check whether the MPTCP model transits

that lead to the fallback 4 ODB traces: fb1, fb2, fb3 and fb4 have been defined. The role

Results

its operation, i.e. completes the handshake, or processes the packet and forms a response.

has been encountered in the second subflow, thus it had to be immediately aborted with
TCP RST message.
4.8
Summary
This chapter described in details the process of MPTCP computer modelling, where
the system analysis led to the statement of modelling goals, being addressed by the
conceptual model.
The concept was in turn implemented in a special software and
verified by a series of simulations. The verification tests proved that the implemented
MPTCP model can establish a multi-path session through which a generated application
data can be transferred reliably and in-order. However, due to the presence additional
model constraints resulting from OPNET-specific programming issues the protocol can
be simulated only under additional assumptions regarding the paths and application
data, and thus for a limited group of scenarios.

where a portion is forwarded directly to the UE, whilst the rest goes to SeNB, and is
sent to the destination from there. In this approach, both eNBs the UE is connected to
have separate buffering, carrier aggregation scheme and physical layer processing[44].
In Dual Connectivity MPTCP connections may be established with the purpose of trans-
port service of packets exchanged between the S-GW and MeNB, as well as between the
UE and both the associated eNBs. This is shown in figure 5.5, presenting UP 3C archi-
tecture variant, where user payload is split at the PDCP level at the Master eNB1.
Payload splitting across multiple disjoint links between UE and eNBs is handled by the
multi-path nature of protocol connections and throughput improvements, presented in
section 5.3 suggest that applying MPTCP would further magnify the benefit of utilising
DC in 5G networks, since these two technologies operate on two independent layers and
thus they are not mutually exclusive.
1Strictly speaking, in the presented example with 2 radio bearers the actual MPTCP design would
have to be extended to 3 subflows per connection, in order to handle data transfer between UE and both
the eNBs.

5.6
Summary
The OPNET implementation of the MPTCP design has been simulated and compared
with TCP and other MPTCP models from literature. The results indicates that the
multi-path successor of TCP serves with higher throughput for FTP service, and may
potentially enhance throughput benefits of Dual Connectivity. However, the limitations
of the design have made it impossible to address all the modelling goals, such as the
resiliency study.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusions
The fifth generation of mobile networks is a big and ambitious project that has been
initiated with the idea of meeting the expectations of customers of the future. Multi-
tude of new services, variety of network requirements and tremendous number of users,
represented both directly as humans and machines or robots serving them, put in front
of final 5G product the task of being highly innovative and robust, at the same time
preserving flexibility and effectiveness of resource usage. A problem of particular signif-
icance is user mobility. People consider high-speed (up to 1 Gbps) Internet as granted,
even when they travel with speeds reaching 500 km/h. On the other hand, the vision
of 5G assumes that 100 Mbps capacity shall be delivered everywhere, and this applies
also for cell coverage edges, for example in rural areas.
From the viewpoint of the
UE, the presence of multiple interfaces opens a possibility of distributing data commu-
nication over different paths that may increase the capacity and, possibly, connection
robustness. As far as the access network is concerned, an idea of Dual Connectivity
comes with a solution of data split between a macro and a small cell serving one UE in
HetNets. The concept seems promising, however the usage of ubiquitous TCP by the
applications remains an issue. The legacy reliable transport protocol is now relatively
optimised for single-homed operation, though it does not provide any mechanisms to
logically associate and manage several TCP sessions, established under the umbrella of
one application. As leaving the old protocol as-is and modifying applications to become
multi-path aware is neither cheap nor reasonable in terms of time, the action of replacing
TCP by a new multi-homed transport protocol has to be undertaken. SCTP is a quite
mature alternative, supporting IPv6 and multi-path connections. The biggest drawback
of such technology is its poor adoption and high complexity, thus making TCP-to-SCTP
transition harmful in terms of money and time. On the other hand, a new multi-path
extension of TCP, MPTCP, offers seamless backward-compatibility and at the same
time implements a similar set of features as SCTP. Thus, MPTCP appears as a serious
candidate for 5G and deserves a more detailed look and performance evaluation study.
A model of MPTCP has been designed with the thought of observing its operation in two
use cases: a simultaneous file transfer over two interfaces, and a connection resiliency
challenge when one of the paths in use goes down. The scope of the protocol has been
limited to double-addressed hosts, corresponding to typical LTE + WiFi smartphones.
Another concept simplification was leaving the congestion control to each of the TCP
subflows independently. The protocol architecture proposed consists of 3 logical sub-
layers and has been implemented in OPNET. The model has been then verified and
simulated in FTP file transfer scenario. The results, juxtaposed with TCP model, show
==PAGE:69==
6.1 Future Work
56
that even 2 sub-flows established by MPTCP provide a considerable throughput improve-
ment, increasing together with growing amount of data to be sent. MPTCP performs at
least as good as TCP for files even as small as 32 kilobytes. The comparison with other
implementations on real hardware revealed, that the results produced by the model are
fairly reliable, yet some work has to be done so that the missing features are added
and the design may be also evaluated in a broader set of experiments, for instance, for
various path capacity ratios.
6.1
Future Work
The next step of the modelling process will be to fix OPNET-related problems and to
supplement the design with the necessary features that are required to fully evaluate
MPTCP both in terms of throughput and resiliency, namely: send/receive buffering
at the MPTCP level, dynamic address discovery and address-to-ID mappings, and the
mode of operating with one subflow as a communication path, and the other one as a
backup path.
As a lot of the experiments on MPTCP, done both for real-hardware deployment and
as computer simulations, have shown that the throughput advantage of the multi-path
protocol becomes severely degraded, when the diversity of paths’ capacities increase.
Therefore, proposing a metasocket scheduling algorithm that distributes the data based
on the latest information about performance of the links, would help mitigating the
aforementioned issue by approaching it from the sender perspective.
