Abstract
Hard water has in many years been an inconvenience for households due to ex-
tra expenses and time used to remove scale from tiles and household appliances
resulting in an increased use of cleaning agents.
In addition, there are sev-
eral assertions that scaling results in an increased energy consumption of water
heating appliances, and that scale can reduce the lifetime of these appliances.
As Brøndby becomes the ﬁrst municipality in Denmark with central softened
drinking water it is highly relevant to examine the formation of scale in a kettle
and the related eﬀects for a household when the drinking water is softened. Ef-
fects of softening was investigated through laboratory work on nine new kettles.
The soft water with hardness 10 and 5 ◦dH was prepared in the laboratory by
the dilution with demineralised water, which was found to be an acceptable
method for the purpose. By softening the water from approx. 20 to 10 ◦dH,
scale formation was reduced by 76 % in the kettles through 110 times of boiling.
The visual eﬀect of softening was also signiﬁcant. Continuous weighing of the
kettles was discovered to be a good indicator for the extent of scale formation.
Based on relevant water quality data, PHREEQC calculations revealed that the
precipitated constituents, determined by AAS from acid-extraction solution of
the kettles, were most likely calcite, dolomite and hematite. Investigation of
the inherent precipitation process showed that precipitation decreased by 88 %
when reducing the hardness with 50 %. The Calcium Carbonate Precipitation
Potential (CCPP) was determined for varying drinking water compositions, and
was overall correlated to the water hardness. By combining measured precipi-
tation in the kettles and CCPP for three levels of hardness, a reduction of 82-91
% in precipitated CaCO3 was estimated when softening the drinking water from
25 to 10 ◦dH in Brøndby. The related eﬀects are a reduction in descaling fre-
quency and use of descaling agents corresponding to a cost saving of 56-155 dkr
household−1 y−1. To test the assertions of an increased energy consumption
due to scaling, the energy consumption of the new kettles and extensively used
kitchen kettles was measured. Through statistical analysis with multiple-linear
regression and ANOVA it was concluded that scale did not cause an increase
in energy consumption. Based on collected data in Brøndby municipality, the
average lifetime of a kettles was determined to 4.9 years using Weibull and Log-
normal distribution with the current water hardness. Due to a reduction in
descaling frequency and use of descaling agents, it is expected that softening
the drinking water will prolong the lifetime of a kettle.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The drinking water supply of Copenhagen is based on groundwater abstraction
from chalk aquifers resulting in very hard water of 362 mg CaCO3 /L (Godskesen
et al., 2012). Hard water has in many years been an inconvenience for households
and companies due to extra expenses and time for families to remove scale from
tiles and household appliances due to an increased use of cleaning agents like
acetic acid (HOFOR, 2016). There are several assertions that scaling results
in an increased energy consumption due to an isolating eﬀect in water heating
appliances.
According to Teknologisk Insitut (2007) limescale deposits were
estimated to reduce the heat transfer by 9 % per 1 mm scaling in water heating
devices. In another study, Van der Bruggen et al. (2009) estimated an increase
of 10 % in energy consumption per 1 mm scaling.
In the Netherlands, they have already taken the consequences of reducing the
hardness of the water. Since the late 1970s central softening has been practised
as a treatment process in the drinking water production to overcome excessive
scaling problems. The reasons for softening covers the environmental beneﬁts
related to the reduction of calcium carbonate (CaCO3) scale formation in water
heating appliances to prevent an additional use of energy, and the reduction of
the environmental burden by chemicals. Other motivating factors for implemen-
tation of central softening in the Netherlands have been social costs, aesthetics
and comfort (Hofman et al., 2006).
A study using Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) to quantify the environmental im-
pact of central softening by chemical precipitation in a pellet reactor showed
environmental beneﬁts when considering both the negative eﬀects at the wa-
terworks and the positive eﬀects arising from the changed water quality. The
impact on the environment and resource consumption were reduced when soft-
ening the water in Copenhagen to a ﬁnal hardness of 254 mg/L as CaCO3 or
145 mg/L as CaCO3 (Godskesen et al., 2012). The kettles were identiﬁed as
one of the domestic appliances aﬀected by the softened water. Three important
aspects related to the eﬀect of softening was identiﬁed: increased lifetime, re-
duced energy consumption and reduced use of descaling agent. At the moment,
most studies have examined the eﬀects of softening by LCA or Cost beneﬁt

1.1
Aim
As Brøndby will become the ﬁrst municipality in Denmark with central softened
drinking water, it is a highly relevant aim to examine the formation of scale in
a kettle and the related eﬀects experienced in the households when the drinking
water is softened. To meet this objective, following questions are to be answered:
• Which method can be used to easily prepare softened water for this study
and can it be accepted for the purpose?
• What is the reduction in scale formation in a kettle after 110 boiling
processes when softening Lyngby-Taarbæk tap water from approximately
20 ◦dH to 10 and 5 ◦dH and how can it be measured?
• What is the inherent precipitation properties of water at 25 ◦C at the
three levels of hardness?
• What is the Calcium Carbonate Precipitation Potential (CCPP) for water
with three levels of hardness and how can it be used to estimate precipi-
tation in a Brøndby household kettle before and after implementation of
softening?
• How does reduced scale formation in a kettle eﬀect descaling frequency
and the use of descaling agents?
• What is the impact on the energy consumption of a kettle due to scaling?
• What is the current lifetime of a kettle in Brøndby Municipality and what
is the expected eﬀect regarding the lifetime when softening the drinking
water?
The questions will be answered based on results from laboratory work, where
tap water and prepared soft water will be applied for scale formation in kettles
by boiling 110 times. Energy measurements on the kettles will be carried out in
parallel with boiling of the water. In addition, three kitchen kettles with exten-
sive use are studied in relation to the energy consumption. Finally, modelling
of CCPP is done in PHREEQC, and data on lifetimes, collected by Godskesen
et al. (2016), is studied.

Chapter 2
Background and Theory
Hard water has in many years been an inconvenience for households and compa-
nies in the Capital region of Denmark. The hard water results in extra expenses
and time for families to remove scale from tiles and household appliances. In
addition, the hard water has a negative eﬀect on the environment, as electricity
consumption and thus carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions increases when heating
elements and hot water tank calcify. To minimise these negative eﬀects, cen-
tral softening of the drinking water at Brøndby Vester waterworks (WW) is
implemented during Spring 2017 by HORFOR. Thus Brøndby is the ﬁrst mu-
nicipality in Denmark with softened drinking water treated by the pellet reactor
method (PRM) (HOFOR, 2016). Using this technology, calcium (Ca2+) and bi-
carbonate (HCO−
3 ) are removed from the water by addition of sodium hydroxide
(NaOH). CaCO3 will crystallize at the surface of sand grains (Hofman et al.,
2006). Due to this implementation, it is highly relevant to investigate some of
the eﬀects achieved from softening as it can potentially reduce environmental
and economic impacts. To substantiate this study, the following sections will
address some of the underlying mechanisms in relation to the hardness of the
drinking water and the formation of scale. An overview of the current hardness
in Eastern Zealand will be presented, and a brief literature review on the pre-
cipitants in water heating appliances as well as recorded eﬀects on the energy
consumption caused by scaling will be included. Finally, lifetime data of the
electric kettle related to water hardness will be introduced in this chapter.
2.1
Drinking water hardness
The hardness of drinking water can be deﬁned as the sum of all multivalent
cations (Nazaroﬀand Alvarez-Cohen, 2001). However, hardness is often an ex-
pression of the concentration of Ca2+ and magnesium (Mg2+) (Winther et al.,
2010). The amount of ions in the drinking water is a result of groundwater ab-
straction from chalk aquifer, where water is in contact with CaCO3 (limestone).

When the product of the ion concentrations exceed the solubility product the
solution is saturated with respect to CaCO3. In other words, an SI value of < 0,
0 and > 0 represent subsaturation, saturation and oversaturation, respectively
(Wojtowicz, 2001).
However, nucleation does not take place immediately when exceeding the solu-
bility product. This is due to stable nuclei, where no precipitation is observable.
For crystals with a high surface energy such as CaCO3, precipitation occurs
when exceeding critical saturation and the activation energy barrier surmounted
(Wojtowicz, 2001). Elevated temperatures will enhance the precipitation, due
to Ksp being temperature dependent. When the temperature increases, Ksp
decreases resulting in an increase in SI (Stumm and J., 1996; Madsen, 2007).
It is a well documented phenomena that heterogeneous nucleation occurs more
easily on a hard surface at a lower saturation state as opposed to homogeneous
nucleation in a bulk solution (Chen et al., 2005). When crystals are formed
during heterogeneous nucleation, they grow faster at a lower saturation than
the critical saturation. In addition, formed crystal may contribute to forma-
tion of new crystals during a secondary nucleation process, resulting in positive
conditions for scale formation (Madsen, 2007).
Calcium Carbonate Precipitation Potential (CCPP)
Although SI is a useful indicator of the potential for CaCO3, it is only a qual-
itative measure as it does not include the extent of precipitation. By use of
mathematical tools, it is possible to calculate the potential amount of CaCO3
precipitation based on aqueous carbonate solutions, namely the Calcium Car-
bonate Precipitation Potential (CCPP) (Wojtowicz, 2001). The CCPP, which
==PAGE:23==
2.2 Water heating appliances
7
is the equivalent of CaCO3 supersaturation, is a generally used index. As the
name implies, the index gives a quantiﬁcation of the potential of CaCO3 precipi-
tation in a solution reaching equilibrium. CCPP can be determined by iteration
using programs like PHREEQC.
Modelling of CCPP can be done for a closed or open system with respect to
atmospheric CO2. In closed system, the amount of CO2 is limited. The system
is open, when CO2 is a continuous source. Thus, a partial pressure of CO2
corresponding to the atmosphere (-log PCO2 = 3.5) is often applied (Appelo
and Postma, 2005).

One of the issues regarding the service life of the electric kettle is the trend of

such as trends and the hassle maintaining the device compared to a relatively
low cost of a new kettle. Thus, lifetime data found in literature may reﬂect the

Lifetime distribution methodology is often applied for statistical analysis of life-
time data. Ragan (1992) discovered that the Weibull distribution function ad-
equately models the failure time distribution of water heaters.
The Weibull
distribution has the ability to assume characteristics of many diﬀerent types
of distributions as it both can model right-skewed, left-skewed and symmetric
data. Thus, Weibull is useful in many ways to model a variety of data. Also
the Log-normal distribution tend to better represent lifetime data. Thus, they
are often called lifetime distribution (Lawless, 2003).

Methods
The eﬀects of changed drinking water composition due to softening were investi-
gated in the form of laboratory experiments and models. The following chapter
describes the methodology used to examine limescale deposits in a practical as
well as theoretical context through laboratory experiments on electric kettles
and PHREEQC modelling of precipitation potentials. The method used to ex-

also covered in this part. In addition, the chapter will include a description of

3.1
Soft water preparation
Due to practical circumstances, it was not possible to retrieve authentic soft-
ened water identical to water treated by the PRM. Thus, the soft water used
to investigate the eﬀects of changed drinking water composition was prepared
in the laboratory. It was decided to use a "dilution method" with demineralised
water to reduce the hardness of the tap water accessible in the laboratory. In
demineralised water, all ions and dissolved gasses have been removed including
Ca2+ and Mg2+ (Hansen, 2006). Thereby, it is assumed that the ratio between
tap water and the demineralised water, used for softening, also can be used to
determine the hardness. Tap water from Lyngby-Taarbæk Supply was mixed
with demineralised water to produce soft water of 10 ◦dH (SW dH10) and soft
water of 5 ◦dH (SW dH5).
Thus, the hardness of the water can be catego-
rized as Slightly hard and Soft, respectively (Lyngby-Taarbæk Forsyning A/S,
2016). The hard water (HW) applied for the study was tap water from Lyngby-
Taarbæk water supply. According to Lyngby-Taarbæk Forsyning A/S (2016),
the drinking water has a hardness of 18-21 ◦dH, and is thus deﬁned as "Hard".
A volumetric ﬂask was used to ensure an amount of precisely one liter prepared

3.2
Limescale formation in kettles
Limescale precipitation was studied through 110 boiling processes in nine new
kettles using one liter of water each time.
Of that, 105 boilings were with
varying initial temperature of the water and 5 boilings with constant initial
temperature of 20 ◦C (see Section 3.5.1). Three of the kettles were exposed
to tap water (HW1, HW2 and HW3), three were exposed to the prepared soft
water with a hardness of approx. 10 ◦dH (SW1 dH10, SW2 dH10 and SW3
dH10), and ﬁnally three kettles with soft water with a hardness of approx. 5
◦dH (SW1 dH5, SW2 dH5 and SW3 dH5).
The eﬀect of softening the water were investigated by studying the formation of
limescale in the nine kettles by the three following methods:
• Visual eﬀect
• Weighing of the kettles
• Determination of precipitated constituents (Ca, Mg and Fe)

Visual eﬀect
To document the visual eﬀect of softening, photos of the nine kettles were taken
during the testing period and at the end of the period (after the 110 times of
boiling). A comparison of the extent of limescale formation between the kettles
exposed to each of the three hardnesses were done.
Weighing of the kettles
Each kettle were weighed before the test, approximately once a week during the
test and once at the end of the testing period. Each of the weekly weighings
were done on Mondays to ensure a minimal amount of residual water at the
weighings. Since the kettles were not in use during weekends, there was time for
evaporation of the residual water and no signs of remaining water was observed
in the kettles after two days since the last boiling campaign. The increased
weight of the kettles could be an indication of the scale formation through 110
boiling processes.
Determination of Ca, Mg and Fe by AAS
For a more accurate determination of the scaling in the nine kettles, AAS analy-
sis was conducted after the testing period. Chemical composition of pellets pro-
duced from pilot scale investigation at Brøndby Vester WW showed that other
macro ions measured in relatively high concentrations are Fe and Mg (Tang and
Albrechtsen, 2016). Thus, in addition to Ca, it was decided to analyse the scale
for these two constituents.
The inside of the kettles were de-acidiﬁed with 1% nitric acid (HNO3) solution
to dissolve all limescale. An amount of 200 mL was needed for kettles HW and
100 mL for SW dH10. For SW dH5, 50 mL solution was used. The kettles
were gently tilted in circular motions to dissolve the limescale on the sides, and
then left for two hours with the solution. A disposable pipette were used for
the sides of the kettles and to ensure that all remaining limescale was dissolved.
The solutions were then transferred to a storage bottle with a lid.
Before analysing with AAS, the solutions were diluted 100-2000 times for Ca,
and 20-1000 times for Mg to stay within the calibration range. No dilution were
needed regarding Fe.

3.5
Energy consumption of kettles
Electric kettles were used to study the potential eﬀect of increased energy con-
sumption due to limescale formation. The study included laboratory test on
nine new kettles and three kitchen kettles of diﬀerent brand used extensively by
employees and students at the Department of Environmental Engineering.
During each boiling process, the energy consumption of each kettle and the tem-
perature of the water were measured using electricity meters (Sparometer) and
thermometers (Thermapen), respectively. The thermometer was tested regard-
ing its accuracy. The standard deviation was 0.1-0.5 ◦C at known temperatures
(Appendix B). Thus, the accuracy of the Thermapen is acceptable for this study.
The initial temperature of the water was measured in the kettle before the ket-
tle was turned on. The plug of the kettle was placed in the Sparometer, which
showed the energy consumption and boiling time. When the kettle switched
oﬀ, the lid was opened and the temperature was measured and notated after
approximately 15 seconds when Thermapen showed a steady value. After notat-
ing the energy consumption and boiling time, the Sparometer was reset to zero
before next boiling. The kettles were emptied through the spout ﬁve minutes

The experienced heat capacity (Ce) represents the amount of the energy needed
to raise the temperature of the water to the boiling point. However, it also
covers the energy used to raise the temperature of the heating element in the
kettle at the beginning of the process. According to Teknologisk Insitut (2007),
limescale deposits were estimated to reduce the heat transfer in water heating
devices. Thus, it is expected that the limescale will have an increasing eﬀect on
the energy consumption resulting in a higher experienced heat capacity.

at the three levels of hardness (Figure 4.5). MPs are based on the masses given

mean precipitations that take place in one kettle per boiling process. Thereby,
it is assumed that the amount precipitation is linear correlated to the number
of boiling processes.
PPs for calcite (CCPP) are highly aﬀected by the system being closed or open to
atmospheric partial pressure of CO2. CCPPs for an open system are signiﬁcantly
higher than the CCPPs based on the closed system (Figure 4.4). For an open
system, CO2 is continuous source due to the contact with the atmosphere. Thus,
equilibrium will occur between CO2 in the water and CO2 in the air. Dissolved
CO2 rapidly reacts with water to form carbonic acid (H2CO3), which then
dissociates into HCO−1
3
(Nazaroﬀand Alvarez-Cohen, 2001). When the water
is heated, e.g. in a kettle CO2 is removed from the water as gas phase and the
equilibrium shifts resulting in Ca2+ ions reacting with HCO−1
3
(de Moel et al.,
2006). The unlimited CO2 from the atmosphere results in a higher concentration
of HCO−1
3
due to ongoing dissociation and thereby a higher potential for CaCO3
precipitation to take place compared to a closed system, where CO2 is limited.
When water is boiled in a kettle, water vapour displaces the existing air in the
kettle, making diﬀusion of CO2 into the water and subsequent dissociation very
unlikely to occur. Due to diﬀusion being a slow process and boiling a relatively
short process, there are strong arguments against calcite precipitation in a kettle
being described as an open system with respect to CO2.
For the closed system, CCPPs for hardness approx. 5 and 10 ◦dH, are negative.
Hence, no precipitation occurs. At approx. 20 ◦dH (HW), the CCPP and MP
are equal, namely around 20 mg/L (Figure ??). Precipitation did occur in the
kettles at 5 and 10 ◦dH making the closed system not an optimal choice for
predicting precipitation of CaCO3. Due to the fact, that CCPPs are based on
equilibrated water and a boiling process not being a system in equilibrium it is

As the PHREEQC modelling is based on equilibrium phases calculations in a
batch reactor as a closed or open system, it does not account for varying tem-
peratures, which takes place at a boiling process in a kettle. Water is heated
over time, and thereby decreases the solubility product of CaCO3 resulting in
an increase in the SI, and thus the precipitation is not constant over time. In
addition, other factors, such as nucleation and crystal growth processes and ad-
sorption to the surface material also play an important role. The amount of
CaCO3 that actually precipitates depends not only on the extent of supersat-
uration, but also on kinetics. Often reaction kinetics of CaCO3 precipitation
is inﬂuenced by a temperature dependent growth constant, a function of SI,
and the available CaCO3 surface (Brink et al., 2004). Therefore, using kinetics
for describing the relatively short precipitation process in a kettle, might be a
better method. Wang et al. (2013) concluded that a larger amount of CaCO3
tended to precipitate at a stainless steel surface compared to silica surfaces due
to the steel surface being rougher. Thus, there are many inﬂuencing parameters
to account for when predicting the actual precipitation of CaCO3. Neverthe-
less, with limited knowledge on kinetics and adsorption mechanisms, CCPP is
usefull tool, which requires little information on the composition of the water
and temperature conditions. To predict the extent of precipitation in heating
appliances such as kettles, CCPP should be used in comparison with knowledge
on measured precipitation studied in the laboratory.

Discussion of energy consumption and scale
A number of sources have stated the eﬀect of scale formation on the energy
consumption due to reduced heat transfer. The eﬀect vary from 9 - 30 % for 1
mm of scaling (Teknologisk Insitut, 2007; VDI, 2005), see Table 2.3. However, no
eﬀect was observed during this study on energy consumption of electric kettles
exposed to water with diﬀerent hardnesses.

The ﬁnal stage of the kettles exposed to hard water reveals that scale does not
cover the entire heat exchanger, and that a part of the scaling has detached from
the bottom as ﬂakes. During the boiling process, this part will lay on top of the
water and not cause any eﬀect on the heat transfer. Even an extensive use of the
kitchen kettle did not show any impact on the energy consumption. It is very
likely that detached limescale, was removed from the kettles when emptying for
remaining water through the lid during the registration period, resulting in no
observable increase of the weights of the kitchen kettles. In addition, removal
of detached limescale by rinsing the kettle with water might have taken place
due to the inconvenience of ﬂakes in the tea/coﬀee.
A continuation of the study with the new kettles in the laboratory with a larger
number of boiling processes to build up the formation of scale on the heat ex-
changer might result in noticeable impact on the energy consumption. However,
this study seeks to reﬂect the use of kettle in an average household. An extensive
use of the kettles is thereby not relevant to include in this study of changing
drinking water composition due to central softening.

the ﬁrst Municipality with central softened drinking water.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
The aim of this thesis was to examine the formation of scale in a kettle and the
related eﬀects experienced in the households when changing the composition
of the drinking water due to central softening at the waterworks. The method
used for preparation of the soft water was successfully implemented. From a
visual point of view, softening of the water from approx.
20 to 10 ◦dH has
an signiﬁcantly eﬀect on the amount of limescale formation in the kettles after
110 times of boiling. Limescale formation was found in quantities of 1941-2371
mg in the kettles with water of approx. 20 ◦dH. By softening the water to 10
◦dH, scale formation was reduced by 76 %. Softening to 5 ◦dH, results in a
reduction of 99 %. The weighing of the kettle could be used as a measure of
scale formation in the kettles. The precipitated constituents were interpreted
into calcite, dolomite and hematite as the most likely minerals using PHREEQC,
where calcite represents 89-94 % of the precipitation in the kettles. The presence
of Fe in kettle HW 3 was found to have a negative eﬀect on the crystal growth
of CaCO3, which shows a signiﬁcant lower mass. The inherent precipitation
properties of water at 25 ◦C was examined. The results showed that formation of
scale decreased by 88 % when reducing the hardness with 50 %. Eventhough, the
amount was very low compared to the precipitation in the kettles, the reduced
scale formation at room temperature might have a great visual eﬀect at e.g. the
sinks in the households.
Calculation of CCPP is a useful index for quantifying precipitation for varying
drinking water compositions and are overall correlated to the hardness. Despite
the CCPP being highly aﬀected by whether the system is closed or open with
respect to atmospheric CO2, it was possible to estimate the precipitation in a
kettle before and after softening in combination with the measured precipitation
in the kettles at three levels of hardness of Lyngby-Taarbæk drinking water. The
results showed a reduction of 82-91 % in precipitated CaCO3 if softening the
drinking water from 25 to 10 ◦dH. In Brøndby this would lead to a reduction in
descaling frequency and agents and an expected cost saving of 56-155 dkr per
household per year.
The energy measurements on the kettles showed no eﬀect in energy consumption

due to scaling. Multiple-linear regression revealed that there is no signiﬁcant
correlation between the number of boiling processes, initial and boiling temper-
ature and the experienced heat capacity, which was used as indicator of increas-

which are extensively used, showed signs of increased energy consumption. An

The lifetime of a kettle exposed the current hardness in Brøndby was determined

electric kettle will increase when Brøndby becomes the ﬁrst municipality with
central softened drinking water.

Chapter 6
Experience, future work
and perspectives
Determining the eﬀects of softening the water for household appliances like the
kettle is challenging as practical circumstances can result in a necessity to in-
troduce more simple methods that do not reﬂect reality. In this study, it was
necessary to come up with a simple method to soften the water. Due to the rel-
atively large amount of water, it was not possible to retrieve authentic softened
water produced from the pellet reactor since this was both time consuming and
required optimal storage options. Nevertheless, using authentic softened water
for studying precipitation would have been more optimal. There is probably
a greater certainty associated with the use of authentic softened water as the
hardness of the water is more deﬁned and does not vary as was the case with the
soft water generated in the laboratory. If repeating the study, the use a greater
number of kettles for each hardness would provide a more stable evaluation of
the precipitation and the reduction due to softening as there is a possibility of
corrosion of the bottom of the kettle as seen in one of the kettles with hard water.
This showed to have an inhibitory eﬀect on precipitation of CaCO3. By includ-
ing a fourth hardness, this would strengthen the understanding of the correlation
between measured and potential precipitation in the kettles. This would also
result in a more accurate estimation of the precipitation, when only the CCPP
is available. In a modeling context, it would be possible to achieve more pre-
cise estimates of CaCO3 precipitation by introducing kinetics to describe the
precipitation process in the kettle. However, this requires more information on
inﬂuencing parameters on reaction kinetics.
To study the inherent precipitation properties of water at 25 ◦C, it is preferred
that the water is stored in vessels with a greater surface contact with the air and
a ﬁlter for retaining the precipitation. In that way, it would be more comparable
to the kettles.
Scaling had no impact on the energy consumption of the kettle. However it
would be interesting to study energy consumption and scale in other water
heating appliances, such as the coﬀee machine. The kettle seemed to "throw"

56

the scale in ﬂakes. Perhaps this is not the case for a coﬀee machine. Additional
work in terms of collecting lifetime data from municipality with soft water, would
be an easy accessible method, to study the eﬀect of hard water on lifetime of
the kettle.
Focus in this study has primarily been of the technical measures of CaCO3
deposit in kettles. However, it also worth mentioning the perceived eﬀects in
the households that can be expected when central softening is implemented.
These eﬀects covers rarer need for descaling, both of he domestic appliances but
also tiles, toilets, and sinks. Reduced scale deposits in domestic appliances may
lead to reduced maintenance and increased lifetimes of e.g. washing machines
and hot water tanks. In addition, the consumption of soap and shampoo is
lowered due to soft water. It is expected that soften water will be beneﬁcial
in many aspects and that the overall eﬀect is perceptible when the households
adjust to the new conditions derived from the soft water.
CCPP calculations in Phreeqc revealed that softening the water in Brøndby to
hardness 10 ◦dH results in a value of 0.3 mg/L for a closed system with respect
to atmospheric CO2 at 25 ◦C. This means that under these kind of conditions,
the water might potentially be corrosive as the water is close to subsaturation
with respect to Ca.
The conditions may be present in pipe systems, where
there is no contact with the atmosphere and where the temperature is relatively
low (< 25 ◦C). Corrosion can both aﬀect the aesthetic quality of the water
and damage piping systems in terms of leakages (Nazaroﬀand Alvarez-Cohen,
2001). In worst case, corrosion processes can result in presence of toxic metals
as copper, lead and zinc (Hilbert et al., 2010). It is therefore recommended that
regular analyses of Ca and Mg are done, where these conditions may exist when
softening is implemented for a possible adjustment of the hardness to reduce
the potential of corrosive water.

Multiple linear regression (MLR) was conducted to model the linear regression
between one continuous dependent variable (Y ) and two or more predictor vari-

ship between the experienced heat capacity and the number of boiling processes,
the initial temperature and boiling temperature. The common relationship is
described as follows:
Y = β0 + β1x1 + β2X2 + β3x3

in the mean response to a unit change in the predictor variable X1, when X2
and X3 are held constant.

H0: β1 = β2 = ... = βk = 0
HA: βj ̸= 0 for at least one j
