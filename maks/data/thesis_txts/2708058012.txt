Abstract
The Danish healthcare system is undergoing reconstruction due to the increasing pressure
on the Danish hospitals. To accommodate the increasing pressure, six new so-called Su-
per Hospitals across the country will be constructed. The New North Zealand Hospital
(NHN), located in Hillerød, is among these six hospitals. One of the goals of NHN is to
gather specialties and resources previously located in other hospitals under a single roof.
However, this has increased the complexity of deciding how the department layouts should
be deﬁned. A project team of decision-makers at NHN faces this exact problem and needs
prescriptive insights into the future NHN system to support their decision-making. To
accommodate this need, two particular problems have been identiﬁed: The sizing and
ordering of departments and the prescriptive insights of how potential department layouts
would perform.
To investigate these problems, this study proposes a Decision Support System (DSS) de-
veloped speciﬁcally for the NHN system. The DSS will be based on granted historical
data of patient arrivals, their length of stay, and distinct NHN system characteristics to
enlighten and evaluate the performance of department layouts. To do so, a simheuristic
framework is at the core of the DSS. The simheuristic combines the methodology of simu-
lations with the optimization nature of metaheuristics. Thereby, the DSS grants insights
to the future NHN system by analyzing how relevant system characteristics and potential
department layouts would aﬀect each other.
Based on the analysis of the various NHN system variations, relevant patterns and similari-
ties are identiﬁed. The results showed that linking sub-departments with shared specialties
as neighbors impacted the system performance most negatively. At the same time, the
hospital’s physical hub areas were found as ﬁtting natural barriers between the depart-
ments. Furthermore, two "what if" scenarios were examined, testing the inﬂuence of fewer
patients versus a shorter length of stay. The results showed that if NHN were only able
to reduce one of them, decreasing the number of patients would have the most impact on
the system performance.
The proposed DSS has given the project team a prescriptive analysis tool for decision-
making. The tool can contribute to valuable insights and position the project team better
in making a more informed decision on the department layout of the future NHN.

Introduction
The increasing population combined with rising life expectancy has led to increased pres-
sure on the Danish healthcare system (Danske Regioner, 2015). Not only do the Danish
hospitals have to solve the problem of increasing number of patients, but they also have
to handle current issues with a lack of resources in the form of doctors, nurses, and other
medical staﬀ(Region Hovedstaden, 2015). Therefore, the demand for new, larger and
more eﬃcient hospitals structured to support the needs in modern healthcare is increas-
ing. The latest signiﬁcant step in streamlining the Danish healthcare system was the new
Structural Reform in 2007, which has or will entail the closing of 25 hospitals across Den-
mark (Nørby & Holgersen, 2018). The idea behind the Structural Reform is that resources
can be used more eﬃcient by gathering resources in fewer and better locations. Besides
gathering resources from closed hospitals in other existing hospitals, six so-called Super
Hospitals are also built. Among these six Super Hospitals is the New North Zealand Hos-
pital (NHN) which is under construction in Hillerød (Nørby & Holgersen, 2018). NHN and
the rest of the six new Super Hospitals are built to prepare the Danish healthcare system
for future needs via new technology, healthcare innovation and, in general, more eﬃciently
use of resources (Fog et al., 2015). The new Super Hospitals also gather specialties that
previously has been spread out on multiple hospitals. This has led to increased complexity
in the combinations of specialties and has raised new questions about how these should be
placed in relation to each other. The increased complexity within the new Super Hospital
calls for operations management (OM) techniques to improve processes, plannings, and
structures (Stevenson, 2020).
Healthcare Operations Management is its own ﬁeld of study and has attracted more at-
tention in recent years. The opportunities are many and include but is not limited to im-
proving the ﬂow of patients and operating room planning and scheduling (Denton, 2013).
The wide range of opportunities within Healthcare operations is partly due to Health-
care 4.0, which is developed from Industry 4.0. The concept of Healthcare 4.0 focuses on
the automation of smart machines facilitated by major digital databases (Kocheva, 2021).
However, most importantly for this study is the digitization of large amounts of data used
in Healthcare 4.0, which is the very foundation of creating meaningful optimization and
granting analytical insights. Analytical tools such as simulation and optimization models
can process Big Data and be powerful tools in problem-solving and decision making at the
new Super Hospitals.

Chapter 1. Introduction
This study will investigate the strategic planning problem of how departments of spe-
cialties in NHN should be allocated in relation to each other to facilitate ﬂexible single
patient room utilization across the departments. A ﬂexible single patient room is deﬁned
as a bed which can be used by any patient regardless of their department. To investi-
gate this strategic problem, a Decision Support System (DSS) (Power, 2007) is proposed
supported by its design considerations, modeling, and experimental study analysis. The
developed DSS emphasizes on a model-driven framework that uses NHN system knowl-
edge and historical patient arrival data to investigate the strategic planning problem in
hand. It is identiﬁed, that the problem necessitates both optimization and prescriptive
analysis, why it is suggested that it should be investigated by both metaheuristics (Tsaia
et al., 2016) and simulations (Rosetti, 2016). While these two techniques are considered
fundamentally diﬀerent, they can still beneﬁt each other to great extends. It is considered
that a framework combining these two is applicable for the DSS developed for NHN (Juan
et al., 2015).
1.1
Aim and Objectives
The study will focus on how departments at NHN should be placed to increase the general
eﬃciency of the hospital. The aim is to place the departments such that patients always
can be allocated to a bed near the center of the patient type’s respective department.
The study is based on historical data from existing hospital systems. Diﬀerent models
and scenarios with changes regarding constraints of the system, the number of arriving
patients, and their length of stay (LOS) will be assessed to investigate how this will aﬀect
the performance of the department layouts. To assess the identiﬁed problem, the following
research question will be examined:
How can a Decision Support System be developed and utilized to propose and evaluate
reasonable bed allocation planning at New North Zealand Hospital’s future setup?
This research question has led to the following sub-questions:
• When designing a Decision Support System, which of NHN’s current processes should
be considered, and how can these be implemented?
• How should historical patient ﬂow distributions from existing hospital systems be
transformed into applicable input for NHN’s conceptualized setup?
• Which system characteristics and scenarios would be beneﬁcial to examine in order
to enable an extensive analysis of potential bed allocation solutions?
This study will answer the objectives by proposing a model-driven Decision Support Sys-
tem (DSS) that, by its core, includes interaction between a metaheuristic optimization
model and a stochastic simulation model. The DSS is developed based on historical NHN

The scope and limitations of the project are addressed in the following section. The scope

2.3
Designing a Solution Framework
The physical plants for already existing hospitals are often outdated, ineﬃcient, and diﬃ-
cult to update. Yet, it is, according to Williams (2006) one of the essential circumstances
to achieve eﬃcient healthcare. With the construction of NHN, it is assumed that at least
the architectural principle of the plant has been designed in a way that can support the
current needs and adapt to the future. When assuming that the physical boundaries are in
place for an eﬃcient hospital, new questions arise regarding the internal setup of depart-
ments and how it should be planned to raise eﬃciency. The NHN team has developed an
initial solution to the department order based on patient ﬂows and the interplay between
departments observed in the existing NOH. One of the main problems with this initial
solution is that it is mainly based on descriptive data without deeper insights into how
the merge of specialties from multiple individual setups will perform in real-life scenarios.
Therefore, the NHN Team strives to be able to make decisions based on a more prescrip-
tive examination, which can enlighten possible issues and result in a more informed and
possibly better solution.
The problem can be considered as two distinct problems; the sizing and ordering of de-
partments and the prescriptive insights of how a potential layout would perform in real
life. Part of the ﬁrst problem is to identify the size of each department and thereby how
many beds each department should consist of. The size of the departments will have sig-
niﬁcance to the number of eligible combinations of departments on each of the two ﬂoors.
As NHN utilizes the concepts of ﬂex beds, the sizing part of the problem is assumed to
have less signiﬁcance than the main part of the problem, which is the ordering of the
departments. For example, suppose a department initially has been allocated some beds
which the respective department rarely utilizes. In that case, these beds will be located

farthest away from the department center and, in most cases, be used by the neighbor
departments. Thus, the main problem is to optimize the department order and ﬁnd rea-
sonable solutions. A reasonable solution is deﬁned by how it performs in real-life scenarios
measured by deﬁned performance metrics. This directly refers to the second problem; that
the NHN Team wants further insights than descriptive analysis can provide, to investigate
how potential department layouts at NHN would perform. This entails the application
of prescriptive analysis through simulation, including the factor of real-world randomness
in the system.
According to Rosetti (2016), real-world randomness can be deﬁned as
stochasticity and is a crucial factor when conducting representative simulation analysis.
While these two problems can be investigated independently, it is believed that it would
be beneﬁcial for the problem case at hand to propose a uniﬁed solution framework. The
goal of the solution framework is to facilitate a system where the solutions of the two
problems are fed into each other in a sequential feedback loop. Resulting in a framework
that can ﬁnd reasonable department layouts based on their simulation performance.
As the case problem is a strategic planning problem, it is proposed that the solution
framework should be deﬁned as a Decision Support System (DSS). Power (2007) generally
deﬁnes a decision support system as a set of computerized information tools that support
decision-making processes. Power (2007) also proposes that there are ﬁve diﬀerent types
of DSS’s: Communication-, data-, document-, knowledge-, and model-driven. Given this
study’s scope and identiﬁed case problem, a model-driven DSS has been identiﬁed as the
most applicable framework. Power (2007) deﬁnes a model-driven DSS to utilize, among
others, statistical, optimization, and simulation models that use data to support decision-
makers to analyze particular scenarios.
The DSS developed in this study will not be
a broad, fully realized software with an associated user interface or connection to data
warehouse. Instead, its underlying foundation will be a collection of speciﬁc programming
notebooks, including the developed models.
NHN speciﬁc data and system characteristics are required to design the applicable DSS
and develop the foundation of the chosen models. As described earlier, these models should
bring analytical insight to the case problem by establishing a framework where the two
identiﬁed problems, Optimizing department ordering and Simulating department layouts,
can mutually beneﬁt each other. In Figure 2.4 below, a simpliﬁed model-driven DSS has
been developed and visualized for the NHN problem case.
The DSS is built upon historical data of patient arrivals and LOS combined with qualita-
tive information of system processes and physical characteristics retrieved from the NHN
Team. By utilizing these inputs, two model modules can be developed, each referring to
an identiﬁed problem of the NHN Team: Optimization of Department Layout and Simu-
lation of Department Layout. The goal of the DSS is to allow investigation of these two
main problems in synergy. A framework will be developed that allows for the output of
a model module to be used as input for the other. As illustrated in the conceptualized
DSS, the optimization module produces solutions to be evaluated in the simulation mod-

3.1
DEGREE Problem-Solving Methodology
This study will follow a modiﬁed version of the DEGREE methodology to ensure that
the overarching process of developing the required solution framework will be with as few
hurdles as possible (Rosetti, 2016). The DEGREE methodology is a general methodology
for solving problems and is an acronym of:
1. Deﬁne the problem.
2. Establish measures of performance for evaluation.
3. Generate alternative solutions.
4. Rank alternative solutions.
5. Evaluate and iterate during process.
6. Execute and evaluate the solution.
While the DEGREE methodology deﬁnes a well-rounded structure for general problem-
solving, it must be modiﬁed to contemplate the steps of a solution that includes simulation
speciﬁcally. Therefore, Rosetti (2016) has reﬁned the general methodology for applying
simulation to problem-solving, which is divided into six distinct phases.

general methodology is visualized in Figure 3.1 below, including which chapters of this

3.2
Deﬁne the Problem
As mentioned in Section 2.3 - the most relevant problems of the NHN Team are the
sizing and ordering of the departments and lack of performance insights of potential NHN
setups. These problems can not be solved solely by an isolated simulation study. Instead,
this study aims to combine the optimization nature of metaheuristics and the stochastic
nature of simulations in a simheuristic solution framework, which will be described in
detail in Section 3.5. This will facilitate a feedback loop where the search for a reasonable
department layout will be inﬂuenced by the simulation performance measures of the same
system. As described in Section 2.3, these processes will be the central element of the
DSS. The DSS will also enable the NHN team to use the simulation part as an isolated
DST to investigate various "what-if" scenarios when they need to gain analytical insights
into potential NHN setups. Therefore, the objectives of this study are to:
• Investigate how the structure of the model inﬂuence the search for a reasonable NHN
department setup.
• Investigate how the ordering and positioning of the departments aﬀect the system’s
overall allocation of patients in relation to their associated department center.
• Compare the performance measurements for diﬀerent relevant system characteristics.
• Investigate relevant scenarios focusing on the inﬂuence of modiﬁed patient arrivals
versus patient lengths of stay.

3.4
Establish Performance Metrics
Given the deﬁned problem and system of the study, performance metrics, also known
as KPIs, have to be established to investigate and assess the solutions (Rosetti, 2016).
Furthermore, the performance metrics will be used to examine solutions in the optimization
process and get insights into the actual performance of the found solutions. The chosen
KPIs are: Distance, Dispersion, and Correlation.
Distance
The primary performance metric is distance, as the NHN Team generally wants to mini-
mize the distance, the healthcare staﬀhas to walk between the patients in a given depart-
ment. Figure 3.4 visualizes a simpliﬁed example of how the distance for a department is
calculated for each time period. The distance is calculated by a distance matrix as the
number of beds from the department center to each bed with a patient from the given
department. The total distance for a solution is then the sum of the distance for each
patient in the system for each time period.

Dispersion
Originating from measuring distance, a performance metric called dispersion is developed.
The concept of dispersion is to establish a measurement that takes the placement of a
patient and its neighbor patients into consideration. This measurement will count the
number of patients with neighbor patients on both sides that are from another distinct
department than their own. The concept of dispersion is visualized in Figure 3.5. The
ﬁgure shows an example in which Department X utilizes most of the beds. Besides pa-
tients from Department X, two patients from Department Y are allocated such that they
have neighbors from distinct departments on both sides. In this case, the dispersion for
Department Y would be 2, as stated in the ﬁgure. Dispersion is expected to happen at
the soft borders between departments to a greater extent.
Figure 3.5: KPI Dispersion
The synergy between distance and dispersion can give insights into whether patients for a
department are placed far from the department center because of many patients or mixed
patient types. A department with a high distance and a low dispersion would indicate
that the high distance results from many patients in that given department. In contrast,
a high dispersion would indicate that the high distance was due to distinct patient types
using the beds between another type of patient and its department center.
Correlation
A diﬀerent approach to minimize distance is to place two departments with opposite trends
besides each other. If this synergy between two departments were perfect, they would be
able to utilize their neighbor departments’ beds in peak periods. This performance metric
is measured as the correlation between two departments. The correlation is a common
statistical measurement that ranges from -1 to 1, where a smaller value, in this case,
indicates that one department has a decrease in patients while the other has an increase,
and a larger value means that both departments experience an increase or decrease at the

Simulations
The following section will cover a short description of simulations in general, including
why and how simulations can be used. Furthermore, the simulation methodology and
approach used for this case study will be introduced.
Simulation modeling is a commonly used tool with various use cases. One of the primary
beneﬁts is that decision-makers can imitate a system or a layout solely based on the given
system’s well-deﬁned assumptions and characteristics. This can often be done both time
eﬃciently and with low cost, compared to if the same experimental studies should have
been performed in real life. Therefore, simulations entail that multiple layouts can be
tested and compared quickly, making it suitable for a wide range of potential layouts for
NHN to be measured. However, the output of a simulation model will only be as realistic
as the characteristics, assumptions, and data the model has been built upon and provided
with (Law, 2015). The NHN system in this study is simpliﬁed with multiple assumptions,
and historical data is used. Therefore, the results from the simulation model will not
represent the real NHN system entirely, which has to be considered when conclusions are
made based on the simulation tool.
As NHN is under construction at the time of writing, it is diﬃcult to know how it will be-
have or perform. By developing a simulation model that can imitate the expected behavior
of NHN, the aim is to get prescriptive insight into how changes to the system will aﬀect
the performance. These insights can enlighten the NHN Team to make a more informed
decisions, even though it may not be able to provide the NHN with a deﬁnite solution.
There exist diﬀerent simulation methods depending on the characteristics of the system
simulated. In this simulation study, the system’s characteristics are that it is a physi-
cal system, as it comprises two ﬂoors with hospital wards. The patient ﬂow is based on
stochasticity, so the system is stochastic. As the system and its states change with respect
to time, it is a dynamic and discrete system.
Observations, in this case, the stochas-
tic patient ﬂow, entails changes to the system. These changes to the system are called
events. Based on the system’s characteristics, the simulation model used in this study can,
according to Rosetti (2016) be described as a Discrete Event Simulation (DES) model.
Metaheuristics
The following section will cover a short description of metaheuristics in general, including
when, why, and how they can be used. Furthermore, the metaheuristic process and ap-
proach applied for this case study will also be introduced.
Metaheuristic methods are a group of algorithmic frameworks that can search for rea-
sonable solutions in complex and computational heavy optimization problems. Instead of
ﬁnding the optimal solution as mathematical optimization models do, metaheuristic mod-
els aim to ﬁnd good approximations in less time. Metaheuristics are most often used to
solve real-world problems that mathematical optimization models cannot solve in proper
22
==PAGE:36==
DTU
Chapter 3. Deﬁning Decision Support System
time due to the complexity of the problems (Burke & Kendall, 2013). Diﬀerent types of
metaheuristic algorithms exist, each with diﬀerent strengths and weaknesses. However,
they have in common that they can combine constructive methods with diﬀerent strategies
for searching the solution space and handling local optima. Metaheuristics have proven
to be a very eﬀective tool for problem-solving in complex healthcare systems, as it can
explore large solution spaces eﬃciently and thus ﬁnd reasonable solutions (Tsaia et al.,
2016). As metaheuristic algorithms are a way to search the solution space, two runs with
the same input can often result in diﬀerent results, which means that metaheuristic mod-
els, in most cases, are non-deterministic. When two runs do not necessarily result in the
same solution, it can be seen as a consequence of the trade-oﬀbetween eﬃciency and the
quality of the results (Torres-Jimenez & Pavón, 2014).
Given the extensive number of possible department order combinations of NHN, it is
assessed that a mathematical optimization model doing an exhaustive search does not ac-
commodate the case problem. While it would have resulted in an actual optimal solution
at some point in time, it inevitably would also result in too long running times. Therefore,
it is proposed that the nature of metaheuristics suits the goal of exploring a wide space of
potential department setups at NHN and instead identify reasonable solutions. This study
has decided to focus on using a metaheuristic built upon a framework from Simulated an-
nealing (SA) with additions such as a memory list inspired from Tabu Search (TS) and
a very simpliﬁed destroy and repair method inspired from Large Neighbor Search (LNS).
SA has been chosen as the foundation of the metaheuristic as Juan et al. (2015) mentions
multiple instances where it has been used in healthcare applications regarding simulation
optimization.
SA is one of the most used metaheuristic algorithms because of its ability to ﬁnd reason-
able solutions for optimization problems eﬃciently. It is a generic probabilistic method
and is an extended version of a standard local search (hill-climber) algorithm. The name
and one of the algorithm’s key characteristics are inspired by the annealing procedure of
metal (Eren et al., 2017). The idea is that the model initiates with a speciﬁed tempera-
ture (T) which decreases with a factor (α) after each iteration. The SA algorithm uses the
temperature to calculate the acceptance probability, which is used to accept worse solu-
tions than the current solution. The acceptance probability is an exponentially decreasing
function of delta (δ). This means that a neighbor solution that is a lot worse than the
current solution will have a lower probability of acceptance than neighbor solutions that
are only a bit worse. In each iteration, a new neighbor solution is generated by diﬀerent
operators. The types of operators can be many and depend on the problem SA is used
for.
The ﬁrst well-described local search metaheuristic algorithm in modern time is TS and
was ﬁrst deﬁned by Fred Glover (Gendreau & Potvin, 2010). The TS algorithm is designed
to escape local optima by changing the current solution or current state with predeﬁned
moves. A move could, for example, be to swap the location of two departments. One of
the main characteristics of TS is to search the solution space without revisiting already
23
==PAGE:37==
DTU

visited solutions by keeping a number of the visited solutions in a tabu list. The tabu list
is a memory list that can contain either complete solutions or moves. When the algorithm
ﬁnds a new neighbor solution based on one of the predeﬁned moves, it checks whether the
solution is in the tabu list or not. If the solution is not in the tabu list, the current solution
and the neighbor solution are compared, and if the new neighbor solution is accepted, it
becomes the new current solution (Glover, 1990).
LNS is another metaheuristic that aims to search large solution spaces and escape local
optima. In the LNS metaheuristic, neighbor solutions are deﬁned by destroy and repair
methods, and the approach is quite diﬀerent from the TS metaheuristic approach previ-
ously described. LNS destroys a part of the current solution, for afterward to rebuild a
new neighbor solution using predeﬁned repair methods (Shaw, 1998). The destroy and
repair methods used in an LNS heuristic are developed to guide the search for the speciﬁc
use case and vary from problem to problem. One of the advantages of the LNS approach
is that the neighbor solutions can diﬀer a lot more from the current solution than neighbor
solutions do with local search algorithms such as SA or TS.
As mentioned, the proposed metaheuristic for this case study is based on SA, but a
few characteristics from TS and LNS will also be incorporated into the metaheuristic
algorithm. From the TS algorithm, the idea of a memory list will be utilized to prevent
the model from searching already visited solutions for a certain number of iterations.
From the LNS algorithm, the idea of a neighbor solution that diﬀers signiﬁcantly from the
current solution is implemented. Combining characteristics from diﬀerent metaheuristics
aims to achieve an algorithm that can ﬁnd a reasonable solution in a relatively short time
span.

Simheuristics

through simulation to search stochastic optimization problems. The concept matches to a
great extent the case problem of identifying reasonable department layouts in a stochastic

metaheuristic framework by evaluating the problems through simulation.
This study will answer the problem objectives by developing a simheuristic framework

3.5.3
Document Model Assumptions
Given the above presented conceptualized model of the DSS, its model assumptions must
be clearly documented. Most of the model assumptions have already been stated in the
previous sections but are now listed below to capture them all:
• No time used for cleaning a bed in between two patients. A bed is immediately free
when a patient is dispatched.
• No distinction is made between the importance of diﬀerent patient types.
• The distance between two arbitrary neighbor beds is always 1 bed unit.
• The two child and youth departments, PÆD and NEO, are excluded from the system.
• A patient can not move through the child and youth departments, meaning that the
only way to get from one side of the departments to the other side is around it.
• There is only one way to get from the 2nd to the 3rd ﬂoor. Elevators or stairs are
not considered.
• When patients arrive in the hospital, they are spawned in their respective department
center.
• A patient can not be moved when ﬁrst allocated to a bed.
• Bed index 211 divides the two ﬂoors in the 1-dimensional line of beds
• All patients generated from the NHN data will be allocated to the closest available
bed to their department center.
• When searching for an unoccupied bed for a patient, the search starts from the
department center and "moves out" in both directions. The ﬂoor at which the de-
partment center is placed will be searched entirely before searching on the other
ﬂoor. However, in the case of a department being placed across the two ﬂoors, this
department is allowed to search across the two ﬂoors simultaneously.
• If all beds are fully utilized in a given time period new patients will be skipped as
no queue system is developed to handle backlogging.
• For all patients arriving in the same time unit, the order of the patient allocation is
randomized.
• Schedule planning is out of scope - a patient enters the system when they arrive at
the hospital.
• Operational levels are out of scope - it is assumed there are always enough human
resources to allocate, treat, and discharge patients.

The acceptance probability of the simheuristic is a tool to accept worse solutions and is

or accepted by the acceptance probability.

Swap Methods
Lastly, the swap methods applied for the simheuristic is presented. The four swap methods
guide the simheuristic through the solution space. Therefore, these operators must be
structured to ﬁnd new and better solutions eﬃciently. Three out of four swap methods
are developed based on the KPIs elaborated earlier. Each of the swap methods focuses
on ﬁnding the worst-performing department or departments measured by the respective
KPIs. In each iteration of the simheuristic, at least one swap method is utilized to ﬁnd a
new temporal neighbor solution, sn, based on the solution space for the current solution,
sc. Each of the four swap operators is weighted equally, which means that the probability
for each operator to be chosen is the same. The four types of swap methods are:
• DistSwap - 25%
• DispSwap - 25%
• CorrSwap - 25%
• RandomSwap - 25%
To prevent the simheuristic to revisit already visited solutions, the swap function is em-
bedded in a while loop that ensures new sn’s are generated until one that is not contained
in the memory list is found. In this case, the list contains the last 15 sn.
The ﬁrst swap method is the DistSwap. The pseudo-code for the operator can be seen
below, and will, structurally, be very reminiscent of Disp- and CorrSwap.

smaller departments if they are positioned poorly in the given solution. The behavior of

The second operator, DispSwap, functions in a similar manner to the DistSwap. DispSwap
identiﬁes the department with the largest measured total dispersion, and swaps it with
one its neighbors. Dispersion was developed to identify departments that experiences their
patients being allocated in-between other types of patients. Therefore, the DispSwap will
hopefully, to a larger degree, be able to identify appropriate swaps on departments of any
size in comparison to DistSwap. The pseudo code for DispSwap is stated in Appendix A.3.
The third operator, CorrSwap, is based on the correlation KPI. CorrSwap identiﬁes a pair
of neighboring departments with the worst total correlation value in between them. The
operator swaps either of the two with a neighbor department not being a part of the pair
set. The goal with CorrSwap is to identify seasonal ﬂuctuations between departments and
divide departments with similar patient arrival patterns. While it was found, that mean-
ingful correlation would be easier to identify in non-aggregated data, it is still assessed as
a relevant method of guiding the simheuristic in its search for better department layouts.
The pseudo code for DispSwap is stated in Appendix A.3.
Lastly, the fourth swap operator is RandomSwap. The operator simply chooses a random
pair of neighboring departments and swaps their location with one another. RandomSwap
stands in contrast to the other three operators that are developed to identify the worst
performing neighboring departments as seen from their respective KPIs. The reason for
adding a RandomSwap is to mitigate the risk of DistSwap and DispSwap targeting the
same pairs of departments across the simheuristic iterations. RandomSwap will thereby
be able to guide the simheuristic in new directions and search for solution spaces that
otherwise would not have been searched.
If the swapping module does not manage to identify a new sn that is both legal and not
already in the memory list based on the above operators, the shuﬄe function is instead
utilized. The shuﬄe function completely randomizes the whole department order and its
purpose is to redirect the simheuristic if its completely stuck. Thereby, it also grants the
model the possibility of getting a completely new starting point if the proposed solution
is actually accepted by the delta evaluation of the simheuristic.

Veriﬁcation and Validation
The last step before the Preliminary Model Study and Experimental Study can be initi-
ated, is to verify and validate the simheuristic framework. This ensures that the developed
model behaves as intended and as close to the system it seeks to imitate as possible. The
importance of veriﬁcation and validation is emphasized in the last two steps of 2nd phase
in Rossetti’s methodology (Rosetti, 2016) and is further elaborated in Law’s techniques for
verifying simulation models (Law, 2009). Law’s techniques are developed for simulation
models, but it is assessed that most of the techniques also can be used on the simheuristic
as a whole. The techniques are primarily seen as general guidelines and can thus be used
to verify non-simulation models and general algorithms as well. Below, the most relevant

techniques have been applied to verify the simheuristic.
Veriﬁcation
The ﬁrst of Law’s techniques to be utilized is regarding the beneﬁts of establishing a sim-
ple model for a beginning, for then to increases the complexity step by step. By following
this procedure, one should be able to avoid debugging a complex code, which often can
be time-consuming when not knowing which parts of the code that works as intended.
Therefore, the approach for developing the simheuristic was to start with only a simple
main function and the essential helper functions. Then, each time a new helper function
or additional details to the main program was implemented, they were tested, traced and
debugged.
Another of Law’s techniques have been used to get familiar with each part of the algorithm
and make the it more manageable to debug. This technique recommends that someone
else than the main creator of new helper functions or new additions to the main function
should review the code and become reliable with it. This has been a natural necessity
because most helper functions depend on each other. Therefore, it has been necessary for
all persons involved in the development of the simheuristic to be familiar with all parts
of the code. As a consequence of this, most of the simheuristic has been developed or
implemented in collaboration.
A third of Law’s techniques is to test, in this case the simheuristic, with diﬀerent inputs
to see if the model behaves as expected. Furthermore, this technique can identify how the
simheuristic reacts when it is stress tested with more patients than it can handle. When
testing the algorithm with 20% decreased LOS and with 20% less patients, the performance
improved signiﬁcantly. This is as expected as fewer patients or shorter LOS decreases the
number of patients in the system at the same time. The algorithm, therefore, have a
greater likelihood of being able to allocate new patients closer to their department center,
resulting in a better objective value. Opposite, the algorithm was also tested with an
increased number of patients (20%) and LOS (20%). The results from these tests showed
that the system is forced to skip some patients as the capacity of the hospital is fully
utilized. These tests resulted in the implementation of a dummy variable, cantAllocate,
which counts the number of patients that can not be allocated because of lacking free beds.
The last of Law’s techniques that has been utilized focuses on debugging. The recom-
mended approach described by Law is a technique called trace. The concept of trace is
about displaying the temporary states the system is going through. The states can then be
compared with the expected state which have been found by manual iterations executed
on parts of the algorithm. The implementation of trace in and after the essential helper
functions, identiﬁed a problem in some of the swap methods in which the algorithm failed.
By displaying the state it was easier to identify when this problem occurred and thus
mitigate the problem with modiﬁcations to the algorithm. It is clear that the concept of

identify where and what to display. It is especially diﬃcult when some errors only occur

Validation
Finally, validation of the simheuristic is made to ensure that the developed algorithm be-

been obvious to validate the output of the developed simheuristic with behavior observed
at NHN, but as NHN is under constriction, this so-called results validation approach is not
possible to perform (Law, 2009). Since the system is built with a unique combination of
departments and with its own previously deﬁned list of assumptions, it has not been possi-
ble to ﬁnd similar algorithms or models to compare results with. Result validation is thus
almost impossible to utilize. However, the results have been reviewed for reasonableness,
as Law describe is an approach to validate the system, whether or not there is an existing
system to compare with. The initial results have been discussed with the NHN Team and
analyzed to check for consistency, but it is worth mentioning that the pure objective value
is a relative value. The value itself and the raw measurements do not tell much unless
compared with other results. Furthermore, as the primary algorithm is developed in Julia
and not in a visual simulation program such as AnyLogic (2021), it is less approachable for
the NHN Team to comprehend and thus validate the behavior of the algorithm. The list
of assumptions made in cooperation with the NHN Team is, according to Law, one way
to increase the credibility of the algorithm. The multiple meetings with the NHN Team,
including the listed assumptions, should ensure that the credibility is increased. Based on
these and the discussion of results with the NHN Team during the development phase,
the algorithm is validated in the best possible way.

Preliminary Model Study
Before the explained DSS framework can be utilized, multiple parameters and user deﬁned
inputs have to be deﬁned. This chapter aims to examine the user-deﬁned parameters and
other model requirements as preparation for the Experimental Study in the next Chap-
ter 6. There is no one-solution-ﬁts-all for these types of parameter variations and tuning
(Joshi & Bansal, 2020). Therefore, the goal is to identify appropriate model inputs in order
and accordance with how they aﬀect one another. Firstly, the preliminary model study
will seek to identify appropriate weights used to calculate the department sizes through
the approach explained in Chapter 4.2.2. Then, multiple simheuristic runs with diﬀerent
combinations of alpha and temperature values are investigated for the acceptance proba-
bility. When the sizes of the departments and the right setting for alpha and temperature
have been determined, the simehuristic is run once for each of the three objective func-
tions based on the KPIs. The performance of these is analyzed, and the weights used for
transforming the multi multi-objective function are identiﬁed based on the ﬁndings and
the opinions of the NHN Team on their respective importance. Given the transformed
objective function, various robustness tests of the model will be run. Lastly, the number of
required replications to reach a steady state for the model’s stochastic module is identiﬁed.
The preliminary model tests are generally examined through a practical approach where
the best-performing parameter values are chosen. There is no deﬁnite "As-Is" scenario
for NHN. Therefore, the tests are conducted on the model variation with the fewest con-
straints, Model 1, which is the foundation of the other models presented in the Experimen-
tal Study, Chapter 6. Furthermore, the stochastic simulation module of the simheuristic
is excluded until the appropriate steady-state has been identiﬁed.

Therefore, the experimental study takes outset in Model 1, which is the variation of the

testing a new relevant system characteristic and constraint. The purpose of testing varying
system characteristics is to investigate their eﬀect on the overall system performance, and
to identify general patterns in the best found department layouts. Each of the models will
be run by the developed simheuristic framework with its found parameter tuning. Then,

The scenarios are chosen with outset in the NHN Teams expectations on the development
of the future health care system in general. They expect that certain patients can be
treated in their homes instead of at the hospital, and that the general LOS of patients
in the hospitals will decrease as a result of faster and more accurate diagnoses through
improving technologies. Therefore, it was agreed with the NHN Team that a relevant case
scenario analysis would be to compare the eﬀect of these two. However, the NHN Team
does not have clear estimations to which degree patients are expected to be treated at
home and to which degree the LOS can realistically be reduced. Therefore, approxima-
tions of the two scenarios were instead created: In the ﬁrst scenario, departments with an
average LOS > 80 hours will have their number of arriving patients reduced by 5%. In
the second scenario, the same departments with an average LOS > 80 hours will have the
LOS of all their patients reduced by 20%. The argumentation for only aﬀecting depart-
ments with larger LOS in the scenarios, is with the assumption that departments with
smaller LOS generally handles more severe operations that are diﬃcult to further reduce
the LOS of. Then, the logic behind having diﬀerent percentages between the two scenarios
is, that the removal of patients will also indirectly decrease the total system’s LOS. So,
to compensate for this behavior, a 20% reduction in LOS is chosen. Also, a smaller re-
duction in LOS would generally have resulted in insubstantial performance gains. Figure
4.2 in Chapter 4, showed that patients generally arrive in the morning or after lunch, and
not in the evenings or in the middle of the night. In reality, patients are also generally
being discharged in intervals as the doctors and nurses makes their route throughout the
department, and not necessarily the exact moment they are healthy. An example of a
shorter reduction of LOS than 20% not working as indented would be the case where a
patient would originally have been released from the hospital in the morning, now with a
small reduction would be discharged in the middle of the night instead. However, as there
is very small likelihood that a new patient will arrive in the middle of the night, this free
bed will not have the intended eﬀect of relieving the system. Therefore, a more signiﬁcant
amount of 20% less LOS is chosen. There are a total of six departments with LOS greater
than 80 hours: 2.PAL, 3.ONK, 7.GAS, 9.END, 10.NEU, and 11.ORT.

Model 2 - Sub-departments
Model 2 incorporates the system characteristic of sub-departments to the modeled system.
The idea of sub-departments is to constraint the system to always have sub-departments
being neighbors to each other. The NHN Team ﬁnds this characteristic highly relevant,
as it is to be expected that the sub-departments share specialties and thereby to some
degree can utilize the same resources more eﬃciently. The list of sub departments are
stated deﬁned as:
• Oncological: 2.PAL / 3.ONK
• Gynecology: 4.GYN / 5.OBS + (NEO)
• Surgical:
7.GAS / 8.KIR
The three sub-departments concerns ontological, gynecology, and surgical. Ontological
and surgical are both simply deﬁned as a pair of departments already considered in the
proposed system. However, gynecology is a bit more complex. Gynecology states that
4.GYN and 5.OBS must be neighbors, but that 5.OBS also must be the neighbor of the
excluded child and youth department NEO on the 3rd ﬂoor. The reasoning makes good
sense from a healthcare perspective, as 5.OBS is the maternity ward of the hospital and
NEO specializes in the medical care of ill or premature infants. Therefore, 4.GYN and
5.OBS will have their position ﬁxed at the very end of the department order in the 11th and
12th spot as neighbors to NEO. However, the reason for the sub-department characteristic
to not be enforced in the basis model, is that the NHN Team also ﬁnds it interesting to
investigate the possible advantages of splitting up sub-departments that do not require
their neighbor-ship to the same degree as 5.OBS and NEO. From a modeling perspective,
a pair of sub-departments are not perceived as one united department. Instead, they are
still two independent departments in the simheuristic, but are simply linked to each-other
in the swap processes. The beneﬁt of this is, that it allows the simheuristic to ﬂip the
internal order between a pair of sub-departments in for example a random-swap or in
a shuﬄe. The pseudo-code for the added sub-departments characteristic can be seen in
Appendix A.4.2.1. The development of the objective function for Model 2 is visualized

6.3
Model 3 - No 2-ﬂoor
Model 3 incorporates the system characteristic of not allowing any department to have
its start and end, earlier denoted as soft borders, across the two ﬂoors of the hospital.
Model 3 is a variation of Model 1 and does therefore not include the characteristic of
sub-departments from Model 2. Model 3 aims to keep departments on their own ﬂoor,
but allows, in the rare case of that ﬂoor being fully occupied, a department to allocate
patients on the other ﬂoor. To make the model more agile in ﬁnding valid solutions, a
helper-function is added that speciﬁcally checks if a department crosses the ﬂoors, deﬁned
as a border department. Essentially, the model checks that if any border department ap-
pears in a proposed solution, that its smallest ﬂoor side must not account for more than
10% of the beds belonging to the department. If a border department has more than
10% of its beds on its smallest ﬂoor side, the proposed solution is deemed as invalid and
the simheuristic will instead attempt another swap. However, if a border department has
less than 10% of its allocated beds on its smallest ﬂoor side, these beds will be removed
from the border department and instead be equally allocated among the departments be-
longing to that ﬂoor. Thereby, by giving away a maximum of 10% of the beds from a
border department, a proposed solution will become valid. With the implemented system
characteristic of no 2-ﬂoor departments, a clear cut between the ﬂoors will be made. The
pseudo-code for the added no 2-ﬂoor department characteristic can be seen in Appendix
A.4.3.1. The development of the objective function for Model 3 is visualized in Figure 6.8
and shows that the simheuristic ﬁnds its best department layout at around 60 iterations.

6.4
Model 4 - Hub Areas
Model 4 incorporates the system characteristic of having hub areas acting as natural soft
borders. Model 4 is a variation of Model 1 and does therefore not include the charac-
teristic of Model 3 and 4. The NHN Team has expressed that hub-areas in their current
NHN layout suggestion acts as natural borders between the departments. While the hub
areas does not have a directly functional eﬀect, it is still suggested that it will bring a
more clear sense of each department’s main operating areas. As stated in Chapter 2 there
are eight hub areas in total at NHN, four on each ﬂoor. However, as the two child and
youth departments take up one of the hub areas on the 3rd ﬂoor, the applied system only
considers seven of them. The idea is to avoid having departments with only a few beds on
any given side of a hub area. Therefore, a helper function has been developed to ensure
that each department has at least 10 beds on each side of a hub in order to be a valid
solution. The exact choice of 10 beds has been chosen together with the NHN Team to
be the appropriate amount. Similar to the denotation of border departments in Model
3, a department is in Model 4 deﬁned as a hub department if it is positioned across a
hub area. To make the simheuristic more agile in its department sizing, some additional
conditions has been added to the helper function. If a department has less than 10 beds
on each side of a hub area, and the number of beds on the smallest size of the hub area
accounts for less than 10% of the total number of beds for the given department, these will
be given to the neighbor department. If the number of beds on the smallest side accounts
for more than 10% but are fewer than 10 beds, the solution is not valid, and a new swap
will be initiated. The reallocating of beds from one department to another changes the
department sizes, but no more than what is assessed to be reasonable given the 10% limit.
The new department sizes also result in recalculating the department centers. However,
this constraint and modiﬁcation do not change the fact that all beds are still ﬂex beds.
Meaning that even though a department is either completely kept between two hub areas
or has at least 10 beds on each side, nothing stops a department from using beds across
multiple hub areas if found necessary. The pseudo-code for the added hub areas char-
acteristic can be seen in Appendix A.4.4.1. The development of the objective function
for Model 4 is visualized in Figure 6.11 and show that the simheuristic ﬁnds reasonable
solutions at around iteration 50 and 100, but ends up with a better solution in iteration 150.

of its beds to ﬁt it within a hub area, while all other instances are kept below 10%. The

Discussion and Outlook
This chapter aims to discuss and present the various ﬁndings of the study from a holistic
viewpoint. This includes clariﬁcation on the methodology choices, model deﬁning deci-
sions, and the inﬂuence of the granted input data of the case study. Lastly, reﬂections and
further relevant work of the study will be presented and embedded into the perspective of
the study as a whole.
7.1
Choice of Method
With NHN still in the development phase, many choices regarding the hospital’s general
processes and characteristics remain to be investigated. Therefore, the nature of this study
is prescriptive as the goal is to grant insight into how diﬀerent NHN layouts could aﬀect
the future hospital to be built. Given the problem of both identifying and measuring NHN
layouts, it was chosen to put a simheuristic framework at the core of the proposed DSS.
Fundamental for the developed simheuristic is its simulation module, and its metaheuristic
constructed with inspiration from well-known metaheuristics and search methodologies.
The metaheuristic was primarily created with the focal point of a local-search SA algo-
rithm. The metaheuristic also took inspiration from distinct characteristics of searching a
problem-solution space from the constructive LNS algorithm and TS. Gendreau & Potvin
(2010) discloses that taking primary outset in a construction heuristic such as LNS could
have resulted in diﬀerent search methods possibly reaching other regions of the solution
space. It would have been interesting to test out diﬀerent destroy and repair metaheuris-
tics to analyze whether better solutions could be achieved than primarily using neighbor
swaps. However, it was found from the experimental study that the proposed metaheuris-
tic managed to ﬁnd reasonable good solutions at around 200 iterations. 200 iterations were
speciﬁcally chosen for the study as it was found that the most signiﬁcant improvement of
the objective value was found within this space when testing on 400 iterations. Given the
natural time limitations of conducting this study, it is necessary to set time barriers for
how long each step of the preliminary parameter testing and experimental study can take.
However, were the models to be implemented in the frame of production runs, it would
have been interesting to let the model run for an exceptionally long time to observe the
searched solution space in hopes of better-performing solutions.
The proposed simheuristic framework of the study was constructed with the inspiration of
the general simheuristic scheme of Juan et al. (2015) with his assumption that deterministic

optimization problems generally identify reasonable solutions for stochastic optimization
problems. The proposed simheuristic model approaches this assumption by conducting
stochastic simulation with the full amount of replications when the simheuristic proposes
a promising solution. This approach diﬀers from Juan’s general framework, where the
stochastic simulation model is only run with very few replications within the simheuristic.
Then at its termination, a full number of replications is run on the best found solution.
Another approach could have been to remove the stochastic simulation module of the
simheuristic framework and instead implement it as an evaluation tool of only the best
found solution. This would most certainly grant the model the beneﬁt of faster running
times but at the cost of stochastic integrity when searching for reasonable solutions. It
could have been interesting to investigate the exact time diﬀerences between the various
approaches and assess their found solutions’ quality. This would have allowed for analyz-
ing the trade-oﬀof faster running times, thereby enabling more iterations to search a more
extensive solution space versus applying stochastic simulations on all promising solutions.
System Characteristics and Data input
The goal of the DSS and the stand-alone simulation DST was to facilitate prescriptive
insights for the NHN Team in their work of designing the hospital layout. The deﬁning
factor, that NHN is not an entirely pre-deﬁned system, brings challenges to constructing a
completely true model of how such a system would be in reality. Therefore, general model-
deﬁning assumptions about the system characteristics were included and elaborated when
deﬁning the DSS.
According to Law (2015) it is crucial to include subject-matter experts of the given case
to settle the level of detail modeled in the system.
The NHN Team is, in this case,
deﬁned as subject-matter experts and has, throughout the development of this study,
been granting insight into the expected system characteristics of NHN and veriﬁed the
included model assumptions.
However, typical simulation problems tend to ﬁnd roots
in existing systems from a given "as-is" perspective. This study analyses a non-existing
system and instead investigates how the implementation of diﬀerent system characteristics
inﬂuences the performance outcome. The ﬁndings of the experimental study have been
well-received by the NHN Team and will hopefully facilitate bracing discussions internally
on how the diﬀerent system characteristics interplay with one another. With that said,
real-world system characteristics have been omitted from the model, which could have
been beneﬁcial and pushed the model closer to a more realistic interpretation of NHN.
The complete removal of the child and youth departments NEO and PÆD resulted in
the hospital structure being modeled as one-dimensional, resulting in the removal of the
physical circular characteristic. This was done to simplify the modeling as a necessity of
this study’s time and resource limitations. However, neglecting the circular characteristics
entails that the modeled system behaves diﬀerently than the real NHN system is expected
to when up and running. The simpliﬁcation has, among other things, entailed that there
is only one path from the ﬁrst bed on the 2nd ﬂoor to the last bed on the 3rd ﬂoor, even

==PAGE:93==

though these two beds, in reality, could be placed nearly right above and below each other.
Another fundamental assumption of the model is that the distance between any neigh-
boring beds is the same. This assumption omits the micro details of the system layout,
given that beds, depending on their location on structure, will have a greater distance to
one another. A more realistic alternative to this assumption would have been to develop
a detailed distance matrix denouncing the actual distance between any two beds of NHN.
The distance should not necessarily be measured in physical distances but could instead
be measured as the time it takes for human resources to transport themselves from one
bed to another. This approach would also enable more realistic crossing from one ﬂoor
to the other. In addition, transportation methods such as elevators located across the
hospital would be possible to incorporate into the model.
In healthcare systems with diﬀerent specialties, it is not unusual that the importance of
diﬀerent types of patients varies to some extent. In the proposed model, any given pa-
tient is deﬁned as equally important. Deﬁning importance weightings for each patient
type could have eased comparing departments of diﬀerent sizes. In cases where a small
department with critical patients, importance weights could have equalized some of the
imbalance of large departments being swapped more often versus small departments when
swapping based on distance and dispersion. Another decision choice is the application of
speciﬁc penalties for patients crossing various hub areas, departments, and ﬂoors. The
dispersion performance measure was developed to caption some of this behavior - but the
addition of appropriate penalties could have resulted in more realistic performance mea-
sures. While these penalties were not applied, the patient allocation rules were instead
captivated by the pre-deﬁned constraints of every model. The procedure of granting more
detailed importance’s to the system processes branches the scope problem in the direction
of more complex decision support and strategic assessment. This would require further
insights into each distinct hospital specialty.
The input data for the developed model has played an essential role in the study, as it is
this data that reﬂects the number of arriving patients and their LOS. While it is assessed
that the data received from the NHN Team was of adequate quality to conduct this study,
there were still some focal discrepancies from reality. Firstly, the data explains the patient
ﬂows of an already existing system, not necessarily how the ﬂow will be at NHN. With
the data being based on NOH’s existing setup, it can only be assumed that the same data
tendencies apply to NHN. This is one of the downsides of simulating a non-existing system,
as some of the potential reality and interplay of the data may not be represented in the
old system to be replaced. However, given the prescriptive goal of this study, proper data
transformations were conducted to bring down the NOH data to a level that is applicable
for the size of NHN.
Unfortunately, due to Covid-19 aﬀecting all healthcare systems in the year 2020 and
thereby skewing the respective data, it was chosen to use the NOH data from 2019. Given
the NHN Team’s statement that the general LOS times are continually improving with

With these system assumptions and the overall integrity of the data taken into considera-
tion, it is assessed that the model, given more detailed characteristics and granular data,
could have been modeled closer to reality. However, the presented DSS and simulation
DST ﬁndings are still found highly relevant for the problem case at hand. Since the system
characteristics are not set in stone for NHN, the output of the DSS should not be consid-
ered as a true reﬂection of reality but instead be perceived as strategic insight into how the
general system characteristics and departments interplay with one another. The proposed
simheuristic framework was speciﬁcally designed and developed for the NHN system, and
the process of doing so was deemed to enlighten a thorough overview of the system details.
The results of the experimental study also proved to present clear patterns of the system
behavior and how they should be perceived when deciding on a NHN department layout.
7.2
Further Work and Reﬂections
As mentioned above, due to the natural time limitations of this study, multiple system
assumptions had to be incorporated into the model development. The natural next steps
of this study would be to expand on these model assumptions by including more accurate
system characteristics, such as the circular layout of NHN, and improve the ones included,
such as a more detailed distance matrix. Also, further decision assessment methodolo-
gies could add further credibility to the overall model by assigning more comprehensive
importance weights across the NHN system. It would also be relevant to look into other
scenarios to investigate more system performances for further work. For example, it would
be interesting to undergo a more precise stress-test of the system, increasing the number
of patients arriving at speciﬁc times during the year. Or, examine the interplay of the
various departments in shorter time frames such as speciﬁc months, weeks, or even days.
It was found in the experimental study that the most restricted model, Model 5, did not
manage to reach an objective value better than the NHN Team’s initial layout. It was
observed from the preliminary model study that the most signiﬁcant improvement of the
objective happened within the ﬁrst 200 iterations. For further work, however, it would be
interesting to let the models run for as long as reasonably possible to enable the search
and investigation of other areas of the solution space. Another take on Model 5’s inferior
solution could be explained by its struggle of sizing the departments appropriately across
the hub areas and the two ﬂoors, given its set of pre-deﬁned rules. In this sense, the
proposed approach of initially sizing the departments given their number of patients and

their LOS to then trade beds between departments given their ordering may be identiﬁed
as naive. For further work, it would be interesting to design a more complex department
sizing process. Here, it would be relevant to expand on the optimization problem of or-
dering and swapping departments by adding a process that measures the performance of
various department resizings for each proposed solution.
The focus of this study has been on the strategic planning of the NHN layout and its
various system characteristics’ inﬂuence on this. However, it is also interesting to reﬂect
on how the study could have unfolded if the scope of the problem had been diﬀerent.
Given the right data, expanding the problem into a system scheduling problem could be
interesting. Hall (2012) states the importance of utilizing resources in accordance to needs
that can be anticipated and making adjustments as these needs change. Since the 2nd
and 3rd ﬂoors of NHN do not include emergency departments, it can be anticipated which
types of patients arrive in which time frames given the right data. Hence, another take
on the study could be to apply forecasting methods to predict how patients should be
allocated given the expectation of which patients are to follow in upcoming time windows.
An example of how this could prove eﬀective in the presented model is that a patient with
a short LOS could be purposely allocated further away from the department center to
make room for a later arriving patient with a longer expected LOS.
Another relevant perspective of the study could have been to examine the operational
level of NHN by focusing on the patient ﬂows with detailed interrelationships between the
various human resources and processes. While very detailed operational insights would
be complex to execute on the whole NHN system at once, it would instead be relevant to
examine the interplay of sub-systems. This could be a single department considering as
close to real-life processes as possible or even a few neighboring departments that share
their ﬂex-beds and their human resources. Similar to this study, Discrete Event Simulation
(DES) would be applicable for such a scope but would necessitate more micro-level details.
Agent-based simulation (ABS), a variant of DES, could also deem relevant if the focus
should be on the behavior of each individual Agent in the system. The core idea of ABS
is to enable the agents to have the capability to evaluate a given situation in the system
and react accordingly through autonomous decision-making and a set of pre-deﬁned rules.
The usage of ABS could especially be useful if the focus of the study were to investigate
the interplay of how and which human resources are helping other departments if they are
in need. These problems, however, do not share the same yearly time frame as the one
conducted in this study. Micro-level DES and ABS would require smaller time windows
given on a weekly or even daily level. Also, operational performance measures would not
be able to highlight the eﬀect of signiﬁcant system characteristics as the proposed DSS.
Therefore, already pre-deﬁned layouts would have to be chosen, which for example, could
be designed with inspiration from the ﬁndings of this study. For both the implementation
of DES and ABS, it could be relevant to look at using a more visual simulation software
than Julia. By animating the simulation behaviors on these micro-level systems, it would
be possible to analyze the detailed states of the system to a greater extent

Conclusions
The latest action to accommodate the demand for modern and more eﬃcient hospitals is
the construction of so-called Super Hospitals. However, constructing a new hospital that
gathers specialties previously spread out has a complexity to it. For decision-makers to
make more informed strategic decisions on how specialties should be placed in relation
to each other, they need prescriptive insights. The necessity for prescriptive insights at
NHN has led this study to propose a DSS framework. With its foundation in a simheuris-
tic framework, the DSS combines characteristics from both metaheuristic and simulation
methodology to investigate two distinct problems: The sizing and ordering of departments
and the prescriptive insights of how potential layouts would perform. The DSS framework
was developed to grant insights into the future NHN system by analyzing how relevant
system characteristics and potential department layouts would aﬀect each other.
The
study was conducted in collaboration with the NHN Team, which provided historical data
for patient ﬂows from 2019 based on the existing NOH hospitals. The provided data made
it possible to generate appropriate patient ﬂows with stochastic probability distributions.
Furthermore, the NHN Team contributed with valuable knowledge sharing regarding the
processes and characteristics of NHN and its departments. In collaboration with the NHN
Team, the most essential characteristics of NHN were identiﬁed to be the relation between
sub-departments, the physical circumstances between the two ﬂoors, and how hub areas
can be used as natural soft borders. Through the DSS, the provided data and the men-
tioned characteristics were tested as constraints in multiple models to investigate how each
characteristic inﬂuenced the system’s performance. Five models were developed, includ-
ing a version that combined all constraints. For each model, two "what-if" scenarios were
deﬁned based on the NHN Team indications of plausible future trends in healthcare sys-
tems. Several similarities of neighboring departments were identiﬁed through an extensive
experimental study of the model variations. The study also made it possible to directly
compare the inﬂuence of the relevant system characteristics, which concluded that placing
sub-departments as neighbors aﬀected the system most negatively. In contrast, the char-
acteristic of hub-areas aﬀected it the least. Given the investigated "what-if" scenarios, it
was also found that if NHN could only reduce either patient arrivals or LOS, the former
had a more signiﬁcant eﬀect on the system performance. While it is assessed that the
DSS managed to facilitate many relevant prescriptive insights, its weaknesses were also
identiﬁed. The DSS’s process of sizing the departments would have beneﬁted from being
more dynamic, and with the correct data, some of the explained model assumptions could
have been depicted closer to reality. These weaknesses relate directly to the further work

of the study, where most noticeably, a more complex version of the department sizing pro-
cess and a detailed distance-matrix of NHN’s beds could reinforce the DSS. Reﬂecting on

levels could bring some nuances to future case problems of NHN. This study has thus,
managed to propose an applicable DSS that grants relevant prescriptive insights from a
strategic perspective to the NHN system.
