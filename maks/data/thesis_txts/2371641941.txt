ABSTRACT 
This master thesis is a part of the scientific project that named as “Studies of 
parameters having influence on aerodynamic barriers used in pharmaceutical 
facilities”. 
The master project had two main parts; the first evaluated the pressurization design of 
pharmaceutical rooms and the second investigated the influences of contaminant 
migration between neighbouring rooms in dynamic conditions associated with door 
swing. 
The first part of the study studied the required safety differential pressure between a 
cleanroom and an airlock, in regard to evaluate the minimum differential pressure to 
ensure that no contamination transport occur between the rooms. The limitation of the 
aerodynamic barriers at different differential pressures between a simulated 
cleanroom and an airlock was determined in situation of a closed door. This study 
investigated the threshold of pressure difference necessary to decrease migration of 
tracer gas from less clean space to a clean space. The results indicated that a +15.0 
Pa differential pressure should be used between clean spaces and adjacent 
anterooms. 
The second part of the study analysed the effect of a person entering into a cleanroom 
on transport of particles from the airlock to the cleanroom. When the door is opened 
the differential pressure disappear and a product or person in a cleanroom can be 
exposed to contamination transported from the adjacent room to the clean room or 
brought into the cleanroom due to the air movement generated by the moving 
personnel. This was studied with particle measurements. The results were compared 
with the results from experiments with a tracer gas in order to investigate whether the 
tracer gas can be used for replacement of particles when such studies are performed. 
A significantly lower value of particle transport was observed when the time of the door 
opening was prolonged. The transport of particles was again reduced when higher air 
velocities applied as 0.224 m/s.

1. INTRODUCTION 
1.1. Definition of Cleanrooms 
The buildings of these days have been designed carefully for the proper uses 
and needs of the residents. Buildings for residents can control the temperature, 
humidity, sound, lighting, and shading and besides these to be environmentally 
friendly. Cleanrooms comply with these requirements, though its foremost purpose is 
to create the adequate medium for production instead of comforting the peoples. In 
order to achieve this, particles and bacteria dispersed from personnel and machinery 
has to be reduced dramatically within the premises in production lines of 
pharmaceutical buildings. ISO standard 11644-1 defines clean rooms as: 
“A room within the number concentration of airborne particles is controlled and 
classified, and which is designed, constructed and operated in a manner to control the 
introduction, generation and retention of particles inside the room.” [ISO 14644-1 
2015] 
Reduction and control of particles can be ensured by increasing the quantity and 
quality of the supplied air, securing the rooms from the dirty airflows from adjacent 
rooms, usage of appropriate building materials that do not generate particles and 
reduce the dispersion of particles and micro-organisms generated by personnel or 
transported due to their movement between the airlock and cleanroom [Ljungqvist & 
Reinmuller 1996]. 
1.2. History of Cleanroom 
Cleanroom design roots back for more than a century when the need for infection 
control in hospitals and clean environment for industrial manufacturing raised 
expressively. Lord Lister significantly reduced infection in his operating room at the 
Royal Infirmary, Glasgow by use of an aseptic solution. As it can be seen from Figure 
1, the first attempt was to neutralize the contaminants in air [Whyte 2001]. 
==PAGE:13==

The inventor of the modern cleanroom concept (patent) was Willis Whitfield in 1960, 
when he created the initial plans for a unidirectional flow type of chamber as shown on 
Figure 2 and Figure 3. He realized that constant airflow could have a protector effect 
while working with producing precocious instrumentations. The floor was raised in 
order to lead the exhaust airflow out of the chamber and the supply air intake was 
placed on the wall in front of the workers next to their hands where they worked on the 
products [Whyte 2001].

The evolution of the clean room technology evolved in the recent years and 
development still on-going. Instruments turned to be more precise and building 
materials quality also improved. Until the demand of produced high quality 
pharmaceuticals and electronics, the clean room technology will continuously increase 
[Buie 2011]. 
1.3. Development of Standards 
Cleanroom standard came into exist for the needs of the expanding cleanroom 
industry. The first standard written for cleanrooms by the American Air force in 1960s 
and was known as Technical Manual 00-25-203. The most influential standard on the 
design and operation of cleanrooms was known as Federal Standard 209 (1963). 
From 1963 to 2001 the FS209 was the only document providing regulation on the 
subject of cleanrooms, thus it became widely used. Its methodology was based on the 
classification of the airborne particulate cleanliness. Because of the large number of 
cleanroom standards produced by individual countries it was necessary to have one 
worldwide classification system. The International Organization for Standards (ISO) 
developed a series of cleanroom standards that covered the themes as design, testing 
operation and bio contamination. In 1999, ISO 14644-1 ‘Classification of Air 
Cleanliness’ was published. In 2001 the international standard ISO 14644 replaced the 
FS209 standard and became the primary used until this time [Whyte 2001].

The airborne conditions in a cleanroom are determined by the occupancy. Occupancy 
is the status of the cleanrooms in ISO 14644-1: 
• As built where the installation is complete with all the services, without 
production equipment, materials or personnel 
• At-rest where the installations is complete with equipment installed and 
operating but without any personnel present 
• Operational where the condition of the instalment is functioning and personnel 
also present and working 
1.4. Contamination 
Pharmaceutical workers generate primarily contaminants in rooms, due to their 
activity. Contaminants are also present in the supplied outside air for ventilation of 
clean rooms. The five categories of pollution sources are: 
1. Occupants who generate skin flakes, oils, hair and cloth fibres 
==PAGE:16==
 
13 
13 
2. External air 
3. Buildings itself as their surface finishers of the construction  
4. Process materials produced during working hours 
5. Equipment used during the cleanroom processes 
People working in cleanroom facilities emit approximately 100 particles per minute 
with particle size of 0.3 μm or greater. This makes them the source of the highest risks 
of contamination and also the hardest ones to be cleaned compared to other sources, 
i.e. facilities and equipment. 
Effective methods for reduction of airborne particles are protective clothing, 
pressurization, air curtains, filters, increased air change rate and complex facility 
design. 
1.5. Description of Particles 
It is essential to give some indication of the size of particles used in cleanroom 
standards. The unit of measurement is a micrometre and one micrometre (1 μm) is 
one millionth of a metre. Figure 4 shows a drawing comparing particle sizes. 
 
Figure 4 The relative sizes of particles [Treviranus 2011] 
There are three types of particles: inert organic, viable organic and inert inorganic. The 
sources of particles are very wide. It could be generated by rubbing one material on 
another, cutting a piece of wood, or simply by human skin activities as it is shown on 
Figure 5. It is commonly known that approximately 1.000.000 particles larger than 0.5 
μm are present in a standard cubic foot of indoor air depending on clothing and body 
activity. Additionally for that, the process of exhaling air of humans produces several 
thousand particles, especially when smoking [Kochevar 2006]. 
==PAGE:17==
 
14 
14 
 
Figure 5 The particle generation by person per minutes [Kochevar 2006] 
The size of particles is relevant in manufacturing where the specific particle sizes may 
cause damage depending on the cleaning process, however the cleaning and filtration 
of air are also important factors. In the cleaning processes filters are used to remove 
most of the particulate contamination suspending in air. That is why knowing the 
relevant particle size allows having the correct type of filter with the correct pore size to 
remove the contamination and increase productivity [Kochevar 2006].  
The types of movement of particles are the following: by ballistic forces, by diffusing 
into liquid or gas, by currents, by thermal variation and Brownian motion. [Kochevar 
2006]. 
In order to prevent the room air from pollution, the directed air should be cleaned from 
contaminants by various types of air filters. The following Figure 6 shows a clear 
overview on the air filters by classes.

1.6. Design of cleanrooms 
A well-designed ventilation system is essential when it comes to the protection of 
indoor environment against outdoor weather circumstances. Control of hazardous 
contaminants exchange between cleanrooms and anterooms is vital. In providing 
building services, it became common strategy to control contaminants transport in 
cleanroom facilities by airflow direction and room pressurization [Whyte 2001].  
Regarding to the generated airflow there are three types of cleanrooms: 
• Unidirectional flow [Laminar] 
• Non-unidirectional flow [Turbulent] and  
• Mixture of these two [Whyte 2001].

1.7. Operational conditions 
In maintaining cleanrooms and clean zones, typically two types of conditions can be 
expected; these are the steady state and dynamic conditions. These are described in 
the following: 
1.7.1. Steady State conditions 
When the doors are closed between the connected rooms, a quasi-steady airflow 
direction state appears by the balance of supplied air and exhausted air. The air’s 
direction of flow is originated from the cleanroom to the airlock’s direction. As the 
balance shifts to the negative or positive scope by supplying less airflow or more 
airflow than the exhausted airflow, negative or positive pressurizations can be 
achieved. Positive pressurization showed on Figure 10 as an example. 
 
Figure 10 Illustration of steady state condition. The ventilated air flows from the diffuser of the cleanroom 
through under the closed door into the airlock where it is exhausted. 
It is commonly known that, when positive pressure is maintained the process and 
product is secured, while negative pressure is kept when the surroundings should be 
protected from the product or polluter. Thus, room pressurization is widely used in 
chemical or biological laboratories, pharmaceutical clean rooms, and isolation rooms 
of hospitals, nuclear plants and space industry. The key factor is to provide the 
adequate airflow between adjacent rooms, which determines the purpose of negative 
or positive pressure in threatened area. This method, called pressurization, works as 
an aerodynamic barrier and ensure that the airflow from an area of high cleanliness 
will flow to an area of lower cleanliness classification [ASHRAE 2011]. 
==PAGE:26==
 
23 
23 
1.7.2. Dynamic conditions 
In contrast with the steady state conditions (when the direction of the airflow is 
constant), it is difficult to entirely prevent incoming to the clean room contamination in 
the case of dynamic situations. When the door between the rooms opens up a flush 
effect occurs which has an impact on transport of contaminants between the rooms. 
This is called as dynamic condition. A very significant factor, which can contribute to 
contamination transferring between the rooms despite pressure difference, is the door 
function [VTT Symposium 2010]. 
 
Figure 11 Illustration of the dynamic condition. The ventilated air flows from the diffuser of the cleanroom 
through the door into the airlock where it is exhausted. Counter flows from the airlock’s direction appear. 
When a door opens between the two neighbouring rooms, the pressure difference that 
was kept in either positive or negative direction becomes balanced and immediately 
after opening causes pressure equilibration in them, shown on Figure 11. In the case 
of an open door the leakage area is increased drastically. The problem of the door 
opening is more severe when the opening is more frequent [Hendinger & Chludzinska 
& Zietek 2016]. 
1.7.3. Movement of personnel 
While only a couple of previous studies [Gustavsson 2010, Sansone & Keimig 1987, 
Tang & Nicolle & Pantelic & Klettner & Kalliomaki 2013] dedicated on the influences of 
transport of contaminants from airlock to cleanroom combined with motion of the door 
and manikin, this study examines how artificially generated airborne particles and 
automatic door motion with manikin motion can influence the transport of 
contaminants at different airflow rates.

4.5. Conclusion 
Results indicated that the ventilation system of pressure differential of +15.0 Pa in the

concentrations of contaminant transport and the pressure differential. The threshold of 
the differential pressure is at +15.0 Pa. It was also observed that the different pressure 
levels as aerodynamic barriers do reduce the migration of contaminants significantly. 
Comparing against the current standards in pressurization, the results confirmed the 
requirements and changes are not needed.

• Penetration of contaminants can be controlled by appropriate pressurization 
and volumetric airflow 
• The threshold of the differential pressure was +15.0 Pa where the decrease in 
concentration was 10 %

5. PART III. IMPACT OF WALKING PERSON [DYNAMIC 
CONDITIONS] 
There have been many studies evaluating the performance of clean rooms with regard 
to the pressure differential across the doors when it is closed [Shaw & Whyte 1974, 
Linden & Simpon 1985, Kiel & Wilson 1989, Whyte 1991, Ziemer & Taschner 2000]. 
However, there have been relatively less studies evaluating how door-opening 
motions and pharmacy worker’s passing through the door can affect the performance 
of production in clean rooms [Gustavsson 2010]. 
5.1. Objectives 
This part of the study aims to investigate the effects of door-opening motion on 
transport of contaminants from the airlock to the cleanroom considering: variety of 
different airflows; door opening with and without passage of a simulated person; with 
slow and fast door opening speed. The most critical point is the time when the door 
opens up between a cleanroom and its adjacent anteroom creating a transition from 
steady state condition to dynamic condition. (Steady state condition and dynamic 
conditions were discussed in 1.7.1 and 1.7.2) 
The main objective of these experimental measurements is to help understand how 
the different airflows and real-life motions affect the airborne particle transportation in 
real time in critical areas at dynamic conditions.  
Finally, the question of which kind of tracking of the particles is more efficient and 
supposed to be used, either the commonly used tracer gas technique or particle 
encounters.  
5.2. Methodology 
Particle contamination through velocities of door-opening and manikin movement was 
monitored in each scenario in full-scale motion and in air. Only isothermal conditions 
were examined with a metal electromechanical door. This was done because of the 
effect of door-opening motions on the movements of the generated particles that were 
much more interesting to capture via different airflows. 
The aerodynamic barrier can create an effective way in reducing the risks and 
intensity of the penetrating contaminated air. The research measured the airborne 
particles that passed through the cross-section of door while aerodynamic barriers 
claimed to protect the critical areas.

behaviour than tracer gas measurements. Consider that the time difference in sensing 
is also significant as for tracer gas is 40-50 sec interval and for particle measurement 
is 6 sec. 
5.5. Conclusion 
The most important implication from this study is that slow door opening speed design 
causes the less airborne particles to enter into critical areas. Moreover, the 0.224 m/s 
air velocity successfully protected the cleanroom from salt particles to invade in the 
range of 0.5 µm. The particle measurement served with much finer and dense results 
than the tracer gas measurements and in case of dynamic scenarios the particle 
measurement should be used. The recommendation from the author in regard to the 
motion of the manikin is that should be simulated with either a real person or with a 
more advanced manikin. 
The conclusions from this part of the study are the following: 
• Slow door opening motion (44 sec) can reduce particle contamination 
ominously. 
• Fast door opening motion (28 sec) caused drastically higher contamination to 
invade even with compared with the personnel movement. 
• Slow door opening motion combined with 0.224 m/s air velocity or higher can 
form an effective aerodynamic barrier in dynamic situations. 
• Slow motion of the manikin further reduced the migration of pollutants.
