Abstract
This project revolves around the preparation of Ni-Mo electrocatalysts for hydrogen evolu-
tion using advanced alkaline electrolysis. The electrodes were prepared with Ni-foam as
support using the SILAR method, electrodeposition and a hydrothermal method. The pre-
pared electrodes were characterized structurally using SEM, XPS, XRD and TGA. They
were also examined using electrochemical tests.
Using the SILAR method, a homogeneous coating was not obtained and the deposited
material was also observed to scale off during the electrochemical tests. Therefore, the
SILAR method was quickly dismissed. Electrodeposition was carried out at 100, 200
and 250 mA/cm2 resulting in a better coating of cauliflower-like Ni-Mo structures with
Ni/Mo ≃5. Significant Mo leaching was observed for the electrodeposited electrodes
during the electrochemical tests, which combined with their electrochemical performance
meant this method was not examined further.
The hydrothermal method proved to be the most promising with overpotentials in the range
of 70-80 mV at 100 mA/cm2 (T = RT, [KOH] = 30 wt%.). A homogeneous coating of thin
fibers with Ni/Mo = 1.0-1.4 was obtained with this method resulting in the highest surface
area of the three examined preparation methods. This method was therefore examined in
more detail by studying the influence of reaction temperature and annealing temperature.
Moreover, the long term stability of a hydrothermally prepared electrode was also proven
in a 250 h full cell test after which the coating was still observed albeit sintering and Mo
leaching had occurred (Ni/Mo = 3.8).

Resumé
Omdrejningspunktet for dette projekt er fremstillingen af Ni-Mo elektrokatalysatorer til ud-
vikling af hydrogen vha. avanceret alkalisk elektrolyse. Elektroderne blev fremstillet med
Ni-skum som support med henholdsvis SILAR metoden, galvanisering og en hydroter-
misk metode. De fysiske egenskaber for de fremstillede elektroder blev karakteriseret
med SEM, XPS, XRD og TGA. Derudover blev de elektrokemiske egenskaber også un-
dersøgt gennem en række test.
Det var ikke muligt at producere en homogen coating med SILAR metoden, ligesom flager
af det aflejrede materiale forsvandt under de elektrokemiske test. Derfor blev SILAR
metoden hurtigt droppet. Galvanisering af elektroderne blev foretaget ved 100, 200 og
250 mA/cm2 resulterende i en bedre coating med blomkålslignende Ni-Mo strukturer med
Ni/Mo ≃5. Der blev observeret en væsentlig udvaskning af Mo i forbindelse med de elek-
trokemiske test for de galvaniserede elektroder. Dette faktum, sammenholdt med deres
elektrokemiske ydeevne, gjorde at galvanisering ikke blev undersøgt mere detaljeret.
Den hydrotermiske metode viste sig at være den mest lovende fremstillingsmetode med
overpotentialer i omegnen af 70-80 mV ved en strømtæthed på 100 mA/cm2 (T = RT,
[KOH] = 30 wt%.). En homogen coating bestående af tynde fibre med Ni/Mo = 1.0-1.4
blev fremstillet med denne metode, hvilket resulterede i det største overfladeareal ob-
serveret for de tre forskellige fremstillingsmetoder. Denne metode blev derfor undersøgt
nærmere ved at variere reaktionstemperaturen samt brændingstemperaturen. Desuden
blev stabiliteten af en hydrotermisk fremstillet elektrode undersøgt i en celle gennem 250
timer, hvorefter en homogen coating stadig var til stede om end sintring og udvaskning af
Mo havde indtræffet (Ni/Mo = 3.8).

Introduction
»Electric power is everywhere present in unlimited quantities and can drive the world’s
machinery without the need of coal, oil, gas or any other of the common fuels.«
- Nikola Tesla (1856-1943) [1]
Nikola Tesla most likely could not predict the future. Nonetheless, his quote remains ever
more relevant with the fossil resources running out and the search for sustainable energy
solutions being one of the most discussed topics of the present. This introduction seeks to
present a brief historical review of water electrolysis and explain why this old technology
might help us in the search for answers to some of the energy challenges the world is
currently facing.
1.1
A Brief History of Water Electrolysis
The history of water electrolysis originates in 1789, when the two Dutchmen Adriaan Paets
van Troostwijk and Johan Rudolph Deiman used two gold electrodes connected to an
electrostatic generator to split water by immersing the two electrodes in water [2]. One
decade later, Alessandro Volta created a voltaic pile (the first electric battery), which con-
tinuously supplied electric current to an electric circuit. This technology was exploited
already a year later in 1800 by the two English scientists William Nicholson and Anthony
Carlisle for electrolytic splitting of water [3]. A discovery which was also made indepen-
dently by German scientist Johann Wilhelm Ritter [4]. The scientific field of water elec-
trolysis was born, but it was not relevant on an industrial scale until the Russian engineer
Dmitry Lachinov developed a method for the industrial synthesis of hydrogen via water
electrolysis in 1888. By 1902, more than 400 industrial electrolyzers were in operation
worldwide [2]. The first electrolyzers utilized aqueous alkaline solutions as their elec-
trolyte, a technology still existing today and the focal point of this thesis. Other technolo-
gies have also been developed as will be addressed later.
The development continued in the beginning of the 20th century, mostly in Norway and
Canada where cheap hydroelectricity could be exploited for electrolysis. However, the
rapid development of water electrolysis technologies ceased due to the availability of low-
cost hydrocarbons. Hence, hydrogen could be produced by coal gasification and natural
gas reforming at a lower cost and therefore the development of electrolyzers stagnated.
It was not until the oil crisis in 1973 that water electrolysis again became relevant as the
attitude around hydrogen changed. Hydrogen was now considered an energy carrier of
the future and one of the keys to solve the problem of renewable energy supply [3]. This
problem is still relevant today most notably illustrated by the increased focus on Power-
to-X technologies. Undoubtedly, the world still faces numerous energy challenges as will
be reviewed in the following section.

Energy Challenges
The industrial revolution began on a small scale in 1750 and has kept raging up until
modern day. Since 1750 the global use of hydrocarbons for fuel has increased nearly 800-
fold. Fossil hydrocarbons are interesting because they have a very high energy density,
which can be exploited to do work. Some might claim that we live in an information age
today, but the truth is that we still live in a petroleum age. Transportation, food production,
plastics, most of our jobs and leisure, much of our electricity, and all of our electronic
devices are dependent upon gaseous and especially liquid petroleum [5]. In Figure 1.1,
the global fossil fuel consumption over time is illustrated distributed between coal, oil and
gas. The consumption of coal seems to have stagnated, but that is mainly because the
graphs only cover the global consumption. The coal production and consumption has
increased significantly in China the last couple of decades, but is relatively stable in a
global perspective [6].

run out in approximately 50 years [6]. The continued increase of fossil fuel consumption

Project Objectives and Structure
The objective of this thesis is to evaluate the performance of nanostructured electrodes
for hydrogen evolution to be used in zero-gap electrolysis cells. The electrodes will be
prepared during the project with commercial Ni-foam as the support. The preparation
of Ni-based electrodes is primarely achieved with wet-chemical techniques on lab-scale.
The electrodes are then tested electrochemically using KOH as the electrolyte to evaluate
their performance for producing hydrogen. The prepared electrodes are tested using a
three-electrode setup as well as a full electrochemical cell. Lastly, different characteriza-
tion techniques such as scanning electron microscopy and X-ray diffraction are utilized
to determine the morphology and structure of the developed electrodes. The approved
project plan is available in Appendix B.

2.1
What is Water Electrolysis?
The definition of electrolysis from the Cambridge Academic Content dictionary is: »the
use of an electric current to cause chemical change in a liquid« [13]. In this project that
liquid is water and the chemical change caused by the electric current is the splitting of
liquid water according to the following reaction:

The energy added to the system in the form of an electrical current is then converted to
storable energy in the form of hydrogen. Oxygen is also produced, but since oxygen is

the charge carriers in the electrolyte. The mobile ions working as the charge carrier are

2.2
Technologies
There are three main water electrolysis technologies being developed: alkaline electrol-
ysis, polymer electrolyte membrane (PEM) electrolysis and solid oxide electrolysis cells
(SOEC). The main characteristics for these three different technologies will be introduced
in the following.
2.2.1
Alkaline Electrolysis
Alkaline electrolysis is the oldest and most mature technology of the three mentioned
technologies. Besides being the most mature technology, alkaline electrolysis also has
the advantage of being capable of using affordable electrode materials such as nickel
due to the alkaline conditions. As the name suggests, an alkaline solution is used as the
electrolyte. Typically, the electrolyte is 20-30 % NaOH or KOH, which are also two afford-
able electrolytes [18]. The operating temperature is usually in the interval 60-80 °C [19].
The reactions taking place at the electrodes have already been introduced in Reaction
(2.3) and Reaction (2.5). These reactions are balanced in OH – and therefore only water
needs to be added during operation to maintain the electrolyte concentration and feed the
reaction [19]. An illustration of the typical features of an alkaline electrolysis cell is given
Figure 2.2.
Figure 2.2: Schematic diagram of a standard alkaline electrolysis cell. Note that this is not con-
sistent with the zero-gap configuration [19].
The configuration shown in Figure 2.2 is the typical configuration of an alkaline electroly-
sis cell, where the two electrodes are separated by an OH – -conducting diaphragm. The
diaphragm also serves the purpose of keeping the evolving H2 and O2 separated to avoid
recombination of these two gaseous compounds. It is seen in Figure 2.2 that the bub-
bles are actually forming between the electrodes and the separator. This can lead to
some problems, as the gas bubbles will start to form a continuous gas film as the current

operating current densities are typically limited to a few 100 mA/cm2 [19]. Moreover, the
permeation of gas products makes hydrogen and oxygen mix and recombine. It is there-

reduction of the Faradaic efficiency [20]. However, a potential solution to this type of prob-
lem is the zero-gap configuration, where the two porous electrodes are pressed against
the separator (in a setup resembling the PEM setup illustrated in Figure 2.4). Here, the

Asbestos was used for many years as the diaphragm, but due to the toxicity of asbestos,
new materials have been developed [19]. Polymer-based composite materials in particu-
lar have undergone a lot of development as a replacement for asbestos with Zirfon being
the most prominent diaphragm. Zirfon is a composite of zirconia and polysulfone, which
is highly stable in concentrated KOH solutions even at elevated temperatures [20]. This is
important as the diaphragm is filled with the electrolyte to ensure conductivity. This leads
to another important point of discussion for alkaline electrolysis, namely the choice of
electrolyte. Commonly, concentrated KOH or NaOH is being used as the electrolyte. But
what is the optimal electrolyte and why does it have to be concentrated? This is a relevant
question to discuss and the simple answer to why it has to be concentrated is to optimize
the conductivity of the electrolyte. As can be seen in Figure 2.3, the specific electrolyte
conductivities as calculated by Brauns et al. [8] suggest that optimum concentration of
KOH is approximately 30 % and that it generally is a better electrolyte than NaOH.

2.2.2
PEM Electrolysis
Polymer Electrolyte Membrane (PEM) electrolysis was introduced in the 1960s by General
Electric. PEM water electrolysis contains an anode and a cathode separated by a polymer
electrolyte membrane (hence the name). They operate at 60-80 °C, which is similar to
the operation temperature of alkaline electrolysis [20]. The two electrodes are pressed
against a proton-conducting polymer electrolyte resulting in what is known as a membrane
electrode assembly (MEA). As PEM electrolysis is based on the conduction of protons,
the reactions taking place at the electrodes are identical to the acidic water electrolysis
reactions presented in Reaction (2.2) and Reaction (2.4). A schematic overview of a
typical PEM electrolysis cell is given in Figure 2.4. Here, it can also be seen that albeit
having the acidic reaction scheme, the MEA is immersed in pure water as the protons are
produced at the anode and conducted through the membrane to the cathode. Therefore,
acid is not needed, but instead pure water is fed at the anode side. Although protons
remain inside the membrane, the acidy is very high (similar to that of a 1 M sulfuric acid
solution). Therefore, only noble metal catalysts can sustain such acidity required at both
the anode and the cathode [19].
Figure 2.4: Schematic diagram of a PEM electrolysis cell. This illustration resembles the zero-gap
alkaline electrolysis setup, where gases are evolved at the back side of the electrodes, but with a
different electrolyte [19].

One of the advantages of PEM electrolysis is the possibility to run at high pressures (pres-
sure can be elevated to about 100 bar). This makes it suitable for up-scaling and com-
pressing hydrogen for transport. However, the downside of running at such high pres-
sures is that the membrane needs to be thick, which increases the conductive resistance,
thereby also increasing the Ohmic losses. Moreover, the catalysts must be able to sus-
tain both the high pressure and the acidic conditions created by the formation of protons.
Hence, platinum is the widely used catalyst for the HER and IrO2 is used at the anode for
the OER [20]. These noble metals unquestionably increase the capital expenses of PEM
electrolysis.
The polymer electrolyte membrane is commonly composed of Nafion, which is a perflu-
orosulfonic acid-type membrane capable of conducting protons. The proton conductivity
of Nafion even allows for high current density operation, as it is capable of achieving cur-
rent densities beyond 2 A/cm2 [20]. This possibility of operating the cells at high current
density is another of the advantages of PEM. Moreover, high efficiencies can be obtained
due to the thin zero-gap configuration, which minimizes the Ohmic losses (depending on
the applied pressure cf. the discussion above). This is also why the zero-gap technology
for alkaline electrolysis is promising. Two final advantages of PEM compared to alkaline
electrolysis is the use of de-ionized water as the sole reactant, which results in produc-
tion of high purity gases and a very high dynamic range, where it is possible to vary the
production range within milliseconds [19]. However, there are also some disadvantages.
The capital expenses for PEM are generally high due to the need of noble metal catalysts.
Likewise, the expenses for purification units are high since high-purity water is needed.
This makes it difficult to keep the expenses at a feasible level when operating on a larger
scale [19].

Since SOEC significantly differs from alkaline and PEM electrolysis, there are some ad-
vantages and disadvantages worth mentioning. First and foremost, SOEC produces hy-
drogen at a Faradaic efficiency close to 100 %, which results from improved kinetics at
higher temperatures. As the temperature is increased to the high operating temperature
of SOEC, the theoretical voltage for splitting H2O falls by 20 to 30 % [21]. However, the
high temperature also implies a challenge for the material stability; particularly at the an-
ode [20]. The problem at the anode arises from the high partial pressure of oxygen at
the anode interface, which causes the electrode to delaminate. The delamination causes
a reduction of the electrochemical surface area (ECSA), which results in a reduction in
efficiency [18]. This problem can be minimized by operating at lower current densities [20].

Hydrogen Evolution Reaction
The reaction pathway for the HER in alkaline medium can be described by two steps. The
first step is water dissociation resulting in the formation of adsorbed hydrogen H* on the
catalyst surface. This is known as the Volmer step and is described by [30, 31]:

desorption step in which the adsorbed hydrogen combines with an electron transferred
from the electrode surface and a proton from the electrode. The Tafel step is a chemi-
cal desorption step, where two adjacently adsorbed hydrogen atoms combine to form a
hydrogen molecule. The reactions are outlined below [30, 31]:
H2O + e−+ H∗−−→H2 + OH−+ ∗

(2.18)

Now that the HER mechanism has been introduced, it is also relevant to look at what
materials are used and why. Platinum is the state-of-the-art catalyst, but also a very

of adsorption of hydrogen atoms, ΔGH∗is introduced. An active catalyst should have

desorption step (Heyrovsky/Tafel) will be limiting, whereas the adsorption step (Volmer)
is limiting if the hydrogen atom binds to weakly. By plotting the Gibbs free energies of
adsorption for a number of species against measured current densities Seh et al. [33]
obtained a volcano relationship as shown in Figure 2.10. Here, it should be noted that all
the reactive metals are on the left leg of the volcano and the unreactive ones, Cu, Ag, and

2.4.1
Experimental Analysis
In order to test the electrocatalytic performance of an electrode, different electrochemical
analysis techniques can be applied. Here, the background for the experimental analysis
performed as part of the practical work of this thesis will be briefly introduced.
When evaluating the performance of an electrocatalyst, it is important to be able to dis-
tinguish between improved active sites and an increased number of sites, which will both
result in improved overall performance. In this project, the electrochemical performance
will be evaluated qualitatively based on cyclic voltammetry in the cathodic region. This
is done by looking at the onset potential. The lower the onset potential, the better the
electrode. Initially, the onset potential is evaluated with the current density normalized to
the geometric surface area of the electrode. However, it is not possible to assess whether
the improved performance of one electrode compared to another is a result of improved
active sites or an increased number of sites using this method. Thus, the electrochem-
ical surface area (ECSA) is introduced. The active sites are an intrinsic property of the
electrodes. The intrinsic activity is commonly defined as the specific activity which is the
current per unit catalyst surface area [39]. Hence, if the onset potentials of two electrodes
are similar when normalizing the current density to the ECSA, the active sites have not
been improved.
24
Novel Hydrogen Evolution Electrodes for Advanced Alkaline Electrolysis
==PAGE:43==
To clarify, if the onset potential is lower for one electrode compared to another when
normalizing the current density to ECSA, the active sites have been improved. On the
other hand, if the onset potential is similar when normalizing the current density to ECSA,
but different when normalizing the current density to the geometric surface area of the
electrode, the improved performance is likely due to an increased number of active sites
as the intrinsic activity is similar. Thus, a reliable technique for measuring the ECSA is
needed. Different methods can be applied as shall be briefly introduced here along with
some of their main advantages and disadvantages. The relevant techniques are listed
below:
Electrochemical techniques
Non-electrochemical techniques
• Surface redox reaction
• Atomic force microscopy (AFM)
• Double-layer capacitance
• Brunauer-Emmett-Teller (BET) method
• Electron microscopy
The listed methods are divided into electrochemical and non-electrochemical techniques.
Starting with the electrochemical techniques, surface redox reaction involves oxidizing the
surface using cyclic voltammetry. The oxidation CV peak is then integrated to quantify the
ECSA. However, the accuracy of this method is low as it is difficult to determine the base-
line for the integration. Moreover, the number of electrons transferred per metal site is not
known and one also risks destroying the catalyst through irreversible oxidation or leaching
of one of the catalyst components. The other method is based on the double-layer ca-
pacitance. Double-layer capacitance measurements are a versatile and non-destructive
way of estimating the ECSA [40]. The double-layer capacitance can be determined based
on either cyclic voltammetry or electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS). For cyclic
voltammetry, the double-layer capacitance is determined by recording CV’s at various
scan rates where there is no Faradaic response. The capacitative current is then plotted
against the scan rate and the double-layer capacitance is determined from the slope of this
curve. The ECSA is then estimated from the ratio between the determined double-layer
capacitance and the specific capacitance. This double-layer capacitance method is more
accurate than the surface redox reaction method, but also have some disadvantages
including a lack of tabulated specific capacitances. Furthermore, these measurements
can be influenced by side reactions or pseudocapacitance, which will lead to an overes-
timation of the actual ECSA [39]. Pseudocapacitance is a Faradaic process arising at
the surface of the electrode, where materials undergo fast and reversible redox reaction.
However, the CV’s look similar to what is observed for the non-Faradaic double-layer ca-
pacitance [41]. The two processes can therefore not be distinguished, but when the redox
reactions occur, the anodic and cathodic currents are larger explaning the overestimation
of the ECSA.
AFM is the most accurate method for estimating the ECSA of the non-electrochemical
techniques. BET and electron microscopy are less accurate and can only be applied to
powder. Moreover, the BET surface area is not equal to the actual electrochemically avail-
able surface area [39]. Thus, it was ultimately decided to estimate the ECSA based on the
double-layer capacitance using cyclic voltammetry. This was decided since this technique
was readily available and could be implemented as part of the electrochemical measure-

3.1.1
SILAR Method
The initial experiments for the preparation of electrodes were made using the successive
ionic layer adsorption and reaction (SILAR) process adapted from Manikandan et al.[42].
Firstly, the nickel foam was pretreated by immersing it into a 10 % HCl solution for 15
minutes followed by washing with DI-water, ethanol and acetone. Two precursors were
prepared, namely a cationic precursor consisting of 0.1 M NiSO4 + 0.015 M (NH4)6Mo7O24

27
==PAGE:46==
and an anionic precursor consisting of 1.0 M Na2S. Details for these chemicals are listed
in Appendix C. 100 mL of each precursor was prepared as shown in Figure 3.2. Once
the pre-treated Ni-foam had dried, it was cooled to room temperature before the electrode
area was immersed into the cationic precursor for 20 seconds. It was then rinsed with DI-
water and dried using a hair dryer. Afterwards it was immersed into the anionic precursor
for 20 seconds, washed with DI-water and dried using a hair dryer once again. This cycle
of dipping, rinsing and drying using both the cationic and anionic precursor constitute one
SILAR cycle. Four different samples were prepared by performing 10, 20, 30 and 40
SILAR cycles.

In attempt to improve the deposition of Mo on the surface of the Ni-foam, the procedure
was adjusted to have the Mo in the anionic precursor in the form of MoS42 – . The cationic
precursor was then simply a 0.1 M NiSO4 solution. For the anionic precursor (NH4)2MoS4
was used, but as it did not dissolve completely in water, the supernatant was transferred
to another beaker and used as the anionic precursor. It is therefore difficult to know the
exact concentration of the anionic precursor, but it was approximately 0.1 M (NH4)2MoS4.
However, an accurate concentration is not that important for this type of experiment as
long as the desired ions are present in an excessive amount, since only a small amount will
actually deposit on the surface. Two different electrodes were prepared using these new
precursors, one with 10 SILAR cycles with rinsing in DI-water and drying as performed
previously and another electrode, where the drying and rinsing procedure between immer-
sion into the cationic and anionic precursor was skipped. This electrode was still rinsed
and dried after being immersed in the anionic precursor thus completing one SILAR cycle.

3.1.2
Electrodeposition
It was also attempted to prepare electrodes using an electrodeposition approach adapted
from Wang et al. [43]. Before the electrodeposition, the electrodes were pretreated using
the following sequence:
1. Anodic degreasing in SlotoClean EL DCG (Schlötter) for 2 minutes at a current den-
sity of 100 mA/cm2
2. Pickling in SlotoClean Decasel 5 (Schlötter) for 1 minute
3. Reverse Ni-strike1 in a bath containing 30 g/L Ni2+ and 120 g/L Cl – made from NiCl2
and HCl for 2 minutes at 500 mA/cm2
4. Ni-strike1 in the same bath as above for 4 minutes at 1000 mA/cm2
The purpose of the reverse Ni-strike was to remove the Ni oxide layer that forms in the
anodic degreasing, which does not completely dissolve in the pickling bath. Moreover, it
roughens the surface, which is desirable as it increases the surface area.
The electrolyte for the electrodeposition was prepared by dissolving 0.017 M NiSO4 · 6H2O,
0.016 M sodium citrate, 3.60×10−4 M (NH4)6Mo7O24 · 4H2O and 0.285 M NaCl in DI-
water (detailed information about the chemicals can be found in Appendix C). The amount
of electrolyte prepared was increased by a factor 10 compared to the recipe by Wang et
al. [43] to match the setup used for the electrodeposition. The pH of this solution was then
adjusted to 8.5 by adding droplets of NH3. The pretreated electrode was then placed in
the electrolyte with two nickel plates as anodes and the electrodeposition was then run
for 1 hour (3600 s) at varying current densities, namely 100, 200 and 250 mA/cm2. The
electrodes were then dried using a hair dryer before characterization and electrochemical
testing.
3.1.3
Hydrothermal Method
As a third method for the preparation of coated electrodes, a hydrothermal method was
applied where a NiMoO4 coat on Ni-foam is formed in an autoclave under autothermal
pressure. The hydrothermal method was adapted from a paper by Zhang et al. [44] where
8 mmol Na2MoO4 · 2H2O and 8 mmol NiCl2 · 6H2O were dissolved in 120 mL deionized
water. These quantities are a factor four compared to the original recipe, but with identical
concentrations. The additional amount of reaction mixture was made in order to secure
the electrodes were completely covered with reaction mixture in the autoclave. The Ni-
foam was pretreated in 5 % HCl for 15 min and rinsed with DI-water before being placed
in an autoclave along with the prepared reaction mixture.

Physical Characterization

SEM/EDS
Scanning electron microscopy is a widely used technique for surface characterization of
solid samples. The sample is placed in a vacuum chamber and then electrons are emitted
from a filament. The electrons are focused towards the sample. The detectors will then
capture the scattered electrons following the interaction of the electrons with the sample.
The signal from the detectors are then transformed into an electrical signal, which can
be quantified and used to create images displaying the surface structure of the examined
sample. Moreover, many SEM’s are fitted with an energy dispersive spectrometer, which
is used for composition analysis [45, 46].
SEM was used to characterize the surface structure of the prepared electrodes. More-
over, it was used for quantifying the surface species using EDS. A Hitachi TM3030 table-
top SEM was used for quick analyses and a ZEISS Merlin was used for more thorough
surface characterization of the most promising electrodes. A Zeiss EVO scanning electron
microscope was also used on one occasion.
The characterization of the electrodes using SEM is a quick and easy way to get infor-
mation about the surface structure of the prepared electrodes both before and after elec-
trochemical testing. Moreover, the EDS analysis provides valuable information about the
elements deposited on the surface and their respective ratios. However, the images and
the quantifications are limited to small areas and should therefore only be used as an
indication of the actual structure across the entire surface of the electrodes. Moreover,
the quantification of molybdenum and sulfur might prove to be problematic as there is an
overlap between the L series peaks of molybdenum and the sulfur Kα peak. This was one
of the reasons that it was decided to use XPS as well to be able to distinguish between
these two elements and confirm the presence of molybdenum.
XPS
X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy is a very sensitive technique, where the sample is irra-
diated with x-rays of energies lower than ∼6 keV and the kinetic energy (KE) of the emitted
electrons are analyzed. Based on the measured kinetic energy, the binding energy (BE)
of an electron can be determined based on the following relation [47]:
BE = hν −KE −spec
(3.1)
Here, hν is the energy of the X-ray and spec is the spectrometer work function, which is
constant. The binding energy of an electron is a material property, which is independent of
the X-ray source used to eject it. The photoelectron peaks can thus be assigned to specific
elements and orbitals, which makes XPS a very powerful tool as the binding energy also
provides information about the oxidation state of the element of interest [47].
XPS was performed using an ESCALAB XI+ X-ray Photoelectron Spectrometer from Thermo
Fisher Scientific. The analysis was made on small pieces cut from the electrode as illus-
trated by the light blue area in Figure 3.1. Charge compensation was used for all elec-
trodes. Moreover, the recorded spectra were corrected using a calibration method based
on the lowest binding energy C1s peak. It is impossible to avoid carbon contamination
of the surface when the samples are exposed to the atmosphere, but this is utilized to

tion method [47]. This calibration along with the analysis of recorded XPS spectra was
performed using the Avantage software provided by Thermo Scientific.
XRD
X-ray Diffraction is a convenient method to characterize materials based on their crystal
structure. The method is typically applied to powder samples, which is irradiated by an
X-ray beam. The beam diffracts and by measuring the intensity and angles of these beam
diffractions, a unique fingerprint in the form of a diffractogram is created. The diffractogram
can then be used to identify crystalline phases present in the powder sample. Thus, one
of the main advantages of XRD is the possibility to identify different crystalline phases
with identical elemental composition [48]. For this project, the XRD diffractograms were
recorded using a Malvern Panalytical Empyrean diffractometer. The recording of diffrac-
tograms and the initial analysis of these were performed by the XRD department at Haldor
Topsøe A/S.
TGA
Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) is a technique where the weight of a sample is moni-
tored over time with changing temperatures. The sample (usually powder) is placed in a
crucible and mounted in the TGA apparatus and the temperature settings for the exper-
iment are programmed. For the TGA experiments performed for this project, a Netzsch
STA 409 was used. Different temperature settings were used for the performed analyses
and will be specified along with the presentation of the results. All TGA experiments were
performed at atmospheric pressure.
The purpose of the TGA experiments was to investigate the annealing procedure of the
hydrothermally produced electrodes in more detail. Specifically, the reduction process
was investigated and therefore a gas flow of 100 mL/min containing 5 % H2 in N2 was used
for the TGA experiments. For the experiments, the precipitate formed in the autoclave
during the reaction step was collected and dried at 60 °C and placed in the crucible.
This compound should be similar to the chemical species on the surface of the prepared
electrodes, but without the Ni-foam.

4.5
Discussion and Summary
The results for the individual experiments have been presented and discussed in the pre-
vious sections. In this section, these results are compared to give a better overview of
the major findings of the research. Thus, it is also the objective to provide an analysis of
which changes have had the most significant effect on the performance of the prepared
electrodes.
4.5.1
Overpotentials
It is difficult to make a direct comparison of the prepared electrodes based on the recorded
HER activity CV’s as several parameters were changed over the course of the project
including the potential limits and scan rate. Hence, the overpotentials measured at the
end of the one hour chronopotentiometry carried out at 100 mA/cm2 has been selected
as a way to compare the electrocatalytic activity of the prepared electrodes quantitatively.
These overpotentials are summarized in Figure 4.52 along with a Ni-foam reference. The
Ni-foam reference is an average of the four different measurements with the standard
deviation also shown.
Figure 4.52: Numerical values of the overpotentials measured at the end of one hour chronopoten-
tiometry carried out at 100 mA/cm2 (MoS4 rinse at 133 mA/cm2) normalized to geometric surface
area. The listed overpotentials have been iR compensated. T = RT, [KOH] = 30 wt%.
90
Novel Hydrogen Evolution Electrodes for Advanced Alkaline Electrolysis
==PAGE:109==
It is evident from Figure 4.52 that the hydrothermal method has resulted in the lowest
overpotentials for the electrodes prepared using the three different methods. Even the
electrode annealed at 700 °C, which has a significantly larger overpotential compared
to the other annealed hydrothermal electrodes, has an overpotential similar to the best
performing electrodes prepared using the SILAR method and electrodeposition.
It is also observed that there are two groups of overpotentials for the electrodes prepared
using the hydrothermal method: one at ∼70 mV and one at ∼80 mV (with the electrode
annealed at 550 °C somewhere in-between). This is likely caused by a small adjustment
in the preparation procedure. Initially, the electrodes were prepared by dissolving the two
reactants, NiCl2 and Na2MoO4, simultaneously in a beaker. However, it was observed
that either the reactants did not dissolve completely or the reaction started immediately
upon mixing resulting in the presence of precipitate. This precipitate would work as seeds
during the reaction in the autoclave, which would constrain the growth of NiMoO4 on the
Ni-foam. Thus, the procedure was changed so the reactants were dissolved separately
in two beakers and mixed in the autoclave upon which no precipitate was formed prior to
closing the autoclave. This adjustment could explain why two overpotential groups are
observed as it is expected to have improved the growth of NiMoO4 on the Ni-foam surface.
The two groups of overpotentials for the hydrothermal method electrodes also indicates
that the changes made have not had any significant influence on the electrocatalytic ac-
tivity. The results are very similar regardless of carrier gas, reaction temperature and
annealing temperature except for annealing at 700 °C. Differences were observed in the
thickness of the fibers with a higher reaction temperature resulting in thicker fibers. How-
ever, this does not appear to have had any effect on the electrochemical performance.
At least not in the experiments performed as part of this project. It is therefore difficult to
conclude which parameters would be the most optimal for preparing a hydrogen evolution
electrode based on this work. Instead, this shows how robust this method has proven to
be, which is an asset of the hydrothermal preparation method.
4.5.2
Electrochemical Surface Area
Another relevant topic of discussion is the estimation of the electrochemical surface area
due to its relevance in evaluating the performance of an electrocatalyst. The ECSA was
estimated based on the double-layer capacitance determined using cyclic voltammetry.
However, the accuracy of this method is questionable based on the obtained results.
This was especially seen for the electrode annealed at 700 °C, where the electrochem-
ical results showed an ECSA approximately an order of magnitude smaller than similar
electrodes annealed at lower temperatures while no significant sintering was seen on the
SEM images. It is therefore debatable whether these ECSA estimations can be trusted.
This also means that the evaluation of the intrinsic properties of the prepared electrodes
becomes problematic. For the SILAR electrodes, the ECSA estimation was not even per-
formed as the data for the double-layer capacitance was not suitable for linear analysis
due to potentiostat overloading. It is therefore impossible to conclude anything relating to
the intrinsic properties of the SILAR electrodes compared to Ni-foam.
Novel Hydrogen Evolution Electrodes for Advanced Alkaline Electrolysis
91
==PAGE:110==
The ECSA was estimated for all the electrodeposited and hydrothermal electrodes and
while these are questionable, it is still considered very likely that the intrinsic properties of
all these electrodes have been improved compared to Ni-foam. Since the onset potential
was observed to be significantly lower compared to Ni-foam for the HER activity CV’s with
the current density normalized to the ECSA (see Figure 4.19 and Figure 4.29), the intrinsic
activity is believed to be better even with the large uncertainty of the estimated ECSA’s.
However, it is not possible to separate the performance of the three electrodeposited elec-
trodes. The same goes for all the hydrothermally prepared electrodes. In fact, it is very
difficult to determine which of the preparation methods results in the best intrinsic prop-
erties of the electrodes due to the significant difference of the estimated surface areas.
However, it can be concluded that the overall performance is best for the hydrothermally
prepared electrodes, but this is mostly due to a significantly larger surface area.
Continuing the discussion of the active sites of the prepared electrodes, the prepara-
tion method should not influence the intrinsic properties of the electrodes, as Ni-Mo is
deposited on the Ni-foam support using both electrodeposition and the hydrothermal
method. However, the Ni/Mo-ratio observed using EDS for the different preparation meth-
ods varies quite significantly for the two preparation methods.
For electrodeposition,
Ni/Mo ≃5 before testing, whereas Ni/Mo = 1.0-1.4 for the hydrothermal method. Thus,
the hydrothermally prepared electrodes have a higher Mo content. The hydrothermal
electrodes were also observed to have a lower onset potential when normalized to the
ECSA compared to the electrodeposited electrodes, which indicates that an increased
Ni/Mo-ratio might lead to improved active sites. However, the uncertainty of the ECSA
estimations meant no definitive conclusions could be drawn.
Considering the uncertainty of the ECSA estimates, it is also relevant to consider the
method used for the estimations. As mentioned in the literature survey, several methods
can be used. Even for the double-layer capacitance approach, impedance spectroscopy
can be used instead of cyclic voltammetry. It could be relevant to include impedance
spectroscopy for double determination of the ECSA if the measurements were repeated.
Another problem related to this method is the pseudocapacitance of these Ni-Mo elec-
trodes. The interference from the pseudocapacitance will lead to an overestimation of the
ECSA. Moreover, the ECSA’s were estimated using the end points of the CV scans rather
than an average between the anodic and cathodic current in the middle of the potential
interval. In the ideal world, this would not make any difference as the double-layer capac-
itance CV’s are expected to be quasi-rectangular, but as hysteresis was observed (most
likely due to oxidation) this approach results in a further overestimation of the ECSA. The
actual ECSA’s are therefore most likely not as high as the reported values.
Based on the above discussion, it is clear that the determination of the ECSA has not
been perfect. However, if the estimated ECSA’s are to be related to the numerical re-
sults reported in Figure 4.52 there are some interesting parallels. It was mentioned that
two groups of overpotentials were observed for the hydrothermally prepared electrodes.
Interestingly, the three electrodes with an overpotential of ∼70 mV are also the three elec-
trodes with the highest estimated ECSA. This would also suggest that the intrinsic prop-

Conclusion
In this project, three different preparation methods for hydrogen evolution electrodes for
advanced alkaline electrolysis has been investigated, namely the SILAR method, elec-
trodeposition and a hydrothermal method. Ni-foam was used as support for Ni-Mo coated
electrodes, which were examined thoroughly through structural characterization including
SEM, XPS, XRD and TGA along with electrochemical tests to evaluate their performance.
With the SILAR method, it was generally not possible to obtain a homogeneous coating
using this method and the Mo content was very low despite attempts to change the prepa-
ration method. Moreover, the coating was observed to scale off during the electrochemical
tests. The SILAR method was therefore not considered to be particularly successful and
was quickly dismissed. Electrodeposition was also tested using three different current
densities (100, 200 and 250 mA/cm2) for the preparation. A better (albeit not homoge-
neous) coating was obtained using this method with Ni/Mo ≃5. This coating was also
observed after the electrochemical tests albeit with a reduced Mo content as a result of
leaching. The intrinsic activity of these electrodes had also been improved compared to
Ni-foam with the electrodes prepared at 200 mA/cm2 and 250 mA/cm2 performing slightly
better than the one prepared at lower current density.
A homogeneous coating of thin fibers with Ni/Mo = 1.0-1.4 was obtained using the hy-
drothermal method resulting in the highest surface area observed among the three at-
tempted preparation methods. Combined with good structural stability and the lowest
overpotential observed at the end of chronopotentiometry, this preparation method was
the most promising. Hence, this preparation method was investigated further by varying
the reaction temperature and annealing temperature. The thickness of the fibers was
observed to increase with increasing reaction temperature, but similar electrochemical
performance was observed with overpotentials in the range 70-80 mV (T = RT, [KOH]
= 30 wt%.) at a geometric current density of 100 mA/cm2 in all cases except for one
electrode annealed at 700 °C. The long term stability of the hydrothermal electrodes were
also proven by a 250 h test in a full cell. After this test, a homogeneous coating was still
observed although sintering had occurred along with leaching of Mo (Ni/Mo = 3.8). The
hydrothermal method is thus concluded to be the most promising of the three methods
attempted in this work given the robustness, superior electrochemical performance and
structural stability.
Finally, the ECSA was estimated using double-layer capacitance CV’s for the prepared
electrodes to evaluate their intrinsic activity. However, the obtained results were debatable
as significantly different ECSA’s were estimated for electrodes looking similar on SEM
images. Moreover, the reported ECSA’s were also believed to be overestimated due
to the influence of pseudocapacitance. It was therefore difficult to evaluate the intrinsic
properties of the electrodes, but the addition of Mo was still believed to have improved the
electrocatalytic activity of the electrodes since the onset potential (with the current density
normalized to the ECSA) was significantly lower compared to Ni-foam.

Future Work
The work done as part of this project has only scratched the surface of potential prepara-
tion procedures for hydrogen evolution electrodes. As the hydrothermal method proved
to be the most promising of the tested preparation methods, this outlook will only focus
on this particular method.
The most relevant starting point for future work would be to perform some more long term
stability tests. The one electrode, which was tested in the full cell test station was tested
as part of two different experiments combining to approximately 250 hours. However, the
lifetime of mature alkaline electrolysis technology is 100,000 hours (11.4 years) [19], but
as such a time frame is not suitable for experiments, shorter stability tests of at least 500
hours would also be relevant. For these tests, exposing the electrode to high current den-
sities would be interesting to stress test it and see whether the cell potential was effected.
Nonetheless, it is difficult designing experiments, which provide good information about
the long term stability without running over a long period of time.
It was observed that the electrode had sintered after the full cell test. Hence, a suggestion
for the first long term test would be to use an electrode prepared with an autoclave reaction
temperature of 180 °C as the fibers were observed to be thicker for this batch. This would
also imply a reproduction of fibers grown at 180 °C thereby investigating whether the fiber
thickness is a function of reaction temperature. If the thicker fibers are in fact reproducible,
it would be interesting to see if thicker fibers result in improved structural stability or a
similar degree of sintering occurs for these fibers.
Another important thing to improve upon further work is the recording of CV’s and esti-
mation of ECSA. The HER activity CV’s were improved significantly by reducing the scan
rate from 50 mV/s to 5 mV/s, but it should be considered to reduce scan rate even more to
get closer to the actual steady-state. Moreover, the double-layer capacitance CV’s should
be conducted in another interval as oxidation of the electrodes was observed for some of
these CV’s. Hence, it is suggested to record the double-layer capacitance CV’s at poten-
tials below 100 mV vs RHE to eliminate undesired oxidation processes and minimize the
influence of the pseudocapacitance, which leads to an overestimation of the ECSA.
The reaction temperature as mentioned above is one of the parameters, which can be
adjusted in a study to control the length and thickness of the grown fibers. In this work,
a reaction time of approximately 17 hours has been used. Whether such long reaction
time is required is debatable as depletion of the reactant will likely occur earlier. Precip-
itation besides the growth of NiMoO4 on the Ni-foam support was also observed. It is
therefore reasonable to assume that an equilibrium has been reached in the experiments
conducted. However, it would be interesting to do experiments with a shorter reaction
time to see if the length of the fibers can be controlled. Other parameters to adjust in this
type of study would be the reaction temperature as already mentioned, but also the con-
centration of reactants. Namely the concentration of reactants could be changed such
Novel Hydrogen Evolution Electrodes for Advanced Alkaline Electrolysis

==PAGE:116==
that the Ni/Mo-ratio of the reactants was varied to investigate the role of Mo on these
electrocatalysts.
Finally, an in-depth investigation of the annealing procedure has not been conducted, but
would be relevant. The annealing temperature has been investigated, but the reaction
time was kept constant at 1.5 hours with a temperature ramp of 3 °C/min. These two
parameters could be varied to get closer to an optimal annealing procedure. It was shown
that increasing the temperature led to a more reduced coating, but also a decreased
ECSA albeit not visually apparent from the SEM images. There might be a similar trade-
off between annealing time and sintering. Moreover, the concentration of H2 used for
the annealing might influence the degree of reduction. In this work, gases containing
5 % H2 were used, but increasing the H2 concentration could possibly lead to a more
reduced coating. However, an increased hydrogen content is also related to potential
safety issues. Likewise, it is unknown whether a more reduced surface results in a higher
catalytic activity.

Background
Hydrogen is going to play a major role in our transition from fossil to renewable energy.
With a recent agreement to construct the world’s first wind energy hub as an artificial island
in the North Sea, the topic of Power-to-X has become ever more important. The renewable
energy is of course going to be integrated on the grid-connected system, but renewable
energy evidently fluctuates. Hence, when the fluctuating renewable energy exceeds the
load, the excess energy can be used for the production of fuels and chemicals with minimal
CO2 emissions. One of the options is to produce hydrogen by water electrolysis and it
is expected that the demand for electrical hydrogen production will increase through the
coming years.
There are three main competing water electrolysis technologies being developed: alkaline
electrolysis, polymer electrolyte membrane (PEM) electrolysis and solid oxide electrolysis
cells (SOEC). Alkaline electrolysis is the most mature technology of the three having been
used for hydrogen production for more than 100 years. Moreover, alkaline electrolysis can
also be operated with cheap Ni-based electrodes, but the technology has been developed
to be robust rather than efficient and the traditional alkaline electrolyzer design and the
materials used as electrodes limit current density. In recent years, research has focused
on developing new electrode materials with improved catalytic activity. At the same time, a
new electrolyzer design - the so-called zero-gap design - has received increased attention.
This research path will be further investigated in this project.

The scope of this project is the preparation and testing of nanostructured electrodes for
hydrogen evolution to be used in zero-gap electrolysis cells. The preparation of Ni-based
electrodes is achieved with wet-chemical techniques on lab-scale. The prepared elec-
