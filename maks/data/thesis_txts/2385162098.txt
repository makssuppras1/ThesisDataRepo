Abstract
Several conditions in a psychiatric hospital must be considered and numerous require-
ments must be met in a feasible schedule of continuity of care. Since the request for reg-
ular working schedules is highly prioritized it is examined in this thesis how to solve the
combinatorial problem of scheduling continuity of care with cyclic scheduling of activities
in patient courses. Mixed integer linear programming will be applied for the modelling.
Te cyclic period is one working week. An activity of a patient course consists of the
sub-activities preparation, confrontation and processing. Te cyclic scheduling is done in
two diﬀerent considerations of activities: 1) an activity consists of disjointed sub-activities
which allows maximum one day between the execution of the sub-activities, 2) an activ-
ity consists of consecutive sub-activities which requires the sub-activities to be executed
successively to each other.
Two models of the case problem are of distinct mathematical formulations. Model A repeats
cycles by duplicating values of binary variables of one cyclic period to the next cyclic pe-
riod. Model B links cycles by an integer variable representing the number of cycles between
an activity to the ﬁrst activity of the patient course.
Each of the two models Model A and Model B is partitioned into three sub-problems and
solves the case problem by applying a heuristic solving the sub-problems stepwise. Testing
showed no signiﬁcant diﬀerence in the performance of Model A and Model B. Te cyclic
scheduling of activities considering consecutive sub-activities is faster than cyclic schedul-
ing disjointed sub-activities of an activity, however, disjointed sub-activities is more appli-
cable in case of much discontinuous available time of employees in the hospital.
In the research of solving the case problem without necessity for applying a heuristic,
simpliﬁcations of requirements in the case problem are examined. Hence, a third model the
simpliﬁed model of the case problem is tested. Cyclic scheduling of the simpliﬁed problem
was solved without applying a heuristic within acceptable computation time. However,
the solutions are only applicable in case of the hospital’s acceptance of simpliﬁcations.
Te feasible schedule is wished to be fair in respect to the distribution of activities to em-
ployees in the hospital. It is suggested to improve fairness by minimizing the maximal
variance in assignments of activities by including a set of sof constraints in the model.
Furthermore, it is suggested to evaluate fairness in terms of an employee’s proﬁciency
(availability and capability) and shared workload (equality) among staﬀ. Examination of
distributions from solutions shows the fairness can be improved by implementing the sug-
gestions in the models. Furthermore, testing showed the time consumption is reduced for
application of the stepwise heuristic for solving Model A and Model B.
It can be concluded that with the researched approach it is possible to achieve a fair dis-
tribution in a feasible cyclic schedule which complies with all requirements (Model A and
Model B), and provide regular working schedules for the staﬀof a hospital, while the com-
putation time is maintained very reasonable.

Introduction
Numerous requirements and conditions makes the scheduling of continuity of care in ex-
amination of a patient a very complicated task in the Danish psychiatric hospitals. Conti-
nuity of care is concerned with the quality of care over time and the patient’s experience
of a continuous caring relationship with identiﬁed hospital employees [10]. Terefore, to
schedule continuity of care several conditions and requirements must be met.
An examination of a patient consists of several activities in a patient course handled
by the employees of the psychiatric hospital. Several given requirements from directives
and stakeholders must be obeyed under given conditions in the psychiatric hospital. Te
scheduling has to consider tasks inclusive of assignment of required competences to activ-
ities, a correct order of activities in each course of patient examination, a patient’s contact
to employees of the hospital and compliance with several time lines of activities. Ensur-
ing all requirements are met in a schedule of a patient course is scheduling continuity of
care which is important for maintaining patient satisfaction, successful examination and
correct diagnosing.
Furthermore, Danish directives of psychiatric diagnosing have been tighten which require
the employees of the hospital to execute activities of patient courses within a stricter time
horizon. Tis increases the need of careful scheduling.
Te psychiatric hospitals have previously used much resources and time on manual schedul-
ing. Currently, the company Qampo Aps manage the scheduling of continuity of care for
many hospital in the Danish region Region Midtjylland. Qampo’s current approach to han-
dle the diﬃcult scheduling is to apply constraint programming and non-cyclic schedules
but it is challenging to schedule all patient courses of the forecasted demand. In this thesis
the solution approach of mixed integer programming and one-week cyclic scheduling -
distinct to the current approach - will be researched. Te aim is to schedule all demanded
courses with continuity of care and develop schedule solutions of high utility for the work-
ing procedure in the hospitals by recognisability in weekly work schedules as requested
by employees.

1.1
Case study: A cooperation with Qampo and Region Midtjylland
Tis thesis is based on a case study. Te case is a real world problem of scheduling conti-
nuity of care for the public authority Region Midtjylland Psychiatric Health Care which is
currently managed by the consultant company Qampo Aps (Qampo).
Region Midtjylland govern regional healthcare functions in Denmark. One is the psychi-
atric function which has Department of Child and Youth Psychiatric (in Danish: Børne- og
Ungdomspsykiatrisk Center) among others. In this paper Department of Child and Youth
Psychiatric will be referred to as BUC.
BUC has over 500 employees and around 4000 new patients yearly at their hospitals [20].
Tey examine, treat and diagnose psychiatric diseases in their currently 11 hospital sec-
tions. One is denoted Hospital Q and another denoted Hospital R in this thesis will be the
reference points in the case study. An overview of the organisation is shown in below
ﬁgure 1.
Figure 1: Several departments and hospitals are part of Region Midtjylland Psychiatric
Health Care
My research approach is to solve the problem in the case study with clariﬁcation of re-
quirements and conditions, insight in relevant theory, application of collected actual data
about the psychiatric hospitals, and formulation of the problem with mathematical mod-
elling, and solve the problem by implementation in a optimization solver.
Qampo has realised the complexity of the problem of scheduling continuity of care. Cur-
rently, Qampo is able to schedule continuity of care for 7-8 patient courses initiated each
week in Hospital Q. Qampo has made a forecast of future demand which requires 10 pa-
tient courses are initiated each week. It is desired to be able schedule the full number of
forecasted demand, and not only schedule the patient courses but ensuring fulﬁlling all
requirements to achieve feasible schedules of continuity of care.

1.2
Research Qestion
As explained, scheduling continuity of care is scheduling of several activities of a patient
course in the psychiatric hospital while maintaining any given requirement and condition
related. In the aim of scheduling the forecasted quantity of patient courses and meet the
request for regularity in employees’ work schedules, this thesis is found on the following
research question:
How is the problem of scheduling continuity of care in psychiatric hospitals solved
with the approach of mixed integer programming and cyclic scheduling?
How is a fair schedule solution achieved and evaluated?
Te aim is to accomplish research such that answers are found to the research question.
Te research question will be addressed with the following described methodology.
1.3
Methodology
In this section the methodology used in this thesis will be argued. First is argumentation
for the data collection and next is the methodology of operation research which is used
in addressing the research question. Te choice is made in order to apply a systematic
approach on the complex case study and maintain a high scientiﬁc level. Furthermore, the
method of operation research enables producing results which are practical applicable to
the real world problem in the studied case.
1.3.1
Data Collection
Empirical data about psychiatric hospitals in Region Midtjylland has been collected by
Qampo by conducting interviews and assembling quantitative data.
Empirical data applied in this thesis has been gathered from Qampo by oral and writen
semi-structured interviews [1]. Qampo has also provided quantitative data sets of two spe-
ciﬁc hospital denoted Hospital Q and Hospital R in this thesis.
Te gathered empirical data represent a real world case. Tis thesis applies empirical data
of general conditions and requirements of psychiatric hospitals in Region Midtjylland and
speciﬁc quantitative data of a hospital refereed to as Hospital Q and a hospital refereed to
as Hospital R.
A secondary source is the thesis of Lauridsen and Mathiassen (2016) [15] who have previ-
ously done research for Qampo of a similar case of scheduling patient courses. Teir de-
scriptions of the psychiatric hospitals in BUC in Region Midtjylland has contributed to the
understanding of current conditions and requirements in the hospitals. Tough, changes
have been made since their research was executed but many descriptions are relevant to
the current situation. Te work and results of Lauridsen and Mathiassen have contributed
to the understanding of the complexity of the case problem and of the diﬃculty in schedul-
ing continuity of care in the psychiatric hospitals in Region Midtjylland.

Conceptualization of case problem
Empirical data of psychiatric hospitals in Region Midtjylland has been collected by Qampo.
Based on the collected data a conceptualization of the case is produced in this section
with the aim of obtaining deﬁnition, clariﬁcation and understanding of extent of the case
problem: scheduling continuity of care in psychiatric hospitals in Region Midtjylland.
2.1
Objectives
A determination of objectives for solving the scheduling problem in psychiatric hospitals
has contributed to the conceptualization of the case problem.
Region Midtjylland desires a smooth work ﬂow in the psychiatric hospitals with satisﬁed
employees and patients (customers). Furthermore, the psychiatric hospitals are governed
by Danish laws and restrictions which Region Midtjylland is reasonable for maintaining.
Employees’ satisfaction is critical for eﬃcient operation in hospitals. With high employee
satisfaction it is achievable to conduct successful patient treatment and sustain the number
of new incoming patients (demand). Employees have requested regularity in their individ-
ual work schedules. A regular work schedule of the activities of patient courses makes the
employee’s own planning of other tasks less complicated.
Te satisfaction of patients is also central to the importance of scheduling continuity of
care. Terefore it must be avoid to keep patients in suspense and the patient course is
wished to be carry out shortly afer the referral from a general practitioner. A regular
schedule will contribute to smooth conduct of activities within a short time horizon in
both patients’, employees’ and Region Midtjylland’s favour.
2.2
A three-part problem
Te case has been analysed and three identiﬁed tasks constitute the case problem of schedul-
ing continuity of care. Tese are described in the following.

2.3
Conditions
8
(i) Ensure consistency of employees in a patient course and right competences
to activities
Each activity of a patient course must be assigned one or more employees with right
competences. Maintaining consistency of employees in a patient course contributes
to patient satisfaction and avoidance of knowledge loss of the patient.
(ii) Ensure capacity and capability
Capacity must not be violated. By capacity is meant quantity and availability of re-
sources: employees and examination rooms. Activities must be held within working
hours of the participating employees. Furthermore, patients and parents atend some
activities of a patient course. Obviously an participant/atendant cannot be present
at more than one activity at one time.
(iii) Ensure time limits
Required orders and time intervals (delays) between activities must be met.
Te complexity lies in aligning feasible solutions of the three tasks in the aim for a feasible
solution of schedules of continuity of care. Te tasks must be solved simultaneously and
not violate the given conditions and requirements explained in following section.
2.3
Conditions
From gathered data requirements and conditions related to continuity of care in psychia-
try are identiﬁed. Te case study in this thesis uses Hospital Q as point of reference and
therefore all presented requirements and conditions are valid for Hospital Q.
Employees in a psychiatric hospital
Employees working in psychiatric hospitals are divided into two categories: Terapists
and Council workers. Furthermore, the employees are divided into position groups. Te
category therapists consist of employees of the following position groups: Doctors, psy-
chologists, medical interns and psychology interns. Te category Council workers consists
of employees of the position group council worker which are among others nurses and
counsellors. Te categories and position groups of employees in a psychiatric hospital are
illustrated in table 1. Some employees are working on temporary contracts which are pri-
marily medical interns and psychology interns.
Activities in a patient course
A patient course can be of diﬀerent types which deﬁnes which activities are included. Type
PB01 and type PB03A are relevant for Hospital Q. Type PB01 consists of one activity clar-
ifying interview which is mandatory for all patients. Type PB03A consists of 10 activities.
Together the two course types constitute what is denoted a patient course in this thesis.
Te activities of a patient course are seen in table 2.

3.1
Scheduling problems
Scheduling is solving the tasks of by whom a job is executed and when a job is executed.
Each job must be assigned to a resource and the time execution must be ensured by aligning
time and capacity of resources with a feasible order of the jobs to be executed. Te amount
of jobs executed must match demand.
Graph formulations in scheduling
A feasible order of jobs is not violating any order restrictions between the jobs, i.e. if one job
must be executed before another. Te order restrictions between the jobs to be scheduled
can be represented with graph theory [19]. Considering a directed graph D the set of jobs
are presented by the nodes (vertices) v ∈V . Te set of arcs a ∈A represents order (and
time) between the jobs (the nodes) to be performed in order to produce a product [14]. See
illustration in ﬁgure 6 of a directed graph. Te directed graph shows feasible order(s) of
the nodes. Te successor node follows the predecessor node deﬁned by direction of the arc
between the two adjacent nodes.
Figure 6: Example of a directed graph
From the directed graph in ﬁgure 6 it can be seen that two distinct sequences {i, j, k} and
{i, k, j} are both feasible paths, i.e. feasible orders of the jobs.
In the following, non-cyclic scheduling and cyclic scheduling are discussed.
3.1.1
Non-cyclic scheduling
A non-cyclic schedule consider each planning period individually such that variations in
capacity and demand can be considered in the scheduling. A schedule for one planning
period may be diﬀerent to the schedule for another planning period depending on changes
in resources (capacity) and request throughput (demand). Short term planning periods in
non-cyclic scheduling enables adoptions in schedules. Terefore, non-cyclic scheduling
must be executed regularly for each new time period.
3.1.2
Cyclic scheduling
Instead of distinct schedules from time period to time period the same schedule is repeated
for every time period. Te set of jobs J scheduled in a certain order and denoted a sequence
of jobs in a time period T. Te sequence of jobs is also deﬁned as basic cycle schedule which
can be repeated inﬁnity many times and with no overlaps of the basic cycles. Te cyclic

might be costly and time consuming for renewing schedule frequently.
A concrete advantage of Qampo’s current approach is their non-cyclic scheduling con-

selves. However, by allowing many variations, regularity in a schedule is lost, and uncer-

In cyclic scheduling it is necessary to assume that resources and demand are steady, i.e.
constant over all time periods. Conditions can be standardized and assumed approximate
constant for a long term planning horizon in many cases. A cyclic schedule can be re-

An advantage with cyclic scheduling is the employees obtain certainty of their future in-
dividual work schedules because of the regularity in cyclic schedules. Purnomo et. al. 2006
[16] argues an advantage is that cyclic schedules reduce some of the instability in the pro-
duction process and claims cyclic schedules ”are easier to manage and generally perceived
to be more equitable”.
One of the advantages of cyclic scheduling is the reduced planning period of time con-
sidered, namely the cyclic period, with a critical lower number of timeslots which means
fewer variables and can make a large combinatorial problem easier to solve. Hence the
number of jobs to schedule may also be reduced since only the demand of the cyclic period
must be considered and not the demand of a full planning period considered in non-cyclic
scheduling.
Draper et al. 2013 [7] argues that a cyclic schedule is not necessarily the most eﬃcient
schedule. With a given ﬁnite number N of required repeated sequences (to fulﬁl full plan-
ning period) it is likely that there exists another more eﬃcient schedule (Hanen 1994) than
the one produced by cyclic scheduling. However, in the mater of a feasible solution, com-
plete eﬃciency is not mandatory.
If a cyclic schedule has a non-equal or un-fair distribution of jobs to the resources (might
caused by changes) the imbalance will increase for each cycle. When larger permanent
changes occur, the cyclic schedule may be updated by a new generated cyclic schedule.
In cases where each planning/time period has a workable size the non-cyclic schedul-
ing is preferable due to its ﬂexibility in regards to variations from time to time period and
eﬃcient schedules can be produced. However, when considering a long term planning pe-
riod the problem size will grow with the increasing number of timeslots, and the cyclic
scheduling can be preferable since the required assumption of constant conditions will
help reduce size of the problem for feasible schedule solutions.

3.2.1
Constraint Programming (CP)
Constraint programming is a relatively new paradigm for computing feasible and opti-
mal solutions to combinatorial problems [17]. Constraint programming is not necessarily
constrained to real or integral values. Constraints does not have to be expressed as linear
(in)equalities contrary to mixed integer linear programming. In constraint programming
non-linear constraints like quadratic constraints can appear, and variables obtain rational
numbers [6].
In constraint programming ﬁrst is the constraint satisfaction problem (CSP) formulated.
Te CSP consists of the decision variables with each associated ﬁnite domain set, i.e. set
of feasible values. Te CSP corresponds to the given constraints which deﬁnes the rela-
tions between the variables. In general, CSP does not consider an objective function but
a requested objective can be expressed as a constraint. However the purpose of CSP is
to rather ﬁnd feasible solutions than an optimal solution but which can be found and is
discussed in following section 3.2.1. Solving the corresponding CSP is expressed by Klein
2009 [4]: ”assigning values to each variable of its domain such that each constraint is satis-
ﬁed”. Assuming ﬁnite domain sets, then a constraint is said to be satisﬁed when variables
included in the constraint are within their respective domains.
Solving with Constraint Programming (CP)
Constraint propagation is a constraint solving method for CP. Te techniques is used for
systematically reduction of the domains to get nearer to values which satisfy all constraints
and thus give a feasible (and optimal) solution. Using algorithms constraint propagation
is usually applied iteratively to reduce the domains of each variable as much as possible
while ensure feasibility by satisfying constraints [12]. Cursory explained, a constraint is
considered and the domains of each involved variable is evaluated such that the reduced
domains only contain values which the variables can obtain in relation to each other and
the constraint. Next is to inspect for each variable the intersection of the reduced domains
obtained from considering each constraint. Such domain reduction algorithms and prop-
agation is ofen deﬁned and editable in optimization sofware, argued by Klein 2009 [4].
Constraint propagation improves the solving since the total search space is diminished,
argued by Rossi et. al. 2006 [17]. Local inconsistencies are removed from the CSP to avoid
unnecessary search and dead-ends in the search. Maintaining consistency is critical.
Several search algorithms has been developed to either determine a feasible solution, if
one exists, or to prove the problem is infeasible. Te purpose of the search algorithms is
to systematically search the solution space, i.e. search through the combinations of values
assigned to the variables. A common algorithm is backtracking and forward checking.

3.2.2
Mixed Integer Linear Programming (MILP)
Mixed integer linear programming (MILP) is a natural extension of linear programming
(LP). Constraints are linear but variables obtain instead for real values R integer values
Z. Here applies also binary values. In general, mixed integer linear problems are NP-hard
and hard to solve while opposite the linear problems can be solved in polynomial time.
An objective function in MILP is either maximized or minimized in order to ﬁnd optimum
which is not necessarily an extreme point, however, in that case the problem can be solved
in polynomial time.
In MILP it is possible to transform hard constraints to sof constraints and punish the
objective function with a penalty at violation of the sof constraints. In this way, a hard
solvable problem can produce a solution anyway. Purnomo et. al. 2006 [16] shows some
applications of this approach.
A mixed integer problem can be described with Minkowski-Weyl’s theorem [2] which
tells the problem can be represented by a bounded non-empty polyhedron of convex com-
bination of extreme points or extreme rays for the unbounded non-empty polyhedron. Te
properties in a bounded non-empty polyhedron is advantageous towards proving optimal-
ity exists and solving the relaxed linear problem (LP) in polynomial time.
Solving with Mixed Integer Programming (MILP)
Mixed integer problems are solved by evaluating upper and lower bounds and comparing
bounds of a LP-relaxation of the original problem.
A general approach to determine bounds is the method of branch and bounds which the
solver Cplex applies. Te function of branch and bound methods is to eliminate solutions
which are not improving the current bounds, i.e. branches can be pruned. Furthermore,
branch and bound search on variables by stepwise ﬁxing more and more decision variables.
With this approach and pre-processing not all possible solution combinations have to be
explored.
Te branch and bound method is initial relaxed and solved as a linear problem. Te method
consists of a branch search tree and each branch is a sub-problem where one or more
decision variables x are ﬁxed to integer values. Simultaneously evaluating bounds and
take actions depending on which of the three outcomes [5]:
(i) Integer solution: A feasible solution is found: x ∈Z. Optionally, search on other
branches. If no gap between primal and dual bound zPRIMAL = zDUAL, i.e. zUPPER =
zLOWER, the solution is optimal.
(ii) Fractional solution: ∃x ∈R Continue searching on that branch if bounds are im-
proved andzUPPER ≤zLOWER is not the case.
(iii) Infeasible solution: Solution is empty. Stop searching on that branch.
Grossman 1992 [9] clariﬁes the important capabilities in branch and bound method:
”… preprocessing techniques that have the eﬀect of ﬁxing variables, eliminating redundant

constraints, adding logical inequalities, tightening variable bounds and/or performing coeﬃ-
cient reduction.”

3.2.3
Discussion of CP and MILP
Te main diﬀerence in solving with each of the two introduced programming methods
is that preprocessing and bounds are decisive for MILP and constraint propagation and
domains are decisive for CP.
An advantage of MILP is an objective function can be handled. Furthermore, it is ofen pos-
sible to achieve good bounds of the LP-relaxation which can steer the search. Te quality
of a MILP-solution can be evaluated in relation to the solution of the LP-relaxation. Te
application of preprocessing in MILP can be eﬃcient since the integral search region is
reduced however it does not guaranteed to reduce the computation time [9].
An advantage of CP is the option of expressing constraints in more simple formulations
and logical formulations. As for example for disjunctive constraints where with MILP you
must introduce additional (binary) variables and there is no need for that in CP. Smith et.
al. 1996 [18] support that the compactness of the model with CP is an advantage.
A ﬁnding by Rossi et. al. 2006 [17] is the option of solving over several optimization cri-
terion with CP which is an advantage in cases of hierarchical objectives. Another ﬁnding
is the constraint propagation leading to a reduction of the search region which can make
large problems easier to solve by some selected algorithms.
A disadvantage of CP is the diﬃculty in measuring the quality of a solution, i.e. the gap to
the optimal solution. Furthermore, computation time can be challenging since the search
process has to be restarted when searching for an improved feasible solution. Smith et.
al. 1996 [18] argues that experience and luck in selecting right heuristic have inﬂuence
in a successful solution. It may be necessary to experiment with several heuristics which
is time consuming and with no guarantee of a solution exists. Tus, a main disadvantage
of CP is the unpredictability of ﬁnding a feasible solution and within an acceptable time
frame.
A disadvantage of MILP is that the models can be become large compared to CP models
because of the need of additional variables and thus also additional constraints to explain
relations between decision variables. Tus models can became quite heavy and be time
consuming to solve. However, advantageously there exists well developed techniques of
heuristics and algorithms for solving MILP which perform reliable. Te techniques can
lead to approximate solutions which can be valuable for the user as well.

4.2
Complexity analysis of case problem
Te problem in the case is clariﬁed with above conceptualization. Te case problem of
scheduling continuity of care in psychiatric hospitals in BUC consists of both an assignment
and a scheduling problem. Te conceptualization also contributes to the understanding of
the complexity of the case problem which is discussed in this section.
From the conceptualization it is evident there is need for integer solutions since it concerns
humans and activities which cannot be set on pause and continued another day. It must be
decided in the assignment problem who participate an given activity and in the scheduling
problem when the given activity is held. Any feasible solution, in other words, feasible
schedule is satisfying to the case problem.
In mathematical model a large number of integer variables and constraints are needed to
meet all requirements in the psychiatric hospitals. Te numerous variables increases the
dimensions of the search space and the many constraints increases the complexity of the
feasible search region.
Te case problem is a combinatorial problem and is NP-hard [11]. Tus the problem cannot
be solved in polynomial time but a solution can be veriﬁed in polynomial time by a yes to
a decision problem.
Te daily interval of general working hours in the hospitals must be divided into multiple
timeslots. Due to the durations of the activities of a patient course the length of one times-

As described in section 2.3 each activity consists of the sub-activities preparation, con-
frontation and processing of individual durations, presented in table 2. It is valid for all
activities, that the sub-activity confrontation must follow preparation and processing must
follow confrontation, if the sub-activity exists for a given activity.
Two considerations of sub-activities of an activity are examined and formulated for the
models. Te two considerations are deﬁned as following:
1. Disjointed sub-activities of an activity: Te sub-activity preparation must be held no
earlier than the day before the confrontation. Processing must be held no later than
the day afer the confrontation. Hence, in this consideration, sub-activities of other
activities can be executed in between. Te durations of sub-activities are considered
individually in the scheduling and each sub-activity must be assign a starting time.
2. Consecutive sub-activities of an activity: Te sub-activity preparation ends in the
timeslot followed by the timeslot where confrontation starts, and confrontation ends
just as processing starts. Hence, in this consideration the sub-activities are held con-
tiguous and only one starting time is assigned where the ﬁrst sub-activity of the ac-
tivity starts. Ten the sum of the durations of the sub-activities of an activity must
be considered when scheduling.

Stepwise heuristic for solving models
Initial testing of solving the models conﬁrmed the diﬃculty in solving the case problem.
Tests of the models did not succeed within 48 hours. Very long time consumption was
expected based on the analysis of the complexity in section 4.2 and the size of the problem
seen in sections 2 and 5. Te expectation of diﬃculty in solving the problem is also based
on previous research done by Lauridsen and Mathiasen [15] whose model succeeded in
scheduling all activities of upto six patient courses.
In order to reduce computation time of solving the very complex scheduling problem in
the psychiatric hospitals in Region Midtjylland, a partition of the models seems to be nec-
essary. A constructed stepwise heuristic is composed in the aim for achieving feasible so-
lutions within an acceptable time frame. A stepwise heuristic shall solve the models from
previous section 5 which each are partitioned into three sub-problems.

6.2
Steps in stepwise heuristic
Te case problem of scheduling continuity of care in psychiatric hospitals is solved with
a constructed stepwise heuristic in which the three sub-problems are solved separately.
Solution output/input exchange between the sub-problems is performed by comply the
following steps:
1. Solve sub-problem I of iteration i.
2. If infeasible: Capacity of employees and examination rooms is not large enough to
meet demand of patient courses.
If a feasible solution to sub-problem I is found: Perform the following steps:
3. Fix variables to values from output of sub-problem I of iteration i in sub-problem II.
Solve sub-problem II of iteration i.
4. If a feasible solution to sub-problem II is found: Go to step 5. If infeasible: Perform
the following steps:
4a. Insert output from sub-problem I of iteration i as not allowed solution for sub-
problem I in iteration i + 1.
4b. Go to step 1 and denote iteration i + 1 as iteration i.
5. Fix variables to values from outputs of sub-problem I and sub-problem II of iteration
i in sub-problem III. Solve sub-problem III of iteration i.
6. If a feasible solution to sub-problem III is found: STOP. Feasible solution to the entire
case problem is found. If infeasible: Perform the following steps:
6a. Insert output from sub-problem II of iteration i as not allowed solution for sub-
problem II in input of iteration i + 1.
6b. Go to step 3 and denote iteration i + 1 as iteration i.
Te stepwise heuristic’s application on the case problem is validated by testing in next
section.

Modelling of simpliﬁed case problem
In previous sections 7 and 8, it was seen that models of the case problem could be solved
within reasonable computation time when applying the stepwise heuristic introduced
in section 6. In applying the heuristic a model was partitioned such that the three sub-
problems of the case problem was solved separately. Te purpose of solving the case prob-
lem without the heuristic is to avoid excluding possible optimal and feasible solutions.
In this section it is examined how the case problem can be simpliﬁed in the aim for solv-
ing a model of the case problem without applying a heuristic and still maintain reason-

having applicability of solutions in mind. By implementing suggested simpliﬁcations the
complexity of the problem is decreased and the partial feasible regions are reduced which
contributes to maintain reasonable computing time.

Discussion of results
In this section, the models of scheduling continuity of care presented in previous sections
will be discussed. Next in this section, a comparison of the achieved results of the re-
searched approach and Qampo’s current solution is conducted. Furthermore, the achieved
results in this thesis will be compared with the results of the thesis of Mathiasen and Lau-
ridsen [15].
11.1
Discussion of models
In section 5.3 Model B of the original case problem was introduced. Te constraints re-
garding patient atendance in Model B are tighter than necessary. As described in section
2.4 the requirement says that a patient must atend maximum one activity each day while
the constraints (5.58)-(5.61) of Model B ensure a patient atend maximum one activity each
24 hours. Te tighter constraints might exclude some feasible solutions and can make it
harder to solve the case problem. However, the time performance of Model B is satisfying.
Model A is also a model of the original problem and was introduced in section 5.2. Taking
the computation time of Model B in comparison with Model A, the tighter constraints have
no insigniﬁcant eﬀect on the time consumption.
Te constraints (5.35)-(5.40) of the allowed time between disjointed sub-activities in both
Model A and Model B are tighter than necessary. Te sub-activities preparation, confronta-
tion and processing of an activity was explained in section 2.3. Te constraints (5.35)-(5.40)
ensure that the sub-activities of an activity are held no earlier or later than the day be-
fore or afer the previous or following sub-activity of the same activity. Since one week is
considered as the cyclic period a weekend or other regular days-oﬀwill not come in be-
tween the sub-activities ensured by the constraints. Such tight time constraints have not
been required. However, it is an advantage in practise that preparation and processing is
not executed long before/afer the confrontation in order for continuous work procedure
for an employee. Te tighter constraints do not make the achieved solutions less feasible
and can be compared with Qampo’s results and results of Mathiasen and Lauridsen.
It is trivial that there is a chance of infeasibility during performing the stepwise heuristic
which can leads to several iterations before reaching a feasible solution which will result

==PAGE:91==

in extended computation time. In applying the stepwise heuristic introduced in section 6.2
a solution to a sub-problem is ﬁxed in the following sub-problem which causes exclusion
of possible optimal and feasible solutions.
Te sof constraints introduced in section 8 are pursuing a fair solution by minimizing
variations in assigned activities to employees. Optimizing fairness in distribution is not a
mandatory requirement, however, including the sof constraints in Model A or Model B
have shown to lead the stepwise heuristic in direction of a feasible solution much faster
seen in the results in section 8.2.3. By including the additional sof constraints and mini-
mize variations in assignments, some feasible solutions might be excluded since the feasi-
ble region is reduced by these constraints.
Te simpliﬁcation of the case problem provides a less complex problem which is solved in
the simpliﬁed model introduced in section 9. Te simpliﬁcations made it possible to solve
the simpliﬁed problem without applying a heuristic since the search space is reduced. An
advantage of the simpliﬁed model is that it can be solved without the stepwise heuristic.
Hence all sub-problems of the case problem is solved simultaneously, and no feasible solu-
tions are excluded from the search space. Te search space of simpliﬁed problem showed to
be too large when testing scheduling of all consecutive sub-activities for more than eight
patient courses. It can be concluded that partition is needed for the large combinatorial
case problem even though simpliﬁcations are implemented.
Qampo has experienced that employees in the hospitals prefer to have lunch break with
colleagues. To meet that wish, employees must have their lunch breaks at the same time
of the day and thus the ﬁxed lunch break at 12 o’clock in the simpliﬁed model will make
great sense in real world. To be precise it is wished from employees that lunch breaks are
scheduled as an activity such that other tasks cannot be planned in the timeslots of the
lunch break. Te models in this thesis handles the lunch breaks in avoiding scheduling
activities in the full valid time interval for a lunch break and leaving at least 30 continuous
minutes available intended for a lunch break. Tis means that other tasks actually can be
planned in these timeslots afer the cyclic scheduling. Tis might be a disadvantage due
to the wish, however, Qampo has experienced that employees anyway ofen change the
times of the scheduled lunch breaks.
Te best achieved results is the two partitioned models (Model A and Model B) of the
original problem solved with the stepwise heuristic succeeded in scheduling consecutive

Lauridsen and Mathiasen [15] have researched a similar case as the one in this thesis how-
ever conditions and requirements have changed since their work. Lauridsen and Mathiasen
[15] used mixed integer programming as well however modelled the problem for a non-
cyclic schedule solution. Te model of Lauridsen and Mathiasen [15] considers diﬀerent
course types. Tese are of diﬀerent combinations of the activities which are constituting
the merged course (PB01 and PB03A), refereed to as patient course in this thesis, as ex-
plained in 2.3.
Lauridsen and Mathiasen [15] do not include all requirements and conditions, as also ex-
pressed in their paper. Lauridsen and Mathiasen do not consider daily lunch breaks for
each employee or neither the other tasks (regular or irregular) individually planned by em-
ployees. As discussed in section 2.3 an activity consists of three sub-activities preparation,
confrontation and processing. Sub-activities are handled in two diﬀerent ways (disjointed
and consecutive) in Model A and Model B of this thesis, discussed in section 5.1.4. Te
sub-activities preparation and processing of an activity are not considered in the model of
Lauridsen and Mathiasen [15] which results in scheduling less time for activities than real
durations.
Lauridsen and Mathiasen [15] achieve to schedule 12 clarifying interviews and six patient
courses of any type (excluding clarifying interview) with start up in the ﬁrst two weeks of
a four-weeks long planning period. Te time consumption is 44.5 hours.
In this thesis, mixed integer programming has been applied as well as in the thesis of Lau-
ridsen and Mathiasen however cyclic scheduling is approached opposite than Lauridsen
and Mathiasen. Tis thesis achieves results of scheduling 10 patient courses and 4 extra
clarifying interview which results in total of 14 clarifying interviews within reasonable com-
putation time. Te feasible solutions are achieved within computation time of less than one
hour using a stepwise heuristic and considering all sub-activities of an activity either as
consecutive or disjointed.

in section 3.2.1, CP is fast to get a feasible solution but not necessarily for an optimal
solution. Qampo schedules a non-cyclic schedule and considers employee’s other tasks,

Outlook
In this section implementation of a cyclic schedules will be discussed. Compared to the
current non-cyclic approach, there exists some diﬀerences in the prioritizing of activities
of patient courses and other tasks. Qampo who is currently responsible for the scheduling
has comment on results from this thesis.
12.1
Discussion of implementation
Tis thesis suggest to apply cyclic scheduling to solve the case problem of continuity of
care. With the approach of cyclic scheduling it is easier to schedule the forecasted demand
of patient courses in psychiatric hospitals. Implementing cyclic scheduling will require
changes in the current prioritizing of employees’ tasks and activities. Current, employees
can plan any task for any timeslot before the schedule of activities of patient courses is
established which to a great extent complicate the scheduling of continuity of care. To
implement cyclic schedules of continuity of care the employees will be asked to plan their
other tasks which are irregular around the activities of patient courses. However, employ-
ees’ regular/cyclic tasks, as weekly research, can be considered in the cyclic scheduling of
continuity of care. Tus non-cyclic tasks, as dentists’ appointments, must be prioritized
afer the activities of patient courses in order to enable scheduling of continuity of care of
full demand. Some employees might not be pleased with the change of have to plan his/her
other (individual) tasks around the scheduled activities of patient courses. However, the
request for a regular and recognisable work schedule of activities of patient courses is met.
12.1.1

Qampo has commented on applying the researched solution approach and implementing
cyclic schedules in the psychiatric hospitals.
Qampo explains that regularity and recognisability in the work schedule of an employee
is highly requested by employees and management in the hospitals wants to prioritise
to meet the request. Terefore, Qampo sees great beneﬁts of the cyclic schedule in that
perspective.

Tis thesis is based on a case study of Hospital Q and Hospital R which is just two branches
among several psychiatric hospitals. Tere exists 11 psychiatric hospitals in the depart-

previously introduced ﬁgure 1. Te model have been constructed for a general purpose

In further research, testing of scheduling in more psychiatric hospitals will be highly pri-

Extending the cyclic period to two weeks is very applicable for including more variances

period of one week the most tight interval is applied.

13
Conclusion
Each of the two formulated models Model A and Model B is partitioned into three sub-
problems and solves the case problem by applying a heuristic solving the sub-problems
stepwise. Testing shows reasonable computation time for scheduling the forecasted de-
mand of 10 patient courses which is the maximum amount of patient courses which can
be scheduled with the current hospital staﬀs. No signiﬁcant diﬀerence showed in the per-
formance of Model A and Model B which was not known before hand. Te cyclic schedul-
ing of consecutive sub-activities of an activity is faster than disjointed sub-activities of an
activity if the individual available time of employees in a regular week enables it.
Te simpliﬁed model of the simpliﬁed case problem is solved without applying a heuristic
within acceptable computation time for the cyclic scheduling of only the most important
sub-activity confrontation. Cyclic scheduling of all sub-activities did not succeed for larger
input sizes. Hence, the simpliﬁed model is only preferred in the case of acceptance of
simpliﬁcations including only cyclic scheduling of the sub-activity confrontation.
Te request for fair schedules is prioritized. Te fairness in a schedule is related to the
distribution of assigned activities to employees of the hospital. If a cyclic schedule is un-
fair, the fairness will decrease over time for each cycle and therefore fairness is even more
important to consider in cyclic scheduling. Suggested sof constraints are included in each
of the models and the maximal variance of activities assigned to employees is minimized.
Tree suggestions of sets of sof constraints pursue diﬀerent fair distributions are exam-
ined due to chosen fairness parameters and time performance. Since the staﬀs in hospitals
consist of individuals with diﬀerent work ability, three parameters are suggested to eval-
uate the fairness of a solution. Te two parameters availability and capability are related
to an employee’s proﬁciency and the third parameter equality is related to the idea of a
common workload to be shared among the employees in the hospital.
Including the suggested sof constraints and objectives in the models, the achieved distri-
butions showed much more fairness due to the chosen parameters compared to the distri-
bution from the models in which fairness was not considered. Te achieved distributions
of activities for 10 patient courses are evaluated for Hospital Q and Hospital R. All achieved
distributions show a satisfying fairness for both hospitals when including one of the sug-
gestions of an objective function. No suggestion showed to perform steady beter than the

other suggestions in all measurement parameters for both hospitals. It can be concluded,
due to diﬀerent staﬀcompositions in hospitals, it is up to the management of a hospital to
choose which fair distribution is preferred.
Minimizing maximal variance in distribution showed beter time consumption in solving
the models with the stepwise heuristic than simply search for a feasible solution. Caused
by the more spread assignments in the solution of the ﬁrst sub-problem I (assignment prob-
lem), which makes the solving of the second sub-problem II (time scheduling) easier and
reduce the computation time of the most time consuming sub-problem (time scheduling).
Both models are applicable; a feasible and fair cyclic schedule is found within reasonable
computation time. Te approach of cyclic scheduling is distinct to the current approach of
non-cyclic schedules provided by Qampo. It is succeeded with cyclic scheduling to sched-
ule continuity of care for patient courses of the forecasted demand and comply with all
requirements which was not reached in the thesis of Lauridsen and Mathiasen [15].
In the current solution approach provided by Qampo, hospital staﬀis allowed to individ-
ually plan other tasks. With the current approach it is not possible to schedule continuity
of care matching demand. Te researched solution approach of cyclic scheduling solved
with a stepwise heuristic gives promising results. However, the following change in ap-
proach must be accepted by the hospital: Only regular other tasks are considered in the
cyclic scheduling of activities of patient courses, and occasional tasks are scheduled in
consideration of the cyclic schedule. When irregular discontinuation as holidays occurs a
rescheduling can be performed with Qampo’s current model - even suggested by Qampo
themselves.
It can be concluded from the results of this thesis, that the researched solution approach
enables a higher utilisation of resources in the psychiatric hospitals, and with the model
suggestions, a feasible and fair solution for cyclic schedules of continuity of care is found
within reasonable time. Furthermore, the cyclic schedules meets the request of regularity
in working schedules for the employees in the hospital.
