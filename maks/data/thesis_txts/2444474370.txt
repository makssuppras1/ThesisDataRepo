Abstract
Background:Bioreactors for drug development and disease modelling are used to
achieve functional mini-organ (tissue construct) models that reect the behaviour
of human adult organs. Local static- and dynamic mechanical strain has proven
to be essential for proper mini-organ tissue development with cyclic uniaxial strain
specically important for heart- and skeletal models.
Method: Growing constructs between a set of exible poles has proven to be an
ecient way to achieve 3D mini-organs but lack the possibility of inducing strain.
This thesis investigates the design, manufacturing and performance of a 3D printed
topology optimized actuator (5x5x0.85mm) used to deect a set of exible pillars to
induce cyclic strain. The actuator and pillars are printed as a monolithic structure
through stereolithography using PEGDA (MW700) and evaluated according to out-
put/input displacement ratio and long term cyclic actuation using visual assessment
and computer simulations.
Results: An actuator designed to deect a single pillar demonstrated an output/in-
put displacement ratio between 2.07 to 2.89 (n=7) using an input displacement of
191 ± 14 resulting in a max. pillar displacement of 448 ± 38µm and the possibility
of cyclic actuation for 106 cycles without signicant decrease in performance.
An actuator designed to deect multiple pillars simultaneously was not able to
generate pillar displacement with an input displacement of 220µm. The choice of
material proved to distort certain dimensions when introducing the structures to an
aqueous environment which decreased performance.
Conclusion: This thesis shows that it is possible to 3D print a mechanical actuator
that can be used to deect a exible pillar with a clear physical distinction between
the input displacement source and potential tissue construct.

Introduction
1.1
Problem Statement
With 31% of all global deaths being related to cardiovascular diseases (CVDs) it is
the leading cause of death, according to the World Health Organization (WHO) [1].
This corresponds to 17.9 million CVD related deaths annually which is only expected
to rise [2]. Thus, pharmaceutical solutions to CVDs are of high priority. With an
estimated cost of 1.8 billion US dollars and an average of 12 years from initiation
to commercial launch, methods for accurate and high-throughput early-stage drug
screening are needed [3].
Advanced, high throughput and functional mini-organ models (organoids) that ac-
curately reect the response of human adult tissue have therefore attracted attention
within regenerative medicine and drug discovery [4]. Organoid development relies on
the maturation of appropriate stem cells and human embryonic stem cells (hESCs)
have shown great promise for maturation of cardiomyocytes (CMs) as they can dif-
ferentiate into any cell type of the body. The high utility of hESC derives a demand
that is dicult to meet since the cells are harvested from the early stage embryo
thereby raising serious ethical questioning [5]. In 2006 Yamanaka et al. found an
alternative to this ethical dilemma by inducing pluripotency in a somatic mouse
cell, referred to as induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs). In 2007 iPSCs were de-
rived from human broblasts giving access to stem cells that can dierentiate CMs
without the morally complicated provenance [5].
Local static- and dynamic mechanical forces have been found essential to guide
stem cell maturation and much current eort is focused on the development of
bioreactors capable of mimicking the mechanical and electrical surroundings of car-
diac tissue [68]. Uniaxial stretch of contractile mini-organs such as skeletal- and
cardiomyocytes have shown to inuence proliferation [9], alignment [10] and con-
tractile behaviour [11]. However, the ability to induce strain on cardiac mini-organs
are limited to 2D cultures which do not accurately represent the 3D organization
of CMs [1214]. In order to avoid necrotic tissue due to the lack of nutrient supply
and to allow a high throughput solution, a bioreactor capable of inducing signicant
uniaxial strains on micrometre sized 3D cardiac tissues is needed.
A method developed by Vandenburgh et al. has shown the possibility to mature
3D cardiac micro constructs between two exible poles cast in silicone [13]. This

Background
This chapter covers the principles of mechanotransduction which is a term used
for the cellular response to the mechanical environment. Several devices, termed
bioreactors, have been designed to mimic the in vivo environment of stem cells in
order to control the mechanotransductive mechanisms. Dierent bioreactors will be
discussed along with their respective advantages and disadvantages to discuss the
requirements for a bioreactor device focused on skeletal- and heart muscle construct
maturation.
2.1
Mechanotransduction
Cells are constantly exposed to mechanical stimulation. The beating heart cycli-
cally stretches the myocardium throughout its lifetime, the blood ow exposes the
endothelial lining of our capillary network to shear stresses and the bones of our
body are being strained, bend and compressed as we move around.
It is now well established that cells adapt to their mechanical environment and
that the developing organs depend heavily on mechanical cues in order to properly
grow [6,15].
The conversion of mechanical cues into biochemical signaling is known as mechan-
otransduction and mimicking the natural mechanical environment is widely acknowl-
edged to be a critical step for guiding stem cell fate determination in vitro [7].

to each other and allows cell-cell communication. Integrin attaches the cell to the extracellular matrix making it

according strain [6].
Mechanotransduction can be aected by external forces that act upon the cell as
well as internally generated forces used to sense the local environment [16].
Figure 2.1 highlights some of the membrane proteins involved in mechanotransduc-
tion including the transmembrane proteins cadherin and integrin.

Cadherin allows cell-cell communication by providing a link between the cytoskele-
ton and cadherin molecules of neighbouring cells. The cytoskeleton comprised of
actin, microtubules and intermediate laments are all important components in up-
holding the mechanical integrity of the cell and act as links between the mechanosen-
sitive membrane proteins and the nucleus allowing mechanical inputs to inuence
gene expression [6,12].
Another class of transmembrane proteins, named integrins, link cells to the extra-
cellular matrix (ECM). Jacot et al. showed that the ECM has a signicant inuence
on cardiomyocyte maturation by seeding rat ventricular myocytes on polyacrylamid
gels with dierent stiness. They showed that the contractile force signicantly vary
with substrate stiness and that maximal force of contraction was obtained with an
ECM stiness of 10kPa [17].
Besides the membrane proteins cadherin and integrin, the cell membrane contains
mechanosensitive channels. A subcategory of such channels include stretch-activated
ion channels (SAC) and are found in both skeletal- and cardiac myocytes [6].
These mechanosensitive mechanisms are not only important during tissue matura-
tion, as heart related diseases such as stretch induced arrhythmia are thought to be
linked to the function of SACs [18].
2.2
Mechanotransduction Inuences Cardiogenesis
Spontaneous contraction of the human heart can be detected a mere three weeks
after fertilization and is the rst functional organ to develop [19].
From the initiation of the rst heart beat, cardiomyocytes are exposed to cyclic
mechanical stretching which is believed to guide cardiogenesis [20]. An extensive
amount of research has therefore been conducted to elucidate the eects of stretch
on cardiomyocyte maturation, however the exact mechanisms are still not fully un-
derstood [16,21].
Vandenburgh et al. tested the morphological response of neonatal cardiac rat cells
to constantly increased uniaxial strain when seeded on elastic membranes [10]. They
found that cells aligned parallel to the stretch direction in contrast to perpendicular
alignment when exposed to 15-20% cyclic stretch which indices that the cellular
alignment is not only aected by strain but also strain type. [10,22]. Additionally,
once alignment in one direction had been obtained, changing the strain type did not
re-align the cells in another direction indicating that cell plasticity decreases during
maturation [10].

Cell proliferation has also shown to be dependent on mechanical load. Late-fetal
and post-natal cardiac mass increases primarily by hypertrophy whereas early em-
bryonic cardiogenesis is driven mainly by hyperplasia [20]. Tobita et al. showed that
cyclic mechanical stretch signicantly increased cell proliferation of 3D constructs
of early embryonic ventricular cells from the chicken [23,24].
However, not only proliferation and morphology is aected by mechanotransduction.
In vitro organ models of contractile tissue is often evaluated by their force of contrac-
tion during either twitch tension or isometric contraction [8,13,23]. Twitch tension
of biopsies from healthy adult human hearts have shown to have a twitch tension of
44 ± 3.7mN/mm2 at a contraction frequency of 174 ± 4 beats per minute [25,26],
whereas the isometric contraction of human myocardium has been measured at
51 ± 8mN/mm2 [27]. The myocardium experiences cyclic strain of ≈20% as the
heart beats.
Several studies have documented the eect of 5 −20% stretch at
0.5 −3Hz on cardiogenesis showing that such strain regimes aect the development
of the contractile units of muscle cells, known as sarcomeres [7, 20, 28]. However,
in vitro matured cardiac tissues have shown signicantly lower contractile forces,
typically ranging between 0.1 to 4.4mN/mm2 with one study by Zhang et al. re-
porting a contractile force of 11.8mN/mm2 measured on a centimetre sized cardiac
patch matured from human embryonic stem cell derived cardiomyocytes. [8,29,30].
Thus, even though much progress have been made no cardiac construct have been
matured with the functionality of adult cardiomyocytes.
Zimmermann and Eschenhagen, pioneers within engineered heart tissue develop-
ment, have explored the eect of strain type on maturation of 3D cardiac tissues [11].
They tested the twitch tension magnitude of actively contracting 3D cardiac tissue
constructs of neonatal rat heart cells exposed to three types of external mechan-
ical resistance; Isometric (xed length), Isotonic (xed resistance) and auxotonic
(external resistance increases during contraction), as illustrated in Figure 2.2a-c.
Auxotonic resistance showed to more than double the force of twitch tension con-
traction compared to isometric and isotonic which indicates that this type of external
resistance is superior to the alternatives [11]. This type of mechanical contraction
has since been used by several groups to promote tissue maturation of contractile
tissues [8,13,26]. However, the lack of bioreactors capable of both auxotonic resis-
tance and mechanical stretch makes it dicult to truly investigate the inuence of
dierent kinds of load- and strain regimes.

2.3
Bioreactor Requirements
Based on the eect of mechanotransduction, a bioreactor for cardiac tissue matu-
ration should be able to create uniaxial cyclic strains of 20% to promote cellular
alignment, proliferation and contractile behaviour while also supporting auxotonic
resistance. Furthermore, adult cardiac- and skeletal muscle cells are organised in
complex 3D tissues and are poorly resembled in 2D in vitro cell cultures [13]. Hence,
a bioreactor is required to support the formation of 3D constructs in order to imitate
the in vivo structure of such tissues.
Due to the limited ability to create vascularized tissues in vitro, nutrient and waste
transportation rely solely on diusion [31]. With the exception of a cartilage, skin
and the cornea all tissues have a dense vascular network such that each cell is within
a 100µm proximity of a capillary [31]. Thus, it is necessary to either incorporate a
vascular network or shrink the tissue model such that sucient nutrient is available
through diusion. Consequently, at the time being a size constraint of 200µm di-
ameter is required.
Mechanotransduction is not the only parameter that aect skeletal- and cardiac
maturation.
Ronaldson-Bouchard et al.
recently showed that electromechanical
stimulation (electrical pacing and auxotonic resistance) inuenced contractile forces,
hypertrophy and sarcomere development which indicates that mimicking mechan-
otransduction alone is not sucient to recreate the in vivo environment [8].

strain a monolayer seeded on the strained side of the beam (Figure 2.3C). The most
widely used commercial bioreactor is the Flexcell Tension System used for both uni-
axial and equibiaxial strain of 2D tissues [32]. This bioreactor works by seeding cells
on a exible collagen-I or elastin coated silicone membrane. The exible membrane
is then pneumatically stretched to create the desired strain as illustrated in Figure
2.3A and D [32].

Instead of going through a time consuming trial and error approach to design an
alternative and ecient solution it was found more relevant to take advantage of a
design method known as Topology Optimization (TO). The TO algorithm used to
generate the structures for this thesis has been developed and executed with the
much appreciated help from DTU Mechanics. A detailed description of how topol-
ogy optimization works is outside the scope of this thesis but has been described in
several books and reviews [3941].
Briey, topology optimization is an approach to create computer generated struc-
tural designs based on an initial design domain with a homogeneous distribution of
material and clearly dened boundary conditions without any presumptions of the
nal design outcome [41]. The material distribution within the design domain is
then iteratively reorganized based on a nite element analysis that calculates the
distributed material's (structure) performance based on an initial optimality crite-
ria. Once further rearrangement of material does not result in better performance
the structure is said to have converged towards the optimal material distribution for
the given criteria [41]. In order to generate the topology optimized design, boundary
conditions and an optimality criteria have to be dened which will be done next for
the structure used in this thesis.

4.1
Manufacturing Method
3D printing was investigated as a possible manufacturing method because of the
complex geometry of the TO-generated design combined with the sub millimetre
sized pillars and the desire to create a monolithic structure in biocompatible mate-
rials.
The rst 3D printing method, also known as additive manufacturing (AM) was de-
veloped in 1986 with the idea that a three dimensional structure could be created by
printing consecutive layers on top of each other [43]. The method has now become
widely accessible with several dierent types of AM methods including extrusion-
based, powder-based, two-photon and stereolithography with subcategories using
slightly dierent methods to deposit material within each category [43,44].
The emergence of AM has been accelerated by the access to computer aided design
(CAD) software for structural design such as PTC CREO and Autodesk Inventor.
A complex structure can now be created on the computer via a CAD software and
quickly materialized via a 3D printer. Additionally, AM procedures which support

As mentioned, the structure is created in a layer-by-layer fashion which requires
that the 3D CAD model is split into a series of 2D slices. The distance between
each slice corresponds to the layer thickness such that each slice is printed once.
The series of slices is created by a software program which converts the CAD-design
to an .stl le. Because each layer is printed individually it is essential to ensure
adequate bonding, adhesion or fusion between layers in order to create a continuous
structure. This also requires that the printed layers stay xed in their position such
that the following layer is printed correctly in relation to the rest of the structure.
Thus, the initial layer is printed on a stable platform which serves as support for the
successive layers and for many AM methods additional pillars are printed to act as
support for free hanging structures which are removed in a post printing procedure.
For this design, the choice of AM method relies on the available materials, printing
resolution, printing time and the layer-to-layer bonding.
The material must be biocompatible with a printing resolution of <100µm with
99.9% dimensional accuracy. In order to nd the most suitable approach for this
specic project a series of printing methods have therefore been evaluated.
Extrusion Based Printing (EB): Currently the most popular AM method for
tissue engineering applications, EB allows printing in a range of biocompatible- as
well as biological materials [44,45]. Polymer laments are extruded through a mov-
able nozzle by a pressure gradient created either pneumatically of mechanically. The
downsides of this method is the increased manufacturing time at high layer com-
plexity and a resolution limited by the size of the nozzle opening (≈100µm) [46].
Moreover, the laments align in the direction of the movable nozzle which generates
an anisotropic structure meaning that its properties are directionally dependent and
the extruded laments rely on a starting- and end point making it unsuitable for
tall structures with limited xy-dimensions [45]. This makes it a poor choice for this
specic design since it includes vertical structures with limited xy-dimensions.
Selective Laser Sintering (SLS): Also known as powder-based printing. This
method uses a powder bed consisting of micro beads (particle size of 10-150µm) and
can be used for both metal and polymers making it attractive for tougher scaolds as
used in tissue engineering concerned with bone matrix formation [47]. Powder-based
printing relies on a laser to heat thermoplastic beads above their glass transition
temperature Tg for amorphous polymers and above the melting temperature Tm for
crystalline polymer [47]. The resolution of SLS rely on the particle size and hatch
distance, which describes the needed distance between laser tracks within the same

Stereolithography (SLA): SLA is the approach used in this thesis and was the
rst developed type of AM by Charles Hull in 1986 [43]. It relies on a spatially
controlled photopolymerization of a liquid resin consisting of a monomer, photo ab-
sorber and photo initiator [43,48,49]. The printer itself consists of a vat to contain
the resin, a light source to activate the photo initiator and a movable fabrication
platform to allow layer-by-layer printing. Two approaches to SLA exist; namely
bottom-up and top-down. The former initiates photopolymerization from the top
and moves the structure downwards through the resin as each layer is produced
whereas the latter initiates polymerization from the bottom [49]. Photo absorber
is added to allow z-direction resolution as it limits the penetration depth of the
light source and layer-to-layer bonding is obtained by securing that the unreacted
functional groups of the preceding layer is polymerized to the newly formed layer.
Thus, mismatching the concentration of photo absorber and slicing thickness/plat-
form movement can result in non-attached layers. On the other hand, an excessive
overlap can cause overexposure increasing the risk of polymerization outside the
specied area [49].
SLA is a dot-based printing method making it useful for vertical structures with lim-
ited xy-dimensions and is known for its high resolution which is primarily limited
by the pixel pitch of the light source (xy-resolution) and absorber concentration (z-
resolution) [49]. Furthermore, production time does not depend on layer complexity
but is limited in the available materials. The photo absorber has to absorb light of
the same wavelength used to activate the photo initiator which must be matched to
the choice of monomer to initiate polymerization.
Two-photon printing (2PP): 2PP is similar to SLA in the sense that printing
relies on photopolymerization of a liquid resin, but can achieve sub micrometre reso-
lution as the only available AM method [50]. However, the method rely on expensive
equipment not commonly accessible and manufacturing time is extremely slow [51].
Since the structure in this project must support a tissue construct in the micro- to
millimetre range, a sub micrometre resolution is not required.
For these reasons, SLA was chosen as the appropriate AM method and a custom-
built printer applying the top-down approach has been used as described by Zhang

Discussion
3D Mini-organ models of contracting tissues can be engineered by maturing my-
ocytes around a set of exible pillars which generates an auxotonic resistance shown
to increase functional performance [11]. The cardiomyocytes of the native tissue
experience constant cyclic stretch as the heart beats which manifests as cyclic uni-
axial strain and has shown to be another inuencing factor in cardiogenesis [7]. This
thesis sought to investigate the hypothesis that an actuating micro device could be
designed to bend a set of exible beams to create a system that supports both uni-
axial cyclic strain and auxotonic resistance. The actuating device was generated
via a topology optimization (TO)-routine and printed via stereolithography using a
50% (v/v) PEGDA (MW700) resin. The TO-design was generated based on the op-
timality criteria of maximizing an inverted output displacement from a xed input
displacement Uout
Uin and resulted in a 2D force inverter design.
Most 3D printing methods require the implementation of support structures, and
stereolithography is no exception. Printing of the structures took 15 minutes with
an additional 10-15 minutes being used for removal of support structures. This was
done manually and posed a certain risk of damaging the prints and creates a poten-
tial bottleneck if this method is to be used in large scale production. One solution
to this problem would be to design support structures that automatically break
when actuation is initiated or by using a dierent manufacturing method that does
not require support structures. Such methods do exist, but was not chosen in this
thesis as it includes other limitations such as lower resolution and the production of
porous structures.
Once the design phase was completed a simulation was used to evaluate the force
inverter performance compared to the raw TO-design. Interestingly, the simulated
design was shown to generate a larger Uout
Uin ratio than the raw TO-design. It was
expected that any design changes would only decrease performance since the TO-
routine is specically designed to nd the most optimal solution. A reason for this
may be found in the fact that the applied TO-routine generates a 2D design which
is then evaluated as a 3D structure. TO-routines have been developed for 3D struc-
tures but are also signicantly more computationally demanding compared to 2D,
and since all forces acting on the force inverter can be described in two dimensions
it was decided that a 2D TO-generated design was sucient [62].
It was decided to print the structures in a 50% (v/v) monomer concentration resin

compared to a 17.8% (v/v) monomer concentration resin. A 90% (v/v) monomer

a non-swelling print surface with a swelling hydrogel was most likely the cause of
the observed dimensional distortion. Kim et al. showed that swelling of PEGDA
networks can be reduced by using a lower molecular weight monomer (PEGDA
(MW258)) serving as a possible alternative material compliant with the same man-
ufacturing method [63].

Uin ratios ranging between
2.07 to 2.89 which results in pillar displacements of 455µm and 635µm which is suf-
ciently large to induce a 20% strain of a 2mm construct. No tests were performed
with actual tissue constructs, but simulations showed that actuating a single pillar
in a set of two, deects both pillars. This creates a non-uniform stretch distribution
and requires additional displacement of the pillar. A more suitable solution might
therefore be to actuate both pillars simultaneously. Alternatively, the non-actuated
pillar could be replaced with a rigid attachment point.
Long term actuation was also shown to be possible with a single output force in-
verter and showed close to constant performance over the course of 93 hours. The
slight decrease may be caused by measurement errors or deformation of the hydro-
gel at the contact point between the ball mill used for input displacement and the
hydrogel structure. A discrepancy between the actual and true input displacement
was found and increasing the surface area of the contact point between the external
displacement source and the force inverter would be a great optimization of the
experimental setup.

with a setup termed I-wire by Sidorov et al. [64]. Instead of using a set of ex-

tissue. This approach can thereby create auxotonic resistance be letting the rod
stay stationary and induce strain by moving it. This approach was briey discussed
as a solution in Section 3, but was not chosen since the actuating mechanism is in
direct contact with the construct. The method chosen in this report creates a clear
physical distance between the external mechanical input and the tissue construct.
Thereby it requires no physical interaction with the cellular compound after cell
seeding.

limited to 2D structures. This is sucient for certain types of tissue but cannot
properly mimic the environment of 3D organized organs which has been the focus
of this report.
The design developed here does not require the use of clamps to xate a construct
and can therefore be used for constructs with limited structural integrity while using
a concept that has been shown to support millimetre sized organoids. However, the
method used in this thesis require that the entire mechanical unit (force inverter) is
changed for each individual construct. Due to the observed variation between prints
manual adjustment of the input displacement is needed to achieve similar strain
magnitudes between prints which is not required with other bioreactors. However, it
must be noted that several parameters of the design can be optimized and achieving
similar output/input displacement ratios across prints may be feasible.

Conclusion & Outlook
This thesis tested the hypothesis that a 3D printed micro actuating device could be
designed to induce a signicant cyclic strain of a tissue construct matured between a
set of exible pillars. A set of functional requirements were derived from a literature
study and used to dene the requirements for the device developed in this thesis.
The design is capable of generating the required pillar displacement for a 20% strain
of a 2mm construct while supporting auxotonic resistance. It was possible to cre-
ate long term cyclic strain without compromising performance while proving the
possibility to mature constructs despite introducing holes in the container used for
cell suspension. Certain design changes are required for long term cell maturation
as cells were found to detached from the current design and it was not possible to
observe if constructs of 200µm diameter could be achieved.
Further optimization is needed for this to work as a high throughput solution since
varying Uout
Uin ratios were found between devices. The post processing process of man-
ually removing support structures and the vertical displacement of the free hanging
structure are both hypothesized to inuence this. Choosing a material that does
not experience swelling or optimizing the resin formula for minimized swelling and
optimal strength would be a great step forward and make it easier to predict me-
chanical behaviour. The design tested in this thesis relies on the displacement of
a single exible pillar in a set of two. To obtain a more uniform stretch of the
construct an alternative approach where both pillars are displaced simultaneously
is most likely the best approach. A multiple output actuator was designed as a high
throughput solution but did not succeed in creating a signicant pillar displacement
but as material choice is optimized this may be achieved as the main cause was due
to dimensional changes observed during swelling.

Cell seeding Procedure
This appendix explains the cell seeding procedure used for C2C12 myoblast seeding.
Preparation of the print
1. Immediately after 3D printing, use ethanol to remove traces from the adhesion tape on the
cover glass.
2. Transfer the print to a 6-well plate lled with milli-Q such that the entire print is well
submerged.
3. Replace milli-Q twice: After 5min and 1h. Afterwards leave the print in milli-Q for 24h.
4. Take the structure out and carefully cut the support structures using a scalpel.
5. Prepare a solution of 5mg/mL LAP using 1.5mL 20mg/mL LAP and 4.5mL milli-Q and
submerge the structures herein.
6. Expose the print to UV-light for 45 min.
7. Remove the LAP solution and suspend the print in milli-Q. Replace milli-Q three times.
After 5min, 10min and 1h.
Sterilization of print
1. Inside LAF Bench wash the print with sterile Phosphate Buerede Saline (PBS) twice in a
well plate before transferring to a new sterile well plate.
2. Submerge the print in PBS with 2% P/S for 10min.
3. Wash the constructs twice with sterile PBS and expose the print to UV light for 10min.
4. Place the print in fresh PBS,
The print is now ready for cell seeding
Preparation of GelMA/C2C12-cell solution
1. Prepare 250µL solution of: 7mg/mL Gelatin methacrylol (GelMA), 1mg/mL LAP and
16 ∗106 cells/mL.
2. Remove all excess PBS but leave the surface of the print hydrated.
3. Suspend 2.79µL of the solution into the well corresponding to 44,640cells.
Expose the print containing the gelMA/cell solution to 365nm wavelenght light for 1 minute. Add
Dulbecco's Modied Eagles Medium (DMEM) high glucose + 2% FBS + 1% P/S.
Change DM every third day.
