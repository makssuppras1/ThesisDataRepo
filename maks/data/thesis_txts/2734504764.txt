Abstract
This master’s thesis investigates the sulfation mechanisms of fly ash from Waste-to-Energy (WtE) power plants through
experimental analysis and modeling.
A new Experimental method was developed to analyze sulfation kinetics on fly ash samples, yielding reproducible results.
Employing Fourier Transform Infrared Spectroscopy (FT-IR) and X-ray Fluorescence (XRF) techniques, sulfation dynamics
were monitored.
There was performed sulfation of fly ash from Maagebjerg Værket under 400 ppm SO2 and with a temperature range of
400 oC to 600 oC. The results indicate that the sulfation of the ash deposits takes place faster than expected, and in the
duration of a couple of hours, most of the chlorine content has been sulfate.
In this thesis, a new model was developed from three sulfation models from literature with the addition of mass transport
terms. The model was able to predict the sulfation of the fly ash to some extent and indicate the presence of small-size
particles of KCl and NaCl.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation and background
With the management of municipal solid waste (MSW) being a global challenge, there is a need for efficient and sustainable
treatment method [1]. WtE(Waste to Energy) technology is a mature technology that can simultaneously address the chal-
lenges of waste management and energy demand[2]. There are, however, technical limitations and challenges that need to be
solved. The environment inside a WtE boiler is more hazardous than in coal-powered plants. The heterogeneous nature of
waste and higher content of critical elements such as chlorine led to higher amounts of boiler corrosion, more deposit formation,
and elevated dioxin levels.
These problems are handled today by having a lower operating steam temperature, as it lowers the rate of corrosion[3]. When
the steam temperature is lower, the efficiency is as well. The efficiency of the WtE plant can be increased by solving the
corrosion issue.
If the sulfur contained in the gas phase is increased, it was found that it could remove chlorine from the particles before
they attach as deposits and lower the amount of chlorine in the deposits; this reduces corrosion[4].
Hunsinger et al.[5] first developed a process to increase the sulfur content by recycling both SO2 and HCl to produce
H2SO4; the sulfuric acid is then sent in with the secondary aris and combed rapidly to SO2. This resulted in a five-time higher
SO2 concentration in the gas phase[5].
Anderson et al[4] developed the process by using SO2 and H2O2 to form the sulfuric acid. This process was installed at a
Danish waste-to-energy power plant, M˚abjergværket. the initial result proved very promising with respect to 50% reduction of
dioxin, and the corrosion rate was reduced to a fourth. The disadvantage of the test was that very hard deposits were formed,
which were difficult to remove. It was suspected that these deposit formations were caused by condensed sulfation(Sulfation
of chloride and calcium particles in the deposit). The sulfation reaction may lead to sintering and hardening of deposits.
In the literature, the sulfation of alkaline chlorides has primarily been studied at temperatures in the interval 450 oC to
900 oC, with experiments conducted at lower temperatures. Condense sulfation of alkali chlorides is primarily focused on larger
KCl and NaCl particles with diameters in the range of 90 µm to 250 µm and higher temperatures of up to 1000 oC[6][7][8] .
The sulfation of CaO and CaCO3 has been studied extensively[9], but primarily in the temperature range of 600 oC to 1000 oC
and with inlet of SO2 above 1000 ppm [9]. In WtE plants, the KCl and NaCl in deposits originate from the KCl and NaCl
sub-micron aerosols[10]. The temperature of the deposit at the superheaters is in the temperature range of 300 oC-550 oC [11].
The condensed sulfation of small size particles of KCl and NaCl aerosols, and there interactions and competition with
CaO/CaCO3 sulfation at temperature range (300 oC-600 oC) have not been investigated experimentally. In the literature,
no model has been developed that considers simultaneously the condensed sulfation of NaCl, KCl and CaO has not been
developed and validated. .
A partnership was formed with MEC(M˚abjerg Energy Centre),BW(Babcock & Wilcox), and DTU CHEC, focusing on
understanding the condensed sulfation of fly ash and deposit. MEC provided DTU CHEC with serval fly ash samples, and
there was a preformed test under sulfur recirculation conditions. The initial measurement campaign at MEC, by Whenhao
indicates that the condensed-phase sulfation of alkali compounds is key to understanding the formation of hard deposits.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
1.2. PURPOSE AND GOALS
1.2
Purpose and goals
The purpose of this master’s thesis project is to gain an initial understanding of the deposit’s condensed sulfation under sulfur
recirculation conditions. There are five concrete goals for this master thesis.
1. Development of experimental method investigate condensed sulfation of fly ash from WtE plants.
2. Development of analytical methods used to follow the sulfation of the fly ash.
3. Intial understanding of condense sulfation of fly ash
4. Validation of known sulfation models with sulfation experiment of KCl,NaCl and CaO.
5. Development of a model to simulate the sulfation of deposits.
1.3
Structure of thesis
The thesis starts off with a literature survey to give the reader the theoretical knowledge needed for the rest of the work.
This is first an introduction to the technology of WtE and sulfur recirculation. A brief introduction to aerosol formation and
deposit formation in WtE plants. The last part of the literature focuses on sold gas reaction and sulfation reaction with a
focus on KCl,NaCl, and CaO. The next two chapters will describe the experimental procedure and the development of the
optimal experiment. After the experimental chapter, the model development chapter will introduce the sulfation models used
for the complete model. The model development chapter will present the PDE(Partial differential equation) model’s progress
and development. In the results chapter, the main findings from the sulfation experiments will be presented, and the results
will be compared with model simulation. In the discussion chapter, the results will be set into the context of the problems
which to be solved by this thesis. In the future work section, the work needed to continue the work of this thesis will be
presented. The thesis will be rounded off with the conclusion.

Chapter 2
Literature study
Introduction and purpose of the Literature study
This chapter will introduce Waste to Energy (WtE) in general, from the overall design of a modern WtE power plant to the
different unit operations to clean the flue gas. This will give the reader the basic knowledge of a WtE plant. The sulfur
recirculation section will introduce the technology that increases the sulfur concentration. The thermodynamics properties
section will give primary melting points pure components and mixtures relevant for the sulfation of fly ash. The gas-solid
reaction will introduce the most common model for gas-solid sulfation reactions to present different modeling approaches that
can be used to develop a model for the sulfation of fly ash.
Finally, the Alkaline choroids sulfation section will introduce the sulfation of chlorine in the gas phase briefly and then in
dealing describe the current model of heterogeneous sulfation of sodium and potassium. These models are those that will be
further on in the thesis.

2.1. WASTE TO ENERGY TECHNOLOGY
2.1
Waste to energy technology
There is a potential to improve sustainability by improving the management of municipal solid waste (MSW). The EU
directive[12] states the desirable options for MSW management. First, waste should be avoided generated, and then reuse or
recycling is preferred. If the first two options are not possible, then waste recovery should be used[13]. Waste to Energy(WtE)
falls into the category of recovery as the waste is burned to generate electricity.
Figure 2.1: Schematise of modern waste to energy plant, adapted from Solid Waste Technology & Management [14].
2.1.1
WtE plant design
In this section, there will be a focus on a typical European waste incinerator. A simplified schematic is shown in figure 2.1.
Preprocessing and waste delivery
The waste is transported by garbage trucks to the WtE plant. The waste is delivered directly to the storage pit or first to a
tipping floor[15]. In the tipping hall, large objects are removed before the waste is tipped into the storage pit. To homogenize
the waste, overhead crane grabs blend the waste and their plastic bags up; the grabs ensure a good mix of the low and high
heating value of waste. The waste enters the furnace by the feed chute, which delivers the waste to the furnace by gravity
while ensuring that the feed chute is airtight.
Furnace
The main technology for waste combustion is moving grate-firing, rotary kiln, and fluidized bed combustion.
The most
commonly used technology in Europa is moving grate[16]. The waste enters from the feed chute on the moving grates, and
it is on the grates that the combustion takes place. The purpose of the grates is to mix the waste and ensure airflow to the
combustion while transporting the bottom ash through the furnace[14], in figure 2.2 there is illustrated a grate system by
Babcock & Wilcox. The grate runs in a forward and reverses motion, which causes the new waste to push the waste down
through the grate by gravity; this is illustrated in figure 2.2a[17]. The height of waste on the grate decreases with the travel
on the grate. The oxygen is supplied in two ways, beneath the grate, called the primary air, and right before the boiler, called
the secondary air.

Combustion process
The two main process schemes of the combustion on a moving grate are downward combustion and upward combustion. The
Combustion scenario depends on the primary air temperature [19]. Downward combustion, also known as the classic model,
was proposed by Rogers[20]. This is where the ignition starts on the top of the waste, and the combustion continues vertically
down until it reaches the grate; the combustion is spread into different zones. First is the drying zone, where waste is heated
by radiation from combustion, and water vapor is released from the waste. Afterward, waste is heated further, and then
the pyrolyze starts, which releases organic compounds that can be ignited, and the ignition of the waste is initiated. The
combustion of the waste goes from the top and proceeds vertical downwards until there is only the bottom ash left and the
the flame ends, the bottom ash is then collect in the bottom of the grate[20][21].
Frey et al.[22] did several measurements on temperature and concentration profiles along the grate that support the model.
Figure 2.3: Illustration of combustion process on a grate, left downwards combustion proposed by Rogersl[20] and right
upwards combustion proposed by Markovic et al. [19]. Illustration adapted from Yan et al[23].
Upward combustion Markovic et al. [19] proposed the upwards combustion model which takes place if the primary air is
preheated to a temperature high enough for auto-ignition at the grate bottom, auto ignition for MSW has been reported as
low as 443 K, but is primarily at a temperature above 493 K[19].In upward combustion, the heat from the primary air heats
the waste up to the point where the drying start. The water content is evaporated, and the drying zone increases upwards
along the grate. The bottom residue is dried and heated to the point where volatilization starts until the waste starts to
burn(called the combustion zone). The combustion zone consumes all the oxygen, and pyrolysis takes place on top of the
combustion zone. The gases released from the pyrolysis are combusted when mixed with the secondary air, which increases
the drying on top by radiation from the combustion of the secondary air.

Furnace geometry
Figure 2.4: Illustration of different types of furnace geometry, Parallel flow, counter flow and center flow, adapted from G¨oerner
et al. [24].
The flue gas is transported up from the grate to the boiler. The entrance to the boiler can be designed in several different
locations. There are three major designs of the flow of the flue gas: Parallel flow, counter flow and center flow. Figure 2.4
illustrates the 3 different design options.
In parallel flow, the flue gas travels parallel to the waste; in the counter-current flow, the inlet to the boiler is at the start
of the grate, and the flue gas travels reverse to the residue. The choice of geometry affects the region of the grate where the
heat transfer is increased by convection[24]. Parallel flow is often used for a design where the after-burning needs more heat,
and counter-current is used when the fulls have a high water content as there is more heat transfer to the drying phase. The
secondary air is located at the boiler entrance and affects the flow going into the boiler.
Boiler
The Boiler’s purpose is to transfer heat from the combustion to increase the pressure and temperature of the steam used in
the turbines. The temperature of flue gas going into the boiler is often in the range of 1273 K to 1473 K. The first part of the
boiler is the heat transfer from the flue is primarily from radiation; the tubes of water are therefore placed along the walls in
the first part of the boiler[25]. The flue gas is passed downwards again, then upwards in the first three passes in a four-pass
boiler. In this section, the water in this part of the boiler is where the vapor is formed; the first part of the boiler is called
evaporates. The vapor is then transported to the superheater that is placed after the four passes. at the fourth pass, the
temperature of the flue gas is drooped to approximately 900 K. In this temperature region, convection begins to dominate the
heat transfer. The superheats are, therefore, placed in the middle for increased surface area with the flue gas.

Ash particles

Thermophoresis
Thermophoresis was first observed in the 1870ties by an experiment where the location of dust depended on which areas were
heated[31]. Thermophoresis takes place when particles travel in a fluid with none uniform temperature field, here the particles
are drawn in to the cold area. This effect occurs when the fluid has a higher temperature than the tube. A higher temperature
means higher mean kinetic energy and, thus, higher kinetic velocity. The gas molecules in the hot area collides with a higher
inertia than the gas molecule in the cold area, resulting in a net force on the particles towards the cold gas area[30].In the case
of a superheater tube, the gas locally around the tube is several hundred kelvin colder than the mean gas temperature, and
there is a large temperature gradient which results in a Thermophoresis force on the particle towards the superheater tube.

2.6.7
Summary
The sulfation of KCl/NaCl is found to be slower than that of the sulfation CaO. In the sulfation of KCl/NaCl, most authors
agreed that the sulfation is reaction-controlled. Several authors have suggested that the particles could form a liquid phase
in the sulfation. However, the same authors still have used the shrinking core model. The shrinking core model has primarily
been used as an empirical model, and further investigation of the sulfation is needed.
2.7
Part conclusion
There has been conducted a litterateur study in preparation for the experimental work. The Sulfur recirculation is a potent
technology with promising results, however the mechanismen of the sulfation of the decrease of the deposit formation is not
understood. The sulfation of CaO,KCl and NaCl has been investigated in the literature unfortunately most model build is
applied only for narrow experimental conditions, there has not preformed experiments with mixtures al compounds present
ash deposit. A survey of the phase diagrams of CaO,KCl and NaCl has showed that is is very possible that in normal WTE
operation condition there is liquid present. There is need of further investigation to identify the mechanism.

Flue gas analysis
To analyze the flue gas, an FT-IR(Fourier transformed Inferred spectrometer) unit is used. The FT-IR unit used in the
setup is a Gasmet DX4000, which can measure industrial emissions. The library used in this setup can measure the flowing
gases:H2O,CO2,SO2,CO,N2O,NH3,HCl,HF,CH4 ,C2H6,C3H8,C6H14,CHOH,HNCO and HCN. The Gasmet unit measures an
inferred spectrum over 5 s; the software fits the known compounds to the measured spectrum and outputs an estimated value
of the given compound. The FT-IR does not directly measure each compound but measures the inferred spectrum of the
mixture of compounds. Gasmet, the company, has used its own calibration gas to select regions of different compounds used
to evaluate the concentration and determine calibration factors.
At the start of each day, the FT-IR unit was flushed with N2 for at least one hour. Then, a background measurement was
measured while flushing with N2. The background measurement is subtracted from all spectra measured afterward. N2 no
vibrational modes that can be measured and is therefore blank; the background measurements should capture all artifacts.
An issue when measuring a mixture of different gasses is the inferred spectrum of different compounds overlaps, thus resulting
in fall values. This is discussed more in detail in the chapter on method development.

3.3.1
BET analyse
Brunauer–Emmett–Teller(BET) analysis is a method for investigating microscopic information about solid samples.
The
assumption is that the gas molecules are bound to the surface in weakly bound (van der Waals bounds) in multiplayer.
Therefore, the absorption gas must be inert with the solid compound[73]. It is a gas absorption/desorption experiment, where
the results from the experiments can be used to calculate the surface area, pore volume of micropores, and other quantities[73].
The experiment is carried out by first heating the sample under N2, where moisture is released. Afterward, the sample is
placed on the machine. The desorption and absorption occur at 77 K, which is achieved using liquid nitrogen.The desorption
occurs when the pressure is lowered, and absorption occurs when the pressure increases.
BET measurement was performed on both the ESP ash T11 and ESP ash T17, and the results are presented in table 3.2
and in figure 3.7. The measured values of surface area and pore volume are of an order of magnitude to fly ash in the literature.

has been measured by Wang et al[75] for several fly ash particle from serval coal-fired plant in the range of 7 m2/g-21 m2/g.

XRF
X-ray fluorescence (XRF) can analyze a sample for most elements, though lighter elements can not be measured with XRF.
XRF’s strength is that it is not destructive; therefore, it can be used on a sample before and after sulfation. In principle, the
Xrays are absorbed electrons in the innermost shell, that are excited to a higher energy level; when this happens, a photon is
emitted when one electron from another shell drops into the innermost shell.

4.1. SO2 SATURATION
4.1
SO2 saturation
During the initial blank experiments, where no ash samples were loaded in the reactor, there was a large time delay of
approximately 1 hour before the SO2 reached a steady state when fed, just through tubes. This would result in unstable
inlet SO2 concentration to the reactor at the initial stage of the sulfation experiment. This was solved by performing SO2
saturation prior to an experiment. The test with SO2 saturation was carried out with a quarts reactor with quartz wool,
where the inlet gas consists of CO2,SO2,O2, and N2. Figure 4.1 shows the profile for CO2 and SO2. The SO2 initial mole
fraction was 150 ppm where after 50 min it reached a steady state of 400 ppm, which was consistent with the fed mole fraction
of SO2. A hypothesis for the slow increase of SO2 is that the SO2 is saturated on the tubes in the system. To investigate how
fast the SO2 can reach the steady state, with a pre-saturation step, after beginning shut off as the reactor is heated, the SO2
flow rate was set to 0 NmL
min at time 55 min and the reactor was heated to 773 K. The SO2 was fed again at time 90 min, where
after approximately 2 min, the mole fraction of SO2 reached the fed level. This test indicates that the method of saturating
the system before heating is efficient. The pre-saturation test is presented in figure 4.1.

The test was repeated 2 times and the results were duplicated. Therefore, before the experiment was performed, sulfation

possible to being constant to the fed level of SO2 during the experiments.
The measured mole fraction of CO2 is affected by the presence of SO2; this is indicated by the slight decrease and increase
in the mole fraction of CO2 when the feed of SO2 is shut off and turned on respectively. This is not because the total molar
flow is decreased; in that case, the mole fraction of CO2 should increase and not fall.
The cause of this can be attributed to the measurement by FT-IR; the principle method of the FT-IR is to measure the
total IR spectra and fit the mole fraction of the gas components such that the sum of all the gases gives the measured spectra.
At the end of this chapter, the FT-IR measurements are investigated further.

Bed material
The ESP ash sample was placed on a thick layer of quartz wool and then on a fixed bed. Two types of quartz wool were
tested. The quartz fiber and then a cheap quartz wool used for insulation, which is randomly packed, was tested, and there is
information about the purity. The test with the insulation quartz wool increased the sulfation and resulted in a much more
rapid release of HCl. The cause of this phenomenon was not investigated, but it was suspected that some interactions with
this in the bed material could cause increased sulfation.In comparison, another quartz fiber (Glass Wool, extra fine, Karl
Hecht™Assistent™) was tested to be inert under-investigated sulfation conditions and thus used for standard experiments.
4.6
Limitations of setup
The initial result in this section has demonstrated that a flow of 1 NL
min and temperatures in the interval of 400 oC to 600 oC
leads to there is a concentration drop over the fixed bed, which means that it is not a differential reactor. For the experiments
to yield a differential reactor, the sample mass must be deceased, the flow increased, or the temperature decreased. If the
mass is reduced, analysis methods would be impossible to perform, and the uncertainty of loading the sample increased from
a large percentage of the sample sticking to the surface of the reactor.
With the current experimental setup, it is possible to go up to 2.5 NL
min; the issue at higher flow is higher pressure. The
temperature of the deposit formation area in a waste incinerator is in the range from 350 oC to 550 oC as the lowest temperature
is the temperature of the water inside of the overheats. These issues could be solved by using a reactor with a diameter of fx
6 cm as it would allow for a larger flow, and there could be used mass flow controllers that allow a higher flow rate used.

5.2
Overall model methods
This thesis uses the model to compare the performed experiment with models from the literature. The model will describe
an ideal sulfation of fly ash under sulfation in a fixed-bed reactor.
The sulfation occurs in a fixed bed reactor, where a constant flow of gas with SO2 is sent through the fixed bed. Given the
concentration profile over the fixed bed, different methods exist to evaluate the sulfation. The concentration profile of SO2
over the bed depends on the reaction rate and amount of solid compared with the gas flow rate. This can be divided into
three scenarios depending on how large the concentration drop is over the fixed bed.
• ySO2,in ≈ySO2,out : this is the case where the reaction rate is very slow compared with the gas flow rate. In this case, the
concentration of SO2 can be assumed constant, and the system can be solved as three uncoupled differential equations,
which in this case can be solved analytically.
•

The central difference method is the most accurate. However, when it was first used, it resulted in numerically unstable
solutions. This could be because the system was initially modeled as a pipe with plug flow, and the concentration moves
as a vertical front through the bed. This vertical front is hard to calculate with a finite difference method, and using the
central method can lead to instability as a point is used in front of the moving front. The backward difference was chosen to
approximate the spatial derivative of YSO2/YHCl as it only relies on previous points.
To use the finite difference method, the system is divided into N number of cells in the spatial direction, where it is assumed
that SO2 is uniformly distributed in each cell. Furthermore, it is assumed that the velocity is constant through the bed and
that all particles are identical in the cell with respect to conversion for each species. The model assumes that all the surfaces
in the bed are smooth. The model does not take into account the consumption of O2 and H2O; this assumption is based on
the fact that there is a large surplus of O2 and H2O.
When using the finite difference method, the five couple particle differential equation system is ”reduced” to a 5 times N
coupled ordinary differential equation system, which is presented in equation (5.12a) to equation (5.12e).

6.5
Discussion on model performance
The developed model in this thesis shows that when using the same parameters for one experiment, the model can somewhat
predict the other experiment by considering the transport of SO2 and HCl through the bed and using models for sulfation
of pure components from literature.
The model needs to consider transport if experiments are performed under similar
conditions as in this thesis. It is quite remarkable that the model successfully predicts the fly ash’s sulfation, considering it is
extrapolating on sulfation models derived from data outside of the experiments in this thesis.
The logarithmic increase of outlet mole fraction of SO2 present in the experimental results, which is not seen in any of the
model predictions, indicates that the initial sulfation reaction occurs quickly. A reason for this observation is that the sample
could contain very fine particles. Particle size In waste power, it depends on the waste that is burned and how the design of
the power plant is designed [Frandsenetaldorcforing]. If there is a distribution of smaller particles, the reaction rate would
initially be higher; as the small particles are more converted, the reaction rate will drop, thus resulting in the outlet mole
fraction of SO2 to increase. This can be modeled by adding a balanced equation for each size chosen for CaO. However, this
would also increase the computation time.
In the experiments, there is an observed maximum for outlet mole fraction of HCl, which the model does not predict. It is
important to note that this maximum is a least after 30 min, which indicates that it is not simply caused by the transport of
the HCl through the pipes. In the pure components test, there was no observed maximum for the outlet mole fraction HCl,
which indicates that the maximum observed in the fly ash sulfation experiments is that the sulfation rate of chlorides has a
maximum. This leads to discuss the limitations of the model.
Sulfation model
All three sulfation models used are shrinking unreacted core models, where the maximum conversion is at X = 0 or t = 0,
which is not observed in the fly ash sulfation experiments. The shrinking core model is used as an empirical model with no
experimental evidence that a shrinking core is a good model for the sulfation of chloride particles. Dam-Johansen[54] developed
3 layered grain model for the sulfation of CaCO3 to take into account the structural change undergoing the sulfation, a similar
approach could be used for the chlorine particle, which would be capable of predicting a HCl maxima. From SEM-EDX

7.1
Characterization of fly ash
In this thesis, two samples from Maagebrjergværet have been analyzed with SEM-EDX, XRF, XRD, Eurofine chemical
analysis, and BET. These analyses have given some initial knowledge, but there has not been enough analysis to give full
information about the sample. The first step would be to increase the number of samples used for each experiment to lower
the uncertainty. This would give important knowledge on the difference between fly ash from the standard operation and, in
the case of sulfur recirculation.
7.1.1
Future characterization methods
The density of fly ash is a critical parameter that this thesis relied on from literature; Mercury porosimetry can be used to
determine the particle density and porosity. In combination with BET analysis, it would be possible to measure both pore
volume and surface area and find a full distribution of pore radius[89].
By first comparing the ash from the reference and ash from sulfur reciucaltoin, mercury porosimetry can be used on fly ash
samples after sulfation. These experiments and analyses will give a quantitative understanding of the structure change during
sulfation.
The model components, tests, and chemical analysis methods to determine the sample’s conversion and exact
amount of chlorine would verify if the XRF methods and FT-IR are accurate enough.
XRF
An improvement for the XRF analysis could be to make an extra step in the preparation to ensure the homogeneity of the
sample. This could be done by pressing a tablet with a mixture of SiO2, wax, and sample. This would ensure that the sample
is always well mixed furthermore, a fixed standard matrix with Cl, S, K, Na, and CaO could be made to make a calibration
specific to be used to measure Cl
S after sulfation experiments[90].
Carbonate analysis
TPD experiments showed the presence of carbonates and sulfur in the sample; TGA and TPD experiments would contribute
to understanding the composition of the fly ash samples.

Future test
Waste feed affect on sulfaiton
Future sulfation experiments need to include tests on fly ash at different seasons and from different waste power plants with
different feedstock to understand the sulfation effect on deposits in general. These experiments will hopefully show the effect
of chemical composition in the ash. The purpose of these experiments is on the sulfation mechanism, whether the sulfation of
each compound happened independently or if the solid reaction is coupled with the other compounds.
Longterm exposure to SO2
The maximum solid residence time of all experiments was 6.5 hours; this is, however, short compared with the time between
maintaining the boiler. Experiments were performed for solid residents of 50 h to see if the ash reached full conversion. To
compare the sulfation experiment with the deposit formation, an experiment where ash from the sulfation experiment is mixed
with fresh fly ash to investigate the effect of new ash arriving at the boiler.
Pure components
The sulfation models in this thesis are developed based on particles larger than the ones present in the fly ash; furthermore,
most heterogeneous alkaline chlorine sulfation models are based on one particle size.The first step would be to develop sulfation
models where the size is taken into account by performing experiments with different particle sizes.
Preformed sulfation experiments on compounds that could be present in the fly ash, such as ZnCl2,CaCl2, Na2CO3, K2CO3.
These experiments will be used to consider which compounds are inessential in the sulfation of fly ash.
Mixed purecomponets
A mixture of known compounds can be used to understand the interaction in the sulfation.
Sulfation experiments with
different mixtures would be used to understand if the sulfation of each compound is affected by other solids. To optimize the
mixed pure component sulfation, there is a need to develop a method of mixing the pure components properly, for solid-solid
reactions to be possible.
Alternative reactor design
The model where transport is considered can be verified by an alternative reactor design, where the fixed bed is split into
three separate reactors. In figure 7.1 there is presented a quick sketch. Here, the reactors are set in series; each reactor would
have its own furnace and would have a bypass valve in each outlet to measure the concentration of the gases between each
reactor.
With the reactor set up, a model with transport can be evaluated to compare the concentration profile through the bed.
Furthermore, the setup can also be used to simulate a temperature profile of the deposit layer, as each reactor would have a
separate furnace.
Fly ash sulfation at lower temperature
This thesis only performed two experiments at 400 oC; these experiments resulted in a conversion similar to the case at 500 oC,
where the models underpredicted the conversion. There is a need for more experiments 400 oC and lower to understand the
sulfation; to do this, there is a need to design a setup that is reliable at low temperatures.
7.3.1
Future modeling of the sulfation
The model developed in this thesis did not consider the structure of the ash or other material properties. To understand the
condensed sulfation, a model that takes the characterization of the fly ash into account must be developed.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
This thesis has thoroughly investigated the sulfation of fly ash from a WtE power plant through experimental and modeling
approaches to gain insight into deposit sulfation.
An experimental method has been developed to conduct sulfation experiments of fly ash to gain an understanding of
condensed sulfation. The method has proven successful as the experiments have been repeatable. FT-IR and XRF have been
used to determine the sulfation degree and the results are consistent..
When the experimental setup was developed, there were conducted 15 sulfation experiments on ESP ash T17 from the
reference case, and 2 on ESP ash T11 that was derived under sulfation conditions. The experiments prove that the sulfation
of chlorine takes place faster than expected and that the sulfation of chlorine is not the primary sulfation reactant. At the
end of the typical experiment with solid resident times over 5 hours, the amount of sulfur was greater than chlorine, which
was backed up by XRPD, XRF, and SEM-EDX analyses. The experiments reveal that there is a sintering process occurring
under the sulfation.
4 experiments were on pure components and two sulfation experiments were on mixtures of pure components were also
conducted. These experiments were used to verify the experimental method and to bed used for developing a model for the
sulfation. A coupled partial differential equation model has been developed that has a balance for SO2,HCl,NaCl, KCl, and
CaO and based on pure components ODE model from the literature. The model was capable of describing the sulfation
of ESP ash T17 at 500 oC and with the use of the same parameters gave a good prediction of the sulfation experiments at
600 oC.However, the model was not in the same degree able to predict the experiment at 400 oC.
The model comparison with experimental results indicates that the high sulfation rate is a result of submicron KCl and
NaCl particles. The model is built on sulfation models that are valid for higher SO2 mole fractions and temperatures, so it is
remarkable that the model can be used to describe the sulfation of fly ash. This thesis is only the beginning of the journey of
condensed-phase sulfation of ash deposits. This gives suggestions to both new experimental and modeling subjects that could
be considered for further investigations.
