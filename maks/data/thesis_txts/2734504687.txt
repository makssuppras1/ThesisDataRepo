Abstract
The present study provides a brief overview on resilience in HVAC systems in
Copenhagen, with a focus on dynamic simulations conducted in IDA ICE. The re-
search explores the impact of different system combinations on resilience and analyses
their behaviour under different critical conditions such as heat waves, on a short-term,
mid-term and long-term periods. The system combinations simulated were: VAV sys-
tem with CO2 + Temperature control, a mechanically assisted natural ventilation with
night cooling, and a radiant cooling floor. The study contributes insights into optimiz-
ing HVAC systems by using customized control schemes for both current and extreme
conditions, enhancing building resilience in the face of climate challenges. The best
cooling performance was obtained by the CO2 + Temperature control system as the
majority of the time the operative temperatures were within the range for Category
II, in compliance with the Danish Standard EN 16798-1:2019. In spite of the fact that
further research should be done to obtain more accurate results, this study aims to act
as a reference for future resilience improvements.

Introduction
1.1
Background and project description
Climate change constitutes a significant threat to human well-being inside buildings. One of
its most pronounced impacts can be seen in the inevitable increase of heat waves, which are
becoming a challenge for buildings to maintain thermal comfort and occupants’ safety. Heat
waves can lead to health issues, scarcity of water resources and disrupted ecosystems.
Even though heat waves are considered a threat worldwide, in this study focus was placed in
European populations that are living in climates that are currently temperate. No group of
people can be said to be immune to the dangers they provide [1], especially the most suscep-
tible population. Buildings are also susceptible to the rising temperatures associated with
these heat waves. In order to withstand the climatic conditions, buildings must continuously
improve their design, construction and operation. Building’s resilience performs a key role in
ensuring occupants’ well-being, as well as energy efficiency and overall global sustainability.
Many building systems are designed to work under current weather conditions. However,
many of these do not perform as expected for extreme critical conditions.
Measures to
improve energy efficiency may limit the building’s ability to cope with extreme weather
events [2].
In the following sections of this thesis, the resilience of indoor terminal units together with
heat/cold source combinations withing Heating, Ventilation, and Air Conditioning (HVAC)
systems was examined. They were simulated under critical conditions such as heat waves us-
ing the IDA ICE software. Not only were current climate conditions taken into consideration,
but also unforeseen climate challenges that might take place in the near future.
The goal of this thesis was to recognize optimal combinations that can cope with both current
and future weather conditions. Dynamic building simulations were carried out in order to
contribute knowledge to the field of cooling. Successive steps will offer the methodologies,
implications and results of this research regarding building resilience, all in accordance with
the dynamic simulation guideline [3], which recommends to use comfort standards to define
the threshold for overheating.

1.2
Literature review
A literature review was conducted on studies related to resilience and heat waves prior to the
simulations. It was conducted to identify indoor terminal units and heat sources that have
potential of being resilient. Different HVAC systems and their characteristics were studied
to decide which systems could adapt better to the situation in scope.
An overall research was made to get more insights mainly on the impact of heat waves on
buildings and, consequently, on people occupying these buildings. The scope was narrowed
down to heat waves, as power outages could also be a direct consequence from these.
Reviews for resilient cooling technologies were also inspected, in an attempt to find the most
suitable components that could conform the system. Natural ventilation also plays a pivotal
role as the system needs to be able to perform under a power outage.
As a way of covering as many system combinations as possible, specific focus was placed into
resilience, passive and active cooling of buildings.
1.2.1
Resilience
This subsection addresses resilient cooling and the standards for evaluating it in addition to
reviewing the definition of resilience and the traits of resilient systems.
According to the UN General Assembly Resolution 71/276 [4], resilience is ”the ability of a
system, community or society exposed to hazards to resist, absorb, accommodate, adapt to,
transform and recover from the effects of a hazard in a timely and efficient manner, includ-
ing through the preservation and restoration of its essential basic structures and functions
through risk management”.
In the context of buildings, it refers to incorporating into the design of a building, aspects and
features that allow the building to carry out its intended functions, now and in the foreseeable
future [5]. Seeking for for a better resilience in the future might as well compromise the
building‘s energy efficiency, and will probably result into an elevated cost, as striving for one
of this characteristics usually means compromising the other one.
Specifically, in the context of this thesis, the focus shifts to the resilience in the buildings.
When it comes to cooling, Carnieletto et al.
[6] defines it as ”the ability of a building-
plant system to withstand and recover after a disruptive event” . The cooling of a building
is resilient when ”the capacity of the cooling system integrated in the building allows it
to withstand or recover from disturbances due to disruptions, including heat waves and
power outages, and to adopt the appropriate strategies after failure (robustness) to mitigate
degradation of building performance (deterioration of indoor environment quality and/or
increased need for space cooling energy (recoverability)”, according to Attia [7].

Zhang et al. [2], for example, classifies the resilience by the following criteria:
• Absorptive capacity is the degree to which a system is able to absorb the impacts
of disruptive events, and minimize their consequences with little effort.
• Adaptive capacity is the ability to adjust undesirable situations by undergoing some
changes. The system can learn from the event, evaluate the system performance and
modify its configurations, and make it more flexible to future disruptions. Adaptive
capacity is distinguished from absorptive capacity in that adaptive systems change in
response to adverse impacts, especially if the absorptive capacity has been exceeded.
• Restorative capacity is the ability to return to normal or improved operation.
• Recovery speed is the speed of the recovery process. Recovery may be accelerated
if absorption activities are well implemented and the system can quickly mobilize and
effectively use all the resources at its disposal.
1.2.2
Passive Cooling
A system is passive when its design includes natural processes for heating or cooling the
interior of the buildings. Energy in passive design moves naturally, without any reliance on
electrical devices that consume power [8]. The main purposes of the natural cooling are to
prevent the heat from entering the building or/and to remove the heat from the building
once it has entered.
As a way of removing the heat from inside the building, some studies include a heat mass
protected by external insulation, which absorbs heat from the people, devices, lighting and
so on during the day. Then the mass discharges the heat during the night. According to
Fortin et al. [9], they were proven to be effective systems for passive cooling in hot and dry
climates, but unfortunately, the performance of such systems is not as high when the climate
is cold and humid as it is the case. Some of the most popular passive cooling technologies
nowadays are shading, natural ventilation or evaporative cooling [10].
Evaporative cooling is a passive cooling method that decreases the ambient inlet air temper-
ature. A dependable, easy, and affordable method of reducing the outside air temperature,
which lowers energy use. Indirect evaporative cooling (IDEC) and direct evaporative cooling
(DEC) are the two primary conventional forms of evaporative cooling. Direct contact with
water cools a secondary air stream in the indirect evaporative cooling system. This form of
heat exchanger uses indirect contact to chill the secondary air stream, which prevents the
primary air stream from becoming humidified. However, due to the fact that the principal
air in the direct evaporative cooling system comes into direct contact with water, it becomes
humidified as it cools [11].

After consideration, evaporative cooling was not included in this investigation as DEC is
mostly suitable for dry and hot climates. According to Amer et al. [12], ”in moist conditions,
the relative humidity can reach as high as 80%, such a high humidity is not suitable for direct
supply into buildings, because it may cause warping, rusting, and mildew of susceptible
materials”.
It is possible to significantly lower operative temperature and lessen the requirement for active
systems by implementing passive control schemes. Passive cooling strategies demonstrated a
good performance in maintaining thermal comfort in the mid-term. Still, it is evident that the
best comfort and energy situations from passive cooling systems are those that incorporate a
variety of controlling actions [13]. Some passive methods might be able to provide cooling on
their own, however, many factors other than just cooling need to be taken into consideration
to provide thermal comfort. Floor cooling, for example, is a cooling system that requires
ventilation in order to provide indoor air quality and to avoid condensation.
In this case, the building structure contains a core zone with no available windows as it is
the main space that connects all other zones. This zone is cooled down by passive means
where outdoor air is driven into the zone.
1.2.3
Active Cooling
Alternatively, active cooling may also be used in an attempt to improve occupants’ thermal
comfort inside buildings. Currently, the climate change is a big concern also in the thermal
design of the buildings. With the inevitable rise of the temperatures in hot climates, active
cooling becomes necessary when decreasing indoor temperatures as passive cooling is not
able to fully cope with this task. According to Bogatu et al. [14], ”cooling energy savings
could be surpassed by the increase in fan energy use, especially in hot and humid climates”.
Passive measures can reduce the risk of dangerous conditions but cannot guarantee safe
conditions for occupants. Active measures are required to provide cooling in order to preserve
occupants’ safety, such as cool energy storage or photovoltaic systems that power cooling
systems [15]. These systems are considered active systems although they use outdoor air, as
it is driven inside the building by fans. Heating and cooling coils are also used in order to
provide the necessary air temperature to the building.
It is evident that during the past few decades, buildings have improved in a number of areas,
including thermal comfort and energy efficiency [16]. These improvements lead to air-tight
buildings where higher polluting concentrations are found. The absence of fresh air redirected
into the building and these increased concentrations are directly linked to health problems
[17]. Due to this fact, recently, indoor air quality has become one of the main concerns when
designing and installing a ventilation system.
A study by Bogatu et al.
[18] shows that air cleaners can decrease the energy use for
conditioning and transporting the ventilation air by 1.9% to 18% while possibly reducing the

pollution level of buildings. In order to provide outdoor air to the building, there are several
methods, one of the most used ones includes the Constant Air Volume (CAV) system, which
supplies constant airflow into the building regardless of the CO2 concentration indoors. This
method causes either an excessive amount of ventilation or poor indoor air quality due to the
lack of ventilation [19], which makes it unsuitable for this study. It could however, become a
better option if combined wit other system for a more acceptable cooling performance.
Variable Air Volume (VAV) systems by definition are ”simply air conditioning (AC) systems
that are designed to promote existence of constant temperature in a conditioned space by
varying the volume of air supplied to the conditioned space instead of varying the temperature
of supplied air” [20]. The controller setpoints defined for the VAV system are further discussed
in subsubsection 2.1.7.
Regarding the energy consumption of air-conditioning systems, the indoor cooling load of
large areas is sufficiently high that an extensive amount of circulating air is needed in all-air
systems to achieve the necessary cooling. According to this conventional approach, fans’
energy consumption accounts for a sizeable percentage (30–50%) of overall consumption,
leading to a high overall energy consumption [21].
On the other hand, a radiant floor was also used in this study.
This system transports
energy and exchanges heat by flowing cooled water on pipelines.
To avoid condensation
on the floor surface during hot and humid weather, the floor surface temperature must be
adjusted [22]. Given that one terminal unit may be used for both heating and cooling, this
technology contributes to a cost-saving option. Additionally, it has been demonstrated to
be a successful method of removing solar loads from interior areas by extracting heat flow
before it enters the indoor environment [23].
Nevertheless, as every system, it also has its drawbacks. Not only its performance is limited
by the presence of obstacles such as carpets or furniture, but the system does not attempt
to remove latent heat in the first place; as a radiant floor surface is strictly for sensible heat
[24].
In the study case, it was combined with a CAV system in an attempt to avoid the previously
mentioned condensation and also for ventilation purposes.

1.3
Objectives
The purpose of this study is to offer many innovative alternatives into HVAC system designs,
with a primary focus on resilience, by examining these various points of view. Research on
more resilient cooling systems—those that can function even in the face of unforeseen and
unanticipated obstacles—could be conducted with the aid of dynamic simulations.
Long-term testing of resilience will also be done to see how effectively buildings adapt and
last in a changing environment, with the goal of getting the cooling system ready for any
extreme or unexpected occurrences that may be carried on by global warming.
This study investigates system combinations of day-time ventilation, night-time ventilation
and overall passive and active cooling strategy. Consequently, in the present study, a mechan-
ically assisted natural ventilation system will be analysed. Kamal [10] states that in order to
apply passive cooling in a successful way; heat transfer must be directed to an environmental
heat sink (i.e. outdoor air, water...) which has a lower temperature than that in the inside
of the building.
In order to complete the objectives, each system’s performance under heat waves will be
studied. Analysis of systems’ performance for different climate files will also be done, and
consequently, suitability of each system combination will be discussed.
The aims of the current thesis project are the following:
1. Compare the resilience performance for different HVAC system combinations
2. Find viable resilient systems that can withstand future extreme events
3. Implement a custom control that allows the ventilation system to cope with the different
system combinations

last longer as time passes by. This topic will be deeply covered later in subsubsection 2.1.3.

2.1.3
Heating and cooling seasons
In order to ensure a year-round comfort and efficiency, HVAC systems need to adapt to
the distinct thermal demands.
Therefore, as the dynamics of indoor environments vary
considerably between seasons, the simulation is differentiated by the cooling and heating
seasons.
The definition of heating-cooling season can be made in different ways [28]:
1. Based on date
2. Based on outdoor temperature
3. Based on calculated needs by dynamic building simulations

2.1.6
Air Flow Rate
The ventilation system plays a pivotal role in providing acceptable air quality. The main
purpose is to minimize health effects that might be caused from pollutans and overall poor air
quality related to the lack of ventilation. In the field of HVAC systems, the determination and
regulation of air flow rates constitute fundamental pillars influencing the overall performance
and resilience of the system. Air flow rates play an essential role in the quality of indoor air,
thermal comfort and energy efficiency of buildings.
Understanding and optimizing flow rates constitute primary objectives when trying to aim
for occupants’ thermal comfort, as it requires precise control that becomes especially critical
in extreme conditions such as heat waves, where the adaptability of the HVAC system is
tested.
In order to calculate the minimum air flow rate, the following formula was used:

2.2
Control
From an engineering perspective, the ventilation systems need to be different depending on
the building characteristics, climate zones and pollution environments. Consequently, pre-
designed ventilation systems might not be as effective as a system with a customized control
in creating a productive and hygienic interior space [31].
Several factors are targeted in an attempt to make the building operate in compliance with
the standards EN 16798-1:2019 and EN 16798-2:2019. By setting up thresholds on parameters
such as CO2 or operative temperatures, the building can be modelled to operate and provide
different airflow rates for different scenarios.
One of the control blocks used was the ”Thermostat (on-off with hysteresis)” with a deadband
of −0.5 in which two variables were inserted: the measure variable and the setpoint variable.
According to Peffer et al. [32], ”the basic function of the typical residential thermostat is to
set a target temperature, see the current temperature, and control the equipment accordingly.
A second and expanding role is to save energy”. They work by turning the connected cooling
equipment completely on or off when the temperature reaches the setpoint. In this case, they
were used so that they would only operate if the operative temperature surpassed either the
ambient temperature or the setpoint temperature, as it can be seen in Figure 10.
2.2.1
VAV system with CO2 + Temperature Control
According to the User’s Guide on IDA ICE, the ventilation system ”VAV CO2 + Temperature
control” uses the supply air to both heat and cool. It also will force this air flow to maintain
the maximum CO2 level (the minimum level will never be used) by using PI controllers
instead of using P ditto.
In this study, the base control system modelled was a ventilation system type similar to
”CO2 + Temperature control” system on IDA ICE. By modelling it, further applications and
conditions could be added if necessary.
For the ventilation system, it was assumed that people did not open windows during the
simulated period as the mechanical ventilation system is capable of providing all the fresh
air requirements. The operating schedule was set from 8:00 to 18:00 hrs during weekdays,
and it was assumed to have a constant air supply temperature of 12.8 ºC. The heating and
cooling coils were set to 1, in order to heat or cool the incoming outdoor air.

4
Discussion
4.1
Interpretation of Simulation Results
4.1.1
Heat Removed
Based on the results obtained for the cooling load, it was observed that in comparison to
the ones obtained for Buffalo, although the cooling load per m2 is similar, the overall heat
removed was lower.
The South zone, however, was observed to have the second highest
values.
Buildings are usually oriented in a way that the South zone receives the most direct sunlight,
which leads to higher internal heat gains per m2 that will need to be removed by the AHU.
This could be one of the main reasons why the Core zone shows the lowest value per area
even if it is the zone that removes the most heat. Additionally, the area is four times bigger
than in the South zone which could also contribute to this fact. As it is located in the middle
of the building and has no direct exposure to sunlight, only heat produced by the occupants
and other internal heat gains need to be removed. The same rationale can be applied to
the North zone, which shows lower values of heat removed. Nevertheless, West and East
zones show high values due to the fact that the area is also half the South and North zones’,
respectively.
Other factors such as thermal mass, insulation, occupancy or even the equipment load should
not be considered as a differential aspect since they were all sized equally for all zones. The
design and placement of the AHU should also be considered, as well as the supply and return
vents, which could have influence in the heat removal effectiveness.
4.1.2
Operative Temperatures
In an attempt to find the occupants’ thermal comfort and acceptable limits of both operative
temperature and CO2 concentration level, a control system based on these two conditions
was designed. A base case was used for all three system combinations, which compared the
setpoint temperatures with the operative temperatures.
Results for the VAV CO2 + Temperature control system showed that more than 50% of the
operative temperatures fell within the range for Category II, regardless of the term simulated.
The system was able to maintain percentages below 30% above Category II, with a continuous
ventilation during working hours. The zones with the less exposure to sunlight obtained the
best results, as mentioned in subsubsection 4.1.1.
The fact that this system includes a cooling coil operating at maximum efficiency, made the
outdoor air flow into the building at one degree higher (due to the AHU design) than the
supply air temperature set, which was 12.8 ºC. By flowing cooled outdoor air, temperatures
could be dropped below the threshold.
As for the Night Cooling system, a night schedule was added to the base control system.
The percentages obtained within Category II were between 30-40%, in other words, most
Technical University of Denmark
==PAGE:56==
Resilience evaluation of different HVAC system combinations
Page 48 of 53
of the occupancy time of the building is outside the range established by EN 16798-1:2019.
Moreover, the highest share of operative temperatures were above 26 ºC, which shows that
the system was not able to remove the heat in any of the climate files studied.
In this case, the outdoor air flows into the building without being previously cooled. Despite
the fact that the implemented control avoids the flowing of outdoor air warmer than the
operative temperature, in the hottest months of the cooling season, the ambient air could
indeed be below but close to the setpoint temperatures. This could result into poor cooling
performance, which combined with the presence of occupants, could make the operative
temperatures rise.
Lastly, the results obtained for the radiant floor cooling system show percentages around
50% for all zones except the South and the West zone which show percentages between 30-
40% within Category II. The share of operative temperatures below the threshold is also the
highest among all systems.
4.1.3
System Limitations
The fact that the design power and the heat transfer coefficients used were the same in all
zones could have an influence in these results. As discussed previously, many factors have an
impact on the cooling performance, and having a fixed design power for all of them, could
indeed result into some zones not being able to cope with the high ambient temperatures.
In contrast, other zones might experience too much cooling instead. Moreover, the cooling
floor needs to be combined with a ventilation system in order to maintain acceptable indoor
air quality, which could be the main factor behind the high percentages obtained for low
operative temperatures.
The cooling season calculations also play a pivotal role at having an influence on the final
results. They were obtained based on the running mean average temperatures calculated and
they are clearly differentiated in the heating and the cooling seasons, neglecting transition
periods. In this case, the cooling season for the different climate files started when the Trm
surpassed 15 ºC, but did not end until it dropped below 10 ºC. These transition periods (10
ºC ¡ Trm ¡ 15 ºC) could influence the results, as the percentages were calculated based on the
total operative temperatures obtained, and by reducing the number of them the percentages
could increase drastically.
The results obtained were based on hypothetical climate files, which predict a rise on the
temperatures but might not be as accurate as they finally end up being. Furthermore, the
percentage analysis was conducted for typical meteorological years (TMY) which do not cover
extreme events such as heatwaves. Consequently, as the South zone had the highest results
above the threshold, it was individually analyzed for heat wave years (HWY). Each system
followed the same trend as in subsection 3.3, where both the VAV CO2 + Temperature control
system and the Floor Cooling system managed to keep operative temperatures at acceptable
limits for almost all months except for July, and the Night Cooling system struggled during
the full cooling season.

In conclusion, mechanical ventilation systems with cooling coils showed more resilient results
than mechanically assisted natural ventilation systems which did not modify the incoming
ambient air temperatures. The floor cooling system also showed high resilience but it should

4.2
Identification of Future Research Directions and Recommen-
dations
From a resilient perspective, the floor cooling system obtained the best performance at main-
taining the operative temperatures under the threshold even if a high share of this percentage
fell under the acceptable limits for Category II. However, the design power of the system,
could indeed be customized for each zone to obtain more optimal results. In this study, the
design power used was assumed based on recommendations, Olesen [34][38], while it could
also have been sized according to the calculated cooling load instead. More accurate inputs
could result into lower over cooling percentages and less thermal discomfort.
As for the radiant floor cooling system used in this combination, a ventilation system is
necessary to remove the latent heat that the floor is unable to take off.
A basic CAV
system with the minimum calculated air flows was simulated for a better understanding of
the system’s behaviour. Other ventilation systems sized according to each zone’s area, could
obtain better performances since the CAV system from this study just provides the minimum
requirements as per EN 16798-1:2019. Other radiant system types from EN 15377-1 could
also obtain more acceptable results.
Regarding the low operative temperatures obtained, the fact that the ventilation system
was not considered when sizing the radiant floor, had a impact on having operative tem-
peratures below 20 ºC during the first occupancy hours. It should also be a consequence
of the ventilation system being forced to start its operation one hour before occupancy, as
per EN 16798-1:2019. The use of the heating and cooling coils should also be taken into
consideration, as they could improve the system’s efficiency.
The VAV CO2 + Temperature control system showed the most absorptive capacity out of all
the analyzed systems, as it was able to maintain the operative temperatures inside the range
for most of the time. System configurations also showed an impact on resilience, as the same
control model was designed for all zones and time periods.
As discussed in previous sections, customized models depending on the zone characteristics,
the environmental conditions, and the geographical location of the building should be con-
sidered. It was assumed that all three system combinations had the same base control, and
as some of them did not operate with cooling coils, it was necessary to limit the system op-
eration and avoid flowing outdoor air when it surpassed the operative temperatures. For this
particular system, that condition could affect the system when the ambient air temperatures
are extremely high, which could indeed compromise the system’s performance. In this case,
ambient air temperature should not be taken into consideration as it will be cooled down to
the supply air temperature set on the AHU.

On the Night Cooling system, results showed no absorptive capacity whatsoever. From a
resilient perspective, this system was found to be insufficient to keep down temperatures at
acceptable limits.
A six-hour night schedule was assumed, 4h prior and posterior to the occupancy schedule.
The control implemented on IDA ICE only allowed the system to provide always either the
minimum or the maximum air flow rate during night time, in other words, even when the
building had operative temperatures below the setpoint, the system was still in operation,
which could result into temperatures below Category II at the start of the occupancy. The
system could be improved by stopping its operation when the operative temperatures are at
acceptable levels.
Some of these assumptions were made to simplify the study, which ended up limiting the
system performances. In order to make a detailed assessment of these system combinations,
further research should be done. Some of the system and control parameters could be calcu-
lated instead, which would indeed provide more accurate results.
This study was performed based on the cooling resilience of the systems.
Nevertheless,
according to Elnagar [39] other variables should also be taken into consideration. Even if
the system combinations show an acceptable performance, they should be further analyzed
from an efficiency and flexibility perspective, and their ability to have secondary systems
integrated should also be considered.

5
Conclusions
This paper provides a qualitative assessment of a VAV system with CO2 + Temperature Con-
trol, a mechanically assisted natural ventilation system with Night Cooling control system,
and a radiant floor system for a medium office located in Copenhagen. The study was based
on a cooling resilience criteria. All three systems were analyzed for different climate files,
in an attempt to observe the ability of these systems to withstand future extreme climate
conditions.
The results show that the mechanically assisted natural ventilation with Night Cooling system
lacked the ability to maintain acceptable operative temperatures during the cooling season
due to the absence of a cooling coil. In other words, the study found that systems which
introduce outdoor air without being previously cooled, show a worst cooling performance on
the cooling season for high temperatures and heat waves. As for the VAV CO2 + Temperature
control system and the radiant floor cooling system, they were able to maintain temperatures
below 26 ºC for 70% of the time. Additionally, the zones that had direct sunlight exposure
showed the highest percentages over the threshold. Upon the start of the occupancy schedule,
the occupants might experience thermal discomfort based on the fact the ventilation system
starts its operation one hour before the working schedule.
In conclusion, the Night Cooling system shows the worst performance out of all systems, and
it would require a cooling coil in order to provide acceptable levels of operative temperatures.
The radiant floor cooling system is the most resilient out of all systems but, due to the
assumed design power, some zones might experience over cooling, which would lead to a high
level of thermal discomfort from the occupants. According to the present study, the VAV
with CO2 + Temperature control system obtained the best performance inside Category II.
Nevertheless, the results could improve if the length of the cooling seasons and some other
assumed parameters were less general and more focused on each zone’s orientation. Using
this system as a reference could improve current building’s resilient cooling performances,
due to the fact that they are usually approached from an energetic efficiency perspective,
which makes them vulnerable to unexpected extreme events that could take place in the near
future.

VAV system with CO2 + Temperature Control without Cooling
Coil
The VAV CO2 + Temperature control system was also simulated without the support of the
cooling coil, to better understand the behaviour of the control system and to identify its
weaknesses. Short-term, medium-term and long-term periods were simulated.
The results obtained for the short-term period can be seen in Figure 34. The majority of the
operative temperatures (percentages around 50%) are above the threshold. The results for
the temperatures below 23 ºC, however, are below 20 %.
Figure 34: Percentage of temperatures that fall into Category II for CO2 + Temperature
control with no cooling coil, 2001-2020.
