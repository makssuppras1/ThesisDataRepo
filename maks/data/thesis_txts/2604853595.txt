Summary
The aim of this master thesis project is to design and analyze a dual performance rotor
customized for Airborne Wind Energy Systems (AWES). The design of rotors for the
AWES is at a great level of importance since it should satisfy opposing requirements at
the same time.
Airborne rotors are subjected to work in low tip speed ratio regions. Since the validity
of the results from the Blade Element Momentum Theory (BEMT) is unknown for the
low tip speed ratio regions, there is a need to validate this model prior to the design of
a dual performance rotor. A model based on the prescribed vortex shape is developed.
Moreover, the vortex model is validated by comparing its results with an analytical
equation describing the shape of the helix. The results from the prescribed vortex method
are valid only for the low induction factor regions where the shape of the wake is mainly
driven by the rotational and ﬂight speeds. To assure satisfying the low induction factor
condition once comparing the results of the vortex method and BEMT, a methodology
to design blade test cases is introduced. The results of the vortex model and BEMT are
compared for the given designed blades and it is proved that using the BEMT model is
valid for the low tip speed ratio regions as well.
An introduction of the Brush Less Direct Current (BLDC) motors is given and a
simpliﬁed model of these motor families is used to design a dual performance rotor.
In this thesis, a BLDC motor is modeled by the deliverable maximum torque at the
given RPM and maximum deliverable power limit. Moreover, all of the numerical and
optimization methods used to model a dual performance rotor is introduced. All of the
described methods are explained using an example of dual performance rotor design. A
model to size the rotor diameter based on the kite geometry and desirable induction
factor is introduced. Also, a new methodology to calculate the eﬃciency of the rotor
during the hovering phase is introduced since the traditional method is no longer valid.
Moreover, NACA 0012 is considered since the experimental and simulation data for this
airfoil are widely available for the full range of angle of attack (AOA). Kite and ﬂight
speciﬁcations are described and used for further calculations.
An ideal turbine and propeller rotors are designed and the results are analyzed. A
comprehensive sensitivity analysis is carried out and the eﬀects of the tip speed ratio,
tip speed, takeoﬀRPM, and mixing gain in the objective function are understood.
Garnering knowledge from sensitivity analysis and ideal turbine and propeller designs,
dual performance rotors are designed for the ﬁxed and variable pitch scenarios.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
An airborne wind turbine is a concept of having a rotating rotor in the air without the
support of a tower [[14]]. Thus, this rotating rotor can be operational in higher altitudes
and have access to higher and more persistent wind speed [[11]]. Having the structure
of the tower eliminated, the application of the airborne wind turbine is considered as
an eﬃcient power generation case in comparison with the conventional wind turbines in
terms of the generated power per invested material. The acronym AWES (Airborne Wind
Energy Systems) is used in this dissertation to identify the entire electro-mechanical
system that transfers the wind power to electricity. The power generation in the AWE
system is done by two diﬀerent approaches. If the power generation is carried out on the
kite, then the application labels as the Flight Gen AWES. The generated power is being
transferred to the ground station through a tether that is holding the kite mechanically.
Therefore, the acronym FG (Flight Generation) is used if the AWE system is mechanically
and electrically connected to the ground station, and the power generation component
is implemented on the AWE. On the other hand, if the AWES is mechanically pulling
the winch located on the ground station - causing the generator’s shaft to rotate, then
the application is labeled as the Ground-Gen approach. Besides, the kite’s tether is only
mechanically connected to the kite and no electricity is being transferred through the
tether. Therefore, the acronym Ground-Gen (Ground Generation) is used to illustrate
this application. A graphical illustration of the above explanations can be seen in ﬁgure
[1.1].
Figure 1.1: A visual illustration of ground-Gen (a) and FG (b)[[21]].
==PAGE:20==
1 Introduction
2
In the FG process, which is the main focus of this dissertation, the electrical power
is being produced on the kite and then transmitted to the ground. This type of power
generation is based on Loyd’s concept of maximum drag during the power generation
mode [[34]]. In this case, the power generation is carried out by the rotor exposure to the
high airspeed forcing the rotor to rotate. Kites are producing power during crosswind or
non-crosswind ﬂight operation and consuming power for take-oﬀand landing cases. In
ﬁgure [1.2], companies are categorized based on the method that their kite is operating
during power generation.
Figure 1.2: Classiﬁcation of the AWES development companies based on their power
generation method. Question mark in front of the name Makani is indicating
that they are not available in the business.
Asides from the power generation method, FG systems can also be classiﬁed by their
ﬂight principles. There are three main ﬂight principles for the FG application, which are
illustrated in ﬁgure [1.3].
==PAGE:21==
1.1 Motivation
3
Figure 1.3: Flight based on the lift from one main wing e.g. KiteX, Makani (a) or a few
main wings e.g. Joby energy (b). Flight based on buoyancy and static lift
(c) ﬂight based on hovering and rotor thrust (d) [[15]] .
There is a common feature in all of the diﬀerent methods in FG systems, which is their
power generation component. They all use a system including a rotor and a generator to
generate power. The idea of using a propeller on a self-erecting system is ﬁrst introduced
by [[39]]. A tethered rotor-craft, a variant of gyro-plane, with conventional rotors to
generate power is designed to have access to the high altitude winds [[43]]. Later on,
Makani power [[6]] has patented an invention to passively customize the pitch angle of
the rotor for power generation and takeoﬀphases [[48]]. Airborne rotors - hereafter called
dual performance rotors - should meet two main requirements. They should provide
enough thrust for taking oﬀand landing and at the same time, they should operate well
in the power generation phase. Although many eﬀorts have been made in modeling and
optimizing the AWES, there is not any paper about designing dual performance rotors
for the kite application. Thus in this thesis, the design, and analysis of such rotors are
discussed.
1.1
Motivation
As AWES has the potential to become a very eﬃcient power production method in the
future, research regarding this topic is of much relevance. This includes the research in
the optimization of dual performance rotors for kite applications. As mentioned in the
previous section, no such work currently exist in any paper, stressing the importance of
the work done in this thesis.
==PAGE:22==
1.2 Research Objective
4
Designing a dual performance rotor is a complicated task since the maximum allowable
drag of the rotor during the power generation phase should be a speciﬁc value according
to the paper published by Loyd [[34]] and at the same time, the performance of such
rotors in the power generation phase is in a high level of importance. It should be
mentioned that the performance of the rotors directly aﬀects the amount of the generated
power and thus the income of the kite. Moreover, the designed rotor should be able to
lift oﬀthe kite during taking oﬀand landing, meaning the rotor should be able to act
as a propeller as well. This process can be done by pitching the rotor blade actively
or passively or even without pitching the blades. There are more considerations into
designing of a dual performance rotor e.g. integration of the generator/motor for instance
Brush-less Direct Current (BLDC) and its eﬀect on the performance of the rotor, eﬀect
of the kite design on the performance on the designed rotor, eﬀect of the BLDC and kite
control algorithm on the performance of the power generation, and limitations due to
noise and thrust considerations. In this work, the author tried to shed light on a few
of these questions and design a dual performance rotor for a speciﬁc kite design known
as KX4. The KX4 is the 4 KW rigid wing kite designed and tested by KiteX. This
AWE system is categorized as a FG system since the power generation components (dual
performance rotors and BLDC motor/generator) are implemented on the kite.
1.2
Research Objective
The main objective of this master thesis project is to design an eﬃcient dual performance
rotor. This can be divided up in the more detailed sub-objectives given the following
bullet points:
• Development and validation of a robust aerodynamic model for evaluation of the
performance of the dual performance rotor in the low tip speed ratio regions.
• Validation of the results from the BEMT model with the developed trustworthy
aerodynamic model in the low tip speed ratio regions.
• Understanding of the contribution of the diﬀerent key design parameters to the
performance of the dual performance rotor.
• Development of a robust optimization framework to design a ﬁxed and variable
pitch dual performance rotor.
1.3
Research Questions
In support of the proposed research as deﬁned the in objective section, the following
questions should be answered.
• Is the BEMT model result valid in the low TSR ratio regions?
• Having considered the kite geometry, what is the best working condition for a dual
performance rotor? e.g. tip speed ratio and tip speed.
==PAGE:23==
1.4 Outline
5
• Can a ﬁxed-pitch rotor be eﬃcient in both the hovering and the power generation
phases?
• What is the contribution of the variable pitch design on the overall performance of
the rotor?
1.4
Outline
In this section, an outline of the thesis is presented including a synoptic introduction of
each chapter.
In the second chapter, a model based on the prescribed vortex method is developed
to simulate the performance of the rotor in low tip speed ratio regions. Moreover, after
introducing the BEMT model, the results of these two models are compared. To justify
the correctness of these two models, a methodology to design a blade based on the desired
axial induction factor is introduced. In the end, based on the analysis of the results of
these two models, one of them is chosen for further study.
The third chapter starts with a brief introduction of the BLDC motors as well as
their control algorithms. These motors are playing an important role in the airborne
wind turbine application. Simpliﬁed know-how of this chapter is used in the optimization
section where diﬀerent important parameters as well as their impacts are discussed.
The fourth chapter is about optimization and numerical methods used in this thesis.
All of the methods are explained using an example from a dual performance rotor. This
chapter is included to give the reader an understanding of the methods used in this
thesis.
The ﬁfth chapter is about modeling the interaction between the kite and the rotors.
An estimated rotor area is thus calculated knowing the geometry of the kite and the
desirable axial induction factor. Also, a new approach to calculate the eﬃciency of the
dual performance rotor during hovering is deﬁned.
In the sixth chapter, all of the developed knowledge is used to design ﬁxed and
variable pitch dual performance rotors. Sensitivity analysis of diﬀerent parameters and
their eﬀect on the eﬃciency of the rotor is carried out. And ﬁnally, the results from the
ﬁxed and variable pitch rotor designs are compared.
Lastly, all of the major ﬁndings are presented in the conclusion together with a few
suggestions for future works.

CHAPTER 2
Rotor Modeling
2.1
Introduction
Blade Element Momentum Theory (BEMT) is used in the wind turbine world to estimate
the performance of such machines for the given working conditions and turbine conﬁgura-
tion e.g. wind speed, tip speed ratio, blade number, blade’s twist and chord distributions,
and blade pitch angle. Although the BEMT is studied and validated widely by CFD
methods [[25]] and reference data [[10]], the performance of the BEMT is unknown for
low tip speed ratio regions. In this chapter, a model based on the prescribed vortex
theory is developed to describe the performance of a rotor. This model is used to validate
the results from the BEMT model in low tip speed ratio regions where the performance
of the BEMT model is unknown. A computational model based on the prescribed vortex
theory is developed. The model is thus validated by comparing it with the result of some
analytical cases. Moreover, for a low induction case, the results from the BEMT and
vortex model are compared. Last but not least, some speciﬁc test cases were developed
as a reference for the comparison between the two of the models.
2.2
Computational Vortex model
Newton’s third law suggests that the rotor also spins the passing ﬂow. The spinning ﬂow
at the down wind of the wind turbine starts to aﬀect the ﬂow at the upwind. A famous
real illustration of the above mentioned example is seen in ﬁgure [2.1].
Figure 2.1: Visualized Wake at the downwind direction of the HAWT for diﬀerent tip

the interaction between the vortex horseshoe lines and the upstream ﬂow, the induced

In this section, Some underlying assumptions that simplify the model are discussed. A

For the high aspect ratio blades the length of the chord is much smaller than the
blade span. Having assumed that the rotor has a much bigger span in comparison with

2.5
BEMT
Blade Element Momentum Theory (BEMT) is a classical tool for calculation of the loads
on the wind turbine blades [[19]]. In this section an innovate implementation of this
method is presented
based on [[49]]. It should be mentioned that the base of the presented the BEMT
model is propeller physics. This means that the positive thrust force is propelling the
rotor and positive power shows that the rotor is consuming power to generate that thrust
force. The BEMT model is based on combining two diﬀerent physics, Blade Element
and Momentum theories. In the momentum theory the rotor is considered by the disk
that causes pressure drop. Upstream ﬂow (W ∗) accelerates as it moves toward the rotor
blade and reaches a speed of (Va = W ∗+ Vi) once it is passing the rotor, and it settles
down at the speed of Vs as seen ﬁgure [2.15].

CHAPTER 3
BLDC Motor
A brushless direct current motor is an electronically commuted motor. The only diﬀerence
with a brushed motor is that it does not need a brush to mechanically commute since
the procedure is being done electronically. These types of electric motors are extremely
eﬃcient in producing a large amount of torque in vast operating rotational ranges.
Commutation with electronics has a large scope of capabilities and ﬂexibility. They are
known for smooth operation and holding torque when stationary [[53]]. BLDC motors
have two-member the rotor and the stator. The rotor is the spinning part that holds the
permanent magnets while the stator is the stationary part that contains the winding.
The BLDC motors can be categorized into two diﬀerent subgroups based on their moving
member. Inrunner motor architecture dictates that the rotor locates in the center of
the motor while the stator winding is surrounding it. Since the rotor magnets are not
insulating the heat, the generated heat can dissipate very fast, making this architecture
suitable for producing a large amount of torque. Inn runner architecture is illustrated in
ﬁgure [3.1].
Figure 3.1: Illustration of an inn runner motor architecture where the spinning part is
positioned at the center of the housing [[53]].
The outrunner conﬁguration is the case where the rotor is surrounding the winding.
Rotor’s magnets trap the heat of the winding inside of the stator, and thus the motor is
==PAGE:62==
3.1 Control and Driving systems
44
not eﬃcient for the application of delivering high torque for the vast operating ranges.
Outrunner architecture is illustrated in ﬁgure [3.2].
Figure 3.2: Illustration of an out runner motor architecture where the spinning part is
surrounding the center coils [[53]].
Due to the diﬀerent architecture of the BLDC motors, they have diﬀerent innate
characteristics. Inrunner motors, normally have a lower diameter comparing to the same
type of motor with the outrunner conﬁguration. This translates to the key diﬀerence in
these two motors. Outrunner motors can deliver higher torque than inn runners due to
having higher moment arm. Outrunners, due to their wide diameter, have more coils per
motor section. In comparison with the inrunner motors which has fewer coils per motor
section, outrunners deliver less voltage when being rotated. Having said that outrunners
have lower KV - RPM \Volt, they can thus deliver higher torque as well. However, as it
has been said, the outrunner needs a more delicate cooling system since the winding is
located inside, and the rotating magnet is insulating the heat. On the other hand, heat
dissipation in the inn runners is amazingly eﬃcient, and there is not a need to design or
implement a sophisticated heat dissipation system.

been used to calculate the quadrature and direct current. The transformation is required
since controlling DC current is much easier than controlling AC phase current.

3.2
Losses
Another important factor in the BLDC motors is the eﬃciency of power consumption
or generation. The BLDC motor loss can be categorized into three main subgroups.
Mechanical losses, Magnetic losses, and copper loss. There are two types of Magnetic
losses, hysteresis loss, and eddy current loss.
3.2.1
Hysteresis
Hysteresis is caused by magnetization and demagnetization of the stator as the current
ﬂows in diﬀerent directions. The idea is that the amount of the change in the magnetic
ﬂux is not following the same rate as the change in the magnetic force. Therefore, once
the magnetic force is zero, the magnetic ﬂux is a positive number. The relationship
between the magnetizing force, H, and the ﬂux density, B, is shown on a hysteresis curve
in ﬁgure [3.11].
Figure 3.11: Illustration of the residual magnetizing force by increasing and decreasing
the ﬂux density, also known as a B-H curve. [Image courtesy: NDT Resource
Center].
3.2.2
Eddy current loss
The eddy current loss follows the Faraday’s law stating that any change in the surrounding
environment causes a voltage to be induced in the stator winding. So by just rotating
in a magnetic ﬁeld, a voltage is induced in the coils. The induced voltage will ﬂow the
circulating current. The power loss caused by this current is named eddy loss.
3.2.3
Copper loss
Copper is not a super conductor material, hence there is an incorporated loss in the
conduction. The more current ﬂows through the wiring, the higher the temperature gets,

aﬀecting the copper loss.
There are also other losses e.g. iron losses, iron loss in the stator teeth, Iron loss in
the stator yoke, and the stator core loss. For more detailed information [[5]].

Thus it is expected that the BLDC motors are operating at the maximum eﬃciency in a

the constraints can harness the objective value. If they are a big number, it means they
are strongly ruling the optimization problem and they prevent the objective function to
decrease in a big quantity. If they are small then the optimization problem is not being
strongly constrained by the respective inequality constrain. If they are zero, it means
the respective constraint is not active although satisﬁed [[47]].

4.2.5
Pareto Front
In the multi-objective optimization problems where there is more than one objective
value to be optimized simultaneously, it is important to understand the change of the
objective values with respect to each other. This study helps to ﬁnd the optimal design
by performing a set of trade-oﬀs between two or more objective values. It could be the
case that objective values are conﬂicting. Thus, it is important to know how much of each
objective value should be considered to achieve the optimal solution. Pareto eﬃciency is
a situation where no objective values can be better without sacriﬁcing the performance
of other objective values. Given the situation, if some of the objective values gain and
no objective value loses, then the process is called Pareto Improvement. Moreover, a
situation is called Pareto optimal or Pareto eﬃcient if no change could lead to improved
performance of all objective values. Pareto Front is a set of points in the design variable
space that have a non-inferior ﬁtness function value. On the other hand, there is no way
to improve the value of one objective value or function by not sacriﬁcing the value of
another one. A qualitative illustration of a Pareto front is shown in ﬁgure [4.10] [[17]].
==PAGE:86==

Figure 4.10: An example of a Pareto front where all of the points on the front are
non-inferior solutions.
There are two diﬀerent algorithms in MATLAB for calculation of the Pareto front,
ParetoSearch and Gamultiobj. Gamultiobj is based on a genetic algorithm and uses a
variant of NSGA-II [[18]] while ParetoSearch uses a pattern search algorithm to search
for the points on the Pareto front [[2]]. In this dissertation ParetoSearch algorithm is
used since it requires less computational resources.

Wrap-up
For highly nonlinear problems, well-known gradient-based algorithms cannot guarantee
that the global minimum value is found. Therefore a complementary algorithm is applied
to make sure that the global minimum value is found. A subprogram named GlobalSearch
in MATLAB is used in order to explorer the entire problem search space to assure
the global optimality. The performance of this algorithm with suitable parameters is
discussed and validated. This framework is used for designing of a dual performance
rotor.

turn deteriorates the eﬃciency of the dual performance rotor.

6.3.0.1

As a part of designing a dual performance rotor, one should consider the rotor performance
during both hovering and power generation phases. There are diﬀerent ways to combine
these two performances into one objective value. In this section, the total performance
of the rotor is gained by linearly combining the power generation and the hovering
eﬃciencies, see equation (6.14).
A combined objective value is thus considered to be optimized. The mixing gain that
deﬁnes the relative importance of each of these two eﬃciencies is altered and the optimal
value for the given mixing gain is calculated.
ηMO(X) = (1 −αM) · ηT(X) + αM · ηHov(X)

It is also important to know how much of the hovering eﬃciency needs to be considered.
A sensitivity test for the ﬁve-bladed rotor has been carried out. Increasing the mixing
gain will increase the chord length as well as the takeoﬀRPM. The eﬃciency of the power
generation drops by increasing the mixing gain since it includes more of the hovering
eﬃciency as seen in ﬁgure [6.16].

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
The results from the BEMT model cannot be trusted in the low tip speed ratio regions
since the physic of the ﬂow is unknown. A physical model based on the prescribed
vortex theory is implemented to describe the performance of a given rotor. Having
developed a bench-marking framework, the results from the BEM model are proven to
be valid in the low tip speed ratio regions as well. Airborne rotors are subjected to
the low Reynolds physics. However, due to the lack of reliable simulated or empirically
measured aerodynamic data that covers the full range of the AOA, a more general
airfoil - NACA0012 - is considered. Moreover, the adverse eﬀect of the Reynolds on
the aerodynamic performance of the sections are not considered. Having excluded the
adverse eﬀect of the Reynolds, increasing the rotor blade number improves the overall
eﬃciency of both power generation and hovering phases. Also, increasing the blade
number, decrease the rotor’s chord since the area of the rotor is considered to be constant.
Increasing the limit of the tip speed improves both performance of the FG and Hovering
phases. By an small increase of αM, hovering performance is dramatically improved. The
stall AOA for the hovering phase on the ﬁxed pitch design should be bigger, meaning
the selection of the symmetrical airfoil is not the right choice for the dual performance
rotors. The positive stall AOA requirement is mostly solved by allowing the optimizer
to pitch the rotor’s blade. Moreover, the variable pitch design boosts up the eﬃciency
of the power generation by 2% and the hovering phase by 1 % in comparison with the
ﬁxed pitch rotor design. Besides, the variable pitch design lets the rotor’s blade chord
distribution decrease in comparison with the ﬁxed pitch design. Last but not least, the
optimal tip speed ratio is smaller for the variable pitch design, meaning the relative speed
at the rotor is lower in comparison with the ﬁxed pitch design.
7.0.1
Future works
Following suggestions are made as topics for future works:
• The adverse eﬀect of the Reynolds number on the aerodynamic performance should
be investigated.
• The eﬀect of using other airfoils on the result of the optimization should be
quantiﬁed.
• A noise model should be incorporated together with the aerodynamic model as a
core processor of the optimizer, so the eﬀect of the noise level of the aerodynamic
design can be justiﬁed.
• A better representation of the BLDC motors/generators should be considered.
Either analytical models or empirical performance maps can be used on top of
