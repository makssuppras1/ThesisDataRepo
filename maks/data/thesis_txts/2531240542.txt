Abstract 
The aim for this thesis is to conduct a sustainable assessment of a proposed idea to create a city park in 
Copenhagen by closing the part of Vesterbrogade between Bernstorffsgade and H. C. Andersens Boulevard. 
Additionally, sustainable assessment of the current situation of the area and how proposed scenarios of car-
free Medieval City in Copenhagen may affect the sustainability assessment.  
The sustainability assessment is based on ten created sustainability development indicators. The process of 
developing the indicators were inspired by various literature on sustainability indicator analysis methods. The 
data was collected by internet questionnaires, structured interviews, participant observation, structured 
observation and secondary data. The indicators were assessed for the current situation with a scale of one to 
five, where five is very high sustainability and one is very low sustainability. The city park situation was 
assessed with a scale of minus two to two, where minus two is very negative impact on the sustainability, 0 is 
no impact on the sustainability and 2 is very positive impact on the sustainability.  
The results of the sustainability assessment showed an average sustainability of 2.7 for the current situation, 
-0.40 sustainability impact in worst-case scenario and 1.0 sustainability impact in best-case scenario for the 
city park situation. The study showed there were too many variables to know the effect the car-free scenarios 
in the Medieval City will have on the indicators.  
The study concluded that the level of sustainability of the current situation of the area was in average a little 
under medium sustainability and it could be improved by other measures than creating a city park, by 
implementing measures to address each indicator. In addition, it was concluded that the city park’s impact of 
sustainability on the area depends on the design of the park and therefore further investigations are needed. 
However, if the city park is well designed it will have a positive sustainable impact on the area and if the park 
is poorly designed then the area will be somewhat negative sustainable impacted. Lastly, it was concluded 
that the effect of car-free scenarios could not be assessed due to too many variables that are uncertain.

1 Introduction 
As part of a project to make the city of Copenhagen greener and more sustainable, the amusement park Tivoli 
has proposed to close down part of Vesterbrogade from Axelborg and into City Hall Square to create a city 
park (see Figure 1.1) (Plank, 2019c). There is no fixed plan for the design of the city park and so far, it is a 
loose idea. Their aim is to provide more space for pedestrians and bicycles, and free the space of cars and 
traffic lights (Gehl, 2019; Plank, 2019c). The idea is that the city park should invite the citizens to use the park 
to take stay in or transport through (Gehl, 2019; Plank, 2019c). The city park will extend over 10,400 m2 and 
become a recreation area in the middle of Copenhagen (Gehl, 2019; Plank, 2019c). Their rationale for creating 
a city park is that it is a congested passage where there are many pedestrians and cyclists with too little space 
and there are only a few cars according to Gehl’s traffic studies (Gehl, 2019; Plank, 2019c). The stretch has 
previously been closed due to the memorial concert for Kim Larsen and New Year's Eve 2018, where the 
citizens seemed happy with it (Plank, 2019c). It will increase security in front of Tivoli's main entrance on busy 
days, e.g. Friday concerts (Plank, 2019c). Additionally, it will play along with the new metro stations (Plank, 
2019c). An investigation shows that 59% Copenhageners agree that Copenhagen should change some roads 
to bike and walking zones and 51% are willing to pay more in taxes or fees (Rambøll, C40 and Københavns 
Kommune, 2019). However, one attitude to the project is that it will hinder car traffic and create problems for 
stores by making it harder for them to get customers, and the inhabitants will choose other jobs, because 
transport will be more problematic in Copenhagen for them (Brandt, 2019). The consequences will be that 
house prices will drop and people will have lower income by choosing other jobs (Brandt, 2019). 
 
Figure 1.1. Proposed city park area. Source of base map (Create your own Google Maps style, no date) 
Tivoli’s proposal will contribute to the City of Copenhagen's goal to make Copenhagen greener and make the 
locals stay more in the urban space in the city in the future. The City of Copenhagen wants to be a green and 
a sustainable capital and has the goal of becoming carbon neutral by 2025 (Technical and Environmental 
Administration, 2012; Københavns Kommune, 2019c). The number of inhabitants in Copenhagen is growing 
larger and larger and as a result, the streets and roads become more crowded and housing prices rise 
(Københavns Kommune, 2019c). There is therefore a need to create more space for the many new citizens 
(Københavns Kommune, 2019c). This creates a challenge in how the city should be designed and developed

so that the quality of life for citizens does not get worse, but better (Københavns Kommune, 2019c). The plan 
to make Copenhagen carbon neutral and develop Copenhagen to become a greener and smarter city is not 
only good for the climate and the environment; it also has a positive impact on the inhabitants (Technical and 
Environmental Administration, 2012; Københavns Kommune, 2019c). The positive impact will be due to less 
noise and air pollution, better housing and green mobility (Technical and Environmental Administration, 2012; 
Københavns Kommune, 2019c). In their climate plan, they have several different initiatives in the transport 
area, which will account for 11% of the CO2 reductions (Technical and Environmental Administration, 2012). 
Some of their major transport goals for 2025 are (Technical and Environmental Administration, 2012, p. 42): 
 
75% of all journeys in Copenhagen are on foot, by bicycle or by public transport. 
 
50% of all journeys to place of work or study are by bicycle. 
 
20% more passengers using public transport compared to 2009. 
Therefore, to meet the goals different measures have to be taken. The City of Copenhagen has the ambition 
to shut down car traffic in the Medieval City, which is a neighbourhood within the inner city of Copenhagen. 
The Technology and Environment Committee of the City of Copenhagen is interested in examining whether 
the inner city should be car-free and have set up two scenarios on how the area should be car-free 
(Københavns Kommune, 2019b). The two scenarios are to reduce the car traffic by 40% and 75% respectively 
with various limitations of driving purposes. There are mixed opinions as to whether the Medieval City should 
be car-free expressed in an interview with TV 2 Lorry. One opinion was that it will create problems for the 
stores in Copenhagen and another opinion was that the car-free initiative is a necessity, but that the issues 
that are in relation to the residents must be addressed (TV 2 Lorry, 2019). 
1.1 
Problem Statement 
Based on the different attitudes about car-free areas and the City of Copenhagen's plan to make Copenhagen 
greener and more sustainable, one main research question and several sub questions have been made: 
1 
Is it a sustainable solution to close Vesterbrogade from Bernstorffsgade to City Hall Square 
and create a city park instead? 
2 
What are the advantages and disadvantages of creating a city park? 
3 
How sustainable is the area now? 
4 
Is it possible to improve the sustainability of the area without creating a city park? 
5 
How will the two car-free scenarios in the Medieval City affect the indicators? 
 
The research questions will be answered by making a sustainability assessment of the current situation of the 
area and the city park situation by creating a sustainable indicator set. The indicator set will cover the three 
sustainability domains: economic, social and environment. The current situation is assessed on a scale from 
one to five, where five is very high sustainability. The assessment of the city park situation will be on a scale 
of minus two to two, where two is very positive impact on sustainability, zero is no impact on the sustainability, 
and minus two is very negative impact on sustainability. 
The assessment is based on a literature study of public urban spaces, benefits and disadvantages of green 
urban spaces and sustainability such as the disadvantages of pollution and measures to improve sustainability. 
The assessment of the city park situation is performed in a best-case and a worst-case scenario in order to

performed during this project were questionnaires and observations. Two questionnaires were developed for

2.2.1 
Mixed Methods Approach 
Mixed methods entail a combination of qualitative and quantitative data collection methods. The combination 
of methods can e.g. be used to complement each other, to interpret and explain research findings, to verify 
research findings or fill in gaps (Saunders, Lewis and Thornhill, 2009). Quantitative and qualitative are methods 
with different approaches and tools to answer different questions (Kvale and Brinkmann, 2015). Quantitative 
methods are used to investigate numerical data or quantify data (Kvale and Brinkmann, 2015). Qualitative 
methods are used to investigate to understand how something happens or are experienced, e.g. thoughts, 
feelings and considerations that respondents make (Stamp and Mikkelsen, 2008; Kvale and Brinkmann, 2015). 
Mixed methods have been used to collect data in this study to provide access to a more thorough investigation 
than a single method would allow in order to have better opportunities to answer the research questions and 
evaluate the findings (Saunders, Lewis and Thornhill, 2009; Brinkmann and Tanggaard, 2015).

2.2.2 
Questionnaire 
Two questionnaires were conducted; to the local businesses and to the users of the street. Questionnaires 
can be carried out differently, and in this study the types ‘internet questionnaires’ and ‘structured interview’ 
(see Figure 2.1) were used.  
 
Figure 2.1. Types of questionnaire (Saunders, Lewis and Thornhill, 2009, p. 363) 
The advantages of structured interview are personal contact, possibilities of explaining questions and ask 
additional clarifying questions if needed (Stamp and Mikkelsen, 2008). The disadvantages are that the 
surroundings can distract the respondents, the sample is not representative and it is time consuming (Aarhus 
Universitet, no date a; Stamp and Mikkelsen, 2008). The advantages of the internet questionnaire are it is 
quick to send out, answers are registered electronically, low cost and it is possible to integrate pictures (Stamp 
and Mikkelsen, 2008). The disadvantages are respondents can easily reject it, no personal contact or 
possibility to explain the questions (Stamp and Mikkelsen, 2008).  
The two questionnaires were created using Google Forms which is a survey administration app. It is free to 
use and can be accessed via Google Drive by creating a free Gmail account. Google Forms were chosen 
because it is free and easy to use for both interviewer and respondents. 
A questionnaire that is too long may seem incomprehensible to the respondent and may take too long to 
respond to, which may cause the questionnaire to be rejected by the respondent (Stamp and Mikkelsen, 2008). 
Therefore, the focus of this study was that the questionnaires should be as short as possible and only contain 
questions that could provide data in relation to the indicators. 
Both questionnaires were reviewed by different people before they were sent out or executed in the street. 
The language, types and number of questions were reviewed and given feedback on by the test individuals in 
order to make sure that the questionnaires would be understood by other people that had no knowledge or 
relation to the investigated topic. 
2.2.2.1 
Local businesses questionnaire (see Appendix A) 
This questionnaire was divided into two categories. Category one as the current situation to investigate the 
businesses’ perception of the number of customers they have now. Category two as the city park situation to 
investigate the businesses’ opinion on the impact the city park may have on the number of customers, economy 
and why they think it will affect them that way. It was stated in the questionnaire that it was anonymous, to 
increase the respond rate and more correct answers.  
It was sent to 16 businesses in the studied area and 27 in the surrounding area. After the first five businesses 
had responded, question five was added in order to get an elaboration of why they answered as they did. The 
businesses within the studied area were sent an e-mail with the questionnaire on 8th November 2019. And the 
businesses in the surrounding area were sent an e-mail on 14th November 2019. A reminder was sent out on 
26th November 2019 to those who had not answered. The e-mail and the reminder can be seen in Appendix 
B. The type ‘structured interview’ were conducted with two businesses that were not accessible with e-mail. 
The structured interviews were performed on 10th December 2019.

2.2.3 
Observation 
The method ‘Observation’ was used to collect both quantitative and qualitative data. The qualitative method is 
‘Participant Observation’ used to observe e.g. an area to describe and interpret people’s behaviour (Saunders, 
Lewis and Thornhill, 2009). Advantages are opportunity of explaining of what is happening in a particular 
situation or area, and opportunity of experiencing the emotions of the observed (Saunders, Lewis and Thornhill, 
2009). Disadvantages are observer bias, time consuming , and false recollections because there may not be 
time to write down notes during the observation which can cause an inaccurate analysis and interpretation 
(Aarhus Universitet, no date b; Saunders, Lewis and Thornhill, 2009). The quantitative method is ‘Structured 
Observation’ used to observe in form of counting and sampling (Stamp and Mikkelsen, 2008). Advantages are 
that the method can be used by anyone, reliability due to being able to replicate it, and first-hand collected 
data and thereby not depended on second-hand data, which can have added interpretations (Saunders, Lewis 
and Thornhill, 2009). Disadvantages are that it is time consuming, and results to draw conclusions from are 
limited (Saunders, Lewis and Thornhill, 2009). Due to time limitations of this study, the qualitative observations 
of the area were done within a limited time period, which can be seen in Table 2.A. The notes from the 
qualitative observations can be seen in Appendix G.

3 Theory 
In public urban areas, the environment affects how sustainability is in the area, as there is high density in cities 
and thus many cars because there are many people. Therefore, this section describes the advantages and 
disadvantages of green areas, impacts and limit values for noise pollution arising from road traffic and the 
consequences and limit values for pollutants that may have been created due to road traffic. In addition, four 
different urban areas are described where either car-free initiatives have been implemented or are currently in 
the planning phase.  
3.1 
Green Urban Spaces 
Green areas are important for the city, people, and the environment. Green areas contribute to e.g. health, the 
environment, and reducing air and noise pollution, but there are also disadvantages to creating new green 
areas such as green gentrification. 
Access and location are important factors to consider in planning and designing green urban areas (Mwendwa 
and Giliba, 2012). According to Katz (1998), for most residents, the distance should be no more than a five 
minute walk to a public green space and the location should be near mixed-use area where public transport, 
shops and public buildings are accessible. Stanners and Bourdeau (1995) suggest a walking distance of fifteen 
minutes or less to a green space in urban areas, as studies have shown that access and quality of green 
spaces are more important in terms of mental and social aspects than the size of green spaces. In addition, it 
should also be considered if the area is good enough in terms of road safety and easy accessibility for people 
when planning for green areas (Mwendwa and Giliba, 2012). 
People near a green area use it more often (Mwendwa and Giliba, 2012). People who have access to courtyard 
garden or other forms of green space around their home are more likely to use public green spaces and for a 
longer period of time than those who do not have green spaces around their home (Mwendwa and Giliba, 
2012). Studies have shown that there is a positive correlation between the distance to green areas and stress 
(Teknik- og Miljøforvaltningen, 2015). A study showed that people who have access to a green area within 50 
meters have less stress than those who have to go further than 50 meters to a green area (Mwendwa and 
Giliba, 2012). 
Green areas have been associated with improving the physical and psychological well-being of urban citizens, 
such as cardiovascular disease, mental health, work productivity, behavioural problems, sleep, social contact, 
physical activity, exposure to noise and air pollution (Mwendwa and Giliba, 2012; Nieuwenhuijsen and Khreis, 
2016). Green areas are important for the psychological health in urban areas, as stress levels decrease when 
humans are exposed to a natural environment and remain the same or increase when exposed to an urban 
environment (Mwendwa and Giliba, 2012). A study has shown that patients in hospital rooms overlooking a 
green area were 10% faster in recovery and had 50% less need for pain medication compared to patients in 
hospital rooms overlooking building walls (Mwendwa and Giliba, 2012). Another study showed an association 
between the amount of available green spaces and depression and suicide indicators (Min et al., 2017). People 
in low-green areas compared to high-green areas were 16-27% more likely to have depression and suicide 
indicators (Min et al., 2017). In addition, the study also found that there was a greater risk of depression and 
suicide indicators in people without moderate physical activity compared to people with moderate physical 
activity (Min et al., 2017). A major advantage of green urban areas is recreation, as people most often meet 
their recreational needs within the area they live in (Mwendwa and Giliba, 2012). 
Green areas also improve air quality, which has a positive effect on people's health (Mwendwa and Giliba, 
2012). Studies have shown that vegetation in parks can filter an average of 85% of air pollution (Mwendwa 
and Giliba, 2012). Studies have also shown that green areas can be used to reduce noise depending on the 
amount and distance of the source of noise, as it is not possible to use measures used for e.g. highways due

to urban density (Mwendwa and Giliba, 2012; Lacasta, Peñaranda and Cantalapiedra, 2018). In addition to 
green areas reducing air and noise pollution, it can also regulate urban climate, such as the urban heat island 
effect. Urban heat island effect can increase the temperature in urban areas by 5ºC due to cities' large areas 
of heat absorbing surfaces as well as high energy consumption (Mwendwa and Giliba, 2012). Vegetation can 
help, depending on design, quantity, distribution and maintenance, to reduce the temperature rise caused by 
urban heat island effect (Mwendwa and Giliba, 2012). In addition, vegetation can reduce the need for air 
conditioning and heating, thereby reducing energy consumption (Mwendwa and Giliba, 2012).  
Studies have shown that for every additional 10 hectares of green space (park or natural area) within 500 
meters walking distance, the value of the land and buildings in the area can increase by up to 10% on average 
(Mwendwa and Giliba, 2012; Teknik- og Miljøforvaltningen, 2015). This is due to that people are among other 
things willing to pay to stay closer to green areas (Mwendwa and Giliba, 2012). By creating new green space, 
this can benefit people as described above, but it can also financially benefit those who own land or nearby 
buildings. However, it can also create green gentrification which is providing a low-income neighbourhood with 
green features that increase property values and attract wealthier residents (Goossens, Oosterlynck and Bradt, 
2019). This can create displacement of local traditional businesses and low-income residents if they have to 
pay more in housing costs (Goossens, Oosterlynck and Bradt, 2019). In order to avoid displacement, it is 
proposed to introduce rent control when new features are added in the low-income neighbourhood (Goossens, 
Oosterlynck and Bradt, 2019)

3.4 
Cities with Car-free Zones 
Cars are a large source of air and noise pollution in urban areas. Congestion does not seem to be reduced by 
building new highway lanes or roads, and the increased amount of parking spaces reduce the space for green 
and recreational spaces (Roberts, 2019d). Therefore, cities in the world have or have started to create car-
free zones to reduce pollution (Bendix, 2019). In addition or instead of car-free zones some cities have created 
more space for bikes and pedestrians and deprioritised the cars, to put cyclists and pedestrians in focus 
(Bendix, 2019). This mean the cars have to give way for them, to increase safety for cyclists and pedestrians 
in traffic and decrease the space used for parking spaces (Bendix, 2019). Studies have shown that increasing 
the number of cyclists reduces the number of active transport accidents (Nieuwenhuijsen and Khreis, 2016). 
In addition to reduce pollution other reasons to create car-free areas in the cities are that cities are created for 
the people and used by the people, This can give more room for the city to create more green and recreational 
spaces for people to use.  
There are some issues with creating car-free zones. For instance peoples driving habits are difficult to change 
(Bendix, 2019). Car free zones can have the effect that the number of cars are not reduced but moved instead 
(Nieuwenhuijsen and Khreis, 2016). A study on car-free Sundays have shown that the air quality is more 
influenced by weather and no statistical significance from being traffic free (Nieuwenhuijsen and Khreis, 2016). 
Furthermore the study also found that when there was car-free Sundays the traffic was often moved to the 
suburbs (Nieuwenhuijsen and Khreis, 2016). Car-free zones can also cause inequalities for some people living 
==PAGE:29==

outside the zone (Nieuwenhuijsen and Khreis, 2016). The people living outside the zone who usually use their 
car will not have the possibility to use it to get to the city centre to access goods, service or experience the city

sales, mostly the specialised shops, where you need a car to transport the goods (Nieuwenhuijsen and Khreis,

Oslo 
Oslo is the Norwegian capital and has the goal to become carbon-
neutral by 2030. To achieve that Oslo has implemented car-free 
measures in the city centre (Bendix, 2019). They wanted to reduce the 
amount of cars by 20% by creating large car-free streets and building 
new bike lanes (Streetfilms, 2017). The first plan was to ban all cars 
from the city centre, but after consideration the plan change to only 
eliminate access for private vehicles in the city centre by 2020 (Bendix, 
2019). They have removed approximately 750 parking spaces for 
alternative use to create more space for pedestrians and cyclists by 
adding green areas, benches and bike lanes instead (Streetfilms, 2017; 
Clugston, 2019). Furthermore, some roads have been restricted to be 
one-direction and other roads open to limited (Shemkus, 2019). The 
restrictions on cars to enter the city have some exceptions; delivery 
vehicles during limited hours, charging spaces for electric vehicles, 
access and parking for people with disability, and emergency vehicles 
(Clugston, 2019; Shemkus, 2019) 
To make up for the restrictions on cars, they have made good and attractive alternatives for people to use 
instead of their car (Streetfilms, 2017). They have e.g. implemented city bikes and the bikes have names 
instead of numbers and the names used are the most common names in Oslo (Shemkus, 2019). They have 
implemented busses with four doors to deduce the time to go in and out of a bus to reduce the time to get from 
A to B (Streetfilms, 2017). In addition, they have made public transport cheaper, added more departures, and 
new trams and new metro lines (Clugston, 2019). Moreover, outside the city centre they have parking garages 
with 9,000 spaces, and traffic restrictions have been made to guide drivers to go around the city and use the 
ring roads instead (Clugston, 2019).  
To create a city centre with fewer cars, the process has been to implement changes gradual (Clugston, 2019). 
They started in mid-2017 and have slowly implemented restrictions and transformed streets (Clugston, 2019). 
The municipality intend to change the priority of transport modes on the streets by increasing the priority for 
pedestrians, cyclists and public transport instead of private cars (Clugston, 2019).  
They have seen some positive changes in the city centre since implementing the car-free measures, such as 
a 10% increase in pedestrians and an increase in the use of bike-sharing (Clugston, 2019; Shemkus, 2019). 
However, some businesses have had trouble and have had to close their shops, one of the reasons was that 
the goods were too heavy to buy without having a car to transport them in (Drivers see red over Oslo’s green

3.4.3 
Barcelona 
Barcelona had serious problems with air pollution in 2014 and failed 
to meet the EU’s air quality targets (Vox, 2016). “Studies were 
showing that air pollution in the region was causing 3500 premature 
deaths every year” (Vox, 2016, 0:56). Furthermore, the traffic also 
created problems by causing noise pollution (Vox, 2016). For that 
reason, Barcelona created a mobility plan to reduce cars by 21% 
(Vox, 2016). The mobility plan is based on the idea of 
“Superblocks”, which is created by Salvador Rueda in order to 
reduce air and noise pollution and create more spaces for green 
and public areas (Roberts, 2019d). The plan of Superblocks is not 
to remove cars from the city but instead reduce the amount of space 
they occupy and the need for people to use their car (Roberts, 
2019c). They have implemented five Superblocks in the city 
(Roberts, 2019a) and plan to create 500 in total, which will be 
around 70% of street space in Barcelona that will be used as mixed-
use public space (Roberts, 2019d). The plan can be implemented 
in the entire city with no increase in vehicle density on the main 
network streets, if the car reduction is only 13%. And if the car 
reduction is 21% which they expect it to be, the plan can be 
implemented and the vehicle density will be reduced in the entire city (Roberts, 2019d, 2019a).  
The Superblocks consist of nine city blocks, in the form of three by three, see Figure 3.8 (Roberts, 2019d). 
The main network streets have a speed limit of 50 km/h but within the block the speed limit is 10 km/h and 
there are one-way streets with no possibility of driving through the block, see Figure 3.9 (Roberts, 2019d). 
Residents and delivery vehicles have permission to enter the Superblock and the rest are to use the streets in 
the main network (Roberts, 2019d). The reduced amount of traffic and the low speed limit within the block 
decrease the noise pollution (Roberts, 2019d). Within the Superblocks the parking has been moved 
underground to take up less space above ground and the streets are shared public space, where people can 
walk, cycle or have social gatherings (Roberts, 2019d). For the Superblock idea to work, the area where it is 
implemented has to be diverse and dense (Roberts, 2019d). Therefore, Superblocks are designed as mixed-
use in order to be semi-self-contained communities where the residents have easy access to services e.g. 
restaurants, shops, health clinics etc. (Roberts, 2019d).  
In addition to the Superblocks, the mobility plan also includes plans for buses and bikes (Roberts, 2019d). 
There will be built separate cycling lanes in the entire city (Roberts, 2019d). The plan for the city bus system 
is to redesign it so the routes are mostly perpendicular around the Superblocks (Roberts, 2019d). Furthermore, 
there will be more buses, fewer routes, stops at approximately every 400 meters and residents will be within 
300 meters of a stop (Roberts, 2019d).

During the implementation of the Superblocks they have had a learning process to use for the future

consultation process with the residents which gave the residents a feeling of ownership (Roberts, 2019b). They 
used this implementation process because the consultation process before the implementing stage would last 
a lot longer (Roberts, 2019b). If they started out with structural changes it would not be possible to change 
later if the residents found out that something else would work better in practice (Roberts, 2019b).  
Some of the challenges they have or had to face are firstly moving the cars instead of reducing (Roberts, 
2019a). In order to reduce cars and not move them, other transport possibilities have to be upgraded to be 
better and more attractive for people to use so people can access the city easily without a car (Roberts, 2019d, 
2019a). Secondly, gentrification where the prices will increase and residents and shops will as a result be 
forced out because the Superblocks will attract wealthier people and tourism (Roberts, 2019a). One of the 
problems with an increase of tourism is that real estate speculators will buy housing and rent out through 
AirBnB instead of renting out to locals (Roberts, 2019a). Barcelona therefore learned that the Superblocks had 
to be implemented with other active measures to avoid gentrification. Measures such as e.g. that transit 
options, access to healthy conditions and public spaces are equal for people no matter what neighbourhood 
they live in (Roberts, 2019d).  
A study of before and after on Superblock number two and three, showed that there was a 10% increase in 
walking, 30% increase in cycling and 26% decrease in vehicle traffic in the inner street (Roberts, 2019b). They 
are attractive and popular which has resulted in gentrification due a large increase in tourism. This has caused 
the prices to increase because boutiques and real estate speculators have taken place in the Superblock 
(Roberts, 2019b). Surveys for the fourth Superblock showed that the traffic around the edge was increased by 
approximately 2-3% (Roberts, 2019a).

5 Sustainable Development Indicators 
Indicators can provide an indication of conditions and problems in different aspects of a situation to focus the 
information on the key issues of interest (Maclaren, 1996; Gudmundsson et al., 2016). The creation of a wide 
range of indicators is often more useful than a single indicator (Maclaren, 1996). However, there is no specific 
definition of the “perfect” urban indicator set (Maclaren, 1996; Diaz-Chavez, 2014). The reason is that an 
indicator set represent a specific given situation and therefore it may not be appropriate to use in another 
situation (Maclaren, 1996; Diaz-Chavez, 2014).  
The following steps present the process of developing the indicators to use in the sustainability analysis for 
the situations investigated in this study. 
5.1 
Scoping 
This study is a thesis; therefore, the main target audience chosen is the people evaluating this thesis. However, 
sub target audience that will benefit from the indicators are Copenhagen Municipality as a preliminary 
evaluation and inspiration for further investigation on the idea. Lastly fellow students to use for inspiration for 
their assignments and theses. The format of presenting the indicators depend on who the target audience is. 
Therefore, the indicators were chosen to be detailed, specific, measurable, and scientific valid.  
Based on guidance from supervisor in order to be able to create a holistic indicator set and time limitations in 
collecting data, the goal was to create eight to twelve indicators. Furthermore, the number of indicators 
depended on available and reliable data and their ability to cover all three sustainability domains: 
environmental, social and economic.  
5.2 
Framework 
For this study, a combination of the goal-based and the domain-based framework were used. Combining 
frameworks can help to overcome some of the weaknesses of the individual framework and also integrate the 
advantages of them (Maclaren, 1996). The creation of the indicators was first done with the goal-based 
framework in order to evaluate the sustainability of the area in relation to the predefined sustainability goals. 
Then, with the domain-based framework, the indicators were categorised into the three domains of 
sustainability to ensure that all dimensions of sustainability were covered.  
The goal-based framework was chosen due to it connects the indicators to the sustainability goals and is able 
to measure if the city park will be moving away from or towards sustainability (Maclaren, 1996). However, the 
weakness of the goal-based framework is “that it does not capture some of the complex interrelationships 
among the various dimensions of sustainability” (Maclaren, 1996, p. 191).

A list of different indicators was made by brainstorming in different steps and connecting them with the

5.5 
Selection Criteria 
There are various selection criteria identified in literature used for sustainability development analyses and the 
amount used varies from study to study (Tanguay et al., 2010). For this study, the following criteria are used:  
 
Relevance: To make sure that the indicators are relevant and actually measure the phenomenon it 
intended to measure. (Advisory Committee on Official Statistics, 2009; Gudmundsson et al., 2016) 
 
Data availability, feasibility and reliability: To make sure that data is available and reliable for 
each indicator and that it can be measured within the time and resource limits of this study.  
 
Available over time: To make sure that the indicators can track change and development over time 
(Advisory Committee on Official Statistics, 2009) 
The process of using the selection criteria were  
1. Sorted by relevance 
2. Sorted by data availability and feasibility 
3. Modification of indicators if needed 
4. Sorted by data reliability 
5. Identifying the dimensions of sustainability for each indicator 
6. Modification of indicators if needed 
7. Sorted by relevance and data reliability to choose the final indicator set.

Indicator 4c – Green Spaces 
Green areas have health and environmental benefits (described in section 3.1) which increase quality of life. 
Green areas can also enhance and strengthen biodiversity, create more recreational experiences for 
Copenhageners and contribute to cloudburst protection (Økonomiforvaltningen, no date; Københavns 
Kommune, 2019c). This can thereby attract more people to the city and the use of public urban spaces (Teknik- 
og Miljøforvaltningen, 2015). Copenhagen aims to have 75% of Copenhageners experience Copenhagen as 
a green city by 2025 (Københavns Kommune Økonomiforvaltningen, 2017). A study showed that for 35% of 
Copenhageners it is very important that there are more green areas (Rambøll, C40 and Københavns 
Kommune, 2019). 
The City of Copenhagen has the goal to have 20m2 of park and natural area with public access per inhabitant 
in Copenhagen (Københavns Kommune, 2013). The goal is currently met; however, they expect the city’s 
population to grow with more than 100,000 people by 2027. Technology and Environmental Management 
therefore proposes that the proportion of green areas follow the development of the population (Københavns 
Kommune, 2013).  
The indicator chosen for another assessment of the goal to promote stay/use in open spaces is “Area of public 
green areas per inhabitants within 15 minutes walking distance”. The fifteen minute walking distance has been 
chosen due to Copenhagen’s goal, which they have met, that 90% of Copenhageners should be able to walk 
to a park, beach, natural area in under fifteen minutes (Teknik- og Miljøforvaltningen, 2015). 
The capita and green areas within fifteen minutes walking were measured by using a distance of 1000 meters, 
which is based on the average walking speed of 4-5 km/h (6 Forskellige måder at gå på!, 2009). The speed 4 
km/h is chosen to include most people. In addition, within the fifteen minutes walking area, a five minutes and 
a ten minutes walking area were also investigated to compare the three areas.

capita are (Københavns Kommune, 2013):

7.3.3 
Indicator 3a – Noise Pollution 
The area will be completely closed off to vehicle traffic in the city park situation. Best-case scenario is that the 
urban space will be designed to minimise traffic noise. According to Gehl (2010), a good urban space is when 
designing, among other things, “protection against unpleasant sensory experiences” (Gehl, 2010, p. 239) (see 
Appendix Q). For example, noise protection can be designed by having vegetation in the area. Due to no car 
traffic the noise level will significantly decrease in the center of the area. The biggest source of traffic noise for 
the area will be from H.C. Andersens Boulevard and Bernstorffsgade. However, there are traffic lights between 
Vesterbrogade and H.C. Andersens Boulevard, and Vesterbrogade and Bernstorffsgade. This cause the cars 
to stop and thereby slow down the speed, which also reduces the noise level. The EAS is therefore rated for 
the best-case scenario to be 2. 
By creating a city park and getting car traffic off the road, the noise level will drop. However, the worst-case 
scenario is that car traffic is increased on surrounding streets such as H.C. Andersens Boulevard, 
Bernstorffsgade and Jernbanegade. This can create more congestion which in worst-case scenario, increases 
the noise level by an increase of people's use of the horn and shouting out the car window. In addition, the 
worst-case scenario is that the city park is designed poorly in relation to noise. The EAS for worst-case scenario 
is therefore estimated to be 1 as the noise level will decrease significantly due to the removal of the car traffic, 
but poor design of the urban space in relation to noise. 
7.3.4 
Indicator 3b – Air Quality 
The air quality measurements in the best-case scenario will be the same since the air measurement station is 
at H.C. Andersens Boulevard. The best-case scenario is that traffic on surrounding streets will not increase 
due to the area of car traffic being closed is small. There are opportunities to design the urban space to reduce 
local air pollution by using vegetation, e.g. by planting trees or flowers or creating green walls. However, it will 
not be possible to measure the impact locally with the same measuring station on H.C. Andersen Boulevard 
as air quality is affected by pollution from surrounding areas in relation to wind. The EAS for best-case scenario 
is therefore 0. 
Worst-case scenario is if there is a displacement of air pollution caused by an increase of car traffic on the 
surrounding roads and creating more congestion which can worsen the air quality. And since the air quality at 
the measuring station is affected by the air quality of the surrounding areas, the results of the measurements 
can worsen in the worst-case. Furthermore, it is also worst-case scenario that design options to reduce air 
pollution locally are not incorporated. Therefore, the EAS for worst-case scenario is -1. 
In addition to reducing car traffic, various policies within transport and urban planning can also help reduce air 
pollution (World Health Organization, 2018a). It can e.g. be “shifting to cleaner heavy-duty diesel vehicles and 
low-emissions vehicles and fuels, including fuels with reduced sulfur content;” (World Health Organization, 
2018a). The City of Copenhagen has the targets for 2025 that “20-30% of all light vehicles use new fuels like 
electricity, hydrogen, biogas or bioethanol.” (Technical and Environmental Administration, 2012, p. 42) and 
“30-40% of all heavy vehicles use new fuels.” (Technical and Environmental Administration, 2012, p. 42).

Indicator 5a – Safety at Night

perceive the safety in traffic due to H. C. Andersens Boulevard being a large and busy road. The EAS is

and bike safely. Additionally, it is badly designed if the cyclists are driving too fast and thereby creating an 
unsafe environment and if they do not know where to safely park their bikes without it creating obstacles or 
clutter in the urban space. The EAS is therefore -1.

8 Discussion 
This section discusses to begin with the used methods in relation to how it may have influenced the results 
and what could have been done differently. Second, the city park in relation to design. Third, other solutions 
that can be used instead of creating a city park and improve the sustainability of the area. Lastly, 
recommendations of further work that could be done to investigate the impacts of the city park. 
8.1 
Methodology 
It has been useful to use both qualitative and quantitative methods, as well as using a combination of primary 
and secondary data to get a more holistic view of the situation. The questionnaire could be extended to include 
what businesses and users think are important in the design and in relation to the indicators. In addition, how 
the design would affect their response e.g. in relation to security or residence. 
The results for the observations and questionnaire, would have been more representative had they been 
carried out over different periods of time. The time interval could have been varied, but there was a time limit 
on the project and thus it was not possible to do it over a longer period. 
It could have been interesting to conduct interviews with several different stakeholders and conduct in-depth 
interviews to gain more background knowledge and more opinions on the proposal to close the road and build 
a city park. 
The season may have had a large impact on the results. When the observations and questionnaires were 
performed, it was cold and windy which could have influenced people’s behaviour in the area e.g. the number 
of cyclists in the area and thus the use of bicycle parking. The cold and windy weather could also have 
influenced people's answers to the questionnaire because the cold is not inviting people to take stay in the 
area. In addition, it may have influenced the responses of the local businesses in relation to their understanding 
of the question of their perception of the number of customers. They may have answered how the snapshot is 
or how it is in average.  
8.1.1 
Questionnaires 
For future work it would be beneficial if more tests were performed with several test groups before the 
questionnaires were taken into use. This can reduce sources of error and thereby increase the chance of more 
qualified and correct answers. 
In addition, for future work, it would produce a more representative result if more responses were collected to 
the questionnaires and that respondents from several different locations were asked. It may have caused bias 
in the results that respondents came from a limited place such as a single company in the user questionnaire. 
However, it can be seen that people from the particular company live in different postcodes but most of the 
respondents are in the age group 45 - 65 years. 
Moreover, the study could be expanded to look at differences in the responses based on geography, gender, 
age, etc. The question about gender was deselected as this was not found relevant to the research questions 
in this study and to limit the scope of the study. 
It can be a problem that the questionnaires contained a response scale from one to five. People may have 
been inclined to answer three because they do not know what they think and have doubts about what to answer 
(Brinkmann and Tanggaard, 2015). If there were four response options instead of five it would have forced 
people to take a stand. This can give a wrong picture of sustainability as people have to decide whether it is 
good or bad, where some have the opinion that it is okay and neither good nor bad. 
==PAGE:82==
8 | Discussion 
 
 
69 of 81 
The answers to the questionnaires may have been influenced by different sources of error. For the online 
questionnaires misunderstanding questions and due to it being a self-administered questionnaire, it is not 
possible for respondents have a question explained, compared to the structured interviews in the street. A 
source of error can be social desirability, which is people's tendency to want to appear as attractive as possible 
(Aarhus Universitet, no date c). Users and businesses may therefore have answered in favour of appearing in 
a positive light. For example, respondents in the structured on-street interview, when asked if they would sort 
street waste if possible. The fact that they are face to face with the interviewer may have influenced their 
response as they may wish to appear in a positive light especially when it is a sustainable trend. A source of 
error in the responses from the local businesses may be that it is an online questionnaire sent to them. It is 
therefore not possible to know who actually gave the response, whether it was the manager, someone else 
who has the knowledge of the number of customers and the economy or if it was an employee further down 
the hierarchy. Sources of error in the answers from the users may be that some of the respondents on the 
street were from Jutland. A disadvantage can be that they are not often in Copenhagen and a city park would 
not make a difference for them personally and their answers may come from a place of being indifferent. 
However, an advantage can be that their answers are based on their first impressions and not influenced by 
e.g. economy or everyday habits in the area. In addition, a source of error in the user questionnaire could be 
wrong answers due to misunderstandings of the questions. For example, question 12 in the user questionnaire 
may have been misleading for the online respondents because only stores and restaurants were written as 
examples of the types of businesses. The area consists of several different types of businesses and the 
respondents may have given a wrong answer compared to their actual use of the businesses in the area. After 
the structured interviews in the streets, some of the questions were considered not applicable because of the 
wording of the questions and the reliability of the results to be assessed for the deselected indicator “% of 
people taking each mode of transportation”.  
8.1.2 
Indicator Development Process 
Due to the time limitations of the study and limited access to data, the number of indicators became ten. 
However, it might produce a better result with more indicators. For example, there could be more indicators 
within the sustainability domain, economy. These could be indicators such as housing prices, property values 
or health costs. Health costs could thereby measure how large the future financial consequences are due to 
noise and air pollution specifically in the area studied. 
8.1.3 
Sustainability Assessment 
Assessing the indicators in whole numbers can make the assessment of sustainability imprecise. Some of the 
indicators were rated by multiple assessments and the sustainability score could therefore have been an 
average rather than rounding up to a whole number. However, it was chosen to use whole numbers for the 
SPS and EAS for each indicator because they should have the same assessment basis due to each indicator 
being assessed equally. 
The results of the sustainability assessment of the current situation and the city park situation could have been 
different if the indicators had not been assessed equally. The average score of the sustainability could e.g. be 
calculated differently with a system that weights the importance and influence on sustainability of each

8.2 
City Park Design 
The design of the city park is important in relation to the results of the indicators. The process can be done in 
different ways. For example, it can be done with public participation extended over a longer period to reach a 
solution that both people and businesses are satisfied with. The advantage is that many views from different 
stakeholders are included, so that the city park can be best designed in relation to satisfying as many as 
possible. However, it can be a disadvantage that after the city park is done that people find that the design of 
the park does not work in practice. This may require changes in order for the park to function better and it can 
be expensive in structural changes. Another solution might be to close the road and implement temporary 
urban design elements. Then analyse the area in terms of how people use the area and what users have 
observed they need and want. This method was used in Barcelona, described in section 3.4.3, where they 
changed the traffic of a Superblock and made temporary urban design elements instead of permanent 
structural changes. 
Before designing the park, it is important to keep in mind if cyclists or pedestrians are prioritised. It is important 
in terms of traffic safety who is the first priority in the city park. It can have an impact on who will use the park 
and how it will be used. In relation to Tivoli's problem and justification for creating a city park, it would make 
the best sense to design the city park where pedestrians are first priority and cyclists are second priority. 
However, it should still be made easy and attractive for cyclists to cycle in the area so that they do not use a 
car instead or opt out of using the area. 
In addition to design of the city park to improve the performance of the indicators mentioned in section 7.3, 
other design measures can be implemented such as cloudburst protection or turning footsteps into electricity. 
The kinetic energy from foot traffic can be harnessed by installed tiles in the pavement and convert it to 
electricity to use for e.g. the street lights in the area (Balch, 2015).  
8.3 
Other solutions 
In relation to the indicators, the sustainability of the studied area can be improved by implementing other 
measures without creating a city park. The indicators are designed to measure the sustainability of the area 
regardless of whether a city park is being built or not. 
 
To reduce the local noise and air pollution the vegetation in the area could be increased by e.g. 
creating green wall or roof gardens. Greening the street can also invite more people to the area and 
increase the attractiveness of the street.  
 
To increase the amount of recycled waste, the regular waste bins could be replaced with street sorting 
waste bins described in section 7.3.10.  
 
To increase the amount of cyclists and the amount of people in the area, the amount of bicycle parking 
could be improved and increased as described in section 7.3.2. 
 
Implementing parking for electric kick scooters to improve the conditions of the bicycle parking by 
decreasing the bicycle parking spots used by electric kick scooters.

8.4 
Further Work 
This study is a beginning in relation to investigating whether the city park is a good idea from a sustainability 
perspective. However, it requires several different kinds of studies with different perspectives in order to have 
a proper decision basis for whether or not to build a city park. The following studies are a proposal of what 
studies can be done to get a more holistic view of the city park proposal. 
Examine and analyse the current traffic flow and future traffic flow if the road is closed to car traffic. Traffic flow 
simulations can be done to find out what effects it has on the city park and surrounding roads. It will investigate 
whether there is an increase or decrease in car traffic or whether it can be displaced. In addition, whether the 
number of cyclists and bicycle trips will increase or decrease and what effect this will have on the bicycle 
parking. 
Perform a cost-benefit analysis to assess if the benefits outweigh the costs of different outcomes to identify 
what action to take. The benefits to different outcomes for the people and companies in the area and for the 
municipality must be investigated. This will also create an overview of the possible consequences for retail 
stores and businesses in the area. 
Studies of possible alternative solutions instead of creating a city park. The problem is that there is too little 
space for pedestrians in front of Tivoli's main entrance and it should therefore be investigated whether this can 
be improved without creating a city park. For example, narrow the road and restrict the traffic on the road so 
that only buses and taxis have access. This allows the area in front of Tivoli's main entrance to be made larger 
and wider. In addition, the restriction of traffic can also have a positive effect on the noise level. 
Further sustainability assessment to extend the study to what design measures will be most beneficial in terms 
of sustainability. As well as expanding the number of indicators by including more economy indicators. 
Public participation by involving businesses and residents to listen to their opinions and ideas. By involving 
them, it can be discovered whether they are for or against and whether there is a real need for an urban park. 
This can affect the development of the design as well as whether a city park should be built. 
The studies must be carried out over longer periods to obtain a more realistic picture. There is a difference in 
the use of an area in relation to weather. The studies should therefore take place in all four seasons; winter, 
spring, summer and fall.

9 Conclusion 
Based on the conducted sustainability assessment of creating a city park it is concluded that the level of 
sustainability depends on the design of the park. Therefore, there is a need to investigate further to be able to 
have a qualified basis for a decision such as, traffic analysis, cost-benefit analysis, benefits of alternative 
solutions to building a city park, further sustainability assessments and public participation. Though, the 
sustainability assessment of the city park showed that in a best-case scenario the sustainability of the area 
would be impacted positively (score = 1). And in a worst-case scenario the sustainability of the area would be 
impacted negatively (score = -0.4). However, the size of the impact in the best-case scenario is 60% higher 
than the size of impact in worst-case scenario. In addition, it is concluded that it has to be decided how the 
design should be done. Whether it should be by a traditional design phase or whether to close the road and 
then analyse the need and use of the area. Moreover, in the design phase, different design measures can be 
implemented to improve the performance of the indicators as well as implement other measures that can 
increase sustainability in other ways. 
Secondly, it is concluded that the advantages and disadvantages of creating a city park depends on how the 
city park is designed. Therefore, if the design of the city park is good in relation to how urban spaces are best 
designed, the advantages will be that the city park can connect people by creating conditions to interact 
socially, increase tourism by making the area more attractive and inviting, decrease health costs by reducing 
local noise pollution and increase security and safety. In addition, implementing a green urban space can 
improve the physical and psychological well-being of the inhabitants close to the city park by e.g. reducing 
stress, increasing mental health, and reducing local air and noise pollution. Green areas can also reduce the 
energy consumption because green areas reduces the need for air conditioning and heating. However, if the 
design of the city park is bad in relation to how urban spaces are best designed, the disadvantages will be that 
it can create more problems due to more people being in the area. This can increase the safety issues in front 
of Tivoli’s main entrance and decrease the safety in traffic. In addition, the disadvantages can also be a 
decrease in retail sales, increased housing costs and rent in Copenhagen, which can cause displacement of 
residents and businesses. Moreover, the city park can also affect the surrounding area because the park may 
cause an increase in car traffic and congestion on the surrounding streets.  
Thirdly, it is concluded that the current area is on average a little under medium sustainable with the score 2.7. 
The air quality in the area measured very high sustainability (score = 5) and the amount of bicycle parking, 
accessibility and condition of public toilets and the perceived safety at night were measured to be high 
sustainability (score = 4). The number of customers in the local businesses and the perceived safety in traffic 
were measured to be medium sustainability (score = 3). The noise pollution, amount of stays in the area, green 
area per inhabitant and waste bins were measured to be very low sustainability (score = 1), which equals as 
unsustainable.  
Fourthly, it is concluded that it is possible to improve the sustainability of the area without having to build a city 
park. This can be done by implementing various measures in the area, such as increasing vegetation, making 
it possible to sort street waste in the urban space, improve and increase bicycle parking, implement electric 
kick scooter parking, improve signs for public toilets and improve and increase traffic signs that cyclists and 
electric kick scooter riders must comply. However, when improving the sustainability of a specific indicator, the 
impact of the implemented measure on the other indicators should be taken into account. This is due to the 
indicators are connected and improvements can increase or decrease the sustainability of the other indicators.

Lastly, it is concluded that it is not possible to give a qualified assessment of the possible effect on the 
indicators from the car-free scenarios in the Medieval City, because there are too many variables that are 
uncertain. Except for indicator 6 (waste bins), which will not be affected by the car-free scenarios. More studies 
of traffic flow, parking spaces, the effect on businesses and property value in the area around the Medieval 
City are needed to assess the effect of the car-free scenarios on the indicators. From the literature study it was 
found that there are different solutions in different cities. This is partly due to the fact that cities are structured 
and function differently. The literature study showed that car-free zones work best in mixed-use areas, as 
people have access to various services within walking distance, thereby reducing the need to use the car. The 
literature study also showed that car-free zones can create problems for stores with heavy goods within the 
zone, where customers need a car to transport the goods. Moreover, it was also learned that in order to reduce 
the use of cars, it is not enough to make restrictions against car use, but to increase and improve alternative 
options. It can for example be to improve and increase the amount of public transport. Additionally, there should 
be more focus on the residents in the area than attracting tourists, to ensure that residents are not forced out 
due to rent increases.
