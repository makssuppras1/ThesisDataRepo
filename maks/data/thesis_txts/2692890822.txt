Abstract
Computer vision (CV) is one of the fastest growing ﬁelds of artiﬁcial
intelligence. Fields of usage of CV is expanding day by day in or-
der to maximize the automation of brute force tasks in human life.
Autonomous vehicles such as cars, trucks or drones can be given as
examples of areas of usage.
Image localization is one of the tasks
deﬁned under CV. More speciﬁcally it is the task of retrieving the
pose and orientation of a camera given a query image in a known 3D
geo-localized scene[1].
In this thesis, we want to explore, how well we can match aerial drone
imagery with images taken from ground-level. The obvious extreme
change of perspective makes it particular challenging. The project
focuses on deep learning strategies for solving the problem. State-of-
art deep learning based approaches[2][3][4][5] are promising but fail
to address our exact problem. Further, our application diﬀer as we
regress the camera pose as well. During this research, we will further
investigate state-of-art diﬀerences and possible improvements.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In the era of artiﬁcial intelligence and digitalization, diﬀerent kinds of tasks are
automatized in order to simplify daily life, increase eﬃciency in processes, etc. As
a human, it is an easy task to interpret the outer world via the signal transfers
between the eye and brain.
We recognize, detect, classify objects around us
without a hitch. Computer vision (CV) is the corresponding technology for the
eye and brain to interpret the outer world via images or videos. CV is one of the
fastest-growing ﬁelds of artiﬁcial intelligence. Autonomous vehicles such as cars,
trucks, or drones are a perfect practice area for CV because autonomous vehicles
are trying to interpret the outer world for guiding themselves and succeeding
in the tasks deﬁned. The usage areas of CV are expanding every day by the
improvements in academia and industries. In Section 2, state of art developments
for our speciﬁc problem is going to be discussed in depth.
In recent years, signiﬁcant improvements has been shown in the digitalization
of businesses and the idea of digital twin. After the enormous strides have been
made in hardware, cameras, computational resources and CV, it has become
opportune to digitalize our world or business cases with relative ease.
Chen
1
==PAGE:25==
et al.[21] described digital twin as a computerized model of a physical device or
system that represents all functional features and links with the working elements.
With the advancements in the computational resources, it has become possible to
create digital twin of the real world whether it is from a ﬂight simulation of a drone
or creating a virtual scene with respect to the real world via CV applications. For
example, using satellites, or cameras mounted on planes, drones or vehicles, it is
possible to systematically cover/scan a construction site, an industrial complex,
or a campus. From this database of image or video footage, it is possible to
create a digital model of the site and ﬁnd various business cases such as planning
of maintenance or inspection.
In this thesis, Dansk Drone Kompagni (DDK)1 is a collaborator as the case
and dataset provider. The case is a speciﬁc business problem and dataset is a
real world dataset of images. It can be coarsely said that business case is image
localization with the camera pose estimation. Their ﬁrst approach was creating a
digital twin via rendering the scene of interest through the images captured with
drones which can be called as a digital twin of the real world scene. DDK could not
obtain the results that they have sought for. There are still technical challenges
in the process of creating digital twins, and in creating value/applications from
them. Because of the results, in this thesis, diﬀerent approach is used in order to
look at the problem from a diﬀerent perspective. Therefore, in this thesis, we are
focused on the image localization problem which has a broad application area in
CV.
With the supposition that there is a geo-localized scene and a placed cam-
era/sensor within the geo-localized scene, image localization is the problem of
1https://www.dronekompagniet.dk/
2
==PAGE:26==
retrieving the pose and orientation of a image given a query image in known
2D/3D geo-localized scene. Image localization has diﬀerent terminologies in lit-
erature such as visual-based localization, image-based localization, visual geo-
localization, camera re-localization, image-based pose estimation, and much more[1].
Image localization problem comes in many forms and variants because of the vari-
ability of problem deﬁnition, scene and pre-collected data. For example in some
cases, parameters such as latitude, longitude or height values of query image are
known. The query images can cover various degrees of scene such as 60◦, 120◦and
so forth. The query images can be panoramic. Diﬀerent scenarios based on the
task can be populated.
In the literature, there are two diﬀerent approaches for image localization
tasks. These approaches can be classiﬁed under direct and indirect methods.
Indirect methods specialize in image retrieval via querying an indexed tree-
structured database. In the survey paper of Zhou[1], there are example researches
for both methods. The work of Zamir et al.[22] can be given as a solid example
for the indirect method application. In this research, image localization is ad-
dressed via creating a structured data set of around 100,000 images which are
retrieved from Google Maps Street View. SIFT descriptor[23] is used as a feature
descriptor. By using SIFT descriptor, Zamir et al.[22] is able to index the SIFT
descriptors in the reference images by using a tree. Then the tree structure is
queried in order to localize the query image.
The diﬀerence between indirect and direct methods is actually a trade-oﬀ
between area coverage and precise image pose estimation[1]. Indirect methods was
a better option if we would be interested in relatively large scale areas. It can be
3
==PAGE:27==
coarsely said that there are three pillars under direct methods which are structure
from motion, feature-based and learning-based[1]. In this thesis, we focused on
learning-based pillar of direct method application of image localization. The main
reason for focusing on learning based pillar comes from the advantages of Deep
Neural Networks (DNN). The real life has dynamic environment which creates
complex variability in the data via changes in lighting, weather, angles etc. Thus,
this complex variability in the data, makes it hard to model with hand-crafted
methods[24].
The learning based methods have important advantages even if
the environment includes complex variabilities. Before diving into the business
case and approach of this thesis, it is important to understand advantages of
learning based methods, why these methods are chosen to be used DNN models
to solve addressed problem in this research. In the survey paper of Chen et al.[24],
three main advantages of learning based methods has been mentioned. The ﬁrst
advantage is being robust to dynamic environmental changes via ﬁnding out the
features required for the task. The second one is the ability of learning from
historical data and adaptation to additional scenarios.
The improvements in
computational power and data obtaining systems enables DNN structures to be
useful from the aspect of covering much of the scenarios, and learning from these
scenarios can be given as the third advantage.
As it is aforementioned, previous approach of DDK1 was creating a rendered
scene via aerial images. Their ground query images has a certain degree such as
120◦or 90◦of angle of view. They queried the region of interest in digital scene with
ground images via descriptor based DNN in order to ﬁnd best matched image.
After ﬁnding out the best matched ground image in the scene, they regressed
the camera pose. Since the results are needed to be improved, problem has been
changed. In the this thesis, aerial images of the given case are satellite images and

In this research, we heavily use neural networks for solving the image localization
problem. Therefore, this chapter provides theoretical background and mathe-
matical explanations for understanding how neural networks solve large scale
problems such as cross view matching (CVM) and image localization (IL).
2.1
Neural Networks
Inspired from the biological systems of a brain, neural networks are composed
of series of neurons. Neurons are the cells in human nervous system that are
tasked with transmitting information. Artiﬁcial neurons are designed for the same
purpose in mathematical expressions and designs so that they can be interpreted
and learned by computers. Deep learning is the concept of learning by data in a
neural network structure.
In deep learning, there are diﬀerent types of neural networks which are dif-

Triplet loss function is introduced to literature by Weinberger et al.[34]. It is
used for the neural network purposes ﬁrst by Florian et al.[35] who were working

3.2.1
Discussion
For the CVM task most discussed topic is the ingestion of orientation informa-
tion into the neural networks. In most of the papers, there is a local feature
extractor backbone neural network such as VGG-16[7]. On top of the backbone
there is a layer which may embeds orientation information or extract orientation
information or extract more global features.
Most challenging part of CVM task is view angle problem. In order to miti-
gate this problem, polar transformation is applied. After polar transformation is
applied, recall rates are increased. (see Table 3.2, Table 3.3)
Since CVM task is aimed to be solved via DNNs, various neural network
conﬁgurations are examined from the literature. For the siamese neural network
design, loss functions create diﬀerences and improved versions of triplet loss and
contrastive loss functions are oﬀered in literature. The DNNs are trained with
a certain batch size in order not to be stuck with local minimal during gradient

6.2 Evaluation Methods
6.2
Evaluation Methods
As it is mentioned in Section 6.1, dataset has positive and negative pairs. Each
pair is labeled as 1 or 0. In order to assess the classiﬁcation each label, metrics
rooted inﬂicted by confusion matrix is used for model performance evaluation
purposes. An observation from a pair either belongs to a class or it is predicted
to be belonging to a class by the model. There are four main deﬁnition for this
phenomenon which are as follows;
• True Positives, TP: Number of observations which are in fact positive
(yi = 1) and predicted to be labeled as positive (ˆyi = 1).[14]
• True Negatives, TN: Number of observations which are in fact negative
(yi = 0) and predicted to be labeled as negative (ˆyi = 0).[14]
• False Positives, FP: Number of observations which are in fact negative
(yi = 0) and predicted to be labeled as positive (ˆyi = 1).[14]
• False Negatives, FN: Number of observations which are in fact positive
(yi = 1) and predicted to be labeled as negative (ˆyi = 1).[14]
There are three model performance evaluation measures used in this research
in order to test the model predictions which are shown in Equation 6.1.
Accuracy =
TP + TN
TP + TN + FP + FN
Precision =
TP
TP + FP
Recall =
TP
TP + FN

6.4
Discussion & Results
In this research, mainly two architectures are used which are as follows;
• A1: VGG[7] backbone
• A2: VGG[7] backbone + NetVLAD[4]
In Section 6.3.1 and 6.3.2 sixteen models are explained and their performance
according to accuracy, precision and recall metrics are reported. For the image
size parameter, only the pixel values of 128x128 and 256x256 are evaluated be-
cause with stacked layers of neural network, above the pixel values of 256x256
caused overﬂow in memory even with 60GB of GPU RAM. Models trained with
pixel values of 256x256 images yielded better result. Number of epochs prolongs
the duration of model training phase, therefore 500 is set to be a standard in this
research. Learning rate is an important parameter in gradient descent algorithm
where 10−5 worked better than 10−3. In the models with A1 architecture, batch
size 30 yielded better results whereas A2 architecture yielded better performance
with batch size 10. Fully-connected layers at the end of pipeline improved model
performances on both A1 and A2 architectures. Dropout layers after convolu-
tional and fully connected layers improved model performance on A2 architecture
because reducing crowded feature vector helped the model performance. In con-
trast with A2, A1 architecture yielded worse results with dropout layer since it
doesn’t have additional feature extractor layer on top.

The best model among the models with A1 architecture is M8. The best

needed. But roughly both architectures have very similar results in evaluation
metrics. A statistical method such as McNemar test can applied for the compar-
ison task of these classiﬁcation models.[14] This study oﬀers both solutions with
aforementioned neural network conﬁgurations.

Chapter 7
Conclusions
The focus of this thesis was to create a Deep Neural Network pipeline for CVM
task. The work which has been done in this thesis concluded a Deep Neural
Network architecture pipeline with satisfactory results. In addition to DNN ar-
chitecture, a state-of-art cross view matching dataset named as CV-DTU dataset
is released.
The CV-DTU dataset consists of aerial images and ground query images. The
aerial images are orthogonal or oblique to the ground. The ground images are
street-view ground images and collected with a special equipment mounted on
a car with the help DDK. According to case deﬁned, orientation information is
known only for aerial images but orientation information is not explicitly used.
The main task was image localization via matching of cross view images from
aerial and ground. For this task, state-of-art solutions are investigated in the lit-
erature. (see Chapter 3) A neural network architecture developed which includes
VGG[7] backbone, NetVLAD[4] layer, contrastive loss function[36] and additional
conﬁgurations with dropout and fully connected layers. (see Chapter 6) As a re-
sult of experiment phase, models with performance of “r@1” recall rates of 97%
103
==PAGE:127==
and 98% are obtained.
In conclusion, this thesis developed an Deep Neural Network architecture
pipeline which can successfully cross view match aerial and ground images as
hypothesized in Chapter 1.

Chapter 8
Future Work
In Section 3.2, researches in state of art are analyzed in deep. Each developed
model could be tailored and applied to CV-DTU dataset for the same image
localization dataset. In this research, CVM-Net[3] is analyzed deeply and applied.
Especially, SAFA[2] and DSM[5] architectures should be investigate deeply.
The CV-DTU dataset can be improved by adding more ground images. In
addition to ground images, aerial images can be increased by time dimension. For
example, aerial image for the same location can be downloaded for a diﬀerent year
to observe new constructions, destruction or development from the open sources
explained in Section 4.2.2. In addition to yearly adds on, seasonal and daily adds
on are beneﬁcial for the model. All the images in CV-DTU are collected in day
light and spring season of a year. For each location in the database, time machine
aerial and ground images can be downloaded as it is studied by Arandjelovi´c et
al.[4] In that sense, more of the cases from real life could be covered.
More
comprehensive dataset would lead to a better result for a real life application.
As it is discussed in Section 4.2, ground images are collected in a time series
manner where a car started collecting ground images at a time and after reaching
105
==PAGE:129==
to its destination, it stopped collecting images. The collected images are bonded
with time series feature since most of the areas covered in the images are the
same between consecutive images. A new model with Recurrent Neural Networks
(RNNs) and CNNs can be developed to investigate time series collected images.
In our research, time series feature of images is erased via shuﬄing the order of
the images and dropping the consecutive ground images which has less than 30
meters of distance between each other.
In addition to CVM task, camera pose regression task can be studied further.
The camera pose regression is the task of predicting the pose of given a query
image[58].
