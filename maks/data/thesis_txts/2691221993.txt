Abstract 
 
This project will cover the different aspects related to how the overall profit of all the parties 
involved in a supply chain, which are the manufacturers and the retailers, can be maximized. 
The report will include disruptions to understand the best way to create resilient decentralized 
supply chains that will still distribute profit and risks between members optimally. 
The previously mentioned objectives are related to many questions, that must be solved to 
reach a conclusion. Those questions would be: 
• 
How do retailers decide on order quantities? 
• 
Is it possible to achieve a balance between the benefits of the supplier and the retailer? 
Can risk also be distributed along members of the supply chain? 
• 
How is it possible to achieve the right collaboration between suppliers and retailers? 
How does a contract change this? Which are the best contracts for each situation? 
• 
Is there a contract that can still coordinate the supply chain under disruptions? 
• 
How can a more resilient supply chain be designed? Based on what term? 
Exploring each and every of the issues above mentioned will help to comprehend step by step 
how to optimize supply chains by coordinating retailers or distributors with the best type of 
contract, that shares risk and profit and is able to adapt to disruptions. This last step of studying 
the behaviour of the supply chain with disruptions is to make the project more realistic since in 
the real world there are always unexpected or unplanned situations and events (e.g., if a supplier 
is not able to meet the demand or customers are not able to go to the shop due to Covid-19). 
After the extensive analysis performed in this report, it has been proved that the revenue 
sharing coordinated contract is the most optimal one and can coordinate the supply chain in the 
best manner. This coordination allows an increase in profit for the retailers, since they can 
collaborate, but also for the manufacturer, which needs to satisfy a unique order quantity, 
minimizing the cost of overstocking and understocking. The coordination is based on the 
revenue sharing parameter, which is a parameter that only exists in this contract and is related 
directly with the wholesale price. Additionally, after testing this contract with and without 
disruptions, it can be concluded that in any of the cases when all retailers collaborate together 
and negotiate as a unique entity with the manufacturer, the profit in the supply chain and each 
member is maximum. The individual profit for each retailer after a coalition is obtained with the 
Shapley value.

Chapter 1. Introduction 
 
This chapter informs the reader about the main ideas around the topics covered in the thesis 
while giving importance to the actual discussion of the project. 
 
1.1 Introduction to supply chains and disruptions 
 
Nowadays, supply chains have become a field of great importance since its proper 
understanding and management can lead to improved profits and better results. Managing the 
supply chain is a theme that covers many different aspects and areas, meaning that it requires 
an understanding of a wide range of fields and characteristics. These fields or characteristics can 
go from optimizing the number of materials received to carry on with production, to leading 
with contract management the relations between retailers and manufacturers, or dealing with 
disruptions. Disruptions in the logistic aspect but also from a market point of view. In the recent 
year, we have seen that disruptions can cause big issues and can compromise all the value chain, 
so good contracts that take into account all possible situations or unexpected events are 
mandatory and necessary. The unexpected, is unexpected until it occurs, so prevention is critical 
if it can be achieved. A simple example of this is the Covid-19 situation, in the one many suppliers 
saw themselves in difficulties to meet the specifications from their contracts since they could 
not produce or distribute their products, resulting in loses on both sides, supplier and retailer 
[1]. Sometimes, even proper forecasting is not enough, and the retailers should have several 
suppliers in different locations to never run out of supplies. 
For this reason and many others, disruption management plays a very important role in all 
supply chains since when things get more complicated, a contingency plan must be ready. In 
addition, disruptions of all levels need to be considered in the supply chain and in the 
production. Situations like the actual Covid-19 have shown that even things that are considered 
as granted, are not. Supply chains might be affected by international laws, global pandemics, 
and machine breakdowns. Moreover, all this depends also on the size of the company and its 
international presence, so the there are many variables to consider. 
 
Maintenance is also a critical aspect of every production line or value chain since it can become 
crucial in terms of cost and time savings. Avoiding downtime is the main idea of maintenance to 
maximize profitability. Selecting the proper maintenance technique can have a huge impact. 
Therefore, an extensive analysis on the different options should be always carried out to 
determine which is the best for each different set up. Having available stock is usually more 
expensive than producing every time a demand is placed, so whenever it is possible, producing 
the right amount at the right time is preferred. To achieve this, the availability of the machines 
must be ensured at any moment [2].

maintenance provides the best results and minimizes production stops, maximizing profits.

1.2 Research objectives of the project 
 
From all the different study areas that supply chain management and supply chain analytics can 
cover, this project will focus on contract management, by comparing different types of contracts 
in order to find the optimal contract that distributes risk and profit between the parties involved 
in a decentralized supply chain. 
 
The parts that can form a supply chain are suppliers/manufacturers and retailers/distributors. 
Therefore, the aim to maximize the profit from these players by proving that if retailers 
collaborate, even the manufacturer can optimize its profit. To prove this, three different 
retailers/distributors that negotiate with a manufacturer will be always considered for all the 
simulations. Based on this, there will be five following possible coalitions, that are indicated 
below: 
 
• 
First coalition: Manufacturer negotiating with retailers 1, 2 and 3 separately. 
 
• 
Second coalition: Manufacturer negotiating with retailers 1 and 2 collaborating and 
retailer 3 alone. 
 
• 
Third coalition: Manufacturer negotiating with retailers 2 and 3 collaborating and 
retailer 1 alone. 
 
• 
Fourth coalition: Manufacturer negotiating with retailers 1 and 3 collaborating and 
retailer 2 alone. 
 
• 
Fifth coalition: Manufacturer negotiating with retailers 1, 2 and 3 collaborating. 
 
It will be demonstrated that when a full collaboration is achieved, profit is maximum for all 
parties involved. Moreover, this will be studied without and with disruption (soft and strong) to 
also provide an understanding of the behaviour of the preferred contract under unexpected 
events. Additionally, this will show how a more resilient supply chain can be obtained by 
calculating optimal order quantities. Since it is difficult to have a clear idea on how the supply 
chain will behave, disruptions will be based on random number generations that will try to 
simulate real life situations with high uncertainty. 
 
Overall, and to keep it simple, the main research questions and/or objectives of the study can 
be summarized in the next bullet points: 
 
 
• 
How is it possible to achieve the right collaboration between suppliers and 
manufacturers? How does a contract change this? Which are the best contracts for each 
situation? 
 
• 
Can a manufacturer also increase its profit when retailers form coalitions? 
 
• 
Is it possible to achieve a balance between the benefits of the supplier and the retailer?  
 
• 
Can risk also be distributed along members of the supply chain?

Chapter 2. Literature review 
 
There are many studies that compare the different types of contracts to evaluate how each of 
them can affect the production process and the supply chain. Companies have changed in the 
modern world and now competition is between separate supply chains and not between 
independent firms [5]. Therefore, in order to optimize the efficiency of a supply chain it is of 
utmost importance to collaborate within members of the same supply chain to meet demand 
and supply, while optimizing profits. Achieving this is not a simple task and important simulation 
and prediction techniques must be carried out, to minimize loses. 
In the process of maximizing profit there are many aspects to consider, like the expected lost 
sales and the expected unsold. Depending on these values, the manufacturer or the retailer will 
end up having different benefits, reason why collaboration becomes critical. The terms are 
distributing profit fairly when things work fine, but also distributing loses and risks during bad 
times. Here negotiations within a supply chain enter in action, by deciding the best values for 
the wholesale or the buyback price. Buybacks are used to pay the retailer if at the end of the 
season there are remaining units [6]. Regarding the wholesale price, it consists of the price at 
which the manufacturer or supplier sells to the retailer a unit or item [7]. 
 
2.1 Contract types and their importance 
 
Contracts are necessary to have all the supply chain working towards the same objective, which 
is more frequently to maximize the overall profit, rather than the individual profit of each party. 
The only way of making this is by means of contracts where the terms to achieve the goals are 
negotiated. Without the use of the benefits from contracts, disputes between manufacturers 
and retailers can occur [1].

2.2 Importance of disruptions 
 
In supply chains, not only relations between all different parties are important. Disruptions and 
the techniques to adapt to new situations are also critical and must always be kept in mind. A

There are many studies that compare the different types of maintenance to evaluate how each 
of them can minimize breakdowns in the production process. The main maintenance strategies 
are further explained in the next bullet points: 
 
• 
Predictive maintenance: When a machine reaches a certain level of breakdown 
probability, then maintenance is carried out. The probability of a breakdown increases 
every time a machine is used and every time a product cycle ends, by focusing the 
attention on specific characteristics of the machine. The typical way of studying when a 
machine will fail is with machine learning, or under the default of data, with random 
statistics. It has been stated that almost 83% of all the manufacturing industries are 
using already in 2021 predictive maintenance to schedule all their production in an 
optimal manner [10]. Furthermore, the market related to this type of maintenance is 
constantly increasing, what shows a clear tendency towards the future predominant 
strategy, as it can be seen in the figure below.

• 
Reactive maintenance: When a machine suffers a breakdown, then maintenance is

•

Chapter 3. Simulation and analysis 
 
After an important description and an extensive literature review of all the different types of 
supply chains and contracts, possible disruptions that may occur and how to treat suppliers, it 
is time to get into the analytics part. Simulations for each type of contract will be carried out to 
obtain the optimal profit and order quantity at every contract, but also to compare the different 
contracts, to see which one provides the best and easiest coordination. Coordination is achieved 
when the profit of the supply chain remains constant, no matter the profit distribution between 
the manufacturer and the retailer or retailers. Based on the literature review, the revenue 
sharing coordinated contract seems the best candidate, but it will be confirmed in this chapter. 
Furthermore, the extraction of figures that show how suppliers and retailers distribute the profit 
provides great insights to choose the most efficient contract. All of this will be studied in detail 
now. This chapter will have three subchapters. 
The first subchapter (3.1) will involve a supply chain with one supplier and one retailer, in order 
to observe which of all the previously mentioned contracts is the most effective one at the time 
of sharing profits and risks. 
The second subchapter (3.2) will consist in evaluating the possible collaborations or coalitions 
that can exist in a supply chain where there is one supplier and three retailers. With this, it will 
be demonstrated that when retailers collaborate the supply chain increases its profit. Obviously, 
for this the best contract from the first case will be used. 
The last part (subchapters 3.3 and 3.4) of this chapter will be like the second one but involving 
disruptions (soft and strong). This will be the most realistic case. As well, different probabilities 
of supply chain and market disruption will be incorporated to contrast multiple options, that will 
distinguish between soft and strong disruptions.

Therefore, in this new segment, this contract (revenue sharing coordinated) will be analysed if 
there is more than one retailer involved (three retailers in this case). This new situation will mean 
the use of strategies to achieve collaboration between all members involved, so that the profit 
is properly shared, and risks pooled. It has been proved that if retailers act together, they will 
have more benefits than if they act separately [13]. 
This is also known as the cooperative game theory. It was first introduced by Lloyd Shapley in 
1953 and corresponds to a method to distribute richness [14]. The main reasoning behind is that 
since some players, in our case retailers, can contribute more to the coalition than others, they 
can have more negotiation power. Overall, the question that Shapley value is able to answer 
are: 
 
• 
What is the influence of each player in the global cooperation? 
• 
Which benefits are the expected to be received by each player? 
• 
What is the difference between collaboration and independent negotiations? 
• 
What is the difference between each coalition?

3.3 Taking into account more than one retailer with disruptions 
(soft disruption) 
 
In this situation, the simulations consider disruptions (soft effect). This means that supply will 
not have the same availability as before due to unexpected events. These unexpected events 
can go from a problem in a containership delivering the products to an issue in the production 
facility. Overall, as simple as a problem in the items to be sold, that will result in not meeting the 
demand in most cases.

now. Shops must be closed, and the customers can’t go physically to the store. This obviously 
affects tremendously the demand, so it is important to take into account all this to align the 
forecast as much as possible to reality and have better predictions that help to improve savings,

Chapter 4. Insights from the results 
 
Once that the presentation of all the calculations and results has been performed, it is important 
to understand what they mean and gain insights in order to use the data in an effective manner. 
Most of the times, it is not getting the data the complex part, but interpreting it to make 
recommendations.   
The key points to be extracted based on all the simulations and supported by the results are the 
following: 
• 
Contracts open the possibility for manufacturers and retailers to negotiate the terms of 
their interactions. In these negotiations both try to make the most profit while 
minimizing the risk. The proper contract can make that all parties benefit in equal 
conditions. This was proved to be the case of the revenue sharing coordinated contract 
due to its simplicity, since it only involves two main terms, the wholesale price and the 
revenue sharing percentage. Additionally, it does not need to assume anything on the 
manufacturer side, just like the buyback coordinated contract, which assumes that the 
manufacturer has a salvage value. In the case of the traditional wholesale contract, 
there is a lack of a term that coordinates the supply chain distributing the risks and the 
the profit between all parties. In the same line, the portfolio contract becomes very 
complex to coordinate when there are several retailers involved (again lack of regulatory 
parameter) and focuses only on cost minimization, not in profit maximization. 
 
• 
Coalitions provide more profit on both sides, the manufacturer and the retailers, even 
during unprecedented disruptions. The manufacturer is able to plan its production as 
well as understock and overstock much better when the retailers coordinate their order 
quantities. Therefore, manufacturers can also be extremely benefited, if not the most, 
as our results show. Therefore, it is always a win-win situation to work with 
collaborations. The table below shows an overview of all the simulations, where the

Retailers decide their order quantities by demand forecasts and depending on the

so that the negotiations are not dominated by one member, usually the manufacturer

• 
Including disruptions allows the creation of a more resilient supply chain that is more 
prepared for unexpected risk events, responding and recovering quickly so that a new 
state can be achieved if disruptions hit [23].  The new state can lead to reduced average 
optimal profits, but it will minimize profit reduction as much as possible. This is 
translated into the optimal order quantity and the expectation for the average optimal 
profit based on the demand, sales, and other important parameters. 
 
• 
The revenue sharing coordinated contract is the best one to split profit between the 
manufacturer and the retailer coalition. It has to be always a coordinated contract which 
includes the revenue sharing. 
 
Although some of the reasonings may seem obvious, a demonstration of why they are obvious 
has to be performed, as the one in this project. What initially were assumptions are now 
confirmations, showing that the outcome of the simulations and analyses has been satisfactory.  
Therefore, it is possible to see that in any case, the more collaboration the higher the profit. It 
is reasonable that when there are disruptions the profit is lower, which also can vary depending 
on the impact of those disruptions. Nevertheless, the main purpose of the project, which was 
identifying the possible benefits in the profit from coalitions, has been extensively verified. 
Additionally, a preferred contract to achieve those collaborations has been proposed, that also 
manages disruption in the supply chain and configurates collaborative planning.

Chapter 5. Conclusion 
 
 
After all the complex analyses performed in order to study the main advantages and 
disadvantages from each contract and the collaboration opportunities between retailers and the 
manufacturer it is time to provide the final conclusion and results. 
Regarding the contract type, the revenue sharing coordinated is the most attractive one, since 
it distributes the profit in an arbitrary manner for all the members, and it can coordinate supply 
chains that the buyback contract can’t. For instance, the buyback contract requires a salvage 
value in the manufacturer that the revenue sharing coordinated contract does not. Moreover, 
when we compare the revenue sharing contract with others, such as the traditional wholesale 
contract, the latter does not coordinate the supply chain as effectively as the revenue sharing 
since there is no term like the buyback price or the revenue sharing percentage to allow this. 
For that reason, that type of contract was instantaneously disregarded. Involving several 
retailers would make negotiations with the manufacturer quite complex and if a collaboration 
between the retailers takes place, sharing the profit would not be very efficient. 
Furthermore, another disadvantage from the buyback contract is that achieving coordination 
with a term such as the buyback price is more complex than simply sharing the profit between 
retailers with the revenue sharing parameter.  
In a summary, revenue sharing is the contract that allows coalitions between as many suppliers 
as required in a “simple” manner, thanks to the revenue sharing term. The biggest plus is that 
the value of this term remains constant under disruptions simplifying reactions and profit 
sharing under difficult situations, and this is the most valuable aspect that the contract provides. 
In terms of the influence of collaboration or not between distributors, it has been widely proved 
that when retailers work together, more profit is obtained since predictions are aligned and the 
order quantity that the manufacturer needs to meet is more optimal, giving benefits that come 
from savings in both sides, the supplier and the retailers. 
Overall, new supply chain models with new contracts that allow profit and risk sharing are 
imperative in the future development of coalitions. In other words, maximizing profits balancing 
the disruption risk.  
Additionally to this, it has been confirmed that the revenue sharing coordinated contract 
behaves well under soft and strong disruptions, since it still coordinates the supply chain 
optimally. If this is taken further, an optimal order quantity can be obtained which adds 
resilience to the supply chain, allowing the obtention of higher profits. 
For future research it would be very insightful to study the same simulation involving more than 
one manufacturer and more than three unique retailers. Also, another complication could be 
assuming different disruption effect and possibilities for each retailer or making the disruption 
probabilities and effects variable as well.
