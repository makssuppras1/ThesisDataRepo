Abstract
Atherosclerosis is a progressive, complex and inﬂammatory disease with lethal consequences for the
cardiovascular function and is quickly becoming the leading cause of death and disability of the western
world. It is estimated that the amount of deaths associated with the disease will increase to 23.3 million
by 2030, underlining the importance of conducting research for this disease.
The best way of conducting research in an ethical, inexpensive and time efﬁcient way is to use in vitro
models of the disease, to get a better understanding of the pathogenesis and progression. This thesis
had the main objective of demonstrating rolling capture of monocytes on an endothelial cell layer, to be
used as a model for the initial stage of atherosclerosis. This was completed by optimising and char-
acterising a 3D printed shear stress device previously designed by another master student at DTU, in
order to obtain the right conditions for a model system of atherosclerosis. The goal was divided into
three aims including the optimisation of this device, the characterisation of the effect on the endothelial
layer provided by the shear stress, and also TNF-α stimulation and ﬁnally to demonstrate the effect of
the system and TNF-α on the rolling capture of monocytes on the activated endothelial layer.
Despite some drawbacks concerning the shear stress modelling of the system and uncertainties in the
quantiﬁcation methods, the results demonstrated that the shear stress device was successfully able to
simulate the conditions of an artery and provide the right type of activation and morphology change of
the endothelial cell layer. Furthermore it was shown that the shear stress device can successfully be
used to model the rolling capture process of the initial stage of atherosclerosis.
In conclusion, the shear stress device was a beneﬁcial model for atherosclerosis and has great potential
for future uses in the creation of dynamic conditions in a cell environment.

Introduction
Atherosclerosis has one of the highest mortality and morbidity rates world wide and is a leading cause
of death and disability in the western world [1, 2]. Atherosclerosis is a progressive complex inﬂamma-
tory disease, that is characterised by the build up of fatty deposits or plaques situated in the wall of the
arteries [3]. This will restrict the blood ﬂow, and increase the risk of developing lethal cardiovascular
diseases (CVD), such as a heart attack or a stroke. The main consequence of atherosclerosis happens
when the plague ruptures, and spill its content into the blood stream which will create a blood clot,
known to have lethal effects (Figure 1) [1, 2, 3, 4]. Statistic show that CVD related deaths is expected to
increase to 23.3 million by 2030 due to the high fat western life style, emphasising the need and reason
for conducting research in atherosclerosis [1].
Figure 1:
Brief illustration of the effect of atherosclerosis. Normal arteries (A) refer to arteries with free blood ﬂow.
When atherosclerosis is initiated, a build up of plague (B) will slowly start to form which will eventually burst, leaking its content
into the blood stream and creating a blockage of the artery, also known as a blood clot (C).
The focus of this thesis is to optimise and characterise an in vitro model system used for the initial stage
of atherosclerosis which can be a simpler and cheaper alternative to currently used models. This is
done in order to complete the objective of demonstrating rolling capture of monocytes on an activated
endothelial cell monolayer, a necessary step for the initial progression of atherosclerosis.

The following section will provide a basic understanding of atherosclerosis and important cell types,
proteins and factors involved in the disease. Furthermore it will brieﬂy exemplify the current models

1.1
Endothelium and arteries
The endothelial cell layer, also known as the endothelium, that lines the inner wall of the arteries together
with the basic anatomy of the artery wall are key to understanding the pathogenesis of atherosclerosis.
The general structure of an arterial wall is build up of three layers; tunica externa, tunica media and
tunica intima (Figure 2). The tunica externa is the outer most layer of the vessel wall and mainly consist
of ﬁbroblast cells, perivascular adipose tissue cells and perivascular nerves which are embedded in a
connective tissue that is rich in collagen [5, 6]. It is known to be involved in numerous processes such
as, cell trafﬁcking in and out of the artery wall, repairing and growth of the vessel wall and communica-
tion between endothelial cells (EC) and smooth muscle cells (SMC) with their local tissue environment
[5]. Furthermore it will provide structural support and shape to the vessel [5, 6]. The tunica media,
is the middle layer of the vessel wall and usually the thickest of the three layers. It consist mainly of
multiple layers of SMC which provide muscular support to the vessel and elastic tissues that enable the
arteries ability to stretch [7]. The innermost layer of the artery, tunica intima is in direct contact with the
lumen of the artery and is generally the thinnest of the three layers. It is mainly composed of the EC,
with a support matrix consisting of collagen and elastic ﬁbers, which together form what is known as the
endothelium [6, 7].
Figure 2: The basic structure of an artery wall. An artery wall consist of three layer, the tunica externa, the tunica media
and the tunica intima. Each layer consist of different cells which will have different functional properties for the well-being of
the artery. The illustration is adapted from Zhao et al (2015) [6]
The endothelium, lines the entire circulatory system and can be characterised as a selective permeable

barrier between the extravascular and intravascular compartments of the arteries, in other words, the
endothelium acts as a barrier between the blood components in the lumen and the surrounding tissues
[6, 8]. The endothelium function is crucial for maintaining vascular health, as studies show that the
dysfunction will lead to the initiation, progression and complications of various vascular diseases [8].
The endothelium regulates transport of macromolecules similarly to most other barriers in the bodies
and can use several mechanisms to do so. Macromolecules can either cross the barrier; through the
EC, via cell-to-cell junctions, between endothelial gaps or by vesicular transport [8]. The reorganisation
of intercellular junctions involving actin and myosin can be seen as one of the primary mechanisms to in-
crease the endothelium permeability to water and various molecules [8]. The actin ﬁlaments are present
both centrally and peripherally on EC. In the center the polymerisation of the actin ﬁlaments are known
as stress ﬁbers, whereas on the peripheral the actin ﬁlaments will thicken and form what is known as a
dense peripheral band [9]. The stress ﬁbers are known as long bundles of actin ﬁlaments which will also
contain myosin, tropomyosin and alpha-actinin and have the main role of maintaining strong adhesion
to the underlying layer. On the other hand the dense peripheral bands are thicker and play a role in
cell to cell adhesion, indicating a role in the permeability of the endothelial layer [9]. Changes to the
surrounding environment of the arteries, for examples changes in the ﬂow direction and shear stress,
will cause the actin cytoskeleton to elongate and reorganise affecting the permeability and protective
properties of the endothelium [8, 9].
Apart from shear stress, other methods of inducing these intercellular changes is to add stimulants such
as trombin, histamine or other acute inﬂammatory mediators, which will act on the endothelium and ac-
tivate it. These agents will act on their protein kinase C, and induce phosphorylation which will cause
actin reorganisation, cell rounding and increased paracellular transport. Furthermore other factors such
as; Vascular endothelial growth factors (VEGFs) affecting the nitric oxide synthase (NOS) regulation,
bacterial endotoxin lipopolysaccharides (LPS) stimulating small GTPase, will also inﬂuence the perme-
ability of the endothelium [8]. Generally the endothelial cells are relatively homogenous, however there
is evidence of regional and species variation giving rise to differences in permeability, responsiveness
and biosynthesis [8]. This could be argued as to give rise to certain predilection sites where the forma-
tion of plaques happen more frequently than others. These sites are usually in the reproducible regions
of the arterial tree, where there is low oscillatory endothelial shear stress but with high turbulent ﬂow
commonly seen near branch points [2]. Furthermore research have shown that the abdominal aorta and
coronary arteries are amongst the most affected arteries for the initiation and progression of atheroscle-
rosis [2, 10].
In summary, the endothelium is the inner most layer of an artery that regulates the passage of molecules
from the lumen into the arterial wall, and is essential for the well being of an artery. The dysfunction of
the endothelium will cause various vascular diseases including atherosclerosis. Inﬂammatory proteins,
shear stress and proteins affecting the nitric oxide production, are amongst the most common aspects
that can inﬂuence the function of endothelium and are also essential in atherosclerosis as discussed in
the following sections.

1.2
Risk factors of atherosclerosis and common treatment options
The exact cause or risk factors of atherosclerosis are unknown, however, it seems to be a multi-factorial
disease.
One of the most discussed risk factors of atherosclerosis is the consumption of a diet that contains high
amounts of cholesterol, most commonly leading to a high concentration of Apolipoprotein B-containing
lipoproteins in the blood. A well known example of such a protein is low-density lipoprotein (LDL), and
the accumulation of these in the wall of arteries is a thoroughly discussed cause of atherosclerosis also
referred to as hyperlipidemia [1, 2, 8]. The cholesterol and LDL will enter the dysfunctional regions
on the endothelium via interactions with proteoglycans in the endothelial wall and accumulate in the
intima of the artery. This together with multiple in vivo correlation studies indicates that LDL is directly
associated with atherosclerosis [1, 8, 11]. However, not all lipoproteins types are considered to have
a negative impact on the progression of the disease as studies have shown that high concentration of
high-density lipoprotein (HDL) will decrease the permeability, of the endothelial cell layer and improve
its barrier integrity, decreasing the risk of developing atherosclerosis [8].
Another risk factor could be lifelong diseases such as diabetes mellitus, which will typically result in
hyperglycemia, and is often discussed to have a direct link to the formation of an atherosclerotic plaque
[12]. The hyperglycemia will provoke the dysfunction of the endothelial cell layer, which means that there
is a decrease in the availability of nitric oxide (NO), which is a critical regulator of the vascular tone of an
artery and a key component in the health of the endothelium [12]. A decrease in NO will amongst others
result in higher degree of integrin activation in platelets which in turn will lead to increased leukocyte
adhesion and ultimately the formation of atherosclerotic plaques, indicating that NO has an important
role in maintenance of a healthy endothelium [12].
Moreover, the lifestyle choices made by a person could also have an effect on the risk of the formation
of plaques, as an inactive lifestyle or tobacco smoking could be regarded as a cause of atherosclerosis,
mainly as the consequences of such actions will result in high accumulation of lipids and fats as well
as an unnatural increase in the blood pressure [3]. Furthermore atherosclerosis could potentially have
a genetic cause as studies have shown that the chance of obtaining the disease is higher if a father or
brother has previously been affected by it [3]. This they claim is due to a genetic disposition of higher
expression levels of proteins relevant for the initiation of atherosclerosis [8, 13]. However the concept is
still debatable, and more research would have to be conducted [3].
The main treatment methods commonly used for atherosclerosis, focus on reducing the amount of LDL
and cholesterol and other harmful lipoproteins in the blood by the means of medication. One example
is the use of statins, which will induce the expression of LDL receptors on hepatocytes in the liver and
thereby increase the uptake and limit the concentration of LDL in the blood and plasma [14]. Another
commonly used drug include ezetimibe which will inhibit cholesterol uptake, which is often taken as a
combination with statins. Other approaches for treatment of CVD is to try and increase the amount of
HDL in the blood, however clinical trials are still in process and as of yet this have not proven to have a
Page 4 of 74
==PAGE:14==
Master thesis: Anna K. B. Mortensen
January 1st, 2020
positive outcome [14].
In summary, it seems that high cholesterol and LDL levels in the blood has a direct correlation with the
development of atherosclerosis and the main method of treatment is focusing on decreasing the amount
of LDL in the blood stream, indicating a crucial role in the progression of atherosclerosis.
1.2.1
Pathogenesis of atherosclerosis
The pathogenesis of atherosclerosis is complicated and involves many proteins, reaction cascades and
stages. To provide a brief overview, the process the disease can be divided into different lesions types
seen in the artery wall. According to the American Heart Association (AHA), the main types of lesions
that will happen throughout atherosclerosis are (1) Adaptive intimal thickening, (2) Xanthoma formation,
(3) Pathological intimal thickening and (4) Fibroatheroma formation, leading to the formation of a plaque
(Figure 3) [10].

Prior to atherosclerosis, the predilection sites are characterised by what is known as adaptive intimal
thickening (AIT) (AHA Type I lesion), which is a natural process that can occur spontaneously from
birth, but is not necessarily dangerous. The lesions characterised as AIT consist mostly of SMC and
proteoglycan matrix with little or no inﬂammatory cells [15]. At these sites, the rate of progression of the
plaque remains higher than other sites of the arteries and can be characterised as a starting point for
the development of atherosclerotic plaque [2]. A possible explanation for the atherosclerotic proneness

==PAGE:15==

January 1st, 2020
of these AIT sites, could be the strong interaction that happens between LDL and proteoglycan found in
the cell membrane, which is deemed to be important for disease initiation [2].
As the disease progresses the lesion sites becomes Xanthomas or fatty streaks (AHA type II lesions)
which are characterised based on their high content of "foamy macrophages" or foam cells, referring to
macrophages with internal lipid droplets containing an excess concentration of cholesterol providing a
foamy appearance [15]. When LDL accumulate in the intima, it may be modiﬁed by oxidation and ag-
gregation, and act as chronic stimulators of the adaptive and innate immune response [2]. This means
that the modiﬁed LDL and their oxidised derivatives will be able to induce the expression of adhesion
molecules on the ECs and SMCs. Furthermore it will activate chemoattractants and growth factors which
will enable the interaction with monocytes and stimulate their homing, migration and differentiation into
macrophages and dendritic cells (see section 1.3.2), a key process in the initiation of atherosclerosis.
The macrophages and dendritic cells will be differentiated into foam cells which is assumed to be due
to, amongst other things, uptake of oxidized LDL (oxLDL) or hydrolysis of free cholesterol from LDL [2].
It is these foam cells that are the tell-tales of a lipoprotein driven inﬂammation, and they can be seen by
the naked eye as yellow coloured fatty streaks [2].
The xanthomas will in atherosclerotic prone regions progress into pathogenic intimal thickenings (PIT)
(AHA type III lesions). This type of lesion is typically lined with layers of SMCs in a proteoglycan-collagen
matrix, that has an underlying accumulation in the intima of large lipid-rich material, also commonly re-
ferred to as lipid pools [15]. Most of these lesions are harmless as they do not disrupt the overall
structure of the intima, however in some cases macrophages will inﬁltrate the lesions and thereby they
develop into conﬂuent necrotic cores, irreversibly disrupting the intima structure [2]. When a necrotic
core is formed, the lesion progress to a ﬁbroatheroma (AHA type IV lesions). The necrotic core will
consist of a lipid rich ﬂuid, which is embedded with cell debris and devoid of any type of matrix [2, 15].
The apoptosis of macrophages will lead to a lack of phagocytotic clearance and thereby the accumu-
lation of cell debris, which will further expand the necrotic core formation [2, 15]. An important feature
of the necrotic core is the surrounding thick ﬁbrous cap, which consist mainly of type I and III collagen,
proteoglycans and SMCs and plays an essential role for the maintenance of the integrity of the plaque
[10].
This can be said as the ﬁbrous cap thickness is an indicator of the plaques vulnerability towards rupture,
where the cut off point has been deﬁned to be ≤65 µm [10]. It is not known as of yet what exactly con-
tributes to the rupture of the plaque however the matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) that macrophages
produce, in combination with shear stress, microcalciﬁcations, iron accumulation and macrophage death
all contribute to the disruption [10]. Once the ﬁbrous cap ruptures, the content of the necrotic core will
spill out into the circulating blood which will initiate and activate the coagulation cascade. The cascade
will respond to the exposure of lipids and tissue factors, and thereby produce a blood clot, also referred
to as thrombosis [10].
This is a brief summary of the key steps in the pathogenesis of atherosclerosis as seen from lesion
perspective, starting from the intimal thickening of the arterial wall to the potential rupture of the plaque,

1.3
Cytokines and monocytes role in initiate phases of athersoclerosis
From the previous section is was mentioned that monocytes play a crucial role in the initial stage of the
atherosclerotic plaque formation, and many studies argue that monocytes homing, capture and migra-
tion are essential for atherosclerosis to be initiated [13]. This is supported by the fact that monocyte
homing, rolling and capture will set off a cascade of reactions, which will ultimately lead to the formation
of a plaque.
1.3.1
Different types of monocytes
Monocytes are a type of leukocyte that are derived from haematopeotic stem cells in the bone marrow
[16]. They have a high plasticity and is a heterogeneous population of cells which means that there
are many subsets presumably with different functions. Furthermore monocytes have the ability to alter
their phenotype depending on which microenvironment they are surrounded by [17]. They are usually
distinguished based on their location, phenotype and function as well as their chemokine receptor and
surface molecules [16]. This can be exempliﬁed as in homeostasis, monocytes will circulate the blood,
are usually short lived, does not undergo proliferation and their exact function is unknown. However
when entering an inﬂammatory environment the monocytes will change phenotype and migrate from
the blood into the tissues in response to chemical signal. In the inﬂamed tissue they are able to phago-
cytose cells and toxic compounds, produce inﬂammatory cytokines (which will change depending on the
type of inﬂammation), and differentiate into different inﬂammatory immune cells such as dendritic cells
(DCs), macrophages or foam cells [17].
Using mice models, researchers have identiﬁed two main monocyte subsets that seem to be involved
in the atherosclerotic process [16, 17]. These are referred to as Gr1+/Ly6ChighCCR2+CX3CR1low
(Gr1+/Ly6Chigh) which are also described as the "inﬂammatory monocytes" as they differentiate into in-
ﬂammatory DCs and macrophages in infection models. The other type is Gr1−/Ly6ClowCCR2−Cx3CR1high
(Gr1−/Ly6Clow) also described as the "patrolling monocytes" as they tend to crawl or patrol the luminal
side of the endothelium in the small vessels [16, 17]. The most important subset for atherosclero-
sis is believed to be the Gr1+/Ly6Chigh, as these have been shown to adhere and inﬁltrate the acti-
vated endothelium and eventually differentiate into atherosclerotic macrophages [17]. The exact role of
Gr1−/Ly6Clow monocytes is unknown, but they are believed to constitutively crawl on the endothelium,
perhaps scavenging microparticles and debris from the luminal surface [16]. In the normal conditions it
is less than 1% of these monocytes that will cross the endothelium, however in the presence of tissue
damage they are believed to quickly extrapolate and produce inﬂammatory cytokines indicating a very
early inﬂammatory response [16].

The largest population of monocytes in the human body is believed to be a population with high con-

low afﬁnty IgG receptor CD16 [16]. These monocytes are known as the classical monocytes, expresses

Another subset of monocytes, referred to as the non-classical subtype lacks the CCR2 but will express
high levels of CX3CR1. It has a lower level of CD14 but higher levels of CD16 on its surface, creating a
method to distinguish between the two subsets. These are also referred to as CD14++CD16−for the

monocytes to mouse monocytes there is evidence that the Gr1+/Ly6Chigh monocytes correspond to the
classical phenotype and that the Gr1−/Ly6Clow monocyte correspond to the non-classical phenotype.
This evidence is based on the function of the monocytes in humans and mice, however it is relevant to
mention that there are signiﬁcant difference between the species and the subject is still under debate
[16].
1.3.2
Cytokines and receptors important for onset of atherosclerosis
As indicated in previous sections, the innate and adaptive immune system, plays an important role in
the progression of atherosclerosis, mainly via the signals provided by cytokines and chemokines [1].
Cytokines are a broad range of small proteins that are in involved in cellular signalling pathways and
generally play an important role in determining which type of immune response is being produced.
There are several categories of cytokines, of which the Interferon (IFN), Interleukin (IL), chemokines,
and tumour necrosis factors (TNF), are the most dominant in atherosclerosis [1]. Especially impor-
tant are the chemokines which are proteins that will attract cell types to a certain location, stimulate
pro-inﬂammatory reactions and exert its effect by binding to cell surface receptors [13, 1]. There are
generally four types of chemokines, which are differentiated based on the position of cystein, these are;
C-C, C-X-C, C-X-3-C, X-C, where C-C and C-X3-C seem to be the most important in the trafﬁcking
of monocytes across an inﬂamed artery as well as recruitment. More speciﬁcally, the most common
chemokines involved in the recruitment, rolling and migrating are CCL2, CCL5 and CX3CL1 [16, 13].
TNF-α is one of the most important cytokines in relation to atherosclerosis as its deﬁciency has been
proven in several studies to have a positive impact on the lesion size and disease progression [13, 1, 18].
TNF-α has the ability to fully initiate the activation of the endothelium, and in the process induce the re-
organisation of actin and tubulin cytoskeleton on the EC, making the endothelium more permeable and
allowing molecules such as LDL to easily pass through [1]. Furthermore TNF-α will stimulate several
cell adhesion receptors such as Vascular cell adhesion molecule 1 (VCAM-1), intercellular adhesion
molecule 1 (ICAM-1) and P/E-selectins, that play an important role in the capture and migration of
monocytes. The activation will happen through an intracellular endothelial cell signalling pathway and
will stimulate the production of several pro-inﬂammatory chemokines, cytokines and other cell surface
molecules.

1.4
Theory of shear stress
As mentioned throughout shear stress and ﬂow conditions are important for the maintenance and
strength of a healthy artery. Therefore it is a natural assumption that changes will have an effect on
the progression and initiation of atherosclerosis.
The permeability of the endothelium is inﬂuenced by the shear stress exerted on it [20, 21]. Shear stress
is deﬁned as the tangential frictional force or stress that is being exerted by the blood ﬂow directly on
the endothelial cells lining the arterial wall [8, 20]. It is usually expressed in units of force per units of
area (for example dyn/cm2, Pa or N/m2).
The blood ﬂow in an artery is either laminar or turbulent, where laminar ﬂow can be further categorised
Page 11 of 74
==PAGE:21==
Master thesis: Anna K. B. Mortensen
January 1st, 2020
into undisturbed and disturbed (Figure 5). Undisturbed laminar ﬂow refer to areas of smooth stream-
lined ﬂow (Figure 5A) and disturbed ﬂow, refer to areas where ﬂow reversal is happening, either seen as
reattachment, ﬂow seperation or recirculation (Figure 5B) [20, 22]. However in some areas, especially
around branches in the arteries or curvatures, the ﬂow is characterised as turbulent, which means that
the velocity at a point will be varying continuously over time despite the fact that the overall ﬂow is steady
[22]. The difference in ﬂow (laminar or turbulent) is characterised by the Reynolds number (Re), where
low Re values indicate a laminar ﬂow and high Re values, typically above 2000, indicate a turbulent ﬂow
[22]. The heterogeneity of the blood ﬂow throughout the body and the complex geometry of the circula-
tion determines which type of shear stress pattern is present at a certain environment, which is usually
characterised by both direction and magnitude [20]. In long straight segments, the shear stress pattern
is pulsating and follow the same direction, with values of shear stress of around 15 to 70 dyn/cm2, how-
ever in disturbed laminar ﬂow or turbulent ﬂow, the pattern of shear stress will be low and/or follow a
oscillating pattern [22]. Low shear stress generally means that it will be unidirectional, but have ﬂuctu-
ating magnitude that result in a force of <10 to 12 dyne/cm2. On the contrary, oscillating pattern will be
bidirectional and result in a very low force of 0 to 4 dyne/cm2. Both low and oscillating shear stress are
closely related to atherosclerosis, and seen as important initiators of the disease [22, 20].

In areas not prone to atherosclerosis and in the majority of the arterial network the blood ﬂow is laminar,
with high ﬂow rate and under high shear stress which is known to be atheroprotective [23]. This can be
Page 12 of 74
==PAGE:22==
Master thesis: Anna K. B. Mortensen
January 1st, 2020
said as studies have shown that high shear stress will downregulate pro-atherogenic and inﬂammatory
genes and signal pathways in the endothelial cells [20, 22, 23]. This downregulation will, inhibit the
expression of adhesion molecules and inhibit apoptosis, proliferation and migration. More speciﬁcally
high shear stress and laminar ﬂow have proven to be able to inhibit highly pro-inﬂammatory TNF-α sig-
nal pathways, which means that less ICAM-1 and VCAM-1 proteins are exposed limiting the amount of
monocytes able to bind and enter and thereby limiting the initiation of atherosclerosis [23]. Furthermore,
pulsative laminar ﬂow is the main stimulant for the continuous production of the aforementioned NO by
the endothelium. Apart from being essential in keeping the vascular tone of the arteries, NO is also an
important anti-apoptotic, anti-mitogenic and anti-thrombogenic agent [12, 23].
In contrast when the ﬂow is disturbed and the shear stress is low or oscillating, the NO production will be
attenuated, and it will upregulate factors that will induce vasorestriction and mitogenic molecules creat-
ing a pro-atherogenic environment [22]. In addition, the permeability of the arterial wall and dysfunction
of ECs have also shown to be higher in areas with low shear stress, which means that for example
the accumulation of LDL particles are larger in such places [24, 25, 23, 22]. Furthermore, low shear
stress will activate transcriptional pathways that will upregulate LDL receptors, cholesterol synthases
and fatty acid synthases, which will increase the accumulation of LDL in the arterial wall [22]. One of
the reasons for the greater permeability could be the effect of shear stress on the endothelial cell mor-
phology. When endothelial cells are exposed to steady rapid ﬂow, they are elongated and aligned with
their long axis in the direction of the ﬂow. However when this changes they obtain a more round shape
in a non-uniform orientation giving rise to the widening of the intercellular junctions and hence greater
permeability [8, 22]. Additionally, low shear stress will induce the production of various pro-atherogenic
molecules, such as reactive oxygen species and oxLDL and activate inﬂammatory pathways such as
NF-kβ, which will activate the expression of various pro-atherogenic receptors and cytokines, where
some of the essential are VCAM1, ICAM1, selectins and TNF-α to mention a few [22].
Low shear stress not only affects the initiation of atherosclerosis but has also been proven to affect the
growth and stability of the plaque [20, 22]. Low shear stress will promote migration and proliferation of
SMC from the media to the intima, where they start to produce the ﬁbrous cap surrounding the plaque.
Additionally, they will also activate the transcriptional pathways that produce MMPs and attenuate the
production of extra cellular matrix, leading to the degradation of the plaque. In combination with the
ability to downregulate NO, this will mean that the risk of developing a thrombosis is higher, increasing
the risk of the disease being lethal [22].
In summary, research indicates that blood velocity, ﬂow and shear pattern has a very big inﬂuence on
the ability of the initiation and progression of atherosclerosis, underlining the importance and the reason
of including this aspect in the model system of this thesis.

1.5.1
In vivo model, intravital imaging
In the pharmaceutical industry it is well known that in vivo models are inevitable when studying disease
and developing drugs. Therefore many researchers focus on optimising or discovering new methods to
study a disease using live animals, such as mice.
McArdle et al (2015), has developed a method for intravital live cell imaging, to monitor disease pro-
gression in atherosclerotic arteries [26]. Intravital optical imaging is deemed to be good for studying
biological and disease processes in vivo, however there are many technical challenges, especially when
investigating cardiovascular diseases, mainly due to motion artefacts [26]. The main goal for the authors
of the study was to ﬁlm and show the recruitment of immune cells from the blood and monitor the mi-
gration across the endothelial barrier.
In intravital imaging techniques, one will usually need to apply mechanical stabilisation, triggering and
postprocessing of the image in order to get an usable result. However mechanical stabilisation in the
arteries is not possible, due to tissue damage and restricted movement. Therefore they developed a
system named ILTIS (intravital live cell triggered imaging system) of which they claim high quality im-
ages with subcellular resolution abilities and no need for mechanical stabilisation [26]. The experimental
set-up consisted of a live mouse, on a heated microscope plate, that has been given anesthesia but is
breathing freely with extra oxygen provided, a syringe pump with RPMI media, a scanhead of a two-
photon microscope that is connected to a arduino microcontroller that is connected to a pulse measure
in the mouse thigh and a piezo-actuator connected to MATLAB to control the position of the objective
(Figure 6). From this setup, the heart rate is used to trigger the microscope camera to take an image
when the heart rate is in diastole. The piezo-actuator allows 3D imaging, by MATLAB code instruct-
ing it to change the focal plane, this made it possible to produce a movie. In summary they managed
to demonstrate the patrolling of monocytes by ﬂuorescent labelling of a Cx3CL1 receptor as well as
demonstrate the migration of macrophages within an atherosclerotic plaque [26].

In vitro models, artiﬁcial artery
Recently, with the advanced technology and knowledge of biomaterials, there have been many studies
proposing the use of human based 3D models of an artery used for transplant purposes, to investigate
diseases in vitro. This is due to the ability of such models to closely resemble the human physiological
conditions.
As an example of such a study, demonstrating monocytes rolling and capture, Robert et al (2013) [28],
engineered a 3D artery model, using cells from humans [28]. They argue that other in vitro models
are not sufﬁcient in creating a multicellular complex system with the right dimension and pulsative ﬂow
conditions of the native arteries. The main aim of their study was therefore to construct a 3D artery
model made of human vascular cells isolated from the umbilical cord, and show the accumulation of
LDL and HDL as well as the binding and transmigration of monocytes [28]. The 3D model consisted of a
biodegradable tubular scaffold matrix with a length of 4 cm and an inner diameter of 0.6 cm. It was made
of non-woven polyglycolic acid meshes and coated with poly-4-hydroxybutyrate. They used wielding
and heating techniques to obtain the right shape, and after sterilisation seeded myoﬁbroblast cells using
ﬁbrin. The cells were ﬁrst grown under static conditions after which they were exposed to pulsative ﬂow
for 14 days. Once a conﬂuent layer of myoﬁbroblast was obtained they applied the same procedure for
their isolated HUVEC cells. For maintenance of the 3D model, a bioreactor was used, with tubing and a
roller pump. The functionality of the artery was conﬁrmed with NO production and monocyte adhesion
after TNF-α stimulation and the integrity of the membrane was evaluated using a stain. Finally their
complete aim was conﬁrmed by labelling LDL and HDL and examined the accumulation by confocal
microscopy.

1.5.3
In vitro models, microﬂuidics
As indicated ﬂow and shear are important factors that determine the fate of an atherosclerotic plaque.
Therefore there have been a lot of focus on trying to implement the concept of microﬂuidics in atheroscle-
rosis, and use this as a basis to create an in vitro model [28].
Microﬂuidic devices seem to be the future of quick diagnostics and in vitro ﬂow based assays, in part
due to the ability of scaling down, lowering the long term costs, and the ﬂexibility of the design to include
single cell based assay as well as co-cultures on the same chip [29, 30, 31].
The devices are known to be able to more accurately mimick the physiological conditions due to the
ability of having perfusion and ﬂow while being easy to automate, easing the work for the researcher.
However there are several challenges associated with microﬂuidics, where perhaps one of the most
challenging and time consuming relates to the manufacturing method [29, 30, 31]. Most of the microﬂu-
idic devices choose to use PDMS slides as they are inexpensive and easy to manufacture for small
experiments however very difﬁcult to mass produce. Furthermore, growing cells in a PDMS environ-
ment has also proven to be problematic, due to the lack of knowledge of the effect of PDMS which is
known to be able to cause toxic by-products [29, 30, 31]. For many years protocols have been standard-
ized for growing cells on glass or polystyrene materials, and the transition to PDMS can create many
challenges and problems, depending on the type of cells. This has to be taken into account, and will
remove the ease of transitioning. Microﬂuidic devices can use glass or polystyrene slides to grow the
cells on however the manufacturing method requires specialised knowledge and expertise as well as
special equipment which can be very costly and can scare off potential users [29, 30, 31]. Furthermore
as of yet, most microﬂuidic devices are not fully portable and come with complicated external controls
to feed and maintain the cell system, again creating a need for personnel [29, 30, 31]. The complicated
process, lack of standardisation and need for expert engineers to manufacture and maintain the system
have slowed down the adaptation of microﬂuidics systems, and more optimisation is needed [29, 30, 31].
An example of a study where a microﬂuidic device was created to study the effect of atherosclerosis,
in the form of a thrombosis is completed by Costa et al (2017) [32]. They explain a method to produce
a microﬂuidic chips that will include an exact 3D model replicate of an healthy coronary artery as well
as stenotic coronary artery. The method relies on a stereolitography-based fabrication, where the de-
sign was created by using computer tomography angiography imaging data of a coronary artery. The
printed models was used as molds in PDMS-based soft lithography and used to create a channel with
the correct geometries in a microﬂuidic device (Figure 7) [32]. The device will thereby contain the correct
architecture of an coronary artery and will also have inlets and outlets that will provide the ability of per-
fusion of the channel. Furthermore they prove that the model can be used for seeding of HUVECs and
further investigate platelet aggregation by perfusing the two moulds with human blood in arterial shear
stress. The ﬁnal result proved that in the model of the healthy artery, no thrombosis would occur where
as in the stenotic model, after perfusion of human blood, thrombosis would occur [32]. They claim due
to the ability of seeding HUVECs in the channel that their system is ideal for modelling other aspects of
atherosclerosis [32].

In vitro model, Co-cultures systems
Due to the complex nature of atherosclerosis, there are many cell types involved in the initiation and
progression of the disease. Therefore many in vitro models argued that it is essential to incorporate
co-cultures of different cell types to try and mimick the physiological conditions [33, 28, 34].
As an example, Noonan et al (2019), have developed a triple cell 2D model to demonstrate the immune
systems role in the phenotypic expression of EC and SMC in atherosclerosis [33]. They developed a
triple compartment cell culture model that consists of EC, SMC and THP-1-macrophage cells, which
can all be isolated and studied independently. They used a transwell system, where they seeded SMC
on the underside of a geltrex coated well insert and EC on the top of the same insert allowing the ﬁlter
to act as a method of communication between the two cell types. The immune cells (THP-1 monocytes)
were grown separately and when all three cells were ready, the well inserts were moved into the THP-1
chamber, leading to a culture system as displayed in Figure 8. This system then allows the isolation of all
three cell types, by mechanically disrupting the EC on the top using a syringe plunger and introducing an
ice treatment followed by mechanically scraping of the SMC. They validated their system by comparing
the phenotypic expression of SMC and EC in double cultures of SMC/EC, EC/THP-1 or SMC/THP-1,
to the expression of them in their triple cell culture and found that the triple cell signiﬁcantly alters the
expression of EC and SMC. All in all, they conclude that the system still lack certain aspects such as
cell-to-cell communication and the ability to migrate through the two cell layer, however they claim to
provide a framework that can be adapted [33].
All of the above mentioned models can somewhat accurately simulate what will happen in atheroscle-
rosis. However there are certain issues with time, costs, ethics and complexity which the model used in
this thesis aim to improve.

1.6
Aim and hypothesis
The lethal consequences of atherosclerosis has made it one of the worlds most deadly diseases, es-
pecially in the western world, and much time and research to try and optimise the current treatment
options and perhaps ﬁnd a method of prevention is used. For this purpose, in vitro models are a great
way to gain insight into the mechanisms and processes involved in the disease. The current models are
either not physiologically similar in that they do not include the essential ﬂow and shear stress conditions
or like microﬂuidics and 3D printing of artiﬁcial models, can be very time consuming, complicated and
expensive.
Based on the background information it is hypothesised that it is possible optimise a 3D printed a shear
stress system, so that it will have similar aspects as many of the models described in the previous
section, but include a simple set up and manufacturing method. Additionally it is hypothesised that
this system can be used to model an atherosclerotic endothelial cell monolayer, and when shear stress
and TNF-α is applied, it will alter the morphology and organisation of the cells. Lastly depending on
the amount of shear stress applied and if stimulated by TNF-α or not, it is assumed that there will be
a change in the induction of the VCAM-1 expression which will affect the amount of monocytes being
caught by rolling capture.
In order to investigate these hypothesis and complete the objective of the study, three aims can be set
up:
1. Redesign and optimise a shear stress device to be used on a 24-well plate.
2. Characterise the effect of shear stress device on a monolayer of endothelial cells as well as the
VCAM-1 expression.
3. Demonstrate and quantify the rolling capture of monocytes under the inﬂuence of stimuli such as
shear stress and TNF-α.
The accomplishment of these aims will hopefully create a simple, reliable method to model atheroscle-
rosis and perhaps be used to help the research of ﬁnding new methods of treatment. The following
sections will describe some relevant background that will help to complete these aims.

1.7
3D printing methods and biomaterials
To accomplish the aim of redesigning and optimising a shear stress device, a 3D printed device was
given by a previous master student Christian Dagnæs, which will be characterised to show if it has the
ability to simulate the environment of an artery. For this it is relevant to understand the principles of two
different 3D printing techniques, fused deposition modelling (FDM) and stereolithography (SLA), and
also the basic properties of two biomaterials used, polylactic acid (PLA) and Resins.
1.7.1
Fused deposition modelling using Polylactic acid
FDM is a nozzlebased 3D printing system, that will print (as most other 3D printers) the designed object
layer-by-layer. A torque and a pinch system will push an amount of thermoplastic ﬁlament into a heating
unit, after which it is melted. The molten ﬁlament will then be extruded from the heated nozzle, and
solidiﬁes once it hits the heated buildplate or previous layer of ﬁlament. The temperature of the heating
unit is set according to what the melting point and glass transition temperature the ﬁlament material has,
one of the most commonly used materials is PLA [35].
PLA is an aliphatic polyester that is known to be biodegradable and have high strength. It has a rel-
atively low melting temperature compared to other polymers of 150◦C to 160◦C which makes it more
energy saving for a fabrication processes [35]. On top of being biodegradable it is also known to be
biocompatible, which makes it a good material for medicinal purposes such as fabrication of medical
devices or as a method of drug delivery [35]. This, together with the cheap price and easy accessibility
makes it an ideal material for printing the shear stress system.
1.7.2
Stereolithography using Dental Resins
The SLA printer is a laser-based printer that will print the desired object layer-by-layer. The printer con-
sist of a building platform which will descend into a large tank ﬁlled with liquid light-cured photopolymer
resin. For each layer, the laser beam will trace-out the pattern on top of of the liquid resin at which point
the platform will be lowered into the tank and the laser will crosslink the resin on the building platform or
the already printed layer. The platform will ascend and the process will repeat itself until all the layers
are printed. It is important to note that the ﬁnish object will be hanging upside down from the platform,
which means that it is necessary to design temporary support systems to resist gravity. Unlike the FDM
system, a post-processing method is necessary in order to remove excess resin and to crosslink even
further in a UV oven, thereby hardening the material [36].
Resin materials can either by synthetic or natural. The natural resins are typically derived from plants
and can help to protect them against insects or can be produced as a response to injury. However for
this thesis the interesting resins are the synthetic resins. In science resins are deﬁned as a vicious
liquid of monomers that when curing for example by light or heat, can turn into hard rigid polymers. Both
natural and synthetic resins have many uses of which an example is Dental resin composites which are
used to make splits or molds for dentistry and also as an alternative for ﬁllings. Dental resin is classiﬁed
as a ClassIIa long-term biocompatible resin, and is deﬁned as biocompatible according to the EN-ISO
10993-1:2009/AC:2010 standard. It will cure via light and UV into a hard material, ideal for medical
device and dental component manufacturing [37].

1.9
Cell types used
To accomplish the aim of the demonstration of rolling capture, two different cell types will be used in
order to model the initial stage of atherosclerosis, Human umbilical vesicular endothelial cells (HUVEC)
and THP-1 cells. THP-1 cells are a human cell line that is taken from patients suffering from acute mono-
cytic leukemia. The HUVECs will be used as a model for the endothelial cell layer or the endothelium,
lining the arteries and the THP-1 will be used to act as a monocyte to demonstrate the phenomenon of
rolling capture.
1.9.1
HUVEC
The morphology of endothelial cells are long and ﬂat which are typically oriented along the axis of the
vessel. The average size of the cell is 20-40 µm long, 10-15 µm wide and very thin only at 0.1-0.5 µm
thick [8]. HUVECS are probably one of the most popular choices when modelling EC in vitro, which is
one of the main reasons why it was chosen for this project. They are easily available to culture, and
have the ability to express important endothelial markers relevant for atherosclerosis such as selectins,
ICAM1 and VCAM1 [40]. Moreover the HUVEC cell lines will also react to certain stimuli like inﬂam-
matory proteins and shear stress [40] and change their morphology and phenotypic expression. This
makes the HUVEC cell line an optimal choice in modelling atherosclerosis.
1.9.2
THP-1
The THP-1 cell line was established in 1980 and these early studies indicate that it is able to resemble
the morphology and differentiation pattern (with the addition of a stimuli) of a human monocyte [41].
Once differentiated they will be able to take up lipoproteins and form foam cells, making them an excel-
lent choice for modelling atherosclerosis [41]. Furthermore, they have also been shown to adhere to a
TNF-α stimulated cell layer, further indicating that they are the ideal cell type to use [41]. Technically
they have some advantages over isolated human primary monocytes when working with them in vitro.
They are a relatively homogenous cell line limiting the variability of the phenotypic expression, they are
more easily transfected and genetically modiﬁed and they can be indeﬁnitely stored in liquid nitrogen
[41]. The THP-1 cells have also been shown to express important proteins like CCL2 and CCL5 which
are important proteins involved in atherosclerosis, further underlining their potential for atheroslcerosis
modelling [42]. However it is important to mention, that for clinical applications in drug discovery, there
are signiﬁcant differences between THP-1 and human primary monocytes, and in vivo validation could
be necessary in studies using this type of cell [41, 11].

Materials and Methods
2.1
Culturing and maintenance of cell types used
As mentioned the two cell types used for this study were Human Umbilical Vein Endothelial Cells (HU-
VEC) (ATCC® PCS100010) to resemble the endothelial lining of the artery and THP-1 cells (ATCC®
TIB202™) which are monocytes isolated from children suffering from acute monocyte leukemia. The
HUVECs were grown in endothelial cell growth medium (ECGM) (sigma-aldrich 211-500) supplemented
with 10% (v/v) Fetal Bovine Serum (FBS) (Biowest, s1810) and 5% (v/v) Penicillin and streptomycin
(P/S). Whereas the THP-1 cells were grown in RPMI-1640 medium (Sigma-aldrich, R8758) likewise
supplemented with 10% (v/v) FBS and 5% (v/v) P/S.
2.1.1
Seeding of HUVEC
The HUVECS used were stored in a cryopreserved tube at -150◦C, and used between passage 3 and 8
for the experiments in this thesis. When seeding the cells, the cell suspension containing approx. 1x106
cells/ml was thawed in a 37◦C water bath for 1-2 minutes and transferred into 10 ml of ECGM in a T75
cell culture ﬂask (Thermo, 156499). The cells were incubated in a 37◦C 5% CO2 humidity incubator.
The media was replaced 24 hours after seeding, and following this every 48h after ﬁrst subculture. The
cells were subcultured 2 times a week.
2.1.2
Subculturing of HUVEC
The subculturing of HUVECS required the trypsinisation of the cells to make them detach from the
T75 ﬂask surface.
The HUVECS were split at around 80% conﬂuency which was conﬁrmed using
microscopy beforehand. First the cells were washed twice with 5 ml Phosphate-Buffered Saline (PBS)
(Sigma-aldrich, D8537) solution after which 3ml of Trypsin (Sigma-aldrich, T3924) were added to the
cells. The cells were incubated for 2-3 minutes at 37◦C and the trypsinisation progress was monitored
using a microscope. After detachment the trypsinisation process was stopped using 3 ml ECGM and
transferred to a 15 ml conical tube. The T75 ﬂask was washed with 4 ml PBS which was likewise added
to the conical tube. The 10ml cell suspension was spun down in a centrifuge at 100xg for 5 minutes and
re-suspended in 6 ml ECGM. From this two T75 ﬂasks were seeded in a concentration of 1:3 and 1:6.
2.1.3
Seeding and subculturing of THP-1 cells
The THP-1 cells were stored in a cryopreserved tube at -150◦C, and used between passage 10 and
44 for the experiments. The frozen THP-1 cells, were thawed in a 37◦C water bath for 1-2 minutes
and transferred to a T75 ﬂask containing 11 ml RPMI-1640 medium. The cells were subcultured twice
a week by transferring 1-2 ml of cells into 10-11 ml new RPMI medium. The cells were grown to a
concentration between 1x105 and 1x106 cells per ml before being subcultured.

2.5
Shear stress system characterisation
The system used to apply shear stress on the cells was designed to ﬁt a 24-well plate (Thermo Fisher,
142475) and consisted of 7 individual components where 4 of these were printed on a 3D printer and 3
were commercially bought. The initial idea and design of this device was made by Christian Dagnæs,
and optimised in this thesis.
More speciﬁcally, the provided shear stress device consisted of a (1) LEGO mindstroms NXT controller
and large servo motor, (2) a top lid for holding the motor, (3) a LEGO Technic Axel, (4) a 24-well lid em-
bedded with (5) 24 ball bearings, (6) ﬁshbone gears (orientated in both directions) including a special
designed gear for connecting the motor with the gears and (7) 24 rotors (for technical drawings of each
component refer to Appendix B).
The device is assembled by inserting the ﬁshbone gears into the ball bearings in the lid, alternating

3.1
Design process of shear stress system
A shear stress device was manufactured and optimised to ﬁt a 24-well plate. During this process, vari-
ous ﬂaws that would affect the functionality of the device were discovered in the original design of some
of the components including the 24-well lid plate, the gears and the rotors.
It was found that the alignment of the holes in the 24-well lid were not centred to the wells in the 24-well
plate. This created problems when trying to insert the gears in the ball bearings and only 12 gears were
able to be inserted into the ball bearings without affecting the smoothness of the rotation. Furthermore
this misalignment skewed the position of the rotors to the point that it restricted the freedom of rotation.
Additionally, the lid did not follow the edge curvature of the 24-well plate, and slightly deviated from the
measurements of the 24-well plate. Therefore the lid was redesigned to follow the edge and corrected
to have a distance of 19.5 mm between the holes (Figure 13, Table 3).
Figure 13: Final design of 24-well lid. The initial design and dimension of the 24-well plate lid was redesigned. Now
all holes are 19.5 mm apart from center to center and the corners of the plate have been designed to ﬁt the curvature of a
standard 24-well plate (Thermo ﬁsher, 142475)
For the gears and rotors, three different designs were created and tested (Figure 14). In design A,
the rotors would detach from the the gears during rotation defying the purpose of the system and also
damaging the HUVEC monolayer at the bottom of the 24-well plate (Figure 14A). Design B solved the
detachment issue by incorporating a "locking" mechanism, however due to the small size, it proved to be
brittle and unstable and would break during assembly (Figure 14B). Design C used a screwing mecha-
nism to attach the rotor to the gear, ensuring stable assembly and preventing detachment (Figure 14C).
Thus design C was the ﬁnal design for all experiments in this thesis. Furthermore it was found that
printing the gears using Clear Resin and the SLA technique gave a higher precision and better strength
compared to using PLA and the FDM method, which proved to be beneﬁcial for connecting rotor and
gear.

3.1.1
Characterisation and optimisation of assembled device
Having optimised the individual components, the device was assembled and applied to a 24-well plate,
with a conﬂuent monolayer of HUVECs. The assembled device had various complications, one of which
was the creation of a "milky" substance in some of the wells (Figure 15).
The milky substance would only be present in wells that had been in contact with the rotors indicating
that the problem was related to the rotors. To exclude a bacterial contamination, several sterilisation
techniques were tried (Appendix A, Table 10) and the wells were investigated using a microscope (Fig-
ure 16). The change in sterilisation technique seemingly did not have an effect on the milky substance,
which kept occurring despite the more thorough sterilisation process. The microscopy images also in-
dicated large still structures with an uneven and strange outline, deviating from the normal morphology
and behaviour of most bacteria, which are smaller then HUVEC and known to be mobile. Furthermore
the endothelial cells on the bottom of the milky substance also seemed to be attached, and alive. These
results in combination with the milky substance occurring within the ﬁrst hour of starting the device and
progressively getting more opaque, led to the assumption that the problem was not a bacterial contam-
ination.

3.4
Potential improvements of the shear stress capability
As explained, shear stress can activate the atherosclerotic pathway, however it can also inhibit it when
the shear stress exerted on the cells reaches above 10 dyne/cm2, as it is in a laminar ﬂow environment.
The shear stress calculated for the device is very low and some extra experiments were conducted to
try and investigate if it was possible to increase the amount of shear stress the device can provide.
As mentioned the viscosity of the liquid is an important factor of the shear stress calculations, there-
fore it was assumed that increasing the viscosity of the liquid would increase the shear stress exerted
on the cells. The viscosity of blood is known to be around 3 cP (0.03 dyn · s/cm2) [45], hence as an
atherosclerosis model, the media was changed to have a viscosity similar to this. Therefore an approx.
8% gelatine solution was made using the ECGM, as it has been previously shown to create a viscosity
of around 3 cP [46].
Additionally the rotor design was also altered, with the goal of obtaining a more symmetrical shape
with less uncertainties for mathematical modelling of the system (Figure 29). The rotor was therefore
changed to have a disk shape to try and create a more laminar ﬂow, and further investigate the effect of
this design change on the shear stress.

4
Discussion
This thesis has demonstrated the ability to successfully create a shear stress environment and morpho-
logical activation of an endothelial cell layer, using a 3D printed shear stress device. Furthermore, the
device has also proved useful as a model for atherosclerosis in the demonstration of rolling capture on
THP-1 monocytes. The following will be a discussion of the design of the device, the ability to exert
shear stress on the endothelial cells and the potential of demonstrating rolling capture of monocytes.
4.1
General design and function of the assembled device
The ﬁnal optimised design of the shear stress device characterised in this thesis, supports the hy-
pothesis that a 3D printed system can be designed to include similar aspects to a microﬂuidic device.
Brieﬂy it can be summarised that a microﬂuidic system is able to provide dynamic ﬂow and shear stress
conditions in a relative easy manner using a pump system to continuously feed liquid into the system.
Furthermore it has the ﬂexibility to including any cell type and is also known to be used as co-culture
system using two different cell types.
This is similarly the case for the shear stress device developed in this thesis. The results support that the
device can be used to create a ﬂow and shear stress environment on a layer of cells and the standard
issue 24-well plate used for culturing cells, suggest that many cell type can be grown providing ﬂexibility.
Additionally, although not proven in this thesis, it is estimated, due to its easy manufacturing and high
ﬂexibility, that with a few minor adjustments the system is able to work in a co-culture environment (Sec-
tion 6.0.1). The advantages compared to microﬂuidic systems, is the quick 30-40 min preparation time
and the ability to run 24 different samples in one setting. Furthermore, one of the biggest challenges
with microﬂuidic system is the lack of standardisation of cell culturing on the material, an issue that is
eliminated by using a polystyrene 24-well plate. The manufacturing method of the device is optimised
to only require one type of equipment and is straightforward so no expertise or engineering is needed.
Moreover the device is fully portable and only require the LEGO NXT motor and servo arm to run, elimi-
nating the need for complicated external controls and tubing. The device also does not need continuous
ﬂow of liquid reducing the overall amount of media needed to maintain the ﬂow.
Thus, from the results of this thesis and previous research, it can be argued that the device show similar
results to what is expected from a microﬂuidic device.
Likewise the shear stress device also has advantages over the other three model systems explained in
section 1.5. In general using an in vitro system can be viewed as more ethically correct and is also less
expensive and time consuming, compared to using in vivo models [26]. This creates a clear advantage
of the system. Furthermore the easy manufacturing process and standardised cell culturing method, is
also an advantage compared to the ﬁeld of artiﬁcial 3D printed organs which can take a long time to
be optimised [28]. Lastly compared to many co-culture studies, the device has the added advantage
of providing a dynamic environment, with a possibility of adapting to a co-culture system with small
adjustments [33].
Although the device has many advantages, throughout the experiments one of the biggest challenges
has been the choice of material used for manufacturing. The main components of the device are made
Page 50 of 74
==PAGE:60==
Master thesis: Anna K. B. Mortensen
January 1st, 2020
with Dental LT resin, Clear resin and PLA. There has been issues with polymer residues and leaching of
resins that perhaps need to be taken into account for future long-term uses of the device. Furthermore
overtime the materials of the gears would become more brittle and would easily chip off or create debris
if higher pressures was put on. Also the PLA of the lid would weaken in increased temperatures and
the ball bearings produce a signiﬁcant amount of rust which could cause potential problems. Another
problem, which is assumed to be due to preciseness of manufacturing, is that the assembly of the rotors
and the gears can be crooked, which can result in height differences throughout the plate. Potentially
affecting the type of ﬂow and shear stress on the endothelial cells.
An easy solution for the issues with PLA and ball bearings is to change the materials of the compo-
nents. The PLA 24-well lid could easily be changed to be printed in Clear or Dental LT resin. This
would actually be an advantage compared to the current system, as it would require less equipment
for manufacturing, improve the precision of the component and eliminate the sensitivity to heat. With
regards to the ball bearings, the high amount of ethanol and liquid used to sterilise the device, will result
in the ball bearings producing rust and damaging the materials of the gears. To solve this issue one
could consider changing to ceramic or stainless materials, although perhaps more expensive it could
prove useful for future purposes.
The issue of wear and tare of the resin materials for long-term use is a bigger problem, and perhaps
requires a bigger change in manufacturing process to a more complicated and expensive procedure.
An example could be to use injection molding, which is a versatile manufacturing method that requires
the injection, under high pressures and heat, of molten thermoplastics or thermoset materials. This
will solidify into a pre-designed mold once it has cooled down. The mold can be designed to ﬁt any
shape and size and will work for very detailed shapes, indicating the usefulness for biomedical devices.
Furthermore injection molding of thermoplastics have been used for the creation of drug delivery sys-
tems indicating that it would be suitable for medicinal purposes and can be made biocompatible [47].
Another suggestion to perhaps ensure a more long-term usage is to change the material to a metal, for
example stainless steel. Similarly injection molding using a stainless steel powder has been used for
biomedical purposes and as materials for implants which support the claim that it could be relevant for
the long-term use of the device designed in this thesis [48]. The change in material and manufacturing
methods would improve the precision and long-lasting properties of the materials however it would be
expensive and would require experts for the manufacturing process, defying some of the advantages of
the current design.
Summary
In summary, although there have been issue with the materials used for the device, the results indicate
that for short-term research projects that require a dynamic ﬂow conditions, the design of the device
would be well suited in creating such an environment as further discussed in the following section. If
the purpose of the device is for long-term usage, it could perhaps be beneﬁcial to consider changing
the manufacturing method and material to ensure components that are more long-lasting and perhaps
have higher precision.

4.1.2
Uncertainties of shear stress predictions
The calculated velocity of the THP-1 monocytes were used to estimate the amount of shear stress that
the rotors can apply on the endothelial cells using equation 2 and 3 in section 1.4.
The results indicated a relatively low shear stress was applied to the endothelial cell layer, however
interestingly the two methods of calculations gave signiﬁcantly different results. The large difference be-
tween the two methods indicates that there are uncertainties in the calculations. The difference between
the two methods are difﬁcult to explain, however it could involve the large amount of approximations and
assumptions made for the system, in order to make the calculations possible.
One method assumed a simple two plane system where a force will create a velocity gradient that is
perpendicular to the plane of shear, whereas the other assume a channel geometry that has a certain
ﬂow rate. Both methods require the approximation that in the plane or cross-sectional area being eval-
uated, at a certain point the velocity of the particles is constant or at least close to.
In the ﬁrst method, calculated based on the difference in velocities of two planes, the images used to
calculate the velocity indicated that more than one plane of ﬂow was visualised based on the difference
in focus and length of the light paths (Figure 33A). To try and exclude this uncertainty from the data only
the highest velocities at a certain point was selected to do further analysis on. However it is difﬁcult to
precisely determine the correct plane based on the microscopy method used. Furthermore, as men-
tioned, the THP-1 cells are also not the standard option for particles to use in a PIV test as the size of
them are too big and they will spin in their own axis, changing the speed and direction of ﬂow (Figure
33A). Moreover the shear stress is calculated based on the fact that the velocity from the rotor to the
bottom will decrease in a linear fashion, however with only two data points, this is not possible to assure,

Due to the mathematical challenges it could be relevant to investigate other methods of determining the

magnitude, and thereby one can create a shear-stress ﬁeld [52].

4.2
Endothelial cell characterisation
4.2.1
Morphological and cytoskeletal organisation and VCAM-1 quantifcation
The results of the thesis, support the hypothesis that the shear stress device has the ability to in-
duce morphological changes and reorganisation of an endothelial cell layer that are in accordance with
atherosclerotic progression. In order to conﬁrm this, bright ﬁeld microscopic images and stained actin
ﬁlaments were analysed. Furthermore the activation of endothelial cells were analysed by the quantiﬁ-
cation of VCAM-1 expression.
The general pattern from the bright ﬁeld images generally support results of how shear stress and in-
ﬂammatory mediators affect the morphological and cytoskeletal properties of endothelial cells in atheroscle-
Page 55 of 74
==PAGE:65==
Master thesis: Anna K. B. Mortensen
January 1st, 2020
rosis. In the control sample the HUVECS have the classic cobblestone morphology which will change
to a more elongated morphology when TNF-α and/or shear stress is added. This supports previous
results and is a well known phenomenon in atherosclerosis [8, 9, 53, 54].
The results further demonstrate that there is a noticeable difference between the activation using TNF-
α only, using shear stress only and using a combination of the two. First of all if ﬂow is applied, the
cells seem to align with the direction of ﬂow, which of course happen to less degree when only TNF-α
is applied. Additionally, the morphology when stimulated with TNF-α also seem more ﬂat, than when
stimulated with shear stress only, which seem to have a more rounded morphology with an elongation
resembling a tail protruding from the cell. Since the arteries are under constant ﬂow and shear stress,
the sample that has been stimulated by both TNF-α and shear stress is deemed to be most physiologi-
cal similar situation.
The more rounded morphology seen when endothelial cell are stimulated with shear stress only, are in
contradiction to previous studies that show that stimulation of high laminar shear stress should give a
ﬂat fusiform, elongated morphology [53]. One possible explanation for the contradiction is supported by
a study performed by Dardik et al (2005). They investigated the difference between orbital shear stress
(turbulent and disturbed) and laminar shear stress on the endothelial cells. The results demonstrated a
rounded and elongated morphology when the cells were inﬂuenced by orbital shear stress, which are
similar to the morphology found in this thesis. Thus, it could be indicated that the morphology follow a
more turbulent ﬂow compared to a laminar which could be an explanation for the more rounded shape
seen in the results [55]. This is positive in regards to modelling atherosclerosis as it is proved that more
prone areas are situated in curvatures with low, turbulent ﬂow [2]. However, it could also be due to low
shear stress, not being able to fully ﬂatten the cells, therefore to fully conﬁrm this theory more research
would have to be conducted.
For the actin cytoskeleton, the results of the thesis similarly is supported by previous research. It is
known that the actin ﬁlaments will undergo elongation and reorganisation, when endothelial cells are
stimulated with TNF-α and/or shear stress [8]. This happens due to mechanosensitive receptors of the
endothelial cells which will induce actin ﬁlament stress ﬁbers to polymerise and prolong in the center
of the cell. The stress ﬁbers will move towards the membrane and in the process change the shape of
the cell, disrupting the dense peripheral bands [9, 56]. When TNF-α or shear stress is applied to the
endothelial cell monolayer in the samples, the results reveal more elongated actin ﬁlaments where the
dense peripheral bands that would normally outline the cell shape, will be disrupted, supporting previ-
ous research.
The permeability of the monolayer is connected to the actin organisation and as such it makes sense
that the permeability is likewise affected by TNF-α and shear stress [8, 21]. Kang et al (2014) conducted
a study where they measured the permeability for LDL of an endothelial layer and how shear stress af-
fects this. They conclude that the permeability of the monolayer is enhanced by short term application
of shear stress but will decrease after prolonged shear stress [21]. The results of the work in this thesis
show that a dense and thick network of actin ﬁbers is present when the cells have been exposed to
shear stress for 48 hours indicating that the permeability is low, thus the ﬁndings of Kang et al (2014)
are supported in the thesis. On the other hand, when TNF-α is applied, the actin cytoskeleton of the

==PAGE:66==

January 1st, 2020
endothelial monolayer show larger patchy areas which could indicate a higher permeability. This is also
in accordance with previous research as when inﬂammatory mediators are added to the endothelial
cells they have the ability to phosphorylate the proteinkinase C pathway which will induce the actin re-
organisation and cell morphology change and open the tight and adherence junctions between cells,
increasing the paracellular transport [8, 57]. However to conclude further on this matter, permeability
studies would need to be conducted.
The results of the VCAM-1 receptor staining, indicated that TNF-α was needed in order for the HUVECs
to induce the expression of VCAM-1 receptor. The expression of VCAM-1 in the presence of TNF-α is
supported by previous studies and is a well known fact in the research of atherosclerosis [58, 59]. When
no TNF-α was added to the sample the VCAM-1 staining would not be expressed. This is supported by
Tsou et al (2008), which indicated that applying shear stress without the addition of a cytokine, would
only slightly induce the ICAM-1 receptor and not any of the other CAM receptors would be effected,
including the VCAM-1 receptor [58].
Additionally it has been demonstrated that the addition of shear stress on a endothelial layer activated
by inﬂammatory cytokines, will signiﬁcantly decrease the induction of VCAM-1 [59, 60]. This is in con-
trast to what is demonstrated by the results in this thesis. The results, indicate that there is no signiﬁcant
difference of VCAM-1 expression when adding shear stress to the endothelial cells. This could be ex-
plained by the high uncertainty of the VCAM-1 quantiﬁcation method. Quantifying expression proﬁles
based on image analysis is difﬁcult and can be inﬂuenced by the focus and quality of the image as well
as the background noise. The large uncertainty of the data can be emphasised by comparing the two
biological replicates of the VCAM-1 quantiﬁcation, which proved to signiﬁcantly different (Appendix C).
The second replicate was a lot higher in expression of VCAM-1, perhaps due to higher staining intensity
or an error in the ﬁrst staining experiment. However due to lack of time, only two replicates were done
which is not enough to conclude further on what could have affected the results. Additionally, from the
uncertainties of the quantiﬁcation methods it can also be argued that using a different method to conﬁrm
the results of this thesis is beneﬁcial adn perhaps necessary for further conclusions. As an example
quantitative real time PCR reaction is a validated method to quantify expression data.
Summary
In summary the importance of including both ﬂow and inﬂammatory mediators are highly emphasised,
as the effect in the morphology, actin reorganisation and VCAM-1 expression are proven to be very
different. Additionally, it is also indicated that the morphology could support a more turbulent ﬂow com-
pared to a laminar ﬂow which is positive in modelling atherosclerosis. For the VCAM-1 quantiﬁcation it
is indicated that TNF-α is needed for the expression. However the uncertainties of the method of quan-
tiﬁcation require perhaps further conﬁrmation of the results by using other methods. In general, it can
be summarised that the shear stress device is able to induce morphological changes to the endothelial
cells, reorganise the actin cytoskeleton and induce VCAM-1 expression under the right stimulation, in-
dicating its usefulness as an atherosclerotic model.

4.2.2
Determination of high or low levels of shear stress
It is known that members of the CAM family (for example VCAM-1 or ICAM-1) are mechanosensitive,
which means that it is sensitive to a change of ﬂow or shear [60]. Therefore the results of the VCAM-1 ex-
pression staining in combination with the above mentioned actin cytoskeletal and morphology changes,
also known to be mechanosensitive, can be used to further discuss the amount of shear exerted on the
cells.
The results of this thesis are conﬂicting when it comes to determining whether the shear stress exerted
on the cells is high or low. According to previous research, a shear stress in the range of 2-4 dyne/cm2
has been proven to be pro-atherosclerotic, and to be able to induce VCAM-1 expression, and is seen
as relatively low. On the other hand shear stress above 8 dyne/cm2, normally in the range of 12-17
dyn/cm2, have proven to be atheroprotective, and is generally considered high [58, 53, 54].
At ﬁrst glance the actin staining and characterisation of the morphology when the cells are under stim-
ulus indicate that the cells are exposed to high shear stress. In the samples where the HUVECs are
exposed to shear stress, the dense peripheral bands are disrupted, the cells are elongated and aligned
with ﬂow and the actin reorganisation provide an illusion of a low permeable monolayer. All four changes
are supported by previous research to indicate an atheroprotective endothelial cell layer [58, 53, 54, 61],
indicating a high shear stress environment. However, a ﬁnding by Tsou et al (2008) [58], states that very
low shear stress, approx. 0.25 dyne/cm2, is all that is needed to observe a signiﬁcant change in en-
dothelial cell morphology and reorganisation of the actin ﬁlaments of an endothelial monolayer. Seeing
as the calculated shear stress is generally above 0.25 dyne/cm2, the changes of morphology and reor-
ganisation is perhaps not the best indicator of the level of shear stress exerted on the cell layer.
Further contradicting results can be seen in relation to the VCAM-1 quantiﬁcation. The shear stress
calculation, seemingly support a low shear stress which could indicate that the shear is perhaps too low
to activate the VCAM-1 expression, which is in accordance with the lack of VCAM-1 expression in shear
stress only samples. However, the lack of VCAM-1 expression in the samples with shear stress alone,
could also indicate that the shear stress is high. Previous research have demonstrated that high shear
stress is be able to inhibit the expression of VCAM-1 [53, 60, 59]. In this context, based on the results
of this thesis it could also indicate that the shear stress is so high that it will inhibit the expression of
VCAM-1 receptor. However based on the previously supported assumption that an inﬂammatory media-
tor is needed for VCAM-1 induction, this too can be categorised as an uncertain method of determining
VCAM-1 quantiﬁcation [58]. A possible suggestion for improving this, is to investigate the expression
of other adhesive receptors such as ICAM-1 or E-selectin. This can be claimed as these proteins have
been proven to be a better indicator of shear stress induced activation [60, 59].
Summary
In summary, the morphological changes and staining results are not ideal in concluding further on
whether the amount of shear stress is high or low, and it cannot be conﬁrmed nor denied which of
Page 58 of 74
==PAGE:68==
Master thesis: Anna K. B. Mortensen
January 1st, 2020
the shear stress calculations are correct based on the data in this thesis. Therefore in order to better
characterise the shear stress of the device, the system requires more thorough mathematical modelling
and computer simulations, which is outside of the scope of this thesis, but would be possible and very
relevant for future uses of the system.
4.3
Rolling capture
The results of this thesis support the hypothesis that the shear stress device is able to successfully
demonstrate the rolling capture of monocytes, and further support, similarly to the previous sections,
that it can be used as a model system for atherosclerosis.
Three different variables; prior stimulation by TNF-α, prior stimulation by shear stress and the application
of mixing, were changed to investigate the effect it had on the ability of the endothelial cell layer to
capture monocytes. Out of these three variables, TNF-α and the application of mixing while monocytes
were added on the cell layer, proved to have an effect on the amount of monocytes caught.
First of all, it was illustrated by the results that when the THP-1 cells were exposed to ﬂow or mixing
after the addition, a signiﬁcant decrease would be seen in the amount of monocytes caught. As already
claimed, the was assumed to be because in a static environment the monocytes would be able to simply
sediment on the bottom and be caught in the endothelial cell layer. Therefore the focus of the results
were put on the THP-1 cells exposed to mixing as these are believed to be caught by rolling capture.
With regards to the lack of TNF-α, this would signiﬁcantly decrease the amount of monocytes being
caught on the monolayer. Comparing the rolling capture results with the VCAM-1 staining, the results
nicely correlate in that when VCAM-1 is expressed, as a consequence of addition of TNF-α, more
monocytes will be caught by rolling capture. This supports the theory that VCAM-1 expression is highly
involved in the rolling capture of monocytes, and that it is induced by TNF-α [1, 13, 18].
However, interestingly when no VCAM-1 is induced, both in control sample and the sample only stimu-
lated by shear stress, the monocytes will still be caught on the monolayer, although in lower amounts.
This could indicate that other receptors are expressed on the endothelial cells in static conditions. Sheik
et al (2003) [62], conducted a study that could explain this. In their study they indicated the expression
proﬁle of HUVEC cells in a static, TNF-α induced and dynamic environment. The results showed that the
HUVECS are able to express, P-selectin, E-selectin and ICAM-1 in static conditions in relatively large
quantities, which are known receptors all involved in the process of capturing the monocytes [62, 1, 18],
supporting the results of this thesis.
Another interesting result, although not signiﬁcant, is the slightly lower amount of monocytes caught
when shear has been applied to the monolayer prior to THP-1 addition. These results support a previ-
ous study performed by Korenaga et al (1997) [63] which demonstrated that pre-conditioning endothe-
lial cells with shear stress will slightly inhibit the cytokine induced VCAM-1 expression, thereby slightly
inhibiting the amount of monocytes being caught [63]. Furthermore, Sheik et al (2003) [62], also demon-
strated that TNF-α addition with shear stress, would decrease the amount of P- and E-selectin as well
as ICAM-1 receptor [62]. Another, perhaps more simple explanation could also be that the mechanical

shear stress has removed some of the endothelial cells, which means that there are less cells for the
monocytes to bind to.
To investigate the distribution of monocytes being caught throughout the plate and if the ﬂow velocity of
the cell had an effect, 9 different positions in the wells were chosen.
The results, in a non-TNF-α stimulated environment showed that the faster the monocytes were rotating
the less amount of monocytes would stick to the endothelial layer. A probable cause of this could be that

capture, compared to when not much resistance is there, as is the case in the center, hence it would be
easier to reach the bottom. Another probability relates to the laminar or turbulent ﬂow. According to the

illustrated that the further from the center of the well, the more laminar the ﬂow seemed to be, support-
ing the results of this thesis [55]. However more investigation into the pattern of ﬂow would need to be

Furthermore the result indicates that the distribution of caught monocytes when TNF-α was added be-

does not have a signiﬁcant effect on the ability of the monocytes to be caught on the layer. One can
assume this is due to a high activation of the endothelium, which means all proteins are expressed and
the mechanosensitive properties of the endothelium are less potent [58, 53, 63].
It could further indicate that perhaps the shear stress exerted on the cells is not high enough to fully
inﬂuence the ability of monocytes being stuck. Another interesting discovery only seen in a TNF-α stim-
ulated environment was a small change in monocyte morphology after being caught on the endothelial
layer (Figure 34). This could indicate that the monocytes are starting to activate by their extension of
pseudopodia, creating a ﬂat shape with protuding structures [64]. However to conﬁrm this more re-
search would have to be conducted.

4.4
Effect of the disk rotor and viscosity change of the media
As indicated from the discussion, one of the main drawbacks of the used system is the difﬁculties in
determining the amount of shear stress that is exerted on the cells. Furthermore the shear stress that
was calculated conﬂicted with the results from the morphology and actin staining, indicating a lower
shear stress than what was expected. Therefore to try and increase the amount of shear stress exerted
on the cells or at least change the type of shear stress, the rotors were redesigned to a disk design and
the viscosity of the media was changed.
The results indicated that there was a clear morphological difference between the type of shear stress
exerted by the paddle rotors and the type of shear stress exerted by the disk rotors. The morphology by
the disk rotors changed from a more rounded elongated cells, to a more ﬂat and fusiform shape, with
larger spacing between the cells. This could indicate that the type of ﬂow has changed to assembly a
more laminar ﬂow compared to the more orbital (disturbed) ﬂow, supported by the study conducted by
Dardik et al (2008) [55]. However the alignment of the cells seem more random when activated with
the disk rotor, than a paddle rotor. Due to lack of time, the disk rotors were not optimised and proved
to be too long, hitting the bottom of the well plate and creating the milky substrance seen in the ini-
tial experiments. This could explain the decrease in cells and perhaps also the slightly more random
alignment. Furthermore only one technical and biological replicate was available to be analysed, which
means that not enough data is present to make a ﬁnal assumption. However based on the initial mor-
phology changes, it could be very relevant to further optimise the design and characterise the effect on
the endothelial cells.
The challenges of the materials meant that the rotors had to be shortened for it not to hit the sides of
Page 61 of 74

the well, which could potentially decrease the amount of shear stress exerted on the cells. Therefore, to
investigate the effect of higher viscosity of the morphology and shear stress calculations, an 8% gelatine
media combination was made. This indicated to have a signiﬁcant effect on the amount of shear stress
being exerted on the cells. The morphology signiﬁcantly changed to indicate higher shear stress on the
cells, changing the shape to a elongated aligned fusiform, similar to samples induced by TNF-α and
shear stress, however with an illusion of a more tightly packed monolayer. The shear stress calculations
also increased signiﬁcantly with 1.24 dyn/cm2 or 5.94 dyn/cm2 being the highest, depending on which

To further characterise the change of the disk rotor and high viscosity media, the VCAM-1 expression
was stained in all samples. This showed similar results to the initial experiments with VCAM-1 ex-
pression being very low and in the same range as the control and shear stress only samples, and no
expression of VCAM-1 was seen. Based on these results the claim made by Tsou et al (2008) [58]
that TNF-α or another inﬂammatory cytokine is needed for VCAM-1 induction is still supported by the
results of this thesis. Nonetheless, it could also be that the VCAM-1 expression is now in range that can
inhibit VCAM-1 proteins, again showing that the VCAM-1 protein is not ideal for shear stress indications.
However, again due to limited time, only one repeat of this was done and more repeats and analysis
would have to be done in order to conclude on a potential effect.
Summary
In summary, the experiment investigating the viscosity of the liquid and the disk rotor design, were
preliminary experiments and should be repeated to determine the true effect. However, both the high
viscosity and the disk rotor show potential for further research as the morphological change seemed
different, perhaps indicating a more laminar ﬂow. Furthermore the shear stress values seemingly also
increased signﬁcantly, indicating an effect. Furthermore the results also support that TNF-α is needed
for the induction of VCAM-1 expression.

Conclusion
This thesis had the main objective to demonstrate the rolling capture of monocytes on an endothelial
cell layer, used for atherosclerosis modelling. This was completed by the optimisation of a 3D printed
shear stress device previously designed by another master student at DTU. The aims included the opti-
misation of this device, the characterisation of the effect on the endothelial layer provided by the shear
stress, and also TNF-α, and to demonstrate the effect of the system and TNF-α on the rolling capture
of monocytes on the activated endothelial layer.
The optimised version of the shear stress device successfully demonstrated the ability to activate and
change the morphology of a monolayer of endothelial cells, simulating the dynamic environment of an
artery. Furthermore it was indicated that the model system can successfully be used to demonstrate
the capture of monocytes in ﬂow conditions. For the morphology change and actin reorganisation, the
results demonstrated that TNF-α in combination with shear stress, had the biggest effect on the change
of morphology and actin reorganisation, underlining the importance of including both in a model system.
Furthermore TNF-α also showed to signiﬁcantly effect the amount of monocytes being caught and the
expression of VCAM-1, supporting the relation of the protein and cell type in atherosclerosis.
The main drawback of the shear stress device was the challenge in calculating the amount of shear
stress exerted on the cell layer. From the uncertainties and conﬂicting results, it was not possible based
on the results of this thesis to further conclude on the exact amount of shear stress. Therefore more re-
search needs to be done for this type of characterisation. Despite the uncertainties in the quantiﬁcation,
it was however still evident from the results that some type of shear stress was exerted on the cells.
In conclusion, the shear stress device is deemed a successful model system for the creation of dynamic
ﬂow conditions and demonstration of rolling capture in the initial stage of atherosclerosis.

6
Future perspectives
The ability of the shear stress to create a dynamic ﬂow environments, opens up the opportunity for
various future applications, for example by transitioning the shear stress device to be used to do barrier
studies. Furthermore the challenges of shear stress calculations also allow for further optimisation and
modelling of the system.
6.0.1
Co-culture system
From the literature study, it has become clear that atherosclerosis is a complicated disease that will
activate many different signalling pathways and involves multiple proteins and cell types. Hence, it is
discussed that an optimal model system of atherosclerosis contains both the ability to create a dynamic
environment and also a possibility to co-culture different cell types. For an atherosclerosis model,it
would be relevant to investigate the co-culture of HUVEC cells with SMC, and investigate the migration
ability of monocytes through a barrier, which is the next step in atherosclerosis after rolling capture.
The results of the thesis demonstrate the ability to create a dynamic environment with the capability to
produce shear stress on the cells, however design of the experiments lacks the aspect of creating a
barrier between two types of cells for migration studies. In literature transwell models are continuously
used to co-culture cells for barrier studies, as both sides of the transwell can be used to culture different
type of cells and the ﬁlter or gel membrane of the transwell can be used as a method of communication
aswell as a barrier. The high ﬂexibility of the shear stress device optimised in this thesis, allows the
easy adaptation of the device to be used in a co-culture environment using a transwell system, where
only a small adjustment of the design of the rotors is needed. A preliminary design was created for this
purpose (Figure 35).

6.0.2
Optimisation, mathematical modelling and computer simulations
As continuously mentioned throughout the discussion one of the biggest drawbacks of the shear stress
device was the challenge of calculating shear stress exerted on the cells. This can be argued to be due
to the asymmetric and, from a mathematical perspective, complicated design of the paddle. Therefore
further mathematical modelling and computer simulations will signiﬁcantly help the usefulness of the
device. Additionally from preliminary results of the disk rotors effect on the endothelial cells, it could be
relevant to further investigate the ability of these rotors to be used to model atherosclerosis instead of
using the paddle. The disk rotor is a more symmetrical model, that will be easier to model and will have
less uncertainties when calculating the shear stress, and perhaps create a more laminar ﬂow on the
cells.
