Summary
The weather service has been one of the essential supports for the uptake of the renew-
able energy in the Nordic electricity market. This thesis study focuses on the effect of
the uncertainty in wind power prediction on the Danish power market. The quantifi-
cation of this effect is the primary task of this work, and the results reflect the added
value that a skillful weather service could bring into the Danish power market.
The Balmorel model, which is a partial equilibrium model widely used in energy sec-
tor for simulation the energy system operation in various European countries, is used
for simulating the power market operation in Denmark. The supreme focus lies in the
electricity price formation and the total cost of the system. A base Balmorel model is
validated for the specific year of 2017. The average modelled price are considerably close
the Nord Pool day-ahead price, and the power balance in each time segment is achieved.
In addition, the wind power prediction generators are created based on the statistical
model (auto-regressive models), the varying uncertainties are added on by applying an
weighting coefficient. The data generators are also created for net exchange, which is
the power exchange between Denmark and the third countries outside of simulation in
Balmorel.
The effect of uncertainties on the Danish power market is analysed from the Balmorel
model outputs, such as the electricity price and system cost.
In conclusion, the study has shown that the increased uncertainty or the variability in
wind production increases the system total cost mildly, especially for the fuel and emis-
sion cost. By contrast, the effect on electricity price per MWh in day-ahead market is
less evident in Denmark. In other words, a higher quality of the wind weather service
will defiantly bring the added value to the Danish power system and market in terms of
decrease system cost and lowering the electricity price.
Key words: Short term wind power prediction, Variability, Uncertainty, Auto-Regressive
Model, Balmorel model, Denmark, Day-ahead Spot market.

Introduction
Renewable energy involves natural phenomena such as, wind, sunlight, water, tides,
geothermal heat and so on. With the climate change and global warming concerns, to-
gether with the continuing falling of the costs in some renewable energy solutions, such
as wind turbines and solar panels, the renewable energy in electricity generation has
grown at an astonishing rate.
The large scale integration of renewable energy into electric power system has brought
inescapable challenges to various stakeholders, including power producers and power sys-
tem operators, due to the stochastic and fluctuating nature of some renewable resources.
Especially, in the regions with high penetration levels of certain types of renewable en-
ergy, it is important to forecast the power outputs in a reasonably accurate manner, so
that those renewable energies can be utilized in the power system. For example, power
system operators use wind power forecast to schedule the operation of other plants.
Wind power has been playing a crucial role in Denmark within electricity sector. This
thesis focuses on the specific type of renewable resource ——wind, and the wind weather
service in this study is defined as short term wind power prediction or forecasting. Dis-
cussing and quantifying the effect of uncertainty or variability in wind power prediction
on the Danish power system is the main task, the added value of wind weather service
are further discussed.
The Balmorel model, a partial equilibrium model specialised in solving linear program-
ming optimisation problem is utilized for the price formation and system simulation
in the wholesale power market. The statistical models are created for generating wind
power prediction and net exchange.

CHAPTER 2
Trading of Electricity
Electric power system is a network, or a grid that provides electricity to a large area, for
example a country. An electric power system can be divided into three key components.
Power suppliers
Power suppliers, or also called generators or producers. They generate electricity by
various methods and sources, for example, wind turbines, solar panels, hydro plants,
steam turbines heated by coal, gas turbines, nuclear plants and so on.
Electricity grid
Electricity grid can be further divided into transmission system and distribution system.
A transmission system transfers electricity generated from power suppliers to the load
centres, while the distribution system is at the load center, and further carriers electricity
into houses, buildings or factories.
Loads
Loads, or also called consumers or customers. They use electricity to perform a function.
It can be home appliances, like a refrigerator, or it can be industrial machinery.
There are many challenges in the electric power system. First, electricity can be seen as
commodities or goods, which is produced and transferred to the customers through the
power system, and the customers pay for it. However, electricity is different from other
goods, because it is not easy to store. Although there is battery which store the electric-
ity, it is not commonly applied in large scale in power system at the moment. There,
thus, must always be balance between electricity generation and electricity consumption
at any time in power system.
Secondly, consumption varies a lot by time, for example, based on the data form En-
erginet, on 11.6.2019, the electricity consumption of Denmark between 2:00 and 3:00 is
2703 MWh, while, on the same day between 10:00 and 11:00, the consumption is 4545
MWh. There is 1842 MWh or almost 70% difference.
Thirdly, when a customer needs electricity, this demand shall always be met, and the
electricity has to be available. If the demand is not matched by the supply exactly, the
producers’ generators speed up or slowdown, which cause the power system frequency
(For example, 50 Hertz) to increase or decrease. The system operator tries all means to
keep the frequency within an acceptable range.

Similarly, the production can also vary by time. The coal-fired power plant could shut
down by mechanical issue, the wind and solar power varies by its nature.
Lastly, electricity provided to the customers can also be considered as public service.
The typical customers’ expectations are the electricity bill shall be low, the system shall
minimise outage times and be quick for the power restoration.
2.1
Eletricity Market
The power market is designed according to the electric power system, addressing the
challenges the power system is facing. However, in the historical point of view, the
challenges are not the same at different periods of time.
2.1.1
Short History
The first commercial large-scale application of electricity may be considered as in New
York City in 1882, when Edison Illuminating Company, a company established by
Thomas Edison, built a power plant and powered 400 streetlamps for 82 customers.
Starting from there, electricity has developed into an essential part of the normal devel-
oped countries.
By the 1950s, electricity could be considered as natural monopoly. The high infrastruc-
tural costs for the power system and many barriers to enter the market give the largest
supplier in electric power industry an overwhelming advantage over potential competi-
tors. At that time, a limited number of companies participated in the market provides
market efficiency, so that it is common to have companies with vertically integrated
structure, who participated in all components in the power system, from the generation,
transmission until distribution and retailing. Usually the government body regulated
the price.
Since the 1990s, many countries in the world have started to deregulate the power in-
dustry, creating power markets in generation and distribution parts of the electricity.
The price is determined by the market. The horizontal restricting and competition lead
to improvement in the efficiency. Unlike the traditional vertical-integrated centralized
power market, the generation, transmission, distribution and retail are separated, and
there are competition in the generation and retailing parts. This increased competition
together with the well-connected markets has brought bunches of benefits in terms of
higher security of energy supply and optimal use of production resources. The transmis-
sion and distribution parts of electric power industry are usually still monopoly, as the
returns on investment are not easy to get.

2.3.1
Day-ahead Market
Day-ahead market is the main place for electricity trading, where contracts are made for
delivery of power the following day with settled price. For Denmark, day-ahead market
is called Elspot in Nord Pool.
Elspot is the main marketplace for power trading, where the trade is organized as two-
sided auction. Sellers and buyers submit the offers or bids to the market operator biding
for the electricity deliver to and withdraw from the grid, respectively, throughout the
next day [2]. The orders consist of 24 volume-price pairs, in which each pair specifies
the amount of energy willing to be sold or bought at the given price.
At 10:00 CET (Central European Time), the Energinet and other TSOs published trans-
mission capacities and market participants study the capacity and make price, supply
and demand predictions and choose auction strategy. Capacity is available to all on
equal terms. There are no explicit capacity auctions on connection capacity and there
is no single party who has access to any trading capacity.
2.3.2
Intra-day Market
Intra-day market is a continuous trading platform between day-ahead and balancing, al-
lowing the adjustment of the original schedule. In Denmark, intra-day market is called
Elbas in Nord Pool. During trading hours, the transactions will be matched automati-
cally when concurring orders are registered in the system.
At 14:00 CET in Denmark [6], once the day-ahead result is available, TSOs submit
available transmission capacity, and the Intraday market is open to trade. The trade
continues until 1 hour before the delivery hour.
Members can use hourly contracts or block contracts. For hourly contract, the members
are free to place or hit orders. The block contract is similar to the regular order in the
day-ahead market, with all or nothing feature [7]. The market acts as first come, first
served principle.
2.3.3
Balancing market
The balancing market is close to the real time operation, and it ensures the power bal-
ance for the market operator. Through Day-ahead market and intraday market, the
supply and demand have set balance in advance by the market operator. It is the re-
sponsibility of the Balance Responsible Parties (BRPs) to keep their plan on production
and consumption as promised. The plans, however, always deviate from what actually

2.4.3
Pricing in bidding area
At 12:00 CET, it is the deadline for submitting order and the results are available at
about 12:42 CET [12]. Then the price calculation process starts. The system price is
calculated together with the market coupling between Nord Pool and continental Eu-
rope and UK. For each bidding area at each hour, the total demand and supply curves
are created, and then the price for each area is formed.
The merit order is used to generate the demand and supply curves. For the supply curve,
all the orders submitted are ranked by price in the ascending manner. The lowest offer
is placed first, and the line is plotted as volume in the horizontal axis and price in the
vertical axis. Then follows the second lowest offer and so on. For the demand curve, it
is the opposite, all the orders submitted are ranked by price in the descending manner.
The highest bid is placed first, then follows the second highest bid and so on. In this
way, those electricity with the lowest offers are the first ones to be brought to meet
demand, and the plants with the highest offers are the last to be brought.
Two curves meet at one point, and this is the equilibrium established between supply
and demand. This point determines the total dispatched volume and price. All the sell-
ers offer price lower than the price at this point will be scheduled to generate electricity
with the volume they offer, and they all will be paid at the price at this point. All the
buyers bid price higher than the price at this point will be scheduled to receive electric-
ity with the volume they bid, and they all will pay at the price at this point. However,
the sellers offer price higher than the price at this point will not be scheduled. Simi-
larly, buyer bid price lower than the price at this point will not receive electricity neither.
Price equilibrium for each bidding area is unlikely to be the same, some of the bidding
areas have higher price and others have lower price. Between the bidding areas, there is
transmission capacity to transfer the electricity from area with lower price to the area
with higher price. Thus, bidding areas can have a balance, deficit or surplus of electricity.
If the transmission capacity is bigger enough to transfer the electricity to flow among
bidding areas, the price for different bidding areas would be the same. However, if the
transmission capacity between bidding areas is not sufficient to pass all the electricity
needed, there is a congestion. Then the bidding areas would have different prices.
For example, there is a bidding area with lower electricity price (20 EUR) than others
(30 EUR). The power will flow from this this surplus area to deficit areas. This can be
considered as buyers from other bidding areas come to buy electricity from this bidding
area, willing to pay price up to the price at their bidding area (30 EUR). As a result,

2.5
Imbalance Settlement
The imbalance cost in the balancing market is another concern apart from the price for-
mation mechanism in day-ahead market. The cost resulted by the imbalance between
planned and actual production, and this is particularly relevant for the renewable tech-
nologies, due to their predictability and variability.
The imbalance results from the uncertainties from generators and failure in consumption,
generation and the grid operation. The eSett Oy (Imbalance Settlement Responsible,
ISR) organisation equally owned by TSOs in Finland (Fingrid), Sweden (Svenska kraft-

tlement. Danish transmission system operator Energinet signed an agreement on 14th
of May that Denmark will join Nordic imbalance settlement and the share capital is
divided equally between the four shareholders [4].
To establish a financial balance in the electricity market after the operation hour is
the main purpose of the imbalance settlement. eSett performs imbalance settlement
and manages invoicing and collaterals towards the BRPs on behalf of the TSO in each
country [14]. The imbalance settlement is conducted by eSett every day starting from
the second day after the delivery day until the final settlement 13 days after delivery [15].

2.5.2
Pricing of Imbalance
Two different price models are applied to the production imbalances and consumption
imbalances. The price for the production imbalance is the less favourable price of the
MBA’s 5 PX market 6 price and imbalance price. This is called the “two price model”.
The price of the consumption imbalance is MBA’s imbalance price. This is called the
“one price model” [16].
2.5.2.1
Pricing of Production Imbalance
Production imbalance is priced according to the two-price model. Thus, positive and
negative production imbalances have different prices. When BRP has negative produc-
tion imbalances, BRP buys the deficits at price A, and at the same time, when other
BRP has positive production imbalances, RBP sells the surplus at price B, price A is
always higher than price B. Furthermore, the system regulation direction at the delivery
hour determines the types of prices applied to the positive and negative imbalances, and
they are described as follows:
If the system is in up-regulation, the system needs more production. If a BRP has
negative production imbalances, BRP needs buy at the up-regulation price, while if a
BRP has positive production imbalances, BRP needs sells at PX market price. The
up-regulation price is always higher than the PX market price.
If the system is in down-regulation, the system needs less production. If a BRP has
negative production imbalances, BRP needs buy at the PX market price, while if a BRP
has positive production imbalances, BRP needs sells at down-regulation price.

Wind Power Forecasting
In the beginning of the development of the wind power, it is usually the utility’s obli-
gation to purchase all the energy produced from the wind farms. However, as the wind
penetration increases, such as in Denmark, the wind energy today is being traded just
like other energies at Nord Pool and the wind farms are asked to deliver the production
plan to Energinet in the same way as the other plants. The wind power forecasting is thus
considered of great importance both in power system balance and for energy trading [22].
There is substantial difference in dispatchability between wind power and the conven-
tional energies. The wind energy cannot be easily dispatched by the request of power grid
operators according to system needs by contrast with the conventional power system,

To cope with the variability, the other power plants can dispatch with higher or lower

• Milliseconds to minutes

• Seasonal/long-term This type of forecasts are for planing of maintenance of wind
farms, conventional power plants or the transmission line. For example, the opti-

as access to the offshore turbine is barely like the quick, easy and safe access to
onshore turbine in almost any weather conditions.

3.3
Forecasting Techniques
The techniques and methods used for wind power forecasts varies. A general model of
the forecasting techniques being used at the present time is described as: The model
typically uses input data from diverse sources, such as numerical weather prediction
(NWPs) models, supervisory control and data acquisition (SCADA) system and local
wind measurement.
Based on the methodology, the wind-forecasting models can be
classified into the following three categories: (i) physical model, (ii) statistical model
and (iii) hybrid model [19].
3.3.1
Physical model
Physical model describes the physical relationship between wind speed, atmospheric
conditions, local topography and the output from the wind power plant. This model
usually includes several sub models, and they provide the wind forecast at a certain
location and height, before transferring into power forecast of a wind farm or each wind
turbine [23]. Thus, two main steps can be defined as: (i) downscaling and (ii) power
conversion.
With appropriate setup in NWPs, the downscaling calculates the wind
speed and direction at the turbine hub’s height. The input data includes the elevation,
roughness, obstacles and the local wind profile. The two main alternatives to do so are:
a) Surface wind is modelled through both the wind profile and the geotropic drag law,
and b) Advanced simulation of the wind field by computational fluid dynamics (CFD),
where the terrain is fully described. A power curve is applied for converting the wind
speed to power. For a NWP model, The calibration or correction is usually applied
by the comparison between predicted variables and observed variables in the statistical
way.
3.3.2
Statistical model
The statistical models aim to establishes statistical relation between historical power pro-
duction, historical wind forecast or wind measurement, instead of describing the physical
steps involved in the wind power conversion process [18], [19]. The statistical relation
finding may involve various input data, such as wind speed, direction result from NWPs,
or wind power, wind speed or direction of actual wind turbine.
The relation finding technique could be linear models, non-linear models, structural
models, black box models, or grey box models. The structural models could be the
models which construct data with diurnal wind speed variations pattern. The black box
models may apply artificial intelligence (AI), which requires little wind related knowledge.
The grey box models could apply AI as well as the experience. In other words, it falls
between structural and black box models. Some of the statistical models commonly
applied are described as follows.

3.5
Wind Power forecasting in Power Market
As described in previous chapter, the wind power is usually traded in day-ahead market
and intraday market, and get penalised in balancing market. The short-term forecasting
is typically used for power trading [26].
In Denmark, the electricity is traded at Nord Pool, Elspot as day-ahead market and El-
bas as intraday market. The bids deadline for Elspot is 12:00 every day for the electricity
production and price throughout the next day. In other words, the forecast horizon is at
least from the next 12 hours to 36 hours, with one hour time step. As for the the bidding
price, it is usually the marginal cost of the plant. As for wind power, the marginal cost
is close to zero or zero. Thus, the wind farm owners typically bid 0 EUR per MWh. The
market price is the price the wind farm owners receive. In this way, the wind production
is the main focus for the forecaster. A typical commercial forecaster may use the latest
one or more NWPs, as NWPs usually update several times a day, and combined with
physical approach, by a downscaling or micro-scale flow model, power curve or wake
effect, which may be pre-calculated, enabling the update of the forecast result in a very
short time. A statistical approach may also be applied, by finding relation with actual
on-site wind measurement, or actual production.
Intraday market starts after the Elspot has ended. Elbas is a continuous market until 1
hour before the delivery hour. When trading in the Elbas, Elspot-price of each delivery
hour is already known, thus, Elbas prices follow the Elspot price trend. As for the wind
power, the closer the hours, the accuracy is usually higher. The new forecast may be due
to the update of NWPs, or pure statistical modelling. The forecast horizon is between 1
and 36 hours of the delivery hour. The wind production prediction for about 2–3 hours
ahead, could have very high accuracy level, and thus, it is great for the wind power
producer to bid with much decreased forecast error. In addition, the successful Elbas
trade also lowers balancing costs. However, the volume in Elbas trade is quite low at
the moment, and Elspot remains as a main market place for trading energy, as more
than 70% of electricity in the Nordic countries is traded at Elspot.
The imbalance cost is a typical revenue loss for an optimal revenue. One way to min-
imise the loss is to improve the accuracy of the wind power forecasting, another way to
compensate is to apply the portfolio effect. It is found that the forecasting error drops
dramatically when several wind farms make a joint bids. Another way to reduce the
imbalance cost is to use optimal biding strategy. The strategy is designed by BRPs to
maximise the profit. In other words, the biding volume is not exactly the forecasting
production anymore.

Balmorel is a model widely used in the analysis of the energy sector, specialising in
electricity and combined heat and power systems. However, it has also been developed

5.2
The Auto-regressive Model Adoption
The auto-regressive–moving-average (ARMA) model is a mix of AR model and MA
model, and they are the basic models for understanding and analysing a stationary
time series. The auto-regression (AR) and moving average (MA) process are described
by a polynomial function in the order of p and q, respectively, note as AR(p) and MA(q).
If a time series model, Xt, is an auto-regressive model of order p, then AR(p) process is
defined as:
Xt = c + φ1xt−1 + ... + φpxt−p + et
(5.1)
Where c is a constant, φ1 to φp are the model parameters, and et is the white noise,
which is a series of random signal with zero mean and finite variance.
If a time series model, Xt, is an moving-average model of order q, then AR(q) process
is defined as:
Xt = µ + et + θ1et−1 + ... + θqet−q
(5.2)
Where µ is a expectation of Xt, and it is usually zero, θ1 to θq are model parameters,
and et is also white noise.
The ARMA model is the mix of AR and MA model, which is defined as:
Xt = c + φ1xt−1 + ... + φpxt−p + et + µ + et + θ1et−1 + ... + θqet−q
(5.3)
The actual wind production during six-year period from 2013 to 2018 with hourly time
step are collected and act as the training data for the ARMA model fitting.

7.1
Validation of Balmorel Base Model
Statistical Analyse of Base Model Results Through the validation of Balmorel
base model for a specific reference year 2017, the results has been shown in Chapter 6.1
and 6.2.
The statistical analysis has shown that the average electricity price from Balmorel is
greatly close to the Elspot day-head average price, and the bias of 1.4 AC/MWh in DK1
and 0.5 AC/MWh in DK2 are considered as a minor difference, because there are many
other phenomena happening in Nord Pool that Balmorel cannot consider and capture
within this scope of work, such as the unit commitment operation, and the biding strate-
gies that BRPs applied in day-head trading.
For example for some power plants, there is time and cost for the start and stop, and
the longer operating time could minimize this cost. This is considered in Nord Pool by
block orders. However, this is not modelled in Balmorel.
In Nord Pool, some of the generators may use strategy to maximize energy production
profits. For example, the companies with sufficient size to influence the price may bid
its production at higher cost in order to raise market prices, even though this may drop
their market share, but increase the profit. However, this is not modelled in Balmorel.
The accuracy indicators of MSE, RMSE and MAE in Table 6.2 show that the simulation
quality are similar in different seasons, although it is found that the average price bias
in Table 6.1 varies notably. The MAE varies from 6.6 AC/MWh to 8.1 AC/MWh, and for
whole year they are around 7.0 AC/MWh in both regions. This is considered as a minor
difference.

The power balance in each time segment for all seasons and regions are shown in Fig-

and demand residuals are achieved in each hour. In addition, the model price (shown

price (shown in red dot lines) in some period, especially the time segments where the

because the variation in Nord Pool are not only affected by the dispatchable generation,
but also with various affecting factors.
Among the three seasons, the season 3 during the period between October and December
particularly shows a violent fluctuation in terms of dispatchable generation and electric-

in DK1 is more than double of that in DK2, based on the data from the Nord Pool in

When there is a plenty of wind blowing, the wind power may meet all demand residuals
(demand-solar-net exchange), and the price falls and even approaches zero in the model
due to the zero marginal cost of wind power. However, in Elspot market, the lowest
price could further drop below zero due to the fact that wind suppliers might be still
profitable to bid at a negative price as they can get financial support from government.
At the same time, the electricity transmitted from DK1 to DK2 increases dramatically,
which are shown as dark blue line in Figure 6.1 to Figure 6.6. In reality, DK1 indeed
acts as an essential electricity exporter to the neighbouring countries, including Norway
and Germany, when wind resources are sufficient.
However, the import to DK1 increases dramatically when the wind is weak. The import
is mainly from Norway and Germany based on analysis of the historical power exchang-
ing data from Nord Pool. The production from domestic conventional plants in this case
rises, which eventually results in higher electricity prices owing to its higher marginal
cost compared to wind power.
To conclude, the Balmorel base mode is considered having the capability for simulating
an approximate power trading process happening in the Nord Pool day-ahead market,
although some variation in electricity price can’t be simulated, which is considered ac-
ceptable for the results that modelled by a simplified linear partial equilibrium model.
Most importantly, the cheapest resource is always scheduled first has been verified.

Limitation of the Study
The data used for investigating the effect of wind weather service is not wind speed, but
the energy that produced by wind turbines, collected from Nord Pool. This is because
the physical transformation from wind speed to wind energy is not the focus of the
study. However, the limitation is also brought into this research topic, as the variability
and uncertainty in wind energy cannot represent the total variability and uncertainty in
==PAGE:88==

wind speed.
The relationship between energy produced by wind turbines and wind speed are not
linear, and it is known that the relationship is usually characterised by the so-called
’power curve’ of the wind turbines or aggregated power curve for a bunch of wind turbines.
One power curve example is shown in Figure 7.2.
Figure 7.2: An simple illustration of wind power curve. Source: [24].
As Figure 7.2 shows, the wind power rises when wind speed is between cut-in speed
and rated output speed. When the wind speed is outside of this range, the wind power
is either equal to zero or a constant at nominal power [28]. Therefore, the variability
and uncertainty in wind speed cannot be totally represented by the uncertainty in wind
energy, because the turbines eliminate the variability in wind speed when it is higher
than rated output speed.
7.6
Effect of Uncertainties in Wind Power on
Danish Power Market
The variability and predictability are the two highly concerned topic in terms of wind
power forecasting. It is important to notice that the method we applied here actually
is discovering effect of the variability of the wind power on the power system respect to
==PAGE:89==
7.6 Effect of Uncertainties in Wind Power on Danish Power Market
73
electricity price and total generation cost rather than its predictability.
Before discussing variation of cost and electricity price, it is important to emphasise
that the weighting coefficient β controls the magnitude of uncertainty in wind predic-
tion through changing the standard deviation of the normal distributed error term, while
the mean value of the error term is not affected considerably. This also explains why
the average value of wind power prediction shows only a slight increase when β increases
dramatically, while the standard deviation of the wind power prediction varies notably.
This fact has also been discussed in the previous sections and proved by Table 6.4. The
effect of β on wind power prediction could also be evidently observed from the plots of
wind prediction with varying β given in Appendix Figure A.37 to Figure A.42.
The elaboration above is essential and helpful when analysing the following results, be-
cause the changes observed in the results hence are mainly caused by the variation of
standard deviation rather than the volume of wind production. In other words, the rea-
sons why the results changes with β is because of variability of wind production instead
of its penetration level.
The total fuel cost and CO2 emission cost over the year increased by 9.35% and 8.91%
in DK1, and 7.29% and 7.13% in DK2 when the uncertainty is changed by 10 times.
This increased cost leads to the rise of electricity price, and a visible increasing trend is
found in electricity price in both DK1 and DK2 presented in Figure 6.16.
Compared to the effect on the cost, the day-ahead electricity price per MWh feels less
sensitive to the variability of the wind generation, when the uncertainty is 10 times
larger, the electricity price increased by 1 euro per MWh, or 4%.
Considering the amplified level in the uncertainty and the increase in the average price,
the 1 euro growth in the average price seems minor, however when it comes together
with the electricity volume traded annually, the sum effect is large. For example, the
total electricity Denmark traded in Nord Pool day-ahead market is around 30 TWh
(calculated from Nord Pool historical data) in 2017. Multiplying this volume with 1
euro/MWh, it is 30 million euro, which becomes considerable amount.
In conclusion, the poorer quality of wind power forecasting has brought negative effect
respect to increased system operation and generation cost and higher electric price in
day-ahead power market.
In addition, as illustrated in Chapter 2, the balance responsible parties (BRPs) is the
one of the main players in power market and responsible for paying the imbalance cost.
If the variability in wind forecasting can be predicted precisely, then they may have more
confidence on the actual wind production in the short term. Therefore, the production
plan submits to the TSO can be as close to the actual wind production as possible.

For TSOs the costs of maintenance of security supply and risk management probably
increase when the variability in wind can not be forecasted properly. With higher vari-
ability, more reserve capacity and flexible response may be needed to maintain the
system frequency with the specific range.

CHAPTER 8
Future Work
A set of synthetic wind power predictions generator have been created by adopting the
historical actual wind productions to AR(2) and AR (3) models. In addition, another
set of net exchange generators are also established based on linear relation between it
and power production predictions. They could be further used as a powerful tool for gen-
erating the wind production and net exchange data without the limit of time for other
projects.
What’s more, the method of generating synthetic data for other resources
might be similar to the solution created for wind power in this study.
The scope of the current work could be expanded to a larger geographical range. For
example, the simulation in Balmorel model can be expanded to the whole Nordic area,
including Norway, Sweden and Finland. In this case, the effect of renewables of wind,
solar and hydro power could be investigated together, especially the hydropower is the
dominant contributor in Nordic power system.
For wind and solar the power potential can vary substantially within hours, while the
variation of hydropower production usually are discussed for a longer term. Therefore,
the added value long-term climates service including precipitation, temperature, wind
speed and solar radiation on the Nordic power system and market would be an extremely
appealing topic to work on.
Moreover, a benchmark research could be conducted beforehand on measuring the qual-
ity the existing climate service offered by public or private providers, for investigating
its effect on power system. Furthermore, the influence of climate climate change on the
climate service is also an important work to be further studied.
The benefit or losses for different stakeholders involved in electricity supplying chain,
like TSO, BRPs and retailers could be further discussed when it is possible to fore-
cast electricity price based on long-term climate service and information. For example,
in financial market, the traders might have more precise price signals for the forward
prices and contracts. Additionally, the day-ahead market price also send the signals for
investment decisions on renewable electricity productions [33].
