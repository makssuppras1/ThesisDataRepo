Abstract
The scope of this research is to explore the various implications of regarding a building as a build-
ing site. Several ideologies in the built environment currently suggest increased transformation and
reuse of buildings. A design methodology based on three principles (Paint, Mirror and Grow) is
presented in this thesis. The method allows for the development of potential design solutions that
supports the creation and maintaining of sustainable and regenerative communities. This research
contributes to the currently on-going re-development of a grain silo in Stavanger, Norway. The ini-
tiate to focus on this project was taken by Norwegian architectural oﬃce, Helen & Hard. Social and
technical design objectives are established for each principle of the method, and these are imple-
mented at an early stage of the design. They suggest continuous assessment, evaluation, mainte-
nance and improvement of the building design in the architectural process. A Public Participatory
Digital Model (PPDM) is constructed as a digital tool emphasising on participation early in the de-
sign process. The PPDM is based on Community Architecture Development Methodology (CADM).
The tool has been tested through information technologies, in a framework that addresses the re-
search question. The test is measured with regards to features that monitor the understanding and
communication of a design. The tool leaves a gap of design freedom for the architect to specify the
solution objectives. The users are allowed to choose design solutions and provide feedback. The ap-
proach of this project from a Participatory Design perspective gives insights into the challenges and
opportunities in the architectural design process. The project oﬀers new knowledge about how par-
ticipatory design techniques work. The combination of the principles of Regenerative Design, Open
building and Participatory Design, embedded within a single method supports the transformation
and reuse of buildings, and all the beneﬁts that come with it, by means of the right strategy and in-
formation technologies. This thesis challenges the way a building is conceived in the digital age and
builds upon the Tabula Rasa concept in architectural practise.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
In our studies at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU), we have been increasingly exposed
to the question of how to make the Architecture, Engineering, and Construction (AEC) industry
more eﬃcient and sustainable. There is a need for reuse of both the building materials and build-
ings themselves. Through several courses, at DTU the emerging discussion has also been a matter of
debate, and it has been concluded with 11526 - Interdisciplinary Design Project, an adaptive reuse
demonstrator with focus on the repurposing of an old warehouse building at Refshaleøen in Copen-
hagen. Refshaleøen is an old harbor for the Danish navy, where old torpedo halls, marine storage
buildings, and big warehouses now are being transformed into a vibrant urban area with a lot of
dwellings, art museums, detail furniture dealers, and world-class ﬁne-dining restaurants. During the
course, we faced the challenge of acquiring a complete 3D model of a building from various scan-
nings together with the process of design with an existing building as the starting point.
It comes the need to understand and ﬁnd an strategy on how to best use an existing structure from
various viewpoints. Here, aesthetics, statics, social and contextual aspects may be included, as well
as the compilation and proper use of the existing data, needed to meet the expected goal.
The Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) are a call for action by all countries. Stakeholders still
lack a shared interpretation on how the 17 SDGs can be operationalized (Sachs et al., 2020). Sup-
portive actions by governments, society, science, and business, will be required to achieve this tar-
get. Many sources claim that cities will need to have good coordination, planning, and management
of their resources, which includes the reuse and conservation of the built environment (Attia, 2018;
Naboni and Havinga, 2019). The approach that legislators, investors, entrepreneurs, architects, and
engineers take to this challenge is paramount to their success.
Naboni and Havinga (2019) and Otovic et al. (2016) call for the implementation of parametric de-
sign analysis early in the design process. Attia (2018) claims that the design process makes out 1%
of the life-cycle cost of a building project, and can reduce up to 90% of the life-cycle energy cost.
And in the early design phases, 20% of design decisions are taken, and they subsequently inﬂuence
80% of the later design decisions. A parametric approach early in the process allows for more ﬂex-
ibility and a better understanding of design options (Negendahl, 2019). Parametric design is an
evolving discipline, based on a digital design approach. The biggest strength is that it is compati-
ble with many diﬀerent aspects and provides a continuous solution space. Both authors of this the-
sis have experienced during their studies, the implementation of parametric design at the early de-
sign phase for sustainability purposes. Therefore, it could be said that there is a pure connection
between a parametric process and sustainability to reuse existing building structures.
A major player in the architectural design and process today is Life-Cycle Assessment (LCA), as it
provides a discrete way of comparing design proposals and assessing the material bank the build-
ing will constitute. Attia (2018) argues that LCA comparison is the highest cognitive level analysis
involving synthesis and evaluation.
In their 2016 report on the future of construction, the World Economic Forum assumes that inte-
grated BIM together with people will have the highest impact on the development (World Economic
Forum, 2016). This includes the digital design process, the evolved synthesis of people and technol-
ogy. Within the ideals of regenerative design and adaptive reuse, this thesis aims to explore the idea
of using an existing building as the building site in the building design process.
Re-imagining the existing industrial, religious, or military spaces for the regeneration of the urban
or rural area is a challenge today. It is generally unusual that an industrial building, a grain silo, or
a warehouse is re-purposed for dwellings.

1.2
Research question: Can a building function as a building site?
The building site in the AEC industry is considered a ﬂat plot of various sizes that a building may
be placed upon. The idea that the site is ﬂat and blank can be associated to the concept of Tabula
Rasa, a utopian blank slate upon which the site can be conceived ‘over again’. This idea originates
from the simplicity in designing on a blank sheet. Designing the building on a ﬂat blank sheet of
paper was never a necessity, but it simpliﬁed calculation and drawing procedures.
In the digital age, this is not necessarily the case. Buildings are both architecturally and struc-
turally designed in a digital medium, that does not sense any diﬀerences between a ﬂat or a com-
plex shape of the site.
For buildings, the idea would be to create a regenerative built environment that prioritizes reten-
tion and refurbishment over demolition and rebuilding. Community participation takes an impor-
tant role here, and it should occur in every single aspect of the design process. The implementation
of diﬀerent techniques should aim for the involvement of future users and people who use the sur-
rounding environment.
At a collaborative edge of design and social science, a shift in the design perspective has been no-
ticed, which goes from a user-centered to participatory experiences approach. ‘It is a shift in atti-
tude from designing for users to one of designing with users’ (B.-N.Sanders, 2002).
‘Each route to experience reveals a diﬀerent story. Listening to what people say, what they are able
to express’ (B.-N.Sanders, 2002). However, listening to what the community says or thinks is not
enough. To succeed in the task of regenerative design, a ﬂexible framework should be deﬁned, which
leaves the social context as a leading feature in a design process based on participation. New ways
of thinking and working are required, and the acceptance that all people have something to oﬀer to
the design process when the right tools are oﬀered.
The implications of this approach have not been deeply explored and are not a common practice
today. Therefore it seems of interest to research and study how to combine the existing theories and
ideologies to come up with a method that gives rise to a better understanding. Is it possible to ﬁnd
a way of having a building designed for continuous change, community participation and adaption
to the needs of this versatile and changing community? Can we create a time-line design process for
the existing building, so that it lasts as long as possible?

The architectural heritage challenged by a lack of resources
Københavns Kommune approved a bill in 2019 with the requirement that all new buildings must
take into consideration the possibility of materials reuse. This could be connected to an increas-
ing trend in construction, the assessment of building materials as a way for environment protection
and sustainable development. Like the oil industry, which already has been given a time of death
in Denmark, we could ask ourselves if there could come a time when new constructions would be a
concept of the past. Reuse and the change of existing structures to new purposes could be the only
way to move forward. The use of resources and area constraints may cause this new paradigm. In
addition to this the world will experience growth in urbanization with 1 million people moving to
cities every week for the next ﬁfteen years, pointed out by architect Alejandro Aravena in his 2014
TED Talk. The task seems huge. Today, when a building is analyzed for possible reuse or demoli-
tion, economic considerations have the highest weight (Sørensen and Oberender, 2019). In both fu-
ture scenarios, this could be argued to be the sub-optimal approach. The future is certainly a com-
bination of these two, and we must be prepared as we are approaching it.
The population expansion calls for resources to support the basics of life and increasing standards
of living. Energy, minerals, water among others, are critical for human existence and are connected
to the context of sustainability (Thompson et al., 2018). The availability of resources for future gen-
erations may come with eﬀorts to improve the eﬃciency of processes and increase the need for re-
cycling and upcycling. To succeed, it seems important to fully understand the environment that
surrounds us, from the critical processes that focus on resources to the environment and climate
that support life (Thompson et al., 2018). In parallel to this, there emerges a need to engage society
through collaboration and intergration. Both needs should be fully understood, from the human and
the context perspective. Also, the strategy to follow should be planned and informed in the right
way, and the gained knowledge should be expanded to support the upcoming generations.
New ways of conserving the design practice should be considered in response to the replacement of
completely new constructions. Relevant theories are increasingly coming up such as regenerative
design, adaptive reuse of architectural heritage, among others; but it comes the time to take ac-
tion against the resource scarcity, while responding to the needs of the user and context. New ap-
proaches are required and despite the scarcity of means, what is available should be intensiﬁed. By
combining the modern practice with the need to reuse, the research question of this project emerges:
Can a building function as a building site?
The AEC industry strongly aﬀects the economy, the environment, and society as a whole. It touches

need for aﬀordable housing and social, transportation, and utility infrastructure.

2.2
The architectural design process in the digital age
The architectural design process is today a cyclic, integrated and interdisciplinary process. Digital
technologies are radically changing how buildings are conceived, designed, and produced (Kolaveric,
2003). Wind, solar and geotechnical conditions are usually analyzed and considered before moving
forward. Thoughts, ideas, and concepts are tested through several digital media. By using drone-
scanning, laser-scanning, and digital 3D-modelling tools an existing building can be visualized through
many perspectives and be further imagined within the four sides of a screen. This is in some compa-
nies standard practice today. Insight by project supervisor Ane S. Dahl, from Helen & Hard Archi-
tects, 2021.
BIM (Building Information Modeling) is an intelligent, model-based process that connects AEC
professionals and they can more eﬃciently design, build, and operate buildings and infrastructures
(Chinnapandian and Babaei, 2020). It is used by AEC professionals as a collaborative software tool,
which enables to create a complete digital description of a building project, from the planning to de-
sign and construction of buildings. It is more than a simple 3D-visual programming software as it
updates the model to reﬂect any change from one discipline to the other in real time.
BIM emerged along with the digital revolution and has been expanding in an equally rapid pace.
The classiﬁcation, according to the NBS (National Building Speciﬁcation in the UK), of this ex-
pansion is termed levels. Level 0 being simple in today’s standards CAD (Computer Aided Design)
software. Level 1 BIM being the ﬁrst emerging tools for 3D modelling, Level 2 is ending approxi-
mately where we are now, and Level 3 is the intelligent near-future BIM where high-degrees of shar-
ing, collaboration and interdisciplinary work occurs. It is characterized by open data and common
frameworks. However it has not yet been fully deﬁned (The NBS, 2021). As BIM is becoming the
industry’s standard way of working, many new possibilities open up. The implementation of site-
speciﬁc conditions, indoor climate analysis, and working in a non-linear clustered process is possible
within BIM. There is a general call-out for providing as much information as early as possible in the
design process (Grobman et al., 2010; Otovic et al., 2016).
The design process in architecture is something that is characteristic to each architect and does not
follow a rule-oriented timeline. There are inﬁnitely many variations and approaches. However, some
general common stages and assessments can be distinguished. The design process starts in many
cases long before the building is built and ﬁnishes when the building is demolished. As an example,
the Parthenon in Athens, Greece, was built between 447 and 438 BCE, however, ornamentation and
detail construction continued to 432 BCE. Even today, maintenance and repairs are taking place
and various events are being held in the building. The design process has been ongoing for approxi-
mately 2,500 years. In the late 20th century repairs were taking place, and through the use of com-
puter aided design and digital models, it was possible to detect older repairs that were misplaced.
The structure was reﬁtted with iron H-pins to strengthen the construction. Within short time after
ﬁtting the iron eroded, creating rust, which is expansive, and therefore cracked the marble.

Regenerative Design
The term regenerative design originates in agriculture and was popularly deﬁned by John T. Lyle
in Regenerative Design for Sustainable Development in 1994. Lyle is a landscape architect who at-
tempts to make a universal sustainable design theory, drawing beyond the Newtonian world-view
and taking inspiration from Einstein, Heisenberg and the GAIA hypothesis among several concepts
(Cole and Robinson, 2015).
Cole and Robinson (2015) builds on Lyle’s deﬁnition, among others, and states that:
Regenerative design is rooted in the natural world in that it tries to mimic the way dif-
ferent species and organisms in a landscape interact and functions together to pursue
and sustain healthy lives. It is an approach that supports the co-evolution of humans
and natural systems in a partnered relationship.
Cole and Robinson (2015)
It is not the building that is ‘regenerated’ in the same sense as the self healing and self-organizing
attributes of a living system; it is about the ways that the act of building can be a catalyst for posi-
tive change within and added value to the unique ‘place’ in which it is situated.
Within regenerative development and design, built projects, stakeholder processes and inhabitation
are therefore collectively focused on enhancing life in all its manifestations (human, other species,
ecological systems) through an enduring responsibility of stewardship. Regenerative approaches are
thus fundamentally about rethinking the role of buildings, the types of questions asked during the
design process, who is the subject asked, and how the discussion is guided.
In their outline Cole and Robinson (2015) makes a distinction between regenerative design and de-
velopment and regenerative sustainability. These are diﬀerent because they build on diﬀerent philo-
sophical foundations. While regenerative development and design is founded on an ecological foun-
dation where absolute truths exists, regenerative sustainability is founded on the idea that truths
are a concept of a constructed reality. Therefore, a solution space exists within regenerative sus-
tainability that does not exist in regenerative development and design. The main diﬀerences can be
seen in Table 1. In our case, it would be more appropriate to talk about regenerative sustainability
due to the fact that the philosophical foundations correspond better to regenerative sustainability.

of its components and the resources it consumed to be built, to perform and to function.

Open Building
The concept of Open Building Architecture, invented by N. John Habraken, has been considered as a
relevant and crucial approach to design, in order to increase the variety, ﬂexibility and quality of
space. John Habraken advocates a clear distinction of the support and the inﬁll and emphasizes
that this is not only technical in nature, but more importantly focused on the ability to facilitate
personal inﬂuence. The supports are part of the public domain and permanent, while the inﬁll be-
longs to the individual owner/tenant and is changeable. Participation and freedom of choice for the
user is the key objective. Open Building redesigns ownership scenarios, (design-)responsibilities and
building engineering. It oﬀers participation and co-creation of inhabitants and users, on the private
(Habraken, 1961). Habraken considered the power the architect has over a design in relation to the
client, which he characterized on three diﬀerent levels. The tissue level being the municipality, leg-
islators and planning process, the support level being the owner of the building block and the inﬁll
level being the actual tenants (˚Agren and Wing, 2014). Habraken stated that:
The uncertainty of the future itself must be the basis on which present decisions are
taken.
Habraken (1972)
Therefore, he argued, the reallocation of power is necessary. It seems important to consider the indi-
vidual freedom within the community. And how, thus, with respect to regenerative design, buildings
and people grow together. Open building methodology also emphasises the need of variety within
the rules. This may refer to the rules of the building regulations, the structural system or the site.
It is important to consider the life-span of structures and systems and to adapt for them to work in
an ideal way. A system with a lifespan of 20 years should not be locked in a in a structure with a
life span of 200 years. This all boils down to considering the architects participation within the built
environment and who holds the control over the diﬀerent building elements. We should consider the
legislative authorities to have power over regulations and sites, the architect over the built form and
function and the inhabitant over the interior use and function of the building.

Adaptive Reuse
The concept of adaptive reuse is increasingly becoming a buzz-term describing development and
reuse of buildings. A common deﬁnition comes from Joachim (2011), and claims that adaptive reuse
is the development of a building preserving its historic features. In other words it describes change
within the boundaries of architectural heritage. Joachim (2011) goes on and explains that due to
environmental issues the term can be widened and its ideology can be used in all kinds of buildings.
It is not clear where he lies the line between buildings of historic signiﬁcance and other buildings,
however he is clear that adaptive reuse can be applied on all kinds of buildings.
In a thesis from Aalborg University Nichols (2020), while spending 10 pages on deﬁning adaptive
reuse, concludes that it is a meeting point of architecture, development, interior design, conserva-
tion and planning, and it is driven and challenged by economic, environmental, cultural, social and
political factors.
Although it is hard to deﬁne precisely, adaptive reuse seems to refer to the process of reusing an ex-
isting building for a purpose other than which it was originally built and designed for. It may be
referred as well with words such as renovation, retroﬁtting, conversion, adaptation, reworking, reha-
bilitation, historic preservation or refurbishment. Adaptive reuse is considered to be an interesting
theory, as many projects have been labelled with this term and beneﬁcial procedures for assessing
historic value and how-to-proceed methods have been developed.
These methods are relevant to this project, bearing in mind the need of transformation, preserv-
ing the architectural heritage, preventing the lack of resources and preparation of a terrain for the
upcoming future. The focus on preserving architectural heritage contains a consideration of the ex-
isting building mass. So to speak, the use of the building as the building site in the transformation
process.
Buthke et al. (2020) suggests a step by step design process for adaptive reuse. The steps can be
summarized as follows:
1. Analysis of the cultural value of the existing architecture
2. 3D scanning of both the exterior and the interior of the existing architecture
3. Select elements that need transformation and create 3D models for further design
4. Compare new program with architectural context to place main functions and technical instal-
lations
5. Analyse strategies for realizing transformation with modular systems
6. Prepare the building for ﬁtting of modular systems simultaneously with fabrication of the sys-
tem
7. Fitting the system to place

Sustainable Development
Sustainable development may on ﬁrst glance seem to be strongly connected to regenerative devel-
opment and design and regenerative sustainability. However, this is a much older term that was de-
ﬁned between 1983 and 1987 by the UN appointed Brundtland Commision. It has its foundation in
international politics and economics (Brundtland, 1991).
The UN Brundtland Commision (1983-1987) deﬁnes Sustainable Development (SD) as:

Life-Cycle Assessment
Life-Cycle Assessment (LCA) is a structured, comprehensive and internationally standardised method
to quantify and assess the environment impact of a goods or service ‘products’. It quantiﬁes all rel-
evant emissions and resource consumption and the related environmental and health impacts and
resource depletion issues that are associated with any product (Hauschild et al., 2018). LCA is com-
posed of four main stages and that are governed by two ISO standards, 14040 and 14044. The four
stages are: Goal and Scope, Life Cycle Inventory, Life Cycle Impact Assessment and Results Inter-
pretation (Hauschild et al., 2018).
11
==PAGE:22==
2
BACKGROUND
For various products a selection of parts of a LCA will be used, and the assessment period will diﬀer
from one to the other. Common periods of assessment are ‘cradle-to-grave’ and ‘cradle-to-gate’. The
goal and scope of the LCA deﬁnes the functional unit and system boundaries for the assessment.
The functional unit describes speciﬁcally what product, to what use and for how long the assess-
ment will determine the environmental impact. The system boundaries decide the unitary elements
included in the assessment (Cavalliere et al., 2020).
Hollberg (2020) states that LCA is considered a time-consuming and complex procedure and is
therefore not common practise at an early design stage today. Cavalliere et al. (2020) argues that
this is due to the diﬃculties in getting speciﬁc input-data in early stages of the design process. Ne-
gendahl (2019); Hollberg (2020); Cavalliere et al. (2020) all argue that incorporating a simpliﬁed
LCA tool into design software will help minimize the eﬀort due to LCA. The LCA impact results
can then be visualized early in the design process.
BIM assists integrated design and contributes to data management and collaboration between the
diﬀerent parties involved in the project. BIM has been also used to investigate diﬀerent options in
regards to materials production, delivery, and construction methods, with the aim of reducing GHG
emissions (Giesekam et al., 2014). The most important eﬀect of LCA is to inform towards a positive
change. LCA can be completely performed only in the later design phases as the full information is
available (Cavalliere et al., 2019). At this stage, however, it is very costly to make any changes.
As Negendahl (2019) deﬁnes it:
LCA should be used to inform and then change design rather than to assess the ﬁnal
solution of design.
The way this plays out is according to Cavalliere et al. (2020):
Using LCA analysis in the early stages of the design process, it is possible to direct the
designer to make choices aimed for improving the environmental performance of build-
ings in order to compare, in real time, diﬀerent technological and geometric solutions. In
addition, the use of LCA analysis in the preliminary design phase leads to a reduction in
the future costs related to possible design variants.
Together with these external limitations there are internal limitations to the use of LCA, especially
when concerning reuse of buildings and materials. Attia (2018) found that assessing the recyclable
materials was diﬃcult and therefore, conﬁdent LCA results were not able to be produced. This may
be connected to Wolf (2019)’s concern that LCA has an in-built limitation to assess reuse of build-
ings. A LCA is dependent on its functional unit which, as the name suggests, is unitary. Determin-
ing how many life-cycles a material can be recycled is not possible and therefore a precise LCA is
not either.
Another issue with reuse emerges in assessing double counting. A LCA for a new construction can
have positive impact both from using recycled components, as well as the old building having posi-
tive impacts for the same components. Where this impact should be allocated is not clear yet (Wolf,
2019). Wolf (2019) accompanies Negendahl (2019); Hollberg (2020); Cavalliere et al. (2020) in call-
ing out for an approach where a simpliﬁed LCA is combined with parametric design inside a BIM
paradigm. As LCA is dependent on its parameters, it could be suggested that a parametric ap-
proach may be suitable in this assessment, in order to investigate the environmental performance
of a building element or the whole building.

Participatory Design
Participatory Design (PD) is a complex concept that has raised awareness in the last years. It is
an evolving practice among design professionals and a research ﬁeld that includes both the design-
ers and the practitioners in the design process (Kensing and Blomberg, 1998). It is deﬁned as the
model of direct involvement in the design of diﬀerent social groups, from functional tools to environ-
ments, social, institutions and businesses (Mahabadi et al., 2014).
Community awareness emerged in the early 1960s in several poor neighborhoods. A new movement
started with the direct involvement of the people in regards to the deﬁnition of their physical envi-
ronment and an increased feeling of power and social responsibility. This movement was followed by
the creation of several community design centers (CDCs) in the United States. These aimed for a
planning and design that gave the low-income communities the right to decide and put into practice
their own planning targets according to their needs (Sanoﬀ, 2006).
In addition, the American professor Paul Davidoﬀpresented in the 1960’s Advocacy planning (AP),
where he suggested that planners ‘should also concern themselves with social justice and not merely
be a technician, but a servant for pluralism’. It was highlighted that planners should look for the
representation of the interests and needs from a variety of social groups. Following him, several de-
sign and planning professionals refused the traditional way and fought for urban redevelopment and
the rights of citizens, and started developing methods based on citizen participation. (Sanoﬀ, 2006)
Federal programs of the 1960s started involving and encouraging the citizen participation in pro-
grams for improvement, where non-professionals were allowed to decide and give opinions about
planning and ﬁnancing. The right to participate in planning and implementation processes was
given through grants and technical assistance. (Sanoﬀ, 2006)

Regarding the built environment, Henry Sanoﬀseems to be the pioneer and leader in the develop-

Following the deﬁnition from Sanoﬀ(2010):
Participatory Design is an attitude about a force for change in the creation and manage-

traditional professional boundaries and cultures. Its roots lie in the ideals of participa-

Sanoﬀparticipated in the development of multiple innovative participatory design tools, methods
and games (Sanoﬀ, 2000) and other publications that strengthen practical and theoretical real project
insights. Since today, these have an inﬂuence in architecture and environmental design research
and bring tangible changes in communities through built environment projects (Hartson and Pyla,
2019).
In Europe several well known architects incorporated the ideals of participatory design in their prac-
tices. With Supports in 1961, John Habraken proposed the method that divided the ‘support’ base
building from the ‘ﬁt out’, which allowed the users to participate and have an impact in the design
of their homes. In fact, in one of his publications, Henry Sanoﬀdescribed a new professional role
for architects in participatory design, ‘where the professional is not redundant but is less dominant’
(Habraken, 2007).
Clement and den Besselaar (1993) reiterates three basic requirements for participation, already
highlighted by (Kensing and Blomberg, 1998), and presents two additional ones. These are based
on a revision of ten PD projects in the 1970’s and the 1980’s:
1. Access to relevant information
2. Possibility for taking an independent position on the problems
3. Participation in decision making
4. Availability of appropriate participatory development methods
5. Room for alternative technical and/or organizational arrangements
One of the preconditions for a successful design as deﬁned by (Kensing and Blomberg, 1998), is to
include participation of all users involved in the technology process. This includes the incorporation
of skills, experiences, and interests of all kind of users in the design system. This way it is possible
to ensure that the system will be useful and well integrated into the work practice.
It is also conﬁrmed by (Kensing and Blomberg, 1998) that researchers in the PD ﬁeld suggest that
design professionals need knowledge of the technology tools use context, while users need knowledge
of the possible technological options. The kind of knowledge needed from both sides is eﬀectively
developed through active collaboration and dialogue between the designers and involved parties.
This should be a continuous dialogue, as technology evolves very fast in time.
There is a strong connection between the architectural practice, participatory design and practice-
based research. Until today, a rising number of architects and urban planners are including par-
ticipatory design as a crucial step in their projects, where the citizen and community engagement
become the heart of the design process (Think Wood, 2021).

Methodology
As the subject of our thesis started to be shaped in early December 2020, it quickly became clear
that the target was to make a theoretical research-based thesis, connected to a relevant topic in the
building industry. This was settled to be the investigation on how to use an existing building as the
building site. The connection between industry and academia is nuanced on this speciﬁc subject
and sometimes diﬀused. Therefore, a need exists for clariﬁcation between the two institutions. De-
sign Science Research is a research and design methodology that lays emphasis on the applicability,
relevance and communication of research, and thus connects academia and industry. This immedi-
ately stood out as an appropriate methodology for this thesis.
Design Science Research (DSR) is a methodological approach concerned with devising artefacts that
serve human purposes. It is a form of scientiﬁc knowledge production that involves the development
of innovative constructions, intended to solve problems faced in the real world, and simultaneously
makes a kind of prescriptive scientiﬁc contribution (Dersch et al., 2015). It is a relatively new ap-
proach to research with the goal to construct a new reality instead of explaining an existing one
(Iivari and Venable, 2009).
According to Dersch et al. (2015) the DSR process includes seven criteria:
1
Design as artefact
Research developed with the design science research method must pro-
duce viable artefacts in the form of a construct, model, method or in-
stantiation
2
Problem Relevance
The purpose of design science research is to develop solutions to solve
important and relevant problems for organizations
3
Design Evaluation
The utility, quality and eﬃcacy of the artefact must be rigorously
demonstrated via well-executed evaluation methods
4
Research Contribu-
tion
Research conducted by the design science research method must pro-
vide clear and veriﬁable contributions in the speciﬁc areas of the devel-
oped artefacts and present clear grounding on the foundations of design
and/or design methodologies
5
Research Rigor
Research should be based on an application of rigorous methods in both
the construction and the evaluation of artefacts
6
Design as a re-
search process
The search for an eﬀective artefact requires the use of means that are
available to achieve the desired purposes, while satisfying the laws gov-
erning the environment in which the problem is being studied
7
Communication of
the research
Research conducted by design science research must be presented to
both an audience that is more technology-oriented and one that is more
management-oriented
The result of DSR is always an artefact (a construct, model, method or instantiation) that enables a
satisfactory solution intended to have a well-founded root in practise. The evaluation of the artefact
and the communication of results are crucial to the method.

DSR is used as a tool and model guidance for the development of a new conceptual method for the
reuse of buildings. This will be the artefact of this research. The method should be considered be-
fore the already predeﬁned key stages of the early design process. The primary intention is to in-
clude the key and necessary elements in the the early design stage in order to succeed in the archi-
tectural design.

4
Design Process
The method presented in this chapter, emerges from the research question and the study of the
state of the art. The combination of selected aspects from various topics, provides a conceptual
framework, an alternative method, that embodies many of the leading themes from the terms de-
ﬁned in chapter 2.
The main ingredient in the method is the combination of 3 principles. These principles have been
formulated from the research question in a way that supports the theoretical background. The method,
drawing on Hollberg et al. (2021), suggests a 3-part division of the design process: The conceptual
design phase, the detailed design phase and the use phase. This is an established and commonly
used approach. Within these stages 3 principles to guide the process are proposed. The principles
have inspiration from the reports we have studied for this thesis and aim to make the design process
of development projects more holistic and inclusive. Albeit being ‘ﬂuﬀy’ terms they have a discrete
meaning and reasoning within the design process, followed by speciﬁc tools.
Starting this thesis we studied several example projects of silo development around the world. We
could extract some common features of these projects. We were also speciﬁcally looking for projects
that had a high degree of reuse. In the examples we could ﬁnd, the most successful ones (this being
our own assessment and the praise received in online magazines and newspapers) used the spaces

3. Grow - growing is the ﬁnal action considered as well as most abstract principle proposed. It

4.1
The building as a building site
When regarding an existing building as a building site there are several implications that follow,
which will unfold themselves as rules of the site. In a traditional project typography will have to be
either ﬂattened or built up from and in relation to. Following from the Tabula Rasa pre-condition
of architectural and structural design. By assuming the conservation value of the Mirror action, it
is suggested to build around the envelope as much as possible. While in a traditional project we
are given a site and have to move the pieces within the site, now it is our pieces that need to be
moved within the building. The site cannot be moved, as the organism already exists. This comes
with some challenges that need to be considered and faced prior to any action.
The application of the Grow action suggests that we make carefully thought-out moves that do
not hinder future development or change. We should not make it harder for the ones that come af-
ter us. By further implications of the typography parallel, wind, sun and geo-conditions, must be
worked out from the building’s existing properties and further development accordingly. By apply-
ing the Paint and Mirror principles, the surroundings and context will be taken into account. This
could make the transformation extent smaller and further enhance the neighbourhood connection.
The built environment, and more speciﬁcally the building site, should be viewed as an artifact that
is never ﬁnished, grounded in convention, and that is the responsibility of non-professionals who
should take over with care and cultivation when professionals have completed their work (Kendall,
1999).
Coming back to the steel-rimmed spectacles, through these the wearer observes the building as build-
ing site and therefore gets some new interesting perspectives. In regenerative design, the distinctive
connection to a place or a site is important. When talking about the building taking the role of the
building site, it is suggested that the entire building assumes this position.
The building site has its shape and form from engineering and architecture purposes. There is no
need of a shape or a form in the digital age, as we can make designs and calculations around the
site without it being ﬂat and empty. Another important, result from looking through our steel-
rimmed spectacles is the constant nature that the building assumes. Through the design process the
site is one out of few elements that remain truly constant. This should be embodied by the method
itself and be prepared for.
Many architectural games start with the ﬁrst idea generation and conceptual development by as-
suming the drawing of the concept or idea on a blank piece of paper. This is hard if the blank build-
ing site is an already complete building. Therefore, the steel-rimmed spectacles should be used in
a digital environment. This digital approach requires the building which shall be taken into view
through a steel-rimmed pair of spectacles as a digital model which can represent the ideas and con-
cepts proposed in an appropriate manner. Thus a ﬂexible pair of spectacles on a computer environ-
ment.
The generation of a 3D-model is therefore one of the ﬁrst requirements that must be solved for the
method to start. This should be done in accordance with the needs that is represented. This may
be the wish to do a daylight analysis, an investigation of alternative geometry, context analysis and
the like. The generation of a digital model can be done from direct measurements, a 3D scanning
of the building space or from drawings of the building. Diﬀerent methods may be better for diﬀer-

Method foundation
The theoretical terms deﬁned in chapter 2 have various underlying implications on the method, re-
sulting in several interesting implication chains. By following this conceptual framework, the method
is formed such that these implication chains are valid. Connected here there might be some degree
of uncertainty, as the chain would not be valid if any step is omitted or altered. This justiﬁcation
will be outlined in chapter 5. There are two implication chains that should be mentioned, as by
highlighting their importance and possible eﬀect, they should be followed in the method. This is
a major player in connecting the theory and practise which, as stated in chapter 3, is a huge part
of the DSR methodology. And the ultimate aim of our method, to connect ‘ﬂuﬀy’ terms to actual
building performance indicators.
By investigating the implication chain of our method, the following can be derived:
Proposed method =⇒Regenerative design =⇒Sustainable development
=⇒Fulﬁllment of user needs
So we could set as a solution objective for our proposal that the method should produce a design
that fulﬁlls user needs. However this becomes vague quickly when considering many inherently dif-
ferent users. Some justiﬁcation towards the wide variation within user needs is required.
Although the above directly follows from the deﬁned terms, there is a high degree of uncertainty
connected within the terms themselves and the broad application scope of the method. Further in-
vestigation on this topic will follow in chapter 6.
Another implication of the method is the embodiment of the LCA within the method itself. Here
another implication chain is active.
Proposed method =⇒Shady Attia’s (Attia, 2018) regenerative design method
=⇒Sustainable material choices in all building elements
=⇒Satisfactory LCA results
Attia (2018) proposes a method that suggests that the building, which is to be designed, should be
regarded as three separate parts. The structure system, the design elements, and the building ma-
terials and products. These three bins will cover the building in its entirety and thus ensure that
every element will have a good LCA score in itself.
Our method does not state the construction system, nor a speciﬁc focus on design elements or mate-
rials. By using the building as a building site a choice of construction system is not required. With
this statement, every construction system will include some value of embodied carbon, therefore
providing a positive LCA score. As this score is not aﬀected, at least not in a negative way, the
implication holds. By utilizing the Grow action the design elements and building materials should
be chosen in such a way, that allows for easy expansion without compromising the existing organ-
ism and the future needs. The concerns stated in section 2.7 remains relevant here. As well as the
uncertainties mentioned for the former implication chain. The representation of these principles
through these chains may not therefore be the ideal way of reviewing this issue.

4.3
Method principles
The three principles embody the building site, community, and context, as a whole within three ac-
tions. They aim for the maximum building potential and the recovery of its essence, which in many
cases could be lost in the design process. They allow for a better understanding of the relationship
between the design intention and the building performance itself. They are a way of justiﬁcation of
every decision in the design process and they allow for a longer lifespan of the building.
By combining the three actions a new design may appear, which intends to answer the research
question:
Can a building function as a building site? Could the building be designed in a way that
allows for permanent change? This is the target of the actions combined, which already includes the
beneﬁt of the community. And in this way the actions become design principles to be followed in a
methodological sense.
These principles should force designers to think about the sustainability and quality of all the ma-
terials. And more importantly, what the essential materials are. This responds to the current needs
of the building and its future life. The three principles lead to Attias approach and together they
aim for longevity, conservation, and healthy neighborhoods, which give rise to improved sustainable
communities. And the conservation of our common architectural heritage.
4.3.1
Paint
The process of blending something into its environment. Both the process of hiding and enhanc-
ing architecture in one action. The action of painting is regarded as a process that aims to make
something more beautiful and at the same time making it look something familiar to the viewer. In
architecture, generally, these two actions are two sides of the same coin.
Paint is the social and contextual connective action. It makes the foundation for the three actions in
the beginning, and it connects Mirror and Grow in the end.
The main goals the Paint action aims to achieve are:
• Adapt the design to the place. Ensure continuity in the area and a design that is not out of
context.
• Prevent alienation towards the design through interaction, collaboration and participatory
processes.
• Preserve the unique artistic qualities of the place.
• Preserve the history and existing essence of the place.
• Shape identity in the area, encouraging a longer life-span of the building.
The Paint action applies its eﬀect by understanding the meaning of architectural quality. Participa-
tory processes takes an important role here, needed to promote interdisciplinary and cross-stakeholder
collaboration in the design. Paint directly suggests participatory design.

4.3.2
Mirror
The idea that the building should be mirrored, as it is through an imaginary mirror of Erised. To
keep the best and most useful parts of the existing architecture, while renewing the ones in need
of renewal. Visualizing the building in its best version through a mirror, satisfying the buildings
deepest desires.
The main goals the Mirror action aims to achieve are:
• Maximize the building’s potential: The building site oﬀers spaces that could have very good
potential if utilized in the right way, and give rise to added value on site.
• Further enhance architectural quality, assessed from a participatory perspective.
• Investigate diﬀerent uses for the building, with regards to environmental and social positive
outcomes.
• Keep as much structure as possible and decrease the need for new elements in the future.
• Evaluate the preservation of existing elements and analyse the potential for conservation.
• Assess the requirements for avoiding museumiﬁcation of the building.
The Mirror action applies its eﬀect by understanding the building as it is. This means to evaluate
the building and using it as a platform to further development. Mirror therefore is assessing the
building on its own and directly. Assessment of existing elements is required. Mirror suggests a
preservation assessment and an analysis of the ratio of preserved elements. Preservation does not
compromise further development and later additions to the structure.
4.3.3
Grow
The investigation on how to make the building a self-renewing, growing entity. Designing systems
so that they can be replaced by future systems without compromising other systems. Material and
component choices that have a positive-outcome reasoning and adaptable properties.
The goals of the Grow action are:
• Design the building for the present and future generations.
• Allow for the structural system to reach its maximum life-span.
• Allow for adaptable and changeable spaces.
• Building systems evaluation and separation.
• Sustainable and ﬂexible material choice.
The Grow action applies its eﬀect by connecting functions, spaces and layout. It suggests a conﬁ-
dent strategy on how the building can achieve its maximum life span and allow for the change of
program. The task aims to design an open building system that has high ﬂexibility and easy adap-
tion of the spaces and systems.
The future is unknown for all of us. The key is to design the building elements, spaces, and layout
to be changeable, adaptable, ﬂexible, being able to adapt for a pandemic, utopian, dystopian, or any
other kind of upcoming future.
This action addresses architecture’s intrinsic material and aesthetic foundations to envision tomor-
row’s built environment. By thinking of strategic modes of operation, the architectural design be-
comes an opportunistic and value-added approach for the inhabited physical environment.

4.6
Design Process Features
In order to assume that the proposed method will be applicable in the majority of renovation cases
and to ensure an easy application, the concept of Occam’s Razor has been applied to all parts of the
method design. Occam’s Razor is the idea that a scientiﬁc method or theory should be shaved down
only to its essential parts. So that there is no repetition or use of unnecessary procedures.
The method therefore does not assume speciﬁc software or tools to achieve a solution, but empha-
sises the use of the appropriate tools. With this purpose, careful attention has been paid to the way
the method is communicated and translated through the design process, same as for the artefacts
that come out of it. To do this in the best possible way, the test of the method in a real case study,
presented in chapter 5, will serve to do an empirical study and ﬁnd the best way to implement and
communicate the design method. To make this possible, the right features that characterize the ef-
ﬁcient communication will be explored and presented. These should have a direct connection to the
principles and aim for community inclusion and interaction.
For the method to be operable within information technologies in a regenerative design setting, its
principles and values need to communicate the design across all disciplines and clients (community,
owner and municipality) in a simple and understandable manner. The involved parties must have
the possibility to give comments, in an agile and easy way, and communicate any issues, which will
reﬂect into the design features themselves. The level of detail given must correspond to the solution
objectives of the dialogue. The involved parties should be able to follow the design and comment
on the correct level as well. These are features that could be implemented on an interdisciplinary
workﬂow today, and from examples seen in the built-environment we believe some have already been
present in the built environment projects.

The history
In the 1950s the ﬁrst big ships, specially designed for carrying vast amounts of grain were designed
and launched. These so-called bulkcarries were in the range of 30,000 - 40,000 tons. Before this, the
grain was shipped by smaller vessels at 6,000 - 8,000 tons, that were used for several diﬀerent pur-
poses and therefore could land at smaller ports. After the second world war, as most of Europe
laid in ruins, Norway was importing most of the grain used for food from USA and Canada and
was suddenly in the need of port that could host these large new ships. As well, almost simultane-
ously the production of animal fodder was increasing rapidly in Norway and a storage was needed.
Stavanger lies on the south west coast of Norway with good sea connections to many places in the
world, the harbour is ice-free all year, which is not common in Norway and there was a big mill

Havnesilo B is located on the east side of the city of Stavanger in Norway, and it is part of the city’s
architectural heritage. Stavanger is the hub for most of the petroleum companies operating in the
Norwegian part of the North Sea. It is located by the southernmost of Norway’s large fjords and
oﬀers spectacular natural scenerios. Stavanger’s population has been in rapid growth since the dis-
covery of oil on Norwegian soil in 1969 and the city has a strong economy and a high standard of
living. During the United Nations’ Architectural Heritage Year in 1975, Stavanger was pointed out
by UNESCO as one of the cities worth preserving in Norway (Emporis, Stavanger Building informa-

the best preserved wooden house area in Northern Europe is located close to the area in question
and has similar architecture.
Havnesilo B is currently surrounded by two additional silos, the mill and storage buildings con-
nected to the silos, and otherwise mainly wooden single family houses. The neighbouring area oﬀers
a variety of parks and bicycle trails. Downtown Stavanger is centered around Breiavatnet, a small
lake with a jet fountain. Nearby is an old shopping area on a peninsula. Also, many galleries, handi-
crafts’ boutiques and sauna facilities are found in the area.
The city embraces several diﬀerent kinds of houses. The majority correspond to wooden housing
from Stavanger’s settlements prior to the Second World War. In the old days, it was a common
thing to bring the house if you moved from one place to another. The houses are constructed with
lafted timber to allow for easy disassembly and transportation when moving. This is the case for
many of the houses in Gamle Stavanger. This way of thinking from the previous times encourages
to the future generations to undertake the same task and have the similar mentality. Moreover,
when it comes to the house aesthetic, an arch shape on top of the roof can be found in many houses.
It is referred to as the Stavanger arch and was built in the time to expand the accommodations (Re-
gion Stavanger, 2021).

6.1
Introduction to the PPDM
The Public Participation Digital Model (PPDM) is a digital data interface that intends to support
participatory design and the development of improved architectural systems. The tool aims to be
used for analysis and to explore a new perspective in the architectural design.
Participatory design together with the knowledge in the ﬁeld of architecture and engineering, aim
to be the foundation of the needs an existing building, and the deﬁnition of the potential for the
building transformation.
Based on the preliminary study of the area and relevant data input presented in chapter 5, where
the inhabitants of Stavanger gave comments on what they were missing from the neighbour area, se-
lected focus parameters to consider in the design proposal have been listed. These are the base of an
open dialogue, that involves the eﬃcient communication between the designer and the community,
primary objective of the PPDM.
Very much similar to the evolving building design, the form and content of this tool has changed as
we kept ourselves open to new possibilities. This has been possible by allowing selected expert users
to test and react to the tool as it has evolved. Their comments and opinions have been part of a
trial and error process to reshape and improve the participatory tool. The improvement of the tool
happens in speciﬁc focus areas in the own performance of the tool, also referred as solution objec-
tives. For example on whether the user can provide active feedback, or if the building is recogniz-
able from real-life in the digital model. The solution objectives will redeﬁne and measure relevant
criteria for the tool and will be evaluated based on a simulation in an ideal case scenario. The tool
performance addresses the representation of selected building elements, as well as the interaction
that govern the chosen building elements with the tool user. The performance of the tool will dic-
tate if further improvement is needed. This allows for a monitored experiment setting at all stages
of the tool development.
To further strengthen the tool, the Community Architecture Development Methodology (CADM)
proposed by McGinley and Nakata (2016) and introduced in chapter 2 is used. It aligns with the
tool development process and has been useful to better understand the exchange and quality of in-
formation between the involved parties. The methodology is typically used to develop systems with
the aim of supporting community stakeholders for participation in the planning of communities.

Grasshopper (GH) is a parametric modelling tool integrated with Rhino’s 3D modeling tools. It is a

Grasshopper has a wide variety of plug-ins that have also helped on the design process development

From Helen & Hard we received the construction drawings from the 1970s, when the silo was con-

grid with grid size of 5,950 mm. See Figure 15. There are 12 big pipes with a radius of 5,200 mm in
a 3x4 array, and 6 small ones with radius 3,215 mm in a 2x3 array. The two arrays are placed such
that the big pipes fully enclose the small ones and the gaps between the big pipes are covered by a
curved wall arc with the same radius as the pipes. The gap between the big pipes are at its narrow-
est 1,500 mm.

6.2.3
ShapeDiver
ShapeDiver is an online tool that allows to create interactive 3D models from Rhino and Grasshop-
per ﬁles, that are accessible through any kind of web browser. The 3D model is combined with pa-
rameters that can be controlled by the user of the tool and are deﬁned by the designer himself (Sha-
peDiver, 2021).
In a traditional architectural project, parametric modelling is usually used to easily produce mul-
tiple design iterations. They are a useful way to explore potential design solutions and collaborate
with the project stakeholders. ShapeDiver is used to utilize parametric models to build product con-
ﬁgurators. The software uses the diﬀerent model parameters to deﬁne alternative options of a spe-
ciﬁc product. This is very handy in furniture design and production for example.
Last, but not least, ShapeDiver was regarded as a very useful tool that actively involves the user in
the design process. Giving the user a certain control and the chance to personalize their choices.
ShapeDiver deﬁnes two points as main advantages of using the possibilities of parametric design
modelling with the users, which connect directly to the target of the developed digital tool (PPDM).
1. The user takes part of the design through simple controls (number sliders, text inputs, drop-
down lists, colors ...) that are understandable for any kind of user, and can be used to explore
several diﬀerent options.
2. The parametric model is always live within a CAD system and allows the designer to retrive
design choices exported in a speciﬁed common ﬁle format.
ShapeDiver has in-built functions for user-control of the model, realistic rendering and agile com-
munication between user and designer. It shows the 3D model in an easy controllable way. The de-
signer can control how complex the parameters and renderings shall be. ShapeDiver is only compat-
ible with GH.

7.1
Design Science Research
Following from chapter 3, Design Science Research (DSR) methodology consists of seven steps.
This thesis presents a design method that includes three guiding principles to the architectural de-
sign process. Their relevance is highlighted in the interconnection to Regenerative Design which is
presented in the following section. The artefact of this thesis are the principles themselves embed-
ded in the method. Direct testing of the principles has not been done as it would require bigger re-
sources and a longer time span, as being implemented from start to end in the architectural process.
The evaluation of the Principles is limited to their representation through three alternative design
proposals, and direct presentation to relevant professionals (Tim McGinley, architect and supervisor
of this thesis; and architects at the Norwegian architectural oﬃce Helen & Hard). The contribution
and rigor of the principles are presented in chapter 4. The principles have been developed through
exploration of the research question and the state of art documented in chapters 2 and 4.
The communication of these principles is at this stage limited to this report and presentation to He-
len & Hard. The aim is to be able to communicate them by reﬂecting in the design process. This
research project oﬀers more than a design method. It produces a digital tool, the Public Participa-
tory Digital Model (PPDM), as artefact of the method. The state of the art in participatory design
as well as the regulations and architectural practise in Norway have been analyzed in detail, so that
the artefact design corresponds to current needs and improvements. These factors are reasoned for
in chapter 2. The evaluation of this artefact is documented in chapter 6, by means of CADM. The
need for such an artefact as the PPDM, was from the beginning of this research stated from an aca-
demic and a professional standpoint. The contribution of the tool to the design process needs to be
further assessed by direct implementation in a real-world project and ﬁnal evaluation.
Even though the tool has theoretical roots in the foundations of design and participatory design
theory. Following CADM, also based on DSR methodology, the presented tool follows research rigor
in its construction. A research process has been conducted where the laws governing the environ-
ment in which the tool will be used have been studied. Therefore, it satisﬁes the requirements gov-
erning the mentioned environment. The communication of the tool follows from the methodology on
which it is built (CADM), is covered in chapter 6. The tool itself, being a participatory design tool,
implies communication as an integral part of the tool. Therefore, the features that qualify it, listed
in section 6.6, have been tested and evaluated in chapter 6. These emerge from an empirical anal-
ysis, and originate within the principles and are measurable to a higher degree. They are a mean
aiming for an improved communication of the intention of design and better dialogue with the user.
The tool is also communicated through this thesis and presented and tested by the relevant profes-
sionals connected to this thesis.
The communication of the principles through the PPDM tool is at this stage limited, however fu-

Evaluation of the method
This thesis draws inspiration from a range of topics, which are all connected through the architec-
tural design process. Design Science Research methodology is followed in order to develop a design
method based on three design principles and the tools to aid the implementation of these. The pro-
cess and principles are connected through the stakeholders involved and the solution objectives of
the project. These stakeholders are connected through human interaction, which can be measured
and monitored in a controlled manner through features of communication within a digital frame-
work. These features have been coined in this thesis, but have roots in other professionals work.
A metaphor could be done, taking the process along with the tools presented in this project. These
could be imagined as a machine that takes the People, the Principles and the Place as input and
produces a design. Employing the works of the machine an iterative participatory process comes
out, along with an evaluation of building elements and systems. The process could be imagined as
a visual programming script. As seen in Figure 31. Design Science Research methodology, regener-
ative and participatory design along with CADM go into the structure of the component labelled as
Architectural Design Process. The component functions like a script itself, taking inputs, sorting the
information, using tools (algorithms) to various building features, where the ﬁve features of PPDM
functions as lubricant in the machinery. The component takes a building, situated in a place as in-
put and produces a developed building.
The scripting of this component would include several ‘for’ loops and ‘if/else’ conditions. First,
it takes all information provided in the inputs People, Principles and Place. People contains all
aﬀected parties as well as all clients of the architect (Support, Tissue and Inﬁll level (Habraken,
1972)). Therefore, the people input contains regulations and building requirements. The Princi-
ples are the ones identiﬁed in 4. The Place contains the building, building system, context and area.
The Place could be rephrased as the ‘building as building site’ and directly connects the inputs to
regenerative design.

7.3
Regenerative design
As previously stated in chapter 2 regenerative design moves beyond green and sustainable architec-
ture and aims to exchange ‘bad with good’ in a way that is both restorative and non-depleting with
regards to human comfort, eco-systems and natural resources. Naboni and Havinga (2019) summa-
rizes that the main goal of regenerative design is to go from less bad to more good, indicating that
the current trends, green and sustainable architecture, focusing on decreasing the bad (GHG emis-
sions and resource depletion), need to move to a regenerative approach, which increases the good
(eco-systems, natural resources and human comfort).
An important focus point on this approach is the measurement and assessment of human comfort in
relation to its respective eco- and societal system. The presented method and tool aim to connect
designers and their clients (Habraken, 1972) through participatory methods and to follow guiding
principles that suggest a re-allocation of power in the architectural design process as well as a re-
allocation of focus in design. As Wolf (2019) states the construction of new structures may need to,
within regenerative design, be replaced or annihilated. Therefore a non-biased, deep conversation of
the use of a building as the building site is needed.
The site or place, is integral in regenerative design. Naboni and Havinga (2019) only includes two
kinds of examples when it comes to the use of regenerative design in the AEC-industry, namely al-
ternative new constructions and adaptive reuse. The method and tool developed in this thesis puts
the site and the eco-system, including humans, as main focus. Regenerative design and its sibling
regenerative sustainability and development, being recent terms, have both not been extensively
used within architecture and construction and their implication on the industry is still unclear. It
is clear however the importance of the place, or site, and this thesis explores this term in relation to
the inhabitants of the place.
7.4
Features later in the process
The development of PPDM measures and tracks ﬁve features that can be monitored outside the de-
sign performance assessment in regards to traditional criteria. These features are based on the de-
velopment of the design process and the eﬃcient implementation of the principles. They are the re-
sult of the ﬁrst application of Paint. However, they should thus continue to be relevant and applied
within the other principles Mirror and Grow of the design method. Mirror states that the building
should be analysed on a structural and architectural level and improvements and development shall
provide a mirroring eﬀect of the building to its developed self through the reuse or renovation pro-
cess. Grow requires the building to be treated as an autonomous entity in the built environment
and therefore could be considered from a biological perspective.
From this perspective, the building could be analysed in the same way in which a doctor analyses a
body. It is easy to convince someone that putting the heart outside the skin is a bad idea. The role
of the architectural engineer could be summarized to consider the architectural cosmetics in a holis-
tic perspective that takes into consideration all the systems in the organism and their interaction.
To imagine the engineering works as complimentary systems to architecture, through assessment of
principles and features of the design. The principles deﬁned in this thesis investigate connections
from the context of the building, the building skeleton and the interior systems of the building. The
features are a means of communication between the involved parties conducting a procedure on the
building. To continue the biological perspective the principles are the theory of how the organism
functions and the features are the tools with which we investigate the internal workings. ‘The prin-
ciple takes the role of a doctor and the feature is his x-ray machine showing him the inside of the
body.’

7.5
PPDM as information technology
Sustainable buildings are a fundamental part of the future urban environment. Diﬀerent actors and
parameters of sustainability can be applied in the building design sector to contribute to sustainable
development. These actors may enable the interrelation of the social, economic and environmental
pillars. Decision support tools are often used too late in the design process and commonly focused
on single parameters of sustainability (Sørensen et al., 2020). PPDM can be included as a holistic
sustainability actor, being a design decision support tool with the added value of participatory pro-
cess embedded in the system.
PPDM is presented in this thesis as an example of informative technology that tries to ﬁll the gap
of the missing active dialogue with all the community stakeholders (clients) in all the design stages.
It mostly highlights the social aspect and how the community user is able to familiarize with a new
system that will allow the direct inﬂuence in speciﬁc design parameters (building components).
In a conceptual stage, it seems to be enough to show the user diﬀerent alternative solutions and get
feedback from them. The level of data deﬁned for a ﬁrst design draft in this stage should be clear
and simple, and understandable to any kind of user. From this stage onwards, the design may in-
crease in detail and accuracy as new iterations appear. The process continues until the designer and
client community are satisﬁed with a solution, which may be the start of a ﬁrst concept for the De-
tail Design Phase, where a higher level of complexity is needed.
Within the diﬀerent frameworks, a tool as PPDM can be modiﬁed and reshaped to open a new dia-
logue involving other personalities and relevant topics. If the dialogue takes place between the AEC
disciplines, such as planners, architects, engineers, construction managers or others, the presented
information may relate to daylight, materials selection, quantities, cost or other environmental pa-
rameters. These can be synthesized and visualized in a tool like PPDM to make achieve a clearer
and more eﬃcient communication, while avoiding the loss of data.

CONCLUSION
8
Conclusion
This thesis has explored current trends in regenerative design and developed a complementary de-
sign method and tool, utilizing recent advances in information technologies applied within the AEC
industry. This method and the complementary tool encourages participation in design. They in-
tended to respond to the research question: Can a building function as a building site?
Through analysis of relevant theory in the architectural design process, three guiding principles have
been formulated. These principles concieve the building as the building site and can be summarized
as:
• Paint: explores the context and aims to create continuity in the area
• Mirror: investigates the building elements and aims to conserve the building structure and
architectural heritage
• Grow: looks into the building systems and aims to prepare for longevity and sustainability
Together they form a holistic approach that puts inﬂuence on every phase of the design process.
The principles follows regenerative design that, by deﬁnition, requires participatory methods and
interdisciplinary collaboration. By investigating the building site with Paint, Mirror and Grow, the
construction of a digital model has allowed the evaluation of the communication of these principles.
This thesis has identiﬁed the vision and goal of a public participatory digital model that comprise
the interaction and understanding of particular building elements. The vision was determined to be
shared solution objectives towards the development of the building. An online model has been con-
structed as a Public Participatory Digital Model (PPDM). The communication of the design and
the understanding of solution objectives were measured through features of this tool. These features
are:
• Simplicity - Allows the fast transformation from idea to digital model.
• Realism - Ensures that the user understands where and what they are participating in.
• User control - Provides the user the ability to freely operate the model and explore design so-
lutions.
• Interactivity - Veriﬁes the eﬃcient communication between designer and user.
• Sensitivity - Supports the registration of nuances of richer semantic of the user preferences
within the model.
The features of the tool and design method were tested with 16 expert users. The test addressed
Havnesilo B, in Stavanger, Norway. This is a real project currently being developed by architectural
oﬃce Helen & Hard. The company supervised and supported the development of this thesis.
Four building elements were included in the test and the expert users were asked to choose a favourite
design solution for each element. We received feedback on the speciﬁc design parameters and the so-
lution objective. The user results were collected by email and were easily accessible to the designers.
The test identiﬁed achievements and limitations of the developed tool. The features showed rele-
vant application within regenerative design in the architectural design process. The user control and
sensitivity of the PPDM were identiﬁed as limitations. The terminology with regards to the design
parameters showed lack of relevance to the expert users. A new iteration, that directly addresses
these limitations is suggested.
Future work should include the identiﬁcation of further the ideal software for a participatory digital
model. The inner workings of such a tool remain to be investigated. Application of the principles
and PPDM in an actual project would be very exciting to this project.

CONCLUSION
The PPDM comprises aspects that seem to be relevant in other design stages and in relation to all
suggested design principles. Through PPDM, clients are given a voice in the design process which
can assist the assessment of architectural heritage. This could increase understanding of, agreement
on and community contribution on design. This way the building is not regarded as a solo organ-
ism, but blended in context and the client desires. It becomes a unique place with its own story,
and understood by all parties involved.
In the end, this thesis provides a means for collaboration between architect, and three levels of clients.
It illustrates an eﬃcient way of measuring and monitoring participation. By controlling and moni-
toring the feedback form, it is possible to focus on the solution objectives intended by the architects.
This aids the registration of speciﬁc opinions on the solution objective. It provides a tool for the
architect to receive speciﬁc feedback on speciﬁc design decisions. As well as providing all levels of
clients a tool to contribute to and comment on decisions taken early in the design process.
