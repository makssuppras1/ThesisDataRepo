Abstract
The aim of this thesis is to evaluate the effect of urban nature, in the shape of shade
trees, green walls, and green roofs on the indoor climate and energy performance of
residential and oﬀice buildings. Cities are growing and densifying, which causes a
significant amount of vegetation to be compromised, but the lack of vegetation en-
hances the urban heat island effect, which makes cities more prone to extreme weather
events like heat waves. The compact city district Nordhavn, located in Copenhagen,
Denmark, was chosen as the case study for this research.
Dynamic simulation models were built in the software IDA ICE and EnergyPlus.
Both software were used to model different kinds of vegetation. Sensitivity analyses
were performed on a variety of parameters relating to vegetation, to investigate their
effect on the thermal comfort, heating, and cooling needs of buildings. The results
showed a positive impact on overheating and cooling needs, but also an increase in
heating consumption.
The cooling effect of plants, caused by evapotranspiration,
mainly had a considerable impact on the cooling needs. The optimal green roof could
reduce the total energy consumption with 2%, while the optimal green wall reduced
it with 12%.
The recommendation was made to enlarge the distance between trees when placed on
the East or West side of buildings, to ensure the provision of daylight inside the rooms.
Green extensive roofs were found to be the worst performing studied component and
were therefore advised against. Green walls were determined to be the most useful
on high-rise buildings.
Finally, the results were analysed and related to the broader domain of urban planning,
in which building performance only plays a small role. A lot of benefits related to
urban nature have been identified, but certain restrictions, prioritisation, and the
costs related to many of the solutions, complicate the advancement of planning more
nature in cities. Nordhavn’s first developments seemed to have neglected nature in
their planning processes, but the future master plans show that there will certainly
be attention for implementing more nature and vegetation in the future.

Introduction
In the context of our post-industrial world, in which the consequences of climate
change have become undeniable, it is clear that great efforts have to be made to
minimize our impacts. Urbanization complicates the challenge of reducing our car-
bon footprint. Problems like the urban heat island effect and air pollution must be
addressed and it is already known that vegetation serves as a powerful tool in this
battle. The importance of nature in cities has become unquestionable and a profound
know-how of urban greening is developing, but there is limited information about the
interactions between the indoor and outdoor climate of buildings. An investigation
covering this relationship will enable different disciplines to collaborate on this uni-
versal challenge and hence, create holistic solutions.
This thesis focuses on the interplay of urban nature and building performance, with
Nordhavn as a case study. Urban nature can exist in a lot of shapes and appear-
ances, for example trees, bushes, green surfaces, pocket-parks, city-forests, etc. In
this project, it refers to three possible forms: street trees, green walls, and green
roofs. Those are the kinds of vegetation that will be investigated, because they can
be located on and right next to buildings in a compact city while they are still of
considerable size to cast shadows on the facade.
Building performance is also a broad field which can relate to a lot of properties,
for example energy consumption, indoor air quality, acoustics, etc. In this thesis,
building performance in the sense of thermal comfort and energy consumption is
investigated. Our research endeavors were guided by these key performance indica-
tors (KPIs). In the case of street trees, daylight availability was assessed additionally.
Nordhavn is a growing city neighborhood in North-East Copenhagen, Denmark, that
is being developed on a former industrial harbor site. It is the case study for this
project, because it is a modern city district with a dense structure and it also serves
as the subject of ongoing projects regarding urban nature. However, the results don’t
have to be restricted to this area, since it can be related to other places with a similar
climate. Two kinds of buildings will be evaluated: a residential and an oﬀice set-
ting, which are the most common typologies in Nordhavn. The residential building
model will be based on an existing building, but pulled from its context by varying
in orientation. This will provide the possibility to associate the findings of the results

2
with other locations as well. The oﬀice will show clear differences with the residen-
tial model to evaluate the generalities of the results, namely if the trends related to
vegetation, are similar across different typologies.
Researching the possibilities of implementation and optimization of urban nature
principles, our technical approach is rooted in the matter of building performance
simulation and complemented by a theoretical approach in the larger field of ecological
urban development. The thesis aims to address the following specific problems:
• The effect of urban greening on indoor climate and on the energy performance
of buildings: Investigating how greenery affects indoor temperatures, energy
consumption, and daylight. Examining the impact of various forms of urban
nature on the indoor climate of buildings in Nordhavn.
• Other valuable aspects of urban nature: Assessing various socio-ecological and
economical factors, and their relation to building performance. It’s essential to
connect the technical aspects with other factors, to see what the results of the
thesis imply for urban planning as a discipline.

Background
1. Challenges related to climate change in densifying cities
It has been validated that human behaviour, since the industrialization, increased
the concentration of greenhouse gases (GHG) in the atmosphere, which altered the
Earth’s climate [1]. Extreme weather events like heat waves, droughts, heavy rainfall,
coastal floods, etc. are amplified by the changing climatic conditions and cities are
the most vulnerable to such disasters [2]. By 2050, more than 60% of the world is
expected to be living in cities [3]. This means that urban areas will either expand
their borders or become more dense. Since urban sprawl has been associated with a
large ecological footprint in terms of energy and land use, loss of biodiversity, and
increased car-use, compact building has become a widely accepted strategy in urban
planning. The compact city relates to mixed land-use with high-density housing and
minimal transport of resources and people [4,5]. Because of the short distances, peo-
ple are invited to walk and enjoy the outside space, while car-use is minimized [6].
However, urbanized areas tend to have higher temperatures than their surroundings:
The phenomenon that is known as the urban heat island (UHI) effect and it occurs
everywhere, regardless of the size and the climate in which the urbanized area is
situated [7]. This is caused by the dominance of impervious surfaces with high ther-
mal capacity in cities, such as concrete and asphalt. These surfaces store the solar
energy that is gained and release it later as sensible heat. In rural areas, vegetation
is often more dominant, which means that a large amount of the energy is absorbed
and released as latent heat, needed for evapotranspiration [8]. The UHI effect can
therefore be related to the lack of vegetation in urban areas. This effect on the lo-
cal climate adds to the global warming and associated extreme weather events, to
which cities are already more vulnerable, because of their high population levels and
building density. To deal with hot weather, mechanical cooling is a commonly used
strategy in cities [1]. Nevertheless, since the building sector accounts for 30% of all
European energy consumption, the importance of meeting occupants’ cooling needs
in more equitable and sustainable ways has become undeniable [9]. Although natural
ventilation often serves as a passive cooling method to maintain thermal comfort,
overheating still occurs in some cases [10].

2. The role of greenery in sustainable urban planning
Vegetation appears to have several positive effects on the urban environment, includ-
ing the sequestration of carbon, reduction of outdoor temperatures, management of
water drainage, and tempering of the climate extremes. Positive effects on the health
of citizens are observed, as well as an improved perception of urban spaces [2,5]. In
Helsinki, a high green view index, which corresponds to the overall greenery level
in streets, was related to lower indoor air temperatures in the residential building
stock [11].
Providing surfaces with a vegetative cover and planting shade trees are known as
methods to control the heat balance and could be seen as mitigation strategies of
the UHI effect. This concept is contrasted with adaptation strategies, which don’t
reduce the UHI effect, but provide measures that make it easier to adapt to the
changes. Large blue and green spaces are considered to be rather adaptation tools,
because they provide cooler micro climates to which people can flee from the hot
city [1]. These cooling effects have also proven to be spread into the urban areas
around it when the spaces are large and well-designed, whereby fresh air flows into
the surrounding neighborhood [12].
2.1 Urban shade trees
Urban shade trees can lower surface temperatures in their surroundings [13]. They
affect buildings’ micro-climate by the following mechanisms: shading, wind shielding,
and evaporative cooling [14]. Large trees with a treetop cross-section of at least 50
m² demonstrated to be effectively decreasing the energy use of family houses in Los
Angeles [14]. Trees have proven to be versatile and can provide cool, shaded spaces
in hot cities [1]. Urban tree planting with careful consideration for the urban design
can regulate the UHI effect greatly [13–15].
Even though vegetation in an urban context is associated with the advantages de-
scribed above, planning for more urban shade trees remains a challenge, because it is
in conflict with the ongoing densification of cities. Problems like a lack of sunlight in
narrow streets, traﬀic constraints, polluted soil, and underground cables/pipes com-
plicate the creation of new green spaces [4]. Moreover, there’s a need for provision
of other facilities as well, like bicycle and car parking, accessibility, fire safety con-
siderations, etc.
These regulations often force a lower priority on urban greening
strategies [16].
2.2 Green walls and roofs
Green roofs and green facades influence the micro climate by similar processes as
shade trees: shading on the facade, evapotranspiration of the plants, wind shielding,
==PAGE:14==
Background
5
but also an extra insulation layer is added to the building facade. It consists of veg-
etation and possibly a soil and/or air layer [17, 18]. Green walls and roofs typically
have a lower surface temperature than bare walls and roofs [19,20]. The vegetation
uses the gained energy for evaporation rather than heating up [1]. These interactions
can lead to less heat flux through the building envelope and eventually, lower indoor
air temperatures or cooling demand [21].
Successful examples of green walls and roofs can be observed in various cities globally.
For instance, green roofs have been effectively implemented on bus shelters in Edin-
burgh, Scotland, improving urban sustainability and offering social and environmental
advantages [22]. Research conducted in Melbourne, Australia, has demonstrated the
beneficial effects of green roofs on thermal comfort, by reducing the UHI effect during
extreme heatwave events [23]. Additionally, a number of cities have included green
roofs into their urban stormwater management plans, demonstrating their eﬀiciency
in controlling stormwater runoff and enhancing water quality [24].
Green roofs and walls provide benefits, but widespread adoption is diﬀicult. High
upfront installation costs, ongoing maintenance needs, structural considerations, and
low stakeholder knowledge are a few of the challenges. Concerns like choosing the
right plant species, irrigation requirements, and making sure drainage systems are
in place, are all essential for green walls and roofs to be implemented successfully.
Furthermore, in order to overcome implementation obstacles and ensure the long-
term sustainability of green walls and roofs, policies and regulations that encourage
the integration of green infrastructure in urban planning are essential [22,25].
3. Nordhavn as a case study
Currently, the cooling effect of urban green infrastructure has been studied more pro-
foundly in cities with hot and humid climates [17,26], where the UHI effect enhances
the rising of already high temperatures. Meanwhile, there is little information avail-
able on this phenomenon in high-latitude cities [12]. Therefore, a city district in the
capital of Denmark was selected as the case study for this research. In Copenhagen,
the climate is temperate without dry season and with a warm summer, rather than
hot [27].
Despite their cooler temperatures, cities with cold climates appear to be more prone
to heat waves than warmer cities and these heat waves will occur more intensely and
frequently in the future [7]. Knowledge on the cooling effects of vegetation in this
climate is rather limited. Moreover, Copenhagen is a coastal city, which means that
the UHI effect might be partly reduced by a sea breeze [8]. A site-specific research is
thus essential for the results to be relevant. The Copenhagen University conducted
a research on the differences in land surface temperature in and around Copenhagen,
comparing rural areas and forests with urbanized areas [28]. The largest observed
==PAGE:15==

temperature difference was 12◦C and the surface temperature differed 10◦C when
comparing an industrial area to a forest. G. Yang et al. conducted a study on the
cooling effect of blue-green spaces in Copenhagen and concluded with the proposal
that tree-covered green spaces are the most eﬀicient for climate adaptation in high-
latitude cities [12].
The city district Nordhavn was chosen as the case study in Copenhagen, because of its
high building density [25], recently built housing and oﬀice spaces [29], and ongoing
investigations regarding sustainable urban development [30]. Nordhavn is a growing
city neighborhood in North-East Copenhagen, that is being developed on a former
industrial harbor site, alongside with the opening of a new metro line [31]. To recover
the high costs of the newly built infrastructure, the city government depends on the
incomes of this new neighborhood, which therefore requires high-density housing and
oﬀices, maximizing land use [25]. This scenario is typical for Copenhagen’s model of
development, in which the city’s financial strength often relies on semi-private devel-
opment with a dense structure [32].
The company By og Havn, that is owned partly by the City of Copenhagen and
partly by the Danish government, carries out the (re)development of this site, which
stretches over approximately 40 years, starting in 2009. By og Havn is assisted by a
number of consulting architecture and/or engineering companies that were involved in
the open international ideas competition for the future development of Nordhavn [16].
The winning entry was designed by COBE, SLETH, and Rambøll. By 2050, there will
be living space for 40 000 inhabitants and working space for 40 000 employees [33].
In the strategy plan of 2009, six themes were established to guide the vision for the
future district: Islets and Canals, CO2 Friendly City, Five-Minute City, Blue and
Green City, Intelligent Grid, and Identity and History [33]. Since 2009, the urban
strategy for Nordhavn was revised multiple times [34, 35]. As the area develops fur-
ther and further, master plans and local plans become more detailed. The six themes
are still present in the master plan of 2023 [35].
In 2023, a small fraction of the district has been fully developed: the Århusgadek-
varteret [35]. There has been allocated minimal space for nature in this area, which
raised concerns regarding biodiversity in the city [25]. A lot of problems, shortcomings
and knowledge gaps have been addressed in the Urban Solutions to Green Transitions
Living Lab of Nordhavn, during its launching workshop. The biggest challenges facing
sustainable urban development in Nordhavn were identified along with their oppor-
tunities and desirable next steps. Two site-specific pilot projects have been initiated
within the Living Lab that will build on these themes: Planning for People, Place,
and Planet, and Sustaining Publics [30].
This combination of a modern city area with a dense city structure on one hand, and
ongoing research projects regarding urban vegetation on the other hand, provides an
ideal case study for this thesis’ research. Nordhavn’s buildings are constructed ac-

city as is praised upon in current urban planning studies. Investigating the possi-

for current, worldwide trends. The Nordhavn Living Lab can furthermore provide
useful data on the social and ecological aspects of green space planning, which will
help make the connection possible with different disciplines of urban planning.

Objectives
As described before, this thesis focuses on the interplay of urban nature and building
performance, with Nordhavn as a case study. The main objective of the thesis is to
answer the following research question:
”How does urban greening in a densely built environment influence the indoor climate
and energy performance of buildings?”
Several objectives are developed to define the scope of the project and help answer
the research question:
• To select relevant building energy software, which will advance research regard-
ing the impact of urban nature on building performance in Nordhavn.
• To build proper models of a residential building and an oﬀice building with an
appropriate level of detail, fitting in the context of Nordhavn.
• To develop methods to model vegetation in the applicable building energy soft-
ware.
• To perform sensitivity analyses, showing the impact of various parameters re-
lated to urban nature on the indoor climate and energy consumption of the
models.
• To assess the uncertainty of the combined results by comparing the models in
the distinct simulation tools.
• To draw a comprehensive conclusion from the results, answering the research
question.
Additionally, a sub-question is formulated, which complements the main research
question by connecting the technicalities of building performance simulation to the
theories of urban planning. The sub-question is:
”What are the socio-ecological, economic, and other aspects of urban nature, and how
do they relate to the influence on building performance?”

Objectives

Establishing a connection between the technical aspect, described in the main research
question, and the human-centered issues is crucial.
What do the findings about
building performance imply for urban planners? This research aims to bridge the gap,
providing urban planners with insights into broader implications of integrating urban
nature into their designs. On top of that, theories of urban planning which might have
implications on the results of this thesis, will be investigated. Examining the social
aspect, for instance, involves understanding the interactions between residents and
their surroundings. What else is important to take into consideration when designing
for more green?

1.0.1
Preliminary work
Before the start of the thesis project, preliminary work was carried out in which pos-
sibilities for simulating vegetation in the building performance simulation (BPS) tool
IDA Indoor Climate and Energy (IDA ICE) were explored. The report of interest is
published in Appendix A.
The study’s conclusion was that simulating vegetation in IDA ICE forms a great
challenge. Standalone trees and plants could be represented in a simplified way with
shading objects, but greenery as integral part of a building’s construction couldn’t be
modelled in the software. The report also contained a list of gathered literature that
was considered useful for the current thesis project.
1.1
Approach
To be able to examine the correlation between urban vegetation, indoor climate and
the energy performance of buildings, the following process was followed: First, a thor-
ough literature review was carried out about topics such as the urban heat island
effect, the cooling potential of urban vegetation, several building energy simulation
software with their advantages and disadvantages, modelling vegetation within these
programs, and how to perform sensitivity analyses. The findings of the literature
study are summarised in the chapter Background.
Secondly, the simulation tools were chosen and appropriate models were built for each
scenario to be studied. Dynamic simulations were performed next with varying pa-
rameters. Afterwards, the outputs of the simulations were assessed and in some cases,
a second iteration of simulations was performed. Lastly, the final results served as
the basis for a detailed conclusion which was related to the context of Nordhavn and
current tendencies in the domain of urban planning. The aim of this connection is to
ensure that the assessment goes beyond the technical factors and takes into account
the socio-ecological and human-centered aspects of urban planning.

1.1 Approach
12
In this chapter, the general approach to the research will be demonstrated. This
includes which building performance simulation (BPS) tools have been used, how the
tools were compared, the structure, materials, building physics, and controllers of the

1.1.1
Building performance simulation tools
Modelling tools for building performance will be used to evaluate indoor climate and
energy use. Still, a major challenge is to realistically simulate vegetation in these
software and this played a big role in finding suitable BPS tools. Eventually, IDA
ICE and EnergyPlus were chosen to use for the dynamic simulations of this research.
IDA ICE was used for the computation of the models with shading objects repre-
senting urban shade trees. This tool was chosen because of a high familiarity with
the interface and existing reference work using this software. IDA ICE has a high
performing zone model fidelity (Climate) which computes the solar radiation more
detailed than the default model fidelity (Energy). This is a useful feature when the
effect of shading objects becomes important.
EnergyPlus was used, together with OpenStudio as helping modelling tool, to com-
pute the models with green roofs and walls.
This tool was chosen because of its
prebuilt components for green roofs and walls. The roof module can be used directly,
while a green wall needs to be modelled from scratch with soil and vegetation ele-
ments. This feature is very important for the research, since IDA ICE does not have
the ability to simulate vegetation objects and thus cannot model green walls or roofs.

1.2
Building modelling
Two separate scenarios were drawn out, one of which is a residential setting and the
other an oﬀice environment. These are the two typologies that are the most common
in Nordhavn [33], but they show considerable differences and thus it is important to
model both. If their results show the same trends, it supports the interpretation that
the results can be generalised for different typologies.
In the residential model, the room is heated by a floor heating system and natural
ventilation is used for cooling measures. On the other hand, the oﬀice model features
water radiators for heating and a mechanical cooling system, facilitated by chilled
beams. Furthermore, every scenario takes into consideration differences in comfort
requirements, temperature ranges, occupancy, internal gains, and solar shading con-
trols. These differences are essential for reflecting the diverse needs and operational
dynamics that are specific to residential and oﬀice settings.
The models were based on an existing building block, but pulled from their context
by changing the orientation, to represent a generic building block in Nordhavn. This
way, the conclusions can be broadened to the whole district.

Residential building
The residential building model is based on an existing apartment complex: Havnekan-

The apartments are heated by a waterborne floor heating system with a design power

1.2.1.4
Internal gains and occupant behaviour
The internal gains have been modelled in detail by S. Wistoft and T. Q. Hoang [36].
A thorough literature study helped them to define the highest probability of people
being present in a certain zone [37,40] and create occupancy schedules for the different
zones. Moreover, moisture production from the occupants and their activity levels
have been defined in compliance with recent studies [41]. Heat gains from equipment
have been modelled according to the Guidelines for Indoor Climate Calculations [42]
and other guidelines [43]. These include heat gains from the lighting as well.
The values for internal gains as defined by S. Wistoft and T. Q. Hoang which were
verified in their thesis and found to be realistic, were adopted for the residential model
of this research.
In terms of occupant behaviour, a realistic profile for opening and closing the windows
and for drawing the solar shading had to be modelled. The Guidelines for Indoor Cli-
mate Calculations [42] suggest that windows should be fully opened at a temperature
setpoint of 25◦C when the residents are at home. This scenario was found to be
unrealistic [36], because it can cause the windows to open and close as much as 24
times in one hour. However, the detailed window opening control macro that was
created by S. Wistoft and T. Q. Hoang, based on probability regressions for opening
and closing the windows, appeared to be too complex to be recreated for the current
study. The simplistic model was therefore used and optimized, for example with an
action delay and a reduction of the effective opening area. More information about
this control will be given in chapter 2 and chapter 3.
The thesis’ detailed solar shading control macro based on two studies [39, 44] was
adopted for the residential model. It includes a linear relationship between daylight
illuminance in the room and position of the internal shading. The blinds will be fully
open below 1500 lx, be drawn gradually when more daylight enters the room, and
fully drawn at 2000 lx.

1.3
Vegetation modelling
In the analysis, careful consideration was given to the different mechanisms of vegeta-
tion: shading, wind shielding, cooling effect, and insulation. Delving into the complex
interactions between plants and their surroundings, efforts have been made to model
these dynamics as accurately as possible.
==PAGE:30==
1.3 Vegetation modelling
21
1.3.1
Shading
A tree will cast shadows on its surroundings. This effect is the most straightforward
to model, because most BPS tools have the possibility to implement shading objects.
One option is to create a detailed 3D model of a tree and let IDA ICE compute the
shadows. To avoid having computationally high demanding models, simplified 3D
models were created instead with a suitable transparency.
A green roof is an integral part of the building construction and thus cannot be
considered solely as a shading object. Depending on the type of green wall, it can cast
a shadow on the wall behind, or it can behave as an integral part of the construction.
It is important to model these components in the right way. Further details about
green roofs and walls will be discussed in chapter 3.
1.3.2
Wind shielding
Regarding wind shielding, the decision was made not to differentiate between a sit-
uation with vegetation and a situation without vegetation. One of the reasons for
this is that building regulations require a high degree of air tightness [49]. Therefore,
wind doesn’t have a great influence on the indoor climate.
Another reason for the simplification, is the complexity of wind pressure [50]. BPS
tools generally use wind pressure coeﬀicients Cp to calculate it, which are empirically
derived coeﬀicients, dependent on the shape and surroundings of the building. Since a
building in an urban setting would be defined as sheltered, adding trees wouldn’t make
a difference, because there are no coeﬀicients defined for ’extra sheltered’ buildings.
1.3.3
Cooling effect of plants
There is plenty of evidence that plants lower the ambient temperature of their sur-
roundings through evaporative cooling [12, 13, 51, 52].
However, quantitative data
on this temperature reduction have hardly been published, which complicates the
modelling of plants’ evaporative cooling effects without a designated micro climate
simulation software. Transpiration of plants occurs mostly when they are foliated and
lighted by the sun. However, a clear correlation between temperature, solar radiation,
hours of sunlight, and the transpiration amount was absent in a study by J. Teshirogi
et al. [52]. Even if the transpiration of a plant is quantified, the reduction of the
surrounding temperature is still dependent on other factors like initial temperature
and humidity of the air.
To evaluate the importance of a proper quantification of this temperature reduction,
four different scenarios were studied. One with original weather conditions and three
==PAGE:31==
1.3 Vegetation modelling
22
with a potential cooling effect of 0.2◦C, 0.5◦C, and 1◦C. These scenarios were mod-
elled by altering the values of the dry bulb temperature in the weather files, used
in both BPS tools. The temperatures were subtracted by the values defined above
during the months April to September, in which plants are foliated [53], and only
during the hours in which solar radiation was detected.
A larger cooling effect wasn’t investigated, because more research would be needed
to avoid big variations between the boundary times early in the morning and late
at night. Generally, there is a temperature rise during the first hour in which so-
lar radiation is detected each day. This increase is partly reversed by applying the
cooling effect during sunlit hours. If the temperature is reduced more than 1◦C, a
temperature drop would often be observed during the first hour of the day, which is
unrealistic. If the plants cause larger temperature reductions, a part of the cooling
effect would supposedly extend into the night. However, Yang et al. investigated the
cooling effect of large blue-green spaces in Copenhagen, and the average difference in
LST was around 1◦C for smaller green spaces [12].
By adding the temperature reductions to the list of variable parameters in the sensi-
tivity analysis, the separate influence of the cooling potential of the plants on indoor
climate and energy consumption can be evaluated. If the results are sensitive to this
parameter, it will show a need for further investigations on cooling effects of plants.
All four scenarios were tested for both regular Danish climate conditions and for
increased temperatures of 3◦C, to imitate the UHI effect in Copenhagen. This value
was chosen as a safe estimation of the UHI effect in Nordhavn. The temperatures in
Copenhagen were found to be 10◦C higher than in the forest [28], but in Nordhavn
this effect is reduced by the coastal breeze [1]. Assessing the results for both normal
and raised temperatures, will help evaluating whether the effect of urban vegetation
on energy consumption is amplified in cities with increased temperatures due to the
UHI effect. The effect was imitated by altering the weather files as well. A general
increase of 3°C was applied to the dry bulb temperature in all seasons and at all
times, because the effect doesn’t disappear in winter or at night [54].
1.3.4
Insulation
Green roofs and walls add an extra insulation layer to the building’s envelope. This
translates to lower U-values of the total construction.
Typically, a waterproofing
membrane on the roof structure is covered with a layer of vegetation and substrate
for a green roof. Thermal resistance from this layer improves insulation by reducing
heat transfer through the roof. Similar to this, green walls are made of vegetation
that grows on the vertical surfaces of buildings, often with the help of a supporting
framework and growth medium. By increasing thermal mass and decreasing heat
gain or loss through the building envelope, these walls provide extra insulation [55].

taking into account variables like the type of vegetation, substrate layer thickness,
and associated heat exchange processes.

Thermal comfort is represented by the amount of hours that the operative temper-
ature in a zone rises above 26°C. This quantity is summed up for each zone to a
total of overheating hours. In dwellings with natural ventilation, 26°C is generally
acceptable and the hours above 27°C would be documented [49], but for a smooth
comparison between the two typologies, 26°C is defined as the threshold for overheat-
ing in both models. In any case, the windows in the residential model are opened
when the operative temperature rises above 25°C. The rooms almost never heat up
to 27°C, so evaluating the time above 26°C might be more relevant, even though it’s
not problematic according to the regulations.
Regarding energy consumption, two KPIs are present per model: heating consump-
tion and cooling consumption. The heating consumption is not included in IDA ICE,
because only the summer period is simulated, which means that the consumption
would be very low and irrelevant. More information on the simulation period will
follow in chapter 2 (section 2.3).
In EnergyPlus, heating consumption is measured in kWh/m². Cooling consumption
is also measured in kWh/m² in the oﬀice models, but the residency uses natural ven-
tilation as cooling measure. For that reason, the total duration that windows were
opened is documented. Also this quantity is summed up for every window in the
dwelling.
In IDA ICE, a global sensitivity analysis was performed to rank the parameters from
most influential to least influential. With global sensitivity analysis, the combinations
of parameter values are chosen randomly in order to take the interactions between the
parameters into account as well as the interactions between the output and each pa-
rameter [56]. In EnergyPlus, the parameters will be ranked as well, but by executing
a local sensitivity analysis. This method was chosen because the software’s built-in
parametric function caused numerical problems and couldn’t be simulated. In a local
sensitivity analysis, the parameters are varied one by one, starting from a baseline.
This reduces the amount of simulations, but still allows to uncover the interactions
between the parameters and their outputs. A simple local method for determining the
parameter sensitivity, is the sensitivity index, which will be explained in chapter 3.

CHAPTER2
IDA ICE
IDA ICE was used as BPS tool to evaluate the effects of urban shade trees on the
indoor climate and energy use of buildings in a densely built environment. Two models
were built, one residential and one oﬀice model. Trees were added to the models as
shading objects. Then, the parameters of the sensitivity analysis were made variable
and parametric runs were performed with these varying parameters. Afterwards, a
sensitivity analysis was conducted on the results of the parametric runs.

One apartment floor is modelled, of which the height will be varied in the parametric

this is a climate model, in which the direct solar radiation is taken into account when
calculating the operative temperature. It provides a higher level of accuracy, which

Moreover, the detailed window object is used for the windows, instead of the regular

opening control macro was created based on a previous study [58] with a delay in
changing behaviour, which means that the window is always kept open or closed for at
least one hour. This way, an unrealistic oscillation of opening and closing is avoided.
The effective opening area is also reduced to 50% in summer and 25% in winter, be-
cause looking at the window sizes, it would make sense that only half of the window
can open like a door. The gain parameter k, in the PI-controller object, was lowered
as well, to allow smaller opening fractions when the temperature rises just above 25◦C.

2.5
Sensitivity analysis
A global sensitivity analysis was conducted to evaluate the influence of the parameters
on the KPIs, also considering the interactions between the variables. In IDA ICE,
there is a built-in function for the Monte Carlo method, which is often performed
before regression analysis [61]. Hereby, a random value is chosen for each parame-
ter. The software takes into account the probability function of the parameters so
that highly probable values will occur more often than less probable ones. However,
most of the variables in this study have discrete values of equal probability. Only the
==PAGE:42==
2.5 Sensitivity analysis
33
transparency of the shading objects’ probability is normally distributed, as shown in
Figure 2.8. IDA ICE does not take the standard deviation in account, but instead
applies a standard Gauss curve for the normal distribution. This means that the
standard deviation of the transparency inputs will be a bit different than the actual
standard deviation.
After obtaining the results of the parametric runs, they were analysed in Excel. First,
some transformations to the raw results had to be made. For a regression analysis,
the parameters need to be formatted in numbers. Moreover, transformations might
be necessary to achieve a linear regression line [62]. The floor levels were expressed
in height from the ground, which didn’t have to change. The higher the value, the
higher the floor level. The cooling effect of the plants was expressed in text, namely
the titles of the weather files. Those were changed to numbers: 0; 0.2; 0.5; 1. The
higher the number, the larger the cooling effect. The orientation was expressed in
numbers, but because the building rotates, the lowest value (0°) and the highest value
(350°) corresponded to close positions, while a middle value (e.g. 180°) corresponded
to the opposite position. With this set-up, finding a linear relationships with outputs
is very hard. For that reason, the orientation was transformed with following formula:
Orientationnew = |180 −x|
(2.2)
with Orientationnew the transformed parameter [°]
and x the orientation as it was entered in IDA ICE [°].
Now, the orientation varies from 0° to 180° in which 0° represents a North facing
facade and 180° represents a South facing facade. The higher the number, the more
South-oriented the building. There is no distinction between East and West. The
transparency parameter was brought back to the transparency of the shading objects
for clarity, using Equation 2.1. The higher the value, the more transmittance of solar
radiation. Finally, the parameter defining the amount of trees was not transformed.
The higher the number, the more trees.
The KPIs were not transformed, but the zone-specific properties were summed up to
one general value. After all these calculations, the data was organised with a row
for each parametric run and one column for each variable parameter and KPI. This
resulted in eight columns during the first iteration of simulations.
The data was
fed to a statistical add-on, Excel Analysis Toolpak [63], and this tool automatically
calculated the results of the sensitivity analysis. Results consisted of values for R2,
Multiple R, regression coeﬀicients, significance values, etc. In the first iteration, an
appropriate sample size was defined by analysing the data gradually, adding more
data in each step and evaluating when the results converged [64].

2.6
Daylight considerations
Adding shading objects in front of a building will compromise the daylight availabil-
ity in the rooms. For that reason, it is also important to assess the influence of the
parameters on daylight. In each model, nine daylight simulations were carried out.
Three different scenarios (0, 7, or 15 trees) were tested on three orientations (East,
South, and West).
The key performance indicator in this case will be daylight autonomy, with 300 lx
as threshold (DA300). It refers to the percentage of time that the illuminance on a
specific location reaches above the given threshold [66]. This is not the same as spa-
tial daylight autonomy (sDA), which refers to the percentage of area in a room that
reaches the threshold of illuminance for a specified number of hours. Spatial daylight
autonomy in IDA ICE will always use 50% of the daylight hours in a whole year as
number for which the illuminance needs to be above the threshold. For that reason,
this property becomes irrelevant when the simulation period is different than one year.
However, instead of having a location-dependent property that gives the DA for every
coordinate, IDA ICE calculates one result for the whole zone. The quantity is named
DA300/50%, which seems to represent the percentage of time that half of the floor
area reaches the threshold illuminance of 300 lx.
Because there is no additional
explanation about this metric and the properties cannot be changed, this value is
used to compare the different scenarios to each other. If the definition of the value
turns out to be different than described above, the comparison will still be meaningful,
because it is relative.

3.3
Greenery modelling
Given their potential to affect indoor climate and overall building performance, close
attention will be paid to simulating green walls and roofs in this part of the research
[69,70]. Through the use of earlier literature studies, the goal is to create models that
accurately represent the intricacies of green walls and roofs, ultimately augmenting
the understanding of their possible advantages in urban settings.
3.3.1
Green roofs
There are currently limited design tools available to help engineers assess the potential
energy savings associated with different implementation alternatives (e.g., plant type,
soil type/depth, irrigation options) [68]. With the help of the Material input object
in EnergyPlus, simulating green roofs is quite straightforward; The computational
model for the heat transmission processes in a green roof has been used. This model
takes into consideration [68]:
• Radiative exchange between long- and short-waves within the plant canopy
• Effects of plant canopy on convective heat transfer
• Evapotranspiration from plants and soil
• Heat conduction (and storage) in the soil layer
Green roofs not only reflect solar radiation, but they also serve as an additional layer
of thermal insulation. Typically, a green roof is built with four levels, each of which
has a distinct purpose. These layers consist of [71]:
1. Protection layer: This layer shields the roof from moisture and is situated right
above the roof structure. To stop water and roots from penetrating the roof
structure, it consists of a combination of root barriers and waterproof mem-
branes.
2. The drainage layer: Situated directly above the protective layer, may have voids
and small moisture-retention reservoirs to let excess moisture escape the green
roof. After that, the moisture is routed into storage systems or drains.
3. Growing media (soil) layer: This layer is usually separated from the drainage
layer by a dense mesh fabric cloth. Typically, a lightweight mixture of sand,
aggregate, and organic matter is used as the growth medium. The depth of the
media layer might vary from 5 to 10 cm to 1 meter, depending on the kind of
plant that is utilized.
4. Vegetation layer: The top-most layer of green roof construction is the vegetation
itself.
The green roof module specifies an ’ecoroof’ as the external layer of a rooftop system,
where it’s possible to define the the thermal properties, the height of the plants, depth
of the growing media, the density of the plant canopy, the stomatal conductance (a
==PAGE:47==
3.3 Greenery modelling
38
biophysical factor controlling the rate at which the plant can transpire moisture), and
the soil moisture conditions (including irrigation). The model’s formulation consists
of [68]:
• A simplified moisture balance that permits irrigation, precipitation, and the
transport of moisture between the top and root zones of the soil.
• An energy balance for soil and plant canopy derived from the Fast All-season
Soil Strength (FASST) vegetation models developed by the Army Corps of
Engineers [72]. The moisture balance of vegetated soil - including ice and snow
- is also monitored by FASST. This tool analyses the effects of different soil and
vegetation types on the stability and strength of the soil at throughout different
seasons.
• The temperature equations for the soil surface and foliage are solved simultane-
ously at each time step.
Similar to a traditional roof, radiative forcing from the sun dominates the energy
balance of a vegetated roof. Long-wave (thermal) radiation to and from the soil and
leaf surfaces, conduction of heat into the soil substrate, and sensible (convection) and
latent (evaporative) heat flow from soil and plant surfaces balance this solar radiation.

Evaporation is the physical process that converts liquid water into water vapor. Tran-
spiration, which mostly happens through the leaves, is the process by which water
that has moved through the plant turns into vapor. Both direct evaporation from
the soil and transpiration from the leaves are included in the term ’evapotranspira-
tion’. The plant must provide the least amount of resistance to the water’s flow from
the roots to the leaves, in order to maximize evapotranspiration. Since the climbing
barrier just has a small patch of soil on the ground, only the process of transpiration
occurs in this vertical greenery system.
The soil thermal mass and LAI value of a heavy system green wall are higher than
those of the green climbing barrier, which is how these two systems in detail vary
from one another. Thanks to the thickness of the substrate, the wall can host larger
species with a greater foliar density [73].

since different software may show modest variations, because they focus on different
aspects of building performance. The trends and key metrics must nearly line up,
even though the programs might differ somewhat in precise numerical numbers.
Step by step, more specific details were added to the base case until the final building
models were created. The comparison of those two models will define the degree of
uncertainty when combining the results and findings from IDA ICE and EnergyPlus.

The cooling measures are also influenced the most by the floor level. The second most

The cooling effect of the trees has a larger influence on the cooling measures than

need for cooling in the buildings, while the amount of overheating doesn’t change as

that the oﬀice cools down the outside air directly in the AHU. If the air is cooler,
less cooling will be needed. While in the residential model, the cooling needs are
dependent on the temperatures inside the building, which will trigger opening of the
window. However, the outside air still has an impact on the window opening duration,
because the windows might be closed sooner if the air outside is cooler.
The parameter which shows the most fluctuations in their standardised regression
coeﬀicients, is the orientation of the site. In the residential model, it has a large influ-
ence on the overheating hours, but a low influence on the window opening duration.
This could be related to the occupancy schedule in this typology. The windows are
closed between 8:00 and 17:00 on weekdays, which has nothing to do with the orienta-

overheating is highly dependent on the orientation, because it relies on the solar gains.

In this model, 15 trees corresponds to a distance of 7.5 m between the tree trunks.
With a crown radius of more than 5 m, this means that the crowns of the trees
overlap. 7 trees corresponds to a distance of 15 m between the trunks, which results
in a distance of 3.5 m between the crowns of the trees.
5.5
Conclusion
The key takeaways from this chapter are:
• Oﬀice and residential buildings with trees in front will have less overheating and
less need for cooling than buildings without trees.
• If the outdoor dry bulb temperature is decreased during sunlit hours, both
buildings will have less cooling needs.
• There are no great differences observed between the scenarios in a normal Danish
climate and scenarios with increased temperatures due to the UHI effect.

5.5 Conclusion
58
• The floor height of a certain apartment or oﬀice will still have a larger effect
on building performance than urban vegetation has. Higher floors have more
overheating and more cooling needs.
• The transmissivity of the trees barely affects the building performance.
• Overheating in dwellings and cooling energy consumption in oﬀices will be the
worst when the building faces South. This is an appropriate location to place
trees.
• When placing trees on the West or East side of a building, more distance between
the trunks will be needed than on the South side. This ensures the access of
daylight in the buildings.

6.1
Preliminary analysis green roof
The Figure 6.1’s left side displays the soil layer thickness of 0.15m, while the right
side refers to the soil layer thickness of 0.7m. It’s clear that a green roof’s increased
soil depth increases thermal mass and insulation, which reduces energy use overall.
However, because deeper soil has more insulation, it might not be able to fully utilize
the evapotranspiration cooling effect during the cooling season with high LAI values.
Nevertheless, in every case, increasing the soil depth leads to a reduced overall energy
consumption.
A cooling effect is created by increasing the LAI, since it improves shading on the
roof and thus lowers solar heat gain, and increase plant transpiration. As a result,
a higher LAI results in less cooling energy consumption, but more energy used for
heating. However, the overall energy consumption still follows the cooling effect’s
trend and drops as LAI increases.
Higher plant height increases wind velocity within the canopy, reducing aerodynamic
resistance and increasing the transpiration cooling impact. Taller plants therefore
contribute to more energy consumption during the heating season and less energy
consumption during the cooling season.
In order to conduct the sensitivity analysis, an intensive green roof with a plant
height of 1.0 m was selected (since this seemed to be a regular value for intensive
green roofs [69]). Based on the findings of this analysis, a 0.7 m soil layer was se-
lected, since a thicker soil layer appeared to be advantageous. Given the diversity of
plants on this roof, a LAI of 3 or 5 seems more appropriate. The value of a LAI of
5 was selected, because this produced better performing outcomes. Because an ex-
==PAGE:69==

tensive green roof only contains small plants, such as sedums and grass, the decision
to choose a 0.1-meter plant height for the extensive roof’s design was made, where a
LAI of 1 or 3 seems more reasonable. Since a higher LAI performed better, the LAI
of 3 was selected. Even though a larger soil layer functions better, this option was
overlooked, since it is impractical to have a thick soil layer and a modest height of
plants.
In conclusion, during the cooling season, energy savings may be maximized with a
greater LAI and taller plants (the graph’s second-to-last blue bar represents the low-
est cooling value). Increasing soil depth is the way to cut down on energy use during
the heating season. The soil depth will be significant, since the case study is centered
in Nordhavn, Copenhagen, where the heating season plays a significant role. Given
that our primary goal is to reduce the building’s total energy demand, a deep soil
depth may be the answer, in which case an intensive green roof should function more
effectively than an extensive green roof.

6.3 Impact analysis
68
Starting with the impact of green roofs, it is evident that they do not significantly
affect the energy consumption of a building. This is probably because the roof’s area
is relatively small, compared to the building’s overall volume. It is quite challeng-
ing to reduce the energy consumption of a six-story building with only a relatively
small green roof. Previous research supports this observation, showing that verti-
cal greenery systems have more potential in urban areas characterized by vertical
development, while green roofs mainly affect the upper stories of tall buildings [73].
Therefore, green roofs will perform better in cities with lower building heights. Ad-
ditionally, the simulated buildings are well-insulated, which reduces the potential
influence of a green roof. Nevertheless, installing a green roof has a more notice-
able impact on poorly insulated structures: A green roof system yields higher annual
savings when installed on a poorly insulated roof rather than a well-insulated one [69].
An extensive green roof raises the need for heat and cooling, resulting in higher total
energy consumption, which goes up by 7%. On the other hand, an intensive green
roof reduces total energy use by 2%.
Green walls significantly reduce air temperature and wind speed near the walls. This
effect is mostly produced by the foliage’s transpiration and evaporation, which con-
vert sensible heat to latent heat. The substantial decrease in cooling energy intensity
is accomplished through several mechanisms. First, vegetation provides shading for
walls and reduces solar heat gain through opaque facades. Second, it reduces outside
temperature through evapotranspiration, which minimizes convective heat gains on
the facade and improves the cooling system performance of the building. Nevertheless,
this leads to a rise in latent cooling load, which is mainly caused by humid outside air
infiltration, and a slight increase in ambient humidity. Third, the green wall reduces
the building’s infiltration rate by attenuating both the stack effect (due to a lower
indoor-outdoor temperature difference) and wind effect (a lower wind speed along
the wall, because of the foliage).
The main ways that green climbing barriers reduce energy use are through transpi-
ration from foliage and shading during summer. The case UHI-0 shows the most
significant reduction, with a 12% decrease for the green wall on the north facade and
a 10% reduction for the green barrier on the north facade.
Furthermore, heavy system green walls have been shown to dramatically decrease the
operative temperature during the cooling season — by as much as 9°C when com-
pared to the base scenario. The green climbing barrier, on the other hand, manages
a 6°C drop. During the winter, heavy systems increase the operative temperature by
2°C, as the soil provides thermal resistance but, being almost always wet, does not
have a low enough thermal conductivity to be insulating. The operative temperature
rises by 1°C, when using the green climbing barrier [73].

advantageous in warm climates. Vertical green systems are then recommended as

Discussion
The impact of urban shade trees and green walls/roofs has been studied separately,
in two different BPS tools. The models were found to be more or less comparable
(chapter 4), but the obtained results are mostly in different formats, which compli-
cates their comparison. What can be compared, however, are the tendencies in the
results and the overall effects of the greenery on building performance.
In both cases, the same trends are observed. Adding trees to the model and imple-
menting the cooling effect of the plants, reduces the need for cooling and overheating,
which is also achieved by adding an intensive green roof and a green wall to the build-
ings. Because the different types of vegetation act on different parts of the building,
the assumption is made that combining the different forms of greenery would result
in greater impacts. However, careful consideration is needed when combining a green
wall with street trees. The green wall will thrive when it receives plenty of sunlight,
while the main purpose of the trees is to provide shading on the facade. Since the
trees often don’t shade higher than the third floors, the green walls could be of great
importance on high-rise buildings.
Moreover, trees take up quite some space. The trees in the IDA ICE models are
placed on a 10 m distance from the facade. This means that the street would have
to be at least 20 m wide, if the trees can be planted in the middle of the street. For
that reason, green walls will often be a great solution for dense city structures with
narrow streets. Trees will be more effective when they are placed on locations with
more free space.
One of the biggest differences with IDA ICE, is that the simulation period in Energy-
Plus included the whole year, which allowed the assessment of heating consumption.
Even though most of the vegetation types caused a reduction in cooling measures, all
of them caused an increase in heating consumption during winter. The total energy
consumption could only be decreased with the heavy system green wall and the inten-
sive green roof. This implies that evaluating the effects of vegetation for the whole
year is absolutely necessary to be able to draw a comprehensive conclusion.
Another difference between the impact of trees and the impact of green roofs and
walls, is the importance of the UHI effect in the residential model of EnergyPlus.

CHAPTER8
Urban planning
The process of designing and organizing cities is known as urban planning, and it in-
cludes a wide variety of endeavors with the goal of producing environmentally friendly,
functional, and aesthetically pleasing urban settings. Urban planning has changed
dramatically during the last several decades in response to modern issues, including
fast urbanization and climate change. Urban green planning, which integrates natu-
ral elements into urban spaces to improve public health, environmental quality, and
overall quality of life, is an important subset of urban planning [83].
In Denmark, several planning documents are made by urban planners, going from
a large scale to smaller scales. The final local plans need oﬀicial approval prior to
implementation. The functions that can be built within a designated area and the
maximum building heights that are permitted are for example, regulated by this plan.
In essence, it describes the general requirements and regulations that apply to the
particular area. Plots are sold to different developers and are bound by the rules set
forth in both the local plan and in the Danish regulations. This guarantees uniformity
and compliance with planning regulations throughout the numerous projects within
the area [16].
The planning and development process usually starts with a structure plan, focusing
on infrastructure and green spaces of a large area. Then the master plans are designed
for the different parts of the area. Master plans are more detailed and descriptive
plans, specifying the locations of buildings, commercial spaces, etc. Finally, local
plans provide the regulations for the area, including zoning laws, design standards,
etc. [16].
The following section provides an overview of the urban planning process of Nordhavn,
with a specific focus on analyzing the integration and presence of vegetation.

Current situation
Copenhagen is well known for its innovative approach to climate adaptation in urban
design, placing a strong emphasis on large green areas, bicycle-friendly infrastructure,

The strategy plan for urban nature [84] and the city’s goal

Numerous concerns have been raised by the Urban Solutions to Green Transitions

areas. Those flaws are addressed because they affect the well being of citizens, as

Sleth Architects, responsible among others for the new developments in Nordhavn,
faced significant challenges to the incorporation of greenery in the local plan for the
Århusgadekvarter. Despite their efforts to implement as much green spaces as possi-

green spaces was further limited by the demand for a dense urban fabric with max-
imised land-use, typical for Copenhagen’s model of development [32].
The primary reason for the lack of greenery in Nordhavn appears to be its low prior-
ity among current planners, coupled with the existing complications of high buildings
and narrow streets [16]. The reason for this low priority has everything to do with the
fact that vegetation costs the government a significant amount, while it doesn’t yield
much that is perceptible. In order to overcome these obstacles, a determined effort
must be made to give priority to green spaces in upcoming construction projects by
investigating and exposing the benefits of urban green.
Through innovative initiatives, the Nordhavn Living Lab is actively tackling these
problems. The creation of the Urban Belonging App, which collects data from pedes-
trians in Nordhavn, is part of their first pilot project, Planning for People, Place, and
Planet. The purpose of this app is to give urban planners useful information on the
priorities and viewpoints of citizens about their urban environment. Planners make
better judgments to improve the area’s sustainability and livability by learning what
the locals value [25].

8.2
Implementation of urban nature
By examining the impacts of urban nature on a small scale (one residential and one of-
fice building), it is now possible to extend the findings from chapter 7 to a larger urban
scale. This analysis will look at how the results fit into the Nordhavn setting and an-
swer the following question: ”What can our results mean for future urban planners?”
Through investigating the possibilities and impacts of various urban configurations,
it was possible to pinpoint general solutions tailored to Nordhavn’s situation.
Given that Nordhavn primarily consists of high-rise buildings, adding green roofs
might not be the most practical solution. Green roofs - especially intensive green
roofs - may be more advantageous in city areas with low-rise buildings.
Furthermore, Nordhavn’s narrow streets make green walls a more reasonable choice,
as they require less space compared to trees. However, another perspective to con-
sider is that the narrow streets may prevent sunlight from reaching lower levels due to
shading from adjacent buildings. As mentioned before, green walls are most effective
when they receive ample daylight. Green walls will have less of a cooling impact in
the summer if they are constantly shaded, since evapotranspiration from the soil and
plants will be minimal. This might result in an increased need for heating in the
winter, which would raise total energy consumption. Thus, combining green walls
and trees at the street level strategically could ensure both are used to their fullest
potential.
The proximity of parks is an essential aspect that was not previously discussed, but is
crucial in this context. Having a park nearby can greatly enhance the micro-climate
and overall quality of life, which highlights the necessity for urban planners to consider
green areas into their designs [1,12].
8.2.1
Other valuable aspects of urban nature
Making the connection with the political, economic, and social-ecological theories is
crucial [25]. What does the impact of urban nature on building performance imply for
urban planning? But also, how do the other aspects influence the conclusions drawn
from the results? A positive impact on energy use and overheating calls for more
urban green. Meanwhile, complications regarding the desirability, might contradict
the recommendations made in these conclusions.
Analyzing the social aspects for instance, involves knowing how locals engage with
their environment. Aside from shade, planting a tree directly in front of a window
could cause problems, because of birds nesting and entering homes when windows are
open. When constructing green walls, insects will easier find their way into the homes
of the people living behind the green walls. The question is, if this is a breaking point

for potential residents. It is important to engage the various stakeholders and hear
their voice, like the Living Lab attempts in their pilot-projects.
Tree planting is not always an easy task. Underground pipelines may interfere root
growth, and suﬀicient space and usable soil are essential [4].
Before installing a green roof, a few factors must be taken into account. Not every
structure can handle the extra weight, and a sturdy roof support system is required.
Extensive green roofs are more commonly used, because they require less care and can
be put onto existing roofs with few structural adjustments [76]. This practicality is
likely why extensive green roofs are favored in Nordhavn, despite their less significant
impact on building performance compared to intensive green roofs. Intensive green
roofs are better for lowering overall energy consumption, but they also come with
higher installation and maintenance expenses, which need to be taken into account
before implementing [55]. Providing the structural capabilities to carry an intensive
green roof will cost extra material as well, which undermines the sustainability of the
building and will result in a worse LCA score.
It may be diﬀicult and noisy to install green walls, which could disturb residents
[25]. Though they demand more sophisticated maintenance, heavy system green walls
reduce energy consumption more than climbing green barriers. While self-sustaining
plant species are common in climbing green barriers, more demanding plant species
are frequently found in green walls. Installation and maintenance costs are crucial
factors and often lead contractors to opt against heavy system green walls [73].

Despite these diﬀiculties, urban greenery has many advantages. In addition to the
impact of greenery on a building’s heat demand, several studies have shown that veg-
etation improves a building’s hydrological eﬀiciency and lowers CO2 emissions [73].
Green spaces make cities more visually appealing and provide pleasant views, com-

Nature has positive impacts on

green areas enhances mental well-being [73]. Parks and other green spaces are also
necessary for leisure activities. Additionally, greenery promotes animal life, which
increases urban biodiversity [88]. The integration of green infrastructure needs to be
a component of an all-encompassing urban planning approach that takes into account
the technical feasibility, maintenance requirements, and the ecological and social ad-
vantages of vegetation.
Because the benefits listed above are not always perceptible and because they don’t
yield immediate gains, choosing for more profitable land-use or cheaper materials is an
understandable choice of building contractors. Allocating a priority to urban nature
should therefore be done by the government. Plenty of regulations exist already to
ensure fire safety, accessibility, heritage protection, etc. Including minimal presence
of vegetation could and should be added to that list. By conducting research, proving
the numerous advantages of vegetation, scientists are able to put more pressure on
the politicians to prioritise urban vegetation.

Conclusion
Two research questions were presented in the introduction of this Master’s Thesis:
”How does urban greening in a densely built environment influence the indoor cli-
mate and energy performance of buildings?”
and ”What are the socio-ecological,
economic, and other aspects of urban nature, and how do they relate to the influence
on building performance?”
The main findings of the research include that urban greening in the shape of shade
trees, green roofs, and green walls have a desirable impact on the thermal comfort
and cooling needs in residential and oﬀice buildings.
The models with trees as shading objects showed a decrease in cooling needs, when the
cooling effect of plants was imitated by reducing the outdoor dry bulb temperature
during sunlit hours. South-facing buildings showed more overheating in the dwelling
and cooling consumption in the oﬀice workplace, and the floor level was often the
most important parameter. Daylight availability was worse for the East and West
facing models, where trees need more distance from each other to allow for daylight
in the rooms.
The models with green wall and roof types showed an increase in heating consump-
tion, which could not be compensated for all models by the decrease in cooling needs.
Stronger impacts of the parameters were observed in the models with higher outdoor
temperatures, imitating the urban heat island effect. Here, the green walls even re-
duced the heating consumption (in the oﬀice). Imitating the cooling effect of the
plants also had a desirable outcome in these models. This implies that simulating the
micro-climate around the building in a detailed way, is important to achieve realistic
results. The extensive green roof performed worse than the other greenery compo-
nents, often causing more overheating and energy consumption.
Other aspects of urban planning, including cost, maintenance, aesthetics, lack of
space, biodiversity, health, recreational benefits, stakeholder engagement, and gov-
ernmental management, were found to enhance the complexity of planning for more
urban nature. The advantages need to be weighed out against the disadvantages be-
fore a holistic evaluation can be made. The disadvantages often come down to higher
expenses, which often outweigh many of the benefits provided.
==PAGE:93==
Further research
Further research concerning the interplay of urban nature and building performance,
could be related to the following subjects:
• Building energy simulations with defoliated street trees in the months October
to March, to evaluate the effects on heating consumption.
• Daylight simulations of urban shade trees with varying transparency, to evaluate
the importance of this parameter.
• Further investigations on the cooling effect of plants, possibly using a micro-
climate simulation software. The prominence of this parameter in the results
shows that the micro-climate around buildings matters for building energy sim-
ulations.
• Further investigations on the effect of urban nature on building performance in
heat waves and future weather conditions.
• Building energy simulations on other building typologies with more varying
parameters. In this thesis, only two specific scenarios were investigated with
set occupancy schedules, heat loads, etc.
• Building energy simulations on a more detailed oﬀice model, with more attention
to the HVAC system. In this model, the same AHU was used as in the residential
model and the air flows were increased without testing, which possibly resulted
in overestimated air flows.
• Obtaining more comparable data for the shade trees and the green roofs and
walls, or performing simulations with different types of greenery combined.

Geometry import
3.1
3D modelling software
If there already exists a 3D model of a certain building, it can save up a lot of time to im-
port this model into IDA ICE instead of recreating it from scratch. However, the simulation
program needs specific information of a model to be able to make it work in the tool.
Different 3D modelling programs are Autodesk Revit, SketchUp, Rhino 3D, and Blender.
These programs can export their models in CAD files (.dwg, .skp, .3dm, .obj, ...) en such
files can be imported into IDA ICE. Although, when a CAD file is opened, it will most likely
appear as an imported object that’s not editable. IDA ICE doesn’t always recognize which
geometries are meant to be walls, doors, roofs, etc.
If you want to import a building that’s recognized by IDA, it’s best to use the tools "im-
port building body" or "import zone geometry". The geometries will be non-editable but
re-sizable [1]. Since dwg-files are only supported for versions of AutoCad up to 2004, using
SketchUp or Blender is recommendable.
Figure 1: IDA ICE is unable to import dwg-files from after 2004.
However, if you want to import a building with more information attached than only the
geometry, IFC-files may be useful. This is a BIM file that contains all materials, spaces, etc.
If this type of file is imported in IDA ICE, the simulation program will immediately recognize
all the objects that need to be inserted and they are completely editable afterwards. Since
IFC contains the most complete information, this method was selected to continue with.
3.2
IFC import
In order to practice the IFC import of a 3D model, a small building was created in Revit,
because this software can export IFC files from their 3D models. It is important that the
Revit model contains all the correct information. Important features here are style names,
clear building storeys, clear spaces, construction materials, simple or simplified geometries,
and a system with SI-units.
Some specific geometries are unable to be processed by IDA ICE, which means that it is
impossible to have them in your model. Building storeys cannot intersect each other, their
footprint cannot contain holes, and facade openings cannot belong to multiple storeys at
once. A building with any of these properties needs to be simplified in order to model it in
IDA ICE [1].

it won’t recognize the building as a building body in which zones can be created (Figure 3).
The mapping allows you to allocate a material from the IDA ICE database to each material
in the Revit model. In this example, generic walls were used for the Revit model. They exist
of just one layer of concrete. This means that only one material needs to be mapped in IDA
ICE. When importing more complex models, more materials will need to be mapped with
the right properties. Possibly, new materials need to be created in IDA so they can match
the materials in Revit.

In summary, the most important considerations for importing an IFC model into IDA ICE
are listed below.
- Make sure the IFC model is clean and contains all information.
- Make sure that spaces are present in the IFC file and that they correspond to the zones
which will be simulated.
- In some cases, IDA will simplify the geometry.
- IFC mapping is essential for the model to function.
- All materials used in the IFC model should be created in IDA ICE with the right
properties.
- Also the glazing material of the windows needs to be created and manually added as
the default for windows, because it cannot be mapped.

Green infrastructure
4.1
Trees and plants
According to H. Akbari, urban trees can have multiple effects on the energy use of buildings.
First of all they provide shading, which reduces the solar radiation on a building facade.
Moreover, the ambient temperature can be reduced because of evaporative cooling. This
can create an oasis effect with significant cooler temperatures. Lastly, trees also serve as
wind barriers, which has different consequences depending on the climate. The lack of wind
can reduce air infiltration with a positive effect, or it can reduce the effectiveness of natural
ventilation because it prevents cool air from entering. Lower wind speeds might also cause
higher surface temperatures on a facade [2].
Figure 5: Tree serving as shading object next to a building.
Taking into consideration the capabilities of IDA ICE, it clearly won’t be possible to sim-
ulate the complex interactions of a tree with its surroundings. However, the program does
recognize shading objects and takes into account the reduction of solar radiation coming
through the window, when the window is blocked by such objects. Since CAD files can be
imported as discussed in subsection 3.1, it is fairly achievable to model a tree as a shading
object. Any 3D model like SketchUp, Blender, or Rhino 3D could be used for that.
After having imported a tree geometry, the object is visible in the 3D viewer and when
clicked on, the position, scale, and transparency can be modified. Assuming that there exist
different tree or plant species with different shading patterns, the transparency tool can be
useful to recreate specific trees as detailed as possible. The concept of green view index
(GVI), which corresponds to the level of vegetation on the sides of the street, the shading
of trees and surrounding buildings at the height of 1 to 5 meters [3], could be used to sim-
plify the model of trees even more. More research on GVI could help define a relation with
transparency of a shading object.

It should be noted that the trees are fixed objects, which don’t differentiate between seasons.

for autumn, spring, and even more for winter. This will require several shorter simulations

As seen on Figure 6, not all trees downloaded from the internet [4] will produce a shadow.
This is an essential characteristic for the tree to function as a shading object and therefore
needs to be checked. Creating a simplified shape in IDA ICE directly could even be more
convenient.
4.2
Green roofs and facades
Even though several studies of the list with literature (section 5) indicate that vertical
greenery systems have a positive influence on the energy use of buildings, there doesn’t exist
a model for green facades or green roofs in the simulation program [5].
There exist BIM models for green roofs and facades of which an IFC file could be created, but
the mapping of all the materials in IDA ICE would be a challenge. Thermal properties of the
different layers might not be consistent in time, but reliant on the amount of moisture and
other factors. The U-value of such a construction would consequently be difficult to calculate.
Again, a highly simplified concept of a green roof or facade could be modelled by only taking
into account the shading properties of the vegetation. According to a study in which a green
facade was simulated, the shading properties of the plant species were crucial and greatly
reduced the cooling load of the building [6]. A shading object with the right transparency,
close to the facade could imitate such a construction.

looking further into this issue.

Conclusion and further research
5.1
Conclusion
Simulating vegetation in IDA ICE is a great challenge and extensive simplifications have to
be made. Modelling a standing tree or plant has successfully worked with shading objects.
However, in the context of densifying cities, it would be very beneficial to be able to simulate
other greenery objects which take up less space in an urban environment.
To look further into the possibilities of simulating shading objects on a facade without win-
dows could be a great start from this point. Other than that, exploring other forms of urban
green or looking into other simulation programs with more possibilities might be necessary.
In the next section of the report, a few articles have been listed, which were found to mention
possibly relevant studies in their abstracts.
