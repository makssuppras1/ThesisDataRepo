II 
Abstract 
 
The curve number method is a globally known and widely used method for estimating direct surface runoff. 
The method is not popular in Denmark but is being included to a Danish online flood screening tool, Scalgo 
Live. The curve number method is based on curve numbers, which are chosen based mainly on land use 
and soil type or calculated from measured rainfall and runoff data. This study aims to find out if curve 
numbers for different land uses and soil types perform better compared to if only two curve numbers are 
used, one curve number for pervious areas and another curve number for impervious areas, when applied 
in Denmark. Further the applicability of the curve number method in its original form and when used with 
Scalgo Live is assessed. 
 
Spatial data was not found to improve the runoff estimations. The curve number method in its original form 
was found to fit poorly to Denmark’s humid conditions. The method was however found to work well when 
using the method together with Scalgo Live. Average curve numbers for pervious areas were much higher 
when using the method in Scalgo Live than when using the method in its original form, mainly since the 
catchment areas defined by Scalgo Live are clearly smaller than the entire catchment areas. Gradual filling 
of depressions in the ground was found to significantly affect the size of the catchment area that 
contributes to runoff by the catchment outlet. The curve number 74 has been recommended for pervious 
areas in Scalgo Live, but based on this study a higher curve number 81 is recommended.

1 Introduction

1 Introduction 
Several areas in Northern Europe are expecting more extreme rainfall events in the future. Flash floods 
caused by extreme rain events cause a lot of destruction to infrastructure and property and can cause injuries 
to humans or loss of human lives. Especially urban areas with a lot of impervious surfaces are vulnerable to 
flooding. Urban areas can try to prevent or mitigate the increased risk of inundations by proper land use 
planning. Good prevention measures require knowledge of the risks, for which good flood models are 
needed. Flood models are often complex, time intensive and spatially limited to smaller areas. 
 
1.1 
Scalgo Live and the Curve number method 
Scalgo Live is a digital flood risk screening tool that aims to meet the need for easy and fast flood risk 
modelling. The tool is made with the intention to be straightforward to use, also for persons without 
experience in flood modelling, since the end user is not required to choose parameters. Scalgo live uses 
detailed elevation maps to route the precipitation to depressions and if the depression gets filled the 
remaining water spills and continues downstream. Scalgo Live calculates catchment areas based on the rain 
event size, giving only the area which is expected to contribute to runoff. Runoff from areas upstream is 
expected to be stored in depressions upstream.  
 
The simplicity of the usage of Scalgo Live is reflected in the simplicity of the modelling. Originally Scalgo 
Live has only used a so-called glass plate model that does not take into account hydrological losses except 
from depression storage. This means that all the precipitation that is not stored in depressions is counted as 
runoff, leading to overestimation of runoff especially from pervious areas. No common agreement of the best 
way to model runoff from pervious areas exists and many other rainfall runoff modelling methods completely 
neglect runoff from green areas, which is another extreme approach since pervious areas contribute to runoff 
at least for extreme rain events (DHI, 2015) 
 
The curve number method (NRCS-CN) by the US Natural Resources Conservation Service is a rainfall runoff 
method that is being implemented to Scalgo Live as a way to include the effect of infiltration to the tool. The 
curve number method is a popular method worldwide since it is easy to use and requires few parameters. 
The curve number method converts mass rain to mass runoff. The method is based on a sole parameter, the 
curve number, which is chosen mainly based on land use and soil type. A curve number shows a steepening 
curvilinear relationship between rainfall and runoff. For a composite catchment several curve numbers can 
be chosen to represent the variability of the catchment. Both the advantages and disadvantages of the curve 
number method lie in its simplicity. In Denmark the curve number method has not been widely used, which 
is why its applicability should be assessed.  
 
1.2 
Aims and objectives  
This thesis studies the usability of the curve number method for pervious areas in Denmark and the 
applicability of the method for Scalgo Live. The proposed way of including curve numbers to Scalgo Live is 
==PAGE:12==

2
by giving impervious areas one curve number and pervious areas another curve number. The original idea 
of the curve number method is that curve numbers should be chosen based on land use and soil type from 
provided tabulated curve number lists. Geographical datasets provide information about land use and soil 
type and could potentially be used to improve the runoff calculations with the curve number method. Scalgo 
Live includes geographical open data layers and if curve numbers that improve runoff estimations could be 
chosen based on spatial data, it would be easy for Scalgo Live to define the curve numbers per pixel based 
on the data layers. 
 
The main objectives of this study are to find out whether freely available geospatial data mainly of land use 
and soil type can be used to improve the runoff predictions by the curve number method in Denmark, 
compared to using a single curve number for pervious and impervious areas, and whether the 
implementation of the curve number method to Scalgo Live improves the runoff estimations. Measured rain 
and streamflow data will be used to evaluate the performance of the method.   
Sub-questions to be answered by the study are: 
• 
What kind of open data can be used for the study? 
• 
Can rain events and corresponding runoff events be identified from rain and streamflow time 
series data and what kind of criteria for the events should be set? 
• 
Does the method for defining the catchment area contributing to runoff affect the results? 
• 
How can the land use and soil type data be used for determining curve numbers and how does 
the modelled runoff perform compared to observed quick flow?

• 
Can curve numbers be calculated reversely for Scalgo Live based on rainfall and runoff data? 
How does Scalgo Live perform using curve numbers compared to the glass plate model?

2   Theory 
This chapter presents the theory behind runoff generation, the curve number method and introduces the 
relevant parts of the flood screening tool Scalgo Live. 
 
2.1 
Rainfall to runoff 
The global hydrologic cycle describes the circulation of water between earth and atmosphere, while the land 
hydrologic cycle describes the path from precipitation to runoff  (Dingman, 2002). This study focuses on the 
land hydrologic cycle, especially the way from rainfall to runoff, which is a complex process affected by many 
hydrological processes as illustrated in Figure 2-1. The water balance equation in its simplest form is 
𝐼𝑛𝑓𝑙𝑜𝑤−𝑂𝑢𝑡𝑓𝑙𝑜𝑤= 𝐶ℎ𝑎𝑛𝑔𝑒 𝑖𝑛 𝑠𝑡𝑜𝑟𝑎𝑔𝑒, this equation is usually applied on a delineated geographical 
location most often to a catchment. 
 
 
Figure 2-1 Land phase of the hydrologic cycle, boxes standing for principal storages (Dingman, 2002)   
 
 
Hydrologic losses 
Hydrologic losses occur both before rain has reached the ground, in the form of interception, and after rain 
has reached the ground as depression storage, infiltration and evapotranspiration. The following section 
briefly introduces interception loss, infiltration and depression storage. 
==PAGE:14==
2 Theory 
 
 
 
4
Interception loss 
When rain falls on vegetation a part of the rain remains on the vegetation from which it evaporates or later 
drips to the ground, the part of the rain that never reaches the ground is called interception loss. The type 
of vegetation and its growth stage significantly affects the amount of intercepted precipitation (Dingman, 
2002). Depending on geographical location and plant species interception loss stands for about 10 to 40% 
of the precipitation (Dingman, 2002). Interception loss is larger for a small storm of long duration than for a 
large and intensive storm  (Singh and Mishra, 2003).  
 
Infiltration 
Infiltration is the water going from soil surface into the soil (Dingman, 2002). The infiltration rate is the rate at 
which water moves into the soil, water input rate is the amount of rainfall that reaches the ground. If water 
input is less or equal to the infiltration rate all water input will be infiltrated, if the opposite happens the soil 
gets saturated from above which leads to ponding. Completely saturated soil can occur if the water table 
rises to the surface also leading to ponding, this time the soil gets saturated from bellow (Dingman, 2002). A 
high water-content in the soil decreases the infiltration capacity. Infiltration rates vary considerably with 
location and is highly affected by soil type. Infiltration and the movement of water in soil are very important 
since they determine the water available for surface runoff. 
 
Depression storage 
Depression storage is also referred to as surface detention. The water that reaches the ground but does not 
infiltrate starts to pond into natural or man-made depressions in the ground surface. How much water can 
be stored in depressions depends highly on the topography of the surface. If the catchment has a low relief 
depression storage is higher (Singh and Mishra, 2003). The water that gets stored in depressions will slowly 
either infiltrate or evaporate (Singh and Mishra, 2003). 
 
Evapotranspiration 
Evaporation is the process of water on the ground and on vegetation that is converted to water vapor and in 
this way returned to the atmosphere, this process is called evapotranspiration. It is a term covering both 
evaporation from surfaces such as soil, plants and water and transpiration from plants (Singh and Mishra, 
2003). Evapotranspiration affects especially the long-term water balance (Singh and Mishra, 2003) 
 
 
Runoff methods 
The rain that remains after hydrologic losses becomes runoff, several types of runoff generation methods 
exist and are explained below. Runoff is usually illustrated as a hydrograph where flow is plotted per time. 
Surface runoff is visible in the hydrograph after satisfaction of interception, infiltration and surface storage, 
ending soon after the storm (Mockus and Hjelmfelt,2004). 
 
==PAGE:15==
2 Theory 
 
 
 
5
Surface runoff 
Surface runoff is either Hortonian overland flow named by Robert Horton or Saturation overland flow 
(Dingman, 2002). Hortonian overland flow occurs when rate of rainfall is higher than the infiltration capacity, 
this usually happens when the soil is dry (Ponce and Hawkins, 1996). Saturation overland flow is the runoff 
occurring when the soil is saturated caused by antecedent moisture or a very large rain event. Saturation 
overland flow usually occurs at the end of a wet season (Ponce and Hawkins, 1996). When ponding occurs 
and the depression storages are filled the water starts to move on the surface down the slope, this is called 
overland flow or surface runoff (Dingman, 2002). Surface runoff is common in small arid watersheds (Mockus 
and Hjelmfelt,2004). 
 
Channel runoff 
Channel runoff is the precipitation straight into the stream contributing to the hydrograph from the beginning 
until the end of the rain event (Mockus and Hjelmfelt,2004). According to Dingman (2002) channel runoff 
can stand for a significant part of the quick flow event, even though the percentage of the total catchment 
area often is less than 1 %. According to the National Engineering Handbook (NEH) by NRCS channel 
precipitation is an insignificant part of the flood hydrograph and usually ignored (Mockus and Hjelmfelt,2004). 
 
Subsurface flow 
There are many different ways in which subsurface flow can quickly contribute to the stream as a response 
to a rain event, both for saturated and unsaturated conditions (Dingman, 2002). Subsurface flow is caused 
by infiltrated water under but close to the surface. It shows in the hydrograph during and close after a storm, 
subsurface runoff is common in humid regions (Mockus and Hjelmfelt,2004). 
 
 
Streamflow 
Any naturally formed channel with waterflow is called a stream, streamflow varies seasonally depending on 
the climate, and spatially based on the time it takes for the input to reach a stream. Streams with water only 
during rain are called ephemeral streams and streams with water year around are called perennial streams 
(Gupta, 2011). The input to the streams comes mainly from the runoff generation methods described above 
or it can originate from groundwater. Streamflow measurements when flows are very low are usually not very 
precise which affects the streamflow curve (Dingman, 2002). For hydrological modelling purposes 
streamflow is often divided into quick flow caused by a precipitation event and baseflow that cannot be linked 
to any specific rainfall event. 
 
Quick flow 
Quick flow, also called event flow, direct runoff, storm runoff and storm flow is the direct response of a water 
input to the streamflow (Dingman, 2002). The main sources causing a quick flow event are channel 
precipitation, surface runoff or subsurface runoff according to Dingman (2002). According to Dingman (2002) 
the ratio between quick flow and storm input for an individual storm is usually less than 0.1. However, a

portion of the water which seems to be quick flow is often water from previous events that is affected by the 
new rain event and transported to the stream as quick flow (Dingman, 2002).   
 
Baseflow  
Most water enters the stream via various delayed ways and forms as baseflow. Baseflow is the part of 
streamflow that is not associated with any specific water input event. It originates from persistent sources 
such as unconfined aquifers (Dingman, 2002). Baseflow originates from aquifers that have been recharged 
by infiltrated water  (Mockus and Hjelmfelt,2004). Baseflow is quite stable mainly formed by groundwater. 
Baseflow during fall and winter often increases when evapotranspiration is low (Koskelo et al., 2012) 
 
Baseflow separation methods 
Baseflow can be separated from streamflow with several techniques and there is a large variety of the total 
amount of baseflow, depending on the method chosen for baseflow separation (Dingman, 2002).  Separating 
baseflow from streamflow is a simplification of many complex processes (Koskelo et al., 2012) Graphical 
baseflow separation methods are the simplest baseflow separation methods. One example of a graphical 
baseflow separation method, where a line is drawn from the initial hydrograph rise until it meets the 
intersection of the hydrograph with a 1.4 l/s is shown in Figure 2-2 (Dingman, 2002). Several different 
baseflow separation methods exist such as smoothed minima approaches, rating curve methods, nonlinear 
baseflow separation, fixed-base method and automated computer-program based methods (Koskelo et al., 
2012) These methods all have their own drawbacks, being expensive, laborious, poorly standardized and 
subjective. Separating baseflow is needed for several hydrological analyses, when separating baseflow it is 
important to stay consistent to the method and be aware that graphical methods cannot actually differentiate 
between sources of the streamflow (Dingman, 2002). Tracer based baseflow methods probably give the 
most accurate results, but the method is laborious and expensive. 
 
Figure 2-2: An illustration of graphical baseflow separation. 
 
 
Catchment 
A catchment is also called watershed, river basin or drainage basin. Drainage area is the area of the 
catchment that drains water gained from precipitation to a stream, marsh, river or lake (Gupta, 2011). The 
lowest point of a basin is always the outlet. Watersheds are traditionally defined with topological maps, 
==PAGE:17==
2 Theory 
 
 
 
7
however nowadays catchments can be defined by computer programs with the help of digital elevation 
models (DEMs) (Dingman, 2002). The size of catchments vary from a few hectares to millions of square km 
and a larger catchment can be divided into an infinite number of smaller catchments (Gupta, 2011). 
Catchment characteristics are most importantly area but also length, width, slope, relief and drainage 
patterns (Singh and Mishra, 2003)The catchment relief is the elevation difference between two reference 
point, the maximum relief is between the highest point in the catchment and the outlet (Adhikari et al., 2014). 
 
2.2 
SCS runoff curve number method 
The SCS runoff curve number method hereafter only called the CN method was originally developed in the 
United States in 1954 by the Soil Conservations Service, nowadays called Natural Resources Conservation 
Service (NRCS) as a response to a risen need to estimate runoff in agricultural watersheds. The aim was to 
develop a method that could be used in ungauged catchments (Mockus and Hjelmfelt,2004). The CN method 
was empirically developed based on rainfall runoff data from small agricultural catchments  (Hawkins, 
Richard H., 2009). The CN method is a conceptual, single-event and originally lumped model however it can 
be used as a distributed model. The CN method models infiltration loss but can include interception and 
surface storage loss with its initial abstraction parameter  (Ponce and Hawkins, 1996), the method is 
developed based on surface runoff caused by infiltration excess  (Mockus and Hjelmfelt,2004), however, it 
is not an infiltration equation (Ogden et al., 2017). According to the CN method Quick flow is caused by 
channel runoff, subsurface runoff and surface runoff and the CN method estimates these (Mockus and 
Hjelmfelt,2004).

The curve number ranges theoretically between 0 to 100, but if the curve number is less than 40 other

The initial abstraction coefficient Ia stands for hydrologic losses. The initial abstraction ratio Ia/S or lambda 
was originally given as  𝜆= 0.2. Lambda can however be seen as geographically variable depending on 
climatic and geologic conditions (Ponce and Hawkins, 1996). Ia/S has been found to be significantly lower 
than 0.2 as originally recommended (Hawkins, 2009). Values between 0-0.3 has been reported in multiple 
studies in the United States and other countries (Ponce and Hawkins, 1996). Originally lambda was not 
recommended to be changed because very little was known about the hydrologic losses. Of all hydrologic 
abstraction infiltration is the most important on short term and evapotranspiration on long term, interception 
and surface storage are seen of secondary importance (Ponce and Hawkins, 1996). The CN method is based 
on infiltration loss but also including interception and storage losses through initial abstraction (Ponce and 
Hawkins, 1996) according to (USDA, 1986)it also considers evapotranspiration. 
 
 
Runoff from heterogenous catchments 
When using the CN method on catchments with several different land uses or HSG groups there are two 
principal ways to calculate runoff, the weighted curve number (weighted CN) method and the weighted runoff 
(weighted Q) method. For both weighted CN and weighted Q methods curve numbers are defined for a 
catchment and weights for each curve numbers are given according to % area.    
 
• 
The weighted CN method multiplies each CN with corresponding area % after which they are 
summed, leading to one weighted CN used for calculating runoff with equation (2-4).  
• 
The weighted Q method calculates first the Q for each CN with equation (2-4) after which each Q 
is multiplied by % corresponding area and summed.  
The weighted Q method is better than the weighted CN method, but the weighted CN method is easier to 
use if the catchment has many different curve numbers (Singh and Mishra, 2003).

The CN method was developed from daily rainfall-runoff data from agricultural watersheds where land use 
and treatment, soil group and hydrologic surface condition were known (Ponce and Hawkins, 1996). 
For the curve number method to be successful the use of accurate curve numbers is needed  (Menberu et 
al., 2015). The main factors affecting the choice of curve number from tabulated curve numbers are 
Hydrologic soil group, land use and cover type, hydrologic condition treatment and antecedent moisture 
condition.

Soil plays an important role in the choice of curve numbers since infiltration is much affected by soil type. 
Soils are divided into four different hydrologic soil groups (HSG) A, B, C and D. The division is based on

infiltration (HSG A) to high runoff potential and low infiltration rate (HSG D). Table 2-1 shows the different 
HSG categories based on transmission rates and texture. Soil texture is usually better available for than 
transmission rates. For the United States HSG maps are given. Neither objective instructions for determining HSG 
nor correlations with soil classifications by FAO exist (Hawkins, 2009).

Instead of AMC classes the new NRCS handbooks have included antecedent runoff condition (ARC) classes. 
The average ARC values are the median values for a site derived from sample precipitation and runoff data. 
The ARC I and III classes are the lower (10%) and upper (90%) limits of a confidence interval, leading to 80% 
of the runoff falling within the range (Hawkins, 2009). The ARC does not aim to account for only soil moisture 
variation but for overall variations in CN, they are so called error bands of CN. The tabulated CN values are 
average ARC’s from the test sites. 
 
The actual moisture condition of the soil depends on many more parameters than just on prior rainfall and 
many different ways of accounting for the variability of CN due to soil moisture have been done. Three 
different indices have been used to describe AMC, the antecedent precipitation index (API), the antecedent 
baseflow index (ABFI) and the soil moisture index (SMI)  (Mishra et al., 2005). The original AMC classes used 
API. The advantage of the AMC classes is their simplicity and many studies still use the AMC classes even 
though NRCS doesn’t recommend it anymore. The AMC classes fail in areas with large seasonal variations  
(Tessema et al., 2014). ABFI is based on the amount of previous baseflow which is affected by groundwater 
flow and the effect of delayed antecedent precipitation, however ABFI is not popular in research due to many 
uncertainties such as baseflow separation (Mishra et al., 2005).

Watershed response changes between seasons and variability in CN have also been found between seasons 
(Hawkins, 2009). Parisay et al.  (2019) used the leaf area index (LAI) and precipitation data to derive monthly 
curve numbers which improved the runoff estimations.  LAI can be used to estimate canopy cover which

The SWAT model that uses the CN method has two different methods for taking seasonal variations into

season and good when wet season, they found modest improvements of modifying the curve number 
between the seasons   (White, Bosch and Veith, 2009). They made the modifications in the riparian zones 
and for woods.  
 
 
Modifications 
Several modifications with different rate of success have been done to the original CN method. A long-term 
CN method has been developed by Singh and Mishra  (2003), taking previous moisture conditions into 
account. Williams and Sharpley  (1990) included slope to the CN method. The slope adjusted method, 
originally developed for the EPIC program (Erosion/ Productivity Impact calculator) has been widely 
implemented in several programs, it is for example used in the Swedish HBV- NP model  (Pers, 2007). 
 
 
Literature about the method 
The CN method is widely used for direct runoff volume computing. It is one of the most popular methods for 
calculating surface runoff volume from small, agricultural, forest or urban watersheds (Singh, Mishra, 2003). 
The CN method is as well as implemented in several computer programs (Ponce and Hawkins, 1996). 
Outside the United States the CN method is popular mainly in India and South Africa but also used in many 
other countries worldwide (Hawkins, 2009). In developing countries, ungauged catchments are common and 
data availability often scarce, which speaks for the popularity of the CN method. The method suits best for 
rainfall runoff estimates in streams with insignificant base flow such as ephemeral streams in arid or semi-
arid regions  (Ponce and Hawkins, 1996). The runoff depth given by the CN method is not the same as flood 
depth, if the runoff volume is wanted in as a volume it should be multiplied with the catchment area (Harbor, 
1994). 
 
For low CN and small rain events the correct choice of CN is more important than correct rainfall for runoff 
estimations, since the method is more sensitive to CN than to rainfall when being close to the threshold of 
runoff creation  (Hawkins, Richard, 1975). The handbook curve numbers were developed from rainfall runoff

data from east and Midwest United States  (Rietz and Hawkins, 2001). The aim was for the method to be 
used for similar catchments as it had been developed in, rainfed agricultural uplands with large storm events 
(Hawkins, 2009). No official catchment area limits are given for the curve number method, but the 
catchments used for the creation of the tabulate curve number tables were between 0.1 to 18650 ha with a 
median of 8 ha  (Hawkins, 2009). According to the NEH the method is not restricted to small catchments, 
but for bigger catchments spatial variability and soil complexes should be considered (Mockus and 
Hjelmfelt,2004). Since rainfall runoff data often is widely available nowadays the curve numbers can often be 
calculated from rainfall runoff data. The popularity of the CN method is mainly due to its simplicity and low 
data requirements (Ponce and Hawkins, 1996). The CN method does not work very well for small rain events 
and low curve numbers, and is therefore not recommended for these, originally the method was developed 
for annual floods  (Mockus and Hjelmfelt,2004). According to TR 55 the procedure is less accurate if runoff 
is less than 0.5 inches (12.7 mm)  (USDA, 1986). The CN method is not suitable for frozen ground (USDA, 
1986).  
 
 
Critics 
Constant curve numbers are not fit for all watersheds, when curve numbers have been determined from 
rainfall runoff data has shown a large variability in curve numbers for similar events for the same catchment 
(Hawkins, 1993). The direct use of tabulated curve numbers has been found very uncertain (Hawkins, 2009). 
There is a parameterization bias towards infiltration excess, when saturation excess is probably a more likely 
runoff mechanism simulated by the CN method (Walter and Shaw, 2005). The CN method is developed for 
agency use without scientific peer review and adopted by practioners (Ponce and Hawkins, 1996). Most of 
the data used for the initial development of the curve number has not been preserved. The maximum 
retention parameter S is given as constant even though it varies from storm to storm, mainly due to soil 
moisture changes  (Hawkins, 2009). Maximum potential retention (S) has been found to vary between rain 
event magnitudes (Hawkins, 1993) The CN method is verry sensitive to the choice of curve number 
especially for smaller magnitude rain events and lower curve numbers close to the threshold of runoff 
generation (Ponce and Hawkins, 1996). Originally the curve numbers were defined for daily rainfall and runoff 
data, the method applies for a 24-hour duration storm, changes in storm duration should require changes in 
curve number  (Meadows, 2016). There are no official storm size limits for the method (Hawkins, 2009).

Scalgo Live 
Scalgo Live is a steady state flood screening tool that uses digital elevation maps (DEMs) to generate detailed 
flow paths. The tool is very user friendly visual and fast, where no input parameters by the user is required. 
Due to its simplicity the tool is primally aimed for initial analysis, it is good for grasping the big picture while 
more complex tools are recommended for more specified analysis. Scalgo Live includes all the main 
geospatial data layers openly available in Denmark.

Depressions 
The depression map in Scalgo live shows depressions in the surface that do not have any natural outlet. The 
depressions will accumulate water when it rains, which is reduced from the total runoff. 
 
Depression-free Flow 
The Watershed depression-free flow tool in Scalgo Live does not take depressions into account when 
defining a catchment area. The watershed depression-free tool shows the entire drainage area upstream of 
a selected point. 
 
Flash Flood Map 
The Watershed flash flood map tool in Scalgo Live calculates surface runoff related to a rain event. The flash 
flood mapping tool first fill the depressions and when the depressions are filled additional water input 
continues downstream. The Flash flood mapping tool allows the user to choose the depth of a rain event. 
The watershed flash Flood mapping tool differs from the depression free tool since it delineates the 
catchment area to an area which contributes to surface runoff for the rain event depth specified. A larger 
rain event leads to a larger area contributing to surface runoff generation, if the rain event is defined close 
to the maximum the catchments are delineated almost as for the depression-free flow. The rain further 
upstream outside the flash-flood map catchment area will only contribute to depression storage upstream. 
 
Curve number method and Scalgo Live 
The original version of SCALGO is a so-called glass plate model meaning that it does not account for 
hydrologic losses. The curve number method will be included to the Flash Flood mapping tool in Scalgo live. 
The watershed flash-flood map tool first calculates the direct runoff with the curve number method and then 
defines the area based on the effective rainfall contributing to runoff. Initially Curve numbers of 98 for 
impervious areas and 74 for pervious areas have been suggested using a lambda 0.1. Runoff will be 
calculated for each pixel in Scalgo, which allows the use of geospatial data for curve number definition. It 
should be noted that Scalgo live is a static tool, not taking antecedent conditions into account. Scalgo Live 
does also not consider intensity of rainfall, a quantity of rainfall is given in mm and corresponding runoff is 
given as a volume

Denmark 
Denmark is a country of 43 000 km2 with a mean elevation of 31 m. Denmark has a coastal climate, the 
weather is mild and humid without radical changes between mean temperatures of winter and summer  
(Cappelen, 2019). The weather is affected both by the European continent and the sea, if the direction of the 
wind comes from the east it typically brings continental weather, warm during summer and cold during 
winter, if the wind comes from west it brings clouds and rain without warm or cold extremes, the westerly 
wind being the most frequent (Cappelen, 2019). Annual average precipitation is 700 mm in the eastern part 
and 800 mm in the western part. In average middle Jutland receives most rain and the region by Kattegat 
the least  (Pedersen et al., 2020). Mean annual temperature is 7.7 C° ranging from 0 C° in January to 16 C° 
in July.  
 
Geologically Denmark can be divided into two main areas based on a line marking the maximal extent of the 
ice during the last Ice age, the western part consisting mainly of sandy glacial outwash plains and Saalian 
moraine and the eastern part consisting of Loamy Weishselian moraine. Most of the eastern and central parts 
are developed in moraine landforms with loamy soils, rich in soil clay content, whereas the western part 
consists of older more eroded landforms and sandy -glacio-fluvial flood plains. The northern parts are of late 
post-glacial marine deposits. The first nation-wide soil database in Denmark was done in the 1980’s, 
simultaneously as the main gas pipe was built providing an opportunity for detailed soil observations.  
 
Agricultural land covers over 60% of the land area in Denmark while forest stands for about 14 %  (EEA, 
2010b). Forest areas have increased due to a governmental afforestation program. Forest is aimed to be 
doubled to 20 – 25 % of the area within the coming 80-100 years (EEA, 2010a). Cereals are grown on 57% 
of the agricultural area, grass and green fodder in rotation on 16%, root crops on 4% and the remaining 
agricultural area is used for pulses, seeds for sowing, horticultural products and fruit trees (Danish Bureau 
of Statistics, 2001). Major crops: wheat, maize, potato and barley and livestock production are very common 
in the west (Adhikari et al., 2014).

streamflow data is converted to discharge from depth measurements with the help of a rating curve. The 
rating curve is not always able to correctly transfer the data especially for more extreme events. The

4 Methods 
The methods section is divided in three main parts. First time series analysis is done for the precipitation and 
streamflow data to define rainfall and runoff events based on set criteria. Secondly different methods for 
curve number definition are set and runoff is calculated, and curve numbers are calculated based on rainfall 
runoff data. Finally, evaluation methods are presented. Spatial data is treated with ArcGIS and the 
calculations are done with Python and excel. 
 
4.1 
Time series analysis 
This part of the methods section goes through the pretreatment of measured precipitation and streamflow 
data to be usable for runoff simulation and validation. The curve number method is a single event method 
and input rain event data is given as a volume in mm. The output of the curve number method, the runoff, is 
also given as a volume in mm.

Several modified methods of the UKIH method have been made depending on the aims of the studies

during the day of quick flow or the previous day.

Precipitation aggregation 
Rain is often referred to as rain events since most of the time is rainless, multiple different criteria for 
separating rain events exist  (Dunkerley, 2008). The curve number method requires rain input as an event 
given as a volume in (mm). Separating rain events is not straightforward especially if rain events occur close 
after each other for a longer time period. Fixed minimum inter event time (MIT) separates rain events based 
on how long MIT is needed before a new rain event starts, the range of MIT in studies ranges between 15 
min to 24 h (Dunkerley, 2008). The amount of time chosen as MIT affects the quantity, duration and depth of 
==PAGE:33==
4 Methods 
 
 
 
23 
rain events and the length of dry periods following a rain event.  Several studies use additional criteria to 
fixed MIT, such as minimum rain event durations and minimum rain event depths (Dunkerley, 2008). Using 
a larger MIT for runoff studies gives a higher number of dry hours between rain events and permits infiltration 
and drying of soil to happen between the rain events resulting in reduced runoff generation (Dunkerley, 
2008). 
 
MIT is used for this study, different MIT’s between 1-7 and 12 hours are investigated for the precipitation 
event aggregation and finally a MIT that fits with the runoff events is chosen. Hourly precipitation data is 
aggregated as an event on the last hour of precipitation defined by the MIT. In this study rain events with 
less than 3 mm of rain are neglected to be able to better separate individual rain events. When rain is 
aggregated to an event the time of the event is set to the last hour of rain. 
 
 
Events from observed quick flow and rain 
Quick flow events that are caused by only one rain event will be combined together. All rain events that end 
within the timeframe 1 hour before the start of the quick flow curve until the end of the quick flow curve are 
considered to contribute to the quick flow curve. 
 
4.2 
Comparison of observed quick flow and modelled runoff 
Runoff is calculated with the CN method with equation (2-4) using rainfall, CN and lambda as input. The CN 
method gives runoff in mm. For comparison also observed quick flow is converted to mm. Observed quick 
flow in l/s is converted to mm using the time of the quick flow curve and the size of the catchment, the 
assumption is that the whole catchment area contributes to the quick flow.  
 
Each observed quick flow event is compared against all runoff events calculated from the rain events falling 
within the timeframe one hour before the start hour of the quick flow event until the end hour of the quick 
flow event.  
 
 
Catchment area contributing to quick flow 
The CN method expects the whole catchment area to contribute to runoff. Scalgo Live has two different tools 
for defining the catchment area, the watershed depression-free flow tool and the watershed Flash flood 
mapping tool as presented in chapter 2.3. Both of the catchment area definitions will be used. The watershed 
Flash flood mapping catchment tool gives the area based on rain event size and ideally the rain event size 
should be according to the observed rain size of each event. For this study a rain event size of 30 mm is 
chosen for all catchments and for all events for catchment area definition with the flash flood mapping tool. 
This area will be used for all events when using the flash flood mapping area even though an individual area 
for each rain event size would be more representable. Hereafter the depression free flow catchment area 
will be referred to as depression free area and the flash flood mapping catchment area will be referred to as 
flash flood area, both area definitions will be used for each catchment for the runoff calculations.

Runoff calculation with uncalibrated curve numbers based on spatial data  
Curve numbers based on spatial data and runoff is calculated for each precipitation event using equations 
(2-4), (2-5) and (2-6). Runoff is calculated with the weighted Q method presented in chapter 2.2.2. Inputs for 
the curve number method are rain events in mm, lambda and curve numbers.  
 
The catchments for this study were chosen with as little impervious areas as possible, the impervious area 
of the depression-free catchments is between 2 to 5 % including waterbodies. The main reason why 
impervious areas should not be included is that the interest of this study lies in pervious areas. The tabulated 
curve number method gives CN 98 for impervious areas, such as paved areas and roofs, however CN 98 
assumes that the impervious areas have a direct connection to the drainage system  (USDA, 1986).  For this 
study the impervious areas should be directly connected to the streams to motivate the use of CN 98 for 
impervious areas. Most of the impervious areas in the catchments of this study are however not expected to 
be directly connected to the stream. The impervious areas are mainly in areas connected to storm sewers 
or discrete locations surrounded by pervious areas. The areas which are covered by combined sewer 
systems could be directly removed from the catchment area since they are not expected to contribute to the 
runoff generation. Separated sewer systems or only stormwater sewer systems, might be led to a stream, 
but it is difficult to find information about to which stream the water will be led and with what retention time.  
 
Seven methods using different datasets or different combinations of spatial data for the choice of curve 
numbers and using different assumptions will be described in the following chapters. The methods presented 
below are uncalibrated and lambda is fixed to 0.1 for all methods. In this study impervious areas are not 
expected to be led directly to a stream, for this reason the curve number for impervious areas including 
waterbodies will be set uniformly to 76 instead of 98 for methods 2 to 5.  
 
Method 1: Impervious and pervious area division 
Method 1 follows the original recommendation of curve numbers for Scalgo Live, dividing the catchments 
into impervious and pervious areas. The curve numbers for impervious and pervious lands are derived from 
tabulated curve numbers. The tabulated curve number for impervious areas is CN 98 regardless of the soil 
type, which is recommended for Scalgo Live. For pervious areas the tabulated curve number tables have 
one category representing specifically green areas in urban areas “Open space (lawns, parks, golf courses, 
cemeteries etc.)”, which is categorized into three hydrologic condition groups, poor less than 50 % grass, 
fair 50 – 70 % grass and good more than 70 % grass as can be seen in appendix Apendix A: (USDA, 1986). 
Green areas in Danish cities would usually fall in the good hydrological category since bare soil is rarely seen  
(Lerer, 2020). The western sandy parts would be given HSG A and the eastern clayey parts would be given 
HSG B or C, HSG C is initially chosen for the whole country, leading to CN 74 for pervious areas seen  (Lerer, 
2020).

4.5 
Evaluation  
Runoff volumes estimated with the methods will be validated against quick flow volumes extracted from 
measured streamflow with the baseflow separation methods. Bias, Mean Absolute error (MAE) and Nash 
Sutcliffe (NSE) are calculated for the different catchments. Bias gives the mean difference between modelled 
and observed runoff, it shows whether the model is underestimating or overestimating, if bias is zero the 
model is unbiased. MAE gives average error between modelled and observed values. NSE is commonly 
used for hydrological models, it can be anything between −∞ to 1, where NSE 1 indicates a perfect fit 
between modelled and observed values, if NSE ≥ 0.5 the model shows a good predictive ability, NSE ≤ 0 
means that the mean of the observed data predicts the model better.

streamflow curve fluctuates a lot with rain input, however the quick flow events remain quite short. As can 
be seen from Figure 5-3 all baseflow separation curves except from the SARR baseflow curve are close to 
zero for the Fredtoften catchment, the catchment has almost no baseflow during summer months. Baseflow 
is more difficult to identify for the Skærvad and Simmelbæk catchments, only average daily streamflow is 
available, which makes it harder to identify the start and end of quick flow curves. Larger time steps are 
needed for these catchments because of the size of the catchments and the availability of only average data. 
For Slåenbæk and Fredtoften catchment either the modified baseflow separation methods using one-day 
and two-day time steps could be chosen. Since quick flow curves caused by many rain events are aimed to

remembered that a large part of agricultural fields in Denmark have subsurface drainage, which affects the

when the soil is dry and can store water for a long time, however when the soil is saturated infiltration is low 
and runoff potential is therefore high (Menberu et al., 2015). Catchments dominated by peat soil have 
compared to mineral soil a higher water storage and shallow ground water (Menberu et al., 2015). Peat has 
for this study been given HSG B classification. Till soils are also missing from the texture based HSG 
classification. Glacial till soils are usually regarded to have low hydraulic conductivity especially for fine 
grained till  (Lind and Lundin, 1990), for this study clayey till has been given a HSG group C and sandy till 
has been given an HSG group B.

Performance of the methods based on spatial data for estimating runoff 
The runoff for the selected events based on the seven different methods is plotted together with observed 
quick flow events and curve number curves in Figure 5-14 for depression-free area and Figure 5-15 for flash 
flood area. It can be noted that the catchment area affects the magnitude of observed quick flow events 
especially for Slåenbæk and Fredtoften catchment where the difference between depression-free area and 
flash flood area is the largest. For example, the same observed event in Slåenbæk catchment which is of 
magnitude 3 mm when using the depression-free area becomes an event of 9 mm with Flash flood area. All 
methods except from method 7 clearly overestimate the modelled runoff for the depression free area. The 
same situation remains for Fredtoften, Skærvad and Simmelbæk catchments with the Flash flood area. 
Events observed in Slåenbæk for flash flood area are scattered around, some are in line with method 3 and 
4, with method 5 and with method 7, and one extreme outlier intersects with the CN curve 90. 
 
Performance parameters calculated for the different methods per catchment are shown in Table 5-13 for 
depression free area and Table 5-14 for flash flood area. Performance is not calculated for Fredtoften, since 
only four events were identified of which two had only a few cubic meters observed quick flow and only one-
hour duration of quick flow. None of the methods perform very well based on the performance parameters. 
This is mainly explained by the large variation between observed quick flow events within the same 
catchment which explains some of the bad performance. Method 7 gives the lowest MAE for each catchment 
with depression free area. For flash flood area method 5 gives the lowest MAE for Slåenbæk and Method 3 
gives the lowest value for Skærvad. MAE is even for the best performing events of similar magnitude as the 
average observed quick flow. Bias shows similar results about the overestimation and underestimation of the 
methods as could be noted from Figure 5-14 and Figure 5-15. NSE does not show good performance for 
any method for any catchment and even gives negative values for the Slåenbæk catchment for both 
depression free and Flash Flood area.

6 Discussion 
The CN method is widely used and has had different rates of success in different studies and at different 
locations. The academic interest towards the method can be noted from the extensive amount of literature 
regarding it and several modifications to the original equation and the original usage of the method have 
been made. Also, many misinterpretations of the model exist (Ogden et al., 2017). No studies using the curve 
number method in Denmark were found, and only a few studies in Nordic countries: in Sweden the CN 
method is implemented in the Swedish HBV-NP model (Pers, 2007). Even though the CN method is popular 
worldwide, the rainfall runoff method has many drawbacks. It has to be remembered that the method is old 
and developed in a different time with different data availability and computing power only giving average 
runoff estimations. The curve number method does not give exact estimates that could be used for planning 
purposes, it rather gives an approximation of impact from different land uses  (Harbor, 1994). Both the 
strength and the weakness of the CN method is its simplicity. 
6.1 
Event selection and catchment area 
The quick flow was separated from streamflow with a modified baseflow separation method based on the 
UKIH and SARR method that aim to find local minima. Baseflow separation is very uncertain and can be seen 
more as art than science  (Koskelo et al., 2012). The methods used in this study assume that a part of the 
streamflow is baseflow, and another part of the streamflow is quick flow. These methods are not actually able 
to define the origin of the flow seen in the hydrograph.  
 
No clear guidelines were found from literature on how rain events should be defined when using the CN 
method, originally the method used daily precipitation data, mainly since more detailed data was not 
available. Daily data is still used by studies e.g. by Menberu et al. (2015) and Harbor (1994), but many studies 
use hourly data with shorter rain event times, however often without information of how the events have been 
defined. The CN method is a single event method where one rain event causes a runoff event, in Texas 
where the method was originally developed in small agricultural catchments single rain events resulting a 
clear runoff event might be common. Denmark, however, has a humid climate with frequent rain resulting in 
compound hydrographs caused by many rain events. For this study a MIT 6 were chosen for rain event 
definition. MIT 6 was chosen since lower MITs often resulted in low depth rain events. Very few quick flow 
events caused by a single rain event could be identified for smaller MIT. MIT 6 allows 6 dry hours between 
rainy hours that is still counted as the same event, this might occasionally lead to rain events of very long 
duration with a low intensity. Bad performance of the CN method has been found when storms have a long 
duration and moderate intensity  (Rozalis et al., 2010), which would support the use of a smaller MIT. To be 
able to find enough events for analyzing from the limited dataset MIT 6 was however chosen. The aim was 
to identify rain events with similar conditions where the runoff response would behave similarly. Only rain 
events larger than 15 mm were considered because the CN method is known to give better results for larger 
rain events. Less than 30 mm antecedent rain during the last 48 hours was also set as a condition so that 
the antecedent soil moisture would be low. Further the year was divided into dormant and growing season, 
of which only growing season was analyzed. The observed quick flow and variability between the events was 
==PAGE:64==
6 Discussion 
 
 
 
54 
higher during the dormant season. The set criteria resulted in only a handful of rain events of moderate size 
between 15 to 37 mm, it was also noted that all events except from one fell into the dry AMC I category.   
 
The CN method is traditionally implemented on an entire catchment. In this study observed quick flow (l/s) 
is converted to runoff (mm) using the duration of the quick flow curve and the catchment area. This approach 
assumes that the whole catchment area uniformly contributes to the runoff at the outlet. Scalgo Live defines 
catchment area with two different tools, the depression free flow tool defining the entire catchment area, and 
the flash flood mapping tool defining the area depending on the rain event size by first filling the sinks after 
which the overflow spills and drains to the outlet as runoff, both methods were used for the study. Scalgo 
Flash flood mapping tool was in this study set constantly to a 30 mm rain event, even though the rain event 
sizes varied and the catchment area should equally have varied between the events. Instead of using a 
correct catchment size the aim was mainly to illustrate how the definition of the catchment affects the study. 
Using the Scalgo Flash flood mapping significantly increased the observed quick flow, as was seen for event 
10 in Slåenbæk catchment where observed quick flow was 2.94 mm when using the depression free area 
and 8.60 mm when using the flash flood area. The times of the observed quick flow curves were found to be 
long in this study even for the smallest Slåenbæk catchment, often the quick flow curves were artificially cut 
if it had not rained more than 1 mm within the antecedent 48 hours.  The long time of the quick flow curves 
could be explained by the runoff being mainly subsurface runoff, many Danish fields are also subsurface 
drained which might affect the runoff generation.  
 
It could be argued that the method in its original form is not very well applied to Danish humid conditions 
with frequent rain events. Most identified quick flow curves were caused by multiple rain events. The 
selection and definition of rain events largely affect the results. The methods used in this study for observed 
rain event and runoff event definition are very simplified and therefore highly uncertain. Further the methods 
are completely based on an automatic code which follows a set criterion. No clear definitions for small and 
large rain events exist for Denmark and intensity should always be considered together with the rain depth 
when categorizing events. The definition for a cloudburst according to DMI is that it rains at least 15 mm 
within 30 minutes. Both the observed rain events and the corresponding quick flow events in this study are 
mostly of rather small magnitude, and therefore not exactly events for which the CN method is developed.  
 
6.2 
Runoff calculations using tabulated and calibrated curve numbers 
The choice of accurate curve numbers is essential for the functioning of the CN method, since the curve 
number together with the parameter is the only input parameters to the model. Especially when rain events 
are small the calculated runoff is more sensitive to CN than to the rain event (Ponce and Hawkins, 1996). 
The methods given in chapter 4.2.2 are based on tabulated curve numbers that are chosen based on spatial 
data of land use and soil type for the runoff calculations. It should be remembered that the tabulated curve 
numbers are developed for a geographically different location and does not necessarily perform well in 
Denmark. Rietz and Hawkins (2001) calculated curve numbers for 15 land use categories from 177 
catchments. Over 95% of the tested catchments had significant differences between curve numbers for 
different land uses and especially land conversion was found to highly affect the curve number (Rietz and 
Hawkins, 2001). The crop types that especially deviated from handbook values were small grain, row crops 
==PAGE:65==
6 Discussion 
 
 
 
55 
and fallow (Rietz and Hawkins, 2001). Tabulated curve numbers could be developed for different locations, 
but it would require extensive research. Since the curve number method is not popular in Denmark, this is 
not feasible. The CN method usually performs well when using calibrated curve numbers and not the 
tabulated curve numbers (Ogden et al., 2017). 
 
The calculated runoff with the different methods does not correlate well with the observed runoff for either 
the depression free catchment area or for the flash flood mapping catchments area. The tabulated curve 
numbers were generally found to be too high. According to Mishra & Singh (2003) the CN method often 
overestimates runoff. Method 7 was found to perform the best for all catchments using the depression free 
flow area. Method 7 is based on a global curve number dataset for ARC I and the global dataset is based on 
the tabulated curve numbers using spatial data. Since all the events except from one would have belonged 
to the dry AMC I group it is logical that Method 7 using ARC I performed the best. The other methods did 
not account for the dry antecedent conditions, which might be the reason for the high curve numbers. 
Method 1 with the curve numbers for pervious and impervious areas recommended for Scalgo Live did not 
perform well overestimating the runoff and therefore an average curve number to represent only pervious 
areas was calculated from rainfall and runoff data. The curve number for impervious areas was significantly 
lowered from the original tabulated CN  98 to 76 since the impervious areas did not seem to be connected 
to the stream. For many events both using the depression free flow catchment area and flash flood mapping 
catchment area, the curve numbers for impervious areas alone stood for more than all of the runoff observed 
in the stream, as can be noted from the curve numbers calculated for pervious areas from rainfall runoff data 
when using the weighted Q method. The impervious areas stood for 2 to 5 % of the depression free 
catchment areas and could also be seen as insignificant. Because of this weighted curve numbers were also 
calculated for the catchments, which can be seen to represent the pervious areas if the impervious areas 
are neglected due to their low ratio. 
 
The calculated curve numbers per event varied significantly between events even within the same 
catchment. The coefficient of variance of curve numbers was only calculated for the weighted curve numbers 
since curve numbers for only pervious areas often resulted in zero, when the impervious areas alone stood 
for all the runoff. The coefficient of variation was lower for Slåenbæk and Fredtoften catchment for the 
depression free area 0.15 and 0.20 respectively than for the flash flood mapping area 0.16 and 0.24 
respectively, where the area was significantly reduced with the flash flood mapping tool. For Skærvad and 
Simmelbæk the coefficient of variance was, 0.09 and 0.10 respectively for both depression free and flash 
flood mapping area. 
 
The average of the weighted curve number for Slåenbæk catchment was tried on both the depression free 
area (CN 58) and the flash flood area (CN 64). The calculated weighted curve numbers for depression free 
flow performed worse than Method 7 based on the global CN layer with ARC I. For flash flood mapping 
Method 7 performed better than the calculated weighted CN for Simmelbæk and Skærvad and for Slåenbæk 
method 5 performed better than the calculated weighted CN. The average calculated weighted CN from 
Slåenbæk catchment did not even perform better in Slåenbæk catchment than in Skærvad and Simmelbæk 
catchments, the reason for this is probably the high variance of CN within the Slåenbæk catchment. Even 
==PAGE:66==
6 Discussion 
 
 
 
56 
though method 7 performed a bit better than the calibrated curve number drawing a conclusion that spatial 
data improves runoff estimations would be careless, since the performance of neither is good. 
 
It is not surprising that the performance of the different methods and the calculated weighted CN is not 
especially good By looking at the observed rainfall runoff plots Figure 5-14 and Figure 5-15, it can be 
observed that the events don’t follow any quick flow curve but are instead spread mainly between curves 50 
to 80. No curve number combination could give accurate runoff for events with this large variability.  
 
When calculating CN values from rainfall runoff data the values will be different for any catchment for different 
storms  (Hosseini and Mahjouri, 2018). The runoff response to rain events naturally varies significantly even 
for events of same depth. The curve number method completely neglects intensity, which significantly affects 
the runoff generation. Initial losses by the CN method are lumped together and given by a constant 
percentage of the Storage value that is based on CN. In reality however initial losses are many complex 
processes affected by different variables such as rain intensity and antecedent conditions of i.e., soil 
moisture.  
 
The rain events in this study were small or moderate events and often close to the threshold for runoff 
generation depending on the specific rain event size and curve number used (see Figure 5-12). For large 
rainfall depths and high curve numbers the sensitivity towards CN decreases, for events with lower CN the 
sensitivity towards the rainfall event is low because of the high amount of infiltration, for CN lower than 30 
the sensitivity to rainfall is almost inexistent  (Hosseini and Mahjouri, 2018). When the rain event is less than 
20 mm runoff is highly sensitive to CN not to rain, a 10 % variation in CN leads to 50 % variation in runoff 
(Hawkins, 1975). When rain events are close to the threshold for runoff generation, errors in CN estimation 
often lead to bad performance (Hawkins, 1975). Rozalis et al. (2010) used uncalibrated curve numbers based 
on spatial data to find out if a runoff event was categorized as extreme, moderate or low, and found good 
performance for detecting extreme events from high rain events but worse performance on detecting low 
and moderate runoff events from low and moderate rain events. Tabulated curve numbers were also used 
in a study in the Czech Republic but concluded that both CN and lambda should rather be seen as flexible 
site-specific values (Caletka et al., 2020). 
 
6.3 
Curve number implementation in Scalgo Live 
Using the curve number method for Scalgo Live differs from its original use. In Scalgo Live the CN method 
is used as an infiltration equation where the runoff given by the CN equation is the effective rainfall, the 
catchment area is then defined based on the effective rainfall after which sinks are filled and the remaining 
rainfall is given as runoff. Depressions are counted twice as a hydrologic loss both by the CN method and 
by the Scalgo Live algorithm when routing the flow. To decrease the effect of double counting the 
depressions, the depression storage calculated by the CN method can be decreased by decreasing lambda 
which will decrease the initial abstraction and by using higher curve numbers. Depression storage calculated 
by Scalgo Live can be expected to be much more accurate than the depression storage calculated by the 
curve number method, since Scalgo uses detailed topographical maps. Since depression storage is not part 
==PAGE:67==
6 Discussion 
 
 
 
57 
of the CN method when it is used in Scalgo Live it can be considered to model the losses mainly caused by 
infiltration but also interception loss.   
 
Average curve numbers per catchment calculated for Scalgo Live are between 76 and 89 when calculated 
for pervious areas only by fixing CN to 98 for impervious area and 77 to 91 when calculating the weighted 
CN regarding the impervious areas as insignificantly small and not connected to the stream. The variance 
between the curve numbers for Scalgo Live was smaller than for depression free and flash flood areas 
especially for Slåenbæk and Fredtoften catchments. The coefficient of variance for weighted CN’s was 0.09 
for Slåenbæk, 0.07 for Fredtoften, 0.06 for Skærvad and 0.08 for Simmelbæk.  
 
Due to the Scalgo Live flash flood mapping catchment area definition the effective rainfall from the whole 
catchment area can actually be expected to drain to the outlet. The catchment areas that are calculated by 
Scalgo flash flood mapping tool for the effective rain are very small and therefore allow the use of curve 
numbers that are much higher than if the curve numbers are used for the entire depression free flow 
catchment. 
 
The CN method fits in many ways very well for runoff estimation with Scalgo Live. Scalgo Live and the CN 
method have some similarities, both are static one event mehods using rain depth without including intensity 
as input for the runoff estimation. The CN method gives runoff volume per pixel and Scalgo Live routes the 
runoff, the CN method and Scalgo Live complement each other in this way and can together improve the 
results for runoff prediction. Using the CN method in Scalgo decreases the overestimation of flooded areas. 
In this study the rain events were categorized by being small with previous dry conditions and during the 
growing season. The magnitude of curve numbers might be different for events with different characteristics. 
Wet antecedent moisture conditions during the dormant season probably require even higher curve 
numbers. On the other hand, curve numbers for small rainfall events are often biased to high values 
(Hawkins, 1993). When curve numbers are calculated from rainfall and runoff data the curve numbers usually 
decrease with larger rain events until they reach an equilibrium  (Hawkins, 1993). However, it seems like the 
curve numbers in Scalgo Live could be higher than the originally recommended 74. An average of the 
calculated curve numbers for pervious areas from Slåenbæk, Skærvad and Simmelbæk catchment, CN 81 
is recommended to be used for pervious areas in Scalgo live. It should be noted that for events with higher 
antecedent moisture condition, for example during the winter the, curve number would probably need to be 
higher. 
If different curve numbers for land use and soil type in Denmark would be found to improve the runoff 
estimations these could easily be implemented into Scalgo Live. This study did however not find sufficient 
proofs that that would be the case with the current data availability. For now, it can be concluded that using 
a single curve number for pervious areas does already significantly improve the results compared to the 
glass-plate model at least for similar rain events as studied here. However, it has to be remembered that 
both Scalgo Live and the curve number method give approximations of runoff generation. Runoff generation 
is a complicated process affected by various uncertain factors.

6.4 
Limitations 
This study uses only open data. Even though the availability of data is constantly increasing it is still limited 
and data availability has been a restricting factor for this study. The availability of streamflow data has limited 
the definition of study catchments. Several small pervious catchments with hourly data were wanted, but 
streamflow data was not found. Streamflow data was also limited to a short number of years, for each study 
catchment the available number of years was used, which varied between 2 to 8 years.  
Precipitation data was in this study expected to be spatially uniform over a large area including the closest 
rain gauge and the whole catchment. In reality the rainfall is not uniform over the whole catchment, rain 
events can be very local. The rain gauges used for each catchment were situated 4 to 23 km from the 
catchment outlet. For more reliable results it could be motivated to use several rain gauges and calculate an 
average for example by using the Theisen’s polygon method or to use radar data. The expectation of spatially 
uniform rain might significantly have affected the results.  
 
Several factors affecting the streamflow response to a rain event are not considered in the original CN 
method, such as seasonal differences. From this study it was clear that curve numbers during the dormant 
season should be higher than during the growing season. Other factors that affect the streamflow response 
are subsurface drained agricultural fields. In Denmark a large part of the agricultural fields are drained with 
subsurface drainage. Subsurface drainage affects the hydrologic conditions in many ways for example in 
higher flood peaks and higher CN for higher rain events (Yuan, Hirschi and Cooke, 2001).

7 Conclusions 
 
 
 
59 
7 Conclusions 
Open data availability is constantly increasing but lacks in availability still exist. No official land use maps and 
soil type maps with topsoil’s that are frequently updated was found for Denmark. Limited availability of 
streamflow and rain event data affected the choice of study catchments. Catchments found for the study 
were Slåenbæk, Fredtoften, Skærvad and Simmelbæk. 
 
The identification of rain and corresponding runoff events was not found to be straightforward, and many 
assumptions had to be made to identify events, that could be used for the curve number method. Streamflow 
can be divided into quick flow, which is the direct response to a rain event and baseflow, which is not directly 
related to a rain event. The baseflow index gives the baseflow ratio of the total observed streamflow. Baseflow 
was found to stand for the largest part of the streamflow in the study catchments, the baseflow index was 
0.77 in Slåenbæk, 0.82 in Fredtoften, 0.90 in Skærvad and 0.85 in Simmelbæk. Rain events where identified 
from time series data by aggregating rain to an event if the time between the rain was not larger than 6 hours. 
Observed quick flow events were plotted together with curve number curves but were not found to clearly 
follow any curve. Events during the growing season were found to scatter around lower curve number curves 
than events during the dormant season. Events during the growing season were chosen for further 
investigation.  
 
Expecting the entire depression-free catchment area to contribute to quick flow by the catchment outlet, was 
found to be unrealistic for non-extreme rain events, especially if the catchment is flat with many large 
depressions. The Watershed flash flood mapping tool in Scalgo Live gives catchment areas based on the 
specific rain event size, considering only the area that contributes to the runoff downstream and not the area 
contributing only to depressions upstream. Catchment areas for a 30 mm rain event with Flash flood mapping 
tool were found to be significantly smaller than the entire depression free catchment areas. The flash flood 
area was for Slåenbæk 34 %, Fredtoften 11 %, Skærvad 79 % and Simmelbæk 76% of the depression free 
area. The catchment area was found to affect the magnitude of the observed quick flow since the flow has 
to be converted to mm using the catchment area to be comparable with the modelled runoff. 
 
Curve numbers based on tabulated curve numbers by the Natural Soil Conservation Services were chosen 
for seven methods using different combinations of spatial data. The tabulated curve numbers were often 
found to be too high, especially when using the depression free catchment area. None of the methods 
performed well, mainly overestimating the runoff. Method 7 using a global dataset of curve numbers for low 
runoff conditions that are based on tabulated curve numbers using spatial open data was found to perform 
best for the depression free flow catchment areas. Observed average and MAE when using the depression 
free flow area was 0.61 mm and 0.45 mm for Slåenbæk, 0.28 mm and 0.24 mm for Skærvad and 0.08 mm 
and 0.07 mm for Simmelbæk. According to the Nash Sutcliffe efficiency none of the methods was able to 
satisfactorily, estimate the runoff. Simmelbæk had the best Nash Sutcliffe efficiency, 0.23, when using 
method 7.  
 
==PAGE:70==
7 Conclusions 
 
 
 
60 
Use of higher curve numbers for impervious areas was not found to be motivated, if the percentage of the 
impervious area is small and not connected to the stream. Instead, when calculating curve numbers from 
rainfall and runoff data the whole catchment area can be considered pervious and a weighted curve number 
can be calculated for each event. Average weighted curve numbers for the depression free catchment area 
ranged between 52 an 65. Weighted curve numbers calculated from rainfall and runoff data did not show 
better performance than method 7 based on tabulated curve numbers. The variance between the weighted 
curve numbers calculated from rainfall and runoff data was so large within the catchments, that an average 
could not represent all events. The coefficient of variance for weighted CN was 0.15 for Slåenbæk, 0.20 for 
Fredtoften, 0.09 for Skærvad and 0.10 for Simmelbæk for the depression-free flow. 
 
Curve numbers were also calculated reversely from observed rainfall and runoff data for Scalgo Live. When 
using Scalgo Live, the catchment areas become very small, since they are delineated based on the effective 
rainfall instead of the observed rainfall. Significantly higher curve numbers were calculated for Scalgo Live 
than when using the CN method in its traditional form, caused by the smaller catchment area and because 
Scalgo Live calculates depression storage. To avoid double counting the CN method should not include 
depression storage, leading to the need for higher curve numbers. Average curve numbers for pervious 
areas per catchment when using Scalgo Live was 82 for Slåenbæk, 89 for Fredtoften, 85 for Skærvad and 
76 for Simmelbæk. Average weighted curve numbers were 83 for Slåenbæk, 91 for Fredtoften, 86 for 
Skærvad and 77 for Simmelbæk. The coefficient of variation was between 0.06 and 0.09 for both weighted 
curve numbers and curve numbers for only pervious areas. Higher curve numbers were in average found 
for all catchments than the curve number 74 proposed to Scalgo Live.  
 
This study did not find improved performance by adding spatial data compared with the dividing the area 
into pervious and impervious areas. The gradual filling of depressions in a catchment was found to 
significantly affect the catchment area that contributes to runoff by the outlet. The catchment area affects 
the magnitude of the total volume of runoff by the outlet when using the curve number method. Low curve 
numbers were calculated based on rainfall and runoff data when using the whole catchment area, high curve 
numbers were found when calculating curve numbers for Scalgo Live mainly since the catchment area was 
significantly smaller and to avoid double counting of depression storage. The CN method was not found to 
perform well in Denmark in its traditional form. The CN method however fits well for Scalgo Live, since the 
inputs are similar and the CN method and Scalgo Live complement each other when calculating the runoff. 
Based on this study a curve number for pervious areas in Scalgo Live would be recommended to be 81.

8 Future work

8 Future work 
Further studies could focus on using more extreme events, using for example storm events with one-year 
return period. Data availability is expected to improve in the close future, radar data that will be available by 
DMI later this year 2021 could be used for rain event definition. Furthermore, antecedent moisture condition 
should be looked into in more detail and for example historical monthly groundwater levels close to surface 
could be considered. This study focused on events during the growing season but events during the dormant 
season would need to be investigated. Scalgo Live is constantly being updated and the curve number 
method is being implemented, when possible it would be interesting to try the CN method in Scalgo Live, by 
giving curve numbers in Scalgo based on land use and calculating the runoff with the flash flood mapping 
tool.
