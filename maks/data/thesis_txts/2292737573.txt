Abstract 
 
 
This project makes an investigation of the possibility of combining an anorectal manometry device with a 
BK medical 2052 endo-anal transducer. To be able to come up with an answer for this topic, research of 
medical and clinical perspective of anorectal manometry is needed to create an understanding of this 
examination method. Another important thing this project studies is the competitive products solutions 
currently at market along with a research for other sensor option, which could be utilized as a new possible 
way of measuring anorectal manometry. 
This entire research process will result in a selection of one or more sensing concepts, and these concepts 
needs to be investigated, designed into concept models and tested in different test case setups. 
The design and implementation process of mechanics, hardware and software for these concept models 
will be completed by utilizing the Rapid Prototyping development method, which will ensure a fast and 
enlightening development phase. 
And finally the test results from the testing of these designed concept models will make it possible to 
conclude whether or not it would be possible and valuable to make a combined solution of anorectal 
manometry and ultrasound scanning.

ultralydsscanning.

1.1 Background 
This section will sketch the background for the feasibility study of a manometric solution for a BK Medical 
endo-anal ultrasound transducer by briefly explaining the reason for using a manometric solution, what it is 
and why to combine it with a BK Medicals endo-anal ultrasound transducer. 
1.1.1 BK Medical’s combinational reasoning 
BK Medical ApS is a company that mainly specializes in ultrasound transducers for various medical 
purposes. They have a history of being the first one the market with many solutions and a reputation of 
developing very high quality product solutions. 
The reason, why BK Medical wants to make a feasibility study of a manometric solution for a BK Medical 
endo-anal ultrasound transducers and not just make a separate instrument, is that they want to make a 
solution for doing an anorectal manometric procedure, while they are doing e.g. ultrasound scanning for 
e.g. bowel cancer or abnormalities such as fistula or cancer in the anal canal with the 2052 endo-anal 
transducer shown in Figure 1.1-1: 
 
Figure 1.1-1 Anorectal 3D 2052 ultrasound transducer1 
The 2052 endo-anal ultrasound transducer is made with a mechanical device that allows both vertical and 
horizontal scanning, and if some sort of manometric solution could be applied affectively somewhere on 
this transducer without (or just minimally) affecting the ultrasound data and without applying too much or 
any extra discomfort at all to the patient, it would make two different examinations as comfortable as one 
and thereby reduce the discomfort both physically and mentally for a patient with the need of both 
examinations. 
Ultrasound is able to detect both fistula and cancer2, which emphasis the question of why a manometric 
measurement has to be applied in the first place. The reasoning for applying manometric measurements 
for these examinations is to examine the active part/length of anal canal and thereby the anal sphincters 
(refers to internal and external sphincter muscle, which are circular muscles around the anal canal. These 
muscles help contain fecal continence both manually and automatically). By knowing the length of the 
active part of the anal canal surgeons can give a good estimation of the side effects of a fistula operation.

Such operation is made by cutting the anal sphincters open from the anus and up to the fistula and, when 
that is done, drain the fistula for infection, which will lead to a degeneration of the fistula. This is a very 
tough operation for the patient, but by knowing the active length of the anal canal the surgeon is able to 
estimate the outcome of the operation for the patient, and they can find a solution for the fistula condition, 
which is a chronically painful condition. Due to the pain patients, well aware of the estimated risks, are 
willing to sacrifice fecal continence to a certain degree. But it is very important to be able to estimate 
approximately what degree of fecal incontinence, the patient can expect to become, and this can only be 
done by knowing the active length of the anal canal of the patient. 
1.1.2 Fecal incontinence 
Fecal incontinence (FI3) is a humiliating and devastating condition to be in for any human of all ages. People 
suffering from a FI condition are e.g. women experienced menopause with severe pelvic muscular and 
neurologic injuries. It is also a commonly known problem for women gone through a vaginal birth 
experience, which includes a massive amount of strain and stress in the pelvic floor region perhaps with 
direct sphincter trauma or neuropathic injuries. And as mentioned earlier this condition can also be caused 
by anorectal surgeries. 
The main reason, why the FI is such a devastating condition to be in, is the lack of control of the anal 
sphincters due to the injuries at these muscles, which can lead to fecal leaking and thereby the inability to 
be too far from a toilet at all time (with respect to how severe the state of the condition is). 
1.1.3 Anorectal manometry 
The active length of the anal canal as well as the origin of a FI condition can both be detected with different 
methods, where one of these is the manometric test method. The manometric test method is a method 
that measures the pressure of both the internal and external anal sphincter in different scenarios: 
- 
The patient tries to relax both anal sphincters as much as possible. 
- 
The patient tries to squeeze both anal sphincters as much as possible, as if the patient is trying to 
keep the measuring catheter inside the anal canal. 
- 
The patient tries to press, as if the patient is trying to make a bowel movement. 
There are also different manometric techniques for taking these pressure measurements, which are: 
- 
the stationary technique, where a catheter of some sort is put into one position in the anal canal 
and contains this position through the entire test. 
- 
the stationary pull-trough technique, where a catheter again is put into the anal canal, but apart 
from the stationary technique, it will sample data for each centimeter interval while the catheter is 
pulled out of the anal canal. 
- 
the continuous pull-through technique, is much like the stationary pull-through technique, where 
the catheter is pulled through the anal canal, the difference comes with the measurements, which 
are taken continuously and the pulling of the catheter has to be done in a fixed speed (e.g. by a 
machine at 25 mm/s). 
                                                          
3 http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC2780206/

There are already several different hardware solutions for doing these anorectal manometric 
measurements, where the most common would be: 
- 
fluid-filled and air-filled balloon systems, where a small balloon in inserted in rectum, and then the 
patient should try to expel the balloon from the rectum through bowel movements. The time used 
to expel the balloon is recorded and afterwards used for calculations of the capability of the anal 
sphincters. 
- 
water-perfused catheters are in this content tubes with a fixed water pressure kept from the water 
supply. This water pressure can be measured at all time, and an externally added pressure will 
cause a momentarily change in these pressure measurements. To keep a fixed water pressure 
inside the tubes, the solution is design with an overflow valve. This valve allows the tubes to leak a 
continuous amount of pressure (a few drops pr. second). This is a relatively cheap way of 
measuring pressure, and due to the fact that it does not require electrical sensors it is completely 
safe for the patient as well. The drawback of this equipment is that it is rather uncomfortable for 
the patient to experience the feeling of water leaking in the anal canal, but that is only mental 
discomfort in the beginning until the patient has gotten used to the feeling and is convinced that 
this feeling is only due to the water and not fecal seepage. 
- 
solid-state catheters are arranged with microtip catheters mounted radially on them. A microtip 
catheter is small, electrical, one-directional pressure sensor. It is very accurate within the 
measuring area (orthogonally to the surface of the probe). It is relatively expensive compared to 
water-perfused catheters, but it much easier to setup and use for people who are not trained to 
use water-perfused catheters.

1.2 Motivation 
As mentioned in chapter 1.1 this project has a great potential for both anorectal preoperative patients and 
patients suffering from the condition, FI. This section will pinpoint the motivational factors for BK Medical 
ApS as well as doctors and surgeons utilizing manometry and ultrasound as well. 
1.2.1 The motivational factors 
The motivational factor for this project in long term perspective is to look for a possible way of optimizing 
anorectal surgeries for e.g. fistula and examination and treatment of FI. This could be done by making a 
combination between anorectal manometry and endo-anal ultrasound. 
This combination is already possible but it is not that beneficial due to the fact that the two data types are 
hard to compare, because they are measured with two separate probes and the location of these probes 
are not that well known. 
 
Figure 1.2-1 Anorectal manometry 3-D visualization of test results from both rest and active squeeze measurements4 
Figure 1.2-1 shows what is already possible within anorectal manometry, where the examination data can 
be visualized as a 3-D plot. This visualization makes it very intuitively for doctors and surgeons to examine 
these data for the entire anal canal, because such data plot gives both the periphery and the length of the 
anal canal, and that makes it a lot easier to localize local maxima and local minima of pressure inside the 
anal canal and thereby find the active length of the anal canal. 
By making a manometric examination, it is possible to see, how much pressure the canal sphincter muscles 
applies and where, but this examination can’t see, why the pressure is as it is at a given place. 
On the other hand with an ultrasound examination it is possible to search for abnormalities, which perhaps 
could cause a problem for the active length of the anal canal, or a tumor or fistula that has to be removed. 
Figure 1.2-2 shows the great features of a 3-D visualization/simulation of an endo-anal ultrasound scanning. 
It is possible to look at the anal canal radially and longitudinally as well as moving in and out of 
visualization/simulation, which gives access to data for each layer both radially and longitudinally.

The problem as stated in chapter 1.1 is though, that it is not possible from anorectal ultrasound alone to 
evaluate what level of incontinence an upcoming anorectal surgery will leave the patient in. 
The motivational factor for BK Medical for coming up with a concept for making an anorectal manometric 
evaluation of a patient while doing an endo-anal ultrasound scanning is to be able to evaluate the active 
anal canal length, while scanning for reasons why this active anal canal length is as long as it is. By 
combining the two measurements in one probe/transducer there will be a perfect synchronization of the 
location of the pressure compared to the ultrasound image. This synchronization makes it possible to see 
how the anal canal looks like and exactly what pressure is applied from which location6. 
With these combined synchronized sets of knowledge available it could be possible for surgeons and 
doctors to give a quite good estimation of the side effects with e.g. a fistula operation due to the 
postoperative level of incontinence for the patient. 
Another thing, which could become a reality with a synchronized set of manometric and ultrasound data, 
would be the possibility to detect FI and perhaps its origin. If that origin was a fractured sphincter muscle or 
a region of scar tissues due to e.g. vaginal birth experience with severe trauma on an anal sphincter, it 
would be possible to locate this damaged part of an anal sphincter muscle and treat this exact location with 
e.g. a stem-cell-like culture injection. 
1.2.2 Vision 
The vision for this project is to do a feasibility study for using sensing options on a BK Medical endo-anal 
transducer, 2052, to detect the pressure applied from both internal and external anal sphincter, and to 
design one or more concept models to be able to make a complete conclusion of whether or not this 
combinational concept is a way of going, and if so which measuring concepts would be applicable.

1.3 Problem formulation 
The task in hand for this project is to make a feasibility study of a manometric solution for a BK Medical 
endo-anal transducer, 2052, and come up with one or more concept models to verify the theoretical 
assumptions. 
To be able to understand the entire concept of anorectal manometry, how it is used and why, this 
feasibility study needs to describe subjects like: 
- 
fecal incontinence, what it is and how to detect and treat it 
- 
anorectal physiology 
- 
clinical anorectal manometry examination, what it is and how it is performed 
With the foundation for this feasibility study laid, this project needs to dive into current hardware solutions 
at the market for manometric examinations and explore pros and cons for these current solutions. This 
exploration needs to be done to be able to make a qualified research for other perhaps possible sensor 
options to make a possible combined manometry and ultrasound solution for a BK Medical 2052 ultrasound 
transducer. 
When this entire research is done there has to be selected one or more concepts for making manometric 
examination in combination with ultrasound examination. This or these concepts should be tested by 
designing (a) concept model(s). A concept model should be able to handle a test specific case, measure 
externally applied pressure and save and/or make a visualization of the collected test measurements. 
To sum up what needs to be done to come up with a possible solution for this task, the report needs to 
cover the following: 
1. Description of fecal incontinence in general, the infective mechanisms of this condition, 
assessment, investigation and management of this condition. 
2. Layout of the anorectal physiology. 
3. Description of the clinical procedure of a manometric test, what is tested and how it is tested. 
4. Description of the current hardware solution for manometric already on the marked with different 
number of measuring channels. 
5. Theoretical evaluation of concepts for measuring anorectal pressure. 
6. Description of the investigated equipment and concepts, their features and usability. 
7. Description of method as it was used for the design and implementation of both mechanics and 
hardware for (a) concept model(s). 
8. Costumer evaluation and feedback on designed and considered concept model(s). 
9. Description of method as it was used for the design and implementation of software for (a) concept 
model(s). 
10. Display and explanation of diagrams for software overview. 
11. Description of test cases for the concept model(s) and the results of those tests. 
12. Discussion of challenges during the project, test results and the value of the final implementations. 
 
By completing the listed tasks it will be possible to evaluate the value of applying a manometric solution on 
a BK Medical endo-anal transducer, 2052.

1.4 Methods and limitations 
This project has a duration of 18 work weeks, which means that developing a complete product solution is 
out of the question. This project is primarily a feasibility study, which means that the concept model(s) for 
this project will be designed in the end second half of the project period due to the fact that a field study is 
needed to make valuable evaluations of current products and selections of future anorectal manometric 
measurement concepts. The concept modeling will be done in a similar way as the rapid prototyping 
method7. 
Rapid prototyping (RP) is a developing approach that focusing on quickly designing a working prototype 
exploring the needed product requirements instead of preparing requirements and design documents in a 
predesign phase, which is the case for a more conventional approach like the Waterfall model. RP is an 
approach that is well suited for projects, where the final product requirements and/or design are not that 
well known. RP is a learning approach, where users are involved very early in the process and is kept 
involved for the entire development phase. This will provide valuable feedback continuously during the 
development phase. In the development phase multiple iterations of a prototype (or concept model in our 
case) are made and evaluated in cooperation with the users. 
When using the RP method it is important to decide what purpose the prototype should have after the last 
iteration of it has been completed. The three most common purposes are: the evolutionary prototype, 
where the prototype through the iterations evolves into a final product; the experimental prototype, where 
the prototype helps exploring the feasibility of an idea, component or design; and the build-and-replace 
prototype, where the prototype serves as a complete behavioral specification, where an operational 
solution can be built from. This is also known as throw-away-prototyping, not because the prototype is 
thrown away, but because the final product is built from scratch after the development of the prototype. 
Figure 1.4-1 shows an overview of all phases of the RP method. The first phase in RP is the project 
definition, which has already made for this entire project in this chapter, but for this project there will be 
applied a RP approach for different hardware and software design developments. For these part of the 
project an individual project definition in the form of a small project management plan (PMP) will be made 
(these will be found in chapter 3.1.1, 3.2.1, 3.4.1, 4.1.1, 4.2.1 and 4.3.1). These PMPs will state; what is to 
be prototyped, specific goals for this development phase, the purpose and the functionalities of the 
prototype. 
The second phase in RP is the tool selection, this is where the hardware components are selected as well as 
the abstraction level of the software language is chosen. The hardware components needs to be kept as 
simple and as general as possible to make it possible to implement fast and to adapt it to changes further 
on in the process. The main tool selection will be stated in chapter 2.3.2, where all sensing options are 
chosen. For the software language a high level of abstraction (like Python or Perl) is often chosen to make 
the development as fast as possible. In this project the programming language will be “C”, which does not 
have as high level of abstraction as wanted for RP. The abstraction level has been compensated for by using 
an Arduino development platform, which has large libraries to help simplifying the development process. 
With this step completed the method will enter, what is called, the RP loop (marked with a red circle on 
Figure 1.4-1). 
                                                          
7 http://www.astm.org/Standards/E1340.htm (Standard Guide for Rapid Prototyping of Information Systems)

The RP loop will be seen in chapter 3.1.2, 3.2.2, 3.4.2, 4.1.2, 4.2.2 and 4.3.2 (plus the testing in 5.1-4), 
where the entire development phase of both hardware and software will be described along with the 
testing of selected iterations of those. 
First step in the RP loop is the analysis. This step is where the task in hand gets analyzed to come up with a 
product behavior for the prototype. As the iterations get completed this product behavior becomes more 
and more clear, which leads to fewer changes to the upcoming prototype. This step can only be repeated 
until there are no more inputs or questions to the product behavior, which have a need for the user to get 
answered through another prototype-iteration. 
The second step in the loop is the synthesis, here the questions and inputs from the analysis will be taken 
into account and modifications to the prototype will be added to solve these questions and inputs. It is very 
important to keep an open structure in this step to keep it easy and fast to make additions, subtractions 
and changes to the prototype. It is also very important to document all development and change to the 
prototype to be able to backtrack a process if an error occurs later on. The documentation could consist of 
e.g. “system development notes”, these are about what decisions was made, options considered and what 
has been changed in the development process along with what should be reviewed. It is also a good thing 
to add drawings of both hardware and software design to visualize the changes and additions made to the 
prototype. 
The third step of the loop is the construction, which builds what was synthesized in the previous step. This 
is where new components and designs are added and other removed and tested. This step is all about 
==PAGE:18==
Side 18 af 103 
 
speed to keep the development going and concepts and designs fresh in memory for both developers and 
users. 
The last step in the loop is the evaluation. This is where reviews of both hardware and software take place 
along. It is also here the RP method has its most valuable feature, which is the user feedback, because this 
step is where the user gets to see, what has been designed, try it, ask questions about it and come up with 
suggestions for further improvements. (Most of the time the users in this project will be R&D people from 
BK Medical and once an anorectal specialist team of two doctors will be attending an evaluation of the 
latest iteration of the concept model(s).) 
The last 2-3 steps following the RP loop will not be implemented in this project because there will only be 
developed concept model(s) which is(/are) not designed to be implemented in a operating product as the 
2052 ultrasound transducer, but they will be explained briefly. 
The first two steps after the RP loop, design and system implementation, is where inefficient components 
are changed to perhaps more complex and integrated components to improve the system performance. 
The design implementation is made if the product has to be built from scratch with the prototype as the 
specification. If the prototype on the other hand has evolved into the final product it only needs a system 
implementation, where it perhaps is integrated into a larger system. In both steps though there will be 
tests of the implementations before moving on.

2.1 Fecal incontinence 
This section will cover the general principles for fecal incontinence, selected parts of the anorectal anatomy 
and its surroundings. Afterwards it will look at some of the originations of this condition, external 
assessment of the problem area, internal investigational methods of the anorectum and some of the 
treatment options, there are to be found at present time. 
2.1.1 Fecal incontinence in general 
Fecal incontinence8 (FI) is defined as a condition, where the patient with the condition is suffering from 
involuntary fecal passage through rectum. 
Clinically the condition is split into three categories: 
1. Passive incontinence, a condition with an inability to control and detect discharge of stool and/or 
gas sometimes. 
2. Urge incontinence, a condition with an inability to control fecal discharge even with active attempts 
to retain the bowel content. 
3. Fecal seepage, a condition where the patient experience leakage of stool and has to go elsewhere 
and make a regular evacuation of bowel content. 
 
FI as a condition have a wide span of severity, from the patient involuntarily expels a flatus to worst case 
scenario, where the patient experience fecal leakage or even uncontrolled complete evacuation of bowel 
content. This is why, FI is a condition to be taken very seriously, first because of the physical complications 
but even worse is the psychological aspect of this condition, which can cause severe humiliation for the 
patient and lead to lowered self-esteem and quality of life and even social isolation. 
A 10 years old report[8] made a very clear point of FI being a somewhat big issue not only for the patient 
physically and mentally but also economically for both patient and community. As mentioned in chapter 
1.1.1 the two primary groups of people, who suffers from FI, is women past menopause and with stressful 
vaginal deliverance experiences. In both UK and US the prevalence of FI spans from 1 to 7.4 percent in an 
otherwise healthy population, and for the institutionalized elderly this prevalence was up to 25 %. 
The report states that in US there was spend annually  ~$400 million on adult diapers, and for the 
institutionalized elderly there was spend annually $1.5-7 billion for incontinence (both fecal and urinal). 
Looking at the outpatient setting in the US there was spend $17,166 annually. This is a relatively large 
amount of money already, but what is really import to mention is that due to the major embarrassment 
following this condition, the patient can become social dysfunctional and inactive for the job market, which 
will lead to a cost for the community. The embarrassment can also cause the patient to wait up to several 
years before requesting for treatment, which can result in the FI condition to be worsened and thereby 
more expensive to control/maintain the level of severity and even try to cure. 
2.1.2 Anorectal physiology 
A normal fecal continence is primarily contained by the neuro-musculature of rectum, anus and adjoining 
pelvic floor with the puborectalis as an important pelvic floor muscle for this purpose.

Starting from the top of Figure 2.1-1 there is the rectum. The rectum is a tube-like muscular composition of 
continuous layers of longitudinal muscles, which cross underlying circular muscles. This muscular design 
enables the rectum to function both as storage for stool and as a contracting/pumping element for a fecal 
discharge routine. 
Surrounding the rectum and anal canal are the anal sphincters. There are two of these anal sphincters, the 
internal sphincter and the external sphincter. The internal anal sphincter is not really a surrounding part of 
the rectum, but more like an expansion of 3-5 mm of the circular muscle layer of the rectum itself. The 
external anal sphincter is on the other hand surrounding the lower rectum and anal canal, it is a 6-10 mm 
expansion of a muscle called the striated levator ani muscle. 
At the lower end of the anal canal comes the anus, which is a 2-4 cm long tube-like muscle. In normal 
conditions the anus is kept closed by a tonic activity of the internal anal sphincter. This form of closing can 
be reinforced by a voluntary squeeze of the external anal sphincter performed by the patient itself in an 
active attempt to retain the content of the bowel. 
The way Figure 2.1-1 have placed the anus compared to the rectum is most likely for a situation, where the 
patient is trying to defecate, because in a state of straining the angle between rectum and anus is 
approximately about 110-130 degrees, which makes it easier to expel fecal material. The angle between 
anus and rectum is called the anorectal angle. The anorectal angle differs between different anorectal 
stages. For an anorectal resting stage the anorectal angle will approximately be 90 degrees, and during a 
voluntary squeeze to actively retaining stool the anorectal angle will decrease even further to around 70 
degrees.

2.1.3 Infective mechanisms and causes 
Chapter 2.1.2 points out multiple mechanisms submitting to maintaining the fecal continence. If one or 
more of these mechanisms are disrupted in a level of severity, that other mechanisms are not able to 
compensate for, the patient will suffer from FI. Most often FI is caused by multiple disrupted mechanisms; 
in fact a prospective study in 2004 showed that only 20 percent registered cases of FI was cause by only 
one disrupted mechanism. 
As mentioned earlier women going through vaginal delivery are especially vulnerable, when it comes to FI. 
Vaginal delivery could injure the internal and/or external anal sphincter and/or the pudendal nerve. The 
most critical cases of vaginal delivery due to causes for FI are: 
- 
 Forceps delivery, where the baby is dragged out of the vagina to assist the delivering woman. This 
will for most cases create a larger stress on the vagina and the surrounding tissue, nerves and 
muscles. 
- 
Prolonged second stage labor, which is the phase of the delivery, where the head of the baby is 
delivered. This is the stage where the vagina experiences the largest amount of stress. 
- 
Large birth weight, which will create a demand for increased expansion of the vagina. 
- 
Occipito-posterior, where the head of the baby is facing the pubic bone in the latter part of the 
vagina. 
Apart from vaginal delivery FI can also be caused by e.g.: 
- 
Failed anorectal surgeries for hemorrhoids, fistulas and fissures 
- 
Fragmentation of internal anal sphincter due to anal dilatation (procedure where a plug-like 
instrument is inserted in the anal canal ease up the tension in this area and thereby avoid fractures 
on the wall of the anal canal post an anal surgery) or lateral sphincterotomy (surgery of the internal 
anal sphincter as a treatment of chronic anal fissure) 
- 
Myopathy and degeneration of the internal anal sphincter, which will make it dysfunctional 
- 
Incorrect functions in the central nervous system due to sclerosis, dementia, brain tumors, spinal 
cord lesions and much more 
- 
Skeletal muscle disorders, which can cause problems for both the external anal sphincter and the 
puborectalis

- 
Rectal tumor 
- 
Incomplete evacuation of bowel content 
- 
Fecal impaction due to prolonged retention of stool, which make a possibility for liquid fecal 
content to get past the fecal impaction and escape through the anal canal. This cause is seen often 
for the elderly and the children. 
2.1.4 Assessment of FI 
A clinical assessment of FI is the first thing to do, when it comes to treatment of this condition. The first 
thing to do in this assessment is to establish a report between doctor and patient. This report/journal 
needs to be filled out by the patient. It is more likely a journal ark for an entire week, where the patient 
needs to document among others: 
- 
Date and time of bowel movement 
- 
Whether or not it was a stool seepage or staining situation 
- 
Whether or not it was controllable (possible to postpone the bowel movement for >15 minutes) 
- 
The consistency of the stool defined in 7 levels (1 = separate hard lumps and 7 = watery) 
Following this report there should also be asked for the patient’s quality of life, medical history for both 
medicine and injuries, which could have affected the fecal continence, a dietary history (as much as 
possible) and under which circumstances FI occurs (to see whether any pattern can be found). 
With these information gathered it is time for the doctor to do a physical examination of the patient. This 
examination will check for: 
- 
Neurological disorders in the back and legs, because such neurological disorders can also cause 
problems to the anorectal region and thereby affect fecal continence. 
- 
Physical and hygienic disorders such as presence of fecal substances, hemorrhoids, scars, skin 
excoriation (traumatized skin from scratching and rubbing) and gaping anus. These conditions could 
suggest, if the sphincters where weakened, or the patient suffers from chronic skin irritation. 
- 
Excessive rectal prolapse or perineal (perineum is between vagina and anus) descent. Here the 
patient needs to press to make a clear view for assessment. 
- 
Perineal sensation, where connections between sensory nerves, skin, spinal cord segments and 
motor innervation of external anal sphincter are inspected to assess the integrity of those 
combined. 
This “external” examination of the anorectum is followed by a digital rectal examination, which is an 
“internal” examination of the anorectum. The digital rectal examination is performed by the doctor by 
inserting an index finger in the anorectum. This finger can then assess: 
- 
Length of anal canal 
- 
Anorectal angle  
- 
Sphincter pressure for a resting and squeezing situation 
- 
Integrity of the puborectalis muscle 
- 
Elevation of perineum due to a squeezing situation

It is important to inform that this “internal” anorectal examination gives the ability for experienced 
surgeons to identify fecal impaction and overflow, but the examination is not accurate enough to 
diagnosing such thing as a dysfunctional sphincter. For such accuracy more technical methods needs to be 
utilized. 
2.1.5 Investigations of FI 
The more technical examination methods mentioned before would most commonly be methods like; 
anorectal manometry (which will be covered individually in chapter 2.2), anal endosonography, the balloon 
expulsion test and the pudendal nerve terminal motor latency (PNTML). 
- 
Anal endosonography is the cheapest and simplest method of assessing the anal sphincters for 
structural defects. The assessment of the anal sphincters is performed with a transducer having a 
focal length of 1-4 cm. This transducer is usually rotating with 7 mHz (millihertz), but in the last 
years transducers with a rotation speed of 10-15 mHz have been proven to have better accuracy in 
the description of the measured anal sphincters. The anal endosonography is widely used because 
of its cheap cost and relatively simple usability. The transducer is very simple to use, because it just 
have to be inserted in the anal canal, but from there on the measuring technique is very individual, 
and it takes a lot of practice and experience to get a good understanding of the collected result. 
When having this experience and practice it is possible to distinguish between injuries at the 
internal anal sphincter from the external anal sphincter, but no matter the experience and practice 
this method of assessment has very poor determination of the origination of the FI condition. 
- 
Balloon expulsion test does not assess the functionality e.g. of an anal sphincter individually. This 
test is to make an assessment of the coordination between the abdominal and pelvic floor muscles 
and the anal sphincters during defecation. Such uncoordinated conditions are seen mostly for 
elderly patients, and this condition makes it hard or even impossible to succeed this test. The 
balloon expulsion test is performed by inserting a 50 ml water filled balloon or an artificial silicon-
filled stool in rectum, which afterwards needs to be expelled by the patient. This test is not made to 
examine FI, because normal patients and patients with FI have little or no problem with evacuating 
the inserted component. What this test can explore is impaired fecal evacuation for patients with 
fecal seepage, impaction or overflow. This is a relatively cheap test method and is used quite often 
to assess the need of e.g. biofeedback training as a treatment for patients with impaired fecal 
evacuation. 
- 
PNTML is a method often used to assessing the anal sphincters prior to a surgery of those 
sphincters and to predict the outcome of such a surgery at the given time. The PNTML method 
measures the integrity the pudendal nerve and the anal sphincter. If the pudendal nerve is injured, 
the anal sphincters will experience denervation, which will be seen in increased reaction time and 
muscle weakness, and this condition might lead to FI. The PNTML method cannot be used to 
determine the origination of the FI condition, but it can be used as a supporting method for 
anorectal manometry and anal endosonography to supply the neurological part of the assessment. 
This support is needed because FI most often is an end result of both muscle and nerve injury. 
2.1.6 Management of FI 
The condition FI can have many different originations and to treat this condition in general, there can be 
done several preliminary changes in the patient’s lifestyle such as: 
==PAGE:27==
Side 27 af 103 
 
- 
Scheduling the toilet visiting, this could improve the stool consistency, when the bowel content is 
evacuated regularly and/or help monitoring patients with dementia. 
- 
Changing/improving hygienic behavior by changing underwear more frequently, using baby wipes, 
instead or regular toilet paper after stool evacuation followed by applying a thin layer of barrier 
cream such as zinc oxide or similar. These hygienic improvements would make an improvement of 
the skin around anus and help preventing any skin excoriation. 
- 
Dietary changes by reducing fiber and/or caffeine intake. A reduction of the patient’s consumption 
of caffeine in general and especially around meals could help improving diarrhea. This 
improvement is due to the problem with caffeine, when absorbed it increases colonic motility, 
which leads to an inducement of fluid secretion and thereby a cause of fecal urgency especially 
after meal intake. 
Increased fiber intake is known as being helpful against watery stool (and thereby diarrhea), 
because it can increase the solidity of the stool. The problem with this “common knowledge” is that 
there is no complete study justifying it. For some extend of patients it is affective, but if the patient 
is unable to absorb all fibers. The unabsorbed fibers could actually be proved worsening for a 
diarrhea condition, because these unabsorbable fibers could increase colonic fermentation and 
thereby e.g. create an extra bloating. 
With the preliminary changes in a patient’s lifestyle covered it is time to look at the actual treatment 
methods: 
- 
Pharmacologic therapy 
- 
Biofeedback therapy 
- 
Plugs, sphincter bulkers, and electrical stimulation 
- 
Surgery 
Pharmacologic therapy is, as the name refers to, a method of treatment, where the patient gets a medical 
drug to help treating the origination of the FI condition. Among the most common drugs there will be 
antidiarrheal agents like Imodium and Lomotil, which have been shown to increase anal resting sphincter 
pressure and reduce the frequency of incontinent accidents and the stool urgency. Other drug types has 
also been proven affective, and the patients have at least temporary benefits of most drug types after a 
few days, but some of these drug types also comes with side effects such as dryness of mouth, cramping 
pain in lower abdominals, which can lead to difficulty with stool evacuation. 
Another example of pharmacologic therapy could be; women with FI due to menopause have been treated 
with estrogen replacement therapy, which had a high successful outcome percentage after 6 months of 
both patients becoming completely asymptomatic or had symptomatically improvements. 
Biofeedback therapy is a very safe and effective way of treating patients with urge incontinence. The 
purpose of this therapy is to strengthening the anal sphincter muscles, improve coordination between 
abdominal, gluteal and anal sphincter muscles during voluntary squeeze and perception of fecal content, 
and finally to improve the anorectal sensory perception. 
Biofeedback therapy is a way of training the muscles and sensory units in charge of fecal continence. This 
training is patient specific, and therefore it has to be performed in clinical facilities with authorized 
==PAGE:28==
Side 28 af 103 
 
personnel. The training sessions (most likely 4-6 sessions is needed) take place in an either visualized 
auditory or with verbal feedback. The exercises are different for the three mentioned purposes. 
For strength development of the anal sphincters the patient is asked to squeeze as tight and for as long as 
possible around a manometric or EMG (electromyograph, recording electrical activity from skeletal 
muscles) probe, and while doing the voluntary squeeze the patient will get either visual and/or verbal 
feedback instantly. This instantaneous feedback helps the patient to understand and feel the muscles 
involved, which can make the patient able to develop some form of control of or response to these muscles 
and nerve endings. 
The improvement of coordination between abdominal, gluteal and anal sphincter muscles is made by doing 
an exercise, where a balloon is inserted in the rectum. When the balloon is in place, it is expanded, and 
then the patient have to make a maximum squeeze in a time frame of 2 seconds after balloon expansion. 
This procedure is performed to simulate an arrival of fecal content in rectum, which the patient, for 
obtaining continence, has to be able to respond to quickly. The patients are taught how to selectively use 
their anal sphincter muscles without increasing the contraction of the intra-abdominal, gluteal and thigh 
muscles, which will help improving the reflex response from the anal musculature by making it 
independent of compensation of other muscle groups. 
When the patient is able to perform the exercise correct, the expansion of the balloon is decreased to 
make an improvement of anorectal sensory perception. The sensory perception improvement comes with 
this part of the exercise, because the patient is told to squeeze as hard as before when sensing an even 
smaller volume inside the rectum to be able to avoid leakage of all kinds of fecal material or else way 
respond in a wanted manner (if e.g. a fecal evacuation is wanted). 
Plugs, sphincter bulkers, and electrical stimulation are primarily on an experimental plan. For the anal plug 
devices, this is meant as a temporary anal occlude especially for immobilized patients, institutionalized 
elderly patients and patients with a neurologically disease, all which are not able to react instantly by 
themselves and cannot be observed at all times. It is practically an anal tampon made of cotton fabrics, but 
the main problem with the supportive device is, that many patients’ anorectal region has not been able to 
tolerate the stress factor, this dry cotton material applies to the anal canal walls. 
The anal sphincter bulkers are meant as more permanent solution, where fat or protein, primarily from the 
individual itself, mostly is injected in the anal canal wall around the location, where the sphincter muscle is 
deficient from degeneration or fractions. 
Where the anal plugs and sphincter bulkers were passive methods, electrical stimulation is a more active 
treatment experiment, where the anal sphincters are exposed to electrical impulses, which cause the 
sphincter muscles to contract. By going through this therapy the anal sphincters’ strength and muscularity 
will be developed, which theoretically should lead to a stronger anorectal resting pressure and thereby 
fecal continence (or at least an improvement of the condition). 
Surgery is only meant as an option, when biofeedback and/or other therapy types have been proven 
unbeneficial for the given patient. For the majority of patients suffering from FI due to a fraction/scar of the 
anal sphincter, an overlapping sphincter repair is enough. This surgery is performed by either removing a 
small part of the damaged sphincter tissue or leave it, and then sew the closest functional muscle tissue of 
the sphincter together and perform an overlapping of the damaged part of the muscle tissue. 
For patients with more severe damage of the anal sphincter muscle a neo-sphincter construction can be 
considered. The construction of a neo-sphincter is made from a skeletal muscle with fast twitch fatigable 
==PAGE:29==
Side 29 af 103 
 
muscle fibers, most often the gracilis muscle which sits on the inside of the thigh. A part of the muscle can 
then be transplanted as an anal sphincter muscle for the same patient. The gracilis muscle is as mentioned 
made of fast twitch muscle fibers, and the anal sphincter muscle is made of slow twitch muscle fibers. To 
make the muscle implant function as an anal sphincter muscle it needs to be stimulated over a long period 
of time. This stimulation is performed by an implanted peacemaker, and when the patient needs to expel 
gas or other fecal content, an external magnetic device will temporarily switch off the pacemaker. 
A more commonly known surgery is the colostomy, which is used when other surgeries have failed or were 
not able to be performed or considered helpful in the specific case, e.g. an immobilized patient with anal 
skin complications or a patient with spinal cord injury. The colostomy is not the preferred surgical option, 
but it is not to be seen as a failure for the patient, because for most of the patients with performed 
colostomy, their quality of life has been increased a great deal. The colostomy is performed by cutting the 
lower part of the large intestine and pulling it out through the abdominal wall to create a stoma, where the 
colostomy bag can be attached to, and thereby an exit for fecal content is created. A colostomy can be 
either temporary or permanent. In case of a temporary colostomy surgery, the problem in the anorectum 
or elsewhere may be considered fixable, and when this problem is fixed, a reverse colostomy can be 
performed, where the large intestine is reattached to the rectum and the hole in the abdominal wall is 
closed. 
Relatively new studies (5-6 years) in the UK12 have shown promising results, when it comes to regeneration 
of a fractioned anal sphincter. This regeneration made by injecting cultured autologous muscle tissue 
directly into the fractioned part of an anal sphincter muscle. These cultured autologous muscle cells can, 
when injected, merge with the surrounding muscle tissue and reconstruct the sphincter muscle. The 
autologous muscle cells are harvested through 1 cm3 (cubic centimeter) biopsy of a pectoral muscle. This 
muscle tissue sample is cultivated in approximately 40 days before injection. Prior to the injection the 
patient has been examined physically and exposed to electrical stimulation of the anal sphincter muscles 
for 15 minutes a day in 10 weeks to enhance the muscular strength. This electrical stimulation of the 
sphincter muscles was continued the following 4 weeks post injection. 
At Herlev University Hospital there is an ongoing study13 for an even simpler method to regenerate a 
fractured/injured anal sphincter muscle. This method is using the same principals of injecting autologous 
substance into the fractured part of the anal sphincter muscle, but instead of cultivating the harvested 
muscle tissue, they chop the tissue sample into very small pieces. The reason for during this 
chopping/blending of the harvested muscle tissue is that by splitting the muscle tissue into such small 
parts, some of these parts will react in an “stem cell”-like fashion when it get in contact with the fractioned 
muscle tissue of the anal sphincter. 
Three months prior to this “blended” muscle tissue injection the patient have to go through a 3 month 
pelvic muscle training program to evaluate, whether or not an injection can be proven necessary, or the FI 
condition can be treated by physical and dietary exercises/changes alone. 
The essential part of these last two treatment cases is, that the faction/injured part(s) of the anal sphincter 
can be located with a good accuracy to be able to make the injection as precise as possible to make the 
treatment most affective.

2.2 Manometry

2.2.1 Clinical procedure of manometric test 
As mentioned in chapter 1.1.3 anorectal manometry is a method to measure the pressure of both internal 
and external sphincter. This is not the complete story, it is just a few things of what an anorectal 
manometry test14 can measure. A full anorectal test could measure: 
- 
Resting pressure both maximum and mean 
- 
Squeeze pressure both maximum and mean and the duration for pressure kept above 50 % of 
maximum squeeze pressure 
- 
Pressure changes during cough reflex 
- 
Rectoanal pressure changes while doing attempted defecation 
- 
Rectoanal inhibitory reflex

In general a manometric test takes all in all one hour; half of the time spend on preparations and the other 
half on the anorectal examination. Prior to the examination the patient usually may have some easy 
digestible food and liquid to eat up to four hours before15, after this point the patient have to enter a state 
of fasting. Two hours prior to the examination the rectum has to be emptied for solid fecal content, which 
can be helped by taking some purgative product like FLEET’S ENEMA. 
At the start of the examination the patient is asked to lie down in a left lateral position with both knees 
bent to 90 degrees to insure a more uncomplicated insertion of the measuring probe in the anorectum. 
With the patient in place it is now time for a digital examination, where the doctor with a gloved and 
lubricated finger makes a rectal examination looking for tenderness, blood or stool, which could affect a 
successful manometric examination. If the digital examination has nothing to apply, then the manometric 
probe will be inserted very gently in the rectum. This procedure has to be completed without any pain and 
the least amount of discomfort, or else it can cause endorectal injuries. 
When the probe is in place, the patient is given a “run-in” time interval, where the patient gets 5 minutes 
to get used to having the probe inside and to get the anorectal pressure more or less back to normal resting 
level. When 5 minutes have expired, the resting sphincter pressure can be calculated as the difference 
between the intrarectal pressure and the maximum anal sphincter pressure in a state of rest. 
With the resting sphincter pressure calculated, the patient is told to squeeze has hard as possible, like 
trying to retain fecal content completely, for as long as possible within a timeframe of 30 seconds. The

A “cough reflex test” is implemented in the examination, if a suspicion about the cause of the FI condition

If dyssynergia, which basically is a muscular incoordination, is suspected to be problem for the patient, he 
or she will be asked, while lying down, to make a pressing movement, like if they were attempting to 
defecate. At this moment two pressures will be compared the rectal and the anal pressure. For a 
normal/correct defecation there would be seen an increase in the rectal pressure and a decrease in the 
anal pressure and if not, e.g. if the anal pressure is also increasing, this will be an indication of dyssynergia. 
This defecation attempt will be repeated once more with a pause of 30 seconds in between to avoid 
uncertainty. 
Rectoanal inhibitory reflex is often examined with children, because they are the majority of patients with 
the Hirschsprung’s disease, which is a condition, where the anal sphincters, due to underdeveloped nerve 
ends, do not respond correctly to a rectoanal expansion. When the anal sphincters do not respond with a 
muscular release on such an expansion inside rectum, this will create an obstruction. The test is performed 
by rapidly expanding a balloon in rectum to 50 mL of air, if a there is not seen any anal sphincter relaxation, 
then the balloon will be expanded further up to a maximum of 250 mL to trigger an anal sphincter 
relaxation. For a healthy patient this anal sphincter relaxation can be detected with small balloon volumes 
even less than 50 mL of air. 
Rectal sensation is basically a testing of the anorectal nerve ends. The test is made to confirm, how 
sensitive the patient is to accumulation of fecal content in rectum. The examination is performed by 
expanding a balloon with 10 mL of air per second until the patient detects the first sensation of this 
expansion, and the volume is noted. Afterwards the expansion will continue with 30 mL increments, each 
increment is contained for 30 seconds follow by a 30 pause, where the balloon is completely deflated. 
These 30 mL increments are continued until the patient reports that a maximum tolerable volume is meet 
or the volume reaches 250 mL of air. This maximum volume is also noted along with the volume, where the 
patient first time reported a desire to defecate for more than a 15 seconds interval. 
A balloon expulsion test is performed to examine whether or not a patient is able to make an evacuation of 
bowel content. The procedure starts by inserting a 4-5 cm long nonlatex balloon inside the patient’s 
rectum. When the balloon is in place, it is filled with 50 mL of temperate water, and then the patient has to 
sit alone on a commode for 3 minutes and try to expel the balloon. A stopwatch is set, when the defecation 
attempt starts and the patient has to stop it, if the defecation is completed within the timeframe. If the 
defecation is not completed within 3 minutes the balloon will deflated and pulled out, and this would be a 
sign of dyssynergia. 
Simulated defecation is made as an additional test, if the balloon expulsion test was not completed 
successfully. It is more or less the same test apart from the fact, that the balloon is mounted on the probe, 
and the anorectal pressure is measured during the defecation.

2.2.2 Common catheter types for manometric test 
As seen in last section a manometric examination has many different test options but they are widely 
spread our throughout the anorectum and not all of them16 are that accurate as a stand-alone test 
procedure. This is why the focus for this report is at the anal sphincters functionality, which is a test, where 
the manometric examination is quite accurate as a stand-alone procedure. 
To examine the functionality of the anal sphincter muscles both resting pressure and maximal squeezing 
pressure needs to be measured along with the functional length of the anal canal. The procedure to 
calculate the resting anal pressure is already mentioned in chapter 2.2.1, and it is generally measured 1-2 
cm from the anal verge17. The resting anal pressure contains primarily the information of the functionality 
of the internal anal sphincter. The maximal squeeze pressure is measured throughout the entire anal canal 
and is primarily containing the information of the functionality of the external anal sphincter. The 
functional anal canal length is found by measuring the entire anal canal, and the functional length is define 
as the length from the anal verge and as fare into the anal canal as the resting pressure do not become less 
than half of the maximal anal resting pressure.  
As mentioned in chapter 1.1.3 the anal sphincter functionality can be assessed with three different 
techniques; stationary, stationary pull-through and continuous pull-through. 
Two commonly used catheters type for anal sphincter functionality assessment are the water-perfused 
catheter and the solid-state catheter. 
The water-perfused catheter18 is as mentioned in chapter 1.1.3 a tube with a fixed water pressure kept 
from the water supply. This pressure water pressure can be measured at all time, and an externally added 
pressure will cause a change in these pressure measurements.

2.3.1 Pressure principals and methods 
The anorectal manometry procedure measures among others the pressure of the anal sphincters. This 
feasibility study is looking for a solution for examine the functionality of the anal sphincters to see, if and

the last part of chapter 2.1.6 it is very critical to know the exact location of a fracture in the anal sphincters 
due to the fact that it is possible to inject cells directly into these areas and make the sphincter muscles 
regenerate quit well if not entirely.

Semiconductive pressure sensors can be split up into two categories; resistive and capacitive. 
The resistive pressure semiconductor could e.g. as it is visualized in Figure 2.3-1 be placed inside an oil-

applied on a relatively small area, which (combined with the possibility size-wise to make multiple 
channels/chambers) could give a good radial pressure position. The reason for encapsulating the resistive 
semiconductor inside an oil (or water for that matter) filled chamber is to both protect it and to ensure a 
good pressure measurement by having enough measuring area. The resistive semiconductor works that 
way, that it will change resistance when experiencing compression.

As explained in chapter 2.3.1 such bending strain can be detected by a strain gauge, which behavior is that

expanding. However this behavior does not complete a digital measurement of the strain applied to the 
lamella. To be able to measure the strain applied, there has to be applied and measuring circuit to this 
variable resistance, known as a strain gauge. This circuit is called a “Wheatstone bridge”. A Wheatstone 
bridge is a design of four resistors paired up and put in a parallel formation as shown in Figure 3.1-2: 
 
Figure 3.1-2 Wheatstone bridge design30 
The Wheatstone bridge is a design that allows an instant output change when a change in resistance occurs 
for one or more of the resistors inside the Wheatstone bridge. 
For this lamella design it would be logical to place two strain gauges in the Wheatstone bridge, one on the 
outside and one on the inside of the lamella. By utilizing two strain gauges both compression and extension 
of the lamella will be measured, and by putting the two strain gauges in the same serial connection will give 
twice the output value. 
Although the output voltage is twice the original size, it is still in the range of millivolts, which means that to 
get a measureable output range from 0-3 V, the Wheatstone bridge output needs to be amplified. Due to 
the small output value no ordinary amplifier will be able to handle this task. The amplifier has to have a 
very low offset voltage and low input offset drift to be able to detect such small input changes and amplify 
them accurately.

Synthesis 
Due to the dimensions of the probe vs. the size of the piezoresistive pressure sensors it is only possible to 
have 6 channels for this device, which would not give a very detailed overview of the entire anal canal, but 
it would be more than enough to make a proof of the concept of instantaneous pressure measurements of 
the entire anal canal. 
These 6 channels will be made of approximate 60 mm polyurethane tube with an inner diameter of 2.5 mm 
and an outer diameter of 3 mm. This tube will be attached to one end of a brass pipe, which has a 
piezoresistive pressure sensor in the other end. Afterwards vacuum is applied to this channel design and 
water is injected, and finally the polyurethane tube is sealed and attached to the surface of the measuring 
probe. 
The reason for choosing brass pipes, and not attach the plastic tubes directly to the pressure sensors, is to 
avoid expansion of the plastic tube inside the probe, due to the fact that the sensors are too large to fit 
inside the probe itself. The reason for choosing brass above other metals was due to the cost and the fact 
that it is possible and relatively easy to bend at room-temperature without kinking. 
The polyurethane was not the perfect choice for the “plastic”-tube part, but it was the only thing thin 
enough to be able to measure such small pressures as needed, and it was possible to buy “off shelf”, which 
saved a lot of time and money for this project. 
To be able to locate the externally applied pressure the measuring channels needs to be kept in a fixed 
position at all times during an examination. This needs to be dealt with by an appropriate mechanical 
design. This mechanical design has to make it possible to implement all 6 channel designs into a measuring 
probe of approximately the same size as a 2052 transducer, while keeping all 6 measuring ring areas in a 
fixed position.

The evaluations for this concept model has been, that the model is very promising, when it comes to 
providing an instantaneous manometric examination of an entire anal canal, which is what at least the

to be implemented at the surface of the probe, which also would make it possible to produce pre-

other assisting personnel. If this cannot be done the concept will probably not get accepted, because it is 
very hard to ensure only fluid and not air inside the measuring channels, which is a “must have” to ensure 
accurate measurements. This evaluation has been done by the writer in cooperation with the BK Medical 
R&D team, who agreed with the writers evaluating comments. 
This concept is made to be exactly what the anorectal specialists did ask for in a compromised product 
solution: an identical probe as the ultrasound transducer with multiple circular average pressure 
measurements through the entire anal canal. This concept will make them able to place the ultrasound 
transducer in one place in the anal canal and do a scanning and afterwards placed the identical manometric 
probe in the same position and do an examination of the entire anal canal’s circular average pressures at 
once. This will make it very easy to detect the functional length of the anal canal and match this length with 
the ultrasound images to locate, where a given fistula would be placed compared to the functional length 
of the anal canal, and how much of the anal sphincters there has to be cut open to drain a fistula in an 
operative treatment. 
However it is very important to emphasis that this is the only possible solution for making this concept 
functional, because it cannot be combined directly with the ultrasound transducer, due to the fact that it is 
not possible to scan through the measuring areas of this concept model with ultrasound.

The test setup for the weights test of the strain gauge concept model was as visualized in Figure 5.1-1: 
==PAGE:73==
Side 73 af 103 
 
 
Figure 5.1-1 Weights test for strain gauge concept model 
As shown in Figure 5.1-1 the mechanical lamella design is put on a surface that only supports the support-
rings in the ends of the lamella design. This setup gives the lamella subjected to a strain (the lamella 
pointing upwards and furthest away from the supporting surface) the opportunity for pull the support-rings 
towards the center of the lamella design, as it would have been possible, if the design has been put outside 
an ultrasound transducer. 
The external pressure is applied by wrapping a thread around a single lamella and attaches a weight in the 
other end of the tread, and let that weight hang in free air. This procedure will create a deflection of the 
lamella like, if it was pressed down by a similar external pressure. 
The weights applied for this test is 25.2 grams, 112.4 grams and 190.7 grams, which approximately 
corresponds local externally applied pressure at 24.7 mbar, 110.3 mbar and 187.1 mbar respectively. 
To compensate for the relatively slow deflection and expansion time for the lamella design each test weight 
has been through a number of 200 samplings. The average of these samplings has been seen as the 
resulting local pressure measurement for each of the 8 lamellas. Finally the average of these 8 average 
pressure values is seen as the resulting circular average pressure for each tested weight.  
The resulting circular average pressure measured was; 25.3 mbar, 126.3 mbar and 214.8 mbar respectively. 
This gives the strain gauge concept model an average accuracy for its entire measuring interval of 
approximately 10.6 %. 
These results do seem fairly good, when it takes into consideration that the strain gauges are glued on the 
lamella surface by hand. This means that they are not placed at the exact midpoint of each lamella, neither 
==PAGE:74==
Side 74 af 103 
 
are they completely in line with the lamella itself. And due to the quick harden time for the utilized glue 
some of the strain gauges became a little wrinkled when mounted. 
With all these factors taken into account an average overshoot of 10 % most be seen as very acceptable. 
 
A speed test has also been applied for this concept model to validate the speed of the software. The result 
of this test would conclude whether or not the software is able to log data fast enough to fulfill the sample 
rate requirements. 
The test was performed for 600 measurements of 8 channels, and the duration was 1-2 seconds for the 
test, which proves that the software has more than enough speed to make the data loggings in a real-time 
mode.

pressure tight. When the applied pressure inside the pressure chamber exceeded 160-190 mbar, the 
sealing of the polyurethane tubes broke, and all channels became leaking. The most optimal solution for 
this problem would have been to rebuild the measuring probe and the channels and come up with a more 
solid sealing solution for the polyurethane tubes. However at this point of time in the project schedule, 
time did not allow such solutions, and therefore it was decided, that the concept model has been proven a 
success in the hand pressure multi-channel test, and the only thing missing was to test the accuracy and 
stability of the sensor choice. This was why the pressure chamber single channels test was designed, 
because it allowed the writer to test only a setup of the piezoresistive pressure sensor connected to a brass 
pipe with an attached and sealed polyurethane tube at the end of it. The sealing of the polyurethane tube 
was for this case a knot, which could not break without tearing the tube apart (which would not be the case 
with the given amount of pressure applied). 
The pressure chamber single channel test was performed with two different lengths of the polyurethane 
tube one at ~20 cm and another at ~7 cm. The reason for testing two different lengths was to test the 
influence of the elasticity of the tube walls to the resulting measurements. The test subjected the 
polyurethane tube to a pressure range from 0 to 200 mbar with increments of ~20 mbar. For each 
increment there were taken 200 samples with a 0.5 second delay in between each sample. This insurance 
was made to counteract the settling time of the polyurethane tube and get a fair evaluation of the sensor 
accuracy itself when utilizing an incompressible fluid as pressure transmitter. 
The average of these 200 pressure values is seen as the resulting circular average pressure for each applied 
pressure values.

5.5 Part conclusion 
In this chapter test cases have been described for all three concept models and the visualization program as 
well. 
The strain gauge concept model has been through a weights test, where three different weights have been 
applied for each lamella, and the average of 200 samples for each lamella and weight has been evaluated. 
The results of these tests showed that the strain gauge concept model for average circular pressure 
measurements was off by 10.6 %, which is very acceptable for a concept model. 
The strain gauge concept model was also through a speed test to verify, whether or not the software would 
be able to perform with a sampling rate of minimum 40 Hz for all 8 channels. This was no problem at all for 
the program. 
The force sensor concept model was through the same weights test as the strain gauge concept model, 
which it failed completely. The sensor was too inaccurate and not consistent with repetitive measurements 
for the same weight. This is probably because the main intention for this sensor is to serve as a switching 
indicator for electronic buttons. 
The piezoresistive pressure sensor concept model has been tested with two different tests. The first test 
verified that the concept model was able to control and measure applied pressure for all six pressure 
channels. Due to a problem will the sealing of the polyurethane tubes it was only a single sensing channel 
that was tested in a pressure tight camber. This test showed that the sensor channel has a stationary error 
factor around 0.5, which could be compensated for software-wise, and this gave that the concept model 
had an accuracy of -8.8 %, which is very good due to the problem of creating a complete vacuum in the 
measuring channel before injecting an incompressible fluid. 
The visualization program has also been tested with two different pressure input .txt files; one with 
pressure input data from the strain gauge concept model, and another with inputs from the piezoresistive 
pressure sensor concept model. The results of these tests have shown that the program is able to create an 
intuitive and quick overview of an ultrasound scanned anal canal and the pressure measurements done for 
the same anal canal. 
With these testes completed and evaluated, this chapter has covered the 11th bullet point from chapter 1.3. 
Now all designs of mechanics, hardware and software has been tested, and it is now time to discuss the 
entire project and evaluate the results from it. This will be done in chapter 6 and chapter 7 respectively, 
along with some suggestions for what can be followed up on, if this project was to be reinvestigated by 
another engineer.

Chapter 6 
Discussion 
 
 
 
This chapter will look at the choices made for the concept model designs and discuss the entire project 
including the test results and the value of the final implementations. This will cover the 12th bullet point 
from chapter 1.3. 
 
 
There have been several choices to make for both the concept models and the visualization software. 
The clearest and most important thing to recognize is that the concept models was presented to two 
anorectal specialists. These specialists are very skilled and do have an impressive amount of knowledge and 
experience within anorectal examination and surgery. These facts make their feedback very valuable for 
the development of the concept models, but it is important to remember that they are just two specialists 
out of many, and the opinion of whether or not to use a pull-through method with or without a pulling 
machine instead of a stationary measurement method, this is hard to conclude. These two specialists favor 
the stationary measuring method and want to have circular average pressure measurements instead of 
radially arranged and separated local pressure measurements. And other specialists utilize a big mechanical 
pulling machine and are happy with only 4 channels and radially arranged and separated local pressure 
measurements. 
 
==PAGE:82==
Side 82 af 103 
 
This is the main reason for designing the strain gauge as a radially arranged concept to measure separated 
local externally applied pressure, which will please the specialists, who prefer the pull through method. And 
on the other hand the piezoresistive pressure sensor concept model is made exactly as the two consulted 
specialists want to have a substitute manometry probe with the same dimensions as the BK Medical 2052 
model. 
By emphasizing this point and taking the test results for both the strain gauge and piezoresistive pressure 
sensor concept model into account, both of these concept models seems very promising in their own way. 
As the developer and designer of both concept models, I will say that both the strain gauge concept model 
and the piezoresistive pressure sensor concept model have shown very good test results, and I will 
therefore recommend BK Medical to take these two concepts up for further evaluation. Suggestions for 
what to investigate will be presented in chapter 7.3. 
The piezoresistive pressure sensor concept model would be very good for preoperative examinations for 
estimating the outcome of a fistula anorectal operation, because it gives a clear and quick overview of the 
active length of the anal canal. 
The strain gauge concept model on the other hand would be very good to detect local minima and maxima 
in the circular anal sphincter muscles, which could be very effective when searching for scar tissue and 
other origins to fecal incontinence. And by localizing a problem area of the anal sphincter muscles it would 
be much easier to treat and maybe even heal/regenerate those local problem areas and thereby increase 
the level of fecal continence for the patient. 
On top of that the strain gauge concept model is actually designed to be combined directly with a 2052 
model endo-anal ultrasound transducer, which was the goal for this entire project itself. The concept model 
design is not applicable for a product type, but by changing the lamella design material to a metal or 
something alike would probably make the mechanical design fast enough to run at a sampling rate of 
minimum 40 Hz, and it would also make the design much thinner, which will increase the applicability for a 
combination/merging of this anorectal manometry device and the 2052 model endo-anal ultrasound 
transducer. 
Looking at the visualization software, it would be preferable to have the manometry data as a 3D output 
applicable to the 3D anorectal ultrasound output. This was neglected due to the fact that the test cases was 
not made with human test subjects, because the concept models were in very premature development 
stages compared to a product model, and it would not have been responsible to attempt such test cases 
involving real persons. Instead the channels for both of the accepted concept models were tested and 
evaluated separately, and therefore it would have been necessary to be created a large amount of fictive 
pressure measurements to be able to create a meaningful 3D visualization of the pressure outputs. 
This was the reason for creating a simple bar graph of the pressure data collected from the individual test 
cases, and the reason for comparing it to a longitude ultrasound image of an anal canal was to show the 
possibility of still combining the two data sets, even though the test cases did not allow it at a concept 
model stage.   
 
This would wrap up the discussion part of the project, and it is now time to make a conclusion for the entire 
project and the value of the results from it.

7.2 Final conclusion 
This project has made an investigation of the possibility of combining an anorectal manometry device with 
a BK medical 2052 endo-anal transducer. To be able to come up with an answer for this topic, there were 
made a research of the medical and clinical perspective of anorectal manometry to create an 
understanding of this examination method. Afterwards competitive products solutions were studied along 
with other sensor option that could lead to a new possible way of measuring anorectal manometry. From 
this research two functional concept models have been designed, a strain gauge concept model and a 
piezoresistive pressure sensor concept model. 
The entire investigation process and the design, implementation and testing process in this report covers all 
listed bullet points in the problem formulation (chapter 1.3). 
The design and implementation process of mechanics, hardware and software have been completed by 
utilizing the Rapid Prototyping development method, which had ensured a fast and enlightening 
development phase. 
The piezoresistive pressure sensor concept model is seen as a very good option for preoperative 
examinations to estimate the outcome of a fistula anorectal operation due to the concept model’s direct 
measurement of the active length of the anal canal. 
The strain gauge concept model could be very good to examine anal sphincters for scar tissue and other 
origins to fecal incontinence. It would be able to localize local pressure maxima and minima for the anal 
==PAGE:86==
Side 86 af 103 
 
sphincter muscles, which would make it much easier to treat and maybe even heal/regenerate these local 
problem areas and thereby increase the level of fecal continence for the patient. 
The strain gauge concept model is designed to be combined directly with a 2052 model endo-anal 
ultrasound transducer, and with change of the lamella design material to e.g. a metal would probably make 
the mechanical design able to run at a sampling rate of minimum 40 Hz as well as more applicable for a 
combination/merging of this anorectal manometry device and the 2052 model endo-anal ultrasound 
transducer. 
The absolute conclusion for this project will be: “Yes, it is possible to combine an anorectal manometry 
device with a BK Medical 2052 endo-anal transducer, and the combination of the two collected data types 
will be very valuable, because it will show the exact state of the anorectal area, and in some cases the 
reason for the anorectal area to be in this condition. Such examination would make anorectal examinations 
more accurate and easier to estimate operational outcomes from.” 
And finally as the developer and designer of the concept models I will recommend BK Medical to make 
further investigations on one or both of these concepts. 
 
7.3 Further work 
If this project at BK Medical is found qualified for further research and development, some subjects to look 
into could be: 
- 
A new material for the strain gauge concept model’s lamella design. This has been mentioned a 
couple of times throughout the report, but by choosing e.g. a metal for the lamella design would 
probably make the response time for the mechanics fast enough to run at a desired sample rate at 
40 Hz for 8 channel measurements. Another mentioned improvement would be the thickness of 
the entire mechanical design, which could be decreased a lot, and this would make it a more 
patient friendly product solution. 
- 
When talking about reducing the size of the mechanical design of the strain gauge concept model 
piezoresistive strain gauges instead of the utilized metal foil strain gauges might be considered. 
These strain gauges are much smaller than the metal foil models and they are a lot more sensitive. 
However it should be mentioned that these strain gauges are much more expensive and more 
difficult to order, due to the fact that there are not that many manufactures of these strain gauge 
type worldwide. But they might be proven worth the money and the ordering time. 
- 
If an entire and instant anal canal pressure measurement from strain gauges is desired, then this 
concept could be applied to a separate manometry probe made of multiple C-shaped “rings”. These 
C-shaped “rings” forming a manometry probe would give in for externally applied pressure, which 
will cause the distance between the two ends of each C-shape to decrease, and a strain in the C-
shaped “ring” will be measurable with an attached strain gauge of some type. Thereby it would be 
possible to measure circular pressures throughout the entire length of the anal canal almost 
instantly. 
- 
To follow up on the piezoresistive pressure sensor concept model the next step could be to make 
an investigation of the cost and effectiveness for extracting the piezoresistive material in the 
piezoresistive sensors and implement this material directly in the pressure measuring area outside 
the separate manometry probe.

void loadMethod(int *intLoad){ 
  byte channel, NoB = 0xFF, count, bufferArray[READINGS]; 
 
  for(channel = 0; channel < NOC; channel++){  // Loop through all 6 sonsors 
    count = 0; 
    Wire.beginTransmission(0x70); // address the I2C bus multiplexer... 
    Wire.write(0x01 << channel);  // ...and select sensor 1 
    Wire.endTransmission();       // Stop the transmission to keep the I2C bus ready for the commands to the  
                                                            selected sensor 
 
    NoB = Wire.requestFrom(ADDR, READINGS);  // Request for two byte readings of pressure data from the  
                                                                                          selected sensor 
 
    while(Wire.available()) 
    { 
      bufferArray[count++] = Wire.read();  // Make each byte reading 
    } 
    Wire.endTransmission();  // Stop the transmission 
 
// Load the raw pressure measurement into a buffer 
    intLoad[channel] = (bufferArray[0] & 0x3F) << 8; 
    intLoad[channel] |= bufferArray[1]; 
  } 
}
