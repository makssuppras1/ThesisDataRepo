Abstract
This thesis presents a novel approach to designing energy-eﬃcient automatic license plate
recognition (ALPR) models tailored for edge devices using Neural Architecture Search
(NAS). With the increasing demand for real-time ALPR in resource-constrained environ-
ments, traditional models, although accurate, often prove computationally prohibitive for
low-power hardware. To address this, the study introduces a comprehensive NAS pipeline
that integrates a customized search space, a Bayesian optimization framework via Ax,
and a specialized latency estimator calibrated on target devices such as the Raspberry
Pi 4B. Initial experiments conducted on a proxy dataset established a robust baseline,
while extended training on a larger dataset further reﬁned model performance.
Addi-
tionally, various quantization techniques (Float16, INT16, and INT8) were applied to re-
duce inference latency without signiﬁcantly compromising accuracy. Benchmarking across
multiple hardware platforms demonstrated that several NAS-discovered architectures not
only achieve competitive intersection-over-union (IoU) scores but also deliver substan-
tial speedups compared to conventional models like YOLOv8n. These ﬁndings highlight
the potential of NAS-driven methodologies to automate the design of lightweight, high-
performing ALPR systems that meet real-time processing requirements on edge devices,
thereby oﬀering a viable and scalable alternative for intelligent transportation and smart
city applications.

Introduction
Advancements in machine learning, particularly deep learning, have transformed the ﬁeld
of computer vision, enabling highly accurate object detection and recognition systems that
were previously challenging or impractical to implement. Convolutional Neural Networks
introduced robust feature extraction capabilities, allowing models to identify complex pat-
terns and structures in images, such as vehicle shapes and license plates, even in varying
conditions. With the rise of high-performance GPUs and optimized frameworks like Ten-
sorFlow and PyTorch, training large, accurate models has become more accessible and
eﬃcient. As a result, the development of Automatic License Plate Recognition (ALPR)
systems has become more feasible, as modern machine learning models can now han-
dle challenges such as varied lighting, angles, and image resolutions that are common
in real-world scenarios. These advancements enable ALPR solutions to operate reliably
and eﬃciently, paving the way for broad application in areas such as traﬃc monitoring,
security, and smart city infrastructure.
With the emergence of neural networks, automatic license plate recognition has been
seen as one of the real world applications of the ﬁeld.
ALPR consists of two major
tasks: (1) ﬁnding cars and their subsequent license plates in images - an object detection
task; (2) decoding the characters on the plate - an optical character recognition (OCR)
task. With the increase of computational power and the advancements of neural network
research, today’s ALPR systems have a wide range of out-of-the-box solutions that show
very promising results on existing databases of still images. [1]. Nevertheless, many of
these architectures are computationally heavy models, with millions of parameters, which
limit their usefulness on older, less performant devices. This, of course, questions their
application in real-time systems, such as speed cameras, where low latency is required
without the beneﬁts of having strong GPUs. As such, striking a balance between accuracy
and latency has become the emerging topic in the ﬁeld of neural network research for ALPR
systems.
1.1
Problem background
The number of vehicles on the road has increased steadily worldwide, contributing to the
rise of congestion, environmental concerns, and infrastructure demands. According to the
International Organization of Motor Vehicle Manufacturers, global car ownership has ex-
ceeded 1.4 billion in recent years [2], with emerging economies experiencing rapid growth
in vehicle usage as access to aﬀordable transportation expands. This growth is particu-
larly pronounced in emerging economies, where expanding middle classes and increased
access to aﬀordable transportation have led to rapid increases in vehicle ownership. For
instance, between 2015 and 2020, Vietnam experienced an annual growth rate of 17% in
its motorization rate, while China and India saw annual increases of 14% and 10%, re-
spectively [3].This upward trend is expected to continue, especially in urban areas. The
United Nations projects that by 2050, approximately 68% of the global population will
reside in cities, up from 55% in 2018, underscoring the need for advanced solutions to
manage traﬃc ﬂow, enhance safety, and improve mobility in increasingly congested urban
environments [4].
ALPR systems play a pivotal role in supporting this need, oﬀering eﬃcient and scalable
solutions for vehicle monitoring and management. These systems enable a wide range
of applications, from traﬃc law enforcement and toll collection to parking management

1.2
Objective and Scope of the Study
The primary objective of this study is to use Neural Architecture Search to develop an
eﬃcient model for license plate (LP) detection. NAS enables the automated search for
architectures tailored to speciﬁc requirements like speed and power eﬃciency, allowing
the selection of lightweight models that can run on hardware-constrained environments
without sacriﬁcing accuracy.
The study aims to establish a NAS pipeline that can identify suitable models for LP
detection and benchmark these models against widely used YOLO-based solutions. YOLO
models are renowned for their high accuracy and speed in object detection tasks, but are
often computationally intensive, which can be a limitation in resource-limited systems. By
focusing on three main aspects: (1) establishing an eﬀective NAS pipeline, (2) identifying
models optimized for LP detection, and (3) comparing performance with out-of-the-box
YOLO models, this work seeks to demonstrate that NAS can yield competitive results for
LP detection with the added beneﬁt of eﬃciency. The ﬁndings will showcase the potential
of NAS to enhance ALPR by providing lightweight, hardware-friendly models suitable for
deployment in existing infrastructure.
1.3
Proposed Solution Approach
In developing this solution, inspiration is drawn from the work presented in License Plate
Recognition Using Neural Architecture Search for Edge Devices, which demonstrates how
NAS can be applied to create eﬃcient edge-compatible ALPR models. Similarly to its
approach, CNN blocks are used rather than single CNN cells as the primary building units
in the NAS search space. By using larger, preconﬁgured blocks from architectures like
MobileNet, this design allows for richer feature extraction and more eﬀective modeling
of complex visual patterns in license plates while keeping computational costs manage-
able. This strategy is essential for fast LP detection tasks for real-time applications, as it
balances eﬃciency with the capacity to handle variations in real-world conditions.
This study diverges by implementing NAS through a Bayesian optimization approach
through Ax, an adaptive experimentation platform designed for scalable optimization. It
adopts the idea of constructing a latency table to optimize the model for deployment on

Literature Review
2.1
ALPR with Classical Methods
Automatic License Plate Recognition (ALPR) systems have been integral to numerous
applications in areas such as traﬃc management, security, and tolling, attracting consid-
erable research interest long before neural networks became a staple. A comprehensive
review of traditional ALPR approaches can be found in [7], which underscores their mul-
tistep framework. Speciﬁcally, ALPR is deﬁned as a four-stage pipeline, where the ﬁrst
two steps: (1) vehicle detection and (2) license plate extraction - especially relevant to
this work - are followed by (3) segmenting the license plate into individual characters and
(4) recognizing those characters.
Historically, many methods relied on edge detection and boundary analysis. For example,
techniques that employ Sobel ﬁlters or vertical edge detection algorithms (VEDA) proved
eﬀective in diverse lighting conditions by reliably identifying the edges of the rectangular
plate. However, they can struggle in environments with numerous irrelevant edges, result-
ing in false positives. Another common strategy, Connected Component Analysis (CCA),
is a binary image technique that groups adjacent pixels to isolate objects like license plates.
Although CCA can be successful, it is highly dependent on correct thresholding to create a
precise binary mask. If the threshold is set incorrectly, the plate may be split into multiple
regions, thereby reducing the segmentation accuracy.
Figure 2.1: Example of clear license plate boundaries in a thresholded image. [8].
Beyond edge-based methods, researchers also explored texture- and color-based techniques.
These aim to detect variations in texture or color at the plate boundary, separating it from
the surrounding area. Color-based approaches often work well in outdoor settings with
ample natural light to enhance contrast, but are sensitive to low or inconsistent lighting,
limiting their reliability under variable conditions.
Despite these progressions, integrating edge, texture, and color features into a single
pipeline to increase robustness inevitably raises computational costs, making such methods
less suitable for deployment on standard low-power hardware. This trade-oﬀbetween ro-
bustness and computational eﬃciency has been a persistent challenge, one that has driven
the transition toward neural network-based ALPR methods that can learn multi-stage
recognition processes with fewer resource-intensive feature engineering steps.

2.2
ALPR with Neural Networks
With the emergence of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), ALPR systems signiﬁ-
cantly improved accuracy, especially in unconstrained environments involving sloped an-
gles, variable lighting, and partial occlusions. By learning intricate patterns directly from
the data, CNNs enable robust feature extraction under diverse conditions. In the realm of
neural network-based ALPR, two primary strategies have emerged: one approach involves
end-to-end models that seamlessly integrate license plate detection and character recog-
nition into a single pipeline, while the other, more traditional method separates vehicle
and license plate detection from character segmentation, using dedicated models for each
stage.
In [9], the authors present a uniﬁed deep learning model that performs both license plate
detection and character recognition in one forward pass. This architecture, as shown in
Figure 2.2, utilizes convolutional layers for feature extraction, incorporates a region pro-
posal network for plate detection, and employs recurrent neural networks (RNNs) com-
bined with Connectionist Temporal Classiﬁcation (CTC) for sequence recognition. The
model achieves a recognition accuracy of 96.13% on datasets such as CarFlag-Large and
processes images in approximately 300ms on a Titan X GPU, considerably outperforming
the traditional two-stage approach that requires about 450ms per image.
Figure 2.2: Combined architecture for end-to-end ALPR from [9].
The paper by Zhenbo Xu et al. [10] introduces CCPD, the largest and most diverse pub-
licly available license plate (LP) dataset with more than 250,000 images annotated with
bounding boxes and vertex locations. To enhance ALPR, the authors propose a novel
architecture: Roadside Parking Net (RPnet), which integrates detection and recognition
into a single forward pass using shared convolutional features. RPnet is designed to be
eﬃcient and accurate, operating at more than 60 frames per second (FPS) with 98.5%
recognition accuracy, signiﬁcantly outperforming existing methods. The study empha-
sizes the importance of large, diverse datasets and uniﬁed models for robust ALPR in
unconstrained real-world environments.
In [11], the authors present a highly eﬃcient ALPR system that leverages the YOLO object
detection framework in a multi-model approach.
This system starts with two YOLO-
based models for vehicle and license plate detection, proceeds with a segmentation model
to locate individual characters, and ﬁnishes with two separate classiﬁcation models for
letters and digits, as illustrated in Figure 2.3. To further improve performance, the authors
applied data augmentation techniques, such as inverting and ﬂipping license plates. Their
approach achieved an impressive recognition rate of 93.53% at 47 FPS on the SSIG dataset,
surpassing commercial solutions like Sighthound and OpenALPR.

2.3
ALPR and Neural Architecture Search
While the previously discussed ALPR models are carefully hand-designed to achieve high
accuracy and speed, Neural Architecture Search (NAS) oﬀers a promising framework to
automate and optimize the architecture selection process, potentially surpassing manual
designs in both eﬃciency and performance.
In [13], the authors present a method to develop eﬃcient ALPR systems speciﬁcally tai-
lored for resource-constrained edge devices. Using NAS, they designed lightweight neural
networks optimized for both accuracy and computational costs, with a focus on real-time
performance and energy eﬃciency. Inspired by [14], they build a latency table of the se-
lected building blocks of future architectures and benchmark them on the selected target
device. Then, they used this latency table to tune the loss function of NAS during model
search. They also explore an alternative method, called partially connected diﬀerentiable
architecture search (PC-DARTS) [15], to create an additional set of hardware agnostic
models. These approaches yield solutions that achieve competitive accuracy while eﬀec-
tively operating on low-power platforms. Their two-stage approach to ALPR is presented
in Figure 2.4. In a subsequent study, [16] the same research group advances this work by
deploying a hardware solution using the previously optimized models.
To conclude, it is evident that NAS-driven ALPR solutions are in their early stages, yet
hold substantial promise for further advancement. With ongoing research and innova-
tion, these methods have the potential to redeﬁne the capabilities of ALPR on resource-
constrained devices, making real-time high-accuracy recognition feasible on aﬀordable
hardware.
This work aims to contribute to this pioneering ﬁeld, advancing the state-
of-the-art in energy-eﬃcient ALPR systems that can be adapted to real-world constraints.
The continuous development of NAS methodologies will only expand the possibilities for
practical and scalable ALPR applications in the future.

Theoretical Foundations
3.1
Neural Architecture Search
Neural Architecture Search (NAS) automates the design of neural network architectures,
shifting away from labor intensive manual architecture engineering.
Described in [17],
NAS seeks to optimize neural architectures for tasks such as image classiﬁcation and ob-
ject detection, often surpassing human-designed networks in performance. NAS operates
within three core components, as shown in Figure 3.1: (1) the search space, which de-
ﬁnes potential architectures, (2) the search strategy, which explores these architectures,
balancing eﬃciency and coverage, (3) performance estimation, which evaluates the ef-
fectiveness of the architectures. This framework enables NAS to discover high-performing
architectures with minimal human intervention, paving the way for enhanced adaptability
and eﬃciency in deep learning models.
Figure 3.1: Illustration of a general Neural Architecture Search pipeline [17].
The search space in NAS encompasses all possible network conﬁgurations deﬁned by a set
of building blocks such as convolutional layers, pooling operations, and skip connections. It
serves as the domain from which candidate architectures are drawn and evaluated. A well-
designed search space is crucial: it must be expressive enough to include high-performing
models, yet constrained enough to allow eﬃcient exploration by the search algorithm.
NAS employs several search strategies to eﬃciently navigate the vast space of possible
architectures. Random Search oﬀers a simple baseline by sampling architectures arbitrar-
ily, though it tends to be computationally ineﬃcient and lacks direction. Reinforcement
Learning-based NAS uses an agent that iteratively learns to design better architectures
by receiving rewards based on model performance, thereby gradually converging on more
optimal designs. Evolutionary Algorithms simulate biological evolution by evolving
a population of architectures through mutation and crossover, retaining only the most
promising designs over successive generations. Meanwhile, Bayesian Optimization uses
probabilistic models to estimate architectural performance, which helps narrow down the
search space to high-potential areas, especially useful when each evaluation is expensive.
A notable study used an evolutionary algorithm for neural architecture search, where can-
didate architectures evolve over successive generations through tournament selection and
a regularization mechanism [18]. This method eﬀectively explores vast architecture spaces,
achieving competitive performance on benchmarks such as CIFAR-10 and ImageNet. Its
inherent parallelism and ﬂexibility allow for diverse architectural proposals without impos-
ing strict assumptions on the search space. However, compared to Bayesian optimization,
evolutionary methods are often more computationally expensive and sample ineﬃcient,

requiring numerous evaluations before converging, and their stochastic nature can lead to
less reproducible results.
Another inﬂuential work using a reinforcement learning framework in which an RNN-
based controller generates architectural proposals that are evaluated and rewarded based
on performance [19]. Through policy gradient methods, the controller eﬀectively navigates
the complex and discrete search space to uncover architectures that rival state-of-the-art
models. This approach excels at capturing non-linear dependencies between architectural
components, facilitating the discovery of innovative designs. On the downside, RL-based
NAS is typically computationally intensive and suﬀers from high reward variance, making
it less sample-eﬃcient and sometimes unstable compared to Bayesian optimization, which
uses uncertainty estimates to guide a more focused search.
The performance evaluation strategy is equally critical, as it determines how eﬀec-
tively the NAS framework can assess each candidate architecture without prohibitive com-
putational costs. Rather than training every candidate to complete convergence, strate-
gies such as early stopping, proxy tasks, and weight-sharing are commonly used to provide
rapid and approximate performance estimates.
A common practice in NAS is to use proxy datasets - smaller or simpliﬁed versions of the
target dataset - to reduce computational time during architecture evaluation. However,
reliance on proxy datasets can sometimes yield models that do not generalize well to the
full dataset. To address this, methods such as ProxylessNAS [20] have been developed;
they directly train and evaluate candidate architectures on the target dataset and hardware
using a diﬀerentiable search process that avoids proxies without compromising eﬃciency.
Similarly, the One-for-All (OFA) approach [5] trains a supernetwork that encompasses
multiple subnetworks of varying sizes and complexities, which can be selected or ﬁne-
tuned based on speciﬁc resource constraints.
Moreover, recent advances such as Diﬀerentiable Architecture Search (DARTS) [21] have
further streamlined the NAS process by enabling gradient-based optimization over the
architecture space, signiﬁcantly reducing search time and computational demands. These
developments, along with other emerging techniques, such as weight-sharing strategies
[22], continue to push the boundaries of what NAS can achieve, making it increasingly
accessible and scalable for real-world applications.
However, it is important to note that despite these advances, NAS remains a resource-
intensive process, often requiring substantial computational power and memory to evaluate
and store large-scale architectures directly on the target dataset or within supernetwork
frameworks. This challenge continues to drive research into more eﬃcient search strategies
and performance estimation techniques. In addition, early adoptions of NAS focused on
image classiﬁcation problems, a trend that has been changing in recent time, with attention
shifting to object detection tasks as well.
3.2
Bayesian Optimization NAS with Ax
Bayesian Optimization (BO) is a powerful framework for optimizing expensive black-
box functions where evaluations are costly or time consuming. It works by building a
probabilistic surrogate model - often a Gaussian Process - that predicts the objective
function’s behavior based on past observations. This model is then used to guide the
search for the optimal solution by selecting new evaluation points that oﬀer a promising
balance between exploring uncertain regions and exploiting known high-performing areas.
In essence, BO provides an eﬃcient strategy to navigate complex, high-dimensional spaces
with minimal function evaluations.

Gaussian Processes
Gaussian Processes (GPs) are a non-parametric Bayesian modeling tool that provides a
probabilistic framework for learning about unknown functions. In NAS with Bayesian
optimization, GPs are used as surrogate models that approximate the performance func-

intersection-over-union (IOU) accuracy in license plate detection. Reducing latency en-

3.2.4
Ax - a platform that brings it all together
Ax is an advanced experimentation platform that leverages the principles of Bayesian op-
timization to streamline the design, execution, and analysis of optimization experiments.
By integrating with libraries such as BoTorch and PyTorch, Ax automates many of the
details of experiment conﬁguration and bookkeeping - such as managing hyperparameters,
logging results, and tracking experiment progress - thus freeing researchers from reinvent-
ing the wheel. This robust framework is particularly useful for complex tasks like Neural
Architecture Search, where exploring vast design spaces manually would be prohibitively
time-consuming.
Ax implements BO by constructing Gaussian Process surrogate models to capture the
relationship between input conﬁgurations (e.g., neural network architecture parameters)
and objective outcomes (e.g., latency and IOU accuracy). These GP models provide not
only predictive means but also uncertainty estimates, which are essential for balancing
exploration and exploitation. Ax uses these predictions to compute acquisition functions,
such as Expected Improvement (EI) and Expected Hypervolume Improvement (EHVI)
[26], to guide the selection of new candidate solutions. In multi-objective scenarios, Ax’s
use of EHVI helps quantify the expected gain in the hypervolume of the Pareto front,
ensuring that the search eﬃciently converges toward a set of optimal solutions.
Ax experiments work with a ﬂagging system, where each experiment can result in one of
four outcomes: COMPLETED - the experiment ran and returned values for the objectives;
FAILED - the experiment did not return values, but can be retried later; ABANDONED -
same as FAILED, but will not be retried; and RUNNING - currently running experiment.
When ﬁtting the surrogate models, only datapoints that have a COMPLETED ﬂag are
used. The importance of these ﬂags will be highlighted further in the results.

This chapter presents the methodology for designing, training, and evaluating energy-

Building Block Selection
In conﬁguring the Neural Architecture Search for license plate detection, a diverse set of

that adaptively recalibrate channel-wise feature responses, improving the networks ability
to focus on informative features and further boosting eﬃciency [28].

for small errors (promoting stability) and L1 loss for large errors (reducing sensitivity to
outliers). This property makes it particularly well-suited for bounding box regression.

optimization task, with the goals of minimizing latency and reducing validation IoU loss

Latency Measurement and Estimation
To conﬁgure NAS to target models that run at a speciﬁed latency on the Raspberry Pi, the
ﬁrst step was to measure the latency of individual building blocks directly on the device.
Initially, a naïve approach was adopted by measuring the speed of each block for a ﬁxed
input size of 224 × 224 pixels in 15 trials. These averaged measurements were compiled
into a latency table, and assumed that the overall latency of a model is estimated simply
by summing the latency of its constituent blocks, i.e.,
lat(arch) =
∑
lat(blocks).
(4.2)
However, as experiments progressed, it became clear that this simplistic method did not
accurately capture the true speed of the architectures.
To address this shortcoming, a reﬁned latency measurement process was developed, recog-
nizing the need to measure each combination of in-out channels in a range of input sizes,
from as small as 1 × 1 to the full 224 × 224. Such benchmarking would have provided
detailed insights into the latency behavior of each building block, but it would also require
an impractical number of measurements and long hours of testing on the Raspberry Pi
and would have also resulted in a less general solution.
To overcome this challenge, it was decided to sample from this large set of measurements
and ﬁt a regression model to the resulting data. 28 equally distanced points were sampled
from 1 to 224 that ran 20 trials for each building block, the ﬁrst ﬁve of which were used
as warm-up steps and were not included in the averaged data point. After experiment-
ing with various regression approaches, ﬁtting a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) yielded
an eﬀective latency estimator. This MLP-based estimator successfully captured the rela-
tionship between input size, in-out channel conﬁguration, and observed latency, enabling
accurate predictions of overall model latency without the need for exhaustive benchmark-
ing. Finally, this MLP-based latency estimator was used to predict the latency of the
NAS-proposed models.

In the experiments, candidate models were primarily evaluated on two metrics: inference

ensuring that the models meet real-time constraints.
To account for variability, each

robust estimate. Detection accuracy is assessed on an unseen dataset using the IoU, which
reﬂects how well the predicted bounding boxes overlap with the ground truth.
The performance of candidate architectures obtained via NAS was initially assessed by
constructing the Pareto front, which captures the best trade-oﬀs between low inference
latency and high detection accuracy. From this Pareto front came the selection of promis-

expanded dataset to verify whether their performance improves or worsens with respect
to the one observed during the NAS process.
After extended training, both the candidate models and the YOLOv8n benchmark were
quantized to evaluate the impact of reduced precision on the inference speed and accuracy.

terms of IoU accuracy and prediction speed on the target device. This evaluation frame-

speed or accuracy performance compared to the established YOLOv8n baseline.

The average inference time measured for the building blocks across 15 runs is shown in

these blocks completely, thus reducing the search space.
The Identity block was also
excluded from further latency experimentation as it’s speed could be considered to be
instantaneous.

Although the GPU estimations are rather okay, the proposed method simply does not

For this new experiment, all building blocks had to be measured for every in/out channel
combination and possible image sizes, which meant 5 × 5 × 224 = 5600 measurements for
a single block, so 5600 × 10 = 56000 total measurements to cover the (reduced) search
space. To gather a statistically signiﬁcant sample size, this process should be repeated
20 times, with the ﬁrst 5 serving as warm-up runs, and omitted from the ﬁnal average.

solution. Instead, the decision was taken to sample the search space and ﬁt a regression
model on the gathered datapoints with which predictions could be made, resulting in a
more generalized solution that could also scale with larger search spaces.

5.2.2
Reﬁned Pipeline
The redesign of the Ax environment draws inspiration from [31]. This approach steps
away from the Ax Client-based implementation and uses a TorchX-based runner with a
Scheduler. A schematic of the working principle is shown in Figure 5.7.
Runners in Ax are responsible for interfacing with the computational infrastructure - they
prepare the environment, launch trials (or jobs), and retrieve their outcomes. Essentially,
a Runner encapsulates all the details necessary to execute a single trial.
The TorchX
runner in Ax builds on these concepts by using the TorchX framework, a tool designed
to orchestrate PyTorch jobs in distributed settings. By integrating TorchX, the runner
enables Ax to submit trials as TorchX jobs, taking advantage of its robust scheduling,
containerization, and resource management capabilities. Instead of relying on functions in
the same environment as the Ax client, TorchX can distribute jobs onto .py ﬁles directly,
calling them in a similar fashion to running a ﬁle in the command line.
The Scheduler functions as the control loop for the optimization process. It interfaces
with the backend to initiate trials, monitor their progress, and collect outcomes. In this
work, it accomplishes these tasks by reading and parsing locally saved logs, whereas in a
remote execution environment it would make API calls.

used in this work is shown in Listing A.6. This ﬁle was called by the TorchX Runner
shown in Listing A.2. The task of the runner is to package and distribute the parameters
proposed by the Ax Experiment. The Search Space was conﬁgured in a similar fashion

• Models that were infeasible due to matrix multiplication errors were marked as

model.
• Model training time was increased to 400 seconds for every trial. Models that did
not train within 200 seconds were skipped. Initial learning rate was set to 0.02, and
was decreased to 0.01 after 200 seconds.
• The training set was reduced to 1500 images, while the validation set was set to
1000. The aim of this was to balance out the increase in train time and allow for
more trials as well.
• The number of trials were substantially increased, initially to around 100, later to
170 trials per experiment. As such, the number of Sobol and BoTorch trials were
also modiﬁed accordingly.
• A total of eight experiments were conducted, each with its own slight tweak.

Experiments 3 and 4 - standardizing the objectives
Building on the knowledge of the previous two tests, the number of trials for Experiment
3 was increased to 150 (80 Sobol + 65 BoTorch), to allow for a suﬃciently large sample
size after the random sampling, but to also leave room for model exploitation. The Inden-
tityBlock - which is a block without any eﬀect on the input - was also removed from the
options, to decrease the search space. This experiment resulted in a a very nice Pareto
front, with high viability of unique models, as shown in Figure 5.10. However, the lack of
improvement in the hypervolume indicates that in this experiment the exploitation phase
did not bring signiﬁcant improvements and there was also a lack of datapoints in the top
right of the latency-loss space.
The GP model in Ax drives the selection of promising candidates by balancing explo-
ration and exploitation based on the observed performance. In this case, the GP might
have focused its exploitation on regions with low latency because the model had lower un-
certainty there, leading the acquisition function to consistently select candidates in that
area. In contrast, high-latency regions, which can span a much wider numerical range,
may have exhibited higher uncertainty or simply appeared less promising in terms of im-
provement, resulting in fewer proposals from that part of the search space. In addition,
the time constrained training also meant that slower models trained for less epochs, thus
their convergence was not guaranteed.
This questioned the diﬀerence in the scales of the objectives: validation loss is constrained
between 0 and 1; latency can vary from 0 to potentially unbounded values. Without proper
scaling, this disparity in magnitudes can cause the GP - and thus the acquisition function
- to be overly inﬂuenced by the latency objective, skewing the optimization toward regions
where latency is already low.

Experiment 8 was designed to test the resilience of GP model ﬁtting when the training
data for each proposed architecture is randomized. Although both experiments used a

introducing additional variability compared to a ﬁxed dataset. Typically, a ﬁxed dataset
ensures that every model is trained and evaluated on the same data ordering, yielding
consistent performance measurements that make it easier for the optimization algorithm
to assess candidate quality. In contrast, randomizing the data introduces noise that can
lead to ﬂuctuations in performance - often resulting in fewer completed trials and a lower
number of feasible models, as shown in Figure 5.14. The higher feasibility rate, on the
other hand,in the Sobol phase is likely because the weights of the building blocks are
randomly initialized. This means that when the same architecture is retrained, the random
initialization can sometimes lead to better performance.

Initial Experiments
Training models on a proxy dataset does not guarantee identical performance on larger
datasets [20]. In addition, given the time-constrained nature of the NAS experimentation
phase, it was fair to assume that to truly evaluate the best performing models, extended
trainings would be required. Sampling from the best architectures during the initial NAS
trials, a complete training pipeline was set-up to evaluate the performance in an extended
training on an increased dataset. For this pipeline the training dataset was increased to
10000 images, while the validation set was raised to 4000, with batch sizes of 24 and 16
respectively.

Initially, a ﬂat learning rate was applied, but it soon became evident that this approach
was not optimal. Consequently, CosineAnnealingLR was adopted as the scheduler, as it
supports repeated cycles of gradual reduction and warm restarts - enabling the model

5.4.1
Quantization
Quantization is the process of reducing the numerical precision of a models parameters
and activations (e.g., from 32-bit ﬂoats to 16-bit or 8-bit integers) to decrease memory
usage, accelerate inference, and lower power consumption. This is typically achieved by
converting the model weights and calibrating activation ranges using representative data,
either statically (post-training quantization) or dynamically. This work uses the ONNX
library for static quantization [32], which consists of the following steps:
• Exporting to ONNX: The ﬁrst code block loads the NAS-derived models us-
ing PyTorch, reconstructs each model architecture, and then exports each PyTorch
model to the ONNX format. This conversion is necessary to leverage ONNX-based
quantization tools, Listing A.7.
• Converting to Float16: The second block iterates over the exported ONNX mod-
els and converts their weights from 32-bit ﬂoats to 16-bit ﬂoats using
float16.convert_float_to_float16 and the converted models are saved for fur-
ther processing, Listing A.8.
• Preprocessing for Quantization: The third section uses a subprocess call to run
ONNX Runtimes preprocessing module. This module prepares the ONNX models
for quantization, ensuring they conform to the necessary speciﬁcations for further
optimization. This step is necessary for INT16 and INT8 quantization, Listing A.9.
• Preparing Calibration Data: The fourth block constructs a calibration dataset
from a custom dataset (in this case, from the CCPD_4k_224 dataset) by loading
a subset of images. A custom calibration data reader (MyCalibrationDataReader)
is implemented to feed these images into the quantization algorithm, ensuring that
the quantized model correctly captures the activation ranges, Listing A.10.

• Static Quantization to INT8/INT16: Finally, the last block applies static quan-

6
Conclusion
In conclusion, this work has successfully demonstrated that Neural Architecture Search
(NAS) through Bayesian optimization can be used to design energy-eﬃcient automatic
license plate recognition (ALPR) models that are well-suited for deployment on resource-
constrained edge devices. By establishing a comprehensive NAS pipeline - from the se-
lection of CNN building blocks and the construction of a tailored search space to the
integration of a latency estimator and the implementation of Bayesian optimization with
Ax - the study has enabled the automated discovery of lightweight architectures that strike
a favorable balance between detection accuracy and inference speed.
The initial experiments provided a solid foundation by validating the end-to-end NAS
process on a proxy dataset, despite the inherent challenges posed by time-constrained
training. These experiments underscored the importance of carefully calibrated hyper-
parameters, appropriate loss function design, and the important role of a robust latency
estimator in guiding the search. The subsequent reﬁned pipeline, which incorporated a
more accurate latency measurement process and increased trial counts, yielded a Pareto
front of candidate models that clearly highlighted the trade-oﬀs between accuracy and
speed. Extended training on a larger dataset further conﬁrmed that many of the NAS-
discovered models can achieve performance levels competitive with, or even superior to,
the benchmark YOLOv8n, particularly when quantization strategies (Float16, INT16, and
INT8) are applied to optimize inference on low-power devices.
Moreover, by benchmarking these models across diverse hardware platforms - including
GPUs, CPUs, and Raspberry Pi 4B - the study has provided a detailed performance
proﬁle that demonstrates the practical viability of NAS-based ALPR systems. Notably,
several NAS models achieved substantial speedups relative to YOLOv8n, making them
compelling alternatives for real-time deployment in edge environments. In comparison with
the LPLite models from a previous studies, the top-performing NAS-derived architectures
exhibit promising accuracy, thereby validating the objectives set forth in the introduction.
Overall, this work not only challenged the state-of-the-art in energy-eﬃcient ALPR by
leveraging NAS methodologies but also establishes a framework for future research in
optimizing deep learning models for edge deployment. The ﬁndings highlight the potential
of automated architecture search in addressing the dual challenges of high accuracy and
low latency, ultimately contributing to the development of more eﬃcient and scalable
solutions in intelligent transportation and smart city applications.
