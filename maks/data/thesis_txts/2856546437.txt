Abstract  
This thesis focuses on energy management for utility-scale wind farms in Spain that 
operate under a combination of merchant exposure and contracts secured through 
Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs). While uncertainties of market conditions and 
wind farm production have been explored in recent research, the dynamic nature of 
these factors presents ongoing complexities. The unique aspect of this thesis is the 
development of a more comprehensive method for accounting for these uncertainties. 
In this approach, decision-making is conducted every quarter to determine the Hedge 
Ratio, which is the volume of anticipated production to be hedged for the short term. 
The hedges are executed using financial derivatives in the Iberian Electricity Market, 
specifically the Quarterly Financial Baseload Future Contract.  
The challenge is tackled using a single-stage optimization model that optimizes the 
hedging strategy for an energy producer across four distinct stages, where each stage 
operates as a single-stage optimization that balances expected revenue with risk 
aversion, using Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR) to manage downside risk. The 
objective is to maximize revenue while controlling potential losses by selecting an 
optimal hedge quantity in each period. Each stage operates independently, 
incorporating realized decisions from previous stages as fixed parameters, thus 
reducing computational complexity compared to a traditional multistage stochastic 
model. The model optimized across scenarios reflecting the uncertainty of the main 
risk factors of a hedging strategy using a Baseload contract, namely the spot price, the 
capture rate, and the production of the wind farm.  
Optimization techniques are employed to solve the problem iteratively, considering 
probability distributions using Monte Carlo simulation. The objective function 
balances revenue maximization with risk mitigation, allowing wind farm operators to 
tailor the hedging strategy to their risk appetite. The optimized hedging strategy 
provides actionable insights for wind farm operators to improve financial 
performance and risk control.  
However, the modelвҖҷs effectiveness is contingent on the accuracy of the input data and 
the assumptions made. Future research could expand on this thesis by incorporating 
additional revenue streams such as intraday market and ancillary services, improving 
input data accuracy with probabilistic forecasts for wind production and market 
conditions, considering colocation with battery storage and/or electrolyzers, and 
investigating the portfolio effect of having multiple wind farms either in different 
locations or incorporating other technologies.  
Overall, this thesis contributes to the field of renewable energy market participation 
strategies, by offering a rigorous framework for optimizing hedging strategies in 
uncertain market environments. By integrating optimization techniques with risk 
management principles, wind farm operators can make informed decisions to 
maximize revenue while effectively managing downside risk, ultimately enhancing 
the financial sustainability and competitiveness of renewable energy investments.

1. Introduction 
The transition to net-zero greenhouse gas emissions by 2050 necessitates a 
significant shift in our energy landscape, with wind and solar energy poised to 
become the predominant sources of power generation. To align with the Net Zero 
pathway, annual global wind capacity additions must increase substantially by 2030. 
In 2022, wind electricity generation rose by 265 TWh (a 14% increase), and wind 
investment reached a record USD 185 billion, growing by 20% in the same year, 
according to IEA [1]. 
However, sustaining and accelerating this growth in wind generation investments 
presents a formidable challenge. The main factor that can decelerate wind integration 
into the energy mix is the uncertainty on the revenues during the lifetime of such an 
investment. As Europe transitions from a fully subsidized economic environment for 
renewables to liberalized and competitive electricity markets [2], effective risk 
management becomes critical to assure investors and creditors of sufficient returns 
and keep the transition toward Net Zero on track. Spain, which has set ambitious 
renewable energy targets including the addition of 90 gigawatts (GW) of renewable 
capacity by 2030 offers a relevant case study. Despite these goals, Spain currently 
installs around 1 GW of wind capacity annually, far below the 2.8 GW required to meet 
its target, highlighting the challenges in achieving rapid scale-up [3] 
Electricity markets are complex, due to the interplay of supply-demand dynamics, 
generation costs, and grid constraints. Price volatility and intermittent generation, 
primarily resulting from weather conditions and fuel prices, are the main drivers of 
revenue uncertainty. Wind output, which varies with wind speed, significantly 
impacts revenues, introducing additional uncertainty. In Spain, these challenges are 
compounded by permitting delays, grid connection issues, and competition with solar 
energy development [3]. To stabilize revenue streams and ensure bankability and 
financial viability for wind generation projects in this uncertain and volatile 
environment, sophisticated risk management is essential. 
While the existing literature provides a comprehensive foundation for understanding 
the complexities of financial risk management in energy systems with high renewable 
penetration, it primarily focuses on specific areas such as electricity market dynamics 
and price forecasting, risk management and hedging in energy trading, stochastic 
programming and optimization in energy systems, wind power forecasting, and 
renewable energy contracts and policy. These areas are crucial for grasping the 
intricacies of the energy market and the strategies for managing risks.

However, there are notable gaps in the current research: 
1. The literature often overlooks the integration of these diverse aspects into a

2. Many studies remain theoretical and fail to capture real-world elements and 
uncertainties affecting wind farm revenue and risk. Specifically, most

The main contribution of this research is to use optimization techniques to enhance 
risk management techniques for wind farms. By applying a multidisciplinary 
approach, this work aligns market and weather condition uncertainties with practical 
risk-mitigating tools, thereby supporting the financial viability and sustainability of 
renewable energy systems (RES). 
This research develops an integrated, modular solution that allows for future 
modifications, extensions, and improvements by other researchers. This modular 
approach ensures that all main uncertainties affecting revenues and risk exposures 
are comprehensively captured, providing a more robust and practical framework for 
financial risk management in energy systems with high renewable penetration. 
The analysis integrates all components affecting the revenue outcome for the wind 
farm, including uncertainties such as wind farm generation, day-ahead market price, 
captured price, and prices of electricity financial derivatives. It also includes inputs 
regarding Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs) and probabilistic forecasts to predict 
uncertain factors. By incorporating volume and profile risks into the analysis, the 
study provides a more accurate and practical framework for financial risk 
management. 
By using a case study of a utility-scale wind farm operating in Spain, the research 
bridges the gap between theoretical models and real-world applications. Although 
actual data will not be used, the data will replicate real-world conditions. 
The objective of this thesis is to develop a more comprehensive method for managing 
the associated financial risks. The research question is: How can wind farm operators 
optimize their hedging strategies in uncertain market environments to maximize 
revenue while effectively managing downside risk? 
While this research aims to provide a comprehensive framework for financial risk 
management in wind farms, there are expected limitations. The modelвҖҷs effectiveness 
is highly dependent on the accuracy of input data and the assumptions made. Future 
research could address these limitations by incorporating actual data from wind 
farms, expanding the model to include additional revenue streams, improving input 
data accuracy with more sophisticated probabilistic forecasts, considering colocation 
with battery storage and/or electrolyzers, and investigating the portfolio effect of 
having multiple wind farms in different locations or incorporating other technologies.

2. Literature Review 
In this section, the dynamics of electricity markets and price forecasting will be 
explored, followed by an examination of risk management and hedging in energy 
trading, a delve into stochastic programming and optimization in energy systems, a 
discussion on wind power forecasting, and finally, a review of renewable energy 
contracts and policy.  
2.1 Price Forecasting and Electricity Market Dynamics 
Multiple challenges and risks affecting RES result from price forecasting and market 
dynamics. In this field, several studies have made significant contributions. Studies 
have investigated the challenges of price volatility and uncertainty posed by 
liberalized electricity markets, smart grids, and high penetration of renewable energy 
sources (RESs)[4]. A novel pricing scheme was developed, called flexibility real-time 
pricing (FRTP), to maximize profits for energy service providers (ESPs) from both 
traditional markets (wholesale/day-ahead and retail) and innovative flexibility 
markets, offering dynamically adjustable financial incentives to users. Meanwhile, 
another study delved into the complexities of predicting electricity prices in the 
European market, known for their volatility [5]. This research explored the potential 
of various machine learning techniques in accurately predicting these prices and 
incorporated previously unconsidered predictive features into the forecasting 
models, such as the price histories of neighboring countries. 
The risk inherent to the interplay between sequential markets using different time 
horizons (future-spot) was explored in [6] and [7]. In [6] an option contract model 
was designed based on a renewable energy price index using data from the Nord Pool 
exchange system and used an auto-regressive integrated moving average (ARIMA) 
model to predict this price index. In the Nordic power market, a study examined the 
relationship between the price of weekly futures contracts and subsequent spot 
prices from January 2004 to December 2013 [7]. The research found that futures 
prices are biased predictors of the subsequent spot prices, and there is a significant 
forward premium in the Nordpool market, especially during winter and autumn 
which is a direct result of the asymmetry in volatility and risk. ConsumersвҖҷ stronger 
aversion to price spikes and producersвҖҷ preference for revenue stability collectively 
secure a risk premium that elevates futures prices, ensuring stability for both sides at 
the cost of a higher forward price compared to subsequent spot prices. This is 
particularly important to the wind industry, as the wind resource is typically higher 
during winter and autumn, aligning with the periods when the forward premium in 
the Nord Pool market is most significant. This seasonal increase in wind resource 
availability means that wind energy producers can benefit from favorable market 
conditions. 
Further studies associate price risk with market maturity. In terms of market 
volatility, a study investigated the volatility of 15 power markets in Europe, focusing 
on the Southeast Europe (SEE) markets [8]. The research found that power price

volatility is higher in new SEE markets compared to more mature European markets 
due to limited competition, regulatory uncertainty, infrastructure limitations, lower 
market liquidity, and dependence on electricity imports. These factors combined 
contribute to the higher volatility observed in these newer markets. Another study 
examined the changes in electrical systems undergoing green transition, particularly 
in Spain, focusing on making them more liberalized and competitive [9]. The research 
developed a tool to predict the next dayвҖҷs electricity price as accurately as possible 
and benchmarked several machine-learning models.  
Lastly, researchers investigated the impact of an increasing market share of Variable 
Renewable Energy Sources (VRES), such as wind or solar power, on spot power price 
dynamics and the forward price premium [10]. The study found that markets with a 
high share of supply from VRES have a significantly lower forward premium than 
markets with a low market share of VRES. These findings underscore the critical need 
for producers, retailers, and other market participants to analyze and interpret the 
market on an individual basis. By doing so, they can better understand the unique 
dynamics and risks associated with wholesale price fluctuations. 
These studies highlight the complexities and challenges of electricity market 
dynamics and price forecasting. Understanding these dynamics is crucial for market 
participants to navigate price volatility and make informed decisions. This knowledge 
sets the stage for exploring risk management and hedging strategies, which are 
essential for mitigating financial risks and ensuring market stability in such a volatile 
environment. 
2.2 Risk Management and Hedging in Energy Trading 
Risk management and hedging play a crucial role in energy trading, helping to 
mitigate financial risks and ensure market stability. Recent research has increasingly 
focused on the impact of different market types (e.g., forward and spot markets) and 
conditions (e.g., liquidity, supply, and demand), yielding insights particularly 
beneficial for the wind industry. 
In the realm of decision-making under uncertainty in renewable energy generation 
offered through electricity markets, [11] introduces a variant of the newsvendor 
problem (which determines the optimal inventory level for a perishable product with 
uncertain demand), referred to as the Bernoulli newsvendor problem. This research 
presents distributionally robust versions of the problem, considering ambiguity 
about both the probabilistic forecasts for power generation and the chance of success 
of the Bernoulli variable. The findings suggest that both versions of the 
distributionally robust Bernoulli newsvendor problem have closed-form solutions, 
demonstrating the benefits of the approach through simulation studies and a real-
world case-study application. 
Simultaneously, the financial challenges faced by wind power producers in day-ahead 
electricity markets are explored in another piece of research [12]. This study 
proposes the use of a separate prediction market as a hedging method, where

5 
participants trade shares linked to specific outcomes, enabling wind power producers 
to offset imbalance costs through prediction market payouts. The research develops 
an optimal trading strategy, where the trading volume in the prediction market is 
analytically derived to minimize potential losses derived from imbalance costs. The 
pricing of shares is determined via an indifference utility condition, demonstrating 
that this approach can limit revenue losses and improve risk metrics. 
Building on this, [13] delves into the financial risk of imbalance costs for wind power 
producers in day-ahead electricity markets. It proposes a similar hedging method 
where producers trade in a prediction market, offsetting imbalance costs with 
prediction market payouts. The research develops an optimal trading strategy, 
minimizing potential loss and determining share prices via an indifference utility 
condition. Focusing solely on volumetric risk management and disregarding the 
market risk associated with electricity price volatility leads to a conclusion. The 
conclusion is that the hedging operation of the industrial portfolio offers substantial 
benefits, particularly when considering the worst-case scenario. 
In the context of the risks faced by electricity producers in electricity markets, [14] 
identifies options among electricity derivatives as an effective tool for managing these 
risks. The research proposes a multi-stage stochastic model to determine the optimal 
selling strategy for a risk-averse electricity producer, incorporating options, forward 
contracts, and pool trading.  
Further, a set of Value-at-Risk (VaR) models and their ability to describe and capture 
price-change risk in the European energy market is examined in a study [15]. The 
research makes in-sample, one-day-ahead VaR forecasts using a simple parametric 
model, a historical simulation model, and a Quantile Regression (QR) model. This 
research is further investigated in another study [16], which demonstrates that the 
QR model can provide accurate VaR forecasts in the European energy market. 
Lastly, [17] investigates the use of various trading strategies, based on Machine 
Learning (ML) methods, to profit from the risk premium of the Nordic electricity 
base-load week futures. The research indicates that the Support Vector Machine 
(SVM) is the most effective method and asserts that this market allows for profitable 
speculation, particularly using combinations of non-linear signal extraction 
techniques. 
In summary, the studies discussed highlight the importance of advanced risk 
management and hedging strategies in energy trading. These strategies, including the 
use of VaR models, prediction markets, and machine learning techniques, are crucial 
for mitigating financial risks and ensuring market stability. 
Understanding these methods sets the stage for exploring stochastic programming 
and optimization in energy systems. These techniques further enhance decision-
making processes by providing robust solutions under uncertainty, which is vital for 
integrating renewable energy sources effectively.

2.3 Stochastic Programming and Optimization in Energy Systems 
Stochastic programming is used to model and solve problems under uncertainty, and 
has shown high potential for optimizing energy systems and trading strategies for 
electricity market participants. While this thesis develops a single-stage optimization 
model, it is designed to implicitly mimic the functionality of a multistage stochastic 
framework. Therefore, the literature related to multistage stochastic optimization is 
assessed as highly relevant and used as a benchmark for evaluating the proposed 
approach. 
Starting with the work presented in [18], the researchers tackle the complex task of 
planning the electricity transition. This is a significant challenge due to the 
uncertainties in renewable energy and the need for extensive transmission 
investment. To address this, they introduce a new multistage stochastic mixed-integer 
model. This model uses a novel decomposition algorithm, demonstrating its 
scalability through its application to ChinaвҖҷs electric power requirements. They show 
that the model can effectively manage uncertainties and coordinate transmission 
investments, providing robust solutions for long-term planning. The insights 
provided by this study are valuable for policymaking, highlighting the modelвҖҷs 
computational tractability. 
Building on the theme of managing uncertainties, another piece of research [19] 
investigates the role of energy hubs in sustainable cities. These hubs are modern 
energy systems that couple different energy carriers to enhance energy efficiency and 
reliability. The researchers propose a two-stage stochastic model for the operation of 
energy hubs. This model operates in conjunction with day-ahead and real-time 
electricity markets, considering uncertainties in demands, renewable power, and real-
time electricity prices. A unique aspect of this model is the incorporation of value-at-
risk, which helps prevent high operation costs in the worst scenarios. This approach 
will be adopted in the thesis as well.  
Shifting the focus to the impact of uncertainties in renewable energy targets, [20] 
explores how these uncertainties affect investment planning in the electricity sector. 
The researchers use a multi-stage stochastic model to analyze the optimal mix of 
dispatchable power plants and system costs. The findings of this study shed light on 
how plants with a medium capital/operating cost ratio have a higher value under 
uncertainty, leading to lower investments in storage units. Despite these challenges, 
the study concludes that the costs induced by the implementation risk of renewable 
energies are relatively small compared to total system costs. 
In the context of forecasting energy production for secure bidding in electricity 
markets, [21] proposes a multistage stochastic decision-aid algorithm based on linear 
programming. This algorithm optimizes the bidding strategy in three different 
electricity markets (day-ahead, intraday, and balancing markets). The research 
represents a Virtual Power Plant with wind, solar PV, and storage technologies. The

findings of this study highlight the importance of accurate tools to handle the multi-
settlement framework of electricity markets and the uncertainties of daily operations. 
As we consider electricity markets with increased volatility, [22] delves into key 
uncertainties. The study aims to optimize day-ahead and intraday trading decisions 
for a portfolio with controllable and intermittent renewable generation, taking risk 
into account. The research includes a reserve market, a day-ahead market, and an 
intraday market in stochastic modeling and develops a multi-stage stochastic Mixed 
Integer Linear Program. The findings of this study assess the profitability and risk 
exposure, quantified by the conditional value at risk metric, of trading strategies 
following different risk preferences. 
Further, [23] presents a methodology to define the trading policy of an energy 
generation agent. This methodology uses stochastic programming to represent the 
uncertainty related to short-term price fluctuation. The researchers incorporate risk 
criteria in the decision-making model, achieved through the use of tools such as Value 
at Risk (VaR) and Conditional Value at Risk (CVaR). 
Lastly, addressing the increased financial risk faced by retailers in deregulated 
electricity markets, [24] proposes a multistage stochastic mean-variance 
optimization model. This model manages a portfolio of electricity derivative 
contracts, offering a solution to hedge against financial risks. The researchers apply 
two approximations to reduce computational complexity: aggregating the decision 
stages and solving the resulting problem in linear decision rules (LDR). The LDR 
approach restricts the set of recourse decisions to those affine in the history of the 
random parameters, leading to convex quadratic programs when applied to mean-
variance optimization models. 
In summary, these studies demonstrate the critical role of stochastic programming in 
managing uncertainties and optimizing decision-making in energy systems. By 
leveraging these advanced models, energy market participants can better navigate the 
complexities of renewable energy integration and market volatility. 
2.4 Wind Power Forecasting 
Wind power forecasting is essential for optimizing energy production and managing 
grid stability, especially with the increasing integration of renewable energy sources. 
A novel methodology for assessing the prediction risk of short-term wind power 
forecasts is presented in [25]. It calculates confidence intervals at a user-defined level 
and introduces two indices known as вҖңmeteo-riskвҖқ (MRI) and вҖңproduction-riskвҖқ (PRI) 
to quantify meteorological risk. These indices, measuring the spread of multi-scenario 
weather and wind power predictions, are used to adjust confidence intervals and alert 
operators about prediction risk. The key finding is a demonstrated link between these 
indices and prediction error levels. Tested on wind farms in Denmark and Ireland, this 
methodology is operational and adaptable to all wind power forecasting models.

The importance of consistent forecasts for decision-making, particularly with the rise 
in renewable energy capacities connected to the power grid is highlighted in [26]. The 
study reevaluates the optimal projection reconciliation approach in a distributed 
setup using the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM). The key 
contribution is the demonstration of the approachвҖҷs effectiveness through three case 
studies involving simulated data, the National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL) 
Wind Toolkit dataset, and a dataset from wind farms in Sardinia, Italy. 
Shifting the focus to the increased uncertainties in electricity markets due to the 
weather dependency on renewable energy sources, [27] quantifies how updates in 
predicted wind energy affect intraday electricity prices. The study uses wind speed-
based wind energy forecasts for its analysis. The findings suggest that wind energy 
updates negatively affect intraday prices, with the effectвҖҷs magnitude depending on 
the position within the merit order approximated by the level of residual load. The 
effect is largest for high residual loads and is augmented in situations of low residual 
load due to negative prices and larger average forecast errors for wind energy. The 
main contribution of this research is the implication that varying predicted residual 
loads necessitate different risk assessments and merit order-based models can help 
anticipate and account for the magnitude of price uncertainties due to wind energy 
forecast updates. 
Examining the cost implications of not utilizing efficient wind forecasting, [28] 
demonstrates in the Portuguese context that intermittent renewable power 
generation has a significant impact on balancing markets. Despite wind power 
fulfilling 23% of demand between 2012 and 2016, the absence of incentives for 
efficient wind forecasting results in consumers shouldering wind balancing costs. 
These costs are found to be approximately 2 euros per MWh, a figure kept low due to 
a robust transmission grid. The study suggests that by holding wind generators 
accountable for their imbalances, in line with practices in other European markets, 
consumers could potentially save millions annually. 
Lastly, the challenge of wind speed intermittency in wind power generation is 
investigated in [29]. The research underscores the value of a 15-minute time step 
series for short-term energy yield estimations. It highlights the importance of 
understanding short-term output variations for grid management, turbine control, 
and wind power optimization. The research analyzes energy yield from an 800kW 
wind turbine using 15-second time-series data, finding that theoretical output often 
underestimates actual yield by 5% - 48%. The study also notes instances of zero 
turbine output despite wind speeds above the cut-in speed, suggesting the impact of 
wind direction on yield. 
These studies highlight the critical role of accurate wind power forecasting in 
managing grid stability and optimizing energy production. Understanding and 
improving forecasting methods is essential for integrating renewable energy sources 
effectively and ensuring a reliable electricity supply.

2.5 Renewable Energy Contracts and Policy 
Renewable energy contracts and policies are crucial for promoting sustainable 
development and integrating renewable energy sources into the market. These 
frameworks provide the necessary support and incentives for both producers and 
consumers, ensuring a stable and sustainable energy landscape. By establishing clear 
guidelines and financial mechanisms, they help mitigate the risks associated with 
renewable energy investments and facilitate the transition to a low-carbon economy. 
In the field of renewable energy strategies, [30] delves into the role of power purchase 
agreements (PPAs) as key elements for sustainable development in large 
organizations. This study emphasizes the role of PPAs in delivering green energy 
efficiently and motivating stakeholders to participate in sustainability initiatives. A 
multicriteria decision analysis method, PROMETHEE, is introduced to evaluate the 
implementation of sustainability strategies using PPAs, offering a comprehensive 
approach to sustainable energy strategy selection and PPA structure optimization. 
Building upon this, the growing popularity of Renewable Power Purchase Agreements 
(PPAs) over the past decade is discussed in [31]. PPAs have facilitated significant 
renewable energy development and are integral to many sustainability programs. 
With a better understanding of the intermittent nature of renewable generation, PPAs 
have been refined to incorporate measures for handling uncertainties in both 
renewable energy production and electricity market prices. This underscores the 
ongoing evolution and complexity of PPAs in navigating the uncertainties prevalent in 
renewable energy markets. 
Transitioning to the significance of Contracts for Difference (CfDs) in incentivizing 
investment in low-carbon projects and new technologies, research [32] explores their 
increasing importance. CfDs have become instrumental in the rapid development of 
offshore wind in the UK and are central to European electricity market reforms. The 
research provides a comprehensive analysis of CfDs and their potential in advancing 
low-carbon solutions beyond electricity generation. 
Lastly, the вҖҳcannibalization effectвҖҷ, where wind and solar power lower market prices 
when their production is highest, is examined in research [33]. Despite its significant 
impact on the revenue of Variable Renewable Energy (VRE) technologies, this effect 
is often overlooked in capital budgeting literature. The study presents an analytical 
framework that captures the correlation between VRE production and electricity 
prices. This comprehensive model takes into account the cannibalization effect, 
thereby providing a more accurate basis for making VRE investment decisions. This 
model could serve as a valuable tool for policymakers, helping them understand and 
address the complexities of the cannibalization effect in renewable energy markets.

3. Fundamentals of Hedging in Electricity Forward Markets 
3.1 Introduction 
In the context of renewable energy assets such as wind farms, managing revenue 
volatility is a critical challenge. This volatility arises from fluctuating electricity 
market prices, intermittent generation profiles, and evolving energy market 
dynamics. Hedging strategies play a vital role in mitigating these risks, enabling wind 
farm operators to stabilize revenues and achieve their cash flow objectives. 
Hedging strategies typically involve two primary approaches: short-to-mid-term 
hedging and long-term hedging. These strategies address different time horizons and 
serve distinct purposes: 
в—Ҹ Long-Term Hedging via PPAs: Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs) provide 
revenue certainty over extended periods, typically ranging from 5 to 20 years. 
They are often essential for financing renewable energy projects, ensuring that 
fixed-price contracts support stable cash flows. However, PPAs usually involve 
trade-offs in terms of volume risk, generation profile alignment, and price risk.  
в—Ҹ Short-to-Mid-Term Hedging: Short-to-mid-term hedging strategies, using 
financial or physical derivatives, manage price risks over periods ranging from 
days to years. These instruments are essential for mitigating merchant 
exposure, where uncontracted output is sold on the volatile spot market, 
exposing revenues to price fluctuations. By locking in prices with financial 
instruments like futures or forwards, generators can safeguard against low 
market prices and stabilize cash flows. However, baseload contracts are not 
perfect hedges for intermittent generation due to production profile 
mismatches. Sophisticated risk management is required to address these 
mismatches and optimize the revenue-risk trade-off. When long-term 
agreements like PPAs cover all generation output, there is no need for such 
hedging strategies. 
This chapter begins by discussing merchant exposure, followed by short-to-mid-term 
hedging strategies, and concludes with an analysis of PPAs. Each section includes 
illustrative examples tailored to the thesis case study, ensuring a comprehensive 
understanding of these foundational concepts. 
3.2 Merchant Exposure 
Merchant exposure refers to a wind farmвҖҷs participation in the day-ahead market, 
where electricity prices fluctuate based on supply and demand dynamics. In the 
Iberian market, the day-ahead market is operated by OMIE1, functioning as a daily 
auction. Generators and consumers submit bids for each hour of the following day, 
and market-clearing prices are determined based on the supply-demand equilibrium.

The Day-Ahead Auction Process 
The day-ahead auction in the Spanish market is a uniform-price auction held daily to 
set electricity prices for each hour of the following day. The process involves the 
following steps: 
1. Bid Submission: 
в—Ҹ Generators, including wind farms, submit hourly supply bids based on 
their forecasted production and according to their anticipated marginal 
cost. 
в—Ҹ Retailers or big industrial consumers submit hourly demand bids 
reflecting their anticipated energy needs. 
2. Merit Order Principle: 
в—Ҹ The merit order principle is used to determine which bids are accepted 
and at what price. Supply bids are ranked in ascending order of price, 
while demand bids are ranked in descending order. 
в—Ҹ The market-clearing price for each hour is set at the intersection of the 
supply and demand curves. This price applies uniformly to all accepted 
bids. 
3. Nomination of Generation Rights: 
в—Ҹ Once the market clears, generation rights are allocated to producers 
whose bids are accepted. Wind farms are typically required to nominate 
their forecasted production as part of this process, reflecting their 
variable output. 
в—Ҹ Any deviation between the forecasted and actual generation is handled 
in subsequent intraday or balancing markets.  
Captured Rate 
For intermittent generators, such as wind farms, the variability in generation profiles 
further compounds this uncertainty, as revenues depend not only on prices but also 
on the timing and volume of production. 
While the day-ahead market sets hourly prices, a wind farmвҖҷs effective revenue is 
determined by its captured price, which reflects the average price achieved based on 
its generation profile. Wind farms often achieve lower capture rates compared to the 
market average due to the timing of their output aligning with periods of lower prices. 
For instance, wind generation tends to peak during off-peak hours or periods of high 
renewable production, driving down prices due to the merit order effect. The capture 
rate over a certain delivery period is a key metric that highlights the discrepancy 
between the market average price and the actual price achieved by the generator.

3.3 Financial and Physical Electricity Derivatives in the Spanish 
Market 
Electricity derivatives are essential tools for managing price risks in energy markets. 
These contracts are primarily traded on regulated exchanges such as OMIP and EEX, 
as well as in over-the-counter (OTC) markets. They fall into two broad categories: 
financial derivatives (e.g., futures and options) and physical derivatives (e.g., forwards 
linked to physical delivery). Their characteristics, such as delivery and trading 
periods, settlement mechanisms, and benchmarks, play a critical role in their 
application in hedging strategies. 
Delivery and Trading Periods and Liquidity 
The delivery period defines the timeframe during which electricity is delivered (or 
settled financially), while the trading period specifies when trading activity for the 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
14 
contract is allowed. Electricity derivatives in the Spanish market cater to different 
delivery profiles, including baseload, peak, and off-peak contracts, designed to match 
varying market demand patterns. 
1. Delivery Period: 
The delivery period ranges from daily, weekly, monthly, quarterly, to yearly durations 
and contracts include specific delivery profiles: 
в—Ҹ Baseload: Continuous electricity delivery across 24 hours of the day. 
в—Ҹ Peak: Delivery during high-demand hours (e.g., 08:00вҖ“20:00 on weekdays). 
в—Ҹ Off-Peak: Delivery during low-demand hours, typically nights and weekends. 
2. Trading Period: 
The trading period starts months or even years before the delivery period and 
concludes shortly before delivery begins. 
Examples: 
в—Ҹ OMIP Baseload Monthly Futures: Trading ends on the second-to-last business 
day before the delivery month. 
в—Ҹ EEX Futures: Trading ends on the second to fifth last trading day before 
delivery, depending on the product. 
3. Liquidity Concerns: 
Liquidity, or the ease with which contracts can be traded without significantly 
impacting their price, is a critical factor in derivative markets. Liquidity levels differ 
significantly across contracts: 
в—Ҹ Contract Duration: Short-term contracts (e.g., monthly or quarterly futures) 
tend to be more liquid than long-term contracts (e.g., yearly futures). 
в—Ҹ Contract Profile: Baseload contracts are generally more liquid than peak or off-
peak contracts due to their broader usage in hedging and trading. 
в—Ҹ Time to delivery: Contracts with delivery periods far in the future often 
experience limited trading activity, resulting in wider bid-ask spreads and 
higher transaction costs. As the delivery period approaches, trading activity 
typically intensifies, improving liquidity and narrowing spreads. 
This variability in liquidity impacts the execution of hedging strategies, especially for 
generators seeking to hedge positions over longer horizons. It is critical to factor in 
these dynamics when designing and implementing hedging strategies.

3.4 Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs) 
PPAs are long-term contracts between electricity generators and off-takers, providing 
revenue stability and price certainty. These agreements are a cornerstone of 
renewable energy project financing, offering more steady cash flows that help secure 
funding and reduce the financial risks associated with volatile energy markets. PPAs 
serve as a risk management tool for both parties, allowing them to share and trade 
volume, profile, and price risks based on the contract structure. 
Risk-Sharing Dynamics and Pricing 
The structure of a PPA impacts its pricing, as the allocation of risks between the 
generator and the off-taker determines the financial trade-offs. For example: 
в—Ҹ Generators typically demand higher fixed prices for baseload PPAs due to the 
volume risk they bear. Conversely, they accept lower prices for Pay as Produced 
(PaP) PPAs since the payments align with actual production. 
в—Ҹ Off-takers prioritize price stability and supply predictability, which may result 
in a premium for contracts that limit their exposure to variability, such as 
baseload or capped PPAs.

4.4 Real-World Applicability 
The case study reflects real-world practices and challenges faced by wind farm 
operators: 
1. Dynamic Hedging: Staggered hedging reduces price volatility exposure, 
mirroring common strategies in liberalized markets. 
2. Uncertainty Management: Models evolving price and capture rate forecasts, 
enabling decisions to reflect improved accuracy closer to delivery. 
3. Alignment with Risk Management: Incorporates Conditional Value-at-Risk 
(CVaR) to address downside risks, meeting investor expectations. 
4. Practical Adjustments: Unwinding positions ensures alignment with 
production and market forecasts, a critical feature in operational risk 
management.

5. Optimization Model  
5.1 Model Overview 
This model optimizes the hedging strategy for an energy producer across four distinct 
stages, where each stage operates as a single-stage optimization that balances 
expected revenue with risk aversion, using Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR) to 
manage downside risk. The objective is to maximize revenue while controlling 
potential losses by selecting an optimal hedge quantity in each period. 
Each stage operates independently, incorporating realized decisions from previous 
stages as fixed parameters, thus reducing computational complexity compared to a 
traditional multistage stochastic model. The model efficiently maintains a cumulative 
revenue impact over the planning horizon by sequentially passing hedge and 
unwinding decisions forward as parameters. 
An opportunity cost term is incorporated to capture potential market dynamics, 
which estimates the potential benefit of delaying hedge decisions based on expected 
future prices. This term encourages lower hedge quantities in earlier stages when 
future prices are expected to increase, providing a simplified mechanism to account 
for anticipated market conditions. 
Market prices, production volumes, and capture rates are treated as uncertain 
parameters within each stage, represented through scenario-based sampling, 
allowing the model to simulate market variability.

achievable market prices for energy sales for intermittent technologies. 
3. Production Volume: Modeled as a normal distribution, representing 
fluctuations in production due to operational or environmental factors. 
These distributions enable scenario generation for each stage t, creating diverse 
outcomes to capture potential variations in price, capture rate, and production.

5.7 Solver and Implementation 
The optimization model in this thesis was implemented using Pyomo, a versatile 
Python-based optimization modeling library, and solved using the GLPK (GNU Linear 
Programming Kit) solver. This combination provided a robust and accessible 
framework for developing, testing, and executing the optimization algorithm across 
multiple scenarios and parameters. 
Solver Choice 
The choice of GLPK was driven by its ability to handle large-scale linear programming 
problems efficiently and its compatibility with Pyomo. GLPK is an open-source solver, 
making it a practical and cost-effective option for academic research while offering 
sufficient computational power to solve the structured optimization problems in this 
study. 
Integration and Settings 
Pyomo was used to define the modelвҖҷs variables, constraints, and objective function, 
ensuring a clear and modular implementation. The library's seamless interface with 
GLPK allowed for straightforward solver invocation and result interpretation. 
GLPK was configured with default settings for solving linear programming problems. 
No specific modifications to tolerance or time limits were required during the 
standard executions. 
Performance Metrics 
The runtime for each stage's optimization ranged between 5 and 10 seconds. The 
solver effectively handled up to 10,000 scenarios without significant degradation in 
performance, validating its suitability for this problem size. GLPK consistently 
achieved optimal solutions, as verified through scenario-based validation and result 
comparisons across multiple executions. 
Challenges and Mitigation 
As an open-source solver, GLPK has limitations in handling advanced non-linear or 
integer programming problems. While these were not encountered in this model, 
transitioning to a solver like Gurobi or CPLEX could be beneficial for models requiring 
more advanced features. 
Summary of Results 
The Pyomo-GLPK framework demonstrated strong performance for the single-stage 
optimization model, providing accurate and efficient solutions for hedging and 
unwinding decisions. This setup enabled seamless integration of real-world

The production profile at an hourly level leads to a deviation between the captured 
price of a generation asset and the arithmetic average of the day-ahead market price 
over a given period. This metric has been previously explained in Chapter 3 under the 
section 3.2 (Merchant Exposure). Since asset-specific capture rates for wind 
generation in Spain are not publicly available, a similar methodology to the one used 
for production data has been employed. Specifically, the capture rates for the entire 
wind generation in Spain have been calculated. 
However, the capture rates for total wind generation in Spain are expected to be 
higher, as they reflect the aggregated production of wind assets across the country. 
This results in a smaller deviation from baseload production (uniform production 
across all hours of the quarter) compared to a single asset or a smaller portfolio of 
assets. To account for this "portfolio effect," a discount factor of 5% has been applied 
to the historic Spanish Wind Quarterly Capture Rate, reducing it to better approximate 
the capture rate of a single asset or a smaller portfolio.

Price Risk Mitigation: 
The hedging strategy significantly reduces the negative impact of drops on realized 
day-ahead prices. Without a hedging strategy, revenue deviations decline steeply in 
scenarios where prices drop below the base case values (e.g., -30%). In contrast, the 
hedging strategy dampens this decline, resulting in less severe revenue losses. 
Changing the Slope for Downside Risk 
The flatter slope in the hedging strategy for negative price deviations demonstrates 
its effectiveness in mitigating price risk. By locking in forward prices, the model 
ensures more stable revenues even when spot prices underperform. 
Sacrifice of Upside Opportunities: 
However, this risk mitigation comes at the cost of reducing upside potential. For 
positive deviations in realized day-ahead prices (e.g., +20% or +30%), the hedging 
strategy results in lower revenue gains compared to the unhedged approach. This 
reflects the inherent trade-off in hedging, where the protection against downside risk 
limits the ability to capitalize on favorable price movements.

7. Policy Making 
The electricity market is characterized by significant volatility and financial risk, 
which can impede the effective implementation of hedging strategies. This chapter 
briefly explores the role of policy making in mitigating credit risk and facilitating the 
upscaling of hedging mechanisms, such as Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs) and 
participation in forward markets.  
7.1 Credit Risk as a Bottleneck 
Credit risk is a critical barrier to the widespread adoption of PPAs and other hedging 
instruments in the electricity market. The primary sources of credit risk include: 
вҖў Long-term Commitments: PPAs often involve long-term commitments, 
exposing both buyers and sellers to significant credit risk. The default of either 
party can lead to substantial financial losses. 
вҖў Market Volatility: The inherent volatility of the electricity market can impact 
the financial stability of companies involved in PPAs, increasing the credit risk 
associated with these agreements. 
вҖў Counterparty Risk: The risk that the counterparty will not fulfill their 
contractual obligations is a major concern, particularly in markets with less 
creditworthy participants. 
Short-term Hedging Strategies 
In the context of short-term hedging strategies, the participation in forward markets 
presents additional challenges: 
вҖў High Costs: Participation in forward markets can be prohibitively expensive for 
a large group of generators and consumers, particularly industrial entities. The 
costs associated with securing forward contracts and managing the associated 
risks can be substantial. 
вҖў Liquidity Issues: Short-term forward markets may lack sufficient liquidity, 
making it difficult for participants to enter and exit positions without 
significant price impacts. 
Policy Interventions 
To address these challenges, policymakers can implement several measures to 
mitigate credit risk and facilitate the upscaling of hedging mechanisms: 
вҖў Credit Risk Guarantees: Governments can provide credit risk guarantees to 
reduce the financial risk for companies entering into PPAs or shorter term 
hedges. For example, Spain has implemented legislation offering credit risk 
guarantees for energy-intensive companies engaging in large-scale corporate 
PPAs. 
==PAGE:49==
 
 
45 
вҖў Regulatory Frameworks: Establishing robust regulatory frameworks that 
enforce strict creditworthiness criteria for market participants can help 
mitigate credit risk. This includes regular financial assessments and 
mandatory credit ratings. 
вҖў Financial Instruments: Developing and promoting financial instruments such 
as credit default swaps and insurance products specifically designed for the 
energy sector can help manage and transfer credit risk. 
вҖў Public-Private Partnerships: Encouraging public-private partnerships can help 
share the risk between the government and private entities, making it more 
feasible for companies to engage in both long-term and short-term hedging 
strategies. 
7.2 Focusing on Spain 
Spain provides a relevant case study for examining the impact of policy interventions 
on the electricity market. The Spanish government has introduced several measures 
to support the adoption of PPAs and other hedging mechanisms: 
вҖў Legislation for Credit Risk Guarantees: Spain's legislation offers credit risk 
guarantees for energy-intensive companies, reducing the financial risk 
associated with long-term PPAs. 
вҖў Support for Renewable Energy: Spain has implemented policies to support the 
integration of renewable energy sources into the grid, which often rely on PPAs 
for financing. 
вҖў Market Reforms: Ongoing market reforms aim to increase liquidity and reduce 
the costs associated with participation in forward markets, making it more 
accessible for a broader range of market participants. 
7.3 Key Takeaways 
Effective policy making is crucial for mitigating credit risk and facilitating the 
upscaling of hedging mechanisms in the electricity market. By implementing 
measures such as credit risk guarantees, robust regulatory frameworks, and financial 
instruments, policymakers can help reduce the barriers to participation in both long-
term and short-term hedging strategies. Spain's experience provides valuable insights 
into the potential impact of these interventions, offering a model for other European 
countries to follow.

8. Conclusion and Strategic Implications 
This thesis developed a robust optimization framework for hedging strategies 
tailored to utility-scale onshore wind farms operating in Spain's volatile electricity 
market. By integrating Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR) with dynamic hedging 
strategies, scenario-based modeling, and probabilistic inputs, the research provides 
actionable tools for improving financial stability and optimizing revenues in the 
renewable energy sector. 
8.1 Optimization Framework 
The single-stage optimization model successfully balances expected revenue with 
downside risk, providing operators with a flexible and computationally efficient tool. 
The inclusion of CVaR ensures the model aligns with varying risk appetites, 
addressing the unique needs of wind farm operators exposed to market volatility. 
Dynamic Hedging Strategies 
The use of rolling hedging strategies allows operators to make incremental 
adjustments to hedge volumes based on evolving market forecasts, mitigating the 
risks of overhedging and underhedging. 
Practical Relevance 
The model incorporates real-world constraints, such as Power Purchase Agreements 
(PPAs) and merchant exposure, making it directly applicable to current market 
practices. Case study and backtesting demonstrate its adaptability to dynamic market 
conditions, including significant deviations between forward and spot prices, high 
production uncertainty, and declining price trends. 
Robustness Under Diverse Conditions 
The extended analysis highlights the model's capacity to: 
вҖў Adapt to scenarios with forward prices both exceeding and underperforming 
forecasted spot prices. 
вҖў Protect against overhedging by dynamically adjusting volumes under high 
uncertainty. 
вҖў Penalize excessive early-stage hedging to preserve flexibility and optionality. 
вҖў Execute unwind decisions to capitalize on dropping prices, reducing overall 
hedging costs.

8.2 Future Research Directions 
Building on these contributions, several promising avenues for further research are 
identified. 
1. Policy Interventions for Market Access: 
Addressing credit risk barriers that prevent smaller market participants from 
accessing hedging instruments is essential. Proposed solutions include: 
вҖў Credit Guarantee Schemes: Publicly funded or regulated guarantees to support 
small operators. 
вҖў Aggregator Platforms: Enabling collective hedging by pooling resources and 
diversifying credit risk. 
вҖў Collateral Reduction Mechanisms: Regulatory incentives to lower collateral 
requirements for hedging instruments. 
2. Granular Contract Integration: 
Expanding the model to include more granular hedging instruments, such as monthly 
or intraday futures, would enhance operational flexibility and improve alignment 
with short-term market dynamics. 
3. Advanced Forecasting and Real-Time Optimization: 
Incorporating machine learning-based forecasting techniques and real-time data 
inputs would allow the model to adapt dynamically to changing market conditions, 
further enhancing its performance. 
4. Broader Revenue Streams: 
Including participation in intraday and balancing markets, as well as ancillary 
services, would capture the full revenue potential of renewable assets, making the 
model even more comprehensive. 
5. Colocation with Battery Storage and Hydrogen Production: 
Exploring the financial and operational synergies of colocating wind farms with 
battery energy storage systems or hydrogen electrolyzers could enhance profitability 
and reduce market exposure. 
6. Policy and Market Design: 
Investigating the implications of emerging regulatory mechanisms, such as Contracts 
for Difference (CfDs) and capacity markets, on hedging strategies would provide 
valuable insights into their effectiveness in stabilizing renewable revenues.
