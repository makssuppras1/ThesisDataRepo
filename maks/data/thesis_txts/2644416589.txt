Abstract
In the past decade, antimicrobial resistance (AMR) in bacteria has become a major
threat to human healthcare system across the globe. Previous research showed that
plasmids and bacteriophages are the main transmitters of antibiotic resistance. As the
genomic origin of antibiotic resistance often determines how easy it can spread to other
bacteria in its proximity, it is crucial to identify whether the detected AMR is carried
by a relatively stable bacterial chromosome, or by a mobile element such as plasmid
or phage. Currently, we are lacking tools that can accurately discriminate plasmids or
phages from chromosomes in a context of metagenomic data.
To predict the genomic origin, we applied machine learning techniques to develop
random forest classifiers using k-mer frequencies, an alignment-agnostic approach and
training the models on random fragments extracted from whole-genome sequences.
When this method was tested on simulated 5000 bp metagenomic contigs, it showed
improved performance over models trained on whole sequences. The model’s sensitivity
for chromosome, plasmid and phage was measured at 0.9, 0.65 and 0.84, respectively,
showing merit of this approach for incomplete data classification.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Surveillance of antimicrobial resistance
In 2016, the UN has released a report stating that “Antimicrobial resistance poses a
formidable challenge to achieving Universal Health Coverage and threatens progress
against many of the Sustainable Development Goals, including health, food security,
clean water and sanitation ...” [1]. Organ transplants, post-surgical treatments, preven-
tion of newborn sepsis and safeguarding patients undergoing chemotherapy are virtually
impossible without effective antibiotics to prevent and combat life-threatening bacterial
infections. Each year, 700,000 people die from drug-resistant infections alone, includ-
ing 230,000 deaths from multiple drug-resistant (MDR) tuberculosis - a figure that is
expected to rise dramatically in the coming decades [2].
Surveillance of antimicrobial resistance (AMR) in human pathogens is traditionally
done using laboratory techniques such as culturing and, in the recent decade, sequencing,
which are routine tests in clinical diagnostics. While these techniques allowed us to
measure and monitor AMR, they are severely limited to the symptomatic population
and pathogens that can be grown in culture. These techniques, although essential, do not
provide the resolution necessary to capture the complex dynamics of AMR dissemination
through horizontal gene transfer [3].
1.1.1
Horizontal gene transfer
Horizontal gene transfer is a process when an organism transfers its genetic material to
another organism other than its offspring. In bacteria, the main mechanisms mediating
horizontal gene transfer are: transformation (uptake of free DNA), conjugation (plasmid-
mediated transfer), and transduction (phage-mediated transfer) (Figure 1.1)[4].
Plasmids are extrachromosomal elements of mostly circular DNA present in bacteria,
which replicate independently of the host genome. The horizontal transfer of plasmids
containing resistance genes is a critical mechanism for the dispersion of antimicrobial
resistance [5]. When resistance genes are carried by plasmids, they can be easily spread
to other bacteria by horizontal gene transfer.
Escherichia coli type 131 (ST131) and Klebsiella pneumoniae ST258 that emerged in
the year 2000, became known as the high-risk clones of multi-drug resistant pathogens
and are classical examples of plasmid-mediated AMR dispersion.
Similarly, bacteriophages and other mobile genetic elements, are significant contrib-
utors to AMR transmission. Bacteriophages display a complex life cycle whereupon
infecting the host cell they can enter into one of two distinct lifestyles: virulent or tem-
perate. In the former, the phage’s genome is replicated and released into the surrounding
environment, killing their host in the process. Conversely, during the temperate lifestyle,
the phage incorporates itself into the bacterial genome or re-circularizes into a stable
plasmid [6]. During the virulence stage, fragments of bacterial DNA can become a part

Occasionally, these fragments can carry whole genes with
antimicrobial resistance that are transferred to the new host. Salmonella typhimurium
phage type DT104 was responsible for outbreaks of non-typhoidal Salmonella from year

showed harboring multiple phages capable of AMR transfer [7–10].
Reliable predictions that could accurately identify and characterize the transmission
of AMR through horizontal gene transfer could provide crucial information for public
policies, treatment decisions and global surveillance.

1.1.2
Metagenomic approaches
In the past two decades, the advancements in DNA amplification technologies, increase
in computational power and developments in bioinformatic techniques, all enabled the
development of tools for metagenomic research and gave rise to a new era in resistome
monitoring. Whole genome sequencing (WGS) of single isolates became a powerful tool
for AMR diagnostics. For example, it was demonstrated that WGS profiles had a high
concordance (>96%) in predicting minimum inhibitory concentrations (MIC) [11–13],
which is a measure of how much antibiotic is needed to effectively prevent bacterial
growth. However, whole sequences provide a partial picture limited to the individual
isolate. In contrast, metagenomics, or sequencing all genetic material directly from the
environment, presents a more holistic approach to studying microbial habitats as it
provides access to all organisms present in the sample, their genome information and
AMR profiles, without the need to be grown in culture [14, 15].
The output of any sequencing experiment is a large set of DNA fragments, or reads,
the length of which is defined by the technology used, such as Illumina, Oxford Nanopore
==PAGE:9==
1.1 Surveillance of antimicrobial resistance
5
or Pacific Biotechnology, to give an example. Each of these platforms has its distinct
chemistry, read length and error rate, with >80% of the current WGS market being
dominated by Illumina technologies [16]. Illumina platforms can sequence samples to a
high depth with a relatively low error rate. However, they produce short reads, from
150 up to 300 bp [17]. Assemblies of these reads do not output whole genomes, but
rather require computational tools to assemble overlapping reads into individual contigs,
and, if the contigs’ orientations can be resolved, they could become the building blocks
of much larger sequences called scaffolds. As metagenomic samples contain reads from
different organisms and genomic origins (chromosomal, plasmid and phages), it can be
challenging to assemble large enough sequences for individual organisms.
The number of tools that work on metagenomic assemblies continues to rapidly grow.
Some of the most popular ones are able to map the chromosomal DNA to organism’s
genera, or even species, and include Kraken, MetaPhlAn2 and GTDB-Tk [18–20]. How-
ever, assembling plasmids and phages has proven to be computationally challenging.
Plasmids display plasticity and exchange their genetic material with the host’s chromo-
somes, they vary in structure, size and gene content, making them hard to be defined
in computational terms [21].
Similarly, bacteriophages conduct a complex life cycle and can be found in any of the
stages described in Section 1.1.1. Due to these structural differences, a variety of tools
is potentially needed that would take these factors into account. Some of these tools are
described below.
1.1.2.1
Plasmid identification
In the past few years, several tools have emerged, that differentiate between chromosomal
and plasmid DNA. One of the first ones were cBar that leveraged pentamer frequencies,
and Recycler that used de Bruijn graphs to resolve circular DNA [22, 23]. Another tool
to mention is PlasmidFinder, which can identify plasmid sequences with high precision,
but is limited by querying contigs against its well-defined replicon database [24]. One of
the most recent tools is metaplasmidSPAdes, which is an upgrade over the previously pub-
lished plasmidSPAdes algorithm that tried to resolve circular genome assembly graphs by
taking into account the non-uniform coverage of various bacteria within a metagenomic
sample [21].
A study which compared the performance between the original plasmidSPAdes al-
gorithm and the three other tools, showed that all had potential for classifying small
plasmids (<10 kbp), but were not robust as they were strongly swayed by the contigs’
lengths and the presence of repeating elements [25].
1.1.2.2
Phage identification
It was estimated that the abundance of viruses exceeds that of other organisms on Earth,
yet the number of available viral sequences is significantly smaller than the number of
available prokaryotic genomes. This is partially the reason why very few viral sequences
find homology hits in the existing databases [6, 26].
Some of the earlier tools for identifying phage genomes in metagenomic samples in-
clude: PHASTER, PhageFinder, PhiSpy and VirSorter as being the most popular one
[27–30]. All of these tools mainly rely on gene-based similarity approaches and, as men-
tioned previously, are limited by our existing databases, even for complete sequences.
==PAGE:10==
1.2 Machine learning in genomics
6
For much shorter sequences, such as metagenomic contigs, these methods become vir-
tually inapplicable. Alternative approaches include k-mer frequency signatures, which
serve as basis for VirFinder [31]. This tool uses a logistic regression classifier and shows
a relatively good performance on shorter sequences.
Alternative approaches for viral classification incorporate machine learning algo-
rithms that help build alignment-independent tools. One such tool is MARVEL, which
“considers a contig sequence to be predicted as part of a previously determined bin (as
opposed to treating contigs as isolated objects)” [32]. In their article, the authors took
advantage of feature engineering from baseline information found in genome sequences
and demonstrated that out of their six features; gene density, strand shifts and hits
against the Prokaryotic Virus Orthologous Group’s (pVOG) database were the most
informative for bacteriophage prediction.
1.1.2.3
3-class classifier
Until recently, all developed tools focused on trying to differentiate between chromosome
and one another genomic element. PPR-Meta is a tool that was developed recently (2019)
and is the first 3-class classifier for chromosome, plasmid and phage contigs without the
use of database referencing.
Instead, it applies a novel bi-path convolutional neural
network (CNN) architecture that is based on codon discrimination within sequences. It
is currently a very promising tool for tackling metagenomic sequences classification [33].
1.2
Machine learning in genomics
The explosion of NGS technologies has decreased the biological bottleneck from generat-
ing data for generalisation of complex biological systems. Instead, the limiting factor has
become the ability to process and extract useful biological insights from large datasets.
In general terms, machine learning is a technique that can find patterns in (often
large) datasets that would be out of reach for “conventional” manual approaches. A
machine learning framework allows to iterate over examples with their specific data
points, commonly referred to as observations and variables, and apply computational
approaches to produce an optimized representation of the data as a mathematical equa-
tion or a model.
For machine learning models it is important to find the balance between a sufficiently
large number of examples, their quality and heterogeneity. The former is important
because machine learning approaches are computationally expensive as they require a
large number of training examples to be able to find meaningful patterns in the data.
The quality is often outside our immediate reach but heterogeneity can be addressed by
removing redundant data (Section 2.2.2). This step ensures a diverse variety of training
examples that can help the model improve its ability to generalize on new data and
prevent it from “overfitting”.
In this project, the task was to define a relationship between individual observations
of genomic elements: chromosomes, plasmids, phages and their variables, represented
here as k-mer frequencies of their contig sequences. For this, we trained the random
forest algorithm on both, whole sequences and random fragments and evaluated the
models’ ability to classify the genomic origin of simulated metagenomic sequences.

1.2.1
Random forest
Random forest (RF) is a popular tree-based ensemble algorithm that have proven to
be successful in many biological applications [34–36]. The algorithm is computationally
light, highly data-adaptive and can handle large datasets, which makes it appealing for
high-dimensional genomics data. In addition, these models are less prone to overfitting.
The algorithm works by constructing multiple decision trees that are based on a ran-
dom subset of features. Each tree is based on a purity measure, quantifying how well
each decision, or split, separates the data into pure classes (Figure 1.2). All trees are
then combined into one “forest”, thereby reducing the effect of random noise from an
individual “tree” but amplifying a consistent signal found in others. Because RF models
incorporate “the wisdom of the crowd”, they are known as the ensemble models.
Figure 1.2:
Random Forest’s decision tree: the algorithm operates by constructing a
multitude of decision trees based on a random subset of available features
(a). Each tree outputs its predicted class (b) and the majority class is
returned (c).
1.2.2
Cross-validation
“Overfitting” is a common term in ML to signify that the generated model applies well
to the data it was built from but generalizes poorly to unseen data. This can be a result
of small or imbalanced training datasets, a poorly chosen algorithm, high cordiality of
variables or inappropriate separation of train and test datasets. Cross-validation is often
used to detect overfitting and involves dividing training data into several partitions, K-
folds, of approximately equal size (Figure 2.2). The model is trained on each of the K−1
folds, while using the leftover one for model testing. An average error across all folds can
then be calculated to provide an objective performance measure across different data
partitions.

1.3
The aim of the study
Metagenomic techniques have a wide range of applications: personalized medicine, foren-
sics, the discovery of new antibiotics or surveillance of resistance to them. In the case
of the latter, it is crucial to identify the source of AMR in a metagenomic context to
monitor their spread and prevent potential disease outbreaks.
Despite the availability of multiple tools that are aimed at classifying phage or plas-
mid from chromosomal DNA, more robust multi-classification approaches are needed.
Except for the PPR-Meta tool, which uses a CNN for its 3-class classification model,
all the other tools specialize in differentiating between two classes, predominantly built
around relying on existing databases, which severely limits their scope.
With high-
throughput technology and a fast-growing amount of metagenomic data, we need higher
performance tools that provide precision and high resolution of the complex microbiome
involved.
Our goal was to address this shortage by constructing a 3-class classifier model that
would handle incomplete information commonly encountered with metagenomic data.
Due to the lack of standard datasets for metagenomic origin identification, the models
will be tested on simulated 5000 bp contigs created from the whole sequences.
If successful, this approach could be used as a stand-alone tool for contig classification
or as part of an AMR identification and/or surveillance pipeline, where in addition to
identifying the resistance genes it can help classify its genomic origin.

CHAPTER 2
Materials and Methods
This project explored the development of random forest models to classify simulated
metagenomic contigs as chromosomal, plasmid or phage. To achieve this, we applied a
compositional approach converting genomic sequences into k-mer counts vectors. We
have implemented a random fragment data preparation approach that involved training
the models on incomplete data sampled from the whole sequences. They were trained
and evaluated in a 5-fold cross-validation framework and combined into ensemble mod-
els. The best of these models were applied to the holdout dataset as an independent
evaluation. The effect of k-mer size, class weight and fragment frequency on the models’
performance were investigated.
2.1
System specification
The implementation of the methods was done using Python 3.6.10 that included pack-
ages: numpy 1.18.1, pandas 1.1.3, scikit learn 0.23.2. Performance analysis was per-
formed in R 4.0.2 using packages: tidyverse 1.3.0 and visualised with ggplot2 3.3.2.
Data acquisition and processing, as well as model computations, were performed on the
Computerome High-Performance Cluster [37]. Additional diagrams were prepared with
Biorender [38].
2.2
Data preparation
2.2.1
Data sources
A large number of training examples of chromosomal, plasmid and phage sequences was
necessary to capture a wide taxonomic diversity and provide sufficient resolution needed
for classification of metagenomic data. For this purpose, plasmid and phage data were
acquired from the PATRIC database while the chromosomal data was acquired from the
KmerFinder database [39–41]. The former resource is a major pathogen genomics data
hub, that links data from major databases, such as the National Center for Biotechnology
Information (NCBI) [42]. Yet, chromosomal records may not always benefit from the
purification steps necessary for sequencing plasmids and viral genomes such as some form
of alkaline lysis or chromatography, respectively [43–46]. Thus chromosomal records
may include contamination from other genomic sources. To mitigate this problem, the
KmerFinder database - a curated source of bacteria chromosomal sequences, was used
instead.
==PAGE:14==
2.2 Data preparation
10
PATRIC Database
The Pathosystems Resource Integration Center (PATRIC) is a centralized resource for
bacterial pathogens [39]. Its database was queried for all available sequence records of
plasmids and bacteriophages amounting to 10853 and 12930 records, respectively (re-
trieved: 06.10.2020, [47]). Hereafter, the PATRIC’s plasmid and bacteriophage datasets
are referred to as PL and PH, respectively.
KmerFinder Database
KmerFinder is part of the Center for Genomic Epidemiology (CGE) tools that is aimed
at identifying species by estimating their relatedness using k-mer frequencies.
This
method relies on a database of reference genomes of annotated species which was assem-
bled from public data and refined through multiple curation steps [40]. The collection
of sequences was acquired from the KmerFinder database (version: 20042020), here-
after CH, and contained 18022 records of chromosomal sequences from a wide range of
bacterial species.
2.2.2
Data clustering
To remove redundant sequences while retaining the diversity for the input data, all iso-
late sequences, within each class, were clustered together using the KMA alignment tool
(version: 1.1.0) with the following settings: −k16, −NI, −Sparse, −ht and −hq where -k
is the k-mer size and -ht, -hq correspond to the template and query coverage thresholds,
respectively. The KMA’s clustering option applies the Hobohm-1 algorithm that itera-
tively estimates the similarity of the new - unclustered sequence, to the representative -
query sequence, of each existing cluster, based on the number of shared k-mers and the
specified coverage thresholds [48]. The same cutoff value was used for the template and
query parameters. The thresholds tested included: 87, 90, 93, 95, 96 and 99.
2.2.3
Input data
The main aim of this project was to investigate the random fragment approach for
classifying the origin of metagenomic contigs. For this, we trained two types of models:
the first ones were designed to use the information from the whole genome sequences,
while the others were trained on random fragments extracted from the whole sequences.
The fragment size was based on the average length of a metagenomic contig which is
commonly found to be in a range 2000 - 10000 bp.
In our experiment, a fragment
size of 5000 bp was chosen. Machine learning models were generated using both whole
sequences and random fragments and tested on simulated metagenomic 5000 bp contigs
according to the following strategies:
• whole sequence: k-mer frequencies from whole sequences were used to train
the models and evaluate their performance on whole sequences. This was done
to estimate how well the algorithm is expected to perform given the full range of
information provided by the sequences. To evaluate the same models’ performance
on shorter contigs usually found in metagenomic data, 5000 bp sequences were
randomly extracted from the whole sequences and used as the basis for the testing
and holdout datasets, which would be referred to as the truncated sequences.
==PAGE:15==
2.2 Data preparation

• random fragment: for this strategy, random 5000 bp fragments were extracted,
for each of the whole sequences, once, five or ten times (x1, x5, x10).
These
fragments were then used as training dataset and the models’ performance was
evaluated on simulated 5000 bp contigs which served as the basis for testing and
holdout datasets.
Although the number of examples of each class was balanced, when sequences were
sampled it was important to account for the difference in true sequence length (complete
information) and the sampling’s coverage (partial information). For that, the average
sequence length within each class was calculated. The length was then used to help
estimate the sequence coverage by these fragments. Based on coverage difference, the
class weights parameter, a ‘penalty’ factor for each class, was calculated. The models’
performances with and without class weights parameter were recorded.

2.3
Random forest
The random forest models were constructed using Python’s scikit learn library (0.23.2)
with a tree depth of 400, the number of trees fixed to 800 and data bootstrap set to
False [50]. These parameters were determined to be optimal based on the application
of a random search algorithm (scikit learn: RandomizedSearchCV(cv=3, n_iter = 100))
to the whole sequence dataset, one for each k-mer size [51]. This was done to estimate
the initial settings for the RF model that would allow capturing the signal in this type
of data. The full list of parameters sampled by the random search algorithm is shown
in (Appendix Table A.1). The remaining settings were left as default.
2.4
Cross-validation
The initial data was split into training and holdout datasets. Here, we applied a 5-fold
cross-validation framework that partitioned the training dataset into 5 folds, training
the model on 4 and testing its performance on the remaining one iterating over all
folds such, that the model is always trained and tested on independent data points
(Figure 2.2). When fragments from the same isolate were present in the data multiple

training or holdout datasets, nor during the CV’s training and testing folds, to ensure
objective performance (Appendix Fig. A.1) [52]. During the CV, 5 different models
were trained, evaluated and their average performance was recorded. These results will
be referred to as test performance.

2.5
Performance evaluation
The performance was evaluated for each model in the cross-validation pipeline specific
to the strategy used (Table 2.1). This included receiver operating characteristic area
under the curve (ROC AUC), Matthews correlation coefficient (MCC) and multi-class
confusion matrix.
The AUC and MCC metrics were used to determine the best performing CV models
within their group: k-mer size, class weight parameter and model construction strategy.
These CV models were then used on the holdout dataset and their combined performance
is presented as an aggregated ensemble metric.
To investigate which of the parameters had the strongest effect on performance; k-
mer size, class weights or fragment frequency, all models were evaluated on the holdout
dataset to obtain the AUC scores for the one-way ANOVA test comparison.
Area under the curve
The AUC represents the model’s ability to correctly identify classes and is displayed
as relationship between true positive rate (TPR/recall) and false positive rate (FPR)
on y- and x-axis, respectively (eq. 2.2 - 2.3). The metric returns a score of 0.0 for an
inverse and 1.0 for a perfect prediction, while the value of 0.5 indicates that the model’s
predictions are random. During the cross-validation, AUCs from each fold were averaged
together. In addition, AUC macro-averages were calculated (AUCmacro), which include
aggregating AUC scores for each class independently, before averaging them across all
classes (eq. 2.4).

Matthews correlation coefficient
Matthews correlation coefficient is used in machine learning as a measure of the quality
of classification.
It takes into account true and false positives and negatives and is
generally regarded as a balanced measure which can be used even if the classes are of
very different sizes. The MCC returns a value between -1 and +1, with −1 coefficient
representing an inverse, 0 - random and +1 a perfect prediction.
Confusion matrix
A confusion matrix allows to zoom in on the model’s ability to predict each class and
provides a more detail view on where the model does well and where it has a hard time
distinguishing between classes. For multi-class classification, a One-vs-All setting was
used. This treats each example as binary, correct or incorrect, and the accuracy(%) is
represented in relation to the true instances of the class (row-wise).

Statistical significance testing
To determine whether k-mer size, class weights or fragment frequency parameters had
a significant effect on the models’ performance, the one-way ANOVA test was applied
comparing the variance found within-groups to the variability between-groups using
mean AUC as a base metric for the comparison (significance level α = 0.05).
The data was controlled for normality and homogeneity of variance across groups
using Shapiro-Wilk’s and Levene’s tests, respectively (data not shown). The null and
alternative hypotheses were expressed as follows:
• H0: “There is no significant difference between the group’s AUC means.”
• HA: “There is a significant difference between AUC means of at least two groups.”
In case of rejecting the H0, a Tukey’s honestly significant difference (HSD) test would
be used as a post hoc analysis metric.

Strategy: random fragments
In an attempt to correctly identify metagenomic contigs, we have implemented a frag-
ment strategy by which randomly selected 5000 bp long fragments from the whole se-

information.

parameter was introduced to counter it. As chromosome sequences were found to be
x100 longer that their counterparts (Figure 3.1b), the parameter was set to reflect that
difference: class weights = {100:1:1}. The models’ predictive power was evaluated in
response to fragment frequency (information quantity), k-mer size and the use of class
weight parameter.

CHAPTER 4
Discussion
Technological advancements have made it possible to generate large quantities of se-
quencing data that can describe the microorganisms found in environmental samples.
Metagenomics, the field that studies the different genomes within these samples, is
becoming increasingly popular, with its real-world applications such as antibiotics dis-
covery, personalised medicine, forensics, and many more. In contrast to whole-genome
sequencing, where assemblies can combine reads from a single organism, this tasks be-
comes increasingly challenging when dealing with reads from multiple species found in
the same sample. Assembly of metagenomic samples, which attempts piecing together
short sequence reads dissociated from their original host, usually results in contigs which
lengths rarely exceed 5000 bp, making them extremely challenging for species identifi-
cation. Where long genomic sequences contain information about open reading frames,
transcription units, associated promoters and transcription factor binding sites that can
aid in sequence’s origin identification, in metagenomic contigs this information is re-
duced to single domains, short operons or a handful multidomain genes. This creates
the need for new computational approaches that can aid metagenomic data classification
and facilitate its further research and application.
Here we explored the relevance of random forest models for classification of simulated
metagenomic contigs into three classes according to their genomic origin: chromosome,
plasmid or phage, based on k-mer frequency distributions. To classify the incomplete
data from these contigs, the models were trained on similarly partial information using
the random fragment approach, in which randomly selected short genomic fragments,
5000 bp in length, were sampled from the whole sequences and used as input data for
model training. Whole sequence models constructed from complete information were
trained alongside as baseline comparisons for the machine learning approach.
The input data consisted of 25790 examples and was balanced between the three
classes. It was split between training (n = 23211) and holdout (n = 2579) datasets.
For the random fragment strategy, the number of training/testing examples were scaled
according to the number of random fragments extracted.
All models were trained using 5-fold cross-validation and further combined into en-
semble models. The best performing ensemble models from their group were evaluated
on the holdout dataset.
4.1
Model performance
The random forest is a powerful algorithm for genomic data modelling. The RF models
trained on k-mer counts from whole genomic sequences showed high results with the best
ensemble model of the group reaching an AUC5−mer = 0.992 ± 0.001. The Mathew’s
correlation coefficient value was similarly high (MCC5−mer = 0.928) strengthening the
argument for the models’ ability to ‘learn’ from this type of data. This model correctly
==PAGE:29==
4.1 Model performance
25
classified whole genomic sequences as chromosome, plasmid or phage with a recall of
0.98, 0.94 and 0.93, respectively.
To simulate metagenomic contigs, 5000 bp sequences were extracted at random from
whole sequences. When these whole sequence models were applied to these contigs, they
were unable to generalise well. The best performing whole sequence model, based on k-
mer size 5, scored an AUC and MCC values of AUC5−mer = 0.876 ± 0.001, MCC5−mer =
0.419 on the holdout dataset. This model was unable to correctly classify chromosomes,
mislabeling them as plasmids and phages in 58 and 41 per cent of the time, respectively.
This misclassification would appear to be unrelated to normalization of the shorter input
vectors as the sensitivity values for plasmid and phage classes were considerably higher
(>84%). Instead, the features that the models’ predictions were based on were possibly
too scarce or completely absent in the shorter contigs for correct classification.
To overcome this obstacle in classifying incomplete data, the approach was to train
the models on similarly partial information. To achieve this, a random fragment strategy
was implemented, that involved extracting single or multiple fragments, of 5000 bp in
length, from the whole sequences in our collection. These fragments were then used as
input data for model training. The models were then tested on simulated metagenomic
5000 bp contigs and demonstrated improved performance over those produced by the
whole sequences strategy.
The best model from the random fragment approach was
constructed using k-mer size 5 with fragment frequency x10 and without the use of the
class weight parameter. This model scored an AUC5−mer of 0.939 and MCC5−mer of
0.713. The model’s recall was measured at 0.9 for chromosome, 0.65 for plasmid and
0.84 for bacteriophage contigs.
When examining the effect of different parameters on the models’ performance, a
general trend was observed.
The class weights parameter, which was introduced to
potentially help with ‘information loss’ in chromosomal fragments, appeared to be detri-
mental to the models, resulting in a significant decrease in AUC scores (p = 0.000241).
This could be a result of inappropriately selected weights, e.g. plasmids showed a much
wider range of sequence sizes and the chosen values were interfering with overall class
predictions. To elaborate, as more sequences’ lengths deviate from the mean values used
for class weights assignment, the ratio becomes unbalanced and no longer represents the
true difference in coverage. A possible test could be to use CH and PH alone, whose
distribution in sequence lengths is more uniform, and testing the effect of these values
on the performance. Alternatively, the models were able to capture the signal given
by the fragments alone and the inclusion of class importance only interferes with the
predictions.
When all models were compared, no significant difference on the AUC metric by
the fragment frequency was observed. As the introduction of class weights only deteri-
orated performance, weighted models were excluded from the comparison and the test
was repeated. This data showed a significant improvement in AUC score by using frag-
ment frequency x10 over x1, suggesting that further gains are possible by increasing the
number of examples in the dataset (p.adj = 0.045). On the other hand, the increase in
computational time and memory needed does not necessarily justify a relatively minor
increase in performance (Appendix Fig. A.4).
When examining the k-mer size, an inverse relationship between size and performance
was observed with no significant difference in the overall performance, neither in weighted
nor unweighted models. An increase in performance by smaller k-mers indicates that the
models are learning frequency distributions rather than unique features. Alternatively, it
might suggest that by increasing the k-mer size, the vectors become more sparse resulting

in less data for the models to generalise on. As short k-mers offer little information
about their genomic function it poses a challenge to translate the models’ predictions
into biological meaning. Nonetheless, a feature importance analysis was carried out by
applying Gini importance and permutation importance techniques to the top scoring
whole sequence ensemble model (Appendix A.6).
All fragment models showed a higher sensitivity for identifying chromosome and
phage fragments. Most of their errors revolved around plasmid identification with many
cases being misclassified as chromosomes, suggesting a similar composition, making it
challenging to identify by such methods. And indeed, the principal component analysis
showed a high degree of overlap between the three classes, even more so between CH
and PL (Appendix A.4). Additionally, plasmid identification is known to be impeded
by their intrinsic genomic plasticity: the constant exchange and shuffling of genes and
could be investigated further [53–56].
Overall, these RF models constructed from partial information showed a promising
result. These models were able to classify simulated contigs by training an RF model
on incomplete data, which could prove to be a valuable approach in metagenomics or
for cases when incomplete data is a hurdle for machine learning applications. The low
fluctuations within CV and between training and holdout results suggest that the models’
performance is uniform with no apparent indication of overfitting.
4.2
Benchmarking against PPR-meta
As described previously, the recently published PPR-meta tool was designed to solve
the same, 3-class classification problem. We were unable to benchmark our tool against
PPR-meta due to it being inaccessible for comparison at the time of the experiment
(accessed: 15.01.2021).
The relevant links specified on the GitHub’s documentation
page were unavailable. Despite a successful installation of this tool on the local cluster,
we were unable to run the executable.
In the article, when PPR-meta was tested on a group of 5 - 10 kbp sequences, the
tool reported a recall of 0.86, 0.875 and 0.992 for chromosome, plasmid and phage,
respectively [33, Fig.2, Group D]. In contrast, our best model achieved a recall of 0.9 for
chromosome, 0.65 for plasmid and 0.84 for bacteriophage when tested against simulated
contigs of 5 kbp only.
4.3
Application
The models were designed to be incorporated into existing bioinformatics pipelines where
identifying the genomic source is of importance. For example in resistome surveillance
where the identification of AMR is carried out by the existing tools, and the genomic
source of significant contigs would be annotated by the models presented here. They
could act as a recommendation system that marks and prioritises risk cases that require
preventative measures.
The models will be hosted on the DTU’s Center for Genomic Epidemiology servers
as a stand-alone tool which accepts a single isolate in a FASTA format and returns a table
with assigned class: chromosome, plasmid or phage (http://www.genomicepidemiology.org/).
The project’s code repository is available at https://bitbucket.org/vlagri/oriclass.

4.4
Limitations
Data quality
Projects like these would not be possible without publicly available data, yet, quality is
always an important consideration. As described before, the sequencing of chromosomal
DNA does not usually require the same purification steps as used for plasmid and virus
isolation. As a result, contigs from other genomic elements can be found present in
otherwise ‘chromosome’ labelled records. For this project we used chromosomal records
from a pre-curated database, yet, the lack of erroneous entries is hard to guarantee and
should be kept in mind for any machine learning applications regardless of the type of
data.
Compositional methods
The method of representing biological data is significant for every machine learning-
based tool. Although compositional methods, specifically k-mer counts, have shown to
be a powerful representation, there are concerns that short sequences can exhibit serious
fluctuations and may present misleading results [57–59].
Sequence orientation
In this study, the train and test datasets were based on fragments extracted from com-
plete sequences and did not account for the unknown orientation of the contigs com-
monly produced by the metagenomic assemblies. This can be addressed by including
the reverse compliment data into model construction.
4.5
Challenges
Big data
One of the main challenges during this project was the 1st ‘V’ of Big Data - volume,
which required adjustments to the underlying programming [60]. For example, python’s
pandas and R’s tidyverse, both powerful data analysis toolkits, were rendered unusable
when scaling the data preprocessing pipeline from whole to fragment strategy. As the
amount of data will continue to grow and the machine learning techniques become more
computationally intensive, it becomes necessary to develop better tools for manipulating
such data, as well as putting more weight on training the new generation of bioinfor-
maticians to handle these challenges.
Workflow management system
The transformation steps for input data can easily turn into an intricate process as
multiple scripts are required to run in parallel as well as sequentially. Using workflow
management systems can significantly improve reproducibility by implementing auto-
mated completion checks and starting new processes as required. During the project,

the transformation steps specified in the data preprocessing diagram (Figure 2.1). An
example of this management system’s execution tree is shown in Appendix Fig.A.6. Un-
fortunately, we were unable to scale this process to the entire dataset. The process was
too slow, possibly due to lengthy sequential checking of job completions, large data files
and potential lack of experience on the user’s part.
4.6
Future work
Experimental data
These models were trained and tested on artificially fragmented sequences of size 5000 bp
that were meant to simulate metagenomic sequences. The next step would be to validate
these models on experimental data, but, as of yet, there are no benchmark datasets
available for metagenomic source classification. These models could additionally get
tested on instances when available sequences are shorter than the used fragment size,
and if so, what is the lower boundary before the model is no longer able to generalise
reliably. Along the same lines, it is worth investigating whether the random fragment
approach can achieve comparable or better results using smaller k-mer sizes.
Hyperparameter tuning
The initial settings for the fragment models were base on a random search algorithm that
sampled 100 combinations from a list of >6000 possibilities (Appendix Table A.1), which
was applied to the whole sequences training set. For the purpose of comparison, the same
settings were used to construct the fragment models but were not explicitly tested on
their suitability for the fragment dataset. Going forward, a more direct optimization
approach could be applied to achieve better performance.
More complex models
The two limiting factors for machine learning model performance are high variance or
high bias. The former is a common product of insufficient data or a choice of overly
complicated model, resulting in overfitting.
It becomes apparent when the model’s
performance during training is much higher than during testing. This is usually rectified
by adding more data for model training. On the other hand, when the model is too
simple to describe the data, this is known as high bias, in which case the model would
not benefit from more training examples. Instead, choosing a more complex model might
help capture an additional underlying signal.
In our study, the models showed a levelled performance across different folds and
validation stages.
Furthermore, an increase in sampling frequency (from x5 to x10)
showed no considerable effect on the overall performance, dismissing the possibility of
suffering from high variance. On the other hand, to evaluate the aspect of high bias,
more complex models, e.g. a feedforward neural network, could be applied.
==PAGE:33==
CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
Random forest models, together with short k-mer count distributions, have proven to
be suitable for predicting genomic origin from whole sequences to a very high level of
recall but were unable to generalize to much shorter simulated contigs.
We have designed and tested a 3-class classification method that makes predictions on
partial data by applying models constructed from similarly incomplete information. Our
random fragments approach showed competence when tested against artificially created
contigs of 5000 bp.
Our best model demonstrated high sensitivity for chromosomal
(0.9) and bacteriophage fragments (0.84) classes. Plasmid sensitivity was lower (0.65)
with the majority of incorrect classifications labelled as the chromosomal class (0.27).
Possibly, this is due to plasmids’ high genetic plasticity and their ability to exchange
genetic material with chromosomes.
Despite this, the random fragment approach has shown to be a useful technique
for metagenomic, and, possibly, other types of incomplete data.
We propose these
models as an extension to the existing antimicrobial surveillance pipelines as a form of
a recommendation system to help sort and prioritize efforts in antimicrobial resistance
management.

A.6 Model interpretation
35
A.6
Model interpretation
One of the approaches for interpretation of tree based models, is the Gini importance
method which aggregates and ranks each feature from all the trees based on its impurity
decrease at each node’s split. This approach is known to have major drawbacks:
• they incorporate a strong bias towards high cardinality features
• in the case of correlated features it can select one of the feature and neglect the
importance of the second one
• because they are computed on the training set, their statistics do not reflect the
ability of feature to be useful to make predictions that can be generalized to the
test set
A second approach is using a permutation importance algorithm, that uses the test
and compares the baseline performance (criterion: accuracy) of a test set to performance
of a model that had one of its input features permuted. Any change between the two
defines the importance of that specific feature on the overall performance of the model.
As our models were used in an ensemble, these methods were applied to the indi-
vidual models of the best whole sequence ensemble and their mean importance metrics
were calculated. The top 10 features, based on their absolute values, are shown in Fig-
ure A.5. A high concordance was observed with 9/10 features overlapping between the
two methods.
Another method based on Shapley values, which takes into account the average
marginal contribution of each permuted feature of the model, was considered but not
implemented due to the time constraint [62].
Figure A.5: Bar plot of top 10 variables with the most impact on the model’s decision
(n = 2080). Gini importance (a) and permutation importance (b) meth-
ods applied to the whole sequence ensemble model (k-mer size = 6). Each
feature’s mean was aggregated and the top 10, based on the absolute val-
ues, are presented. The two methods showed a 9/10 overlap in top-scoring
features. The remaining features are highlighted in red.
