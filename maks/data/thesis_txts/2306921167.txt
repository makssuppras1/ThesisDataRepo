SUMMARY 
World population is expected to double by the end of the 21st century to 16 billion which means 
that global demand for energy is likely to increase. As a consequence, there is a growing concern 
regarding energy-related environmental issues such as acid precipitation, stratospheric ozone 
depletion and global climate change. This, together with the variability of oil prices shows the 
need for new and more sustainable processes for producing energy and chemicals.  
Biorefineries have unique characterisitcs compared to conventional chemical processes. Lots of 
different raw materials can be used to produce a specific product. Also, several products can be 
obtained from one raw material. Thus, biorefineries are multi-raw material multi-product 
problems. Also, their solution is location dependent due to the variability of the availability and 
prices of raw materials. 
This project shows different applications of a methodology for synthesis-design of biorefinery 
processing networks, which has been developed following a three-stage approach. The three-stage 
approach consists of decomposing the design procedure in three stages: (1) synthesis, (2) design 
and analysis and (3) innovation. This way, the problem complexity is managed by screening 
through a larger number of alternatives using simpler models first (in stage 1) and performing 
more detailed analysis the top ranked flowsheets afterwards (in stage 2). The third stage (stage 3) 
involves the analysis of intensified flowsheets to find innovative solutions; this stage is outside 
the scope of this thesis. 
Process synthesis and design-analysis has been performed in two stages for two different case 
studies in the field of biorefineries: production of ethanol and production of a mixture of gasoline 
and diesel from lignocellulosic biomass in various locations. The superstructure considered for 
the production of ethanol case study consists of 9 steps, 36 intervals, 34 compounds and 7 
locations. The one considered for the production of a mixture of gasoline and diesel consists of 10 
steps, 19 intervals, 18 compounds and the same 7 locations. 
The results show location-dependency of the solution. For the case of ethanol, Thailand has been 
found to be the optimal location where to produce ethanol, mainly due to the low cost of Cassava 
Rhizome. For the case of gasoline and diesel, Thailand has also been found to be the optimal 
location, which means that the major economic driving force is given by the price of raw 
materials.

1. INTRODUCTION                                     
 
1.1 MOTIVATION 
Climate scientists have observed that carbon dioxide (CO2) concentrations in the atmosphere have 
increased significantly over the past century, compared to the pre-industrial era. The 
concentration of CO2 in 2014 was about 40% higher than in the mid- 1800s, with an average 
growth of 2 ppm/year in the last ten years. In fact, energy-related CO2 emissions have reached 
historic highs as can be seen in Figure 1 [1]. Among the many human activities that produce 
greenhouse gases, the use of energy represents by far the largest source of emissions [2]. The 
actions human beings are having on the environment are clear and well reported. The Fifth 
Assessment report of IPCC 2013, on its representative concentration pathway (RCP) 8.5 climate 
scenario, projects a rise of the global mean temperature of 3.7ºC by 2100, as well as an increase 
of the sea level of about 0.9 meters (global average).  
 
FIGURE 1 – GLOBAL ANTHROPOGENIC ENERGY-RELATED GREENHOUSE GAS EMISSIONS (IEA 2015) 
Increasing demand for energy comes from worldwide economic growth and development, caused 
by the continuous increase in world population, which is expected to double by the end of the 21st 
century to 16 billion people [1] . This will affect energy demand and greenhouse gas (GHG) 
emissions, which are both likely to double by 2050 [1]. This increase in energy demand will raise 
oil demand as well, which will heighten concerns over security of supply [3]. 
==PAGE:22==
1. INTRODUCTION 
 
2 
 
There are several shared socioeconomic pathways (SSPs), which are intended to analyze the 
feedbacks between climate change and socioeconomic factors, such as world population growth, 
economic development and technological progress (See Figure 2). In SSP5, which is a scenario 
considering a high technological development in the next years but a low environmental 
consciousness from society; the energy supply will still mainly rely on fossil fuels. Currently, 
approximately 80% of total primary energy supply (TPES) comes from fossil fuels [2]. 
Furthermore, there is an increasing tendency in TPES, since it increased by almost 150% between 
1971 and 2013. Such huge reliance on fossil fuels, together with the narrow set of technologies 
used is a threat to energy security, stable economic growth and global welfare, as well as to the 
environment [1]. We have to avoid reaching a deadlock when it comes to sources for energy 
supply, and continue investigating other sources than the current ones derived from fossil fuels.  
To sum up, it is clear that current trends in energy supply and use are unsustainable – 
economically, environmentally and socially. With each year that passes, a clearer consciousness 
of the high costs associated with energy systems driven by the combustion of fossil fuels is 
emerging. That is why there is a growing awareness of the urgent need to move towards more 
sustainable processes for producing energy. 
Biofuels can help reducing the current dependency on oil and providing a way of shifting to low-
carbon, non-petroleum fuels. While improving vehicle efficiency is an important and low-cost 
short term manner of reducing CO2 emissions in the transport sector, biofuels will need to play a 
significant role in replacing liquid fossil fuels suitable for planes, marine vessels and other heavy 
transport modes that cannot be easily electrified. Production and use of biofuels can also provide 
benefits such as increased energy security in a long term basis by reducing dependency on oil 
imports, and reducing oil price volatility. In addition, biofuels can support economic development 
by creating new sources of income in rural areas [3]. 
Currently, biofuels provide around 2% of total transport fuel worldwide, but there are new 
emerging technologies which offer a considerable potential for growth over the coming decades. 
The roadmap developed by IEA forecasts that by year 2050, through the use of new technologies 
and feedstocks, the global use of 32 exajoules of biofuels can be achieved. This would mean that 
biofuels would provide 27% of the total world transport fuel, which represents an increase of 25% 
with respect to the current situation [3].

1.2 BIOREFINERIES: AN OVERVIEW 
Biorefineries have been defined in many different ways depending on the author. They were 
defined by [4] as a conversion pathway to produce products from feedstocks, via platforms and 
processes, being the platforms intermediates from which final products are derived. Biorefineries 
have also been reported to be “integrated biomass-conversion processes used to generate biobased 
products such as biofuels (e.g., biobutanol and bioethanol), bioenergy (heat and power), and 
biobased chemicals and materials” [5]. They have also been described as facilities that ”address 
the concept of converting biomass materials into biofuels or bio-based chemicals via the fusion of 
biotechnology and physico- chemical technology” [6]. In this project, a genetic concept of 
biorefineries has been considered. They are considered to be processing pathways which convert 
different types of biomass into chemical products, fuels or energy. Thermochemical, biochemical 
and conventional chemical processes can be used in biorefineries. Their main difference with 
respect to otber chemcal processes is that they use bio-based raw material instead of chemicals.  
There are different classification approaches for biorefineries in the literature, but the one 
considered more applicable to all biorefineries is the one proposed by Aslanzadeh et al [7]. The 
basic idea of this classification approach is that each individual biorefinery system can be 
classified using the following four main features: Platforms, Products, Feedstock and Processes. 
From here, a network where the different individual biorefinery systems are combined can be 
built. Platforms are intermediates which link feedstocks and final products. The platform concept 
is similar to the one used in the petrochemical industry, where the crude oil is fractionated into a 
large number of intermediates that are further processed to final energy and chemical products. 
The most important platforms are the following: biogas, syngas, H2, C6 sugars, C5 sugars, lignin 
and oil [7].  
Biorefineries can produce both energetic and non-energetic products, and thus, they can be 
grouped in two categories: energy-driven and material-driven biorefinery systems. In the first 
one, biomass is primarily used for transportation fuels, energy or heat, and in the latter, 
biorefineries primarily generate bio-based chemicals [7].  
Biorefineries can be classified upon the carbon source used to produce biofuels. Depending on the 
type of feedstock used, biofuels are classified into first, second, third and fourth generation 
biofuels. First generation biofuels are produced directly from food crops. The most contentious 
issue with first generation biofuels is that since the majority of biofuels are produced directly 
from food crops, the rise in demand for biofuels has led to an increase in the volumes of crops 
being diverted away from the global food market. This has been blamed for the global increase in 
==PAGE:24==
1. INTRODUCTION

food prices over the last couple of years. Also, there has been much debate over their actually 
benefit in reducing GHG and CO2 emissions due to the fact that some biofuels can produce 
negative net energy gains, releasing more carbon in their production than their feedstock’s 
capture in their growth [8]. Second generation biofuels have been developed to overcome the 
limitations of first generation biofuels. They are produced from non-food crops such as wood, 
organic waste, food crop waste and specific biomass crops. Life cycle assessments of second-
generation biofuels have indicated that they will increase ‘net energy gains’ overcoming the main 
limitation of first generation biofuels. Also, they have been reported to be more cost competitive. 
Third generation biofuels refers to biofuel derived from algae. One of the major benefits of algae 
is that they can use a diverse array of carbon sources. Most notably, it has been suggested that 
algae might be tied directly to carbon emitting sources (power plants, industry, etc.) where they 
could directly convert emissions into usable fuel. This means that no carbon dioxide would be 
released from these settings and thus total emissions would be reduced substantially. As with 
everything, algae have a down side. Even when grown in waste water, algae require large 
amounts of water, nitrogen and phosphorus to grow. So much in fact that the production of 
fertilizer to meet the needs of algae used to produce biofuel would produce more greenhouse gas 
emissions than were saved by using algae based biofuel to begin with. Fourth generation biofuels 
are aimed at not only producing sustainable energy but also a way of capturing and storing CO2. 
Biomass materials, which have absorbed CO2 while growing, are converted into fuel using the 
same processes as second generation biofuels. This process differs from second and third 
generation production as at all stages of production the carbon dioxide is captured using processes 
such as oxy-fuel combustion. The carbon dioxide can then be geo-sequestered by storing it in old 
oil and gas fields or saline aquifers. This carbon capture makes fourth generation biofuel 
production carbon negative since it is ‘locks’ away more carbon than it produces. 
Finally, in biorefinery systems, four types of processes can be applied to convert biomass into 
desired products: (i) mechanical/physical, which do not change the chemical structure of the 
biomass; (ii) biochemical, which occur at mild conditions using microorganisms or enzymes; (iii) 
chemical and (iv) thermochemical, where feedstock undergoes extreme conditions [7].  
In order to implement the biorefinery concept, systematic synthesis and design methods are 
needed due to the large amount of alternatives that it is needed to deal with. Biorefineries have 
unique characteristics compared to conventional chemical processes. The economic 
competitiveness of biorefineries is based on the production of large volumes of ‘low value’ 
biofuels and a lower volume of ‘high-value’ chemicals as co-products. This means that it will be 
really interesting to study multi-raw material multi-product biorefineries. Also, the economic

feasibility of biorefineries is highly dependent on the geographical location where the biorefinery 
is built. This is due to the fact that many biomass feedstocks are location-specific, with many 
types confined to specific geographic locations [9]. For example in Thailand there is a high 
amount of Cassava Rhizome available while in the US there is a very limited amount of this

causes a change in the raw material chemical composition from location to location, which will 
have influence on the processing pathway to produce a desired product. Furthermore, even the 
same feedstock may have different characteristics based on the region and climate. Also, due to 
the difference in abundance of feedstocks in varying locations, their cost can also be significantly 
different, influencing the overall economics of the process. That is why, the optimal biorefinery 
network problem, unlike conventional chemical processes, needs to have location-dependent 
solutions [10]. This means that the existing synthesis methods will have to be adapted in order to 
be able to use them in the field of biorefineries. Thus, new suitable methods and tools for 
synthesizing, evaluating and selecting process alternatives as well as for designing highly 
promising processes are needed for biorefineries [10] .  
 
1.3 PROCESS SYNTHESIS AND DESIGN OF BIOREFINERIES – 
CURRENT STATE OF THE ART 
As mentioned in the previous section, there is a clear need of adapting the existing synthesis 
methods to the field of biorefineries due to their unique characteristics. Systematic process 
synthesis methods need to be developed in order to deal with the large amounts of data needed 
and the location-dependency characteristics of biorefineries compared to conventional chemical 
processes. Regarding design methods, there has been more research carried out. Thermodynamic 
models for the many biological species involved in biorefinery processes that were not needed in 
conventional chemical processes are being found in order to be able to simulate and design in 
detail the processes. A review of the available methods to solve synthesis problems is presented 
in the next section. A literature review of the design methods available is not presented in this 
project, since well-established and no innovative design methods are going to be used.  
 
1.3.1 METHODS TO SOLVE THE SYNTHESIS PROBLEM 
The objective of process synthesis is to find the best processing route from among many 
alternatives, to convert feedstocks into desired products, subject to predefined performance 
==PAGE:26==
1. INTRODUCTION 
 
6 
 
criteria [11]. There are numerous scenarios of the process synthesis problem. For example, given 
a set of raw materials, determine the best raw material blend and the best route to produce a 
desired product, or, given a set of products, determine the best processing pathway to produce 
each of them as well as the best proportion of products in order to obtain the highest profit.   
The problem of synthesis and design of processing networks involves, thus, the generation, 
evaluation and screening of numerous process alternatives with respect to raw materials, process 
technologies and product portfolio. This problem is complex and multi-disciplinary since 
different aspects such as technical, economical, regulatory or logistical need to be taken into 
consideration simultaneously in order to be able to identify the optimal design [12] . 
Numerous methods have been proposed to solve the process synthesis problem. They belong to 
three main classes: heuristic or knowledge-based, mathematical programming and hybrid 
methods.  Heuristic methods are based on process understanding and know-how. In mathematical 
programming-based methods, the best flowsheet alternative is determined from network 
superstructure optimization (this method is especially useful when the system to be studied is well 
defined and many combinations of alternatives need to be considered). Finally, hybrid methods 
use a combination of process insights and know-how from heuristic methods and mathematical 
programming [13].  
Heuristic and knowledge based methods use previously acquired know-how of existing processes 
to create rules that help to obtain favorable results. However, results obtained from applying these 
rules might not be the optimal. Furthermore, the results should be verified in order to confirm that 
the rule applied is applicable to the specific process, since they do have a limited application 
range. An advantage of these methods is that they are useful when improving already existing 
processes. However, their main limitation is that not all heuristic rules are of general application 
and therefore, careful consideration and verification of the results must be done when applying 
these rules. Furthermore, it is impossible to ensure that the optimal solution can be achieved when 
heuristic methods are employed [14]. A heuristic method for synthesis of initial sequences for 
sloppy multicomponent separations was proposed by  Chen et al [15]. A hierarchical approach for 
conceptual design of process flowsheets was also proposed by Douglas et al  [16], which consists 
of solving design problems by first developing very simple solutions and then adding successive 
layers of detail. As shown in Table 1 a hierarchy of decisions to be made is developed in order to 
obtain a systematic approach for process design. One great advantage of this approach is that it 
allows the calculation of the equipment sizes and estimation of the costs as we proceed along the 
hierarchy of decisions.

Mathematical programming methods imply the generation of a superstructure containing a 
number of processing intervals (PI) with their corresponding interconnections [11]. The synthesis 
and design problem is usually decomposed in 3 steps. The first consists of representing the 
solution space of the studied problem by means of a superstructure. In the second step, the 
synthesis problem is cast as a mathematical optimization problem. This implies the definition of 
an objective function and a superstructure model which describes each process alternative 
considered as well as the material flow through the network. The objective function to be 
minimized or maximized is subject to both linear and non-linear constraints. As a result, the 
overall synthesis problem is a Mixed Integer Non Linear Program (MINLP). Binary variables are 
used to represent Boolean decisions to represent the different possible choices within the 
superstructure. A mathematical formulation based on big-M notation is used to model the logic 
intrinsic to design choices [17]. In order to solve the problem, it has to be reformulated into an 
MILP in order to take out the non-linearity of the system. This can be achieved for example by 
fixing operating conditions. Also, linear equations can be derived from mass and energy balances 
[12]. In the third step the problem is solved, in order to determine the optimal design according to 
the specifications given by the objective function [12]. 
Numerous studies have described the basis for solving process synthesis methods via 
mathematical programming using a systematic framework [18] [19] [20] [21]. Also, solution 
methods and algorithms have been reported by Floudas et al and Grossman et al [22] [23]. This 
systematic approach has been applied to different process synthesis problems. A synthesis 
problem for biogas production from animal waste was studied by Biegler et al [24]. An 
aggregated mathematical model with an economic objective function, formulated as a MINLP 
problem, was used.  
Mathematical programming methods perform simultaneous optimization of the flowsheet 
structure and the operating conditions, which has shown to provide better results than the 
sequential approach used by heuristic methods [25]. However, in order to obtain the best solution, 
==PAGE:28==

the superstructure generated must be adequate, meaning that the optimal pathway must be 
embedded into the search space constituting the superstructure. Also, the solution of the 
optimization problem is difficult due to the fact that the optimization problem consists of a 
number of non-linear equations. Therefore, the models used need to be simplified [26]. 
Hybrid methods use the mathematical programming techniques based on optimization together 
with the knowledge-based approach. They keep the simple structure of heuristic methods, but 
replace the non-generally applicable rules with guidelines based on physical insights [27]. With 
the information taken from the physical insights, the search space of flowsheet alternatives can be 
narrowed by removing infeasible solutions. Thus, the final mathematical problem to be solved is 
smaller. 
 
1.3.2 SYNTHESIS AND DESIGN OF BIOREFINERIES FOR BIOFUELS PRODUCTION 
The main focus of this thesis will be the production of different biofuels from lignocellulosic 
biomass, mainly composed of cellulose, hemicellulose, water, lignin and ash. There have been 
numerous studies using mathematical programming methods applied to the synthesis and design 
of biorefineries to produce biofuels. Zondervan et al [28] proposed a biorefinery optimization 
model based on PI to build a multi-product superstructure, which contains alternatives for 
producing ethanol, butanol, succinic acid and blends of these chemicals with fossil fuel based 
gasoline. This idea was further developed by Quaglia et al [29] by developing an integrated 
business and engineering framework for synthesis and design of processing networks. The 
framework includes a software infrastructure for integrating different methods and tools needed 
for problem definition, formulation and solution of the problem, reducing thereby the time needed 
to generate and solve the synthesis problem. A hybrid optimization model including 
superstructure-based optimization was presented by Shabbit et al [30]. The aim was to study the 
economic viability of biorefineries producing both chemicals and biofuels, taking into account 
their environmental performance as well. A reaction flux network analysis (RFNA) was 
introduced by Voll et al [31] as a rapid screening method for synthesis and design of biorefinery 
processing pathways, considering not only techno-economic but also environmental impacts. 
Possible reaction paths to convert biomass to biofuels were generated by means of this approach, 
and thus, the optimal pathways were identified through the formulated MINLP problem. 
Martin and Grossmann [32] studied results of a biorefinery design using mathematical 
programming to systematically evaluate a large number of alternatives to identify the optimal 
solution in terms of economics and sustainability (including energy and water consumption, and

process integration). An MINLP model for superstructure-based optimization of biodiesel 
production from microalgae biomass was proposed. Their suggested methodology was tested by 
choosing on a specific case different objective functions. The optimal flowsheet design of a 
biorefinery producing liquid transportation fuels was studied by Babilan et al [33]. A systematic 
approach to improve existing biorefinery designs was proposed by Abdelaziz et al [34] . This

Abdelaziz et al [34] proposed a hierarchical approach to improve the efficiency of the existing

factors when designing a sustainable biorefinery. Garcia et al [36] proposed a large superstructure 
for producing biofuels from biomass. They developed an optimization model that determines the 
most cost-effective and environmentally sustainable production pathway, taking into 
consideration supply chain aspects. Also, Gonzalez-Delgado et al [37] have introduced a 
combined methodology applied to the synthesis problem of a micro-algae based biorefinery. The 
methodology uses a hierarchical approach that starts with the most important data and based on 
that, focuses on the most promising pathways. 
The aforementioned studies have not only provided interesting methodological approaches, but 
have also generated many promising biorefinery configurations that might be considered for 
commercial scale exploitation in the future. However, none of them have included the 
geographical location factor, which is of crucial importance when studying biorefineries due to 
the variability of raw material availability in different locations, which means different raw 
material composition from location to location. Furthermore, the different prices of biomass in 
every location depend both on their demand and on their availability, and will certainly have an 
effect on the overall profit of the process.

A three stage approach for sustainable process synthesis, design and innovation was proposed by 
Babi et al [13]. This approach consists of decomposing the process design problem into three 
stages (See Figure 2) in order to identify more sustainable and innovative process alternatives. In 
the first stage, which is the synthesis stage and the main focus of this thesis, the problem is 
defined in terms of objective function and process constraints and the search space is determined 
and represented. The top-ranked alternatives are selected to go to the next stage, which is the 
design stge. In the design stage, the top processing routes from the first stage are designed in 
detail and analyzed in terms of economic factors, sustainability metrics and LCA factors. Finally, 
in the innovation stage, the results from the analysis performed in the second stage are used to 
find process bottlenecks and generate more sustainable innovative designs. 
In Figure 2, the methodology that has been used along this project with the integrated methods 
and tools together with the inputs and outputs from each step is presented. The different stages are 
presented by means of different colors, and the steps within each stage are also shown. The first 
stage, which is the synthesis stage consists of 3 steps. The first step, which is problem definition 
consists of translating an existing need into a problem with its respective objective function. The 
second step is the superstructure generation and data collection, where all the data needed to solve 
the problem is gathered. It is the most time consuming step within this first stage. The last step is 
the solution of the optimization problem in GAMS by means of a generic model. The second 
stage, which is the detailed design and analysis stage, consists of 4 steps. The first one is a 
rigorous process simulation where all the equipment involved in the process is included and 
thermodynamic models for each piece of equipment is selected. The second step is an economic 
evaluation of the process, in order to see if the studied alternative would be economically feasible 
to implement. The third step is a sustainability analysis where the sustainability metrics for the 
process studied are obtained. With the data obtained in these three steps, the fourth step, which is 
the analysis of process bottlenecks is performed. The third stage, which is the innovation stage, 
where process intensification is carried out consists of five steps. The first step will be defining 
the objective function, followed by the identification of desirable phenomena, generation of 
feasible flowsheet options, screening of all the feasible options using process constraints and 
finally solution of the optimization problem.

In order to solve the optimization problem, the user-defined objective function and all the logical,

constraints and logic constraints among others) defined are collected to formulate the MI(N)LP 
problem. To do that, the mathematical representation of the superstructure has to be developed.

The solution of the MI(N)LP or MILP optimization problem gives the optimal value of the 
optimization variables (where discrete variables represent the optimal strategic decisions and the 
continuous variables represent the optimal tactical decisions) together with the objective function 
and the performance indicators calculated at the optimal solution [29]. 
The model adapted by Bertran et al (2016) [10] is generic and data independent, which means that 
the user does not need to further edit it in order to solve the problem. All the necessary data needs 
to be provided as an input file and the problem will be solved using an external solver, GAMS.  
 
2.1.2 MATHEMATICAL MODEL  
The synthesis problem can be defined as an MI(N)LP problem. The generic modelling approach

Materials of construction have a significant influence on the capital cost of equipment. Therefore, 
a material factor ( 𝐹𝑚 ) will be included in the calculations for capital cost. Operating pressure of 
process equipment will also have a great influence on its capital cost as a result of thicker vessel

cost. This is caused by, amongst other factors, a decrease in the allowable stress for materials of 
construction as the temperature increases.

model has been used. The flowrate used for capital cost is equal to the summation of the flowrate 
divided into pieces for linearization. 
 
2.1.4 DATA MANAGEMENT 
In order to build a superstructure, a lot of data is required. On the one hand, the raw materials 
available in each location need to be found, as well as the product’s demand in each geographic 
location. Also, all the data related to the processing technologies available as well as the possible 
interconnections between the different intervals considered in the superstructure needs to be 
collected in order to correctly formulate the problem. This need called for the development of a 
database where to store, search and retrieve data when needed. The data structure needs to 
provide a common format to all the data, and it needs to be flexible enough to be applicable to 
diverse sets of problems.  
To achieve that, ontology-based data architecture was developed, around which specific databases 
are built. In this case, it is the ProBioRefine database that has been used and some data has been 
added as well so that other users can look it up. As shown in Figure 7, the developed ontology 
consists of two main sections; the units and the material sections. The unit section classifies the 
units in types and subtypes, and contains all the information about each unit. The material section, 
==PAGE:44==
2. METHODOLOGY AND TOOLS 
 
24 
 
as with the units, is divided into sections and subsections. It contains the list of all the compounds 
and each of them is assigned to raw material, intermediate or product, creating a link between the 
material and one of the material sub sections. The material subsections correspond to a raw 
material subsection, an intermediate subsection and a products subsection. The link between the 
raw material and the unit sections is done by assigning one material of the materials list as an 
inlet and another one as an outlet of each unit. Therefore, connections between units are inferred 
by comparison of inlet and outlet of the material names, hence obtaining a superstructure of 
alternatives. 
In this project, the database has been increased, adding all the data related to the production of 
ethanol and a mixture of diesel and gasoline with methanol as a possible co-product.

2.1.5 TOOLS  
 
o SOFTWARE TOOL: SUPER-O 
In order to automate the application of the synthesis method within the 3-stage approach 
described previously, a software tool developed in C# environment named Super-O was 
developed [10]. It is a user interface for the synthesis of processing networks, which allows a 
great reduction of the time needed to formulate and solve the network optimization problem. It 
enables the systematic storage and retrieval of the necessary information for different problem 
types. A schematic representation of how is the methodology when implementing Super-O with 
the associated tools is shown in Figure 8. Super-O integrates the previously explained ontology-
based database, from where information needed to formulate and solve the synthesis can be 
retrieved saving a lot of time to the user. The problem is formulated by the user, and all the 
necessary data will be retrieved from the database, including the problem specifications. In that 
way, a superstructure is created, and a GAMS-readable binary file is created with all the input 
data necessary to solve the optimization problem. Then, Super-O calls an optimization software, 
in this case GAMS [42] , where the model has been detailed. All the generic equations described 
are included in the model file, and so, GAMS solves the optimization problem and generates an 
output file with all the results obtained. Within these results there are the selected route, raw 
material and products, as well as the values for all the model variables so that they can be used in 
the subsequent stages of the 3-stage approach method, which are detailed design and analysis, and 
innovative and more sustainable designs.

require dramatically more mathematical computation than those for similarly sized pure linear 
programs. Many relatively small integer programming models take enormous amounts of time to 
solve. For problems with integer variables, CPLEX uses a branch and cut algorithm which solves 
a series of LP, sub problems. Because a single mixed integer problem generates many sub 
problems, even small mixed integer problems can be very compute intensive and require 
significant amounts of physical memory.

GAMS/Cplex is a GAMS solver that allows users to combine the high level modeling capabilities

2.2.1 WORKFLOW 
The detailed design and analysis stage starts by taking as input the most promising alternatives 
obtained from the superstructure optimization problem in Stage 1.  Rigorous process simulation 
with all the equipment involved in the process and not just the major piece of equipment as used 
in Stage 1 is carried out. A thermodynamic model for the overall process and for every specific 
piece of equipment involved in the process is going to be selected. As an output of the process 
simulation, mass and energy balances are obtained. With the results from the rigorous simulation, 
sizing of all the equipment is carried out as well, in order to obtain the capital cost expenses 
required for the process. With all that, an economic evaluation of the process is performed, in 
order to see if the process is economically feasible or not. In case it is not, a sensitivity analysis 
changing the prices of the raw materials used or the products obtained can be carried out to see 
how much prices would have to go up or down in order to obtain an economically feasible 
process. Then, a sustainability analysis in order to obtain the sustainability metrics is carried out. 
This will allow a comparison of the different process alternatives in terms of sustainability. 
==PAGE:50==

With all this data, an analysis of the process bottlenecks in order to identify targets for 
improvements is carried out. These potential targets for improvement will be further studied in 
the innovation stage of the 3-stage method, which is out of the scope of this project. 
2.2.2 TOOLS  
The tools needed for the design and analysis stage are presented in this section. 
o PRO/II 
The simulation of the selected alternatives has been done using Pro/II as the simulation software. 
PRO/II, which is from the Schneider Electric SimSci brand, is a steady-state process simulator for 
process design which deploys sequential approach calculations. It includes a chemical component 
library, thermodynamic property prediction methods, and unit operations such as distillation 
columns, heat exchangers, compressors, and reactors as found in the chemical processing 
industries. It can perform steady state mass and energy balance calculations for modeling 
continuous processes. 
o ECON 
The software tool that has been used to do the economic evaluation of the most promising 
alternatives obtained in Stage 1 is ECON. It needs as input data the amount of raw material 
needed to produce the amount of product desired. Also, it needs all the required data necessary 
for sizing all the pieces of equipment involved in the process and thus determine the capital cost. 
Furthermore, all the utilities required for the process need to be provided, so a calculation of the 
operating cost can be performed. With all this data, a detailed economic evaluation is obtained. 
Also, it provides results showing the sensitivity of each factor in the process. 
o SUSTAINPRO 
The software tool used to evaluate the sustainability metrics of the process alternatives is 
SustainPro. It is a tool that consists of three parts. The first part is formed by five different steps. 
The first one is data collection concerning the equipment and streams involved in the process. 
The second step consists of decomposing the flowsheet into mass closed path (MCP), mass open 
path (MOP), energy open path (EOP) and energy closed path (ECP).  Step 3 consists of the 
different indicators that will be used to determine the sustainability metrics: Material value added 
(MVA), energy waste cost (EWC), accumulation factor (AF), energy accumulation factor (EAF), 
demand cost (DC) and total demand cost (TDC). In step 4, an indicator sensitivity analysis is 
carried out, and finally in step 5 a designs according to sensitivity analysis are obtained. The 
second part consists of obtaining the sustainability metrics (SM) and safety indexes (SI). Finally, 
in part 3 a new design alternative is obtained.

3. CASE STUDIES 
 
In the recent years, there has been an increasing popularity regarding the use of bio-based ethanol 
as a partial alternative to gasoline derived from fossil fuels. Thus, a lot of research concerning the 
development of new enzymatic strains which will be able to break down more easily or faster the 
cellulosic biomass or to be able to work under a lower water load has been carried out. However, 
many companies have discovered that producing bio based ethanol in specific countries will not 
be feasible under the current raw material-product price scenario. Had they known that in 
advance, they would have saved a lot of money and effort in trying to develop new technologies 
that are not going to be useful for them due to the current prices of biomass, utilities and the cost 
of ethanol. 
That is why, the study of the production of ethanol from lignocellulosic biomass in different 
locations has been chosen as the main case study for this project. With the presented 
methodology, a fast screening can be performed, showing the scenarios under which ethanol 
production will be economically unfeasible. Thus, for those scenarios there is no point in trying to 
develop newer technologies in order to improve the process performance. However, the most 
promising ones can be further studied and analyzed in detail in stage 2 of the proposed 
methodology and after that new innovative and more sustainable solutions can be proposed in 
stage 3. In this project, focus will only be put on the synthesis and detailed design and analysis 
stages. 
Since many countries are making it mandatory to blend a minimum amount of ethanol with 
gasoline (most of them not higher than 10% since up to 10% it does not imply an engine 
modification), the production of  gasoline from biomass has also been studied.

In the recent years, ethanol has been widely being used as a transportation fuel. Ethanol, when

Bio-based ethanol has several advantages over conventional fuels. It comes from renewable 
resources i.e. agriculture residues, wood and crops. Also, compared to fossil fuels, it greatly 
reduces the GHG emissions. Road transport fuel accounts for 22% of all greenhouse gas 
emissions and through the use of ethanol, these emissions will be reduced since crops absorb the 
CO2 they emit by growing [46]. Currently most of the ethanol produced is being used in blends at 
different percentages with gasoline. It has quite high octane number i.e. 129 and its addition to 
gasoline results in an overall increase in the octane number. Ethanol can be blended up to 10% by 
volume with gasoline and be used in unmodified vehicles but it can also be blended up to 85% 
with gasoline in engine-modified vehicles. 
To illustrate the use of the aforementioned methodology, the production of ethanol from different 
feedstocks in different locations is considered in this case study.

3.2.1 INTRODUCTION 
Gasoline and diesel are two of the most used fuels around the world. Their main difference is the 
carbon chain length, which directly affects their density, being 0.83 kg/L for diesel and 0.75 kg/L 
for gasoline. There engines required for both fuels are not the same. Diesel engines have no spark 
plug. They need high compression ratios to generate the high temperatures required for the auto 
ignition of the fuel (the higher the cetane number, the better the ignition). Compression is much 
higher in diesel than in gasoline engines (25:1 vs 14:1). Gasoline engines use lower compression 
ratios in order to avoid fuel auto ignition, which would cause an engine knock. Higher 
compression ratios lead to higher thermal efficiencies and better fuel economies. 
Both bio based and biogasoline have several advantages over the sane fuels produced from fossil 
fuels. Biodiesel is a green fuel and has desirable characteristics compared to conventional diesel 
fuel. For example, it is non-toxic, biodegradable and has a very low concentration of sulfur. In 
addition it has a high cetane number (above 60 as compared to 40 for regular diesel), a high flash 
point (<130 °C) and it emits ~70% fewer hydrocarbons, ~80% less carbon dioxide and ~50% less 
particulates [48] .  
The process of biomass to gasoline consists of obtaining gasoline (and diesel as coproduct 
depending on the operating conditions) from biomass. The biomass will firstly be gasified to 
obtain syngas. From the syngas both methanol and a mixture of gasoline/diesel can be obtained. 
The methanol can be further converted to gasoline by a process named Methanol to Gasoline 
(MTG). The advantage of this process is that all the raw material except the ash is used. In 
fermentations on contrary, ash and lignin are not used and the yields for mannan, galactan and 
arabinan are really low.

4. CONCLUSIONS  
 
In this work, a systematic methodology for process synthesis has been applied to two different 
case studies; production of ethanol and production of a mixture of diesel and gasoline from 
lignocellulosic biomass. The methodology followed is based on the three-stage approach 
proposed by Babi et al [11]. The first stage has been carried out following the synthesis 
methodology developed by Quaglia et al [12] and adapted to the field of biorefineries including 
location dependency by Bertran et al [10]. The second stage has been carried out following the 
methodology used in [38]. Due to the limited time, only the first two steps of the second stage 
have been done, which are process simulation and economic evaluation. 
In the synthesis stage, a generic model proposed by Bertran et at [10] has been used. The model 
has several parameters that need to be provided as input data in order to be able to solve the 
problem. Most of them have been found in the literature and technical reports. However, others 
such as the capital cost have been estimated. An order of magnitude estimate (with around 40%) 
has been calculated. The obtained results are inside the range of error corresponding to this type 
of estimate. It has been demonstrated, that the capital cost cannot be extrapolated from the curve 
obtained by plotting flowrate vs cost. However, the results obtained by setting a maximum 
capacity for the equipment and calculating the number of equipment that would be needed are 
much more accurate. This however, has been done by hand since in order to implement it in the 
generic model, a discontinuous function should be added. Several scenarios have been solved. In 
order to make it easier to reuse the data already found, it has been included in an ontology-based 
database.  
Location dependency of the solution of biorefinery network synthesis problems has been 
confirmed through the developed case studies. It has been shown that depending on the location 
considered, both the raw material and the processing pathway vary. This is due to the difference 
in raw material price and availability, product price and demand and utility prices from location 
to location.  
The case study of production of ethanol has been solved location by location, and it has been 
observed that the raw material and processing route vary from location to location. This is due to 
the difference in raw material price and availability, product price, utility price and chemicals 
price from location to location. The problem has also been solved including all the locations. It 
has been obtained that the best location where to produce ethanol would be Thailand using 
==PAGE:91==
4. CONCLUSIONS 
 
70 
 
Cassava Rhizome as raw material. The case study of production of a mixture of gasoline and 
diesel has been solved location by location, and it has been observed that the raw material 
selected varies from location to location. However, the processing route obtained is the same for 
all the locations. Again, the optimal solution would be to produce a mixture of gasoline and diesel 
in Thailand  using Cassava Rhizome as raw material. 
The most promising alternative obtained for the ethanol case study has been simulated and 
analyzed from an economical perspective. The capital cost has increased with respect to the 
calculations done in the synthesis stage. However, the increase is within the 40% of error 
acceptable for an order of magnitude estimate. Similarly, the operating costs have also increased 
with respect to the values obtained in the synthesis stage. It has been observed that the factor that 
has a higher impact on the capital cost are the fermentation reactors due to the high amount of 
water needed for the bacterial fermentation to take place. The factor that has a higher impact on 
the operating cost is the reboiler duty of the beer distillation column. This column eliminates most 
of the water coming out of the fermentation reactor. Due to the high volumes of water involved, 
the reboiler duty of the column is really high. 
 
4.2 FUTURE WORK 
 
As future work, in order to improve the design obtained after stage 2, the third stage could be 
carried out in order to find more sustainable and innovative design for the production of ethanol 
from lignocellulosic biomass.  
A combination of the two developed case studies could be done. A problem including both the 
production of ethanol and of a mixture of gasoline and diesel from lignocellulosic raw material 
could be solved. All the input data needed to solve both problems has been stored in the ontology-
based database. By using the database, all the data is uniform and easily combinable, which 
makes it easier to combine both problems. 
Also, more scenarios could be proposed for each case study. An option would be to study the 
possibility of diverging some of the product to produce something else. In the case of ethanol for 
example some of the sugars could be diverged to produce a high value product such as succinic or 
lactic acid. In the case of producing a mixture of gasoline and diesel some of the syngas could be 
diverged to produce methanol, which despite its lower conversion, has a much higher selling 
price. In order to do that, the divider fractions should be optimized and the problem would be 
transformed into a non-linear problem.

More factors related to location dependency could be taken into account. It could be taken into 
account that due to different climate conditions in each location, biomass will have slightly 
different compositions depending on the location where it is taken from. This could be 
implemented in the generic model. 
Finally, the capital cost taking into account that depending on the flowrate more than one piece of 
equipment is going to be needed could also be implemented in the generic model.
