Abstract
Second trimester anomaly screening is a complex task, and capturing Standard Plane
(SP) improperly can lead to misdiagnosis. This is especially important for heart SP since
congenital heart diseases are the most common birth defect. While the use of machine
learning systems in healthcare is gaining momentum, explainability is often lacking, lead­
ing to distrust toward such methods.
This motivates the development of a UNet­based model that jointly segments and pre­
dicts quality scores of 21 anatomical structures present on 5 common heart SP obtained
during the anomaly screening. The segmentation and quality scores are further used to
classify SP based on clinically relevant rules. This intermediate step ensures human in­
terpretability. Thus, such a system finds application in automatic SP retrieval from video
data and as a feedback system for clinicians.
The model performance is compared to expert intra­ and inter­observer variability. Eval­
uating the dice score on correctly classified structures is shown to be on par with experts;
however, anatomies that are underrepresented in the data remain challenging to classify.
Furthermore, the system is tested on SP retrieval task from a video and is shown to be
capable of retrieving high­quality planes.
Moreover, the annotation of medical images requires a skilled professional; therefore, the
suitability of unsupervised pretraining is studied to assess if the number of labels required
can be reduced. It is shown that this approach is advantageous if less than 90 training
images per SP are used.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Congenital Heart Disease (CHD) are the most common birth defects. It affects 0.8% to
1.5% of live births, while major CHD account for 0.15% to 0.28%. Still, the detection rate
of major CHD in Denmark is 71%, and the proportion of correct diagnosis is 88.2% [1], [2].
This means that between 64 and 120 potentially life­threatening major CHD are missed
in Denmark each year. The low detection and diagnostic rates are primarily attributed to
the inherent difficulty of the scanning task, which requires a lot of expertise. The chal­
lenge comes from the fact that only a single plane through the body can be viewed at any
given time during the examination, and based on that view, an examiner has to obtain
the correct Standard Plane (SP) used for diagnosis. The SP is characterised by a proper
presentation of specific anatomical features and ideally, should have good contrast and
limited artefacts. Figure 1.1 shows a visualisation of how the heart is viewed during an
ultrasound examination.
Since the SPs are a basis for clinical workflow, an automatic method to obtain said planes
have the potential to improve its quality and consistency and benefit the diagnostic rate.
Figure 1.1 – Visualisation of the correspondence of 3D heart and 2D ultrasound image.
Source [3]
1.2
Background
The literature on classifying standard planes of ultrasound images is plentiful [4, 5, 6, 7,
8, 9]. However, most do not provide explainability, which is considered a disadvantage in
the medical field. Most often, neural network models are trained end­to­end. Given input
data X and labels Y , a function f(X) = Y is found with little regard for how the task is
accomplished. This may result in inference rules that differ from our expectations. For
example, markings placed next to skin lesions were shown to interfere with predictions
[10].
Some attempts have been made to guide the network toward a representation that is hu­
man interpretable, notably concept bottleneck models [11]. However, this approach does
not produce the expected results [12]. Moreover, saliency map based explainability [13,
14, 15] makes it possible to verify that the prediction is based on the relevant parts of the
image. However, it is too crude to distinguish between anatomical structures and deemed
unsatisfactory for clinical application. An example of saliency map based explainability of

Some research suggests that detecting relevant structures and scoring the standard plane
prediction aids interpretability.
This is promising, but there is room for improvement.
For example, approaches that perform object detection and quality prediction [16, 17]
utilise separate networks to perform this tasks which adds unnecessary complexity and
increases the inference time. Other work determines the quality of a standard plane based
on the predicted structures, but the presentation quality of individual structures is not
taken into consideration [18]. Furthermore, outlining the exact boundaries of anatomies
of interest is better accomplished with segmentation, which enables a straightforward
implementation of biometric measurements that aid in the verification of abnormalities.
Segmentation of fetal heart was already described in the literature [19, 20], but it was not
done in the context of providing explainability, and the quality measure was omitted.
Unfortunately, segmentation is more labour intensive than providing image­level labels
useful for classification or bounding boxes for object detection. Since the annotation pro­
cess requires a skilled professional and takes 1.5 to 2 minutes per image, minimising the
amount of annotation needed is beneficial. Research suggests that unsupervised pre­
training can aid label efficiency [21] and has shown some promising results in the medical
imaging domain [22, 23, 24].

1.3
Approach
Despite the significant amount of work dedicated to the standard plane classification task,
only a small fraction utilise clinically meaningful explainability or provide a QS. Moreover,
existing approaches are not suitable for biometric measurements.
Therefore this thesis aims to develop a model that jointly predicts expert­assigned quality
scores and segmentation masks for 21 anatomical structures on 5 common heart stan­
dard planes from 2nd trimester scannings. Furthermore, the model output will be used to
classify the SPs using clinically relevant rules. The benefits of this approach include:
• Utilisation of a single model to perform segmentation and quality score prediction
• Direct interpretability of the classification results
• Straightforward implementation of biometric measurements
Note that this work does not cover the use of active learning, 3D Ultrasound (US) data,
nor the use of temporal correlation present in US videos.
The solution to the explainable standard plane classification problem is accomplished in
the following steps. Firstly, unsupervised pretraining utilising the SWAV method [25] is
used to obtain an encoder network, and its quality is compared to ImageNet baseline and
evaluated on the 2nd trimester classification task. Next, a decoder network is attached to
the pretrained encoder to form a UNet [26] architecture; additional outputs for the quality
score predictions are appended. Finally, this model is trained to segment 21 anatomical
structures and output corresponding Quality Score (QS) on 5 fetal heart SPs. The pre­
dictions, together with clinically relevant rules, are used to classify the standard planes.
Additionally, evaluation of the segmentation performance as a function of the number of
annotated images is shown for pretrained models and the ImageNet baseline. Moreover,
intra­ and inter­observer variability is shown to allow for the comparison of the model per­
formance to experts. Lastly, the explainable standard plane classification system is tested
on a video obtained during the 2nd trimester examination.
This work is meant to pave the way for a system that could be connected to US scanner
and provide the sonographer with feedback on the visualisation quality and anatomical
completeness of the SPs, which would act as quality assurance.

Related Work
In this chapter, the developments in image classification, object detection, and segmen­
tation will be described. Next, the field of semi and self­supervised learning will be intro­
duced. This will be followed by work done in the context of fetal ultrasound images.
Finding objects on images can be split by how precise the localisation is. If only infor­
mation about the presence or absence of an object on the image is obtained, it is called
classification. Object detection refers to locating the given feature with a minimum height
and width bounding box containing the entire object. The next step is segmentation; in
this case, the precise object boundary is outlined. While the segmentation is the best in
terms of localisation, it is also the most labour intensive to annotate.
2.1
Classification
The use of deep learning models in the computer vision field started with the introduction
of the AlexNet [28], which achieved top­5 test error 10% lower than previous methods.
This has spurred research into designing new network architectures, which grew more
extensive and more complex with the increase in Graphics Processing Unit (GPU) perfor­
mance [29, 30, 31, 32]. Recently, the focus shifted from manual architecture development
to Network Architecture Search (NAS) [33], which aims to choose the most suitable archi­
tecture for the task at hand from a given design space. However, such a method requires
a lot of computing power. In contrast, RegNet[34] focused on obtaining so­called design
spaces that generalize to different problems.
While Convolutional Neural Network (CNN)s dominate the domain of image processing,
it has been shown that transformers [35], which are most famous for natural language
processing, can provide competitive results. However, this approach has not matured
yet.
2.2
Object detection
Object detection is an extension of image classification where the location of an object of
interest is highlighted by a bounding box. Most of the solutions to this problem are based
on network architectures that consist of an encoder that extracts image features, which
is often just a network pre­trained on a classification task followed by a part that predicts
bounding boxes and corresponding labels.
The most naive approach to object detection is to input crops of the original image into a
classifier. This approach is utilised in the Region­based Convolutional Neural Networks
(RCNN) [36]. It uses a selective search to find objects on an image, which are sub­
sequently passed through an ImageNet pre­trained AlexNet with the classification layer
removed. This yields a feature vector representation that is subsequently classified with
support vector machines. Since the original publication, the design was iteratively im­
proved. Firstly, by removing the need to pass each region through a classifier [37], then
by replacing the selective search with a trainable region proposal network [38], and finally,
by training for multiple values of IoU [39]. As a result, RCNN is one of the most popular
object detection architectures that can also serve as a base for segmentation tasks [40].
However, the fact that it requires a two­step approach spurred research into alternative
architectures that remove the need for the region proposal network and perform the detec­
tion in one step, hence the name ”single­shot” detectors [41, 42]. An important realisation
Automatic Detection of Anatomical Structures in Fetal Hearts
5
==PAGE:20==
was that combining features from different scales to form so­called feature pyramid net­
works (FPN) leads to improved performance [43, 44]. This idea is also extensively used
in segmentation networks. Moreover, it has been shown that transformers can succeed
in object detection tasks; however, the authors point out that the approach has to mature
first [45].
2.3
Segmentation
Segmentation increases the granularity of object localisation from bounding boxes to in­
dividual pixels. This approach can be further subdivided into semantic and instance seg­
mentation. The former labels pixels based on the class it belongs to; for example, a
picture of two people would result in both being marked as ”person”, while instance seg­
mentation would mark it as ”person 1” and ”person 2”. This can be obtained by extending
the RCNN network with a layer to predict the segmentation mask [40]. However, this ap­
proach also inherits the limitations of the former method. Another approach is to remove
the global pooling layer from a classifier to form a fully convolutional network and treat the
feature map as a coarse segmentation that can be interpolated to the desired size [46].
An extension to this idea utilises a decoder network to upsample the features [47]
However, these were superseded by methods that utilise the Feature Pyramid Netowrk
(FPN) idea to combine features from different scales [48]. UNet [26, 49]. is another
popular architecture that utilises the same idea. Recent work in image semgentation
include NAS [50], use of dilated convolutions [51], and use of transformers [52]
2.4
Unsupervised learning
Firstly, non­deep learning approaches were omitted for brevity. Likewise, generative mod­
els are only briefly mentioned due to the generally higher computational demands, making
them impractical in this project.
Unsupervised, self­, semi­supervised, representation­, manifold­, feature­learning and so
on. It seems that the vocabulary used to describe the problem lacks consistency.
Starting with the ”unsupervised” term, which in the context of deep learning is a misnomer.
After all, gradient descent requires a supervisory signal to generate the error surface to
descend on. Therefore the term ”unsupervised” must refer to the signal’s origin. Is it
provided externally by an expert, like in supervised learning, or is it obtained from the
data itself, like in autoencoders? If we decide to take that route, we must consider what
differentiates self­supervised and unsupervised learning. It would be easier to discuss
the problem if the focus is shifted away from the naming conventions to the underlying
principles the solutions are built upon.
Firstly, starting with an assumption that the semantic meaning (is it a cat or a dog, small
or big) of the high­dimensional data (image) is expected to have a lower­dimensional
latent representation. Furthermore, this representation is continuous, which means that
neighbouring points are semantically similar. Lastly, the cluster assumption states that the
points form distinct clusters separated by low­density regions. This is illustrated beautifully
by the reconstruction of latent space with GANs or VAEs. By interpolating the latent space
between two classes, it can be seen how those continuously deform from one into the
other, as shown in Figure 2.1.
The above problem can be solved by finding a mapping from input space X to embedding
space Z, in which the above assumptions hold. Many authors argue that the semantic
meaning of the image remains unchanged if some reasonable augmentation is applied,

e.g. random crop. This, together with the cluster assumption, allows us to train a network
that performs the mapping from X to Z by either minimizing the distance in embedding
space between different augmentations of X or by passing the embedding through a clas­
sifier and ensuring the augmentations result in the same class. On its own, this approach
fails due to the existence of a trivial solution known as mode collapse, in which everything
is mapped to a single cluster or point. Therefore, the cluster separation must be enforced
in some way. Hence, the solutions to this problem could be categorized by the approach
taken to enforce clustering of similar and separation of dissimilar data points.
However, one notable exception to this approach is the use of pretext tasks. This ap­
proach does not attempt to cluster the data in any way; instead, it utilises tasks that allow
the model to learn a representation that carries some benefits for the downstream task.
Some authors propose colourisation of grayscale images [54], predicting relative rotation
[55], and position of image crops [55, 56].
To ensure consistency throughout the rest of this report, all pretraining approaches that
enable reconstruction of the original or generation of novel data from the embedding Z
are referred to as generative models. Moreover, methods that do not utilise externally
supplied labels as in supervised learning are referred to as unsupervised methods. Lastly,
approaches that incorporate labelled samples along with unlabelled ones are referred to
as semi­supervised methods.
Generative models
Generative models try to obtain a mappings f(X) = Z and f′(Z) = X′. The inverse
function f′ is used to generate novel data from the latent space. This explicit invertibility
constrain prevents the mode collapse. Autoregressive models aim to predict the next out­
put of a sequence conditioned on previous terms; this approach is mainly used in natural
language processing. With some modifications, it can be applied to image generation
problems[57, 58]. However, it generates pixels sequentially; therefore, it suffers from
poor performance. Flow models explicitly model the complex underlying data distribution
using normalizing flow [59, 60]. Autoencoders work under the assumption that the data
comes from low­dimensional latent space. This approach compresses the input to the
latent representation Z and reconstructs X′ form Z. [53, 61] GANs model the data distri­
bution implicitly by generating X′ samples from Z through a decoder(generator). At the
same time, a classifier (discriminator) is trained on real samples from the dataset and the

==PAGE:22==
generated ones to discriminate between them. There exist modifications to GAN that also
train an encoder suitable for downstream tasks [62]
Unsupervised
The idea of contrastive learning attempts to minimise the distance between embeddings or
enforce the same classification output of similar data, for example, multiple augmentations
of the same image. At the same time, the distance is maximised, or class assignment is
ensured to be different between dissimilar points. The dissimilar data can be obtained
randomly from the dataset under the assumption that it is unlikely to sample the same
class twice.
DeepCluster [63] attempts to generate embeddings Z and subsequently cluster them after
each epoch. In addition, the authors propose to combat the mode collapse by weighting
the sample loss with the inverse of the size of its cluster assignment. Further work focused
on improving the efficiency by replacing the once per epoch clustering with memory banks
[64] and queues [65] to store previously obtained Z. Hyperparameter and augmentation
optimisation [66] together with the inclusion of multiple views [67] have shown to produce
better results.
BYOL[68] and Mean Shift [69] pass two or more augmentations of an image through two
networks and try to match the prediction. The twist in this work comes from the parameter
update rule; specifically, one of the networks is updated with an exponential moving aver­
age of the parameters belonging to the other network instead of regular gradient descent.
Authors hypothesise that this prevents the mode collapse. However, it has been shown
experimentally that this is not the case [70], and another hypothesis was proposed.
SWAV [25] aims to enforce the same cluster assignment for different augmentations of
a single image. The mode collapse is explicitly prevented by penalizing the non­uniform
distribution of cluster assignments. It has also been shown to work with smaller batch
sizes compared to previous methods.
Semi­Supervised
The availability of some labels during training can simplify the problem. This allows us to
think about the problem in a more supervised learning way as the mode collapse should
not occur if the number of labels is greater than one and those are not severely unbal­
anced. Therefore, the primary concern of this approach is how to obtain a more robust
representation. This is obtained through ”consistency regularisation”, which is another
way to say that augmentations of the same image should result in the same class assign­
ment or close distance in the embedding space. Intuitively one can think about this as
expanding cluster boundaries from the labelled samples. Firstly the boundary expands
from a single point thanks to the augmentations. Once the boundary is sufficiently close
to another unlabelled point, it is assigned a pseudo­label, which is subsequently used
similarly to the regular one.
Confirmation bias is a potential problem arising from this approach [71]. If a pseudo­label
is incorrect, this prediction would be reinforced in subsequent iterations. Possible solution
to this problem include soft label assignments [72], combination of augmentation and
confidence thresholding [73], and enforcing that interpolation in input space corresponds
to interpolation in embedded space [74].
Another method called temporal ensembling [75] generates pseudo­labels by averaging
predictions over multiple epochs. Subsequently, authors of the Mean Teacher[76] argue
that it is better to use two networks, with one updated with gradient descent and the other
with the moving average of the first. Noisy Student [77] utilizes the teacher­student idea
8

by generating pseudo­labels on unaugmented images with the teacher network and sub­
sequently training a bigger student network on the heavily augmented pseudo­labelled im­
ages. Then the student becomes a new teacher, and the process repeats. Meta Pseudo
Labels [78] proposes to optimize the teacher network based on the student’s performance
on labelled samples; this method avoids generating a new network on every iteration
Currently, PAWS [79] shows promising results. It is a variation of SWAV applied to a
problem with partially labelled data. Authors argue that the mode collapse is avoided if
labelled data is balanced in mini­batches.
2.5
Fetal Ultrasound
Classification of standard planes in fetal ultrasound is well researched subject that in­
cludes both classical [80, 9] and deep learning methods [6, 7, 8]. In this context, explain­
ability can be provided with saliency maps. For example, one work applies this idea to
CHD classification task [81]
Some authors [4, 82, 83] proposed tackling problem of object detection with weakly­
supervised learning. In this context, a model is trained on a classification task, and the
localisation is obtained from layer activations or gradient­based saliency maps. Similar
results can be obtained by including the attention layer, which provides localisation and
explainability similar to saliency maps [5]. Other authors [84, 85] utilise object detection
models to analyse axial sweeps. In this approach, a timeline is generated which shows
the detected structures as a function of the ultrasound probe position. Comparing a given
timeline to one generated from healthy hearts can be used to find abnormalities. This
approach allows human interpretability as the network predictions can be evaluated for
correctness, and this was shown to be beneficial from the examiner’s perspective.
Further work [19] describes the implementation of a classifier network for 5 standard
planes 3 Vessel Trachea (3VT), 3 Vessel View (3VV), LVOT, 4CV, and Abdomen/Situs.
The interpretability of the model is provided with gradient­based saliency maps. While
highlighting the relevant features, the authors mention that it is impossible to infer the real
reason for misclassification. The same work describes a segmentation model for 4CV,
which includes the following classes: thorax, atriums, ventricles and spine. The study
included 107 823 images of normal and abnormal hearts from 1 326 patients.
Another work [20] on fetal heart segmentation reported IoU and dice scores. However,
the paper has several weaknesses that raise suspicion about the integrity of obtained
results. 1149 images from 50 patients were used to train a Mask­RCNN to segment 4CV,
3VT, LVOT, RVOT. Authors claim that 17 objects are segmented; however, this way of
counting assumes that, e.g. ascending aorta visible in RVOT, LVOT and 3VT corresponds
to 3 distinct objects. Excluding the defects, the number of labelled anatomical structures
is 9, including ascending aorta, ductus arteriosus, superior vena cave, main pulmonary
artery, left and right ventricles and atriums. Furthermore, the study evaluated the intra and
inter­patient variation for CHD detection. However, the description of the results suggests
that the intra­patient variability was conducted with the same images in the train and test
set. This would raise the question if the model was overfitting the data.
In medical ultrasound, obtaining a correct standard plane is only part of the task. Addi­
tionally, it has to be ensured that the SP is a high­quality one. Hence, quality assessment
is crucial for utilising the models in clinical settings. Prior work on this subject includes
quality assessment of transthalamic SP. This is accomplished by scoring an image based
on the presence of relevant anatomical structures [18]. Moreover, one study developed
a quality control method for 4CV. The relevant anatomical structures are detected on the

work includes quality score prediction for abdominal SP [16].
Automatic biometric measurements is a less researched area that focuses on the head
and abdominal SPs used for weight estimations as it is a part of standard practice [86, 87]
The medical field has already started embracing the potential of unsupervised pretrain­

3.1
Limitations
Some of the design choices and evaluation methods were affected by external factors.
The first major constraint was GPU availability. Due to chip shortage, the ordered hard­
ware was delayed several times. Since the GPUs on the server have to be shared with
other users, and the data cannot be transferred to another server, the unsupervised learn­
ing part had to be limited in scope and some of the experiments could not be repeated.
Another problem was related to data availability. Since it became available relatively late
in the project and was supplied in batches of 100­200 images, it was necessary to perform
initial tests on a subset of the final data. In normal circumstances, the initial tests could
be repeated on the entire segmentation dataset close to the end of the project, but GPU
availability made it impractical.

set and the remaining ones.

SWAV Implementation
Unsupervised pretraining based on the SWAV approach relies on enforcing the same clus­
ter prediction for augmentations of a given image. The cluster assignments Q are obtained
by multiplying learnable cluster prototypes C with an image embedding Z. This is illus­
trated in Figure 3.6 where the SWAV approach (right) is compared to contrastive learning
(left). Note that cluster assignments Q are called ”codes” in the figure. Authors argued
for the use of a multi­cropping strategy, which generates so­called global views (large
crops) that contain a large portion of the original image, and local views (small crops) that
are relatively smaller. Then, the cross­entropy loss is computed between all N pairs of
cluster assignments originating from global and local views.

of cluster assignments Q; in this approach, it is accomplished with the Sinkhorn­Knopp

The SWAV implementation described in the original paper had to be modified for this
specific problem. The first significant change is the departure from the ResNet50 encoder
architecture. In the unsupervised learning community, it is used as a standard to allow for
easy comparison between methods, but such comparison is impossible in this work. This
enables the use of RegNet, a more optimised architecture.
Additional changes were necessary to accommodate for training on a single GPU. The
batch size and learning rate were reduced, LARS optimiser was changed to SGD with
momentum. Due to smaller batch sizes, a queue was used as recommended in the paper.
Furthermore, it was observed that the recommended cropping parameters (0.14­1) and
(0.05­0.14) were unsuitable for this problem, leading to mode collapse. Therefore the
initial training utilised a less aggressive cropping strategy Aug1 shown in Table 3.4 along

3.3.3
Encoder
As mentioned in the Related Work chapter 2.1, automated network design has yielded
multiple well­performing architectures, while the EfficientNetv2 [90] was shown to outper­
form RegNet [34], it was decided to use the latter due to its availability in PyTorch frame­
work. Specifically, the implementation from [91] was used, as it can be easily modified to
output intermediate layer activations, which is useful for segmentation task.
The RegNet does not describe any novel neural network architecture, and it can be con­
sidered a slight variation of the ResNet [30]. Instead, the authors optimised the network
parameters (number of layers and convolutional channel width) for a given number of
floating­point opeartions. This approach replaces the heuristic part of choosing network
parameters with ones sampled from the experimentally derived design space.
Therefore the outputs of the network are E1 to E5, which correspond to intermediate out­
puts with sizes [(H/2, W/2)...(H/32, W/32)] and embedding Z which is a result of the
global average pooling from E5. While literature advocates using a large model for unsu­
pervised training, it was imperative to use one that can be trained in a reasonable amount
of time on the available hardware. Hence, only the RegNetY 800mf, 1.6Gf and 3.2Gf
architectures were tested. The values denote the number of floating­point operations.
To monitor the encoder’s quality during training, it was evaluated on a linear classification
task on the 2nd trimester dataset. This evaluation was performed at the end of each
epoch by passing the images from 2nd trimester train, validation and test set through the
encoder to obtain Ztrain, Zval, Ztest. Subsequently, the embeddings Ztrain and Zval were
used as an input to a linear classifier to predict the corresponding classes. Finally, the
performance on Ztest was plotted for each epoch to evaluate the performance change.
The initial tests were performed on RegNetY 1.6Gf with 128 prototypes. It was found that
the suitable training parameters include: a variable learning rate between 0.2 and 0.0002,
batch size 128, queue length 512, one warmup epoch, freezing prototypes for the first 7k
iterations, and use of augmentation level Aug1 as shown in Table 3.4. Keep in mind that
the batch size denotes the number of unique images used at each iteration, but for each
image, the multi­crop augmentation yields 2 large and 4 small crops. This means that the
effective batch size is 6 times larger. Further tests were performed on RegNetY 800Mf
with 64 prototypes and Regnet 3.2Gf with 256. The batch size was scaled to 192 and
90, respectively, to fit in the GPU memory, and the number of iterations without updating
prototypes was set to 5.2k and 11k to accommodate the change in batch size. Other

Model Architecture
Due to its simplicity, it was decided to use the UNet approach to segmentation instead of
Mask­RCNN. The latter method shows excellent results; however, it is less straightforward
to implement and has additional hyperparameters that must be tuned to the problem at
hand.

==PAGE:35==
The segmentation is accomplished by combining the encoder with the decoder and adding
skip connections between feature layers with matching width and height. This is shown
in Figure 3.9, where E1−5 denote encoders block from RegNetY 1.6Gf architecture, D1−5
denote decoder stages and || represents concatenation along channel dimension. Each
decoder stage consists of an upsampling block and two layers with 3x3 convolution, batch
normalisation and dropout, followed by a Leaky ReLU activation function. Dropout prob­
ability was set to 0.2, and the number of output channels was set to half of the input. Ad­
ditionally, B1 shown in the figure uses the same block architecture but takes a grayscale
image as an input and outputs 16 channels. Lastly, B2 is a convolutional layer that out­
puts 23 channels, 22 corresponding to segmented classes (including background) and 1
quality score prediction, which will be described in section 3.3.5.
Two upsampling strategies were evaluated. One relied on transposed convolution with
kernel size 4 and stride 2, and another used bilinear interpolation. The former has led to
less stable training and worse overall results. Hence bilinear interpolation was used for
upsampling.
Finally, the last stage of the encoder E5 is passed through a global average pooling layer
to obtain embedding Z. This is processed by two MLP blocs that output additional class
and quality score predictions used in some evaluations. Each block consists of a 3­layer
fully connected network with 128 features each and ReLU activation in between.
3.3.5
Quality Prediction
Two ways of predicting quality scores were evaluated. The Method 1 QSemb utilised one
of the MLP blocks to process embedding vector Z and output a vector of quality score
predictions. The last layer did not include any activation function. The quality score of
absent structures on a given image was set to 0, and mean absolute error loss was used
for this task.
Since the quality score is based partially on the quality of the boundary of segmented
structures, the Method 2 QSpx relied on predicting the QS values for each pixel. This was
implemented by extending the segmentation output with an additional channel treated
separately as a regression task and optimised with the mean absolute error loss function.
This approach makes the quality score prediction only meaningful at the segmentation
boundaries. Therefore the loss originating from pixels away from the boundary was set
to 0. The boundary area and the target value were obtained with morphological opera­
tions performed on a given segmentation label. Each label was dilated with a 5x5 square
structuring element to generate M1 and eroded with the same element to generate M2.
The boundary mask corresponds to M1 XOR M2. The same procedure is followed dur­
ing inference to obtain the boundary masks for each predicted segmentation label. An
average of the individual pixels belonging to the mask yields a quality score for a given
anatomical structure.

Quantitative Segmentation Results
The encoder pretrained in an unsupervised manner on the augmentation level Aug2 was
used as a base for the segmentation model. It was trained for 5000 epochs using aug­

parameters were outlined previously in Table 3.3.
The model output was evaluated to check whether it predicts the correct anatomy without
focusing on the precise outline, this approach is treated as a classification task, where
True Positive (TP) refers to dice score 1 > Dice >= 0.5 between prediction and annota­
tion, True Negative (TN) indicates dice score equal to 1. This only happens if there is no
expert annotation or model prediction. Furthermore, in this case the False Negative (FN)
is defined as dice < 0.5, and False Positive (FP) as a non­zero predicted area without
corresponding expert annotation. For brevity, the simplified results for each anatomical
structure are included in Table 4.2, and poorly performing cases are highlighted. A more
detailed breakdown of the classification results is included in the appendix C. The high­
lighted issues arise for the following reasons:
• Aortic/Pulmonary Valve ­ Underrepresented and small­sized structure
• Left Pulmonary Artery ­ Relatively less common than other structures on the RVOT
plane.
• Outer Skin/Heart Boundary ­ Dice score is a poor metric for thin and long structures.

across different scales (thickness is the same regardless of zoom).
• Pulmonary Veins ­ Ambiguous and difficult to outline. This will be shown in the next

• Right Atrium ­ Underrepresented on the LVOT plane.
• Ventricular Septum ­ Only partially labelled on the LVOT plane

5
Discussion and Conclusion
The results presented in the previous chapter are discussed in the section below, following
the conclusion of the thesis.
5.1
Discussion
Firstly, the results related to unsupervised pretraining are discussed. Next, the model
performance is split into sections dedicated to quantitative evaluation, label uncertainty,
and qualitative results.
5.1.1
Unsupervised Pretraining
As shown in section 4.1, the unsupervised pretraining yields an encoder capable of sepa­
rating the relevant heart standard planes from the rest of the data. However, distinguishing
between specific heart SP proves to be difficult with this approach. This is especially true
for 4CV, VSV, and LVOT that form one cluster; and 3VV and RVOT that form another,
see Figure 4.1. This is consistent with the visual appearance of the SP listed above and
highlights the task’s difficulty.
Moreover, in section 4.2, it was shown that the fine­tuning of the pre­trained encoder leads
to better performance on 2nd trimester data than the ImageNet baseline. This indicates
a clear benefit to unsupervised pretraining for the classification task. While this does
not offer any direct benefit to the segmentation task, a good classifier helps to ease the
annotation burden by discarding irrelevant planes that otherwise would have to be looked
through.
As shown in results 4.3, for the segmentation task, pretraining offers a clear benefit when
the labelled data is scarce. However, when 90 training images per class are used, the dif­
ference between unsupervised pretraining on ultrasound dataset and Imagenet pretrained
network is insignificant. Considering that it takes an annotator 1.5 to 2 min to annotate
an image, matching the performance of the model pretrained in an unsupervised manner
can be achieved with 11 to 15 hours of annotator effort. This means it takes less effort
to annotate the images than to implement the unsupervised pretraining pipeline. The lim­
ited effect of pretraining is possibly caused by the specific pretraining method, which was
designed with the classification task in mind. To rule out this possibility, further study is
needed to evaluate the effects of approaches specifically designed for segmentation.
In retrospect, it is not advisable to devote a significant amount of effort to unsupervised
pretraining if the number of distinct SP is small. Such an approach is likely more suitable
for cases with many different planes for which top performance is not required. Given the
clear benefit of unsupervised pretraining in scarce annotation scenarios, future research
could evaluate how to couple this approach with active learning.
5.1.2
Model Performance
Quantitative evaluation
The segmentation task of the heart is not trivial due to the inherent ambiguity in the la­
bels. As shown in 4.5.1, experts generally agree on the presence of anatomical structures
with some notable exceptions. For example, there was a considerable disagreement on
the placement of pulmonary veins in 4CV between experts due to different annotations
approaches. However, even the same expert only agreed on the annotation 67% of the
time, which means this is inherently difficult. More ambiguities between experts are shown
between pulmonary and mitral valves, and descending aorta in 3VV plane.
Automatic Detection of Anatomical Structures in Fetal Hearts
45
==PAGE:60==
Moreover, the dice score between experts and the model appears to be on the same level
when computed only on the anatomies where there is an agreement. This suggests that
the model performs well in this task, and no further performance gains can be expected
by training with more annotations or increasing the input image resolution.
However, there is room for improvement in regards to identifying the correct anatomy.
Most notably, the model ignores aortic and pulmonary valves that appear small and are
underrepresented in the dataset. Attempts were made to alleviate this problem by ad­
justing the corresponding weight parameter in the loss function, but this has led to overall
worse performance. Subsequently, the input size of the network was increased from
224 × 224 to 288 × 288 to evaluate if larger size helps with segmentation of small struc­
tures. This did not help, and the problem requires further investigation. Oversampling the
images with the valves present or changing the loss function are other possible solutions
that do not rely on labelling more data.
Furthermore, the poor detection rate of the ventricular septum on the LVOT SP is caused
by inconsistent labelling, only part of the training data has this anatomy annotated. This
problem will be corrected during the next round of annotation with the addition of the
missing labels.
Quality score was shown to correspond in intra and inter­observer test, and the mean
correlation coefficient was 0.7424 and 0.7784, respectively. However, the correlation co­
efficient between the expert and model was only 0.3242 or 0.3900 for method 1 (QSemb)
and method 2 (QSpx), respectively. This suggests that outputting QS for each pixel along
the segmentation mask yields better results, but there is still a significant performance
gap compared to the experts.
However, the reliability of the intra­ and inter­observer evaluation can be questioned due
to a low number of images per SP. This could be improved by including more images and
performing significance tests.
Uncertainty
In the section 4.6, expert uncertainty was qualitatively compared to model uncertainty
estimated using two approaches, Monte Carlo dropout and augmentation, as shown in
Figure 4.10.
The former approach has shown only the boundary uncertainty and provided little useful
information. However, augmentation has led to more perceptible visualisation. In the case
of LVOT and 3VV planes, it has shown weak correspondence with expert uncertainty. The
significant expert uncertainty of Main Pulmonary Artery (MPA), Ductus Arteriosus (DA),
and LVOT is caused by the inherent difficulty of the task and partially by inconsistencies
in labelling. The rules defining where an anatomy ends were not sufficiently well defined.
Lastly, inspection of the calibration curves reveals that the reliability of the segmentation
probability score obtained from the model varies and can be considered acceptable for
LVOT, Superior Vena Cava (SVC), while the reliability of Left Pulmonary Artery (LPA),
Outer Heart Boundary (OHB), and Outer Skin Boundary (OSB) is poor. This suggests
that the probability obtained from the model cannot be used for uncertainty quantification
without calibration.
While it is interesting to evaluate the model uncertainty to gain insight into whether it
corresponds with experts or if the predictions are reliable, in the current implementation,
it offers little tangible benefit. However, future work is likely to include measurements of
the anatomical structures, and in such a scenario, the segmentation uncertainty will be
important.

5.1.3
Qualitative evaluation
To qualitatively evaluate the model on standard plane retrieval task, a video was pro­
cessed to obtain 3 candidate images for each SP as described in 4.7. The expert con­
firmed that most of the retrieved images are high­quality standard planes, and at least
one out of the three is an acceptable one. However, RVOT proved challenging, with two
planes being unacceptable, as shown in Figure 4.12. One issue arose from a failure
on off­plane image that was misclassified. This highlights that the system is not robust
enough to handle images it was not trained on. Further work is planned to address this
issue by adding off­plane images.
Moreover, the expert confirmed that displaying the segmentation of the anatomical struc­
tures found on an image with corresponding quality scores provides meaningful feedback
as seen in Figure 4.13. Furthermore, the rule­based standard plane classification helps
build trust in machine learning systems. However, in some cases, it may be impossible
to obtain a quality SP fulfilling the predefined rules. For example, this may be caused
by patients being overweight or a fetus being placed unfavourably. In such cases, it has
to be ensured that the SP requirements are not too rigid and the best possible plane is
obtained in given circumstances.
5.2
Conclusion
This thesis focused on developing a segmentation model for 21 anatomical structures
and prediction of corresponding expert­assigned quality scores on 5 fetal heart standard
planes. Moreover, the model output was used to develop an explainable SP classifier
based on clinically relevant rules.
Since the annotation of medical images require a skilled professional, it was investigated if
unsupervised pretraining on ultrasound images can lead to better performance with fewer
labels. It was shown that for the segmentation task, it offers a clear benefit when using
less than 90 training images per SP. However, this number of images did not lead to
satisfactory performance and additional training samples were included.
Two methods for predicting the quality score were evaluated; method 1 (QSemb) obtained
a single prediction per anatomy. Method 2 (QSpx) assigned QS to every pixel. The pixel­
wise predictions and the segmentation mask were used to post­process the result and
obtain a single QS per anatomy. This method was shown to correlate better with expert­
assigned QS
Furthermore, the agreement between experts themself and the model was shown in terms
of label agreement and the dice score. It was shown that once the model identifies the
correct anatomy, the dice overlap is on par with experts. However, in regards to de­
tecting correct anatomy, there is still room for improvement; most notably, the small and
underrepresented aortic and pulmonary valves are not detected at all. Additional work is
required to improve it.
Lastly, the model was set to retrieve the 3 best candidates for each of the 5 standard
planes from a video recording of the 2nd trimester examination. According to the ex­
pert, the majority of obtained images were high­quality ones. However, there is room for
improvement in terms of robustness. Furthermore, it was reported that visualisation of
the segmentation and quality score provides a satisfactory level of explainability, and the
rule­based standard plane classification has the potential to increase the trust of health
professionals toward the system.
