Abstract
In higher education activities, formative evaluation is increasingly valued by educators.
In the current Danish context, all universities still use summative evaluation to evaluate
teaching activities as a part of institutional educational quality assessment process. How-
ever, summative evaluation has the characteristics of hysteresis and uncertainty due to
the form it takes. These characteristics lead to a lack of integration between teachers’
teaching quality and evaluation results, and students do not feel that these evaluations
valuable. Formative evaluation, as it accompanies the whole process of educational activi-
ties, can help teachers to understand the students’ learning status dynamically. Therefore,
formative assessment has become a better way of teaching assessment and has been paid
more and more attention.
At present, the general method for conducting formative evaluation is questionnaire sur-
vey. The questionnaires are mainly released through Google form or Microsoft office, and
then scored by students. Due to the characteristics of these platforms, teachers need to
analyze the results of questionnaires by themselves, and students receive delayed or no
feedback on the results of the surveys. As a result, students and teachers are less moti-
vated to participate in assessments. On the other hand, teachers are unable to provide
personalized feedback if they cannot analyze the true motivation behind students’ com-
ment based on the evaluation results.
On the other hand, the communication between teachers and students in formative eval-
uation is also an important factor affecting the quality of evaluation. In some scenarios,
the communication between teachers and students is not timely and sufficient due to the
lack of platforms or information.
The main purpose of this thesis is to provide an improved solution to help students and
teachers perform formative evaluation and enable teachers to give students targeted and
personalized feedback during the evaluation process.
This study, in cooperation with Wyblo[1], improves the interface and functions on the ba-
sis of their existing products, and combines learning preferences and personality analysis
models to give both teachers and students insights on learning status.

Abstract

This study is mainly based on design thinking methodology, using desk research, user
empathize interviews and other methods to understand user needs, and using Point-of-
view, HMW and other methods to try to find solutions to user problems. First, this
study investigates the academic research status, strengths and weaknesses of formative
evaluation by searching for keywords on the Internet, reading related blogs and articles,
and investigates existing solutions. At the same time, this study supplements the findings
on user needs by conducting interviews and questionnaires with users within a selected
scope. Secondly, this study adopts the method of storyboard to demonstrate user sce-
narios, brainstorm new solutions and filter them with methods such as Point-of-view and
HMW. Finally, this study uses Figma to create a high-fidelity prototype for student and
teacher respectively, and test it against different users. Based on feedback from testing,
the prototype is continuously iterated.
This thesis verifies the usability and feasibility of this study by designing a improved
clickable prototype and using it to conduct a series of user tests. The prototype designed
for this study uses the VARK[2] learning preference analysis model and the MBTI[3] per-
sonality analysis model, combined with data from the results of the formative evaluation
survey, to create personalized profiles of students. At the same time, the prototype pro-
vides dynamic and visual insights for both students and teachers based on the different
personalized profiles of different students.
During the process of designing and building the prototype, this study used a combi-
nation of research and testing to design an optimized user flow and data dashboards.
Additionally, this study introduced more new functionalities which can provide more de-
tailed insights on top of Wyblo’s original products.
Judging from the user test results, the most of users expressed a positive attitude towards
this study and expressed their willingness to try the products designed this time. Follow-
ing user testing, the prototype underwent a series of iterations to ensure that it satisfied
both the user’s flow and their essential needs better.
This study’s solution fills the gap in the utilization of formative evaluation. It combines
learning preferences and personality analysis to provide both students and teachers with
insights into learning activities and learning situations, enabling personalized teacher-to-
student feedback. This is also the innovation of this study.
Keywords: Formative evaluation, Personalized feedback, Learning preference, Personal-
ity test, User experience

Glossary
Formative evaluation
Formative assessments are used to provide feedback
to instructors so that they can enhance their edu-
cation. Formative evaluations can be used at any
point during the educational process to assess the
value and impact of instructional methods, as well
as to provide feedback on teaching strengths and
weaknesses.
Personalized feedback
Personalized feedback differs from Differentiation in-
struction in that it responds to students’ individual
interests and requirements in the form of feedback
on a draft. When an instructor refers to a student
by name, background, personal characteristics or at-
tributes, and/or hobbies, this is known as personal-
ization. This information is included in comments
on an assignment, a discussion, or class material by
an instructor.
Learning preference
Learning preferences refer to a range of theories that
aim to account for differences in individuals’ learn-
ing. Many theories share the proposition that hu-
mans can be classified according to their ’style’ of
learning, but differ in how the proposed styles should
be defined, categorized and assessed.
A common
concept is that individuals differ in how they learn.

1.1
Background
In higher education, evaluation (assessment) of educational activities is normally done by
a survey after all of the activities have been completed; or by tutors in the classroom do-
ing an overall questionnaire. Although these approaches, which include the evaluation of
educational quality and educational methods, can get students’ feedback on the course to
some extent, it is frequently hard to identify the true reason for some negative feedback.
Lack of participation, contradicting assertions, and ambiguous judgments based only on
recalls might lead to a lack of authenticity and accuracy in course evaluation systems[4][5].
At the same time, variances in learning preferences and learning methods across students
will reduce the validity of the course evaluating system[6][7].
Typically, there are two types of assessments: summative assessment and formative as-
sessment. Compare to summative assessment(e.g. final exam or survey in the end of
semester), formative assessment is also very important[8]. Formative assessment is re-
lated to feedback. In formative assessment, assessment tools are used in teachers’ forma-
tive evaluation to provide actionable feedback, then students can learn through this in-
formed feedback[9]. When Tutors are performing education, they must provide more than
just insight into outcomes; but also provide functional (i.e., performance-related) feedback
that illuminates changes in practice, predicts professional learning needs, and monitor the
learner’s progress during the course. Thus, assessment tools can be considered as learn-
ing tools designed to enhance insight into current and desired levels of performance and,
based on this, provide possible or alternative learning pathways[10]. The learning-oriented
formative assessment must be distinguished from a summative one, sometimes externally
initiated or mandated, the assessment that records and evaluates work performance in

1.2
Users’ pain points
Based on the above introduction, the object (core users) of this study is identified as
teachers and students in higher education activities.
From the perspective of tutors, they encounter a number of challenges, despite their
desire to improve their own teaching skills through student‘s feedback. The first is that the
teachers do not receive feedback from students after they sent out surveys, and the second
2
==PAGE:15==
is that it is often difficult for teachers to analyze the effectiveness of teaching activities
based on student feedback (for example, the real reason behind negative feedback from
students). Last but not the least, teachers desire an effective and intuitive interface to keep
track of how learning activities are progressing and to provide instructions to students
timely[16][17].
Patterned questionnaires, on the other hand, usually fail to achieve the desired outcome
from students. It is difficult for the tutor to acquire useful information from these statistics
if the content of the questionnaire has not changed in several years. Simultaneously, it is
difficult to gather comprehensive information on students just through summative surveys,
because some students might have lost interest in continuing course-related work, or forget
their experience when they finished all courses[18].

2.1
Formative evaluation
The purpose of formative assessment is to follow student progress and provide constant
feedback that helps students understand their strengths and shortcomings, as well as ar-
eas where they need to improve. It also assists teachers and staff in identifying where
students are having difficulty and resolving difficulties as quickly as possible[19].
Karee E. Dunn & Sean W. Mulvenon pointed out the difference between formative and
summative evaluation in their study. They believe that formative evaluations are bet-
ter at helping students make progress in learning, especially for those who are under-
performing[20]. As shown as figure 2.1 The data presented in Black and William’s (1998)
review[21], as well as the other research all showed that formative assessment has a posi-
tive impact on student achievement. It does, however, strengthen the case for conducting
research that uses more efficient procedures and designs to produce more decisive re-
sults and a better understanding of the impact of formative assessment and evaluation
on student progress. Basically, dis-aggregating student accomplishment data, filling out

2.2
Personalization
In formative evaluation, Personalization, i.e. personalized study, is particularly impor-
tant. Because one of the purposes of implementing formative evaluation is to give feedback
and help suitable for different educator’s own conditions.
Personalized study is a learning method that tries to tailor the learning experience to the
individual’s specific requirements, goals, and skills. It can be accomplished by utilizing
current instructional technology that gives unique learning experiences in a variety of
learning environments. Even though technology is available to tailor the learning experi-
ence, there is still a lack of united consensus on what components should be included for
a dynamic personalized learning approach that can provide each student with a unique
and successful learning experience[27].
Consider the online platform "Netflix" as an example. In order to help its users find
content that suits them, Netflix has undertaken extensive personalization attempts in a
variety of areas, including suggested videos and their ranks, videos sorted by row and
page ways, and much more. Personalization begins with the homepage but extends inside
and outside of their product as well. For example, they can choose what information
to provide Netflix subscribers as personalized feedback to keep them informed and inter-
ested[28].
In terms of academic research on personalized feedback, Sun et al.(2019)[29] performed a
real-time analysis of learning activities to motivate students who were reading on-task to
keep going and to caution those who were reading off-task to pay attention to the text. To
encourage disappointed students to keep working on the tasks, Rajendran et al.(2019)[30]
provided them personalized feedback messages. In a series of geography exercises, Jones
and Castellano (2018)[31] coupled domain feedback to raise task scores with input to raise
student self-regulated learning.
Overall, it can be seen that the prospect of personalized learning is very broad. In order
to achieve the goals of personalized study, the following requirements need to be met[32]:
• Using learner profiles. Educational institutions must keep current student records
and have a thorough awareness of each student’s unique skills, needs, motivations,
progress, and objectives. These profiles require more frequent updates than stan-
dard reporting records. They also assist teachers in making decisions that benefit
8
==PAGE:21==
students’ learning. Students can use learner profiles to keep track of their progress.
It allows teachers, students, in many schools to assess if students need to adjust
their learning strategy or goals before they fail or under-perform.
• Using personalized learning routes. Each user can create their own learning
route, responding to or altering it based on their progress, motivation, and objec-
tives. For example, a tutor may establish a student’s schedule based on weekly
updates on academic achievement and hobbies, or the schedule may be developed
by the student.
Each student’s timetable is different. It may, however, comprise a variety of learning
modalities. It can be a small group of peers, independent work on specific abilities
or challenging tasks, and one-on-one tutoring with a teacher are examples of this
combination.
• Using flexible learning environments.Tutors must adapt the student’s learn-
ing environment to his or her preferred learning style. For instance, the physical
layout of the classroom, the school day’s schedule, and the distribution of learning
resources.
On the other hands, tutors should alter the way of space, time, and resources are
used in the classroom if students demand more time for group teaching. Students
can use design thinking to modify the learning environment.
2.3
Learning styles and preferences
To perform a better formative assessment, we need to provide personalized feedback based
on students’ diverse learning styles, help students with unique learning difficulties, and
point them in the correct direction. Various models can be used to categorize learning
styles: We can use many models to classify them.
2.3.1
VARK model
The VARK model[2] was introduced by Neil Fleming in 2006[33]. This model classify
different students to four categories: Visual; Auditory; Read and Write; Kinesthetic.
Dr. Walter Leite of the University of Florida’s Research and Evaluation Methodology
program verified the VARK model[34]. Visual learners (V) learn by looking at images,
graphics, and movies with a lot of detail [7]. Auditory learners (A) start paying attention
9
==PAGE:22==
to the words spoken by teachers[35]. They prefer to listen rather than take comprehen-
sive lecture notes; they enjoy debates and seminars, as well as listening to professor mp3
recordings. [36] Read and write learners (R) receive information through reading written
texts. Lecture notes, handouts, and textbooks are among their favorites. They’re also
good note-takers[35]. However, Hands-on experience, practical application, model use,
and real-life experience are preferred by kinesthetic learners (K). They prefer hands-on
learning and prefer to combine touch, movement, and engagement into their classroom[35].
2.3.2
The Kolb learning style inventory
David A. Kolb proposes his experiential learning style theory by a four-stage learning
cycle: Concrete Experience; Reflective Observation of the New Experience; Abstract
Conceptualization; Active Experimentation[37]. Learners either have a new experience or
have a version of an old experience in the first stage. This prompts the learner to consider
the next step in the process. The learner’s unique understanding of this experience is
completely dependent on it. The learner experiences abstract conceptualizations based on
this understanding, in which new concepts are produced or existing ideas are transformed.
Everything learned in the first three levels is suggested in the final stage. Learners put
these new skills to the test in real life, and the results lead to new cycles.

Witta and Lee’s variables
Witta and Lee used factor analysis and internal consistency reliability tests to identify 10
variables or elements that show learners’ preferences in the online environment in order
to achieve personalized goals. The factors or elements are Sociability (face-to-face other
students), Student Organization (set-schedule study), Authority Dependence (need in-
structor feedback), Avoidance (Class boring), Communication (good written communica-
tion), Reading (prefer reading), Concrete (like concrete examples), Recognition (Teacher
recognize work), Action (participate in course), and Instructor Organization (need clear
instructions)[38].
2.3.4
Abell, Cain and Lee’s model
Self-motivation, self-management, instructor feedback, social contact, reading visual text,
reading visual graphic, hearing, and technology were used by Abell, Cain, and Lee[15]
to create a learning preferences profile. A customised feedback was provided to a single
student based on the generated scales for their A’ scores in the eight factors.
2.3.5
MBTI model
The Myers-Briggs Type Indicator(MBTI) personality inventory was created to make C.
G. Jung’s theory of psychological types intelligible and relevant in people’s lives[3][39].
The idea states that much of what appears to be random variance in behavior is actually
quite regular and consistent, owing to fundamental variations in how people prefer to
use their sense and judgment. With MBTI, we can provide teachers with insights into
learning preferences and learning situations in terms of students’ personality preferences.
2.3.6
Big 5 personality test
In research of psychology, the big five personality traits are the most widely used model
of personality. It is the basic study in college course of personality psychology. The "Big
five" is based on a statistical analysis of responses to personality questionnaires. Psychol-
ogists can use a technique called factor analysis to look at people’s responses to hundreds
of personality measures and ask, "What is the best way to characterize an individual?"
This has been done with many samples around the world, and the basic conclusion is that,
although there appear to be an infinite number of personality variables, five stand out
in terms of explaining a lot of a person’s answers to personality questions: extraversion,
11
==PAGE:24==
neuroticism, agreeableness, conscientiousness, and openness to experience. The "Big five"
isn’t linked to a single test; instead, a range of tests have been devised to assess it. In
1992, Goldberg improved this test by using The Big-Five Factor Markers from his Inter-
national Personality Item Pool[40].
After a thorough analysis of numerous models, this study adopted the VARK model as
the main learning preference classification approach, with MBTI providing supplemental
information. The reasons for choosing these two methods are: VARK and MBTI’s indi-
cations are more intuitive, making it easier for users to form an initial impression; they
can provide significant support for the future development of personalized feedback.
2.4
Learning experience
It can be seen in the entry explanation provided by "edglossary.org[41]", the learning
experience provided to learners by formative assessment and summative assessment is not
the same. For learners, formative assessment is more suitable for the current learning
trend, it can effectively help teachers provide personalized feedback and help to learners.
According to the learning styles and preferences of different students, it can introduce the
concept of LXP(Learning experience platform).
LXP was developed from LMS(learning management system). Simply explained, it’s a
platform that allows users to choose from a variety of tailored or recommended learning
experiences. LXP was established to solve the flaws of the LMS, which is its predecessor.
Prescriptive "push" learning was common in older LMSs. This indicates that the system is
designed with learning administrators in mind. LMS, in particular, assists administrators
in creating mandated learning pathways for their students to follow. As a result, LMS
is suitable for compliance-focused training. It has, however, contributed to a widespread
distaste of the online learning experience. After all, it is not a good experience to have
users’ actions dictated to the platform.
LXP has three primary features[42]:
• User-Centric Experience: Anything that makes the learning process go more
smoothly is referred to be a user-centric experience. LXP focuses on the learner’s
comfort. Incorporating online games, social learning, customisation, gamification,
and adding narrative to formal learning could improve the experience. All of these
factors contribute to optimal user engagement.
• Personalised Recommendations: Many LXPs, including Netflix and Amazon
12
==PAGE:25==
Prime Video, can contextualize material via artificial intelligence. This AI is fed
into a recommendation engine that aids learners in their path. The engine does not
inform learners that they must complete the content; rather, it informs them that
it is likely to be interesting.
• Social Interaction: Generally speaking, LXPs are good at facilitating informal
learning experiences. In fact, according to the 70:20:10 learning model, social learn-
ing accounts for 20% of all learning.[43] Users enjoy the full social experience on an
LXP. This usually comprises by: Collaborating via social media news feeds; The
chance to speak with experts; Taking part in customized discussion groups; and A
live chat feature for real-time participation and comments.
Compared to LXP, the E-learning platform[44] is more like a complete system integrating
learning experience and formative assessment. It integrated a group of interactive services
that give information, tools, and resources to teachers and students within a certain scope
to assist the management and delivery of teaching and learning activities. Therefor, for
this study, the E-learning platform is a worthwhile research object. In order to better
carry on the comparative narration, this thesis will discuss in detail in the Chapter 4.
2.5
Key takeaways
From the literature review above, it is clear that personalized educational feedback can
give users a better teach-and-learn experience and is more suitable for the formative as-
sessment.
In terms of the many learning experience platforms on the current market, the purposes
for which they provide services are mainly oriented toward students, and the information
and data provided to teachers is typically insufficient. To put it another way, it’s difficult
for teachers to identify the true reasons for students’ evaluations from the data obtained,
and it’s also difficult for teachers to provide personalized learning feedback to assist stu-
dents make progress. In other word, most of the current methods are good for enriching
students’ learning experience and simplifying the process of formative assessment, but
they do not give both students and teachers an insight into learning status.
For example, according to the VARK model, a student who gives a low grade in a course
is more likely to have learning preferences that are incompatible with the teacher’s teach-
ing style. Satisfaction surveys are sometimes ineffective in obtaining this information.
Therefore, it is important to conduct a survey of their learning preferences or personali-
ties, before students participate in educational activities. And it also help tutors provide
personalized teaching feedback to students.

Desk research
Desk research is a method to help me gather information and collect data[50]. It was
done online, mainly for related articles, blogs and websites, etc.
Desk research also named secondary research. Usually, there are two types of research
activity: primary research (discover stuff yourself); and secondary research (review what
other people have done) [50].
Desk research was conducted on the topic before conducting face-to-face interviews. The
reason for this is that personalized learning is a new topic for me, and before my inter-
viewing with users, it is necessary to conduct some research on the concept, which will
allow me to come up questions with more in-depth and precise customer pain points.
Furthermore, desk research can assist me in determining the scope of users.
On the other hand, after completing empathize interviews with users, I’ll conduct new
desk research using the keywords I’ve obtained. As a result, the desk research and em-
pathize interview are not done in any particular order. Both methods are used at the
same time and simultaneously exchange supplementary data.
The selected key words were: "personalized feedback"; "formative assessment/e-
valuation"; "learning style" and "learning preference", they were researched via

3.3.1
Point-of-view madlib
To turn the data I gathered from earlier interview and survey into relevant problem
statements, I used the "point-of-view madlib" (POV madlib) method[53].
Point of View (POV) Madlib is a problem-reframing approach that helps an individual
or group to transform a specific problem into a manageable problem statement. Madlibs
are phrase pattern word games in which one player asks for a list of words to fill in blanks
in a story. A madlib format, in this case, provides a foundation for developing the POV
by defining three elements: a user, a need, and an insight into that user’s POV.
This method uses a structured approach to describe the right challenges, allowing for
goal-oriented solution development that meets the needs of the users.
In summary, a POV statement consists of three components: User; Need; Insight, as
shown in figure 3.5.
User refers to a specific user, here I defined it as students and
teachers. Secondly, Need refers to what users need and what they are concerned about.
The last component is Insight, which refers to how to synthesize the gathered information
into a key takeaway. The "insight" statement can be used to design a solution.
After I defined the users as teachers and students in formative evaluation, I tried to
find out the problem statement based on the POV statement. I made a priority list of
these statements according to the degree of concern to users.
The core needs took a
higher priority. With this method, I can define the motivation of each user’s need and
distinguish the difference in needs between the two types of users.

MoSCoW
In the initial stage of prototyping, the MoScow[57] approach was used in this study, based
on the list of features obtained in the previous stage. In the initial stage of prototyping,
the MoScow approach was used in this study, based on the list of features obtained in
the previous stage. This method can prioritize the functions obtained from different user
perspectives. This approach also helps clarify the functional distribution of the prototype
during the design process.
MoSCoW contains four components:
1. Must have: features that are necessary for the application to function.
2. Should have: features that are essential to the application’s success.
3. Could have: features that are good to have but not necessary.
4. Won’t have: features that will not be implemented but may be useful in the future.
By interviewing different users and translating their needs into a feature list, and using
MoSCow, two feature priority lists for both users can be obtained. When developing the
prototype, this study will develop high-priority functions first according to the obtained
priority list, which helps to complete an MVP and put it into testing within the specified
time.

Methods in Test stage
The main task of this stage is to use the high-fidelity prototypes obtained in the previous

Two test methods were used in this study: Think-aloud test and Constructive inter-

3.6.1
Think-aloud test
Think-aloud methods involve participants to think aloud while carrying out a set of tasks.
As they finish the assignment, participants are invited to say whatever comes to mind.
This could include things like what they’re looking at, thinking about, doing, and feeling.
This allows observers to see the participant’s mental processes (rather than just their final
result), allowing them to be as explicit as possible during test completion[67].
At the end of the Think Aloud test, some open-ended questions will be asked to the tester.
For example: What do you think is the most attractive thing about the prototype and
why? What features do you think need to be added/removed and why? Testers are free to
express their opinions, while all feedback is recorded on audio or notes for further study.
The thinking aloud test was conducted in six rounds, all in the form of face-to-face in-
terviews. Among the six testers, one is a master student and two are Ph.d in Computer
science at DTU, two are software developers, and one is a DTU teacher. The reason for
choosing these testers is that teachers and students, as the core user group, can give the
most intuitive feelings and insights. And software developers can give their insights from
the perspective of technical feasibility. During the test, the testers were first informed of
the main purpose and background of this study, and then the observers first demonstrated
the main functions and operation of the prototype. Finally, the testers interact directly
with the prototype on Figma through the computer.
3.6.2
Constructive interaction
The constructive interaction approach is used with two individuals to avoid the problems
of usability testing that come with the think-aloud test. The two individuals are given a
29
==PAGE:42==
task to do together, which generally results in disagreements over what to do next and
how to do it, as well as explanations of why they did what they did to each other. Because
this kind of contact is more natural than the think-aloud test, and because interaction
between the researcher and the tested individuals is limited, it usually give a higher
significance[60].
In this method, subjects are selected as two groups: one was two DTU computer science
masters students, and one was two software developers. The reasons for selecting subjects
and the testing procedures are mostly the same as for think-aloud. The difference is that
the subjects are not told in detail the steps to use the product, but need to discuss and
discover for themselves how to complete the test. Likewise, at the end of the test, subjects
are given open-ended questions, and all feedback is recorded with audio or notes.

Users in formative evaluation
• Tutor
Typically, at the end of a course, the tutor will send a questionnaire to the students in
the hopes of eliciting the students’ opinions and evaluations of the course in order
to improve the teaching quality and level. As a result, evaluation and feedback
systems are widely used in educational activities.
The educational institution’s

Taking DTU as an example, students’ satisfaction with the course; what students

goals in the course; workload and curriculum settings, and so on are all covered in

• Students
When students finish a course and are given a course evaluation survey, the survey
usually takes 5-10 minutes to complete. However, based on my interviews, course
evaluations are frequently done at the end of a semester, and many students have
forgotten a lot of what they experienced in this course. After completing the course,
some students have lost interest in continuing course-related work, making it im-

• User Relationships under the New Situation
The COVID-19 epidemic has had a significant influence on the education sector. In
2020, the traditional school model was turned on it’s head, and millions of children,
parents, and teachers had their first encounter with home learning. In most schools
and districts, this crisis has shown the strengths and shortcomings in our readiness

Students no longer need to wait for teachers to evaluate worksheets or essays in
order to demonstrate their understanding of a concept. Rather, individuals can
monitor their development in real time from any location because it is accurately
tracked through the use of technology. This strategy will continue to push learning
beyond the pages of a book, the four walls of a classroom[68].

4.2.1.2
Users’ needs in formative evaluation
Despite the great benefits of formative assessment for teaching activities, there are a
number of challenges, or to put it another way, a number of user’s needs in formative
assessment, that deserve our attention[69]:
1. Tutors need a quick and simple tool to simplify the process of conducting assess-
ments, monitor student learning status easily, and gain intuitive insights into learn-
ing. Because while conducting formative evaluation, tutors would have a lot of work
before and after course, for example, editing and preparing preview course ware, de-
signing questions in assessment, give feedback to students and so on. Although the
digital teaching method greatly shortens the time for teachers to do these processes,
some processes that need to be analyzed have caused the burden of the teacher’s
work. (e.g. finding out the reason why students feel bored in the class or why
students give negative feedback)
2. Although formative assessment can assist teachers in obtaining an accurate informa-
tion on student learning, students require a simplistic, easy-to-complete assessment
system since formative testing requires more research and interaction than summa-
tive assessment. It will assist students in increasing their enthusiasm for taking the
evaluation and in better understanding their own learning status. On the other
hand, it requires students’ change from passive to active learners. It also has sig-
nificant needs for self-control, learning capacity, learning desire, and pheromone
cultivation from the perspective of learners. Formative evaluation does not relieve
students of their responsibilities; rather, it is easy for students to get fearful of
challenges and even tired of studying.
3. Formative evaluation is a systematic process that involves the information literacy
of tutors and learners, as well as the ability to informatization teaching and learning,
in order to shape and construct the teaching system and some business processes.
It also includes hardware and software teaching platforms, resources, learning and
teaching management, instructor evaluation, and the ability of students to learn
independently. As a result, a platform that can meet the needs of users as well as
improvements in formative evaluation is necessary.
4. Usability is an important aspect in higher education. The convenience with which
a user can use a product is directly related to the product’s market and advertis-
ing. As a result, through formative evaluation, I intend to provide users with a
platform that is clear, straightforward, and accessible at any time and from any
location. For example, students can quickly complete the assessment using their
33
==PAGE:46==
mobile phones within 5-10 minutes in their commute. Teachers can also learn more
about evaluation in a shorter time period.

Formative evaluation, as indicated by current research, can engage tutors and students in

4.2.1.4
Challenges in formative evaluation/assessments
Lee Crockett [25] mentioned 5 common problems with formative assessments in his blog.
These 5 problems are:
• Teachers don’t see a need for it. Differentiation requires formative assessment.
We can never be certain that all students will be on the same page. Therefore, a
suitable formative assessment system can change the teachers’ perception effectively.
• Formative assessment is too time-consuming. With a suitable formative as-
sessment system, we can greatly reduce the time to conduct formative assessments
and data maintenance, as well as data collation costs. This question shows the value
and market potential of the formative assessment system from the other side.
• Teachers see formative assessment as a graded component. Different from
summative assessment, formative assessment focuses on giving students differenti-
ated and personalized feedback and help in the process of learning according to the
situation of students at different stages. How to simplify and automate the process
of formative assessment has become the main goal of creating this system.
34
==PAGE:47==
• Teachers feel they lack guidance on how to use it effectively and effi-
ciently.Lee Crockett mentioned in his blog that to solve this problem, teachers can
adopt three different paradigms: individual, partner, group. Individual refers to the
personal information that the teacher knows enough about the students, including
learning preferences and personal characteristics. Partner refers to organizing stu-
dents who need help with other students who are proficient in the course to become
learning partners, because "Students who are taught self-evaluation skills are more
likely to persist on difficult tasks, be more confident about their ability , and take
greater responsibility for their work.”(Rolheiser and Ross 2001) [70]
• Formative assessment involves too much data tracking. This is also a pain
point in the current use of formative assessments. We know that formative assess-
ments are based on more data than summative assessments to give students more
appropriate feedback. For some users who do not use third-party evaluation sys-
tems, they need to organize and classify the evaluation result data by themselves.
This greatly increases the cost of formative assessment for users. Whether the data
can be organized to give a clear insight is also an important criterion for measuring
whether a formative assessment system is helpful.

4.4.2.2
Learning experience
Learning experience including two aspects:traditional academic settings (schools, class-
rooms) and nontraditional settings(outdoor education). Therefore, there are two different
interactions: traditional ones(students learning from teachers and professors) and nontra-
ditional ones(students learning through online platform and interactive software apps).
In the context of modern education, new technologies have substantially increased and
diversified the methods in which students can learn from and engage with educators, as
well as their level of learning independence. Students now communicate with tutors via
email, chat, or online meetings, and they also use online course management systems to
organize and share learning materials which given by tutors. In other words, students
can learn on their own time, at their own speed, and without the supervision of tutors
by using software programs, apps or online platforms[41]. Under such a general trend, a
better environment for formative assessment and personalized evaluation is given.
The starting point of my design of this system is not only to give feedback to teachers
and students, but also to help students in learning activities. Therefore, it becomes nec-
essary to study the learning experience here. Through research on learning experience,
in order to attract learners to participate in formative assessment actively and make the
system enter a virtuous circle of teacher-student communication, I have determined that
the platform designed this time should follow three principles:
1. The system can give tutors insight of students’ context.
2. Tutors can respond to the learner’s based on their existing level of knowledge.
3. Tutors provide adequate and appropriate feedback over time.
The first and second points above are achieved by creating student profiles, and the third
point is the need to design a suitable feedback communication function, which will be
summarized at the end of this chapter.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
This chapter outlines the goal of this study and the answer to the research question in
section 1.4 based on Chapter 3;Chapter 4. Here it evaluated the thesis’s contribution to
formative evaluation and personalized learning. At the same time, it analyzed the limi-
tations and offered recommendations and objectives for further research in this chapter.
In summary, this study examines the feasibility of building up a personalized feedback
application and presents a way to make course evaluation and self-reflection more effec-
tive. The study also includes a proof-of-concept prototype that depicts the process of
designing the application. The concept of this study is providing an effective platform for
implementing formative assessment and personalized feedback.
This study comprehensively analyzes the current state of formative evaluation and user
needs, and proposes a reasonable solution for formative evaluation implementation that
meets the needs of core users. On the other hand, this study presents a clickable prototype
showing showing the user flow and user-prototype interaction.
5.1
Summary of the problem
Formative evaluation is becoming increasingly important in today’s teaching activities. In
order to help teachers better understand students’ learning in formative evaluation, and to
give teachers and students more information to understand their learning preferences, so as
to provide a personalized learning plan suitable for students, it is very necessary to design
a solution which can show the students’ learning situation and their own characteristics,
and to provide a way for teachers and students to communicate. At the same time, in
formative evaluation, whether teachers can obtain learning insights based on students’
own characteristics is also an important factor.
76
==PAGE:89==
Based on extensive desk research and user interviews, this study proposes and improved
designs one mobile application that is suitable for use in a Danish context in a university
scenario. Because the user scope is set in the university campus and does not involve
factors such as the commercial market, user needs in the corporate context, etc., this study
may lack consideration and investigation of commercial factors that affect the research
direction.
5.2
Contributions of this study
According to desk research, whether in the commercial market or in school education ac-
tivities, formative evaluation is receiving more and more attention, which is also a change
in learning activities under the new situation. The research shows that there are few
software or tools that are commonly used on college campuses specifically for formative
evaluation. At present, in the university context, summative assessment is more often
used. Sometimes teachers conduct surveys on students through oral, Google form, etc.
These solutions aren’t well-suited to users’ formative evaluation needs, and they don’t
provide teachers with personalized information.
This study’s mobile solution fills the gap in the utilization of formative evaluation. Fur-
thermore, it incorporates features such as personalized analysis and feedback, teacher-
student communication, and much more, allowing users to complete all formative evalua-
tion tasks on the same platform rather than switching between applications. This solution
allows users to create targeted learning programs, whether they are students or teachers.
The study’s user profile, which is generated by merging learning preferences and per-
sonality analysis models, is also an innovation that distinguishes apart from most other
learning software/platforms.
5.3
Proposed solution
Through desk research and user interviews, this study uses the Point of View method to
summarize the core needs of users, and tries to brainstorm solutions to users’ pain points
through the HMW method. The scheme is sorted through the Affinity Diagram, and the
overall direction of the study solution is obtained after that. Based on this direction,
one high-fidelity prototypes was designed in this study, and a series of user tests were
conducted using the prototypes.
The solution proposed in this study designed the entire flow for users to complete for-
mative evaluation and personalized feedback through the APP. The solution is aimed at
student users and teacher users respectively. The user scenarios are determined in uni-
77
==PAGE:90==
versity learning activities, and the main test scope is within the DTU school.
Throughout the design thinking process, this study investigated the pain points of stu-
dents and teachers, and compared the assessment methods commonly used today and
the acceptance of formative evaluation by different users. According to user feedback,
users are generally optimistic about formative evaluation, and their main concerns are:
whether they can get feedback timely; and whether it will cause additional workload due
to formative evaluation. Therefore, the design direction of this research mainly focuses
on these two issues.
The mobile application designed and improved in this study requires users to log in with
an account in the same domain, for example, in the same domain of a university. At the
same time, the anonymity of the user is maintained, it means only the back-end knows
the exact identity of the user, and the users in the front end communicate through a cus-
tomized name, which ensures the comfort of the user during the communication process.
Student users need to take the learning preference test and the MBTI personality test
the first time logging in and it can be done multiple times. Through the test results,
both students and teachers can understand whether they are compatible with each other
in teaching activities, and then make improvements to a certain extent. On the other
hand, users can also see their past learning/teaching situations through the app. It will
give users insights based on learning preferences and personality characteristics based on
the results of formative assessments which is a good function for users to understand the
learning situation.
At the same time, the application designed in this study integrates features which meet
users’ need, such as survey templates; one-click publishing of surveys; comment-reply
function; real-time chat function; and publishing and browsing of course materials, etc.
Considering that different users have different needs for same features, users can also
choose to block some features that are not useful to them, it means that the apps pro-
vides: switch on/off message notification, block real-time chat, etc. These features meet
the user’s needs and also provide a high degree of freedom.

5.5
Limitations
This study was limited in the following aspects:
• The scope of this study is limited to DTU, and the scope is selected for the conve-
nience of conducting research and interviews. Therefore, this study lacks the survey
and analysis of user samples outside the scope of DTU. This may lead to a change
in research direction.
• This study did not collect a large number of user samples.
Except for face-to-
face interviews, the data for this study were mainly obtained through published
questionnaires. Based on the results obtained, most of the questionnaires sent out
have not been answered. This is due to the instability (such as holidays or final
exams) of the publishing platforms and user groups, most users don’t have time to
finish it so they choose to ignore the questionnaires.
80
==PAGE:93==
• The investigation and testing of teacher users in this study are not sufficient. Only
four teachers provided feedback during the study, and the remaining teachers who
were contacted did not respond to emails inviting them to participate.
• This study does not consider commercial factors and the competitiveness of existing
product markets. This is because these factors are, to some extent, outside the scope
of this study, and the addition of factors like market competitiveness will further
confuse the issue and blur user needs.
• Only using learning preference and personality analysis as the basis for evaluating
students’ learning situation is not comprehensive, and the actual situation is often
affected by many factors.
• According to interviews with developers conducted throughout the testing phase,
some features of the APP designed in this study (such as real-time chat) are tech-
nically difficult to implement. This could have implications for the study’s design
in the future.
5.6
Future works
From the results of the previous section, the future work of in this study can start from
the following aspects:
• In addition to the desktop study, this study was discussed with experts and re-
searchers in formative assessment to further determine the usability of this design
in the future.
• In this study, many users provided fresh ideas about user scenarios during interviews.
Teachers and students are the stakeholders of educational evaluation in university
education, and their connection is not particularly close. The reason for this is that
educational evaluation is not mandatory, and evaluation does not provide significant
benefits in any case, so educational evaluation participants’ excitement for partici-
pating in evaluation activities is low.
On the contrary, customers are typically more eager to provide feedback on this
consumption behavior in some commercial consumption circumstances because they
have made a purchase. For example, in some sports clubs’ teaching activities or in
some organizations’ training operations.
This study could attempt to extend user scenarios and apply formative evaluation
and personalized feedback to a wider range of industries.
81
==PAGE:94==
• Limited by the scope of users of this study, the sample size of this study is not
satisfactory. In the future, this research will broaden the scope of user surveys and
collect additional user data in order to improve the design scheme.
• As reference analysis tools for formative assessment and personalized feedback,
VARK and MBTI can help teachers to build students’ profiles to a certain extent,
but students’ individual situations are often dynamic and cannot be fully embod-
