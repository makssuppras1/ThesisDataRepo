Abstract
Offshore structures for wind energy are a growing industry, due to the increasing demand of sustain-
able energy solutions, because of global warming. In this relation, residual stresses are of interest
to investigate. The fatigue life at welded connections of the structures is of special concern, due to
the cyclic loads offshore. Residual stresses from welding are assumed to be at the magnitude of the
yield strength. If sufﬁcient knowledge of residual stresses is obtained, then the costs for offshore
structures, such as monopiles, could be reduced in terms of less material consumption. However,
this is if the residual stresses are less than assumed.
This thesis deals with welding of can sections (hollow sections) as used for monopiles on a smaller
scale (diameter: 1200 mm, thickness: 5mm). The steel grade i S355. Residual stresses are measured
and evaluated near the weld, to see the link between welding and stresses.
The hollow sections are welded with multiple welds by use of submerged arc welding. First, two
specimens in the longitudinal direction. Afterwards, multiple welding in the circumferential direc-
tion of two other hollow sections is performed.
Following that, the residual stresses in the surface are investigated by use of the Hole Drilling
Method. The stresses are evaluated and compared to the yield strength of the hollow sections,
which is measured from experimental tensile strength tests.
The longitudinal welding with resulted in a joint with 29 welds and another with 19. The circumfer-
ential welding needed 17 welds. The stresses are investigated for the hollow section with 19 welds
and the at the specimens with circumferential welding.
The yield strength of the hollow sections has an experimental mean value of 450.3 MPa. For the
longitudinal welding, residual stresses are measured with a maximum of 97& of the yield strength i
the longitudinal direction, and 95% in the transverse direction. For the circumferential direction the
maximum longitudinal stress measured is 92%, and the maximum transverse stress is 83%.
Due to limitations of Hole Drilling Methods, the measured stresses are only reliable to a maximum
of 80% of the yield strength. Hence, the maximum magnitudes of the stresses cannot be concluded
for sure. Nevertheless, they are concluded to be in the order of magnitude of the yield strength.

Chapter 1
Introduction
During the last decades the demand for sustainable energy solutions have increased. One of the
solutions, especially in Denmark, is wind energy. Denmark is one of the world leaders in this
industry. Due to the increasing demand for sustainable energy, it is important to have knowledge
of theses kinds of structures. The research conducted in this thesis will mainly be in relation to
monopiles, which is one of the most common used substructures for wind turbine structures.
The design criteria for monopiles and other offshore structures is often the fatigue life in the founda-
tion structure. Residual stresses induced from welding are important in the fatigue life assessment.
However, knowledge of residual stresses in relation to magnitude and distribution is limited, and it
is still on a research level. Now the residual stresses are in guidelines (Eurocode 3, DNV etc.) as-
sumed to be up to the magnitude of the yield strength for welded structures. If sufﬁcient knowledge
of the residual stresses is obtained, the design guidelines might be optimized on a long term basis.
If it is proven that residual stresses lie under the yield strength, material savings can be earned. This
is beneﬁcial from a ﬁnancial perspective, but even more for the perspective of the still increasing
over-spending of materials world wide.
As contribution to the research of residual stresses, the present work is conducted. Experiments
are carried out in relation to welding of circular hollow sections and investigations of the residual
stresses created from this process. The welding process is submerged arc welding (SAW) and the
residual stresses are measured and evaluated by the hole drilling method. The specimens to be
welded could be representative as a part of a monopile on a scaled-down basis. Hence, the research
conducted is mostly in relation to monopiles.
To create a starting point, some background knowledge of monopiles and offshore substructures is
brieﬂy presented. After that an introduction to the general design and development of the design
of monopiles is presented. Hereafter, the objective of this thesis and the scope of work will be
introduced.

waves, wind and rotation of the wind mill itself. The cyclic loads are in combination with residual
stresses able to create high tensile stress concentrations, which cause crack ignition. Therefore,
investigation of welding and residual stresses is important for fatigue life assessment. The term
fatigue from a structural point of view is elaborated in chapter 2.
1.2
Objective
The overall objective is to gain knowledge about residual stresses from welding. Earlier projects
have considered submerged arc welded ﬂat plates with varying plate thicknesses. The residual
stresses have been examined by numerical simulations and experimental measurements. This project
considers bigger specimens with circular shape (can thickness of 50 mm and diameter of 1200 mm.),
in order to simulate welding of a monopile as realistic as possible. It is interesting to investigate
whether there is a difference in the development of residual stresses, when the test specimens have
a design similar to the reality.
Some of the secondary objectives are listed below. It is desired to:
• Obtain knowledge of submerged arc welding and how the welding process affects develop-
ment of residual stresses.
• See if there is a difference between longitudinal welding and circumferential welding of cir-
cular hollow section.
• Investigate the magnitude of the residual stresses with respect to the yield strength of the
material.
On long term basis the objective is to optimize the fatigue design of offshore structures, which are
strongly depending on knowledge of residual stresses.

Scope of work
The scope of work conducted in this thesis consist of two main ﬁelds; a theoretical study of the
problem and experimental work. The thesis is structured on basis of that, starting with the theoretical

The theoretical background begins with an elaboration of the fatigue mechanism and the relation

After that residual stresses are deﬁned, and the development of residual stresses induced by the

Following the theoretical background, the experimental investigations are presented. Initially, pre-
liminary tests with the SAW device on a 70 mm plate were performed. Hereafter, welding of the

way around. This is chosen because it feels more natural to investigate the yield strength of the

to determine the yield strength. However, other parameters are investigated as well.

mental execution follows. This includes installation, drilling and calculation. As a result of that the
stresses calculated for each hole, are presented in the form graphs.

Chapter 2
The Fatigue Mechanism
Fatigue is one of the main concerns in the design process of offshore steel structures. This is due to
the large cyclic loads from wind, currents and waves at open water. In general, fatigue failure must
be considered for all types of metal structures, however welded structures at sea are one of the major
concerns.
In this chapter the fatigue mechanism will be elaborated. Firstly, a general deﬁnition of fatigue
and fatigue crack propagation is presented, followed by a description of the relation to welding
and residual stresses and the major concerns from this. The fatigue guidelines are in accordance to
Eurocode 3: Design of steel structures - Part 1-9: Fatigue [3].
2.1
Deﬁnition of Fatigue
Fatigue is a failure mode occurring in cyclic loaded structures. The vulnerable places in relation
to fatigue damage are often construction details from machining, welding or connections. At these
locations, cracks are likely to appear. The changing stress state from the cyclic load will cause the
cracks. One of the special things of fatigue life, is the fact that a construction detail can be designed
with a much higher ultimate limit strength than other places at the structure. However it may still
be critical in relation to fatigue. Hence, engineers have to be focused and aware of the danger of
fatigue, and take the design requirements serious. Fatigue life is often described as the number of
cycles required to initiate crack propagation until failure [47].
The opening of the cracks can be divided into three different modes, see ﬁgure 2.1. Mode I shows
the crack opening mode forced by tension stresses. Mode II and Mode III shows the opening mode
for shear stresses in-plane and out of plane, respectively. However, Mode II and Mode III are only
present for small cracks, and even if cyclic shear stresses are present, the crack initiation will quickly
follow mode 1 after a short while [47].

macro crack instead, and the crack is now in the crack growth phase [47].

Chapter 3
Welding Theory
In this chapter welding theory will be presented. Submerged arc welding is the method used in
this thesis. Therefore, SAW will be described in detail in relation to the whole process and how
different welding parameters inﬂuence the ﬁnal result of the welds, created from this SAW process.
Furthermore, a little theory of thick plate welding and welding defects will be elaborated.
3.1
Submerged Arc Welding
Submerged Arc Welding is a method widely used for offshore structures. SAW is part of fusion
welding and goes under the subcategory of Arc Welding. Arc welding is one of the most common
types of welding processes, because it implies a medium heat input and a medium power density of
heat source compared to other welding types, see ﬁgure 3.1.
Figure 3.1: Power density as function of heat input with indication of welding type and damage assessment
[48].
In SAW, an electric current ﬂow conducted through an electrode ﬁller wire is used. The resistance
of the ﬁller wire, the arc gap and the base metal will cause heating, and a local area of the base metal
will melt [29].
13
==PAGE:38==
3.1. Submerged Arc Welding
When the metal is melting the merging process of the elements happens. The "submerged" part of
the name is due to the presence of the granular ﬂux covering or shielding the electric arc and weld.
The process beneﬁts from the ﬂux, since the ﬂux e.g decreases the effect from the surrounding air
in addition to heat loss or spatter. The ﬂux is likewise able to prevent light radiation, and the arc is
easier to stabilize when it is submerged [48].
The setup of SAW and the processes present during welding are depicted in ﬁgure 3.2.
Figure 3.2: a) The SAW process and the different components used in the process b) Closer look on SAW
near the weld [48].
The process starts by covering the electrodes with ﬂux. The ﬂux is constantly feed from the ﬂux
hopper. It is of high importance, that the ﬂux is added in front of the electrodes in relation to the
welding direction. Otherwise, the arc is visible. Turning on the welding device will make a circuit
between the electrode and the base metal, and a kind of short circuit will happen and create the arc.
The heat from the arc melts the base metal. Also, some of the ﬂux will melt. Metal droplets from
the electrode will fall into the metal pool due to heat. After a short while, the weld pool will solidify
and create the weld metal. The melted ﬂux will create a slag layer on top of the weld metal, which
often will lose itself from the weld during cooling. If not, the slag is removed with a hammer or
similar. Flux which did not solidify is recycled.

3.1.1.1
Electrode polarity
Welding with direct current (DC) is often divided into Direct Current Electrode Positive (DCEP) and
Direct Current Electrode Negative (DCEN) polarity. DCEP polarity will often give the most optimal
surface appearance, bead shape and resistance to polarity. While DCEN polarity often will cause
fast burn off and decrease the penetration depth. This is because the maximum heat is developed
in the electrode tip instead of the plate surface. In relation to ﬂux and wire consumption it is often
DCEN polarity, which has the lowest consumption.[30]
Alternating current (AC) is rarely seen as the only polarity in SAW. However, it is very often seen as
part of multiple wire systems together with DCEP or DCEN. In relation to the arc voltage, DCEN
requires the highest voltage and DCEP requires the lowest. AC has a requirement of voltage between
DCEN and DCEP. DCEP is the most common type of direct current used in SAW.[48][30].
In all welding settings, it is always crucial to compare the different possibilities described. On ﬁgure
3.3 the weld bead shapes for DCEP, AC and DCEN are compared.
Figure 3.3: Different polarities and corresponding bead shape [51].
Using DCEP polarity will lead to a deep weld pool and a relative slender surface. DCEN will be the
exact opposite, with a low penetration but a wide surface. AC will be somewhere in between.[30]

to cover electrodes for SAW.[30]
The electrode diameter varies depending on the situation and the work pieces. However, for SAW
the wire diameter usually lies in the order of 2.0-6.0mm. In most cases the AC wire(s) are thinner

3.1.1.5
Electrode extension (stick-out)
The electrode extension is governing for the amount of resistance heating in the electrode. A small
extension will lead to relatively small heat effect and a deep penetration. A long extension will on the
other hand lead to a large heat effect and small penetration depths. This is because the heat effect is
increased by increasing the extension, and the penetration is decreased by increasing the extension.
Furthermore, the deposition rate is increased with the extension increasing. It is recommended to
use a stick-out of 25-30mm for "normal welding"[30].

Cracks in the weld material is the most dangerous defect, and they are never acceptable. Cracks are
caused by three different phenomena; segregation induced cracking, bead shape induced cracking
and surface proﬁle induced cracking. However, they all induce the same types of cracking. Usually,
it is bead shape cracking (center line cracking) which is interesting for SAW. This type of cracking
will occur, if the melted material on each side of the arc do not solidify together [39]. Cracks can
also be present due to impurities. The same is present for porosity [30].
There are two common causes of porosity. If the joint is contaminated with oil, paint, grease or
similar. These products will decompose and create gasses when in contact with the arc. Another
thing causing porosity is wet ﬂux. If that is the case, the steam from the wet ﬂux can act as porosity
[30]. In relation to ISO 5817 [5], which is the European standard for quality levels for imperfection
in weld, porosity is at a small level acceptable. Hence, it is not as dangerous as cracks in small
quantities. This is general for other defects as well.
Undercutting is generally caused by incorrect welding parameters. The most affecting parameters
are the travel speed and the arc voltage. Increase of the travel speed will increase the risk of un-
dercutting, and also the size of undercutting. On the other hand, too low arc voltage can lead to
undercutting as well. Often, undercutting can be taken care of, when welding with multiple welds,
because a new weld can be placed on top of the one with undercutting (assuming burn through at
the undercut position).
Lack of fusion can also be seen in welding. It occurs when there is not sufﬁcient fusion between the
weld metal and the base metal. For single pass welding, it is often due to poor welding technique.
Lack of fusion for multipass welding is seen when one bead is just too narrow to cover the hole
width of the groove. Then a split welding technique should be used (welds next to each other). The
same is present if two welds are too narrow, then three welds must be made etc.[17].
From the SAW process one of the most common errors is slag inclusions. Sometimes the slag
is difﬁcult to remove. If not everything is removed then some of the ﬂux or slag will cause slag
inclusions, if it is not destroyed from the heat.

Chapter 4
Residual Stresses
Residual stresses are important in relation to welding and fatigue. They often develop during the
welding process, and sometimes they can be beyond the yield strength even before the structure is
subjected to any load. In the following chapter a general description of residual stresses is presented,
and after that the subject welding residual stresses are explained.
4.1
Deﬁnition of Residual Stresses
Residual stresses are deﬁned as the stresses existing in a body, if all external load is removed.
This is described as "locked-in" or sometimes internal stresses [45][48]. An important feature of
the residual stresses is the self-equilibrating feature. This means that the total sum of tensile and
compressive stresses results in zero force and moment over the total volume of the body. To get a
better understanding of residual stresses, ﬁgure 4.1 is useful. It shows a diagram of the cross section
of a sheet of toughened glass, and it is seen that residual stresses can occur without external load.
Figure 4.1: Different stress distributions of cross section of toughened glass for non-loaded, externally
loaded, and residual stress situation respectively [45].
Residual stresses occur in almost every manufacturing processes, and typical mechanisms creating
residual stresses in the manufacturing process could be [45][10]:
27
==PAGE:52==
4.1. Deﬁnition of Residual Stresses
• Non-uniform plastic deformation: This typically comes from a manufacturing process, where
the shape of the material is changed. Examples of that are forging, rolling, bending, drawing
and extrusion.
• Surface modiﬁcation: Usually it comes from the manufacturing process also. Examples are
machining, grinding, plating, peening and carburizing
• Material phase and/or density changes. Comes from manufacturing processes as welding,
casting, quenching or similar processes.
On the other hand, residual stresses could however, also occur in service. Surface deformations can
cause non-uniform plastic deformations. Surface modiﬁcations will often be caused by oxidation or
corrosion. Lastly, material phase and density changes can be caused by e.g. nuclear radiation. All
this are some of the most common processes causing residual stresses in different materials.
By use of a length scale residual stresses can be categorized, because they are equalized over the
length scale. The categorization of residual stresses could be explained as three types of residual
stresses, as is done by Schajer et al. in [45]. Or it could be done as the distinction made by Radaj
presented in [21]. In the following the distinction by Radaj is presented.
Residual stresses are usually considered in three different orders, σ1, σ2 and σ3. The ﬁrst order
residual stresses are described as macrostresses. They are present for distances which extends over
1 mm. Macrostresses usually explains the residual stresses from loading strain, stress, temperature
and displacements. Second order residual stresses are described as microscopic stresses, and they
are in the order of magnitude between distances of 1 mm to 0.01 mm (10−3 −10−5 m). This ﬁeld
is between each grain and adjacent grains. Inﬂuencing factors on the residual stresses are grain
morphology, rate of grain/crystal growth and nucleation rate.
The third order residual stresses are described as microstresses as well. However, this case concerns
stresses on atomic scale, which leads to an averaged distance of 10−5 −10−9m. Dependent ef-
fects and variables are e.g. stresses which corresponds to imperfections or dislocations inside the
crystals. In ﬁgure 4.2 the categorization and range of the residual stresses can be seen. Figure 4.3 de-
picts an illustrative division of macrostresses and microstresses and what happens from the different
processes.

4.2. Welding residual stresses
4.2
Welding residual stresses
As aforementioned, residual stresses are a direct cause from the welding process in metals. During
arc welding the materials near the arc will be exposed to high temperatures. The heat is so strong,
that a molten region will be created, and this region wants to expand. However, the molten region is
restraint to expand, because of the presence of cold adjacent base material. The is applicable when
the weld is cooling, and thereby wants to contract.
A material heated in the elastic region only will go back to its initial state, when is is cooled down.
However, during welding is it difﬁcult to keep the temperature on the same level at. Hence, some
of the material will go into the plastic region because of non-uniform temperature changes. When
a material has reached the plastic region it can never go back to its initial state. Usually, residual
stresses from welding are assumed to be in the magnitude of the yield strength of the given material
welded. However, it can also be much less, or much higher, and residual stresses will develop no
matter if the material is in the elastic or plastic region.
A way to explain the thermal processes and development of residual stresses caused by welding is
the "Three-Bar Arrangement" presented in [48]. The method is based on heating the middle bar with
non-uniform temperature changes, and no presence of plastic deformations. Which means that after
heating and cooling, it is not present that the middle bar has been subjected to heating. And that is
exactly the danger of residual stresses.
The three-bar arrangement considers both heating and cooling under constraint. As the name implies
three bars are considered, and they are all identical. The bars are considered in three different phases,
see ﬁgure 4.1. The three bars are connected with two rigid blocks preventing longitudinal movement.
The three phases consist of a) initial conditions with room temperature, b) heating of the middle bare,
c) cooling of the middle bar.
Figure 4.1: Three stages of the thermal induced stresses in the three-bar arrangement. Sketch self-made but
inspired from [48],[53] and [11].
.
During heating of the middle bar, it wants to expand, but the cold side bars restrains it to do that. Due
to that, compressive stresses will develop as function of the heat in the middle bar. The compressive
stresses will increase with increasing temperature until the yield strength is reached. The side bars
will be tensioned to balance equilibrium, which means each value in side bar corresponds to half of

==PAGE:55==

the absolute value of the compression in the middle bar.
Reaching the yield strength in the middle bar, the bar is leaved to cool down. During cool down the
compressive stresses will decrease, and eventually turn in to tensile stresses, due to the restraining
of thermal contraction caused by the side bars. When the middle bar is cooled down to its initial
temperature, the tensile stresses will be equal to the yield strength. The side bars will this time be
compressed, and it is yet again half the value of the middle bare. [48]
Comparing the three-bar arrangement to welding, it is observed that the distribution of stresses is
similar, this can be observed on by comparison of ﬁgure 4.1 and ﬁgure 4.2, where the side bars after

is still molten, and it has not obtained any strength. Compressive stresses are developed in the heat
affected zone, because of the colder surrounding material, which prevents the zone to expand. Small
tensile stresses are developed just outside the heat affected zone in order to balance equilibrium.
Section C represents a location which has been welded, but it is still hot and the temperature needs
to cool down to room temperature. During cooling the weld will try to shrink. Hence, tensile
stresses will develop near the weld, but compression stresses will be present in the heat affected
zone, because the temperature is high. Some tensile stresses will develop in the coldest areas to

The welding parameters does also have a huge effect on development on residual stresses. As stated,
heating and cooling inﬂuence the residual stresses. However, other parameters do also inﬂuence the
heat added. This is present from the heat input, which is calculated from the electric current, voltage
and travel speed, see (3.1.2). From and experiment conducted by Amin et. al. [8] the effect from

is seen, that the test with the lowest voltage and current, but with high travel speed induced low
residual stresses. On the other hand the experiment with high voltage and current but low travel
speed induced the highest residual stresses. Thus, the higher heat input the higher residual stresses.
Hence it is important to chose the welding parameters carefully in relation to development of residual
stresses.
Other factors inﬂuencing residual stresses are different material properties. The elasticity modulus,
E, yield strength, fy or the thermal expansion rate, α. Since the parameters are temperature de-
pendent, they will affect the development of residual stresses when the temperature is increased of
decreased.

5.2.1
Temperature measurement and drilling of holes
Due to the thermal processes during welding it is important to measure the temperature near the
weld bead. First of all, it is used to identify if there are any errors. A signiﬁcantly high temperature
compared to others is often an indication of an error. Furthermore, the temperature measurements are
of high importance in relation to development of residual stresses (the more heat the bigger residual
stresses). The temperature is also necessary to establish numerical simulation of the welding process.
Thermocouples of type-k are used to measure the temperature, see ﬁgure 5.2. They have to be
inserted into holes drilled in the specimen.
Figure 5.2: Thermocouple of type-k used for temperature measurements

located in t/2, in this case 25 mm deep holes. Because of the size and round shape of the specimen,
it does not ﬁt-in into the same drilling tools as ﬂat plates. From experts it was told, that for drilling of
a hole in structural steel with a drill of 1.5 mm, a device with around 10.000 revolutions per minute
should be used. A device with that kind of power, cannot handle the size of the specimens. However,
with a lot of carefulness, the holes were drilled with a radial drill device with only 1500 revolutions
per minute, see ﬁgure 5.3 and 5.4. Regarding the carefulness in the drilling process, it is important
to use a sufﬁcient amount of lubricant and to cool the drill, so it want get too hot. Otherwise, the

First of all, the hollow section welded needed to be prepared for the circumferential welding. This

some problems occurred in order to construct a proper connection between section 1 and section 2.
Welding of hollows sections in the circumferential direction is subjected to some requirements.
Firstly, the longitudinal weld grooves at each section have to be separated. From recommendations
from technical staff at DTU, the grooves must at least be separated with a distance 5 times the width
of the weld groove, or 45°. Unfortunately, the longitudinal welding process caused deformation of
the hollow sections. Thereby, the sections were not perfectly circular anymore. Several approaches
and attempts to combine the two section have been tried. One technique did show good appearance
in the beginning. By local heating at some points on the sections and pressure from a hydraulic pres-
sure device, the maximum misalignment decreased from 24 mm to 12 mm, see approach in ﬁgure
5.1. Nonetheless, the magnitude of the hollow sections was too big to handle, with the equipment
available at DTU at the moment. Furthermore, it would cause serious time consumption to force
back the original shape. If the equipment on the other hand was available, the stresses in the sec-
tions might be rearranged due to heating and pressure. This would lead to unreliable results anyway.
At this point it was impossible to make a good connection between the section 1 and section 2.

Chapter 7
The Hole Drilling Method & Process
The hole drilling method is used widely for measurements of residual stresses. From the study in
[10] it was concluded that hole drilling is the method which requires less practical knowledge and
experience, but also in general the easiest method to use, compared to Deep Hole Drilling, The
Contour Method and the Neutron Diffraction. Furthermore, is the cheapest method and it is less
destructive than the others, which also requires special laboratory facilities.
7.1
Basis of the Hole Drilling Method
The Hole Drilling method is known as an accurate and reliable method for measuring residual
stresses. The method is applied by drilling one or more small holes in the workpiece. It is im-
portant to drill the hole in the area, where residual stresses are to be evaluated. As the material is
already stressed, a redistribution of the residual stresses will occur, because of removal of material
by drilling. Because of the redistribution of the stresses, some localized deformations around the
hole will be formed. The edge of the hole will expand. This is caused by the stress release from the
drilled hole, see ﬁgure 7.1.
Figure 7.1: Principal of hole drilling and deformation caused by stress redistribution [46].
The local surface around the hole will rise due to Poisson strain. These deformations are measured
with strain gauges or optical methods. Hence, residual stresses present in the original conﬁguration
(before drilling of hole) can be determined from the measured deformations. This is done by using
calibration coefﬁcients [46] [10].
67
==PAGE:92==
7.2. Strain gauges
Hole drilling can measure in-plane residual stresses to a depth a little less of the radius of the drilled
hole. These stresses are known as "near surface" residual stresses. For a nominal diameter of 2 mm,
which is often used, the penetration depth is around 1.4 mm [46].
7.2
Strain gauges
As aforementioned, strain gauges are used to measure the local surface deformations. Strain gauges
used for this purpose are often a part of a hole drilling rosette. This contains usually three radial
strain gauges [46]. On ﬁgure 7.1 some standardized strain gauge rosettes are shown.
Figure 7.1: Standardized hole-drilling strain gauge rosettes [46].
Type A is the most general design of strain gauges, and it is suitable for a lot of different cases. The
internal angle between the gauges is 120 degrees, which makes it able to measure deformation all
around the hole. The design was developed by Rendler and Gigness in the 1960’s.
Type B is a little different, since the gauges are located on the same side of the hole. The conﬁgura-
tion is usually used, when the measurement is located near obstacles, for example close to a ﬂange,
wall or a weld. However, it should only be used, when the conditions are as described, because of
the location of the gauges at the same side. This can make the measurement sensitive to eccentricity
errors around the hole.
The last type, Type C, is accurate for measurement in materials with low residual stresses and low
thermal conductivity. This makes it unsuitable for steel, and thereby there will be no further expla-
nation.
It is obviously of high importance to choose the most suitable strain gauges for a given project.
The location of the residual stress measurement area is near the weld groove, and thereby type B
might be most suitable. However, type A, might be better for some measurements far away from the
welds. Nevertheless, type B is chosen, since some of the holes are near the weld groove, and it is
more accurate to have the same kind of gauges measuring on one specimen.
The strain gauges chosen are manufactured from HBM and they are of type 1.5/120R RY61 or type
1.5/120R RY61-3L-0.5M. They can be seen on ﬁgure 7.3 and ﬁgure 7.2. It is the same type of
strain gauge; the only difference is that 1.5/120R RY61-3L-0.5M is equipped with 0.5 m of wire to
connect to the measurement machine. Type 1.5/120R RY61 must be equipped with wire manually
by soldering.

7.3.4
Converting strains to stresses
The strains measured is converted to stresses by the Eval 7.14 software. However, there are several
different calculation methods available. The most common used and recommended methods accord-
ing to SINT Technology, are the HDM method and the Standard ASTM E837-13 method for both
uniform and non-uniform stress ﬁelds [49][7]. The other methods available are deselected because
of e.g. high sensitivity to errors, design for speciﬁc cases etc. At this point however, calculation by
the uniform part of the ASTM E837-13 can be discarded too. The Eval 7.14 software has the ability
to check uniformity, and none of the holes can be assumed to have a uniform stress ﬁeld. This has
also been the conclusion in similar test performed at DTU [9][7][31]. The two remaining calculation
methods are brieﬂy explained in the following. For further elaboration of the methods see "EVAL
Back Calculation Software Manual" by SINT Technology [49].
7.3.4.1
Standard ASTM E837-13: Non-uniform
This methods is based on the assumption that the stresses vary with distance from the surface and
into the specimen. Usually, the method is used for calculation of the stresses in a hole. Numerical
coefﬁcients are established by the ASTM E837-13 for the calculation. The method is able to calcu-
late the stresses in a depth of 1.0 mm, and this has to be done by use of a strain gauge with strain
diameter of app. 5.13 mm. The calculation is performed in 20 steps, which gives a stress result for
each 0.05 mm. The method has a validity of a maximum of 80% of the yield strength for thick plates
[49].
7.3.4.2
HDM Method
The HDM method is based on the Integral Method which is also accessible in the software. The
Integral Method and HDM is used for residual stresses which vary signiﬁcantly through the depth,
or at least when expected. The Integral Method is very sensitive to test errors, and therefore the
HDM method was developed. HDM can apply eccentricity corrections, caused for instance from
the end mill. The method should be applied for hole depths of app. 0.2 times the strain gauges
diameter or below. Which for the strain gauges used in this project is 5.13 mm, and the calculation
depth is thereby app. 1.00 mm as for the Standard ASTM E837-13: non-uniform method.
This method does also have a recommended validity of 80% of the yield strength for thick plates
[23], since it is evaluated by recommendations from the Standard ASTM also.

Chapter 8
Discussion
In this chapter the results obtained from the experimental investigations will be discussed. The
results will be compared with theory and some with results from other investigations. Furthermore,
different processes during the experimental investigations are discussed.
8.1
Submerged Arc Welding
The submerged arc welding process is an important topic in this thesis. It is the welding process
which creates residual stresses, and several features can have an effect, as elaborated in Chapter 3.
Furthermore, it is also of high importance that the welds are installed at the right location and with
the right parameters. This is both in relation to fatigue and in respect to the overall strength of the
welded connection.
One of the major concerns regarding submerged arc welding is the fact, that the it is impossible to
see the weld during the welding process. In the longitudinal welding process, one weld was made
at the time. These welds were approximately 500 mm. Due to the massive heat, it is impossible to
remove the slag before the whole length of the weld is made. Thus, it is exciting to see the result,
when the slag is removed. Especially, in relation to fusion between base metal and weld metal it
would be great to see if there is a proper fusion in the beginning, and if not, the process can be
stopped. It is believed that some of the errors occurring in the longitudinal welding could have been
avoided if the weld was visible.
For the circumferential process it was easier to see since the slag was removed as a combination of
gravity and man power from using a slag hammer.
Usage of a PQR is a great help during SAW. Nevertheless, this welding process has shown that it is
necessary to have exact knowledge about welding parameters. Particularly, because the welding pa-
rameters are changed throughout the process. Meaning that the bottom welds have "low" parameters
with respect to power, the weld in between has "high" and the ﬁnal welds has again a little lower.
Several times during the process, it is also necessary to evaluate the welding parameters from the
previous weld with respect to the next weld. Thus, a PQR can be followed, but it is only a guideline.
83
==PAGE:108==
8.1. Submerged Arc Welding
If mistakes happen, it is crucial to know how to correct the problem from for example adjustment of
welding parameters.
It is considered that training and preliminary tests are extremely important features. Of course, it is
obvious that welding requires some basic training. However, the welding device used in this thesis
is semi-automatic, and the usage of it might be considered as "easy" before start. This is not the
case.
Longitudinal welding requires less training than circumferential welding. The longitudinal part de-
mands that the welder can adjust the welding device correctly before welding. After that, the process
is more or less automatic. To obtain knowledge of the longitudinal process, it is recommended to
conduct a study with equal parts of theoretical studies and practical studies. This means that it is
recommended to control the welding theory by small welding experiments.
Circumferential welding requires extended training, because the welder must control the location of
the weld by a joy-stick. Thus, it is not as automatic as circumferential welding. However, another
factor is suspected to have caused problems during the process of circumferential of the hollow
sections.
The factor is regarding the placement of roller beds and placement of the hollow sections on top of
these. Handling this type of large equipment is sometimes difﬁcult, e.g. each hollow section has a
weight of app. 500 kg.
Figure 8.1: Roller beds with a set of hollow sections on top.
Placement of the roller beds take some time due to size and weight. Even though controls measure-
ments are made in relation to correct and parallel installation, there might be a small error, which is
in the order of magnitude of 0-10 mm. Furthermore, placement of the hollow sections on the roller

Also, a comment in relation to the temperature measured, during the welding process, must be made.
The maximum values measured are for section 1: 710°C , section 2: 440°C and for circumferential

assessed that measurement no. 5 for specimen 2 is not entirely reliable for the middle of the thick-
ness. Hence, establishment of a numerical model in ABAQUS, ANSYS or similar, should be done
carefully with respect to the temperature, and maybe some of the data measured with no. 5 should
be neglected.
The deformation of the hollow sections from longitudinal welding was not expected. Even though
the specimens are large and circular, the welding process will cause deformation. The hollow sec-
tions have to be restrained. Otherwise, they are difﬁcult to connect. Restraining might lead to
increase of residual stresses, however it is the only way to make an acceptable circumferential con-
nection. Studies for that should be conducted as further investigations.
It is believed that welding of hollow sections with dimensions of real scale magnitude will not have
the same problem. These elements are much bigger, and the welding might not be able to deform it
as much. Thus, it is assessed to be a problem for low-scale test. However, it has not been studied in
detail, and no ﬁnal conclusion of this can be conducted.

specimens in the longitudinal direction can be conducted faster than in the circumferential direction.
This is due to the length of the welds. Thereby, the welds in the longitudinal welding process can
be made relatively quick after each other, of course without exceeding the inter pass temperature.
This means that a random point in the longitudinal weld groove will experience a higher tempera-
ture during the process, than a random point in the circumferential direction. Simply, because the
longitudinal process is faster than the circumferential. A random point in the circumference weld
groove, will cool more, before is it subjected to heat again. Considering the temperature curves,
it is also seen that the average temperature is higher for welding of section 2 (ﬁgure 5.7) than the

residual stresses would be appropriate, or at least an assessment of the stresses above 80% in the top.
For another assessment, methods such as x-ray diffraction, contour method or neutron diffraction

Chapter 9
Conclusion
In this chapter conclusions regarding the investigations conducted in this thesis are presented. The
conclusion is stated on the basis of the results and the discussion.
9.1
Submerged Arc Welding
Circular hollow sections with a thickness of 50 mm has been welded in the longitudinal and circum-
ferential direction using submerged arc welding. Section 1 required 19 welds, section 2 required 27
and the circumferential process required 17 welds. Hence, the number of welds will increase with
the size of the weld groove, if the welding parameters are in the same order of magnitude.
Specimens of this size are able to deform. This was a result of the longitudinal welding process.
Artiﬁcial restriction is necessary to keep the specimens in place during welding. Unfortunately, this
might increase the stresses, which are already of a high magnitude.
Regarding the temperature during welding, there is a tendency which shows that the mean tempera-
ture during welding is higher for longitudinal welding of hollow sections than for the circumferential
welding. For the longitudinal welding of section 1 and section 2 the mean temperature is app. 125°C
for both. The mean temperature for the circumferential welding is app. 80°C during welding. This is
despite that the heat input is the same. The peak temperatures are in general higher for longitudinal
welding too.
9.2
Tensile Tests
Tensile strength test has been performed for the steel of the specimens and for the weld metal pro-
duced in the circumferential welding process. The yield strength for the steel was measured to 450.3
MPa, and the ultimate strength and E-modulus was 541.7 MPa and 186.9 GPa, respectively. For
the weld material the yields strength was 507.5 MPa, the ultimate strength was 556.5 MPa and the
E-modulus 206.0 GPa. It seems that the weld metal is stronger than the steel. However, there is a
lack of data for the weld metal, since one specimen was destroyed.

9.3. Hole Drilling & Residual Stresses
9.3
Hole Drilling & Residual Stresses
The residual stresses have been evaluated for the specimen corresponding to "section 2" and for the
specimens welded in the circumferential direction.
The difference between the calculation methods HDM and Standard ASTM E837-13: non-uniform
has been investigated. No signiﬁcant difference between the methods have been noticed. Some of
the disagreements are discussed, and the biggest difference is related to the transverse stresses in the
circumferential direction. Furthermore, some tendencies do also show, that for increasing distance
away from the weld edge the difference between the methods does also increase. Nevertheless,
none of the methods is preferred above the other, since they show the same tendencies and stress
distributions for all measurements. Both methods are recommended to use for similar investigations.
The maximum longitudinal stresses are in the same order of magnitude for both welding directions,
with size of 94-97% for longitudinal welding and 91-92 % for circumferential welding with respect
to the yield strength. From this investigation, it could be concluded that change in specimen shape
and welding direction will have less effect on longitudinal residual stresses than on the transverse.
Despite what is given in the theory, the longitudinal stresses and the transverse stresses (magnitude
of 94-95% yield strength) are in the same order of magnitude for the longitudinal welding process,
especially close to the weld. The reason for that is believed to be because of the circular shape,
which makes at difﬁcult to deform in the transverse direction. Hence, transverse stresses will be
bigger due to the restriction from the shape itself. This is concluded, since the transverse stresses
from the circumferential welding are lower (maximum of 70-83 %), and they lie along a horizontal
part of the specimens.
In general, there is a tendency which indicates that the longitudinal welding process induced higher
stresses then the circumferential process, especially regarding high tensile stresses. However, this
might also be due to the lower number of welds for circumferential welding or the lower temperature,
since higher temperature often induces higher stresses. To make a ﬁnal conclusion on this, similar
investigations must be conducted. Still the tendency is interesting.
The calculated stresses with magnitudes above 80% of the yield strength should be evaluated care-
fully. For the calculation methods used, the reliance of the stresses is limited to 80%. Above that
they are considered as less reliable. Thereby, some of the measurements have to be rejected since
they are above 80%. It could only be concluded that stresses are present above 80% of the yield
strength, and to be conservative they might be concluded to be in the magnitude of the yield strength.
However, the tendencies are still trustful, since the measurement methods predicts nearly the same
results. Furthermore, the conclusions made above are still believed to be correct, for example that
the longitudinal stresses are higher for longitudinal welding than for circumferential welding. But it
is accepted that the values for each measurement might not be the exact correct values at that speciﬁc
point.
As a result from these measurement the hole drilling procedure is assessed to be a good way of
evaluation of residual stresses. However, the limitation of the method for only reliable results up

to 80% of the yield strength is a problem. The stresses from 80-100% are of high importance,
and other methods such as x-ray diffraction, contour method or neutron diffraction might be used
to make a better assessment of the residual stresses. Establishment of a numerical model is also
possible for assessment of the residual stresses. Still the hole drilling method is an excellent tool, to
give indications of residual stresses, and it is a good method to use as a starting point for preliminary
investigations. Establishment of a numerical model is also possible for a deeper assessment of the
residual stresses.
Finally, the investigations do not show the exact same tendencies in relation to thickness effect of
residual stresses as concluded by Sørensen and Ali [7]. The transverse stresses do ﬁt into the con-

it would be appropriate to investigate more circular specimens with varying plate thickness.

Chapter 10
Further Work
In relation to the investigations conducted in this thesis, plenty of further investigation could be
executed. Some of the most important, from the author’s perspective, are given below.
First of all, the number of measurements with the hole drilling device is low. More measurements
with the hole drilling device on the welded specimens should be conducted near to already measured
areas. The residual stresses at hollow section 1 should also be measured. It could also be interesting
to measure the stresses with another measurement method, and then compare the methods.
After that a numerical model should be established. A numerical model could give in-depth knowl-
edge of the residual stresses, and it would be possible to see the contribution from each weld. If
more time was available this would have been the next step in this thesis.
Fatigue tests of the weld should also be performed. This would also give an idea of the quality of
the weld. Comparison of experimentally found fatigue strength and a calculation example using
Eurocode 3 is also interesting to see the differences or similarities between them. This could all be
related to residual stresses.
Other test such as Vickers hardness test or fracture toughness test is also appropriate. A comparison
of the steel materiel and weld material should be made.
For similar experimental investigations it is recommended to develop equipment to avoid deforma-
tion of the cans during welding. This could be a kind of rings avoiding deformation. However, they
must have a high strength compared to the can section.
When a solid knowledge and an almost full assessment of the residual stresses in the specimens
handled in this thesis, is obtained, other specimens should be tested. This should ﬁrst be done for
varying plates thickness. Followed by that, the can diameter could also be varied. Implementation of
other welding processes could also be applied, to see if they induce a lower limit of residual stresses.
