Abstract
There has been a constant rise in waste production in the last decades with seri-
ous environmental and social impact following that. The projections estimate that
the numbers will grow further the following years, making the problem of waste
management a crucial challenge. Not only waste impacts negatively the environ-
ment and the societies but there is great value lost in the materials that end up in
landfills. Solid urban waste management is one of the most important parts of the
waste management sector since it is directly connected with the quality of the life in
urban areas, where most of the people live nowadays. A more effective sustainable
treatment of solid waste from urban areas can have great environmental benefits for
the societies, as well as, crucial financial effects from the utilisation of the value that
is lost now. In order to achieve a more effective and sustainable system, it is evident
that new and innovative solutions have to be implemented in the sector and gov-
ernments and institutions can help with the proper regulations and policies in order
to optimise the transition. A substantial part of literature refers on the effects of
regulations on innovation but it has been identified a knowledge gap for the effects
of regulations on innovation for the solid urban waste management sector. With
this thesis, we seek to understand the interconnection of these three fields, regula-
tion, innovation and solid urban waste management. To achieve this, a thorough
literature review on the existing knowledge was conclude and a series of interviews
were held to relevant stakeholders in Danish and European Union ecosystems. As a
result, a series of regulation mechanisms that affect innovation in the sector of solid
urban waste management have been identified and how entrepreneurs utilise them.
A qualitative analysis was performed, which made possible to highlight key findings
in the interview data. By combining the findings with relevant theory from the lit-
erature review, new recommendations and explanation on the relationship between
these 3 fields are presented that contribute to fill in the knowledge gap that was
identified.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Nowadays, society is characterised by extreme consumption habits that lead to ex-
tensive waste production. Urban waste is an interesting case of waste that seems to
be produced in urban areas and directly impacts human lives. Unfortunately, there
is no indication that the figures will slow down; the estimations approximate 70%
in worldwide urban waste production [48]. With the waste management issues, a
great deal of value is lost in the materials that end up in landfills.
This thesis aims to investigate the sector of solid urban waste management
(SUWM) and how regulations of the European Union (EU) affect processes of in-
novation. It seems that the sector needs to change at fast pace in order to deal
with the latest environmental issues that are connected with it. The involvement of
entrepreneurial activities as the main representative of innovation in business can
be a catalyst towards this aim.
Therefore, this thesis will investigate the existing knowledge of the connection
between innovation and regulation and will apply the result to the SUWM sector.
The target is to connect the dots between regulation, innovation and the SUWM
sector in order to add to the existing knowledge. This will happen by studying
and analysing existing scientific papers to identify the interrelationships, as well as
interviews with relevant stakeholders will be conducted to better understand the
situation in a practical manner.
1.1
Problem Statement
1.1.1
Contextualise the Problem
Humans produce large amounts of waste every year due to their activities. Waste
is an unavoidable result of life, and it can be detected in nature since every living
1
==PAGE:12==
Chapter 1
organism produces waste as a result of the processes that keep it alive. The natural
waste produced from living organisms follows a circle in which it is used as a useful
source by other organisms, such as animals, plants, or bacteria. Human-produced
waste, especially after the Industrial Revolution, during the 18th century, when the
production processes were industrialised, is produced on large scales and in forms
that could not become useful sources for other organisms or processes of production.
As a result, there is a huge footprint on the planet from the waste humans produce,
and as the world becomes more consumeristic and the living standards of countries
elevate, the amount of waste rises with negative results for the environment and so-
cieties. Appropriate Waste Management processes have become necessary in order
to tackle these problems.
One crucial sector of Waste Management is SUWM or Municipal Waste Man-
agement, which is about the waste that has a solid form and is produced in urban
areas, specifically cities. As the world faces urbanisation and environmental con-
cerns, SUWM has emerged as a crucial issue. Especially in the region of Europe
and, more specifically, in the EU, which is a highly urbanised part of the world, 338
million people are living in urban areas, which translates to 75% of the total EU
population.
In order to treat the increasing amounts of waste produced by the EU every year,
a series of solutions have been proposed and implemented with the goal of providing
more sustainable practices that will have a positive effect on the environment and
the societies that formulate the EU. In order to achieve these sustainable goals, the
EU passed Green Regulations and Policies that try to swift the traditional linear
economic model of produce, use, and throw it away to a circular model where we
produce, use, and reuse. These Green Regulations and Policies aim to revolutionise
the SUWM sector by promoting innovation. A lot of studies have been conducted
through the years that confirm that regulations have the capacity to promote inno-
vation under certain conditions. Furthermore, Entrepreneurship has the potential to
be a fundamental factor in this transformation since it can be a means of innovation
and address the challenges of SUWM sustainably.
Even though there is extensive literature and research on the effectiveness of
regulations on innovation, there is an information gap specifically on how regulation
affects innovation in the context and sector of SUWM and, as a result, the relation-
ship between regulations and policies on entrepreneurship in the sector of SUWM.

Chapter 1
1.1.2
Why it matters
Understanding the relationship between EU Regulations and Entrepreneurship in
SUWM is crucial for several reasons. Firstly, Entrepreneurship can play the role
of the driving force that will accelerate innovation in the sector and help transform
the processes into becoming sustainable with almost no negative impact on the
environment and the countries that will achieve that, with huge benefits for their
people.
Entrepreneurs are willing to take risks and try different approaches to
existing problems by introducing novel technologies, breakthrough business models
and solutions to address solid urban waste challenges effectively.
Secondly, EU
Regulation not only shapes the regulatory environment in which companies can
operate and conduct business but also has the potential to influence market dynamics
and investment decisions, which also work as influential factors in entrepreneurship.
Therefore, a better understanding of how EU Green Regulations and Policies affect
Entrepreneurship can lead to a more effective regulation framework from the EU
that could lead to more effective changes in the sector of SUWM.
1.1.3
Aims and Objectives
The primary aim of this thesis is to investigate the impact of EU Green Regulations
on Entrepreneurship by examining the impact on the promotion of innovation in the
sector of SUWM. More specifically:
• It will present a thorough Literature Review of existing theories and frame-
works of the connection between regulations and innovation.
• Assess the current regulatory environment in the EU in Green Regulations
and Policies with connection to Waste Management and Sustainable Goals.
• Analyse the impact of these regulations on Entrepreneurship.
• Fill the knowledge gap regarding Innovation and Entrepreneurship in the sec-
tor of Solid Urban Waste Management by conducting interviews with relevant
stakeholders.

2.1
Background Information for Waste Manage-
ment
Over 2 billion tonnes of municipal solid waste are generated globally each year, and
this shows no signs of slowing. In the EU, municipal waste generated has increased
by 7,4% from 1995 to 2019, of which approximately 60% was neither reused nor
recycled [80]. Figure 2.1, it is displayed the creation of urban waste in the member
states of the EU. The data captures the trend of waste augmentation from 2004 to
2022.
Figure 2.1: Urban Waste Generated in EU 2004-2022 (kg per capita) [47]
Waste Management is the term used to describe a stream line process used by
organisations to dispose of, reduce, reuse and prevent waste, it includes the collec-
tion, transportation, disposal/recycling and monitoring of waste materials produced
as a result of human activity [45]. SUWM is the term that refers to the section of
the overall waste management, specifically targeting the waste that is produced in
urban areas and has solid consistency. This section is of great interest because the
waste is produced in areas with great population density, which means it has a great
5
==PAGE:16==

social and environmental impact and at the same time, the waste is reusable, since
it is not heavily toxic like the waste production in factory units, and can be treated
through relatively sustainable practices such as incineration, landfill, compost and
recycling. That means that it can also produce added value through these processes
and contribute to the economy of the region.
What is waste, though? Waste is a product or substance which is no longer suited
for its intended use [98]. Whereas in natural ecosystems, waste, such as oxygen (by-
product of the photosynthesis process), carbon dioxide and dead organic matter,
is used as food or reactant, and that way, the waste becomes a useful resource for
another natural process by creating a circle where the waste of the ’first’ process
becomes an item of utilisation for the ’second’ one. Waste material resulting from
human activities is often highly resilient and takes a long time to decompose, and
as a result, it can not be a useful matter for another process [98].
2.1.1
Definition of Solid Urban Waste
The Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) defines
solid urban waste as waste that is collected and treated by, or for municipalities. It
covers waste from households, including bulky waste, similar waste from commerce
and trade, office buildings, institutions and small businesses, yard and garden, street
sweepings, contents of litter containers, and market cleansing. Waste from municipal
sewage networks and treatment, as well as municipal construction and demolition,
is excluded [85].
2.1.2
Examples of Solid Urban Waste Management
The first step of sustainable SUWM is the reduction of waste produced.
That
requires the proper education of the citizens through educational programs that
emphasise the importance of more sustainable practices and the proper design of
products in order to last longer.
The collection process of waste is crucial and presents many challenges and op-
portunities for improvement. The collection process includes the selection of proper
containers, the utilisation of trash truck fleets and gathering points where the waste
is treated. This is an important part of SUWM because it constitutes the first stage
of waste sorting, and if done properly, it potentially adds value to the waste. In
the collection process, the implementation of Artificial Intelligence systems is able
to elevate and optimise parts of it [83]. For example, machine learning algorithms
could aid the sorting process of waste into the proper categories, the optimisation

of the trash truck routes for waste collection and smart waste bins that can predict
when they need to be emptied [83].
The recycling process is a major part of SUWM. It is considered a sustainable
practice that enables materials that consist of waste to be used again. Nowadays, a
great deal of new approaches and practices are introduced that tackle a number of
the challenges that the sector faces. For example, one of the most eminent challenges
is the treatment of the plastics that are used everywhere and in huge quantities, a
new approach to the problem suggests the utilisation of plastic-eating enzymes that
can consume and break down plastic material in the waste that once was considered
non-recyclable [74].
Lastly, the incineration process, waste-to-energy solution, is considered a sus-

of its produced by-products, for example, the fly ash, that is characterised as haz-
ardous and requires special treatment [100]. For example, the Amager Bakke incin-

its efficiency and environmental benefits, which have not been fully delivered due to
political, financial and environmental reasons [46]. Recently, a new waste-to-energy
solution, called gasification, has been introduced that promises to produce energy
from solid urban waste without the negative by-products of the incineration [94].
2.2
Background Information for Regulations
Regulation is considered a rule or directive made and maintained by an authority.
For the purpose of the thesis, EU and Danish Legislation will be investigated and
covered. The importance of a regulation or a policy is that forces a certain behaviour
that otherwise would not be implemented or followed. If done correctly, a regulation
can fix or optimise a certain behaviour for the common good.
A lot of times,
it can be frustrating and make people or groups of people uncomfortable since it
changes the norm by excluding certain levels of freedom. However, the way societies
formulate and organise themselves makes it necessary for policies and regulations
to be implemented in order to promote the common good and stabilise the different
dynamics that every society holds within it. Here, it is important to mention that
there is extensive academic research on the mechanisms of regulation and how it can
be effective and provide the necessary results. One important aspect has to do with
the fact that it is possible to utilise regulation to promote innovation. Even though
that sounds paradoxical, innovation seems to excel in environments of freedom where
people can act independently and without following any rules. More details will be
7
==PAGE:18==

presented later in the thesis on how regulation achieves that goal and, furthermore,
cases where it can be an obstacle to innovation.
A focus will be placed on the
sector of SUWM to investigate how regulation can promote innovation. The waste
management sector is of interest since it seems that regulations and policies are
necessary elements in order to function properly and provide effective management
for society and the environment.
Apart from the catalyst role regulations can have for innovation, they can also
introduce new markets that otherwise wouldn’t emerge. By regulating and, as a
result, formulating the way companies can conduct business, policymakers have
the capability to minimise certain business activities which are considered to be
negative for the common good and navigate companies into new areas of operation.
For example, for many years it was unregulated the process of hazardous waste
treatment. Companies were making decisions based on financial benefits and were
releasing the hazardous by-products into rivers or to the ground with catastrophic
consequences for public health and the environment. When it became evident that
these practices were harmful to humans and the environment, policymakers, all
around the world, with the assistance of organisations and scientists, decided to act
on it. They introduced a series of regulations and policies on how companies that
produce such waste should treat it. As a result, new markets emerged since there
was the need for someone to treat the hazardous waste in a respectful manner and
make sure that it would not negatively affect the environment and the health of
people.
The ability that regulations have to swift human behaviour holds a great power
and it can be used to tackle serious social and environmental problems.
In the
last decades it has become evident that the planet can not sustain the way of life,
the level of consumption and the practices of production that humankind follows.
So, societies, companies and governments work on solutions that could provide an
alternative approach. The way governments and authorities contribute to the so-
lution is by using one of the most effective and powerful tools they possess: the
ability to decide and implement regulations and policies. Apart from the obvious
impact that such practices have on the behaviour of people and companies, they
can become effective with indirect results by promoting innovation on the industries
they try to transform. Also, they can create the necessary circumstances for new
markets to emerge and create the incentives for investors to allocate their resources
to companies and startups that operate to these newly established markets.

2.2.1
Definition and Main Features
As already mentioned regulation is a tool that governments can utilise to enforce
a certain behaviour. In the dictionary is defined as a rule or order issued by an
executive authority or regulatory agency of a government and having the force of
law [76]. The most typical precondition for the use of regulation comes up in cases
where the market forces do not lead to the social optimally result [87]. These cases
are called market failures in economics. Market failure refers to a situation defined
by an inefficient distribution of goods and services in the free market. In an ideally
functioning market, the forces of supply and demand balance each other out, with
a change on one side of the equation leading to a change in price that maintains the
market’s equilibrium. In a market failure, however, this balance is disrupted. When
markets fail, the individual incentives for rational behaviour do not lead to rational
outcomes for the group. In other words, each individual makes the correct decision
for themselves, but those prove to be the wrong decisions for the group as a whole
[97]. Therefore, a social market failure goes beyond pure economic measurements
and can have broad societal consequences. For example, market failure in the health-
care industry might leave a significant portion of the population without access to
essential services, leading to public health crises or increased mortality rates or in
the case of social market failure in the waste management industry is the plastic pol-
lution in which the market produces and sells single-use plastic straws due to each
low cost and durability and the consumers purchase them in big quantities due to
the economic benefits in comparison with alternative and environmentally friendly
straws.
As a result, plastic straws end up in landfills without the proper prior
waste treatment and are not biodegradable, causing catastrophic consequences for
the natural environment, the ocean, wildlife and human health. This social market
failure takes place because the market fails to take into account the environmental
and social costs. Therefore, the government steps in to fix this situation by provid-
ing regulations and policies. Some governments have banned or imposed levies on
single-use plastics. At the same time efforts to improve the levels and the process
of recycling are being made.
Of course, social market failures are not the only cases that governments decide
to regulate. Among many reasons that a regulatory authority, like a government,
decides to intervene, it is important to mention the regulatory failures [87]. For
example, when existing regulations fail to accomplish their goal, intervention is
needed to fix the problem and initiate a different approach.

2.2.3
How EU Policy is Decided
The members of the EU hold the right to legislate at a national level, but it is evident
that a major part of the policies and regulations come from the EU itself. The EU
consists of 3 main institutions in decision-making: the European Parliament, which
represents the EU citizens; the Council of EU, which represents the EU governments;
and the European Commission, which represents the EU’s general interests. EU
legislate in harmony with these three institutions, which means that in order for a
decision to be finalised, they have to come to an agreement.
Based on the official website of the EU [33], the first step towards a new leg-
islation is the Drafting of the EU law. The European Commission is responsible
for conducting thorough research regarding the potential social, environmental and
economic impact of the new legislation and publishes a report in which it is dis-
played the results of the research. The report is called ’impact assessment’ and
sets out the advantages and disadvantages of the policy options. During the pro-
cess of developing the report, the European Commission seeks help from a variety
of institutions that have direct relevance to the subject and can provide expertise
and valuable insights on the matter that is being investigated. Such institutions
or organisations can be non-governmental institutions, national authorities and in-

dustry, as well as expert groups that give advice on technical issues. At the same
time, further stakeholders have the opportunity to be included in the process and
provide knowledge and feedback. Stakeholders such as individuals, businesses and
organisations have access to the development of the report through public consulta-
tions (questionnaires) of the European Commission. Also, national parliaments can
formally express their opinion about the upcoming legislation by expressing their
reservation that the issue could be legislated at a national level rather than at the
European level.
The second and last step is the Review and Adoption.
After the European
Commission concludes the development of the impact assessment, shares the report
with the European Parliament and the Council of EU in order to be reviewed.
Both of them can propose amendments by meeting and discussing over the report

propose further amendments. In the case that both parties agree, the proposal can
be adopted, otherwise a conciliation committee is set up in order to find a solution
by providing an alternative proposal. Both Parliament and the Council have the
right to block the committee’s proposal. Finally, a proposal is adopted into law
when the Parliament and the Council agree on a common text and afterwards, it is
published in the EU’s Official Journal.

are the more important ones.
EU countries implement and Commission supports. Member States have the

At the same time, the Commission acts in a helpful manner and tries to

2.2.5
Types of EU Law
The EU is based on the rule of law [35]. This means that every action taken by
the EU is founded on treaties that have been approved democratically, through its
institutions, by its members. EU law’s purpose is to achieve the EU’s objectives for
the common benefit of its members and help to implement EU policies into practice.
There are two main types of EU law, the primary and the secondary.
The primary law consists of the treaties that embody binding agreements be-
tween the EU and its members that establish the nature of the relationship between
them, set out the EU objectives and the EU rules for institutions and how decisions
are made. These treaties act as the starting point for EU law upon which the rest
will follow. The regulations, directives, decisions, recommendations and opinions
that are structured upon the primary law are called the secondary law.
The EU treaties lay down the objectives of the EU, the rules for EU institutions,
13
==PAGE:24==
Chapter 2
how decisions are made and the relationship between the EU and its member states.
The treaties can be changed from time to time in order to meet the needs of an
ever-changing world. A lot of times, the duties of the EU institutions have to be
renewed, and new areas of responsibility have to be added. Also, a lot of times,
these changes are due to the fact that new member states are joining the EU.
Regulations are legal acts that apply automatically and uniformly to all EU
countries as soon as they enter into force, without needing to be transposed into
national law [35]. They are binding in their entirety on all EU countries.
On the other hand, directives follow a different approach. Directives set a certain
goal for the member states that have to be achieved but leave the implementation
open to be decided by them. Member states can develop the strategy that they
will follow in order to adopt the measures to incorporate the directives into national
law, meaning to transpose the directive into national law. National authorities must
communicate these measures to the European Commission and meet the adoption
deadlines. In case they fail to meet them, the Commission has the right to initiate
infringement proceedings.
In the case of a decision, member states are obliged to follow it in its entirety.
A decision which specifies those to whom it is addressed shall be binding only on
them [35].
Recommendations allow the EU institutions to make their views known and to
suggest a line of action without imposing any legal obligation on those to whom it
is addressed. They have no binding force [35].
An ’opinion’ is an instrument that allows the EU institutions to make a state-
ment, without imposing any legal obligation on the subject of the opinion.
An
opinion has no binding force [35].
Delegated acts are legally binding acts that enable the Commission to supplement
or amend non-essential parts of EU legislative acts, for example, in order to define
detailed measures. The Commission adopts the delegated act and if Parliament and
Council have no objections, it enters into force [35].
Implementing acts are legally binding acts that enable the Commission, under
the supervision of committees consisting of EU countries’ representatives, to set
conditions that ensure that EU laws are applied uniformly [35].

2.3.1
Approaches to Define Entrepreneurship
Although, a unified definition would help to solve the ambiguity around what ex-
actly constitutes entrepreneurship, research activity provides a number of different
approaches. Based on the article of 1991, ’Defining Entrepreneurship’ [37], there are
6 schools of thought based on personal characteristics, opportunities, management
and the need for adapting an existing venture.
The ’Great Person” School of Entrepreneurship tries to answer the question
of whether successful entrepreneurs and great leaders are born or are made.
It
states that by studying biographies of great people, there is a tendency to identify
a series of character traits, such as the strong drive for independence and success
or being inspirational and motivational. This approach presents an explanation of
charismatic leaders with power, success and wealth that created their way to the
top. Leaders like the Fords or the Kennedys, Enzo Ferrari and Rockefeller. These
people seem to be endowed with certain traits that differentiate them from others
[50] and are able to present ideas in a way that people want to follow them.
The Psychological Characteristics School of Entrepreneurship approaches the
analysis of entrepreneurial traits from the aspect that one’s needs, drives, attitudes,
beliefs and values are primary determinants of behaviour [37], and as a result, an
entrepreneur is a creation of these deeper aspects of his or hers life experiences.
Entrepreneurs can be differentiated from non-entrepreneurs by personality charac-
teristics [37]. The focus is on three personality characteristics: (1) personal values
such as honesty, duty, responsibility and ethical behaviour, (2) risk-taking and (3)
the need for achievement. The personal values of honesty and responsibility are
essential for an entrepreneur since they enable him or her to be trustworthy. Trust-
worthiness is important for someone who tries to convince people to follow him or
her on a new venture, either work or invest in it.
It is common to portray en-

to step on other people, and actually, there are numerous cases to support this
idea. However, studies of entrepreneurs indicate that many are highly ethical and
socially responsible, compared to the general population [37]. This school generally
supports that entrepreneurs are not developed in the environment of school or by
studying rather than their behaviours are developed in the early stages of their life
by interacting with their parents and teachers, when people formulate the base of
their character and identity. The risk-taking willingness is a trait that can differen-
tiate entrepreneurs from managers [79]. It is important to clarify that risk-taking
in entrepreneurship is completely different from the way gamblers in casinos choose
to take risks. It is a risk-based on careful analysis and established data and not
based on chances and luck. Entrepreneurs choose to take risks in cases where they
know they have a certain degree of control over how things will unfold. However,
it is impossible to avoid it at all since they operate in new fields and markets with
new ideas that haven’t been tested or verified. The innovation that entrepreneurs
initiate holds a certain level of uncertainty. The need for achievement is a trait that
directly correlates with cultural values. Especially in capitalistic cultures, where
self-achievement is highly promoted as a way to succeed in your life, people tend
to evaluate their lives based on their personal accomplishments. In summary, the
psychological school of entrepreneurship believes that certain individual values and
needs are the necessary preconditions for entrepreneurship and that values and needs
are cultivated in the early stages of the human’s life prior to adulthood, and there-
fore, schools and universities are not able to replace that process.
The Classical School of Entrepreneurship approaches the definition of entrepreneur-
ship by providing an explanation of the etiology of the word and by making the con-
nection of entrepreneurship with creativity and innovation. The word entrepreneur
derives from the French verb ”entreprendre”, meaning to ”undertake” and was trans-
lated from the German verb ”unternehmen” which also means ”to undertake” [37].
The term was used in the early sixteenth century to describe people who bore
risk and uncertainty in order to make innovations [63]. As the years went by, the
term started to be related more with the innovation part of the definition and in
1934, Schumpeter specified that the key ingredient of entrepreneurship lies in the
innovation of the individual and the ownership is not necessary [91]. Eventually,
innovation, creativity and discovery became the essential factors that best describe
the entrepreneurial activity for this particular school of thought, and they are an
irreplaceable part of the definition of the word entrepreneur.
The Management School of Entrepreneurship is an approach to entrepreneur-

2.3.2
Measuring Entrepreneurship
The ambiguity of the definition of entrepreneurship and the difficulty of providing
a universal description is part of its nature. It may create frustration and difficulty
for people when they refer to it since the same word can have different meanings
for different people, but it is important to understand that it is inherited from the
nature of entrepreneurship. On a high and abstract level, someone could argue that
this ambiguity is what makes entrepreneurship what it is. Starting with some prior
knowledge and experience in a field, an entrepreneur tries to create something he
is not sure what it is. It is an ambiguous journey that passes through uncharted
waters.
Another challenge that comes with the definition of the word is the effort to
measure it, meaning how entrepreneurial an environment is or how successful a
19

country is in terms of entrepreneurial activity. How to measure something that it
is not always definitive, something that is not certain and that people don’t always
agree upon.
For many reasons, including industry competition, tax contributions, job cre-
ation, revenue generation and innovation, policymakers rely on entrepreneurs. Be-
cause entrepreneurship is a diverse field, choosing the right metrics is important
because it affects how current and future policies are evaluated, analysed, predicted,
and understood. Essential aspects to measure include the methods (such as self-
employment and new business creation), motivations (necessity versus opportunity)
and outcomes (growth).
Over the past decades, significant progress has been made in the field of en-
trepreneurship measurement. Right now, there are 2 main types of measurements,
the quality aspects of entrepreneurship and the quantity measurements [95]. The
quantity type keeps track of the number of new firms in terms of business ownership
that are created and the self-employment entries within populations.
The Global Entrepreneurship Monitor (GEM), creates measures of entrepreneur-
ship activity based on the rate of new ventures formation and degree of ownership
involved [52]. Such measures provide a simple and easily understood number that
is comparable and therefore it is useful. However, there is an essential problem with
the approach of new venture formation. It fails to take into account the quality of
these ventures in time. Policymakers, with their programmes targeting economic
growth, may only increase the number of firms rather than catalyse the destruction
process [95]. It is possible that for a year, the number of new ventures formation will
increase, but at the same time, ventures from previous years that stay in business
will decrease due to an increase in the number of ventures that fail after the first
year. By taking into account only the number of new ventures formed, a distorted
image of reality is created.
The quality aspects of entrepreneurship can be measured by taking into account
the growth potential of a venture since it is the factor that provides information re-
garding the potential of the venture and the impact it will have on the socioeconomic
level. The measurement of growth is considered to be a difficult and challenging task.
Measuring growth in entrepreneurial firms can be done in several ways. One ap-
proach is to measure the change in the number of full-time employees [38]. One
challenge that arises here is the diversity between countries on what constitutes a
full time employee. At the same time, a negative change in this number could po-
tentially be an indicator of growth in certain cases. For example, in entrepreneurial
ventures that try to automate processes within the company, such a decline is a
20
==PAGE:31==
Chapter 2
reflection of growth, but it could be translated the opposite way. Another approach
to measuring entrepreneurial growth is to take advantage of the traditional tools of
accounting, such as sales, revenues and profits [38]. This approach can be problem-
atic in certain cases. Accounting standards tend to differentiate between countries.
These numbers are heavily dependent on the level of corruption in a country. It is
possible for entrepreneurs to underreport these numbers in corrupt countries and,
in cases of proper regulatory enforcement, be more reliable.
Another important factor when it comes to measuring entrepreneurship and its
effectiveness and impact has to do with the duration that the analysis takes place.
The implementation of policies that influence entrepreneurship or the utilisation of
technologies or even cultural factors may take long periods of time to be evident
in the entrepreneurial environment.
The length of time a longitudinal study of
entrepreneurship spans is, therefore, likely to be a critical factor itself [51]. If, for
example, the effect of technological change on entrepreneurial activity spans a 50-
year cycle, then a 20-year study of entrepreneurship is not very likely to identify
this influence [51].
In summary, measuring entrepreneurial activity is a challenging task that re-
quires careful consideration and deep knowledge of the difficulties that may rise.
It is a task that has to be approached from different angles in order to take into
account all the possible factors that affect the phenomenon. Time duration, quanti-
tative data and qualitative measures are necessary in order to conclude a safe result
that depicts reality as it is.
2.3.3
Importance of Entrepreneurship
Entrepreneurship, on a high and abstract level, is the process of turning a concept
into a developed product. Entrepreneurship is then best viewed as a creative pro-
cess that stimulates an individual to undergo the stages of ideation (the process of
generating ideas and becoming thoroughly familiar with them), conceptualisation,
enterprise creation and commercialisation [40]. This process is beneficial for the
people who participate since they have the chance to develop themselves profession-
ally and personally and for society on many levels. It can have a positive impact on
the creation of job opportunities through the creation of new businesses and growth,
innovation, living standards, research and development, and the creation of national
wealth and social welfare.
Innovation and entrepreneurship are indissolubly connected since the first is a
constituent element of the latter. Being an entrepreneur means that you propose a
new, different, innovative approach to a problem and offer a solution to a problem

entrepreneurial activity is bound to think, act and make decisions in an innovative

2.4
Background Information for Circular Econ-

There is a pressing need to transition to a more sustainable model when it comes
to how to produce and consume products and services. The current linear model
follows the philosophy of ”make-use-dispose” with a heavily negative impact on the
environment and extensively on humans, as well.
Environmental problems such
as biodiversity loss, water, air and soil pollution and resource depletion bring the
planet to the point of no return for humankind, and climate change has become one
of the most crucial challenges for the current and future generations, if not the most
crucial.
In an attempt to change radically the established system of production and con-
sumption, it has become evident that at a core change is necessary in order to achieve
a more sustainable future with respect to the natural resources and secure a safe
future for the following generations. Therefore, the concept of Circular Economy,
while not entirely new, has recently gained importance in the academic world, as

Circular Economy presents a sustainable approach to production and consump-
tion, where it becomes evident the elimination of waste in the form of closed loops.
Closed loops are a different idea from the linear model, where the by-products of
productions are used as materials in other processes, the waste is almost minimised
and in cases that it is unavoidable, waste is utilised in order to extract every useful
resources it can provide, such as energy production through the process of gasifica-

2.4.1
What is Circular Economy
The circular economy is a regenerative system in which resource input and waste,
emission, and energy leakage are minimised by slowing, closing and narrowing ma-
terial and energy loops. This can be achieved through long-lasting design, mainte-
nance, repair, reuse, remanufacturing, refurbishing, and recycling [53].
Therefore, the goal of the circular economy is to present a different scope of
view where production and consumption are interdependent and create a close loop
with the aim to eliminate or minimise as much as possible the material and energy
leakage from the loop in the form of waste. In order to achieve that people have
to change their way of thinking and behaving as producers and as consumers and
embrace the methods of that are presented in the aforementioned definition.

2.4.2
The Difference between Sustainability and Circular
Economy
The modern conception of sustainability has its origin in forestry [53] and comes
from the French word ”soutenir” which means ”to support or hold up” [21].
It
was an effort to mention the importance of harvesting quantities of wood from the
forest at a pace that would allow the forest to regenerate the harvested quantities
[22]. Later, the meaning of the word was transferred to the context of ecology as a
principle of respecting the ability of nature to regenerate itself [42]. Nowadays, an
accepted definition of the word is ”development that meets the needs of the present
without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs”
[61]. Instead of merely setting common goals, sustainability opens up the scope for
multiple expectations about, for example, what should be developed and what is to
be sustained, for how long, and for the benefit of whom [3].
Therefore, taking into account the definition of the sustainability and circular
economy, it is possible to identify the difference between these two terms, that a lot
of times are used wrongly interchangeably. Sustainability acts as an umbrella for
every practice that strives to sustain the future, while circularity is more about the
practices which lead to sustainability.

According to the ”weak” version of the Porter Hypothesis strict environmental regu-

Environmental regulation, especially command-and-control regulation, has been
particularly suspected as a source of crowding out effects [56].
Command-and-
control regulation is a type of environmental regulation that allows policymakers
to specifically regulate both the amount and the process by which a firm should
maintain the quality of the environment, and crowding out effects are considered
the economic theory that argues that rising public sector spending drives down or
even eliminates private sector spending. To spend more, the government needs added
revenue. Therefore, it is important to mention that regulation can have a negative
effect when done the wrong way. Too much governmental involvement can damage
the goals of policies and regulation and since the current economic model of the
world is capitalistic a combination of free market principles with the right amount
of state involvement could mitigate the regulation risks. In a paper study, it was
identified a trade-off between resource allocation for R&D to improve productivity
over R&D for pollution reduction [73]. Therefore, such a company could potentially
face productivity issues and the margins for profits could be affected negatively.

process since a big part of the resources are allocated to regulation matters rather
than productivity R&D. Finally, a potential crowding out of other innovation could
be a barrier to competitiveness and economic growth in the long run for a company

Figure 3.1 presents the Waste Management Hierarchy, which is a conceptual
framework designed to guide and rank waste management decisions at both the
individual and organisational level [12]. It gives top priority to waste prevention,
followed by reuse, recycling, recovery and finally disposal.
The hierarchy helps
individuals and organisations rethink their relationship with waste based on five
priorities ranked in terms of what’s best for the environment. In more detail, the
five steps that are displayed in the Waste Management Hierarchy have a specific
goal and the higher in the hierarchy, the more important they are. The prevention
has top priority and importance and the meaning is that the first step is to try
and prevent the creation of waste as much as possible.
The second step is the
preparation for re-use in which organisations could use materials that used to be
treated as waste, as they are to another production process without added action.
The third step is the recycling process, which aims to transform waste into valuable
materials. It’s a process that takes place later in the life cycle of a product when
it’s already become waste, but after a specific process, it is possible to turn it into
a useful material again. The fourth step in the hierarchy is the recovering process
in which waste is considered that further recycling is not practical or possible and
energy or materials could be recovered through processes such as anaerobic digestion

3.3.3
Landfill Waste and Landfill Directive
EU, based on the waste hierarchy, has decided that landfill is the least preferable
option and that it should take measures to limit, to the necessary minimum, the
quantities of waste that end up in landfills [29]. In 2018, 24% of all municipal waste
generated in the EU was landfilled. This can potentially be extremely dangerous for
the health of humans since landfills are exposed to weather conditions which enables
the generation of leachate.
Leachate is contaminated water that has percolated
through solid waste and has accumulated a number of contaminates and potentially,
if not treated properly, it can further damage the environment and human health by
polluting underground water sources [23]. At the same time, landfill sites produce
quantities of methane, which is a potent greenhouse gas, and it is considered harmful
to humans and the environment when concentrated in large quantities.
For these reasons, the EU has introduced the Landfill Directive, which sets out
strict operational requirements for landfill sites with the objective of protecting both
human health and the environment [27]. A concise version of the Directive’s aims,
which encapsulates the overall objective, includes the following rules. The Land-
fill Directive introduces restrictions on landfilling of all waste that is suitable for
recycling or other material or energy recovery from 2030, limits the share of munic-
ipal waste landfilled to 10% by 2035, introduces rules on calculating the attainment

quality control and traceability system for municipal waste landfilled, requires the
European Commission, with the European Environment Agency, to draw up early
warning reports 3 years before each deadline to identify shortcomings in attaining
the targets and recommending action to be taken, and allows EU countries to use
economic instruments and other measures to encourage applying the waste hierarchy

3.3.5
Danish Legislation Framework for Waste Management
Danish waste legislation is characterised by a close interplay between EU regulation
and national regulation. The EU regulation lays down the overall frameworks and
principles for waste management.
The organisation of waste structures and the
implementation of EU regulation in Danish legislation is a national task [5].
The Danish waste model is based on a combination of traditional administrative
instruments (Acts, Statutory Orders, Circulars), and various other instruments such
as taxes and charges, subsidy schemes and agreements [5].
Waste management in Denmark is regulated by the Environmental Protection
Act [60]. The Act sets the framework to prevent and control pollution of air, water,
soil, and subsoil, as well as vibration and noise nuisance. The Act also contains
hygienically justified rules of importance to the environment and population and
limits the use and waste of raw materials and other resources. It contains rules
to promote the use of cleaner technology as well as rules to promote recycling in
waste management [44]. It is important to be mentioned that the Environmental
Protection Act provides only a legal framework, and the more specific provisions
of the Danish waste legislation and policy are presented in various statutory orders
[60].
The legal framework laying down the obligation of local authorities to manage
waste is given in the Danish Environmental Protection Act and subsequent Statutory
Orders and Circulars. The most important Statutory Order is the Statutory Order
on Waste [5]. Under the provisions of the order, local authorities are responsible
for the management of all waste in Denmark and the Environment Act states that
local authorities are responsible for SUWM, as well [6].
The local authorities have the power and freedom to make decisions on how to
implement the tasks regarding waste management within the limits that the policies
of the Environmental Act and the statutory order provide.
Their duties are to
display a short-term waste management plan covering 4 years and a long-term plan
40
==PAGE:51==
Chapter 3
covering 12 years. Also, local authorities are obligated to prepare a specific set of
actions in the form of waste regulations detailing the schemes of waste management
and make sure that the schemes are following environmentally friendly practices [6].
The schemes are environmentally friendly when the local councils make decisions
according to the Danish Environmental Protection Act and the Statutory Order on
Waste.
When the waste management schemes have been established, citizens, freeholders
and enterprises are under an obligation to use the schemes. The local council can
have the option to assume the task itself, to confer the task to an inter-municipal
waste company or to contract operation to a private company [6].
Furthermore, the Danish legislation system, in addition to the environmental
Acts and Statutory Orders, utilises a series of extra tools. Waste tax has the objec-
tive of differentiating the waste management practices by creating financial incen-
tives for environmental practices [6]. Also, agreements are another tool to achieve
environmental objectives by agreeing on a set of rules and actions between the state
and private organisations for the setting of specific processes [5]. For example, an
agreement has been entered with the Danish Contractors’ Association on the selec-
tive demolition of building materials.

There are 2 main approaches to qualitative analysis: inductive and deductive. Fur-
thermore, the inductive methods of analysing transcripts can further be divided into
the thematic analysis and the narrative analysis [89].
For the purposes of the current thesis, the inductive thematic analysis was se-
lected. The thematic analysis provides a versatile method for identifying, analysing
and reporting patterns within data [10]. This particular method is not tied to any
specific philosophical framework [10], therefore providing a level of freedom to be
applied in the current qualitative research since it is a method that does not require
a predetermined framework and encourages the identification of common themes
and patterns in the data [89]. Additionally, due to the clear, easy-to-follow pro-
cesses outlined by Braun and Clarke [19], researchers have suggested that thematic
analysis is an ideal analytic method for novice qualitative researchers [84].
The process involves six key stages: familiarisation with the data, generating ini-
tial codes, searching for themes, reviewing themes, defining and naming themes, and
producing the report [10]. The familiarisation with the data took place by studying
the transcripts one time in order to acquire a general overview of what was discussed
and start creating some patterns that had been repeated in the interviews. That
was extremely helpful since it enabled the creation of the first thematic categories
but without the implementation of them during the first stage, yet. After the first
reading of the transcripts, the first version of categories was produced, which in-
cluded the categories: product, innovation, market and regulation. Since the main
categories existed, a second study of each transcript took place, and in parallel,

Discussion
6.1
Research Problem and Research Questions
Countries, especially the developed ones, are characterized by highly consumeristic
habits due to the capitalistic tendencies and culture embedded in people’s lives over
the last decades. The phenomena of overproduction and over-consumption have led
the planet to its limit, with natural resources being eliminated and huge quantities
of waste being produced, with catastrophic impacts on the environment and living
organisms.
At the same time, the materials that end up being waste in landfills hold great
value that is lost. Therefore, the waste management sector has faced a transfor-
mation in the last decades to tackle the problems of rising waste quantities and
material value loss. More specifically, the waste management industry responsible
for the solid waste treatment for urban areas, which is named SUWM, is of great
interest since it is responsible for generating 2.01 billion tonnes of municipal solid
waste annually [16]. For these 2 problems to be treated more effectively, govern-
ments and institutions have proposed and implemented a series of legislation orders
to promote sustainable practices for waste.
The current thesis aims to investigate how regulations affect innovation from
the entrepreneurship standpoint in the sector of SUWM and fill in the knowledge
gap that connects these 3 areas: regulation, entrepreneurship and SUWM. In order
to do that, a thorough literature review was conducted on existing frameworks
and theories that connect regulation and innovation. Also, the current regulatory
environment in the EU and Denmark was investigated, as well as how the policies
and regulations of the EU are implemented at the national level. Lastly, a series of
interviews were conducted with the aim of understanding better the relationship of
these 3 areas and especially the effect of regulation on innovation processes.
64
==PAGE:75==
Chapter 6
The research questions that will be answered in this chapter, by combining the
knowledge that has been accumulated are: 1) Discover the mechanisms of EU reg-
ulations that affect innovation, positively or negatively, 2) Explain the implemen-
tation of EU regulations in member states legislation systems and 3) Explore the
way entrepreneurs in the sector of SUWM utilize the opportunities that derive from
regulations.
6.2
Summary of Key Findings
The data suggest EU regulations have an impact on innovation. The impact can be
both negative and positive, although the positive side of the effects outweighs the
negative. In entrepreneurial ventures, the cost of regulations seems to have different
effects based on the phase the venture is in. By interviewing 2 start-up founders,
it has been identified that 1 start-up founder believed that the resources that are
necessary for navigating through regulation could be a reason to end their venture
if it wasn’t for the resources that the incubator provides them and enable them to
identify the key point of these regulations. On the other hand, the other start-up
had a different opinion on the matter.
Being in the process of developing their
prototype, the regulations and policies regarding sustainability seem to be out of
scope and without any effect on them. For established companies, the necessary
resource allocation for the sustainability reports could be a reason to slow down
the innovation processes in the company. At the same time, even though the cost
of regulation creates these difficulties, it does not stop innovative processes. Only
slows them down. By providing specific constraints, companies are forced to think
differently and innovate by choosing different materials for their product, different
designs or even by changing their business plan.
The data support the idea that EU regulations provide flexibility in the imple-
mentation of legislation systems in the member states. The directives constitute a
non-binding legislation framework that provides guiding directions and specific tar-
gets that have to be met but leaves the implementation of the goals to be decided
by the member states. On the other hand, an EU regulation is a specific legislation
that member states have to implement. This flexibility provides the opportunity to
meet each country’s special circumstances and promote policy innovation.
The analysis identifies that EU regulations can create opportunities for en-
trepreneurs. The regulations regarding environmental taxation create new markets
where entrepreneurs can promote their products. These taxes disrupt the status quo
and force companies to adapt to more sustainable practices. The example of the
65
==PAGE:76==

packaging directive where companies will be taxed higher based on the packaging
material that they use, provides the opportunity for Ynkvig to sell their sustainable
packaging material. Also, EU regulations create incentives for funds to be redirected
towards sustainability projects, which is a great opportunity for entrepreneurs to get
funded. Therefore, by innovating in the sector of SUWM, entrepreneurs can get in-
vestments from sustainable funds.

6.3.1
Regulation Cost
Regulation costs can have different forms. It can be a financial cost, administrative
cost or compliance cost. In the interview analysis, two different approaches on that
matter were identified by the start-up founders. The Ynkvig founder mentioned
that the help they get from the Skylab incubator is vital for them to deal with
the regulation regarding the waste management sector to the extent that otherwise
would be a challenge for them to allocate the necessary resources to do it. So, it
seems like a start-up that has limited resources and has to allocate them carefully
into the most crucial processes can face problems regarding the proper allocation of
their resources. For a start-up, these processes have a direct connection with the
innovative solution they want to bring into the market, which is a very demanding
task in terms of technology development and finding the best market fit for their
product. Therefore, it would create vital problems if they had to allocate these
limited resources to tasks that do not have a direct connection with these product
development and market fit processes. That is an interesting case because it states
an important issue that regulation can create.
On the other hand, Acckon’s founders demonstrated a different picture. It seems
they are in an earlier stage and still focusing on the development of their prototype.
Even though they also mentioned that they are receiving beneficial help from the
66
==PAGE:77==
Chapter 6
Skylab incubator regarding mentoring and regulation advice, they do not believe
that there is a high regulation cost that would cause them problems regarding prod-
uct development and resource allocation.
Furthermore, the Force Technology employee mentioned that the number of new
regulations that are currently published is a problem for established companies. The
problem with the fast rate and the large quantity of new regulations in the waste
management sector creates difficulty for them in keeping up with the obligations they
have to comply with. It takes time and human resources for a company to study
a legislation document, identify the key elements that refer to their business, and
later develop the necessary reports. This way a lot of companies end up reporting
on sustainability targets but they do not invest in actual innovative process. Also,
a lot of times, these regulations require a thorough reform of their business plan or
even production processes. These changes take time and financial resources to be
achieved. This creates a problem for established and big companies, as these changes
affect a complicated bureaucratic hierarchy and interconnections between different
divisions. Therefore, these changes require a lot of effort in terms of establishing
what the regulations require from the company, how the company can achieve the
targets and obligations, and how to implement these changes later.
A constant
flow of new regulation creates the need for the companies to be in constant reform
in order to meet the new regulation obligations, and as a result, it can create a
situation where the companies allocate the necessary resources for these changes
and to produce the sustainability reports without really innovate.
Here, it is wise to draw a connection with the theory.
In the paper ”How
does environmental regulation promote green technology innovation? Evidence from
China’s total emission control policy”[103], it is mentioned that ”According to the
”weak” version of Porter Hypothesis strict environmental regulation will increase
production costs, suggesting that firms must meet environmental regulations of pro-
ductivity loss”, which makes a direct connection between regulation costs and pro-
ductivity loss. The interview data from the Ynkvig founder and the Force Tech-
nology employee verify the theory and, at the same time, make an extension of the
negative impact. For the start-up, the data make evident that regulation costs could
potentially become such a burden that the venture will cease to exist and the estab-
lished company could derail their production processes to the extent that instead
of innovating, they will try to keep up with the reporting. At the same time, it is
important to mention the case of the Acckon start-up, where the founders seemed
to have a different experience regarding regulation costs. It seems that they are
not affected by them. The reason for this contradiction with the other two cases

the general goals of the EU regarding sustainability and sets specific targets for the
member states. For example, its ultimate target is to set the direction order for the
EU to become the first climate-neutral continent by 2050.
The Force Technology employee said about the European Green Deal and its

on something specific. For example, the European Green Deal aims to create a
climate-neutral continent. This declaration itself provides the necessary informa-
tion for entrepreneurs to focus their attention towards actions that could assist this
target. Now, it is their own responsibility to understand and envision what actions
and decisions could enable them to develop an entrepreneurial venture that could be
part of this target, but at the same time, they know where to focus their attention

and efforts.
The creation of incentives, as the EEA employee mentioned, is another mecha-
nism that regulations provide in order to enhance entrepreneurial activity and inno-
vation. The incentives can have many forms. As it is mentioned in the paper, ”How
does environmental regulation promote green technology innovation? Evidence from
China’s total emission control policy” [103], ”From an industrial perspective, envi-
ronmental regulation may incentivize potential entrants that have identified market
opportunities for green technologies”. Here, it is possible to identify an alignment
between interview data and the theory where both sources agree upon the benefits of
regulation by providing incentives for companies to participate in green innovation.
The theory goes one step further by indicating specifically green technologies and
incentives for companies that have already identified potential markets.
Furthermore, in the same paper [103], there is a reference to how green regu-
lations and policies become positively impactful, ”the policy has a substantial and
positive impact on green innovation thanks to its precise policy objectives focused
action plans and strict reward and punishment systems. Furthermore, we discov-
ered that the policy encourages more companies to develop green technologies and
raises the average degree of green innovation in cities and industries. Environmen-
tal regulations, in particular, may encourage businesses with low stocks of green
knowledge to enter the market. This suggests that the policy not only encourages
green innovation but also helps to increase the adoption of green technologies in a
variety of industries and geographical areas”. First of all, the aforementioned refer-
ence aligns with the feedback the Force Technology employee provided. The theory
mentions that the focused action plans with strict reward and punishment systems
are the reason why regulations are effective in promoting and enhancing innovation
in green technologies. Just as the Force Technology employee mentioned the com-
mitted goals of regulations. In addition, the theory suggests that regulations have
the potential to encourage companies with low stocks of green technologies to enter
the new green markets. This could be understood as an incentive for companies to
start participating in the green transition.
6.3.3
Regulations and Investments
One important factor for entrepreneurial ventures’ success is their ability to concen-
trate funding. It is not a secret that many promising start-ups die because of a lack
of funding. So, during the investigation of the regulation mechanisms that could
affect innovation processes, investment processes were also investigated.
The Acckon founder demonstrated indifferent behaviour towards investments and

time, it seems that all the efforts and energy is concentrated on developing their
innovative technology. As the founders mentioned, ”In our startup, we are right
now primarily focused on the development of the machine”.
Furthermore, their
involvement with regulations and investment processes seems to be out of scope,
with no direct benefit for them, and it does not affect their effort to innovate, as
they said, ”and like not having been taking so much into the essence of the EU and
the Danish Government politics”.
On the other hand, Ynkvig’s founder had a different opinion and approach to
the effect of regulations in investment schemes and frameworks and the importance
of funding for their start-up. As he mentioned, ”There is a great push from the
EU right to invest in sustainable solutions. As I recall it, it’s the horizon initiative
where they’re trying to. They are creating like a big pool of funding that can go
out to sustainable solutions”. Even though Ynkvig is part of the Skylab incubator,
benefiting from the help and support it provides to them and is sufficient from the
soft funding from the university, they have already started investigating the invest-
ment environment and how they could benefit from investment schemes directed to
start-ups with sustainable goals. Their interest in these investment schemes indi-

Therefore, it is safe to say that investment schemes that are directed to sustainable
start-ups, like the ones that operate in the sector of SUWM, draw the attention of
the start-ups as possible funding sources. That fact proves the impact that such
schemes could potentially have on start-ups and innovation processes.
At the same time, the Force Technology employee provided some insightful per-
spective on the matter and how regulation can affect investment processes towards
sustainable and waste management ventures. She made a direct connection between
regulations regarding sustainable goals and investment schemes. She said, ”Move
more money into sustainable investments, and in order to do so, they have to define
what is a sustainable investment, and that is definitely a thing that they’re trying
by regulation”. There is a chance that, in some cases, there is ambiguity regard-
ing what exactly is sustainable and what is not, which creates confusion towards
to which start-ups and innovation the investments should be directed. By regulat-
ing this ambiguity and providing a clear definition of sustainability, the investment
processes are becoming easier to happen. That is because regulation can create a
trustworthy environment where people know what they should invest in and what

6.3.4
Application of Regulation
The EU has 2 main kinds of legislation, the regulation and the directives.
As
the EEA employee explained during the interview, ”the countries usually have to
implement what’s coming from the EU into their national rules, and they also have
some freedom”. The fact that EU legislation can be in a binding and non-binding
form provides the EU with the choice to enforce the regulations to the member
71
==PAGE:82==
Chapter 6
countries or let them choose the optimal way to implement the changes. The freedom
that comes with the second choice is crucial in order to take into account the special
circumstances that exist in every member state. That can result in more effective
implementations and better results.
Unfortunately, no relevant theory could be
found that supports or cancels the statement that a level of freedom regarding the
implementation of the regulation provides better or worse results in the SUWM
sector. Therefore, it is not possible to derive a secure conclusion regarding the effect
that the freedom of implementation provides on the regulations and, as a result, the
innovation in the sector of SUWM. Probably, the effectiveness of the implementation
and whether there is going to be an impact on the innovation process is a matter
for each member state to decide.
At the same time, it is important to mention a mechanism that the EU imple-
ments in order to provide better and more effective policies. The EEA employee
mentioned that policies tend to be reviewed after the implementation in order to
decide on the effectiveness and whether there are things that need to be changed.
As she said ”OK, well what we had in mind in the beginning, how the market reacts
is maybe not exactly how we had anticipated it ... there are some loopholes and
then usually it gets revised and changed”.
In brief, these are the two mechanisms that were identified during the analysis of
the interview data regarding the implementation of EU regulations in the member
states legislation systems and as a result to the Danish system as well. Unfortunately,
there is no further information to back up the effectiveness of the innovation of these
approaches apart from the data that were collected during the interviews. Based on
this data, the provided freedom to implementation and the revision of implemented
regulations are two important and necessary traits.
6.3.5
New Market
Regulations affect the creation of new markets. One way that this can possibly be
achieved is by the proper targeting of taxes. Ynkvig founder mentioned, ”petroleum-
based packaging is very cheap, and I don’t think there’s going to be a time soon
where these newer alternatives can match in pricing”, therefore as it is right now,
companies have the financial incentive to use petroleum-based products for their
packaging. Ynkvig’s product is a sustainable alternative that uses food waste as the
basis of its product. As it is a new technology, it is really difficult to compete with
established and cheap petroleum packaging solutions. EU has recognized this issue
and will publish a series of regulations that will impose high taxes on companies that
use petroleum-based packaging material with the aim of creating financial incentives
72
==PAGE:83==
Chapter 6
for the companies to start using environmentally friendly materials. As mentioned in
the article ”Waste 21 - Waste Management Plan” [6], ”Waste tax has the objective
to differentiate the waste management practices by creating financial incentives for
environmental practices”. As it is right now, companies have the financial incentive
to use petroleum-based packaging materials because they are cheaper. By imposing
higher taxes, the EU aims to reverse the financial incentive towards environmentally
friendly solutions.
That creates a new market where start-ups like Ynkvig can
penetrate and start selling their products. Therefore, we can see that taxation can
be a mechanism to promote innovation in the SUWM sector.
While these new markets are being created and innovative ventures enter and
sell their products, established companies will start to lose market share. The Force
Technology employee said, ”I think what we will see is that if the big companies
that exist today if they’re not transitioning, I think we’ll see more new players in
the market. That will take market shares because they have other solutions to it”.
That fact will create the incentive for established companies to start innovating in
order to regain the lost ground in terms of market share. Therefore, the creation
of new markets through the implementation of green regulations in the sector of
SUWM, could promote the innovation processes for established companies, as well.
Lastly, it is important to mention that the creation of new markets based on
sustainability criteria has created the problem of greenwashing from established
companies. These companies try to capture market share by ostensibly following
the green regulations instead of practically producing innovative solutions for their
clients. As the Acckon founders mentioned, ”upgrading the LCA and everything
like that, it would help them. There are more political interested in the course ...
maybe somewhere greenwashing and make a statement that you have to use it”.
In brief, regulation through taxation practices can become a mechanism of in-
novation promotion and positively affect entrepreneurial activities, both for new
ventures and established companies. At the same time, entrepreneurs can utilise
these opportunities to innovate.
6.3.6
Innovation in Solid Urban Waste Management
Regulation can promote innovation in the SUWM sector in other ways apart from
utilizing taxes. The circular economy principles can provide useful directions to-
wards a more innovative sector. The Force Technology employee mentioned, ”Cre-
ating more and more like financial incentives to actually change and use resources
better, the companies will have to change, and that requires innovation. At least
think more modular design”. Therefore, the utilisation of circular economy prin-
73

ciples to create a modular design for products requires and promotes innovative
processes for companies. She continues by providing an example, ” we can change
the things are the parts that have a limited time lifetime for instance, on a phone
like the battery is the first thing that dies. So can we build in the opportunity to
easily change the battery and the processes so we can keep the screen and keep some
of the other things”. This example demonstrates the important effect of regulations
because they refer to changes in the product design and the business plans of the
companies. The change happens if companies embed circular economy principles
into their process in order to create products that last longer and are repairable if
part of them fails.
Regulations can be used to provide financial incentives for companies, but they
can also be used to educate critical personnel. As she mentioned, ”they didn’t come

report that often the both the board and the CFO and the CEO have to sign they
also have to get some kind of knowledge that they didn’t have before”. Therefore,
by incorporating sustainability reports, like the Corporate Sustainability Report,
indirectly, higher-level management personnel are forced to be educated in order
to be able to understand what criteria make decisions. The educational effect of
regulation has the potential to be the most important because it has long-lasting
effects on how people think and how they make decisions. By regulating a more
sustainable business environment, companies learn how to take into account the
totality of the stakeholders that their business affects. These stakeholders can be
environmental and social.
In brief, regulations can promote innovative behaviour by providing financial
incentives for the incorporation of circular economy principles and enforcing decision-
making personnel to consider sustainability factors, which could lead to further
education on the matter.

6.4.1
Data Sample Limitations
As has been mentioned in the Methodology and Data chapter, the initial aim was to
interview 4 stakeholders. The interviews that took place included start-up founders
(Ynkvig, Acckon), intermediary company (Force Technology) and consultant agency
(EEA). It is important to mention that the EEA is considered a consultant agency
because it provides consultant services to the European Commission. It is a gov-
ernmental agency that has no similarities with business consultant agencies, but the
employee interviewed had a consultant role rather than a policymaker.
As a result, an important knowledge gap has been created due to the lack of
interviews with policymakers and employees from established companies. The poli-
cymakers were contacted by the Danish Environmental Ministry and the European
Commission. The employees from the Danish Environmental Ministry were con-
tacted through Linkedin and email, and the European Commission through email
and phone. Both governmental agencies provided negative answers to the possibility
of an interview. For the established companies, an outreach to 13 Danish companies
took place in the duration of 2 months. Unfortunately, either no reply or a negative
reply due to time constraints were received back.
The interview data from the established companies would have been useful since
it would have provided meaningful insights into how innovation takes place in estab-
lished companies. Probably, the kind of challenges and opportunities green regula-
tion creates for them and how they overcome and utilise them. The knowledge gap
that was created due to the lack of interviewing established companies is important
but partially covered. The fact that there is interview data from 2 startups, provides
a crucial chance to investigate how businesses are affected by the green regulations.
On the other hand, the lack of interview data for policymakers created a crucial
knowledge gap that could not be covered. Initially, it was thought that the inter-
view with the EEA employee could cover parts of the policy makers’ agenda but it
became clear that the agency provided only consultation services to the European
Commission without participating in the process of decision-making regarding the
policies. Therefore, critical feedback from the policy-making viewpoint has been
lost.

Theoretical Limitations
The thesis lacks some important theoretical viewpoints that could have enriched the
interpretation of the results. Even though, the interview data indicate great bene-
fits for innovation processes from the proper utilization of taxation, the theoretical

6.5
Research Recommendations and Future Re-
search
6.5.1
Recommendations
The interpretation of the results suggests that regulations can have an important
impact on innovation. First of all, it is crucial for policymakers to take into account
the quantity and pace at which they introduce new regulations and policies in the
SUWM sector.
The interview data showed that an over-regulated industry can
create obstacles for new firms to enter the industry and at the same time, established
companies will be forced to spend time and resources in order to comply with the
new regulations instead of innovating. Therefore, it is important for policymakers to
take into account the factor of quantity and pace when they publish new regulations
and policies. They have to be careful and think about the time that is required in
order to implement changes that derive from regulations.
Secondly, another mechanism that regulations have is taxation. It is possible
to affect innovation by creating new market opportunities for start-ups.
As the
interview data suggest that is the case for Ynkvig. Therefore, it is possible to assume
that entrepreneurs should take into account future policies and regulations that will
create new markets through taxation. That is important because entrepreneurs are
people who are looking for new opportunities and they try to fill in the gap and
provide innovative solutions to problems humans are facing.
Thirdly, the regulation can provide strategic planning for the future. The inter-
view data showed that European regulations and policies, like the European Green
Deal, that set long-term sustainability goals have a positive influence on innovation
processes. Especially for the SUWM sector, where changes require time to be imple-
mented in many cases, the long-term nature of such policies can be a clear indicator
76
==PAGE:87==
Chapter 6
of which challenges and problems the start-ups should aim for.
Lastly, regulation influences investment processes, which in turn is one of the
most influential factors for innovation. The interview data suggest that regulation
can provide a clear indication of what constitutes sustainable investment which will
enable funds to be directed to the proper ventures. Entrepreneurs who operate in
the sector of SUWM can take that fact into account and investigate what constitutes
sustainability and try to build, based on those principles, their ventures.
In brief, the recommendations that have been presented are practical examples
of how regulations affect innovation from the entrepreneurs’ viewpoint, how poli-
cymakers can implement more efficient regulations and how entrepreneurs in the
SUWM sector can use these mechanisms to better identify upcoming opportunities
in the industry.
6.5.2
Future Research
The research of the thesis covered a significant proportion of the effect regulations
have on innovation in the SUWM sector. Also, it managed to fill in the knowledge
gap between regulation, innovation and SUWM by providing concrete examples of
the overlap and by proposing practical actions for policymakers and entrepreneurs.
Though, as has already been mentioned, there are some limitations as well as pos-
sibilities for future research.
The lack of policymakers in the interview data is considered a significant short-
age. Future research could provide important insights by accumulating data from
policymakers about how they make decisions about policies and regulations and
whether innovation is an aspect they consider.
Also, during the analysis of the data, some shortages from the theory standpoint
were identified. Theoretical background on taxation and its effect on innovation
could provide interesting results that could enrich our understanding of the topic.
Furthermore, one knowledge gap that has been unexplored is about the implemen-
tation of EU policies from the member states and whether the practice that is being
implemented currently has any effect on innovation. In addition, the interview data
suggested the great importance of education, which is derived from regulations and
promotes innovation in the SUWM sector. That suggestion wasn’t investigated in
depth, but it has great promise.
Lastly, future research could try and identify specific SUWM technologies. It
was out of scope to do so in the current thesis, but research on the matter could
provide interesting insights and enrich the understanding of the sector of SUWM
and the interrelation it has with innovation and regulation.

Conclusion
The current thesis aimed to provide insights into the interconnection between regu-
lation, innovation, and SUWM. More specifically, it sought to fill in the information
gap regarding how EU regulations affect innovation processes in the SUWM sector.
The research questions around which the thesis was developed and aimed to help
us provide this information are: 1) Discover the mechanisms that EU regulation
affect innovation, positively or negatively, 2) Explain the implementation of EU reg-
ulations in the Danish legislation system, and 3) Explore the way that entrepreneurs
in SUWM industry utilise the opportunities that derive from regulations.
Interviews with relevant stakeholders were conducted to answer the aforemen-
tioned questions. The interviews were recorded, and later, the produced transcripts
were analysed. The analysis that was selected was the inductive thematic analysis,
which is optimal for qualitative data and the identification of patterns in it. The
results of the analysis were based on the themes that were developed during the
analysis.
The analysis showed a crucial connection between EU regulations and innovation
in the form of regulation costs or regulation opportunities, such as the creation of
new markets due to taxation. However, further research is necessary to identify
the effects of regulations for specific technologies used in the SUWM sector and
overcome the limitations of the current thesis.
