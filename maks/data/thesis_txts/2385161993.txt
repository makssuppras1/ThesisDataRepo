ABSTRACT 
The use of robots for power line inspection is a fast growing research field due to increased 
demand for faster and more cost effective inspection methods. The two main robotic solutions in 
use today are Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs) for visual inspection of power lines and line-
crawling robots for close-proximity inspection of various transmission line equipment. A new UAV 
solution is proposed that combines the two methods by having the UAV fly up to and attach to a 
live conductor taking advantage of the practicality of the UAV while enabling the close proximity 
inspection of a line-crawling robot. The design for a proof-of-concept prototype is introduced and 
an initial prototype is developed. The UAV takes advantage of the flight capabilities of existing 
flight controller software for general flight operations but a supplementary control method based 
on Image-Based Visual Servoing (IBVS) is implemented as an aid for the UAV operator in aligning 
the UAV underneath a transmission line conductor. The UAV uses tube rollers to attach to and 
traverse the conductor and propulsion is achieved by rotating the flight motors to thrust the UAV 
along the conductor. The UAV is designed and tested for any harmful effects of the high voltage 
on the sensitive flight electronics and the control method chosen is evaluated based on results 
from flight tests performed in a controlled test environment. The results form a basis for future 
prototype development.

1 INTRODUCTION 
 
A reliable power transmission network is essential to transfer the electricity generated in power 
plants to the homes and cities of the world. Any failure in the transmission network can have a 
severe impact on the daily life of people and industries that need a reliable electricity source such 
as transportation networks, factories, hospitals and security. To minimize this risk energy 
transmission companies perform routine maintenance of their transmission networks to 
preemptively detect and repair faults before they reach the point of having an adverse effect on 
the electricity transmission. 
Developed economies all over the world have made significant investments in their power 
transmission network over the past decades. With parts of the power infrastructure aging rapidly 
and nearing the end of its active service life along with ever increasing rises in electricity 
consumption, large investments in renewable energy sectors and less reliance on fossil fuels there 
is an increased demand for faster, more cost efficient and more reliable inspection methods to 
ensure safety and reliability of power transmission networks.

1.1 INSPECTION METHODS 
 
Ground inspection is the oldest and most common inspection method used today (Danielsson, 
2006). The inspection crew relies mainly on visual inspection aided by the use of binoculars and 
thermal cameras to assess the status of the transmission equipment. Inspection workers also 
regularly have to climb the support towers to get a closer look at isolators and conductor 
connections. Since transmission lines often pass over difficult terrain with restricted vehicular 
access the inspection crews often have to traverse the length of the power line on foot over rough 
terrain. This can be very time consuming and is better suited for other inspection methods such 
as aerial inspection. 
Aerial power line inspection gives the advantage over ground inspection of being able to inspect 
the power line in less time and with more efficiency (C. Whitworth, 2001). Airplanes are used for 
inspecting long stretches of power lines. The pilot flies over the power lines at a low altitude while 
a spotter inspects the line from above. This method enables fast inspection of long stretches of 
power lines but is not suited for detecting anything but the most obvious structural faults such as 
broken insulators, damage to the supporting tower or severely broken and unraveled wire 
strands. Helicopters have the advantage over airplanes that they can hover in place and fly at a 
slower speed but suffer from the decreased fuel efficiency and higher maintenance cost. Since 
helicopters are flown in closer proximity to the power lines there is also a high risk of accidents. 
There are numerous cases of helicopters used for power line inspection striking the power lines 
with the main or tail rotor of the helicopter and crashing. This risk is further increased when 
helicopters are used to transport line inspection workers to the power line for inspection or 
repairs since the helicopter needs to hover in very close proximity to the power line, leaving little 
room for pilot error.  
The power line itself, referred to as the conductor, is often inspected by line workers riding in 
gondolas suspended on a live conductor or crawling along a conductor to inspect for faults and 
damage often not visible using aerial or ground inspection.

1.2 ROBOTIC INSPECTION 
 
Due to the high cost as well as inherent danger to inspection workers associated with traditional 
inspection methods the use of autonomous or semi-autonomous robotic systems for inspection 
is fast becoming the new standard, inspiring research and development of innovative robotic 
inspection systems (Jaka Katrasnik, 2010). 
 
1.2.1 
UAVs 
 
The use of Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAV) for aerial visual inspection of power lines is gaining 
traction as a replacement for the more traditional aerial inspection methods, with established 
UAV companies such as Delair tech (Delair Tech, 2017) offering long range autonomous inspection 
with fixed wing UAVs with a range of up to 100km and DJI (DJI, 2017) offering a complete quad 
copter UAV solution capable of inspecting power lines using regular and thermal cameras.

1.2.2 
Line robots 
 
Another method for robotic inspection is in the form of a line-crawling robots. Inspection robots 
have been a topic of research since the early 1990 (Jun Sawada, 1991) as a means of replacing the 
line workers for close inspection of conductors. Modern line robots such as the LineScout 
(Montambault, 2010) and Expliner (Paulo Debenest, 2008) are capable of traversing along the 
conductor while shifting its center of gravity to navigate itself over obstacles situated along the 
conductor.

1.2.3 
Motivation 
 
The two robotic inspection methods have pros and cons that make them suited to different use 
cases.  
The downside of UAV inspection lies in the difficulty of detecting faults that present in ways other 
than obvious structural faults that can be seen from a distance or increased heat in components 
that can be detected using a thermal camera. UAVs are however light and practical, can be flown 
or carried into the field for inspection in remote areas and are quick to set up. Line-crawling robots 
are less practical and need to be manually mounted on a conductor. They have a limited range 
due to battery life restrictions (Montambault, 2010) but deliver better fault detection due to a 
closer proximity to the conductor surface. 
The gap between practicality and precision between these two methods is the main motivation 
of this thesis. A new UAV solution is proposed that is a hybrid between the two technologies, 
taking advantage of the practicality of the UAV while enabling the close proximity inspection of a 
line-crawling robot. The proposed solution is a UAV that has the following basic requirements: 
 
The UAV can attach and detach to the conductor. 
 
The UAV can traverse along the conductor. 
 
The UAV can carry sensors for conductor inspection. 
 
The UAV can operate autonomously or semi-autonomously. 
 
The UAV can autonomously align itself underneath a conductor before attachment. 
 
Actual fault sensing and detection is left to future prototype development. This thesis focuses on 
the development of the robotic UAV platform capable of carrying sensors for line detection. 
1.3 OBJECTIVE 
 
The objective of this thesis is to: 
 
Analyze the intended operating environment around a live high voltage conductor and 
define required operating capabilities suitable for a proof-of-concept UAV prototype.  
 
Design and construct a prototype capable of fulfilling the requirements. 
 
Incorporate a position controller to align the UAV to the conductor. 
 
Evaluate the feasibility of the UAV design and the methods used for future prototype 
development. 
This report goes through the prototyping process from start to finish where elements from various 
engineering fields come together to form a finished prototype. The prototype serves as a proof-
of-concept for the design choices, construction method, use of materials and control approach 
used.

2 ANALYSIS OF OPERATING ENVIRONMENT 
 
The first step in designing a prototype for the proposed UAV solution is to analyze the intended 
operating environment of the UAV to form a more specific list of requirements. The challenges 
the UAV will face when in operation are defined, ranging from objects such as vibration 
dampeners situated on the power lines that could inhibit the UAV from traversing along the wire 
to the effects of the electromagnetic field generated by a live transmission wire on the operation 
of the sensitive electronics on board the UAV. The results are simplified to suit a proof-of-concept 
prototype and form the design requirements for the UAV. 
  
2.1 CONDUCTOR CONFIGURATIONS 
 
High voltage transmission lines stretch across fields, forests, mountains and lakes, suspended up 
to 40 meters in the air supported by transmission towers spaced between 300 and 500 meters 
apart. A single circuit AC system (Overhead Power Lines, 2017) has three separate conductors in 
either a horizontal or vertical configuration, one for each phase, which can be made up of one to 
four bundled transmission lines separated by isolation clamps. Grounding lines stretch between 
the transmission towers which shield the conductors and intercept lightning strikes. Where 
greater transmission capabilities are required there can be up to four separate circuits on one 
tower line.  
 
Figure 5: Example of single circuit in horizontal configuration with 4 conductors bundled together 
(Skies). 
 
==PAGE:22==

2.1.1 
Line slope 
 
The conductor itself is most commonly made up of aluminum strands twisted around a steel core 
(Overhead Power Lines, 2017). The sag of the conductor depends on temperature variations 
contracting and expanding the metal in the conductor, the span between towers, weight, tensile 
strength, tension and additional load such as ice and snow. The UAV must be able to traverse the 
full length of the conductor including up and down sloped conductors.  
 
2.1.2 
Objects on line 
 
Depending on the configuration of the transmission lines there are a number of obstacles that 
can hinder the UAV in traversing along the conductor. Some common obstacles include: 
● Insulators 
● Bundled conductors with spacers 
● Vibration dampeners 
● Tension clamps 
● Aircraft warning spheres 
● Splices

9 
 
2.1.3 
Effects of close proximity to wire 
 
Since the UAV will be required to operate on a live transmission line careful consideration has to 
be taken to the electromagnetic environment surrounding the conductor and the potential effects 
it can have on the sensors and electronics of the UAV.    
A low-frequency electromagnetic field at 50Hz surrounds the conductors and is comprised of 
decoupled electric and magnetic fields. The magnetic field can be sensed with a typical UAV 
magnetometer (John Matthews, 2013) and therefore the use of magnetometers for precise 
heading determination under a live conductor is not recommended for this particular case due to 
potential interference.  
As the UAV approaches the line there will be a difference in the electric potential of the UAV and 
the line. The intensity of the electric field around the conductor is inversely proportional to the 
distance to the conductor and as the UAV approaches the line the electric field intensity grows 
due to the presence of the conductive UAV frame. The conductive UAV frame distorts the electric 
field until the intensity exceeds the dielectric strength of the air and an arc forms between the 
conductor and the UAV. The current of the arc is limited by the overall capacitance of the UAV 
frame but it is important that the electronics can handle the current generated in the frame. A 
constant electric connection while attached to the line equalizes the electric potential to eliminate 
any further arcing between any parts of the UAV frame and the conductor.  
Although the low-frequency electric field is not likely to have any effect on the electronics there 
is potential of Radio-Frequency (RF) noise from corona discharges. A corona discharge is “an 
electrical discharge brought on by the ionization of a fluid such as air surrounding a conductor 
that is electrically charged” (Corona discharge, 2017). A corona discharge can be produced from 
sharp points in a conductor so the UAV should be designed to limit any sharp points that can cause 
corona discharges to form. The corona discharges can produce RF noise with frequencies orders 
of magnitude higher than the electric field frequency but the effects of RF noise and potential EMI 
shielding is left for future prototype development.  
The use of GPS localization in proximity to power lines is possible and no detrimental effect from 
the electromagnetic fields are expected (J. Michael Silva, 2002).

● Single-circuit configuration, one conductor for each phase in a horizontal configuration.  
● Objects that can impede the movement of the UAV on the conductor are confined to

● Maximum slope in span: °30 
● Maximum conductor or splice diameter: 50mm

2.2.1 
Extended design requirements 
 
The design requirements in section 1.2.3 are then extended with the following requirements: 
● The frame structure must be electrically conductive and maintain a constant connection 
to the conductor to minimize potential differences between parts of the UAV and the 
conductor. 
● The UAV must not rely on the magnetometer to determine heading when in close 
proximity to the line due to potential interference from the magnetic field. 
● The UAV must be able to traverse the conductor at the defined maximum slope and 
conductor diameter.

2.2.2 
Proposed flight and traverse methods 
 
The proposed flight and traverse methods of the UAV are two-fold: Fully autonomous and semi-
autonomous.  
The autonomous method is intended as a suggestion for a future final version of the UAV where 
the UAV can autonomously fly to a point underneath a conductor, traverse along the conductor, 
detach from the conductor, fly underneath the isolators and tower equipment, inspect the 
equipment using an upwards facing camera and attach to the next conductor section. 
The semi-autonomous manual flight envelope includes the capabilities required of the first UAV 
prototype. The operator flies the UAV manually with the aid of a position controller for aligning 
and attaching to the conductor after which the UAV traverses along the conductor using manual 
control.

3 DESIGN AND CONSTRUCTION 
 
The UAV prototype is fully designed and modelled in SolidWorks 2016. The UAV is a quad copter 
in an H frame layout measuring 500 mm between the front and back motors and 460 mm between 
the left and right motors.  
 
 
Figure 7: Final SolidWorks design of the UAV prototype 
==PAGE:27==
13 
 
 
Figure 8: Dimensions between motors in mm. 
 
The UAV is designed to approach the conductor from below using an upwards facing camera to 
detect and align the UAV to the conductor. The V-shaped channel guides the conductor to the 
correct position and a sprung lever with a potentiometer at the lever axis of rotation measures 
the vertical position of the conductor with regards to the UAV. As the conductor makes contact 
with the bottom of the V-guides the potentiometer value reaches a set point and engages the 
rotation of two tube roller arms ensuring they have enough clearance to rotate over the 
conductor. The sprung lever includes a hinged metal slider that keeps constant electrical contact 
with the conductor regardless of conductor diameter or lever angle while a high friction roller 
rolls along the underside of the conductor measuring position and speed using a rotary encoder. 
After attachment the motors are rotated 90° along the motor boom axis, the two front motors 
pointing towards the front and the two back motors pointing towards the back to thrust the UAV 
along the conductor in both directions. 
 
Detailed descriptions are found in the sub-assembly sections in 3.2.

3.1 RAPID PROTOTYPING 
 
Rapid prototyping (Rapid Prototyping, 2017) is an umbrella term covering a group of technologies 
that enable quick fabrication of physical components from computer aided design data. The rapid 
prototyping technologies used in the construction of the UAV prototype are 3D printing, 
otherwise known as additive manufacturing, and CNC routing. The ability to design, fabricate and 
test individual structural components during the design of critical sub-assemblies proved to be of 
great value, resulting in numerous design changes identified in the sub-assembly testing phase. 
 
3.1.1 
3D printing 
 
Structural parts were 3D printed on a Zortrax M300 3D printer. With a 300x300x300 mm printing 
area and print resolution between 140 and 300 microns it is capable of producing the high-
precision structural components required. All parts were printed in High Impact Polystyrene with 
a layer resolution of 0.14 mm. All parts except the motor arm control horns were printed with a 
grid shaped infill to reduce printing time. Test parts printed with solid infill were considered but 
the increased printing time was determined to not be ideal for rapid prototyping.     
 
3.1.2 
CNC 
 
Carbon fiber parts were milled from a 500x480x2 mm carbon fiber sheet on a High-Z S-1000/T  
CNC router. The tool path was generated from the SolidWorks CAD file using SolidCAM and the 
resulting G-code was fed to the WinPCNC software controlling the CNC router. A 3 mm diameter 
Tungsten-Carbide routing bit at 20.000 rpm with a feed rate of 400mm/min produced clean parts 
with no fraying at the edges. 
Note! Cutting carbon fiber produces a fine dust that is harmful when inhaled so proper ventilation 
and dust masks are required.

3.2 SUB-ASSEMBLIES 
 
The four main sub-assemblies that together constitute the mechanical design of the UAV 
prototype are designed and constructed to meet the necessary requirements set forth in sections 
1.2.3 and 2.2.1. For each sub-assembly the analysis behind the chosen design is presented after 
which the mechanical operation and construction is detailed. 
 
3.2.1 
Traversing the conductor 
 
The method of how to drive the UAV along the conductor is the most influential design choice on 
the general UAV frame layout and design of subsequent sub-assemblies. Two solutions are 
considered, the first solution being motor driven tube rollers with a high friction surface coating 
such as silicone or rubber to drive the UAV along the conductor and the second solution to rotate 
the existing brushless motors 90° from a horizontal to a vertical configuration to thrust the UAV 
along the conductor. In comparison the benefits of using the already existing flight motors for 
propulsion far outweighs the use of motor driven tube rollers in the following key areas: 
 
Weight 
Each added component increases the weight and should not be included unless critical to 
the operation of the UAV. By using the existing flight motors for both flight and conductor 
propulsion the only weight added is the two servos and pushrods used to rotate the 
motors. 
 
 
Reliability 
Each added component adds a possible point of failure that can affect the operational 
reliability of the UAV. By using the existing flight motors for on-wire propulsion the only 
added points of failure are the two rotation servos. 
  
 
Ease of integration 
Both the Electronic Speed Controllers (ESC) and the rotation servos are controlled by the 
same type of Pulse Width Modulated (PWM) control signals. Integrating servo operations 
is therefore expected to be within the capabilities of the chosen flight control computer.

blocks, each of which has an internal needle roller bearing to facilitate smooth rotation of the 
motor tube. The right corner block is designed to hold a standard sized servo that connects to a 
3D printed control horn on the outer right section of the motor tube through a pushrod linkage. 
When the UAV is in flight configuration the propeller plane is horizontal and the pushrod linkage 
passes over the servo rotation axis providing a mechanical rotation lock on the motor tube as seen 
in Figure 11. After attaching to the conductor the servo rotates the motor tube 90° with the front 
pair of motors thrusting the UAV forwards and the rear pair of motors thrusting the UAV 
backwards. This enables the UAV to traverse in both directions, pull itself up a sloped wire and 
apply reverse thrust for braking down a sloped wire.

3.2.2 
Conductor attachment 
 
The UAV was designed to approach, align and attach to the conductor from below. By approaching 
from below the conductor is seen as a dark straight line contrasted against the sky, dramatically 
reducing the complexity and processing required for reliable detection when compared to 
approaching the conductor from above. Attaching from below is also preferred when traversing 
the conductor, keeping the center of gravity below the conductor and giving the UAV a naturally 
stable configuration. 
 
 
Figure 12: Conductor attachment sub-assembly. 
 
The front and back attachment sub-assemblies are identical and mirrored around the center of 
the frame. The attachment mechanism features a tube roller with a compound curve compatible 
with conductors of up to 50 mm diameter. The tube roller has three internal 6 mm ball bearings 
and is connected to a rotating arm on a 6 mm steel axle. The rotating arm is constructed from 2 
mm carbon fiber sheet on either side of a 3D printed core that pivots around a 6 mm steel axle. 
An 85x25 mm slot in the upper frame plate allows the arm to recess into the main box structure 
where the incorporated control arm is connected to a servo using a pushrod linkage. 
 
 
==PAGE:33==

The attachment sub-assembly relies on the conductor being positioned along the longitudinal 
center line of the UAV with a minimum vertical clearance between the conductor and tube rollers 
while they rotate in place. The outer pivot axle block holds two perpendicular 10 mm carbon fiber 
tubes that form a V-shape channel guiding the conductor to the correct position for attachment. 
The V-shaped guide channel tubes have a secondary purpose of protecting the exposed propellers 
from accidentally hitting the conductor as well as decreasing the reliance on perfect alignment 
underneath the conductor. The 395x460 mm rectangular area between the end points of the V-
shaped carbon tubes allows the UAV to safely attach to the conductor with an angle error of up 
to 40° when the conductor is centered over the midpoint of the UAV and a positioning error of up 
to +-190 mm when the conductor is parallel to the longitudinal axis of the UAV as seen in Figure

3.2.3 
Conductor positioning 
 
The conductor positioning sub-assembly has three functions 
● Measuring the vertical position of the conductor in the V-channel guide. 
● Measuring the position and speed of the UAV while traversing along the wire. 
● Keeping constant electrical contact between the conductor and the UAV. 
 
A 22 kΩ potentiometer with a 6 mm plastic axle functions as the pivoting axle for the positioning 
lever and a rubber band is used to pull the lever upwards and allow it to keep constant contact 
with the conductor. As the conductor makes contact with the high friction roller the positioning 
lever is pushed down through a slot in the upper frame plate. When the conductor reaches the 
lowest position in the V-guide channel the potentiometer reaches a threshold value engaging the 
tube roller arms which rotate over or away from the conductor depending on which position the 
tube roller arms are in. The contact slider is wired to the upper frame plate creating an electrical 
contact between the conductor and the UAV frame. A secondary hinge on the positioning lever 
itself allows the contact slider to keep constant contact with the conductor as the positioning 
lever is pushed down. This secondary hinge also allows the UAV to keep a constant electrical 
contact while traversing conductors with varying diameters from 30 to 50 mm. While the UAV 
traverses the conductor the high friction roller turns a rotary encoder that keeps track of the 
position and speed of the UAV along the conductor.

3.4 FRAMES OF REFERENCE 
 
The UAV is a 6 Degree of Freedom (6-DOF) rigid body moving in three-dimensional space. To 
describe the movements of the UAV three frames of reference are defined that follow the right 
hand rule for orthogonal coordinate systems: 
 
Body-fixed frame: 
The body-fixed frame of reference is attached to the UAV center of mass with the following 
conventions: 
● The x axis points in the UAVs forward direction of travel (roll axis) 
● The y axis points to the right (pitch axis) 
● The z axis points downwards (yaw axis) 
 
Rotations in x, y and z (roll, pitch, yaw) in relation to the earth-fixed frame are defined in Euler 
angles. 
 
Camera frame: 
The camera frame of reference is attached to the UAV center of mass with the following 
conventions: 
● The x axis points to the right. 
● The y axis points to the UAVs forward direction of travel. 
● The z axis points upwards. 
 
 
Earth-fixed frame: 
The earth-fixed frame has the origin at the takeoff position with the following conventions: 
● The x axis points towards the North pole. 
● The y axis points east. 
● The z axis points downwards to the center of the earth.

5.1 IMAGE PROCESSING 
 
The image processing and feature detection is done in Python using OpenCV 2.4.9 (OpenCV, 
2017). The OpenCV library in Python is a C/C++ wrapper so no significant benefits would come 
from using C/C++ while implementing the script in Python can take advantage of the DroneKit-
Python functionalities to send the position and angle information to ArduCopter as explained in 
section 5.2. 
The Raspberry Pi camera board resolution is set to 320x240 pixels (downscaled from 640x480 
capture resolution) with the frame rate set to 90 FPS. The resolution of the processed image is 
one of the main factors for the computational speed and due to the simple nature of the image 
received from the camera a high resolution image is not needed to achieve a sufficient detection 
rate. The shutter speed is set to automatic and is determined by the included auto-exposure 
algorithm of the camera board.  
The first step is to capture the latest image frame from the camera. Reading a frame from the 
camera sensor is an I/O-bound task (I/O, 2017) so to avoid the effects of I/O latency on the 
detection rate the reading of frames from the camera sensor is placed in a separate thread.  
The Python code can be seen in Appendix A. The PiVideoStream class spawns a new thread, 
continuously polls the camera, grabs the most recent frame from the video stream and stores it 
in a variable. Calling vs.read() returns the latest frame to the main thread for further processing.

5.1.1 
Canny Edge Detector 
 
 
Some feature extraction methods such as the Hough lines transform only look for white pixels in 
a binary image. To reduce computation time it is therefore beneficial to pre-process the image to 
only include two edges of the conductor as white lines on a black background. One method to 
accomplish this is to use the Canny edge detector algorithm (Canny, 2017).  
The Canny edge detector algorithm consists of 5 steps: 
● Reduce noise in the image using a Gaussian filter. 
● Find intensity gradients in the image by filtering with a Sobel kernel. 
● Apply non-maximum suppression to the gradients to “thin” the edge. 
● Apply double threshold to discard weak edges and keep strong edges. 
● Hysteresis tracking to evaluate edges that fall between the low and high threshold. 
 
OpenCV combines the 5 steps in one single function, cv2.Canny(). The input image needs to be a 
single-channel 8-bit image so before applying the Canny Edge Detector algorithm the frame is 
converted from a 3-channel BGR (Blue, Green, Red)  image to a single channel grayscale image 
using cv2.cvtColor(frame,cv2.COLOR_BGR2GRAY). 
 
Using the following parameters the output image is seen in Figure 24: 
● Lower threshold value: 50 
● Upper threshold value: 150 
● Aperture size for Sobel kernel: 3 (default) 
● L2gradient: False 
 
The L2gradient parameter defines which method is used to calculate the image gradient 
magnitude in step 2 of the algorithm. Setting this parameter to True uses a more accurate method 
that makes the algorithm slightly slower which is counter-productive for this particular 
application.

The image is now ready for feature extraction using the Hough Lines transformation (HoughLines, 
2017). The standard Hough Lines transform uses the Hesse normal form (Hough Transform, 2017) 
shown in Equation 2 to describe a detected line:

θ is the angle between the x-axis and the line going through the origin normal to the straight line 
as seen in Figure 25. The (ρ, θ) plane is referred to as the Hough space.  
 
==PAGE:55==

Each white pixel in the Canny output image has a pair of (x, y) coordinates. The Hough line 
algorithm takes each pixel and using Equation 2 calculates ρ  for every θ value between [0;π] 
determined by the θ resolution chosen. These values result in a unique sinusoidal line for each 
pixel in the Hough space. The algorithm calculates these values for each white pixel in the image 
and the resulting sinusoidal lines can be visualized by plotting them in a Hough space graph as 
seen in Figure 26. If the pixels lie on a straight line their sinusoidal lines intersect with the 
intersection point in the Hough space representing the Hough space values for that line. The 
strongest line has the most amounts of intersecting lines at the same point and thus the most 
votes. The output of the OpenCV Hough line function is a list of lines ranked in order of votes, 
with the strongest line with the most amount of votes at the top.  
 
An example can be seen in Figure 26 where the two lines in a Canny output image result in two 
distinct intersections at (204,37) and (238,37) in the Hough space graph. Note that in this example 
the origin is in the top left corner of the Canny output image.

Figure 26: Example of Hough Lines  transform showing the intersections in the Hough space. 
 
 
The normal Hough Lines transform checks every white pixel in the input image which is an 
unnecessarily computationally heavy method to detect two straight lines. The probabilistic Hough 
lines transform (HoughLinesP, 2017) is an optimization of the normal Hough lines transform that 
takes a random subset of the white pixels that is sufficient for line detection. Due to the algorithm 
checking fewer pixels and therefore resulting in fewer intersecting lines in the Hough space the 
vote threshold needs to be lowered compared to when using the normal Hough lines transform.  
 
The probabilistic Hough transform is implemented with the following parameters: 
● ⍴ resolution: 1 pixel 
● θ resolution: π/180 
● Vote threshold: 50 
● MinLineLength: 100 pixels 
● MaxLineGap: 30 pixels 
 
==PAGE:57==
43 
 
The MinLineLength parameter determines the minimum length of detected lines with line 
segments shorter than 100 pixels being rejected. The MaxLineGap parameter determines the 
maximum allowed gap between points to be considered part of the same line. Due to not 
expecting any other line segments in the image other than the two edges of the conductor these 
parameters along with the vote threshold are quite generous which has the added bonus of 
increasing robustness against unwanted objects on the conductor disrupting the straightness of 
the white lines from the Canny edge detector.  
Due to the simple nature of the input image in this case the probabilistic Hough lines transform 
yields less than a 0.06% increase in pure processing speed over the normal Hough lines transform 
in initial tests. The probabilistic Hough lines transform is used due to the different outputs of the 
two functions in OpenCV, with the normal cv2.HoughLines() returning Hough space values (ρ,θ) 
and the probabilistic cv2.HoughLinesP() returning the endpoints of the found lines in (x,y) pixel 
values which suits better for extracting the desired position value in section 5.1.3. 
 
5.1.3 
Calculating position and angle of found lines 
 
The output from the OpenCV probabilistic Hough transform is a list with each found line 
represented by a 4-element vector (𝑥1, 𝑦1, 𝑥2, 𝑦2), where (𝑥1, 𝑦1)and (𝑥2, 𝑦2) are the end points 
of each line. The output list in this case includes the two lines representing both edges of the 
conductor and the strongest line with the most votes is selected for calculating the angle and 
horizontal position of the line at the center of the image plane. This can result in a discrepancy in 
the calculated horizontal position equal to the width of the conductor depending on which edge 
results in the strongest line. During testing this discrepancy did however not have a significant 
enough effect on the positioning of the UAV to warrant any further selection processing between 
the two detected lines.

parameters and attributes have been downloaded from the connected vehicle before returning.  
By reading the referenced DroneKit-Python API documentation it is clear that it is a powerful tool

6 FLIGHT CONTROL 
 
From a control perspective a quad copter is a classic force-controlled under-actuated system 
(Inkyu Sa, 2012) using 4 control inputs to control movement in 6 degrees of freedom. Movement 
on the vertical earth-frame z axis is possible by changing the angular velocities of each rotor by 
the same amount but translational movement along the earth-fixed frame x and y axes and 
rotation around the z axis requires the UAV to change its attitude by varying the angular velocities 
of the rotors to induce changes in the roll, pitch or yaw Euler angles. 
The rotational and translational motion can be approximated as a double integrator system from 
actuator command to attitude angle or translational motion in the earth-fixed frame. Stable 
control therefore requires some form of artificial damping through rotational and translational 
velocity feedback (Inkyu Sa, 2012). This is important for the development of the position 
controller in Section 6.2. 
The position control is implemented as a proof-of-concept, for details regarding the dynamics and 
modelling of a quad copter for future controller development the reader is referred to the 
referenced report (Inkyu Sa, 2012) as well as the excellent description of UAV control systems 
design by Wil Selby (Selby, 2017). 
 
6.1 ATTITUDE CONTROL 
 
Arducopter uses two nested control loops that run at 400 Hz (Attitude Control, 2017) to stabilize 
the UAV to a desired target roll and pitch Euler angle received from the RC transmitter. The outer 
loop converts the angle error between the target angle and the angle measured by the internal 
IMU sensors into a desired rotation rate using a P controller. The inner loop is a PID rate controller 
that takes in the rotation rate error and converts it into high level motor commands. A mixer 
contains information about the physical setup of the UAV and translates the motor commands 
into absolute motor outputs in the form of PWM signals sent to the ESCs.  
The yaw control differs from the roll and pitch in that it receives a target yaw rate input in angular 
velocity to avoid oversensitivity with yaw movements. 
Since even small attitude errors can cause unwanted translational movements an outer position 
control loop is required to control the position of the UAV in the world coordinate system. 
Arducopter includes a position controller that uses GPS localization to control the position of the 
UAV, enabling off-board navigational control in the from the Mission Planner ground station or 
by navigation commands from the Python control scripts using the DroneKit-Python API. 
 
==PAGE:64==
50 
 
To enable the UAV to position itself underneath the conductor the addition of a second position 
control loop is required that takes in the yaw and horizontal position errors from the camera for 
IBVS control and converts it into the desired roll and yaw Euler angles that are then fed into the 
attitude control system.  
A visual representation of the general software architecture is shown in Figure 32. Sensor readings 
are fed to an Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) that estimates the current attitude and position of the 
UAV. Combined with the desired attitude set point from the RC transmitter or position set point 
from off-board control this results in the error signals fed to the position and attitude controllers. 
 
 
 
Figure 32: Visual representation of ArduCopter system architecture.

6.3 POSITION CONTROL 
 
The position control is implemented in the Altitude hold flight mode code in control_althold.cpp 
as seen in Appendix B.  
The altitude hold flight mode takes in attitude and altitude set points from the RC transmitter 
while holding a fixed altitude above ground using altitude data from the EKF. The altitude set point 
can be adjusted from the RC transmitter by raising or lowering the throttle input around a dead-
zone of 30% around the mid-point throttle value. 
When the UAV maintains a stable altitude while hovering in place the net thrust vector divided by 
the UAVs mass is equal to the acceleration due to gravity. The altitude hold flight mode is 
therefore ideal for position control development since the existing altitude controller 
compensates for the offset of gravity and the deflection of the generalized thrust vector at non-
zero roll and pitch Euler angles. 
In this UAV prototype the altitude and pitch is controlled manually from the RC transmitter giving 
the UAV semi-autonomous position control to aid the operator in aligning the UAV to the 
conductor.  
 
 
 
==PAGE:66==

6.3.1 
Yaw controller 
 
A Proportional (P) yaw controller is implemented that takes in the yaw error in degrees received 
from the CAMERA_DATA message and outputs a target yaw rate in centidegrees/s to the attitude 
controller. A proportional controller is sufficient for stabilization to a target yaw rate due to the 
output being an angular velocity around the body-frame z axis. This also removes the need for 
using a magnetometer sensor to determine the current heading for generating a yaw error signal 
which matches the design requirements in Section 2.2.1 when operating a UAV inside the 
magnetic field generated by the current running through the conductor. The target yaw rate is 
compared to the angular velocity around the body-frame z axis from the internal IMU sensors.

The UAV is fitted with the test electronics described in Section 3.3.8. The UAV is connected to a 
100kV transformer through a wire to a 3 mm bolt in the top frame plate of the UAV in a high-

Figure 41.

Measurement data is sent over a 433 MHz serial link to a laptop computer. The voltage is

a prototype with the materials and production capabilities available and the corona discharge 
resistance of the UAV. This needs to be addressed in future prototypes to limit the amount of 
sharp conductive points that can produce corona discharges from the UAV.

8 CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK 
8.1 SUMMARY OF ACHIEVEMENTS 
 
In this project the development of a functional proof-of-concept UAV prototype has been 
achieved. The design and construction of the UAV prototype has succeeded in responding to the 
required operational capabilities and during testing all mechanical features performed as 
designed in a safe and reliable manner. The performance of the visual tracking system is sufficient 
to deliver a high rate of yaw and roll error data to the positioning controllers with a mean 
detection rate of 20 frames per second during flight testing. The yaw and roll controllers were 
successfully implemented in the existing open-source flight controller firmware and the results 
from flight tests indicate that the use of Image-Based Visual Servoing as a control method is 
possible and can successfully position the UAV semi-autonomously underneath a transmission 
line conductor given the operating environment simplifications done in section 2.2.  
The UAV is successful in operating and traversing along a live conductor with an electric potential 
of up to 50 kV without any detrimental effects on the electronics situated in the conductive UAV 
frame nor the streaming of Serial data over a 433 MHz wireless link. 
 
 
==PAGE:79==
65 
 
8.2 FUTURE WORK 
 
The results achieved in this thesis result in several improvements that need to be considered in 
the development of future prototypes.  
With regards to the design and construction the UAV achieved good results for an initial 
prototype. Further work should investigate the possibility of shielding the camera against 
environmental effects such as rain that can have detrimental effects on the clarity of the image 
received. The battery life and operating time of the UAV was not considered as such but future 
work should include investigation of methods to harness power from the magnetic field 
surrounding a conductor to improve the operating time and enable fully autonomous inspection 
over long stretches of transmission lines. The box structure that houses the electronics has 
potential to act as a Faraday cage and the EMI shielding effects and corona generation of the UAV 
frame should be optimized by material choice and frame design, limiting any sharp points on the 
UAV frame. 
With regards to the position control the method used is sufficient for proof-of-concept purposes 
but can be further improved. The addition of an Integral part in both the yaw and roll controllers 
is recommended before operating in an outside environment. External disturbances such as wind 
are likely to push the UAV to either direction perpendicular to the conductor and an integral part 
is needed to compensate for the steady state error introduced by a steady wind pushing on the 
UAV. Sensor fusion using a Kalman filter or other high-level control methods would improve the 
position control as well as modeling and simulation of the UAV dynamics for optimal controller 
gain values.  
A method of detecting the distance between the UAV and the conductor can be achieved by use 
of wide-beam range finders or an upwards facing laser scanner that is resistant to direct sunlight. 
Magnetometers that detect the magnetic field could also be used for conductor detection and 
ranging if precise data about the magnetic field is known.  
The rolling shutter of the camera used could result in image distortion during fast UAV movements 
under the wire. This did not have an effect on the conductor detection of the visual tracking 
system but the use of global shutter cameras would eliminate this effect and should be 
investigated before improving the detection processing or controller design further.
