Abstract
Channel characterization is a crucial step at the time of planning a mobile network.
Attenuation added to the transmitted signal can produce severe limitations in the
coverage and capacity of the network. One of the key parameters at the time of char-
acterizing a channel are Path Losses (PLs). A good characterization of them arises as
a main step to fulﬁll the increasing demand of capacity in upcoming networks. At the
same time continuously changing environments along with this higher data demands
requires live updates of the PLs prediction. Nowadays a wide range of models are
used to predict PLs. They have proven to be reliable and they are widely used in
current network deployments. Nevertheless higher accuracy in the prediction is paid
by an extensive ﬁeld measurement and large environment characterization.
Some researchers have started to use Machine Learning (ML) tools to predict
PLs. They try to improve the prediction while reducing the number of measurements
needed by the high accuracy methods. Nevertheless the data gathering process tend
to be also extensive and the input needed to the models is similar to the traditional
ones. This thesis tries to constrain the inputs to the models to location data infor-
mation. The idea is to train the models with location data extracted from mobile
phones/User Equipment (UE). Therefore we could use these methods in any scenario
without the need of previous tailoring to the environment. We compare two popular
ML algorithms: Support Vector Machines (SVMs) and Neural Networks (NNs). We
succeed at obtaining a good accuracy in the prediction and generalizing with NNs in
two diﬀerent datasets. Also we conclude how, out of two datasets implementation,
generalization arises as a key step at the time of characterizing PLs with ML tools.
Afterwards we implement a NN to assess whether is its possible to use it for online
purposes. This implies to create a low complexity size NN that is able to update the
PLs prediction model with incoming location measurements from mobiles phones/UEs
(online scenarios).
We perform speed tests and complexity size comparisons with
existing literature to show that we are able to create a low complexity model to
calculate PLs usable in continuously changing environments. Also we conclude that
the optimal input space to the model should have information about what Base
Stations (BSs) the UEs are connected to.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Wireless communications(Figure 1.1) relies on a complex transmission medium that
is continuously changing and degrades the signal propagation. This degradation is
caused by many impairments present in the transmission link: obstacles, eﬀects of
atmosphere and noise among others makes the air a propagation medium with many
losses.
At the time of planning a mobile network one of the most challenging tasks is the
channel characterization. When addressing the losses that the channel introduces we
talk about attenuation[1]. This is caused by the eﬀect of many impairments summed.
One of them are the PLs. PL describe how the signal power is attenuated with the
distance. For a ﬁxed distance the PL value remains constant and it describes how
the transmission link attenuates the signal(further details on Section 3.2).
PL has both impacts in the coverage and capacity of mobile networks. First the PL
increases with the distance so it will limit the coverage of the network. Then at some
point the power received will not be enough to provide a good service. Also it is
related with the Signal-to-Noise (S/N) ratio. Basically S/N measures the strength
of our signal(in terms of power) compared with the background noise of the channel.
Therefore an attenuated received signal will lead to worse S/N that has an impact on
the maximum Capacity (C) that we can transmit. The capacity of a link is related
with the Bandwidth (BW) available and the S/N ratio (Shannon theorem)[1]:
C = BW ∗log2(1 + S/N)
(1.1)
The more the signal is attenuated the higher bandwidth we will require to provide
a high data rate. Also capacity has to do with the modulation used( Nyquist band-
width)[2]:
C = 2BW ∗log2(M)
(1.2)
where M is the modulation order. Higher capacities require higher order modulation
but if the channel attenuates a lot the high capacity is limited by a low S/N ﬁgure.
In this sense, the channel modeling is so crucial that mobile standards such as Long
Term Evolution (LTE) uses Adaptive Modulation and Coding (AMC) techniques that
work with a Channel Quality Indicator (CQI) to assess the quality of the channel and
change the modulation to provide the best possible data rates with error free trans-
missions[3]. Therefore a proper characterization of the channel’s PLs plays a mayor

role at the time of designing mobile networks both for coverage and capacity concerns.
On top of that upcoming mobile networks demand an increased capacity that
requires a tight planning of the network resources[4]. This puts an extra eﬀort in

ios. This requires the implementation of live optimization techniques of the channel
to address continuous changing environments (online features).

CHAPTER 2
State of the art
To characterize the channel’s PLs there are a wide range of models.
Traditional
propagation models used to calculate PLs oﬀer a trade-oﬀbetween accuracy and
the needed input data (diﬀraction obstacles, height of antennas and terrain elevation
among many others). The simpler the model, the less accuracy in the prediction we
will obtain.[5] Higher accuracy is paid by an increase of the computational complexity
and the input data needed (eg. deterministic models).
To improve the accuracy of the prediction without increasing the complexity of
the models and the data gathering process, some researchers have started exploring
ML tools to calculate PLs. Nevertheless the input data used does not diﬀer from the
previous models requiring a extensive characterization of the environment (further
details in Section 2.1)
Nowadays the most accurate way of obtaining PLs is through network design soft-
ware where complete geographic information systems are incorporated and allow an
accurate estimation of them. This software not only oﬀers useful tools to plan net-
works but also optimize them with incoming data. Indeed some of them, in the case of
PL models, allow to tune the traditional models with incoming signal measurements.
The main drawback is the expensive price of these programs (ASSET, ATOLL or
NetAct software)[6][7][8]
ML leaves room to explore open source techniques that oﬀers high accuracy/low
complexity models that can be used to plan PLs in mobile networks and suit online
scenarios. At the same time training these models with less inputs would ease the
data gathering process allowing to build more ﬂexible tools usable in any scenario.
2.1
ML and PLs prediction
The ﬁrst point to address is the current use of ML to predict PLs: How many research
has been done and are the results satisfactory enough to consider them an alternative
to traditional PLs calculation?
Most of the study carried out mainly consists on comparative works to test some
ML algorithms and compare them with the traditional PLs models or the in situ PLs
==PAGE:19==

measurements.
The most spread ML method in literature for predicting PLs are NNs. It has
been proved its accuracy for urban[9], suburban[10], rural[11] and indoor[12] environ-
ments. Another method is SVMs. Usually it is found compared with NNs. Some of
the reasons oﬀered to use SVM are the convexity properties of the solution and the
faster training period compared with many layers NNs. The ﬁrst reference to SVM
is [13] for an urban environment research. It is challenging to extract a conclusion
from some of these comparative works since sometimes they oﬀer opposite results
on the optimal ML method to use.[13][14] Thus it is tempting to admit that the
choice of method is case dependent. Also some researchers has used decision trees
to predict PL[15]. However this last algorithm remains quite unexplored in literature.
In all these cases it is clear that the prediction using ML is better (in terms of
evaluation metrics.
eg: Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE)) than the traditional
models. For example, in [14] they compare Okumura Hata versus the ML algorithms
and ML tools have less error in the prediction. Nevertheless Okumura Hata it is
not the optimal method in non Line-of-Sight (nLoS) scenarios because it does not
address diﬀraction losses in a complete way [16]. So taking into account the number
of models available it would make sense to seek one that could really approach the
ML prediction or even improve it.
All the above mentioned researchers use similar inputs to the ML methods than
the one used for the traditional models. Having information about the antenna tilt,
terrain elevation, distance, horizontal and vertical attenuation of the antenna and
obstacles in between among others proves that the ML prediction of the PL will be
accurate enough as seen in all these studies. Also these studies address a Point-to-
Point or Point-to-Multipoint scenarios missing the complexity of a multi BSs network.
In the recent years, some other researchers have tried to reduce the number of
measurements needed as input of the models. Reducing the number of inputs allows
optimization of the network in real time (online fashion). Special attention has been
paid to location data. Some researchers have proposed to use user location informa-
tion as the only input to the models. This is motivated by the easiness of collecting
geolocation data from the mobile phone users[17]. Then, the idea is to continuously
update the models with the incoming measurements. The question to answer here is
how far can we go in terms of accuracy in the prediction only with location data and
if so works, how many users would we need to start relying only on location based
models?
In this line, ML plus location data has been used to optimize other features of a
mobile network[18][17]. In terms of PLs calculation the work that excels is [19]. Here,
the author use location data as input to train the models. Additionally he uses user
trajectories data to improve the accuracy of the calculation. The purpose of this is to
provide a high estimation accuracy under real-time constraints and practical channel
impairments. He implements the online location update with an Adaptive Projected
Subgradient Method (APSM) and a Multi-kernel algorithm. Mainly because they are

As a result of the kernel methods used in both algorithms he implements the

PL network planning is addressed statistically using traditional models.
2.2
Beyond State of the art
The target of this thesis is to investigate whether it is possible to calculate PLs for
a network planning with only location data extracted from UEs. This would allow
us to deploy ML tools in any scenario without the need of previous tailoring to the
environment and pre-data gathering.
To do so, comparative work with NN and SVM is performed in several datasets.
The motivation to carry out the analysis with these two algorithms is the proven PLs
prediction accuracy in existing literature and the ﬂexibility that they oﬀer(explained

3.2
Attenuation and PLs
Attenuation is a complex propagation phenomenon that can be seen as the summed
eﬀect of several individual impairments(Figure 3.1). We can divide them into large
scale and small scale ones:
1. Small scale attenuation: they are called like that because their eﬀect occurs
in distances of the order of the signal wavelength. They are also called multipath
attenuation and they are caused by the several paths that the signal takes due
to reﬂections with terrestrials objects and atmospheric ducting[20]. In the Re-
ceiver (Rx) the diﬀerent waves are added either constructively or destructively.
This lead to ﬂuctuations in the amplitude, phase and delays on the received
signal. They are time variant impairments because either the Transmitter (Tx)
or Rx is moving and the multipath nature change over time. Usually they are
modelled using random time-varying impulse responses.[1]
==PAGE:23==
8
3 Communication and ML background
2. Large scale attenuation: they are appreciable in large distances. We can
distinguish between PL and Shadowing eﬀects. PL describes how the signal
power is attenuated with the distance. Shadowing is caused by the variations
in the attenuation introduced by obstacles in the transmission link. These vari-
ations tend to be random and usually are estimated with statistical models (e.g
log-normal shadowing).[1]
Figure 3.1: Attenuation events
On measurement data we obtain the summed eﬀect of all types of attenuations (Fig-
ure 3.2). In this thesis we are interested in predicting PLs. In order to identify the
PLs we can average the total attenuation of the link and obtain an averaged value of
the PL. Also we can make many measurements from the same point and since PL is
constant(for a speciﬁc distance) we can average all of them and obtain a loyal value

3.4.1
PLs models
PLs models are used in every day networks and provide a reliable estimation of PLs.
But as we mentioned before, they oﬀer a trade-oﬀbetween the model complexity and
the accuracy of the prediction[5].
Usually they are classiﬁed in diﬀerent ways, depending on the literature. A general
classiﬁcation can be into three diﬀerent groups: empirical (also called statistical meth-
==PAGE:27==
12

ods), deterministic and semi deterministic.
Empirical models are based on statistical calculations obtained from measure-
ments from real environments[27]. Simple empirical models use parameters such as
distance between Tx and Rx antennas, frequency of emission or heights of Tx and Rx
antennas. More complete methods address diﬀraction losses and other impairments.
There are many empirical methods developed and usually they work for a certain sce-
nario (urban, semi urban, rural) and a speciﬁc frequency range. Some famous ones
are Okumura-Hata or COST 231 which incorporates a more complete obstacle losses
prediction. The main advantage of these models is that they are not too complex
and allow a fast calculation of PLs[5]. For a ﬁrst approach of the PL value (e.g in the
Nominal planning step of Figure 3.3) they might be enough.
Deterministic models use physical properties of waves to obtain PLs. They
are considered the most accurate way of calculating PLs. Nevertheless, they require
complex calculations since waves propagation prediction with obstacles tends to be
challenging. Then in search of accuracy we need much more input data addressing
every obstacle. The calculation is computationally heavy and even unfeasible in some
scenarios.
Ray tracing models are popular deterministic methods.
Finally, semi-
deterministic models are a combination of the two previous methods.[13]
A thorough comparison of these methods, out of the scope of this thesis, can be
found in [28][29]. Its worth to point out that there is not a best model for all cases;
the accuracy and optimal use of these methods is case dependent. Despite there are
some advises to use them; in terms of frequency of operation or conditions that we are
addressing it is common to use them in several scenarios with diﬀerent speciﬁcations
that the recommended in the model.

3.5.1.1
Bias-Variance trade-off
After choosing a proper ML algorithm, training our model and obtain the predictions
for our test subset (steps 3,4 and 5 in Figure 3.4) we can perform evaluation tests
(step 6 in Figure 3.4). The idea behind it is to assess what is the error in the pre-
diction for the test and training subsets.At this point we introduce a basic concept
to take into account when doing predictions with ML: the Bias-Variance trade-oﬀ.[31]
We call bias to the assumptions made by a model to make the target real dis-
tribution of our response easier to learn. A higher bias may indicate that we are
making more assumptions about the shape of the target distribution. This can leads
to under ﬁtting the model, meaning that we are simplifying the real distribution of
the response.
Variance refers to how our prediction would change using diﬀerent training sub-
sets. When we have a model with high variance smaller changes in the training set
will lead to big changes in the prediction. This can lead to overﬁt the model. Mean-
ing that the model is potentially more sensitive to every observation in the dataset
and some of them may not be representative of the real distribution of the response
variable (e.g outliers).

3.5.2
Support Vector Machines
SVM is a popular technique among statisticians that was initially conceived to solve
classiﬁcation problems.[32] Its popularity comes from the several settings and ﬂexi-
bility that it oﬀers as well as the convexity property of the solution. The most basic
SVM classiﬁer is called Maximal Margin classiﬁer and it is linked with the use of
hyperplanes. In a n-dimensional space an hyperplane is a ﬂat subspace of n-1 dimen-
sion. The idea is to use an hyperplane to separate classes. In a n dimensional space
an hyperplane is deﬁned as [32]:

the largest. The hyperplane is chosen after solving an optimization problem and it
is called maximal margin classiﬁer. The closest observations to the hyperplane(the
ones that deﬁne the margin) are usually called support vectors.
However we can work with datasets where the observations can not be separated
by a maximal margin classiﬁer(some samples can fall in the wrong side of the margin

vector classiﬁers and are regulated by diﬀerent parameters. Basically they determine
the number of observations that can lie within the margin. In the case explained
above (Linear hyperplane) it is regulated by the Cost parameter which determines
the width of the margin. This has a high inﬂuence in the bias-variance trade-oﬀof our
model[32]. When the Cost value is large the width of the margin is larger. Therefore
more observations are allowed to violate the margin. This make the model to reduce
its variance, since the model it is not so aﬀected by individual observations, but in
exchange it is likely to increase its bias, leading to oversimplifying the model.

3.5.3.4
Mini-batches
So far we have consider working with the whole training set (batch method). An
alternative is to work with online(stochastic) tools. Instead of calculating the update
of the weights from the summed error term of all the observations we can compute
the gradient and the update sample by sample. In redundancy datasets this method
handle data much more eﬃciently since the gradient of redundant terms tend to be
the same. Nevertheless it requires more computation to update all the weights. So
as a further improvement we ﬁnd a popular method called mini-batch technique[40]
The idea behind it is to divide the training subset into several mini-batches of a spe-
ciﬁc size and run them individually through the network. We have to calculate the
gradient n times which corresponds to the number of iterations( training size divided
by the size of the mini-batches). Once we run forward and backwards all the training
dataset we have completed one epoch. Also as we will see later mini-batch technique
is better to work with our matrix notation NN than the batch method.
A possible drawback from this technique is that decreasing the training size the
gradient might not point at the right direction to decrease the loss function. A trade-
oﬀhas to be found. For example by increasing the mini-batch size and decreasing
the learning rate. Nevertheless they are considered the most eﬃcient way of updating
==PAGE:40==

the parameters and therefore we will use this approach in this thesis.
Tuning a NN is a complex task and there are many other tools that can be stud-
ied. For instance, we can use more eﬃcient methods to optimize the NN than the
gradient descent such as conjugate gradients,Newton and quasi-Newton methods or
Levenberg Marquardt methods.[41].These methods are out of the scope of this thesis.
Nevertheless the reason to choose gradient descent is that it is the optimizer with less
memory use since it only works with ﬁrst order derivatives. Here we have presented
all the techniques that will be implemented in our NN analysis.
3.5.4
Validation Methods
Finally we introduce the validation methods. They are popular techniques used in
ML to improve predictions.
The simplest validation method is the validation set
approach. Mainly consists on randomly dividing the dataset into a training subset
and a validation subset. The validation subset in which we test the models works
as an estimate of the test error. The main drawback of such approach is that if we
change the initial random selection of observations we will obtain diﬀerent test error
results. This leads to a high variability depending on this initial sample distribution.
Moreover we are only using half(or whatever size we decide for the training subset)
of the samples to train the model. This also leads to an overestimation of the test
error.
Cross-validation (CV) is a popular choice at the time of improving predictions. The
idea behind it is to divide our dataset into several folds (n folds) of the same size
and train the model with n-1 folds and test it into the remaining fold (validation
fold). After training the model we compute the evaluation metric chosen( eg. MSE
or RMSE) in the validation fold. This process is repeated n times and ﬁnally we
obtain the k-fold CV estimate by averaging all the n estimates obtained. The choice
of the number of folds is not trivial. A popular choice in literature is to divide the
training subset into 5 or 10 folds.[32]. E.g: for visualization purposes the 5-fold CV
method is shown in Figure 3.12.
One variant from this method is Leave-one-out-cross-validation (LOOCV) which
consist in dividing the dataset in n folds equal to the number of observations. Then we
train the model with n-1 observations and validate them in the remaining observation.
Despite this method can work in some scenarios it is likely to produce overﬁtting be-
cause all the models obtained after the n training’s are highly correlated. Thus when
computing the average estimate of all the n evaluation metrics, the high correlation
will produce overﬁtting of the ﬁnal model. Another drawback is the computational
complexity of training the model in large datasets.

We will build a 2 layer NN with the following features: backpropagation algorithm

To make the code scalable (needed to analyze results with several hidden units)
we will work with matrix notation. Also that’s the reason to implement mini-batch
techniques. Since we are working with some large datasets, matrix operations leads
to large values for the update of the error terms when backpropagating. This leads
to obtain extreme values after the sigmoid function (either 1 or 0) and therefore the
output error term is very large. To avoid that, we separate the data into several
mini-batches. In the same line we also scale the data between 0 and 0.25. Also we
scale to have all the inputs with the same importance. If we do not scale some weights
will have much higher value than others leading to biased results.

Future work
First comparative analysis has to be done. The next step would be to compare the
outcome of this ML models, trained with location predictors, with existing PL models
and made some evaluation tests. This would allow us to understand what is the PLs
accuracy pursued and lead us to discard some of these methods in case they don’t
fulﬁll the accuracy in the prediction needed. At the same time comparative analysis
with the top-notch software mentioned [6][7][8] could also be an interesting analysis.
==PAGE:80==
7.1 Future work
65
Another interesting approach would be to train one method in a speciﬁc dataset
(corresponding to a particular scenario) and try to predict the PLs in another diﬀer-
ent one(assuming both of them use the same mobile standard and frequency band).
For the online application purposes some real ﬁeld tests can be performed. This
would imply to update the network with incoming data(every speciﬁc time) and an-
alyze the performance in our NN. Also this would help us to realize about what are
the needs of such a deployment (e.g is it enough the accuracy of a local minima ob-
tained with gradient descent or is it really memory use such a concern at the time of
updating NNs).
Another point to address is the feature extraction needed for the methods. This
implies to study how the network receives the location data and how would it calculate
the desired predictors that we need in our model.
