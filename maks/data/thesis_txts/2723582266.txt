Abstract 
 
 
In this thesis, three different enzyme systems have been extensively investigated for their 
ability to undergo liquid-liquid phase separation. Moreover, advanced biophysical instruments 
were applied to demonstrate how phase separation can be quantified and examined in high 
concentration enzyme solutions. Laser light scattering, absorbance, and fluorescence 
measurements were utilized to examine the type of phase separation and the conditions under 
which it is induced. Quantification of phase separation was primarily performed using the 
fluorescence-based method known as Taylor dispersion induced phase separation (TDIPS). 
Initial experiments were conducted on the well studied protein lysozyme, followed by 
investigation of two different ⍺-amylases (Bacillus licheniformis ⍺-amylase (BLA) and 
Bacillus halmapalus ⍺-amylase (BHA). Mainly NaCl and pH were investigated for the effect 
on phase separation. Lysozyme results suggested a NaCl driven phase separation at four 
different pH levels (4.2, 6.4, 8.2 and 10) with a reduced amount of phase separation as a 
function of higher pH. Opposite lysozyme, BLA results showed a complete NaCl dependency 
to stay in solution. Interestingly, for unclear reasons, this protein also displayed a dilution 
induced precipitation. With TDIPS it was shown that phase separation of the BHA enzyme was 
induced by pH reduction. In addition BHA exhibited reversibility of assemblies at high pH and 
irreversible assemblies in low pH.  
Phase separation was measured in all three enzyme systems using TDIPS, with or without NaCl 
or by the induction of pH. Moreover, spectroscopic analysis was used to measure reversibility 
in phase separated samples to gain information about the characteristics of the insoluble phase.

1 Introduction 
 
 
1.1 
Background 
 
Proteins are dependent on their structure to fulfil their function inside a cell. Enzymes with 
catalytic importance are no different and are also functionally dependent on remaining in their 
native state. Their native state also implies the mobility of the protein and the ability to interact 
with their surrounding environment contributing to their stability. Therefore, being in solution 
is a necessity for protein functionality. A protein can in specific instances undergo phase 
transitions which means that the solution enters a state with a new composition of proteins and 
solvent. In some cases, this leads to a change of the native state resulting in aggregation, but in 
other cases functional assemblies can be produced. The underlying principle of protein phase 
separation involves the concept of solubility. The solubility describes the ability of the protein 
to dissolve in solution. In an elementary sense, the protein exists in an equilibrium state 
between a soluble and an insoluble phase 1. The solubility is affected by protein/protein 
interactions such as electrostatic and hydrophobic forces, among others which all contribute to 
the Gibbs free energy of the system 2. Generally, colloidal stability is dependent on interactions 
between every element present in a solution, such as solvent composition, protein, 
concentrations, pH etc 2. Whether the transitioning into another phase results in crystallization, 
aggregation, or other types of insoluble states is highly dependent on specific protein/protein 
interactions and favourable conditions. Phase transitioning in protein solutions is driven by 
systemic energetically favourable interactions. In a solution where the proteins are dissolved 
and a one phase regime is in effect, the G is negative given that this is the spontaneous 
composition of the solution. The change in partial Gibbs free energy per change in number of 
molecules in a solution is defined as the chemical potential µ under constant temperature, 
pressure, and number of other valent species, where n is the number of molecules 3:

Thus, the chemical potential provides information about the stability of the protein solution 
dependent of the number of proteins. Increasing the concentration of protein and the number 
of molecules (n), the chemical potential increases which is essentially unfavourable since G 
will be positive if  𝑛 is positive. A higher chemical potential means that the proteins have a 
higher capacity and potential for inducing change is the system 3. When a protein solution 
enters a two-phase regime, the systemic chemical potential changes, and there is now an 
identical partial chemical potential of the system in each phase. The G changes given that the 
proteins are now in a different composition with each other and the solvent, and the number of 
proteins is also different in the two phases. What drives the phase transition is essentially a 
more stable solution. This means that for phase separation to occur, multiple thermodynamic

factors must contribute favourably to the G in a higher degree in the two-phase system than 
in the one phase regime.  
The concept of phase separation has been a topic of research in the biophysics and biochemistry 
field for a long period of time. A phenomenon like crystallography is a big part of this type of 
research and has been investigated intensively for structural purposes 4. More recently, the 
concept of liquid-liquid phase separation (LLPS) has gained increased attention in the 
biophysics community. The study of LLPS has gained its momentum due to a relevance in a 
wide range of biological, pathological, and biophysical questions 5, relevant not only to the 
academic world in basic research, but also for the industry. The pharmaceutical and 
biotechnology industries working with high quantities of large molecules such as proteins, all 
face solubility issues at some point. Although the concentration of protein for a 
biopharmaceutical can vary widely, in many cases it requires several hundreds of milligrams 
per millilitre 6. Having a protein in such a high concentration can compromise the stability and 
thus solubility of the protein 7. The industry mentions opalescence, a cloudy solution, that can 
be mistaken for a turbid one and often thereby mistaken for a precipitated solution 6. Mistaking 
a turbid solution for a precipitated one could potentially result in discarding an LLPS solution. 
Thus, determining the actual phase of the solution and its behaviour in terms of reversibility or 
aggregation can be of great value. Somewhere on the spectrum between proteins in solution 
and precipitated amorph aggregates are phases that, if understood correctly, can be informative 
or perhaps even taken advantage of. Liquid-liquid phase separation is somewhere in that 
spectrum and could be the key to understanding how the proteins interact and remain stable at 
high concentrations.  
Research in LLPS has been done on proteins and polymers such as amyloid fibrils due to the 
connection it has with neurodegenerative diseases like Sclerosis, Alzheimer’s disease, and 
Parkinson’s disease 8,9. Enzymatic systems like lysozyme have also previously been studied 
for its phase separating properties 10, however, not in the same approach as proposed in this 
project. This thesis aims to investigate which factors influence specific enzymes to undergo 
LLPS and to improve our understanding of the techniques and instruments used in the 
department laboratory for the study of phase separation in highly concentrated enzyme 
solutions.  
 
1.2 
Liquid-liquid phase separation 
 
Liquid-liquid phase separation describes the coexistence of two phases, just like the separation 
of oil and water 11. This process occurs naturally in cells where membrane-less organelles 
compartmentalize to organize the proteins which are supposed to interact with each other and 
avoid unwanted interactions 12. It is a dynamic process that occurs when certain conditions are 
met for a solution such as, pH, salt, temperature, crowding effect, etc 13. These effects can be 
manipulated and induced in vitro to push a protein into undergoing LLPS. The phase separation 
happens for a particular system when a threshold concentration is surpassed together with 
specific conditions. This threshold is correlated to the state of supersaturation. A supersaturated 
solution is an unstable solution where there is more than the maximum amount of solute 
capable of being dissolved 7. The conditions for which proteins undergo LLPS and their ability 
to do so can vary from protein to protein. The characteristics of a protein influence how it will 
==PAGE:7==
 
6
react to a change in pH, ionic strength etc. Nonetheless, proteins share the fact that phase 
separation only takes place if it is more thermodynamically favourable for the solution 3. The 
result of LLPS is the formation of structures generally described as droplets or condensates 
which then constitute a denser phase of the solution. Even though the entropy of a solution 
typically increases in a mixed state, and thermodynamic systems tend to favour higher entropy, 
the system is lower in energy after droplet formation 14. The entropy increase in the dilute phase 
upon phase separation makes up for the entropy loss in the dense phase. The dense phase 
contributes favourably to G by the enthalpic binding energy. The droplet formation is a 
reversible process making it easier to work with and identify which sort of phase the solution 
is in 12. 
An LLPS phase diagram is constructed by the binodal line (Figure 1 A). The binodal line is a 
function of the protein concentration and environmental conditions, that will drive the solution 
from a one-phase to a two-phase regime. The concentration of protein inside the droplets and 
in the dilute phase does not change by an increase in protein concentration, only the volume 
fraction of the phase will increase. This concept is described by the lever rule from which the 
volume fractions of the phases can be calculated. A tie line can be drawn to the binodal line, 
and the volume fractions can be determined using the length to each side of the binodal line 3. 
Theoretically, the solution will at some point shift the dilute phase to be inside the droplets, 
and the dense phase will be the surrounding solution until the protein concentration is so high 
that the solution enters the one-phase regime again (Figure 1). The binodal curve describes 
where the two-phase regime starts for a liquid solution, however, the two-phase regime does 
not only constitute condensates. The spinodal line indicates the areas of the diagram where the 
proteins are nucleation prone to form droplets (Figure 1 B). Below the spinodal line, spinodal 
decomposition occurs leading to a fluid stochastic liquid phase separation without the need for 
nucleation. The model that best describes the thermodynamic argument for the process of LLPS 
is the Flory Huggins theory. This model considers the mixing of two components, one of them 
being the solvent and the other being a homo polymer. The free energy of the system can be 
calculated based on the energy needed for dispersing the polymer and solvent and the volume 
fraction of the two components (entropy) 15. This will show that the energy of the solution in a 
phase separated state is lower than in one phase. As mentioned, the Flory Huggins theory is 
based on homo polymers and not folded proteins, therefore this is not fully translated for phase 
separation of the enzymes in this thesis. The general underlying thermodynamic rationale for 
enzymatic phase separation involves the nucleation and growth rate of condensates among 
other factors that contribute to a favourable process.

1.3.1 Taylor dispersion induced phase separation (TDIPS) 
TDIPS has earlier been applied on the IDP Ddx4 which phase separates at concentrations much 
lower than the ones achieved in this thesis 22. Here TDIPS is put to the test for its application 
for quantification of LLPS in high concentration folded enzyme systems. First the capillary is 
filled with buffer, and afterwards a small volume of protein sample is pushed into the capillary 
which we call the “plug”. A buffer is now injected into the capillary behind the plug at a certain 
pressure causing the plug to disperse (Figure 3).

droplets and the green area represents the plug containing protein and NaCl. The blue colour in the bottom half of the figure 
represents protein that has dispersed from the plug.  
TDIPS uses Taylor dispersion within the capillary to separate a part of the plug into the analyte 
solution, which is being tested for its phase separation effect on the protein. Imagine the protein 
is monomeric and in solution inside the plug where a high concentration of NaCl is present. 
When pressure is applied to the system, the tip of the plug is exposed to the environment 
==PAGE:10==
 
9
surrounding the plug which could have lower or no NaCl at all causing the protein to undergo 
LLPS or another form of phase separation. Black dots in Figure 3 represent the formation of 
larger particles formed when the solution dispersed into the buffer indicating phase separating 
conditions. Large particles stuck in the centre of the capillary meet the detector first and appears 
as spikes on the output curve, and those formed near the sides of the capillary, where the flow 
is very slow, will fall behind 23. The NaCl in the capillary diffuses quick and evenly and will 
therefore be more compactly dispersed during the run. The monomeric protein separated from 
the NaCl disperses more than NaCl due to lower diffusion rates. Particles formed due to phase 
separation can have different characteristics, some of them are large enough to have close to 
zero diffusion, and some of them are still small enough to diffuse slowly. The black interrupted 
line in Figure 4 represents the distribution of large non-diffusive particles starting at T1/2. Flow 
focusing is a theory of the flow profile for particles formed in the middle of the capillary which 
slowly diffuse into a slower flow layer and reach the detector between Tavg and T1/2. The same 
phenomenon is observed if the assembly is formed in the back of the plug but diffuses towards 
the centre of the capillary, where it picks up speed, goes through the plug and meets the detector 
before the Tavg.

1.4 
Probe Drum 
 
The Probe Drum is a good tool for investigating the effect of temperature on phase separation 
in a system and on the reversibility of a two-phase solution. The Probe Drum has been exploited 
for the ability of measuring absorbance, fluorescence, turbidity, and scattering. Absorbance 
measures how much light that penetrates the solution and reaches the detector on the other side. 
If less light than was emitted has reached the detector this gives rise to a signal in the 
absorbance. Specific aromatic residues absorb light at 260-280 nm 24. The absorbance detector 
can be utilized for measuring the turbidity by recording at a wavelength where nothing absorbs. 
Thus, a signal on the absorbance at e.g. 600 nm would therefore not be a result of protein 
absorption but a result of scattering of the light. The more turbid the solution is, the less light 
will penetrate giving a higher absorbance which is in fact turbidity. The laser light available in 
the Probe Drum provides a coherent light source, which is measured at a 90 º angle (Figure 5).

1.5 
Prometheus Panta & Nano differential scanning fluorimetry 
 
The Panta provides the option to measure thermal unfolding of a protein or produce a size 
analysis among other functions. It measures unfolding by monitoring the laser scattering, 
fluorescence, and turbidity of a solution under the treatment of heating or cooling. The little 
brother to the Prometheus Panta is the Prometheus, where the technique Nano differential 
scanning fluorimetry (NanoDSF) is available. This instrument was used in one case instead of 
the Panta due to technical issues with the Panta. The NanoDSF can also monitor protein 
unfolding but only measures the turbidity and fluorescence. The size analysis exploits dynamic 
light scattering (DLS) to generate a hydrodynamic radius.  
 
1.5.1 Dynamic light scattering  
As opposed to the static light scattering measurements on the Probe Drum, where the intensity 
is analysed, the DLS considers instead the development of the intensity over time. In specific, 
the DLS measures the rate of Brownian motion of particles in dispersion and thereby the 
diffusion. When the laser light illuminates the molecules in a solution, the detector measures a 
scattered light intensity. Because the molecules in the solution are dynamic, the intensity will 
be different over time, creating a fluctuating intensity signal (Figure 6). The fluctuation signal 
is different depending on whether the solution contains smaller or larger molecules. With faster 
diffusion from small molecules also comes rapid fluctuations and vice versa. A correlation 
coefficient is considered by taking snapshots of the fluctuation curve with nano- to 
microseconds apart to see how the fluctuation curve correlates. The curve of a slow diffusive 
solution correlates longer than a rapid diffusive solution. From the autocorrelation function, 
the translational diffusion coefficient can be derived, and by using the Stokes-Einstein 
Equation, the size of the molecule in solution can be calculated 25.

1.6 
Enzymes of interest  
 
The project began with a focus on the well-researched enzyme lysozyme to familiarise with 
the laboratory instruments and to learn the techniques. The protein weighs 14.3 kDa and can 
be dissolved in water, HCl, buffer etc. at hundreds of milligrams per millilitre of concentration 
providing great flexibility. Combined with information about which conditions lysozyme phase 
separates under, made it a good test system to show if the method strategy was sound. After 
initial experiments were tested on lysozyme, two similar -amylases were provided by 
Novozymes for investigation. Based on a brown-like colour in one of the powdered proteins, 
it appeared impure, but one of the two appeared whiter than the other. Thus, only the protein 
called Bacillus licheniformis -amylase (BLA) was utilized for the project. After working with 
BLA, a third enzyme was investigated with LLPS experiments which was also provided by 
Novozymes. This enzyme is called Bacillus halmapalus (BHA) and is also an amylase, 
however, not commercially available at the purity provided for this project.

2 Materials and methods  
 
 
2.1 
Enzymes 
 
The hen egg white lysozyme was purchased from MP Biomedicals as a crystallized, freeze-
dried powder of light beige color and stored at -20 °C. The Bacillus licheniformis ⍺-amylase 
(BLA) was purchased from Sigma Aldrich (Merck), expressed, and purified by Novozymes 
and stored in lyophilized form. The protein purity was estimated to be > 93 % with SDS-page 
by Merck. The total protein content in the formulation was determined with the Bicinchoninic 
acid assay (BCA) to be 60 - 80 % with 500 - 1500 units/mg 26. It is unspecified what the 
remaining percent are in the formulation. 
The Bacillus halmapalus ⍺-amylase (BHA) was provided by Novozymes who expressed and 
purified it before triple recrystallisation of the enzyme. 
 
2.2 
Preparation of protein stocks  
 
The lysozyme stocks were prepared by dissolving the powder either in milli Q water or in 50 
mM HCl. The 86 mg and 75 mg of the lysozyme powder were weighed using a Sartorius scale 
and dissolved in 229 µl HCl or 200 µl milli Q water (milli Q) respectively to obtain a theoretical 
target concentration of 300 mg/ml in both stocks. After dissolving the powder, the stocks were 
centrifugated at 16.6 rcf for 10 minutes to remove the insoluble fraction. The final protein 
concentration remaining in the supernatant was determined by UV absorbance to be 
approximately 255 mg/ml in both stocks. A lysozyme stock was produced and purified by size 
exclusion chromatography (SEC) to remove potential impurities. The stock was made of 82.5 
mg of lysozyme dissolved in 231 µl 10 mM NaPi pH 6.8 50 mM NaCl and eluated in the same 
buffer. Purification was carried out using HiLoad Superdex 75 pg preparative size exclusion 
column (Cytiva) connected to an Äkta pure FPLC system (Cytiva) with a flow rate of 1 ml/ml 
using a 5 ml loop. Fractions obtained from SEC were up-concentrated by using a 6 ml spin 
filter with a 5 kDa membrane cut-off to 46 mg/ml. The retentate from the ultrafiltration (ca. 
700 µl) was flash-frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at -80 ℃ in 50 µl aliquots. Each thawed 
aliquot was centrifuged for 10 minutes at 16.6 rcf before use.

2.3 
Concentration determination  
 
Protein concentrations were determined by UV absorbance at 280 nm using a Nanodrop 
spectrophotometer (Thermo Scientific). Stocks were diluted 100-200 times to avoid errors due 
to the light scattering or detector saturation. The protein concentrations were calculated 
according to Lambert-Beer’s law using the extinction coefficient of each protein obtained from 
their sequence using the online tool ProtParam (ExPASy, Switzerland). Protein concentration 
measurements were done in triplicates or duplicates to ensure reliable estimates. 
 
2.4 
Probe Drum Spectroscopic analysis  
 
Lysozyme phase separation was investigated in three experiments by measuring the absorbance 
and static light scattering as a function of temperature using the Probe Drum instrument. 
Concentration details for the experiments are specified in the results section. The absorbance 
of the samples was measured at 600 nm in all Probe Drum experiments. 50 µl of NaCl induced 
phase separated lysozyme in acidic pH were placed in a 3.00 mm light path quartz cuvette 
(Hellma Analytics), and the turbidity and scattering were monitored during a thermal program.

14
The sample in the cuvette was gradually heated from 20 to 60 ℃ and cooled back at a 2 ℃/min 
rate. In an additional experiment, a phase separated lysozyme sample was placed in the cuvette, 
which was preheated to 40 ℃ and cooled down to 14 ℃ and heated to 40 ℃ again at a 2 ℃/min 
rate. In a third experiment, the phase separated lysozyme sample was cycled through a program 
where the temperature increased from 20 to 40 ℃ and then back to 20 ℃ again. Approximately 
15 minutes went by between each cycle.  
Dilution induced phase separation was examined for BLA. A stock of 211 mg/ml in 40 mM 
HEPES buffer pH 5.7 (Table 1) was diluted 50 times and heated from 20 to 40 ℃ and 
subsequently cooled to 20 ℃ again. The same program (20-40-20 ℃) was used to assess 
crowding induced phase separation for BHA using PEG 8000 (Sigma, USA). 
 
2.5 
TDIPS 
 
The FIDA 1 instrument (FidaBio, Denmark) was used to conduct TDIPS experiments to

Buffers and Opentrons 
 
The OT-2 pipetting robot (Opentrons) was employed for filling the 96-well plates for TDIPS 
experiments and capillary loading for the Panta to ensure excellent reproducibility and reduce 
the potential source of errors. The robot was programmed with a custom-made script from the 
referenced paper 16.  
Four main buffer stocks were produced (Sodium carbonate, NaPi, NaAc, and HEPES). The 
666 mM phosphate buffer at pH 6.8 was created by mixing 1 M monobasic and 0.5 M dibasic 
phosphate stocks in the ratio 1:2. The dibasic stock was made by dissolving disodium hydrogen 
phosphate (Sigma, USA) in milli Q, and the monobasic stock was made by dissolving sodium 
dihydrogen phosphate (Sigma, USA) in milli Q. The produced 666 mM stocks were aliquoted 
and kept at -20 °C. A 0.5 M HEPES buffer pH 5.7 was produced by dissolving HEPES (Sigma, 
USA) in milli Q. The NaAc stock was made with 1 M in concentration pH 4.4 by dissolving 
anhydrous sodium acetate (Sigma, USA) in milli Q. A 0.2 M sodium carbonate stock at pH 10 
was made by mixing sodium bicarbonate (0.2 M) and sodium carbonate (0.2 M) in a 1:1 ratio 
and titrated with a 1 M NaOH stock to reach pH 10. Subsequent dilutions of all the buffers 
were diluted with milli Q and titrated with 1.5 M HCl or 1 M NaOH to alter pH. A 5 M NaCl 
stock was made by dissolving NaCl in milli Q and used throughout the project  
 
2.7 
Protein sizing and stability analysis  
 
The molecular sizes and thermal stability of all proteins used in this thesis were measured by 
dynamic light scattering and differential scanning fluorimetry (nanoDSF) using Prometheus 
instruments (NanoTemper, Germany). Protein stocks (10 μL) were loaded into standard grade 
capillaries (NanoTemper, Germany), which were sealed in long experiments to prevent 
evaporation during incubation. The capillaries were placed in a closed thermally controlled 
compartment of the machine. The excitation power of the laser was set automatically by the 
software to obtain the best signal. The DLS, SLS, and fluorescence intensities of all samples 
were measured automatically by the instrument at a constant temperature (20 °C) for 16 hours, 
or during gradual heating from 25 to 95 °C at a 2 ℃/min heating rate. Solvent was indicated to 
account for viscosity in DLS accordingly.

3 Results and discussion  
 
 
The results and discussion sections are divided into three main parts, one dedicated to each of 
the studied enzymes, starting out with lysozyme and followed by the two ⍺-amylase results.  
 
3.1 
Lysozyme 
 
The following section consists of the results from LLPS condition screenings and LLPS 
characterization on lysozyme. The first sub-section presents TDIPS data generated on the FIDA 
1 with the program specified in materials and methods (Table 3 and Table 4), followed by 
results generated with the Probe Drum. 
 
3.1.1 TDIPS 
Since lysozyme is a well studied enzyme, initial experiments were inspired by literature 
describing LLPS in the system 10. Lysozyme has been reported to undergo LLPS in low pH 
and with a significant effect of NaCl. In Figure 7, TDIPS has been run with a plug of 40 mg/ml 
lysozyme in NaAc buffer pH 4.4. Five different 100 mM NaAc buffers of the same pH with 
increasing NaCl concentration were used as analyte buffers. The plots indicate very little phase 
separation from 1.32, 1.44 and 1.8 M NaCl concentrations. Few spikes are observed, however, 
these are very low in intensity and barely significant. The slight flattening of the signal in the 
upslope of the peak is a better indication of formed assemblies.  
 
Figure 7: TDIPS with a plug containing 40 mg/ml lysozyme in 50 mM HCl run with 100 mM NaAc analyte buffers with 
increasing NaCl concentrations.  
By increasing the concentration in the plug to 250 mg/ml and running the experiment again, a 
clear difference is noticed (Figure 8). This observation is expected, since the theory tells us that 
phase separation in protein systems is concentration dependent 7. This is partly due to the 
increased protein/protein interactions that occur in higher concentration solutions. In Figure 8, 
we observe signal spikes with high fluorescence intensity. It appears that the peaks are 
flattening as the NaCl increases. This is partly due to assemblies formed early in the experiment 
==PAGE:18==
 
17
that reach the detector and generate these signal spikes. The higher the signal, the bigger the 
assembly, and the flatter the peak due to much of the protein already reaching the detector 
ahead of the average peak. These initial TDIPS experiments suggested that 40 mg/ml was too 
low, and a 250 mg/ml concentration was too high for the purpose of gaining the most 
information from the plots as possible. Notably the time normalization in Figure 8 could be 
better. The poor normalization could be attributed to the very high protein concentration which 
increases the viscosity and is not accounted for.  
  
Figure 8: TDIPS with a plug containing 250 mg/ml lysozyme in 50 mM HCl run with 100 mM NaAc pH 4.4 analyte buffers 
with increasing NaCl concentrations. 
Knowing that lysozyme undergoes LLPS at acidic pH with NaCl, a screening experiment was 
conducted where both NaCl concentration and pH were varied. In this experiment, a stock of 
lysozyme dissolved in 50 mM HCl with a concentration of 112 mg/ml was used. Four different 
buffer systems were applied to screen for the effect of pH at 4.2, 6.4, 8.2 and 10. The buffers 
were only 10 mM to make sure that the salt effect on the phase separation came from the NaCl 
and not the buffer. The plots in Figure 9 show phase separation for lysozyme like expected at 
low pH and with NaCl exposure. The NaCl titration indicates that a higher concentration 
induces more phase separation. In the right panel of Figure 9, seven spikes are observed 
meaning that assemblies large enough to diffuse little to none have formed. Spikes are observed 
from 1.4 M NaCl, and they increase in number or intensity towards 1.7 M NaCl. As the NaCl 
concentration increases, the curves begin to flatten towards the left side of Tavg compared to 
the reference peak with 0 M NaCl. This suggests that lysozyme has assembled in a way that 
has decreased the diffusivity of the protein, since an increased population of protein at a higher-
than-average velocity in the capillary is seen. An increased flattening towards the end of the 
curve is also observed. What this part represents has previously been described as an effect of 
phase separation that has diffused into a slow flow velocity layer in the capillary. However, 
this can also be an effect of the proteins interacting and sticking to the capillary wall.

Figure 9: TDIPS with 112 mg/ml lysozyme in 50 mM HCl with 10 mM NaAc pH 4.2 in analyte buffers with increasing NaCl 
concentration.  
Figure 10 shows the TDIPS results at pH 6.4 in NaPi buffer. It appears that the salt still has an 
effect at pH 6.4, however, this is also slightly acidic. Three small intensity spikes and three 
higher spikes are observed in the right panel, which does suggest reduced phase separation 
compared to the TDIPS with NaAc buffer. The lowest concentration of NaCl that still induces 
a spike signal is 1.4 M, but the intensity is low.  
 
Figure 10: TDIPS with 112 mg/ml lysozyme in 50 mM HCl with 10 mM NaPi pH 6.4 in analyte buffers with increasing NaCl

phase separation for lysozyme, however, it is reduced compared to the lower pH buffers. Fewer 
assemblies which are smaller in size reach the detector to form the spike signals suggesting an 
effect of the pH. Lowest NaCl concentration inducing the spike signal is now 1.5 M compared 
to the acidic buffers where spikes were observed at 1.4 M.

Even at pH 10 in the carbonate buffer, the lysozyme shows phase separation (Figure 12). 
However, only four spikes are formed which are all low in intensity compared to the acidic pH 
experiments. Three of these spikes are from the 1.7 M NaCl run suggesting that a higher 
concentration of salt is needed to induce phase separation at higher pH. Interestingly, a spike 
is observed at 1.5 M NaCl, but no spikes are seen at 1.6 M. However, at 1.6 M NaCl a higher 
population of slow diffusing assemblies than any of the other concentrations have formed 
(Figure 12, right panel). 
 
Figure 12: TDIPS with 112 mg/ml lysozyme in 50 mM HCl with 10 mM carbonate pH 10 in analyte buffers with increasing 
NaCl concentration.  
Generally, the data imply that lysozyme phase separates under a wide range of pH, indicating 
NaCl driven phase separation. On the other hand, since the lysozyme stock is in 50 mM HCl, 
and we know that the enzyme phase separates at low pH, there could be an issue with the pH 
regulation. If the pH of the lysozyme stock is around 1-2, and the concentration of enzyme is 
almost 8 mM, using an analyte buffer of only 10 mM can complicate the pH regulation in the 
timeframe of the experiment. Optimally, a buffer should be several times higher in 
concentration than the molecules it buffers. To clarify this issue, a new stock was prepared, 
and higher concentration buffers were used.  
 
==PAGE:21==

The same TDIPS screening was conducted but with a stock of lysozyme dissolved in milli Q 
with 168 mg/ml, pH of 4.6, and the strength of the analyte buffers was now 50 mM. The pH in 
the stock was 4.6 because of the acidic formulation of the powdered protein. As expected, the 
enzyme still shows increased phase separation in low pH by the effect of increasing NaCl 
concentration (Figure 13). However, the degree of phase separation is reduced compared to the 
runs with the HCl stock. Instead of seven spikes observed for NaAc, we now only see a total 
of four, one small spike and three higher in intensity. In addition, no spikes are observed on the 
signal for 1.4 M NaCl like in the HCl stock (Figure 9).

Figure 14: TDIPS with 168 mg/ml lysozyme in milli Q with 50 mM NaPi pH 6.4 in analyte buffers with increasing NaCl

the upslope of the plots when comparing with the reference peak (0 M NaCl). This again 
suggests that something has been assembled and diffused slower than the proteins would do in 
a non-phase separating environment.

The concern about the pH regulation being an issue by using low concentration buffers in 
TDIPS with high concentration of enzyme seemed to be rational. Generally, the peaks are 
deviating less from the reference peak for the milli Q stock TDIPS suggesting less phase 
separation than in the HCl stock. The pH of the plug is the only difference in the two stocks 
apart from the concentration, which would induce more phase separation. Therefore, the pH 
must be a deciding factor, but whether it is a regulation issue, or another symptom of pH 
difference is hard to conclude. By comparing a run with the HCl stock with 10 mM and 100 
mM in the analyte buffers with the same concentration of NaCl, a significant difference is not 
observed (Figure 17).  
==PAGE:23==

Figure 17: a: TDIPS with 112 mg/ml lysozyme in 50 mM HCl with 10 mM carbonate 1.7 M NaCl as analyte buffer. b: 
TDIPS with 112 mg/ml lysozyme in 50 mM HCl with 100 mM carbonate 1.7 M NaCl as analyte buffer. 
Lysozyme phase separates at pH 4.2 - 10 in all four of the investigated buffer systems, by the 
induction of NaCl. Given that the pI of lysozyme is around 11, the protein is fully positively 
charged at pH 7 (+8). Even at pH 10 the protein could still be slightly positively charged. With 
the Debye Hückel theory in mind, the negative ions of NaCl in the solution interact with the 
proteins and decrease the charge on the protein surface. This would minimize the repulsion 
between the positively charged proteins and make protein/protein interactions more frequent. 
This effect can be observed for all the buffers, but mostly at lower pH. The reason for this 
difference in the lysozyme HCl and milli Q stock results is unclear. Some of the data are 
pointing in opposite directions. The titration data with the two stocks suggest a pH regulation 
issue, however, Figure 17 indicates that it is not the issue. Moreover, subsequent TDIPS 
experiments on BHA indicate that 10 mM is sufficient. Assuming that 10 mM is enough for 
pH regulation, something else must be inducing the difference in the lysozyme stocks. The fact 
that lysozyme exhibits most phase separation in low pH can potentially explain why it seems 
to do so predominantly in the HCl stock, as it is already in a state that is prone to phase 
separation. As soon as the protein disperses into the analyte buffer, the protein phase separates 
instantly, and by the time the pH is regulated it is too late. However, the net charge of the 
protein should not be different from pH 1-2 to 4.4, only the ions of HCl differ from the two 
stocks. Another notable difference between the two stocks is the fluorescence intensity signal. 
The signal is higher for the HCl stock even though the concentration is higher in the milli Q 
stock. This could be attributed to a slight structural difference in structure of the protein in the 
two respective stocks. The fluorescent aromatic residues like the tryptophanes might be in 
different environments because of structural differences due to pH. Fluorescent intensity in 
terms of the general height of the peaks throughout the project is hard to gain information from. 
The width of the peaks and maybe fluorescence quenching have an effect on this trend 27. The 
lysozyme in the milli Q stock appears more stable in the HEPES buffer compared to the 
carbonate buffer. This could be due to the high pH in the carbonate buffer, which is getting 
close to the pI of lysozyme causing less repulsion between the proteins.

3.1.3 LLPS reversibility 
Turbidity and static laser scattering were measured on a phase separated lysozyme sample 
during three different thermal programs. The lysozyme sample was 15 mg/ml, 100 mM NaAc, 
and 1.2 M NaCl. In the first run, a thermal program going from 20-60-20 ℃ was used. The 
sample was turbid at first, which is also observed by the y-value at the start of the experiment 
at 20 ℃ (Figure 20). As the temperature increases, the turbidity begins to decrease already after 
the temperature rises a few degrees. At around 36 ℃, the turbidity is close to 0 and stays this 
way until the temperature reaches around 50 ℃. At this point, the turbidity starts to increase 
again. When the temperature starts to decrease, the turbidity only decreases slightly. The fact 
that the turbidity was close to 0 at 40 ℃ when the temperature increased but remained high 
upon cooling down passed 40 ℃, indicates that a new type of phase separation has been 
produced which keeps the sample turbid at all temperatures upon cooling. The laser scattering 
shown in panel b has the same general pattern where it decreases as the temperature rises but 
increases again at about 50 ℃. The scattering curve ends at a different value than where it 
started supporting the idea that a different form of phase separation has occurred compared to 
the original state of the sample. Although lysozyme melting temperature is reported to be 82 
℃ in water 29, the turbidity and scattering are still most likely due to thermal unfolding followed 
by aggregation. The sample is high enough in concentration to phase separate, why the stability 
and the melting temperature might be lower. It seems that the temperature has a clear effect on 
the turbidity of the sample. The fact that the turbidity goes down with an increase in 
temperature is a good indication that the phase separation could have been liquid, since we 
know from literature that droplets are reversible 12.  
 
Figure 20: Start and end values are indicated in the figures.  a: Turbidity was measured against temperature. b: Laser 
scattering at 636 nm was measured against temperature. 
Another program was run at lower temperature to measure the reversibility better. Results from 
the other thermal program is shown in Figure 21. The program started with cooling from 40 ℃ 
to 20 ℃ and heating up to 40 ℃ again. The turbidity increases as the sample is cooled to around 
20 ℃ and continues to do so until 14 ℃ is reached. The turbidity looks steady until the 
temperature rises to 20 ℃ again, where the turbidity starts to decrease. The turbidity returns to 
the same values as it started, which suggests a 100 % reversible phase separation. However, 
looking at the scattering in panel b, it is observed that the end value is not the same as the start 
value. This suggests that another kind of aggregate has assembled scattering in a different way 
than initially in the cuvette. Thus, the laser scattering provides information that is not 
==PAGE:26==
 
25
obtainable in the turbidity data. The assemblies which scatter are possibly not large enough to 
produce a turbidity signal.  
 
Figure 21: Reading direction is given in plot a with arrows, where turbidity was measured as a function of temperature. b: 
Laser scattering at 636 nm is measured as a function of temperature changes.  
Considering that lysozyme is not fully reversible from phase separation and that residual 
assemblies which scatter are still left in the solution, a thermal cycling experiment was 
conducted. Since phase separation is concentration dependent, and some of the effective 
protein concentration is lost to aggregation after each cycle, it is expected that phase separation 
will cease at some point. The sample consisted of 17 mg/ml lysozyme in 100 mM NaPi 1.5 M 
NaCl. After the first cycle, 15 minutes went by before initiating the second cycle to let the 
insoluble fraction fall to the bottom of the cuvette. The cycling program started with heating 
from 20 ℃ to 40 ℃ and then cooling down to 20 ℃ again. Figure 22 shows the results from 
the thermal cycling experiment. In the first cycle, the turbidity and scattering decrease with 
temperature increase as expected from the initial Probe Drum experiments (Figure 20). In the 
second cycle, the turbidity remains very low after the sample is cooled down. At the end of the 
second cycle, in Panel b, we can observe that some sort of particles are still scattering, even 
though the turbidity tells us it is a clear solution. This is an indication that the effective protein 
concentration has been reduced. The low turbidity, at the end of the first cycle, suggests that 
the concentration is low enough for the solution not to undergo phase separation again upon 
cooling.

3.2 
-Amylase Bacillus Licheniformis  
 
This -amylase was purchased by Novozymes from Sigma Aldrich. The enzyme was 
investigated for the effect of NaCl, pH and a “dilution factor” on phase separation of the system. 
The dilution factor was an effect that caused the enzyme to precipitate upon dilution without 
the addition of NaCl. Whenever a stock of the enzyme was diluted e.g. 100 times for 
concentration measurement on the nanodrop, it precipitated. A protein stock which was 
dissolved only in a buffer without any NaCl, precipitated when diluted with its own buffer 
without NaCl. Diluting the stock with the same buffer including NaCl kept the protein in 
solution but would also precipitate after a couple of hours. Using a stock with 229 mg/ml in 40 
mM HEPES buffer pH 5.7, a DLS measurement was done on the Prometheus Panta. The size 
analysis showed that there were populations in the sample much bigger than the monomer of 
the protein (Figure 23). This suggests that the protein oligomerises at high concentrations. The 
reason why the protein produces oligomers may be due to induced stability compared to the 
monomer at these concentrations. A theory on the dilution issue could be that the monomer 
becomes more frequent upon dilution, and if the monomer is less stable, it would be more prone 
to phase separation. However, a dilution would generally result in an entropy increase which 
is thermodynamically favorable.

3.2.2 Phase separation reversibility 
A thermal program was set up on the Probe Drum to investigate the nature of the dilution 
induced precipitation. The BLA stock of 211 mg/ml 40 mM HEPES pH 5.7 was diluted 50 
times without any NaCl and run with a program that heated from 20 ℃ to 60 ℃ and down to 
20 ℃ again (Figure 27). Heating the sample appears to reverse the turbidity to an extent. Both 
turbidity and laser scattering decrease as the temperature increases. This is an indication that 
the phase separation could have been liquid-like. Both scattering and turbidity start to increase 
again around 40-50 ℃ suggesting a new sort of phase separation. Turbidity and scattering are 
stable when the sample is cooled down again suggesting that insoluble aggregates were formed. 
This could possibly be due to unfolding of the protein as a result of heating.

3.2.3 Thermal stability  
A larger experiment based on the same concept as the Probe Drum experiment above was 
conducted on the Prometheus Panta. However, in this experiment also NaCl was inspected to 
see if there was an effect of this on the thermal stability of the enzyme. A stock with 211 mg/ml 
BLA 40 mM HEPES pH 5.7 was diluted 100x, 50x, 6.6x and 4x in the same buffer, but the 
samples had varying NaCl concentrations of 25 mM, 100 mM, 200 mM and 400 mM for each 
dilution (Figure 28). In panel a, the turbidity curve for the 100x diluted sample is shown. Only 
the sample without any NaCl is turbid at first and starts to decrease in turbidity as the 
temperature rises and begins to increase again around 50 ℃, similar to the Probe Drum data 
above (Figure 27 a). The samples including NaCl are not turbid at first and only start to increase 
in turbidity around 60-70 ℃. The increase in turbidity is a result of thermal unfolding followed 
by protein aggregation. Notably the onset where the turbidity starts to increase for the 0 M 
NaCl sample is significantly earlier than for those with NaCl. This is also the case for the 50x 
diluted samples in panel b. The solutions that were turbid and phase separated at first are less 
stable and start to aggregate at lower temperatures than the samples with NaCl. The stability 
of the protein in solution can be partly attributed to the presence of NaCl, which contributes to 
this onset difference. Panels c and d show the results from the turbidity measurements of the 
6.6x dilution and the 4x dilution. For these samples only a slight difference in the onset 
temperature is observed. This supports the idea that NaCl not only solubilizes the protein but 
also has a stabilizing effect. Interestingly, the difference in effect of NaCl is bigger from 0 M 
to 25 mM NaCl compared to 100 mM and 200 mM which are quite similar. As mentioned 
earlier, the stabilizing effect of NaCl could be attributed to a binding in an ionic pocket of the 
enzyme. The reason a small difference is observed between 100 mM to 200 mM could be a 
result of saturation of the binding site.

3.3.1 Stability and solubility assessment 
Early solubility issues were met with this enzyme, and since the protein was tough to keep in 
solution, a stability study was done. Eight different stocks were produced, two stocks in 40 mM 
HEPES buffer pH 8.2 with and without 0.5 M NaCl and two in NaPi pH 6.8 with and without 
0.5 M NaCl. Four NaAc stocks were made, two of which had a large pellet after dissolving and 
centrifuging specified (S) for saturated, and two with very little pellet specified (Un) for 
unsaturated. A dilution series was made with four concentrations for each stock. Turbidity was 
measured at 20 ℃ over 16 hours. Unfortunately, two of the stocks (NaPi and NaAc (Un) 0.5 
NaCl) were precipitated before protein concentration could be measured. The same amount of 
protein was weighed for all the stocks except the saturated ones, which is why their 
concentrations have been estimated according to the other buffer stocks (Figure 30). 
NaCl appears to have a stabilizing effect on the enzyme at pH 8.2 based on the result difference 
in the HEPES buffer with and without NaCl. The sample with 4.2 mg/ml protein without NaCl 
in the solution increases in turbidity over the course of the experiment. The NaCl also seems 
to have a stabilizing effect in pH 6.8 with the NaPi buffer. Only the highest concentration in 
the NaPi 0.5 M NaCl stock precipitates. Previous work on BHA has shown that the enzyme 
crystalises between pH 6-9 32, suggesting that it could be a crystallisation curve and not an 
amorph aggregation occurring. This was confirmed by microscopic observations. Interestingly, 
the turbidity increases almost immediately for the NaPi stock without NaCl, and with NaCl in 
the stock, the turbidity increase occurs later. The NaCl appears to stabilise the protein and stall 
the crystallisation. In NaAc pH 4.4, the NaCl seems to have a different effect. The enzyme in 
the unsaturated NaAc stock without any salt remains stable throughout the experiment whereas 
the turbidity increases for the solution with 0.5 M NaCl for the two highest concentration 
samples. In the two saturated NaAc, stocks turbidity is observed both with and without NaCl. 
These samples are supposedly supersaturated, and only the energy barrier for nucleation stands 
in the way of precipitation. The 22 mg/ml sample of NaAc (S) could also be a crystallisation 
curve. The 11.35 mg/ml sample is slightly turbid and remains the same throughout the 
experiment. This could also be a result of crystallisation, which occurred before the 
measurements began. The fact that the turbidity does not increase could be because the 
equilibrium concentration was reached. In the bottom right panel, it is observed that the two 
low concentration enzyme stocks increase in turbidity over time where the two highest 
concentration stocks remain stable. This is quite unexpected and difficult to interpret. 
Fluorescence intensity at 330 nm confirmed that the data had been plotted correctly.

3.3.3 Crowding induced LLPS 
The effect of crowding on phase separation was assessed with the Probe Drum instrument. The 
inspected sample consisted of 36 mg/ml BHA in HEPES buffer pH 9.5 and 8.8 % PEG 8000. 
The sample was put through a thermal program cooling from 40 ℃ to 20 ℃ and heating up to 
40 ℃ measuring turbidity and scattering. The results are shown in Figure 37. The high turbidity 
in the sample at the start of the experiment suggests that the sample is phase separated. As the 
temperature increases, the turbidity drops as well suggesting that the heat is melting the phase 
separation. The laser scattering shows the same tendency where it decreases as the temperature 
rises. From both the turbidity and the scattering, we see that the phase separation is not only 
dependent on PEG, as we see significantly lower values of turbidity and scattering from a 
sample with a lower protein concentration of 23 mg/ml (Figure 37). Moreover, the fact that the 
turbidity do not change with temperature indicates that the sample was not LLPS and perhaps 
the turbidity and scattering come from a crowding induced aggregation. Note that PEG can 
also phase separate on its own in solutions at high pH 33.

As mentioned, a reference peak is important when interpreting TDIPS data. Since proteins 
diffuse differently and can be affected by many factors, the Tavg of the reference peak must be 
determined for proper analysis. For the NaCl vs pH screening experiments done on lysozyme, 
a run with 0 M NaCl has been included as a reference peak. The reference peak for BHA was 
produced by running with milli Q as the analyte buffer, which is the same solvent as the one 
the protein is dissolved in. The FIDA analysis software provides a fit to the output curve and 
generates a hydrodynamic radius based on the fit. The RH provided by the software for BHA 
was 1.48 nm (Figure 52). Since this is too small for a 55 kDa protein according to the online 
weight to size estimation tool nanoComposix34, this raised a concern about the integrity of the 
experiment. In addition, the peak is too narrow to fit the description of a gaussian curve. On 
the other hand, since the peak is symmetrical and the enzyme should not phase separate when 
exposed to milli Q, I assume the peak is representing the stochastic diffusivity of the monomer. 
The reason for the narrow peak is most likely due to the high protein concentration, the low 
==PAGE:42==

buffer concentration, and the pH of the solution. In a high protein concentration, the Debye 
length increases, dependent on the ion concentration, making the proteins feel each other 
already at long distances. Given that the buffer concentration was 10 mM, and the protein 
concentration was roughly 7-8 mM, the number of ions available for screening the 
protein/protein interactions is much lower than in usual protein solutions. It should be noted 
that crystallised proteins are precipitated with counter ions in order to produce a neutral crystal. 
This means that the ionic strength in the capillary is higher than 10 mM, and enough ions are 
available in the solution to neutralise the protein. Nonetheless, the Debye length is still 
relatively long with this ion to protein ratio. With a pI around 6, the protein would be highly 
charged (-12) in pH 11. At a high concentration of 96 mg/ml, intermolecular repulsion forces 
are expected to cause the proteins to diffuse significantly faster than normally, especially with 
a long Debye length. Nevertheless, a better gaussian fit with a more correct RH would have the 
same Tavg which is an important information from the reference peak. The narrow peak 
represents something that diffuses fast, and given that the rate of diffusion is size dependent, 
the RH is low. Thus, the diffusion affects the signal distribution but not Tavg in this case. The 
reference peak for the BHA TDIPS results was important to obtain in order to conclude whether 
one of the peaks in the peak splitting data from the runs with carbonate and HEPES buffer 
(Figure 33, Figure 34) was the average peak. 
Dynamic light scattering was used to measure the RH of BHA to see if the size was 
concentration dependent. In Figure 40, the hydrodynamic radii, diffusion coefficients and 
polydispersity indexes are plotted for BHA in concentration ranging from 1 - 96 mg/ml. In 
panel a, the cumulant radius is plotted, where we see a decrease in size when the concentration 
increases. This supports the idea that the repelling forces are causing the protein to diffuse 
faster at higher concentration. In the lower concentration samples, the protein diffusion is closer 
to what it should be since the size determined by diffusion is higher. At 40-50 mg/ml, the 
diffusion does not increase further and appears to decrease a bit again at 80 mg/ml (Figure 40 
b). Perhaps the viscosity of the solution now has a bigger effect on diffusion than the 
intermolecular repulsion forces. The viscosity has been corrected in the Panta control software 
in terms of the sample solvent (milli Q), however, the viscosity provided by the protein itself 
is not accounted for.

4 Conclusion 
 
 
The phase separation behavior of three different enzyme systems were investigated. Figuring 
out if the enzymes could phase separate and under which conditions they did so, was one of 
two goals. The second goal was to demonstrate how the instruments could be applied to 
quantify phase separation in highly concentrated enzyme solutions and how to analyze it. By 
using TDIPS it was illustrated that the phase behavior of each enzyme was highly dependent 
on the specific conditions they were exposed to. Initial experiments showed the effect of NaCl 
in lysozyme phase separation and revealed the relevance of the protein concentration in the 
process. Furthermore, TDIPS could be used to illustrate the effect of an additional factor on 
the system. Two different stocks of lysozyme were screened for the effect of pH along with 
NaCl. This showed that phase separation was driven by NaCl but reduced at higher pH. With 
spectroscopic analysis on the Probe Drum, it was possible to examine the reversibility of NaCl 
induced phase separation for lysozyme. Turbidity and scattering data suggested that it was 
reversible to a certain degree, where insoluble aggregates were formed along the process. The 
scattering data revealed that turbidity was not sufficient to determine if the solution had an 
insoluble fraction.  
The BHA enzyme was also investigated for the effect of NaCl and pH using TDIPS. This 
showed a pH driven phase separation for the protein with minimal effect of NaCl. A peak split 
was observed at pH 8.2 and 10 which indicated reversibility of the pH induced assemblies. The 
formation of spikes in the fluorescence signal for pH 6.4 and 4.2 suggested no reversibility of 
large assemblies before reaching the detector. The diffusion of BHA was investigated by 
measuring DLS on the Prometheus Panta. This revealed that the diffusion coefficient was 
concentration dependent.  
BLA was also investigated for its phase separation properties. A dilution induced phase 
separation was identified in the system, and by using TDIPS it was illustrated how the protein 
was dependent on NaCl to stay in solution. A TDIPS screening with NaCl and pH revealed that 
the ions of a NaPi buffer could induce the same stability as observed with NaCl.   
The Panta was applied to describe the effect of NaCl on thermal stability of BLA. Here the 
results showed NaCl induced thermal stability of the enzyme and a certain degree of 
reversibility in phase separated samples. Moreover, thermal stability was reduced in samples 
that had previously undergone phase separation.   
Experience showed that TDIPS experiments was only half the work. In order to analyse TIDPS 
correctly, the importance of a reference peak cannot be stressed enough. Moreover, 
normalizing the data in a way that allows for proper data interpretation is essential to give 
TDIPS its value. The method enables the opportunity to screen for endless phase separating 
conditions and provides a rational argument in regard to inferring whether phase separation 
inside the capillary occurred or not. However, it does have its limitations with regards to 
identifying the type of phase separation responsible for diffusion differences in the capillary. 
Nevertheless, TDIPS along with spectroscopic analyses, were used successfully to illustrate 
and quantify phase separating conditions and reversibility in the three enzyme systems.
