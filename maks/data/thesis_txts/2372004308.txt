Abstract 
This report presents the process and results obtained in the development of a sustainable surfboard, using 
natural fibers and biopolymer. Natural fiber composites have gained a lot of attention in the recent years, due 
to their good mechanical properties, competitive price, and overall low environmental impact. Very few bio-
based and biodegradable resins are available on the market, and usually present weaker fiber/matrix adhesion 
properties than synthetic resins. But the recent development made by the Danish startup Pond ApS, in the 
elaboration of a thermoplastic bio-based and biodegradable resin system with high bonding properties, opens 
up new opportunities in the area of sustainable product development.  
The three main objectives of this project have been to: (1) identify a suitable combination of materials to 
reduce the environmental impact and improve the disposal capabilities of a surfboard, (2) use the selected 
materials to design a sustainable shortboard, and (3) assess the economical viability of this particular 
sustainable surfboard. The project is divided into three main parts presenting the context of the product 
development (technology, market, user), the design proposal, and the commercialization plan. The 
development of the new product has been conducted by using user-centered design techniques and iterative 
prototyping methods. 
This report concludes that a combination of bio-based resin, flax fibers, and PLA foam shows some 
promising potential in the creation of sustainable and biodegradable surfboards. Nevertheless, the 
temperature required to process the bio-resin is believed to be the main limiting factor in the creation of the 
intended product. With the assumption that the processing temperature will be reduced in a near future, this 
report shows that it could be economically viable to produce and sell the sustainable surfboard designed at a 
moderated price.

1. Introduction 
Sustainability is critical concept to understand and implement in the different aspects of our society. Mostly 
viewed as an add-on to a product justifying a price increase, it is still not believed to be the industry stand. 
But the general understanding and consciousness in regards to environmental issues is rising. The challenges 
faced do not only address the governments and industries, but also the ordinary people, affecting their way of 
living and consuming. Most citizens living in developed countries are now experiencing changes in their 
everyday life in order to reduce their environmental impact. Cars are heavily taxed, plastic bags in 
supermarkets are not free anymore, and recycling glass and plastic bottles has become a habit. With all these 
changes, consumers are becoming more and more aware of the impact of their acts, and demand to be offered 
sustainable alternatives in most aspects of their lives. 
Sustainable practices are often negatively perceived by companies who generally consider nature as a 
constraint to development and a challenge to competitivity (Alves et al., 2009). This is the case in the 
sporting good industry, where most of the improvements are brought to sport apparels, and are mainly 
carried out by few leading companies (Subic et al., 2009). When it comes to sports equipment, the level of 
performance asked by athletes has pushed the manufacturers to constantly develop and use more advanced 
materials, such as composites. This complexity creates multiple problems when the product reaches the end 
of its useful life or gets broken. The components cannot be taken apart, therefore cannot be recycled, and 
often end up in landfills or burned. But the recent growth in the creation and use of sustainable materials, 
such as natural fiber composites, is expected to positively influence the environmental impact of many 
industries around the world, therefore creating opportunities for sporting goods manufacturers. 
This report presents the process and results obtained in the development of a sustainable surfboard, using 
natural fibers and biopolymer. The learning objectives of this project are presented in appendix 1.

2. Project overview 
  
2.1 Problem deﬁnition 
Since the 50s common surfboards have heavily relied on non-renewable and health-threatening resources, 
such as Polyurethane (PU) and Expanded Polystyrene (EPS) foams, in combination with polyester or epoxy 
resins. Most manufacturers took for granted the rapid popularity of the discipline, which counts nowadays 
approximately 5.4 million enthusiasts worldwide, to leave the materials almost unchanged. It is estimated 
that more than 400,000 surfboards are produced around the world every year (TechNavio, 2016), leading an 
important number of old or broken surfboards to be thrown in landfills. Many factors can be responsible for 
that; surfboards are exposed to heavy usage conditions, the repairs attempt often lead to weaker surfboards, 
and as every other discipline, the surf culture is subject to passing fads constantly pushing the surfer to 
acquire the latest products.  
  
Noticing the environmental benefits, the interest of the surf community, and the business opportunity, few 
companies have started to produce “sustainable surfboards” under the supervision of the US based 
association Sustainable Surf, who classifies the level of sustainability of the products. Being considered as a 
relatively emergent trend, the producers of sustainable surfboards are for now only replacing some of the 
constituents of the board with less harmful materials. This has the effect of slightly reducing the 
environmental impact of the surfboard, but still relying on non-renewable resources. Furthermore, they are 
only addressing part of the issue, as most of the constituents of a surfboard are bonded together, therefore 
always leading to the same problems at the end of the useful life of the product. 
  
This project aims at developing a sustainable surfboard, with the end property being biodegradable under 
specific conditions. The development of this product is supported by the recent creation of a new bio resin 
system intended to replace the traditional resins used, such as polyester and epoxy. This new bio resin system 
has been developed by the Danish startup Pond ApS, and opens up new possibilities in the creation and use 
of natural fiber composite. 
  
The objectives of this project are the following: 
• Identify a suitable combination of materials to reduce the environmental impact and improve the disposal 
capabilities of a surfboard. 
• Use the selected materials to design a sustainable shortboard. 
• Assess the economical viability of this particular sustainable surfboard. 
  
2.2 Project methodology 
  
The methodology applied in this project is highly inspired by a classic model used by many companies and 
in particular by the world known design consultancy firm IDEO (Tidd, & Bessant, 2013). It consists of a 
series of steps used to develop and design products with an iterative approach. Nevertheless, the initial 
process has been adapted to fit the specific context of this project and consists of four steps: 
  
1. Understand the technology, the market and the users. 
2. Visualize concepts and the customers who might use them, using prototyping, models and simulations. 
3. Evaluate and refine the prototypes in a series of quick iterations. 
4. Implement the new concept for commercialization.

underlying structure in which the product will be developed. It ensures that the right product is developed on 
the basis of data and information gathered from the different dimensions. Each exploration areas are based 
on scientific studies, online information, personal communications, and personal knowledge. Figure 2.1 
illustrates the dimensions.

At the second step of the development process, a detailed analysis of the social context will be used to 
complete the initial findings. The ideas captured from the two first steps are then translated and presented 
under the form of concepts and iterative prototypes. Lastly, an evaluation of the end product provides the 
necessary support to draw some conclusions on the feasibility of the implementation of the product with its 
technology, and the associated go-to-market strategy.  
  
2.3 The Collaboration with Pond ApS 
  
Pond ApS is a Danish startup, founded by Mechanical Engineer Thomas Brorsen Pedersen, and Chemist 
Martin Jensen, in July 2015. Established at the harbor of Aarhus, the young company aims at replacing the 
intensive use of petroleum based resins in composite materials by a fully bio-based and biodegradable one 
developed by them. The environmental aspect of their resin in combination with natural fibers offers endless 
possibilities in the creation of sustainable product. Currently, the startup is composed of four permanent 
members, the two founders, a business developer, Pernille Alberg Pedersen, and a sustainability expert, 
Jingjing Gao. Besides the core members, five interns are working on various aspects of the startup, from the 
communication and marketing of the products, to the development of new resin systems.   
  
At the moment, Pond’s portfolio is composed of three products. A bio resin, available in various densities 
and used in combination with synthetic or natural fibers to create composite materials. Bio-based adhesive 
resin fibers, mainly used for airlaid purpose. Lastly, bio granulates premixed with fibers, to create light and 
eco-friendly injected molded components (Pond, 2017). 
  
The activities of the startup mainly consist of improving and developing new resin system, and 
commercializing their products to various customers, either already working with plastics or composite 
materials, or simply willing to reduce the environmental impact of their products. The materials developed at 
Pond gained a lot of attention by being selected among the 10 most promising sustainable materials at the 
LAUNCH Nordic challenge in 2015 (Launch Nordic, 2015). But even more, by winning the “startup 
booster” competition held at the 2017 JEC World Composite Fair in Paris, in the category biomaterials (JEC 
composite, 2017), and the Entrepreneur of the Year in Business Region Aarhus in 2017 (Pond, 2017). 
  
Besides having created a really innovative product, the startup needs to face the barriers implicitly present in 
the commercialization of biopolymers, such as high cost and limited performances. If these factors can be 
overcome, the chances are high that the product will be accepted as substitute to traditional non-
biodegradable polymers (Mohanty et al., 2002). 
  
In the context of this project, Pond acts as the material supplier and supporter in the use of their new bio 
resin. Having recently developed new generations of their bio resin, the project is also good way for them to 
get feedback on the processability and the behavior of the resin.

2.4 The bio resin 
  
The bio resin systems invented are mostly based on starch, a naturally occurring polymer, and present the 
property of being fully biodegradable under controlled conditions (heat, humidity, and bacteria). The 
company has the ability to modify the degradation rate of the resin in order to extend or reduce the product 
lifespan, but also to adapt the resin to a specific type of natural fibers, in order to increase the bond between 
the two materials. Being almost made out of 100% bio-based and renewable content, the resin is not 
dependent on any finite, or highly pollutant resources. The use of bio-based resins also greatly improves the 
working environment of the people exposed to it. Unlike epoxy, the bio resin only presents minor danger 
when getting in contact with the skin, or when inhaled. Similar benefits can be observed with the end user, 
who can avoid being exposed to endocrine disrupter, as bisphenol, used in epoxy resin. Furthermore, two 
European patents filed in 2015 and early 2017 protect the chemical formulation and the production method 
of the bio resin. 
  
In the context of the project, the use of a bio-based and biodegradable resin is crucial in reducing the 
environmental impact of the end product, greatly improve the working environment of the shapers , and do 
1
not compromise the quality of the end product. 
  
2.5 Deﬁnition of sustainable product 
  
When designing new products aiming at being more sustainable than the competitive products, it is 
interesting to define the evaluation criteria, and the borders of the classification. In a study conducted by 
Ljungberg (2007), sustainable products are defined as “a product, which will give as little impact on the 
environment as possible during its life cycle” (p. 467). The author also suggests that no products can reach a 
level where no impact is done on the environment, and that the success of a product also relies on customer 
acceptance and satisfaction. This definition goes along with the principle presented by Subic et al. (2009) 
pointing out that sustainable design does not only address functional and aesthetic requirements, but mainly 
aims at meeting present needs without compromising the needs of future generations. Nowadays, it is 
accepted that sustainability covers three main areas: social, economic, and environmental (Subic et al., 
2009). 
  
In order to fully understand the extent to which a product or a material can be considered as sustainable, 
Mohanty et al. (2002), present the concept of sustainable bio-based products. In order to be considered as 
sustainable, a product should initially be derived from renewable resources, have the capability of being 
recycled, and include triggered biodegradability capacities. Furthermore, Mohanty et al. (2002) outline the 
importance of the product to be commercially viable and environmentally acceptable. Figure 2.2 regroups

3.1. History of the discipline 
  
The British explorer James Cook, who arrived in 1779 in Hawaii, reported the first signs of people seen 
surfing. He reported that the native Hawaiians were riding waves on top of giant wooden boards (Club of the 
wave, n.d.). For the Hawaiians, surfing was more a matter of spiritual affairs, and social differences, than 
purely an entertaining activity. The chiefs and noblemen were riding “olo” boards, whereas the common 
people used the “alaia” (Schultz, 2009). The only major difference remained in the size of the surfboard 
itself, influenced by the social classes. The boards were made out of plain local woods, leading to very heavy 
and hard to maneuver surfboards (Club of the wave, n.d.). 
  
After being adopted by the Westerner missionary, and exported to California, the first changes in the 
construction of the surfboard started to appear in 1926, with the developments made by Tom Blake (Club of 
the wave, n.d.). He was the first to introduce hollow boards with fins to the public. The reduced weight and 
increased maneuverability of the surfboard, made the sport accessible to a wider audience. At that time, the 
boards were mainly produced out of redwood, balsa, or plywood (Schultz, 2009). The next major changes in 
the surfboard construction were seen after World War II, with the development of new materials, and 
especially resins which became abundant (Club of the wave, n.d.). Therefore, in the 50s, the shapers got hold 
on fiberglass, lightweight foams, and resins to create a new generation of surfboards that are still dominating 
the market today. It was also around that time that surfing became popular in the rest of the world, especially 
in Australia, and kept on attracting more and more people. Few other changes appeared in the 80s, with the 
introduction of the “thruster” boards, a surfboard with three fins for a higher control (Orbelian, 1987). Since 
then, most of the developments are primarily affection the shape and the aesthetic aspect of the surfboards.

3.2 Materials and construction 
  
Originally entirely constructed in wood, the technological development made in the 50s has allowed the 
producers to create lighter, cheaper and better performing products. The rapid popularity of these new 
boards, and the new possibilities that they opened up, makes it very difficult for the wooden boards to be 
competitive. This section will only focus on analyzing the materials and construction used in the creation of 
shortboards, as they are the better performing and most demanding board in terms of mechanical properties. 
For these reasons, if suitable materials and construction can be used to create a shortboard, it is then easy to 
apply them to any other shapes of surfboard. 
  
The major characteristics of a shortboard come from its short size, generally 5’6’’ to 7’ ft. (~1.68 to 2 
meters), and high maneuverability on the wave (Surf science, n.d.). The main inconveniences of these boards 
come from the low flotation properties, due to the reduced volume of foam. Which, on the one hand, helps 
the surfer to dive under the wave when going away from the shore, and on the other hand, makes it 
complicated to paddle when catching a wave and especially the small ones (Surf science, n.d.). For these 
reasons, shortboards are mainly used by surfer with levels ranging from high intermediate to professional, 
and allow them to easily perform tricks while surfing medium to large sized waves (Surf science, n.d.). 
  
The materials used to create these boards remain almost unchanged since their first introduction. The core of 
the shortboard is composed of either PU, or EPS foam. PU foams have been used since the 50s, and are still 
the most commonly used core material. They are light, easy to shape with sanding paper, inexpensive, and 
available in many different sizes and density (Tactics, n.d.). EPS foams have been developed more recently, 
and are lighter than PU foams, also relatively cheap, and can be directly molded into specific shapes, partly 
due to the more difficult shaping process (Tactics, n.d.). Both types of foams present water absorption 
problem, and are dangerous for the shapers’ health (Tactics, n.d.). It should also be taken into consideration 
that PU and EPS foams are chemicals synthesized from benzene, obtained through oil refinery (Design Life-
Cycle, 2014). In order to increase the rigidity of the board, one or multiple wooden stringers are added to the 
middle of the core. To do so, the foam is cut in half, and the stringer is glued in-between the two foam parts. 
The stringer is usually made out of balsa wood, as it is one of the lightest existing woods, and is fairly easy 
to process. Once the foam is shaped, either manually or with a CNC machine, one or multiple layers of 
fiberglass fabric are used to wrap and protect the core, but also isolate it from the water. The number of glass 
fiber layers will depend on the type of foam used as core material, the dimensions of the surfboard, and the

intended performance (Surf science, n.d.). Shortboards typically feature two layers of 4 oz (approx. 115 g/

hold on the waves, and drag and speed of the board. Either fixed or removable, fins are attached to the 
surfboard by means of the “fin plug” (Surf science, n.d.). The leash is the element that keeps the surfboard 
attached to the surfer’s ankle in case of fall. Usually about the same length of the surfboard, the leash is

3.3 Production methods 
  
The surf culture is prone to many “traditions”, which remain unchanged since the first introduction of 
modern surfboard materials. There is still a large amount of shapers creating surfboards in a craftsmanship 
manner, with the aim of being recognized as the creator of great surfboards. The main differences in the 
production method used by independent shapers and large brands mainly remains in the foam shaping 
process (Madehow, n.d.). Large manufacturers are using CNC machines to transform the foam blanks into a 
surfboard shapes, whereas small shapers are usually doing this process manually, by using divers tools 
(Madehow, n.d.). The use of computer-assisted techniques presents the advantage of being more reliable, 
faster, and with high volume production drastically reduce the costs per units (Eppinger & Ulrich, 2012). But 
this creation process is sometimes badly perceived by surfers who rely more on the work of famous shapers. 
When it comes to the glassing, both the shapers and the large manufacturers use manual techniques, such as 
wet layup (Madehow, n.d.). This technique is known to be wasteful, as a lot of excess resin is spilt around the 
intended area. Only very few companies are impregnating the fiberglass using Vacuum Assisted Resin 
Transfer Molding (VARTM). Figure 3.5 illustrates the production process of a shortboard both in a

3.4 Environmental issues 
  
The general perception of surfing as a sport still remains in the common minds as a clean and admirable 
practice. The image of the lone surfer catching waves in clear blue water leaves the admirers, and the surfers 
themselves, to forget everything else, even the most important part of the sport, the surfboard. The 
technological improvements and constant demand for better performing equipment has led the producers to 
focus their work on the aesthetic aspect of the product, rather that on the production or assembly techniques. 
Thus unfortunately, most parts of a surfboard are harmful for both the environment and the producers of the 
surfboards, who are exposed to hazardous chemicals like isocyanates, VOCs, and styrene (TechNavio, 2016).  
  
In a Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) conducted by Schultz (2009), the two most common construction types of 
surfboards are assessed and compared. The first one is a combination of a PU foam core with polyester resin-
soaked fiberglass (UPR), and the second is an EPS foam core with epoxy resin-soaked fiberglass. Both 
surfboards were shaped with shortboard dimensions, and only differ on the specific use of both resins. Epoxy 
resins require the use of more hardener, and polyester resin the use of a surfacing agent. The LCA was 
composed of three parts aiming at defining the carbon footprint, toxic by-products from manufacturing, and 
local toxic exposure in board construction. Table 3.1 presents the results of the LCA on the two first parts. 
Due to a lack of data, and large variability in the chemical stock, the local toxic exposure in the board 
construction is not fully representative of the situation, and will not be presented in this project. Despite this, 
it is known that the shapers are exposed to styrene monomer and methyl ethyl ketone peroxide, which are 
known to be easily evaporated and inhaled, but also to fine foam particles when sanding down the blank 
(Schultz, 2009).

4.1 Composite materials 
4.1.1 Deﬁnition 
Callister (2012) presents that modern composite materials are born from an industrial demand for lighter, 
stiffer, shock resistant and more robust materials. Traditional materials, such as metals, ceramics or 
polymers, could meet none of these characteristics. A composite material is the combination of two or more 
materials with the aim of combining the properties of its constituents. A majority of composites are only 
composed of two different materials, the outer and continuous one called the matrix, and the inner one called 
the reinforcement. The proportion and properties of both constituent, and the geometry of the reinforcement 
determine the properties of the final material. In the case of composites reinforced by fibers, the shape, size, 
orientation, distribution and amount of fibers are influencing the characteristics of the final material (figure 
4.1) (Callister, 2012). 
Figure 4.1: Illustration of different composite materials’ geometry reinforced by fibers 
Note. From Fundamentals of materials science and engineering: an integrated approach, P.462, by W. D. Callister 
  
The composites reinforced by particles are characterized by the size and distribution of the particles. The 
most common one is the concrete, composed of cement (matrix) and aggregates (particles) (Callister, 2012). 
Composites reinforced by fibers are classified by the size and orientation of the fibers. They are commonly 
used in high performance automobiles or sports equipment (Corona et al., 2016). The last category, structural 
composites, defines a type of materials composed of a homogenous core and a composite material (Callister, 
2012). They are divided between the laminate and sandwich structures. In all three cases, the atomic or 
molecular interaction between the matrix and the reinforcement is a determining element in the quality of the 
composite (Callister, 2012). The following sections will be focusing on the composites reinforced with 
fibers, as they represent the main interest area of this project. Figure 4.2 presents three categories of

4.1.2 Performance / Properties 
Callister (2012) further explains that for a fiber-reinforced composite, the ability of the matrix to transfer a 
load to the fibers is highly influenced by the fiber length. Glass or carbon fibers are produced in a continuous 
matter, meaning that the fibers will be as long as the piece cut. If the fibers are considered too short for a 
specific application, the matrix will take up most of the load and break. After the length of the fibers comes 
the orientation. Fibers can be aligned, crossed, or woven with specific angles (e.g. +90/0, +45/-45). The 
selection of the orientation is highly dependent on the application and the constraints to which the material 
will be subject to. Another criterion that influences the mechanical properties of the composite is the fiber/
matrix ratio. As the fibers have the biggest elastic modulus, it is common to try to maximize the fiber content 
to improve the elastic modulus or the tensile strength of the final composite (Callister, 2012). 
  
Over the years, numbers of synthetic fibers have been created or improved to meet the most demanding 
applications (Corona et al., 2016). The most commonly used fibers are the glass fibers, used for their good 
mechanical properties and low price, the carbon fibers, known for their high specific modulus and light 
weight, and the aramid fibers, also known as Kevlar fibers used when a high tensile resistance is required 
(Callister, 2012). Table 4.1 presents the main mechanical properties of the three most common synthetic 
fibers used in composite materials. 
  
Table 4.1: Mechanical properties of the most common synthetic fibers

As presented by Callister (2012), the matrix of a fiber composite material can be made out of metal, polymer 
or ceramic. For this project, the most interesting matrix material is polymer, and it plays three essential roles. 
Firstly, the matrix bonds all the fibers together and spread the constraints between all the fibers. Secondly, it 
protects the fibers from wear due to abrasion or from chemical corrosion. And lastly, it isolates the fibers 
from one another, and avoids the propagation of cracks between fibers. There are many different types of 
polymer matrices suited for composite materials, the most commons are: polyesters, vinyl esters, epoxies, 
bismaleimides, polyimides and phenolics. These matrices can either be thermosets or thermoplastics. The 
thermosets are composed of a resin and a curing agent. Once the two components are mixed the curing 
reaction forms cross-links between the molecules, and ends in a solid material. At the opposites of 
thermosets, thermoplastics are already reacted and do not cross-links on heating (Callister, 2012). This 
particularity gives it the advantage to be heated and reprocessed multiple times (Campbell, 2003). Figure 4.3 
presents the most common polymer matrix, with their mechanical properties. Finally, composite materials 
offer a relatively long lifetime, do not require any specific maintenance, and are not prone to corrosion 
(Campbell, 2003).

4.1.3 Production methods 
Throughout the years, a wide variety of production methods have been developed to meet the requirements 
of a wide range of applications. It is precisely the application, the properties needed, the type of fiber, and the 
matrix used that defines the production process to use (Khosravani, 2012). Every processes are driven by the 
same goal, combining the resin in a liquid form with the fiber, and let the resin solidify in a define shape. The 
most common production processes are: Injection molding, wet layup, spray up, Resin Transfer Molding 
(RTM) which include the VARTM, autoclave molding, pultrusion molding, filament winding and 
compression molding (Khosravani, 2012). All these production methods have different advantages, results, 
costs and time requirement. It can be outlined that composite materials are easier to process in comparison to 
metals, and require less assembly labor and fastener (Khosravani, 2012). 
  
4.1.4 Limitations 
The downside of the impressive mechanical properties of the synthetic composite material is expressed by 
energy intensive production processes, high pollution rates, threat for human health, non-renewable 
resources usage, and a poor recyclability (Corona et al., 2016). Other limitations concern the design 
characteristics of the composite. As the properties of fiber composites are highly influenced by the fiber 
direction, if a composite is created to be stiff in a certain direction, it might limit the potential secondary uses 
of the product (Campbell, 2003). Furthermore, Subic et al. (2009) outline that “the sustainability issues with 
using composites is a major challenge to the sports industry in the face of government limits on pollution and 
the consumer demanding ‘greener’ products” (p. 73). Usually, the most interesting elimination process is the 
incineration of the product with a high focus on energy recovery. However, the general public sometime 
perceives incineration as a negative option, as it can contribute to the emissions of toxic gases. Campilho 
(2016) presents that the fiber-reinforced composites are not the most appropriate materials for energy 
recovery mainly due to their high fiber content, leading to a low calorific value.

4.2 Natural Fibers 
4.2.1 Deﬁnition 
The interest raised around natural fibers has been steadily increasing during the last decades; three main 
factors can be identified as the promoters of this trend. The first one being that more and more people are 
becoming aware of the human impact on the environment and therefore, many industries are now pointed out

4.3 Natural Fiber Composites 
4.3.1 Deﬁnition 
Natural Fiber Composites (NFC) are the association of natural fibers with any types of matrix. NFC are born 
from the global awareness of the human impact on the environment, the steadily decreasing availability of 
petrochemical resources, and the improvement of the natural fibers dedicated to composite materials. The 
years spent researching and enhancing the performances of NFC brought them to be competitive with 
fiberglass composites, in terms of mechanical strength and low price. Yet, many areas are still subject to 
various improvements, such as the fiber extraction, the fiber-matrix adhesion, processing conditions or the 
chemical treatments (Campilho, 2016). In his recently published book, Campilho (2016) defines the 
characteristics of NFC as: “An ideal natural fiber composite is fully biodegradable under controlled 
conditions and is composed only of short-cycle renewable plants” (p. 2). This quote is highly dependent on 
the type of matrix used. In most cases, natural fibers are combined with non-biodegradable matrix. The use 
of synthetic polymer matrix in NFC has been generalized due to the known properties of the matrix, and to 
address few of the known limitation of natural fibers. Recent solutions are using PolyLactic Acid (PLA), a 
starch based plastic, and polyhydroxy-alkanoates (PHA) as matrix, in order to create 100% biodegradable 
composite, therefore limiting the lifespan of the product. Nevertheless, the most important challenges faced 
by these biodegradable matrix remains in the fiber wetting and fiber bonding (Campilho, 2016). 
 
 
Due to the recent popularity and interest raised around renewable resources, many companies are now 
considering the use of natural fibers in their composite with regards to their low weight and sustainable 
aspect. It is estimated that the global market on natural fiber composites has reached USD 550 million, with 
a Compound Annual Growth Rate (CAGR) of approximately 11% in 2016 (Campilho, 2016). Most of the 
applications concern the use of wood and nonwood fibers. The majority of the wood fibers are used for 
construction purposes by the North American market, while the European market is essentially organized 
around the use of nonwood fibers, with an important growth in the automotive industry (Campilho, 2016). 
Not only natural fibers are prone to high growth rates, bio-based plastics too, and up to the point that the 
expected fabrication capacity reaches 3.45 million tons in 2020 (Campilho, 2016). But bio-matrices are 
prone to many controversies in regards to their sustainable aspects. Table 4.6 presents the advantages and 
disadvantages of bio-based matrices in comparison to conventional synthetic matrices.

It is important to detail that the use of natural fibers in a composite material does not make it implicitly a

by nature biodegradable, unlike bio-based polymer matrixes, which can be both biodegradable and non-
biodegradable. 
  
4.3.3 Limitation of Natural Fiber Composites 
As composite materials benefit from the performance of both materials (fiber and matrix), they often suffer 
from their disadvantages. Few general problems of natural fiber composites that limits their usage are: 
Strength prediction during structural loading, uncertainties about long-term performances, moisture 
absorption, lower fire resistance, lower mechanical properties and durability, limited processing temperature, 
larger scatter in the cost and properties than synthetic composites, and some difficulties in the use of well-
known fabrication processes (Dicker et al., 2014). It is also possible to list few of the general problems 
occurring in the design phase and manufacturing process of composite materials, both synthetic and natural: 
The general properties of the fibers, the void content, the properties of the resin, the fiber/resin ratio, the 
geometry and orientation of the fibers, and the manufacturing processes (Campilho, 2016). Of course, the 
limitations presented in the above sections are highly dependent on the application.

equipment on eCommerce platforms, and product improvement through R&D (Persistence, 2015;

5.2 Perception of sustainable sports equipment 
  
In order to materialize the need behind the creation of sustainable sports equipment, a survey has been 
created to gather quantitative and qualitative data from sports enthusiasts. Surveys can be used in multiple 
phases of the design process and can reflect the enthusiasm of the eventual target audience (van Boeijen et 
al., 2014; Eppinger & Ulrich, 2012). The questions were aiming at testing the three following assumptions: 
               
 
1.
Sports enthusiasts are concerned and demand the creation of sustainable sports equipment. 
2.
Sports enthusiasts are ready to pay more for sustainable sports equipment. 
3.
The performance and aesthetic aspects of sports equipment are the most valued purchase criteria 
  
The survey consisted of 11 questions with various objectives: question 1 to 3 were the “screener” questions 
to define the profile of the respondents, 4 to 6 segmented the respondent based on their sports and frequency 
of exercise, 7 to 10 gathered their opinion of sustainable sports equipment, and 11 provided qualitative 
feedback. Before being sent out, the survey has been tested and improved on the basis of feedback gathered 
from two sports enthusiasts. The survey has been posted on various sports related groups on Facebook and 
LinkedIn, as well as to the U.S. based association Sustainable Surf. The survey has been made available for 3 
days and has generated 180 replies. The full version of the survey can be found in appendix 2. 
  
Based on the first six question, approximately 95% of the respondents were between 18 and 35 years old, 
with a majority being student and more than a third working. It has been made on purpose to only present 
sports that required equipments often made out of composite materials (skiing, tennis, golf…), to narrow 
down the scope of answers to the interest area of this project, and facilitates the creation of interest groups 
based on specific sports. More than 40% of the respondents claimed that they were doing sports more than 3 
times per week, and that the performance and use of sports equipment are the two most valued criteria, with 
42% and 33% of the votes respectively. This last result challenges the third initial assumption in the way that 
the use is more valued than the aesthetic aspect in sports equipment. The two other assumptions have been 
confirmed with more than 60% of respondents being ready to pay more than the average price for sustainable 
sports equipment, which of course do not provide any guarantee of purchase. This previous number is 
supported by the fact that almost half of the people surveyed are aware that sports equipments are produced 
from non-renewable resources, and acknowledge the fact that they only have a limited amount of other 
options at the moment. Mixed opinions have been collected in regards to the way of marketing sustainable 
sports equipment. Two thirds of those surveyed believed that the green aspect of the product should be 
shown, either on the packaging or on the product itself, and the other third showed only a low interest in 
having the product labeled as sustainable. 
  
As previously mentioned, questionnaires can be used to identify the most interesting target audience and find 
out their preferences and motivations. In this case, the people stating that they were practicing sport more 
than three times a week are the target audience, as they could be considered as early adopters. Out of the 73 
people in this group, 60% would be ready to pay more than the average price for sustainable sports 
equipment. This percentage is slightly inferior to the one from the overall respondents. It could be explained 
by the fact that a majority of the people who took the survey are still studying, therefore with limited budget, 
or that they might already be in possession of relatively expensive equipment and that a price increase might 
be a limiting factor. The second interesting parameter to analyze is the most valued feature in sports 
equipment. Here again, with more than 52%, the performance is the first parameter that is prioritized, 
followed by the use, with 26% of the votes. Nevertheless, the three other parameters, price, design and 
innovative aspect, are to be considered equally important when developing a product, and especially in the 
marketing strategy. 
  
As a whole, the use of a questionnaire in the preliminary phase of the development shows that there is an 
interest behind sustainable sports equipment, and that only limited sustainable options are available at the 
moment. The last question written as an open question invited the respondents to add a comment on 
sustainable sports equipment. This had the benefit to provide some qualitative data to the research. Few 
respondents outlined the importance of using a sustainable approach in the whole supply chain, as one 
claimed: “I tend to buy more easily if I know that I contributed to a good cause, especially if it's equipment 
that I'm going to use for a long period of time”, or “I would also like to know the carbon footprint (or any 
other calculation) of my equipment's manufacture. To know the total impact of my equipment on the 
environment”. Other opinions illustrate the central part that the performance is playing in sports equipment: 
“The fact that is “green” shouldn't get in the way of the athlete and I think if professionals athletes would be 
sponsored with such product and still be competitive I'd be even down to pay 25% more to get that product”,

When analyzing the surfboard industry, it is almost impossible to avoid the fin industry, which evolves 
hidden from the general public and holds the surfboard manufacturers in hostage. The fin industry is 
composed of many different companies, where two of them stand out from all the others, Future Fins and 
FCS. The two brands are constantly competing to gain market shares one over the other by imposing their 
removable fin system to the surfboard manufacturers. Composed of two elements, the removable fin and the 
fixed fin plugs, once a surfboard is equipped with the system from a brand, it is remains unchangeable. As 
fins are relatively costly, from US $40 to $110 a set of three, the surfers tend to buy new surfboards onto 
which they can attache their old fins, meaning the fin plug from the same brand (Surf FCS, 2017). This factor 
forces the surfboard manufacturers to make each surfboard available with both fin plug system. This 
customization has no impact on the selling price of the surfboard, but surely creates troubles in the sales 
forecasts and production room. FCS has patented the design of their fin plugs, as well as the connection part 
on the fin itself. This implies that as long as their patent is still in force, no one can create compatible fins.

In order to understand the competition and profitability within the surfboard industry, the Porter’s five forces 
analysis presents the industry’s underlying structure in a simple way (Porter, 2008). The products analyzed 
are sustainable surfboards from all categories. Within this specific industry, the competition is considered as 
global. No matter where, the structure in terms of rivals, buyers and barriers are similar in most countries.

5.4.1 Threat of new entrants 
  
The threat of new entrants is relatively high in the surfing industry. Based on the analysis method proposed 
by proposed by Porter (2008), the incumbency advantages independent of size and the supply-side 
economies of scale are considered to be the main market barriers. It illustrates the advantages that seasoned 
brands have over newcomers, such as preemption of the most favorable geographic locations, established 
brand identity, cumulative experiences, or the production of larger volumes at lower costs (Porter, 2008). The 
capital requirements to enter the market are relatively low, and the accesses to distribution channels are open. 
It is demonstrated by the fact that the surfboard industry is composed of few large players and dotted with 
numerous independent shapers. Despite having a reduced annual production and higher sale prices, the 
independent shapers act on a more local scale and are often perceived as authentic and part of the experience 
of the surf culture. As the annual growth rates are moderated, it gives the advantage to newcomers to grow 
by taking market shares to incumbents (Porter, 2008). 
  
5.4.2 Bargaining power of buyers 
  
The bargaining power of the end customer is considered as moderate. The surfing industry can be divided 
into two segments, the business-to-customers (B2C) and the business-to-business (B2B). The independent 
shapers are mostly operating in a B2C configuration, where the customers have a low bargaining power due 
to the small amount purchased and the uniqueness of the product. When large manufacturers are selling their 
products through surf shops or sporting goods stores (B2B), the bargaining power of the distributors is 
relatively high. They can directly influence the purchasing decisions of the end customer and usually buy in 
important quantities. It is common for larger brands to distribute their products through small local channels, 
such as surf shops, which usually offer a wide variety of surf brands, models and prices, and sometimes offer 
multiple selling points nationally. Some strategies adopted by the large manufacturers tend to diminish the 
influence that the resellers have one the end users, by directly marketing the products to their target audience 
through advertising videos, sponsorship of contests and pros, with the aim of creating preferences for their 
products. 
  
As a whole, a large part of the customers, and especially the younger ones, will tend to be more price 
sensitive if the performances are not a major concern. The other part of the end customers, less price 
sensitive, are willing to pay more as long as it is justified in some ways (quality, materials, performances, 
uniqueness) (M. Ring, personal communication, April 28, 2017).

5.4.3 Threat of substitute products or services 
  
The analysis of substitutes to a sustainable surfboard can take many different forms, and is considered as 
high. The main form of substitution is the other so called “sustainable surfboards”, and especially the ones 
benefiting from the Sustainable Surf recognition. This category of surfboard is only sold by 54 certified 
board builder around the world (The EcoBoard Project, 2017). The second substituents are regular 
surfboards. They represent the larger part of the competition, have a global presence, and can compete on 
price. Regular surfboards also benefit from the status of the “industry standard“, meaning that they are the 
default choice of customers and present very low switching cots. The third substituent group is composed of 
the other sports that are practiced in the water, and require any other type of board, such as SUP, bodyboard, 
skimboard, kite surf, wakeboard, wakesurf, and so on. Basically, these substituents perform a similar 
function by a different mean, and usually involve low switching costs. With a more distant look and by 
considering the surfboards as being only a leisure activity, kayaking, sailing or even jetskiing might be 
considered a substituent to a sustainable surfboard. These substitutes are directly threatening the surfboard 
rental agents, which indirectly affect the surfboard manufacturers. 
  
As the threat of substitutes is high, the industry profitability suffers. Porter (2008) explains that to increase 
the profitability and growth potential of a product in a highly competitive market, one must differentiate 
itself through product performance, marketing, or other means. He argues that competition on different 
dimensions than on price will improve the customers’ value and justify higher prices. 
  
5.4.4 Bargaining power of suppliers 
  
As the constituents of regular surfboards are well known and developed by many companies around the 
world, the bargaining power of suppliers is relatively low. This allows the surfboard manufacturer to switch 
very easily from one supplier to the other without having to change any production process. This is mainly 
due to the well-developed market for fiberglass, epoxy resin and petroleum based foam. When it comes to 
sustainable surfboards, the bargaining power of suppliers is high. Fewer producers of natural fibers and bio-
resin exist, leaving only little options to the sustainable surfboard producers. In the case of natural fiber 
producers, the recent entry of the automotive industry as a customer reduces the power of other industries. 
But all the interest around natural fibers shows positive signs of growth, as it is expected that the European 
annual production of natural fiber rise up to 830,000 tons by 2020, which represents a ~260% increase in 10 
years (Yan et al., 2014). 
  
As previously explained, the two main suppliers of fin systems have a very high influence over the surfboard 
manufacturers, as their fin systems influence the customer’s buying decision. Despite being offered at low 
price, different fin plugs from different manufacturers impose specific installation methods to the surfboard 
manufacturers, and continuously change as the new models are released. 
  
5.4.5 Analysis 
  
The level of profitability in the surfboard industry is moderated to low. Most of the materials are available, 
cheap and do not require any specific training before usage. Moreover, due to the over abundance of products 
on the market, the differentiation between the products is hard to make. When it comes to sustainable 
surfboards, the threat of substitute products and the power of suppliers are the most powerful forces. 
Sustainability being a recent trend, both in terms of marketing strategy and product development, allows the 
creation of new and different products that might compete, or even be superior to the industry standard. This 
analysis is supported by multiple international initiatives aiming at reducing the human impact on the planet 
and supporting alternative solutions, as the 17 United Nation global compact sustainable development goals 
(UN, 2015). These kinds of initiatives prove that sustainability is a viable path to follow in the long-run. 
Furthermore, having access to the Pond bio resin represents a strategic advantage over the competitors, 
facilitating the technological and aesthetic differentiation of the products. 
  
As a whole, the introduction of a new sustainable surfboard should make use of the following guideline: 
Grow by taking market shares to the large manufacturers, market the product directly to the customers and 
avoid distribution channels with many participants, differentiate the product and its commercialization from 
the others, and ensure the access to a reliable supplier network.

5.5 Customer proﬁle 
  
Defining the customer profile and its needs ensures that the product is designed to meet a specific goal for a 
specific customer, and provides a fact base for justifying the product specifications (Eppinger & Ulrich, 
2012). Two types of data-gathering method have been used: an online survey, and face-to-face interviews. 
The online survey served as starting point to establish the general characteristics of surfers, by understanding 
when do they buy, what do they buy, and how do they buy. The face-to-face interviews provided the 
remaining information to fine-tune the customer profile that enabled the segmentation of the target 
customers. 
  
The online survey was exclusively dedicated to surfers, and has been published on various social networks, 
surfing dedicated websites, and emailed to personal contacts. Unfortunately, the survey did not generate a 
sufficient amount of answers, less than 30, to be useful in gathering quantitative data. Nevertheless, three 
main information have been used out of this survey: ~65% of the respondents are buying a new surfboard 
within two years, ~70% of them are promoting the performances of the surfboard in their future purchase, 
and ~50% of them value the advice of a friend or the seller more than the brand reputation or the pros 
choices. The complete survey can be found in appendix 4. 
  
To better understand the surfers in the target audience, five face-to-face interviews have been conducted 
during a field research in Klitmøller (Denmark), also known as “cold Hawaii ”. The interviews were 
conducted in an informal setup, with the use of an interview guide to structure the dialogue and document the 
interactions. The duration of the interviews varied from 15 to 30 minutes. The interviewees had been selected 
on the basis of the surfboard they were surfing, a shortboard. The interview guide can be found in appendix 
5. 
  
As the information gathered with both the survey and the interviews did not provide sufficient clues enabling 
the creation of a customer profile, further researches have been conducted to understand the difference 
between “normal” surfer and shortboard surfers. As shortboards require a certain level of skills to be 
maneuvered, it makes sense to deduce that a shortboard user has a surfing level ranging from high 
intermediate to professional. After that, when looking at the design characteristics of shortboards, it was 
difficult to find relevant guidelines that would define which criteria the professionals were looking for when 
selecting a shortboard. Their decisions are based on the weather condition (wind, heights of the waves, shape 
of the waves) and the surfing style they wanted to adopt this specific day (aggressive, relax, performing 
figures). This is one of the main reasons why advanced and professional surfers have an important number of 
shortboards, 3 to 7, each varying in their designs. Most of the time, they would bring 1 to 3 surfboards with 
them on daily trips to prevent any damage occurring on one of their surfboards or to be able to face different 
weather conditions. As it was not possible to define any patterns that could guide the design characteristics of 
a shortboard used by the very advanced to professional surfers, the other range of shortboard users has been 
analyzed, the higher intermediate and early advanced. It became interesting to notice that the surfers in this 
range were owning fewer boards, 1 to 3, and did not have the chance to go surfing as often as the higher 
advanced and professionals. Therefore, the board they own should be slightly more polyvalent in order to 
match most weather conditions and surfing moods. Having a more versatile surfboard also requires fewer 
skills to be maneuvered. Figure 5.4 show where the target groups stands in the whole range of surfers. Based 
on a logical deduction, it became clear that the majority of surfers using shortboard is encompassed in the 
target group, as there is only a handful of professional surfers in the world, and that the personal investments 
required to reach an advanced level implies that the surfer spend a significant amount of time surfing. This 
first segmentation of the shortboard users facilitated the creation of five sub-segments on the basis of 
information gathered during a co-creation session, described further down in this report. The sub-segments 
characterize surfers who are: eco-sensitive, financially limited, enthusiasts about new developments, 
frustrated due to the reduced surfing opportunities that are offered to them, or simply followers of the major 
trends. Figure 5.5 presents the five sub-segments within the target group. This analysis is summarized by the 
creation of a second persona, James, which is described in detail in the appendix 6.

visualize the position of the stakeholders in every phase (van Boeijen et al., 2014). The process tree analysis

6.4 Design task formulation 
  
The design task formulation aims at illustrating the need, intention, opportunity, and relation to the past 
products that will be achieved by the new one. When used in a development where many stakeholders are 
involved in the design process, it helps to put everyone on the same page and serves as guideline when 
decisions need to be taken. The task formulation should let a certain degree of freedom in its understanding 
and leave room for potential reformulation during the development due to co-evolution or changing 
conditions (Andreasen, Hansen & Cash, 2015). The design task regrouping most of the information collected 
in the previous phases of the project has been formulated as: 
  
“Surfers are now aware of the environmental impact caused by the surfing industry, and are demanding 
sustainable alternatives without compromising the performances and the perception of the current products. 
The new product should be inline with the surf culture, and keep on carrying its values and codes. It should 
reduce the environmental impact of the surfing industry, as well as the risks threatening the health of the 
workers. The new shortboard should be adopted as a mainstream product, due to its fair price and 
polyvalence. The involvement of the brand in the end-of-life stage represents a strategic advantage to 
improve the customer relationship. Resellers and environmental NGOs are important partners in the 
implementation of a sustainable alternative.”

7. Product Design 
  
This section will present the development undertook to design the final product. It should be taken into 
consideration that the overall development of this project consists of a market-pull strategy, starting from a 
market opportunity to which the appropriate technology is selected, as the bio resin (Eppinger & Ulrich, 
2012). Therefore, the interest area remains in the right association of materials to reach the end-goal, rather 
than on radically changing the way of surfing. The first part introduces the product specifications, by 
describing the selection process applied to each part of the shortboard in order to select the most appropriate 
material. The second part presents the design of every element composing the final product, and is supported 
by multiple human-centered design methods aiming at refining the final concept. Lastly, the prototyping 
process undertook describes and presents the various tests conducted on the materials and the production 
techniques all along the project. 
  
7.1 Product speciﬁcation 
               
 
7.1.1 Technical speciﬁcations 
  
Before presenting the product specifications, table 7.1 shows a decomposition of the system of activities 
composing the shortboard by using the domain theory as presented by Andreasen, Hansen, and Cash (2015). 
The domain theory identifies the interaction between three-linked system: the organs, the parts and the 
activities. It is used to define how the product is used (activities), how it functions (organs), and how it is 
built (parts), and allows the designers to “spell” a product in different ways (Andreasen, Hansen & Cash, 
2015). Here, the definition of the parts, organs, and activities is based on the existing constituents of a 
surfboard, as the new product can be considered as an incremental design. Viewing a product from the organ 
domain gives an overview of the interactions between the different parts composing the organ, but also the 
relation between the organs and the user. The following parts of the report only address the design 
specification of the core and interface organ, as they are the primary focus of the project and that the other 
organs are less environmentally problematic.

7.1.3 Core material selection 
  
One of the biggest challenges addressed by this project is the replacement of the conventional core material, 
PU and EPS foams, used for the creation of shortboards. Their low price, ease of processing and lightweight 
has made them the most desirable and almost irreplaceable core material. As shown in section 3.4 
Environmental issues, the core material accounts for a large part of the CO2 emissions in the production of a 
surfboard. It is therefore fundamental to find an alternative solution to reduce the environmental impact of 
the core material. 
  
In order to find a light, stiff and biodegradable substituent, the material database CES EduPack has been 
used. The research has been conducted on all the 3,900 bulk materials available in the database, by applying 
filters to eliminate the materials that are not suited for this application (CES EduPack, 2016). Figure 7.2 
shows the overall materials available for each category, and sorted by density and young’s modulus. Figure 
7.3 shows the biodegradable materials, which are composed of natural materials and polymers. Out of this 
restrained list, the cork and balsa wood can be identified as being the lightest. Adding to the material 
identified, some research conducted on PLA polymers have outline the existence of PLA foam, that can be 
used as substitutes to regular EPS foam.

functionality and aesthetic appeal of the product, both to differentiate it from the competitors and match the

the time incorporated throughout the whole product development process (Eppinger & Ulrich, 2012). This 
section starts by an initial concept proposal regrouping the attributes of the product, which are then refined 
two times by using human-centered design method, and finally presents a final concept proposal.

As mentioned earlier in this report, the intent of the project is to create a polyvalent shortboard that could be 
used by high intermediate and early advanced surfers. Translated into design a characteristic, having a 
polyvalent shortboard means that its volume should be bigger that the shortboards for advanced users, with 
the aim of improving the buoyancy of the board. By maintaining a standard shortboard size, such as 6’0’’, 
the volume and the buoyancy can be increased by changing the width and thickness dimensions of the board. 
Furthermore, the front rockers also influence the buoyancy, as it defines the promotion of the surfboard that 
is in the water. The other parameters mainly impact the control of the shortboard over different type of 
waves. As for many other sports, the dimension of the equipment depends on the measurements of the user. 
In this case the shortboard will be designed for an average male user of approximately 1.70 to 1.75m for a 
weight ranging form 65 to 70 kg.

this project, all the prototypes were either analytical or physical focused prototypes, and were mainly used 
for learning and communication purposes. Figure 7.8 presents the types of prototypes that have been created

7.2.3 Accessory integration 
  
The accessories to add to the shortboard are the fin plugs and leash plug. As stated at the bottom of the 
section 5.3 Global surfboard market analysis, the fin market is controlled by two companies, therefore 
making it very difficult for newcomers to enter the market and establish a brand. Furthermore, due to the 
patents that protect both the fin plug and the compatible fin design, it is not possible to design, produce, and 
sell fins that are compatible with the fin plugs from the existing brands. Therefore, the fin plugs and leash 
plug should be bought from the existing manufacturers and integrated to the new design. Doing so enables 
the shortboard to be compatible with the existing accessories of the surfing industry, and ease the adoption of 
the future customers. In regards to the sustainable aspect of this strategy, it gives the opportunity to the new 
customers to reuse their fins, therefore avoiding over consumption behaviors. Adding to this, the fin plugs 
and the leash plug are fairly easy to remove from the core of the surfboard once it reaches the end of its 
useful life. 
  
7.2.4 Final design 
  
After having gone through the design of each individual components of the shortboard, the final design 
proposal regrouping all the characteristics of the product is presented in figure 7.14. Andreasen, Hansen, & 
Cash (2015) present that a design proposal aims to answer to the need and intention articulated during the 
research phase, but also to present the attributes, target group and positioning of the product. The final design 
proposal has been formulated as follow: 
  
“The Orbis Board is designed to meet a general demand coming from the surfers and other important 
stakeholders, for reducing the environmental impact of the surfboards in general. In order to reach that goal, 
and compete with the products already on the market, all the materials have been selected for their 
performances and low environmental impact, as well as their property of being biodegradable under 
controlled conditions. The design specificity of the shortboard makes it perfectly suited for what is 
considered to be the largest group of active surfers, characterized by surfing levels ranging from high 
intermediate to early advanced. It features a wide distance at chest heights and a bigger volume (36l) than 
the similar sized shortboards. The improved buoyancy leads to a more pleasurable surfing experience in 
small waves conditions, where the paddle power and good speed will be its main assets. The evolving profile 
of the rails, and dynamic bottom contour, ensures a good hold and a high stability on the face of bigger 
waves. The thruster fin system is designed to improve the maneuverability of the shortboard, and enable the 
surfer to easily perform shot turns. The shortboard is offered with the fin plugs from the two most prominent 
brands, making it compatible with most fins on the market. The outer layer of the surfboard composed of 
Flax fibers and bio resin protects the PLA foam core from impacts and water infiltration. The unique 
appearance of the Orbis Board makes it recognizable even in the most crowed surfing spot.” 
  
The name of the shortboard “Orbis” comes from the latin word for world, which is what the shortboard aims 
at protecting. The combination of materials, apart from the fin plugs and leash plugs, are all completely 
biodegradable under controlled conditions, and rely on resources renewable on an annual basis. 
On a general notice, it should be acknowledged that one of the foundations of human-centered design is to 
keep iterating, and almost never consider that a product is ready (Design Kit, n.d.). But as in any project,

7.3 Prototyping of NFC 
  
Besides the design of the shortboard, an important part of the development has been allocated to the creation 
of NFC prototypes. The prototyping phase has been conducted by using a heuristic approach of 
experimentations aiming at reducing the uncertainties around the use and the performances of the bio-resin 
developed by Pond ApS, by creating proof-of-concept prototypes (Eppinger & Ulrich, 2012). Prototypes can 
be used to test the technological feasibility through functional testing, reduce the uncertainty of the economic 
viability of the product from a market point of view, and finally reduce the uncertainty on the desirability 
from a user perspective (den Ouden, 2012). This part of the project has been conducted alongside the other 
parts, and was spreaded over three month. 
  
7.3.1 Prototyping method 
  
The first parts of the prototypes were all focused physical prototypes, oriented toward learning and 
communication purposes. A total of 13 small-scale plates combining the flax fibers and the bio-based resin 
were created (prototype 1 to13). Different mixing and curing techniques were tested and compared on the 
basis of the results obtained. The second part consisted of creating small-scale sandwich structure using the 
flax fibers, the bio resin, and a solid core material, generally wood. In this second part, a total of two small-
scale comprehensive physical prototypes were initially created to be used for learning, communication, and 
integration purposes, but their quality did not enabled them to be used as integration prototypes (prototype 14 
and 15). Finally, the last part aimed at creating small-scale sandwich structures combining all the selected 
materials of the final product, with the aim of being used for integration purposes. Unfortunately, the last part 
has not been conducted due to persisting failures encountered during the two first parts of the prototyping 
phase, but also due to some technical limitations of the materials, which is explained further down in this 
section. The picture in figure 7.15 presents in a chronological order the 11 tests conducted at the DTU

As presented earlier in the report, many production methods have been developed to create composite 
materials. In the context of this project, the VARTM method has been identified as the most accessible one,

use of heat resistant equipments. In addition to the VARTM method, five prototypes have been created by

7.3.3 Future developments on the NFC 
  
The numerous attempt realized to obtain a satisfying prototype have outlined some of the limitation of the 
VARTM technique when high temperature and long curing periods are required, as well as the importance of 
using accurate and adapted tools. One of the biggest factors limiting the combination of the resin, the natural 
fibers and PLA foam arises from the different temperature limits of the materials. The PLA foam has a Heat 
Defect Temperature (HDT) around 110°C, above which it starts to soften and loses its mechanical properties. 
The resin can be melted at temperatures between 100°C - 110°C but requires higher curing temperatures to 
reach its final stage, which could be problematic in regards to the temperature limit of the PLA foam. Based 
on the experiments conducted throughout the whole duration of the project and numerous discussions with 
the founders of Pond ApS, two possible paths to reach the desired outcome have been identified. Either 
investigate for other and more advanced production methods, or consider that future technological 
improvements will be made on both the HDT of the PLA foam, and the processing and curing temperatures 
of the bio resin. In the context of this project, the assumption that the technological improvements made on 
the PLA foam and the bio resin will enable them to be processed together at reasonable temperatures is 
pursued. Few reasons being in favor of this option are that: Pond ApS is continuously working on reducing 
the processing temperature of their resin system, PLA foams are relatively new to the market and still going 
under many researches and development projects, and that more sophisticated production methods do not 
guarantee the combination of these two materials. 
  
The following sections of this project will be conducted by assuming that the future versions of the bio resin 
system will enable it to be processed and cured at 80°C over a period of four hours, and that the HDT of the 
PLA foam is at 140°C. This last assumption is based on developments made by the joint venture of Total and 
Corbion in the creation of a heat resistant PLA bioplastic, currently not available as foam, but which supports 
the path followed in this project (Total-Corbion, 2017). Furthermore, in order to speed-up the development 
process of the materials, a close collaboration with Pond ApS should be sustained to assist them in the testing 
of their bio resin system, as well as supporting them in the promotion of their products to increase the 
number of experiments conducted using different manufacturing methods. Engaging with the raw material 
producers as BASF or Total-Corbion can support the improvements made on the PLA foam, but also with 
their customers using the PLA resin or granulates. Contributing to the increase of the need is a way to boost 
the development of a technology. Further details on the risk associated to the development of the project are 
presented in section 8.5.4 Risk analysis. 
  
Based on the above-mentioned assumption, the most suitable production method is still believed to be the 
VARTM. It is thought to be more adapted than the current hand laminating method used in the surfing 
industry for the three following reasons: 
  
- Reduces the exposition of the workers to the resin. 
- Only uses the least minimum amount of resin required. 
- Improve the mechanical properties of the end product. 
The interest in using the VARTM technique also relies in the low costs associated to it, and its endless 
adaptability to new shapes, which is crucial in the surfing industry. A total of five processes have been 
identified as essential to produce the shortboard: CNC machining of the foam, manual finishing (placement 
of the accessories and natural fibers), VARTM, curing, and surface finishing. Figure 7.22 presents a timeline 
featuring each process for the production of one shortboard and includes the number of worker at each stages 
and the nature of the task, which leads to a total production time of 5 hours 50 minutes per unit. Table 7.10 
presents all the tools required in the production process, with their maximal production capacity per day, by 
taking into account 8 working hours per day. All the information presented are based on values given by 
existing manufacturers or distributors located in Europe, U.S.A., and Australia. It can be seen that the 
maximal daily production reaches 16 units per day, and is limited by the VARTM technique, which depends

Doing so would increase the annual product from initially ~3,800 units to ~11,500 units. These kinds of 
possibilities should be addressed few years after the introduction of the product on the market.

8. Commercial Feasibility 
  
8.1 Methodology 
  
The evaluation of the commercial feasibility has been conducted by using both causation and an effectuation 
approach. The causation approach consists of achieving a predefined objective by using a set of given means 
(Effectuation, 2016). This has been done by searching and applying management theories, in order to 
articulate a commercialization strategy for the shortboard designed in the previous section. On the other side, 
the effectuation approach reflects the use of evolving means to achieve new and different goals. This logic of 
entrepreneurial actions presented by Saras Sarasvathy consists of four principles applicable in the uncertain 
start-up phase of a company to mitigate the failure costs for the entrepreneur (Effectuation, 2016). This part 
of the project has been driven by the “bird in hand principle”, illustrating the situation of an entrepreneur 
starting with his own means to shape the new venture (Andersen, & Howard, 2016). Each four principles are 
guided by the general logic that the entrepreneur is a “pilot in a plane” acknowledging that the future is 
fundamentally unpredictable, but controllable to some extend through human actions (Effectuation, 2016). It 
has been applied in this section of the project by building the case with accessible resources in the time frame 
available. 
This chapter of the report will refer to the sustainable surfboard created previously as “the Orbis Board”, and 
the team in charged of the commercialization of the Orbis Board as “the company”.  
  
8.2 Business idea 
  
As presented previously in section 5.5 Customer profile, the target groups are the surfers within the high 
intermediate and early advanced levels. One of their characteristics is the average number of surfboards they 
own, usually 1 to 3. This observation conveys two basic information: Firstly, they are willing to acquire 
polyvalent surfboards that can be used in multiple weather conditions. Secondly, they might also be limited 
financially, or simply do not want to invest too much in their surfboards, as they only have limited amount 
opportunities per year to use them. Based on my own analysis, this category of surfer is also the larger in 
terms of active participants. The first parameter of the target group, using polyvalent surfboard, is directly 
met by the design of the shortboard, whereas the second one, keeping the expenses down, should be 
addressed by the strategy developed to produce and bring the product to the market. When considering the 
general and implied objective of this project, developing a biodegradable surfboard to reduce the 
environmental impact of the industry, it makes sense to create a product that is accessible to the largest 
population as possible. Thus, to avoid creating a high-end product used by a small amount of elite surfers and 
environmentally sensible persons. One of the most common ways to reach this goal is to compete on the 
price of the product, without compromising its quality. 
  
Therefore, in order to minimize the risk, and articulate a practical strategy that would define the place of the 
product in the market space, the strategy canvas developed by W. Chan Kim and Renée Mauborgne in the 
context of the blue ocean strategy has been used. This tool aims at defining the places of a product on the 
market where value can be created, usually by reducing costs, by comparing it to competitive products (Tidd, 
& Bessant, 2013). Figure 8.1 shows the strategy canvas applied to the shortboard market, by comparing the 
Orbis Board to three competitive shortboard presented earlier in this report (5.3 Global surfboard market 
analysis).

The previous estimations give an overview of the total number of potential customers in the target group, but 
to establish sales forecast, it is essential to have an overview of the number of shortboard purchased by the 
target group in the different geographical location. Starting from the known amount of surfboard sold in the 
world, 400,000 units, and the share that the shortboard represented in 2013, 38%, the total number of 
shortboard sold each reach is around 150,000 units (TechNavio, 2016; S. Smith, personal communication, 
April 24, 2017). As presented in section 5.3 Global surfboard market analysis, it is forecasted that the total 
number of shortboards sold in the world reaches 280,000 units by 2020. Therefore, an up-to-date estimate 
could establish that roughly 200,000 shortboard are sold every year. This would represent a CAGR of 8.33% 
on the shortboard sales between 2013 and 2017, which is in line with the forecasted CAGR of 9.46% 
forecasted between 2016 and 2020 by the market research analysis institute TechNavio (TechNavio, 2016). 
Adding to this, shortboards are known to have a shorter life expectancy than the other surfboard designs due 
to their dimensions and intensive usage, leading to a higher consumption rate than the other surfboard 
designs. The geographical distribution of the estimated 200,000 shortboards sold per year is presented in the 
last column of the table 8.1. 
  
In order to estimate the amount of shortboard expected to be sold during a time period, usually one year, the 
model proposed by Eppinger and Ulrich (2012) for the sale potential of durable products is used. It consists 
of calculating the quantity Q, as the multiplication of N, the number of potential customer or current sales 
with A, the fraction of customers that are aware of the product and to which it is available and P, the 
probability that the product is purchased. In the case of the shortboard, N takes the value of the number of 
shortboards sold per years, 200,000 units. A is based on the assumption that 1/3 (33%) of the surfer in the 
target group will be aware of the product during the first year, and finally P has been set at 5%, meaning that 
one surfer over 20 would buy the shortboard. 
Q = N X A X P = 200,000 X 0.33 X 0.05 = 3,300 units/year 
  
This estimation represents a total market share of ~1.6% per year. Based on the production method selected 
which can support an annual production accounting for 1,9% of the market, and on the sales forecast, it is 
expected that the total sales could reach 2.8% of the total addressable market at the fourth year on the 
market, with an organic growth the tree first years. The third year on the market is expected to be a milestone 
to evaluate the performances of the company, as it will be the year of the Olympic games where the 
marketing strategy aims at sponsoring an athlete competing in any of the categories present, thus is expected 
to positively impact the sales. Further details on the implementation plan and on the marketing strategy are 
presented in section 8.5.5 Implementation plan and 8.5.6 Marketing strategy. Nevertheless, this estimation 
should be interpreted with caution and take into account multiple factors that could affect the forecasted 
sales; as the importance of word-of-mouth, the fidelity of the concept description, the pricing, or the level of 
promotion (Eppinger & Ulrich, 2012).

northern hemisphere market active from May to September, and the sudden hemisphere from October to 
April. This factor also serves as guideline to select the market onto which it is relevant to focus on at a given 
time of the year. Based on the table 8.1 it is clear that the U.S.A. and Australia are the most attractive market

As the desired position of the product in the market has been defined in the previous section, the following 
part will define its practical application. One of the main selling arguments of the new product should be its 
competitive price compared to the industry standard. One way to reduce the costs is to bypass the current 
value chain system, as the one presented in figure 5.3. It should aim at reducing the value captured by all the 
intermediary actors in the whole chain. During the field research and in the Porter five forces analysis, it 
came out that one of the most important participants in the value chain is the surf shop. They represent the 
default choice of the customers to acquire surfboards, but they are also the ones capturing an important part 
of the value of the products. One of the major problems faced by the brands in the current setup of the supply 
chain is related to the differentiation of their products in the surf shops. As they usually represent an 
important number of different surfboard brands, as Rider Shack, a famous surf shop in Los Angeles who sells 
surfboards from 17 different brands (Rider shack, 2017). It is almost impossible for a brand to ensure proper 
in-store marketing with the current situation, where an important part of the purchase decision of the 
customers is in the sellers’ hands. This phenomenon has also been identified in the key stakeholder analysis. 
Based on this reasoning, the strategy used to advertise and sell the products should be executed via an online 
store. This solution has the advantage to simplify the supply chain and therefore reduce the costs associated 
to the products, as well as empowering the brand with the complete marketing of the products. Using an 
online strategy to sell surfboards has nothing new, as the online market already exists, either to buy new

illustrates the fact that surfers dare to buy surfboards online from almost “unknown” manufacturers on the

8.4 Value proposition 
  
According to den Ouden (2012), the development of every innovative solution is bringing value at different 
levels. In his study, the author presents an analysis framework looking at four different levels of value aiming 
at defining where and what kinds of values are created. The following levels are presented: The values for 
the users, the values for the organization, the values for the ecosystem, and the values for the society. These 
levels of values are then analyzed from four different value concept; economics, psychology, sociology and 
ecology. The use of this framework in the context of this project provides a deeper level of understanding on 
the integration of the Orbis Board in its context, and helps to articulate an initial value proposition presenting 
the tangible and intangible benefits of the new solution, as the economical, functional, emotional, and 
symbolic dimensions (den Ouden, 2012). The complete value framework analysis conducted on the Orbis 
Board can be found in appendix 10. 
  
The initial value proposition has been formulated as: 
  
“The Orbis Board is targeted at surfers with good surfing skills who want a shortboard suitable for most 
wave conditions. As they don’t have the chance to surf very often, or an important budget, they are keen on 
paying a moderate price for a shortboard with a good learning curve. During their previous surfboard 
purchase, they have experienced some difficulties in finding the right shortboard for their level, especially 
due to the abundance of choice. They are used to purchase all sorts of products online, and especially if it is 
price competitive to the products in brick-and-mortar stores. They are concerned about the environmental 
impact of the surfing industry and are willing to be part of the solution, but they don’t want that the green 
aspect of the product gets into the way of the surfing properties of the shortboard.” 
8.5 Business Model 
  
Johnson, Christensen, and Kagermann (2008) present that a business model is a combination of four 
interlinked elements that create and deliver value when associated; the customer value proposition, the profit 
formula, the key resources, and the key processes. Every successful business model starts with a well-
defined customer value proposition; addressing the “job” the customers are hiring a product or service for. 
Then the profit formula defines how the company is capturing value from the value it is offering. The key 
resources and key processes detail what the company needs to deliver the value and how it will do it 
(Johnson et al., 2008). 
  
The commercialization of the Orbis Board will only be executed through direct-to-consumer channels, as 
online sales and direct sales. This strategy is adopted to avoid the inclusion of various intermediate 
participants in the supply chain capturing value before reaching the customers, but also to ensure a total 
quality control of the service, and provide better value through an improved customer experience. Being able 
to sell directly to the customers represents a strategic advantage over the incumbents who are stuck with 
existing channel partners and resellers limiting their reactivity to changes (Teece, 2010).

8.5.1 People and organization 
  
Building up an effective team is a crucial aspect of the implementation and success of a new company. The 
cross functionality of its members improves the capacity of the firm to address a number of challenges in the 
major functional areas: marketing, finance, and operation (Allen, 2010). Five core members have been 
identified as essential to the creation and growth of the company in its early years, and is composed of: a 
CEO, a mechanical engineer, an industrial designer, a marketing manager, and a logistician. Adding to the 
core team, six consultants are shown to be essential to the good development of the company. And lastly, the 
production methods of the shortboard have determined that 5 workers are needed. 
  
The CEO should be a lead entrepreneur with experiences in early stage startups and the development of 
manufacturing plants in Asia. He should contribute to the vision of the company, by combining a strong 
financial background with good analytical skills. He will also be playing the role of emblematic figure in the 
promotion of the product and the communications with various important stakeholders, as the politicians and 
the environmental NGOs. The mechanical engineer needs to have competences in fluid dynamics, composite 
materials, and manufacturing processes. By bringing his technical knowledge to the product development 
process, he will improve the performance of the products, and ensure the competitiveness of the company 
from a production standpoint. Many tasks will be executed in close collaboration with the industrial designer. 
The industrial designer will take care of the new surfboard designs and the graphical line of the brand, as 
well as ensuring the quality of the end products. He will use his creative skills to solve the problems faced by 
the company, and engaged with end-users to constantly improve the products. In collaboration with the CEO 
and marketing manager, he will assist to trade shows and contribute to the promotion of the products, as well 
as ensuring that the surfboards are the stat-of-the-art products in terms of design, trends, and environmental 
impact. The marketing manager will be in charge of the communication of the products, as well as 
understanding the market trends and evolution over the different geographical locations. He should have 
strong experiences in online marketing and online store management, as well as mastering the use of social 
networks to advertise the products. The last member of the core team is a logistician, responsible for the 
planning, implementation and control of the distribution system of the products from the manufacture to the 
end customers. He will ensure an effective distribution flow throughout the growth of the company. One of 
his main focuses will be to minimize the use of resources, for cost and environmental reasons. In a joint 
effort with the marketing manager, they will build up the distribution network in the different key markets. 
Finally, a selection criteria for the core members of the company will be their high interest for sports in 
general, not only surfing. There is a tremendous amount of surfboard companies relating themselves to the 
deeper roots of the surf culture, or how surfing is their way of living. This personal and professional 
involvement in the surf culture may be the cause of a lack of innovation, by clustering the perception of the 
discipline. The core members should be hired on the basis of network recommendations, or through the 
services of a headhunter. 
  
To support the core team, additional resources and competences will be needed at critical moments faced by 
the company. Six members have been identified as being part of the extended team. Before the end of the 
research and development phase, a web developer and User Experience (UX) designer, will be assigned to 
create the website and eCommerce platform, to ensure a perfectly controlled user experience before the

8.5.2 Strategic partners 
  
Tidd and Bessant (2013)  present that alliances can be characterized in various ways, but are usually 
horizontal or vertical, and represent a way of accessing or acquiring market or technical know-how. 
Horizontal relationship describes the alliances between two companies, sometimes competitors, benefiting 
from each other, through cross-licensing, consortia, or joint venture, to name a few. Vertical relationships on 
the other hand, are mainly used for cost reduction purposes, and are characterized by subcontractor or 
supplier relationships (Tidd, & Bessant, 2013). 
  
The implementation, adoption, and future development of the shortboard are highly dependent on multiple 
types of alliances. Horizontal alliances are expected to be concluded with Pond ApS, the associations 
Sustainable Surf, and the WSL. A partnership alliance with Pond ApS would benefit both companies, as it 
would in one way generate direct feedback related to the use, performances and customers’ perception of the 
bio resin, and on the other way from direct improvements of the bio resin system to the specific use. In the 
case of Sustainable Surf, one of the most famous association promoting sustainable approaches in the surfing 
industry, a type of partnership should also be found to contribute to the education of the general public on the 
environmental impact of the surfing industry, but also benefit from the notoriety and international 
recognition of the association. Lastly, the most important partner for the growth of the company is the WSL, 
responsible for the organization of the highest-level surf competitions in the world. It is possible for brands 
to financially sponsor specific events on the championship tour, in return of gaining an impressive visibility 
and being able to communicate directly with the customers (World Surf League, 2017). Furthermore, the 
WSL advertise itself as being in favor of environmentally friendly solutions, as their core business relies on 
the oceans (World Surf League, 2017). 
  
As presented previously in this report, the environmental NGOs and politicians in the key markets can play 
an important role in promoting the eco-friendly aspect of the shortboard. Environmental NGOs as Green 
Peace, are not only pointing out problems that should be addressed, they are also advising the general public, 
who are the consumers, on the environmental impact of their consumption habits, as it is the case with their 
“guide to greener electronics”, informing the customers on the environmental impact of their electronic 
accessories (Green Peace, 2012). So these organizations would not advertise the product, but could play a 
role in communicating its benefits. In a similar manner, politicians also have some influences on the 
purchase decisions of the consumers, and are generally in favor of sustainable approaches as it contributes to 
their promotion. 
  
8.5.3 Intellectual Property Rights 
  
In order not to limit the benefits derived from an innovation, the usage of Intellectual Property Rights (IPR) 
as protection measure should be balanced between the desire to conserve the knowledge, and the desire to 
share them to promote innovation (Tidd, & Bessant, 2013). The IPR strategy is largely dependent on the 
global vision of the company and the nature of its market and technology (Tidd, & Bessant, 2013). The aim 
of the project being the reduction of the environmental impact of the surfing industry implies the use of 
moderated to little protective measures to foster the adoption of the innovation by other players in the 
industry. Prior to the creation of the protection strategy, all the possible areas that could benefit from a 
protection have been mapped in figure 8.5, and presented to an Associate Patent Attorney from Zacco, one of 
the largest European IP firm. On the material side of the shortboard, the only possibility would be to patent 
the combination of material for the specific intended use (H. Kuhre, Personal communication, May 19th, 
2017). This would imply that no competitors would be allowed to use the exact same material combination 
for the same purpose. This option is not considered to be relevant as it is easy to get around it, would limit 
Pond ApS in its commercial development, and would slow down the adoption of the innovation. Sharing the 
knowledge behind the Orbis Board can be inserted in a model of open innovation and organizational culture, 
allowing both the customers and the competitors to be fully aware of the current situation and contribute to 
the changes. For the manufacturing of the shortboard, the production steps and finishing techniques should 
be kept as a trade secret, contributing to the intellectual capital of the company, and protected by 
nondisclosure agreements (NDAs) with third party involved in the manufacturing process (Allen, 2010). The 
brand should be at the core of the protective strategy, as it sends signal to the customers, which are associated 
with the extrinsic value of the product (Wells et al., 2011). Therefore, a trademark should be used to protect 
the brand and its logo, but also the distinctive mark on the tip of the shortboard, as it can be seen in figure 
8.5. The two last elements involved in the IPR strategy are the design of the surfboard and the website. The 
design of the surfboard cannot benefit from design rights, as it does not fulfill some of the required criteria to 
be protected, as novelty or inventive step (Tidd, & Bessant, 2013). Concerning the website and its potential

The most important risks that have been identified are in the manufacturing and employees categories. The 
manufacturing risk can be addressed by developing early prototypes, but also in collaborating with various 
suppliers to find the best match between the raw materials and the manufacturing process. The risks 
associated with the employees remain in the time allocated to find the right people. Starting early on and 
make use of existing networking opportunities are key elements in ensuring that the right human resource is 
found in time. This risk analysis implies that the most important risks are prioritized over the others. 
  
The use of an online store as only mean to sell the shortboard implies multiple risks and opportunities. The 
main risk being a poor desirability of the customers to acquire the product online. This risk can be assessed 
before having a physical product by creating “fake” landing-page to measure the customer's interest and 
likelihood to acquire the product online. This method is part of the early validation process proposed by 
Laura Klein (2013), aiming at validating the market and the product as early as possible. Adding to this 
strategy, a crowdfunding campaign would practically demonstrate the desirability of the product, and provide 
incomes prior to the production of the product. If successful, the key assumptions in the customer's 
acceptance would be validated, leading the project to the next stage, building the final eCommerce platform. 
The whole success of the eCommerce platform relies on the website quality and its way of influencing the 
consumers’ perception of the product quality (Wells et al., 2011). The signaling theory proposed by Wells, 
Valacich, and Hess (2011), offers a framework to consider the parameters influencing the customers 
purchasing intention on eCommerce platforms. It considers that consumers assess the quality of a product on 
three criteria: the product asymmetries of information, the web site quality, and the signal credibility. The 
asymmetries of information are described by the scarcity of pre-purchase information and the clarity of post-
purchase information. With experience goods, as a surfboard, the scarcity of pre-purchased information often 
requires direct experiences through usage to assess the quality of the product. Similar to a brick-and-mortar 
store, the quality of the website will guide the users when they lack competences to asses the quality of the 
product, and will base their decision on the cues that are readily available. Finally, the signal credibility 
represents the extrinsic and intrinsic product attributes, such as the brand, price, warranties, or size and 
weight of the product, respectively. The nature of an eCommerce platform prevents it to convey the intrinsic 
values of the product, leading the consumers to rely on the information that are accessible, the extrinsic 
values. The website must leverage the informational signals to allow the user to assess the predictive value of 
the product with the confidence value of the signal. In most cases, the brand and the price benefit from a high 
confidence value. As the target group consists of experienced surfers, who have already tried several types of 
surfboards, the chances are that they know what they like from the various designs, and more importantly, 
what they are looking for. This means that with the right signals on the extrinsic value of the product, they 
will be able to relate to their own experiences to assess the intrinsic value the shortboard. These elements of 
the eCommerce platform should be developed using the competencies of a UX designer and the marketing 
manager. 
  
8.5.5 Implementation Plan 
  
In order to be able to understand the rate at which sales will occur, the implementation plan defines the 
actions that should be taken at each stage of the adoption-diffusion cycle (Allen, 2010). The implementation 
strategy and marketing approach are highly dependent on the market segment targeted, and its relative size 
(Allen, 2010). It makes sense to choose a market segment where the sales are expected to be quick, therefore 
where the need is the biggest (Murdock, 2016a). 
  
Figure 8.9 shows the estimated sales forecast over four years on the market. Three distinctive stages can be 
identified: the early adoption, the growth, and the peak. Each stage is carried by a specific segment of the

8.5.7 Economies of scale 
The economies of scale illustrates the fact that manufacturing costs of a product are dropping as the 
production volume increases (Eppinger & Ulrich, 2012). This phenomenon occurs for two main reason: 
firstly because the fixed costs are divided among more units, and secondly because the variable costs become 
lower due to larger quantities purchased and more efficient production processes (Eppinger & Ulrich, 2012). 
Figure 8.12 shows the decrease of the fixed costs association to the manufacturing equipment as the 
accumulated production volume increases over the years. If the sales forecast are correct, the fixed costs per 
unit due to the manufacturing equipment will be of ~EUR 9.6 for a total production of 12,060 units over four 
years. The fixed costs could even go below the indicated value as the production equipment could easily 
produce more units over a longer period of time. The decrease in the variable costs is not taken into 
consideration in the figure 8.12, as they should be negotiated individually with each material supplier.

8.5.8 Pricing 
  
When considering a pricing strategy for a novel product, it is often not advised to seek to compete on price 
due to the risk of undermining the real intended value of the product, but also due to the rapid price decline 
of new technologies (Allen, 2010). Unless the company has the ability to compete on price due to an 
advantage sustainable over a long period of the time that cannot be equaled by the competitors (Allen, 2010). 
A reactive pricing strategy will be adopted in order to offer the same price to every customer. This will also 
be reflected in the simple pricing structure, enabling the customers to compare the price among the different 
competitive products (Allen, 2010). The calculation of the minimum price has been established by using a 
cost-plus approach, starting from the known production cost of the product and the desired profit margin 
(Eppinger & Ulrich, 2012). The final price has been set on the basis of an analysis of competitive products, 
the intended position of the product on the market, and the minimum selling price. The manufacturing costs, 
C, have been estimated to be EUR 116 per units (detailed version in appendix 12), from which the company 
should have a gross profit margin equal to the standard in the sporting goods industry, M, of 50% (Eppinger 
& Ulrich, 2012). This leads to the following calculation of the minimum selling price, P, of the shortboard: 
  
P = C / (1 - M) = 116 / (1 - 0.5) = 231 [EUR] 
  
The first competitor analysis has been conducted on 12 shortboards with a similar size (5’10’’), from 8 
different known brands, and the second competitor analysis on 5 budget shortboards with sizes ranging from 
5’8’’ to 6’0’’, all from the same brand. The average price of each analysis has been calculated, from which 
the average value has been calculated, and reaches EUR 476. This value indicates the position of the product 
based on the strategy presented in section 8.2 Business idea. As the product brings new types of values to the 
customers, the final price of the shortboard has been set to EUR 500, which approximately corresponds to a 
reduction of 25% from the average price of the products sold by known brands. Table 8.4 presents a 
summary of the competitive product analysis with average prices of both categories and the final price of the 
product, the detailed analysis are presented in appendix 13. The following equation presents the gross profit 
margin, M’, on the product based on the selling price, P’, and the production cost, C. 
  
M’ = (P’ - C) / P’ = (500 - 116) / 500 = 77 [%]

Eppinger and Ulrich (2012) present that quantitative analysis, such as the sensitivity analysis, are powerful 
tools to support product development decisions, but are subject to many limitations. These kind of analysis 
tend to focus only on measurable quantities, depend on the validity of the assumptions, and the accuracy of 
the data. Detailed financial models tend to fool product development teams, by presenting every alteration 
made on the project with a precise variation of the NPV. Adding to this, having such a precise level of 
measuring every change can constrain the product development process, and limit the risk-taking attitude of 
a development team. Finally, quantitative analysis should be used in combination of qualitative analysis to 
address complex and uncertain factors, such as the competitiveness of the market, the customers 
expectations, the suppliers stability, but also potential economical shifts, government regulations, or social 
tends (Eppinger & Ulrich, 2012). Most of these qualitative factors have been addressed earlier in this report. 
  
8.7 Evaluation of the commercial feasibility  
The quantitative evaluation of the commercial feasibility assesses the viability of the assumptions made 
earlier in this chapter by measuring their influence on the NPV. The first assumption was that the shortboard 
should be sold at an inferior price than the products from large brands. The estimations have shown that with 
a moderated yet, growing amount of sales each year, it was viable to offer the shortboard 25% cheaper than 
the average price of competitive products. The second assumption concerned the distribution channel that 
has been defined as direct-to-customer via an eCommerce site. By assuming that the selling price would be 
maintained at EUR 500, the NPV would start to be negative if the retailers asked for a gross profit margin 
higher that 30%. It outlines that the assumption was correct in the sense that the common gross profit margin 
of a retailer is around 40% to 50%. The same reasoning can be applied to the production method, which was 
assumed to be taking care of in-house, instead of having it done by an OEM. Lastly, the country into which 
the factory has been chosen to be established is Thailand, due to the low costs of labor and low business 
taxes. A test has demonstrated that the NPV would only be affected by -9% if the factory was setup in 
Portugal, thus maintaining the business profitable. The test has only been based on variations of the cost of 
labor and business taxes. As a whole, the quantitative analysis shows that the assumptions made throughout 
this chapter all lead to a profitable business, and that the factory location leaves some freedom to the 
company if a better opportunity appears.

9. Conclusions 
  
This report presents the process and results obtained in the development of a sustainable surfboard, using 
natural fibers and biopolymer. Three objectives have been formulated at the beginning of the project in order 
to (1) identify a suitable combination of materials to reduce the environmental impact and improve the 
disposal capabilities of a surfboard, (2) use the selected materials to design a sustainable shortboard, and (3) 
assess the economical viability of this particular sustainable surfboard. The project is divided into three main 
parts presenting the context of the product development (technology, market, user), the design proposal, and 
the commercialization plan. The development of this project has been supported by the startup Pond ApS, 
with their new bio-based and biodegradable thermoplastic resin system. 
The context of the product development presents the surfboard history and the evolution of the surfboard 
designs over the years, and emphasize the negative environmental impact that the use of petroleum based 
materials is causing, but also that combining the materials altogether prevent them from being recycled 
individually. The foam blank and the synthetic resins are shown to be responsible for the majority of the CO2 
emissions, as well as for threatening the human health during the production processes by releasing 
isocyanates, VOC, and styrene. All these initial information have been regrouped in a problem statement. 
Then, a review on composite materials outlines the growing use of natural fibers as substitutes to fiberglass, 
carbon, and aramid fibers, mainly due to their low environmental impact, high renewability rate, 
biodegradability, and lightweight. It indicates that the mechanical properties of some natural fibers are close 
to the ones from fiberglass. Forecasts expect that natural fibers will account for 28% of the total market 
shares by 2020, supported by the important engagement of the automotive industry.  
In order to evaluate the interest and the need behind sustainable sports equipment, an online survey has been 
used to collected quantitative data over 180 sports enthusiast. A majority of them were ready to pay more 
than the average price for sustainable equipment, as long as the performances and use characteristics are 
equal or better than regular sports equipment. The information are regrouped in the creation of a persona. 
Then, a general market analysis of the surfing industry presents that the expected CAGR will be of 9.46% 
between 2016-2020, representing a global market of USD 9.5 billion in 2020. The number of participants is 
estimated to be ~5.4 million worldwide, and spreaded over six main geographic locations: the U.S.A, 
Australia, Brazil, France, Portugal, and Asia. Among the different surfboards designs, the shortboards are the 
most consumed type, with sales expected to be around 280,000 units in 2020, in comparison to the estimated 
400,000 units sold annually regrouping all types of designs. The use of a Porter Five Forces analysis 
establishes that sustainable surfboards should make use of the most direct distribution channel as possible, 
apply different commercialization and marketing techniques than their competitors, work with reliable 
suppliers, and grow by taking market shares to the incumbents. This analysis of the context and the surfing 
industry concludes that the new product should be a shortboard, due to the popularity and high mechanical 
requirements of this type of design, but also due to the significant environmental impact resulting from the 
higher consumption. On the basis of this decision, the target audience has been identified as regrouping 
surfers with levels ranging from high intermediate to early advanced, and segment them into five categories. 
This identification and segmentation are based on online survey, face-to-face interviews and online 
researches, and are regrouped in the creation of a second persona.   
By looking at the social context in which the new product will evolve, the surfboard resellers and 
environmental NGOs have been identified as being the most influential stakeholders in the promotion and 
successful adoption of a sustainable surfboard. To complement the stakeholder analysis, a customer journey 
map has been created to identify the stages through which the two personas created earlier were experiencing 
their surfboard. It came out that both customers were starting their journey for different reasons and only 
shared the same experiences after the purchase of the product. It illustrates the uneven experiences in relation 
to the geographical location of the customer. The customer journey mapping has also been used to define the 
influence that a surfboard brand has on the experience of its customers, and revealed that the brands were 
almost never present at the very beginning and end of the journey. By taking a step back, a process tree 
served as a tool to provide an overview of the four main activities, and every related stages, a surfboard is 
going though during its life cycle, and reveled that the surfers are the only stakeholder involved in every 
activities of the lifecycle. The first part of the project has been concluded with a design task formulation 
regrouping most of the findings in a short statement illustrating the need, intention, and opportunity of the 
future product. 
!101
==PAGE:104==
The design proposal starts by presenting some general technical specifications of competitive products and 
broadly defines the initial target specifications of the new product as being in-between the high-end and low-
end shortboards. This introduction is quickly followed by the selection of the materials composing the future 
shortboard. The selection of the natural fibers has been based on information collected from the material 
database CES EduPack, and shows that Flax fibers are the most suited natural fibers for this application. The 
arguments in favor are: the high mechanical properties, the range of fabric available for composite use, the 
scientific interest around these fibers, and the moderated price. The same methodology has been used to 
select the potential biodegradable core material, and presents them with their physical properties. On the 
basis of its biodegradability, low density, processing methods and heat resistance, a specific PLA foam 
composed of 70% PLA and 30% PBHD is define as the most adapted core material. Nevertheless, it has been 
outlined that the Heat Defect Temperature (HDT) of ~110°C of this foam might be a limiting factor in the 
production phase, but has been considered as the best option available.  
The design process has been conducted in an iterative manner, starting from an initial design created by the 
mean of a morphological chart regrouping the different possible designs for each section of the shortboard. 
This initial design has then been improved two times, firstly on the basis of five one-to-one interviews 
conducted with experienced surfers and relevant stakeholders in Klitmøller, Denmark. And secondly during a 
co-creation session executed with three surfers from the target group. These iterations allowed the design to 
evolve and move closer to meet the needs of the target audience, but also the intended surfing characteristics. 
Many different types of physical and analytical prototypes have been created for learning and 
communication purposes through the process. The final design proposal is presented in the form of a 3D 
rendering, showing the dimensions (178 X 51.8 X 7.3 cm) and features of the shortboard, such as an 
evolving rail shape or bottom contour. This part of the report also presents the future tests that should be 
conducted in order to reduce the customer acceptance risk.  
In a similar risk reduction perspective, 15 tests have been conducted to create a “look-like” physical 
prototype to demonstrate the technical feasibility, test the customer acceptance, and assess the economical 
viability of the materials selected. All the tests were aiming at combining the bio resin created by Pond ApS 
with flax fibers. The prototypes have either been created by using a vacuum assisted resin transfer molding 
technique (VARTM), or by a wet layup followed by a curing under vacuum. The nature of the bio resin 
implies that it should be cured at temperatures ranging from 130°C to 160°C to reach its final stage. It is 
precisely the elevated temperature and the related effects that have been identified as the source of high 
failure rate, as only one test out of 15 has been considered as successful. It should also be considered that a 
vast majority of the tests were conducted in an non-optimal setup, where the lack of temperature control 
means and the size of the curing oven were reducing the chances of success. Nevertheless, the satisfying 
prototype has been evaluated by the mean of an assessing method inspired by the Dreyfus’s five critical goals 
for industrial design, and though a co-creation session with three surfers from the target group. These 
assessment methods have revealed that visual aspect of the prototype transmits the right values and create a 
good differentiation from the industry standard, but also that the use of resources is appropriate. On the 
opposite, the aspect of the surface (hardness and porosity) and reparability of the composite are perceived 
negatively. 
All the tests conducted have outlined the limitation of the combination of all the materials selected. Mainly 
due to the low HDT of the PLA foam, and the elevated curing temperatures of the bio resin. The design 
proposal has not demonstrated through a proof-of-concept prototype that is was possible to combine the 
materials together. Therefore, this part is concluded by the assumption that the future improvements made on 
the processing and curing temperature of the bio resin, as well as HDT of the PLA foam, will enable to 
overcome the current limitations preventing the materials to be combined. 
!102
==PAGE:105==
The commercialization plan is build around three assumption: the shortboard should be sold at a moderated 
price to be adopted by a large number of surfer to have a real environmental impact, the shortboard should 
only be made available through an online store, the shortboard should be produced in-house. The two last 
assumptions aimed at bypassing the current supply chain to cut the profit margin of the actors currently 
involved in it, and offer the product at a moderated price. In order to achieve the preset goals, most of the 
elements composing a company are taken into consideration and reflected in a budget established over a 
five-years period. By assuming that the selling price of the shortboard is of EUR 500 and that the product 
benefit from a fixed window of opportunity for sales, the annual profit after five years is forecasted to be of 
EUR 1,675,838. But in order to achieve this result, an initial investment of EUR 650,000 is needed. Finally, 
the use of the Net Present Value (NPV) calculation method enables the creation of tradeoff rules, to facilitate 
evidence-based decision-making in the development of the project. It has been used to show that the sale 
volume is the most influential factor in the profitability of the business, but has also been used to validate the 
three assumptions made at the beginning of the commercialization plan. 
All in all, the development of this project has (1) identified a theoretical combination of materials (bio resin, 
flax fibers, PLA foam, wood) positively impacting the environmental impact of a shortboard. Which all have 
the end property of being biodegradable under specific conditions (heat, humidity, and bacterias), therefore 
improving the disposal capabilities of the product. (2) These materials have been used to design a sustainable 
shortboard, meeting the needs of a specific target group. And finally, (3) a scenario has proven that it could 
be economically viable to produce the shortboard designed with the selected materials. 
Lastly, This project has contributed to make a (small) step forward in the development of sustainable 
alternatives in the surfing industry.
