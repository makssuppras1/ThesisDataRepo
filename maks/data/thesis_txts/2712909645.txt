Motivation
With this 30 ECTS points thesis, my education as a Master of Science in Engineering in Indus-
trial Engineering and Management at the Technical University of Denmark is concluded. The
case study presented in the thesis was formulated in collaboration with the Department of Digi-
talization and Analysis at the North Zealand Hospital, Dyrehavevej 29, 3400 Hillerød, Denmark.
Hospitals are facing a growing demand from the expanding population to increase their
efficiency and productivity.
To overcome this challenge, society is gaining more interest in
the physical layout of a hospital and its impact on said efficiency. This has contributed to
renovations of hospitals and construction of new hospital buildings to provide a better flow of
patients, staff and materials. Additionally, the idea of sharing bed resources between depart-
ments is gaining traction.
The objective of this thesis is to investigate the staff flow in a hospital based on the placement
of departments and subsequent allocation of patients when there are no physical boundaries
between departments and patients may be allocated to any bed in the hospital. The evalua-
tion is carried out with a simulation model representing the hospital of the case study, New
North Zealand Hospital, Hillerød, which is currently under construction. The Department of
Digitalization and Analysis at the North Zealand Hospital has provided aggregated data in
the period 2018-2021 from the hospitals in Fredrikssund, Hillerød and Helsingør which are to
be replaced by the upcoming hospital. A sim-heuristic combining the simulation model and
the adaptive large neighbourhood search meta-heuristic has been implemented to visit and
evaluate several potential solutions in an attempt to find the optimal placement of depart-
ments. Potential constraints to the layout and changes to patient allocation raised in the case
study have been incorporated in scenarios and tested with the sim-heuristic. The layout pro-
posed in the case study and potential layouts found with the sim-heuristic have been compared.
The thesis is structured as a scientific paper with the intention for it to be written for a
journal within operations research such as the European Journal of Operations Research or
Computers and Operations Research and/or within healthcare operations management such as
Health Care Management Science or Operations Research for Health Care. Due to the paper
format, not all procedures or theory will be discussed in depth as it is expected that an intended
reader of a scientific journal already has prior knowledge to simulation and optimization. Ad-
ditionally, the intended reader is expected to have been introduced to mathematical graphs
and take an interest in hospital layout problems or general facility layout problems. Following
the bibliography which completes the paper is an appendix. The content of the appendix is
included for the purpose of the thesis but is intended to be excluded for a potential submitting

Research Question
This thesis studies the following research question:
How can a simulation optimization model be developed and utilized to propose
and evaluate a reasonable bed allocation plan for the New North Zealand
hospital’s future setup?
– Based on historical patient data and other relevant input, how can a model of the system
be developed and utilized to evaluate potential solutions?
– How should a proposed solution for the layout be evaluated?
– What is the performance of the best solution found by the model and solutions found in
scenarios which constrain the layout and/or change the patient allocation compared to
the currently proposed solution?
Learning Objectives
1. Explain the concepts of hospital planning and flexible beds.
2. Model a bed allocation at New North Zealand hospital with a fully flexible bed setup and
stochastic patient data.
3. Create a framework which combines simulation and optimization nature to find a good
solution to the bed allocation problem.
4. Design scenarios which introduces constrains to the layout and/or change the patient
allocation and compare solutions found by the framework with the currently proposed
solution.
5. Discuss the applied method and its limitations and consider potential future research.
6. Write a thesis with the structure and format of a scientific paper.

Allocating departments in a hospital layout with flexible
beds
Emilie Lund Schrøder

Abstract
A well-planned hospital layout has shown to improve the efficiency of hospitals and
enables staff to focus on providing healthcare services instead of travelling between pa-
tients. The problem of optimizing hospital layouts by allocating departments has been
studied before, with it often being formulated as a quadratic assignment problem and
departments having fixed sizes. In this paper, we consider the allocation of departments
in a hospital layout which utilizes fully flexible beds. We propose to locate department
centres in the hospital and allow the subsequent patient allocation to recreate the depart-
ments and the layout of the hospital. For this, we consider the Danish case study of New
North Zealand Hospital, where we aim to minimize the distance travelled by staff to reach
patients. A sim-heuristic approach is adopted, which combines a simulation model of the
hospital and the adaptive large neighbourhood search meta-heuristic. Compared with the
currently proposed layout in the case study, the sim-heuristic has found a solution which
decreases the objective value by 83.7%, both when departments can be placed anywhere
in the hospital and when subdepartments must be neighbours. The approach opens up
for new ways of solving the hospital layout problem and more research is needed in the
future to investigate how the approach can be applied for other hospitals.
Keywords: Hospital layout problem, flexible beds, sim-heuristic, adaptive large neigh-
bourhood search

Life expectancy is rising globally and an increasing number of people are living with diseases and
disabilities for several years. These trends can be linked to the improving healthcare systems

years, hospitals have faced a growing demand from the expanding population and number
of chronic patients for technological innovations and increased efficiency and productivity. To
accommodate this, countries all over Europe have implemented healthcare reforms to streamline
care delivery to improve quality and reduce costs (Garel and Notarangelo, 2020). This has led

especially when presented with the stochasticity of arriving patients and their length of stay
(LOS) as well.
Allocation of resources in hospitals has also been investigated as a way to improve efficiency.
Within this is the allocation of clinical beds, where hospitals often reserve a number of beds
for each department. An idea gaining traction is the notion of using flexible beds. This term
describes beds which may be used by several different departments, and it is proposed to in-
crease the utilization of beds in the hospitals (Bekker et al., 2017; J. Wang et al., 2022).
The aim of this paper is to address the combined problem of optimizing and simulating
a hospital layout in a stochastic setup where all clinical beds are flexible. We wish to find a
layout such that patients are allocated to beds as close to their department as possible such
that the flow of the staff is optimized. The main contributions are the representation of this
type of setup and the design of a decision support tool (DST) to evaluate and analyse the staff
movement and patient allocation using the case study of New North Zealand Hospital (NHN)
in Hillerød, Denmark, with stochastic patient arrivals and LOS.

Literature Review
The problem presented in this paper regards the allocation of departments in a hospital, which in
literature falls under the umbrella of hospital layout planning along with problems of allocating
labs or operating rooms as well as defining patient room sizes and capacity (Benitez et al.,
2019).
In this literature review, we will summarize recent research on the hospital layout
planning considering location of departments in hospitals or sub-departments within emergency
departments. Furthermore, we will consider the term of flexible beds. With the literature
review, we aim to establish contributions of this paper.
2.1
Department allocation in hospital layouts
The problem of optimizing a hospital layout in regards to departments can be seen as a sub-
category of the facility layout problem (FLP). The FLP is an optimization problem applied
to find an efficient layout design. Quadratic assignment problem (QAP) and mixed integer
problem (MIP) methods are often used with the objective of minimizing cost of material flow
between departments or machines (Cubukcuoglu, Sariyildiz, et al., 2022; Cubukcuoglu, Tasge-
tiren, et al., 2021). Literature mainly discuss the FLP in regards to manufacturing, however,
the methodology is very applicable to allocating departments within hospitals as well. Like the
FLP, planning of departments in hospital layouts have often been formulated as a QAP, with
one of the first to do this being Elshafei (1977). The same method was used by Cubukcuoglu,
Tasgetiren, et al. (2021), Rizk-Allah et al. (2021), and Tongur et al. (2020) who used meta-
heuristics to solve it due to the QAP being an NP-hard problem. The studies considered the
renovation of hospitals and reallocated departments as blocks to locations within the hospital.
Sizes of each department and location where given, although Cubukcuoglu, Tasgetiren, et al.
(2021) allowed a department to be split between locations if no area was big enough to accom-
modate the entire department. This was done even if the chosen locations were not neighbours
due to the potential of a higher-level of satisfaction for the logistic requirements.
Huo et al. (2021) also considered the renovation of a hospital building but formulated it as a
MIP before solving it with a meta-heuristic as well. Meanwhile Cubukcuoglu, Sariyildiz, et al.
(2022) presented it as an architectural layout planning problem for constructing new hospital
buildings and proposed a three step solution using both a graph theoretical approach (GTA)
and MIP. Units consisting of several departments were grouped together using a weighted graph
==PAGE:9==
2

to define which units should be on the same floor, while MIP was used afterwards to allocate
the departments on the floor. GTA was further used by Arnolds and Nickel (2015) with the
use of an adjacency matrix to allocate departments based on closeness ratings.
A different approach to the mathematical methods, QAP, MIP and GTA, was adopted in
a study by K.E.K et al. (2021). They utilized surveys and the mutli-criteria decision tool,
ELECTRE, together with Apple’s layout procedure. Meanwhile, Rismanchian and Lee (2017)
considered the layout of subdepartments within an emergency room and proposed a process
mining and goal programming approach.
The studies by Cubukcuoglu, Tasgetiren, et al. (2021), Huo et al. (2021), Rizk-Allah et al.
(2021), and Tongur et al. (2020) all considered the minimization of the cost of patient flows
between departments, but they used deterministic data based on log data or averaged data.
To accommodate for stochasticity of patient arrival and LOS, simulation should be utilized.
Like some of the studies above, Zuo et al. (2019) formulated the hospital layout problem as
a QAP but included simulation as a second step. They presented pareto optimal solutions
for minimization of patient flow and maximization of closeness rating based on deterministic
data and used simulation afterwards to evaluate the chosen layouts based on patients’ arrival
rate, service time and amount of staff. The two-stage approach of finding a layout and then
evaluating with simulation was proposed by Butler et al. (1992). This study proposed a solution
where department sizes depended on bed allocation. Departments were given a target allocation
of space and a lower and upper bound of space but were otherwise flexible in size. Munavalli
et al. (2022) fully combined simulation and optimization, thus evaluating all potential solutions
through simulation. Meanwhile, Sasanfar et al. (2021) and T. Wang et al. (2015) did not use
mathematical models for optimization, but proposed layouts based on predefined shapes and
interviews respectively which were evaluated through simulation.
Wurzer (2013) did not consider the optimization aspect but presented a simulation tool in-
stead to account for dynamic behaviour and perform spatial analysis which users could utilize in
the early stages of hospital planning and include in the design process. Cubukcuoglu, Nourian,
et al. (2020) proposed a simulation tool to test and validate potential layouts found by the user.
In the studies above the combination of optimization and simulation has been either an
approach with two separated stages or evaluating each potential solution. In this paper we
combine optimization and simulation in a sim-heuristic as presented by Juan et al. (2015) where
selected solutions are evaluated with simulation and stochastic data during the optimization
process. The simulation model can also act as a DST on its own to evaluate potential solutions
like Cubukcuoglu, Nourian, et al. (2020) did.

2.2
Flexible beds
Flexible - or floating - beds in healthcare is a term given to beds which can have multiple
types of patients assigned to it. Several studies have recognized it as a key concept in efficient
management of beds due to its capability to increase the utilization of beds(Bekker et al.,
2017; Bruin et al., 2010; Hall, 2012). In the study by Bruin et al. (2010), they considered the
merging of bed resources between departments. They concluded that fewer beds were needed
to withhold a maximum percentage of refused patients when all departments could utilize the
beds. Bekker et al. (2017) considered ’flexible departments’ which contained flexible beds with
dedicated multi-skilled nurses. They tested different bed allocation policies mixing no flexibility
- all beds are reserved to designated departments only - and full flexibility of beds - all beds
presented as one flexible department where all patients share beds. The study concluded that
it was infeasible for large systems to incorporate full flexibility due to the required amount
of multi-skilled nurses, but including some flexibility provided the system with the ability to
handle peaks in demand and reject fewer patients. Izady and Mohamed (2021) studied the
same setup with flexible departments and multi-skilled nurses, but included the cost of training
the multi-skilled nurses. They concluded that full flexibility inquired too high training costs.
However, by allowing clusters of departments to have reserved beds and share a pool of flexible
beds, the utilization of beds was increased, the number of rejected patients decreased and nurses
did not require training within all specialities thus lowering training costs. Full flexibility was
also commented on by Holm et al. (2013) as the hospital in their case study had implemented
full flexibility a few years earlier which led to physicians travelling long distances between their
patients and presented problems nurses specialising in specific patient groups.
Rather than designating specific areas with flexible beds, J. Wang et al. (2022) proposed
an overflow-strategy for allocation of patients post operations. Compared to assigning specific
areas with flexible beds and multi-skilled nurses, they allowed patients to be assigned to other
departments if their designated department was fully occupied. The allocation to other depart-
ments incurred a monetary overflow cost. They concluded that the overflow strategy decreased
the waiting time for patients requiring operations, however, depending on the overflow cost the
strategy might be too expensive for some hospitals.
Flexibility has been incorporated in different setups and there has often been a focus on
the monetary cost of training nurses or allocating patients to other departments. Especially
full flexibility is noted to having several disadvantages in the studies above including its affect
on staff movement (Holm et al., 2013). However, in this paper we will consider a fully flexible
setup and use it to design the hospital layout. By placing department centres in the hospital,
we propose to allocate patients as close to their designated department centre as possible and
thereby recreate the department layout through patient allocation instead of placing department

blocks of fixed sizes. While Butler et al. (1992) allowed for some flexibility in department sizes,
the approach of recreating departments entirely through patient allocation and full flexibility
has not been studied before to the best of the author’s knowledge. Thus, this paper proposes
a new setup of combining hospital layout planning and flexible beds and implements a sim-
heuristic model to account for stochasticities in patient arrivals and LOS.

Problem definition
In this paper we consider a multi-floor hospital with a set of specialised departments which
utilizes fully flexible beds in a dynamic and stochastic setting. Each floor contains a number of
single patient bed rooms - interchangeably denoted as rooms or beds - and elevators connecting
the two floors. There are no physical boundaries to divide the floors into different locations.
We wish to locate the different departments in the hospital as to optimize the flow of the staff
travelling to their respective patients.
3.1
Case Study of New North Zealand Hospital, Hillerød
As mentioned, there is a growing demand for hospitals to increase their efficiency and produc-
tivity and Denmark is no exception to this development (Danske Regioner, 2015). The demand
for new and more efficient hospitals led to the Danish government attempting to streamline
the Danish healthcare system with a new Structural Reform in 2007. The reform entails the
closing of 25 hospitals across the country and instead gathering the resources - nurses, doctors,
etc. - at fewer and better hospitals. Including moving the resources to other existing hospitals,
six new Super Hospitals are being built (Regeringen Lars Løkke Rasmussen III (2016-2019),
2019). One of these hospitals is the New North Zealand hospital, which is to replace NOH, the
collective term for the Northern Zealand hospitals in Fredrikssund, Hillerød and Helsingør. It is
currently under construction in Hillerød, and it is estimated to be completed in 2024. The case
study of NHN is presented by the department of digitalization and analysis at North Zealand
hospital henceforth denoted as the NHN Team.
NHN will be a medium sized hospital with 14 speciality departments and 456 single patient
bed rooms (excluding the emergency department) spread across the second and third floor of
the hospital. The two floors share an identical structure and the rooms will be placed in two
lines with a hallway between them on both floors. The building is characterized by its snake-
like structure in the shape of a quatrefoil, which contributes to the lack of physical boundaries
on the floors. A floor plan of NHN can be seen in Figure 1 along with the areas containing
elevators and administrative areas.
At NHN, they will apply fully flexible beds to allow patients to be placed anywhere on the

3.2
Modelling of NHN
A graph theoretical approach is adopted to model the hospital as a mathematical weighted
graph. Each single patient room along with the elevators used to transport patients and staff
between the floors are represented by vertices with weighted edges to represent distances. How-
ever, the only spatial data acquired are floor plans depicting the rooms and elevators on the
two floors with no measurements as seen in Figure 1. Edges between vertices are therefore
based on conjecture and have been manually inserted with a fixed weight for edges connecting
neighbouring rooms and rooms across from each other on the hallway. Edges between elevators
have been given a larger fixed weight to account for the waiting time as Huo et al. (2021) did
in their study when representing crossing between floors. The same weight is set for edges
crossing the administrative areas as per the NHN Team’s request. The distance between all
rooms is calculated as the shortest path like Cubukcuoglu, Tasgetiren, et al. (2021) did. A slice
of a graph example can be seen in Figure 3.
3.3
Key performance indicators
For this problem, the proposed model will only consider flow of the staff and will not include
costs related to staff wages, material, etc. To measure an efficient staff flow, we propose to look
at two factors: the distance travelled by staff to reach their patients and the distance where
staff of two different departments must cross each other in the hallway to reach their respective
patients. These two factors are formulated as two KPIs:
 Centre-KPI: The distance between a department centre and patients belonging to the
given department.
 Component-KPI: The distance between the connected component of a department and
patients belonging to the given department.

A department centre is located within a patient room, which means that the vertex rep-
resenting the room in the modelled graph also represents the given department centre. The
term ’connected component’ is from graph theory and is defined as a subgraph of an undirected
graph where each pair of vertices is connected with each other via a path. In the model, a
subgraph containing all rooms (vertices) which are either empty or have a patient from a given
department is found. In the subgraph, the connected component which contains the depart-
ment centre is defined as the ’connected component of the department’. If the room of a given
department centre contains a patient from another department, the connected component will
default to only containing the department centre and function the same way as the centre-KPI.
In addition, elevator vertices are not included in the subgraph created to find a connected
component. As these few edges connecting the floors could be in use by other people such
as patient families, it was deemed that this would break the connected component due to the
sharing of the edges.

3.4
Patient data
As NHN is not active yet, the data provided by the NHN Team has been collected from the
three hospitals coined as NOH in the period 2018-2021. Due to the effect of COVID-19 on
hospital admissions in the years 2020 and 2021, data from 2019 has been utilized. For each
department, average data is provided on patient arrivals and length of stay in a week for each
of the four seasons. According to the NHN Team, 90% of the patients being admitted to the
speciality departments at NOH are arriving from the emergency departments. Thus, they are
termed as acute patients meaning they were admitted without a prior scheduled appointment.
Due to the high number - and lack of data on scheduled patients - all patients will be modelled
as acute. The patient arrival is modelled as a Poisson distribution with time-varying intensity
as has been done in literature before (Bekker et al., 2017; Cubukcuoglu, Nourian, et al., 2020;
Holm et al., 2013). The intensity is based on the average patient arrival. The provided data on
LOS consists of the mean and standard deviation from a normal distribution. A zero-truncated
normal distribution is used to fit the data, though to further, we would use a lognormal, gamma
or exponential distribution which is more common in literature (Cubukcuoglu, Nourian, et al.,
2020; Harini et al., 2018; J. Wang et al., 2022).

4
Simulation model
In the following, we present the implemented simulation model of NHN. This includes the sim-
ulation process and assumptions related to the model. The model is used to evaluate potential
layouts for NHN and can be incorporated in an optimization model to evaluate solutions or
used as a DST on its own.
4.1
Simulation process
The simulation process consists of three main steps: locating department centres, allocating
patients and calculating the objective value. The input required by the simulation model is
the order of department centres and estimated sizes of each department. While no beds are
reserved to each department, the estimated sizes are used as an auxiliary tool to ensure spacing
between department centres.
Locating department centres
Given a solution order and department sizes, the model initiates by allocating rooms to each
department to match their estimated sizes. Firstly, all rooms on the second floor are allocated
followed by the allocation of rooms on the third floor. This setup allows one department to
have rooms on both floors, essentially splitting the department in two and stacking them on
top of each other. This is allowed to avoid potential additional constraints on the placement of
departments. Using the allocated rooms, the department centre is located in the room, which
has the smallest total distance to all other rooms allocated to the given department.
Patient allocation
When the department centres have been located, the process continues to the patient allocation.
The model utilizes the allocation process in two stages: a warm-up period and a full scale
allocation. The warm-up period is used to initialize the system such that it is not empty at the
beginning of the full scale allocation. The allocation functionality is the same in both stages,
however, the warm-up period only considers patient data during the winter, while the full scale
allocation considers all four season. The allocation is done for a number of time steps, with
each time step corresponding to a number of hours based on the provided data. The allocation
process begins by generating a list of arriving patients for each time step. These lists contain
information on which department a patient belongs to and their LOS. For each time step, the
list is shuffled to ensure the model does not consider arriving patients for one department at a
time. Considering the list of arriving patients one by one, the model adopts a greedy patient
allocation policy and attempts to allocate each patient to a room as close to their department
centre as possible. If the hospital has reached full capacity and there is no room available,
==PAGE:17==
4
Simulation model
12
the patient is rejected. The procedure is repeated until the list of arriving patients is empty.
Afterwards, the LOS of all patients in the hospital is decreased by the number of hours in the
time step. If the LOS of any patient reaches zero or below, the patient is discharged and the
room will be available in the next time step. The allocation process is repeated until the model
has been through all time steps.
Evaluating solution
Throughout the full scale allocation, it is continuously tracked which department has a patient
allocated in a room in each time step. When the allocation process for all time steps is fin-
ished, the objective value of the solution is calculated using the solution’s subsequent patient
allocation and the equations presented in Section 3.3. Thus, patients which were discharged
during the warm-up period are not included in the calculation of the objective value.
The presented simulation process follows some assumptions which have been made in order
to reduce the complexity of the model. These are:
 There is always enough staff to admit, treat and discharge patients.
 Patients always arrive at the beginning of a time step and leave at the end of a time step
if their LOS has decreased to zero or less.
 If there is no available room in the hospital, a patient is rejected and not assigned to a
queuing system. There is no penalty related to rejecting a patient.
 When a patient has been allocated to a room, they will remain in that room until they
complete their LOS, even if a room closer to their department centre becomes available.
 When a patient is discharged, their room becomes available immediately in the next time
step.
To capture seasonality fluctuations, the model utilizes the data provided from each season.
However, to reach stability in a reasonable time, each season only consists of four weeks rather
than 13 weeks, which could have been used to simulate an entire year.

Verification of the model was done through testing of the implemented code. Each function is
tested in a small scale setup to ensure it works as intended. The final simulation model has gone
through 400 replications with 7 different seeds to ensure the results are stable for different seeds
and that the model does not encounter failure. Variations on the amount of arriving patients

Sim-heuristic
To search for potential solutions, we have implemented a sim-heuristic, which combines simu-
lation and a meta-heurstic. The adopted sim-heuristic methodology is presented by Juan et al.
(2015). They proposed that solutions which did well based on deterministic data, would also
do well on stochastic data. All visited solutions would be evaluated through a single simulation
replication using deterministic data, while promising solutions are evaluated through several
replications of the simulation process using stochastic data.
In this paper, the chosen meta-heurstic is the adaptive large neighbourhood search (ALNS)
presented by Pisinger and Røpke (2010). The meta-heuristic incorporates several destroy and
repair methods and uses weights to track which methods find the best solutions. These weights
are used to determine which methods are chosen in each iteration to move the search for a so-
lution forward. All solutions found with the meta-heuristic are evaluated using the simulation
process described in Section 4.1. The pseudocode of the sim-heuristic combining the ALNS and
simulation model can be seen in Algorithm 1.

5.1
Destroy and repair methods
For the ALNS we implemented five destroy methods and three repair methods. Most of the
methods attempt to intensify the search by removing departments which have a critical impact
on the solution due to their added cost compared to other departments. In comparison, there
is only one destroy and repair method which attempt to diversify the search.
Destroy methods
The following contains a description of the destroy methods. Many of the methods chooses a
random number of departments to destroy, however, the number has a maximum to ensure no
more than half of the solution is destroyed. Additionally, if a method compares costs related
to KPIs between departments, it considers standardized cost to account for some departments
having more patients in the hospital than others.
 Random destroy method: This method is used to diversify the search. The method
chooses a number of random departments and removes them from the solution.
 Worst centre-KPI destroy method: This method attempts to intensify the search
by removing departments which have a critical impact on the centre-KPI. The method
removes the n departments with the highest cost in regards to the given KPI, where n is
a random number.
 Worst component-KPI: The method follows the same principle as the destroy method
above, but regards the component-KPI instead.

 Worst neighbour destroy method: This method chooses either the centre-KPI or
component-KPI at random. From the solution order, it removes the neighbours of the
department which has the highest impact on the chosen KPI. Note that this method
always considers departments as neighbours if they are next to each other in the order
of department centres, even if the resulting layout places the two departments on top of

 Worst correlation destroy method: This method uses a correlation matrix based

It considers the pair of
neighbouring departments in the order with the highest correlation and removes one of

Repair methods
The repair methods insert missing departments in incomplete solutions. This is always done
from the end of the incomplete solution. The methods consider the last department in the
given solution order as the ’current last department’. When a missing department is inserted,
it attains the status as ’current last department’. Additionally, once again standardized costs
are used for methods considering KPIs.
 Random repair method: Given an incomplete solution, this method inserts the missing
departments at random.
 Best correlation repair method: Using the correlation matrix, the method evaluates
the correlation between all the missing departments and the current last department. The
department with the lowest correlation is inserted and the process is repeated until the
solution is complete.
 Best historical repair: This method considers the average cost for both KPIs when
two departments are neighbours in a solution order.
This number is updated for all
neighbouring departments in every visited solution to track the historical impact of having
a pair of departments as neighbours. Given an incomplete solution, the method inserts
the department which shares the lowest historical cost with the current last department.
The process is repeated until the solution is complete.

6.4
Experimental scenarios
The sim-heuristic used to find a solution above does not consider any constraints on the physical
layout or any specific patient allocation policy. However, the NHN Team has presented potential
limitations to the layout and allocation, which they are considering to implement for NHN. To
include these changes, three scenarios are designed. For each scenario, the sim-heuristic has
been modified to accommodate the changes to layout constraints and allocation policy. As
before, the modified sim-heuristics has been run several times with 10.000 iterations each time.
In the section below, the three scenarios will be presented followed by a discussion comparing
the found scenario solutions, the original sim-heuristic’s solution and the solution proposed by
the NHN Team.
Scenario 1 - Subdepartment constraint
Within the hospital there are departments which are termed as subdepartments and together in
pairs of two represent one main department. At NHN these subdepartment pairs are palliative
and oncology (2 and 3), gynaecology and obstetrics (4 and 5) and surgical gastroenterology
and surgical (7 and 8). Additionally, the NHN Team is strongly considering to place obsetrics
next to the children’s department, neonatology, on the third floor. In this scenario, a solution
is only feasible when subdepartments are neighbours in the layout and obstetrics is next to the
neonatology. To be a neighbour in the layout, the departments must either be next to each
other on the same floor or on top of each other on the two floors. Departments are considered
as being on top of each other if the distance between their centres - when assuming movement
between floors costs zero and can be done from anywhere in the hospital - is no more than
half the estimated size of the largest of the two departments. However, to avoid the additional
waiting time for elevators, it was decided that obstetrics must be on the third floor next to
neonatology.

Scenario 2 - Allocation constraint
It is being considered whether NHN should adopt a patient allocation policy, which aims to
allocate a patient to the same floor as their department centre before searching for available
rooms on the other floor. This would allow staff to refrain from spending time on waiting for
elevators, although it will also increase the distance they have to walk to reach their patients.
For this, the patient allocation policy is changed such that the allocation process must search
an entire floor containing the department centre for an available room before moving the search
to the other floor. However, as seen in both solutions presented above, there are cases where
the department at the end of the second floor has been assigned rooms on both floors. This
assignment often affects the location of the department centre, as the model will try to place
the center close to an elevator to reach the rooms on both floors. In such cases, we allow the
allocation to search through all of the assigned rooms of a department even if they are on a
different floor than the department centre. The allocation may not search the rest of the floor
outside the assigned rooms before it has searched the floor of its department centre. In this
scenario, the constraint on subdepartments presented above is not included.
Scenario 3 - Combining constraints
For the final scenario, the two scenarios presented above are combined. Thus, subdepartments
must be neighbours in the physical layout and the patient allocation policy is changed to search
an entire floor of a department centre for an empty room.

Further research and perspectives
The implemented model presents a new way of using flexible beds as a tool to allocate depart-
ments within a hospital, and while it captures some overall details of NHN, there is room for
improvement. Particularly the conjecture on distances within the hospital limits the simulation
model as this information is essential for how patients are allocated and thereby the objective
value representing staff movement. Whether it is distance in metric units or the time it takes
to walk between rooms, the information would be relatively simple to implement in the model
as new edge weights. Additionally, the distance walked by staff to reach a patient is always
considered as related to the department centre. However, in the case of the layout proposed by
the NHN team where several patients from the LIM department where moved to the other floor,
some nurses would move their rounds to the other floor, thus decreasing the walked distance.
==PAGE:32==
7
Further research and perspectives
27
While it raises the complexity of the model, it is a scenario which could be of interest in the
future.
Limitations are also seen in the sim-heuristic, especially in its tendency to revisit solutions,
where a TABU-list may be implemented to avoid this or at least avoid passing the same solutions
through the simulation process to find steady state. If the computational time of the model
and thus the time required to reach stability could be lowered, it could also be considered to
allow each visited solution to be evaluated through the simulation process as Munavalli et al.
(2022) did.
From the literature it was seen that full flexibility has often been discouraged due to the
increased walking distance incurred by staff (Holm et al., 2013). While this model mostly uses
the flexible beds as a way to recreate departments, it will still affect the distance walked to
reach patients. It might be interesting to change the patient allocation policy and reserve some
beds around the department centre to a given department. This would present a somewhat
similar setup as Butler et al. (1992) who allowed be allocation to help determine the size of
departments, although in this case a department would only have a minimum size and not a
required size within a range. Such a study would, however, have to consider the effect of the
implementation on the bed utilization or number of patients rejected as Izady and Mohamed
(2021) did to be able to compare the two patient allocation policies properly.
The current model only considers data from one year, namely 2019 in this case. Patient ad-
mission rates and patient LOS might change over the years though, as was the case of patients
admitted to neurological rehabilitation. By forecasting future admission rates and including
this data, the model could evaluate the potential of a layout based on future demand as well.
Additionally, if the model expanded to include a cost for moving departments, it might be used
to determine if a renovation of the hospital would be beneficial when comparing the change in
staff flow and the renovation cost. This may allow the model to suggest changes in the layout
over the years instead of proposing a single layout.
With the model configured specifically to the setup at NHN with no physical boundaries,
there may be limited use for it by other hospitals as it is. However, it does propose a new way
of planning hospitals. Even if a hospital presents some physical boundaries, the model could
be adapted to consider fewer blocks for locations able to contain several departments. Flexible
beds would still be used to recreate the departments, albeit in a smaller space compared to
NHN which considers the entire hospital as one large location and pool of beds. Additionally,
the model only considers flow of staff. In other studies, it may be adjusted to consider patient
flow instead.
Depending on the success of the setup at NHN, other hospitals may try to adopt a similar
layout through renovations or by constructing new hospital buildings. More research is needed
==PAGE:33==

28
in this type of hospital planning to find how hospitals could benefit from allowing simulation
of patient allocation to decide the layout of departments.
8
Conclusion
This paper presents a new way of placing departments in a hospital layout planning which
utilizes fully flexible beds. It considers the case study of New North Zealand Hospital currently
under construction in Denmark where there will be no physical boundaries between departments
and the departments may allocate their patients to any bed in the hospital.
By locating
department centres in the hospital rather than blocks of departments and allocating patients
as close to their department centre as possible, we propose to let the patient allocation recreate
the departments and the hospital layout. A simulation model representing the hospital in the
case study has been developed to consider stochasticities in patient arrivals and their length
of stay. Using a sim-heuristic approach, the simulation model is used to evaluate proposed
solution layouts, while the adaptive large neighbourhood search meta-heuristic is implemented
to search for potential solutions.
The simulation model can also be adopted as a decision
support tool on its own. Compared to the currently proposed layout for the hospital, the sim-
heuristic presented a solution which lessened both the distance walked by staff to reach their
patients and the distance where staff of different department may cross each other. In total,
this meant a 83.7% decrease in the objective value compared to the current proposed solution.
An equally good solution was found when subdepartments were forced to be neighbours in the
hospital. However, the simulation model does not include actual distances of metric values from
the hospital, but is based on conjecture instead. As the model is evaluated on the distances
walked by staff, there could be significant changes to the evaluation of the presented solutions if
correct distances were included. While it does provide a simple visualisation of how a solution
may shape the hospital layout, proper distance measurements would significantly elevate the
model.

Zero-truncating length of stay
The data provided by the NHN team, states the mean and standard deviation of the length of
stay for patients based on a normal distribution, which could essentially allow patients to have a
length of stay less than zero. To mitigate it, the data has been zero-truncated. When a normal
distribution is zero truncated, it increases the mean and decreases the standard deviation.
However, to avoid skewing the distribution too much compared to the observed data, we have
calculated a new mean, which - when zero-truncated - results in the same mean as observed.
While it would be favourable to do the same for the standard deviation, it was not possible
for our solvers to solve the equation system of two equations with two unknowns due to the
complexity of the equations. Instead the observed standard deviation will be used, even though
this will decrease it in the resulting zero-truncated distribution. The new mean is isolated from
the following equation which is normally used to calculate the mean of a zero-truncated normal
distribution:
µobs = µnew + σobs · ϕ(−µnew
σobs

σobs
))

where µobs = µnew when the standard deviation is zero or missing in the data set. Figure 8
shows an example of a normal distribution and truncated normal distribution when using the
observed mean and the new mean as input. The mean of the zero-truncated normal distribution
is written in the figure, where it is seen that the use of µnew as input results in the truncated
normal distribution having a mean equal to µobs.

Estimating department sizes
As NHN consists of a number of rooms, estimation of department sizes actually considers the
estimation of how many rooms a department should consist of. The hospital has 456 rooms,
where 55 are reserved for the children’s departments, leaving 401 beds available to the 12 other
speciality departments. The estimation was done by treating one hour in a bed as a resource.
With 401 beds available 24 hours a day for 365 days, the total amount of resources are:
401 beds · 365 days per year · 24 hours per day = 3, 512, 760 bed hours per year
By multiplying the observed number of admitted patients and the average LOS from the
provided data, it was calculated how many bed hours each department required. However, the
sum of required resources was larger than the amount of available resources. To accommodate
this, the scale between the available resources and sum of required resources was found by
diving the two:
3, 512, 760 bed hours per year
sum of resource requirements = 3, 512, 760 bed hours per year
3, 626, 622 bed hours per year = 0.9686
This number was multiplied with each department’s resource requirement to scale their need
to the amount of available resources. This number was used to calculate the percentage of
resources allocated to each department, which in turn was used to calculate the number of
rooms given to the department:
required resources
available resources · number of beds available = number of beds assigned to department
The assigned calculations and the assigned number of rooms can be seen in Figure 9. The
method for calculating the number of assigned rooms and the results were discussed with the
NHN Team, who deemed them reasonable.
