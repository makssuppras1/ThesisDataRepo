Abstract
This Master Thesis presents a mathematical formulation of a version of the Unit Com-
mitment problem used by Ørsted’s Bioenergy Department to plan the production of the
Combined Heat and Power (CHP) plant located in the city of Esbjerg. The model op-
timizes the schedule of the power and heat generation depending on the costs, demand
and capacity available. The model takes into consideration physical constraints such as
ramping constraints for heat and power generation, storage limit, demand fulfilment, etc.
The problem is formulated as a large-scale, highly constrained and mixed integer prob-
lem. With the use of different state-of-the-art mathematical tools and frameworks, it is
investigated why there are instances where well-known commercial solvers such as Xpress
or CPLEX struggle to solve the model.
Through an extensive literature review, implementation process and testing, four different
methods have been implemented to improve the performance of the solution procedure:
Model re-formulation, Lazy Constraints, Lagrangian Relaxation and Benders Decomposi-
tion. The results showed that the different methods improve differently the model. The
revision of the model formulation allows the re-definition of different variables and con-
straints that relieve the load of the solver during the solution procedure. The Lazy Con-
straints approach shows how shorter solution time and faster convergence can be obtained
all over the test instances. The Lagrangian Relaxation showed great improvement in solve
time, and Benders Decomposition obtain a better convergence for the diﬀicult-to-solve
instances.
A set of 122 test instances is defined with prices and demand data from 2021. All methods
are tested with the instances and a detailed description of the results is presented. In order
to compare the different methods and implementations between each other, a discussion
of the results and recommendations is provided. The different methods applied improve
the performance of the model in either solve time, relative gap or a combination of both.

Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
Ørsted is one of the biggest energy-producing companies in Denmark.
Their purpose
is to provide energy, as their service, to nowadays society in the most sustainable way.
In order to accomplish that they need to pay special attention to all costs related to
their operations. Those costs include human resources, maintenance operations in their
facilities, planners for the energy production, prices and consumption of different fuels
used in that production, etc. There is a need to continuously revise and optimize the
operations being executed.
One way in which Ørsted optimizes the use of the aforementioned resources is by the util-
isation of mathematical models that assist the decisions making and guide the operations
to be executed optimally. In this case of energy production, optimally is referred to as the
best balance between cost of production and operation, and market prices, i.e. the point
where the organisation can get the maximum amount of revenue out of their production.
On many occasions, the production has to meet some requirements in terms of demands to
fulfil, minimum and maximum rates of production, etc. The revenue has to be optimized
fulfilling those constraints.
As the energy market is growing and more competitors are arising, there is a need to
improve their operations in order to compete with the rest of the energy production
organisations. Ørsted is aware of that need, the reason why they have entire teams working
on the development of tools to maximize their revenue by optimizing the operations in
their power plants.
In the case of Denmark, CHP plants have become very popular in recent years. At the
present time, over 60 % of the district heating in the country is co-produced with electricity
[1]. For the CHP plants, the tools used for its optimization generate a schedule for the
production in the plants that will maximize the benefits and reduce the costs by utilizing
the available resources optimally. The inputs of the tools can be either demand, forecasts
of electricity prices, operational and fuel costs, etc. With that information, the schedules
with expected profits are generated and used by the operators and planners to determine
how the plant must be operated.
The energy market in Denmark [2] allows energy providers like Ørsted to adjust the
production plans so that the demand is reflected.
The system works with a bidding
approach where bids are placed on a daily and hourly basis so that the energy contracts
are secured and the market is kept balanced. In Denmark, there are two different bidding
zones: DK1, which include Jylland and Syddanmark, and DK2, including Sjælland and
Hovedstaden (Figure 1.1). The electricity market is composed of the day-ahead market
where production schedules are submitted on a daily basis, and the intraday market where
the plans can be adjusted every 15 minutes. Ørsted has the so-called ”Trading Floor”
where traders and planners are located. Those are the users in need of the optimization
tool to get assistance in their decision when planning the production.

The tool Ørsted has created to assist in scheduling energy production is called Octopus.
The software includes different mathematical tools and models for a variety of purposes
such as power production scheduling, network distribution optimization, integration of the
production for multiple plants, etc.
The model implemented to optimize the operations at CHP plants is a version of the Unit
Commitment (UC) problem. In the electrical power production field, the UC problem is
an optimization problem that coordinates the production of electrical generators to fulfil
energy demand with minimum associated costs [4]. The common modelisation includes
several thermal units that are required to meet the forecast demand at minimum cost.
The version implemented at Ørsted does not follow the common problem configuration.
It optimizes over one single thermal unit.
The model takes into account the physical
limitations of the plant such as ramping constraints for power and heat production, storage
usage, load balance for demand, maximum and minimum production rates, etc. As Ørsted
has different configurations of CHP plants since some of them include electric boilers,
contracts for auxiliary services, etc, the models for each plant are adjusted to fit the
needs of each individual case. The inputs taken by the model are the production costs,
electricity prices and demand for the following 5 days (120 hours). The solution of the
model presents then a schedule for the generation at the power plant that will maximize
the revenue, reducing costs and maximizing benefits based on the input data.
The day-ahead planners solve the model between 09:00 and 10:00 in the morning, produc-
ing a plan for the operation of the power plant for the following day. The solution of the
model is obtained through two steps. First, it computes the optimal production schedule
with a simplified version that makes use of an approximation for fuel consumption. Once
the schedule is obtained, the solutions is used in a subsequent model that does small ad-
justments in the working points so that the non-linearities of the fuel consumptions are
taken into consideration.
The solution of both models is used as input parameters for other subsequent models that
are needed to integrate the production of different plants in the network as a whole. Is
important to note that the information from the solution of all the models is used by
Ørsted to trade and place bids in the electricity market, making it valuable information.
Having that information before the competitors allows the corporation to have an economic

advantage that could potentially affect the development of the business. Since different
models need the schedule produced by the model, being able to solve it as fast as possible
would allow the other subsequent models to be solved before, obtaining an advantage
against other competitors.
As mentioned, the optimal schedule is obtained in two steps. Those steps face different
diﬀiculties during the solution procedure. The first one, which produces the production
schedule, includes the formulation of the 1-unit version of the UC model.
There are
different sets of binary variables that control when and how the unit is operating, as well
as continuous variables to control the amount of power or heat produced. The second step
consists of a formulation that takes the binary variables from the former and implements
the non-linear relation for fuel consumption to obtain the accurate amount of fuel needed
to meet the production schedule.
The modelisation team at Ørsted reported that when optimizing the first step, there are
several instances that do not obtain the optimal solution. The solution procedure requires
such a long time that they have to stop the execution in order to meet the schedule to
send the production plans to the plants. When that happens, they are forced to use a
non-optimal solution. It becomes an issue because the solution obtained needs to be used
in other models, leading to chained non-optimal solutions that can affect the economic
performance of the organisation. For the second step (model including the non-linearity
constraints), the solution procedure finished in a few seconds. There are no instances that
make the solution procedure of the second step slow.
1.2
Aims and Scope of the project
The project aims to get to know the implementation of the model and investigate the cause
of the slow convergence of the solution procedure in problematic instances. Once the causes
of the slow convergence are established, it will be investigated which mathematical tools
and frameworks can be used in order to improve the performance of the model.
For the purpose of the project, the model used is the one belonging to Esbjerg’s power
plant in Jutland. There are several cases during the years 2020, 2021 and 2022 where the
model did perform very poorly.
As mentioned, the schedules for the CHP plants are produced with a two-step approach.
The model of the first step consists of a Mixed Integer Problem (MIP) model, and the
second one consists of a Mixed Integer Quadratically Constrained Program (MIQCP). As
the step with poor performance is the solution procedure of the MIP model, the project
will be focused on that one, leaving the MIQCP out of the scope.
1.3
Methodology
The model being used and optimized on a daily basis is going to be implemented using
Julia [5] with the library Jump for mathematical modelling [6]. Commercial solvers such
as Xpress [7] and CPLEX [8] are used in order to solve and explore the model, getting
insight into the solution procedure and solutions obtained.
The methodology followed includes getting familiarized with the model, understanding
the solutions obtained, the diﬀiculties faced, and the context in which the model is used.
The exploratory part seeks to find why there are instances that the model requires a
long time to get the optimal solution, and which methods could potentially improve the
performance.

Prior Work and Literature Review
Following the prior introduction, a literature review of different versions of the UC prob-
lem for CHP plants, and formulations alike, is performed.
Studies researching several
approaches and their findings are presented.
Firstly, power and heat production have been key factors in the everyday lives of humans
for many years. It is no surprise that as technology has been advancing through the years,
making use of it to optimize the production of heat and power has also increased. Mathe-
matical models representing the functionalities of physical plants are used to optimize the
different operations involved in power and heat production. The model that this project
focuses on improving its performance is a MIP implementing a version of the UC problem.
The UC problem has been a hot topic for more than 20 years now. New and different
implementations of the base UC model including new types of constraints, network inte-
grations, different unit configurations and production needs have been done. Those new
approaches keep the research on the topic active because as the models and requirements
grow, so does the complexity of the solution procedure.
Ørsted has tried several techniques to improve the performance of the model such as warm-
starting the model with historical data or modifying the formulation of some constraints
[9]. The former did not improve the performance of the model. The latter obtain solutions
in less time, but there were found scenarios with infeasible solutions.
The related literature shows different approaches to how the UC is being solved. Common
approaches to try to improve the performance of the problem are different heuristics and
metaheuristics approaches, decomposition and relaxation of constraints.
Heuristic and Metaheuristics approaches
Regarding heuristics and metaheuristics approaches, due to the increased computation
and memory capabilities of nowadays computers, computationally demanding solution
procedures have become popular. [10] implements three different methods: Evolutionary
Programming, Hybrid Evolutionary Programming and Gbest based on an Artificial Bee
Colony. It shows a comparison within those methods and the results conclude that Gbest
obtains minimum operation cost and execution time compared to the two evolutionary
algorithms. The instances are tested with a maximum of 200 iterations and the execution
time are 36, 100 and 30 seconds for Evolutionary Programming, Hybrid Evolutionary
Programming and Gbest respectively.
On the same branch of algorithms, [11] implements a genetic algorithm and a crown search
and compares the performance against the classic and well-known Dynamic Programming
(DP) approach.
The model includes demand constraints, ramping constraints for the
generators, and minimum time up and down. The objective includes start and stop costs,
fuel consumption and maintenance costs. The results show that both algorithms perform
better than the DP approach in terms of execution time and objective value. The test
instances were of 10 generating units, wind generators and electric vehicles depending on
their battery state of charge. It is worth mentioning that the DP approach is slower, but is
more reliable in the sense that it will obtain the optimal solution when it ends executing,
but there is no guarantee of the other two algorithms.
Another popular approach is the particle swarm algorithm. [12] implemented that ap-
proach and with systems with a notable percentage of binary variables it gives a better
performance than approaches like genetic algorithm and simulated annealing.

[13] implements an Adaptive Dynamic Programming approach to solve large-scale UC
problem. It makes use of an approximation of the optimal value function from Bellman’s
equation instead of exploring the entire search space. Evaluating a few states instead of
enumerating all possible states for all units reduces exponentially the dimensionality of the
problem, and hence the time. The results obtained showed that the implementation tested
with instances from 10 to 1000 units presented good-quality solutions with polynomial
complexity.
[14] presents an improved Branch and Cut (BC) methods to accelerate the solution proce-
dure of the UC model. The methodology consists of solving smaller MIP models to reduce
the Branch and Bound (BB) iterations needed, getting tighter upper bounds with less
time. The small MIP models are formulated when an Upper Bound (UB) is found in the
BC procedure. A set of binary variables is selected using heuristics and then the model is
solved with those variables fixed. If the bound is improved, the solution is injected into
the BC procedure and the procedure continues. The solution is tested for different cases
158 and 320 units over 24 and 96 periods. The solution time improved on average 2.06.
Decomposition approaches
For the decomposition approaches, one of the most well-known and studied approaches is
Benders Decomposition. It was proposed in 1962 by Jacobus Franciscus (Jacques) Benders
[15]. The approach has been used to successfully solve large-scale mixed-integer problems
such as production planning, facility location, project scheduling, vehicle routing, etc. [16].
Benders’ decomposition approach is based on, given a set of complicating variables, for-
mulating a sub-problem that generates cuts for the constraints involving the diﬀicult
variables. Usually, the dual formulation is used. The cuts are then added to the master
problem, which is a relaxation of the original problem. The relaxation involves removing
the constraints including the diﬀicult variables.
The sub-problem iteratively guides the master problem to the optimal solution through
the generated cuts. The most common is the classical Benders decomposition. It assumes
that the sub-problem can be defined as a linear problem. Another well-known approach
is the Generalized Benders decomposition [17]. This generalized approach is an extension
of the classical approach. It does not require a linear sub-problem.

The green variables represent the variables that are shared across the

structure. For this case, a sub-problem could be formulated using the blue variables and
taking fixed values from the green ones involved in the respective constraints. The master
problem would take care of the green variables, and the solution would be guided by the
cuts generated from the sub-problem.

As per other famous and extensively studied problems such as facility location problems,
workforce scheduling, etc, there are cases applying a Benders’ decomposition approach to
improve the performance of the Unit Commitment problem. [18] implements a Benders’
decomposition to solve the Network Constraint version of the UC.
For this case, the
master problem is formulated so that it takes care of the commitment of the units. Then,
the sub-problem is used to take care of the network constraints. The approach implements
the generation of cuts from the sub-problem with BB and BC to solve the master problem
and improve both feasibility and bounds. It is shown that the network constraints are
the ones that, in this case, make the solution procedure diﬀicult, contributing to bad
convergence and longer execution times. The study concludes that the proposed approach
has to evaluate fewer nodes, and hence less overall execution time than the original problem
solved with CPLEX solver. The instances to solve have a planning horizon of 24 hours and
a total of 43128 binary variables. The instance needed originally more than 2000 seconds
to solve the root node of the Linear Problem (LP) problem. CPLEX solver was not able
to locate an integer feasible solution, even running for more than 3 hours. The Benders’
decomposition improved framework implemented reduced the feasibility cuts by 14.35 %,
and the execution time is reduced to 1057 seconds.
Another approach to a Benders decomposition application is the one proposed in [19].
The approach is similar to the one presented above, where the master problem takes the
scheduling decisions of the unit, and the sub-problem generates feasibility cuts in order to
improve the bounds of the master problem In this case, the approach does not solve the
master problem to optimality but to the first feasible solution. For the sub-problem, the
approach suggests finding the balance between deeper and computationally costly cuts
with lighter but faster cuts. The implemented cuts pursue a multiple high-density cut
generation. Overall, the framework obtains an improvement of the solve time by around
5 % for large-scale systems.
A different decomposition approach is the Danzif-Wolfe decomposition. First introduced
by George B. Dantzig and Philip Wolfe [20], it has been used in the solution of large-scale
problems. The approach is best suited for problems where the structure of the constraint
matrix is separated into independent groups that are linked by a different set of constraints
and variables. The Column Generation consists of exploiting the block structure that some
problems can have. The constraint matrix could be similar to the one presented in Figure
2.2. The coupling constraints would be the ones linking variables across the entire model.
The different blocks are the constraints that depend on different subsets of variables, being
independent of the rest.

The original problem is separated into two different levels: one determined by the structure
of the constraint matrix, separating the variables and constraints that just depend on a
subset of them. That is referred as the pricing sub-problem. The other level is the so-
called restricted master problem, and it takes care of the linking variables. The approach

consists of iteratively solving the restricted master problem and the sub-problem. The
pricing sub-problem tries to find columns (variables) with negative reduced costs so that
the optimal objective value can be improved.
[21] introduce an approach that formulates both the master and the pricing sub-problems.
The framework suggested makes use of a heuristic method to select a subset of the whole
set of columns that can be added. The publication makes use of the discretization of
the production levels to solve the sub-problem using a shortest path algorithm.
The
approach presented separates the power production scheduling problem into two different
sets: global constraints, which include demand and incompatibility constraints linking the
power plants, and the production of the thermal units. The local constraints separate each
of the nuclear power plants. The solution proposed then involves finding a production
plan for each of the plants. When solved, each of the columns with negative reduced
costs is added to the master. The heuristic to solve the model based on a subset of the
columns generated is implemented in a way that only an approximation of the pricing sub-
problem is solved, as mentioned, with the shortest path approach. With that, the column
generation implementation finds the optimal solution of the relaxed problem on the subset
of columns that can be produced. The last step on the suggested approach involves taking
the optimal solution found with the iterative pricing procedure and solving a final master
problem. The final master problem consists of a MIP that includes the global constraints,
and constraints generated dynamically during the prior iterative process.
The approach concluded that the solutions obtained reported a higher objective value due
to solving the pricing sub-problems to an approximation instead of to optimality. Overall
the approach produced good solutions that could be used as starting points for, e.g. local
search procedures.

[22] presents a warm-started Dantzig-Wolfe decomposition approach. The solution frame-

the column generation procedure, and from that the procedure of iterative solving the
master and sub-problem goes on until the optimal solution is found. The test instances
go from 200 to 1000 units and the planning horizon is 48 hours. The time limit is set at
5 minutes, and for the instances with fewer generators it converged to a GAP lower than

generate solutions. The Dantzig-Wolfe decomposition showed to work and converge to the
optimal solution, but with the warm-start approach to initialize the column generation
the execution times.
Another popular approach for solving UC models is the Lagrangian relaxation. It was
studied years ago by [23] where they found great performance but a bottleneck was found
with the limitations in computing capacity at that time. [24] present a new and actual
approach for a different Lagrangian relaxation to a UC model. The studied model has a
planning horizon of 24h with 15 minutes resolution, and the number of units to commit
is around 1100. The original model is decomposed into a master problem with relaxed
constraints in the demand which are penalized in the objective with adjustable penalty
coeﬀicients. The sub-problems are formulated for each area (geographically) where a spe-
cific demand has to be served. Also, the sub-problems include virtual transactions and
demand bids. That can make the sub-problem more sensitive to the Lagrangian multipli-
ers. To cope with the fact that sub-problems can become complex and diﬀicult to solve for
large sub-hourly instances, it is only solved to a ”good-enough” feasible solution solving a
relaxation of the sub-problem and then adjusting it to be feasible through heuristics ap-

Mathematical Model
This chapter aims to present a description of the MIP model that is nowadays imple-
mented in Octopus for the optimization and scheduling of the CHP plant in Esbjerg. The
definitions and assumptions involving the model are also presented, as well as the actual
performance and limitations of the model.
3.1
Decription and Formulation
The power plant modelled consists of a CHP that can work in different modes providing
different proportions of heat and power and an electric boiler that can produce heat using
electricity.
The model is used to obtain the schedule for the unit, its operation conditions, level of
heat storage, electricity production to be sold, etc. The main purpose of having this model
is to provide a tool for the day-ahead planners, that allows them to plan the production
at the plant generating the most revenue possible. As mentioned in the introduction, the
model is optimized once a day between 9 and 10 in the morning. The reason to do that
is that the solution of the model is going to be used to determine how the CHP plant is
going to operate the day ahead. Once that plan is established, other dependent models
are used for the network distribution of the heat and power, enabling the integration of
the production coming from different power plants.
The model takes into account that the storage of heat, which store warm water, needs to
be between minimum and maximum limits. The storage is used to meet the demand for
the same or following days. The electric boiler is able to deliver up to 25 MJ/s of heat. It
can run either with power generated by the unit if it is producing power, or can also run
with auxiliary services. Those consist consists of buying external electricity if the unit is
not producing power.
The planning horizon of the model is 120 h (five days). It is fed with initial conditions
such as the planned storage level and scheduled production at the initial time step of the
planning horizon. Those parameters are fixed by the intro-day plan. The heat demand is
another input. It is already established for the following 24 hours, and forecasted for the
remaining 4 days. It is important to note that the actual model is forced to fulfil the heat
demand that is fixed from the day before, but there are no demand requirements for power
production, meaning that all power production made is going to be a revenue added. The
need to fulfil the heat demand comes from the contract that the plant has with the local
authorities. That demand is used as input for the model together with the prognosis of
the electricity prices for the 120 hours of the planning horizon.
Once the model is solved, the best solution found outputs: when the unit should be on; in
which mode should it operate; and the amount of heat and power that should be delivered
with an hour resolution. An estimation of the fuel consumption is also computed, but the
accurate values are computed with the MIQCP model, which is left out of the scope of
the project.
The schedule of the first 24 hours is used by the day-ahead planers to create and send
the production plans to the plant.
The remaining information about heat and power
production is then used to guide and obtain an overview of how the forecasts on the prices
and demand can affect the operations at the plant. Moreover, it is relevant to note that,
Modelling of Energy Production
11

usually, the price forecast is fairly accurate in the first 24 hours. After that, the markets
can be changed. That is the reason why the model is solved every day, to obtain the best
and most accurate plan for the day ahead.
It must be mentioned that sometimes the schedule obtained from the solution of the
model is not followed exactly because of unforeseen incidences in the plant, maintenance,
or uncertainty in markets, where the operators prefer to execute in a slightly different
way, guided by their experience. Power plants are complex environments, as well as the
electricity markets, so it is fair for those responsible for the execution of the plans to use
their experience in a way that will get the best outcome, even if that means not following
an optimal power and heat production plan produced by the model.
The MIP model takes into account all physical constraints regarding max and minimum
production, working points of the unit, ramping constraints, switching modes constraints,
and minimum and maximum level of heat storage. All those constraints ensure that the
plan produced by the model produced can be executed in the real plant. Of the different
elements integrated into the model, the heat and power unit is the more diﬀicult to operate
due to the variety of working conditions and configurations that it can deal with. Is the
element with more related constraints.
There are four different modes in which the heat and power unit can operate. The different
modes are created because the eﬀiciency when the unit is working on one or another mode
can vary, and it is relevant to be able to get the best performance out of it. By dividing the
operational area into different modes it is possible to determine how the unit needs to be
operating and set costs accordingly to optimize the eﬀiciency of the plan provided. Every
mode has limitations on how much it can produce, as well as specific ramping limits. The
different modes of operation can be represented in a power and heat (PQ) diagram as the

a fixed amount of 131.77MWh divided over 24h a day.

Equations 3.10 and 3.11 are fictionally associated with the unit entering or leaving on

unit changes the mode of operation, making the plan easier to run on the physical plant.
Finally, equation 3.12 is the cost associated with the unit starting to produce heat. It is
also a fictional cost used to minimize the time that the unit starts and stops producing
heat, making it an easier schedule to follow when being brought to reality.

3.2
Implementation, Performance and Limitations of the
Model
The model has been implemented in Julia Programming Language [5] using the library for
mathematical programming language JuMP.jl [6] to write the mathematical model and
call for the optimization. Since the implementation done at Ørsted is solved using FICO
Xpress Solver [7], the library Xpress.jl [26] is used to be able to solve the model under
the same conditions that the real tool does. The objective values and execution time can
be obtained and used to assess the solution, as well as to be compared with the solution
obtained using Octopus, i.e. the solution that the day-ahead planners would obtain. With
that, the aim is to obtain an overview of how the actual model performs and what are the
limitations.
To be able to run the model, the real parameters of the power plant have been obtained,
as well as the time series from 2020, 2021 and 2022 regarding prices, demand, costs and
storage level. With that, the model has been solved for the data starting at 00:00 on
January 1st of 2020 until December 26th of 2022. The time horizon has been fixed to 5
days (120 hours), reason why it can only be run until the 26th of 2022 since it needs 120
hours of data ahead of the starting time. Moreover, during the summer months there is
no heat demand, so the model is not used. Instead, a different version that only optimizes
over the production of electricity is used, but it is left out of the scope of the project. For
that reason, the model is not solved during those periods.

4
Theory
In order to determine which frameworks can improve the performance of solving the unit
commitment problem presented in Section 3.1, the literature review presented in Chapter
2 has been performed, giving valuable insights on how the problem is located in the
actual literature. Apart from that, to get to understand the actual model, the first results
obtained when solving it have been analyzed. The way they have been analyzed is by
displaying graphically the constraint matrix, active constraints after solving, number of
cuts generated by the solvers, type of cuts, etc.
Firstly, in order to determine how constrained the model was, the structure of the con-
straint matrix and active constraints, the built-in function XPRSGetCols of the Xpress
library is used. The entire model has 7200 variables and a total of 9571 constraints.
After solving the model both to feasibility and to optimality, the average active tight con-
straints were 6600 and 6587 respectively. It must be mentioned that during the months
of higher averages in execution time (January to March 2020) the active constraints, once
the model was solved to optimality, were 7400.
Moreover, it has also been looked into which constraints were the ones with more active
rows at the optimal solution. Leaving out the equality constraints, it has been found that
the majority of constraints with binary variables regarding the status on or off, start and
stop for all modes (Equations 3.17 to 3.22) and also for the entire unit (Equations 3.33
to 3.38), were inactive in most of the instances tested. No correlation has been found
between the number of active constraints and the execution time.
4.1
Relaxation and Tightening Bound
Knowing that the problem is a highly constrained model with 9571 constraints, of which
3956 are equality constraints, and also knowing that the model includes 2280 binary
variables out of a total of 7200 variable, the first approach to improve the performance of
the model would be to optimize the formulation. An optimized formulation could imply
fewer constraints and variables, making the matrix describing the model smaller, and
reducing then the load of computation for the solver.
Commercial solvers like Xpress and CPLEX provide the possibility to apply a procedure
called pre-solve. That procedure will analyze the model and, depending on how the pre-
solve is configured, redundant or implied constraints will be removed, and some bounds
for inequality constraints will also be tightened.
Regarding the inequality constraints, tightening the bounds of the constraints will decrease
the solution space. With that, it can become more diﬀicult for the solver to obtain a
feasible solution, but when it does obtain a solution, it is faster to prove the optimality of
that solution.
Regarding binary variables and the implication of having implied binary variables, if there
are variables that do not require to be declared as binary because they are bounded to
either take the value ”0” or ”1” due to the nature of the constraints, the definition of the
variables can be relaxed. That would reduce the total number of binary variables and
would lead to alleviating the computational power required by the solver when performing
the branching procedure.

With that, utilizing the pre-solve tool from CPLEX, as well as a manual revision of the

can be done. If there are redundant constraints or implied binaries, the model can be
re-formulated, so that a simplified and easy-to-solve option is obtained.
4.2
Lazy Constraints
From the analysis of the state of the constraints presented at the beginning of the section,
it can be seen that there is a significant amount of constraints that are not active in the
optimal solutions found. That leads to the hypothesis that it could be possible to improve
the performance of the model if the constraints that are not going to be active in the
optimal solution were removed from the model. That would make it a simpler model,
the solution procedure could be simplified and the optimal solution could be found in less
time, or at least get a better solution for instances that timeout.
A way to implement that behaviour is by the use of lazy constraints. Lazy constraints
are used in the literature to describe constraints that are included in the model but only
checked when a feasible solution is found.
Then, the lazy constraints are checked to
see if there are fulfilled or not.
In case a constraint is not fulfilled then is added to
the model so that it will be fulfilled in the following solution candidates nodes.
[27]
use the lazy constraint approach with a Security Constraint Unit Commitment Model.
Great improvements were reported when implementing the approach, together with warm-
starting the model.
The solver Xpress, as well as other well-known such as CPLEX and Gurobi, has the
capability to implement lazy constraints to the model. In the case of Xpress, the notation
”lazy constraint” is not used. Instead, it uses ”delayed rows”, which are described as as: a
set of rows (constraints) that will be satisfied by any integer solution, but will not be loaded
into the active set of constraints until required [28]. That means that when a constraint is
loaded into the model as a ”delayed row”, it will be treated like what is commonly known
as a lazy constraint.
The selection of constraints to be considered as lazy constraints must be done by taking
into consideration which types of constraints the model is leading with and which are
active or inactive. In the case of the model presented, there are 3956 equality constraints
out of a total of 9571 constraints. Those equality constraints will always be active, but
that indicates that there is a remaining 5615 constraints that will not necessarily be active
in a feasible optimal solution. The constraints defined as ”delayed rows” are presented in
Chapter 5 - Implementation.
4.3
Lagrangian Relaxation
The Lagrangian Relaxation approach to solving computationally hard problems has been
popular and useful for many applications since it was presented around the 70s. The main
framework when applying the Lagrangian relaxation consists on partition the constraint
space of the problem to solve into two groups. One group of constraints is the group to
relax and is the one that is added to the objective function with the so-called Lagrange
multipliers. The other set of constraints is kept in the model. With that, the aim is to
obtain a simplified easy-to-solve version of the original model, which will be referred to as
the Lagrange Model.
The aim of introducing the Lagrange Multipliers is to penalise the objective when a con-
straint of the relaxed ones is violated. The process to solve the model then consists of
iterative optimizing the Lagrange Model and modifying the multiplier to make it converge

to an optimal feasible solution for all constraints in the original problem. The termination
conditions can vary depending on the application, but as a general rule, the algorithm runs
until a feasible solution is obtained from the optimization of the Lagrange Model or the
time limit is reached. Moreover, some other approaches can include the use of heuristics
to compute a feasible solution from an unfeasible one.
The way in which the Lagrange Multipliers are updated can affect the convergence of the
algorithm, as well as the quality of the solutions. The best choice of the multipliers would
be the optimal solution to the dual problem [29].

4.4
Benders Decomposition
Benders decomposition is usually used to solve large-scale mathematical models. It allows
breaking the model into a master and a sub-problem. The core concept consists of keeping
a set of variables in the master problem, usually, the variables that make the model
diﬀicult to solve. The remaining variables are put into the sub-problem, with its respective
constraints. With that, the procedure consists of solving the master problem and obtaining
values that might or might not, be feasible for the original problem.
The sub-problem is formulated with the constraints and objective terms including the
variables that are removed from the master problem. The variables that are left in the
master problem are used as parameters (fixed values) in this sub-problem’s constraints.
With that, the sub-problem will find an optimal solution with the condition of those fixed
values.
The dual of the sub-problem is formulated so that the solution space does not depend
on the values of the variables coming from the master problem. Assuming that the dual
of the sub-problem is bounded, it will obtain an optimal solution that will generate an
optimality cut. The cut is added to the master problem as an upper bound for a variable
accounting for the value that the variables on the sub-problem would add to the objective
function.
In the case that the dual sub-problem cannot be solved due to being unbounded, which
would indicate that the values provided from the master problem are not feasible, the
ray version must be solved. The ray version has a formulation quite similar to the dual
sub-problem, with the exception that the objective function if forced to be a constant
value so that it is solved to feasibility only. It also has a constraint forcing the objective of
the dual sub-problem to be equal to the constant value that is forced now in the objective
function of the ray version. When solved, the dual values obtained are use to generate a
feasibility cut which is added to the master problem.
The procedure iterates solving the master and sub-problems until no more cuts are gen-
erated or the difference between the upper and lower bounds obtained is smaller than the
required tolerance. When that point is reached, the solution found is feasible and optimal.
Must be noted that global optimality would be reached when no optimality or feasibility
cuts are generated, meaning that the bounds cannot be improved any further.

of constraints, and also a combination of groups of constraints. Some problems started
arising when ”too many” constraints were to be checked and added to the model. Using
the right constraints might be diﬀicult because if the iterative procedure of adding violated
constraints to the model gets too demanding for the solver because it ends up having to
add almost all delayed constraints to the model, it will take longer than the original way
of solving the model. For that reason, the approach implemented only includes the set of
constraints listed above.
5.3
Lagrangian Relaxation
The implementation of the Lagrangian Relaxation consists of different parts:
First, a parameterized formulation of the model has been done where the index of the
constraints to relax and put into the objective is passed as an array. With that, the model
is formulated without including those constraints. Then, the objective function is defined
with all the original terms, as resented in Equations 3.1 to 3.12. Then the terms for the
constraints are added with the form: u · (A · x −b). The parameter u is defined such that
it is possible to be modified from other functions. That formulation is referred as the
Lagrangian Formulation.
Second, a function to obtain an initial feasible solution is implemented. It formulates the
model with all the constraints but the so called objective sense of the objective function
is set to FEASIBILITY_SENSE. That allows the solver to exit the solution procedure as
soon as a feasible integer solution is found. Must be noted that it has been implemented
in this way and not following another approach because, when tested with all available
instances, a feasible solution can be found, on average, on the first 4 to 6 seconds. With
that it is ensured that there is going to be a feasible solution available to return in case
the relaxation does not converge to absolute feasibility.
Third, a function to check the feasibility of the solution is also implemented. The input
for that function is a pointer to the solved model. The function retrieves then the primal
values of the variables in the solution found and checks the feasibility of the constraints
which have been relaxed. The function returns an array with the constraints that are being
violated, as well as the value of A · xk −b. That information is used both for recovering
feasibility if needed, and update the multipliers as indicated by expression in Equation
4.2, if needed.

5.4
Benders Decomposition
For a Benders decomposition, there are multiple possibilities and combinations of variables
to keep in the master problem/send to the sub-problem. The variables that are to be sent
to the sub-problem can be categorized as ”low-level” and ”high-level” variables.
The
difference between both types is which part of the model the variables affect to. The
”high-level” variables are variables that expand over the planning horizon (T) but do not
Modelling of Energy Production
39
==PAGE:52==
have an effect on the operating points of the PQ diagram, mode of operation, etc. Those
are ruled by the named ”low-level” variables.
Variables that go into the sub-problem belonging to ”high-level” sets can be Qtotal or
LevelStorage.
Those variables are defined in the T dimension, meaning there is one
variable for each time step.
Generating cuts involving those variables will shape the
solution along the entire planning horizon since the heat, power production and the amount
of heat stored will be required to be modified.
Those changes are ruled by ramping
constraints, operating points of the PQ diagram, amount of heat and power produced,
binary variables, etc. The effect of those constraints expands both the planning horizon
and the modes of operation. With that, the cuts will have a great effect on the solution
generated due to bounded decisions involving those variables.
On the other hand, ”low-level” variables can be Pm,t, Qm,t and Alphapq,m,t. When being
sent to the sub-problem it is expected that a higher amount of cuts will be generated in
comparison with the ”high-level” ones. The ”low-level” nature of the variables implies
that the cuts will shape the solution with less aggressive cuts.
For the ”low-level” variables, the relationship between the load that the variables remove
from the master problem when send to the sub-problem, and the number of cuts needed to
get feasible/optimal solutions, will determine how fast the decomposition converges. If the
variables (and constraints) removed from the master problem do not allow obtaining fast
solutions and, on top of that, the number of cuts to generate is high, the implementation
will have a slow convergence. On the other hand, if the master problem can be solved fast
enough, even if the number of cuts to generate is high, the convergence could be faster.
With that, four different decompositions with different approaches are presented below.
The aim of the different decompositions is to reduce the complexity of the model by
targeting different types of variables and constraints.

6.1
Test Setup and General Overview
To assess the performance of the methods implemented, a set of instances have been
defined so that all methods are tested with the same conditions.
As one of the main
objectives of the project is to improve the performance of the model by getting faster and
better solutions, all test instances are to be run with a time limit of 240 (4 minutes). That
time has been determined based on the real applications of the model. When the day-
ahead planner in charge of running the model to schedule the production cannot obtain
a solution in the first, approximately, four minutes, they stop the procedure and get the
best current solution. It does not matter if the solution has a small gap or not. With that,
setting the timeout to four minutes and comparing the relative gap, or objective value,
obtained with the value found with the original formulation and solution procedure can
be used as an indicator for the convergence of the methods utilised. If the gap is smaller
than originally, then the convergence is better, if it is greater, then the convergence has
been worsened.
The instances that the methods are tested include all days in January, February, March
and December of 2020, setting up a total of 122 test instances. Table 6.1 presents the
main metrics for the test instances solved with the original model. It can be seen that
concerning the solve time, the instances are spread over all the groups.
Regarding the objective value, as the electricity prices were low in comparison with the
ones during 2021 and 2022, and the head demand was at its peak since it was winter
season, the plant does not produce revenue during those months (positive objective value).
Lastly, only the instances that timed out have a value different to zero for the relative
gap. Otherwise, the optimal solution is obtained.

approach obtains optimal solutions faster or slower. With the Gap, it can be seen if the
instances are solved to optimality, and for the ones that time out, the convergence of the
approach can be assessed. If the gap obtained is smaller at timeout than with the original
procedure, then the convergence has been improved, leading to better solutions.

if there are any errors observed during the solution procedures, those will also be reported

6.5
Benders Decomposition
The following section presents the results obtained with the implementations described in
Section 5.4.
Must be noted that during the implementation and verification of the methods proposed it
has been observed that the classical version of Benders’ decomposition was not well suited
to the model. Even though the implementations were generating cuts and converging, all
the instances timeout out, most of them without even reaching a single feasible solution.
That slowness is caused by the fact that solving the master problem to optimality takes
too much time in comparison to how deep the cuts are generated and the effect they have.
For that reason, the results presented are obtained with the Callback version and the
CPLEX built-in approach.
6.5.1
First Approach - Qtotal
The following section presents the results obtained with the Benders’ decomposition send-

In Table 6.12 can be seen that the instances are not solved faster with the implementation
of Benders’ decomposition. Mean values, maximum and minimum values have, overall,
increased. On the other hand, the relative gap obtained for the instances that did originally
timeout has been decreased. The mean value has decreased a 15.34%. Must be noted that
not all instances have been improved, but as the average decreases, it is conceived that
the approach leads to a better solution for the originally ”diﬀicult-to-solve” instances.
It can also be seen in Table 6.12 that the implementation is not able to solve all the
instances. There are three instances, which correspond to the days 3rd, 7th and 13th of
March 2020 that do not obtain a single feasible solution. Those instances were solved with
the original approach without timing out, i.e. obtaining the optimal solution, so missing
a solution is not related to those instances being the hardest ones to solve.
Looking at the solution obtained for those instances seems that the diﬀiculty lies in the
high variability of the head demand in that period. Due to the ramping constraints and
the requirements for the working points of the unit, it becomes costly to keep the unit on
during the low-demand hours once the heat storage is already at capacity. The optimal
solution provided by the original approach turn on and off the unit several times during the
planning horizon. Also, for the operating points, the solution switches between producing
only heat to producing power when the storage is at capacity and the demand is in a
valley. After the valley, the heat production is turned on again and eventually, the unit
is stopped so that the storage is used. Since the constraints binding the amount of heat
production are the ones that the sub-problem is formulated upon, in order to obtain a
feasible solution a great amount of feasibility cuts will be needed to obtain a feasible
solution. Even after obtaining a feasible solution, optimality cuts will be needed to keep
improving the solution.
Regarding the solve time for the instances, generally, it has been extended. That indicates
that the procedure affects the solve time of the fast instances by making them slower. That
is because the solution procedure, as described in the implementation section, requires
the solution of a sub-problem at the integer nodes found and adding the necessary cuts
generated. Those operations consume computational resources and clock cycles that for
the fast instances would not be needed.

Exploring the solutions obtained it has been noticed that the explanation for that is that
CPLEX solver falls into a locally optimal solution from which it cannot escape, giving the
termination status LOCALLY_SOLVED. That status can be reached when using JuMP.j by
up to 20 different termination status reported by CPLEX. In this case, the termination
status reported from CPLEX was CPXMIP_FAIL_FEAS.
From the oﬀicial CPLEX documentation, the status CPXMIP_FAIL_FEAS indicates that
there has been some sort of error during the solution procedure but a feasible solution
is available. This error can be triggered by either reaching a limit of iterations or by
problems in the sub-problem. In order to determine the origin of the error, the status of
the last sub-problem can be retrieved by using the function CPXgetsubstat. By doing
that, the status reported that the sub-problem was correctly solved, so the sub-problem
is not the origin of the error. To check that the number of iterations is what causes the
problem, the parameters are adjusted to the maximum value that CPLEX allows. The
value was retrieved at the moment of failure, to check if the limit was reached, but it was
not. With that, some further research on this type of problem has been done, especially
in the community of users of CPLEX. More people found the same issue. From CPLEX
support, no solution has been found so far, but different users reported different responses
when changing to prior versions, trying different devices, etc. With that, seems that there
is some kind of bug when using CPLEX built-in approach.

For the second approach, variables Pm,t and Qm,t are sent to the sub-problem.
The
numerical results are presented in Tables 6.18, 6.19 and 6.20 for the Callback version, and

Table 6.18 shows that, overall, the solve time obtained is increased over all the groups.
Moreover, the relative gap has also been increased. That indicates that the decomposition
does not provide a faster convergence than the original model solved with Xpress.
It must be noted that the implementation does not provide feasible solutions to all the
instances. Only 21 out of 122 (17.21%) end the solution procedure with a feasible solution,
the remaining 101 (82.79%) do not obtain a feasible integer node.
That behaviour can be explained due to the amount of cuts to be generated. Formulating
the sub-problem with variables Pm,t and Qm,t involves a total of 1680 constraints. The
higher the number of constraints relaxed from the sub-problem, the more potential cuts
might have to be generated in order to get a feasible solution. Needing the generation of
many cuts imply that the sub-problem is solved more times, which will use a greater part
of the solve time. With that, the callback implementation of the second approach to a
Benders decomposition does not present an improved performance.
On the other hand, the results obtained with the built-in CPLEX Benders’ decomposition
do behave differently than the callback version. Table 6.21 displays that all instances have
been able to be solved, at least, to feasibility.
For the instances that originally timed out, the relative gap has been improved. The aver-
age relative gap has been decreased from 0.01745 to 0.00354, which represents a reduction
of 79.72%. That indicates that the built-in approach converges faster on the originally
slow instances. For the solve time, it has been reduced by 7.46% on average.
On the other hand, the solution of the instances that were originally solved in less time
(120 to 5 seconds groups) does require more time. Similarly to the first approach, that
indicates that the implementations improve the bounds of the harder instances, but the
easier ones get worsened in solve time.
From Table 6.22 it can be seen how with the implementation all instances are moved to the
top three time groups (240, 120 and 60). For the number of benders cuts, if compared with
the ones generated with the callback version, the built-in approach generated an average
of 905.17 cuts (Table 6.23) and a maximum value of 2595, as per the callback version
generated an average of 853.15 (Table 6.20) and a maximum of 1374. That indicates that
the built-in approach solves more times the sub-problem and generates more cuts than
the callback implementation.
From table 6.22 it can be seen that there are 30 instances that timed out for the CPLEX
Benders’ decomposition implemented, 12 more than originally. The mean value of the
instances timing out is 0.0088, which originally was 0.01745. There are more instances
timing out, but the solution obtained is, on average, better than originally.

6.5.4
Fourth Approach - Alpha
For the fourth approach, both the callback and CPLEX built-in versions did not converge
to a feasible solution after the timeout for the test instances. The maximum time was
changed to 3600 seconds and the instances still timed out without reaching feasibility.
Different instances from 2022 which were originally solved in less than a second have been
tried, but the observed behaviour was the same.
As the cuts generated inequality constraints depending on pq, m and t, the number of cuts
to generate was extremely high (several thousand). That is caused because the cuts are
generated on the working point of the unit at every time and in every mode. That allows
that after a feasibility cut pushes the solution away from a working point, the next one
is located right next to it since those points are determined by the linear combinations
of continuous variables in the convex hull of the PQ diagram. For that reason, in order
to move enough of the working points to a feasible combination at each time step of the
planning horizon, many iterations including solving the sub-problem and adding the cuts
are required. As the working points depend on the mode of operation, the amount of cuts
gets multiplied by the times that the solution changes from using one mode to another.
In the case that the solution procedure oscillates between being in one mode or another,
the number of cuts will be increased.
With that, the decomposition does not converge faster than the original solution method.
All instances timed out without reaching feasibility. The decomposition is now well-suited
for the model.
6.6
Combination of procedures
As it has been presented in Section 6.2 when the model is simplified according to the
information provided by the pre-solve procedure, the performance is improved. As the
modification of the model consists of the relaxation of binary variables to be continuous,
modification of constraint formulation and changing the upper/lower bounds of several
constrain, the underlying structure of the model is not changed.
For that reason, it
is possible to modify the models used with the different approaches implemented: lazy
constraints, Lagrangian relaxation and some of the Benders decomposition.

model obtains 6% more improvement. The solve times for the 240, 120 and 60 groups
have been improved 2% more compared to the first initially obtained values with the lazy
constraints implementation.
Overall, the modifications of the model obtained faster and better solutions when applied
together with the lazy constraints.

Discussion and Future Work
The following chapter discusses the results obtained in Chapter 6. The assumptions and
simplifications that lead to those results are considered, and the scope of possible future
work is presented.
The first aspect to consider is that not all implementations and frameworks utilized have
improved the performance of the solution procedure of the model. The Benders’ decom-
position that formulates the sub-problem with variable LevelStoraget and the one with
Alphapq,m,t did not improve the solution quality of the originally diﬀicult instances, nor
improved the solution time of the rest of the instances. The former was able to obtain fea-
sible solutions within the timeout established (240 seconds). The latter did not converge
to feasible solutions even with an extended timeout of up to 3600 seconds.
The main drive behind both decompositions was based on the effect the constraints in-
cluding those variables did. Both, LevelStoraget and Alphapq,m,t, highly constraint the
model due to several equality constraints that they impose. They are related to the gen-
eration, demand and load balance. Due to the costs related to changing operation modes,
on/off costs, etc., it was expected that once the cuts started pushing the generation to
meet the demand in certain time steps, the neighbouring ones would tend to also fulfil the
demand. Once the results were obtained it was observed that the performance was not as
expected. On the other hand, for the implementation using QTotal
m,t
and the implementation
using Pm,t and Qm,t the performance obtained did improve. The difference between the
implementations is that the former two are indirectly bounded to the production while
the latter two are directly related. Directly related constraints generate cuts that shape
the solution more aggressively than indirectly related constraints.
The implementations that did provide an improved performance either in solve time or
solution quality are the Lagrangian Relaxation, the Lazy Constraints approach, and the
mentioned Benders’ decomposition formulation with QTotal in the sub-problem, and the
one with Pm,t and Qm,t.
Figure 7.1 provides a general overview of the solve time and the relative gap obtained for
all the approaches that in one way or another have improved the performance of the model.
It must be noted that the values displayed in the figure are obtained from the modified
implementations presented in Section 6.6. Those implementations have been chosen over
the original ones because the results obtained are better.

Looking at the four implementations in a big picture, it can be seen that to reduce the
solution time the Lagrangian Relaxation and Lazy constraints approaches are the ones
obtaining the best results. Shorter solution time over all the instances (on average) with
improved solution quality for the originally hard instances.
For the Lagrangian relaxation, as a negative point, the relative gap obtained is not optimal
in most of the instances.
With that, the Lagrangian Relaxation would be a suitable
implementation if the goal is to obtain a solution as fast as possible, putting the solution
quality in the second plane.
Must also be noted that the implementation of the Lagrangian Relaxation does involve
the formulation of a new model with fewer constraints and different objective functions,
handling the multipliers, checking the feasibility of the primal model, and also implement-
ing the function to recover the primal feasibility. On the other hand, the Lazy constraints
implementation does not require great changes in the model formulation, additional func-
tion and algorithm implementation. It is based on handling the parameters of the solver
and selecting the constraints to be marked as Lazy. In that regard, for a fast implementa-
tion that can deliver improved results, the Lazy Constraints approach would be the best
suited. On the other part, the Lagrangian Relaxation approach would be well suited if
reducing the solve time is critical, even sacrificing solution quality.
For both Benders’ decompositions, the implementations would be well suited in the case
where the critical need is to reduce the relative gap of the diﬀicult-to-solve instances. The
solution time is not improved overall, but the originally hard instances have shown to
obtain a better solution quality.
A different approach that could be implemented to obtain the maximum potential of
the frameworks investigated would be to implement a parallel solution that would include
different methods. A first solution would be, e.g. to implement the Lagrangian Relaxation
and Benders 1. From the tests, it could be expected that the Lagrangian would obtain

a quick and good enough solution, but at the same time, it could be compared with the
solution obtained by the Benders’ decomposition. In the case that the solution procedure
starts getting slow and no improvement is obtained for the Benders, the planners would
have available a still good solution obtained with the Lagrangian. A drawback of the
proposed approach is that it would require the maintenance of multiple models, and hence
more resources.
Additionally, there are a variety of frameworks that could improve the solution procedure of
the model. A proposal to explore based on the performance observed with the Lagrangian
Relaxation could be a Danzig-Wofe decomposition approach. It involves solving a master
and sub-problem iteratively until no further improvement can be achieved.
The role
of the sub-problem is to find which variables can improve the objective value and add
them to the master problem. The connection within the Lagrangian Relaxation and the
column generation approach is that both obtain the same upper bound (for minimization
problems) [30]. The Lagrangian formulation relaxes constraints from the original primal
model, adding them to the objective function.
On the other hand, the Danzig-Wolfe
approach keeps the master constrained but relaxes the space by convexifying it. As the
Lagrangian has provided good results in terms of improved solve time, but not as good with
optimality, it seems that a Danzig-Wofe decomposition approach could benefit from the
rapid solution of the dualisation and improve the bounds with the generation of columns.
As the Danzig-Wofe decomposition requires a block structure to decompose the master
problem into different sub-problem, a possible scheme of decomposition would be tempo-
rally decomposition. There is a great number of constraints that include variables at time
t and t + 1, but there is no correlation of variables further than a single time-step. That
would allow the division of the planning horizon into different blocks. As there would be
variable sharing in between blocks, it would be needed to split those and take the master
problem to force the relation with those. A simple implementation with variable sharing
has been carried out during the project, but due to diﬀiculties in the implementation and
time limitations it has been left out of the scope of the project.
Overall, implementing some changes in the formulation of the model improves the perfor-
mance of the solution procedure. On top of that, the performance of the model is improved
by using Lazy Constraints, implementing a Lagrangian Relaxation algorithm or different
types of Benders decomposition. Breaking the model into smaller easy-to-solve does show
to make it easy for the solvers to obtain good solutions.

Conclusions
This thesis dealt with a poor-performing version of a Unit Commitment problem. The
model is used in a CHP plant to schedule its operation so that the revenue produced is
maximized depending on price and demand predictions.
In order to find the underlying reason for the poor performance of the model, a thorough
investigation of the formulation and sample solutions was carried out. It was found that
the long solve times were caused because of the conditions in demand and the variables
controlling the unit. Those conditions were constraining the solution space and making
it diﬀicult to find the optimal production plan. Not all instances were affected, only the
ones with higher demand and low prices had long solve times and non-optimal solutions.
To improve the model’s performance, first, the formulation has been changed to reduce the
number of binary variables and constraints. That approach proved to improve the solve
times and solution quality across the test instances. Another approach that has been im-
plemented is lazy constraints, which solved the model faster and improved the convergence
by getting smaller gaps. Moreover, the formulation of a Lagrangian Relaxation showed
the best improvement in solve time, but not in solution quality. Lastly, different Benders’
decompositions have also proven to improve the convergence and solution quality of the
diﬀicult-to-solve instances.
Overall, different frameworks have been presented and all of them showed strengths and
weaknesses. The main takeout is that the approaches simplifying the model got the best
improvements. For the scope of future work, the real implementation and testing of the
different solutions presented are to be done. Also, different approaches such as a Danzing-
Wolfe decomposition, or the combination of the different presented approaches could be
addressed as further work to explore how the performance of the model could be improved.
To conclude, the main goals of exploring the model and improving the performance of it to
schedule the production at the CHP in Esbjerg has been achieved. Apart from that, the
journey has involved getting to learn about different tools and techniques used in real-life
models, which provided valuable insights and takeaways.
