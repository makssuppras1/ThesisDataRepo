Annealing behaviour of additively manufactured AlSi10Mg 
Abstract 
 
Thanks to their light weight and good mechanical properties, aluminium alloys are of interest to 
the transport and aerospace sectors. Among these alloys, the AlSi alloys and in particular 
AlSi10Mg present a low shrinkage during cooling and a high fluidity and are therefore suitable for 
casting or additive manufacturing (AM).  
The AlSi10Mg alloy studied in this work is widely used for laser powder bed fusion (LPBF), an AM 
technique. This process is effective in rapid production of net-shape parts, without requiring part-
specific tools (e.g. moulds). The final microstructure of the AlSi10Mg part is very fine scaled which 
leads to good mechanical properties. This fine microstructure is a consequence of high cooling 
rates inherent in the process, which however also are responsible for large residual stresses. 
Subsequent stress relief heat treatments (HTs) are therefore often carried out to reduce the part’s 
brittleness. 
This study focuses on the evolution of stresses and microstructure before and after annealing at 
520°C. As the microstructure is highly anisotropic, 3D techniques such as atom probe 
tomography (APT) and synchrotron microdiffraction but also X-ray diffraction (XRD) are included 
in the analysis. These characterisation techniques are also combined with 2D scanning electron 
microscopy and light optical microscopy.  
In the as printed (AP) samples, the APT allowed to estimate the exact composition of the Si and Al 
phases in the eutectic network. The knowledge of these compositions in combination with XRD 
characterisation provided a thorough analysis of the stresses. Furthermore, these stresses, in heat 
treated samples, were studied using synchrotron X-ray energy scans. Synchrotron 
microdiffraction along with 2D imaging techniques allowed to understand the eutectic network 
degradation and the Si particles coarsening in relation with their position in the Al matrix.  
In this work, two samples manufactured with different printing parameters are characterized, to 
learn about what effects they have on microstructures, stresses and their evolution during 
annealing. The results revealed significant stresses in AP samples which reduce upon HT. The 
development of a substantial and similar coarsening of Si particles in the two samples was 
observed. Furthermore, results related to phase composition, crystal structure and orientation 
relationships are discussed.

1. Motivation  
AlSi alloys represent 80% of casted aluminium alloys. Due to their light weight and good 
mechanical properties, they are widely used in the automotive and aerospace industry (1). 
AlSi10Mg is a common grade of AlSi, now in the spotlight for its suitability for Laser Powder Bed 
Fusion (LPBF), one of the most utilized Additive Manufacturing (AM) processes. The advantage of 
this technology is that not only it can produce very complex parts, but it can also produce parts 
with better mechanical properties than those produced using traditional methods such as casting. 
For example, in all directions, its yield strength and ultimate tensile strength (2), (3), (4), (5), (6), 
(7) were found on average to be 50% higher than the minimum requirements from its casted 
equivalent (8). This increase in mechanical properties is due to the very fine microstructure 
produced by the very high cooling rates (often up to 106K/s) (9), (10), (11). However, the same 
cooling rates may result in significant residual stresses and brittleness which limit the use of the 
printed parts for a majority of potential applications (12). Post-heat treatment (HT) has been 
identified as a way forward. 
 
Until now, the main focus has been on optimizing the AM process. The microstructural 
characterization is still rather poor, both before and after HT. In particular, 3D information about 
the morphology and topology of the phases is lacking. To the writer’s best knowledge, only few 
papers report findings based on 3D characterization techniques, and most of them focused on the 
sample porosity in as printed (AP) samples. Concerning heat treated samples, almost all works 
have examined the effects of HT post-mortem and without any 3D analysis.  
To perform the correct HT, leading to minimized brittleness while maintaining the other 
desirable mechanical qualities, it is necessary to evaluate the evolution of the microstructure 
during HT. The thesis therefore aims at analysing the residual stresses and the microstructure 
evolution during HT using both 2D and 3D characterization techniques, including electron 
microscopy, advanced synchrotron techniques and atomic probe tomography (APT). In particular, 
the formation of Si particles and their coarsening are analysed as it leads to important changes in 
the final mechanical properties.  
Two AlSi10Mg samples manufactured by LPBF with different parameters were studied. 
The comparison of the two samples allows to observe the influence of the printing parameters on 
the initial microstructure and eventually on the behaviour during HT.  
 
In this report, the literature review on LPBF AlSi10Mg is presented in the introduction 
(section 2). The microstructure and crystal structure are described for both AP and heat-treated 
samples, and the influence of printing parameters on AP microstructure is discussed. The 
methodology section 3 details the characterization techniques, parameters and the sample 
preparation procedures applied, and introduces the principles of different synchrotron 
techniques, of focused ion beam (FIB) and of APT. Finally, the results obtained with these 
techniques and their analysis are presented in the section 4. First, the samples were examined in 
their AP conditions. For the microstructure, after confirming general observations from other 
studies, the focus is put on the Al-Si eutectic network. This eutectic network is not well 
characterized in literature as it is on the scale of a few tens of nanometres. It is however critical as 
it dissolves and leads to the formation of large Si particles during HT. The AP microstructure is 
then compared with heat treated samples to understand the Si particles formation and coarsening. 
Synchrotron energy scans along with laboratory X-Ray Diffraction (XRD) measurements enabled 
the investigation of residual stress and 3D micro-diffraction allowed to study orientation 
relationships between Al-matrix and Si particles.

2. Introduction 
1.1 Additive manufacturing  
AM encompasses a group of manufacturing techniques building parts layer by layer. Depending 
on the technique, diverse feedstocks (eg powder or wire) can be used as the base material to be 
melted or deposited by layers of similar thickness. Each layer has the shape of one 2D slice 
extracted from the 3D Computer-Aided-Design (CAD) model used to design the part (13). The final 
part is composed of all the layers printed and bonded together during curing or melting (14). 
AM can be utilized for all types of materials and its wide range of techniques offers a lot of 
flexibility. As it allows to rapidly build net-shape (or near net-shape) parts, ie not requiring any 
machining, and without shape-specific tools (by opposition to moulds in casting), it is often 
applied in R&D for fast prototyping. It is now also used in robotics, aeronautics or bio-printing 
thanks to its ability to create precise and complex parts. However, the high cooling rates in each 
thin printed layer often lead to high residual stresses and therefore to parts more brittle than 
when using conventional manufacturing. This brittleness is sometimes remedied with post HT 
especially for metal parts. 
1.1.1 
Laser powder bed fusion 
This master thesis focuses on parts manufactured by a specific AM technique, namely LPBF. This 
method is also known under several commercial names such as selective laser melting (for the 
equipment manufacturers SLM Solutions, Matsuura and Renishaw), direct metal laser sintering 
(for EOS Gmbh), LaserCusing (for Concept Laser) and Direct metal production (Phenix, 3D system) 
(6), (15). 
Compared to other techniques, LPBF is more adapted to metals and is widely used for printing Ti 
and Al alloys, Ni superalloys, steels, and high entropy alloys (16). It allows to manufacture parts 
with very unique geometries and complex internal structures. 
Additive Manufacturing routine for LPBF 
Similar to other AM methods, LPBF requires three pre-processing steps, see Figure 1. The first 
step is to create a 3D CAD model of the expected “printed” or manufactured part. This model is 
then converted to a compatible file (.stl) (second step), from which the slicing is realized (third 
step). Slicing consists in splitting the initial 3D volume in 2D slices corresponding to each layer to 
be printed. A support structure can be added to the CAD model to ensure that overhanging 
structures or bridge structures will not be bent during the printing (13), (14). Adding support 
structure is less often required in LPBF as the powder bed can act as support for small 
overhanging structures (6), (15), (17) . 
The fourth step, the LPBF manufacturing itself, can also be decomposed in three sub- steps. First, 
a powder feed container is pushed upwards while the build platform holding the base plate 
(where the part is being printed) is moved downwards by the height of a powder layer. A 
deposition system can then spread the powder on the build plate which will be scanned by the 
laser beam following the 2D slice geometry. This powder melts and solidifies very fast typically 
leading to fine microstructures and therefore high mechanical strength and hardness (15), (16), 
(17).

Parameters 
In LPBF, several parameters determine the final microstructure and texture. It is therefore 
essential to understand their influence and optimize them.  
The main parameters are: 
• 
h : Hatch spacing, applied distance between the laser scan lines, typically from 100 to 200µm 
(18), (19) 
• 
t : Layer thickness, thickness of each added powder layer, typically from 20 to 100 µm (19) 
• 
v : Laser or scanning speed, typically from 10-2 to 2 m/s (1) but can go up to 10 m/s (19) 
• 
P : Laser power, usually from 20 W to 1 kW (19) 
• 
T0: Base plate preheating temperature, temperature of the plate on which the part is built 
• 
Laser spot size, usually from 50 µm to 180 µm 
• 
Laser scanning pattern, directions of the laser which can be varied while printing one layer 
or between the layers

Protection gas

1.2 AlSi10Mg 
AlSi alloys typically contain between 3 and 25% of Si but also Cu or Mg for hardening. Their light 
weight and low cost make them interesting for the aerospace and automotive industry as well as 
heat exchangers (1). The versatility of these alloys along with their flexible post-processing 
capabilities can allow further uses especially when applying powder metallurgy techniques (21), 
(22). 
The alloy studied in this work, AlSi10Mg, is composed of around 10wt% of Si, making it a near 
eutectic alloy, and of 0.25 to 0.50wt% of Mg, see Table 1, (6), (12). The Mg added in this alloy is 
expected to contribute to precipitation hardening via the formation of Mg2Si during natural or 
artificial ageing (16). Other elements such as Fe Pb, Sn etc. are considered as impurities (23).

1.2.1 
As printed AlSi10Mg  
The small solidification range (around 40°C) of AlSi10Mg ensures its good weldability thus also 
its suitability for AM (16), (21). LPBF was found to be a good manufacturing technique for this 
alloy as it leads to a fine microstructure and therefore better mechanical properties than casting 
where the formation of coarse and acicular Si particles can be problematic (16), (21). The lower 
thermal expansion coefficient of this AlSi alloy compared to others also provides better 
dimensional accuracy and lower residual stresses. This is a major advantage since residual 
stresses are one of the main drawbacks of AM (12). 
Porosity obtained from LPBF  
Along with residual stresses, part density was one of the biggest concerns when LPBF was first 
used and more generally AM. In order to control the porosity but also the final microstructure, 
many articles focused on printing parameters optimization.  
The volumetric energy density (Ev) was often utilized to provide indications on the part 
density and on the type of pores formed. A low Ev and in particular when associated with a low 
laser power or high scanning speed, leads to lack of fusion pores. These pores correspond to 
residual interparticle spacing ie cavities initially present between the powder grains. On the 
contrary, a high Ev leads to spherical pores from trapped gas during solidification and to keyhole 
porosity (2), (24), (25) . Oxides at the surface of the melt pool and high reflectivity of aluminium 
can contribute to an increase in porosity and to the formation of other defects (16). 
With optimized parameters, densities above 99% were achieved ( 99,6% (11), 99,2% (16), 
> 99% (19) etc.). Nevertheless, different studies present different Ev as the optimal value, 
therefore Equation 1 might not be sufficient to predict the final porosity. The main parameters 
described in paragraph 1.1.1 should therefore be varied and studied independently. As an 
example, for a fixed Ev, different porosities are obtained when varying the layer thickness and the 
scanning speed, see Figure 2.

Microstructure originating from the LPBF manufacturing 
The microstructure of AlSi10Mg part made by LPBF can be ‘decomposed’ in several scales: 
• 
Melt pools (MPs) (~101-102 µm) 
• 
Columnar grains (~100-101 µm) 
• 
Al cells and eutectic network (resp. ~of 10-1-100 µm, ~101 nm-102 nm) 
The MPs are the results of the local melting of the powder bed. Their shape is directly linked to 
the gaussian energy distribution of the laser beam, see Figure 3.  
In the plane including BD and TD, the new MPs present a half cylindrical shape. When printing 
another layer, previous MPs partially remelt leading to a superposition of MPs. This superposition 
is sometimes described as a “fish scale” like microstructure, see Figure 4b, (11), (26). In the CS 
(plane perpendicular to BD), MPs form stripes in SD within each layer. These stripes can give an 
indication on the laser scanning pattern used for the printing, see Figure 4a. In the last plane, BD-
SD, the MPs shape is half cylinder elongated on one side.

In the MPs, columnar grains can be observed. These grains come from an epitaxial growth in the 
direction of the maximum thermal gradients. They therefore often initiate near the melt pool 
boundary (MPB) and extend towards the centre of the MP surface in the BD-TD or BD-SD planes 
(see Figure 5)  but appear equiaxed in CS (12), (16) .  
These MPs and columnar grains are composed of Al-cells. The cells usually have a size from few 
hundreds of nanometres to a few micrometres but, depending on their position in the MPs, they 
can be of different shapes and dimensions. Overall, the MPs can be divided into three zones that 
determine the cells characteristics: the remelted zone (RMZ), the heat affected zone (HAZ) and 
the fine structure zone (FSZ). In the RMZ which are at MPBs, the Al cells are coarser, and some 
dendrites can be observed due to partial re-melting of the previously solidified layer. Towards the 
MPs centre, the cells are finer and more equiaxed in the HAZ and even more so in the FSZ, see 
Figure 6. As an example, Thijs et al. (25) found a cell size of around 0.7 µm at the MPB and 0.4 µm 
in the FSZ. The very fine cellular microstructure at the FSZ originates from the high cooling rates 
which depend on the printing parameters (12), (16), (19), (21). Hyer et al. (19) showed that, by 
varying the laser power from 150W to 250W and its speed from 1200mm/s to 100mm/s, the cell 
size can go from 0.130µm to 0.719µm in the FSZ.

During the solidification of the MP, the α-Al cells are the first to solidify and compose the Al matrix. 
After the cells, the remaining liquid solidifies at their boundaries into a submicrometric (10–20 
nm (10)) fibrous eutectic network of Al-Si (12), (16), (21), (29), (30), see Figure 7. This coral-like 
network of fibrous Si and nanoscale silicon particles is not closed allowing epitaxial growth of the 
Al-matrix cells into columnar grains (29). 
From LPBF, very heterogeneous samples are obtained. This heterogeneity was already observed 
in the mechanical properties especially for the elongation to failure. Further heterogeneities can 
be observed along the BD. Indeed, because of a variation in thermal gradients between the top of 
the part and its bottom on the building plate, the average cell size varies as well as. These 
heterogeneities can be critical as anisotropy in mechanical properties is unavoidable. Controlling 
the base plate and atmosphere temperatures might allow to prevent these variations. 
Influence of printing parameters on microstructure 
In LPBF, both solidification and cooling rates are high in the MP as elevated temperatures 
are reached at its surface due to the laser beam while the surrounding material acts as a heat sink.  
Most articles present cooling rates between 103 and 106K/s (9), (10), (11) and some 
mention even higher values e.g. 108K/s (29). From a finite element model, Li et al. estimated 
cooling rates from 2 to 6x106K/s (with h and t =50µm, P =200W and v from 100 to 400mm/s) 
(31). Compared to cooling rates in castings which are from 100 to 102K/s and during which the Si 
can precipitate in a relatively coarse form (26), the high cooling rates in LPBF lead to solute 
trapping of Si in Al.

 Concerning the solidification rates, in the SD, they are often considered to be at the same 
order of magnitude as the laser scanning speed which can be easily up to 2m/s. Since, from 
experience for near eutectic AlSi alloys, a cellular structure is expected for solidification rates 
above 0.2m/s (28), LPBF AlSi10Mg should have an ultrafine metastable cellular microstructure.  
 
Tang et al (28). as well as Hyer et al. (19) used two equations (Equation 3, Equation 5)

(Equation 4) describing the change in temperature of a moving point source (33). The Rosenthal 
equation presents some limits as it allows only to get approximations of the cooling rates. Indeed, 
it considers the material properties temperature independent, excludes all heat transfers except 
from the conduction of the power input at the surface of the material and assimilates the heat 
source to a point source.

The high cooling rates leading to fine microstructure are also responsible for phases’ 
compositions away from the equilibrium conditions in both the Al cells and eutectic network. 
Figure 11 shows that the highest solubility of Si at equilibrium is reached at 577°C and is equal to 
1.4at%. This equilibrium solubility is only of a few ppm at room temperature (11). However, in 
the AlSi10Mg parts made by LPBF, the fast-cooling rates (106 to 108 °C/s) lead to Si solute trapping 
in the supersaturated Al-phase. 
Based on APT measurement, its content was evaluated at 2.2at% by Maeshima et al. (35) 
and 3.08 at% by Lefebvre (10). It is interesting to note that these studies present very similar 
cooling rates according to Equation 5 (resp 2.0 and 2.7x106 K/s) and therefore a closer Al-matrix 
composition would have been predicted. The lower solute content in the sample of Maeshima et 
al. is not expected when comparing the layer thicknesses used in the two studies. Indeed, the 
smaller layer thickness applied by Lefebvre et al. could have led to less solute trapping because of 
remelting or reheating of the cells (ie potential diffusion Si). Other parameters such as the location 
from which the Al-matrix was studied might explain these results. As an example, the different 
cooling rates expected between the MPBs or the MPs centres are likely responsible for a non 
negligeable variation in the solid solution of Si in Al matrix even within the same sample.  
Other articles also estimated the solid solution of Si but give even higher values such 4wt% 
or 8wt% based on Vegard's law applied to XRD peaks shift (36), (21). However, they do not take 
into account the peak shifts due to stresses. Overall, all the values seem to indicate that the solid 
solution of Si is at least twice the maximum of 1,4wt% at 577°C at equilibrium. 
The Mg content in the matrix was found by APT around 0.1at% and 0.14at% (resp (35) 
and (10)) which is lower than its solubility in Al at all temperatures and lower than the alloying 
content (between 0.28 and 0.50at%). Mg is therefore clearly segregated into the AlSi network. 
The presence of particles or precipitates in the matrix is debated in literature (10), (12), (21),  
(29). The reported disagreement can be due to differences in measurement methods, in the 
resolution and/or be related to different printing parameters. Si nanoparticles were observed by 
Alghamdi et al . (21), Albu et al (29) and Santos Macías et al. (34). They are near spheres of 5–10 
nm in diameter and also, in some samples, in rod-like or triangular shape of 20-30 nm (29), (34). 
Amorphous Si areas of about 200 nm and Si clusters of about 1–2 nm were identified as well by 
Albu et al.. Some particles also identified Mg2Si particles in the matrix (12).   
However, the presence of the Si nanoparticles observed by both Alghamdi et al., Albu et al. 
and Santos Macías et al. is not confirmed by Lefebvre et al. with APT measurements. Indeed, they 
did not detect any clear precipitation of either Si or Mg2Si particles in the matrix but only identified 
Si and Si-Mg clusters. This result led them to conclude that the cooling rates are likely fast enough 
to prevent long-range diffusion of solute elements and phase separation (10). As Maeshima et al. 
(35), they observed Mg clusters in the matrix. The analysis of the Mg neighbours revealed that 
they are mostly in Mg-Si co-clusters which should promote the formation of β-Mg2Si during ageing 
(35).  Lefebvre et al.  also identified Si clusters (twice as many as the co Si-Mg clusters) which were 
not distinguished by Maeshima et al. (according to Lefebvre et al., probably because of a low 
signal-to-noise ratio). Following the ternary phase diagram, these Si clusters should lead to the 
formation of diamond-Si particles when reaching equilibrium conditions upon ageing.  
From these different articles, it can be concluded that traces of Si or Mg2Si nanoparticles or 
clusters are found in the Al matrix which indicates some short-range diffusion during printing. It 
can be noted that the base plate temperature might have a large influence on this short-range 
diffusion and whether or not the Si particles are observed. Indeed, Santos Macías et al. (34) 
identified noticeably larger (but still on a nanometre scale) Si-rich precipitates with a base plate 
temperature of 200°C than with a temperature of 35°C, see Figure 12.

Concerning the fibrous eutectic network, Albu et al. (29) observed by scanning transmission

highly twinned crystalline Si particles. Using APT and high resolution-TEM, Lefebvre et al. also

Fe and Mg concentration are higher than in the matrix, see Figure 13. It is therefore not surprising 
that nanoparticles with Mg can form while only clusters were observed in the matrix by APT. 
Different cooling rates in the network might also have enabled short-range diffusion and rapid 
crystalline ordering at the nanometre scale. However, the Mg2Si “precipitates” in Lefebvre et al. 
study (10) correspond to regions with an Mg:Si ratio over 2 and an Al content of about 20 at.%. 
The authors consider them as compatible with a Mg2Si-type precipitate of a non-equilibrium 
composition (10). Overall, it appears that the temperature of the powder or the building plate and 
the scanning strategy (formation of more or less large HAZs) might be responsible for the 
formation of β-family particles or clusters (29).

Thermal treatment and microstructure evolution 
Thermal treatments 
Thermal post processing treatments of LPBF AlSi10Mg samples are mainly performed to relieve 
stresses, homogenise the microstructure and eventually reduce the porosity (12) . 
Several types of HT are often done on AM AlSi10Mg samples: 
• 
During annealing, the samples are exposed to temperatures between around 300°C and 
520°C, leading to the formation and coarsening of Si particles and therefore a decrease in 
hardness. 
• 
Solution HT is performed at temperatures around 530°C-535°C. The samples are usually 
quenched to maintain the solid solution which is responsible for a lower hardness and 
higher ductility.  
• 
For ageing (artificial), the samples are heat treated at low temperatures (100°C-200°C) for 
a long time in order to trigger precipitation hardening. The formation of Mg2Si is expected. 
Along with hardness, an increase in fatigue resistance was observed after ageing (38). 
==PAGE:22==
16 
Annealing behaviour of additively manufactured AlSi10Mg 
• 
T6 HTs gather both Solution HT followed by quenching and ageing. Large Si particles form 
as well as Mg2Si particles (12), (30). This was found to improve plasticity/ductility without 
a significant loss in the tensile/bending strength (21). 
• 
Other HT such as T4 (natural ageing) or T5 (stress relief treatment) are also often 
performed (21). 
 
These HTs are common also for cast AlSi10Mg parts, however the initial microstructure in LPBF 
parts leads to a different behaviour during these treatments. For example, contrarily to cast Al-Si-
Mg alloys, the ageing response highly depends on the duration of the solution HT (30). Machining 
was also found to have an influence on the fatigue resistance and can be coupled with HT to get 
rid of the roughness of the LPBF parts (38). 
Porosity evolution 
Most articles about the effects of HT on the microstructure do not mention any variation in 
porosity or conclude that there is almost no effect (11). The highest increase in porosity found in 
literature is around 13% from 2% in AP to 2.2% after a T6 HT (39). 
Microstructure evolution 
The evolution of the microstructure during HT is dominated by the formation and coarsening of 
Si particles. Three steps are often identified:  fragmentation or degradation, spheroidization and 
coarsening. 
Using in-situ annealing with a heating rate of 5°C/min, Albu et al. (29) observed these three 
mechanisms, see Figure 17. Si particles at the network start to coarsen at 200°C while the 
microstructure is relatively stable until 260°C after which the degradation becomes clear. By 
maintaining the sample at 280°C for 2h, Chen et al (11) obtained discrete Al–Si eutectic particles 
with size of ~200 nm which is relatively close to what can be seen in Figure 17 at the same 
temperature (but with a shorter time). They assume that these particles are due to precipitation 
of Si from the supersaturated matrix at the cell boundaries. Figure 17 indicates that it is only at 
320°C that the network is completely degraded and broken up.

When following the degradation of the Si network for an isothermal HT at 520°C , the network 
appears fragmented after one hour. Alboukhair and al. (30) assume that the difference in 
expansion of the α-Al phase and the Si phase (with resp. coefficients of thermal expansion of 
26.1x10-6/K and 2.6x10-6/K), leads to brittle cracking of the Si. 
The spheroidization happens second. It is induced by surface self-diffusion and Al-Si 
interdiffusion, two time and temperature-dependent phenomena (21), (29).  
Finally, the Si particles coarsen.  At 520°C (after 2 hours), no traces are left from the initial 
microstructure (11), (30) and Si particles reach sizes from 1 μm to 10 μm “due to Ostwald 
ripening”  (11), (21) . The particle size is stable after 3-4hours with an average of 1.40 and 1.42 
µm, respectively (30). They are faceted with sharp corners and edges as the eutectic-Si is diamond 
cubic which preferentially grows in (111) directions (21).  
It can be noted that the Si particles have a bimodal size distribution during the spheroidization 
step; coarse particles originate from the network meanwhile fine particles are from fine 
precipitates (29) and/or Si nanoparticles in the matrix (21).  
From Albu et al. in situ HT see Figure 18, the nanoparticles initially appear fully coherent 
in order to reduce the interfacial energy. Up to 280°C, they grow and become partially coherent 
to have a lower lattice strain. At 300°C, the sample bends leading to higher dislocation density and 
allowed pipe diffusion of the atoms. The nanoparticles dissolve in the matrix to coarsen Si eutectic 
particles. It is important to mention that the authors highlight the potential influence of the sample 
thickness (TEM sample) on the activation energy for Si diffusion as it is only 0.81 eV for Al thin 
films against 1.36 eV for wrought Al with a lower dislocation density. To ensure the validity of 
these results, 3D measurements are essential.

Principle 
Ion-solid interactions between an ion beam and the sample allow to perform milling of the sample, 
imaging, and depositing of atom layers. All three result from similar mechanisms but using 
different gas precursors and ion beam current (40). 
 
When ions from the ion beam (often ion of Ga, Ga+) hit the target material, the inelastic 
interactions can lead to the emission of phonons, plasmons or secondary electrons (SE). If these 
primary ions have a kinetic energy sufficient to overcome the sample surface binding energy, they 
can sputter surface atoms. These atoms ejected from the specimen can be ionized as secondary 
ions (SI). These emissions, and in particular the SE and SI, can be used for imaging.  
 
By increasing the current, more sputtering occurs. The milling process is based on this 
sputtering and can be performed by targeting a specific zone with a high current ion beam to 
obtain a localized sputtering, see Figure 23. 
 
The deposition of external atoms layers relies on chemical vapor deposition (CVD), see 
Figure 23. The precursors for deposition, such as tungsten hexacarbonyl (W(CO)6) or 
(methylcyclopentadienyl)trimethyl platinum (CH3)3(CH3C5H4)Pt, include both volatile and non-
volatile components. The volatile component allows the precursor to reach the specimen surface 
and to be adsorbed. Once adsorbed, the precursor must decompose rapidly under the ion beam 
(faster than it can be ejected) into its volatile and its non-volatile components. The volatile 
component escapes from the surface while the non-volatile component chemically bonds to the 
surface (chemisorption). To avoid sputtering, a lower current than in milling is applied (40). 
During milling, this adsorption phenomenon is also observed but not wanted as it contaminates 
the sample. The milling precursor should therefore be very volatile.

3.6 Characterization by synchrotron 
Synchrotrons produce very bright and high energy X-ray beams allowing to study the samples at 
the nanoscale level (41). The high energy of the X-ray beam and its brightness allow respectively 
to obtain information from deeper in the material and to have a small bright spot leading to more 
detailed results in a shorter time period. Many different characterization techniques are available 
such as tomography, microdiffraction, X-ray scattering, phase contrast imaging, X-ray absorption 
etc. (42). 
 
Figure 33: Synchrotron at the Argonne national laboratory, Advanced photon source. The electron Gun, linear accelerator, booster are 
found in the center of storage ring from which 35 beam lines allow different types of measurements for different types of samples (42) 
3.6.1 
Principle 
A synchrotron is composed of 5 main components: the electron gun, the linear accelerator, the 
booster, the storage ring and the beam lines, see Figure 33. In the electron gun, electrons are 
emitted by thermionic emission. The cathode is heated under vacuum until its thermal energy is 
above the minimum required to extract an electron from the material surface. The electron is then 
accelerated from the cathode to the anode in the linear accelerator. The very high voltage between 
==PAGE:34==
28 
Annealing behaviour of additively manufactured AlSi10Mg 
the electrodes increases the electrons’ speed almost until the speed of light, reaching an energy of 
a hundred million electron volts. 
The accelerated electrons are injected into the booster of the synchrotron. In the booster, they are 
further accelerated in straight lines by a radio frequency voltage source. In curves, a magnetic field 
increases with the electron energy to maintain a fix curve radius. The few GeV electrons are then 
sent to the storage ring. At the Advanced Photon Source (APS) in the USA, the maximum energy is 
7 GeV (41). 
The storage ring consists of straight and curved sections almost forming a circle. In the curves, the 
bending of electrons’ trajectory by Lorentz Force in the magnetic fields, make them decelerate. 
Their energy loss takes the form of a continuous light band potentially from infrared to X rays 
depending on the tightness of the curve. This light is directed towards the beamline where various 
optics will select on certain wavelength and focus the light directed onto the sample (42), (43). 
3.6.2 
Microdiffraction 
Samples from this study were analysed at the beam line 34-ID-E at the APS, see Figure 33. This 
beam line allows to perform microdiffraction measurements and ,with the differential-aperture 
X-ray microscopy (DAXM), it is possible to measure the microstructure, grain orientation and 
stress/strain with submicrometer resolution in three dimensions (44). 
A polychromatic microbeam is focused onto the surface of the sample at a 45° angle. Keeping this 
same surface illuminated, a platinum wire with a knife-edge is moved parallel to the surface at a 
distance of 0.3 mm. The differential aperture is obtained by moving the wire; at each position, the 
knife-edge hides specific reflections. To record the diffracted signals, a CCD detector is mounted 
parallel to the beam direction. The analysis of intensity variations in the reconstructed Laue 
pattern at each wire position allows to trace back the sample “voxels” responsible for each 
reflection and to obtain depth information, see Figure 34.

3.6.5 
Data analysis procedure 
From the microdiffraction data, the aim was to study eventual orientation relationships between 
Si particles and Al grains. To do so, the number of indexed peaks and the Euler angles for each Al 
phase or Si phase voxel were analysed. This data was processed by adapting an already made 
MATLAB code allowing to identify grains. This code can be decomposed into three main parts: 
loading the data, identifying the grains, and exporting the data.  
The data is first loaded, sorted and reshaped. The imported matrix containing the Euler 
angles is rotated to correspond to the sample coordinates while the number of voxels in x, y and z 
directions are calculated (from the know step size and the maximum and minimum coordinate 
values in x,y,z obtained from microdiffraction). The Euler angles can then be added, along with the 
==PAGE:36==
30 
Annealing behaviour of additively manufactured AlSi10Mg 
number of indexed peaks, in the fourth direction of a 4D matrix containing the x, y, z indexes of 
each voxel as the three first directions.  
From this matrix the grains are detected using a function “detectgrains3” which takes the 
Euler angles of each voxel, the minimum number of voxels and the selected minimum grain 
boundary misorientation as arguments. The output takes the form of a list containing the number 
of the grain identified for each voxel and must be reshaped into a 3D matrix with the grain number 
of each x,y,z voxel. From the reshaped matrix, the function “regionprops3” allows to obtain the 
number of voxels in each grain and therefore to filter out the grains smaller than the minimum 
size defined.  
Finally, the IPF colors are created and the matrix containing the grain information on each 
voxel can be exported (.h5 and .xdmf). The software Paraview was then utilized to visualize the 
final grains and their properties in the 3D volume. 
This code was adapted to the specific case of Al grains and Si particles. One of the 
challenges was to separate the two phases as Al and Si present similar diffraction patterns and 
therefore a lot of voxels were indexed as both Al and Si phase (Figure 36). As the Si phase is 
expected to present more peaks, the solution found was to use the number of indexed peaks (see 
Figure 36c/d). Using a simple binary threshold such as keeping only the voxels with a minimum 
number of indexed peaks to obtain the Si phase was not the correct approach. Indeed, it prevented 
from keeping the parts of the particles near boundaries etc. as the Si particles are composed at 
their centre of a few voxels with a high number of indexed peaks which are surrounded by voxels 
with less many indexed peaks. Therefore, the alternative was to keep only the identified Si 
“particles” presenting at least one voxel with more than 15 indexed peaks. In the version of the 
code dedicated to treat the Si phase, a simple loop to filter out the Si “particles” not respecting this 
condition was added to the initial code.  
With the code for the Si phase, the Si indexed voxel data is loaded, grains are found (by 
detectgrains3) for a minimum grain boundary misorientation of 0.1° and filtered out if they are 
smaller than 3 voxels or do not present a voxel with more than 15 indexed peaks. The results are 
exported in .h5, .xdmf files but also, thanks to another added part, as .txt. This .txt file contains a 
matrix with Si particles’ voxel position and the associated Euler angles. 
The exported .txt file is imported in another version of the code to process the Al phase data. The 
import is necessary as it allows to transform all the Si particles voxels into non identified voxels 
for Al phase matrix. Voxel of Si misindexed as Al will therefore not be considered for the Al grains. 
Following a similar procedure as Si, Al grains are found (with a minimum grain boundary of 2°) 
and filtered but, this time, only by their size which should be above 20 pixels. These Al grains are 
also exported as .h5 and .xdmf files.  
When all Al grains and Si particles are identified, the grain boundaries misorientation angles 
between the two phases can be evaluated. For each Si particle, a neighbouring Al grain was 
considered to be an Al grain at a maximum radial distance of 3.75µm (ie  5 pixels) from the edge 
of the particle. The grain boundary misorientation between Al and Si was then calculated between 
the two closest voxels of the grain and the particle. This misorientation was obtained by 
transforming the Euler angles into orientations with the function “orientation.byEuler” and, from 
these orientations, calculating the misorientation with the function “angle”. To ensure that this 
grain boundary misorientation angle is representative, internal misorientation angles were 
calculated in Al grains by using these same functions (orientation.byEuler, angle). The results from 
this code are found in a table gathering the Si particles associated with all their neighbouring Al 
grains, the grain boundary misorientation between the phases, size and internal misorientation 
of the Al grains.

3.7 Characterization by atom probe tomography 
3.7.1 
Principle 
The first step of APT is the controlled field evaporation of atoms from the sample surface. 
The sample, in needle shape with a very sharp tip (in the order of 101 nm), is cooled down before 
being put under a biased voltage (3-15 kV). This leads to a very high electrostatic field (tens V/nm) 
which, when associated with laser or HV pulsing, causes the atoms’ ionization. These ions are 
accelerated by the electrostatic force and projected onto a Position Sensitive Detector (PSD), see 
Figure 37. 
The second step is the detection and position determination. The detector records the time 
of flight, which allows to determine the mass over charge ratio, and the positions of impact of the 
ionized atoms, which enables to reconstruct the original position of the atoms on the tip (46), (47). 
The last step consists in the data reconstruction and analysis: the information about the 
detected ions' positions, masses, and arrival times are be processed to obtain the chemical 
composition and spatial distribution of the atoms within the sample.

ratio. The known peak positions available in a database of the isotopes of expected elements (Al, 
Si, Mg, Fe, Cu etc.), along with the different possible oxides or particles (Al2O, MgO, SiO2 etc) were 
compared with the peaks obtained. The vast majority of the peaks were identified and associated 
with their ion.  
Finally, the volume was reconstructed, and composition profiles plotted. By changing the 
isovalues (concentration threshold) of the elements, it was possible to visualize the phases and 
eventual clusters.

4.1.3 
Residual stresses 
The XRD measurements confirmed the presence of the Al phase and the Si phase in both samples 
A_AP and D_AP while no other phases/precipitates were identified, see Figure 45. The lattice 
parameters were quantified as described in paragraph 3.4 (with coefficients of determination R²> 
0.999998). They are presented in Table 12  along with the values for pure phases and the expected 
values for stress free phases with the compositions found from APT.  
When comparing the two samples’ Al matrix lattice parameters, a difference of 0,0074Å is 
observed (Table 12). Such a difference can originate from different solidification and cooling rates 
in the printed layer or from different thermal cycles caused by the printing of the following layers.  
 
For a same base plate temperature, cooling rates are expected to be slightly higher in the 
A_AP sample compared to the D_AP sample (Table 5)  which could lead to a small increase in solid 
solution in A_AP sample Al matrix but also more residual stresses. A higher compressive strain 
could explain the smaller lattice parameter of the A_AP sample.  
The power and speed, considered in the cooling rate determination, are not the only 
parameters that are different between the two samples. The layer thickness and hatch spacing 
which appear in the Ev might also affect the lattice parameter. As the A_AP sample has a higher Ev, 
the layers should tend to be more remelted and reheated during the process. The thermal cycles 
could therefore relax stresses and allow diffusion of Si. However, this diffusion is probably too 
short-range to create any noticeable variations in lattice parameter. As for the relaxation of 
stresses, it should lead to an increase in lattice parameter (as shown later) meanwhile the A_AP 
sample has a smaller lattice parameter than D_AP sample.  
It can thus be concluded that it is likely that the difference between the two samples is 
mostly related to higher compressive strains due to higher cooling rates in A_AP.

Texture 
The pole figures obtained from the EBSD measurements show that the two samples present a Goss 
texture (52), with a strong <100> orientation along the z axis which corresponds to the BD, a 
weaker <110> orientation along the y axis but also an even weaker <110> orientation along the x 
axis (Figure 47). From optical micrographs (Figure 39), it was observed that the SD might have 
been rotated. However, as more MPs appear in the shape characteristic of the BD-TD plane, in 
particular for A_AP sample, the x axis was associated to the TD and the y axis to the SD for both 
samples, see Figure 48. 
For the EBSD maps, fine equiaxed grains appear at MPB while grains become coarser towards the 
MP centre before being replaced by the columnar grains, Figure 48. The equiaxed grains do not 
present the same <100> orientation along the BD or <110> along the SD as the columnar grains, 
on the contrary, their orientation seems random. These columnar grains also appear to cross 
MPBs, especially on A_AP where the MP superposition prevents from identifying as clearly the 
MPBs as in D_AP. 
The (100) pole figures presented are very similar to what was obtained by Dong et al (37) for all 
their samples, (110) and (111) pole figures cannot be compared as they are not presented in their 
article. Even though D_AP and A_AP samples present higher indexes than what was found by Dong 
et al., a similar trend is observed: increasing the Ev leads to a stronger texture. Indeed, the A_AP 
sample presents a max index equal to 3.51 with Ev= 50 J/mm3 comparing to resp 3.14 and 34 J/mm3 
for D_AP sample. This might be explained by the equiaxed grains (with random orientation) that 
occupy larger zones in the larger D_AP sample MPs than in A_AP sample.

For the Al matrix and Si phase (that changed from fibrous structure to Si particles), the XRD 
showed non negligeable variations in peak position (Figure 58, Table 15 ) and in peak broadening 
(Figure 59). The changes in peaks position are likely due to two competing phenomena: the 
relaxation of macro-stresses and the diffusion of Si from the supersaturated matrix to form 
particles.  
 
The lattice parameter for the Al matrix increases between the AP samples and the HT5 min 
samples. These changes are likely mostly due to stress relaxation as diffusion is often slower than 
stress relaxation. In 5 minutes, the diffusion of Si in solid solution to coarsen Si particles should 
not lead to change in lattice parameter of 0.0090 Å in A sample and especially in an increase since, 
according to the Vergard’s law, decrease in solid solution should lead to a decrease in lattice 
parameter. The stress relaxation observed can be caused by the degradation of the AlSi network. 
In AP samples, the network exercises compressive stresses on the Al cells and therefore its 
degradation leads to the reduction of compressive strains and an increase in lattice parameter in 
D_HT5 and A_HT5 samples.  
 
The Al matrix lattice parameter decreases between the HT5 samples and HT120 samples. 
The diffusion of solid solution Si is, this time, probably responsible for the decrease.  
The Al matrix lattice parameters of the two samples are very close to each other after HT for 120 
minutes. Between A_HT120 and D_HT120 lattice parameters, the difference is of only 0.0007 Å 
against 0.0074 Å for AP samples.

decreases from D_AP to D_HT5 and from D_HT5 to D_120 but increases slightly from A_AP to

expected pure Si phase lattice parameter 5.4310 Å by 0.0089 Å. As the silicon particles are not 
expected to present solid solution of any other elements this difference the change in lattice

Si phase. Indeed, as the balance in the volume should be equal to zero, tensile strains should have 
been found in the Si. If the idea that the Si particles should be pure Si is reconsidered, the peak

5. Conclusion 
The microstructure and residual stresses of two AlSi10Mg samples manufactured by LPBF with 
different parameters were investigated before and after annealing for 5 minutes or 2 hours at 
520°C.  
1. The microstructures observed in the AP samples correspond to what is expected from the 
LPBF process; MPs contain equiaxed grains at their boundaries and columnar grains towards 
the MP centres. Comparing the two samples studied showed that despite its higher volumetric 
energy density, shallower MPs were formed in the A_AP sample compared to D_AP sample (42 
µm against 59µm). This can be explained by the overlapping of the MPs caused by thinner 
powder layers in A_AP. In the grains, small Al cells with low misorientation angles are 
separated together with an AlSi eutectic network. In the D_AP sample, the eutectic network 
width was found to be in a range of 50 to 500nm varying, like the cell size, as a function of the 
position in the MP, RMZ, HAZ or FSZ. The smallest average size of 0.55µm was found at the 
FSZ. This size is not in agreement with the 0.45µm average value found for the same cooling 
rates by both measurements in the work by Hyer et al’s (19) and from an empirical equation 
(Equation 3). The 0.10µm difference might be an example where the increase in laser power, 
from 250W to 650W (resp for Hyer et al. and D_AP samples), actually has more influence on 
the cooling rates than the corresponding increase in laser speed (from 710 to 1850 mm/s). 
This suggests that the Rosenthal equation might not be completely accurate.  
 
2. In the eutectic phase, APT at a MPB revealed that the Si contains 9.4at% Al and 1.9at% Mg 
with a depletion at its boundary, while the Si and Mg contents in solid solution in Al are only 
of 0.4% and 0.1%. This is likely due to the partition coefficients.  
 
3. Upon HT, the eutectic network breaks down to form small Si particles (typically less than 
0.15µm) after 5 minutes at 520°C which coarsen to reach an average of 0.7 µm in DTI and 
AMEXCI samples after 2h of HT at 520°C. The HT showed that samples printed with different 
parameters behave similarly during annealing and lead to similar average Si particles sizes. 
After the 2 hours HT, the particle size distribution is broad. Particles at the grain boundaries 
are found to be larger than inside the grains, probably because the AlSi network is coarser at 
the grain boundaries and because they act as fast diffusion channels. 
 
4. The analysis of synchrotron microdiffraction data showed that there is no clear orientation 
relationship between Si particles and Al grains in the HT samples. 
 
5. The AP samples present a Goss structure with the <100> orientation parallel to the BD and 
the <110> orientation parallel to the TD. The A_AP sample has a slightly stronger texture than 
the D_AP sample, the same conclusion was reported by Dong et al (37); a higher volumetric 
energy density leads to stronger textures. However, this could also be related to the deeper 
MP in D_AP sample which display more equiaxed grains of random orientation. After HT, no 
recrystallization is observed, and the printed texture is maintained even though some grain 
coarsening is seen at the MPBs.  
 
6. From the solid solution obtained by APT in this, and other works, coupled with XRD peak 
shifts, stresses were estimated as compressive in Al phase and tensile in Si phase in the order 
of 10-3 for the AP samples. For the first 5 minutes of HT, a relief of residual stresses is likely 
responsible for the observed increase in the Al matrix lattice parameter between the AP 
samples and the HT5 samples. Further HT allows the diffusion of Si, initially in solid solution,

out of the matrix and causes the decrease of the lattice parameter. Unlike for the Al phase, the 
lattice parameters of the Si phase in the D and A samples studied didn’t vary the same way 
during HT. However, both samples presented very similar lattice parameters for each phase 
after 2 hours HT at 520°C. Compressive strains were found locally in both phases from XRD 
data while synchrotron energy scans indicate in these HT samples 10-4 compressive stresses 
in two Si particles studied and 10-4 tensile strains in a Al neighbouring grain. 
 
7. The reduction of dislocations, and the uniformization of the phases’ composition along with 
the formation of larger Si particles (despite the presence of twins) upon HT induced a decrease 
in peak broadening for both the Si and Al phase.

6. Outlook 
Further analysis and measurement would allow to understand better four main aspects: 
 
The influence of the printing parameters 
 
The phases composition evolution 
 
Residual and thermal stressed in the sample 
 
The development of Si particles 
 
A first step to understand better the influence of printing parameters on the microstructure would 
be to measure the cell and network dimensions in the A_AP sample and compare them with the 
D_AP sample. The comparison with cell size estimated from Equation 3, using the cooling rates 
calculated by the Rosenthal equation derivative, Equation 5, might also allow to validate if 
Rosenthal equation is applicable for high printing power and speed. More statistical cell size 
measurements than done in the present work, possibly in combination with grain orientations, on 
both A_AP and D_AP would be required.  
To further advance the comparison between the two samples, several APT measurements on both 
AP samples in the Al matrix and the eutectic network would allow to correlate, or not, the solute 
content and cooling rates in D_AP and A_AP. It is expected that higher cooling rates would lead to 
more solute trapping and therefore, there should be more solid solution of Si in Al in A_AP sample 
than D_AP. In each sample, the different cooling rates should also lead to higher Si content at the 
centre of the MP compared to the boundary. More APT measurements for the 5 minutes and 2 
hours heat treated samples (at least on D samples), would be valuable to understand the diffusion 
of Si from the Al matrix and gain more knowledge on Si particles purity and Al matrix Si and Mg 
solid solution content.  
 
A better understanding of the solid solution of Si in Al and ensuring a more accurate and statistical 
estimation of the phases ’composition than done in the present work, would allow a more precise 
analysis of the XRD peaks shifts. The XRD measurements could be performed a second time for all 
samples to ensure the validity of the ones determined in this work, and their analysis with precise 
phases composition on all samples would provide values of residual stresses in AP samples and 
thermal stresses in HT samples. Strains could also be measured again by synchrotron energy scans 
after the synchrotron upgrade where the as printed samples may also be characterized. 
 
Concerning the analysis of Si phase, in order to analyse more closely the Si network degradation 
and Si particles coarsening, in-situ measurements at a lower temperature such as 350°C would 
slow down the different mechanisms (compared to 520°C) and provide more detailed 
information.  
HT for 12h at 350°C was already performed for the A sample (Figure 66) and revealed very slow 
coarsening giving a good indication that this temperature could be used for further studies of the 
coarsening mechanisms. A preliminary study using synchrotron nano-tomography at the 
Brookhaven National Laboratory NLSL II during in situ annealing at 520°C showed that it was 
possible to retrieve the two phases after some time despite their low contrast, see Figure 67.  
 
From these measurements, the evolution of Si particles and also their spatial distribution could 
be analysed. The particles distribution in the sample after different HT times can also be realized 
by X-ray computed tomography but previous work showed that the low contrast between Al Si 
remain an important challenge. On the contrary, EBSD characterization coupled with EDS allows 
to clearly identify the Si phase, but only in 2D.

Nanotomography principle 
Nanotomography is based on X-ray computed tomography using the transmission of the hard X-
rays through the sample to study its microstructure in 3D. The needle-like sample (20-30µm 
diameter) is rotated in order to acquire images from all around the sample. At each rotation, a 
photon flux I0(E) is sent on the sample and partially absorbed or scattered following Beer-
Lambert’s law:

Where µ, the linear absorption coefficient, depends on the material properties (density ρ, atomic 
number Z), but also the rays’ energy E. Variations in the composition and microstructure in the 
section (from 0 to s) will lead to a change in the transmitted flux I(E). This flux is converted by the 
detector in electrical signal giving one projection. Using all the projections, the 3D volume is 
reconstructed (53).
