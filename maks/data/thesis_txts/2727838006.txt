Abstract 
Piezoelectric materials can be used in several different areas such as capacitors, transducers, actuators, 
memory storage devices and so on. Such a wide range of usability results in a huge and growing market 
for piezoelectric materials. However, due to their high performance, the most common piezoelectric 
materials are PbTiO3 and PbZrO3, which contain toxic element, lead, which threats the worker’s health 
and causes problems in recycling and disposal of the materials. Therefore, EU restricts the use of PZTs 
and steers the focus towards lead-free piezoelectric materials. To increase the performance of lead-free 
piezoelectric materials, different approaches have been tried and one of them is the synthesizing them 
in high aspect ratio needle-like morphology which allows their domains to be aligned and textured so 
that when an electric field or mechanical stress is applied along the aligned direction, their piezoelectric 
response is enhanced. In this work, different methods, hydrothermal synthesis and molten salt synthesis, 
have been studied for synthesis of needle-like BaTiO3 particles. Moreover, upscaling of the processes 
is studied for checking compatibility for mass production. With both synthesis methods, high aspect 
ratio BaTiO3 needles are synthesized and SEM, EDS, XRD, and TEM characterization techniques are 
done to analyze morphology, crystallinity, growth direction and phase purity of the particles. Along the 
way, different process parameters such as temperature, pressure, milling conditions, mixing methods, 
salt-to-reactant ratio, pH of the mixture, washing procedure etc. are examined and their effect on the 
end product, needle-like BaTiO3, are discussed.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Background and aim of the work 
The piezoelectric effect refers to generation of electrical charges on the surface of non-conducting 
dielectric material as a response to an applied mechanical stress, or conversely, generation of strain in 
the material when an electrical field is applied. Piezoelectric materials can be used in different 
applications from high sensitivity sensors, actuators, transducers, memory storage devices, capacitors 
to electric generators. Lead oxides such as PbTiO3 and PbZrO3 are the most used piezoelectric materials 
due to their high performance such as having high dielectric constants (almost double of the 
conventional lead-free competitors) and high Curie temperature [1]. PZTs contain more than 60%wt 
lead, which is a highly toxic material that makes the handling, disposal and synthesis of them harmful 
for both the workers’ health, soil and water environment. Piezoelectric materials and devices market is 
USD20$B annually, which corresponds around 2500 tons of PZT production that is equivalent to usage 
of 1600 tons of elemental lead [2]. Therefore, European Union restricts the use of leaded piezoelectric 
devices which accelerates the synthesis and improvements of environmentally friendly lead-free 
piezoelectric materials such as BaTiO3 and K0.5Na0.5NbO3. One of the most impactful improvements is 
the morphology control of synthesized piezoelectric particles. For example, synthesis of piezoelectric 
materials in needle-like morphology allows one to align them according to needles’ growth orientation 
using tape-casting, robocasting and extrusion techniques [3]. Then, by templated grain growth (TGG) 
method, aligned needles’ grains grow by the Ostwald ripening procedure within the matrix so that when 
electric field or mechanical stress is applied along the direction, highest piezoelectric response is 
obtained. 
The aim of the work was to investigate several different methods for synthesis of needle-like BaTiO3, 
characterization of the synthesized products via SEM, XRD and TEM for investigation of crystallinity, 
aspect ratio, phase purity and growth direction along the needle. Additionally, the route for scaling up 
the two-step molten salt synthesis of K0.5Na0.5NbO3 particles by keeping the needle-like morphology 
and the phase purity was investigated. 
1.2 Introduction to the techniques for synthesizing needle-like 
BaTiO3 
1.2.1 Hydrothermal synthesis 
Hydrothermal synthesis is a process which allows utilization of single or heterogeneous phase reactions 
in aqueous media, in general water. Reaction occurs at elevated temperatures and pressures, for mild 
conditions up to 350°C and 100MPa, to crystallize anhydrous ceramic materials directly from the 
mixture. Several different additives can be used other than the precursors for promotion of solubility of 
the precursors. One of the most common additives is called mineralizers. They can be inorganic or 
organic, used to control the pH of the mixture for morphology guidance or can be used excessively to 
increase the solubility of reactants [4,5]. Nowadays with the improvements of manufacturing 
technology of autoclaves, hydrothermal synthesis can be done over 1000°C and 500MPa pressure [4]. 
However, energy consumption during the reaction, maintenance costs and reliability of the vessels are 
the struggles needed to be overcome for such applications to be used in industry. 
==PAGE:8==
2 
 
On the other hand, several chemical systems in the form of 0D, 1D, 2D and bulk can be synthesized in 
mild hydrothermal conditions with low or no maintenance cost, low energy consumption (compared to 
other methods) and high reliability and repeatability [6]. Examples include nano particles – needlelike 
– nanotubes of BaTiO3 [7-12], nanowires of KNbO3 [13,14], nanobelts of LiNbO3 [15], nanotubes and 
needle-like PbTiO3 [16-17], needle-like and nanoparticles of Na1-xKxNbO3 [18,19], nanorods of 
Na0.5Bi0.5TiO3 [20], nanowires ofNaNbO3 [21], single crystal bulk quartz [22], zeolites [23], CNTs [24] 
and diamonds [25] and so on. 
One can see the basic flow chart of the hydrothermal synthesis process, see Figure (1) above. First, 
precursors and mineralizers are weighed stoichiometrically and then transferred into the solvent. 
Afterwards, soluble precursors are dissolved in the solvent and non-soluble precursors (such as 
templates) are dispersed via different methods such as stirring or ultrasonification.  Then the mixture is 
transferred into the inliner, and autoclave is closed tightly to withstand the elevated pressures. 
Afterwards, at elevated temperature and pressure values precursors crystallize with the reaction 
controlled by thermodynamics and/or kinetics. In the final step, products are washed with DI water, 
ethanol and diluted acid for removal of the residual precursors or formed intermediate metastable 
byproducts.  
For very complex chemical systems Edisonian trial and error methods are used for the design of 
processes [4]. However, with the recent developments, hydrothermal processes were enabled to be 
designed thermodynamically favored to form the desired products after kinetics limitations are resolved. 
Such thermodynamical methods can perform many functions for example effects of concentration, 
temperature and pressure can be explored over the interested phase of the product, effect of different 
types of precursor can be compared for given temperature and concentration values so that for industrial 
applications lower energy consumption and hence cost effectiveness can be achieved [5]. Additionally, 
as phase spaces are defined well, reaction conditions can be further explored for the desired morphology 
of the material such as nano rods, sheet-like, nano particles or single crystal bulk. Determination of the 
equilibrium concentrations and calculations of stability and yield diagrams are done via the OLI 
software which is based on thermodynamic model that combines Helgeson-Kirkham-Flowers-Tanger 
(HKFT) equation of state for standard-state properties of aqueous species with a non-ideal solution 
model based on the activity coefficient expressions developed by Bromley and Pitzer [5].

The scalability, cost effectiveness, usability of wide range of precursors and being able to control the 
morphology of the products are the key advantages of the hydrothermal process. However, estimating 
the formation of each intermediate product which drives the abovementioned thermodynamical model 
and the determination of the kinetics of reaction is challenging, making it practically easy, but 
theoretically hard to synthesize with hydrothermal synthesis.  
1.2.2 Molten salt synthesis 
One of the other common, robust and reliable anisometric ceramic particles synthesis techniques is the 
molten salt synthesis. In this method, precursors are mixed with a low melting point salt, which acts as 
the solvent when it is melted. Compared to the similar synthesis technique, solid state method, molten 
salt brings advantages such as increased reaction kinetic rate, decrease of reaction temperature, better 
control of crystallite size and the product morphology and increase of the precursors homogeneity 
[26,27]. In this method, precursors dissolve inside the molten salt and ions of the precursors have much 
higher mobility compared to other conventional methods which increases the reaction rate and 
homogeneity of the solution so that stoichiometry is preserved and purity of the product is increased 
[28]. 
The ratio of salt plays a huge role in controlling the morphology of the synthesized particles. 
Statistically, higher salt ratio will decrease the amount of nucleation points of the crystals as salt melts 
since reactants are further apart. This allows crystals to grow in larger sizes and depending on the crystal 
structure, aspect ratio of the synthesized particles can also be tuned by changing the salt to oxide ratio 
[28]. 
Temperature is another parameter that affects the aspect ratio of synthesized particles in molten salt 
synthesis. As a rule of thumb higher temperatures results in higher aspect ratio of the synthesized 
particles since the diffusion rate of ions are increased inside the molten flux and lowest energy facet 
grows faster. A study shows evolution of aspect ratio of plate-like SrTiO3 particles with temperature. It 
is shown that as reaction occurs at 1050°C synthesized particles are at 1 aspect ratio with cube-like 
morphology while at 1250°C aspect ratio is found as 20 with elongated platelet morphology [28]. 
However, drastic increase in the reaction temperature also results in evaporation of sodium or potassium 
depending on the type of flux and hence the solubility of the oxides which affects purity of the product 
and the morphology. 
Type of salt is also important parameter since it acts as the solvent when it is melt. Precursors should 
have a similar dissolution rate in the salt so that stoichiometry is preserved. Additionally, if lower 
reaction temperatures are required, eutectic point of salts can be utilized [27,28]. For example, mixing 
1:1 molar ratio of NaCl and KCl decreases the melting point to 657°C, from 801°C and 770°C 
respectively [29].

1.3 Piezoelectric theory 
1.3.1 Properties of piezoelectric materials 
The piezoelectric effect refers to generation of electrical charges, on the surface of non-conducting 
dielectric material as a response to an applied mechanical stress, or conversely, generation of strain in 
the material when an electrical field is applied as illustrated in Figure (3) below. The former is referred 
to as direct piezoelectric effect while the latter refers to converse piezoelectric effect.  
Piezoelectricity can be seen as a linear electromechanical interaction of mechanical and electrical states. 
Hence the piezoelectric equations can be written as;

Sij = 𝑑𝑘𝑖𝑗
∗ . Ek  (2) 
Where Dx is the electric displacement, Sxy is the strain component, Txy is the stress component and 
𝑑𝑘𝑖𝑗 and 𝑑𝑘𝑖𝑗
∗ are the piezoelectric charge or strain constant, respectively. Both 𝑑𝑘𝑖𝑗 and 𝑑𝑘𝑖𝑗
∗ are called 
piezoelectric constants and numerical values of them are the same but with different units pC/N and 
pm/V respectively, since these coefficients connect the electric field and strain which are equal to those 
connecting the stress and polarization. Additionally, as convention they are generally expressed in Voigt 
matrix notation, dkm and d*km where k represents the displacement D or field E in Cartesian reference 
system, and m denotes the stresses (in the case of normal stresses, x1, x2, x3; in the case of shear stresses 
x4, x5, x6 for T23, T13 and T12 respectively) [30,31]. 
One can see the relationship between the classes of materials from Figure (3) above. Dielectric materials 
are insulator materials which can be polarized by for example placing them in between the plates of 
capacitor. Piezoelectric materials on the other hand, is a subgroup of dielectric materials which when a 
stress applied polarization occurs. Pyroelectrics are materials which generate voltage with changing 
temperature. Ferroelectric materials are a subgroup of piezoelectric materials which show spontaneous 
electric polarization that can be reversed by application of external electric field. Crystal symmetry of 
ferroelectric materials governs the mentioned spontaneous electric polarization. In total there are 32 
crystallographic point groups, 20 of them show piezoelectric effect and 10 of them shows ferroelectric 
effect [31].

While direction of dipoles cannot be changed for all piezoelectric and pyroelectric materials, for 
ferroelectric materials it can be reversed by external electric field. This characteristic of ferroelectric 
materials allows reversion of spontaneous polarization when electric field is applied in the opposite 
direction. Additionally, reverse polarization also depends on the polarization history of the material it 
has experienced so that it creates a hysteresis loop when external electric field is applied on to them. 
When a ferroelectric material is synthesized, as it is cooled down to Curie temperature, ferroelectric 
phase transition occurs which is a structural phase transition which displacement of ions produce lattice 
distortions and changes the symmetry of the crystal. In some regions in the crystal, polarization is seen 
uniformly and different than its surroundings, these regions are called as domains. Saturation 
polarization, Ps, is the maximum level of polarization achieved when all domains are directed in the 
same place under applied electric field during poling, see Figure 4-(b). Remnant polarization Figure 4-
(c), Pr, is the remaining macroscopic polarization after poling and coercive field, Ec, is the required 
electric field to complete polarization and depolarization of the material [32], see Figure 4-(d). 
Materials synthesized in this study are ferroelectric single crystal 1D, needle-like, BaTiO3 and 
K0.5Na0.5NbO3 which can be used in many different applications from memory storage devices to 
capacitors, solar cells to nonlinear waveguides for photonics, high sensitivity sensors to electric 
generators and so on. 
1.3.2 Motivation for synthesis of lead-free ferroelectrics and needle-like 
morphology 
In 1880, piezoelectric effect was found by Pierre Curie and Jacques Curie in quartz crystals and many 
decades later, in 20th century oxide ceramics were found. During world war II, the first oxide 
piezoelectric material BaTiO3 is found and used as dielectric materials for solid condensers. Afterwards,

(PZT) systems have twice dielectric constants of BaTiO3 and the curie temperature is higher as well. 
However, European Union restricts the use of lead in piezoelectric devices since manufacturing, 
handling, and disposal of PZT ceramics causes harmful influences of workers health and soil 
environment and water supplies since they contain toxic lead more than 60% in weight [2,30,31]. 
Therefore in recent decades, piezoelectric researches focused more on lead-free systems such as BTO,

1.4.2 Growth mechanism of molten salt synthesis of BaTiO3 
In this process two step molten salt synthesis is followed. The first step is to synthesize needle-like 
BaTi2O5 (BT2), and the second step is the conversion of BT2 needles into BaTiO3 (BTO) needles. The 
crystal structure of BT2 shows that there are two possible sites where barium atoms can lie on and 
located along (002) and (001) planes while there are three possible sites for titanium to be placed in 
which two of them form octahedrons [Ti1O6] marked as blue, [Ti2O6] marked as yellow and one 
pentahedron [Ti3O5] marked as green, see Figure (11) below. 
While Ti2 octahedrons are linked to each other, to Ti1 octahedrons and to Ti3 pentahedrons by edge 
sharing; Ti1 octahedrons are linked to each other and to Ti3 pentahedrons by corner sharing and 
additionally Ti3 pentahedrons are linked to each other by corner sharing also. By Pauling’s rules, edge 
sharing octahedrons decreases the stability of the structure since cations are placed closer compared to 
corner sharing octahedrons so that repulsion forces are increased exponentially which hinders the 
stability of the crystal. The edge sharing nature of the BT2 makes it a good template for conversion into 
BTO since it is only stable at limited temperatures [47].

Atomic coordinates on the b-axis of the BT2 unit cell are not exactly at points 0.0 and 0.5, and this 
displacement forms the polarization on the axis. When small BT2 crystals starts to nucleate in the 
molten salt during the reaction, the isotropic symmetry breaking occurs and these small crystal 
aggregates polarizes themselves and force the growth direction in [020] direction, therefore BT2 
crystals grow in needle-like morphology naturally [47,48]. Due to natural needle-like morphology, 
sharing the same titanium octahedrons as BTO and instability due to edge-sharing octahedrons, BT2 is 
chosen as the template for conversion into needle-like BTO. 
In the topochemical conversion process, barium ion diffuses into crystal lattice of BT2 and when it is 
placed next to Ti3 pentahedron (see figure (12)), Ti3 pentahedron forms [TiO6] octahedron by shared 
oxygen atom from neighboring edge-sharing Ti2 octahedron. During that process, Ti2 octahedron 
transforms into energetically more favorable corner sharing mode. These small local reassembly 
processes allow the conservation of the morphology during conversion of the crystal [48].

is defined as diffusion of barium ions in between the layered titanium octahedrons and ion exchange 
with hydrogen atoms [50]. Due to charge differences, this ion exchange leads to promotion of 
rearrangement of edge-sharing octahedrons hence the structural transformation into BaTiO3 perovskite. 
Local and consecutive rearrangement of octahedrons allow conservation of morphology during 
topochemical conversion process [49,50]. 
1.4.4 Growth mechanism of molten salt synthesis of K0.5Na0.5NbO3 
Molten salt synthesis of needle-like K0.5Na0.5NbO3 (KNN) is done in two steps, first synthesis of needle-
like K2Nb4O11 (KN), then conversion of KN into KNN. Synthesis of KN is followed by the reaction in 
molten salt as: 
K2CO3 + 2Nb2O5  K2Nb4O11 + CO2 (g) (5) 
Nb2O5 has much lower solubility in alkali chlorides (in this case the flux is KCl) compared to K2CO3, 
so that Nb2O5 particles act as nucleation sites [51]. From these nucleation sites, growth takes place in 
the thermodynamically most favorable direction, perpendicular to the facets with the lowest surface 
energy. In the case of K2Nb4O11, the facet is along the direction of corner-sharing NbO6 octahedra which 
corresponds to [001] direction, see Figure (14).  
 
For the synthesis of needle-like KNN, conversion is done on KN needles. The reaction for that 
synthesis is as follows: 
K2Nb4O11 + Na2CO3  4K0.5Na0.5NbO3 + CO2 (g) (6) 
The conversion process is explained in two possible ways; topotactic conversion and dissolution-
reprecipitation. However, it is believed that reaction is governed by dissolution-reprecipitation process 
since the early stages of the product demonstrates polycrystalline crystals. The solubility of alkali 
sources are much higher compared to KN templates and dissolution of KN templates is slower compared 
to reprecipitation of KNN particles. In that manner, KN template act as a heterogeneous nucleation site

2.2 3-step hydrothermal synthesis of BaTiO3 
Another approach followed to synthesize needle-like BaTiO3 is the three-step hydrothermal synthesis. 
In this method, instead of using needle-like rutile TiO2 templates, first needle-like Na2Ti3O7 is 
synthesized, then it is converted to H2Ti3O7 needles by ion exchange and in the last step H2Ti3O7 needles 
are used as a template for the topochemical conversion to BaTiO3 needles.

hydroxide (Sigma Aldrich) are used as precursors. 24g (0.6 moles) of sodium hydroxide (NaOH) and 3 
grams (0.0375 moles) TiO2 are mixed in PE bottle with 50mL distilled water and stirred for 12 hours 
for complete dissolution. Then the mixture is transferred into 100mL Teflon inliner autoclave.

2.3 Molten salt synthesis of needle-like BaTiO3 
Molten salt synthesis of BaTiO3 needles comprises of two steps. The first step of the process is to 
synthesize BaTi2O5 needles which are then used as the templates for conversion into BaTiO3 needles in 
the second step. 
For the optimized synthesis of BT2 templates, precursors are stored at least for three days in 120°C 
oven for evaporation of the moisture to weigh materials precisely. To start with, 6.3032g analytical 
grade (99%) BaCO3, 5.1020g anatase 99.9% TiO2, >99.5% 18.6711g NaCl and ACS reagent grade 99% 
23.8131g KCl are put into PE bottle containing 50mL 99.9% ethanol with YSZ milling media. 
Afterwards, precursors are ball-milled for 24 hours and precursors are transferred into crystallization 
dish. After evaporation of ethanol is done, precursors are transferred into alumina crucible and reacted 
at 875°C for 5 hours. As a remark, since weighing of precursors is done in fume hood environment, 
because of the humidity in air, especially the BaCO3 is gaining weight during weight measurement. To 
be precisely sure of the weighed precursor is 6.3032g (e.g. for BaCO3), weight gain for 1g of BaCO3 is 
measured in 10 seconds intervals for 2 minutes. Then, the gain is noted down specially for that day’s 
conditions (such as humidity and temperature), and when the actual weighing process is started a few 
minutes later, by the help of chronometer required weight of the precursor including the absorbed 
humidity is calculated, so that desired stoichiometry is preserved. This process also applies for the 
conversion (2nd) step.

3. Characterization 
SEM, EDS, XRD and TEM characterizations are done on samples synthesized by MSS and 
hydrothermal reactions. For this purpose, Zeiss EVO MA10 and Zeiss ULTRA are used as SEM for 
collecting topological and morphological information about the samples. For EDS analysis, Zeiss EVO 
MA10 with AZtec detector is used for obtaining chemical composition of particles by point analysis. 
Malvern PANalytical Aeris Powder diffractometer with Cu Kα wavelength of 1.5418Å is used for all 
the samples for the XRD analysis to determine crystal structure and phase of the synthesized particles.  
Rietveld analysis is done without changing any automatically assigned values of parameters in Match! 
software. FEI TECNAI T20 TEM is used for analysis of the growth direction and crystallinity of the 
needles by measuring distances in lattice fringes in high resolution TEM (HRTEM) images and applying 
Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) to the image. All XRD spectrums are normalized and all SEM images are 
taken at 5Kx magnification unless specified otherwise. 
4. Results and discussion 
4.1 1-step hydrothermal synthesis of BaTiO3 
In this synthesis method, needle-like rutile TiO2 particles are used as the template particles for 
conversion into BTO, while keeping the morphology. As mentioned earlier in the growth mechanism 
section, the process is driven by diffusion of Barium ions into the crystal lattice of TiO2 which shares 
the same crystal structure, tetragonal. However, in contrast to the literature [9], complete conversion is 
never achieved and almost all experiments resulted in needle-like core-shell (TiO2-BaTiO3) particles.  
Only the experiments conducted at high pH levels for long reaction durations gave 98% conversion rate 
to the BaTiO3 however, the needle-like morphology is not maintained due to the dissolution of TiO2 at 
high pH levels which resulted in high conversion rate in the first place.

4.1.1 Effect of temperature 
Reaction temperature is one of the parameters that affects the diffusion rate of Barium ions both in the 
liquid phase inside the mixture of autoclave and in the crystal lattice of rutile TiO2 needles. Therefore, 
the effect of temperature on the synthesis of BTO needles is investigated for the one-step hydrothermal 
process. 
In Figure (16), one can see the evolution of morphology of synthesized core-shell (TiO2-BaTiO3) 
structures with linearly increasing temperature values (from (a) to (f)). At relatively low temperatures 
(a) and (b), 160°C and 180°C respectively, BaTiO3 particles are formed on the surface of the TiO2 
needles. This is believed that, at low temperatures, lattice vibrations are not enough for relatively very 
large barium ions (0.536 nm in diameter) to diffuse inside the rutile TiO2 lattice (with largest spacing 
of 0.325 nm). On the other hand, the reason why BaTiO3 is formed at the surface of the TiO2 is believed 
that caused by the diffusion of titanium atoms from the lattice towards the surface where barium ions 
act as the nucleation site. Then particles grow in the direction in which thermodynamically the most 
favorable, under that reaction conditions. The grown particles have the shape of four-leaf-clovers 
starting from a point, a nucleation site, growing perpendicular to each other in four directions 
considering [100] and [010] directions. Figure (17) shows the XRD spectrum of the samples synthesized 
at different reaction temperatures. As these two low temperature experiments are compared, one can 
see that conversion rate is much higher at 180°C compared to 160°C. It is mainly because at comparably 
high temperatures, even though barium still cannot diffuse inside the TiO2 lattice, the diffusion rate of 
titanium should be increased so that four-leaf-clover shaped particles grew faster. 
As temperature is increased to 200°C, it is seen that number of four-leaf-clover shaped particles is 
decreased significantly which indicates diffusion of barium ions into the TiO2 lattice is enabled by 
increased stretching of the TiO2 lattice. However, Rietveld analysis showed only 29.1% conversion to 
the BaTiO3, see Figure 56 in the appendix. As temperature is further increased to 220°C and 240°C, 
secondary phases started to form, as one can see from the XRD spectrum, Figure (17) below. 
Synthesized sample at 220°C, shows only 24.1% BaTiO3, see Figure 57, with secondary phases of TiO2, 
BaTi2O5 and Ba4Ti11O26. Secondary phases can be seen from Figure (16)-(d) with a sheet-like 
morphology. Secondary phases might be formed due to disturbance of local stoichiometry caused by

4.1.2 Effect of reaction duration 
With the determined reaction temperature, 200°C, reaction duration is studied. For this purpose, several 
reaction durations have been tried from 24 hours to 144 hours, see Figure 18 below. One can see from 
the SEM images in Figure (18) that longer reaction time increased the formation of BaTiO3 at the surface

Further increase of the duration led to the growth of the thickness and grains of BaTiO3, see Figure (18)-

4.1.11 General discussion on 1-step hydrothermal synthesis of BaTiO3 
Even though the article [9] suggests that full conversion from needle-like TiO2 to needle-like BaTiO3 is 
achieved at 160°C for 4 hours, the trial has been done in the same conditions resulted in no conversion 
at all see Figure (67) in appendix. As mentioned in the growth mechanism section of 1-step 
hydrothermal process, there are a few reports [11,12] published by different research groups of using 
TiO2 as a template for conversion into BTO. However, in each of them TiO2 template is very thin 
(compared to FTL-300 and FTL-400 (2.5um and 5um respectively)), with a diameter of 5 nanometers 
up to several hundred nanometers so that diffusion of barium ions into center of TiO2 is not inhibited. 
Moreover, some of the groups used different methods for conversion such as MSS where high 
temperature nature of the reaction and low pressure (compared to hydrothermal) result in higher lattice 
vibrations hence the stretch of the lattice, therefore higher diffusion of barium ions into the TiO2 needles. 
Additionally, even in these reported MSS syntheses, as TiO2 source, anatase polymorph is chosen, 
instead of rutile, which has continuous 3.78Å wide and tall channels lying next to distorted TiO6 
octahedrons which facilitates barium ions’ diffusion. After changing process parameters such as 
temperature, reaction duration, pressure, pH of the mixture, barium concentration, precursor size etc. 
full conversion into BaTiO3 is not achieved while keeping the needle-like morphology which 
contradicts with the results of article [9].

4.3 Molten salt synthesis of BaTiO3 
Synthesis of BTO needles with MSS technique requires two step process: first the synthesis of needle-
like BaTi2O5 (BT2) templates, and second the topochemical conversion of BT2 templates into BTO 
needles. In this synthesis method, two different set of experiments are conducted, one for comparison 
of the ball-milling conditions and other for the washing the sample (post-synthesis) for achieving pure 
phase of BTO needles. 
4.3.1 Comparison of ball milling duration 
As the first set of experiments are conducted for the synthesis of BT2 and BTO, 30 minutes of ball 
milling is done on the precursors for mixing and particle size decrease of precursors for increased 
mobility of ions in the molten flux during the reaction. However, as one can see from Figure (45)-(a)-
(b) above that BT2 particles synthesized with 30 minutes BM have secondary phases which are also 
marked in the XRD spectrum in Figure (46)-(a) below with “*”. As ball milling duration is increased to 
24 hours, it is clearly seen from (c) and (d) above that the impurity phase is mostly removed. Increasing 
the ball milling duration increases the homogeneity of the mixture which ensures the conservation of 
local stoichiometry when the salt starts to melt during the reaction. Additionally, longer ball milling 
duration decreases the particle size of the precursors which increases the dissolution rate and mobility 
of the ions inside the flux, hence probability of variation of stoichiometry within the flux decreases, 
therefore purity of the synthesized product increases. As SEM images of synthesized BTO are 
compared, 30 minutes of BM sample (e), shows very large sized impurities while 24h BM sample (f) is 
showing comparably smaller particles of the impurity. By the XRD spectrum shown in Figure (46)-(b)

5. Conclusion 
In this work, three different synthesis methods for needle-like BTO and upscaling synthesis of needle-
like KNN has been performed. It is shown that one-step hydrothermal conversion method using rutile 
TiO2 needles is not possible. Several different process parameters such as temperature, reaction 
duration, pH of the mixture, pressure, different size of the template particles etc. have been tried and 
none of them resulted in full conversion of TiO2 templates into BTO while preserving the needle-like 
morphology. It is believed that conversion starts at the surface of the TiO2 needles, however diffusion 
of barium ions towards the center of the needle decreases exponentially and stops regarding the reaction 
duration experiments. For higher the diffusion rate of barium ions, temperature is increased but it 
resulted in the formation of secondary phases, concentration of barium inside the mixture is increased 
but it did not change the conversion rate at all and additionally pressure is decreased for the same 
purpose and even though it increased the conversion rate, it did not result in full conversion. On the 
other hand, since increasing the reaction duration and diffusion rate did not work, the required diffusion 
distance has been tried to be modified. For that purpose, TiO2 templates are dissolved in high pH 
solutions with different molarity of KOH, it either resulted in the formation of secondary phases, 
destruction of needle-like morphology or at comparably lower pH values it did not result in full 
conversion into BTO. For similar purpose, halved sized TiO2 source, FTL-300, has been tried with 
2.5um diameter and characterizations of it showed an increase of the conversion rate but still complete 
conversion has not been achieved. 
On the other hand, after optimization of the reaction parameters, 3-step hydrothermal synthesis resulted 
in high aspect ratio, single-crystalline pure BTO needles. For that purpose, first Na2Ti3O7 needles are 
synthesized by hydrothermal reaction. Then resulted sodium titanate needles are successfully converted 
into H2Ti3O7 needles by ion exchange in diluted HCl solution. Conversion is confirmed by peak shifts 
in XRD to higher scattering angles, indicating that crystal lattice parameters got smaller by replacement 
of sodium ions with smaller hydrogen ions. Afterwards, H2Ti3O7 needles are converted into BTO while 
keeping its needle-like morphology. The phase, crystallinity, purity and the growth direction of the BTO 
needles are characterized with SEM, XRD and TEM (with HRTEM and FFT analysis). It is shown that, 
synthesized BTO needles are single crystal with growth direction along the needle is [100] and 100% 
pure. It is concluded that, the best mixing method for precursors is first mixing them in Turbula shaker 
for 24 hours, then using ultrasonic bath for 2 hours. As the reaction parameters, 210°C and 12h duration 
gave the best conversion and highest crystallinity. Although stirring hydrothermal process at 200°C and 
1.5h duration also resulted in pure BTO phase, morphology of the particles consists of smaller particles 
with needles which indicates magnetic stirrer broke some of the needles. 
Synthesis of BTO needles is also done via molten salt synthesis technique. Similar to the 3-step 
hydrothermal process, synthesized BTO needles are characterized by SEM for morphology, XRD for 
phase and purity and TEM for crystallinity and growth direction. SEM images showed that synthesized 
needles have high aspect ratio, HRTEM and FFT analysis showed that synthesized needles are single 
crystal with growth direction of [100], same as the hydrothermal results. XRD results of synthesized 
needles showed that, there is a remaining phase, found to be as witherite (crystallized BaCO3). It is 
washed with different concentrations of HCl solution for varying durations and it has been seen that 
removal of the impurity phase is achieved even in 0.2M HCl solution for 30 minutes. Further increase 
in concentration of the acid or the duration of soaking resulted in increase of BT2 peak intensity which 
indicates BTO needles are converting back to BT2 phase because of the exchange of barium and 
hydrogen atoms in the crystal, resulting in deficiency of barium hence the phase change. Phase 
transformation into BT2 at high concentration of HCl is also confirmed with EDS analysis done on the 
sample washed with 1M HCl for 24 hours. 
==PAGE:55==
49 
 
Upscaling of synthesis of needle-like KNN has also been done successfully. Changing the synthesis 
environment from glovebox to fume hood brought some challenges such as weighing hygroscopic 
materials e.g., sodium and potassium carbonates. As explained in the experimental section with the 
changes of way of weighing the materials, the problem has been overcome, and it plays a crucial role 
in stoichiometry, morphology, and phase purity of the synthesized products. Additionally, with the 
optimization of the mixing methods for almost 200 grams of precursors pure, high aspect ratio KNN 
needles were synthesized. Morphology of the synthesized needles are investigated with SEM and phase 
purity and growth direction of the needles are characterized with XRD. 
Considering the fact that both 3-step hydrothermal and 2-step molten salt synthesis resulted in single 
crystalline BaTiO3 needles with growth direction of [100] along the needle, for scalability of the 
production and ease of synthesis 3-step hydrothermal seems a better choice. However, due to the nature 
of hydrothermal process, dissolution-recrystallization, there are also small BaTiO3 spherical particles 
together with the needle-like morphology. Considering the needle templates are going to be used in 
texturing, if these small BaTiO3 particles are not a problem (if they can act as the matrix), the best 
choice is 3-step hydrothermal process. However, if having only the needle-like morphology is the 
priority, then MSS should be the option and additionally, it is proven that molten salt synthesis can be 
scaled up for large quantity of synthesis without losing the purity and morphology of the product. 
6. Future Work 
After successful synthesis of needle-like BaTiO3 particles with hydrothermal and molten salt synthesis 
techniques, as the future work scalability of the synthesis can be studied. As demonstrated with needle-
like KNN particles, upscaling with molten salt synthesis technique is possible. However, since it 
requires different types of precursors and different salt-to-oxide ratio upscaling of BaTiO3 needles 
should be investigated for molten salt synthesis. Additionally, since upscaling with hydrothermal 
synthesis requires larger volume of autoclave pressure, heating rate and reaction duration parameters 
should be adjusted. For example, even though the filling ratio of the autoclave is the same for two sizes 
of autoclaves, the larger autoclave will be at lower pressure during reaction compared to small one, 
since the compressibility of gases are not linear and driven by the Van der Waals non ideal gas equation. 
Therefore, filling rate and hence the pressure value should be adjusted in accordance with the standard 
synthesis. Moreover, autoclaves are made by stainless steel with thick walls for withstanding high 
pressure values. Even though the heat capacity of stainless steel (502 J*kg-1K-1) is low compared to 
water (4184 J*kg-1K-1)  and Teflon inliner’s (1500 J*kg-1K-1) [54] since the weight of it will be increased, 
it will take longer to reach reaction temperature hence the heating rate will be slower compared to small 
autoclave. Therefore, heating rates should also be investigated for upscaling the hydrothermal process. 
Even though, SEM, EDS, XRD and TEM characterizations are done for identifying crystallinity, phase 
purity, morphology, and the growth direction of synthesized BaTiO3 needles, piezoelectric response of 
them are not measured since the piezo-response force microscope (PFM) was under maintenance during 
the thesis period. Basically, PFM is a characterization method to image ferroelectric domains in 
ferroelectric materials by using the inverse piezoelectric effect. As conductive cantilever scans the 
ferroelectric material’s surface, it applies an alternating current through the material and measures the 
resulted deflection in the cantilever to determine piezoelectric coefficient and also measures the phase 
shift between applied field and sample deformation to determine the domain orientation at the local 
point in the material. This allows obtaining detailed domain mapping and studying domain switching 
dynamics and visualization of material’s ferroelectric hysteresis loop [55,56]. 
As a novel idea to increase the aspect ratio of synthesized needles hence to increase the performance of 
devices made with them, pre-synthesized needles can be used as seeds in the first steps of synthesis 
==PAGE:56==
50 
 
processes. For example, using synthesized BaTi2O5 needles in the first step of molten salt synthesis of 
BaTiO3 needles, will not disturb the stoichiometry of the process and since the first step of the synthesis 
is based on crystallization of dissolved precursors in needle-like morphology, addition of needle-like 
target phase theoretically should result in growth of needle seeds of BaTi2O5. Therefore, aspect ratio of 
the BaTi2O5 needles is increased. In the second step of molten salt synthesis where BaTi2O5 is used as a 
template, topochemical conversion will be done on higher aspect ratio templates which will increase 
the aspect ratio of synthesized BaTiO3 needles. Similarly, it can be applied to 3-step hydrothermal 
synthesis by using Na2Ti3O7 needles as seeds in the first step of the synthesis. Increase of aspect ratio 
can be analyzed using ImageJ software by measuring several hundreds of needles’ length and diameter 
to see the effect of using seeds on aspect ratio. 
Even though 1-step hydrothermal procedure did not work for synthesis of pure phase BaTiO3 needles, 
TiO2 - BaTiO3 (core-shell) needle-like structures are synthesized with different length, diameter, and 
shell thicknesses. In literature it is reported that such structures improve the performance of 
photoelectrochemical photoanodes by enhancing charge separation efficiency and photocurrent density 
[57], enhances the energy storage capability of polymer materials by embedding such structures inside 
the polymer and creating a nanocomposite [58], increases the degradation of organic pollutants by 
increasing the oxidation rate constant [59] and increase the onset potential (Voc) and increase of electron 
density in dye-synthesized solar cells by creating an energy barrier against the electron transfer from 
TiO2 to electrolyte [60]. Since the purpose of the thesis was not to investigate such core-shell structures 
and their possible usage areas focus of interest was shifted towards other synthesis techniques for 
synthesis of needle-like BaTiO3. However, it is demonstrated that different shell thicknesses can be 
achieved via 1-step hydrothermal process by controlling the pressure, temperature and pH value of the 
mixture that properties of such structures can be further characterized and implemented into such 
devices by easily optimizing the core-shell thickness values according to the requirements of devices.

8. Appendix 
8.1 Experimental details of 1-step hydrothermal synthesis process 
Standard procedure for 1-step hydrothermal synthesis is as follows (e.g. FTL-4-200C-24h-40%): 
1- 30mL distilled water is added into 100mL PE bottle. 
2- 3.94 grams of Ba(OH)2.8H2O is put inside the PE bottle. 
3- Mixture is heated to 50°C and stirred for 1 hour on the heating plate with a magnetic stirrer. 
4- Then 1 gram of needle-like TiO2 source is added into the mixture (FTL-400). 
5- Then PE bottle is transferred into 3D Turbula shaker and mixed for 1 hour. 
6- Afterwards, to ensure needle-like TiO2 particles are dispersed, PE bottle is put into ultrasonic 
bath and ultrasonicated for 30 minutes. 
7- Then mixture is transferred from PE bottle to 100mL Teflon inliner. 
8- PE bottle is washed with 10mL distilled water and it is also transferred into Teflon inliner to 
ensure all precursors are transferred without loss. 
9- In the next step, Teflon inliner is put into stainless steel autoclave, and it is tightened. 
10- Then autoclave is put into furnace and temperature is set to 200°C, and reacted for 24 hours. 
11- Afterwards, when furnace is cooled down to room temperature, autoclave is placed in fume 
hood for it to be cooled down to room temperature as well. 
12- Then the product is divided into two falcon tubes and centrifuged 3 times with distilled water 
and 3 times with ethanol at 4500rpm for 10 minutes each repeat. 
13- Then synthesized products are dried inside the 60°C oven by covering the falcon tubes by 
aluminum foil with holes. 
 
8.2 Experimental details of 3-step hydrothermal synthesis process 
Standard procedure for 3-step hydrothermal synthesis is as follows (e.g. Hydrothermal BTO 210C 
12h): 
1- 24 grams of NaOH, 3 grams of TiO2 and 50mL distilled water is put inside a 100mL PE 
bottle. 
2- Then the mixture is mixed with a magnetic stirrer for 12 hours. 
3- Afterwards mixture is transferred into 100mL Teflon inliner. 
4- The PE bottle is washed with 10mL distilled water and it is also poured into the Teflon inliner 
to transfer the precursors completely. 
5- In the next step, inliner is put into autoclave and it is tightened. 
6- Then autoclave is put inside the oven and reacted at 200°C for 72 hours. 
7- Then the product is divided into 4 falcon tubes and washed with water, ethanol and 0.1M HCl 
3 times each for 10 minutes at 4500rpm. 
8- Then washed Na2Ti3O7 needles are dried in an oven at 60°C for 2 days. 
9- Afterwards, 8.3mL of 37% HCl is measured with pipettes and poured into 1L of distilled 
water inside the PE bottle and stirred to prepare 0.1M HCl solution. 
10- In the next step, Na2Ti3O7 needles are put into the 1L 0.1M HCl solution and put into 3D 
Turbula shaker for 24 hours for ion exchange process for synthesis of H2Ti3O7. 
11- Then H2Ti3O7 products are collected by centrifuging the solution and dried at 60°C for 2 days 
in an oven. 
12- Afterwards, 1.025 grams of Ba(OH)2.8H2O is put into 50mL distilled water containing 100mL 
PE bottle and dissolved by stirring at 50°C. 
==PAGE:63==
57 
 
13- Then, 0.140 grams of H2Ti3O7 needles are added into mixture and it is mixed by 3D shaker for 
an hour and then transferred into ultrasonic bath for 30 minutes of ultrasonification to 
disperse needles inside the mixture. 
14- Afterwards, the 50mL mixture is transferred into 100mL Teflon inliner and PE bottle is 
washed with 10mL distilled water to transfer precursors completely.  
15- Then precursors are reacted in an oven at 210°C for 12 hours. 
16- Synthesized BaTiO3 needles are washed with distilled water, ethanol and 0.1M HCl for 5 
times each at 4500rpm for 10 minutes. 
17- Then BaTiO3 needles are dried in an oven at 60°C for 2 days. 
8.3 Experimental details of molten salt synthesis of BaTiO3 
1- Firstly, enough amount of precursors are transferred into a glass bottle and placed in a furnace 
at 125°C at least for 3 days before the weighing to evaporate any moisture absorbed by 
precursors from air. 
2- Then 6.3032 grams BaCO3, 5.1020 grams anatase TiO2, 18.6711 grams NaCl and 23.8131 
grams KCl are weighed precisely.  
3- For carbonate sources, since they are highly hygroscopic, weight gain is measured and noted 
down per 10 seconds for 5 minutes. 
4- Afterwards, weight gain ratio is calculated from the results and required amount is calculated 
for the synthesis assuming that weighing process will be finished exactly at 5 minutes. 
5- In the next step, precursors are transferred into 250mL PE bottles and weighing boats are 
washed with ethanol to make sure precursors are transferred completely.  
6- Then, 70 cylindrical shaped 1cm diameter, 1cm long YSZ milling media and 50mL ethanol 
added into the mixture. 
7- Afterwards, PE bottle is put into 3D Turbula shaker and milled for 24 hours at gear 75. 
8- In the next step, mixture is transferred into crystallization dish and to make sure everything is 
transferred bottle is washed several times with ethanol and in each step ethanol is poured into 
the same crystallization dish. 
9- Then the slurry is heated for evaporation of ethanol at 65°C until it is fully dry. 
10- In the next step, dried powder is scraped with the help of a spatula and transferred into 200mL 
alumina crucible. In this step it is important to break powder agglomerates since as ethanol 
evaporates, heavier particles tend to sediment at the bottom and agglomerations might disturb 
the local stoichiometry. 
11- Then crucibles are put in a chamber furnace and they are covered with alumina plates to 
prevent evaporation of salt. 
12- Then they are reacted at 875°C for 5 hours with heating rate of 120°C/h. 
13- In the next step, around 40mL 70°C distilled water is poured onto product and shaken. 
14- Then mixture containing dissolved salt and dispersed needles are poured into vacuum 
filtration system which uses 42 number filter paper. 
15- These last two steps are done until all salt is dissolved which requires around 2L distilled 
water. 
16- Afterwards, filtered BT2 product is divided into 4 falcon tubes and centrifuged with distilled 
water and ethanol 3 times each to further purification. 
17- Then BT2 needles are dried in an oven at 60°C for at least two days. 
18- For the conversion step, required amount of precursors are calculated in accordance to the 
amount of BT2. 
19- To convert BT2 needles into BTO, all steps are repeated with these exceptions: 
20- Instead of ball milling all precursors, carbonates and salts are ball milled and then they are 
mixed with BT2 needles via ultrasonic probe (at 1W, 20 amplitude, 50% pulse) in 1L beaker 
for 15 minutes (first 5 minutes dispersion of BT2 needles in 300mL of ethanol and last 10 
==PAGE:64==
58 
 
minutes together with carbonates and salts with addition of ethanol). Then mixture is 
transferred into crystallization dish same as the first step for evaporation of ethanol. 
21- After paper filtration, in the centrifuging stage in addition to distilled water and ethanol, 0.2M 
HCl solution is also used.
