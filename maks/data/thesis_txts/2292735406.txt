Abstract 
The flows past spheres and cylinders are classical and well covered cases in fluid mechanics. 
However, even though studies have been made on impulsively started cylinders, no studies 
have been published in the case of impulsively started spheres. In this project this flow has 
been investigated for Reynolds number ranging from 100 to 10000. The goal was to find and 
describe patterns in these flows. The focus was on the non-dimensional timespan of 
0 ≤𝑡∗=
𝑡∙𝑈
𝐷≤5 
From results of numerical analysis some patterns have been described concerning development 
of eddies, wake shape and size, drag coefficient and more. An optical method for velocity 
measurements were used to acquire experimental data on the flow. These data show decent, 
but not perfect, compliance with the numerical data.  
Different photographic flow visualization techniques were tested.

1. Introduction 
Flow past a sphere is a well-established field and has been studied in depth by e.g. Sadatoshi 
Taneda who has written papers (Taneda, 1956)concerning flows past spheres at various 
Reynolds numbers. However, to our knowledge no studies on impulsive flow past a sphere are 
published.  
Impulsively started flow past a cylinder, on the other hand have been studied by R. Bouard and 
M. Coutanceau in their paper “The early stage development of the wake behind an impulsively 
started cylinder” from 1980. The study brought light on some, by the time, unknown 
phenomena. Among the findings were the development of a bulge in the recirculating flow 
along the cylinder surface and the further development of a pair of new eddies. Another finding 
was that the diameter of the wake behind the sphere in some cases exceeded the diameter of 
the sphere itself. The study described the development of these flows in both a qualitative and 
quantitative manor.  
One incentive for this analysis is to find out if these patterns exist for spheres as well as 
cylinder. If so, how do the vortical structures develop in time and how do the structures differ 
from those of the cylinder.  
The flow analysis is done by numerical as well as experimental methods. The numerical method 
includes transient CFD analysis of flows for a Reynolds numbers varying between 100 and 
10000. The experimental method includes PIV measurements. Also, different flow visualization 
methods are tested.

2. Numerical analysis 
Computational Fluid Dynamics 
Computational Fluid Dynamics, usually abbreviated CFD, is a method for simulating fluid flows. 
The fundamentals of CFD are the Navier-Stokes equations which governs any gas or liquid flow.  
Typically a flow of interest is defined by geometry and boundary conditions in either 2 or 3 
dimensions in space. The space is divided into cells and the Navier-Stokes equations are solved 
for each cell and its interaction with surrounding cells.  
The popularity and use of CFD has increased as computer processing power has become better 
and more available. CFD is now widely used in both academic and industrial applications due to 
its ability to accurately estimate complex flow problems. Another benefit of CFD is the effective 
communication that lies in the visualization tools that most software provides.   
Purpose 
The purpose of the numerical analysis is to investigate the flow of an impulsively started sphere 
at different Reynolds numbers. The focus is on the development of vortical structures, and the 
time history of the drag coefficient.

The problem is transient since the flow develops in time and we are interested in the solutions 
in the time immediately after acceleration of the sphere body. This type of numerical analysis 
requires some steps in order to give a good result.  
• Mesh. The analysis should in this case be made in 3D since the problem is 3 Dimensional 
and the velocities cannot be assumed to be axisymmetric at all stages of the flow. A 
mesh should be created with sufficiently fine cell sizes.  
• Models: Relevant models should be used. Irrelevant models should be excluded.  
• Ramp up time. A Ramp up time for the velocity will greatly increase the stability and 
convergence of the first time steps. However, the ramp up time should be relatively 
short to simulate a close to impulsively started flow.  
This section covers the methods and models used to obtain good simulation results.

Transient analysis 
Models 
Implicit unsteady 
The flow of interest is an unsteady one and the development in time is of interest. This calls for 
an implicit unsteady solver which will ensure the quality of the solution at each time step.  
An implicit unsteady solver will solve for the time steps with inner iterations to get a 
convergence at each time step. The residual values of values such as continuity and momentum 
should converge to some fraction of the initial value e.g. 10−4 of initial value.  
In some cases, where the time factor is not of interest, the solutions do not necessarily have to 
converge for all time steps but in this case we need a good convergence from time step 1.  
Segregated flow 
Since we have low Mach numbers, the flow can be assumed incompressible. The segregated 
solver is ideal for cases with incompressible or weakly compressible flow.  
The governing equations will be solved sequentially as opposed to the coupled solver which 
solves the governing equations simultaneously. The segregated solver is faster and requires less 
computation time and memory but needs iterations to solve each time step.  
Turbulence 
The Reynolds numbers go as high as 10000 in these simulations. This flow will with time 
develop into flow with areas of turbulence, but in the short time span of the impulsive start the 
turbulence will not develop and therefore the simulations are run as laminar flow.  
Some simulations were made with a turbulence k-e model and the results were far from the 
experimental results for Re = 10000. 
Ramp up of velocity.  
An impulsive start can be interpreted as one with infinite acceleration to the desired velocity. 
However, in any practical application this is not the case. So how big should the acceleration be. 
For the experiments that were performed for this project it was possible to accelerate the 
sphere to Re = 10000 over 2.461 % D, where D is the diameter of the sphere. Considering that 
the flow of interest develops from t* = 0 to t* = 5 or within a 5D stroke this value of 2.46% D is 
sufficiently low that the difference between the ramped up flow and one with infinite 
acceleration will be relatively small.  
An acceleration which yields the same acceleration distance is chosen for ramping up the 
velocity in these analyses. The acceleration is calculated from the equations of motion.

In this case we are clearly in a well converged scale of step sizes. The deviations never exceed 
1% on any of the evaluated values. The relation between step size and computational time 
would be linear if the number of iterations were kept the same. However, a smaller time step 
will typically make the residuals decrease faster. The initial guess for a time step uses the final 
solution for the previous time step. Since the difference in physical time is smaller, the 
difference in solution will also be smaller which causes the residuals to decrease faster and thus 
allowing for less inner iteration per time step. So even though the computation time could be 
shortened somewhat by increasing time steps, it was kept at ∆𝑡* = 0.0015.

The increase in wake length intuitively makes sense relating to the definition of the Reynolds 
number which is the ratio of inertial forces to viscous forces.  When Re decreased the viscous 
forces gain power which gives larger shear stress and thus lower velocity gradients across the 
flow. This ultimately results in a longer wake. The shortening seen when going to Re = 100 is 
not intuitive and this should be investigated further.

3. Experiments 
This section contains description of the experiments that were carried out in order to get 
experimental data from an impulsive start of a sphere.  
Setup and equipment 
This section describes how the experiments are set up and executed. 
The flow of interest is a flow past a sphere. There are 2 general ways of achieving this flow.  
1. Keeping the position of the sphere constant and moving the fluid past the sphere. This 
requires a quite large setup and requires accelerating a large amount of liquid rather 
fast.  
2. Moving the sphere. This is by far the simplest method since it can be done with a static 
fluid container and a movable mount of the sphere.  
The flow of interest is at Re ≤ 10000. This Reynolds number can be achieved by using water as 
the fluid, spheres of diameter 30 - 80 mm and velocities of  𝑣≤0.4 𝑚/𝑠 Available at DTU is a 
linear actuator that can achieve these velocities with a rather high acceleration.  
The flow is to be investigated in a cross section plane across the center of the sphere and 
parallel to the flow direction.   
The direction of the sphere motion in the experiments will be vertical. However, all illustrations 
from both simulations and experiments are shown with sphere motion from right to left (fluid 
motion from left to right)

The spheres were purchased are designed for use with pin ball like machines. They are 
produced to have very accurate and fine surface quality as well as an accurate mass.

Proposed mount types 
A metal mount was proposed. The sphere would be mounted directly on a special beam, so

airfoil at the section closest to the sphere to obtain a laminar and attached flow around the 
sphere. The beam should be connected at the opposite side of the sphere as the cameras.

a lot thinner than the version showed). 2; the machining would be difficult, and 3; asymmetry in 
the mount would require counter weight on the opposite side of the sphere to ensure a linear 
motion.

String 
The most important properties of the line are stiffness and thickness. The line should be stiff to 
minimize vibration of the sphere and thin to avoid affecting the flow.

excited at all frequencies when accelerated from zero velocity. 
Even though all frequencies will be excited the lower frequencies will be excited with smaller 
amplitude than the higher frequencies because the velocity will be lower at the time of 
excitation.

small (20ᵒ). See first part of Figure 36. This had the unfortunate effect that the sphere would

translate into 3 moments with different directions and thus a rotational resistance.

Particle image velocimetry  
Particle image velocimetry, usually abbreviated PIV, is a method for visualizing and quantifying 
velocity fields in flows. The method allows for measuring of velocities by tracing particles added 
to the fluid. The particles should be nonintrusive to the flow. This is achieved by 1st Using small 
particles compared to the scales of the geometry in the experiment and 2nd Using particles with 
the same density as the fluid.  
The particles of interest are illuminated. This will typically be a 2D section of the flow and the 
illumination can be achieved with a laser sheet. The particles are traced using by a camera 
positioned with the lens direction perpendicular to the section plane.   
The camera takes 2 subsequent images. Each particle will appear on both images and the time 
between image captures is specified so that the particle will have moved far enough that the 
motion can be quantified but not so far that any one particle cannot be recognized from other 
particles at the second picture.  
The two positions of the particle together with the time between image captures can then be 
translated into a velocity vector. The whole image is divided into smaller areas and different 
==PAGE:49==
46 
 
particle velocities are averaged out and a velocity vector is created for each area in the image. 
This yields a velocity field for the entire frame of the image.   
PIV Equipment 
The PIV System consists of 2 cameras (Dantec HiSense MkII) that are synchronized with a 
pulsing laser (Dantec Nano L200-15PIV) so that the laser illuminated the particles at the same 
time as the camera shutter is open. The laser and the cameras are controlled from the software 
DynamicStudio v3.30 which due to complications with newer versions of Microsoft Windows 
cannot be updated. The data is processed in DynamicStudio v4.10 on a different computer.   
Purpose 
The purpose of the experiments carried out is to get experimental data. If the experiments are 
executed correctly the results will be a representation of reality. These results will be held up 
against the results of the simulations.  
Methods 
Motor Control 
The motor is controlled by defining position vs time curves and loading them into the 
controller. The curves can then be executed from the software LinMot Talk v4.3.  
Control of Laser and Cameras 
DynamicStudio has a mode named acquisition which lets the user control the laser and camera 
simultaneously in order to acquire the data needed. When setting up cameras and laser one 
can control the two manually from the software interface and when the frames are looking as 
desired the triggering of image acquisition can be set to on external trigger. This makes it so 
that the first frame (set of 2 images with a defined, small time gap) will be triggered by a signal 
from the motor control system when the motor begins the motion of the sphere. The following 
frames will be taken with a frequency from this time.  
From comparison between the first frame and frames taken manually without movement of 
the sphere it was found that sphere was in the exact same position on the two frames. This 
indicates that the first frame of the motion was indeed taken at t = 0. The pictures cannot be 
taken before t = 0, since the PIV system is triggered by the motor motion.

To avoid light pollution and improve the image quality, the setup was covered with dense 
blankets which kept a large portion of the surrounding light away from the system.

thing that became evident was that the smaller spheres (spheres of size D = 30 mm and D = 60

When processing the images DynamicStudio works with a grid of cells of a certain size (in this 
case 32x32 pixels). At the points of the highest velocities the movement of the particles 
between the 2 images should be approximately a fourth of the width of these cells. 
Furthermore there should be at least one visible particle in each cell. See Figure 39. This will 
ensure a good conversion into a velocity vector field. If the dislocations of the particles are not 
in the right scale the time between the two images should be corrected accordingly. The 
number of particles should also be corrected before acquiring the data.

Masking and processing 
In the adaptive PIV processing an interrogation area size is defined. This is the size of the area 
that is actually investigated for each cell. This area is larger than the cell area. In this case it was 
set to minimum 32x32 pixels and maximum 64x64 pixels. This means that the investigated area 
for a cell that is close to the edge of the sphere will be created using image data from within the 
sphere region.  
As seen in Figure 38 the sphere is illuminated to some degree and this will give disturbances in 
adaptive PIV processing. This is solved by masking out the sphere region in each image by 
setting the pixel values to 0. The adaptive PIV processing tool can then be set to interpret these 
black areas as walls and will thus not use the data in these regions for calculating velocities for 
cells close to the sphere.  
Before running the PIV analyses the system is calibrated from an image of a ruler placed in the 
cross section. This will enable the adaptive PIV tool to convert the pixel velocities into metric 
velocities. Figure 40 shows an image used for calibration. The points A and B are placed in the 
image and the actual distance between them is set.

Reference frame transformation 
As mentioned the images were originally taken without complete knowledge of the exact 
position and angle of the cameras. The things that were easily controllable and therefore

tolerance) and that they were perpendicular to the cross section (within a 2ᵒ tolerance). 
Therefore the vector fields should be transformed to 1; correct the skew frame angle and 2; get 
the velocities in the reference frame of the sphere.  
In the earlier process of masking the images, the pixel-coordinates of the sphere center at each 
frame were noted for each camera view. These coordinates were used to determine the motion 
of the sphere in the reference frame of the camera. With this motion a transformation program 
is created which had the functions of:

• Shifting the position coordinates 
• Subtracting the sphere velocity from the velocity components.

center is now fixed at [x , y]=[0 , 0]. The skewness each image is the result of the transformation

Sources of error 
Among the sources of error are 
• Image quality: The image quality can be insufficient for different reasons. Too much light 
pollution will make the particles indistinct and hard to recognize by the adaptive PIV 
tool. Out of focus images will have the same effect.  
• Cell size: the cell sizes used in the adaptive PIV are rather large compared to the 
structures of the flow. This gives a coarse resolution of the vector velocity field. 
Especially close to the surface the coarse cell size result in inconsistent velocities 
• Particle concentration: Too few particles in the flow can result in inconsistent velocity 
fields, if each cell will not contain at least 1 particle at all times.  
• Unsettled flow: If the flow does not have sufficient time to settle between runs. 
Practically there is way to reach a completely still fluid with the setup available. 
Temperature differences and convection will make the fluid circulate at some rate.

Flow visualization 
A photographic visualization of the actual flow can offer an alternative way of understanding 
the flow. Different methods for visualization were tested – some with more success than 
others. This section will cover the different experiments with flow visualization.  
Particles and long shutter time 
The idea is to take photos of the flow with a low aperture (high f-number) and a long exposure 
time. Particles will be added to the fluid and a laser sheet will illuminate these particles. The 
long shutter speed will draw out each moving particle and the result is an image with pathlines 
(not to be confused with streamlines) from the flow.  
The Particles were illuminated by a laser sheet made with a strong laser pointer and a 
cylindrical lens.  
   
 
Figure 53: Laser pointer and lens for creating a laser sheet 
Unfortunately the long exposure time resulted in blurred image where the sphere itself was 
drawn out. Lowering the exposure time would make the pathlines shorter to the point where 
they were no longer visible. Another problem with the setup was that the pathlines are in the 
cameras reference frame and since the camera was mounted statically, the pathlines mostly be 
in the spheres movement direction. An example of a photo taken with this technique is shown 
in Figure 54. To improve this technique, the camera should be moving with the sphere either by 
mounting it on the linear motor slider together with the sphere mount or by mounting it on a 
separate linear motor.  
==PAGE:63==
60 
 
 
Figure 54: Photo of sphere and illuminated particles and long exposure time. 
Visualization with fluorescein 
To give a spatial perception of the flow structures photos were taken of the sphere moving 
through the water with a fluorescein applied to the surface as a tracer. Fluorescein is a highly 
fluorescent synthetic organic compound. It is available as a dark orange powder which turns 
green when dissolved in water. The fluorescein has to stick to the surface while lowering the 
sphere into the water tank so the fluorescein is mixed with a liquid that will dry up and stick to 
the surface. Two different liquids were tested; syrup and condensed milk. Both work in the 
same way; mix the fluorescein and the fluid and apply it to the surface of the sphere. After a 
while it will have dried up and the sphere can be lowered into the water tank.  
Unfortunately none of the two were able to stick to the surface while lowering the sphere. 
Different lowering speeds and drying times were tested and the results were not improving by 
much. Both the syrup and the condensed milk dissolve rapidly and the Fluorescein pollutes the 
water. This means that it is difficult to tell which of the visible structures are flow structures and 
which are pollution.  
==PAGE:64==

Figure 55: Fluorescein covered sphere. 1; Applied with syrup, 2; Applied with condensed milk. 
Examples of the photos taken are shown in Figure 55 and show the vast amount of fluorescein 
in surrounding the sphere. The two images both for Re = 10000 but are not taken at the same 
time t*, thus showing different structures. The spatial dimension adds little to the visualization 
of the flow structures because it blurs out the eddies and make them appear less distinct.   
To counter this, new runs are made where the fluorescein mixture is only applied in a ring along 
the cross section plane perpendicular to the camera view. This results in a 2D-like image and 
the structures stand a bit more out. They are still not clear and the surrounding fluid is visibly 
polluted with fluorescein. See Figure 56.

To optimize further the laser sheet setup, is used in this application. The fluorescein mixture is 
again only applied along the cross section. This eliminates most of the pollution and gives a 
clearer picture. The sharp and concentrated light from the laser makes the areas with high 
concentrated stand out but the low concentration areas are less visible. The technique depicts 
the structures close to the sphere surface well but the large structures of the main eddy are not 
as well defined.

The techniques that include syrup and condensed milk mixtures have a flaw with regards to the 
fluid properties. Both syrup and condensed milk have higher viscosity and density than water. 
This results in differences in viscous and inertial forces where the concentration is higher. 
Furthermore, it makes the gravitation act with different forces in different areas of the flow.   
This technique works best with higher velocities where the pollution becomes less dominant 
and where the higher shear will draw more of the fluorescent mixture out from the surface and 
into the flow.

5. Conclusion  
The flow has been investigated for impulsively started spheres at Reynolds numbers ranging 
from 100 to 10000. From the numerical analysis some interesting phenomena were observed. A 
pattern in the movement of the main eddy was found; the sphere is initially developed close to 
the sphere surface and then moves downstream and away from the flow axis. This pattern was 
fairly consistent for 200 ≤ Re ≤ 5000. For the cases of Re = 100 and Re = 10000, the pattern 
breaks and the main eddy center moves in different patterns. For Re = 10000 the main eddy 
moves in a pulsing motion closer to and away from the flow axis.  
For Re > 500 a secondary eddies develop on the sphere surface which eventually breaks the 
main eddy into two separate eddies. The new eddies seem to allow the main eddy to become 
more circular which result in a short but wide recirculating zone. The wake, in at high Reynolds 
numbers, becomes larger in frontal area than the sphere itself.  
The drag coefficient does not converge before t* = 5 in any of the investigated cases. A local 
maximum in the drag coefficient appear for 500 ≤ Re ≤ 5000, which coincide in time with the 
first appearance of the secondary eddy.  
A test setup was established in order to get experimental data on the flow.  
The flow for Re = 10000 was analyzed experimentally with Particle Image Velocimetry (PIV) and 
the result had decent compliance with the numerical results. However, some differences were 
noticed. It is uncertain whether the differences are caused by inaccurate PIV measurement or 
inaccurate CFD analysis. Among the sources of error for the PIV method none have a major 
impact on the results with regards to the larger structures. The smaller scale structures proved 
difficult to capture with this technique.  
Different flow visualization techniques were tested including long exposure time photographs 
of illuminated particles and short exposure time photographs of fluorescein covered sphere. 
Unfortunately none of the techniques yielded good results and did not contribute much to the 
flow analysis.   
Possible improvements can be made to the analysis methods used in this project. The PIV 
measurements could be improved by placing the cameras closer to the sphere. This would 
allow for better resolution and processing especially closer to the sphere wall. This would 
require more camera views to get the same coverage. The numerical analysis could be 
improved by refinement of the mesh. This would result in longer computation time but most 
likely also slightly more accurate results. Flow visualization with long exposure and particles in 
the fluid could be made using a moving camera that moves parallel to the sphere. This would 
give pathlines in the spheres reference frame.
