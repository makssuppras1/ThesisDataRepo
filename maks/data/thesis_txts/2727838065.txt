Abstract
Glazing plays an important role in building fire safety as a new vent is formed when glazing
falls out, changing the duration of the fire and, therefore, the exposure time of the struc┬¡
tural elements. Modern buildings are increasingly characterized by low┬¡energy windows
formed by multilayered glazing panes to decrease carbon dioxide emissions. However,
such glazing type can not easily fall out in fire, which results in a longer fire and thus higher
thermal solicitation of structural elements. Therefore, it is critically essential to investigate
the breakage of such glazing to have a better fire resistance design of structural elements
and thus ensure a safe building environment. Therefore, this project aims to start address┬¡
ing this gap by testing different types of glazing panes and investigating, in particular, how
thickness and size influence the breakage of two single┬¡layered traditional glazing types
(float glazing and toughened glazing), and one low┬¡energy glazing type (double┬¡layered
laminated glazing).
In total, 42 experiments were included in this project. Three types of glazing were in┬¡
vestigated: Float glazing, toughened glazing, and laminated glazing. Four different thick┬¡
nesses were included: 3 mm, 4 mm, 6 mm, and 8 mm. For float glazing, five varied sizes
were also studied: 200 mm ├ù 200 mm, 300 mm ├ù 300 mm, 200 mm ├ù 400 mm, 400 mm
├ù 400 mm, and 500 mm ├ù 500 mm.
The glazing receives radiant heat from H┬¡TRIS, with a distance of 430 mm, receiving a
heat flux of 19.5 kW/m2 at the center point of the glazing surface. The heat flux is designed
from the minimum heat flux that can fulfill the flashover, ensuring the fallout of glazing. All
glazing had the same exposure level and shading width of 20 mm. The glazing panes
were exposed to fire until the first breakage or were subjected to fire for 30 minutes. The
primary conclusions are:
ÔÇó The thicker glazing tends to have a longer crack initiation time, a higher critical tem┬¡
perature difference, and thus larger fracture stress.
ÔÇó The larger glazing has a shorter crack initiation time, a lower critical temperature
difference, and thus smaller fracture stress.
ÔÇó Thicker glazing has a larger fallout percentage at crack initiation time, while no trend
is found with varied sizes. Both thickness and size do not seem to have an influence
on the breakage patterns and breakage positions.
ÔÇó The average fracture stress of float glazing is about 43.57 MPa, and the calculated
apparent tensile strength of toughened glazing is around 220 MPa.
ÔÇó No fallout was observed in toughened and laminated glazing under the given ex┬¡
perimental setup. Thus, if these toughened and laminated glazing can be used as
window glazing in the building compartment, these glazing would not provide venti┬¡
lation to the compartment during a fire.
ÔÇó Laminated glazing can not be regarded as float glazing with a thickness equal to the
sum of the two glass layers.
Although this research has some limitations, such as considering constant heating flux,
applying fixed shading width, having limited repetitions for the tests, not considering very
large windows that are normally used in buildings, and lacking over┬¡pressure, the results
can still illustrate that for the glazing that was investigated in this project, some can be
considered as a vent and the others cannot. In general, fire engineers should pay atten┬¡
tion to the smaller and thicker glazing, which may break after the flashover because the

Introduction
1.1
Background and motivation
Considering climate change, it is very urgent to decrease the emission of greenhouse
gas, especially carbon dioxide, to satisfy the Paris AgreementÔÇÖs 1.5┬░C target [1]. It is
reported that the building sector makes up 40% of the global energy consumption, and
the buildingsÔÇÖ energy demand is predicted to continue growing worldwide in the next years
[2]. Therefore, decreasing the energy consumption in the building sector can become the
main method to reduce carbon dioxide emissions. For this reason, modern buildings are
characterized by a high level of insulation and low┬¡energy windows [3]. Due to a lower U
value, the low┬¡energy glazing can help reduce the heating and cooling loss, thus reducing
the electricity for heating and cooling and reducing carbon dioxide emissions.
The behavior of glass window panes is important in building fire safety. A new vent is
created when the glazing falls out, increasing the fire growth rate[4]. Furthermore, differ┬¡
ent fallout times and fallout percentages of glazing make the ventilation conditions in the
compartment more complex. In particular, the high ventilation caused by the breakage
of traditional window panes in the early fire phase led to shorter fires and lower heating
temperatures of the elements. However, unlike traditional windows, new low┬¡energy win┬¡
dows with multiple glass layers are not expected to break so early, thus leading to a longer
fire and higher thermal solicitation of the structural elements. It can be seen that in the
parametric fire curve (more details are introduced in section 2.2.2), decreasing the open┬¡
ing factor results in a longer fire and a lower gas temperature. However, the temperature
of the structural elements, such as the insulated steel, and insulated concrete, reaches
a higher temperature with a smaller opening factor. Therefore, the structural elements
are more likely to be exposed to a critical temperature and collapse when the opening
factor is smaller. Thus, a reduction in the fire safety of modern low┬¡energy buildings can
be expected unless new design methods are adopted, capable of properly accounting for
such lower ventilation during the fire.
However, predicting the behavior of glass in fire is a quite challenging task. Although the
main mechanism of cracking is known, predicting the breaking time and amount of fallout
remains an open research and design question.
Previous research on the topic can be classified based on the different investigated pa┬¡
rameters that affect the breakage of glass in fire, such as the number of layers, shading
width, frame types, thickness, type of glass, etc. Shields et al. [5][6] performed exper┬¡
iments with single┬¡layered and double┬¡layered float glazing successively under varying
heat flux in the corner fire. Klassen et al. [7] tested seven different types of multi┬¡pane
glazing (tempered glass and heat┬¡strengthened glass are included) to get the breakage
properties of single and multi┬¡pane glass. Jonathan J├©rgensen [8] investigated the ther┬¡
mal resistance of the float glass with four different shading widths. Seindal and Jensen [9]
studied the breakage properties of double and triple┬¡layered windows with wooden and
wood/alu frames.
For the study of the different thicknesses, Qiyuan Xie et al. [10] analyzed the crack and
fallout of toughened glass with different thicknesses of 6 and 10 mm in ISO 9705 fire test
room by being exposed to different pan sizes of fires (540 mm ├ù 540 mm, 600 mm ├ù 600
mm, 700 mm ├ù 700 mm, 800 mm├ù 800 mm and 900 mm├ù 900 mm). The toughening
stress of the glass was about 130 MPa, and the glazing was shaded with a 20 mm wooden

frame. The study observed two different sizes: 870 mm ├ù 870 mm and 1820 mm ├ù 870
mm. The study found the critical breakage temperature difference for the 6┬¡mm┬¡thick
toughened glass was about 330ÔÇô380 ┬░C, while it was approximately 470ÔÇô590 ┬░C for 10┬¡
mm┬¡thick toughened glass, suggesting that the critical breakage temperature difference is
larger for thicker glazing. The study compared the results with float glazing and concluded

may be better to prevent complete fallout compared with the whole larger and tall glazing.
Later, Yi Zhang et al.[11] performed a series of experiments to study the breakage behavior
of float glass with different thicknesses of 4 mm, 6 mm, 10 mm, 12 mm, and 19 mm. The
specimens had a size of 600 mm ├ù 600 mm and were heated by the thermal radiation
heat with a certain increasing rate of 15 ┬░C/min. The width of the steel glazing frame was
20 mm. It was observed that the time at the 1st cracking with glass of thickness 19 mm
was longer than the thinner glass, and the time of thicknesses 6 mm, 10 mm, and 12 mm
are close under the thermal radiation, and the temperature difference increased with the
thickness of the glass increasing.
Qiyuan Xie et al. [12] directly measured the critical breakage stress for float glazing with
6, 8, 10, and 12 mm through mechanical tensile experiments based on MTS 810 appara┬¡
tus with a room temperature (25┬░C) and a hot environment (200┬░C). The results showed
that the critical breakage stresses of thicker float glass are larger than that of 4 mm float
glass under the condition both with a room temperature (25┬░C) and a hot environment
(200┬░C). In addition, the comparison between the two different environment temperatures
also suggested that the critical breakage stresses in a hot environment are smaller than
at room temperature.
Yuzhuo Yang et al. [13] conducted experiments with frameless float glass panes of dif┬¡
ferent thicknesses (3 mm, 6 mm, 7 mm, 10 mm) under the same radiation exposure (35
kW/m2 at the centre and 29 kW/m2 at the edge). The experimental results showed that
thicker glazing needs more time for a break, and the critical temperature difference was
larger for thicker glazing. A transient 2D heat transfer model was also presented to ob┬¡
tain the transient temperature fields in the glass samples. In addition, the temperature
fields were loaded to ANSYS to obtain the thermal stress fields. It is found that under
a strong heat flux, in the initial heating period, the maximum stress was mainly caused
by the temperature gradient component across the thickness, and the stress value was
larger for thicker glazing═¥ then the maximum stress was gradually dominated by the tem┬¡
perature gradient component along the planar direction. Therefore, for thicker glazing
under a strong heat flux, the thermal bending stress caused by the thermal gradient over
the thickness must be addressed and considered when calculating the time to the first
crack.

2.2.1
Fire stages
Predicting how temperatures developed in the compartment fires are of great significance for the
protection of human life and property [4]. As shown in Figure 2.6, without fire control, the full
phase of a compartment fire generally starts from an ignition stage, followed by the growth or pre┬¡
flashover period. After the flashover occurs, the fire is steady and is ventilation controlled, referred
to as a fully┬¡developed fire or post┬¡flashover fire. After the fuel is burned away or there is a lack of
ventilation, the fire will experience a decay phase. However, this is only an idealized description.
In reality, all fires include the ignition stage, although many may fail before they go through the
whole stages [4].

Post┬¡flashover fire curves
After the flashover, the occupants are all assumed to evacuate successfully, so the structural
integrity, property, and safety of the firefighters or others who offer the rescue service become
essential. Therefore, predicting the temperature history is more important during this stage [19].
The post┬¡flashover fires are ventilation controlled in compartments with small or medium sizes.
Still, when there is a big opening and a limited combustible surface area, the post┬¡flashover fires
can become fuel controlled [21]. Post┬¡flashover fire curves are temperature┬¡time curves composed
of nominal design and parametric fires. The nominal design fires increase monotonically and do
not include the cooling phase. The most common nominal design fire is the ISO 834 standard
fire. However, parametric fires are defined by the fire properties (the fuel load, opening factor,
and thermal inertia of the compartment) and have a cooling phase. For parametric fires, Swedish
parametric fire, Danish parametric fire, and European parametric fire are the most common ones.
In this project, post┬¡flashover fire design curves play a more important role since the post┬¡flashover
fire is ventilation┬¡controlled. When the glazing falls out, the ventilation condition in a compartment
will change, influencing the fire duration, gas temperature, and structural element temperature.

This standard fire curve (Figure 2.8) is an international time┬¡
temperature fire curve with the reference of ISO 834 standard, defined by the formula:

cellulosic materials such as wood. The standard fire is used to evaluate the required time of fire
resistance of the elements when the requirement is expressed as Rclass [22].

Unlike the standard fire, the Swedish fire considers the fire decay phase,

The Swedish fire curves are derived by solving the heat balance equation for the one┬¡zone com┬¡
partment fire model using the burning rate of ventilation┬¡controlled fires [23]. A reference thermal

the fireÔÇÖs peak.

2.3.3
The crack mechanisms of the window glass in fire
Thermal stress is the reason for the crack of glass under fire. Thermal stress is created due to the
change in the temperature of the material, and it can be generated due to thermal shock, thermal
gradient, thermal expansion, and thermal contraction.
In 1986, Professor Emmons [43] first mentioned the mechanism of glass breakage, which gave
a simple explanation of why framed glass breaks in a fire. In his explanation, the non┬¡uniform
thermal expansion puts the edge into tension, and cracks start at the edge and finally extend to
the whole glass pane. Later in 1988, Olavi Keski┬¡Rahkonen [44] proposed three mechanisms that
can cause the breakage of window glass in a fire:
Rapid heating
When one side of the glass pane is suddenly heated by intensive heating (called thermal shock),
there will be a large temperature difference across the thickness of the pane. The thermal shock
and the steep thermal gradient create thermal stress, leading to the crack of the glass.
Boundary conditions
The thermal gradient across the paneÔÇÖs thickness then causes the paneÔÇÖs deformation. However,
the frame of the glazing is strong enough to restrict the movement of the edges of the glazing pane.
The glass pane can have different boundary conditions due to different glass construction . The
boundary conditions can be anywhere between a simply supported edge (edge deflection is zero,
but the slope of the plate can vary depending on the loading) and a clamped edge (edge deflection
and edge slopes are both equal to zero) [45]. These boundary conditions bend the pane, causing
the greatest stresses close to the corners of the plate.
Non┬¡uniform heating
When the glass is without a frame, it is difficult to shatter in fire since it receives uniform heating
and there is no temperature difference between the edge and the center (sometimes it may crack
since the radiation is not entirely uniform) [43]. However, in the present day, normally, glass is used
as a window, and nearly all window is framed with different materials, such as wood, aluminum,
and fiberglass. Thus, when the window glass is exposed to intensive heating due to the non┬¡
uniform thermal expansion, the edge bears the tensile force, resulting in small cracks and, finally,
the fracture of the whole window glass [43].
In Olavi KeskiRahkonenÔÇÖs study, an edge┬¡protected glass pane was exposed to a uniform heat flux
given by thermal radiation, and a heat conduction equation was solved with linearized boundary
conditions to calculate the thermal stress fields, which means the thermal stresses in the pane
were determined by the temperature gradients. KeskiRahkonen then generalized the stress re┬¡
sults at the pane edge since there is where the maximum temperature difference occurs, and the
generalizing results yield:

As shown in section 2.2.2, the heat release rate keeps climbing during the pre┬¡flashover phase.

Though there is a large standard deviation in the glazing samples with a thickness of 8 mm, it can
be observed in Table 4.1 and Figure 4.1 that the first average breakage time increase with the
thickness. The same trend was also observed by Yi Zhang et al. [11] and Yuzhuo Yang et al. [13].
Based on the theory in section 2.1.1, I believe this increasing trend can be explained as thicker
glazing needs more time to conduct the heat to the backside and, therefore more time is required
to reach the critical breakage stress. Furthermore, according to section 2.3.1, larger tensile stress
is needed to open the flaws on the surface when the glazing is thicker. According to Yuzhuo Yang
et al., [13], it can also be explained as the temperature gradient along the glazing length grows
faster in the thinner glazing, resulting in an earlier crack in thinner glazing. It is noted that the
influence of temperature gradient can also influence the crack initiation time of the thicker glazing
and make the thicker glazing break in the initial period. However, since the heat flux is not strong
enough to generate a large temperature gradient over the thickness, even in the study of Yuzhuo
Yang et al. [13], which applied a heat flux of 35 kW/m2, no glazing fell out due to the temperature
gradient over the thickness. Therefore this influence can be neglected.

As mentioned in section 3.5, the center part receives a heat flux of 19.5 kW/m2, while the

pane is more obvious. The edge part will receive less heat flux═¥ therefore, the glazing can
reach the critical temperature difference for breakage more quickly, resulting in a shorter
breakage time.
ÔÇó Larger glazing has more imperfections, which leads to smaller fracture stress.
Glazing with larger sizes includes more flaws on the edge. Therefore, larger glazing is more
likely to have a more severe flow and, thereby smaller fracture stress═¥ thus, the larger glazing
has a smaller critical temperature, leading to an earlier breakage.

It can be observed that the maximum temperature difference decreases with increasing size. The
larger the glass, the larger the tensile stress needed to equilibrate the same compressive stress,
as the compressive stress will act on a larger area. This means that the tensile stress is larger
for larger panes for the same heating temperature of the exposed glass. This also means that
the same tensile stress will be reached for lower heating temperatures when the pane is larger. If
we consider this tensile stress to be the glass strength, the larger glass pane will break at lower
temperatures (and therefore earlier).
Another consideration concerns the trend of the temperature┬¡size curve, which decreases less
than linearly. This is because the effect explained above is mitigated by the fact that compressive
stresses in the glass depend on the part of the thermal expansion, which is hindered by the cold
glass. Since the shading width (cold part) is always 20 mm, the degree of the expansion that this
cold part can hinder is lower for larger panes with a larger heated area (see section 2.5.3).

Finite element modelling
Mechanic problems are governed by a set of partial differential equations that are valid in a certain
domain, and these equations need to be solved to evaluate the stress condition of mechanical
components [65]. Although analytic methods can be employed to solve linear problems involving
partial differential equations, their use to analyze complex structures may be challenging [65].
However, the finite element method is a popular numerical method for solving partial differential
equations in two or three space variables.
To solve a problem, the FEM subdivides a large system into smaller, simpler parts called finite
elements, which is achieved by constructing a mesh of the object. The PDEs are usually solved
in each finite element by assuming basis functions that interpolate the unknown variables over the
finite element. The simple equations that model these finite elements are then assembled into a
larger system of equations that models the entire problem. The FEM then approximates a solution
by minimizing an associated error function via the calculus of variations.
In this project, the finite element method was conducted in Ansys 2022 R2. The aim of applying
FEM is to investigate how tensile stress changes with increasing size and compare the FEM results
with the analytical method and experimental results.
The properties are assumed to be temperature independent. The definition of glazing properties
in Ansys 2022 R2 is shown below in Figure 5.1:

6
Conclusion
6.1
Highlights
A series of experiments were conducted to investigate the influence of varied thicknesses and
sizes on the breakage of the glazing under the same fire exposure.
The glazing surface receives radiant heat from H┬¡TRIS. A heat flux range of 15 kW/m2 to 20
kW/m2 is decided as this heat flux level can cause a flashover, ensuring the fallout of glazing. After
calibration, the heat flux applied on the center of the glazing surface is 19.5 kW/m2, with a 430 mm
distance from the glazing surface to the radiant panel. In total, 42 experiments were conducted.
Float glazing toughened, and laminated glazing were investigated with varied thicknesses. Four
different thicknesses were included in the study: 3 mm, 4 mm, 6 mm, and 8 mm, while for float
glazing, five varied sizes were also studied: 200 mm ├ù 200 mm, 200 mm ├ù 400 mm, 300 mm ├ù
300 mm, 400 mm ├ù 400 mm and 500 mm ├ù 500 mm.
Generally speaking, the results clearly illustrate that the thickness and size influence the glazing
breakage. For fire engineers, it is suggested to pay attention when the thickness of the windows
is increasing, or the size of windows is decreasing. That is because thicker glazing or smaller
glazing can delay the breakage time, and they may fall out after the flashover. Therefore, the
design method will be wrong if such windows are counted as vents in the fire design. Furthermore,
toughened and laminated glazing with the parameters investigated in this project should not be
considered vents when designing a fire.
Float glazing
The crack initiation time, fracture stress, and critical temperature difference increase with the in┬¡
creasing thicknesses, that is because when the glazing is thicker, a larger tensile stress is needed
to open the flaws at the glazing edge, and thicker glazing needs more time to reach the critical tem┬¡
perature gradient. However, the crack initiation time and critical temperature at the first crack time
decrease with the increasing sizes═¥ that is because the larger glazing needs more time to reach
the critical tensile stress, and non┬¡uniform heating can also have some influence. The last reason
can be larger glazing has more imperfections, resulting in smaller fracture stress and, therefore,
earlier breakage.
The fracture stress of float glazing has an average value of around 43.57 MPa with varied thick┬¡
nesses and sizes.
Nearly all float glazing with varied thicknesses and sizes fell out at the first breakage time. Thicker
glazing tends to have a smaller fallout percentage at the crack initiation time, while no trend is
found between fallout percentage and sizes.
Toughened glazing
The apparent tensile strength of toughened glazing is around 220 MPa, and the critical breakage
temperature difference is 2.5 times to 3.5 times higher compared with float glazing with equivalent
thickness.
No cracks and fallout were observed in toughened glazing since the toughened glazing can not
reach the critical temperature difference under the given experimental setup. Thus, if this tough┬¡
ened glazing can be used as window glazing in the building compartment, this window glazing
would not provide ventilation to the compartment during a fire.
Laminated glazing
Laminated glazing can not be regarded as float glazing with a thickness equal to the sum of the
two glass layers. The lamination foil has a big influence in delaying the cracking onset, increasing
the critical temperature difference, and avoiding glass fallout.
Due to the PVB bonding, the laminated glass remained intact after the crack, and no fallout was
observed, making it capable of preventing new vents from forming in fires. Therefore, the lam┬¡
inated glazing with the parameters investigated in this project should not be considered a vent
during the fire design process.

6.2
Future research
ÔÇó More tests should be conducted at different positions of glazing panes to understand better
how heat flux varies over the glazing surface due to various view factors, which will be
essential, especially when performing experiments with different sizes.
ÔÇó A better temperature measurement method needs to be applied. In this current study, the
positions of TCs inside the frame can not be directly seen and can not be ensured whether
they are at the right place, which results in an inaccuracy in the temperature measurement.
ÔÇó Float glazing is suggested to be exposed to fire until the exposed area falls out completely
to investigate and compare the whole fallout process with varied parameters. Furthermore,
room pressure should be considered since it can influence the fallout percentage.
ÔÇó Although how the thickness and size influence the glazing breakage has been figured out,
a correlation between these two parameters and time to breakage is worthy of being estab┬¡
lished in the future, and a possible relation between these two parameters with the open┬¡
ing factor is expected to be built, which can be used for the current parametric fire design
method.
ÔÇó Rectangular glazing is more common than square glazing in real life. They can be placed
both vertically and horizontally. With an irregular shape, the breakage time can differ from
the square shape when considering non┬¡uniform heating. Different position directions can
also exert an impact on the breakage behavior. Therefore, it is suggested to do further
research regarding this part.
ÔÇó Laminated glazing can generate toxic smoke during exposure. The amount of smoke from
the burning glazing should be quantified since the toxic gas impacts occupant safety.
ÔÇó Different experimental setups should be applied to investigate the fall out of toughened
glazing with different parameters since the toughened glazing falls out entirely when any
parts break and a large new vent can suddenly be formed.
ÔÇó According to the latest Danish regulation, only triple┬¡layered glazing can fulfil the U┬¡value
requirement. Compared with double┬¡layered glazing, triple┬¡layered glazing is even more
difficult to fall out. Therefore, more research should be conducted on this type of glazing
with different parameters.
