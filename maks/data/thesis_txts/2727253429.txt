ABSTRACT 
With an increasing need for subsurface development in civilization, a new metro line “Cityringen 
branch off to Sydhavn is under construction led by Metroselskabet. The metro line will go through 
Paleogene Copenhagen limestone, which is characterized by variably indurated beds formed by 
the process of cementation in sedimentation history. Layers or nodules of highly indurated flint 
are also observed in Copenhagen limestone. These attributes make characterization and 
determination of rock properties more difficult, and there is always uncertainty on the inputs of a 
physics model. In this case, a probabilistic model describing the uncertainty on the parameters, 
and a multivariate model additionally describing the cross correlations between available test 
results from UCS test and Brazilian test, are of great interest. The current report describes the 
process of developing a multivariate model for the characterization of Copenhagen limestone. The 
main steps include analyzing experimental data and interpreting mechanical properties, 
establishing the database, analyzing data using statistics and describing uncertainty on the 
variables by probability theory, while in the end developing cross correlation and a multivariate 
PDF. The database includes geological descriptions concluded from borehole logs and reports, 
index (density, water content, void ratio, etc.) and mechanical parameters (strengths and stiffnesses) 
from UCS and Brazilian test. In addition, rocks like flint, bryozoan limestone, and chalk found in 
several boreholes are also characterized, interpreted, and discussed in comparison with 
Copenhagen limestone. The database is further combined with respect to borehole numbers, the 
depths of extracted specimen, and bulk densities. The database is also used to validate the model 
generated in this project and the model created by E. Panagiotis based on Hoek-Brown criterion 
and the triaxial test results, as well as to provide the material constants in the tensile zone. Two 
commonly used correlation coefficients are used, and the performance is discussed based on the 
results. Box-Cox transformation is used to transform data from physical space into normal space. 
Ten thousand data are generated and transformed back to their physical space using the power 
parameter obtained to validate the multivariate model. Moreover, a similar multivariate modelling 
is carried out for separated Database C with respect to three bulk density ranges to compare the 
results with the initial one that include all data points and evaluate the performance of this method. 
The material constant 𝑚𝑖 of Hoek-Brown criterion in tensile zone is obtained and validated, and 
the failure envelope in tension zone is completed. The correlation coefficient and multivariate 
PDFs present a good correlation among index and mechanical properties as well as the variety of 
Copenhagen limestone. The results will also contribute to a better understanding of the uncertainty 
on those properties and will serve as good references for the planning and design of metro line 
projects in Copenhagen area and further subsurface exploitation in the future. 
Keywords:  Copenhagen limestone, rock characterization, correlation, probabilistic modelling, 
multivariate modelling.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Background 
As an increasing need of transportation in urban areas, the development and utilization of 
subsurface space are always ongoing in Copenhagen City. Recently, a 4.4 km-long 
underground line based on Citytingen that is already in operation, is under construction in the 
Sydhavn area (Figure 1.1). Currently, the process of building five metro stations in Sydhavn is 
ongoing. When the metro stations in Sydhavn open in 2024, the southern part of Copenhagen 
will be connected to the metro network and a stronger public transport network will be created. 
This project is carried out in relation to the Sydhavn metro line and the recent construction of 
Cityringen and aims at providing useful information for the underground constructions in the 
Copenhagen area. 
 
Figure 1.1. Sydhavn Metro Line. Cf. hmeng.gr. 
Copenhagen is located in the eastern part of the Danish-Norwegian Basin with a WNW-ESE 
trend. The basin consists of 10 km of deposits of mainly Mesozoic limestone and chalk, and it 
is constricted by the Ringkøbing-Fyn fault zone where large-scale block faulting along several 
major faults took place (Jakobsen et al. 2015). These faults were active during the late 
Cretaceous to early Paleogene (around 66 Mya). The limestone was raised by the NW-SE 
trending Carlsberg fault, which is located about 40 km west of the Sorgenfri-Tornquist zone 
(Fallesen 1995), and it is gently folded to the east of the fault along WNW to NW trending axis. 
The area was lifted and around 500 m of Paleogene deposits were eroded during Neogene 
(23.02-2.58 Mya). Several glaciers from N, NE, and SE eroded into the surface of limestone 
during Weichselian and caused the folding of subjacent glacial deposits (Hansen et al. 2015). 
The geological ground conditions of this project are dominated by the bedrock of Paleogene 
limestone. The limestone is deposited in a shallow marine environment and contains the 
remains of planktonic organisms with diagenetically precipitated calcite crystals.

Copenhagen limestone is a carbonate rock dominantly composed of calcite (Katic et al., 2015). 
One of the most commonly used classification parameters of Copenhagen limestone is the 
induration. The induration of Copenhagen limestone varies from H2 to H4 due to different 
degrees of silicification and cementation, and layers and nodules of highly indurated flint 
(generally H5) can be observed in limestone (Hansen et al. 2015). The carbonate content in 
most of the Copenhagen limestone is above 95%, which can be very muddy and soft, and this 
leads to varying induration. The more elastic and strong flint is made of micro-crystalline 
quartz through heating of stabilized opal-CT, and it is irregularly spread in the Copenhagen 
limestone and causes further variation on rock classification and mechanical properties (Katic 
et al., 2014). 
Rocks, including Copenhagen limestone, are a favorable medium for tunneling, but it is 
difficult to characterize and determine relevant properties of rock mass (Hansen et al. 2015). 
On the one hand, the geological history mentioned before determined the inhomogeneity of 
rock, and fractures and discontinuities also result in anisotropy and spatial variability of the 
rock mass. On the other hand, the ground characterization techniques that are based on borehole 
logging and field testing can rarely illustrate the real in-situ conditions of rocks, which may 
cause a higher degree of fracturing and core loss. Some researchers have classified rock mass 
using face logging, and some also tried to obtain applicable relations among strengths and 
elastic properties on the Copenhagen limestone. Foged et al. in 2008 argued that the rock 
classification of Copenhagen limestone based on in-situ observations like televiewer logging 
or face logging yields RQD values 3~5 times larger than obtained from traditional core logging. 
Jakobsen et al. and Hansen et al. in 2015 also used face logging and stated that face logging 
provided a better estimation compared to core logging. They also used the Rock Mass Rating 
system (RMR) to classify the rock mass of Copenhagen limestone. RMR was developed by 
Bieniawski (1973) and utilizes six parameters—UCS, RQD, spacing of discontinuities, 
condition of discontinuities, groundwater conditions, and orientation of discontinuities. The 
classification is based on the RMR value by summing up six parameters. According to their 
face logging results and RMR, Copenhagen limestone was interpreted as “Good rock” of which 
the RMR value is between 61 and 80. By using the traditional RQD system, the rock mass 
cannot be considered good rock due to drilling disturbance and core loss. Katic et al. in 2014 
and 2015 presented a combined interpretation of laboratory and field testing of a highly 
variable Copenhagen limestone, and derived rock mass moduli utilizing Hoek and Brown 
model. They also remarked that in addition to the bulk density and the induration, the size of 
the specimen and the measuring technique also affected the moduli of elasticity. Instead of 
using the representative generalized length, the characteristic length is used. The characteristic 
length is calculated by the cubic root of the volume of the cylindrical specimen. They analyzed 
elastic moduli of different characteristic lengths of specimen categorized by induration H2-H4, 
and they found that as the characteristic length increases the mean value of the moduli tends to 
decrease for each induration group except for the 95% fractile for H4 because H5 was enclosed 
in H4.  The correlation between characteristic length and elastic moduli is not strong because 
a large scatter is observed for characteristic lengths within 6 cm, and different measures are 
used for different characteristic lengths.  
Since the last century, many deterministic equations have been created and used to link the 
physical and mechanical properties of rock. Two famous equations are Gassmann’s equation 
(Gassmann, 1951) and Hashin-Shtrikman's (1963) bounds. The former allows the prediction of

saturated bulk moduli and shear moduli of isotropic porous rock, the latter gives the tightest 
bounds for the composite moduli of a two-phase material. A higher porosity will lead to a lower 
bulk density, and lower strength and stiffness. However, there is always uncertainty in the 
inputs of a physics model and such uncertainty results in a variation in outputs. To describe the 
uncertainty of inputs and make extreme outputs considered, the probability theories are applied, 
where the uncertainty can be expressed by a distribution function that can be mathematically 
applied in deterministic equations. Grana in 2014 combined commonly used Gassmann’s 
equation, with the uncertain porosity and saturation, the latter being described by assumed 
probability distributions like the normal distribution. In the end, a probability distribution 
function for bulk modulus is obtained analytically. Ching & Phoon (2018) constructed a 
multivariate probability density function (PDF) for nine intact rock parameters based on the 
ROCK/9/4069 database to capture the correlations, and the prior PDF is updated with site-
specific information by Bayesian analysis. Liu et al. (2022) applied multivariate probability 
distribution functions and obtained the correlation among resilient modulus and cone 
penetration test parameters of cohesive subgrade soil. Panagiotis et al. (2022) constructed two 
multivariate models for stiff clay till in Copenhagen, one based on triaxial and soil 
classification tests while the other based on oedometer and soil classification. In the works by 
the authors mentioned above, all selected parameters were converted to a standard normal 
space using one of the transformation models like Johnson transformation, Box-cox 
transformation, or Nataf transformation. 
1.2 Objectives and Process 
The forementioned multivariate probabilistic analysis procedure and method provide great 
potential for modelling dependencies among parameters. Copenhagen limestone is the 
dominant rock in the construction of metro lines in Copenhagen area, and there do exist 
uncertainties in the characterization and determination of mechanical properties. A multivariate 
model not only enables a distribution for the uncertainty but also illustrates the correlation 
among parameters.  In this case, a probabilistic model that describes the uncertainty of 
properties of Copenhagen limestone and considers a multivariate model describing the 
correlation among those properties is of great interest. The purpose of the project is to evaluate 
the factors that influence the mechanical properties of Copenhagen limestone and construct a 
probabilistic model for the correlated rock properties.  
To achieve the objectives, a database needs to be established first. The database is constructed 
based on the available data provided by Metroselskabet. The work of available data is part of 
the extensive ground investigation from campaigns carried out as part of the construction of 
two metro lines—M3 and M4. Theories regarding ground investigation, laboratory tests, 
statistics and correlations, univariate and multivariate probability distribution, and failure 
criteria for rocks are applied. 
The main steps of analysis include: 
1. Collect data from ground investigation reports and laboratory tests. 
2. Reinterpret the mechanical properties of rock specimens to evaluate test results.  
3. Construct 2 databases for the UCS test and Brazilian test respectively, and combine two 
databases (A and B) and arrange merged datapoints into a new database (C). 
4. Use statistics theory to analyze the central tendency and dispersion of data in Database 
A, B, and combined Database C.

2 Theory 
2.1 Ground investigation and rock characterization 
Ground investigation is usually the first step to obtain the information of subsurface and 
provides firsthand data for the database of an area. This section provides the basic theory of 
ground investigation and rock characterization, including currently used ground investigation 
techniques, and the guidelines of operations, tests, and descriptions.  Techniques including 
borehole logging and geophysical logging are described, and the geological description 
regarding rock samples is reviewed based on the knowledge of engineering geology.  
2.1.1 Boreholes 
A borehole is a narrow shaft bored into the ground. It can be in different directions. The 
borehole is an important part of a geotechnical investigation because it is a widely used method 
to collect soil, water, and rock samples, or to install monitoring wells or piezometers. 
Subsequently, the sample can be tested and classified, and the properties of soil or rock can be 
interpreted to provide information for engineering. The drilling process and completion, as well 
as the transportation and preservation, must be following related standards. The machinery and 
techniques to drill a borehole may vary according to the manufacturer, geological conditions, 
and intended purpose.  
2.1.2 Geophysical logging 
Geophysical logging is the practice of making a detailed record of the geologic formations 
penetrated by a borehole (Wiki, 2023). The log is based on physical measurements made by 
instruments lowered into the borehole. Geophysical logging can be performed in boreholes 
drilled for ground resource exploration and geotechnical studies. 
The equipment includes measuring probes, a winch with logging cables, depth meters, and a 
recording system and software. Different probes have different measurement functions, for 
example, a gamma-gamma probe is used to measure the density of formation, and a neutron-
neutron probe is used for the porosity of formation. The field journal of logging consists of 
administrative information (borehole no., date, etc.), the location of the groundwater table, 
specifications on probes and pump, remarks to the logging program, and the drillers’ notes on 
casing or screen settings. For the Sydhavn metro line project, the probes including 3-arm 
caliper, Focused guard resistivity, Gamma-gamma, Neutron-neutron, Temperature or 
Conductivity for borehole fluid, Induction, Optical televiewer, and HiRAT acoustic televiewer, 
have been used according to ground investigation reports. 
2.1.3 Geological description 
All geological descriptions were carried out in the laboratory in accordance with standards in 
DGF-Bulleting No.1. The descriptions on rock samples include the type of rock, environment, 
age, fissuring, induration, total core recovery (TCR), rock quality designation (RQD), and color 
photos of the intact samples. 
Regarding rock samples, the geological description includes: 
1. Describe the texture of grains for the classification of igneous rock. 
2. Observe if there are any signs of metamorphic microstructure like foliation or mineral 
growth.

2.1.4 Classification test 
The classification tests are used for the determination of moisture content, unit weight, and

2.1.5 In-situ test 
In-situ tests were carried out in geotechnical boreholes. Standard penetration tests (SPTs) for 
every 1m in sandy and clayey layers according to standards in DGF Bulleting 14. During core 
drilling in limestone, the temporary 8” steel casing was advanced into the upper layer of the 
rock formation to secure the recirculation of the drilling fluid. The cores were retrieved down 
to the requested depth. A single core run is maximum 15 m long. The length is adjusted to suit 
the drilled formation to assure the highest possible core recovery.

2.2 Mechanical testing 
Mechanical testing is to determine the mechanical properties of a specimen. There are two most 
common mechanical properties regarding a rock specimen—Strength and Stiffness. The 
strength of rock is its ability to withstand the applied load without failure or plastic deformation, 
and the stiffness describes the stress-strain relationship showing the resistance of rock to the 
deformation in response to the applied force. In this section, the theory and methodology of 
several widely used mechanical testing methods for rock specimens are introduced based on 
corresponding standards and guidelines. For this project, the Brazilian splitting test and 
unconfined compressive strength test are used, but several other testing methods like the 
triaxial test and direction extension test are also mentioned in this part. 
2.2.1 Brazilian splitting tensile strength test 
The Brazilian test is an indirect method to measure the static and dynamic tensile strength of a 
rock specimen (Figure 2.1).  
According to ISRM suggested method: Part 2, the geometry of the specimen is a circular disk, 
and the diameter is related to the average grain size by the least ratio—10:1 or is close to 50 
mm. The thickness should be approximately equal to the radius of the specimen. The specimen 
should be cut and prepared with clean water, while obvious tool marks and any irregularities 
are not allowed. Additionally, two end surfaces should be parallel and flat. 
In the static test of the Brazilian test, the disk is loaded in two special steel jaws. These jaws 
are designed to be an arc of contact of 10° at the failure of the disk. The ultimate stress shown 
in Figure 2.1 is considered the Brazilian splitting tensile strength of rock. The tensile stress at

In this section, the theory of UCS test introduced by ASTM is explained. Apart from ASTM 
D7012, ISRM suggested method and DGF Bulleting 15 are also widely used standard test 
methods for UCS test.

According to ASTM D7012—Standard Test Methods for Compressive Strength and Elastic 
moduli of Intact Rock, four methods named A, B, C, D are introduced. Methods A and B 
determine triaxial compressive strength, while methods C and D determine the unconfined 
uniaxial strength. Furthermore, method C is used for strengths of intact rock core specimens 
while method D is used for elastic moduli where a stress-strain curve is produced. 
The core specimen is in the form of a cylindrical shape. It is cut to length and the ends are 
machined flat. The desirable length-to-diameter ratio is between 2.0 and 2.5, while this ratio 
suggested by ISRM is between 2.5 and 3.0. The specimen is installed into a frame to apply the 
confining stress. If needed, the temperature is also changed before the test. The axial load is 
increased and measured continuously, and the axial stress can be calculated by dividing the 
axial load by the cross-section area of the specimen. The deformation or strain is measured by 
strain gauge (SG) or LVDT when available. The design of strain measurement devices shall be 
at least two, so that an average value can be determined. Usually, two strain gauges are mounted 
at the side surface of the core specimen in the axial and lateral directions to capture the axial 
strain and radial strain. When the specimen is deformed, the length of the strain gauge changes, 
and the resistance changes. The electrical signal of the change is captured in the program and 
converted to the strain using the strain gauge factor. The LVDT, a linear variable differential 
transformer, is also an electrical transformer for measuring linear displacement. At least two 
channels of each measurement should be prepared, and axial strain by LVDT is calculated by 
dividing average deformation by the original length of the specimen.

2.2.3 Other rock mechanical tests 
1) Triaxial shear test 
The triaxial shear test is also a very common test to determine the mechanical properties of 
rock specimens. “Triaxial” here means that apart from vertically applied stress, the fluid 
pressure is also applied to the specimen in the perpendicular directions. During the test, the 
fluid is pressurized, and the stress applied by the platens is increased until the specimen reaches 
failure. It can be considered that UCS is a special case of the triaxial test when confining stress 
is zero. There are three basic types of triaxial test—unconsolidated undrained test (UU), 
consolidated undrained test (CU), and consolidated drained test (CD). The shear strength in the 
first type is measured in terms of total stress, while in the other two types, it is measured in 
terms of effective stress. The triaxial test usually goes through three stages—Saturation, 
Consolidation, and Shear. The consolidation can be isotropic or anisotropic, by increasing the 
cell pressure and maintaining constant back pressure. The drained conditions are controlled by 
allowing or not allowing pore pressure to develop. Along with the obtained strength (deviatoric 
stress at failure), the failure envelope and parameters, for example, friction angle and cohesion 
of Mohr-Coulomb criterion, which describes the critical state at different stress states (like 
different confining stress) can also be obtained. In addition, the transducers that are pre-
mounted before the test allow the deformation data to be recorded and used for the 
interpretation of stiffness. The triaxial test is also used to determine the shear resistance of a 
discontinuity.

2.4.1 Descriptive statistics 
Descriptive statistics is a summary statistic quantitatively describing features from a collection

the dispersion, like the range, quantile, variance, standard deviation, percentile, etc. In addition, 
to describe the shape of the distribution, the skewness and kurtosis are also important.

value of the two middle values.

2.4.5 Central Limit Theorem and Data Transformation 
The Central Limit Theorem states that the probability distribution for the sum of a number of 
random variables approaches the Normal distribution as the number becomes large. According 
to Faber (2014), “large” implies that the sum may not be dominated by one or a few random 
variables. If the distributions are skewed, which is very common in engineering, the required 
number of variables in the sum for the theorem increases. 
The transformation of the data from their physical space to normal space is required, to meet 
the assumption of normality and decrease the effect of homoscedasticity. One of the well-

in this study, the original one-parameter version of the Box-Cox transformation is applied (Eq.

2.4.7 Positive definite correlation matrix 
The eigen value of 𝑪 contains only positive values if and only if 𝑪 is positive definite (Ching 
& Phoon, 2014). The correlation matrix 𝑪 of transformed datasets may not be positive definite, 
so a bootstrapping technique introduced by Efron and Tibshirani (1993) can be used to obtain 
a positive definite correlation matrix. The idea is to obtain a large number of correlation 
coefficients for the random samples of transformed data, and then the 5% and 95% percentiles 
and the median of 𝛿𝑖𝑗 based on these samples are extracted. By checking the eigenvalues, 
whether the correlation matrix is positive definite is determined. According to Ching & Phoon 
(2014), the mean values of accepted 𝛿𝑖𝑗 is very similar to the median values, so the median 
value from 𝛿𝑖𝑗 sampling can be representative and positive definite in their case.

2.5 State of the art in multivariate modelling of subsurface 
This section lists four recently applied multivariate models for soil and rock, which provide 
valuable references for this research project. The database and the main methodology that is 
used in their study are introduced. Then the results like correlation coefficients and marginal 
distribution of each parameter are discussed. Firstly, a multivariate probability distribution for 
some intact rock properties studied by Ching et al. in 2018 is explained in detail. The pivotal 
results of this study are also shown in tables and figures. For the other three studies, which are 
studies of soil from different regions, the figures are not shown but are still discussed as the 
process of analysis is similar. The difference mainly lies in the database and the techniques that 
they used to deal with datasets and the construction of the multivariate model.   
To begin with, Ching et al. constructed a multivariate probability distribution model for nine 
intact rock properties, including unit weight (𝛾), porosity (𝑛), L-type Schmidt hammer hardness 
(𝑅L), Shore scleroscope hardness (𝑆h), Brazilian tensile strength (𝜎bt), point load strength index 
(𝐼s50), uniaxial compressive strength (𝜎𝑐), Young’s modulus (𝐸), and P-wave velocity (𝑉𝑝). In 
the analysis, parameters (𝜎bt, 𝐼s50, 𝜎𝑐, 𝐸, 𝑉𝑝) are applied using their natural logarithmic values 
due to the pretty wide range of datasets. This paper also contributes to summarizing useful 
posterior PDFs in tables presented in this paper for the design properties of intact rock. This 
allows practitioners to look up these tables to find posterior PDFs of design parameters. 
The database they used is named ROCK/9/4069, which consists of 4069 intact rock data points 
for 9 parameters mentioned above from 184 studies. The mean, COV (Coefficient of variance), 
minimum value, maximum value, and percentile value of 1%, 5%, 95%, and 99%, were 
calculated. The database includes data points of igneous rock, sedimentary rock, and 
metamorphic rock, so mechanical properties in this database cover a wide range. In this case, 
the last five parameters mentioned above took their natural logarithm values for analysis.

3 Database 
This chapter describes the structure of databases that are constructed for the study, as well as 
the key methodologies used in the database construction. Metroselskabet provides all the 
information for this project, including all the available raw data sheets, borehole logs, and 
relative in-situ classification reports. To begin with, an overview of the raw database is 
described. “Raw” means that this database is directly provided and contains test raw data and 
interpretations by other technicians. To meet the need of this study, data from other types of 
rock and invalid results are removed, and all mechanical properties are re-interpreted. Database 
A for Brazilian test results and Database B for UCS test results are constructed. One main rock 
type is Copenhagen limestone (CL), which is the object of the study. Bryozoan limestone (BL) 
is kept in the database, but only Copenhagen limestone will be considered for analysis. The 
difference between the two will be discussed in the chapter of discussion. The two databases 
include classification parameters, physical properties, and mechanical properties of 
Copenhagen limestone, and a combined database (denoted as C) is established for the 
multivariate analysis.

4 Methodology 
This section introduces the procedure of the analysis and how the theory described in Section 
2 is applied to this project. The main steps include: 1) Collect data, re-interpret rock properties, 
and construct database; 2) Calculate statistics and describe the central tendency, dispersion, 
and distribution of datasets, visually and quantitatively; 3) Illustrate and evaluate the 
correlations among datasets using scatter plots and correlation coefficients; 4) Construct a 
multivariate model for Copenhagen limestone using obtained marginal distributions and 
correlations; 5) Validate this multivariate model by simulation; 6) Validate another available 
multivariate model based on an independent database of CL; 7) Estimate the material constant 
𝑚𝑖 and complete Hoek-Brown failure envelope in tension zone. To achieve the goal of the 
study, 2 multivariate models are constructed for a better understanding of the probabilistic 
modelling, 1 multivariate model based on an independent database of triaxial test results is 
validated using the database obtained in this study, and method selection and implementation 
are discussed with supervisors of this project. 
4.1 Construction of Database 
The methodology of constructing the databases is described in Chapter 3. The quality of the 
database has a determinative effect on the probabilistic modelling for Copenhagen limestone. 
A different database used can lead to different results like different marginal distributions and 
different degrees of correlation. On the other hand, the results of modelling can in turn test the 
quality of the database in the end and provide useful ideas for improvement for database 
construction.  
4.2 Statistics and Probabilistic modelling 
For the start of the analysis, descriptive statistics of Database A, B, and C are calculated based 
on the theory in 2.4.1. The mean, median, maximum, minimum, variance, coefficient of 
variance, and percentiles (1%, 5%, 95%, 99%) are included. The correlation coefficients based 
on Pearson’s and Spearman’s theory are calculated, and the correlation matrix based on their 
theories are constructed. 
The scatter plots and correlation coefficients described in 2.4.2 are used to illustrate the 
relationships between mechanical parameters and index parameters. For Database A and B, 𝜎𝑐𝑖, 
𝜎𝑡, 𝐸50,tan
𝑆𝐺
, and 𝐸50,tan

the correlation of interest, a scatter plot is also a very useful tool to visualize the error of 
generated data by the simulation model. Curve fitting (2.4.3) is applied when a quantitative 
relationship is needed for model validation. For example, a 2-parameter exponential function 
is used to quantify the relationship between UCS and bulk density, then a linear regression is 
made for the UCS-tensile strength relationship. The effect of specimen size on the mechanical 
properties of rock is also investigated by scatter plots based on Database A and B. The upper 
and lower bounds of the data are also stated.  
Histograms are used to describe the distribution of variables in Database A, B, and C. For 
Database A and B, the tensile strength and UCS of CL and BL are plotted into three groups, 
corresponding to three ranges of bulk density: ≤2.10 g/cm3 , 2.10~2.40 g/cm3 , and >
2.40 g/cm3. The marginal distribution functions for 12 variables in Database C are estimated 
based on the histograms obtained. The calculated bin number is 6 or 7, corresponding to 59 
data points. The bin number is not fixed, and it is sometimes modified for a favorable

distribution shape. Distributions like Weibull, Lognormal, Gamma, Extreme Value, 
Generalized Extreme Value, and Normal distribution are taken into consideration. The 
goodness of fit is tested by the 2-test, which is explained in 2.4.3 and 2.4.4. The Distribution 
Fitting APP provided by MATLAB is a very useful tool to visualize the goodness of fitting 
and estimate the parameters of each distribution function. In addition, the 2-test for goodness 
of fitting is implemented by the command chi2gof, where the observed dataset and the tested 
distribution function extracted from the APP are compared. The goodness of distribution fitting 
is also evaluated based on the tail of a distribution function. For example, the bulk density of 
rock can rarely be lower than 1.5 g/cm3, and water content and porosity cannot be lower than 
zero. If the tail of the distribution function covers the area mentioned above, the prediction of 
the value based on the distribution function should not be considered. 
Before constructing a multivariate PDF, the data in Database C is transformed from their 
physical space to normal space using Box-Cox transformation (2.4.5). This is done by 
implementing the boxcox command, where a vector of transformed data and a power 𝜆 are 
obtained for each variable. The quality of transformation is detected by eye and the SW test 
described in Section 2.5. In addition, the natural logarithm of datasets is tried based on the 
suggestion by Ching et al. (2018) A multivariate model with 12 variables could be overstaffed, 
so seven representative variables from Database C are considered. Based on the Box-Cox 
transformation model and the lower triangular Cholesky decomposition of the transformed 
correlation matrix, 10,000 data are generated for each variable and transformed back to their 
physical space. The generated datasets are compared with the observed datasets to evaluate the 
multivariate model based on different correlation theories.  
The thesis also gives a try to carry out similar multivariate modelling for separated Database C 
with respect to three bulk density ranges mentioned before, to compare the results with the 
initial one that includes all data points, and to evaluate the performance of this method. The 
new modelling uses the correlation coefficient that gives the best simulation between Pearson’s 
and Spearman’s coefficient through the previous model. The methodology of new modelling 
is the same, and the results of intermediate steps like Box-Cox transformation parameters and 
distributions of transformed datasets are put in Appendix. Two models with different 
methodologies are compared based on the quality of modelling data distribution, correlation 
between datasets, and engineering practices. 
4.3 Model validation for multivariate model based on triaxial test results of CL 
and Hoek-Brown criterion (Unpublished work) 
A multivariate model based on an independent database comprising triaxial test results of 
Copenhagen limestone has been generated by E. Panagiotis. This model has not been published, 
but it has been accredited by the author to generate data points. The data points include 8 
parameters, including 𝜌bulk, 𝑒0, 𝑉𝑝, 𝑉𝑠, 𝐸50
𝑟𝑒𝑓, 𝜎𝑐𝑖, 𝑚𝑖,𝑐, and depth. The first five parameters are 
concluded from the triaxial test. 𝜎𝑐𝑖, 𝑚𝑖,𝑐 are interpreted by curve fitting based on the Hoek-
Brown criterion. All parameters are separated with respect to bulk density into three groups (≤
2.10 g/cm3, 2.10~2.40 g/cm3, and > 2.40 g/cm3). The reason is that the goodness of fitting 
considering all data points is quite low, but after separating data into three density groups the 
goodness of fitting increased a lot and more reliable 𝜎𝑐𝑖 and 𝑚𝑖,𝑐 were obtained. The modelling 
is conducted individually for three density groups. For each density group, three techniques of 
regression are used, including linear regression, Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC), and

Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE). The reason for using three regression techniques is

transform data into normal space. 1000 data points are generated for each density group, so in 
total 3000 data points are generated for each variable. The generated UCS and void ratio by 
this model are plotted with bulk density, and compared with the observed data from Database

compared to Database A. The 𝑚𝑖 will also be compared to the 𝑚𝑖 based on Database C. 
4.4 Estimation of 𝒎𝒊 in tension zone 
Based on the theory mentioned in 2.3.4, 𝑚𝑖,𝑡, approximated by the ratio of UCS and tensile 
strength, is obtained based on Database C. By plotting 𝑚𝑖,𝑡 and 𝑚𝑖,𝑐, the difference of 𝑚𝑖 in 
tension zone and compression zone can be visualized. A linear relationship between UCS and 
tensile strength can also be analyzed by linear regression. The range and distribution of 
observed 𝑚𝑖,𝑡 will be compared with empirical values proposed by other authors. It needs to 
be noted that the simulated 𝑚𝑖,𝑡 is not simulated directly but calculated by simulated UCS and 
tensile strength. The representative value will be compared to the observations in Database C, 
the generated 𝑚𝑖,𝑐, and literature. 
4.5 Hoek-Brown failure envelope 
The failure envelope of Copenhagen limestone of different bulk densities will be plotted based 
on the obtained 𝑚𝑖. For the compression zone, the 𝑚𝑖,𝑐 generated by Panagiotis’s model is used. 
Regarding tension zone, the tensile strength and 𝑚𝑖,𝑡 generated based on the initial multivariate 
model are used. For better visualization, representative 𝑚𝑖 (like median) corresponding to 
several typical bulk densities are used in the Hoek-Brown criterion. By creating vectors of 
minor principal stress, major principal stress is calculated and plotted. The shape of the failure 
envelope and the typical values on the curve are compared with the literature.

5.8 Material constant 𝒎𝒊𝒄 and 𝒎𝒊𝒕 
The main purpose of this section is to estimate the material constant 𝑚𝑖 in the compression 
zone and tension zone so that the failure envelope of the Hoek-Brown criterion for Copenhagen 
limestone can be further studied. The idea is not only to estimate 𝑚𝑖 just based on the test 
results, but also to investigate the difference between 𝑚𝑖,𝑡 and 𝑚𝑖,𝑐 through simulation. For the 
tension zone, the test results database (Database C) and the related multivariate model are 
already available. For the compression zone, only the multivariate model is available. So, the 
observations in Database C and the simulation of the related multivariate model are compared 
with the simulation results of the multivariate model based on triaxial test results in 
compression zone. It should be noted again that the approximation of 𝑚𝑖,𝑡, as well as the 
simulated 𝑚𝑖,𝑡, is calculated by the ratio of 𝜎𝑐𝑖 and 𝜎𝑡, or simulated 𝜎𝑐𝑖 and simulated 𝜎𝑡. 
Figure 5.19 shows the simulated 𝑚𝑖,𝑡 based on the assumption described in 2.3.4, together with 
the observed 𝑚𝑖,𝑡 calculated from Database C. It is seen that both Pearson’s and Spearman’s 
correlation coefficient simulate 𝑚𝑖,𝑡 well. As for the area of low bulk density, Pearson’s 
correlation coefficient is better because it considers some high 𝑚𝑖,𝑡 and presents a better 
simulation that Spearman’s coefficient does not. More importantly, the representative value of 
𝑚𝑖,𝑡 and 𝑚𝑖,𝑐 need to be decided based on the results shown below. According to Figure 5.19, 
the median value is more representative than the mean, so 6.67 is defined as the representative 
𝑚𝑖,𝑡 for Copenhagen limestone.

indicates a stronger relationship than Pearson’s correlation when the relationship is curvilinear 
and monotonic. The correlation matrix obtained by Ching et al. (2018), which is shown in

that strong and the Spearman’s correlation coefficient is lower than Pearson’s. One reason is

and the database covers three types of rocks which in no doubt results in higher variability 
compared to a regional database.

similar results eventually. According to Liu et al. (2022), 124 data points are available for each 
variable, which is about 2 times larger than Database C of CL. In their case, the hypothesis of 
a normal distribution after transforming data using Box-Cox is acceptable and the quality of 
transformation is good.

6.4 Constant 𝒎𝒊 and Failure envelope 
The material constant 𝑚𝑖 controls the slope of the failure envelope of intact rock. The material 
constant 𝑚𝑖 depends not only on rock type but also on mineral content, foliation, and grain size. 
The related Hoek-Brown criterion is explained in Section 2.3.2. In order to estimate the 
material constant in the tension zone, Database C and the multivariate model constructed in 
this project are used. According to Figure 5.19, the median of observed 𝑚𝑖,𝑡 is 6.67 and the 
mean is 8.32, and the 95% and 5% percentile are 17.14 and 3.04. The error of approximating 
𝑚𝑖,𝑡 by strength ratio is lower than 5% according to Figure 2.8, so it is acceptable to use this 
approximation for 𝑚𝑖,𝑡. The representative value of 𝑚𝑖,𝑡 is the median. The variance of 𝑚𝑖,𝑡 is 
quite large, especially when referring to the empirical value of Micritic limestone and Sparitic 
limestone, which is 7~11 and 8~12 according to Table 2-1. The distribution of 𝑚𝑖,𝑡 obtained 
from Database C is shown in Figure 6.10, where the distribution of 𝑚𝑖,𝑡 is positively skew and 
the mode is smaller than empirical values. One possible reason is that the 𝜎𝑡 obtained by 
Brazilian test is about 10% larger than one obtained by direct tension test, causing the ratio 
𝜎𝑐𝑖/𝜎𝑡 (≈𝑚𝑖,𝑡) smaller than the real value. The simulated 𝑚𝑖,𝑡 is satisfying, and Pearson’s 𝑟𝑥𝑦 
presents a slightly better simulation than Spearman’s 𝑟𝑠. In the low-density area, several 
datapoints with extremely high 𝑚𝑖,𝑡 are observed, the reason is related to the residual UCS with 
zero tensile strength. When tensile strength becomes zero, the 𝑚𝑖,𝑡 becomes nearly infinitely 
high, so when modeling the 𝑚𝑖,𝑡 of rock with low bulk density, a very high 𝑚𝑖,𝑡 can be

7 Conclusion 
A large variation is observed in rock characterization parameters according to Database A and 
B, which are constructed based on site investigations, laboratory classification, and mechanical 
testing. According to the scatter plots and correlation coefficients, strong correlations are found 
between seven selected variables (𝜎𝑐𝑖, 𝜎𝑡, 𝜌bulk, 𝑤, 𝜙, 𝐸50,tan
𝑆𝐺
, 𝐸50,tan
𝐿𝑉𝐷𝑇) and it can be concluded 
that higher induration leads to higher strength and stiffness of intact rock based on three widely 
used percentile values. As the induration increases, the mechanical properties become more 
scattered. The tensile strength of Copenhagen limestone reaches 9 MPa, and the maximum 
unconfined compressive strength reaches 60 MPa. The most representative modulus of 
elasticity of Copenhagen limestone among 4 moduli obtained from testing is the tangent 
modulus measured by strain gauges, and it ranges from 17 GPa to 59 GPa. Bryozoan limestone 
is also observed in the Sydhavn area, and its mechanical properties are also included in the 
database and compared with Copenhagen limestone. It is found that the mechanical behavior 
of bryozoan limestone is very similar to Copenhagen limestone, and higher strength is observed 
for bryozoan limestone with high bulk density. 
A multivariate model is constructed based on the combined database consisting of 59 data 
points of 7 selected parameters, based on estimated marginal distribution functions and 
correlation coefficients of Pearson’s and Spearman’s theory. The model is validated by the 
simulation of ten thousand generated data which are compared with the database. The model 
with Spearman’s coefficient predicts the nonlinear correlations better than Pearson’s, while for 
variables with linear correlations, both methods can work well. The limitation of this model is 
that the tail of the marginal distribution functions is not well controlled, leading to data points 
beyond the bounds of reason during simulation, especially for bulk density and water content. 
By constructing a new multivariate model based on separated Database C corresponding to 
bulk densities, the tail of the variable distribution is better controlled, and the model performs 
a better prediction for the intact rock properties of Copenhagen limestone. The material 
constant 𝑚𝑖 introduced by the Hoek-Brown criterion is investigated. The median value is 6.67, 
and the mean value is 8.32. The median is considered the most representative 𝑚𝑖,𝑡, which is 
lower than the empirical value suggested by Hoek and Brown. It needs to note that the 𝑚𝑖 can 
be extremely high for Copenhagen limestone with low bulk density, and a roughly estimated 
upper bound of 𝑚𝑖,𝑡 is found to correlate with bulk density according to the database and 
simulation. 
The databases constructed in this project also help to validate the multivariate model 
constructed by E. Panagiotis. Similarly, 3000 generated data points (1000 per density group) 
based on three types of regression models are compared with the database. Firstly, the MLE 
model predicts the unconfined compressive strength best, and all three models predict the 
reference stiffness well for Copenhagen limestone. Secondly, the multivariate model does not 
predict several specimens with high strength and stiffness, and the index properties like bulk 
density, water content, and void ratio are slightly overestimated by this model. In addition, 
according to the generated 𝑚𝑖, the 𝑚𝑖 in compression zone is significantly different from that 
in tension zone. Based on the Hoek-Brown criterion, the failure envelope in tension zone is 
completed. It has to be noted that there is not a single 𝑚𝑖 or failure envelope that is feasible for 
CL. They are correlated to properties that can be highly variable and should always be analyzed 
from the viewpoint of probability.

8 Future studies and acknowledgement 
Regarding the limitations of the multivariate model that is constructed in this project, additional 
techniques for controlling the tails of marginal distributions need to be studied. It is also of 
great interest to separate datasets according to degrees of induration and do multivariate 
modelling with similar procedures. Bayesian updating techniques and other useful 
transformation models can be studied and implemented for a better performance in multivariate 
analysis. The stress state of the Hoek-Brown failure criterion in the tension zone needs further 
study, and the tension cut-off suggested by Griffith's theory is also valuable to discuss.  
As more subsurface projects are carried out in the Copenhagen area, the database will be more 
robust and sufficient for further multivariate analysis to simulate the uncertainties of 
Copenhagen limestone properties better in the future.
