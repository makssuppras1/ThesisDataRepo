Riassunto
Nel mondo, 2.6 miliardi di persone non hanno accesso a fonti pulite per cucinare
gli alimenti e devono utilizzare combustibili inquinanti.
La combustione in foco-
lai e stufe ineﬀicienti rilascia in atmosfera gas serra ed inquinanti di breve durata
(SLCPs), che impattano l’ecosistema, la biodiversità e la salute umana. In partico-
lare, l’inquinamento dell’aria all’interno degli edifici provoca 4.3 milioni di morti ogni
anno.
Le risorse di energia rinnovabile assumono un ruolo cruciale nel costruire un futuro
sostenibile; tra di esse, l’energia solare è considererata la più promettente.
L’applicazione di materiali in cambiamento di fase nell’ accumulo termico è una
soluzione adeguata per ottimizzare la flessibilità di sistemi, dove vi è discrepanza
tra la disponibiltà di energia e la relativa domanda, come nelle fonti rinnovabili.
La tesi è stata svolta in collaborazione con il gruppo di ricerca ”Solar Heating Group”
del Dipartimento di Ingegneria Civile, presso l’Università Tecnica della Danimarca
(DTU).
Lo scopo di questo progetto consiste nell’analisi sperimentale dell’innovativo modello
di cucina solare, SunStore, costituito da un accumulo termico con materiale in cambi-
amento di fase. Una panoramica sulle varie tipologie di cucine solari è indispensabile
per evidenziare le caratteristiche di SunStore. La qualità distintiva di questo pro-
totipo è la flessibilità; questa tecnologia consente di cucinare anche all’interno degli
edifici, durante le ore notturne o nelle giornate nuvolose. Inoltre, detto sistema è in
grado cucinare, friggere il cibo, oltre che a pastorizzare l’acqua.
L’attuabilità di SunStore è verificata eseguendo esperimenti sulla durabilità del PCM,
sulla carica di SunStore sul collettore solare ed analizzando il profilo termico di Sun-
Store, dopo essere stato caricato e collocato nel contenitore isolante.
Il PCM utilizzato è costituito da una miscela eutettica di Acetato di sodio ed Acetato
di potassio.
Il calore accumulato dal PCM è determinato con il metodo ”Heat loss method”. La
stabilità del nel lungo periodo, rispetto all’accumulo termico, è stata studiata sotto-
ponendo il PCM a 90 cicli termici a 240°C; appena sopra la temperatura di fusione
della miscela. Mentre, l’influenza di temperature elevate sulla stabilità della miscela
eutettica è analizzata compiendo 10 cicli termici a 270°C. In entrambi gli esperimenti,
non è emersa una variazione della capacità di accumulo termico del materiale in cam-
biamento di fase.
La radiazione solare diretta per caricare SunStore sul collettore solare, ovvero per
portare la miscela al di sopra del suo punto di fusione, è di 4.27 kWh/m2. Inoltre,
tra la carica e l’uso, SunStore, collocato nel contenitore insolante, mantiene una tem-
peratura superiore a 200°C per un periodo di oltre 16 ore.

Abstract
Worldwide over 2.6 billion people lack access to clean cooking facilities and rely on pol-
luting fuels to prepare meals. The combustion over open fires or ineﬀicient cookstoves
emits greenhouse gases and short-lived climate pollutants. In addition, it is related
to desertification, deforestation, and it is responsible for severe health conditions. In
particular, it is regarded that indoor air pollution causes 4.3 million deaths every year.
Renewable energy sources play a crucial role in building a sustainable future, and
among them, solar energy is regarded as one of the most promising.
The use of phase change materials as thermal energy storage is an effective solution
to optimize the flexibility of the systems, where there is a mismatch between energy
supply and demand.
The aim of this project is an experimental analysis of the novel solar cooker Sun-
Store, constituted by a latent heat storage. An overview of the different types of
solar cookers is used to highlight the characteristics of SunStore. The flexibility is
the distinctive quality of this solar cooker. Indeed, this technology allows cooking
indoors and during cloudy days or late evening hours. Additionally, it is able to cook,
boil, fry food, and pasteurize water.
The feasibility of the product is verified by performing durability tests on the PCM,
carrying out a charging experiment of SunStore on the solar collector and analyzing
the heat loss between charge and discharge of SunStore, when it is located in the
insulation case.
The PCM used is the eutectic mixture of Sodium acetate and Potassium acetate. The
heat content of the PCM is determined using the ”Heat loss” method.
With respect to the heat content, the long-term cycling stability is studied performing
90 thermal cycles at 240°C, just above the melting point of the PCM. The influence
of high temperatures on the eutectic mixture cycling stability is investigated with 10
thermal cycles at 270°C. In both experiments, no variation in the ability of the PCM
to store heat has been detected.
The beam solar radiation necessary to charge SunStore on the solar collector is 4.27
kWh/m2.
The heat loss of SunStore, when it is located in the insulation case between charge and
discharge, is analyzed and the top plate maintains temperatures higher than 200°C
for over 16 hours.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The two words ”Ability” and ”Sustain” combined form the term ”Sustainability” [15].
Indeed, in the United Nations document ”Our Common Future” from 1987, sustain-
able development is described as ”development that meets the needs of the present
without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs” [16].
On a global scale, the majority of energy consumed derives from fossil fuels. Particu-
larly, energy consumption in developed countries is increasing at a pace of about 1%
per year, whereas, in the emerging countries, it is growing at a rate of 5% .
The global energy demand is forecast to rise, and fossil fuels are not foreseen to meet
the growing demand, due mainly to the decline in world oil production and environ-
mental concerns. Therefore, renewable energy sources play a core role in building a
sustainable future[3]. Solar energy is widely regarded as one of the most promising
clean energy solutions, owing to its low cost and abundance [6]. The total yearly so-
lar radiation falling on the Earth’s surface is more than 7500 times the overall yearly
primary energy consumption of the planet[3]. Solar energy has a wide variety of ap-

2.1
Access to clean cooking
The World Health Organization has defined guidelines for indoor air quality that set
a standard for clean cooking. In particular, a cookstove is considered ”clean” if its
emissions are within limits indicated in the WHO Guidelines [28]. Clean Cooking
technologies at the point of use are powered by electricity, natural gas, solar energy,
Liquefied Petroleum Gas (LPG), and processed biomass in highly eﬀicient stoves [29].
Worldwide over 2.6 billion people have to rely on biomass, kerosene, or coal to cook
their meals. Nevertheless, globally, the number of people without clean cooking fa-
cilities has been decreasing gradually over the years. For instance, the recent LPG
programs and clean air policies have allowed 450 million to access clean cooking since
2010 in India and China. However, the Covid-19 pandemic is an additional challenge
that can reverse [1] or amplify this modest progress.
In developing Asia, almost 1.6 billion people lack clean cooking technologies, seven
times the number of people without access to electricity in this region. Nonetheless,
according to the 2018 statistics, 670 million people obtained access to clean cooking
since 2010, reaching access percentages of 49% in India and 71% in China [1].
In sub-Saharan Africa, only 17% of the population has clean cooking facilities. Ac-
cording to the International Energy Agency, 900 million people lacked access to clean
cooking in 2018, making sub-Saharan Africa the only region in the world where the
number of individuals without access is steadily increasing[1].

2.2
Global warming and pollution
Household air pollution contributes significantly to both global warming and overall
air pollution. Indeed, cooking over open flames or ineﬀicient stoves is the source of
greenhouse gases, such as carbon monoxide and carbon dioxide, and short-lived cli-
mate pollutants (SLCPs), like methane and black carbon [17]. Methane and black
carbon are both responsible for global warming, but they are characterized by a short
atmospheric lifetime, varying from days to decades[30] .
Indeed, black carbon(BC), methane CH4, tropospheric ozone O3, and many hy-
drofluorocarbons belong to the category of short-lived climate pollutants (SLCPs).
SLCPs are the leading cause of near-term climate change, with a disproportionate
impact on vulnerable areas of the world. Moreover, they have negative effects on
health, agriculture, and the environment. SLCPs are responsible for altering rainfall
patterns, like the West African and Indian Monsoon, and accelerate the ice melting
of the poles and mountain glaciers. These pollutants are also the cause for harvest
losses of 30 million tonnes per year [30].
The Global Warming Potential (GWP) is a parameter used by the IPCC (Inter-
governmental Panel on Climate Change), useful to understand the global warming
impact of various greenhouse gases. The GWP is measured in relation to the warm-
ing effect of CO2. In particular, the Global Warming Potential of a gas compares
the energy absorbed by 1 ton of that gas to the energy absorbed by 1 ton of carbon
==PAGE:26==
6
2 Motivation
dioxide released in the atmosphere. Therefore, the GWP of CO2 is always equal to
one. Moreover, to evaluate the GWP, it is necessary to define a time horizon; usually,
the time period used to determine the GWPs is 100 years[31].
The GWP of methane CH4 is evaluated 28-36 over a 100-year timespan.
Furthermore, CH4 is a precursor to the formation of tropospheric ozone [31], a
greenhouse gas that is both created and destroyed by photochemistry within the
atmosphere[32].
Tropospheric O3 is an air pollutant [30], which damages human
health [18]. Moreover, tropospheric ozone represents a threat to food security be-
cause it alters the growth of crops, decreasing their ability to absorb CO2 [30].
According to the IPCC, the Global Warming Potential of Black Carbon is 900 over
a time span of 100 years [33].
Black Carbon is a product of the incomplete combustion of fossil fuels and biomass,
and it is released in the atmosphere with other co-pollutants [30]. Specifically, the
incomplete combustion emits carbon dioxide, carbon monoxide, volatile organic com-
pounds, organic carbon, and black carbon [34]. The complete combustion of solid
fuels, such as wood or biomass, is only a theoretical concept [35].
Black Carbon is the principal constituent of fine particulate matter (PM2.5) air pol-
lution; its particles are nearly pure elemental carbon with some oxygen and hydrogen
bound into layered, hexagonal structure [36]. Black Carbon is the second most impor-
tant greenhouse gas after carbon dioxide, and household combustion is responsible
25% of its global emission [18].
BC contributes to global warming in two ways. When it is suspended in the atmo-
sphere, Black Carbon absorbs sunlight, generating heat, altering circulation patterns,
regional cloud formation, and rainfalls [37]. When it deposits on snow and ice, BC
absorbs sunlight and produces heat [37], but also darkens the surfaces, decreasing
their albedo [17], as it is shown in Fig.2.3.
BC is responsible for the accelerated melting of mountain glaciers and Arctic ice[37].
Indeed, Black carbon is regarded as one of the principal causes of the thinning and
retreat of the glaciers in the Himalayan region [18], which are a vital supply of fresh
water for millions of people [37].
Furthermore, since 1890 Black Carbon has con-
tributed 0.5-1.4°C to Arctic warming [38]; and the disappearance of Arctic sea ice
would lead to faster warming and perhaps an irreversible climate change [37].
Black Carbon is a powerful warming agent [33]; however, it exerts its effects over
a considerably shorter period of time compared to CO2, which can vary between
days and weeks [34].
Wet and dry deposition are the only atmospheric sinks for
Black Carbon. Wet deposition happens when BC particles are incorporated in cloud
droplets, ice crystals, and precipitation, whereas dry deposition is caused by gravi-
tational settling, interception, and diffusion [39]. The consequences of black carbon
are felt on a regional scale, in locations close to the emission source. Since BC has a
short lifespan in the atmosphere, reducing the emissions of black carbon can lead to
an immediate slowdown of warming.

2.3
Health impact
Household air pollution is recognized as the single most dangerous threat to human
health, even when compared to the lack of clean water and sanitation. Indeed, ac-
cording to data collected by the World Health Organization, exposure to indoor air
pollution causes 4.3 million premature deaths every year, corresponding to 7.7 % of

the annual global mortality, higher than the yearly deaths of tuberculosis, malaria,
and HIV combined. Indoor air pollution is considered responsible for 25% of all deaths
from stroke, 15% of deaths from ischaemic heart disease, 17% of deaths from lung
cancer, and more than 33% of all deaths from chronic obstructive pulmonary disease
in the developing countries. Moreover, 25% of the cases of cataracts, the primary
cause of blindness in developing countries, are related to household air pollution [18].
The health consequences of cooking with fuels such as charcoal, wood, coal, and
kerosene affect disproportionately women and children, resulting in tragic numbers.
Household air pollution is the cause of almost half a million women’s deaths each
year from chronic obstructive pulmonary disease and of more than 50% of the yearly
deaths from pneumonia of children under five (around 0.5 million). Overall, in 2012
children and women constituted over 60% of all premature deaths related to indoor
air pollution [18].
Open fires and ineﬀicient stoves emit numerous toxic substances to human beings,
such as fine particulate matter, carbon monoxide, methane, carcinogenic polyaro-
matic hydrocarbons, volatile organic compounds. Nevertheless, particulate matter is
the pollutant mainly responsible for the majority of the health impacts. In particu-
lar, small diameter particles (PM2.5) are the most dangerous because they can reach
deeper in the lungs and deposit on the alveoli [18].
Many clean cooking initiatives promote eﬀicient biomass cookstoves, which require
less fuelwood for cooking, reducing deforestation and climate-warming emissions.
Nonetheless, highly eﬀicient cookstoves have not been demonstrated to diminish fine
particulate emissions significantly (PM2.5), which is the primary health concern of
burning biomass.
The review of clean cooking programs with eﬀicient cookstoves
shows that the health gains are lower than expected. For instance, in 2016 in Malawi,
it has not been observed a reduction of pneumonia cases after the distribution of
advanced biomass stoves[25]. To obtain significant health benefits, clean cooking ini-
tiatives should promote the use of clean fuels, like liquefied petroleum gas (LPG),
biogas, ethanol, electricity [25] and solar energy.
Indoor air pollution is a pressing health issue that must be addressed globally. Clean
cooking programs on a large scale can prevent millions of premature deaths each year,
enhancing the quality of day-to-day life and elongating life expectancy.

In most societies, women are assigned the role of caretaker and food provider in
the family, leading women and children to be disproportionately affected by the
health consequences of indoor air pollution. For instance, a study in rural India as-
sesses that the daily average concentration of fine particulate matter to which cooks,
mainly women, are exposed is 0.226 mg/m3, compared to 0.172 mg/m3 for non-cooks

In most households in low-middle income countries, fuel collection and wood gather
is typically considered women and girls duty. For instance, Ghanaian women spend
three times more hours per week collecting firewood and ten times more hours cooking
than men[18]. Moreover, in India, a survey realized by the Self-Employed Women’s
Association among women living in the rural area of Gujarat reports that they spend
40 % of their waking time collecting fuel or cooking [18]. Time consumption is an often
underrated negative impact of cooking with open fires and ineﬀicient cookstoves. In-
deed, women face significant constraints on their available time for income-generating
and educational opportunities, which could contribute to alleviating poverty [26].
In developing countries, women and girls need to walk long distances carrying large
bundles of wood on their backs or heads. They are often subjected to muscle strains,
spinal injuries, headaches, backaches due to the heavy weight of the wood, which can
reach up to 40 kg.
In addition, while collecting firewood, women face serious safety risks; they are ex-
posed to animal attacks and threats of physical violence and sexual assault [18].
These issues are particularly heightened in crisis scenarios like refugee camps.
In
the majority of refugee camps, the food is usually provided by humanitarian pro-
grams, although cookstoves and fuel are rarely supplied [20].
According to the UNHCR, women refugees in Ugandan camps fetch firewood around
eight times per month, traveling an average distance of 5.7 km. In particular, in 2014,
in Uganda’s Nakivale refugee camp, 41% of violence cases on women occurred during
fuel collection trips. The reported accidents included confiscation of firewood (23%),
bodily injury (12%), attempted rape (5%), and rape (4%) [40].
The situation in Chad is even more critical. Refugee women need to travel an average
distance of 13.5 km to collect firewood at least once a week, during which they are
subject to rape and other physical violence. Indeed, in the Farchana refugee camp in
Chad, 90% of confirmed cases of sexual assault occurred during fuel collection trips
[40].
Furthermore, in humanitarian settings, the inability to cook meals due to the lack of
fuel exposes women to a higher risk of domestic abuse[41].
The violence to which women are subjected in and outside the house, especially in
already critical scenarios, represents a public health challenge[41] that must be ad-
dressed.

Solar cookers
The sun represents a clean alternative to firewood as cooking fuel in the developing
world. Solar cookers can play a crucial role in building a sustainable future.
Solar cookers working principle is the simple conversion of the solar energy into heat
[42], which can be utilized to cook food, as well as other relevant processes such as
pasteurization and sterilization [3].
Worldwide there are countless models of solar cookers, and they are continually mod-
ified and improved by researchers and companies [6]. Solar Cookers International
(SCI), the leading NGO in the solar cooking sector, keeps track of all the new designs
and coordinates the work of more than 250 NGOs promoting these devices all over
the world [4].
3.1
History of solar cookers
The first solar cooker dates back to the eighteenth century. In 1767 French-Swiss
Physicist Horace de Saussure cooked food, specifically fruit, using solar energy for
the first time. He built a primitive solar box cooker that achieved temperatures of
88°C [4], made of 5 layers of glass boxes turned upside down on a black table [6]. De
Saussure is considered the grandfather of solar cooking [42].
It has been reported that during an expedition to South Africa in 1830, the English
astronomer Sir John Herschel prepared a meal in a similar insulated box. In 1876, W.
Adams stated that he cooked rations for seven soldiers in two hours with an octagonal
solar oven provided with eight reflecting mirrors. In the 1930s in India, the concern
for deforestation led to a growing interest in solar energy. In the same period, France
supplied numerous solar cookers to its African colonies [6].
In 1945, the Indian pioneer Sri M.K. Gosh developed the first commercial box type
solar cooker [6].
The current design of solar cookers began to evolve in the 1950s [42].
During the 1970s oil crisis, the rising fuel prices sparked a worldwide interest in re-
newable energy technologies, particularly in China and India.
Metcal and Longvin were the first to conduct water pasteurization using a solar cooker
in 1979.
In the 1980s, the American Barbara Kerr constructed and tested several types of solar
cookers [6]. Kerr helped develop the panel-type solar cooker, CooKit [4], and she is

3.2.1.1
Box cookers
A solar box cooker is essentially an insulated box with a clear multiple or single glass
ceiling, with the inner surfaces painted black to maximize sunlight absorption. In ad-
dition, booster mirrors are used to maximize the direct incident solar radiation into
the cooking chamber [6]. A schematic representation of the different components of
a box cooker is reported in Fig.3.2.
Usually solar box cookers can accommodate more than one pot inside and achieve
temperatures around 100°C, allowing to boil food [3].
The operating principle of this type of solar cooker is the greenhouse effect, the glaz-
ing is transparent to the incoming solar radiation, but it is opaque to the infrared
radiation coming from the heated objects placed inside [5].
Some advantages of the box solar cookers include simplicity, structural stability, the
necessity of minimal attention throughout the cooking process, the ability to keep
the food warm over an extended period of time. Additionally, there is no production
of glare and no risk of fire. The main disadvantage of this type of cookers is the
slow cooking process [5]. Nevertheless, box-type cookers perform quite well even with
diffuse radiation, wind, intermittent clouds, and low ambient temperatures [6].
Figure 3.2: Components of a box solar cooker [3]
Each component of this type of solar cooker has a significant impact on the overall
eﬀiciency of the system [6]. In particular, the optimization of the inclination of the
booster mirrors can radically enhance the cooking power [3]. Moreover, the use of

towards the surrounding environment and increase the eﬀiciency of the cooker [3].

3.2.1.2
Panel cookers
Due to its ease of assembly and cheap materials, the panel cooker is regarded as
the most widespread solar cooker. In panel cookers, inexpensive reflective equipment
directs the sunlight to the cooking pot, which is usually enclosed in a clear heat re-
sistant plastic bag [6] or an inverted glass bowl[4].
They employ the same working principle of the solar box cookers: the greenhouse
effect [45].
Indeed, the plastic bag, or glass bowl, retains the heat because it is
transparent to the incoming short-wavelength solar radiation, but it is opaque to the
long-wavelength radiation emitted from the cooking vessel [5]. However, due to their
design, solar panel cookers mainly rely on beam solar radiation; hence they perform
quite poorly in cloudy sky conditions [45]. Overall, it can be stated that this type of
solar cooker provides limited cooking power [6].
The ”CooKit”, widely considered the most popular solar cooker [4], was designed
by Roger Bernard, in cooperation with Solar Cookers International, in 1994 [46].
Arunachala states that it is used by more than 2 million families worldwide[4].

3.2.2.1
Parabolic solar cookers
In the early 1950s, at the National Physical Laboratory in India, Ghai developed the
first model of a solar parabolic cooker [6].
Solar parabolic cookers achieve temperatures up to 200 °C [42] in a short amount of
time, and, unlike the panel and box cookers, they do not require a special cooking ves-
sel [6] or a clear cover to retain the heat [8]. Parabolic cookers are very flexible since
they can cook, boil, fry, and bake all types of food [4]. Nevertheless, in a parabolic
cooker, the food must be stirred frequently because the concentrated power can burn
it if left unattended [6].
The sharp focus point, that characterizes the parabolic solar cookers, makes them
the most popular type among concentrating cookers. However, it implies that these
cookers are extremely sensitive to even a slight change in the position of the sun;
therefore, the use of parabolic solar cookers requires constant tracking [5].

When there is a discrepancy between the supply and the demand of energy, like in
solar cooking, energy storage plays a crucial role [5] in ensuring the flexibility of the

categorized in three categories [51]:
• Sensible heat storage (SHS)
• Latent heat storage (LHS)

The following paragraph presents an overview of the different methods of thermal
energy storage.
4.1
Thermal energy storage
4.1.1
Sensible heat storage
The most used type of sensible heat storage is hot water heat storage. Indeed, it is
widely employed in domestic heating and domestic hot water systems [10].
The operating principle of sensible heat storage consists in increasing and decreasing
the temperature of the storage medium, which can be both in the solid or liquid form

4.1.2
Latent heat storage
Following the 1973–1974 energy crisis, thermal energy storage (TES) acquired an im-
portant role in energy management. In addition, recently, the concern over global
warming and the limited reserves of fossil fuels made the eﬀicient utilization of energy

a crucial issue. In many domestic and industrial sectors, the use of phase change ma-
terials (PCMs) as thermal energy storage is an effective solution to optimize the use
of energy and the flexibility of the systems [11].
The most eﬀicient technique for storing thermal energy is latent heat storage (LHS),
using phase change materials that release both latent and sensible heat. In particular,
the working principle of LHS is based on the storage medium releasing and absorbing
heat as it changes phase, from solid to solid, solid to liquid, solid to gas, or liquid to
gas or vice versa [11]. Therefore, the first categorization of the PCMs may be based
on their mode of phase transition.
Despite the high latent heat, the applications of solid-gas or liquid-gas PCMs are
quite rare in TES systems because of the significant volume changes involved in the
transition, thus, high pressures.
Solid-solid PCMs can be regarded as an alternative to solid-liquid PCMs because
they avoid the significant technical problem of PCM leakage in the liquid phase [11] .
However, the phase change from one crystalline form to another is quite slow [7], and
it is associated with heat of phase transition lower than that of solid-liquid PCMs [11].
In the following section, the most common category [49], solid-liquid PCM, is pre-
sented.
4.1.2.1
Solid-liquid phase change
In the phase change material solid-liquid, the melting and solidification can, respec-
tively, store and release large amounts of thermal energy.
In general, melting is characterized by a volume change smaller than 10%; thus, the
pressure does not vary significantly if the container’s dimensions are appropriate to
fit the phase with the larger volume.
The processes of melting and solidification are considered isobaric [10].

4.1.3
Thermo-chemical heat storage
A chemical reaction can be endothermic or exothermic, depending on whether it
absorbs or releases heat towards the surrounding environment.
In a chemical reaction, the difference between the enthalpy of the products and the
enthalpy of the reagents is known as heat of reaction.
Thermo-chemical heat storage employs a reaction with a high heat of reaction. The
thermal energy is stored in the products and the heat is then released when the reverse
reaction occurs [10].

PCMs are classified into different classes with similar properties, presented in in

Organic PCMs can be further classified into a wide range of materials such as Paraf-

In practice, all of the indicated criteria are not fully met by the majority of PCMs [11].
Subcooling is the effect that the PCM starts solidification only at a temperature
below the temperature of fusion. Thus, decreasing the temperature, sometimes sig-
nificantly under the melting point, is necessary to release latent heat.
The process of crystallization is caused by nucleation. Nucleation is the growth of a
small solid particle, called nucleus, by crystallizing the liquid PCM on its surface.
Subcooling occurs when the nucleus radius is very small, so the energy necessary to
increase the nucleus surface is higher than the heat released by solidification [10].
4.3
PCM in solar cooking
The heat storage in a solar cooker is subjected to several constraints, such as lack of
space and eﬀiciency in the cooking.
Consequently, the adoption of PCM heat storage in solar cooking is particularly in-
teresting, because of their high density storage and constant operating temperature
in the appropriate temperature range, according to the user needs [48].
In literature, several examples of solar cookers with different PCM storage units
have been reported and investigated. In general, the adoption of PCM heat storage
enhances the system’s flexibility, allowing cooking or keeping the food warm in the
late evening hours and on cloudy days, depending on the device and PCM used [48, 7].
Numerous PCM with different characteristics have been employed in various types of
solar cookers; some examples are reported in Table 4.1 [7]. The melting temperature
can vary from 25°C of coconut oil up to 253 °C with LiNO3. In particular, Kani-
mozhi used coconut oil as PCM in a box-type solar cooker, obtaining a reduction in
the heating time of 15% compared to the performance without a latent storage unit.
LiNO3 was employed in the heat storage unit of Wilson solar cooker, a device devel-
oped by MIT professor David Wilson and some graduate students that uses Fresnel
lenses to harness the sunlight and melt the PCM unit [53]. According to Akinwale,
the LiNO3 maintained the temperature of the cooking vessel around 258°C for six
hours, and Wilson solar cooker was able to boil water for 25h [7].

In this chapter, it is investigated the cycling stability of the eutectic mixture of the
organic salts CH3COONa and CH3COOK, with respect to the stored heat.
A widespread method to determine the temperature-enthalpy curve is DSC analy-
sis (Differential scanning calorimetry) [10], but the measurements are performed on
small samples of milligram quantities [66]. Particularly, when the PCM is a mixture,
the use of small samples can lead to diﬀiculties in making the mixture with the correct
precise percentages of the various components. In addition, possible phase separation
can not be easily detected in small quantities.
Instead, in this study, the PCM samples sizes used to analyze the cycling stability
are considerably bigger, varying between 200 and 300 grams; hence it is possible to
examine the material behavior in quantities that represent the operating conditions
of SunStore. The cycling is carried out in Memmert ovens, and the temperature-
enthalpy curve is evaluated using the ”Heat loss” method.

The approach employed in all the experiments is based on the ”Heat loss” method.
In the ”Heat loss” method, the heat content is determined by evaluating the heat
released by the PCM sample during the cooling process in an insulated box. The
heat loss coeﬀicient of the insulated box is calculated by cooling down a reference
medium with well-known thermal properties [70].
Solid and liquids are considered incompressible, in usual circumstances, therefore
the term V · dp in the first law of thermodynamics is negligible.
dH = dU + d(p · V ) = dQ −pdV + pdV + V dp = dQ + V dp
(6.6)
Consequently
dH = dQ
(6.7)
For solid and liquids, variations of enthalpy dH and heat dQ are considered equals
[10].
Moreover, since the bottle fit perfectly the phase with larger volume and the eutec-

∆V = 1.16 ± 0.1% [55] during transition, the pressure does not change significantly.
Thus the melting and solidification processes are considered isobaric [10].
The overall heat content of the PCM sample is calculated as the sum of the heat lost
during transfer from the oven to the box Qtr and the heat lost in the box Qbox,P CM.
Qstored,P CM = Qbox,P CM + Qtr

fer, the top and the bottom of the bottle are covered by gloves. Hence, the surface
considered in the convective heat transfer AM is the mantle surface of the bottle.

6.4
Long term cycling stability
A solar cooker should maintain optimal operative conditions over a considerable num-
ber of usages, so the eutectic mixture should be able to keep its ability to store heat
over several thermal cycles.
Bottle 1 and 3 are cycled 90 times at 240°C, an equivalent of approximately three
months of daily use. The bottles are closed with PPS lids, minimizing the influence of
oxygen on the possible degradation. Nevertheless, it is recommended for additional
investigation to use Argon blanketing and tight aluminum lids to exclude the effect
of oxygen completely.
Each thermal cycle is constituted by 4 hours and 45 minutes in the oven at the
set temperature of 240°C and 3 hours to cool down in the oven; during the cooling
process, the oven temperature is 20°C. At the end of the cycle, the PCM reaches an
overall temperature of around 90°C.
The thermal cycles are performed in a Memmert oven, programmed with the software
AtmoCONTROL.
After 90 cycles, it is possible to observe a change of color of the PCM, both in
molten and solidified state, shown in Fig.6.23, Fig.6.24, Fig.6.25 and Fig.6.26. This
phenomenon is because the lids do not seal perfectly the bottles, and a minimal
amount of oxygen flows in the bottle during the cool down, causing carbonization.
Before the cycling, the solid PCM is white, and the molten PCM is light brown. How-
ever, the mixture gains a grey-ish and dark brown color after the cycling, respectively,
in the solidified and molten state.
There is not an abrupt color change, but a gradual transition over the various cycles.
Nevertheless, after 20 thermal cycles, the color variation becomes apparent, especially
in the molten state.

6.5
High temperature cycling stability
The experiment of charging Sunstore on the tracker, described in Chapter 7, leads to
interesting information on the feasibility of the product and on the PCM behavior.
Overall, seven temperature sensors are used during the charging experiment, four of
which are inserted inside the thermal storage in different positions, and three are

plate. However, the corresponding temperature of the sensor inside the PCM at 40
mm from the top plate is 248 °C, because of the low thermal conductivity of the
eutectic mixture.
A small part of the molten salt is in direct contact with the top plate because during
the charging Sunstore is tilted. According to the literature, the decomposition tem-
perature of Potassium acetate is 296°C.
Consequently, an investigation on the durability of the PCM at higher temperatures
is performed.
The chosen set temperature of the oven for the thermal cycles is 270°C and not 300°C,
because the PCM is characterized by a small thermal conductivity and only a thin
layer of PCM is in direct thermal contact with the top lid, which is an aluminum
plate of 10 mm of thickness.

SunStore can store heat for several hours when located in the insulation case. There-

Around the world, 5000 children pass away every day from diarrhoeal diseases due to
contaminated drinking water [78].
To obtain water pasteurization, it is not necessary to boil water, but it is suﬀicient
that the temperature at the bottom of the water container is around 65°C or higher,
to kill pathogenic microbes.
The general recommendation to boil water to pasteurize it is due mainly to the diﬀi-
culty of accurately determining the temperature of the heated water [79]. However,
there are inexpensive thermometers that detect the water pasteurization temperature,
such as Water Pasteurization Indicator (WAPI), that uses melting wax to indicate
when the water is pasteurized and safe to drink [80].
The high operating temperature could also allow the application of this device for
the sterilization of medical instruments in rural health clinics without access to grid
electricity. Indeed, a hot air sterilization process requires temperatures of 160°C for
at least 97 minutes [81].

Chapter 8
Conclusions
The durability of the eutectic mixture of Sodium acetate and Potassium acetate is
investigated, with respect to the heat content, using the ”Heat loss” method.
The stored heat is calculated by assessing the heat released by the PCM during the
cooling process in an insulated box.
The PCM long-term cycling stability is analyzed with 90 thermal cycles at 240°C.
Instead, the influence of high temperatures on the durability of the PCM is studied
with 10 thermal cycles at 270°C.
The temperature-enthalpy curve is evaluated before and after the cycling. Then, the
measures are compared to detect a possible degradation of the phase-change material.
The results of the temperature-enthalpy curves confirm the experimental observations
that, even though the melting temperature is 232°C-235°C, an initial change in the
PCM structure starts around 200 °C and the curve changes its slope drastically.
Overall, the results of the durability tests show that the eutectic mixture of Sodium
acetate and Potassium acetate does not degrade, with respect to the heat content,
after 90 thermal cycles at 240°C and after 10 cycles at 270°C.
An effective solar cooker must maintain optimal conditions over numerous usages.
Further tests should be carried out to investigate the durability of the PCM over
several additional thermal cycles.
The method employed to evaluate the heat content measurements has been progres-
sively developed and improved during the project. Therefore, several recommenda-
tions for further studies are suggested because it was not possible to execute them,
due to the length and nature of the experiments.
In particular, the bottles should be purged with Argon and then closed with Alu-
minum lids to eliminate the influence of oxygen on the possible degradation of the
PCM. The heat loss coeﬀicients should be evaluated with additional temperature sen-
sors on the glass and on the lid, and UA functions should be determined for each
box used. Finally, investigations on the effect of oxygen on the PCM cycling stability
should be performed.
The charging of SunStore on the solar collector provides information on the feasi-
bility of the solar cooker and on the PCM behavior in operative conditions. Indeed,
seven temperature sensors are used in the experiment, four of which are collocated
inside the thermal storage. The charging experiment highlighted the necessity to in-
vestigate the durability of the PCM at higher temperatures.

8 Conclusions
Overall, the beam solar radiation necessary to melt the PCM inside the thermal
battery is 4.27 kWh/m2, but a reduction of the height of SunStore could radically
decrease it. Additional experiments with different geometries of SunStore should be
performed to assess the different beam solar radiation needed to melt the PCM.
The heat loss between charge and discharge of SunStore, when it is located in the
insulation case, is investigated. The thermal battery is charged in the oven at 250°C
and later transferred in the insulation case.
The temperature of the top plate is
higher than 200°C for over 16 hours. However, it is recommended to develop a lighter
insulation case, to allow the user’s autonomy.
SunStore allows independence between the charging site and the cooking unit, it
enables cooking indoors and even in late evening hours or cloudy days. Due to the
high transition temperature of the eutectic mixture of Sodium and Potassium acetate,
SunStore is able to boil, fry food, pasteurize water, and it can also be used for steri-
lization of medical equipment.
Furthermore, the experimental investigations show that chosen PCM maintains unal-
tered its capability of storing heat over an equivalent of three months of daily usage.
