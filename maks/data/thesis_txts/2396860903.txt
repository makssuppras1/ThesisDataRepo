Abstract
The insertion of ﬁsh farms in any fjord will cause an increase in organic pollution and
if the pollutants are not swept out of the fjord, the consumption of can cause oxygen
depletion. In this study, the ﬂow pattern of the Lysefjord in Norway was investigated by
the use of 3D modelling and CFD.
At the mouth of the fjord there is a sill where it is only 14 m deep, whereas at the
deepest point the fjord is 460 m deep. This causes the fjord to have a basin-like geometry,
which limits the water exchange of the fjord. The presence of both fresh and salt water
causes the fjord to be severely stratiﬁed. It would thus be desirable to develop a model
which could compute how the pollutants are transported and consumed in the fjord.
In the CFD study of the fjord, issues with the hydrostatic pressure of the opening bound-
ary condition caused the ﬂow ﬁeld close to the mouth of the fjord to be un-physical. This
issue was circumvented by only deﬁning the top 20 meter of the opening as an opening.
The qualitative tendencies of the simulated salt content in the fjord was shown in this
work to be in agreement with experimental results. However, the thermocline which is
present at a water depth of ≈20 m, was not achieved in the simulations resulting in
higher temperatures in the fjord. The ﬂow pattern was found to be inﬂuenced by gravity
vector, which was slanted towards the mouth of the fjord. This caused the less dense fresh
water to run up towards the end of the fjord while salt water moved towards the mouth.
The water was also found to be in movement closer to the sea ﬂoor than expected. It
was discussed whether the increased mobility could be due to the higher temperatures
which would cause the viscosities to decrease. The biological model for the conversion of
Chemical Oxygen Demand in the fjord was tested in a simpliﬁed geometry at constant
conditions. The results were reasonable and generally showed slow kinetics which would
make a future implementation into the fjord signiﬁcantly easier. Generally, it was dis-
cussed that the simulations may show that the pollutants will not be spread out, but will
accumulate in the fjord.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Since the 1970’s, ﬁsh farming in Norway has grown signiﬁcantly.
Approximately one
million metric tonnes of ﬁsh are produced each year [3] and the industry is in constant
growth [4]. The environmental impact of the farming has been a topic of discussion for
years since there is a signiﬁcant waste from these farms in the form of feed pellets and
faeces. The ﬁsh do not consume all the food pellets with which they are provided and
the remaining pellets will therefore either sink or be swept out of the fjord. Furthermore,
the faeces from the ﬁsh contain undigested organic matter and inorganic nutrients and
account for the majority of the organic waste from ﬁsh farms. The organic pollutants
are of interest since they will be degraded by the biomass which in the process consumes
dissolved oxygen [5]. If the oxygen demand is too high, oxygen depletion of the water can
happen. The depletion of oxygen is called hypoxia and when there is a more severe lack of
oxygen, it is called anoxia [5]. Hypoxia alone has severe consequences for the other species
in the fjord, and anoxic conditions opens up for production of hydrogen sulﬁde and more
permanent damage to the eco-system. Any oxygen dependent species will disappear from
any areas without oxygen and only anaerobic substances will be left [5].
The fjords of Norway are for the most part ideal for ﬁsh farming. Fish farming requires a
certain level of water exchange around the cages due to the amount of organic pollution
they produce. If the exchange of water is too low, the organic matter may pollute the
body of water and in the worst case cause anoxia [5]. The farms also require a somewhat
sheltered location away from severe waves. The Norwegian fjords generally have decent
water exchange and thus pollution by organic matter is therefore not currently an issue in
Norway, even if ﬁsh farming is widespread [6]. However, in the 1980’s the majority of ﬁsh
farms was placed at shallow (< 20 m deep), quiet waters, and sedimentation rates were
found to be signiﬁcantly higher in close range of the farming area. This accumulation of
organic matter on the sediment was found to cause large problems, and the ﬁsh farms were
instead moved to deeper waters [6]. In the last ten years, the more severe consequences,
such as hypoxia and thus a decrease in biodiversity, are rarely seen. This is due to the
2
==PAGE:11==
increased awareness of the location of the ﬁsh farms [6]. In stead, it has been determined
that the organic matter from the farms are distributed over a larger area [7].
Fish farming is one of the fastest growing industries in Norway, and they are rapidly
running out of space in the big fjords, which have the best water exchange. As a conse-
quence, the industry is now considering the smaller fjords as hosts to ﬁsh farms. In the
biggest fjords, i.e. the Sognefjord or the Hardangerfjord, the additional organic matter
might not be an issue, but in the Lysefjord, there is a bigger risk that the organic matter
will accumulate and cause problems. This is due to the diﬀerences in water exchange of
the fjords. In order to determine if the water exchange is suﬃcient to ensure a healthy
eco-system of the fjord, it is necessary to determine how the topography of a fjord may
inﬂuence the dispersion of the eﬄuent organic matter in the fjord.
The many fjords of Norway are often of a speciﬁc topographical type. They are gener-
ally located by steep cliﬀsides and are thus very deep and long. Furthermore, it is typical
for a Norwegian fjord to have an area near its mouth where the sea ﬂoor rises towards the
surface. The area is called a sill and it creates a basin inside the fjord below the sill depth,
which limits the exchange of water. The sill depth at the Hardangerfjord is 150-200 m
[3]. For reference, the Lysefjord is only 38 km long, approximately 480 m at the deepest
and the sill depth is only 14 m. Therefore, poor water exchange is to be expected at the
Lysefjord.
The distribution of the eﬄuent particles, both feed pellets and faeces, from the ﬁsh farms
can be explored by the use of Computational Fluid Dynamics. By simulating this distri-
bution as well as the ongoing biological degradation of the organic matter, it can possibly
be determined if any zones in the Lysefjord could be in the risk of hypoxia if a ﬁsh farm
is inserted.

Chapter 2
Theoretical background
2.1
Fish farms and their eﬀect on Norwegian fjords
Aquaculture is one of the fastest growing industries in Norway, and the fastest growing
food industry in the world [4; 8; 9]. Aquaculture covers a number of ways to cultivate
ﬁsh and sea food and one of the most popular ways of doing this is by the use of ﬁsh
farms. Fish farms are large cages, which ﬂoat on the surface of the water. They are often
rectangular and approximately 20-40 m on the sides, and can be 20-35 m deep [10]. They
can be circular as well, with circumferences of 90-160 m and go as deep as 50 m [10].
Fish farms are used across the globe, but in Norway, the industry has been growing
rapidly the past decades. Some speculate that the reason for the success in Norway is the
special geography of Norway, which provides a high amount of available coastal area [11].
Many species of ﬁsh are produced this way, but the Atlantic Salmon is especially
popular in Norway. The industry was just beginning to spread in 1970 and the global
production was just 297 tonnes [10]. In 2009, 865 000 tonnes of salmon were produced
globally and it was estimated that about 700 sites along the coast of Norway produced
salmon [11]. In 2013, the global production had increased to 2 087 000 tonnes [10]. It is
expected that by 2030, more than 60% of ﬁsh intended for direct human consumption will
be produced by aquaculture [12].
2.1.1
Distribution of organic matter
The growing industry provides work and food to an increasing population, but it also
poses a threat to the environment in which the cages are placed.
This is due to the
notable amounts of organic matter which is released from these cages. It is estimated
by Skoien et al. [10] that the feed loss from a commercial ﬁsh farm is ≈7%. A typical
Norwegian cage can hold around 200 000 salmon of approximately 5 kg each, and they
are usually fed about 1% of their weight every day. This results in a feed per cage per
day of ≈10 tonnes. With the aforementioned feed loss of 7% it is approximately 700 kg

of feed per day, which is released into the surrounding water [10]. This waste is obviously
an economical issue for the industry, but more pressing, the high amount of waste will
likely aﬀect the ecosystem of the body of water in which the cage is placed. Furthermore,
it should be noted that it is not only the unconsumed feed which is released into the
water in notable amounts, but also faeces from the ﬁsh. The faecal matter is released
into the surrounding water in signiﬁcant amounts and settles at a rate similar to that of
unconsumed feed pellets [9].
In the 1980’s, most cages were placed in calm, shallow waters (less than 20 m) and
signiﬁcant increases to the sedimentation rates under the cages were reported. It was
seen that the sedimentation rates were 2-10 times higher than the normal rates in the
same areas [6]. The increase in sedimentation rates was both due to the settling of the
unconsumed feed pellets and the faecal matter from the ﬁsh. It has been documented how
the increase in sedimentation rates caused signiﬁcant problems especially to the benthic
climates [6].
Today, ﬁsh farms are mainly placed at deeper waters with better water exchange than
before. This ensures, for the most part, that the feed pellets and faeces will be carried
away from the site. If the polluting particles do not get swept out of the fjords, they will
at least be distributed across a larger area. However, to my knowledge, no experimental
ﬁeld work has been done on the distribution of eﬄuent particle from ﬁsh farms on deeper
waters [6].
At the deeper sites, i.e. > 50 m deep, there are a number of ways the eﬄuent particles
can be distributed. The larger particles, e.g. feed pellets or faeces will sink fast and have
a higher risk of ending up at the bottom. The smaller particles can be suspended in the
near stagnant ﬂow in the middle of the fjords. Any dissolved nutrients from the feed and
faeces will, however, more easily be transported with the ﬂow at the top of the fjord.
The feed pellets and faeces provide ideal substrate for a number of organisms in the
fjord, e.g. ﬁsh, bacteria, plants etc. The bacteria will consume the organic matter by
both aerobic and anaerobic processes, which will be explained in greater detail in section
2.1.2. This means that if the organic matter accumulates in somewhat stagnant areas
of a fjord, the oxygen in the fjord will be used by the bacteria in this process. Thus,
there may be generated zones within the fjord where oxygen depletion can be observed
in various degrees depending on the availability of the organic matter, bacteria as well
as other nutrients. Under such anoxic conditions, the fauna and ﬂora of the fjord will be
severely aﬀected.
Furthermore, if the particles are suspended close to the surface, they will be in the
euphotic zone. The euphotic zone is the topmost layer of any body of water, where the
sunlight can penetrate the water, resulting in enough available sunlight for photosynthesis
to occur. This is illustrated in ﬁgure 2.1. It can stretch as far down as 200 metres in
the ocean and be as shallow as a few centimetres in stagnant lakes which are severely
polluted with algae [14].
Below the euphotic zone, the aphotic zone is placed where
some photosynthesis may still occur, but it is not enough to compensate for the oxygen

If the visibility of the water is compromised, e.g. by suspended organic matter, it will
result in reduced amounts of sunlight in the water and thus a reduction of the euphotic
zone [14]. About 90% of life in the oceans lives only in the euphotic layer, and thus any
disturbances to the euphotic layer will inﬂuence the ecosystem signiﬁcantly [5]. This will
in turn limit the photosynthesis of the plant algae in the water. The formation of oxygen
in the water will therefore be limited, and what little oxygen is available will be consumed
in the decomposition of the organic matter.
Furthermore, as the suspended organic matter will be consumed by bacteria, the
oxygen will be used locally around these areas [15]. This will result in pockets of low
oxygen were the organic matter will accumulate, suspended in the water.
Pollution by organic matter is therefore a multi-pronged issue which can create a
number of diﬀerent problems based on how it is distributed in the fjord. It may pose no
threat to the ecosystem if it is swept out of the fjord, but if the organic matter sinks or
is suspended for a long time, it can result in areas of hypoxia (oxygen depletion).

2.1.2
Modelling of oxygen depletion
Organic matter is introduced into the ecosystem in the form of unconsumed feed pellets
or ﬁsh faeces. The organic matter contains diﬀerent nutrients, and as the organic matter
enters the water, some of it will be dissolved, while the remaining portion will stay in the
form of particulate matter.
An important point when it comes to modelling oxygen depletion is that it is most
easily done by using a reaction model which is based on the amount of oxygen depletion
caused by the reactions. In this report, the model which is used is based on the amount
of Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD) in the water. COD is the amount of oxygen which
is consumed in the water during the decomposition of organic matter and the process of
oxidation of inorganic chemicals, i.e. NH4
+ and PO4
3–. A sub-group of the COD is the
Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD), which only considers the oxygen depletion from the
decomposition of organic matter by aerobic bacterial processes. This is also a common
factor to use in these types of models.
The conversion model which has been used in this case is however based on the amount
of COD in the water, and it is presented in ﬁgure 2.2. This conversion model both con-
siders the growth and decay of autotrophic and heterotrophic organisms. An autotrophic
substance is capable of surviving and growing purely on the basis of inorganic nutrients.
The carbon source of the autotrophic bacteria is the dissolved carbon dioxide of the water.
The concentrations of carbon dioxide have not been modelled in this biological model,
as it is assumed to be in abundance in the fjord. On the other hand, a heterotrophic
organism is dependent on organic matter as a carbon source. In ﬁgure 2.2, these two
growth processes are shown as; 1) µhet, which requires organic matter here shown on the
basis of biodegradable, soluble COD, SS, and 2) µaut, which requires ammonium, NH4
+,
phosphate, PO4
3–, and carbon dioxide, CO2. Furthermore, it must be noted that the
heterotrophic growth can occur both aerobically and anaerobically. The rates of reaction
are presented in table 2.1. The total kinetic model is shown in more detail in appendix
B.1-B.2.
Furthermore, both the heterotrophic and autotrophic organisms can deteriorate by
the two rates of decay, raut,dec and rhet,dec. Both types of bacteria will deteriorate into the
same species; XI, XS and SI. XI is particulate COD that is not biodegradable and thus
inert in this situation, SI is equally inert soluble COD, and XS is particulate COD, which
is hydrolyzable. Hydrolyzation is a chemical process in which a bond in a compound is
split by the addition of H2O to the structure. The hydroxyl group, OH– will attach to
one end of the split bond, and the hydrogen atom, H+, to the other end of the bond.
Eﬀectively, this process will break particulates down until the remnants are dissolved
nutrients in the water. Therefore, it will slowly degrade into the biodegradable, soluble
COD, SS, which a heterotrophic organism in turn can feed on. In the hydrolysis, the
inorganic nutrients, phosphate and ammonium, are formed as well. As expected, neither
of the inert substances XI or SI are of any use further in the process.

Challenges of the Lysefjord
The Lysefjord is a 38 km long fjord in the region of Forsand in Norway. It is currently

It is the density diﬀerence between the light fresh water and the denser salt water which
causes the fresh water to rise above the salt water in the water column. Stratiﬁcation is
of high importance when studying the ﬂow patterns of fjords.

2.2.1
Water exchange in the Lysefjord
The Lysefjord is a much smaller fjord than the fjords which are typically used for ﬁsh
farming, i.e. the Hardangerfjord or the Sognefjord. There is a number of challenges which
arise when the fjord is smaller.
In the second largest Norwegian fjord, the Hardangerfjord, the sill depth is around
150-200 m and the fjord is around 900 m deep at the deepest. Thus, the sill leaves at
least 17% of the water column open to the sea. In the Lysefjord the sill depth is only 14
m and the fjord is nearly 460 m deep at the deepest point [2]. This geometry only leaves
3% of the deepest water column open. The water exchange in the Hardangerfjord is more
than likely inﬂuenced by the sill, but it can be assumed that the eﬀect is signiﬁcantly
more drastic in the Lysefjord.
In the Lysefjord, the sill may cause the water velocity to be greatly limited the further
below the sill depth you get. This can have severe consequences if the biomass from the
ﬁsh farms sinks below the sill depth before it can be swept out of the fjord and into the
sea. If this is the case, the biomass might sink to the bottom of the fjord, or it may be
suspended in the water. This will depend on the buoyancy of the biomass. As the biomass
is degraded by microbial activity, the oxygen in the water is consumed and depending
on the degree of biomass pollution there might be local areas which can be completely
depleted of oxygen. This will have severe consequences to the ecosystem either locally or
for the entire fjord depending on placement and grade of the pollution.
The water exchange in the fjord is also limited by the fact that there is a mixture of salt
water from the sea and fresh water which runs oﬀthe surrounding highlands and into the
fjord. Due to the density diﬀerence of the ﬂuids the water is parted into two zones in the
fjord. The denser salt water lies in the deepest part of the water column. This part is
by far the biggest in the fjord, whereas only approximately the top 20 meters consist of
water with a lower salt content due to the fresh water from the mountains [2].

2.2.2
Environmental situation
In a report about the Lysefjord written in 2007, the ecological status of the fjord was
determined by a group of biologists for a land-based ﬁsh farming site, Eidane Smolt A/S
[2]. In this report, it was concluded that the ecological environment of the Lysefjord could
be categorized as an ”oxygen-depleted and strongly fresh water inﬂuenced fjord” [2]. This
is due to a number of factors, of which the most notable is that it is permanently layered
(stratiﬁcation), with the deeper waters being mostly stagnant. Furthermore, the deep
water of the fjord has a very low level of oxygen (≈2 −5 mg/L [2]) and there is a long
time frame for total water exchange. The total water exchange, which was described in
section 2.2.1, has been estimated to only happen once every 8-11 years, and this factor also
contribute to the low rating of the Lysefjord. Another factor which lowers the ecological
status of the fjord further is the very weak tidal eﬀects.
As with any Norwegian fjord with a sill as pronounced as the one in the Lysefjord,
the water column in the fjord is signiﬁcantly stratiﬁed.
This means that the oxygen
concentration in the deeper parts of the fjord, especially in the aphotic zone, is very low.
This will mean that any notable pollution with organic matter may cause zones of hypoxia
to occur in the fjord. It may even cause euxinic zones, where hydrogen sulﬁde, H2S, can
be formed. This only occurs when a body of water is depleted of oxygen while being
acidic. Euxinic situations has not occurred much in modern history, but an example can
be Mariager Fjord in Denmark or Framvaren Fjord in Norway [5].

2.3. Introduction to ﬂuid dynamics and modelling
2.3
Introduction to ﬂuid dynamics and modelling
Fluid dynamics is the study of moving and stationary ﬂuids.
It is of great scientiﬁc
importance in a number of diﬀerent ﬁelds. Originally it has been studied by mechanical
engineers, but the ﬁeld has uses in many other areas of study; e.g. physics, chemistry,
biology, oceanography, environmental and atmospheric studies. The growth of the ﬁeld
of ﬂuid dynamics builds on a foundation of mathematics, simulations and experiments,
since purely analytical solutions can only be found to idealized situations [17].
However, by a combination of these ﬁelds the advances made by the use of ﬂuid
mechanics are rather notable. One of the biggest steps in the ﬁeld of ﬂuid dynamics is
the idea of boundary layers close to surfaces, and it was conceived by Ludwig Prandtl.
He was one of the founders of modern ﬂuid dynamics, and the idea of the boundary layer
was based solely on physical intuition [17]. One of the more recent developments has
been that of turbulence modelling. There is still no model which can describe the exact
turbulent behaviour, but the exponential development of computational capacities over
the last few decades has made it possible to compute averaged eﬀects of turbulence. The
ﬁeld of ﬂuid dynamics has thus seen signiﬁcant development as a consequence of progress
in other ﬁelds [17].

2.3.2
Boundary conditions
In this section the theory behind some of the boundary conditions which has been used
in this project will be presented. One thing, which is of great importance is the deﬁnition
of the boundaries of the system which is to be modelled. There is a number of diﬀerent
boundary conditions, of which the four most common types will be described in this
section; 1) The wall, 2) the in- and outlet, 3) the opening and 4) the symmetry.
Boundary conditions are determined by observing the physical boundaries of the mod-
elled system. As an example, when dealing with a tank reactor, the boundary conditions
can be determined by studying the tank.
Wall The wall surface in a tank reactor, will be stationary, and the ﬂuid will most likely
not slip on the walls. This is due to the shear stress, which the wall induces on the
ﬂuid. In that case, a no slip boundary condition for the radial velocity in a domain
with cylindrical coordinates should be;
uR|R=Rwall = 0,
(2.7)
where Rwall is the radius of the reactor. However, the wall could also be a moving
wall, where the right hand side of equation (2.7) would be equal to the velocity
of the moving wall. Furthermore, if the system is not adiabatic, heat conduction
across the wall should be taken into consideration.
Inlets and outlets Inlets and outlets are somewhat similar when deﬁning them. They
are both often deﬁned by the mass ﬂow rate or the ﬂuid velocity at the in- or outlets.
If the energy balances are solved, these boundary conditions are most often deﬁned
as the temperature of the ﬂuid at the inlets. In this project, all inlets were deﬁned
as the mass ﬂow rate into the system which all had ﬁxed temperatures.

is not necessarily clear whether the ﬂuid is going in or out of the geometry. An
example of this might be a pressure regulating valve in a tank reactor, or the top of

2.3.3.1

Modern turbulence modelling is still based on the Reynolds Averaged Navier-Stokes

equations are a modiﬁcation of the Navier-Stokes momentum balance from equation (2.3).
The modiﬁcations are based on the assumption that the velocity of the ﬂow, ux can be
approximated as the sum of the average velocity, ux, and the velocity of the turbulent

2.4. Computational Fluid Dynamics
2.4
Computational Fluid Dynamics
Fluid dynamics can provide fairly realistic results when the governing equations, which
were presented in section 2.3.1, are solved analytically. However this is only possible in
a few idealized situations, whereas the more realistic, and thus complicated, situations
must be solved numerically [17].
When computers were ﬁrst developed in the middle of the 20th century, the possibilities
for the use of Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) were very limited, but the ﬁeld
expanded quickly. In the 70’s and 80’s, the ﬁeld of CFD saw signiﬁcant growth within
several ﬁelds, and by the 90’s, the ﬁeld was well established. Today CFD is an important
tool when it comes to prototyping and testing within a number of ﬁelds. The tool has
been used mostly in the ﬁeld of mechanical engineering, as a way to test the eﬀects of
aerodynamics on diﬀerent structures, but over the years, CFD has become an integral
part of many engineering ﬁelds [17].
In chemical engineering, CFD is generally used in optimization and testing of reactors
in multiple sizes and shapes.
From micro-scale reactors to full-scale operational tank
reactors. It is a tool which helps to limit the need for prototypes and testing. CFD
however cannot completely replace these stages of production yet, but the cost of them
can be reduced signiﬁcantly.
2.4.1
CFD software
CFD can be expressed as a way to numerically solve the Partial Diﬀerential Equations
(PDEs) of the governing equations described in section 2.3.1. There are several steps to
solving a CFD problem and these steps will over-all be the same no matter what software
is used in the process. The ﬁrst step is the generation of the geometry of the ﬂuid which
is to be studied. This can be done by using 3D sketching tools such as SolidWorks, which
has been used in this project. However, simple geometries can more easily be sketched
directly in ICEM CFD, which is also used for meshing.
Meshing is the discretization of the volume into smaller volumes, called elements. In
this project, the software ICEM CFD, which is a part of the ANSYS program has been
used. The programs for the next steps of the process are found in the CFX package as
well. The next step is setting up the problem, which is done in the CFX Pre program.
Then follows the solving of the problem, which has been done in the CFX Solve program.
At last the results can be processed in the CFX Post program. The speciﬁcs of each step
are shown in the following sections.
2.4.1.1
Meshing
The geometry should be a 2D/3D model of the ﬂuid domain which is to be studied. By
meshing the volume, it is divided into smaller sub-volumes called elements. The corners
22
==PAGE:31==
2.4. Computational Fluid Dynamics
between any two sub-volumes are called nodes.
The meshing can be done in one of two ways, and in ﬁgure 2.7, the two diﬀerent types
of meshing are shown. The unstructured mesh consists of tetrahedral elements and the
structured mesh consists primarily of hexagonal elements. The unstructured mesh can be
generated automatically by the use of e.g. ICEM CFD. However, the unstructured mesh
is not very eﬃcient compared to the structured mesh when solving the ﬂuid problem. This
is mostly due to the use of hexahedral elements in the structured meshes and minimized
element distortion. Therefore, an unstructured mesh is mostly used when the geometry
is too complicated to do the structured mesh. The structured mesh takes much more
work to generate, but it requires a much simpler geometry for it to be realistic. Pipes,
tank reactors and such geometries should be meshed by using the structured approach.
As mentioned, the structured mesh is more time consuming to generate, but it results
in better computational times. The hexagonal elements will for the most parts lead to
shorter computational times, and the node density can be lower than an unstructured
mesh while achieving the same results.
In this project, an unstructured mesh is used for the general fjord, but for the simpler
test cases showing smaller sections of the fjord, a structured mesh is used. This is due
to the very complicated geometry of the Lysefjord, whereas the simpliﬁed geometries are
cubic and thus very easy to mesh using a structured approach.
When meshing any geometry, it is important to think of the ﬂuid problem at hand.
Ideally, the mesh would be incredibly ﬁne in the entire geometry. However this would
prolong the time it would take to compute the problem. Therefore, it is necessary to
be ”economical” about the amount of elements one chooses to work with. The size of
the elements should be varied in such a way that the elements are ﬁner where the most

2.4.1.2
Deﬁning the problem
When a mesh has been generated, it is loaded into the program CFX Pre, where the
simulation is set up. In this program, the ﬁrst step is to deﬁne the domain, which should
be studied. Here it is important to deﬁne the problem by choosing which ﬂuids are present,
whether or not buoyancy should be activated etc. At this step all the assumptions which
are made with respect to the momentum and energy balances are deﬁned.
As with
the mesh it is very important to be aware of the correlation between the complexity
of the problem to be solved and the computational time needed. Therefore, the more
assumptions one can reasonably make, the better this will be w.r.t. computational time.
Furthermore, each surface of the geometry should be assigned a boundary condition,
such as inlets, outlets, walls and openings. These boundary conditions and the cases in
which they are usable have been presented in more detail in section 2.3.2.
It is also in this program where it should be decided whether the simulation is a steady
state simulation or a transient simulation. A steady state simulation is a simulation where
the program will seek to reach a point where there is no accumulation anywhere in the
geometry. The ﬂow ﬁeld will be constant in the solution to this type of problem, and this
can be fairly hard to reach in certain cases with a complex set-up. Therefore it might be
a better choice to run the simulation as a transient problem, where time is a variable and
by doing this you monitor the development of the ﬂow ﬁeld and/or reaction.
A common way to minimize computational time is typically to develop a ﬂow proﬁle
by using a steady state simulation. Then the ﬂow proﬁle in the geometry is ”frozen”
before e.g. releasing a tracer or activating a reaction and then study this by using a
transient simulation. In this way, the program is not solving the momentum balance
but only monitoring the transport of e.g. a tracer. This will signiﬁcantly reduce the
computational power needed and thus reduce the time required for the simulation. It is
24
==PAGE:33==
2.4. Computational Fluid Dynamics
however only usable in problems where the ﬂow ﬁeld can be assumed constant, such as
a well mixed tank reactor. A case such as the fjord, where tidal ﬂow will change the
inlet/outlet of water at the mouth of the fjord two times a day, cannot be assumed to
have constant ﬂow ﬁelds for prolonged time. It can however be a necessary assumption
to make to gain some insights into how the ﬂuid will move around the fjord.
If a transient simulation is chosen, it is highly useful to monitor the results at certain
points along the simulation, e.g. every 10th minute. Depending on the mesh size these save
ﬁles can take up a lot of storage space and therefore it is a good idea to carefully consider
which variables are interesting to monitor in the simulation in question. An example
can be that in certain simulations, it might be of no interest to study the pressure, or
temperature, and therefore these variables should not be saved. This way the amount of
storage space needed can be minimized.
When the problem is fully deﬁned, a deﬁnition ﬁle is exported which contains all the
data of the problem.
2.4.1.3
Solving the problem
The next step is the solving, which is performed in the program CFX Solve. It is of course
possible to run simulations locally on the desktop, however the results can take up a large
amount of space on the computer, and the computation itself can take a lot of memory
to solve. Therefore it is nearly always beneﬁcial to solve the bigger problems by using
multiple cores for the simulation.
When loading the solver an initial condition ﬁle can be included, which is usually a
result ﬁle from a previous simulation. This makes it possible to use a ﬂow ﬁeld which was
found in earlier simulations as a starting point.
Once the simulation is running it is possible to change the conditions deﬁned in CFX
Pre while it is running in CFX Solve. Examples of factors which are most often changed
during simulations, are e.g. the time step of a transient simulation or the Root Mean
Square (RMS), which is the success criteria of a steady state simulation. However it can
also be used to change inlet velocities or other more comprehensive changes.
It is also possible to monitor the development of RMS values and monitoring points
and by doing this to determine how the simulation is progressing. During simulation,
it is possible to save back-up ﬁles which contain all data of the system, and which can
therefore be used as a starting point in a new simulation. The back-up ﬁles can also be
studied in CFX Post, to determine how the simulation is progressing.
2.4.1.4
Post-processing
The post processing program used in this project, is CFX Post, which is a program that
visualizes the results of your simulation.

It is furthermore possible to calculate certain variables, e.g. inlet and outlet concen-

2.4.2
Buoyancy modelling
Buoyancy covers the gravitational eﬀects on the ﬂuid. In a signiﬁcant majority of the
applications for CFD in chemical engineering, the gravitational eﬀects can be neglected
or just substituted by a simple pressure diﬀerence. However in this project where two
ﬂuids of diﬀerent densities are studied, the eﬀects of buoyancy cannot be neglected.
In the Lysefjord, there is a mixture of salt water from the ocean and fresh water from
the mountainsides. The salt water is more dense than the fresh water, and thus the fresh
water will be at the top of the water column. In order to be able to model the stratiﬁcation,
i.e. the density driven separation of fresh and salt water in the water column, the two
ﬂuids should be deﬁned in CFX Pre. These two ﬂuids are however completely miscible
which eases the computation signiﬁcantly, since the modelling of liquid-liquid interfaces
is computationally challenging.
The buoyancy force can also be calculated if e.g. the density of a ﬂuid is set as variable
w.r.t. temperature. This makes it possible to study the thermal eﬀects on ﬂow properties
as well.
The core concept in the use of buoyancy is the hydrostatic pressure. When the buoy-
ancy is activated in CFX Pre, the calculated pressure is the so-called moving pressure,
which does not include the hydrostatic pressure gradient. The moving pressure is respon-
sible for driving the ﬂow. However the variable ’Absolute Pressure’ which is possible to
monitor in CFX Pre, does contain the hydrostatic pressure gradient. It is the absolute
pressure which is used for any calculations of pressure dependent properties.
When the buoyancy model is activated in CFX Pre, the gravitational vector of the
system is to be deﬁned as well as a reference density (for multi-component ﬂows). By the
activation of the buoyancy, a source term, which is shown in equation (2.20), is added to
the momentum balances.
SM,buoy = (ρ −ρref) g
(2.20)
In this equation, ρref is the reference density which has been chosen. The pressure will
be diﬀerent depending on the chosen reference density. E.g. in an air-water ﬂow; if the
reference density is that of water, there will be a larger change of the pressure in the air
phase and vice versa. In the case of the Lysefjord, there is not much diﬀerence in the two
densities, and thus it should not make much of a diﬀerence.

The absolute pressure, Pabs, in equation (2.21) is calculated primarily in order to
compute properties which depend upon the pressure. In CFX, the pressure, upon which
the ﬂow of the ﬂuid is calculated is the regular pressure, P, which is popularly called the
”moving pressure”. This is the pressure which acts as a driving force upon the ﬂuid.
When using an opening (see section 2.3.2) in CFX Pre to deﬁne a boundary condition,
which covers an area that reaches a signiﬁcant depth in the water, some issues arise with
the activated buoyancy. There is consensus in the CFX Pre community that when dealing
with density diﬀerences and buoyancy, any opening boundary conditions should be deﬁned
with the static pressure being the hydrostatic pressure. However, an opening boundary
condition is also deﬁned by the fact that pressure diﬀerences between the opening and the
geometry will cause the ﬂuid to be pushed into the geometry (when Popening > Pgeometry)
or out of the geometry (when Popening < Pgeometry).
The hydrostatic boundary pressure is constant, but even if the hydrostatic pressure
is deﬁned as an initial condition across the fjord, the pressure proﬁle in the fjord quickly
returns back to only calculate the moving pressure, as described earlier in this section.
The problems with understanding the buoyancy has caused signiﬁcant issues with the
openings. It was determined that openings which cover a smaller area are very useful and
in general work well, but if the openings cover a greater area the interaction between the
ﬂuid pressure and the opening pressure causes signiﬁcant issues.
In this project, it has been observed how the ﬂuid ﬂows out of the domain in the
upper part of the opening, where the opening pressure is somewhat low. However, as the
hydrostatic pressure at the opening increases, the ﬂuid is pushed into the domain, since
the pressure inside the domain is lower. This pattern results in an extreme amount of
water being pushed into the domain in the bottom of the opening, and directly out again
in the upper part of the opening. This tendency and the potential causes of it is described
further in section 4.1.
Therefore, in this project, it was decided to divide this opening in such a way that
only the top 20 m of the boundary is an opening, while the lower part is approximated as
a wall. This was done to minimize any possible eﬀects of the atypical ﬂow pattern close
to the opening, which was not a physical solution with water velocities as high as 39 m/s.

3.1.2
Meshing choices
As described in section 2.4.1.1, a good mesh should be adapted to the simulation it is
used for. Depending on the shape of the geometry either a structured or an unstructured
mesh should be applied. The high complexity of the fjord makes it very time consuming
to produce a structured mesh. This could have been done but due to the limited time
available in a masters project, it was decided to do an unstructured mesh with the use of
tetrahedral elements.
In section 2.2.1, some general knowledge of the ﬂow pattern in the Lysefjord was
presented. Since it is known that in the depths of the Lysefjord (> 20 m) the water
is mostly stagnant, it presents a good reason to increase the node density close to the
surface, while decreasing the node density drastically towards the bottom of the fjord. In
this way the changes close to the surface can be predicted while avoiding ”wasting” nodes
on modelling mostly stagnant ﬂow closer to the bottom.
When testing the simulations, signiﬁcant issues with the opening boundary condition
at the mouth of the fjord arose. Across the opening, which is 120 m deep, the hydrostatic
pressure changes signiﬁcantly. This caused large amounts of water to be pushed into the
fjord towards the bottom, just to be pushed out closer to the surface. Due to these issues,
it was decided to close the bottom of the opening as a wall at the mouth of the fjord and
only leave the upper 20 m as an opening. In this way, the result will more accurately
show the stagnant situation at the depths of the fjord.
It would have been a good idea to deﬁne the geometry by the shallowest part of the
sill in order to minimize the area which the opening had to cover and thus minimize the
issues in this area.

3.3. Strategy for simulations
3.3
Strategy for simulations
The strategy, w.r.t. simulations in this project, was to ensure a realistic ﬂow proﬁle of
the fjord. Once this was achieved, the next step was to generate a number of meshes in
diﬀerent sizes in order to be able to run a mesh study. The mesh study was based on
the quality of the layering between the two ﬂuids; I.e. salt water and fresh water. It
was decided to use the mesh which was both accurate enough whilst it was small enough
to have realistic computational times. Due to the signiﬁcant size of the fjord, it was
not expected to be able to observe the point where an increase in mesh size would no
longer mean an increase in the quality of the results. However, it was expected to see the
tendency of the poor quality results from the smaller meshes and the increase in quality
as the mesh size increased.
The ﬂow problem could only be solved with time steps below 1 s and thus the com-
putational times were greatly inﬂuenced. As can be imagined with a geometry as large
as the Lysefjord, it took a long time for any of the simulations to reach a steady state
situation with the very low time steps. Therefore, the computational time has been of
great importance to the choice of the mesh.
Once a suﬃcient mesh had been chosen the next step was to determine how the
pollutant, i.e. dissolved COD, would be distributed from the ﬁsh farm. This was done by
freezing the ﬂow ﬁeld which had been achieved during the mesh study. At the expected
placement of the ﬁsh farm, a tracer with similar diﬀusion coeﬃcients as the dissolved
COD could then be released, and the distribution of the COD would be computed.
Simultaneously, the kinetic model should be tested in a simpler geometry before it
should be added to the ﬂow simulation. This was done in a very ﬁnely meshed geometry,
in order to decrease the amount of computational error which might be caused by a coarse
mesh.

4.1
Buoyancy and Pressure
The opening boundary condition (B.C.) can be set up in diﬀerent ways with varying
results. In this section, the process of understanding the mechanics of the opening B.C.
is presented. In order to ﬁnd the the B.C. which yielded the most realistic results, a
number of diﬀerent scenarios were studied. To study the eﬀects of the diﬀerent opening
boundary conditions (B.C.), an arbitrary line were deﬁned in the CFX Post program half
a meter from the opening. In ﬁgure 4.1, the velocity of the ﬂuid along this line close to
the opening is shown. In this ﬁgure a signiﬁcant and un-physical amount of back ﬂow
is observed. Note the velocities of up to 39 m/s. The signiﬁcant amount of back ﬂow
recirculates rapidly and runs out of the fjord again. Therefore, the highly un-physical
velocities are restricted to the outer basin, close to the opening.
As described in section 2.4.2, an opening boundary condition is typically deﬁned with
a pressure at the outlet. It describes the pressure, which would be experienced by the
ﬂuid right on the opposite site of the opening. It was also described that the consensus
in the ANSYS community was that in simulations where buoyancy eﬀects were modelled,
any opening boundary condition should be deﬁned by the help of the hydrostatic pressure.
This was the case in one of the simulations of the entire fjord, of which the result is shown
in ﬁgure 4.1. In ﬁgure 4.2, the velocities at the aforementioned reference line in ﬁgure
4.1 are plotted alongside the hydrostatic relative pressure at the opening and the moving
pressure at the line. The moving pressure is the pressure which drives the movement of
the ﬂuid. It is shown in equation (2.21), where it is denoted P. This equation shows that
the absolute pressure of the domain is the sum of the reference pressure, the hydrostatic
pressure and the moving pressure. The absolute pressure, is mainly calculated in order to
determine values of material properties which depends upon the pressure of the domain.
The relative pressures at the reference line and the opening are both relative to the
atmospheric pressure, which has been deﬁned in CFX Pre.

to being sucked in, at depths greater than 30 m. This is shown in greater detail in ﬁgure
4.3. The ﬂow is caused by the pressure gradient between the domain and the opening
pressures. The pressure upon which the movement of the ﬂuid is calculated is the moving
pressure as it was described in section 2.4.2. It can be seen in ﬁgure 4.3 how the diﬀerence
between the moving pressure and the opening pressure is only positive until a depth of
approximately 30 meters. At this point the pressure outside the opening becomes higher
than the moving pressure inside the domain. Therefore, at this depth the ﬂow direction
shifts and water is pushed into the domain through the opening. Thus:
Popen > Pmove : Flow out of the domain.
Popen < Pmove : Flow into the domain.
This was also tested for a case where the relative opening pressure and the initial pressure
across the domain were set at 0 Pa. These results were un-physical as well, but in a
diﬀerent, and not as signiﬁcant, way. The results are presented in appendix D.2.
What is noteworthy is the fact that there was no signiﬁcant diﬀerences in the way
the ﬂow proﬁle across the fjord developed in spite of the ﬂow diﬀerences at the opening.
Furthermore, it is worth noting that both results have been running for half an hour of
simulation time, and thus they have not reached steady state completely. However, it was
generally observed in this project that the ﬂow ﬁeld rapidly reached a point where there
were only minute changes over time. Therefore, the results for the opening were used at
this time to underline the qualitative tendencies of the opening B.C.
Since it was known that the ﬂow would stagnate towards the sea ﬂoor, it was decided
to try to simulate a simpliﬁed case, where the opening was limited to the upper 20 meters
of the boundary, while the stagnant ﬂow at the bottom part of the boundary below 20
m was replaced by a wall boundary condition (B.C.). The depth of 20 m were decided
on due to the literature. 20 m has been recorded as a depth where the water is more or
less completely stagnant [2]. Another sgument for the 20 m limit was motivated by ﬁgure
4.3, where it can be seen that the back ﬂow does not begin until a depth of ≈32 m is
reached. Thus, it was expected that by setting the new limit at 20 m, back ﬂow would
be avoided, while still simulating a realistic case.
The opening B.C. until a depth of 20 m resulted in a signiﬁcant decrease in back ﬂow
and smaller velocities as well. However, the results were still un-physical with velocities
up to 19 m/s as shown in ﬁgure 4.4.
In order to ﬁx the un-physical results, it was considered whether the ﬂuid behaviour at
the opening would have any inﬂuence on the ﬂow pattern in the inner part of the fjord.
The sill might have acted as a barrier and has probably been the cause of the rapid
recirculation of the back ﬂow out of the domain. The diﬀerence of the inner ﬂow pattern
of the inner fjord between the full opening and the 20 m deep opening were tested to
determine whether the sill might limit the eﬀects of the opening velocities on the inner

4.2. Mesh study
4.2
Mesh study
A mesh study was done by using the simulations with only 20 meters opening at the mouth
of the fjord. In a mesh study the achieved accuracy is compared with the quality of the
mesh. As the size of a mesh increases, so will the achieved accuracy of the obtained results.
Usually there will be a point at which the quality of the results no longer increases with
the size of the mesh. At this point, it would no longer be sensible to use bigger meshes.
When considering the increase in computational time, as the meshes increase in size, it
can even be necessary to use smaller, less precise meshes to achieve any results at all. In
this case the achieved results must be analysed while keeping the inaccuracies in mind.
It was not expected to reach the point where an increase in quality no longer resulted
in improvement of the results. This tendency can typically be documented in regular
reactor tanks or similar issues, were signiﬁcantly higher node densities can be achieved in
the meshes. However, since the fjord covers a much larger volume, the element sizes are
too large in even the ﬁnest of the generated meshes to reach a point where the results will
not be signiﬁcantly blurred by the poor quality of the meshes.
The velocity ﬁelds and temperature gradients of the mesh study simulations with the
20 m opening never really reached a satisfactory level of agreement with experimental
results. However, the layering of the fresh water on top of the salt water is something,
which shows reasonable results when compared to experimental results. Therefore, it was
decided to do the mesh study based on the layering along a line which was placed close to
a site were experimental results were generated by Johnsen et al. [2]. It was chosen to test
along line 3, which is shown in appendix A.3. This line is fairly close to the experimental
site at ”Mulen”, which is shown in the same appendix.
The results of the simulations are shown in ﬁgure 4.6. In this ﬁgure it can be seen how
none of the meshes reached a volume fraction of salt water of 1 before a depth of 100-150
m. However, it can be seen how the very coarse meshes, has lower salt water fractions
further into the depth, and as the meshes become ﬁner, the gradient of the salt water
fraction becomes sharper and sharper. At the same time, as the mesh size increases, so
does the surface volume fraction of fresh water. This is in line with what was expected,
but as only the top 20 meters are expected to be lower in salt water content, the results
are not exact, but as previously stated, this was not expected.
Another factor was considered when choosing a mesh is the computational time. The
coarsest of the meshes which were tested had computational times of approximately a day
or two, whereas the largest mesh shown here took approximately 11 days to reach steady
state. This is shown in ﬁgure 4.7
The quality of the results cannot count for much if there is no time to achieve the
results. Ideally, much more computer power would be available and thus a much ﬁner
mesh would be used. However, as computational power is limited, decent results must
be weighed against computational time in order to determine which mesh would be the
sensible choice.

Flow pattern
After the mesh study described in section 4.2, it was decided to study the 866k mesh
in greater detail, since this mesh size was adequately accurate for a study of the general
tendencies while not being too time consuming to get results.
It was expected to see movement in the fjord in approximately the top 20 meters while
the water in the deep layer would be stagnant. However, the water of the fjord was found
to be in movement even at signiﬁcant depths. This can be seen in ﬁgure 4.10, where
the velocity is depicted in a plane along the fjord. The simulated velocities close to the
bottom are very low, but at those depths the water is expected to be completely stagnant.
This behaviour can to some extend be explained by the eﬀects of the hydrostatic
pressure upon the two ﬂuids with diﬀerent densities. The upper layer of fresh water has
lower density than the deeper layer of salt water, and the gravity will therefore work to
pull the salt water down, which in turn will push the fresh water upwards. As described
in section 3.1.1, the gravity is slanted due to the diﬀerence in altitude from one end of
the fjord to the other. This will cause the ﬂuid to behave as sketched in ﬁgure 4.11.
This eﬀect was not expected, but it is mathematically valid according to how the
domain was initialized. In sections 4.3.1.1-4.3.1.2 the eﬀects on the ﬂow pattern will be
described in depth by adding tracers to the ﬂow.

Chapter 5
Discussion
During this project, issues arose with the understanding of the pressure in the domain
vs. the hydrostatic pressure at the opening boundary condition at the mouth of the fjord.
The issues are described in detail in section 4.1, and to sum up, the pressure diﬀerence
between the opening and the domain caused a signiﬁcant amount of back ﬂow to occur
with very high velocities. It is not certain to which degree the high velocities in the outer
basin has inﬂuenced the ﬂow pattern in the inner fjord, i.e. inside the sill.
The severity of the pressure diﬀerence at the boundary was lessened to some degree
by assuming the bottom part of the opening could be approximated as a wall, since
experimental results have shown the water to be stagnant at this depth. This did not
remove the back ﬂow, but lessened the amount considerably, as the maximum ﬂow rates
at the outlet went from 39 m/s to 19 m/s. These results were still un-physical, however
it was decided to move on with the results due to time limitations.
It was tested whether the ﬂow pattern of the inner fjord was inﬂuenced by the change
between the two cases, i.e. with a full opening and a 20 m opening(see appendix D.3).
There were no discernible diﬀerences between the ﬂow ﬁelds of the inner fjord generated
by the two diﬀerent opening boundaries. It can thus be understood that the extreme
ﬂow rates seem to be limited to the outer basin due to the sheltering eﬀect of the sill,
which may, to some extent, have caused any back ﬂow to exit the fjord again immediately.
However, this should be tested with a more shallow opening B.C.
It can, furthermore, be recommended to put the focus either on fully understanding
the opening boundary condition and its uses when it should cover large depths, or on the
ﬂow pattern itself. In the last case, it would be an advantage to put a wall boundary
condition in the depth of the opening. The depth should be decided based on tests of
diﬀerent opening sizes in the range of 1-10 m closest to the surface which could allow the
water to ﬂow out as unhindered as possible. An opening depth should then be chosen
based on the results which showed the most realistic ﬂow pattern out of the domain. I.e.
the focus should be on achieving realistic velocities and limited recirculation of ﬂuid due
to the opening being too narrow.
64
==PAGE:73==
It would also be beneﬁcial to use the experiences from this project and regenerate
the geometry and the meshes in such a way, that the opening is directly above the most
shallow part of the sill. As described in section 2.2, the literature explains that the fjord
has two sills, and in the process of trimming the geometry the sills were exchanged by
mistake, and the fjord was cut oﬀjust upstream of the outer, more shallow sill, which has
a depth of 14 meter. In stead, the sill which was studied in this thesis, was the inner sill,
which reaches a depth of approximately 45 meter [2]. By regenerating the geometry to
include the outer sill, and simultaneously placing the opening at the most shallow point
of the fjord, the more correct geometry along with a more shallow opening will result in
more realistic results.
The mesh study was done on the basis of the distribution of fresh water across the depths
of the fjord. It was described in detail in section 4.2, and the results did not diﬀer from
what was expected. Even the biggest mesh that was tested, showed signs that the quality
of the results would still increase as the mesh increased.
However, there was a clear
diﬀerence in quality of the results from the coarsest to the ﬁnest mesh, and this proved
that less numerical diﬀusion of the two ﬂuids into one another occurred when the mesh
got ﬁner.
In section 4.2.1, the salt concentration of the fjord were calculated from the volume
fraction of fresh water and the total salt content of the salt water in the fjord.
The
salt concentration was then plotted against experimental results from a similar location
in the fjord.
This comparison showed that there was a signiﬁcant diﬀerence between
the simulation results and the experimental values. The experimental results showed that
only the upper 20 meters had lower salt content due to the fresh water, and in comparison
the simulation results showed a lower salt content, which reached down from 150 m to
200 m. The qualitative tendency of the simulated salt content was however in agreement
with the experimental data, as the salt content at the surface were ≈20 mg/L both in
experiments and the simulations.
It was therefore considered whether the un-realistic depth of the fresh water layer were
due to the initialization of the domain. In these simulations, it the initial concentrations of
the domain were based on a step function (see section 3.2.1.3), and at the top 20 meter of
the fjord consisted of 100% fresh water. The experimental results showed a steep gradient
of the salt content in the top 20 meters from 21 mg/L to 32.5 mg/L, which corresponds
to a volume percent of pure salt water (32.5 mg salt/L) of approximately 60% to 100%.
Therefore, the assumption that the upper 20 meters were 100% fresh water, was likely
not an ideal assumption, and it was therefore decided to test a diﬀerent case were only
the top 5 meters were considered pure fresh water. However, these results showed a high
distribution of the fresh water through the water column as well as signiﬁcantly lower
amounts of fresh water at the surface. It is likely a mix of the following factors which has
caused the layering to be less sharp than expected:
Mesh size The new initializations were only tested for the medium-coarse meshes (595k
65
==PAGE:74==
and 866k) and therefore the amount of numerical diﬀusion could be an explanation.
However, the larger meshes were unlikely to yield more precise results (see ﬁgure
4.6).
The slanted gravity This eﬀect caused the two ﬂuids to run in opposite directions, and
thus the velocity gradient would be signiﬁcant which would increase the turbulence.
Thus the mixing between the layers was increased.
The temperature In the simulations, the temperature which were calculated where too
high compared to experimental results throughout the domain. This is described in
section 4.3. This would cause the viscosity of the ﬂuids to be higher than it would
be in reality, and thus the mobility of the water would be increased. It would thus
be interesting to study the layering of the fjord when the temperature is held at
7.5◦C throughout the domain.
Furthermore, it would be interesting to only study the fjord geometry from the water
surface to a depth of 50-100 m. Since it is known that the fjord is stagnant at depths
below this level, the assumption would not be too erroneous. By simplifying the geometry
in this way, the mesh density could be increased, and the over-all ﬂow pattern could be
studied in-depth. This type of simulation could determine how the ﬂuid moved in the
fjord.
Another factor, which has proven to be troublesome, was the gravitation used in the
simulations. The gravitation should be slanted in this geometry, as explained in section
3.1.1, since the water level in fjord is not completely level due to the height diﬀerence
of the two ends of the Lysefjord. I.e. the water surface is not completely level and the
bottom of the fjord is approximately 1.5 m higher up than the mouth of the fjord. It
is this factor which should cause the water to ﬂow out of the fjord. However, as the
two ﬂuids; Fresh water and salt water has diﬀerent densities and the separation of the
layers were dependent on the buoyancy eﬀects, the ﬂow pattern became inﬂuenced in an
unexpected way. The eﬀect of the buoyancy on the ﬂow depended on the gravity vector,
as described in section 2.4.2. Thus, when the gravity was slanted towards the mouth of
the fjord, the lighter ﬂuid, i.e. the fresh water, would collect at the bottom of the fjord.
This was described in depth in section 4.3, where it was shown in detail how the fresh
water were found to stream upwards toward the bottom of the fjord.
It is highly likely that this ﬂuid behaviour can be observed to a muted degree in the
fjord, as described in section 4.3. It should also be considered that the system might
not have reached a full steady state proﬁle, after only two hours of simulation time (see
appendix D.4).
The results of the simulation of the biological model are presented in section 4.4. The
results were good, and mostly showed the expected tendencies. However, the rapid hy-
drolyzation of the particulate COD, XS, were unexpected, as this process is actually a
66
==PAGE:75==
rather slow process. It is however not currently known how long time the hydrolyza-
tion would be expected to take. Thus, it is diﬃcult to determine whether the model is
over-predicting the rate of reaction, or if the process is that fast.
It would have been very interesting to implement the results in a more complex situ-
ation, e.g. within a slow ﬂow ﬁeld with a constant but low, replenishing of the nutrients,
e.g. phosphate and ammonia, which would be present in the run-oﬀfrom the mountains.
It cannot be advised to add it to the fjord model directly, since the ﬂow model still has a
number of issues, which must be dealt with before such an implementation. But in a sim-
pliﬁed geometry, the performance of the biological model in a non-stagnant environment
could be very interesting.
There are a few factors which should be added to the biological model before studying
the performance of it in a ﬂow situation. First of all, the model is in fact temperature
dependent, but this dependency has not been implemented in this project. Second of all,
the eﬀects of higher or lower concentrations of single species on the development of the
processes would be very interesting to study further. Speciﬁcally, the concentrations of
oxygen as well as dissolved COD would be interesting test subjects. This were, however,
beyond the scope of this project. It could in this way be determined if e.g. the anoxic
heterogeneous growth rate would dominate the conversion of dissolved COD at lower
concentrations of oxygen.
Now, when considering the fail safes which were integrated into the reaction model by
the use of if conditions, the results showed that the fail safes in the individual reaction rates
worked satisfactorily. Both the concentrations of the auto- and heterotropic substances, as
well as the concentration of hydrolyzable particulate COD reached zero, without becoming
negative. Furthermore, the production of the species by the reactions independent of the
specie itself still occurred and thus the results seem to be decent.
However, the fail safe which was installed in the total rate of change for PO4 was
never triggered, and as such the performance were never evaluated. The if condition used
for the rate of change of phosphate would evaluate the rate of change of phosphate as
0 if the concentration of phosphate ever dropped lower than 1 × 10−9 kg/m3, and this
would eﬀectively stop the decreasing concentration of phosphate. However, this would
also negate the eﬀects of the hydrolyzation of particular COD (XS) which also produces
phosphate.
As this rate of reaction is independent of the concentration of phosphate
this would be a signiﬁcant ﬂaw, as any production of phosphate from this time would be
ignored. Thus, the if condition should ideally be changed in a way so a lack of phosphate
would only aﬀect the rates of those reactions which converts phosphate, i.e. the aerobic
and anoxic heterotrophic growth rates.
When considering the results achieved in this project, it is evident that a conclusive
report on the eﬀects of inserting a ﬁsh farm cannot be achieved at this point in the
process. However, the results in general can still describe some of the tendencies of the
ecological impacts of a ﬁsh farm in the Lysefjord.

The simulations of the ﬂow pattern of the inner fjord resulted in velocities which were
close to the range of experimental values of the velocity magnitudes in the fjord.
In
section 2.2.1 the average velocity at the sill was calculated to be 0.089 m/s based on data
from Johnsen et al. [2]. The average velocity at the sill can be seen as the maximum
velocities which should be seen in the fjord over-all, as the sill has the lowest cross-
sectional area. Thus, when evaluating the results of the simulations shown in ﬁgure 4.10
and appendix D.4, the velocities which were achieved are larger than would be expected,
when compared to the average velocity of the outer sill. However, they are still within the
same order of magnitude as the average velocity at the sill calculated from Johnsen et al.
[2]. Furthermore, the result in ﬁgure 4.10 emphasizes the tendency of larger velocities at
the sill and lower velocities in the basin.
With these somewhat larger velocities in mind, the results of the tracer simulations
are interesting. In ﬁgure 4.15 the tracer was released from the site of a potential ﬁsh farm
and then tracked for three days. The distribution of the tracer was greater than expected,
but when considering the larger velocities which the simulations showed, the distribution
can be assumed to be smaller in the actual fjord.
Furthermore, the tracer for which
the distribution was computed had the transport properties of dissolved COD, since the
tracing of dense particulate matter were beyond the scope of this project. Since dense
particles would sink towards the bottom of the fjord more rapidly than a dissolved specie,
which would be swept with the ﬂow, the distribution of such particles can be expected to
be even less pronounced.
With this in mind, the biological model, of which the results are presented in section
4.4, provides interesting insight into a resulting oxygen depletion. The results points to
a relatively rapid conversion of the hydrolyzable particulate COD, XS, and in general
rather fast kinetics. In 10 days, the oxygen concentration in the test case had decreased
0.5 mg/L from the initial concentration.
In summation; As the simulated velocities are above the average sill velocity estimated
from Johnsen et al. [2] and the transport of organic particulates will be slower than that
of the dissolved COD (see ﬁgure 4.15), the distribution can in conclusion be expected to
be over-predicted in ﬁgure 4.15. In combination with the relatively rapid rate of reaction
of oxygen which were simulated in this project, it implies that the pollution with organic
matter will result in an over-all negative impact on the ecosystem of the fjord.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
In this study, the ﬂow properties of the Lysefjord (Norway) was investigated by the use of
CFD simulations. This was done with the purpose of studying the eﬀects of insertion of
a ﬁsh farm in the fjord. The Lysefjord is 38 km long and has a water depth ranging from
460 m at the deepest place to 14 m at the sill. This geometry results in a limited water
exchange and therefore the additional organic pollution from a ﬁsh farm is suspected to
threaten the environment of the fjord. The geometry of the fjord was found from 3D
scans of the Norwegian coast and it was meshed with a ﬁner mesh closer to the surface
and the 18 inlets, where the most activity was expected.
The stratiﬁcation of the water column is severe. Not only because of the sill, but also due
to the immobility of the dense and cold salt water in the deeper waters, in comparison to
the less dense and warmer fresh water which would have greater mobility. The purpose
of the project was to simulate these characteristics.
The qualitative tendency of the
salt content of the fjord was found to be similar to experimental results. However, the
simulated depth of the fresh water layer were signiﬁcantly thicker than the experimental
results showed. The simulated temperature proﬁle of the fjord were not similar to the
experimental results either, as the temperature throughout the depth of the fjord were
much too high. It is highly likely to be a reason for the increased mobility towards the
depth of the fjord which was seen in the simulations.
It can be concluded that the ﬂow pattern of the fjord in general was not successfully
simulated even though some qualitative tendencies were decent. The opening boundary
condition used at the mouth of the fjord evaluated to un-physical velocities (i.e. 19 m/s).
It was discussed that the eﬀects of the opening mechanics on the ﬂow patterns of the rest
of the fjord were minimal, since the sill acted as a barrier. Tests were run to determine
if this hypothesis was possibly true, and the results showed good agreement with the
hypothesis.
However, the general ﬂow pattern of the fjord was tested by inserting tracers two
places; 1) the largest inlet from the power station, Lyse Kraftstasjon, and 2) the approxi-
69
==PAGE:78==
6.1. Future study
mate placement where a ﬁsh farm might be inserted. The tracer studies showed that the
fresh water streamed upwards toward the bottom of the fjord. This can be concluded to
be due to the slanted gravity vector which was implemented as a driving force of the ﬂuid,
since the water surface was not completely level.
The biodegradation model of dissolved Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD) by auto- and
heterotrophic biomass was implemented in a simpliﬁed, cubic geometry, in which no ﬂow
was simulated. It was necessary to modify the biological model slightly by implement-
ing if conditions which would shut the reaction rates down when the reactants reached
concentrations below 1 × 10−9 kg/m3. In this way, the kinetics of the biological model
became more robust, and it was possible to run the simulation with large time steps of
15 min. The simulation was initialized with primarily regular sea water concentrations of
the diﬀerent species, and the behaviour of the kinetics was found to be in agreement with
the expectations.
In conclusion, the simulations cannot be accepted as good solutions, however the
tendencies of the results can show that there could be an impact to the environment of
the Lysefjord.
6.1
Future study
During this project a number of ideas were thought out, which would be very interesting
to study further. In general a more computationally stable and realistic opening solution
would be desirable. A hydrostatic pressure at the opening boundary yielded highly un-
physical results, while a boundary pressure of 0 bar showed the opposite ﬂow tendencies.
Therefore, it would be highly interesting to study whether a relative boundary pressure
of 1-2 bars at the opening might yield results which were in a sweet spot between the
results of the hydrostatic B.C. and the 0 bar B.C.
In order to improve the computation in general it would be a good idea to attempt
to produce a structured mesh of the fjord. It might be diﬃcult to achieve a satisfactory
quality of the structured mesh. However, if a good structured mesh was achieved, it might
help bring down the computational times.
The kinetics of the biological model should furthermore be improved. As described
earlier, a number of processes in the fjord were neglected in this simpliﬁed model. In
order to get a full view of how the kinetics would work, some, or all, of these processes
should be implemented as well. The additions to the kinetic simulations could be;
1. Modelling of the pH situation in the fjord by adding the alkalinity of the water into
the biological model. This would make it possible to study the formation of H2S in
the fjord.
2. Mass transport of oxygen to the water at the surface of the fjord as well as the forma-
tion of oxygen by planktion and algae in the fjord. This would provide signiﬁcantly
70
==PAGE:79==
6.1. Future study
more realistic views of the oxygen levels in the fjord.
3. Formation of H2S in the areas without dissolved oxygen and with low pH. In this
way, signiﬁcantly oxygen depleted zones could be discovered.
A number of general areas which could be studied further are:
Particle experiment The dissolution of the particles and the eﬀects on the buoyancy
properties of them would be an interesting experiment to do, in order to improve the
distribution of the particles from the ﬁsh farm. This project might be done along
with CFD modelling of a part of the water column where the particle transport
experimental data were utilized.
Oxygen transfer at inlets In the current model all the inlets are very wide and reaches
far into the fjord, whereas a signiﬁcant number of these are water falls. It would
be interesting to do a detailed study of the ﬂow patterns at these inlets in order to
elevate the quality of the results.
Tidal waves The carying ﬂow direction caused by tidal waves would increase mixing in
the fjord, and aﬀect the distribution of particles. It could be a possibility to use
time as a variable in a step function to adjust the pressure and determine whether
the opening should push water into the geometry or let it ﬂow out.
Seasonal changes In the summer, the viscosity of the warmer fresh water is even higher
than that of the underlying salt water. In the winter, when the temperature is more
uniform throughout the water column, the densities might level out to some degree
which should lessen the degree of stratiﬁcation. It would be very interesting to study
these eﬀects.

diameter can be calculated for the approximated height, H, and width, W, of the fjord.
For the duct, the hydraulic diameter is the ratio of the cross-sectional area of the conduit
to the length of the wetted perimeter (see equation (2.9)).

Appendix D
Simulations
D.1
Initial pressure vs. pressure after 10 s
During this project a number of diﬀerent cases has been studied to try to understand
the pressure behaviour. Primarily, the opening pressure was deﬁned as the hydrostatic
pressure. While keeping this constant the domain was initialized in two ways; 1) With
a pressure proﬁle of 0, and 2) with a hydrostatic pressure proﬁle. In case 1) there was
low expectations to the velocity ﬁeld at the opening, since there would be signiﬁcant
diﬀerences between opening and domain pressures. In case 2) the pressure in the domain
and in the opening would be equal, and therefore a more physically valid solution was
expected.
However, after a short period of simulation time, the pressure proﬁle only
reached a maximum of 3 bar where 12 bar was expected (see ﬁgure D.1), and the results
in the two cases became identical.
