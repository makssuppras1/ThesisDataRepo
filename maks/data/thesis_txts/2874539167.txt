Abstract 
Corynebacterium glutamicum is a key microbial workhorse for amino acid and metabolite 
production, yet its preferential utilization of glucose and the resulting diauxic growth remain 
major bottlenecks in mixed-sugar fermentations from renewable feedstocks. These substrate 
transitions impose energetic costs that affect growth and productivity, but the intracellular 
ATP dynamics underlying this behavior are not well understood. In this study, genetically 
encoded fluorescent biosensors, QUEEN-7u and QUEEN-2M, were introduced into C. 
glutamicum CR099 and CR099::U strains via ectopic plasmid integration to enable real-time 
monitoring of cellular energy status. Sensor functionality was first validated in Escherichia 
coli before transferring into C. glutamicum. Growth and fluorescence were monitored using 
microplate assays, BioLector cultivations, and plate reader measurements under defined 
glucose, xylose, and mixed-sugar conditions, with and without IPTG induction. Baseline 
experiments confirmed compatibility of sensor expression with growth, while comparative 
analyses revealed variant-specific differences, including leaky expression and greater 
responsiveness of QUEEN-7u relative to QUEEN-2M. In glucose–xylose co-utilization 
experiments, diauxic shifts were observed, accompanied by transient ATP fluctuations during 
substrate switching. Although genomic integration attempts were unsuccessful, ectopic 
sensor expression provided a functional platform to probe ATP homeostasis in vivo. Together, 
these results demonstrate the feasibility of applying QUEEN biosensors to investigate energy 
metabolism in C. glutamicum, while highlighting current limitations of plasmid-based 
expression. This work establishes a framework for future development of stably integrated 
biosensors and process strategies aimed at reducing lag phases and improving mixed-sugar 
utilization in industrial fermentations.

1. Introduction 
1.1 
Background 
The microbial production of bulk and fine chemicals from renewable resources, such as 
lignocellulosic biomass and residual by-products from agriculture, algae, and forestry, is 
essential for driving the green transition in industrial manufacturing. However, biomass 
hydrolysates and residual streams present significant challenges for microbial cultivation due 
to their complex mixtures of sugars and organic acids (1,2). 
Corynebacterium glutamicum is a widely used industrial microorganism, particularly for the 
production of amino acids such as glutamate and lysine. It has also been engineered to 
produce other valuable metabolites, including organic acids such as succinic acid. Despite 
these capabilities, C. glutamicum naturally lacks the ability to metabolize lignocellulosic 
materials, limiting its potential for biomass-derived feedstocks. 
To address this limitation, C. glutamicum has been genetically engineered to metabolize 
diverse feedstocks, including those from lignocellulosic sources, for chemical production  
(1,2). In industrial applications, it is crucial to maintain consistent product quality, stable 
titers, and low by-product levels. However, efficiently and simultaneously utilizing multiple 
carbon sources remains a challenge, as microbes often display preferential or sequential 
substrate utilization. 
One explanation for this preference is that it may be energetically more efficient for a cell to 
produce only the enzymes required for metabolizing a single substrate. In mixed-sugar 
environments, this substrate preference often manifests as diauxic growth — a biphasic 
pattern in which a preferred carbon source, such as glucose, is consumed first, followed by a 
lag phase before utilization of a less-preferred substrate like xylose. During the lag, cells 
undergo metabolic shifts, adjusting transporter expression, enzyme activity, and regulatory 
networks to switch to the secondary substrate. In C. glutamicum, diauxic growth has been 
reported for several substrate combinations, including glucose–xylose mixtures, where 
glucose repression and inducer exclusion mechanisms play key roles in sequential sugar 
uptake (3). These shifts can influence intracellular energy status, as ATP demand changes 
during the transition, potentially impacting growth rate, metabolic yield, and product 
formation. Understanding the energetic basis of diauxic growth is therefore essential for 
designing strains and processes that minimize lag phases and improve mixed-sugar utilization 
efficiency in industrial fermentations. 
Understanding this occurrence requires precise measurement of intracellular energy status. 
Traditionally, metabolite concentrations in bacteria have been measured using disruptive 
methods, which suffer from limited temporal resolution, sample dilution, and contamination 
from extracellular solutes. More recently, genetically encoded sensor proteins have been 
developed to monitor energy metabolism in real time, enabling measurement of intracellular 
ATP levels in individual bacterial cells (4). 
==PAGE:10==
9 
 
This study investigates how engineered C. glutamicum strains equipped with fluorescent ATP 
sensors can be used to reveal the energetic basis of substrate preference during diauxic 
growth on different carbon sources. 
1.1 
Corynebacterium glutamicum 
Corynebacterium glutamicum is a gram-positive soil bacterium widely used in industrial 
biotechnology and known for its ability to synthesize amino acids (2,5). The organism is easy 
to handle genetically and easy to grow as well as being safe as it is recognized as a GRAS 
organism, it is non-sporulating, non-motile and non-pathogenic (6). It is also robust against 
oscillations in substrate and oxygen supply in large fermenters; thus, it serves as a producer 
and a model organism to study the cell metabolism (7). 
Monosaccharides such as glucose and fructose are preferred substrates for C. glutamicum 
amino acid production, however a shift towards novel and sustainable carbohydrate sources 
is seen, the complexity of sustainable substrate sources, e.g. hemicellulose derived sugars, 
has a negative influence on the process performance. Thus, glucose and sucrose are 
preferred for amino acid productions (1,8).  
Many isolated C. glutamicum strains exist, however, two strains are widely used in basic 
research, namely strain ATCC13 032 and strain R, as other C. glutamicum strains have a 
paracrystalline protein surface layer which has shown to interfere with the DNA transfer by 
electroporation in the Caulobacter species, of the two strains ATCC13 032 is used most by 
researchers. The Corynebacterium species genome is known to be very stable in large scale 
applications (6).  
The C. glutamicum genome contains a large number of mobile genetic elements (MGE), 
which may cause instability in genetic levels and in plasmids. Thus, removal of mobile genetic 
elements and prophages has proven to enhance the stability of the genome. The prophage 
removal results in better survival under stress conditions and increased transformability of 
ATCC13 032. In this project, strain CR099 is used (6). CR099 is the C. glutamicum MB001 
strain, the MB001 strain is prophage free to avoid cell lysis when it is present in high copy 
numbers. Furthermore, insertion sequence (IS) elements ISCg1 and ISCg2 were removed from 
the strain, as they are the most active IS elements  and to ensure stability of both plasmids 
and heterologous genes (6). This study furthermore uses the CR099::U strain which has been 
engineered for optimized mannose utilization and carries heterologous genes for Xylose (9).  
 
1.2 
The metabolism of C. glutamicum 
Glucose is extensively utilized as a primary substrate in the fermentation industry due to its 
affordability and widespread availability. The major uptake system for glucose transport in C. 
glutamicum is the phosphoenolpyruvate-dependent glucose phosphotransferase system 
(PTSglc) which is widely found in prokaryotes, the system is involved in import and 
phosphorylation of glucose (10). 
==PAGE:11==
10 
 
The phosphotransferase system (PTS) consists of a membrane-associated, sugar-specific 
EIIABC complex (EII) along with two cytoplasmic components—enzyme I (EI) and histidine 
protein (HPr). While PTS is common in bacteria, it is absent in archaea and fungi, allowing 
efficient transport and phosphorylation of various sugars simultaneously. For instance, 
glucose is taken up and converted to glucose-6-phosphate via PTSGlc. However, this process 
requires phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP) as a phosphate donor, depleting intracellular PEP levels 
and reducing the availability of PEP-derived metabolites such as L-lysine, succinic acid, and 
aromatic compounds. Additionally, the high efficiency of glucose uptake through PTSGlc 
disrupts the balance between glycolysis and the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle, leading to 
overflow metabolism and impaired cell growth. As a result, researchers have explored 
alternative, PTS-independent glucose transport mechanisms (non-PTSGlc) to enhance 
glucose utilization efficiency and maintain higher intracellular PEP levels (10). 
1.3.1 Phosphorylation – The glucose uptake 
Phosphorylation by the phosphoenolpyruvate-dependent phosphotransferase system (PTS) 
occurs in five sequential steps (see figure 1) involving EI, HPr, and the EII complex, with 
reversible transfer of phosphate groups. EI is a conserved protein containing two functional 
domains—PEP synthetase and phosphopyruvate kinase—and a histidine active site that 
enables transfer of phosphate from phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP) during autophosphorylation 
to form EI-P. The phosphocarrier protein HPr, with a single domain and a conserved histidine 
at position 15, accepts the phosphate group from EI-P (10). 
 
Figure 1. Glucose uptake via the PEP-dependent phosphotransferase system (PTS) in C. glutamicum, showing 
sequential phosphate transfer from PEP to EI, HPr, and the EII complex during transport, yielding glucose-6-
phosphate (Ruan, Yu and Xu, 2020). 
The EII complex, which can be specific to one or more carbohydrates, consists of two 
hydrophilic cytoplasmic domains (IIA and IIB) and a membrane-bound permease domain 
(IIC). HPr phosphorylates IIA, which subsequently transfers the phosphate to IIB. This 
phosphorylation triggers conformational changes in IIC, allowing the carbohydrate to enter 
==PAGE:12==
11 
 
the cell. In the final step, the phosphate originally derived from PEP is transferred to the 
transported carbohydrate molecule (10). 
The glucose PTS operates with high efficiency, but this can result in overflow metabolism due 
to an imbalance between glycolytic flux and the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle. For this reason, 
PTS-independent glucose uptake systems are being investigated to conserve intracellular PEP 
and improve its availability for other metabolic processes (10). 
 
1.3.2 Hemicellulotic substrate sources (Xylose metabolism) 
C. glutamicum cannot naturally metabolize xylose because it lacks the genes required for 
xylose catabolism. To enable xylose utilization, a metabolic pathway from Escherichia coli 
was introduced, comprising xylA (xylose isomerase) and xylB (xylulokinase) in a study by 
Kawaguchi et al. (2006). In this engineered pathway, xylose is first converted to xylulose by 
XylA, then phosphorylated by XylB to xylulose-5-phosphate, which enters the pentose 
phosphate pathway. This engineering allowed growth on xylose as the sole carbon source (1). 
For efficient xylose uptake, the inositol permease IolT1 was identified as a native transporter 
capable of importing xylose into the cell. This eliminated the need for heterologous 
transporters in some engineered strains. The system was further optimized for industrial use, 
enabling C. glutamicum to metabolize xylose from lignocellulosic hydrolysates and mixed 
sugar substrates (1). 
When cultivated on mixtures of glucose and xylose, engineered strains displayed sequential 
sugar utilization, preferring glucose first. Overcoming this required further genetic 
modifications to adjust uptake rates and regulatory controls, improving co-utilization. These 
developments make xylose metabolism in C. glutamicum an important tool for valorizing 
renewable biomass feedstocks in biotechnological processes (3). 
 
1.3 
Circularly permuted enhanced green fluorescent protein(cpEGFP) 
Genetically encoded fluorescent indicators based on fluorescent proteins are used to 
visualize and quantify conformational state of proteins, enzymatic activity and changes in 
biophysical parameters or the concentrations of particular molecules. Furthermore, the 
GEFIs are the preferred choice, when doing experiments requiring real-time imaging (11). 
EGFPs are green fluorescent protein (GFP) that are genetically modified and emits 35 times 
more fluorescence than GFP when excited with ultraviolet or blue light, thus giving a brighter 
signal. The enhancement occurs by a change in the amino acid composition in the 
chromophore region of the protein which is facilitated by a specific mutation in the nucleic 
acid sequence (12).  
The polypeptide chain of GFP-like proteins forms a beta-barrel which consists of 11 beta-
sheets accommodating an internal distorted helix which is resistant to conformational 
==PAGE:13==
12 
 
changes. cpEGFP consists of a modified form of GFP in which the N and C-temini of GFP is 
connected by a peptide linker and a new termini is formed near the chromophore. Circularly 
permuted proteins are more flexible than the non-permuted protein and the chromophores of 
the circular permutants are more accessible to external media (11). This study uses the 
cpEGFP QUEEN ATP sensors. 
 
1.4 
QUEEN ATP sensors 
ATP functions as a signaling molecule both inside and outside cells. The QUEEN ATP 
biosensors, fluorescence-based sensors, measure absolute ATP concentrations in individual 
cells without being influenced by growth rate, unlike FRET-based sensors which can show 
signal shifts due to growth changes (13,14).  
In this study, the QUEEN biosensors used are QUEEN-7u and QUEEN-2M. QUEEN-7u, derived 
from the epsilon subunit of Bacillus PS3, has the highest ATP affinity, while QUEEN-2M, a 
variant with a different epsilon subunit from Bacillus subtilis, has a lower ATP affinity, making 
it suitable for measuring physiological ATP levels (14).  
The QUEEN ATP sensors are genetically encoded fluorescent indicators that monitor real-time 
ATP concentrations in single cells. These sensors integrate a circularly permuted enhanced 
green fluorescent protein (cpEGFP) into the epsilon subunit of bacterial ATP synthase. This 
integration allows for conformational changes upon ATP binding, leading to a measurable 
change in fluorescence. The sensor structure includes cpEGFP inserted between two alpha-
helices of the epsilon subunit from Bacillus subtilis ATP synthase, connected by two amino 
acid linkers (figure 2). This design ensures that ATP binding induces a conformational shift in 
the epsilon subunit, affecting the fluorescence signal (14).

1.5 
The pEKEx2 shuttle vector 
The shuttle vector pEKEx2 is an IPTG-inducible expression vector compatible with both 
Corynebacterium glutamicum and Escherichia coli is widely used for protein synthesis in C. 
glutamicum. It carries the C. glutamicum origin of replication from pBL1, the E. coli ColE1 
replicon, the lac repressor allele lacI^q, a kanamycin resistance gene, and the tacI promoter 
for IPTG-inducible gene expression (Bakkes et al., 2020). Although pEKEx2 is effective for 
expression, the presence of duplicate sequences may contribute to plasmid instability 
(Bakkes et al., 2020). In this study, pEKEx2 is used for ectopic integration of the QUEEN ATP 
sensors (15). 
1.6 
Aim 
This project aims to adapt a previously developed real-time ATP quantification sensor, 
originally designed for Escherichia coli, for use in Corynebacterium glutamicum, an important 
microorganism in microbial chemical production. The sensor, which combines circularly 
permuted green fluorescent protein (cpGFP) with the ATP-binding domain of a Bacillus 
species, will be introduced into C. glutamicum and its expression verified. ATP levels will be 
measured under various growth conditions, with particular focus on diauxic shifts from 
glucose to less-preferred carbon sources such as xylose. These real-time measurements will 
provide insights into microbial energy status and metabolic adaptation to different substrates. 
If time allows, the ATP sensor gene will be integrated into the C. glutamicum genome under a 
constitutive promoter to ensure continuous expression. Ultimately, this work will contribute to 
optimizing microbial processes for industrial chemical production from renewable resources, 
supporting the green transition by improving the efficiency and stability of bioprocesses.

Raw materials, strains and media 
2.1.1 Medium, buffer etc. 
LB medium and CGXII minimal medium were used for cultivation. The CGXII minimal medium 
consisted of MOPS [21g/L], ammonium sulfate [10 g/L], Urea [5 g/L], KH2PO4 [1 g/L], K2HPO4 [1 
g/L], the pH was adjusted to 7.0 with KOH (16).  The minimal media was additionally 
supplemented with stock solutions of biotin, CaCl2, MgSO4 and trace elements. The complex 
LB medium consists of Bacto Tryptone [10 g/L], Yeast extract [5 g/L], NaCl [10 g/L), the 
medium was pH adjusted to 7.0-7.5 using NaOH. Brain heart infusion (BH) medium was used 
for plasmid transformation into the cells. The BHI medium consist of Brain heart infusion 
broth [37 g/L].

The shuttle vector pEKEx2-QUEEN-7u and pEKEx2-QUEEN-2M was used for expression of the 
fluorescent ATP sensor in Corynebacterium glutamicum. pEKEx2 is a dual-host expression 
vector carrying replication origins for both E. coli (ColE1 ori) and C. glutamicum (pGA1 ori), 
which enables maintenance and propagation in both organisms. Selection in both hosts was 
achieved using the kanamycin resistance gene (KanR).

Gene expression was placed under the control of the Ptac promoter, a hybrid promoter 
inducible with isopropyl-β-D-thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG). Regulation was provided by the 
lacI repressor, encoded on the plasmid, which ensures tight control of promoter activity. The 
plasmid additionally carries a 6xHis affinity tag downstream of the promoter for potential 
purification of the expressed protein.

2.8 
Real time monitoring of ATP sensor fluorescence 
Real-time fluorescence measurements of biosensor-equipped strains were performed using 
a BioLector® microcultivation system (manufacturer, model) to monitor the relationship 
between growth and intracellular ATP levels, and to assess changes in ATP during diauxic 
shifts. Strains were prepared as described in Section 2.2. 
The BioLector was equipped with a GFP filter set (excitation 480 nm / emission 520 nm) for 
biosensor fluorescence detection and a biomass filter (620 nm) for optical density 
estimation. 
Cultivations were carried out in 48-well FlowerPlates (Greiner Bio-One) with a working volume 
of 800 µL per well, at 30 °C and 1,200 rpm shaking. Measurements were recorded every 2 min. 
GFP fluorescence was recorded at gain settings of 1, 3, and 5, while biomass was measured 
at gain settings of 3, 5, and 7 to ensure appropriate signal resolution across all growth phases 
and to avoid detector saturation.

3 Results  
In this study the QUEEN ATP sensors were equipped ectopically into C. glutamicum, to 
observe the absolute ATP of the strains, in presence of pentose sugars. The growth of the 
biosensor strains was tested to assess whether an impact on the growth rate is observed. The 
biosensor plasmids were tested prior to insertion. 
In the presence of the inducer IPTG, IPTG binds to the LacI repressor protein, preventing it 
from binding to the Ptac promoter. This allows transcription from Ptac to proceed, leading to 
the expression of the biosensor construct (7u or 2m) encoded on the pEKEx2 plasmid. The 
biosensor protein then detects its specific target molecule and triggers a downstream 
fluorescence response. In the absence of IPTG, LacI remains bound to Ptac and blocks 
transcription, preventing biosensor expression and thus reducing fluorescence output. 
 
3.1 
Baseline in E. coli 
To confirm the suitability of the ATP sensor plasmids prior to transfer into Corynebacterium 
glutamicum, pEKEx2-QUEEN-7u (JS12) and pEKEx2-QUEEN-2m (JS13) were expressed in E. 
coli DH5α and cultivated in shake flasks with LB medium. Both strains displayed similar 
growth, reaching stationary phase after approximately 8–9 h, with JS13 showing a slightly 
faster early exponential phase (Figure 5). No growth defects or morphological abnormalities 
were observed, indicating that the presence of the sensor constructs did not impose a 
significant metabolic burden under the tested conditions. These results confirm plasmid 
stability and provide a baseline for subsequent experiments in C. glutamicum.

3.3.1 Growth in LB and CGXII media 
After confirmation of the plasmids and transformation the C. glutamicum CR099, CR099-7u, 
and CR099-2M carrying biosensor plasmids was monitored using the Growth Profiler system 
to assess whether the plasmids influenced cell growth under different carbon source and 
induction conditions. Strains were cultivated in LB medium and CGXII medium containing 
either 1% glucose (Figure 9, c and d) or a mixture of 0.5% glucose and 0.5% xylose (Figure 9,c 
and d), without IPTG (panels 1 and 3) or with IPTG induction (b and d). 
On xylose as the sole carbon source, all strains reached similar maximum OD₆₀₀ values, 
indicating that the presence of the plasmids did not markedly impair growth. Minor 
differences in growth rates were observed, with CR099-7u and CR099-2M generally showing 
slightly faster initial growth compared to CR099. IPTG induction did not significantly change 
the growth dynamics under these conditions. 
When grown on glucose–xylose mixtures, all strains displayed a clear diauxic growth pattern, 
with a first growth phase on glucose, a transient lag phase, and a second growth phase on

xylose. CR099-2M maintained the highest biomass accumulation in the second phase, 
whereas CR099-7u showed a reduction in growth rate during the substrate switch. Although 
IPTG induction slightly affected the amplitude of the second growth phase for some strains, 
the diauxic pattern persisted in all cases.

Strains cultivated on 1% xylose as sole carbon source (Figure 10, a,b) showed no appreciable 
growth in either the presence or absence of IPTG, indicating that xylose alone was insufficient 
to sustain biomass formation under the tested conditions. 
In contrast, when cultivated on a mixed substrate of 0.5% glucose and 0.5% xylose (Figure 10, 
bottom panels), strains reached a maximum OD600 of 6–8. Growth peaked at approximately 
800 min, followed by a gradual decline in OD600. This decline likely reflects substrate 
limitation as glucose is consumed first, followed by slower xylose utilization.

trends under the conditions tested. 
It would have been informative to test higher substrate concentrations to determine whether 
the observed decline in OD600 was due to substrate depletion or whether other physiological

To examine biosensor behavior under partial catabolite repression, strains were cultured in a 
1% total carbon mixture composed of 0.5% glucose and 0.5% xylose (Figure 15, a and b). In 
the absence of IPTG (Figure 15, a), fluorescence signals were relatively low across all strains, 
with only a modest increase observed for CR099::U-7u and CR099::U-2M, suggesting partial 
basal activity. Compared to pure xylose, basal expression was slightly reduced, indicating a 
potential dampening effect from glucose, likely due to residual repression of the lac promoter 
system. 
Following IPTG induction (b), a clear differentiation between sensor strains emerged. 
CR099::U-7u again produced the highest fluorescence per biomass, showing a sharp increase 
in signal throughout the growth phase. CR099::U-2M also responded positively, though to a 
lesser extent, while CR099::U showed mild induction. Parental strains remained 
unresponsive, as expected. The results confirm that both biosensors are inducible under 
mixed-substrate conditions, with the 7u variant consistently yielding the strongest output. 
Compared to pure glucose, the presence of xylose appears to enhance the dynamic range of 
induction, while still allowing for IPTG-mediated control. These observations suggest that 
mixed carbon sources may provide a balanced environment for maintaining cell growth while 
enabling biosensor responsiveness.

4 Discussion 
4.1 
Sensor performance in C. glutamicum 
The functionality of the QUEEN biosensors (7u and 2M) was first validated in E. coli to 
establish a baseline prior to transfer into C. glutamicum. Both constructs supported normal 
growth and C. glutamicum produced detectable signals, with QUEEN-7u consistently showing 
stronger responses due to its higher ATP affinity. Upon transfer into C. glutamicum, 
fluorescence remained tightly linked to IPTG induction, confirming that the pTac promoter 
system successfully controlled expression. In the absence of IPTG, only low fluorescence was 
observed, corresponding largely to cellular autofluorescence, with some evidence of leaky 
expression in 7u constructs (15). However for laboratory scale IPTG inducible promoters 
provide a controlled overexpression of the gene as opposed to the constitutive Ptuf promoter 
which was part of the genomic integration, and constantly transcribes the sensor gene, 
without the need for induction (19).  
As previously described the CR099 strain, was engineered to lack ISCg1, ISCg2, and prophage 
regions, which should favor stable plasmid maintenance (15). Although, experience from 
metabolic engineering of C. glutamicum indicates that genomic integration offers more 
stability compared to ectopic integrations. Thus, growth studies with genomically integrated 
sensor fragments may have shown more stable expression. 
 
4.2 
Growth behavior on single and mixed sugars 
The growth profile (GP) data suggests possible nutrient limitation during later stages of 
cultivation, other possibilities is potentially depletion of trace elements or other growth 
==PAGE:32==
31 
 
factors. High glycolytic flux during glucose metabolism may also create metabolic 
bottlenecks, particularly at the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle (1). In mixed glucose–xylose 
cultures, a clear diauxic growth pattern was observed, with glucose consumed before xylose. 
This sequential sugar utilization is consistent with earlier reports for C. glutamicum (3), likely 
results from transporter specificity and regulatory mechanisms such as inducer exclusion or 
transcriptional repression. 
The second growth phase observed for biolector graphs grown in glucose (see Appendix A3), 
may be linked to acetate utilization (20) or as a result of the cells not being washed enough 
prior to main cultivation leaving LB residue. Diauxic growth has primarily been reported for 
Glucose-Glutamate co-cultures (20), but reports for other substrate combinations are 
limited. If confirmed, this observation expands the known diauxic growth behaviours of C. 
glutamicum and could inform mixed substrate feeding strategies (20). 
When cultivated on xylose as the sole carbon source, CR099 strains carrying the sensor 
plasmids (7u and 2M) showed no detectable growth, irrespective of IPTG induction. This 
outcome is expected, as the parental CR099 strain is unable to metabolize xylose. 
Consequently, biomass formation cannot be supported under these conditions, confirming 
that xylose utilization was not introduced in this strain background. 
In contrast, strains cultivated on mixed glucose–xylose medium displayed robust growth, 
reaching OD600 values between 6 and 8 before entering a decline phase. The sharp decline in 
OD600 after the growth peak is likely due to substrate depletion, with glucose being 
consumed rapidly, followed by the inability to utilize xylose. The delayed growth on xylose 
alone (maximal OD600 only after ~1600 min compared to ~800 min in glucose–xylose 
mixtures) further shows the metabolic bottleneck in xylose utilization. 
IPTG induction had no major impact on growth under either substrate condition, suggesting 
that sensor plasmid expression did not impose a significant metabolic burden or confer an 
advantage under the conditions tested. This observation points to substrate availability, rather 
than plasmid expression or induction, as the dominant factor shaping the observed growth 
profiles. 
 
4.3 
Energetic implications of diauxic shifts 
Diauxic growth phases require extensive transcriptional and metabolic shifts, processes that 
are energetically costly. This study captured ATP fluctuations during these shifts using QUEEN 
biosensors, revealing potential increases in ATP demand during the transition from glucose to 
xylose. Higher fluorescence signals observed during xylose metabolism, particularly with the 
high-affinity QUEEN-7u sensor, suggest that ATP is consumed more rapidly during xylose 
utilization. This may reflect the additional enzymatic steps in the xylose pathway 
(isomerization and phosphorylation) compared to direct PTS-mediated phosphorylation of 
glucose (10). 
==PAGE:33==
32 
 
Understanding the energy cost of these transitions is particularly relevant to lignocellulosic 
feedstocks, where multiple sugars are present. Excessive ATP use during substrate switching 
could reduce yield and productivity, making energetic profiling a valuable tool for strain 
screening and optimization (21). 
key aspect in understanding the evolutionary significance of the lag phase has been 
highlighted, which showed that the transition to a secondary carbon source begins even 
before depletion of the primary nutrient. This early "preparation phase" was found to play a 
decisive role in determining the length of the subsequent lag phase(21). 
In this study, diauxic growth of C. glutamicum on glucose–xylose mixtures showed a distinct 
lag phase after glucose depletion, reflecting the metabolic reprogramming required for xylose 
utilization. Similar to findings in E. coli (Chu et al., 2016), this shift was heterogeneous across 
the population, likely influenced by intracellular energy states. Using genetically encoded ATP 
sensors, we linked ATP fluctuations to growth profiles, highlighting ATP’s role in substrate 
transitions. These insights are relevant for industrial bioprocessing, where reducing lag 
phases and improving mixed-sugar utilization are critical for efficiency in lignocellulosic 
feedstocks (21). 
The data here have direct relevance for bioprocessing applications. In large-scale 
fermentations with mixed carbon sources (e.g., lignocellulosic hydrolysates), diauxic shifts 
can cause production downtime, redirection of metabolic flux, and reduced product yields. 
Engineering strains to co-utilize glucose and xylose without sequential uptake could improve 
process efficiency. ATP profiling via biosensors like QUEEN could become an important 
selection criterion for robust industrial strains, enabling the identification of metabolic 
configurations with lower energy costs during substrate switching. 
 
4.4 
BioLector and fluorescence data interpretation 
BioLector experiments provided real-time monitoring of growth and fluorescence but 
measure only single wavelength scattering and emission. Without ratiometric analysis, these 
signals cannot be directly interpreted as absolute ATP concentrations and are influenced by 
biomass density. This limitation was addressed using the Clariostar plate reader, which allows 
ratiometric measurement and provides more accurate intracellular ATP quantification. 
Baseline fluorescence in the absence of substrate switching suggests leaky expression from 
the IPTG-inducible tac promoter on pEKEx2. This could be mitigated by lowering IPTG 
concentrations, using a more tightly regulated promoter, or switching to chromosomal 
integration with a constitutive but balanced expression level. 
In summary, this work demonstrates the feasibility of applying QUEEN-based biosensors to 
monitor ATP dynamics in C. glutamicum. While ectopic expression enabled functional 
analysis, genomic integration remained unsuccessful, highlighting a key technical bottleneck. 
Growth experiments confirmed expected diauxic behavior and revealed distinct sensor 
==PAGE:34==
33 
 
responses, particularly for QUEEN-7u. Together, these results establish a foundation for future 
efforts to stably integrate ATP biosensors, optimize cultivation conditions, and ultimately 
improve energy monitoring in industrially relevant processes.

5 Conclusion 
This study analyzed ATP dynamics in Corynebacterium glutamicum during diauxic growth 
using genetically encoded fluorescent biosensors. While ectopic expression of the QUEEN 
sensors (7u and 2M) in C. glutamicum was successfully achieved, genomic integration 
attempts were not successful, limiting full experimental validation. Nevertheless, 
fluorescence-based assays and growth experiments provided insight into the functionality of 
the biosensors and their responsiveness under different nutrient conditions. 
The results confirm that the sensor plasmids maintain detectable activity in C. glutamicum 
when expressed from plasmids. Growth experiments in media containing glucose, xylose, or 
mixtures thereof revealed diauxic growth patterns consistent with substrate preference, 
accompanied by measurable differences in fluorescence response between biosensor 
variants. These findings highlight the potential of QUEEN sensors to probe ATP levels in real 
time and underscore the physiological impact of nutrient shifts on cellular energy 
metabolism. 
Despite technical challenges, the work contributes to establishing a framework for energy-
state monitoring in C. glutamicum and provides methodological advances for future studies. 
Optimizing genomic integration strategies, testing under higher substrate concentrations, and 
extending biosensor applications to dynamic fermentation setups will be essential next steps. 
Understanding ATP dynamics during nutrient shifts could guide metabolic engineering 
strategies to minimize lag phases and enhance mixed-sugar utilization in industrial 
bioprocesses. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:36==
35 
 
Future prospect 
Although this study established the first steps toward real-time ATP monitoring in 
Corynebacterium glutamicum, several avenues remain for further exploration: 
• Stable genomic integration – Efforts should be directed toward establishing reliable 
genomic integration of the QUEEN biosensors, as this would ensure long-term stability 
without the metabolic burden of plasmid maintenance. Alternative integration 
strategies or CRISPR-based approaches may overcome the limitations encountered in 
this work. 
• Optimization of cultivation conditions – Experiments with varying substrate 
concentrations, feeding strategies, or controlled bioreactor conditions could provide 
deeper insights into ATP dynamics during diauxic growth, and minimize artifacts 
observed in shake-flask or microplate formats. 
• Sensor calibration and validation – Quantitative calibration of QUEEN fluorescence 
to absolute ATP concentrations, possibly through permeabilization assays or in vitro 
standards, would enable more robust comparisons across conditions and strains. 
• Expansion to mixed-substrate environments – Extending these studies to more 
complex sugar mixtures or lignocellulosic hydrolysates would provide industrially 
relevant insights into how ATP dynamics influence productivity in mixed-carbon 
utilization. 
• Application in metabolic engineering – Ultimately, integrating ATP monitoring into 
strain engineering pipelines could guide the design of optimized strains with reduced 
lag phases, improved energy efficiency, and enhanced yields of target metabolites.
