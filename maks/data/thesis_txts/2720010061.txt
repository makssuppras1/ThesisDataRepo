Abstract
Sleep is an essential process with a major impact on health and quality of life. One way
to study sleep disorders such as chronic insomnia and narcolepsy is to use mouse models,
in which sleep disorders can be reproduced in a controlled setting thanks to the genetic
tools available. When sleep studies are conducted, EEG and EMG signals are recorded
to detect the different sleep phases. This process is known as sleep scoring. However,
sleep scoring is usually done manually and it is a tedious and time-consuming task subject
to scorer subjectivity. In this thesis, automatic sleep scoring models are investigated to
address some of the problems of manual scoring.
Two deep learning models are studied in collaboration with the Molecular Sleep Lab of the
University of Copenhagen. The first model, SPINDLE, is particularly relevant because of
its ability to generalize to different mice strains, surgical procedures and recording setups,
which remains one of the main challenges in the field. The second model, SleepTrans-
former, introduced the deep learning transformer architecture in sleep scoring in humans.
Thanks to the transformer architecture, SleepTransformer achieved outstanding perfor-
mance and addressed another major challenge in automatic sleep scoring: explainability.
Therefore, it is of interest to test SleepTransformer on mice.
First, it was shown that it is possible to approximately replicate both models when they
are trained on the data used in the original publications. In addition, the performance of
SPINDLE was evaluated when trained on a larger and more diverse dataset provided by
the Molecular Sleep Lab, obtaining similar performance but improved in terms of general-
ization. Furthermore, SleepTransformer was tested on mice by training it from scratch on
the data from the Molecular Sleep Lab, with high overall accuracy. The performance of
the two models was compared under the hypothesis that SleepTransformer would outper-
form SPINDLE due to the transformer architecture. However, no significant differences
were found. In addition to sleep stage prediction, both models were evaluated for arti-
fact detection. It was concluded that the prediction of artifacts is particularly difficult
because the criteria used by human scorers to define an artifact are very subjective, which
is propagated to the training data.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Humans spend approximately a third of their lifetime sleeping. Sleep is an essential pro-
cess in life and, although its functions are not fully understood, it is believed to have an
important role in a wide range of areas, such as brain development [1], memory consoli-
dation [2], brain waste removal [3] and the immune system [4].
Its importance is further supported by the effects of sleep deprivation, which is linked to
numerous life threatening diseases. For instance, incidence of coronary heart disease and
stroke was respectively found to be 48% and 15% higher in those who sleep less than 6
hours [5], while 75% of depression patients suffer from insomnia and insomnia in non-
depressed subjects could be a risk factor for later development of depression [6, 7]. In
addition, sleep deprivation severely affects the daily life of those who suffer from it: it
leads to overall reduced cognitive performance, slowed working memory and lapses of at-
tention [8, 9], which can even increase the risk of fatal accidents [10].
However, several elements threaten the sleep health of the general population. One of the
elements is poor sleep hygiene, which not only includes insufficient amount of sleep but
also common habits such as alcohol, caffeine and nicotine consumption or the increasing
use of screens before bedtime [11–13]. Sleep disorders such as narcolepsy, insomnia or
sleep apnea, which can affect a considerable portion of population, are another threaten-
ing element for sleep health. Estimates of the prevalence of chronic insomnia in different
countries range between 10% and 40% of the population [14–17], while for sleep apnea is
between 10% and 30% [18, 19].
Consequently, expanding our knowledge about sleep would have numerous benefits, includ-
ing better management of sleep disorders, improved individual sleep quality, and potential
advancements in different areas from medicine to productivity.
Animal models are typically used to study sleep. The mouse model is particularly interest-
ing, as the development of genetically-modified mouse strains [20, 21] allows to reproduce
sleep disorder phenotypes in a very controlled setting [22]. In sleep studies, electroen-
cephalography (EEG) and electromyography (EMG) are used as the main tool to classify
1
==PAGE:13==

sleep into different vigilance states, a process that is known as sleep staging or sleep scoring.
In mice, the vigilance states in which sleep is scored are wakefulness, rapid eye movement
sleep (REM) and non rapid eye movement sleep (NREM). Although there is not a strict
consensus, the EEG bands in mice can be defined as delta (0.5–4 Hz), theta (4–10 Hz),
sigma (10–15 Hz) and beta (15–30 Hz) [23]. Wakefulness is typically characterized by
high and varying EMG power and a mixed frequency EEG signal without a clear peak in
the power spectrum; REM by low-amplitude EEG theta waves and low EMG power; and
NREM by high-amplitude delta waves in EEG and low EMG activity [24, 25]. Classifi-
cation of sleep into sleep stages is critical for measuring parameters of sleep, such as the
sleep cycles, the time spent in each stage, sleep latency, wake after sleep onset, etc. [26].
Moreover, the underlying physiological processes of sleep disorders manifest themselves
in the sleep stages, which could allow for identification of biomarkers for sleep disorders
[27–30].
The recorded EEG and EMG are scored in short windows (named epochs) that typically
last between 4 and 30 seconds in mice [31–33]. This process is usually done manually by
sleep experts and, given that a single recording can contain thousands of epochs, it is a
tedious and time-consuming task. In addition, a large consensus on the criteria to score
sleep in mice is still missing [22, 34], which is a potential source of subjectivity in the
process and disparity between studies [31]. The inter-scorer variability in mice has been
quantified in the range 83-96% [35]. But even the introduction of well-defined standards
does not remove the possibility of introducing bias: in humans, where the AASM and
R&K guidelines [36, 37] are commonly used, it has been reported that the average inter-
scorer variability is 83% [38]. Automatic sleep scoring could circumvent these problems,
which would be greatly beneficial for sleep experts.
Automatic sleep staging in mice has not been as explored as in humans, so it would be
reasonable to look for inspiration in models developed for human sleep. However, humans
and mice do not have the exact same sleep patterns and the way experts score their sleep
is different too, which questions whether models for humans are directly translatable to
mice. Mice are nocturnal animals with a polyphasic distribution of sleep (meaning that
their sleep is distributed in multiple intervals throughout the 24 hours day) and they do
not have a regular NREM-REM cycle as humans do [22, 39]. In addition, in mice just 1 or
2 EEG channels are normally used [31–33], whereas in humans polysomnography (PSG)
is used, which includes more EEG channels and other signals such as heart rate and EOG
[40]. Another difference is the length of the epochs and the number of sleep stages that
are recognized. Epochs in mouse studies are 4-30 seconds and they are scored into 3 sleep
stages, while in humans epochs are normally 30 seconds long and scored in 5 sleep stages
(REM, NREM1, NREM2, NREM3 and wake) [41, 42]. Additionally, as mentioned before
there are no well-established scoring rules for mice. Despite all these differences, mice and
humans still share many EEG signatures and characteristics in sleep [22], so knowledge
transfer between models might be possible.
Early attempts to automate the sleep staging problem in mice used traditional feature
extraction approaches.
These methods relied on handcrafted features in the time [43]
or in the frequency domain [44] extracted from EEG or EMG. For instance, a common
strategy was to extract a power-related feature from each of the EEG frequency bands.
Then, these features would be used in a machine learning classifier such as naive Bayes
2
==PAGE:14==

[44] or support vector machine [45] for sleep stage classification. However, these methods
showed some limitations. It is very difficult to handcraft features robust to individual
differences and noise [33]. Often, they are not able to generalize because class-specific
features may differ across data samples, mutants, or species [31]. In addition, features
inspired by domain knowledge might not be the best possible features, which puts a limit
to model performance [41]. On the other hand, deep learning (DL) models might learn by
themselves new and better features than those inspired by domain knowledge [46].
Within DL models, convolutional neural networks (CNNs) were the first models used for
mouse sleep scoring. The approaches are similar and they just differ in the architecture
used for the CNN [24, 34, 39, 47]. Short windows of several epochs around a target epoch
are used as input to provide contextual information, and they can be in the form of spec-
trograms [31] or in the form of raw signals [47]. Grieger et al. [47] is especially interesting,
since they included another sleep stage (pre-REM sleep) in addition to the three stages
that are normally used in mice. They obtained a low F1 score (0.48) for this category,
concluding that the identification of more than three stages in mice is challenging. Even
though CNNs are able to model temporal dependencies through dilation [48], most CNNs
fail to model temporal dependencies such as transition rules [25]. In Miladinovi´c et al. [31],
they addressed this by placing a hidden Markov model (HMM) after a CNN that predicts
sleep stages, so the HMM would suppress unlikely transitions. Their model, named SPIN-
DLE, achieved state-of-the-art performance (99% accuracy) outperforming all previous
methods and being able to generalize to different cohorts of mice and rats.
The need to model temporal dependencies led to the use of recurrent neural networks
(RNNs) (such as LSTM [49] and GRU [50]). Yamabe et al. [33] developed an RNN-based
model named MC-SleepNet. This model consists of a CNN-based feature extraction block
followed by a scoring block, which is composed of a bidirectional LSTM [51] and a fully-
connected network, achieving an overall accuracy of 96.6%. In Tezuka et al. [32], they
developed a model that consists of a CNN that scores each epoch. Then, an LSTM net-
work uses the prediction of the CNN for the current and previous epochs and gives a final
prediction. Their overall model accuracy was 89.5%, and they concluded that the addition
of the LSTM network was an improvement compared to having just the CNN (88.1%). In
Akada et al. [52], they used two different models to analyze EMG and EEG. They use a
bidirectional LSTM on the raw EMG signal and a CNN on the spectrogram of a single
EEG channel. They obtained a better result when combining the outputs of both net-
works into a fully connected network that gives the prediction (95.07% accuracy), rather
than having a rule-based system that first detects wake/sleep with the EMG and then
detects REM/NREM with the EEG (93.91% accuracy). This suggests that simultaneous
learning of EEG and EMG data in adjacent epochs could be effective in increasing the ac-
curacy of the algorithm [52]. Even though LSTM approaches prove to have a competitive
performance, the computational cost of LSTMs due to their recurrent nature and high
complexity should be taken into consideration [53]. In addition, RNNs are good at mod-
elling short-range dependencies but have a limited memory span, so they have problems
modelling longer-range dependencies [25].
A more recent model, called transformer [54], has been shown to outperform RNNs with
impressive results for sequential problems such as audio recognition and natural language
processing [54, 55]. It is based on the attention mechanism and is completely convolution-

The transformer architecture was recently introduced for human sleep stage classifica-
tion in Phan et al. [56] with the SleepTransformer model. SleepTransformer is especially
relevant due to its transformer architecture and its state-of-the-art performance (87.7%
accuracy in the human benchmark dataset SHHS [57]). The self-attention mechanism re-
lates different parts of the input by assigning an attention score, and in SleepTransformer
this is leveraged to model inter- and intra-epoch temporal dependencies. Thanks to the
attention scores the model learns what is relevant, which allows for longer term depen-
dencies. However, SleepTransformer has been only tested in humans, so its performance
in mice is an open question.
Another strong point of SleepTransformer is its intrinsic interpretability, since the at-
tention scores can be used to interpret the decisions made by the model. The lack of
interpretability is precisely one of the main obstacles for the adoption of sleep stage clas-
sification models [58], since DL models are difficult to interpret and they often work as
a black-box. This is very limiting for their application in fields like medicine, where the
models’ decisions need to be coupled to a reasonable explanation in order to be trusted.
In humans, some new approaches have been tested to interpret deep learning sleep scor-
ing models.
This includes intrinsically interpretable models such as SleepTransformer,
but also Explainable AI methods applied to sleep scoring models, such as layer-wise rel-
evance propagation [41, 59], saliency maps [42] and Grad-CAM [60]. On the contrary,
in mice only one attempt to interpret a sleep staging model was found (Kam et al. [61],
where Grad-CAM is used), making SleepTransformer even more interesting to test in mice.
One of the other problems for the adoption of DL sleep staging models is generalization,
especially in animal models. As explained in Liu et al. [25], normally there is a drop in
accuracy when a method is used to identify vigilance states for rodents across mice and
rats, or laboratories. One crucial factor is the heterogeneity of sleep patterns across dif-
ferent rodent models. The variations in EEG or EMG sleep patterns typically originate
from genetic background, disease models, experimental differences, recording device noise,
surgical techniques, electrode implantation and different rodent species (i.e. mice or rats).
Only few works have reflected on this (Svetnik et al. [34], Liu et al. [25] and Yamabe
et al. [33]), while most of the works in mouse sleep classification are evaluated on small
and homogeneous datasets, leaving the generalization aspect without a proper verification.
This is what makes the SPINDLE model especially relevant. SPINDLE showed ability to
preserve predictive performance across animals from different experimental settings, labs
and species, and it was also able to detect mutation-induced sleep alteration. However,
their model was trained on a particularly small dataset (2 mice) and mainly evaluated
on epochs were experts agreed. That leaves out of the evaluation the most challenging
epochs, so it would be interesting to see how it behaves in a new dataset.
The aim of this thesis is to study in healthy mice two of the mentioned DL sleep scoring
models: the CNN-based model for mice named SPINDLE [31] and the transformer-based
model for humans named SleepTransformer [56]. The two models are studied with mouse
EEG and EMG data from the Molecular Sleep Lab of the Department of Neuroscience
of University of Copenhagen (Kornum Lab), which studies the influence of the immune
system in sleep, especially in the autoimmune sleep disorder narcolepsy. The research
questions addressed by this thesis are:

3.1
Artificial Neural Networks
Artificial neural networks are a class of machine learning models that stand out for their
capacity of learning non-linear patterns in big amounts of data.
Neurons are the building blocks of neural networks. Neurons receive information from
other neurons, process it, and send it further to other neurons. The way a neuron pro-
cesses information is through a weighted sum of all the inputs (illustrated in figure 3.1).
A bias term is added to the result of the weighted sum and then the result goes through
an activation function, yielding the output of the neuron. The activation function is typi-
cally a nonlinear function such as the rectified linear unit function (ReLU), the hyperbolic
tangent function or the sigmoid function (figure 3.2). The introduction of non-linearity
is one of the keys to the great power of neural networks, since without them the model
would just be a very complex linear combination of the inputs [66].
The simplest neural network is the feed forward network (FFN) or fully connected network
(FCN). It is structured in fully connected layers of several neurons each, which can contain

The weights and the biases of the neurons are the learnable element of neural networks.
The process that optimizes the weights and biases is called backpropagation [67]. First,

the network (going from the output layer to the input layer) with respect to the network
parameters of each of the layers. This is done by means of an optimization algorithm,

3.2
Convolutional Neural Networks
Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) are a type of neural network typically used for
grid-like data, primarily for image tasks [70, 71].
The main element of CNNs are the convolutional layers, which extract features from the
data. In case of dealing with image data, the extracted features would be characteristics
of the image such as edges or textures [66]. The features are extracted by convoluting a
kernel over the data (figure 3.4). The input data is multiplied element-wise by the kernel,
the bias is added and the activation function applied. The weights and bias of the kernel
are the learnable element. This process is repeated along the whole input data to produce
a feature map, which is the input to the following convolutional layer. A convolutional
layer can have multiple kernels, which results in multiple feature maps that are stack to-
gether.
Figure 3.4: Convolutional layer with ReLU activation.
As shown in figure 3.4, the dimensions of the feature maps are reduced by the convolu-
tional layers (unless padding is added to prevent it), so the representation of the data gets
smaller as the network goes deeper [66]. However, CNNs often contain another type of
layer to reduce the size of the feature maps, named pooling layers. These layers also con-
volute the grid, but instead of multiplying they take the maximum or the average element
in the receptive field of the kernel (figure 3.5).

was applied specifically in SPINDLE follow in subsection 3.6.3.
To solve the problem of finding the optimal sequence given the model parameters and the
data, a dynamic programming algorithm, named Viterbi algorithm, can be used.

3.4
Attention mechanism
The attention mechanism is usually explained through an analogy that involves three ele-
ments [76]: a set of queries, a set of keys and a set of values, with the last two in a paired
form (i.e., each key has a unique value and the other way around). The elements qi, kj
and vkj of each of the sets are vectors, which are stored as rows in the matrices Q, K
and V respectively. For each of our queries, we want an output value, which is the output
of the attention mechanism and is what we call the attention value. The output for a
specific query is simply a weighted sum of all the values, where the values with a key that
is more related to the query are weighted higher, and the values with a less related key
are weighted lower. Then, we do this for each of the queries.
The two most common attention functions are the additive attention and the dot-product
(multiplicative) attention [54].
Transformers use the latter.

3.5
Transformers
Transformers are a deep learning architecture that have had a significant impact in se-
quence modelling tasks, especially in the field of natural language processing (NLP) [54,
55]. They were introduced by Vaswani et al. in 2017 [54], and they are one of the called
seq2seq models that transform a sequence into a different sequence. They have an encoder-
decoder structure (figure 3.8). The encoder transforms the input sequence into a latent
representation, which is decoded by the decoder into the output sequence. Each of the
elements of the transformer model is explained in the following subsections.
The key aspect of transformers is that they primarily rely on the attention mechanism and
they are completely free of convolutional and recurrent layers. By getting rid of convolu-
tional and recurrent layers, transformers are able to process the observations of a sequence

in parallel. On the other hand RNNs need to process the elements of a sequence one by
one and in CNNs the kernel needs to convolute the input sequence. As a result, transform-

transformers faster to train [54]. On the other hand, transformers are large models with
a huge number of parameters, so as a result they need to be trained on massive amounts
of data [77].
Thanks to the attention mechanism, transformers weight the relative importance between
the different observations in a sequence, which makes them more efficient at capturing
long range dependencies in the data. This is explained by the fact that the path between
observations of the input and the output sequence in transformers is shorter than in CNNs
and RNNs because of the same reasons stated in the paragraph above: RNNs process the
data sequentially and CNNs convolute the data, whereas the attention mechanism relates
all observations from the sequences at once [54]. The shorter these paths are, the easier it
is to learn long-range dependencies [54].

3.5.5
Decoder
Only the encoder part is used in this thesis, but for the sake of completeness the decoder
will be briefly described.
The decoder (right part of figure 3.8) produces one element of the output sequence at a
time. It consists of Ndec concatenated decoder layers, which can be a different number
to the number of encoder layers. It has two multi-head attention blocks. The masked
multi-head attention block (at the bottom in figure 3.8) takes the output of the previous
decoder layer but it is allowed to attend just to earlier predictions in the output sequence.
This is done by setting the corresponding scores to −∞before the softmax function in
figure 3.9 and equation 3.13. The second multi-head attention block receives the queries
from the previous decoder layer and the keys and values from the output of the encoder.
In this way, it relates the full input sequence with the sequence predicted so far. Lastly,
it has a FFN, normalization layers, residual connections, and positional encoding as the
ones described for the decoder [54].
The output of the decoder is linearly transformed and passed to a softmax function that
yield the output probabilities.
3.6
SPINDLE model
The SPINDLE model (Sleep Phase Identification with Neural networks for Domain-invariant
LEearning [31]) was the first deep learning model for sleep scoring in mice. It consists of
3 main parts: (1) a data preprocessing block; (2) a CNN model, composed of 2 CNN net-
works and responsible for predicting the class of an epoch; and (3) a HMM that includes
expert knowledge and that corrects the sequence of stages predicted by the CNN part.
A diagram of the model in shown in figure 3.11. The model takes as inputs three signals
(step a in the figure): two EEG and one EMG signal. The three signals are preprocessed
to produce a spectrogram (step b). This spectrogram is the input to the two CNN net-
works. The first CNN (CNN1, step d) predicts a sleep stage for that input. The second
(CNN2, step e) predicts whether the input is an artifact or not. Once the whole signal has
been analysed and a sequence of predictions is produced by CNN1, the sequence is passed
to the HMM, which corrects the sequence by suppressing unfeasible transitions (step f1).
Additionally, the original SPINDLE model assigns a sleep stage to epochs predicted as
artifacts (step g), but this is not done in this thesis since there are not stage labels for all
artifacts in the KornumLab dataset.
1All CNN1 predictions are passed to the HMM, also those of epochs predicted as artifacts by CNN2.
This allows for keeping the time continuity of the sequence. However, given that the artifact epochs are
more likely to be scored wrongly by CNN1, there is a higher risk of passing wrong predictions to the HMM
than if the epochs predicted as artifacts were excluded in the HMM. In any case, both approaches were
tested in this thesis and the results were almost identical.

Data preprocessing
The preprocessing pipeline of SPINDLE, illustrated in figure 3.12, consists of the following
steps:
1. The EEG and EMG signals are resampled to 128 Hz to set all signals from the
different datasets to the same sample rate.
2. The whole signals are transformed to spectrograms by using a short-time Fourier
transform (STFT) along the whole recording, on overlapping windows of 256 samples
(corresponding to 2 seconds) with a step size of 16 samples. A Hamming window
is applied to every frame in the STFT to reduce edge effects.
This results in 3
spectrograms (EEG1, EEG2 and EMG).
3. The spectrograms are sliced to keep just the frequency range of interest. The rows
that belong to the range 0.5-24 Hz for EEG and 0.5-30 Hz for EMG are kept, the
rest of the rows are discarded.
4. The power spectral density (PSD) is calculated in every spectrogram by squaring
the real part of the spectrograms element-wise.
5. Just for the EMG channel, the spectrogram is reduced to a 1-dimensional vector
by summing all the rows across the frequency axis. Since the 3 channels need to
have the same dimension to be passed as input to the CNN, the 1D-vector is stacked
vertically to match the dimensions of the EEG spectrograms.
6. A power to decibel conversion is applied element-wise as 10 · log10 x.
7. Each spectrogram is standardized per recording and per frequency component by
subtraction of the mean and division of the standard deviation in each row of the
spectrogram.
8. The resulting spectrogram for the whole signal is sliced in windows corresponding to
the individual epochs. To capture contextual information, the 2 neighboring epochs

3.6.3
Hidden Markov Model (HMM)
As explained in section 3.3, a HMM is used to find the most likely sequence of hidden states
Z = {z1, . . . , zT }, based on the sequence of observations X = {x1, . . . , xT }. In the context
of the sleep scoring problem, the hidden state in some moment t is the sleep stage of the
brain in that moment, so the space of hidden states S is S = {WAKE, REM, NREM}.
The observable variable xt is the corresponding region of EEG/EMG signal at time t.
In SPINDLE, the HMM is used to correct the sequence of predictions produced by CNN1.
As explained in section 3.3, a HMM is defined by the initial state probabilities, the emission
likelihoods and the transitions probabilities. These are defined in SPINDLE by making
several assumptions, which incorporate domain knowledge with the intention of suppress-
ing unfeasible transitions. The assumptions that give rise to the HMM parameters are the
following:
• We assume that all initial probabilities p(z1 = si) are equal for all three stages.
• To define a transition matrix, SPINDLE’s authors take advantage of the fact that
some transitions are not feasible in healthy mice. These are REM →NREM and
WAKE →REM.
To include this information in the transition matrix, the two
corresponding entries are zeroed out. The rest of the entries in the transition matrix
are empirically estimated by counting transitions in the training data. The resulting

Experiments
First, the SPINDLE model will be reproduced to verify the implementation and see how

3.7
SleepTransformer model
SleepTransformer is the first model to use the transformer architecture in sleep scoring.
It was proposed by Phan et al., achieving state-of-the-art performance in human sleep
staging. In addition, they leveraged the attention mechanism to show what parts of the
data are more relevant to the model in a specific prediction. This is done at two levels:
intra-epoch and inter-epoch. At intra-epoch level, the attention scores point to the parts
of the input epoch that were more relevant to classify that epoch. At inter-epoch level,
the attention scores can be used to highlight the influence that other epochs had in the
prediction of a given epoch [56].
SleepTransformer has been tested only on humans. However, in this thesis we are inter-
ested in investigating how it will perform in mice when the model is trained from scratch.
In Phan et al., EEG, EOG and EMG are used as input data. However, EOG is not com-
monly measured in mice, given the difficulty of placing the electrodes in them. Therefore,
the EOG channel will be substituted by a second EEG channel. In addition, Phan et al.
only attempted to predict sleep stages, but since we also have an artifact label in our data,
a fourth class will be added to the model. In this way, both SPINDLE and SleepTrans-
former will be compared in artifact detection too.

To replicate the model in humans, the same preprocessing as in the original SleepTrans-

2. The signals are transformed to spectrograms by applying STFT separately to each

spectrogram.

in mice (17 time frames and 128 frequency bins). In mouse data, the spectrograms
of EEG1, EEG2 and EMG are concatenated along the frequency axis to produce a
single matrix of size 17 × 384, that will be the input to the model.
3.7.2
Architecture
A diagram of SleepTransformer’s architecture is shown in figure 3.16. As it can be seen,
only the encoder of the transformer model is actually used. The architecture of Sleep-
Transformer can be divided into four levels.
The first level is the epoch level (at the bottom of figure 3.16), which learns the intra-epoch
relationships in the data by relating the spectral columns at different time points. The
second level is the feature vector representation level. It is a linear transformation that
compresses the 2-dimensional output of the epoch level into a 1-dimensional representation
of the input epoch by taking a weighted average of the spectral columns. After that, the
compact representation of every epoch in the input sequence is stacked together, yielding a
sequential representation of the data. This sequence of representation vectors is the input
to the third level, the sequence level. Here, the inter-epoch dependencies are learned by
the encoder. Finally, the output of the sequence level for each of the epochs is presented
to a classifier, that produces the prediction for each epoch. The four levels are further
explained in the following subsections.

Discussion
In this chapter, some possible reasons behind the obtained results are discussed in the first
subsection. In the second subsection, the nature of the methods used is discussed.
5.1
Results discussion
In general, the overall performance obtained is a promising result for the adoption of au-
tomated sleep scoring. In fact, although the agreement rate between the models and the
human scorers from the Kornum Lab has not been evaluated, the results obtained and the
agreement rate between two scorers from the Kornum Lab can be compared (table 4.10
and table 5.1). Even though the metrics are not exactly the same, it can be said that
the performance is quite comparable, especially considering that the agreement rate from
the Kornum Lab comes from the comparison of a scorer trained by the other one. This
confirms the potential of deep learning models as a replacement for manual scoring. In
this sense, it is reasonable to take further steps towards automatic sleep scoring in the
Kornum Lab.
The performance in the REM stage is lower than in the other two stages throughout the
results chapter. A possible explanation when the KornumLab dataset is used as train-
ing data, is that the REM stage in underrepresented compared to WAKE and NREM
(table 2.2), so there is less data to learn from.
In fact, this could be the reason why
the performance in the REM stage is lower in SleepTransformer than in SPINDLE while
the performance in NREM and WAKE is almost identical in both models. Transformer
models need larger amounts of training data because they are huge models with a large
number of parameters [83]. Therefore, it would be reasonable that SleepTransformer is
more affected by the low prevalence of REM epochs. In SPINDLE, class-weighted cross
entropy (CWCE) was used to compensate for the class imbalance. In addition, the HMM
of SPINDLE has been shown to improve the performance in the REM phase. Although in
SPINDLE the performance in REM is also worse than in the other two stages, both HMM
and CWCE likely contributed to SPINDLE being better than SleepTransformer at REM
prediction (table 4.10). A question worth exploring would be whether CWCE and HMM
would have a similar effect if they are integrated in SleepTransformer. Another possible

reason for the lower performance in REM is that it is the most difficult stage to predict in
mice and therefore the most subject to inter-scorer variability (see table 5.1). In humans,
REM detection is aided by the rapid eye movement, which is detected in EOG, but in
mice only EEG and EMG are available. In addition, scorers can use video, but this is not
available in the models. When this issue was discussed with the head of the Kornum lab,

in itself. The problem is scoring the transition epochs to and from REM, because it is not
easy to determine when the REM stage begins and ends. This actually shows a structural
limitation of sleep scoring, as some epochs may reflect a mixed brain state and the current
framework of single-label epochs does not take this into account. To overcome this limita-
tion, there is an increasing support for moving away from the single-label model towards a
mixed representation of sleep through the use of hypnodensities, where an epoch is scored
into the probabilities of all stages rather than into a fixed label [84, 85].

contains annotations from five different scorers and 33 mice, making the model more ro-

data of the BrownLab dataset contains only two mice, the good generalization of SPIN-

might explain why the generalization is worse than in SPINDLE is the aforementioned
fact that transformers require large amounts of data, which makes them more prone to
overfit [83]. Since artifact prediction is already poor on its own, not much can be said
about its generalization, except the accuracy is much worst in the BrownLab dataset due
to the larger number of artifacts.
There are several possible reasons for the low performance in artifact detection. Firstly,
the criteria for scoring artifacts is even more subjective than for sleep stages (see tables 4.2
and 5.1). This makes it particularly difficult to learn the patterns of an artifact. One solu-
tion would be to include data from many different scorers, and indeed in the KornumLab
dataset the labels come from 5 different scorers. However, this would need to be coupled
with a considerable amount of data in order for the model to ignore the subjectivity in the
data. This leads to the second problem: the amount of artifacts in the KornumLab dataset
is too low (0.4%, table 2.2). Even if the data was not biased, the artifact class is very
underrepresented. This could the reason why SleepTransformer is extremely conservative
in predicting artifacts. Nevertheless, in the BrownLab dataset there is a representative
amount of artifacts (17%, table 2.2), but the fact that the artifact detection is also poor

5.2
Method discussion
The original hypothesis was that SleepTransformer would outperform SPINDLE, since the
transformer architecture allows for longer range dependencies than CNNs, and it has shown
state-of-the-art performance in humans in Phan et al. However, no significant difference
was found between the models (table 4.10). The t-test measures statistical significance by
comparing the accuracy in each recording. This results in a low statistical power, since
the models are evaluated on 11 and 4 recordings in the KornumLab and BrownLab data
respectively. A way to get around this would be to use the McNemar’s test, where the
comparison is performed per sample (i.e. per epoch), which would boost the statistical
power.
One possible reason for SleepTransforming not outperforming SPINDLE is, as mentioned
above, that transformer models require larger datasets. But an important point is that
the architecture of SleepTransformer is optimized for human data. As Phan et al. explain,
in a transformer-based architecture choosing an appropriate number of transformer layers
can be crucial ((NE and NS in figure 3.16). Although they later show that it does not
have much impact in their case [56], this may not be the case for mice. The same can be
said for the length of the input sequence (L in figure 3.16), as the value from the original
paper was used (L = 21) and this value was optimized for humans.
In RNNs for human sleep scoring, the optimal length of the input sequence is typically
found to be around 20-30 epochs [83], and in SleepTransformer it was found to be 21
epochs. Given that the epochs in Phan et al. are 30 seconds long, the model had a recep-
tive field of 360 seconds (21 times 30), or 10.5 minutes. Since the mouse epochs used in
this thesis are 4 seconds long, the receptive field is reduced by a factor of 7.5 (the receptive
field is 84 seconds). At the time of using SleepTransformer in mice, this raised the question
of whether the receptive field should match the one of Phan et al. in terms of number of
epochs or in terms of time span. In mice, the epochs are usually shorter just because more
resolution is needed in basic research, but the transition speed is the same in humans and
in mice [personal communication with Birgitte R. Kornum]. This would be a reason to
match the receptive field in time. However, the stage dynamics in mice are very different
from those in humans: in 10 minutes a mouse can go through all the states several times,
whereas in humans there is a more stable cycle. This would be a reason not to have a long
receptive field in mice. In fact, in subsequent work on SleepTransformer [83], Phan et al.
increased the input sequence to 100 and 200 epochs arguing that covering a full human
sleep cycle in the receptive field would be beneficial. They actually obtained a worse re-
sult in SleepTransformer. Nevertheless, they argue that a negative effect of increasing the
receptive field in sleep scoring seems implausible and that this effect is likely because a
longer sequence makes SleepTransformer more prone to overfitting, as the epochs are seen
more frequently during training. Even though there are no sleep cycles in mice, it would
be interesting to see the influence of increasing the receptive field, together with testing
different numbers of transformer layers as mentioned above. In SPINDLE, it would also
be interesting to expand the receptive field by including more neighbor epochs in the input

Another interesting thing to try in SleepTransformer would be to take the model trained
on humans and test it directly on mice. This was not done in this thesis since the main
goal was to train SleepTransformer on mice. Most of the architecture SleepTransformer
depends on the length of the frequency axis of the spectrograms and not on the time axis.
The time axis is precisely the only dimensional difference between the human and mouse
data of this thesis, so it is something doable. In figure 3.16, the whole epoch, feature vector
representation, and sequence levels could be reused with the exception of the positional
encoding. The classifier would also need to be replaced and retrained on mice because of
the different number of stages in humans and mice. This experiment would be particularly
interesting because is a very straightforward way to test how translatable human models
are to mice and it could give an intuition of how similar human and mouse sleep are.
The model could be fine-tuned by further training it on mice to improve the performance.
In addition, doing transfer learning from the human model would be a good way to get
around the lack of mice data, since much more human data is available. However, the
issues mentioned above would still exist, since the number of transformer layers and the
length of the sequence would still be the optimal for humans and not for mice.
Something that stands out in SPINDLE is the use of the HMM in such deterministic way.
The HMM is a trainable model, so better parameters can be found instead of empirically
estimating the transitions and prior probabilities from the data. In addition, the assump-
tion of having equal prior probabilities for the states comes from the fact that CNN1
was trained in a class-balanced way by using CWCE, but this might not be completely
sustainable. More importantly, the whole HMM is built on top of the assumption that
the transitions REM →NREM and WAKE →REM are not possible in healthy mice.
In fact, there is no consensus on this rule and in the Kornum Lab sleep experts do not
follow this assumption when scoring. It is true that by looking at the transitions in the
KornumLab data (table 3.1), the prevalence of these two transitions is close to zero, but
this does not mean that they are completely infeasible. In addition, it should be noted
that this assumption applies only to healthy mice, and would not be valid for mice with
a sleep disorder that alters sleep transitions, such as narcolepsy. In general, this applies
to all the work carried out in this thesis. In the same way that it would be interesting to
study transfer learning between humans and mice, it would be interesting to do the same
between disease models. Especially, since sleep scoring is a tool to study sleep disorders.
Another interesting point of SPINDLE is the use of 2 separate CNNs for sleep stage pre-
diction and artifact prediction. Having a a single network that predicts the four labels
(as in SleepTransformer) would be a perfectly valid approach though. Miladinovi´c et al.
mainly argue that this setup worked better, but they also give other interesting reasons.
One of them is that this setup allows predicting which sleep stage an artifact epoch be-
longs to. However, this could also be achieved in a single CNN network as well by using a
softmax activation in the output of the three sleep stages and a sigmoid activation in the
output of the artifact neuron. The last reason they argue is that sleep stages are classified
according to specific patterns, whereas artifacts are outliers, so having two separate net-
works would be more reasonable [31]. This leads to the question of whether the use of two
separate models is what makes the artifact detection in SPINDLE and SleepTransformer
so different.

In addition, it would be very interesting to address the problem of interpretability. Sleep-
Transformer is interpretable by itself. The attention weights could be exploited to plot a
map of the input, highlighting the areas the model pays more attention to. This would
give a direct insight into what the model is looking at. This could be used to find new
patterns for sleep scoring, to improve the model and to support the model’s predictions.
With the purpose of supporting decision-making, Phan et al. also attempted to quantify
the confidence of the prediction with an entropy-based metric. This entropy-based metric
can also be applied to any model, so it would be interesting to see how confident Sleep-
Transformer and SPINDLE are in the same set of epochs. SPINDLE itself does not have
a mechanism to interpret its predictions, but some approaches already tested in human
scoring models could be applied. Some of these methods are layer-wise relevance propa-
gation [41], grad-CAM [60] and occlusion perturbation [87].

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
In this thesis, two deep learning sleep scoring models were investigated for mouse sleep
scoring. The first, the SPINDLE model, is a mouse sleep scoring model which was se-
lected because of its state-of-the-art performance and outstanding generalization, which
is one of the major challenges in mouse sleep scoring models.
The second, the Sleep-
Transformer model, is a model that has only been tested in humans and was selected
because it is the first sleep scoring model to use the transformer architecture, which opens
the door to address another important challenge in automatic sleep staging: explainability.
The first research question was whether the models were replicable. SPINDLE was repro-
duced using the same data as in the original work, with the same architecture and training
parameters as reported. The results obtained were similar in terms of overall accuracy and
in the WAKE and NREM stages. However, in the REM stage the results obtained were
not as good as in the original work, which could indicate that further tuning is needed
in order to achieve the same performance. The artifact detection results were also sim-
ilar. SleepTransformer was trained on a smaller sample of the original dataset used for
human sleep scoring, which was mainly done as a way to verify the training and testing
setup. The model was considered to be replicable, considering the difference in sample size.
The second goal was to train and test SPINDLE on data from the Molecular Sleep Lab
(Kornum Lab), which is a larger and more diverse sample than the dataset it was trained
on in the original work. This resulted in a similar performance and improved generaliza-
tion. Here, the effect of each major component of SPINDLE was studied in more detail.
The HMM was found to have a small impact on the overall accuracy of sleep stage pre-
diction, but with a relevant improvement in REM stage. In addition, it was shown to
effectively incorporate domain knowledge by suppressing the restricted transitions. On
the other hand, the performance of the artifact detection network was poor.
With the third research question, the performance of SleepTransformer trained from
scratch on mouse data from the Molecular Sleep Lab was studied.
The overall sleep
stge prediction was good, though the performance in REM was more mediocre than in the
other stages and than in SPINDLE. On the other hand, the artifact detection was shown
47
==PAGE:59==
CHAPTER 6. CONCLUSION
to be ineffective, since almost no artifacts are detected.
Fourth, the two models were compared under the initial hypothesis that the transformer
architecture would improve the performance in mouse sleep scoring. However, statisti-
cally significant differences were only found when artifacts were included, but in sleep
stage prediction the performance is pretty much equal. Although not statistically signif-
icant, SPINDLE showed much better generalization than SleepTransformer. One of the
reasons why the hypothesis was not proved could be that transformer models need much
more data than CNNs. In addition, the architecture parameters used in this thesis were
the ones reported in the original work, which were found to be the best working parame-
ters for human data and only for sleep stage prediction (i.e. without artifacts).
Finally, although not compared in detail, the performance of the models studied in this
thesis is on par with the agreement rate of two human scorers from the Kornum Lab.
Therefore, the results obtained are a solid argument to take further steps in automatic
scoring models in mice.
