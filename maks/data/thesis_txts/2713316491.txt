1 Abstract   
 
Wound management is a prevalent issue in the health care system due to the emergence of non-heal-
ing wounds that can lead to bacterial infection. Therefore, there is a need to improve wound dressings 
available on the market in order to reduce healthcare costs. This project aims to develop a smart 
wound dressing to detect bacterial infections. To address the aim, the first step will be identifying 
biomarkers associated with wound infection and inflammation, including pH, temperature, and oxy-
gen levels. The project will consist of several experiments with different objectives, focusing primar-
ily on the pH indicator. Furthermore, this project will investigate the possibility of incorporating a 
colorimetric sensor into wound dressings to detect the pH. An electrochemical sensor will also be 
examined to determine whether a signal can be obtained in a wound dressing.  
 
Infected wounds have an alkaline pH, while inflammatory wounds have a neutral/acidic pH. The 
temperature difference between infected wounds and normal tissue is >3°C. Inflammatory wounds 
have an oxygen level greater than 40 mmHg and a temperature difference of <3°C. Based on the 
composition, function, and results of the experiments, Fusion 5 proved to be the most suitable mem-
brane for incorporation into a smart wound dressing. The experiment conducted with natural dyes 
demonstrates that pH can be detected using a colorimetric sensor. Fuchsin and carmine are the two 
best dyes due to their leaching properties. Additionally, an electrochemical signal can be obtained 
through Fusion 5 and detect the presence of bacteria.

2 Introduction  
 
 
Wounds can be defined as injuries to the skin's surface that usually involve damage to the underlying 
tissues, and they can be classified into acute- and chronic wounds. Acute wounds progress through 
the normal stages of wound healing within four weeks. However, chronic wounds remain in an in-
flammatory phase for an extended period of time, and in worst-case scenarios, they can result in a 
bacterial infection. For example, Staphylococcus aureus (S. aureus) is a prevalent bacteria in wounds 
and can potentially result in sepsis, meningitis and bone infections and even result in amputations or 
death. 
 
Non-healing wounds are increasing each year posing a significant threat to public health care and the 
economy. Chronic wounds are estimated to cost the United States of America over $28 billion annu-
ally. Hence, wound dressings need to be made smarter by being able to warn patients and healthcare 
professionals when an infection is present in a wound, and even better, treating the infection auto-
matically. This will improve the efficiency of the healthcare system and provide a more comfortable 
treatment strategy for patients. 
 
There are currently several wound dressings available designed to promote wound healing. However, 
they have several limitations, such as being passive and incapable of responding to changes in wound 
environments. Additionally, the dressings available cannot distinguish the type of bacteria that caused 
the infection and do not provide information on the healing rate or state of the wound. Consequently, 
the current practice for wound care involves visual inspection of the wound and manual removal of 
the wound dressing, potentially increasing the risk of infection.  The following could lead to hospital-
acquired infections, a major problem worldwide involving the development of an infection during a 
hospital stay. These processes are not only time-consuming for the medical staff, but they also disturb 
the natural healing process of the wound.  
 
The abovementioned leads to the specific aim of this project, which is to develop a smart wound 
dressing for the identification of bacterial infections. To accomplish the following there are three 
steps that must be investigated. The first step toward developing the ideal smart wound dressing con-
sists of monitoring biomarkers such as pH, temperature, and oxygen levels to detect wound infection 
and inflammation. The second step will be to identify specific biomarkers that can be used to deter-
mine whether the infection is caused by S. aureus or Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus 
(MRSA), which are the two most common bacteria associated with wound infections. The third and 
final step is to integrate a drug delivery system to treat infections automatically. This project will 
address the first step in the development of a smart wound dressing prototype, focusing specifically 
on the pH indicator.

3 Background   
 
3.1 Staphylococcus aureus and Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus 
S. aureus is a facultatively anaerobic, gram-positive, and nonmotile bacteria. The bacteria are part 
of the human's normal flora, mainly occurring on mucous membranes and the skin. Humans are a 
major reservoir for S. aureus. [1]. The optimum pH range for the growth of S. aureus is 7.0 to 8.0 
and the ideal temperature is 37-40°C [2]–[4].  
 
The slime capsule is the outer layer of S. aureus and is responsible for protecting the bacteria from 
phagocytosis and enables the bacteria to adhere to artificial surfaces, such as joints, catheters, and 
prosthetic valves. The cell wall consists of a thick peptidoglycan layer which ensures structural in-
tegrity and protects the cell against osmotic lysis [5], [6]. The cell structure of S. aureus can be seen 
in figure 1. A number of virulence factors are expressed by the bacteria, including surface proteins 
called 24 wall-anchored proteins (CWAs), which are attached covalently to the peptidoglycan layer. 
There are 24 CWAs on the surface of S. aureus. Surface proteins are essential for the organism to 
survive as both a commensal bacterium and a pathogen [7].  Protein A is a CWA protein that binds 
to the FC portion of immunoglobulin G (IgG), preventing complement activation (phagocytosis). In 
other words, protein A is a virulence factor that inhibits the body's capability to activate phagocytosis, 
thus preventing the clearing of S. aureus from the body [8], [9].  The virulence factor, toxins are also 
produced by S. aureus including α-hemolysin (lysis of red blood cells), enterotoxin (food poisoning), 
exfoliative toxin (scalded skin syndrome), and toxic shock syndrome toxin-1 (TSST-1) (multiple or-
gan failure and death). S. aureus also produces enzymes such as coagulase, which converts fibrinogen 
into fibrin, causing blood clotting [10].

The bactericidal antibiotic, Methicillin, is commonly used to treat most strains of S. aureus. Methi-
cillin inhibits the synthesis of bacteria's cell walls by inhibiting transpeptidation enzymes (Penicillin-

binding proteins) during peptidoglycan cross-linkage. Consequently, the cell wall will lose structural 
integrity, resulting in bacterial lysis. S. aureus is a challenge to treat due to the emergence of MRSA. 
Some strains of S. aureus possess the staphylococcal chromosome cassette Mec element. The peni-
cillin-binding protein 2a (pBP2a) encoded by MecA prevents ß-lactam antibiotics, including Methi-
cillin, from inhibiting transpeptidation enzymes, rendering S. aureus impermeable [11], [12]. 
 
Most MRSA strains are resistant to all ß-lactam antibiotics [13]. Vancomycin and Teicoplanin are 
the most used antibiotics for treating severe MRSA infections [14]. 
 
3.2 Wounds  
3.2.1 Stages of wound healing   
Anatomically, the skin serves as a barrier against biological, chemical, and mechanical agents. There 
are several layers of skin, including the epidermis, dermis, and subcutaneous tissue [15]. Wound 
healing is a natural process of tissue damage to restore the skin's normal structure and function. In the 
case of skin damage, the healing process depends on the depth of the injury [16]. Skin wounds, such 
as minor abrasions and burns, do not penetrate the dermis. In this case, healing occurs by a simple 
process in which surrounding basal epithelial cells proliferate and migrate across the wound to reseal 
the damage [16]. The healing process for wounds that reaches the dermis is more complex and con-
sists of four stages, as illustrated in figure 2 [17]. 
 
 
Figure 2: An illustration of the four phases of wound healing. Hemostasis involves stopping the bleeding in a wound, inflammation is 
the removal of pathogens and debris, scar tissue is formed during proliferation, and the remodeling phase aims to strengthen the 
wound tissue. Created in BioRender. 
The first stage, hemostasis occurs immediately following an injury and is characterized by forming 
fibrin clots by platelet cells at the injury site, thus preventing blood loss [18]. The wound clot and 
surrounding tissue will release proinflammatory cytokines (IL-1, IL-6, IL-8, and TNF-α) and growth 
factors (TGF-β, PDGF, FGF, and EGF) to activate the inflammatory cells. These cells will migrate 
into the wound by chemotaxis, which initiates the inflammatory response [18]. 
==PAGE:9==
 
7 
 
Inflammation is the second stage of wound healing, which involves neutrophils, macrophages, and 
lymphocytes being sequentially infiltrated. Invading pathogens and cellular debris are cleared by neu-
trophils through phagocytosis, reducing the risk of infection [17], [18]. Macrophages are also respon-
sible for the induction and clearance of apoptotic cells, resulting in the resolution of inflammation 
[18], [19]. The damaged tissues causes mast cells and basophils to release histamine, which triggers 
vasodilation (causes redness) and increases the blood vessels permeability (causes swelling) [18].   
 
Proliferation is the third stage and involves forming granulation tissue, characterized by three phases: 
granulation, contraction and epithelialization, as illustrated in figure 3. In the wound, granulation is 
generated and this tissue consists of fibroblasts, capillaries, and neutrophils [20]. The tissue produces 
fibroblasts that stimulate the production of collagen, which gives the tissue its tensile strength. Col-
lagen synthesis begins with the fibroblast, which secretes procollagen. Growth of the procollagen 
fibers is a complicated process and involves macrophages and platelets [20]. Procollagen fibers ma-
ture into collagen fibrils. The fibrils are then linked together into a very strong rope-like collagen 
fiber [21]. As granulation fills the wound site, the wound contracts decreasing its size. Fibroblasts 
also stimulate the proliferation of endothelial cells surrounding the wound and the regrowth of injured 
blood vessel (angiogenesis) [18], [20].  
 
 
Figure 3:  An illustration of the three phases involved in proliferation. Phase 1 is the process of generating granulation tissue. Phase 
2 is the contraction phase, during which the wound size decreases. Phase 3 is epithelialization, which involves the proliferation of 
endothelial cells. Created in BioRender. 
Remodeling is the last phase of wound healing, during which the granulation tissue matures into scar 
tissue [18], [22]. The extracellular matrix is reorganized, degraded, and resynthesized to increase its 
tensile strength. Collagen type III is replaced by collagen type I, which is the main fibrillar collagen 
in the skin. The scar tissue differs from normal skin in that it has a denser arrangement of collagen 
fibers and is less elastic. After several months, the repaired tissue will reach about 80% of normal 
tissue's tensile strength [17], [18]. 
 
3.2.2 Infection vs. inflammation 
Wound infection is defined as the presence of a pathogenic agent resulting in tissue damage. An 
infection may not present symptoms and therefore may be subclinical or clinically apparent [23]. 
Fever, pain, swelling, purulent drainage, and redness are the most common signs of infection. More 
recently documented signs are tissue discoloration, friable granulation, and wound healing delays 
==PAGE:10==
 
8 
[24].  First-line defense against pathogens is the innate immune system, composed of non-specific 
defense mechanisms activated immediately following infection. The innate immune cells are acti-
vated by pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) that recognize pathogen-associated molecular patterns 
(PAMPs) on the pathogen's surface. In the event that the anatomical barriers have been breached and 
the pathogen enters the tissue, the innate immune response activates the second line of defense, in-
flammation, which is the body's natural response to harmful stimuli and injuries in an attempt to 
restore balance [25]. The following cardinal symptoms are typical of inflammation: dolor (pain), calor 
(heat), tumor (swelling), rubor (redness), and function laesa (loss of function) [23].

3.3 Biomarkers 
The term biomarker refers to quantifiable physiological parameters that change within a wound in 
response to infection and inflammation. In recent years biomarkers such as pH, temperature, and 
oxygen levels have been identified as essential indicators of wound healing [26]. It is imperative to 
monitor the parameters to interpret the wound status and develop a more efficient smart wound 
dressing. The following section will identify the current research on pH, temperature, and oxygen 
levels within infected wounds and wounds in the inflammatory phase. In this project, the specific 
biomarkers are selected based on their availability in the literature and accessibility to detect wound 
changes. 
 
3.3.1 pH 
Intact skin usually has a pH of 4.0 to 6.0. The acidic environment serves as a natural defense mech-
anism against pathogens, but the pH environment changes when skin integrity is compromised [27], 
[28]. Infected wounds have been associated with an alkaline pH and a slower healing process. 
Therefore, monitoring the pH within a wound can provide an early indication of infection, making 
pH an essential parameter in wound healing [29]

3.3.2 Temperature 
Temperature is a fundamental parameter that affects every cellular function in the body. The param-
eter can assist with an understanding of whether a wound is infected or an immediate immune re-
sponse (Inflammation). [28]. Extreme hypothermia has been associated with poor wound healing and 
a greater risk of infection [41]. Consequently, it can inhibit the activation of platelet cells as well as 
decrease the supply of oxygen to the neutrophils to combat infection. Studies have shown that < 33°C 
is the critical temperature for regular cellular activity, including the immune cells [42]. The temper-
ature of normal tissue is between 36.5°C and 37.5°C. An increase in temperature >+1.11°C may 
exhibit the presence of bacterial infection [43]. Table 3 summarizes the results of the studies regarding 
wound temperature in infected wounds and wounds in the inflammatory phase.

3.3.3 Oxygen tension 
Oxygen is a vital parameter in wound healing to regenerate healthy tissue. A normal oxygen level 
(75-100 mmHg) is necessary to promote wound healing because oxygen is required for a number of 
wound healing processes, including fibroplasia, collagen deposition, epithelization, fibroplasia, and 
angiogenesis [47].  The insufficient delivery of oxygen-rich blood to wound tissues hinders wound 
healing [48].  Poor wound healing has been associated with oxygen tensions below 20 mmHg, 
while adequate wound healing requires oxygen tensions greater than 40 mmHg.  
 
In the literature, wounds in the inflammatory phase are identified by oxygen saturation levels exceed-
ing 40 mmHg [41], [49].  High oxygen tension is necessary for immune cells to phagocytose invading 
pathogens [50]. In a study by Allen et al., Phorbol myristate acetate was used to stimulate neutrophils 
in human blood and wounds. An oxygen consumption measurement was conducted between 30 and 
300 mmHg. The study demonstrated that neutrophils lose their capacity at a level of oxygen <40 
mmHg [51]. A prospective study involving 130 surgical patients was also conducted by Hopf et al. 
A correlation was found between wound oxygen level and the risk of wound infection. Therefore, 
improving oxygen levels may facilitate the reduction of infection and promote wound healing [52]. 
Grief et al. conducted a prospective study in which 500 patients undergoing colorectal surgery re-
ceived 30% or 80% oxygen. The infection rate was half as high when patients were given 80% oxy-
gen. The results indicate that the higher the oxygen level, the lower the risk of infection[53]. In addi-
tion, it can be estimated that infected wounds have lower oxygen levels, while wounds in the inflam-
matory phase have a higher oxygen level.

3.4 Sensor technology 
Colorimetric sensors can incorporate into a wearable device that can be used to detect the bi-
omarkers described in section 3.3. It is a sensor that changes color when influenced by external 
stimuli. The sensor possesses the following advantages: easy to use, robust, high accessibility, cost-
effective, fast detection, and does not require the integration of electrical components [54].   
Several studies have been conducted on pH sensors based on colorimetric detection over the years. 
The use of pH-indicator dyes is one of the easiest and most cost-effective methods for monitoring the 
pH of wounds. Synthetic dyes (chemically synthesized) have potentially toxic effects that can lead to 
skin sensitivity and irritation. Due to their safety profile and ability to change color across a wide 
range of pH values, natural dyes are a better alternative [55]. The disadvantage of the colorimetric 
method is that external stimuli such as physical or chemical changes in the wound environment may 
influence the color change. Furthermore, pH sensors present the challenge of ensuring a close adhe-
sion between wound dressings and dyes to prevent the dye from leaching onto the skin. The color 
matrix readout is mainly derived from image-processing captured by a smartphone or estimated by 
the naked eye. A naked-eye assessment can be subjective therefore, researchers are increasingly re-
lying on image processing in order to identify the color change. Image processing is also prone to 
errors due to several factors including, image quality and lighting. Therefore, research is still being 
conducted to find ways to account for lighting and camera conditions [41].    
An electrochemical sensor is an analytical technique that utilizes electrodes to generate a signal (cur-
rent) based on the presence of an analyte [56]. The rapid development of wearable electrochemical 
sensors has been facilitated by techniques such as inkjet- and screen-printing on substrates. The sen-
sor has a number of advantages, such as high sensitivity, low cost, reproducibility, low waste, large-
scale production, and fast detection. his technique also has some disadvantages, such as the necessity 
of external power (batteries) and the difficulty of miniaturizing the potentiostat. Electrochemical tech-
niques include amperometric, voltammetric, and impedimetric techniques. The first method is based

3.5 Drug release  
To develop an effective smart wound dressing, it should be capable of releasing antibiotics into 
wounds upon infection. Few smart wound dressings currently incorporate drug release mechanisms, 
which presents a significant challenge [64]. To prevent the overuse of antibiotics, it should be sensi-
tive enough to release the drug only if it detects S. aureus or MRSA. For this reason, the delivery 
mechanism of the drug should be precise and controlled to improve the quality of wound care. In 
order to fabricate an effective drug release system, further research is required. 
Drug delivery technologies can be classified as passive or active based on their ability to control the 
release profile of the drug externally. In passive drug release systems, antibiotics are released from 
bandages or drug carriers in a controlled manner. The system can easily be incorporated into wound 
dressings to deliver specific antibiotic dosages. Despite their simplicity and effectiveness in deliver-
ing antibiotics, the release profile cannot be modified [41].  In active drug delivery systems, the 
timepoint and location of drug release can be precisely controlled. In general, these systems release 
drugs only when they are stimulated by an external stimulus. Various mechanisms have been used to 
design active drug delivery tools for wound care, including thermally actuated thermoresponsive sys-
tems [41].   
In wound care, thermal stimulation is one of the most popular methods for triggering the release of 
drugs. Temperature control and heating require an external power supply and a microheater. A ther-
mally responsive system releases drugs into the environment by heating thermo-responsive materials 
such as hydrogels and polymers. By altering the temperature of these materials, their hydrophilic and 
hydrophobic properties can be altered, enabling their on-demand release of drugs [65].  
Bagherifard et al. used an integrated microfluidic system to fabricate thermo-responsive poly(N-iso-
propyl acrylamide) particles. Hydrogel sheets containing drug-loaded particles were attached to 
wound dressings with integrated, flexible heaters. Thermal stimulation has the disadvantage that the 
temperature used for drug release may cause low-temperature burns. Furthermore, thermoresponsive 
drug delivery platforms are prone to self-trigger in warm environments and are unable to deliver 
zero/no releases at room temperature [41], [66].

4.2.3 Culturing on paper 
The objective is to investigate bacteria's ability to grow on paper. The idea is to supply the bacteria 
with nutrients by adding agar solution to the paper, which can promote bacteria growth. An investi-
gation of this matter is relevant in connection with wound dressings since a wound infection may 
result in a delayed or non-healing wound. In this regard, it is essential that when a bandage is applied 
to a wound the bacteria can grow on the bandage rather than inside the wound, reducing bacterial 
growth and facilitating wound healing. Growing the bacteria on a bandage can eliminate the need to 
take a sample from the wound and send it to a laboratory where it must be grown on agar and counted

sensor. Considering a wound will be continuously moistened, the pH sensor must be reversible.

relation to integrating a wound dressing. Ideally, the colorimetric sensor must be able to change color

pad, which assists in guiding the fluid in one direction, in this case, downwards. The absorption pad 
layer is essential to ensure that the entire sample passes through the vertical flow chip and prevents 
backflow. On the other hand, if the absorption pad were not present, the flow would immediately stop 
once it reached the end of the membrane. The double-sided tape is applied in the sixth layer to secure 
a good contact between the absorption pad and the plastic. The plastic layer is the bottom layer of the 
chip and serves to stabilize the chip and prevent contamination since an exposed chip is more suscep-
tible to contamination.

4.9 Leaching tests 
Several leaching tests are performed using the dyes prepared in section 4.6. It is imperative to perform 
the test in order to determine the dye's leaching behavior and its stability. Moreover, it can be used to 
==PAGE:42==
 
40 
identify which dyes are more and less susceptible to leaching, thereby providing information on the 
best and worst dyes.  
 
Two types of leaching tests are conducted: the first involves merely dyes, and the second involves 
dyes and hydrogels. It is anticipated that hydrogels will be able to reduce the leaching of dyes. In 
order to determine which membrane is most suitable to incorporate into a wound dressing, leaching 
tests are performed on both Fusion5 and CF1. 
 
Both lateral and vertical chips are tested during the first part of the test. In the second part of the test, 
only vertical chips are used. This is due to the fact that it is more convenient to apply hydrogels to a 
vertical chip than to a lateral chip. In addition, a vertical chip is more similar to a wound dressing, as 
it comprises of different layers that are more compatible with wound dressings.  
 
Dyes dissolved in either H20 or EtOH are expected to leach because they are both water-soluble. 
Ethanol is soluble in water due to its -OH group allowing it to form hydrogen bonds with water 
molecules. Different parameters, including molecular weight, influence the leaching of dyes. Ethanol 
has a molecular weight of 46.07 g/mol, whereas H20 has a molecular weight of 18.01 g/mol [77], 
[78]. Larger molecules are less soluble in water, which may explain why leaching tends to increase 
in dyes dissolved in water. A higher temperature produces a greater degree of solubility. It is further 
anticipated that dyes dissolved in H20 will leach more readily due to their higher boiling point (100 
°C) compared to EtOH (78.29 °C) [77], [78].

4.11.3 Fabricating electrodes on Fusion 5  
The objective is to investigate the possibility of fabricating electrodes on Fusion 5. The idea is to 
draw electrodes onto the membrane and check if a signal is detected. Firstly, electrodes are drawn 
on the Fusion 5 using an ink pencil, as shown in figure 57. 
 
Figure 57: Fusion 5 drawn on with ink pencil to obtain a signal.  
By using a voltmeter, we determined that the ink used was not conductive and, therefore, no signal 
could be obtained. One possibility is to empty and remove the ink from the pencil and replace it with 
any conductive metals. Another option would be to create a stamp containing the electrodes structure 
and conductive paste. The following cannot be completed due to time constraints.   
It was also intended that half of the membrane containing the draw electrodes would be covered with 
wax paper in order to make this part of the membrane hydrophobic, thus preventing short circuits. 
First, the wax paper was placed on top of the Fusion 5, which was heated in an oven for 2, 5, and 
9 minutes at 120 and 150℃, but the wax paper was not absorbed.  The following could be due to a 
lack of contact between Fusion 5 and the wax paper. Thus, it was decided to apply the same principle, 
but this time using an iron to ensure better contact between the layers, but this did not improve the 
results. A wax printer machine would have allowed Fusion 5 to be printed directly onto wax paper, a 
method known to work; however, due to a lack of the machine in the laboratory, this was not possible 
to test. 
Considering the time constraints, this experiment was not able to proceed further.  The final product 
of fabricating an electrode on Fusion 5 should have been similar to the skeleton shown in figure 58.

5 Conclusion and outlook 
 
Globally, wound management is a major problem, and therefore making the wound dressing smarter 
can significantly improve wound healing. The first step in developing a smart wound dressing is 
distinguishing between an infection and inflammation by monitoring pH, temperature, and oxygen 
levels. Monitoring biomarkers is essential for interpreting wound status and developing an efficient 
smart wound dressing. 
 
There have been evidence that infected wounds have an alkaline pH and a temperature difference of 
>3 between normal tissue and infected wounds. Wounds that are in the inflammatory phase are 
characterized by a neutral or acidic environment, a temperature difference of <3 and an oxygen 
level that is greater than 40 mmHg. Fusion 5 is the most suitable membrane for incorporation into 
wound dressings based on its composition, functions, and results obtained from the experiments. 
Bacterial cultures have a pH of 7, while human serum containing bacteria has a pH between 8,5 to 
9, which is more consistent with the results reported in the literature. Bacteria can grow on paper, 
reducing the workload of healthcare professionals and allowing them to detect the type of bacteria 
responsible for the infection more quickly. A colorimetric sensor can be used to detect the pH of a 
wound using natural dyes. The dyes, however, tend to leach, and the intensity of the color de-
creases. Carmine (H2O) and fuchsin (EtOH) are the two dyes that leach the least, while orange G is 
the dye that leaches the most. It has been demonstrated that hydrogels can reduce the leaching of 
dyes significantly. Electrochemical sensors can be incorporated into wound dressings to detect the 
presence of bacteria. Fabricating electrodes on Fusion 5 could be a more compatible method in rela-
tion to a smart wound dressing to detect a wound infection. 
 
Currently, no wound dressing available on the market can detect whether a wound is infected or in 
the inflammatory phase and which bacteria have caused the infection and automatically treat the in-
fection. Future research will involve a second sensor that can detect specific surface proteins on bac-
teria to identify which bacteria caused the infection. Lastly, the infection can be treated automatically 
by incorporating a drug delivery system. Studies could focus on the biomarkers related to infected 
wounds and wounds in the inflammatory state to improve the reliability of the literature search results. 
 
This project represents a crucial step toward developing a smart wound dressing that can help pa-
tients detect, treat, and defeat infections at an early stage before they result in severe consequences. 
Thus, promoting wound healing, reducing health care costs, and enhancing patients' quality of life. 
 
5.1 Future outlook 
This project represents the first step toward developing a smart wound dressing. Based on the meas-
ured biomarkers pH, temperature, and oxygen level, the sensor/bandage will be able to identify 
whether there is an infection in the wound.  
 
The second step is to determine which bacteria is responsible for the infection. This can be accom-
plished without opening the smart wound dressing by incorporating a second sensor. As mentioned 
==PAGE:64==
 
62 
previously, S. aureus and MRSA have distinct surface proteins. One possible possibility is that anti-
bodies could be present in the bandage to bind to specific markers on the surface of bacteria, thereby 
providing a signal. There is also the option of using nanoparticles that bind to the proteins on bacteria, 
but this would require further research because it is not readily available. As nanoparticles cannot 
simply be incorporated into bandages, another option should be investigated that does not require 
nanoparticles. 
 
The third step in this project will involve integrating a drug delivery system into the bandage. An 
option would be to include materials in the smart wound dressing that will dissolve in response to 
changes in temperature. Another option may be to incorporate a heater into the bandage, according 
to X et al. In this way, materials such as hydrogel and polymers would be able to be heated and trigger 
the release of drugs. Additionally, blister packages may be incorporated into a smart wound dressing 
so that drugs will be released from the blister into the wound in response to a positive signal. The 
only challenge in this approach is the need for external stimuli, such as a telephone.  This could be 
accomplished by developing an iPhone application that responds to pH, temperature, and oxygen 
levels to release drugs.  
 
In order to successfully integrate a drug delivery system, it must be ensured that it is controlled and 
that the appropriate amount of dosage is released into the wound. There should be only one antibiotic 
included in the wound dressing that is effective against both S. aureus and MRSA. Due to its sim-
plicity, it will initially be easier to integrate into a smart wound dressing than two different antibiot-
ics. The formulation of the drug must also be considered since it must be absorbed by the wound in 
order to be effective. Since liquid dosage forms are known to be quickly absorbed, this may be an 
option.  
 
The abovementioned is an outlook of how this project can proceed in the future.
