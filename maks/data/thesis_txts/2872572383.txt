ABSTRACT
1
Abstract
This thesis examines the flow inside the sensor cassette of Radiometer’s ABL90 FLEX PLUS
Blood gas analyser. The flow is examined using Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) created
in the commercially available software STAR-CCM+, which is compared to the results seen
in experiments conducted at Radiometer. To ensure safety during testing, a blood substitute
called safePICO demo solution was used instead of real blood samples.
Some key fluid properties, namely contact angles, viscosity, surface tension and density, where
either measured or gathered otherwise to ensure accurate simulations of two use-cases: Filling
and cleaning the sensor cassette.
A preliminary experiment and simulation was conducted by filling the sensor cassette with
water.
Once an acceptable simulation was achieved, simulations and experiments of filling
the cassette with blood were conducted. Once adequate results were reached, the rinse cycle
became the focus, as this is where Radiometer sees most potential gain.
Due to long solution times, a shortened version of the sensor cassette was created and used for
the rest of the simulation. Differences between the simulation and the experimental data led to
the discovery of a discrepancy between the code used for the experiment and the actual process
being done during testing.
After adjustments, simulations more closely matched experimental behaviour and revealed the
presence of a thin fluid film around the air bubbles, which was confirmed to be present during
experiments too.
Finally, simulations were used to explore potential improvements in the cleaning cycle, and
three potential improvements were found.
Due to time constraints, the full rinse sequence
was not simulated, but the findings provide a valuable foundation for Radiometer’s continued
development and optimisation of the sensor cassette cleaning process.

INTRODUCTION
2
Introduction
Radiometer was founded in 1935, entering the growing radio industry in Denmark. Two years
later they were contacted by Carlsberg Laboratory to help develop a device to detect pH levels
in liquids. In 1952, using Radiometer’s pH meter, Poul Astrup and Bjørn Ibsen discovered a
way to improve diagnosis and treatment of patients with polio, by measuring the level of pH
in their blood. This discovery led to Radiometer entering the world of medical technology,
and in 1954 they released the worlds first commercially available blood gas analyser. Today,
Radiometer is a global medical device company that develops products that provide healthcare
professionals with acute care diagnostics solutions, which remain critical in hospitals all over
the world.[2]
One of their flagship devices is the ABL90 FLEX PLUS, also referred to as just ABL90, an
in vitro diagnostic, portable, automated analyser. It quantitatively measures pH, blood gases,
electrolytes, glucose, lactate and oximetry, in heparinized whole blood and neonatal bilirubin in
capillary, venous and arterial whole blood[1]. Currently, more than 30.000 units are worldwide.
The ABL90 measures up to 19 different parameters which are used for different diagnostics.
This versatility is what makes the ABL90 a critical device in hospitals all over the world. The
device works by receiving a blood sample and pumping it through a sensor cassette, which
has all the sensors needed for measuring the critical parameters. A cross section of the fluid
transport system can be seen in Figure 1 with an explanation of the different numbered sections
in Table 1.

2.1
Goal of the thesis
The goal of the thesis is to examine the flow in the ABL90 sensor cassette using Computational
Fluid Dynamics (CFD) simulations, which are verified by experiments. After identifying prob-
lems with the flow in the sensor cassette, simulations with proposed solutions are conducted to
attempt improvement of the flow.
2.2
Outline of the thesis
The thesis starts by explaining some background information on the sensor cassettes and liquids
used by the ABL90. Radiometer’s proposed cases are then described, before diving into the
theory used in the thesis. Some liquid properties were then tested, and implemented into the
simulations. The simulation setup and the experimental setup was described, before moving on
to the implementation of both. Three different types of simulations are run, before potential
improvements are investigated.

BACKGROUND
3
Background
To understand the problem and come up with a suitable improvement some general knowledge
of the product and its use is required.
3.1
Sensor cassette
The ABL90 uses a sensor cassette consisting of either 17 or 19 different sensors, which are
placed in rows on two boards. The flow in the sensor cassette is laminar. The blood sample
flows in a channel between these two boards and the sensors measure the contents of the blood
sample. The channel is then rinsed with a rinsing solution using the procedure described in
section 8.2. The sensor boards can be seen in Figure 2.
Figure 2: The sensor boards used in the sensor cassette
Currently, there are some challenges with the sensor cassette. Sometimes after use, the results
of the measurements deteriorates. There are different reasons for these degrading results. One
of the issues Radiometer has discovered, is the intake of silicon which occurs with the use of
certain syringes where the blood breaks down a silicon part in the syringe. The silicon is then
dragged into the sensor with the sample, where it can end up affecting the flow in such a way
that some of the sensors are blocked off when the blood has to flow around the silicon residue.
Another issue occurs when some leftover proteins from the blood sample remain inside the
sensor cassette. This interrupts the flow by blocking parts of the flow path, thereby redirecting
the flow. In some cases, this results in some of the sensors not being able to measure the sample.

3.2
Liquids
There are two different types of liquids in the solution pack as seen in Figure 1 and Table
1. There are three different Quality Control solutions (QC) and three different Calibration
solutions (CAL). CAL1, CAL2 and CAL3 solutions are used for calibrating the sensors and
contain known concentrations of the different parameters measured by the sensors. CAL1 is
also used as part of the rinse process. After a measurement is completed, the sample is removed,
the system is rinsed with a combination of air and other gases, as well as the CAL1 solution,
and finally the system is filled the CAL1 solution to prepare for the next sample.[1]
QC1, QC2 and QC3 are used for quality control, which is automatically done every 8 hours
rotating between the three quality control solutions, resulting in one QC measurement with
each solution being done daily.
Blood samples are inserted using the sample inlet (9 or 10 in Figure 1) and analysed in the
sensor cassette. Blood and CAL1 are the two liquids used during testing and simulation in this
project.

THE CASES
4
The cases
Radiometer wishes to investigate different cases of varying complexity. The cases are:
1. Clear cassette
2. One sensor
3. Multiple sensors
Case 1 is the simplest case. It involves a custom built sensor cassette where the two sensor
boards are substituted with smooth clear plastic boards with no sensors on them. This case
is used to verify the simulation using the experimental setup, as the simulations are relatively
quick with such a simple geometry.
Case 2 introduces a sensor to the board. This case is used to explore how long it takes for the
flow to stabilise after interacting with the sensor.
Case 3 introduces the rest of the sensors, and aims to simulate real life. All the sensors are
introduced to the geometry, and the real life case is simulated.
This project only focuses on Case 1 as the simulations revealed some underlying issues at this
stage.

5.1
Surface tension
A liquid will form an interface with another fluid. This interface is a result of the forces created
by molecules inside the liquid. In the middle of the liquid the molecules are tightly packed and
will repel each other, but at the surface the molecules are less tightly packed and will attract
each other. Since half of the neighbouring molecules of the surface molecules are missing, the
resulting forces will create surface tension.[11, pp. 27–28]

the interface.[11, pp. 27–28]

5.2
Wettability
The wettability of a surface is described as the ability of a liquid to maintain contact with the
solid surface. A surface with a high wettability is described as hydrophilic, and a surface with
a low wettability is described as hydrophobic. A hydrophilic surface will result in a low contact
angle, which will be explained further in section 5.3, as the intermolecular forces allow the
liquid to easily spread out over a surface. A hydrophobic surface will result in a high contact
angle as the intermolecular forces will try to contain the liquid in droplets, thus preventing it
from spreading. [4]
The wettability of a surface can also be described by the spreading coefficient S, which is
calculated by equation 6
S = γs −(γsl + γl)
(6)
where γs and γl are the surface tension of the solid and the liquid, respectively and γsl is the
interfacial tension of the solid/liquid interface. The spreading coefficient describes the wetting
of a surface. Complete wetting occurs when S > 0 and partial wetting occurs when S < 0 .[12]

5.3
Contact angles
The contact angle θ is the angle between a liquid surface and a solid surface, and it serves as
a measure for quantifying the wettability of a surface. The angle is measured by the tangent
to the liquid/vapour interface, and the tangent to the solid/liquid interface at the intersection
point of all three phases. The contact angle is important when looking at fluid flow in narrow
channels since the flow interacts with potentially different solids at the same time, which will
shape the flow. Since the contact angle depends on the combination of liquids and solids, its
important to collect data for all combinations used in flow. The contact angle describes the
wettability of a solid surface by a liquid.[4]
5.3.1
Young-Dupr´e
In 1805 Thomas Young described the trigonometric relationship between the forces acting on
a liquid drop in equilibrium on a solid surface and the contact angle as seen in equation 7
γs −γsl = γl cos θ
(7)
where γs and γl are the surface tension of the solid and the liquid, respectively, γsl is the
interfacial tension of the solid/liquid interface and θ is the contact angle. [8]
In 1869 Athanase Dupr´e defined the work of adhesion for two immiscible liquids in contact
based on Youngs work in equation 7. Dupr´es definition can be seen in equation 8
Wab = γa + γb −γab
(8)
where Wab is the work of adhesion, γa and γb are the surface tension of the two immiscible
liquids and γab is the interfacial tension of the two liquids. Equation 8 also applies to the work
of adhesion for a solid and a liquid in contact. However, when a or b is a solid, the surface
tension cannot be measured directly. The work of adhesion for a solid and liquid in contact is
described in equation 9

5.3.2
Measurement techniques
The sessile drop method is one of the primary methods for measuring the static contact angle.
The most common way of implementing the sessile drop method is where a tangent is placed
at the point of contact of the sessile drop profile with the solid surface. This tangent is placed
using an optical tensiometer or a contact angle goniometer which is connected to a computer
with image analysis software that analyses the image and calculates the angle.
This used
to be done manually using a telescope equipped with a goniometer eyepiece or by taking a
photograph of the drop profile and manually adding the tangent,[9] but both of these methods
have been mostly outdated since the software is more precise and reliable. This is the most
straightforward method of measuring the contact angle, and can be used to determine if there
are large differences in the solid surface by placing droplets at various locations. A disadvantage
of this method is the fact that the setup only allows the contact angle to be measured in one
plane or cross-section of the droplet and will not be able to detect if there are differences in
the contact angle along the circumference of the droplet. This method is also subjective and
depends on the skill and experience of the operator as well as the precision of the image analysis
software and the image quality which is being analysed.
The Wilhelmy plate method is another method used to measure the contact angle of a liquid
on a plate. For dynamic contact angles a plate is immersed into or withdrawn from a pool of
liquid with a controlled, known speed and the contact angle is calculated. The static contact
angle is calculated while the plate is kept stationary in the pool. The setup can be seen in
Figure 5
Figure 5: Wilhelmy plate method without viscous model[5]

a pool of liquid with surface tension γ and density ρ and using the gravitational acceleration
g the contact angle being measured θ can be calculated using the force balance described in
equation 15

5.4
Multiphase flow
A multiphase flow refers to the flow and interaction of multiple different phases within the
same system, where distinct interfaces exists between the phases. A phase usually refers to
either a solid, liquid or gas.[10] The flow in the sensor cassette is a multiphase flow consisting
of three different phases. The phases are blood, air and rinse liquid, which is called CAL1. The
ABL90 is programmed to pump these fluids through the sensor cassette in specific intervals, as
described in section 11.
5.4.1
Implementation in STAR-CCM+
When implementing a multiphase flow into STAR-CCM+, a phase refers to a quantity of matter
within a system that distinguishes itself from other phases in the system by its unique physical
properties. [10] This could be differences in density, bubbles of different sizes, different surface
tension and viscosity and so forth. STAR-CCM+ has eight different multiphase models that
can be implemented. Those models are:
• Dispersed Multiphase (DMP)
• Eulerian Multiphase (EMP)
• Fluid Film
• Mixture Multiphase (MMP)

• Two-Phase Thermodynamic Equillibrium (TPTE)
• Volume of Fluid (VOF) Multiphase
• Discrete Element Method (DEM)

These eight models have different strengths and applications. Some models, such as DMP and

flows such as the Fluid Film model which predicts dynamic characteristics of wall films using
boundary layer approximations and assumed velocity and temperature profiles across the film
depth[10].
5.4.2
Volume of Fluid
The simulations in this thesis is done using the Volume of Fluid (VOF) model. This model is
used for systems containing two or more immiscible fluids which all form large structures in the
system. This model focuses on the movement of the interfaces of the fluids, and is a popular
choice in marine applications.[10] An example of the suitability of VOF application can be seen
in Figure 6

This model is suited for simulations with phases in large structures with a relatively small total
contact area between the phases due to its numerical efficiency. [10]

5.5
Adaptive mesh refinement
An adaptive mesh refinement (AMR) is used in simulations where sensitive regions change
dynamically during the simulation time using mid-point subdivision. The adaptive mesh is
given an adaptive mesh criteria with a max refinement level, which is the limit on the amount
of times a cell can be refined before the simulation moves on. The adaptive mesh criteria used
in this project is the Free Surface Mesh Refinement (FSMR). To make sure the interface stays
sharp, the FSMR criterion does not refine cells that already contain the interface, but instead
attempts to identify cells downstream where the interface will end up, and refines these cells
instead to ensure a high mesh resolution when the interface moves to these cells.
The simplified version of how the algorithm works is as follows:
• Identify which cells contain the interface.
• Use the flow field to predict where the interface will be next time the mesh is refined.
• Extend the predicted cells by a safety layer.
• Check if predicted cells are at the current interface or not. Refine cells that are not at
the current interface, and keep those that are.

the time step or the delta time.[10]

7.3
Multiphase flow
The simulations were done using multiphase flow and Volume of Fluid (VOF).
Three different phases were defined and implemented in STAR-CCM+. The three different
phases are:
• Air
• Blood
• Rinse liquid
These phases were defined using their density and viscosity. The phases of the flow were then
defined in Volume of Fluid by field functions. These field functions were defined as binary
functions of time, based on the code from the ABL90 software seen in Appendix A. The inlet
was set to have a Volume Fraction, which is a composite of the field functions describing the
flow.
Multiphase interaction is defined by selecting a primary phase, a secondary phase and defining
the surface tension of the primary phase in the secondary phase. Other values can be tweaked
to improve accuracy, but are kept at the standard values for this project.
7.4
Contact angles
To ensure an accurate simulation, the contact angle between the different liquids used and the
different materials they interact with are implemented in the simulation. For an interaction
between phases, one fluid is set as the primary phase and the other as the secondary phase.
A contact angle smaller than 90° means that the primary phase is wetting the sensor cassette
wall.[10]

7.5
Adaptive mesh refinement
A free surface mesh refinement was implemented on the simulations. No prism cell refinement
is applied. The initial mesh is trimmed and the transitional width is set to 1. The face validity
is checked with a minimum face validity of 0.9 to ensure high cell quality.
Face validity describes the quality of a cell by creating an area weighted measure of the face
normals relative to their cell centroid. If the face normals are pointing outwards the quality of
the cell is high. If one or more of the cell normals point towards the cell, the quality is low.[10]
An example of a high quality and a low quality cell can be seen in Figure 12.
Figure 12: Examples of a good and a bad cell as described by face validity[10]
The further below 1.0 the face validity is, the worse the quality of the cell is. This is why a
minimum face validity is set to 0.9.

Adaptive time step
An adaptive time step was also used in the simulations. As described in section 5.6, the adaptive
time step works by calculating the time step as shown in equation 21 using a user-specified
Courant number C, which for this project is defined as C = 0.4.

CONCLUSION
13
Conclusion
This project explored the flow inside a sensor cassette used in the ABL90 blood gas analyser.
The two fluids primarily used by the ABL90 is the CAL1 calibration solution and rinse liquid, as
well as blood samples from patients at hospitals. Since working with blood requires following
a strict protocol, to prevent blood borne diseases from spreading, a blood substitute called
safePICO demo solution was used instead. The contact angle of the rinse liquid and safePICO
was measured on the interfaces they interact with inside the sensor cassette. The viscosity
of the rinse liquid was unknown and had to be measured. The measured contact angles and
viscosity was entered into simulations, along with the known quantities of both liquids, such as
surface tension and density, and the viscosity of blood.
Two different scenarios were simulated: Filling the sensor cassette, and cleaning the sensor
cassette. The filling simulations were done for water first, while the safePICO demo solution
was being acquired, and validated by an experiment. The simulation was then done for filling
the cassette with blood, which gave a good enough result to focus on the cleaning of the sensor
cassette. The cleaning is where Radiometer has been having some problems, so they wanted to
focus on this part of the use-cycle.
When simulating the rinse cycle of the sensor cassette, solution time exploded due to the many
different interfaces inside the flow. To reduce solution time, a shortened version of the sensor
cassette was introduced and used for the rest of the project. The initial part of the simulation
was examined, and the results were behaving much differently than expected. These results
were compared to the experimental data, and it turned out that the code provided to build
the rinse sequence did not correspond with what was happening in the experiments. The air
segments were too long, likely due to the hardware restrictions not being able to swap between
fluids quickly enough, and there was an extra segment of both air and rinse solution at the
beginning of the sequence, which was unaccounted for.
The simulations were tweaked to mirror the experiments, and the resulting simulations were
much better. The simulations revealed a small fluid film encompassing the air bubbles, which
were confirmed to be there in the experiments as well.
New simulations were created to try and minimise this fluid film, and three possible improve-
ments were discovered:
1. A simulation slowing down the velocity of the fluids at the inlet.
2. A simulation changing the shape of the inlet from being a sharp corner, to a gradually
increasing inlet diameter.
3. A simulation combining the other two improvement points
All three of these simulation showed promise, but due to the long simulation time, the simula-
tions were not finished in time for this thesis. However, Radiometer will be able to take these
findings and test the changes with further simulations and experiments.
