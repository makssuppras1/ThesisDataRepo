Abstract
Operational faults represent an important source of reduced indoor environmental qual­
ity and unnecessary energy use in residential buildings. This study investigates these
effects in two single­story houses by combining building simulation, indoor climate mea­
surements, and supervised machine learning. Building models were developed in IDA
ICE to represent four home office and hobby rooms, and three common operational faults
were introduced: low setpoints, high setpoints, and unintended window openings during
the Danish heating season. Their impacts on thermal comfort and heating demand were
quantified and used to train Random Forest and Extreme Gradient Boosting classifiers.
The models accurately detected setpoint­related faults in simulated data, whereas the
detection of short window­opening events was less reliable and strongly dependent on
the availability of control signal data. When applied to measured data, model perfor­
mance declined due to sensor noise, seasonal variations, and differences between build­
ings, resulting in limited generalization across houses. Severity analysis showed that low
setpoints caused substantial degradation of thermal comfort, reflected in both estimated
comfort categories and recorded occupant votes, while providing only minor energy sav­
ings. Unintended window openings resulted in significant losses in both comfort and en­
ergy performance, whereas high setpoints had the smallest impact on both comfort and
energy­saving potential. Occupant feedback further supported the finding that reductions
in indoor temperature lead to disproportionately larger comfort penalties.
The findings demonstrate both the potential and current limitations of using simulated data
and machine learning for fault detection in residential buildings, highlighting the impor­
tance of sensor availability, occupant behavior, and room­specific thermal characteristics
when applying data­driven methods in practice.

1
Introduction
Buildings account for 32 % of global energy consumption [1], with heating, ventilation,
and air conditioning systems responsible for approximately 38 % of the energy used in
the building sector [2]. This indicates a substantial potential for energy optimization, sup­
ported by growing financial and regulatory incentives for improved building performance
[3]. Approaches such as commissioning, fault detection and diagnostics, and advanced
control algorithms have demonstrated the ability to reduce energy use and operating costs
while improving occupant comfort, indoor environmental quality, and the longevity of build­
ing systems.
Most existing research and applications within fault detection and diagnostics focus on
large commercial buildings, where comprehensive management systems provide exten­
sive data for analysis. Residential buildings, in contrast, typically have fewer sensors,
simpler control strategies, and limited data availability, which makes data driven fault de­
tection considerably more challenging and far less explored. Addressing this gap is in­
creasingly relevant as residential buildings constitute a major share of the building stock
and are gradually adopting more automated heating solutions.
This project investigated occupant related operational faults in residential buildings using
sensor data collected through the RePUP project by Climify ApS. Simulated data were
used to train fault detection models and to quantify the impact of these faults. The mod­
els were subsequently evaluated on real sensor measurements to assess their ability to
generalize. The study also examines how a model trained on one zone performs when
applied to other zones within the same building and to comparable rooms across different
houses.
Two single story residential houses in Høje Tåstrup Municipality in Denmark, both without
mechanical ventilation, were analyzed. Simulations were used to quantify the influence of
specific operational faults on thermal comfort and heating energy use. Two machine learn­
ing algorithms were trained on labeled simulated data to establish baseline performance
before being applied to the unlabeled sensor data. Based on the initial thermal comfort
assessment, the potential improvements achievable through fault detection were evalu­
ated, and several challenges related to generalizing training strategies across residential
buildings were identified. The potential improvements in thermal comfort are further com­
pared with collected occupant votes to assess their alignment with expected values and
the severity of the faults.
1.1
RePUP
The Real time Performance Upgrade of Buildings (RePUP) project is a research initiative
focused on advancing sustainable building operation through data driven, real time op­
timization. The project investigates how buildings can actively adapt their performance
by continuously monitoring indoor environmental conditions, outdoor climate, and system
operation. By considering buildings as responsive systems rather than static structures,
RePUP aims to improve energy efficiency, enhance occupant comfort, and reduce envi­
ronmental impact.
Data for RePUP are collected through collaborations with Climify ApS, Panasonic, and
Velux under data processing agreements with DTU Sustain. Climify provides detailed

1.2
Literature study
A study on the operational conditions of air­conditioning units in residential and commer­
cial buildings in California indicated that faults were present in 65 % of the units [5]. An­
other study estimated that reductions of 16 % and 13 % in whole­building energy con­
sumption for existing and new buildings, respectively, can be achieved through building
commissioning [6]. A separate study reported that applying FDD can yield energy savings
of 5–30 % relative to baseline consumption without compromising thermal comfort [7].
Due to climate change, a significant increase in the demand for air conditioning (AC) is pro­
jected across Europe, where mechanical AC systems have historically seen limited use.
Additionally, regions such as Asia and North America are expected to experience further
growth in AC use within their existing building stocks, where AC is already widespread
[8]. In this context, ensuring energy­efficient building operation by reducing faulty system
behavior becomes increasingly important. Effective fault detection and diagnostics (FDD)
can play a central role in achieving this, contributing to both improved indoor environmen­
tal quality (IEQ) and reduced energy consumption.
The literature identifies a range of faults commonly occurring in commercial buildings,
primarily related to the operation of the technical systems. These faults have been cat­
egorized into four areas: Hardware, Software, Human factor and Unspecified, with the
largest share within software with 31.3 % with faults in programming. 21.4 % are coined
to human factors with operator interference being the dominant cause[9].
In a case study conducted at the University of Colorado Boulder, addressing control is­
sues and programming­related faults in a central cooling system without storage tanks
resulted in a 25 % reduction in energy consumption over the two­year period from 1994
to 1996. The study investigated the integration of computational models into the build­
ing automation system, enabling dynamically optimized setpoints and valve positions that
better matched the cooling demand compared to the conservative estimates originally
implemented. Furthermore, the control strategy allowed the cooling system to operate
without storage, making it feasible for implementation in older systems [10].
Within the field of applying machine learning (ML) to FDD, most research has focused on
large­scale HVAC systems in commercial buildings, with some attention given to multi­
story residential buildings, and small residential buildings being outside their relevant
scopes.
1.2.1
Machine learning modeling
To differentiate between various data modeling approaches in fault detection and diagnos­
tics, the literature commonly classifies models into three simplified categories. White­box
2

==PAGE:11==
models, which are digital or physics­based, are well suited for simple rule­based model­
ing of equipment with few sensors. Black­box models, which are data­driven, machine
learning, or empirical, are effective for equipment with many sensors. Grey­box models,
which are hybrid or semi­empirical, combine features of both approaches [7].
In practical implementation, white­box models are often rule­based and require detailed
expert knowledge of system behavior and operational dynamics.
Black­box models have the potential to detect not only major system failures but also minor
faults that may accumulate and degrade performance over time. Their effectiveness is
highly dependent on the quality, quantity, and representativeness of the training data as
well as the required computational resources, which directly influence model accuracy
and reliability.
Grey­box models share similar data dependencies but offer the added advantage of com­
bining physical insights with data­driven methods, potentially reducing computational time
while maintaining interpretability and accuracy [5]. Grey­box models are widely used in
the literature, while experiments with black­box models are increasingly feasible as com­
putational power grows.
Although much of the literature emphasizes the theoretical potential of these methods,
often through benchmarking against similar buildings or comparing simulated and mea­
sured energy usage, real­time implementation remains limited.
1.3
Machine learning
Two distinct approaches are used in machine learning: supervised and unsupervised,
each highlighting strengths for different tasks.
Unsupervised learning models are often associated with large datasets and situations
where there is little or no prior understanding of the data. In unsupervised learning, three
main tasks are typically performed: clustering, association rule learning, and dimension­
ality reduction. These techniques enable the analysis of large datasets for purposes such
as anomaly detection, improving energy efficiency, and identifying system faults. Several
studies, such as those presented in [11], have applied these methods to chillers, heating
systems, and ventilation systems with the goal of supporting building service and mainte­
nance teams.
Supervised learning models are trained using established labeled data, which provides
the desired output for specific combinations of inputs. This allows the model to learn the
relationship between input variables and their corresponding output labels. Once trained,
the model can generalize this pattern to make predictions on unseen and unlabeled data.
Supervised learning models are commonly categorized into two approaches: regression
and classification, with the latter being used in this project.
Within classification models, a further subdivision can be made depending on the number
and specificity of the output labels. The desired output labels, also known as classes, de­
fine the structure of the classification task. Based on these specifications, classifications
can be grouped as follows:
Binary classification: Assigns data instances to one of two categories, typically repre­
senting normal and abnormal states. This approach is widely used in anomaly detection,
for example, to determine whether a system is operating as intended or experiencing a
fault.

Multiclass classification: Assigns data instances to one of multiple mutually exclusive
categories. Each instance belongs to exactly one class with no overlap. This method
is useful for distinguishing between specific fault types, such as sensor failures, control
malfunctions, or maintenance issues.
Multi label classification: Assigns each data instance to multiple categories simulta­
neously. A single instance may belong to more than one class at the same time. For
example, a system may experience multiple faults concurrently, such as combined air­
flow and temperature regulation failures, which can lead to reduced comfort, increased
energy use, or both.
As presented in [12], fault severity at the whole­building level for a mosque was detected
and classified using various algorithms. Among these, Random Forest (RF) and Extreme
Gradient Boosting (XGB) demonstrated superior accuracy, computational efficiency, sta­
bility with noisy datasets, and recall, resulting in overall reliability higher than the other
models investigated. Based on their reliability and proof of concept, these two models
have been selected for use in this project.
Random Forest (RF) is an ensemble learning algorithm designed to perform both clas­
sification and regression tasks by aggregating the results of multiple decision trees. It
employs a non­linear data mining framework, allowing the simultaneous use of classifica­
tion and regression methods.
In the RF procedure, each tree is constructed from a bootstrap sample of the original
dataset, introducing variability and reducing the risk of overfitting. At each node split, the
algorithm considers only a randomly selected subset of features rather than the full set
of predictors. This random feature selection reduces inter­tree correlation and enhances
the generalization capability of the ensemble [13].
Extreme Gradient Boosting (XGB) is an ensemble machine learning algorithm that ex­
tends the gradient boosting framework by improving computational efficiency and incor­
porating enhanced model regularization. Like traditional gradient boosting, XGB builds
an additive model in a forward stage­wise manner, where each tree is trained to correct
the residual errors of its predecessors.
In this approach, decision trees serve as base learners, and their predictive performance is
iteratively optimized using gradient descent on a specified loss function. Unlike standard
gradient boosting, XGB introduces additional regularization terms that penalize model
complexity. This helps mitigate overfitting and produces a simpler yet effective predictive
model while maintaining strong generalization performance across diverse datasets [14].

Simulated data
Each building was modeled in IDA ICE version 5.1, with the primary building elements
summarized in Table 2.1. The two buildings employ different heating systems. House 1 is
equipped with two floor­heating loops and water­based radiators, House 2 likewise utilizes
both floor heating and water­based radiators, in addition to a fireplace. The fireplace,
however, was excluded as an active heating source in the simulations, as occupants
reported that it was primarily used for aesthetic and comfort purposes rather than for
space heating.
As indicated by the sensor data, the heating setpoint was estimated at 21.5 °C.

are expected during this period. To account for this, an additional schedule was imple­

A site visit to both houses was conducted on October 8, 2025, to verify the accuracy
of the floor plans and confirm the location and type of heating systems implemented in
the models. During the visit, interviews were carried out with the occupants to gather
information on behavioral factors such as shading control and internal door usage. Based

To simplify the modeling process, both houses were represented as single level buildings.
Although the actual houses have a ground level difference of approximately 40 cm, this
elevation offset was neglected. The total room volumes were still preserved to maintain
geometric and thermal accuracy by increasing the ceiling height in the lower section so
that it matched the true ceiling height.
For Zones E and J, the floor heating control was simplified by linking the heating directly
to the zone level sensors. In the real buildings, these zones share a common heating
loop that is controlled by the return water temperature. For the simulation, however, the
zones were decoupled to enable independent single zone operation, which improves the
applicability and interpretability of the model. The four selected rooms all serve similar
functions as office or hobby spaces, although they differ in their geometric and thermal
characteristics.
Zone E: This zone is a 16.5 m² single­level room extending from floor to roof, with two
windows on the south façade. The zone is heated via floor heating and is connected to
an adjacent entrance/hallway. External obstructions, such as outdoor furnishings, have
been represented as shading elements, while nearby vegetation has been modeled as
vertical shading devices with 50 % solar transmittance.
Zone J: This zone is a 16.5 m² single­level room extending from floor to roof, with one
window on the north façade and an operable skylight. The zone is heated via floor heating
and is connected to an adjacent entrance/hallway. No external obstructions have been
included in the model.
Zone H: This zone is a 19.8 m² room with a ceiling height of 2.35 m. It was previously
two separate rooms, with one heating device removed during renovation, and features
two windows on the west façade. Heating is provided by a single radiator, and the zone
is connected to a corridor. No external obstructions have been included in the model.
Zone K: This zone is a 6.5 m² room with a ceiling height of 2.35 m and one window on the
south façade. The zone has no direct heating source but is connected through an open

2.4
Fault implementation and features
Three distinct operational faults were implemented: low setpoint, high setpoint, and win­
dow left open. To implement these faults, a heating macro was used to control the water
radiators and floor heating, while an opening schedule was applied to the window con­
currently with its standard operation. Each fault was individually present with no overlap
to simplify fault detection and focus on multiclass detection.
The heating control macro consists of a thermostat with a dead band of 2 ◦C. The setpoint
was normally controlled by a lower temperature limit of 21.5 ◦C, which was then adjusted
according to selected dates and times for the faults, as presented in table E.2. For the
low setpoint fault, the temperature was reduced to 7 ◦C during the specified period, corre­
sponding to the ”frost safe” setting on a standard thermostat. For the high setpoint fault,
the temperature was raised to 26 ◦C during the specified period, corresponding to the
”fully open” setting on a thermostat. Concurrently, the upper temperature limit was in­
creased from 25 ◦C to 27 ◦C to prevent the window from opening while the temperature
was still rising. Outside the heating season, a multiplier and a schedule, with the heating
season defined from May 15 to October 1, set the heating point to 0 ◦C, effectively turning
off the heating. The thermostat reacts to the mean air temperature in the zone where the
heating device was connected for simplicity.
The third fault involves the window opening while heating was active, which lead to re­
duced indoor temperatures and increased energy consumption. Under normal conditions,
the window operates according to its standard control sequence, but during the fault a
schedule override forces a maximum opening signal at predefined dates and times. This
results in the window opening to 20 % of its maximum area, causing a notable drop in
mean air temperature and indoor CO2 concentration while the heating system continues
to operate.
The control macro from IDA ICE is shown in Figure 2.11, where the purple components
represent the modifications applied to generate the faulty window behavior. These ele­
ments override the normal window­control logic by replacing the original signal strength
with a full­strength opening signal whenever the fault is active.
To ensure the high­setpoint fault operates as intended, without increased window open­
ings, the maximum temperature limit was increased by an offset of 2 °C, resulting in an
opening threshold of 27.5 °C half a degree above the heating setpoint of 27 °C.
As shown in the window control schedule 2.11, the strongest signal, either high tem­
perature or elevated CO2 concentration determines the window opening level. During
nighttime hours, the signal strength was reduced by half, while in summer the opening
temperature setpoint was lowered to encourage increased natural ventilation.

2.4.1
Hyperparameter Tuning and Optimization
The fault detection strategy prioritizes minimizing false positives rather than maximizing
recall. This approach ensures higher confidence in detected faults, thereby supporting
the reliability and trustworthiness of the system, particularly important when occupants
are expected to manually respond to and resolve detected issues. However, this strategy
may lead to an increased number of false negatives. In this context, the potential impact
of missed detections is considered less critical than the consequences of false positives.
The sensor data contain more noise than the simulated data, requiring a relaxation of the
fault detection criteria to account for measurement uncertainty. In addition, the sensor
data exhibit stronger seasonal variations due to external weather conditions and occu­
pant behavior. Since such effects were less pronounced in the simulated data, careful
consideration was necessary to differentiate genuine faults from normal seasonal varia­
tions.
The models were configured using the hyperparameters and post­processing threshold
adjustments described in table 2.4 for RF and table 2.5 for XGB respectively, taking these
considerations into account. The four operating conditions were encoded as follows: 0 =
Fault­free, 1 = Low setpoint, 2 = High setpoint, and 3 = Window opening.

Discussion
The primary aim of this study was to assess the potential of machine­learning­based fault
detection using both simulated and measured data, and to evaluate the impact of com­
mon operational faults on indoor thermal comfort and zone­level energy use in residential
buildings.
By combining detailed building simulations, indoor climate measurements, and super­
vised learning models, the study quantified the energy implications of faults, such as low
and high setpoints or unintended window openings, and assessed their effects on occu­
pant comfort.
The Random Forest (RF) model detected a larger number of faults but showed lower pre­
cision due to more false positives, suggesting it may overestimate fault occurrences in
practice. In contrast, the Xtreme Gradient Boosting (XGB) model was more conservative,
detecting fewer faults while maintaining high precision, providing a more reliable identifi­
cation of critical deviations. These results highlight the trade­off between sensitivity and
reliability and emphasize the need for careful calibration when applying fault detection
algorithms in residential settings with variable occupancy patterns and control behaviors.
For this project, fault detection outputs are intended to be communicated through a build­
ing surveillance system, such as a mounted display or a mobile application. To ensure
successful implementation, it is essential to prioritize high precision over recall. Incorrectly
flagged faults may prompt unnecessary responses, undermine occupants’ confidence in
the system, and ultimately reduce its overall effectiveness.
Detecting low­setpoint faults remains particularly critical, even though such events may
temporarily reduce heating energy. Willingness­to­pay studies in office buildings, examin­
ing the benefits of raising temperatures from 19 °C to 21 °C, indicate that occupants value
comfort far more than potential energy savings. Applied to residential settings, failing to
detect low­setpoint conditions could result in discomfort that outweighs minor reductions in
heating energy. Analysis of measured data confirms that addressing low­setpoint events
improves thermal comfort while maintaining energy efficiency. Occupant questionnaire
responses further show a consistent tendency to rate slightly “too cold” conditions as less
acceptable, reinforcing the importance of this fault type both for practical detection and
for weighting in machine learning models.
Feature analysis revealed that combining climate sensor measurements, control signals,
and derived features substantially improved detection capability. In particular, including
valve position data for heating control alongside current indoor air temperature and its
rolling mean was critical. This combination not only enhances the identification of true
faults with higher recall but also helps distinguish whether deviations arise from low­ or
high­setpoint faults, window openings, or other factors such as solar gains or internal heat
gains.
Assessing potential energy savings based on the Zone E results (Table 3.13) and scaling
them to the full building yielded estimates between 4.5 % and 15.4 %. Compared with
literature estimates for HVAC faults in mechanically ventilated buildings (5–30 %) [7, 23],
these values lie in the lower half of the reported range, reflecting fundamental differences
in building systems and ventilation strategies.
Modeling of building operational faults for improved IEQ and energy efficiency
49
==PAGE:58==
In the present study, the implemented faults are inherently zone­bound, affecting only local
heating control within individual rooms. In contrast, many faults reported in the literature
occur in centralized HVAC systems, such as air­handling units or cooling plants, where
a single fault can influence the thermal performance of the entire building. As a result,
the associated energy impacts in centrally ventilated systems are expected to be more
severe.
Furthermore, scaling results from a single zone to the entire building introduces additional
uncertainty. In Building 1, for example, several zones such as halls and entrance areas
were not equipped with dedicated heating systems and instead relied on heat transfer from
adjacent zones. This limits the applicability of directly extrapolating zone­level energy
savings to the whole building, as the origin and distribution of the delivered heat cannot
be clearly attributed to individual zones.
Applying a model trained on Zone E in Building 1 to a corresponding zone in Building 2
resulted in a substantial decline in performance, highlighting the importance of training
datasets that closely reflect the target building’s occupancy patterns, heating configu­
ration, and operational behavior. Differences in user behavior, control strategies, and
system characteristics across buildings therefore limit the transferability of fault detection
models without additional calibration or retraining.
These findings are particularly relevant for the RePUP project, which aims to advance
sustainable building operation through data­driven, real­time optimization. By demon­
strating that machine­learning­based fault detection can simultaneously improve thermal
comfort and reduce energy use, this study contributes directly to RePUP’s goal of enabling
buildings to adapt their performance dynamically in response to indoor environmental con­
ditions and system operation. The remaining uncertainties and practical considerations
associated with deploying these methods in residential buildings are discussed in the fol­
lowing sections.
4.1
Influence of Simulation Assumptions on Feature
Behavior
One major challenge in detailed indoor climate simulation is achieving an appropriate
balance between model fidelity and realistic assumptions about building use. The required
level of detail should be guided by the study objective, whether the aim is to closely match
measured time series, to reproduce critical peak conditions, or to comply with established
industry standards. In this study, a combination of industry­standard assumptions and
case­specific adaptations was applied to better represent residential occupancy patterns
and control behavior in the investigated dwellings.
The simulation setpoint was derived from measured data, which may not be fully rep­
resentative, as undetected or unlabeled faults could already be present in the sensor
measurements. Consequently, these faults may have influenced the simulation inputs,
particularly the assumed heating setpoint of 21.5 °C. The simulated dataset was there­
fore developed primarily to support the training of models for detecting setpoint­related
faults, and this inherent uncertainty should be considered when interpreting the results.
In addition to temperature, both relative humidity and CO2 concentrations exhibited larger
oscillations in the simulations than in the measured data, resulting in systematic discrep­
ancies between simulated and observed behavior. Despite these differences, these fea­
tures were assigned relatively low importance in the models, as their overall dynamics fol­
lowed physically plausible and expected patterns. Specifically, colder outdoor air, when
50

==PAGE:59==
heated indoors, resulted in lower relative humidity, and rapid decreases in indoor temper­
ature were typically accompanied by corresponding reductions in CO2 concentrations with
similar temporal scaling. Such patterns are indicative of natural ventilation and heating
behavior.
During summer periods, increased window opening in the simulations further contributed
to lower indoor relative humidity levels. These tendencies are illustrated in Figures G.3
and G.4. While the influence of relative humidity and CO2 concentrations could be inves­
tigated further using more detailed modeling approaches, the simplified representation
adopted in this study provides a reasonable balance between model fidelity and the re­
production of expected physical behavior.
In addition to the physical differences between the simulated and measured environments,
internal heat gains were represented using a repeating occupancy schedule with an as­
sumed equipment load of 100 W per zone corresponding to a laptop, two screen, a work­
ing lamp and a tablet or phone. This simplified approach was adopted to establish a
consistent baseline for internal heat gains; however, it does not capture short­term peaks
associated with multiple occupants, pet activity, or intermittent equipment use such as
vacuum cleaners. While these transient gains were not explicitly modeled, the repeating
schedule was intended to reflect the typical operational pattern of the zone rather than
day­to­day variability.
Consequently, the simulated data exhibit more stable usage patterns than the measured
data, which may include extended periods of low or no occupancy. The impact of this sim­
plification is considered limited because a consistent, normalized heating­demand repre­
sentation was applied across both datasets. In contrast, the true energy usage could
have exhibited a larger discrepancy between the simulated and measured data, poten­
tially leading to reduced precision.
4.2
Limitations of Cross­Building Fault Detection
When the model trained on Building 1, Zone E was applied to data from Building 2, a sub­
stantial increase in detected faults was observed. Approximately 290 hours were flagged
between 6 April and 18 April, corresponding to a period when the dwelling seemed un­
occupied, likely due to a holiday trip, and the heating setpoint was intentionally reduced.
Excluding this period, the fault markings in Appendix C reveal a clear tendency for detec­
tions to cluster at the lower temperature range.
To evaluate whether the model’s assumptions align with Building 2, the heating setpoint
was estimated using the same method applied to Building 1, excluding the unoccupied
period in April. This analysis yielded an average setpoint of 20 °C, suggesting that a model
specifically trained for Building 2 would likely benefit from adopting this lower setpoint,
thereby better capturing the building’s characteristic temperature behavior.
For the evaluation on Building 2, the Random Forest (RF) model struggled to produce
meaningful results, completely missing fault type 1, low­setpoint, when valve position
was unavailable and fault type 2, high­setpoint, when valve position was included. This is
likely due to the high decision thresholds used to minimize false positives, combined with
differences in thermal behavior between the two buildings. Building 1 employs floor heat­
ing with high thermal mass, whereas Building 2 relies on water­based radiators serving
multiple zones. These radiators respond more quickly to control inputs, creating distinct
thermal dynamics that limit the RF model’s ability to generalize across buildings.
Additionally, to compensate for the lower temperatures observed in some zones of Build­

==PAGE:60==
ing 2, the heating system may have overcompensated due to internal air exchange be­
tween spaces, which quickly adjusted the valve position to meet the heating demand.
This behavior could have contributed to the excessive number of high­setpoint detections
produced by the XGB model in Zones H and K.
Furthermore, the temperature control behavior in the training data from Zone E placed
high importance on the valve­position feature. Since valve position was unavailable in
Building 2, this likely introduced a bias in the estimation of high­setpoint faults. The model
may have misinterpreted normal temperature recovery, such as following open­window or
low­setpoint periods, as high­setpoint behavior, particularly when temperatures returned
rapidly to comfort Category 1.
The results suggest that practical implementation is most effective when a dedicated train­
ing zone is used for systems with similar heating configurations. The current approach
exhibits clear limitations when applied to zones with different heating setpoints, as demon­
strated by the sensor data from Building 2.
An investigation into the use of derived features decoupled from raw measurements could
potentially improve transferability. Greater emphasis could be placed on features such
as the running mean indoor temperature or other engineered features that respond to
changes rather than absolute conditions, potentially bypassing differences in setpoints
and building­specific heating system characteristics.
Furthermore, the narrow thresholds used during training in Zone E pose challenges for the
RF model, particularly in detecting minority fault categories. In contrast, the XGB model
relies heavily on the valve­position feature, which complicates cross­system comparisons
when identifying abnormal behavior, such as high­setpoint faults.
Using a more balanced threshold is expected to improve generalization across different
zones, although it would likely increase the number of false positives.
4.3
Residential Comfort Perception and Setpoint
Assumptions
For the thermal comfort assessment, comfort categories defined in EN 16798­1 were ap­
plied. The study assumed that the temperature ranges specified for office buildings could
be reasonably transferred to residential environments. However, in dwellings, thermal
comfort is more strongly influenced by individual preferences and the greater scope for
behavioral adaptation, rather than differences in perception itself. In residential settings,
comfort conditions are often determined by one or two occupants, meaning that prefer­
ences for warmer or cooler temperatures can have a pronounced impact on perceived
comfort. Previous studies indicate that residents tend to accept a wider range of indoor
temperatures, often slightly cooler conditions, partly because they directly pay for heating
and therefore tolerate lower temperatures than is typical in office environments [22]. In
contrast, office buildings involve larger occupant groups, where comfort assessments rely
more heavily on aggregated responses, reducing the influence of individual preferences
on overall comfort evaluations.
In Building 2, indoor temperatures were generally lower than in Building 1, indicating a
lower preferred heating setpoint while still falling within the “just acceptable” to “accept­
able” comfort range reported by occupants. For the simulated testing, a uniform setpoint
of 21.5 °C was applied across both buildings. While this simplification facilitated the evalu­
ation of fault detection performance, it may have led to overestimated precision and recall
compared with measured data reflecting building­specific setpoints. The lower preferred
52

==PAGE:61==
temperature in Building 2 suggests that residential comfort preferences may fall below the
lower bounds of Category 1. Consequently, assigning comfort scores based on higher
assumed setpoints can bias assessments toward increased heating demand relative to
actual occupant preferences, potentially leading to unnecessary heating even when com­
fort is already achieved.
Across both buildings, conditions perceived as too cold consistently received lower accep­
tance ratings than those perceived as too warm, even for relatively small deviations. This
indicates a greater sensitivity to cold discomfort and aligns with the notion that heating
setpoints are typically positioned toward the lower end of the comfort band. These obser­
vations highlight the importance of preventing low­setpoint faults to maintain acceptable
thermal comfort.
4.4
Limitations of the Study
Both the RF and XGB models assigned high importance to the valve­position signal, which
facilitated fault detection when unusual control behavior occurred under the current zone
conditions. Unfortunately, this feature was not available in the sensor dataset used for
the project.
Another feature with high importance, the whole­building water heating energy, excluding
domestic hot water, was also unavailable. This feature had been considered crucial for
ensuring that the models focused on the heating season and did not incorrectly infer faults
during summer months.
Because this information was missing, a simulated, normalized fault­free water heating
energy was used instead. Consequently, discrepancies between the simulated energy
data and the actual indoor conditions recorded by the sensors were expected. These
inconsistencies may have caused the models to follow incorrect decision paths, reducing
the accuracy of fault detection and diagnosis when applied to the real sensor data.
In the zone­level modeling, heating control was simplified by assuming that each zone
was regulated independently by its own sensor. In the actual building, however, multiple
zones were connected to a shared floor­heating loop controlled based on the return water
temperature. This configuration limited the ability to control each zone individually. Even if
one zone reached its desired temperature, another zone connected to the same loop could
still require heating. Consequently, the system increased the flow through the shared loop,
which could cause overheating in zones that would otherwise not require additional heat.
This resulted in apparent setpoint deviations, as the influence of return water temperature
and shared control signals was not accounted for in the simplified model.
The performance of the RF and XGB models indicated that both were capable of reli­
ably detecting excessive open­window faults. However, only the XGB model successfully
identified low­setpoint faults when applied to data from a separate building, whereas both
models tended to overestimate the occurrence of high­setpoint faults, with the RF model
performing slightly better in this regard. A combined testing strategy, using the XGB model
to detect low­setpoint and open­window faults and the RF model for high­setpoint faults,
could therefore be explored. Such an approach might reduce false positives while main­
taining robust fault detection. Using the XGB model for window fault detection would likely
result in fewer detected window­opening events in both the original building and the exter­
nal test case. Nevertheless, the individual test results indicated that the models needed
to clearly distinguish between low­setpoint and open­window faults to achieve improved
combined performance. In the simulated data, the XGB model demonstrated superior
recall and precision in making this distinction.

4.5
Perspectives on Advancing Data­Driven FDD in
Residential Buildings
Future perspectives for fault detection and diagnosis (FDD) of operational faults in resi­
dential buildings can be grouped into three main areas: integration and response, cross­
building transferability, and model fidelity with labeled measured data.
First, the machine­learning­based FDD framework investigated in this study represents
only one component of a broader operational solution. To achieve practical impact, de­
tected faults must be effectively integrated into building operation and occupant interac­
tion workflows. Future research should therefore examine how occupants perceive and
respond to automated fault notifications, including their willingness to trust and act upon
such information. Depending on detection reliability and fault severity, different communi­
cation strategies may be appropriate, for example, real­time alerts through user interfaces,
periodic summaries, or fault information that is only displayed when occupants actively
engage with building control systems.
Second, the results of this study highlight significant challenges associated with transfer­
ring FDD models across buildings. Performance degradation was observed when models
trained on one building were applied to another with different heating systems, control dy­
namics, and preferred setpoints. This suggests that effective deployment of data­driven
FDD may require training data that closely matches the target building, or alternatively,
more robust feature engineering with greater emphasis on decoupled features that rely
less on absolute measurements and more on integrated trends and tendencies.
A promising direction for future work is the application of similar FDD strategies within
more homogeneous building stocks, such as apartments within the same residential com­
plex, where construction types are more consistent. Within such settings, variations in oc­
cupancy patterns could also be investigated to determine whether training benefits from
room­type­specific models or whether a unified model at the whole­building level is suffi­
cient.
Finally, further research into model fidelity and data availability is needed. In particular,
access to labeled measurement data would enable a more robust evaluation of model

future studies could compare models trained on simulated data with those trained directly
on measured data. Such comparisons would help clarify whether real­world dynamics,
such as seasonal variation, irregular occupancy, and occupant behavior, degrade fault
detection performance or, alternatively, improve it by capturing patterns that are difficult
to represent in simulation­based training datasets.

Conclusion
This project demonstrates the potential of applying fault detection and diagnosis (FDD) in
residential buildings to identify operational faults during the heating season in a Danish
single­family house. By utilizing zone­level climate sensors and deriving relevant features,
operational faults can be detected and, when communicated to occupants, enable im­
provements in thermal comfort and energy efficiency through continuous, real­time moni­
toring of building operation and performance. Furthermore, the integration of climate sen­
sor data with building control signals, such as valve positions, was shown to enhance both
fault detection accuracy and the overall effectiveness of the FDD system. To fully realize
these findings in practice, future implementations should include an occupant­oriented
interface that allows residents to access, understand, and respond to detected faults.
The use of simulated training data applied to unlabeled sensor data proved effective, as
demonstrated by immediate and plausible fault detections in comparable zones. How­
ever, a key challenge arises when transferring simulated training data between buildings,
primarily due to variations in occupant thermal comfort preferences. Differences in build­
ing infiltration rates and setpoint schedules were found to significantly influence the pre­
cision and recall of detecting setpoint­related faults. In this project, transferability across
two similar zones within Building 1 showed promising and consistent results, indicating
that the approach performs well when applied to zones with comparable thermal behav­
ior. A promising direction for future work in residential FDD is therefore the investigation
of apartments within larger building complexes that share identical floor plans and facade
orientations. In such cases, more consistent building performance is expected, allowing
the strengths of this method to be more fully realized and potentially improving overall
FDD performance.
For individually implemented faults, open­window events were less reliably detected, par­
ticularly by the XGB model. In contrast, the multiclass detection approach, where multi­
ple fault types were considered simultaneously, improved the XGB model’s performance.
Similar trends were observed for the RF model, which achieved higher accuracy for de­
tecting high­setpoint faults when evaluated on zones other than the training zone. These
results suggest a degree of overfitting in the XGB model, where small deviations from the
trained high­setpoint conditions reduced its ability to correctly detect and classify faults.
The impact assessment of individual simulated faults indicated that detecting open­window
faults offers the largest potential for energy savings. In comparison, lowering the heat­
ing setpoint provided relatively limited energy benefits, which were unlikely to outweigh
occupants’ preferences for maintaining thermal comfort.
Perceived thermal comfort in residential buildings is highly dependent on individual prefer­
ences, which complicates a direct application of the adaptive thermal comfort categories
defined in EN 16798­1. The standard was primarily developed for office environments
with larger occupant groups, where comfort categories represent aggregated responses.
In contrast, residential buildings typically reflect the preferences of only one or two oc­
cupants, similar to single­occupant offices with individually controlled zone setpoints. In
Building 1, sensor data predominantly fell within the lower range of Category 1, while
Building 2 fluctuated between the lower end of Category I and Category 2. This pattern,
resembling slightly reduced setpoints observed in single­person offices [24], suggests a
relatively narrow comfort band, where small temperature variations may lead to increased
56
Modeling of building operational faults for improved IEQ and energy efficiency
==PAGE:65==
perceived discomfort, particularly when combined with a building­owner mindset focused
on reducing heating costs.
To improve the robustness of estimated energy savings and comfort benefits from FDD
implementation, training models on labeled sensor data would enable more accurate per­
formance evaluation without relying solely on trends derived from simulated data.
While this project focused on FDD in single­person home offices and hobby rooms, ex­
tending the approach to a full­building scale across rooms with varying occupancy pat­
terns could reveal broader trends in building optimization. Such an analysis would also
help determine whether a generalized training model can be applied across multiple zones
or if zone­specific models are required to capture inherent thermal and operational differ­
ences. Results from the second building indicate that even minor differences in setpoints
can significantly reduce the ability of models to detect high­setpoint faults. In particular,
the XGB model exhibited substantially lower detection rates under these conditions, while
the RF model detected very few high­setpoint faults overall. This highlights the sensitiv­
ity of trained models to setpoint variations and suggests that zone­specific calibration or
more robust training strategies may be necessary for reliable fault detection.
The inclusion of heating system control signals alongside climate sensor data represents
a promising extension of this work. Such signals may improve fault detection performance
sufficiently to justify their inclusion, both in terms of detection accuracy and potential en­
ergy savings. They also provide a basis for evaluating whether the added complexity and
implementation challenges associated with monitoring control signals in residential build­
ings yield meaningful benefits, or whether relying solely on zone­level climate sensors
remains a practical and effective approach.
Overall, this thesis demonstrates that machine­learning­based fault detection and diagno­
sis can be applied in residential buildings using a combination of simulated faults, indoor
climate measurements, and occupant feedback. While simulated training data provide
a viable starting point for identifying operational faults, reliable practical deployment de­
pends on building­specific thermal behavior, occupant preferences, and the availability of
relevant control signals. By linking fault detection performance with both energy impacts
and perceived thermal comfort, this work highlights the importance of prioritizing faults that
meaningfully affect occupants, such as unintended window openings and low­setpoint op­
eration. These findings support the use of data­driven FDD as a complementary tool for
residential building operation, while emphasizing the need for zone­specific calibration,
improved sensor integration, and occupant­oriented interfaces to enable practical and
trustworthy implementation.
