                                                             Abstract 
 
Additive Manufacturing (AM) of metals represents an advanced method for creating complex, 
three-dimensional (3D) parts with detailed features on both large and small scales. While AM 
has many advantages, the use of Vat Photopolymerization (VPP) for metal fabrication is still a 
relatively underexplored area traditionally used for producing polymer-based components with 
high precision, VPP has recently been adapted for metals and ceramics due to technological 
progress. However, producing dense metal parts with tunable microstructures through 
conventional powder-based AM processes presents significant difficulties. This thesis aims to 
establish a reliable and optimized approach for fabricating intricate metallic structures using 
Mask Projection (MP) with metal salt precursors—specifically copper sulphate (CuSO₄)—
incorporated into a photosensitive resin and characterizing its porosity. This method ensures a 
stable and printable slurry composition. The study explores the ideal slurry formulations, 
processing conditions, and post-processing techniques for producing high solid-loading 
components, contributing to the advancement of metal additive manufacturing (MAM). It 
highlights the potential of using metal salts in VPP to create precise metal parts more efficiently 
and affordably, expanding the scope of VPP in industrial applications. 
 
Keywords: Additive Manufacturing (AM), Vat Photopolymerization (VPP), Digital Light 
Processing (DLP), Metal Salt Precursors, CuSO₄ (Copper Sulphate).

CHAPTER 1 
                                                           Introduction 
 
The process of designing and producing the components that have been totally changed by the 
AM, which is also known as 3D printing that provides the design freedom and the capacity for 
building the difficult structures and geometries. VPP is one of the AM process which is famous 
by its accuracy and resolution. Traditionally, VPP has been applied in producing polymer-
based components, but recent advances have expanded its use to metals and ceramics, opening 
new possibilities for the manufacturing of micro-scale metallic parts. [1] 
 
Due to the extraordinary properties of copper both in electrical and thermal conductivity, 
resistance to corrosion it is widely used in the domains of electronics and other precision 
engineering applications. Despite of its great properties fabricating Cu micro-components 
directly with VPP had numerous challenges when it comes to well-formed and high resolution 
parts with microstructure. Normally 3D printing techniques for Cu requires high sintering 
temperatures or exhibit issues with porosity, shrinkage, and the formation of weak bonds 
between particles.[2] 
 
This thesis introduces an optimized method for the production of copper-based micro-
components via Vat Photopolymerization using a metal salt precursor approach. By employing 
an oxide/sulphite route with copper sulfate (CuSO₄) as the precursor, followed by thermal and 
chemical reduction. This approach also ensures the slurry’s stability and printability, allowing 
for the precise fabrication of copper parts at micro scale. 
 
This research focuses on optimizing the slurry composition, printing parameters, and post-
processing techniques to achieve high porous to high-density copper microstructures. The work 
illustrates the potential of using metal powders in VPP and shows how to produce complex 
metallic micro-components with greater coherence and cost-effectiveness. [3]

1.1 Background and Motivation  
“AM is a process of combining materials to manufacture the 3D models data usually layer upon 
layer.” Which was introduced by Charles Chuck Hull in the year 1986 in such a way to resolve 
the issues with other manufacturing processes. AM also reduces material wastage, lead time 
and provides the flexibility in manufacturing. With the introduction of AM process there is a 
huge saving of expenditure and time, making it viable for many applications and explicitly for 
product development. [4] 
 
Copper (Cu) is a critical material in fields such as electronics, micro-electromechanical systems 
(MEMS), and aerospace due to its excellent electrical and thermal properties. Existing AM 
methods, such as Selective Laser Melting (SLM) and Electron Beam Melting (EBM), generally 
comes with issues like high production costs, slow speeds, and the need for extensive post-
processing, especially for micro-scale parts.(reference2) 
 
In order to solve these challenges, this thesis optimised the approach using metal salt 
precursors, mainly copper sulfate (CuSO₄), in a photo-resin. This method, combined with post-
processing techniques such as thermal and chemical reduction, aims to overcome the 
challenges accompanying with existing metal AM processes while maintaining the high 
resolution and surface quality of the manufactured samples. 
 
The motivation for this research arises from the need to improve metal additive manufacturing 
for micro-scale applications, specifically for manufacturing high-precision, copper-based 
components with optimized microstructures. Existing metal AM techniques often struggle with 
resolution, porosity, and surface quality, especially in the fabrication of small-scale, intricate

1.2 Challenges in this Project 
The challenges in this project on VPP of Copper based Micro Components via Oxide/Sulphite 
Route has been divided into several key categories: 
 
1. Challenges in Material Development 
a) Slurry Stability: Creating a stable slurry that includes copper sulfate (CuSO₄) and 
photopolymer resin is tough one. Need to make sure that the metal salt remains are 
equally distributed and does not agglomerate over time. The viscosity and flow 
behaviour of the slurry must also be optimized for printability. 
b) High Solid Loading:  It is a challenging task to increase the solid loading into the resin 
because increasing solid loading can lead to issues like clogging in the printer or poor 
layer adhesion. 
2. Printability and Process Parameters Optimization 
a) Layer-by-Layer Alignment: Making sure that the layer alignment while printing copper-
based micro-components is important. Misalignment of layers can cause the structural 
defects or incomplete prints, especially when alternating between different materials 
(e.g., base resin and CuSO₄ slurry). 
b) Curing Efficiency: Metal salts in the resin can affect the curing depth and efficiency of 
the UV light during the VPP process. By controlling the exposure time and light 
intensity the curing of each layer can be done evenly.

2.1 Metal Salts 
Metal salts, such as copper sulfate (CuSO₄), play a crucial role in AM as precursors for 
manufacturing metallic components. The use of metal salts in AM processes like VPP 
introduces an approach to create metallic structures, particularly in high-detail applications. 
The main advantage of using metal salts in VPP is their high light penetration at the projector 
wavelength which makes this method differ from other techniques, also the increased light 
absorption enhances good curing precision during printing. Compared to the cost of pure 
Copper powder CuSo4. is less expensive also available widely in the market. [5] 
One main characteristic of metal salts is their ability to dissolve in solvents, dissociating into 
metal and non-metal ions. This dissociation is particularly important in additive manufacturing 
because it allows for a uniform distribution of metal ions in a liquid medium such as resin or 
slurry. This distribution is critical when printing with high precision, ensuring that the final 
product has the intended metallic properties after undergoing thermal or chemical reduction. 
For example, CuSO₄, when dissolved, releases Cu²⁺ ions, which are later transformed into solid 
copper metal during post-processing. [6] 
Metal salts in VPP provide a safer, more cost-effective way with improved precision. By 
incorporating metal salts into a photosensitive resin, structures can be selectively cured layer 
by layer. After the part is printed, the metal salts can be reduced into a pure metallic state. 
To transforming the metal salts into metals requires a reduction process. In the case of copper 
sulfate, the Cu²⁺ ions are reduced to metallic copper through thermal or chemical means. 
Thermal reduction involves high temperatures under a reducing atmosphere, while chemical 
reduction uses reagents such as sodium borohydride. Both methods allow the metal particles

2.2 VPP Additive Manufacturing 
The core principle of VPP involves the selective curing of liquid photopolymers using 
ultraviolet (UV) light in a layer-by-layer process. This technique offers superior accuracy 
compared to many other additive manufacturing (AM) methods shown in the figure 2.1, 
making it suitable for creating detailed micro scale components.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
                     Figure 2.1: Different types of Additive Manufacturing Methods [7] 
The process works by projecting UV light through a transparent base plate onto a membrane 
containing photosensitive resin. As the UV light hits the resin, photopolymerization occurs 
through a free radical mechanism as shown in figure 2.2, which cures and solidifies the resin 
in pre-determined areas based on the CAD design. The liquid resin typically consists of 
monomers, oligomers, and a photoinitiator, which together undergo chemical transformation 
when exposed to UV light with wavelengths ranging between 380 to 405 nm. As the exposed 
zones of the resin polymerize, they reach a hardened state, forming a solid structure.

This curing process is carefully controlled by adjusting two key parameters: the exposure time 
and the intensity of the UV light. Longer exposure times and higher light intensities result in 
more extensive curing, while shorter exposure times allow for finer control of the resin’s 
crosslinking. By meticulously managing these factors, the curing depth and material properties 
of the printed layers can be optimized for specific applications. The hardened layers represent 
cross-sections of the final 3D object, and the process is repeated layer by layer, with the build 
platform moving incrementally to allow new layers of resin to be cured. 
What sets VPP AM apart is its ability to produce parts with extremely high surface quality and 
fine detail. Its capability to precisely cure thin layers of material ensures that even complex 
geometries and intricate patterns can be manufactured with minimal post-processing. 
Additionally, advancements in material formulations have broadened its application beyond 
traditional polymers, enabling the inclusion of ceramics and metal precursors, such as metal 
salts. This versatility and precision make VPP AM a valuable technique for industries that 
require high-performance, detailed micro-components, such as electronics, medical devices, 
and aerospace. By refining the control over photopolymerization and optimizing resin 
formulations, VPP AM holds the potential to revolutionize the way complex metallic and 
polymer-based parts are produced, offering significant advantages over conventional 
manufacturing methods. [8]

In VPP there are mainly two approaches for additive manufacturing which are the Top-Down 
and Bottom-Up methods. Each approach has its own working characteristics and advantages, 
which influence the process of manufacturing 3D objects from liquid photopolymer resins. 
The Top-Down approach involves starting the printing process from the top of the object. In 
this method, the build platform is initially submerged in a vat of liquid resin, and the UV light 
source, which can be a laser or projector, is directed downwards to cure the resin. As each layer 
is cured the build plate moves down allowing the fresh resin to be added for the next layers. 
This method is characterized by its ability to achieve high precision and fine details, making it 
ideal for applications that require intricate geometries. The controlled curing process allows 
for smoother surface finishes, as each layer can be precisely defined. However, one of the 
challenges of the Top-Down approach is the increased resistance encountered as the part grows.

bottom of the vat filled with resin. In this design, the UV light is projected from below the build 
platform, curing the resin upward. The light passes through a transparent window with 
membrane on it allowing the resin to be cured starting from the base. As each layer is cured, 
the build platform moves upward, which draws fresh resin into the curing area, facilitating the 
next layer's formation. This approach generally reduces the suction forces associated with

2.3 VPP AM of Cu Salts 
2.3.1 Additive Manufacturing of Copper in Industries 
VPP AM has emerged as a promising technology for the manufacturing of complex geometries 
and functional materials. This process becomes more interesting when it comes to the 
incorporating of Cu salts into the resin due to its unique properties.By adding Cu salts in the 
resin, VPP AM can be able to produce metal-based structures, which can be later used in 
different domains after carefully done with the post processing treatments and sintering 
process. 
In industrial applications, utilizing copper (Cu) salts in VPP AM offers various advantages, 
especially in sectors like electronics, energy, and manufacturing. Here are the key industrial 
benefits: 
1. Complex Geometries and Lightweight Design: The ability of VPP machine is to create some 
complex and difficult structures like gyroids, Voronoi cells, and Schwarz lattice surfaces 
allows industries to design lightweight yet mechanically robust components. By introducing 
copper salts, into these structures makes the samples exhibits the characteristics of the copper 
such as electrical conductivity or enhanced heat dissipation. These manufactured structures 
plays an important role in the domains like aerospace, automotive, and energy industries where 
maintaining light weight structures and functionality is critical for performance, energy 
efficiency, and fuel savings. 
2. Incorporating of metal salts in Manufacturing: Introducing Cu salts into VPP offers the 
industries the ability to manufacture multi-functional materials with the benefits of both 
combined metals and polymers. After sintered at high temperatures the parts exhibits high 
electrical conductivity while maintaining the complex geometries. This is particularly useful 
in industries requiring functional materials, like energy storage (batteries, capacitors),

2.3.2 Different Metal AM Techniques for copper 
                    Figure 2.3:   Comparison between different AM techniques [10] 
Different AM techniques are present running in the manufacturing of components filed with 
each of them has its own properties are explained below. 
1. Selective Laser Melting (SLM) / Laser Powder Bed Fusion (LPBF) 
SLM/LPBF is one of the metal AM technique which melts the Cu powder layer by layer by 
using high energy laser. This technique allows for the creation of complex geometries by fusing 
the powder based on a digital design. It’s widely used in applications requiring high precision 
and detailed metal parts. 
2. Binder Jetting (BJ) 
Binder jetting is a process in which a liquid binding agent is selectively deposited onto a bed 
of copper powder. After printing, the part undergoes a sintering process to fuse the powder into 
a solid part. It is typically used for large-scale production of metal parts.

3. Directed Energy Deposition (DED) 
DED is the process which uses laser or electron beam for simultaneous melting and deposition 
of Cu powder or Cu wire for creating the parts and also repairing the existed ones with the 
ability to handle large-scale components. 
4. Electron Beam Melting (EBM) 
EBM uses an electron beam to selectively melt copper powder layer by layer in a vacuum 
environment. This process is typically used for fabricating high-purity copper parts in 
applications requiring excellent thermal and electrical conductivity. 
5. Cold Spray Additive Manufacturing (CSAM) 
In cold spray, copper particles are accelerated at high velocity by a gas stream and deposited 
onto a substrate, bonding together upon impact. Unlike other MAM techniques, no melting 
occurs, and it is typically used for coating or repairing copper components. [11] 
2.3.2 Why Metal Salts? 
In this research, the metal salt route is chosen instead of the metal powder route for vat 
photopolymerization of micro-metal components. The metal salt selected is CuSo4., which will 
be used to produce pure copper through post-processing. The metal salt route offers several 
advantages over the metal powder route in the context of digital light processing (DLP) for 
additive manufacturing of metal parts. When CuSo4. salts are ball milled they are grounded 
into fine particles similar to metal powder. However there will be some composition 
differences between the pure metal powders and metal salts even after milling and grounded to 
powder in terms of their behaviour in AM mainly in VPP and after post processing. These 
differences are listed below, 
1. Composition:- Metal Salts: These are the substances which are made up of chemically 
bonded with non-metal substances like sulphate present in the CuSo4., which are chemically 
different from pure metal.

printing.  
   - Metal Powders: Pure metal powders, after printing, may require only sintering or heat 
treatment to fuse the particles together.  
 
 4. Handling and Safety: 
   - Metal Salts: Even after ball milling, metal salts tend to be less reactive compared to pure 
metal powders. They are more stable in air, less prone to oxidation, and less hazardous to 
handle, which makes them safer to process and store.

3.2 Geometry and Slurry Development 
The Schwarz P is the geometry chosen for this project because of its triply periodic minimal 
surface (TPMS), known for its complex, continuous structure that divides space into two 
interwoven, yet distinct, regions. This surface is characterized by its minimal surface area, 
meaning it has zero mean curvature at every point, making it an ideal structure for various 
applications requiring high strength and low material usage.[13]

The Schwarz P surface is a well-known example of a triply periodic minimal surface (TPMS), 
characterized by its smooth, continuous structure that repeats infinitely in three dimensions. It 
was first described by the German mathematician Hermann Amandus Schwarz in the late 19th 
century during his study of minimal surface that minimize area for a given boundary, much 
like a soap film. The “P” stands for "primitive," referring to the simple yet intricate repeating 
unit of this surface. 
 
3.2.1 Advantages of Schwarz P Lattice 
The Schwarz P lattice has several advantages because of its distinct properties, mainly in fields 
like material science, architecture, and additive manufacturing, some of them are listed below, 
High Strength with Light weight structures the geometry of Schwarz P lattices allows for 
efficient load distribution which is why these are suitable for applications where requiring high 
strength to weight ratios. The lightweight components can be created without compromising 
the mechanical strength [14]. 
Nearly Isotropic Properties Regardless of their TMPS structure Schwarz P lattices display 
similar features in all directions. These lattices gain nearly isotropic mechanical properties 
which allows for steady strength and stability under varying loads. Due to this they can be used 
for load bearing applications where uniform performance is critical [15]. 
Enhanced Heat and Mass Transfer Schwarz P surfaces uniformity and continuity enhances 
the optimum fluid flow and heat dissipation. These properties are mainly advantageous in 
applications such as porous electrodes and filters, heat exchangers where maximum surface are 
and low flow rate are essential. 
Less Material Consumption The optimization of less material consumption is due to the 
geometrical properties of these TPMS and Schwarz P structures which makes sure the material 
is only used where it is mostly needed such as along the surfaces that carry the load. Yet 
consuming the less material it offers high strength and durability also.

3.3 Slurry Development  
The goal of the slurry development is to formulate and optimize slurries using copper sulfate 
(CuSO₄) metal salts as the primary metal precursor for effective application in VPP processes. 
The entire process, from the formulation of the slurry to the production of the final product, is 
outlined below. 
Step 1: Selecting Materials and Preparations The preparation of basic slurries using copper 
sulfate involves mixing CuSO₄ with a photopolymer resin to create a stable suspension. These 
slurries are formulated with varying concentrations of CuSO₄, specifically 50 vol%, 60 vol%, 
and 65 vol % to explore the effects of different loadings on the final properties.

Step 2: Ball Milling of Metal Salt 
Materials and Equipment: The ball milling (Pulverisette 6, classic line, Fritsch, Germany) 
process employed a zirconium jar and yttrium-stabilized zirconium oxide beads (10mm in 
size). These materials were chosen for their durability and minimal risk of contamination, 
which is essential to maintain the purity of the metal salt, copper sulfate (CuSo4.5H2O).The 
copper sulfate pentane hydrate used is from sigma Aldrich [17]. 
 
Initial Process and Challenges: The milling was conducted in the zirconium jar with the 
Zirconium oxide beads which are taken into amount based on the ball to powder ratio [18], 
initially wet milling is done by adding isopropanol to CuSo4.5H2Oat a speed of 240 RPM for 
a duration of 12 hours. This method often faced some issues like uneven grinding and 
agglomeration of the metal salts. Here the liquid medium of IPA is introduced which helps in 
absorbing of some kinetic energy during milling and also reduces the temperatures raise. 
However, the initial wet milling did not achieve a fine grind, so the RPM was increased from 
240 to 270, and the CuSo4.5H2Owas ball milled for an additional 3 hours. Even after this, there 
is no significant improvement was observed. The milling time was then extended to 13 more 
hours at 270 RPM, which resulted in finer powder, but some crystals still remained. So before 
ball milling dehydration of CuSo4.5H2Owas chosen. This process is particularly used for salts 
like copper sulfate pent hydrate (CuSo4.5H2O), which naturally contains water molecules. The 
dehydration temperatures were meticulously determined based on Thermo gravimetric 
Analysis (TGA) of CuSo4.5H2O [19]. TGA offers precise data on the temperature ranges 
where water loss occurs, allowing for the determination of optimal dehydration conditions that 
effectively remove water without degrading the chemical integrity of the metal salts. The 
dehydration was performed in an oven named Nabertherm [20] at a ramping rate of 300°C/hr., 
ranging from 30°C to 300°C and cooling down from 300°C to 30°C. After dehydration the 
CuSo4. is ball milled with IPA for 15hrs at RPM 240 which exhibits a fine powder evenly 
grinded.

Step 3: Sieving Process Following milling, the powder underwent sieving using the vibration 
sieving machine with 60 microns sieve. Here dry sieving is done and the powder below 60 
microns size is collected finally and stored for further process. 
Step 4: Preparation of photopolymer blend Progressing to the creation of a polymer blend, 
this step involved mixing a UV blocker (LB1), a photoinitiator (FT1), selected acrylic 3D resin, 
and a dispersant. In advancing VPP, selecting the right resin is crucial for developing effective 
metal slurries, as it directly influences the quality and consistency of the final printed parts. 
The process began with the careful selection of a photopolymer resin, which acts as a binding 
agent for the metal salt particles. The resin's viscosity is key to ensuring good impact on the 
final print quality, while its reactivity is crucial for effective curing and bonding with the metal 
particles. 
Initially, a "Fun To Do" unpigmented resin was used and after post processes the samples are 
subjected to structural damage. As a result, the resin was switched to 3D Resyn CDP for 
improved performance. This particular resin was previously validated for its effectiveness with 
ceramics prompting its experimental application with metals by a senior Marina Artemeva in 
her master’s thesis on ceramics [21]. The preparation phase involved an optimization process, 
where adjustments were made to the slurry composition to achieve a balance between flow 
ability, stability, and printability which have be already studied in the Master’s Thesis done by 
Anamika [22]. The objective was to create a slurry that remained stable with uniform 
distribution while possessing the ideal flow ability for the Vat Photopolymerization (VPP) 
process. Each component was carefully calibrated and tested with trails, and the final 
concentrations were optimized to 1.75% for the photoinitiator, 1.5% for the UV blocker, and 
2% for the dispersant. As these concentrations has been observed to be ideal which enhanced 
the curing process and improving the overall quality of the final printed product. Disperbyk-
111 (BYK Chemie, Germany), with a density of 1.16 g/cm³, was employed to decrease the 
slurry’s viscosity and ensure a homogeneous suspension. The dispersant played a crucial role 
not only in improving particle dispersion but also in preventing particle settling and modifying 
the slurry’s flow behaviour and viscosity [23]. The incorporation of UV blocker LB1 was 
essential as it controls the curing process by establishing the curing in the calculated areas only.

Meanwhile, the photoinitiator, FT1 plays an important role in starting the polymerization

Step 5: Mechanical Stirring and Ball milling The homogenization of the resin and metal 
particles was further addressed by employing a combination of mechanical stirring and ball

resyn is mechanically stirred continuously at 360 RPM with no heat for good viscosity.

3.6 Reduction Process 
During reduction, the copper components are placed in zirconium crucibles with platinum 
plates to prevent contamination and any unwanted chemical reactions between the copper and 
the crucible material. The samples are shifted to tube furnace and the heating is taken directly 
to 600°C as the samples are already exposed to the higher temperatures so in the presence of 
H2-N2 gases. The H2 gas acts as a reducing agent which will reduce CuO to pure metallic Cu 
at elevated temperatures. The hydrogen reacts with CuO to form H2O as a by-product and 
reduces CuO to pure Cu. 
                                      CuO + H2  → Cu + H2O  
The reduction process causes these particles to bond which increases density and mechanical 
strength. The generated H2O vapour from the reduction reaction may need to be continuously 
purged to prevent re-oxidation of any exposed copper sulphate. However the nitrogen (N2) in 
the atmosphere helps the pure copper from re-oxidation and maintains the stable reaction 
environment.

In the initial slurry formulation with 50 vol% solids, a dispersant concentration of 5% was used

4.1.2 Sieving Process 
The sieving process significantly influences the homogeneity and size distribution of particles 
in a slurry. Wet sieving, which involves adding liquid during the sieving process, promotes a 
more uniform particle size distribution. This uniformity is essential as it affects the flow and 
packing behaviour of the particles, ultimately influencing the density and structural integrity 
of the final product, initially the ball milled CuSo4. was sieved while wet using 20 microns 
sieve which makes the powder pass through easy while it is wet with IPA, but this method 
raised concerns as IPA evaporation during oven drying process was deemed hazardous 
following a risk assessment. To mitigate this issue an alternative method was adopted. The ball 
milled CuSo4. is now sieved without IPA in its dry state using a 60 micron sieve and stored for 
subsequent processing. 
4.1.3 Ball Milling of Photopolymer Slurry 
The next challenge after sieving is the ball milling of the slurry. With the particle size now 
reduced to below 60 microns further reduction to below 20 microns is necessary for smooth 
layer deposition and precise resolution also smaller particles pack more densely which helps in 
reducing the voids in the printed structures. To achieve this, the photopolymer blend is placed 
in zirconium jar along with zirconium balls of 10mm size (powder to ball ratio is considered) 
and subjected to ball milling. Initially the slurry is milled for 24hrs at 400 RPM and it is rested 
for some time in order to prevent any heating of the slurry due to high RPM and then it is milled 
for next 12hrs with same speed. Once this process is complete the slurry is ready for use in 3D 
printing.

CHAPTER 5 
                   Conclusion and Future Work 
5.1 Conclusion 
In this thesis it is fully demonstrated the VPP potential as an innovative approach for fabricating 
the copper based micro components using the CuSo4. as the precursor. Initially the goal is to 
create the high density structure but it is then optimized for the creating of high porous structure 
to the denser ones by carefully adjusting the debinding and sintering parameters. With a 
systematic approach and considering the previous work done some significant progress was 
made at various stages of the process from slurry development to printing, post processing and 
characterization. 
In slurry development the slurries were formulated with different solid loading of CuSo4. 
starting from 50 vol%, 60 vol% and 65 vol% with the composition of different photopolymer 
resin and additives like UV blocker, photoinitiator and dispersant and tested while solving the 
challenges arising like viscosity and homogeneity management, resulting the final green parts 
with good quality and structure. 
Characterization of the fabricated samples using highly advanced instruments like SEM and 
EDS confirmed the preservation of geometric integrity declaring the presence of CuO and also 
revealed that the porosity of the structure can be controlled by altering the annealing 
temperatures. It is also resulted in the study of microstructure which helps to understand the 
final sample structure for the application uses. 
Despite the progress made the study also highlighted the challenges of thermal stresses which 
lead to the cracks and reaction of CuO with the crucible, all these are the limitations that needs 
the further optimization of the process parameters for achieving the controlled porosity and 
crack free components. 
At 600˚C the reduction of the CuSo4 to pure Cu was carried out successfully. By considering 
the time and equipment availability the reduction temperature was constrained. Unfortunately

In conclusion this project work advances the application of VPP in metal AM particularly for

5.2 Future Work 
 For the future work one should perform the optimization of slurry composition and 3D 
printing parameters like exposure time and print speed for improving the quality and 
also for the better structure layers. 
 Investigating the influence of resin additives and dispersants to enhance final product 
properties. 
 Perform reduction temperatures higher that 600°C to evaluate their impact in the final 
Cu Properties such as microstructure and conductivity. 
 Exploring other heating ranges and holding times during post processes to understand 
their effect on the material. 
 Testing of electrical conductivity, thermal conductivity and corrosion resistance of the 
pure Cu to evaluate its performance in applications. 
 Testing for mechanical properties to know the strength and stress with stand of the final
