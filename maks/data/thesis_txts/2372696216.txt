ABSTRACT 
 
A structural health monitoring system comprising of an Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV) deployed with a 
Laser Doppler Vibrometer device is assessed for its robustness. Vibration based Modal Analysis is 
performed for numerical models in single and multiple degree of freedom subjected to dynamic loads. 
Modal response characteristics are extracted from the same using Frequency Domain Decomposition 
(FDD). A theoretical model development and quantification of the measurement velocity uncertainty of 
the system of Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV) deployed with a Laser Vibrometer is conducted and 
probabilistic modelling is applied to update the existing velocity response time series. The updated modal 
properties are extracted and compared to the original modal properties. The comparison yields the result 
that there is no change in the modal properties of the structural models for either case. In this way, it is 
established that an Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV) deployed with a Laser Vibrometer device is a robust 
technique for Structural Health Monitoring.

1. Introduction 
 
Structure and infrastructure systems are of paramount importance for the welfare and sustainable 
development of modern societies. However, the harsh environment they are exposed to, including man-
made and natural hazards as well as the degrading mechanisms (i.e., corrosion), may severely undermine 
their structural integrity and increase the risk of failing the intended performance objectives related to 
either serviceability or even collapse prevention criteria. To this point, the reliable and systematic 
inspection and monitoring of the structural performance is essential to keep them fully functional and 
safe during the foreseen lifetime. For more than 40 years, Structural Health Monitoring (SHM) attracts 
significant scientific and industrial attention. Several advanced techniques have been already proposed 
aiming to optimize the monitoring and inspection strategies applied for structures and infrastructures of 
varying size, geometry and complexity. Nevertheless, the use of conventional SHM methods, either 
related to human-driven inspections or based on the establishment of an extensive instrumentation grid 
(strain gauges, accelerometers. etc.) on the infrastructure, has been frequently found to lead to time-
consuming and costly solutions with potential risk for life. 
In recent past, Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAV’s) have gained a lot of interest in both the industry and 
the research community as the field of robotics and artificial intelligence has advanced to new heights. In 
the field of Structural Health Monitoring (SHM), where robust inspection techniques are critical to damage 
identification, attempts are being made to leverage the properties of an Unmanned Aerial Vehicle in 
carrying out these inspections. Application of a UAV can be critical to structures where manual inspections 
are a challenge and pose a risk to life and safety and may be a reliable alternative waiving the 
aforementioned drawbacks of the existing SHM methods. 
Along these lines, the current MSc Thesis is intended to focus on the use of the Unmanned Aerial Vehicles 
(or the so-called drones) for SHM purposes and more specifically, for the identification of the dynamic 
characteristics (natural frequencies) of simplified structural systems. 
Therefore, the motivation of this study is to find if using an Unmanned Aerial Vehicle system deployed 
with a Laser Doppler Vibrometer is a technique that can be successfully applied towards the inspection of 
our civil structures for Structural Health Monitoring.

2. Structural Health Monitoring (SHM) 
 
2.1 SHM Overview 
 
Structural Health Monitoring is the technique used by civil, aerospace and material engineering disciplines 
for effectively identifying damage and suggesting its control for their infrastructure systems. This process 
involves the observation of a structure or mechanical system over time using periodically spaced dynamic 
response measurements, the extraction of damage-sensitive features from these measurements and the 
statistical analysis of these features to determine the current state of system health [1].  
In general, SHM makes use of in-situ sensing that allows for an analysis of the response characteristics of 
the structure in consideration. SHM can be used for near real-time or short-term evaluation, such as for 
structural condition screening after an extreme event, as well as long-term evaluation during which the 
performance of a structure is periodically updated to reflect any damage accumulation. 
For long-term SHM, the output of this process is periodically updated information regarding the ability of 
the structure to continue to perform its intended function due to the inevitable ageing and degradation 
resulting from the operational environments. Under an extreme event, such as an earthquake, SHM could 
be used for rapid condition screening, to provide, in near real time, reliable information about the 
performance of the system during the event and about the subsequent integrity of the system.  
SHM is the technology that allows the time-based maintenance approaches to evolve into condition-
based maintenance philosophies. The concept of condition-based maintenance is that a sensing system 
on the structure will monitor the system response and notify the operator that damage or degradation 
has been detected. Life-safety and economic benefits associated with such a philosophy will only be 
realized if the monitoring system provides sufficient warning such that corrective action can be taken 
before the damage or degradation evolves to some critical level. The risk associated with implementing 
such a philosophy is that it potentially requires more sophisticated monitoring hardware to be deployed 
on the system and more sophisticated data analysis procedures to interrogate the measured data.

2.1.1 Steps in Structural Health Monitoring (SHM) 
 
Structural health monitoring involves the following steps, (a) diagnosis, which includes damage 
identification, damage localization and damage level assessment and (b) prognosis, which includes 
structure’s residual capacity estimation and residual life forecasting [2] . Historically, the monitoring of 
structures has involved many ingredients of the modern SHM paradigm, such as data collection and 
processing followed by diagnosis. At the simplest level, recurrent visual observation and assessment of 
structural condition (cracking, spalling and deformations) could be viewed as SHM, yet the aim of present-
day research is to develop effective and reliable means of acquiring, managing, integrating and 
interpreting structural performance data for maximum useful information at a minimum cost while either 
removing or supplementing the qualitative, subjective and unreliable human element. The motivations 
for and recent history of SHM applications to various forms of civil infrastructure and case studies on 
specific types of structures have been provided by [3].  
A comprehensive review of the technical literature concerning the detection, location and 
characterization of structural damage through techniques that examine changes in measured structural 
vibration response is provided in [4]. The study provides with the application of methods that use changes 
in modal properties (i.e. modal frequencies, modal damping ratios and mode shapes) to infer changes in 
mechanical properties. 
In another review [5],the authors believe that the SHM problem is fundamentally one of a general 
statistical pattern recognition. Therefore, the damage detection studies reviewed are summarized in the 
context of a statistical pattern recognition paradigm. This paradigm is described as a four-part process: 
  
1. Operational Evaluation,  
2. Data Acquisition, Fusion and Cleansing, 
3. Feature Extraction and Information Condensation, and  
4. Statistical Model Development for Feature Discrimination.  
 
Operational evaluation begins to set limitations on what should be monitored and how the monitoring 
can be accomplished. This feasibility analysis will impose limitations on the SHM scheme with regards to 
the parameters that will be selected for monitoring, the types of instruments that will be used for 
monitoring, and the way in which monitoring will be performed.

The data acquisition portion of the SHM process involves selecting the excitation methods, the sensor 
types, number and locations, and the data acquisition/storage/transmittal hardware. The locations, 
quantities and types of data acquisition sensors will be determined. The data storage media along with 
the sampling rate will also be selected at this stage. The selections will be driven mainly by the operational 
environment of the structure and cost considerations. 
 
Data cleansing is the process of selectively choosing data to pass on to or reject from the feature selection 
process. The data cleansing process is usually based on knowledge gained by individuals directly involved 
with the data acquisition.  
 
The identification and extraction of structural features that provide insight into the condition of a 
structure is performed in the feature extraction step of the SHM scheme. The best types of features to 
extract are specific to each structure and its operating conditions. The more common feature extraction 
algorithms are based on relating the structural output response to structural damage observations made. 
Other methods apply engineering flaws to a structure in order to determine the parameters that are 
sensitive to damage. Finite element models are sometimes used to perform numerical simulations and 
gain insight into the condition of the structure. Lastly, due to the large amount of data that will be 
collected from a given structure, robust data reduction techniques should be implemented. 
 
The last step in an SHM scheme is that of damage detection and model development. The type of damage 
detection algorithms that are used depend on the type of SHM scheme and the data that is available for 
use. Commonly extracted features used for damage detection include the modal properties (modal 
shapes, frequencies and damping) and structural properties (stiffness and flexibility). Finally, a damage 
model of the structure will be developed. 
 
The concept of machine learning enters into this paradigm primarily in steps 3 and 4 [6]. Machine learning 
algorithms can be implemented for damage detection if there is incomplete or insufficient knowledge of 
structural features. Here, the idea of machine learning can be simply stated; it is to ‘learn’ the relationship 
between some features derived from the measured data (step 3) and the damaged state of the structure. 
If such a relationship between these two quantities exists, but is unknown, the learning problem is to 
estimate the function that describes this relationship using data acquired from the test structure – the

training data. This estimation process is the focus of step 4. Learning problems naturally fall into two 
classes. If the training data comes from multiple classes and the labels for the data are known, the problem 
is one of supervised learning wherein the machine learning task is of inferring a function from labeled

Based on information published in this extensive literature, the field has matured to the point where 
several fundamental axioms, or accepted general principles, have emerged and have been described in 
detail by [10]. 
 
2.1.2 Structural Health Monitoring (SHM) Technologies 
 
These days, technology is being developed to replace qualitative visual inspection and time-based 
maintenance procedures with more quantifiable and automated damage assessment processes. These 
processes are implemented using both hardware and software with the intent of achieving more cost-
effective condition-based maintenance. Due to this, several sensor network paradigms for SHM have 
immerged, and [11] very well summarizes and compares these paradigms.  
A fundamental premise regarding data acquisition and sensing is that these systems do not measure 
damage. Rather, they measure the response of a system to its operational and environmental loading or 
the response to inputs from actuators embedded with the sensing system. Depending on the sensing 
technology deployed and the type of damage to be identified, the sensor readings may be more or less 
directly correlated to the presence and location of damage. Data interrogation procedures (feature 
extraction and statistical modeling for feature classification) are the necessary components of a SHM 
system that convert the sensor data into information about the structural condition.

The responses of structures under external loads are conventionally measured with sensors that are 
directly attached to the structures. For example, linear variable differential transformers (LVDTs) and 
strain gauges are typically used for displacement and strain measurements. Each of the attached sensors 
only provides one local measurement at a time, and many them are often required for a complete 
structural evaluation of a large-scale civil infrastructure, which could amount to a cost-prohibitive sensing 
system in practical applications. In addition, installing sensors on a large structure requires hours of labor 
from professional technicians particularly for difficult-to-access areas, including the physical attachment 
and protection of sensors, and power and data transmission cabling.  
In the state of California, 61 of the states 22,000 bridges have been instrumented with costs reported to 
be well over $300,000 per toll bridge for a 60- channel system. A large portion of this cost includes the 
laying of conduits needed to protect wires from harsh weather conditions at a cost of $10 per linear foot 
[12]. For structural monitoring, the wired systems throw up a host of issues, the primary problems being 
those of installation and maintenance. Laying out the cabling is expensive and time consuming. This is 
primarily because of the large sizes of structures and because the points where the sensors are installed 
are generally hard to reach. For example, installation time of a moderate size monitoring system can 
consume over 75% of the total system testing time with installation costs approaching over 25% of the 
total system cost [13].  
Much research has therefore, been conducted such as done by [14], [15], [16],[17] on the development 
of  novel wireless structural monitoring systems and techniques for this purpose. 
2.2 Vibration based Structural Health Monitoring (SHM) 
 
For civil structures, SHM schemes that implement global vibration-based methods for assessing damage 
have become most popular. These SHM schemes utilize the dynamic characteristics of the structure to 
assess the overall condition. A typical vibration-based SHM scheme uses an excitation source that serves 
as an input to the structure to observe its output vibration response. The scheme then proceeds to extract 
various features from the vibration response, which will be used for damage detection. 
Vibration-based SHM processes may use features such as the modal parameters of the structure, or 
implement matrix methods or machine learning algorithms to detect damage. Figure 1 is a flow chart 
depicting the various steps involved in a vibration-based SHM scheme.

2.2.1 Sources of Excitation 
 
Techniques that enable the dynamic testing of structures can be classified as either input output methods 
or output only methods. Input-output methods involve applying a known input, such as a Dirac impulse 
load, to the structure and measuring its output vibration response. To create a desired forcing function, 
devices such as impact hammers, shakers or drop weights are used. Output only methods involve 
measuring the output vibration of the structure in response to ambient excitation.  
 
2.2.2 Data Acquisition Instruments 
 
The most commonly used data acquisition instruments for vibration-based damage detection are 
acceleration and displacement transducers.  
For civil structures, accelerometers tend to be the most widely used data acquisition instruments. This is 
due to their ability to operate over a broad range of frequency responses, their ease of installment and 
high measurement accuracy.  
There are two types of accelerometers commercially available, one which measures acceleration based 
on capacitance measurements and another which uses piezoelectric deformations.

2.2.3 Damage Detection based on Modal Parameters 
 
Modal parameters may be extracted from a dynamic analysis of a structure to serve as inputs to a 
vibration-based damage detection technique. The types of modal parameters used can be modal 
frequencies and/or modal shapes. 
Methods that are based on modal frequencies have the inherent assumption that any shifts in the natural 
frequencies of a given structure indicate that a change in its structural properties has taken place. The 
advantage of using modal frequencies is that it allows for damage identification to be performed with 
relatively easy implementations. 
The use of modal shapes for damage detection allows for spatial information on damage to be obtained, 
but this requires a minimum number of measurement points to properly characterize the mode shapes. 
The number of measurement points increase with increasing mode frequency. 
The use of both modal frequencies and shapes is commonly implemented in SHM schemes as it allows for 
the detection, localization, and estimation of damage in a structure.

3. Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAV’s) 
 
3.1. Overview of UAV Systems 
 
As, the domain of wireless structural health monitoring gained momentum [18], there was also rapid 
development in image acquisition systems/cameras and computational algorithms such that non-contact 
measurements with image analysis gained increasing acceptance in experimental mechanics. For 
example, the digital image correlation (DIC) technique has been applied to monitor the dynamic 
characteristics of structures with camera and computer vision techniques as explained and applied in [19], 
[20],[21].  
Recently, vision based techniques have been coupled with the implementation of the wireless system of 
an Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV) and have been applied to the field of Structural Health Monitoring. 
According to the unmanned vehicle system (UVS) international definition, “an unmanned aerial vehicle 
(UAV) is a generic aircraft design to operate with no human pilot onboard”. 
Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs) have attracted significant interest in recent years. In the past, the 
development of UAV systems and platforms was primarily motivated by military goals and applications. 
Unmanned inspection, surveillance, reconnaissance, and mapping of inimical areas were the primary 
military aims as described by [22].  
However, due to improvements in embedded computing, communications, and sensing technologies, 
UAVs have become increasingly capable of carrying out sophisticated tasks. The reasons for this are: 
(i) 
UAVs can acquire detailed object data from different perspectives quickly,  
(ii) 
they can get close to objects without any physical contact,  
(iii) 
they can overfly inaccessible areas and 
(iv) 
they can fly fully or almost fully automatic.

3.1.2. Sensors in UAV. 
 
The UAV is equipped with different navigation sensors, stabilized by a microcontroller-based flight control 
and basically can be operated by GPS waypoint navigation or manual remote control. An unmanned aerial 
vehicle system includes two parts: the vehicle and the sensors.  
Five kinds of sensors must be equipped on board, namely i) a microprocessor, ii) a GPS sensor, iii) 
gyroscopes, iv) a radio receiver, and v) a barometer. The GPS sensor receives the coordinates for localizing 
the aircraft. The aircraft can maintain its position in the air stably by utilizing the gyroscopes. In addition, 
the radio receiver can receive the signal sent from the manipulator on the ground so that the pilot can 
control the aircraft immediately. Meanwhile, the barometer can assist with estimating the altitude of the 
UAV. Finally, the microprocessor serves as the computing unit. It combines all the information gathered 
from the other sensors and processes the flight algorithm. With those sensors, the aircraft can be 
controlled autonomously in the air.   
A summary of the wide range of available sensors present are: 
1. Vision Based: These are for example, still HD images, video sensors, 3D sensors for large scale 3D 
reconstruction, multispectral imaging sensors, tactile sensors and force/torque sensors. 
2. 3D Sensors: These include for example, Stereo, structured light from an Xbox KinectTM, LIDAR- 
Light Detection and Ranging sensor.

3.3 Summary 
 
An overview of several types of UAV’s is presented. It could be seen that primarily UAV’s were 
implemented for military inspection and reconnaissance purposes in the beginning. As the field of 
robotics, embedded computing, communications, and sensing technologies enhanced, it paved way for 
drones to be used in a commercial setting and for common purposes. This led to the development of a 
wide range of classes of UAV’s, especially the mini UAV’s in carrying out these tasks.  
The use of a UAV for civil infrastructure inspection was seen to give multiple advantages over traditional 
inspection method by: 
1. Reducing work accident risk. 
2. Budget reduction: less logistics and less working hours.   
3. The infrastructure will ultimately not be closed for traffic. 
4. The possibility of using nondestructive techniques for damage detection. 
5. The drone operator can work from a considerable distance and not worry about natural or 
artificial barriers to the sight lines. 
6. Images are high resolution and serve a variety of users. They can be transmitted, shared and 
printed. Software can turn them into topographical maps, heatmaps and more. 
Therefore, this need for better inspection techniques for civil infrastructure, wherein manual inspections 
have been traditionally challenging, prompted the application of UAV’s to solve this issue in a better way. 
Research has been carried out to better the flying algorithms for control of these UAV’s and also to extract 
properties from the structure using vision based approaches. Further, application of UAV’s in crack 
detection, photogrammetry and 3D mapping has also been described through various researchers (Refer 
Section 3.2). In this way UAV’s are effectively being used for structural health monitoring purposes. 
In this study, a theoretical approach is pursued to first identify and then quantify the uncertainty 
introduced by using the UAVs to record the response time series. Evaluation of the accuracy and efficiency 
of the UAV-based system identification is done by comparing the dynamic characteristics of the structural 
models obtained from the numerical modeling approach, wherein simulation of the structural response 
of a simplified structural model is done and response time series of structural response is calculated. The 
dynamic response is used to further extract the natural frequency of the structure. These are finally 
compared by the modal properties extracted from the UAV based recordings of the response time series.

4.2 System Identification in SHM 
 
An important application of structural system identification is in health monitoring, where it is applied for 
damage detection. This is the practice of conducting non- destructive tests or inspection of a structure to 
determine the existence, location, and extent of damage, and in some cases, decide as to the remaining 
life of the system. A summary review of vibration based damage identification methods has been given 
by [48]. 
Model-based techniques utilize changes in response functions or modal parameters such as natural 
frequencies, mode shapes, or their derivatives, to identify damage locations and levels. Analysis of 
changes seen in parameters between sequential tests over time is used to determine damage 
characteristics. An excellent review of model-based health monitoring methods has recently been 
compiled such as in [4]. A toolbox for damage identification has also been developed for experimental 
and statistical modal analysis to measured or simulated modal vibration data in [49]. The toolbox also 
contains a unique capability that utilizes the measured coherence functions and Monte Carlo analysis to 
perform statistical uncertainty analysis on the modal parameters and damage identification results. 
Extreme Value Statistics has also been used to classify structural damage with respect to Structural health 
monitoring in [50].

4.2.2 Frequency Domain Decomposition (FDD) Identification 
 
The technique of FDD is an extension of the classical frequency domain approach often referred to as the 
Basic Frequency Domain (BFD) technique, or the Peak Picking technique. The classical approach is based 
on simple signal processing using the Discrete Fourier Transform, and is using the fact that well separated 
modes can be estimated directly from the power spectral density matrix at the peak[51]. In the FDD 
identification, the first step is to estimate the PSD matrix. The estimate of the output PSD known at 
discrete frequencies is then decomposed by taking the Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) of the matrix. 
It has been shown that this decomposes the spectral response into a set of single degree of freedom 
(SDOF) systems, each corresponding to one individual mode. 
The frequency domain decomposition (FDD) technique that is based on singular value decomposition 
(SVD) of the power spectral density (PSD) matrix takes the classical frequency-domain approach some 
steps further and makes it possible to analyze cases with closely spaced modes. Further, the FDD approach 
==PAGE:42==
 
 
   
 
41 
Modelling of Measurement Uncertainty of a UAV based SHM System 
makes the frequency-domain technique more user friendly because it concentrates all information in one 
single plot; that is the plot of singular values of the SD matrix. The technique was introduced by Brincker 
et al and the theoretical basis for the same can be seen given in [52]. 
In any frequency band, the number of singular values that clearly raises above the noise level is most likely 
a modal response, at least if it has a peak as a modal response. At the vicinity of a resonance, the FRF is 
dominated by the contribution of that vibration mode and the contributions of other vibration modes are 
negligible. If this assumption holds, then the Frequency Response Function (FRF) from a multiple degree 
of freedom (MDOF) system or a real structure can be treated as the FRF from an SDOF system 
momentarily. The SDOF mathematical model can then be used with curve fitting to derive the modal 
parameters. 
The simplest version of the FDD only estimates the mode shapes and then provides an estimate of the 
natural frequency, normally the frequency of the frequency line corresponding to the peak where the 
mode shape was estimated. This process is also denoted as peak-picking FDD and was the first version of 
the FDD introduced by Brincker et al [52]. 
The major limitation to this technique is its inability to differentiate between modal frequencies that are 
very close in value. Furthermore, it also requires the structure to have low damping values. However, for 
many practical applications where damping is not an issue, the peak-picking FDD is to be considered due 
to its close relation to intuition and its user friendliness. This technique has also been used subsequently 
in this thesis.

6. Uncertainty Model of a Laser Doppler Vibrometer based UAV for SHM 
 
6.1 Overview of a Laser Doppler Vibrometer Device 
 
The Laser Doppler Vibrometer (LDV) operates by measuring the velocity of a point addressed by a focused 
laser beam, using the Doppler shift between the incident light and scattered light returning to the 
measuring instrument. With a LDV, response measurements can be obtained successively at hundreds or 
thousands of designated points. This can be done without any of the structural changes so often 
introduced by accelerometer loading, and without multi-channel instrumentation. The LDV scanning 
methods give response measurements for excitation at a single input frequency. It is clearly possible to 
repeat this process at a series of frequencies, as in a stepped-sine test, to establish a frequency response 
function over a range, and with sufficient frequency resolution, to enable any conventional modal analysis 
process to be used to evaluate natural frequencies, modal damping and modal constants[54]. 
The laser vibrometer offers the advantage of being a non-contact method avoiding the intrusive character 
of the accelerometer and rather complex devices needed to place sensors on the targets. 
Laser Doppler Vibrometers (LDV) significantly extend measurement capabilities with respect to traditional 
vibration sensors (such as accelerometers, strain gauges, etc.), as they allow ‘‘remote’’ nonintrusive, high 
spatial resolution measurements with reduced testing time and increased performances (high frequency 
bandwidth up to 20 MHz, velocity range of 730 m/s, resolution of about 8 nm in displacement and 0.5 
mm/s in velocity) [55].  
Furthermore, LDV is a ‘‘remote’’ measurement technique, in the sense that it can measure also if 
positioned far from the target, with sufficient accuracy (1–2.5% RMS of reading) at a distance larger than 
30 m. This feature is not common to all the non-contact sensors and seems to be very useful to extend 
the boundary of vibration analysis to several applications, as the testing of large civil structures.

8 Conclusion 
 
This study commenced with an exhaustive literature review in the subject of Structural Health Monitoring. 
The review expound in detail the concept of Structural Health Monitoring and described its step by step 
application and relevance on civil infrastructure. It also briefly stated the concept of machine learning into 
this paradigm of SHM and how machine learning algorithms can be implemented for damage detection if 
there is incomplete or insufficient knowledge of structural features. The current technologies used for 
SHM were also a part of this review. Several of these indicated towards shifting towards a wireless mode 
of inspection to save both costs and achieve higher levels of safety in operation.  Since, SHM schemes that 
implement global vibration-based methods for assessing damage have become most popular over time, 
the review further expands on the subject of Vibration based Damage Detection and lays its theoretical 
background for implementation in the subsequent sections. 
Next, the topic of Unmanned aerial vehicles and their application in civil infrastructure as a tool for 
inspection and damage detection was covered exhaustively through literature study. Different classes of 
UAV’s were described and their growing relevance in civil inspection was elaborated. The studies were 
indicative towards a growing consensus in using UAV based SHM methods, as they were likely to be more 
cost effective, accurate and provided for elevated levels of manual safety while inspection.  
 The task for this study has been to find out the robustness of a UAV based SHM method for inspection. 
The first step in this involved the generation of simplified structural models subjected to dynamic loads 
This part involved the simulation of the structural response of simplified models when subjected to 
dynamic loads for which a solid theoretical background was also provided. The structural models were 
taken to be a Single degree of freedom model, typically representative of a long bridge and a three story 
building as a multi degree of freedom model. 
Subsequently, numerical modeling was used to calculate the time series of structural response 
due to the imposed dynamic loading for both the models. The dynamic response of the simplified 
structural model, obtained after the numerical simulation, was used to extract the natural frequency of 
the structure by using Modal Analysis technique in the Frequency Domain using Frequency Domain 
Decomposition. 
The next step was to develop a theoretical approach that was used to identify and then quantify the 
uncertainty introduced by using the UAV deployed with a Laser Vibrometer system in recording the 
==PAGE:74==
 
 
   
 
73 
Modelling of Measurement Uncertainty of a UAV based SHM System 
response time series of the generated structural models. A Probabilistic model was developed to update 
the response time series and derive the modal response characteristics for the same.  
The study found that the combined standard measurement velocity uncertainty of an Unmanned Aerial 
Vehicle (UAV) deployed with a Laser Doppler Vibrometer to be 2.94% with expanded uncertainty of 8.9%. 
Subsequently, comparisons were made prior and post application of the Uncertainty model.  
Application of this uncertainty to generated models both in Single Degree of Freedom and Multiple Degree 
of Freedom was performed. However, there was no change in the modal properties of the system as was 
indicated by the results of successfully performing modal analysis in the frequency domain using 
Frequency Domain Decomposition. 
Therefore, the result identified the following aspects to be justified, which are presented as follows: 
1.) The measurement velocity uncertainty of a Laser Doppler Vibrometer mounted on an Unmanned 
Aerial Vehicle does not necessarily influence in measurement of the modal properties of the 
system to be monitored. 
2.) Current Modal Analysis is robust in the detection of the modal parameters and is not influenced 
by the uncertainty developed because of using a Laser Doppler Vibrometer mounted on an 
Unmanned Aerial Vehicle. 
Therefore, it can be suitably concluded as a result of this study, that the structural health monitoring 
technique of using an Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV) deployed with a Laser Doppler Vibrometer is a 
robust technique even after accounting for the given uncertainty of the system.

global vel; 
global vel_mod; 
global accn; 
global accn_mod; 
  
global m; 
global n; 
  
prior_mu=zeros(m-4,n); 
prior_std=zeros(m-4,n); 
post_mu=zeros(m-4,n); 
post_std=zeros(m-4,n); 
x_bar=zeros(m-4,n); 
  
 
u_c=2.94; 
kp=3; 
NoS=1000; 
% sum=zeros(m+1,n); 
% vel_mod=zeros(m+1,n); 
  
std_velt=0.1*ones(m,n); 
vel_new=zeros(NoS,1); 
  
for j=1:1:n 
    for i=1:1:(m-4) 
        prior_mu(i,j)=vel(i,j); 
        prior_std(i,j)=0.1*vel(i,j); 
        std=abs(0.01*u_c*vel(i,j)); 
        rand_error=kp*normrnd(0,std,NoS,1); 
        for sim=1:1:NoS 
            vel_new(sim,1)=vel(i,j)+rand_error(sim,1); 
        end 
        n_dash=1; 
        x_bar(i,j)=mean(vel_new(:,:)); 
        
post_mu(i,j)=((prior_mu(i,j)/NoS)+(x_bar(i,j)/n_dash))/((1/NoS)+(1/n_dash)); 
        
post_std(i,j)=((std_velt(i,j)^2/n_dash)*(prior_std(i,j)^2/n)/(prior_std(i,j)^
2/n_dash+(prior_std(i,j)^2/n))); 
        vel_mod(i,j)=post_mu(i,j); 
        vel_new=zeros(NoS,1); 
    end 
end
