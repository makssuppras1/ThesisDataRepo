Abstract 
Identifying the mood or the emotion in a song is an interesting ongoing research involving different 
parameters of an audio sample. This thesis is an attempt to gain clarity on the subject using Indian 
Classical music and its concepts. A Raag, which is a central concept in Indian Classical music, is said by 
the learned to adhere to a specific set of emotions. It is therefore hypothesized that the emotional 
content of an audio can be correlated to the melodic content. Thus, the moods of pop songs based on 
certain Raags are tested and correlated to the moods of the respective Raags. This comparison creates 
a requirement of identifying Raags in pop compositions. To achieve this, Machine Learning algorithms 
involving Support Vector Machines and Neural Networks are used to match the Pitch Class 
Distributions, Bi-gram Distributions, and Tri-gram Distributions of pop compositions to certain Raags 
present in the dataset in order to identify those Raags in the compositions. The comparison also 
creates a requirement to test the emotional content of Raags and pop songs. It is achieved by 
performing self-report tests using the Circumplex Model of Affect. The Pitch Class Distribution 
algorithm using Neural Networks achieves 100% accuracy in identifying Raags in pop songs. The testing 
of moods in songs achieves 80% accuracy when comparing the Valence axis of the Circumplex Model 
of Affect for Raags and songs based on it. The accuracy when comparing the Arousal axis of the 
Circumplex Model of Affect for Raags and songs based on it was not seen to be satisfactory with 40% 
correct matches.

Chapter 1. Introduction 
1.1 
Context 
1.1.1 Raag 
The fundamental theory of Indian Classical music (both Carnatic and Hindustani) revolves around the 
concept of Raag. A musical scale is defined as ‘a collection of pitches, ascending or descending in f0, 
which adheres to a system of organization allowing or to define a mode, key area, or some other pitch 
collection’ [1]. A Raag, although is a collection of pitches is not only that, which makes it different 
from a scale. An analogy to the concept of scales in Western music, Thaats are the collection of pitches 
which can be called scales. 
To begin defining a Raag, an essence of what it means is required. Swami Prajnanananda writes, “A 
Raag is a psycho-material object, as it is an objective expression of the subjective feeling of the mind. 
It is first designed ideally in the mind and then projected outside in material tonal form. And so, in the 
process of the construction of a Raag, both mind and matter act together” [2]. Thus, a Raag is designed 
to convey various emotions to the listener. A Raag material is composed of different notes including 
the tonic and two other notes called Vadi Swar and Samvadi Swar. These are the notes which the 
performer emphasizes the most during a presentation of the Raag. Another aspect is the ascending 
scale and the descending scale of the Raag called Aarohan and Avarohan respectively. It is important 
to note that the ascending scale and the descending scale of a Raag can have different musical notes. 
For example, Raag Des has B in the ascending scale and Bb in the descending scale instead of the B 
(see Figure 1). Catch phrases or motifs are another important aspect in the identity of a Raag. Trained 
musicians are known to identify the Raag by listening to these catch phrases. They are called Pakad.  
 
Figure 1 Raag Des, Aarohan and Avarohan 
Taking into consideration all the mentioned aspects, a Raag can be defined as – A collection of 
independent ascending and independent descending notes, having an important and a sub-important 
note, performed with a certain catch phrase, all of which combined associate to a certain emotion or 
a mood and a certain time of the day. 
1.1.2 Performing Indian Classical music 
Indian Classical has always been a performance art. Particular emphasis is given to live performance 
and improvisation. The performer is expected to be confined by the rules of the Raag and improvise 
within the confinements. The main focus is the Khyal form of performance. Many other forms exist 
==PAGE:10==
Introduction 
2 
 
but they are not discussed here. The performer starts the performance with an ‘Alap’. An Alap is the 
introduction to the Raag wherein the emotion of the Raag is set for the audience. A peculiar thing to 
note about the Alap is that it is not bound by rhythmic structure. An Alap is a free flowing river with 
the rules of the Raag as the banks but the speed varying according to the slope of the terrain which is 
the will of the performer.  
Every performance has a ‘drone’ constantly accompanying the performance. The notes of the drone 
are defined for every Raag. A ‘Bandish’ is the next part of the performance wherein the rhythmic 
instrument (the Tabla being the most popularly used) gives structure to the performance after the 
mood is set by the Alap. Drone is constant in both the Alap and the Bandish. A peculiar thing to note 
about the Bandish is the changing tempo. The Bandish usually starts with a slow tempo (‘vilambit lay’) 
or medium tempo (‘madhyam lay’) after the Alap and then fast tempo (‘drut lay’) towards the end. A 
Bandish can be compared to the flow in a canal where it is controlled but the twists and turns are the 
will of the performer.  
Indian Classical music has been primarily vocal based, therefore emphasis still prevails in the vocal 
parts of typical Indian pop compositions. To add to this, the tuning system of Indian Classical music is 
known to be closer to the just intonation tuning system [3]. The notes in the scale would be adjusted 
based on the concept of ‘Shrutis’. The octave is divided between 22 such Shrutis which are intervals 
smaller than that of the semitones [3]. Not each Shruti is played, it just provides the base for tuning 
the 7 notes of a Thaat.  
1.1.3 Difficulties in analysing Indian Classical music 
A precursor to this thesis was a special course on Music Information Retrieval for Hindustani Classical 
Music [4]. It was found in the project that there were certain limitations in analysing Hindustani 
Classical music, for instance, the tuning system used in traditional Indian Classical music was found to 
be closer to the just intonation tuning system whereas the Music Information Retrieval tools for 
Western music were designed for the equal temperament tuning system.

1.1.4 Mood Taxonomy 
The (now established) definition of a Raag ascertains its adherence to a mood. This leads to the 
question of what exactly are moods. Superficially speaking, moods are a temporary state of mind. 
Scientifically speaking, moods and emotions are a complex cognitive phenomenon often intertwined 
amongst themselves. There have been approaches to identify emotions based on core affect. Russell 
and Barrett [5] suggest that core affect refers to consciously accessible elemental processes. Certain 
models for the classifying moods have been built on this concepts. This helps create a standard 
taxonomy to better define the emotional states. It should be noted that there is a thin line between 
the meanings of moods and emotions and therefore, the two terms will be used interchangeably in 
this report. 
The approach to emotions or sentiments in ancient Indian literature is based on the concept of ‘states’ 
or bhava of the mind. This concept is clearly defined in the Natyasastra [6]. The Rasa is the experience 
of the emotions which is experienced as a combination of sthayi-bhava (dominant states) and bhava. 
The Natyasastra defines eight Rasa or sentiments namely, Erotic, Comic, Pathetic, Furious, Heroic, 
Terrible, Odious and Marvellous. The sthayi-bhava or the dominant states are, love, mirth, sorrow, 
anger, energy, terror, disgust and astonishment. Other than that, there are thirty three Transitory 
States and eight Temperamental States. All these states together combine to ultimately become one 
experience of a sentiment. Each Raag is said to be attributed certain states, which are central to the 
experience of that Raag [6]. 
1.1.5 Indian Pop Music 
Pop music, defined as commercially popular music, has many facets in the Indian subcontinent. With 
influences from jazz and Western Classical music to Indian Classical music and Raags, it is a quite a 
wide genre. The Indian folk drama had songs as an addition to the art of acting. This has always been 
an influence on the movie culture in India thus the songs in movies. The Indian film industry, now

1.1.6 Machine Learning 
1.1.6.1 Introduction 
Data analysis and extraction is an important aspect of music information retrieval. After successfully 
extracting data, it needs to be assigned to a specific identity. This helps in analysing the patterns 
observed in the data which can be compared to a new dataset. In the context of audio samples, pitch 
is the data which was obtained. The recurrence of notes, 2-grams (two-note sequences) and 3-grams 
(three-note sequences) are the patterns that can be observed in the dataset. This section introduces 
only the intuition behind these methods used for assigning an identity to the observed datasets 
(training) and the methods which can distinguish between the identities (classification) which is 
broadly called ‘Machine Learning’.  
1.1.6.2 Hidden Markov Models 
The first order Markov assumption states that the probability of a state depends on the probability of 
its immediate predecessor [7]. This is the basis of the Hidden Markov Models (HMM) algorithm. Thus 
the probabilities of states are obtained and an identity is assigned to it. HMMs are relevant in case of 
identifying Raags as Raags can be assumed as sequential data and thus be modelled as Markov chains 
[8]. It should be noted though that in the age of parallel processing, there are more efficient ways of 
analysing sequential data such as Neural Networks as was discussed with the author of [9]. Thus 
modelling the Raags using HMM was studied but not implemented.

A widely used algorithm in machine learning, Support Vector Machines (SVM) classifies two data 
classes by representing them in an n-dimensional space (where n is the number of features) and 
separating them by a hyperplane [8] [10]. Figure 4 showcase an example of SVMs in action.

1.1.7 Motivation 
A term ‘light classical’ is used by musicians in India to denote music which deviates to some extent 
from the strict rules of a Raag but has a major influence of the Raag on the composition. The older 
pop music could be loosely termed as light classical as the vocal melodies are said to be heavily based 
on Raags. The songs would not have an Alap as is necessary in an Indian Classical composition. The 
song would start with a definite rhythmic structure and the tempo would not change during the course 
of the song, again unlike a classical performance. A giving of less importance to the Vadi and Samvadi 
notes is quite likely. Since a Raag has a definite mood, a question arises that which specific aspect 
gives the Raag its mood. It is argued that the Raag as a whole is responsible for the conveyance of the 
emotion. A lot of research has being done on understanding the mood of music and similar research 
needs to be extended on this wide subgenre of Indian pop music [13] [14] [15] [16] [17].  
For musicians, the philosophical question of ‘what do we want to say with this song?’ is a way of 
communicating the felt complex emotions. Although these emotions are a combination of the Core 
Affect, music has been an important part in conveying them. Music is created with or without a specific 
aim. Often musicians get stuck in the ‘whys’ and ‘hows’ of creating music. This research is an attempt 
to get an insight into the aspects of compositions and how to convey the intended emotion to the 
listener.  
Another commercially important aspect of this research is the possibility of creating a mood-based 
search engine for songs. Such a service is not present in the context of Indian music and the research

1.2 
Hypothesis 
1.2.1 Objective 
The objective is to gain some insight about the mood of a song. Emotions felt after the experience of 
a song can be attributed to the melody of it. Since a Raag has a clear definition of the melody, it can 
be said that it can point towards a specific emotion. Therefore it can be hypothesised that the mood 
of a Raag on which a pop composition is based can be attributed to the composition depending on the 
difference/similarity of the composition to the Raag. Pop compositions are polyphonic and/or 
heterophonic and that creates a challenge for analysing a Raag in the composition. The Raag is usually 
used in the vocal melody thus the comparison should be made between the Raag and the vocal 
melody. 
To achieve this comparison, techniques which can classify Raags need to be studied, implemented and 
developed for the context of pop songs. In addition to that, prior to the extraction of the Raag, 
separation techniques need to be implemented which can separate the vocal melodies from the 
polyphonic audio signals in context of Indian pop. Separation of vocals from the polyphonic audio is 
out of the scope of this thesis and therefore a dataset of vocal performances of Raags and pop songs 
needs to be created for analysis. Tests of emotional response should also be conducted to test the 
hypothesis thoroughly. For this, an approach needs to be developed for participants to efficiently 
articulate the emotions they have felt from the experience of a Raag or a song.

2.1 
Raag Identification by Segmentation and Singer Identification 
As mentioned earlier, the tonic is difficult to retrieve and necessary to identify a Raag. Similar is the 
case with meter. Sridhar and Geetha proposed a system based on database to tackle these two 
difficulties [18]. The difficulty of detecting meter is tackled while segmenting the signal using a 
database of 175 defined rhythms which are used in the South Indian Carnatic music. The difficulty of 
detecting the tonic is tackled after extracting features to predict the singer and thus using the singer’s 
fundamental frequency from the database, the scale is found.  
The algorithm is shown in Figure 6. The signal separation is achieved by analysing the timbre and in 
turn the Harmonic Structural Stability (HSS) of the signal. For vocal performances, the timbre varies 
by a larger degree than that for the instruments thus the HSS for vocal performances is relatively 
higher [19]. After separating the vocals, onset and offset are detected as a part of the segmentation 
process. The signal between an onset and an offset is assumed to be an integral multiple of the meter, 
thus retrieving information about the meter after applying a fitting algorithm. To accommodate 
quarter notes, each meter segment is further segmented into four frames of equal size in the temporal 
context.  
 
Figure 6 Flow chart for Raag Identification [18] 
After analysing the frequency components in each segment, the frequency component with the 
highest energy is treated as a tone for that segment. Subsequently, the fundamental frequency of the 
signal is found by identifying the singer and thus, mapping of the frequencies is achieved (see Figure 
6). The retrieved notes are then compared to the Raag database and the Raag is determined.

This way poses an issue in the case of pop music as Raag identification is done for only 4 singers 
whereas in pop music, the number is much larger. Another issue is the low accuracy achieved in this 
analysis due to incorrect assumption of the fundamental frequency [18].  
2.2 
Hidden Markov Models 
Using mathematical tools such as Hidden Markov Models (HMM) to increase the accuracy was a way 
indicated in [18]. A Raag was modelled as an HMM which is a set of states (all the notes of three 
octaves being a state) with a probability distribution [20] [21]. The transition probabilities and initial 
state probabilities were trained for a set of Raags with the probability distribution of each state were 
predefined for each Raag in consideration. It was noted that due to use of extended notes in the Indian 
Classical context, transcribing musical notes was a challenging task. Another challenge again was to 
track the tonic or the fundamental frequency.  
In [21], to track the fundamental frequency, the tempo was first tracked. This was done in two stages, 
onset detection and estimation using Kalman filter. Onsets were detected where a transition in 
frequency was observed. The positions of onsets were converted in beats per minute and the average 
value was treated as the tempo of the composition. The tempo in a musical performance varies by a 
certain error. This error was included in the state transition model by introducing noise. Subsequently, 
the error covariance was minimized by using a Kalman filter. The extracted tempo was used to 
segment the signal to have a single pitch in each segment. An algorithm which selects ten indices in 
the descending order of their amplitude after performing the FFT is implemented and then, the 
greatest common divisor of each pair is taken. The mode of the greatest common divisors is taken 
which is the fundamental frequency of the segment.

After finding the fundamental frequency, all the frequencies are correlated to the just intonation 
tuning system to obtain the notes in the scale. These notes are then mapped to the trained HMMs to 
get the Raag. An accuracy of 100% was achieved on the selected dataset in [21].  
The musical samples analysed in [21] were monophonic in nature. In case of pop music, the 
environment is not monophonic, thus additions will have to be made to this algorithm to separate the 
relevant melodies from a polyphonic sample which is accomplished in [19] and [22]. 
To tackle the challenge of note transcription in the context of Indian Classical music, two methods 
were prescribed in [20]. The first is called The Hill Peak Heuristic. In this, a significant change in slope 
or a reversal of it is observed to be an occurrence of a note. At the point of the occurrence, the note 
is identified with the closest characteristic pitch value. If the change in slope is not seen to be 
significant, the previous note is assumed to continue. The second method is called The Note Duration 
Heuristic. For Indian Classical music, an assumption is made that the note continues for at least 25 ms. 
By comparing the current note with the history list of last ‘k’ number of notes, it is accepted as a note 
when it is different from the note which occurred ‘m’ times. The sample values chosen were 10 and 8 
for ‘k’ and ‘m’ respectively [20]. 
Another addition to the algorithm to identify Raags is Pakad Matching. Pakad or the catch phrase is 
used by trained musicians to identify Raags. Pakad is treated as a string of notes. This string is not used 
as a whole in an instance in a performance. It is rather spread out by the performer in substrings 
throughout the compositions. One method of identifying the Pakad is by comparing individual notes 
from the Pakad to the notes in the complete sample. A note in the complete sample is identified as 
belonging to the Pakad if there is a maximum difference of δ between the current note of the complete 
sample and the next note of the Pakad and if the position of occurrence of the next note of the Pakad 
in the complete sample is displaced from its ideal position by α [20]. Since it is not necessary that all 
the notes of the Pakad are in the complete sample due to errors in note transcription, a score, which 
is the ration of notes identified in the sample to the notes in the Pakad, is maintained. Another method 
of identifying the Pakad was matching the n-grams of Pakad. The score in this case is calculated by 
adding the number of times every n-gram occurs in the signal.

Pitch Class and Pitch Class Dyad Distribution 
Another altogether different attempt at classifying Raags was done using Pitch Class and Pitch Class 
Dyad Distributions (PCD and PCDD) [23]. Raags were treated as a collection of melodic atoms which 
then combine to form longer phrases. This creates a tonal hierarchy with some notes appear more 
often. This is in line with the concept of Vadi Swar and Samvadi Swar mentioned earlier. Thus to 
classify Raags, both PCD and PCDD were used. This study used a vast set of (thirty one) Raags for the 
analysis in their database along with twenty one hours of unaccompanied Raags. The tonic for each 
Raag was found manually. During pitch detection, errors in octaves and those due to accompaniment 
were observed. Onset detection was subsequently done using a detection function.  
Histograms of the pitch track, and not the onsets, were used to compute PCD. Just intonation tuning 
system was used to define the bins for the PCD as the tonic was found beforehand manually. Folding 
of five octaves and normalisation led to the creation of PCD. In case of determining PCDD, the detected 
onsets were used. Each onset was used to create PCD which were then separated as dyads. A similar

Chapter 3. Pitch Extraction 
3.1 
Introduction 
Pitch is an auditory sensation represented by two characteristics namely the tone height and the 
Chroma. The tone height showcases the rise in pitch as the frequency rises and the Chroma showcases 
the perceptual similarity between two notes separated by an octave [28]. While extracting pitch from 
an audio sample, both the dimensions should be considered. Having said that, it should be kept in 
consideration that both the dimensions at a certain time can be obtained by extracting the 
fundamental frequency at that time. Due to this, the algorithms which primarily detect the 
fundamental frequency are oftentimes called pitch extraction algorithms. 
Pitch extraction algorithms are often used in speech prediction and music information retrieval. In the 
context of this thesis, the audio samples in consideration were all monophonic vocal audio samples. 
When extracting pitch from audio, care needs to be taken with regards to voiced and unvoiced sounds. 
The spectral analysis of vocal audio show formants influencing the audio sample in that even though 
the fundamental frequency of two vowels is same, the harmonics might have different energies. This, 
along with several other reasons creates difficulties in the extraction of pitch. There exist some robust 
algorithms which are discussed in this section and their performances are also compared in different 
cases.  
3.2

According to the context established earlier, the methods chosen for detecting the fundamental 
frequency of a segment of the audio did not need to be extremely complicated as the audio samples 
are monophonic with good signal-to-noise ratio. Therefore, two methods have been discussed and 
implemented here namely, Harmonic Product Spectrum (HPS) and Autocorrelation.

Chapter 4. Pitch Class Distribution 
4.1 
Introduction 
Any musical piece is a collection of pitches arranged in a way to achieve musical meaning. The Pitch 
Class Distribution (PCD) is a distribution of the probability of the occurrence of these pitches. This 
distribution enables the differentiating of Raags in two scenarios. Firstly, if the Raags have different 
pitches and secondly, if the pitches are same but their occurrence is different. This resonates with the 
fundamental of a Raag as two Raags with same notes have differences in the importance of the notes 
as was mentioned earlier. This makes PCD an extremely relevant parameter in identifying Raags. This 
section attempts to determine if it is a complete differentiator in the context of songs as the 
occurrence of notes can hint multiple Raags in the domain of pop songs. 
4.2

Two methods were tested to obtain the Pitch Class Distribution of the audio samples which are 
explained in this subsection. In order to assign frequencies to a pitch value, the pitches were assigned 
a pitch band. The bandwidth of a pitch band was centred at the frequency of the pitch and separated 
by logarithmic mean of two centre frequencies. 
4.2.1 Without extracting pitch

Thus the energy density of each pitch value was obtained. The octaves were then folded to obtain the 
energy density of a Chroma. This gave the distribution in an octave [33]. The distribution was divided 
by the total energy to obtain the Pitch Class Distribution.

Chapter 5. N-gram distribution 
5.1 
Introduction 
An n-gram represents the sequence of the occurrence of ‘n’ notes. Thus, a 2-gram would represent a 
two note sequence and a 3-gram would represent a three note sequence. N-grams showcase 
important information about the melody structure. In the context of Raags, this exact feature is 
maintained while performing and composing. The melody structure differentiates two Raags which 
can otherwise be seen as extremely similar. This makes N-grams extremely relevant for identifying 
Raags as it relates to the concept of Pakad in a Raag. It is an interesting insight to check if the same 
can be extended to identifying Raags in pop songs. 
5.2 
Temporal Coherence Boundary 
In [23], an attempt was made to obtain what was termed as ‘Pitch Class Dyad Distribution’ which hints 
at the distribution of bi-grams. Detecting bi-grams posed an issue,  
“A significant complication of calculating PCDDs in this musical context is the occurrence of 
notes that are played by sliding up or down to that pitch from a previous note, without any clear 
onset(…). In the current work, this problem was not solved, and so the PCDDs entail a certain level of 
abstraction, as some of the values recorded in them are in actuality bi-grams for the closest pairs of 
clearly articulated notes, rather than simply bi-grams of adjacent notes.” [23] 
 
Figure 29 the temporal coherence boundary and the fission boundary, influence of tempo and pitch interval [35]  
A solution to this could possibly be the temporal coherence boundary (Figure 29). Instead of finding 
pitch at the onsets, the pitches with duration less than 150 milliseconds (the temporal coherence 
boundary average) should be ignored as they will be perceived as a stream or portamento [24]. Thus, 
same pitch having a longer duration does not pose the risk of being divided in bi-grams and all the 
stable note regions can be included in the determination of bi-grams. It also makes the process 
efficient by avoiding the need of detecting onsets. 
5.3 
Method 
To determine the melodic structure, the distribution of both bi-grams and trigrams was determined. 
The determination was conceptually similar with only a minor difference at the end of the algorithm 
which determined the distribution of bi-grams or tri-grams. The pitch information was extracted as

mentioned in Section 4.2. The octaves were then folded to obtain the Chroma at every time step which 
was subsequently divided into bi-grams and tri-grams to determine the respective determinations. 
 
Figure 30 N-gram Distribution algorithm 
An integral part of the algorithm presented was determining Chroma at every time step. This gave 
valuable information about the temporal aspect of the pitch. It can observed from Figure 31 that 
certain notes are stretched for longer periods and at certain time, there is fast variation due to 
ornamentations in the performance. Thus, there is clearly no need for detecting onsets in determining 
stable pitch zones. Stable pitch zones were determined by analysing the duration of each occurrence 
of the pitch. If the pitch lasted for more than 150 milliseconds, it was tagged as a stable pitch zone as 
the possibility of it sounding coherent was quite less [35]. The zone further ended at any variation in 
the pitch. The process was repeated after a variation was detected. If a variation in pitch was detected 
before 150 milliseconds, then next occurrence of a stable pitch was tagged as the next stable pitch 
zone. 
Subsequently, a matrix containing all the possible bi-grams (144 in numbers) or tri-grams (1728 in 
numbers) was obtained. The stable pitch zones were assigned to a sequence. Every possible bi-gram 
or tri-gram was then searched for in this sequence. The number of occurrence of the bi-grams or tri-
grams was thus obtained and divided by the total number of occurrences to get the probability of each 
bi-gram or tri-gram. This resulted in the distribution of bi-grams or tri-grams as can be seen in Figure 
32 and Figure 33.

Chapter 6. Modelling emotions 
6.1 
Introduction 
The hypothesis mentioned creates an integral need to model the emotions felt during the experience 
of a Raag or a song. This is a central concept for comparing a pop song to a Raag. This section aims at 
establishing the method used for modelling emotions while listening to the dataset. By doing this, the 
correlation between the emotion of Raag and the emotion of a pop composition based on the Raag 
can be observed. 
There are many ways to observe the emotions felt, simplest of which is self-reporting tests. In the 
context of music, listeners can rate the emotions felt or the musical intention based on a set standard 
which was discussed earlier (Section 2.5). The detailed methodology for the same is discussed in this 
section. 
6.2 
Method 
To model emotions or moods, self-reporting using the Circumplex Model of Affect [26] was used. A 
survey website was developed which firstly gave information about the test and the Circumplex Model 
of Affect [36]. This was necessary as the participant needed to have some idea about the model for 
self-reporting. Once that was established, the participants were asked to report the musical intention, 
that is, the emotion which the musician might have intended to portray from the composition. They 
could play the audio and subsequently rate the core affects of Valence (Sad – Happy) and Arousal 
(Calm – Alert) on a scale of -10 to +10, the negatives indicating ‘sad’ and ‘calm’ parts of the axes and 
the positives indicating ‘happy’ and ‘alert’ parts of the axes. 
A major difficulty in the testing was observed to be the explanation of the distinction between the 
emotion felt and the musical intention. For the context of this thesis, the musical intention was of 
more relevance for self-reporting as the emotions felt depend a lot on many other factors such as the 
bias of existing emotional state and an emotional bias towards a certain song and its lyrics. Although 
this difficulty was anticipated and steps were taken to reduce its affect, it was seen that a survey 
website could not be the best environment for this kind of analysis. A more controlled environment 
would have yielded much better results. Another difficulty was the emotional bias induced after 
listening to an audio sample and then listening to the next audio sample. The effect of this bias could 
not be analysed in this thesis. 
6.3 
Analysis and Results 
The entire dataset of the results is included in the Appendix below. The survey entries which indicated 
a lack of understanding were omitted from the analysis. At least 60 meaningful survey entries (where 
the participant got an idea of the survey) were aimed but the response was much higher with 111 
meaningful survey entries.  
6.3.1 Analysis and results of participants trained in music 
The analysis was divided in two parts. Those trained in music (moderately trained to graduate in music) 
were separated and the data analysed. This was done with the aim of having an insight into the 
perception of the judgement of the musical intention. Those learned in music were anticipated to 
have a better idea about the musical intention. Taking a hint from [37], the averages for the majority 
of the respondents were taken. Meaning, if the majority of respondents had responded as negative 
for a core affect, the average of only these negative values was considered in the determination of 
the trend of the core affect.

Chapter 8. Summary and Conclusion 
Making the computer identify the mood of a song, or in other words, understand the emotion behind 
a music piece is a topic of curiosity for many. Mainly because there is so little that humans themselves 
understand about emotions and thus, for some, this becomes a topic of self-discovery. This report was 
a similar attempt inching closer to the understanding of two extremely complex phenomenon, i.e. 
music and emotions.  
Indian Classical music theory presented some insight into the connection of emotions with music. The 
very definition of a Raag was seen to include emotions. This led to further research in understanding 
emotions. The models of emotions presented an insight into this. It was thus hypothesized that the 
Raags if present in a song attribute their defined mood to that song. In order to test this hypothesis, 
some knowledge was required in identifying Raags. Lot of research using concepts such as Hidden 
Markov Models and Machine Learning were therefore studied which presented algorithms relevant 
to the study. There seemed a need to extend these algorithms to identifying Raags in songs as the 
structure of a Raag and a song are not necessarily similar and the algorithms were developed 
specifically for identifying Raags from Raag performances.  
This need firstly led to pitch extraction. Various algorithms were tested which fit the context set by 
the hypothesis. It was found out that the autocorrelation algorithm as used in ‘Praat’ presented the 
best results for the use case scenario. The need then led to analysing the Pitch Class Distributions 
(PCD). Two methods, one including pitch extraction and one excluding it, were tested. It was found 
out that obtaining PCD using pitch extraction and later identifying the Raag in a song using Neural 
Networks provided the most accurate results. Another aspect of a Raag was the melodic structure. 
The need suggested that this aspect should also be tested in a song. Thus, the bi-grams and tri-grams 
were analysed. The distribution of bi-grams and tri-grams in a song and a Raag were compared to 
identify the Raag in a song. The results obtained were not as promising as those with PCD. It was 
therefore concluded that pop songs did not necessarily followed the strict rules set for a Raag 
performance and that some liberty was taken in composing. It can also be concluded that PCD using 
pitch extraction coupled with Neural Networks was the best method for identifying Raags in songs. 
After identifying Raags, in order to completely satisfy the need, a test for understanding which 
emotions were felt was conducted. It was observed that those trained in music were better in 
identifying the musical intention which was the main question of the test. Another important 
observation was that musical training held an advantage in the judgement of musical intention thus 
neglecting a cultural bias which was hinted by familiarity of Indian Classical music. It can be concluded 
that one dimension (Valence) of the model of emotions experienced while listening to a Raag and a 
song based on it was correlating with decent accuracy. 
To build on this analysis, an algorithm for predicting the range of valence experienced while listening 
to a song was proposed. It was observed that the tests conducted for modelling emotions were 
coinciding with the range of valence predicted by the algorithm. It can therefore be concluded that 
this algorithm can be extended significantly to study the valence in Indian pop music by improving the 
database of Raags and conducting detailed tests for modelling emotions.

Chapter 9. Scope for Future Research 
The research presented in this report opens up gates for many subsequent possibilities. The 
correlation of Valence in a Raag and a song is observed. This leads to the question of what it is in a 
Raag that induces specific emotions. Thus, the melodic content can be deconstructed to further 
analyse which pitch intervals attribute which set of emotions. This could break the cultural bias (if any) 
and provide significant insight in modelling the valence in music. 
An important dimension of the model of emotions was observed to not correlate when it came to a 
Raag and its song. This dimension, Arousal, is speculated to depend on the temporal aspects of the 
musical piece. A further analysis into the influence of tempo and timbre in music could provide insight 
into modelling the arousal experienced while listening to music. 
Smaller projects such as building a database of different performances of Raags and using them for 
the same analysis can also be executed to improve the current results. The testing of emotions could 
be improved manifolds. That is another interesting research area. 
A research having major relevance in the music industry around the world can be helped with this 
analysis. It might be able to render musical genres as we know obsolete by classifying music based on 
emotions rather than music styles. This could increase music exploration thus giving the entire 
industry a boom as more people will be listening to more kinds of music. The existence of clustered 
audience promoted by genres will cease which could create unimagined possibilities.

Definitions 
The definitions are presented in the order of appearance in this report. 
Raag: A collection of independent ascending and independent descending notes, having an important 
and a sub-important note, performed with a certain catch phrase, all of which combined associate to 
a certain emotion or a mood and a certain time of the day. 
Thaat: Fundamental heptatonic scales defined in Indian Classical music. There ten popular Thaats in 
practice. It is said that all the Raags are composed in a specific Thaat. 
Swar: A musical note. 
Vadi Swar: The most important note of a Raag. It is used in performances as a stop in between 
ornamentations and long vocal solos. 
Samvadi Swar: The second most important note of a Raag. It is used as a transitory note in 
ornamentations. It is also used as a stop if the Vadi Swar is not reachable. 
Aarohan: The ascend of a Raag, central to its definition. It can contain five to seven notes. 
Avarohan: The descend of a Raag, central to its definition. It can contain five to seven notes similar to 
the Aarohan. 
Pakad: The characteristic catch phrase defined for a Raag. Every Raag has this catch phrase assigned 
and performers are supposed to build the performance based on this catch phrase. Learned musicians 
are said to identify Raags by searching for the catch phrase in a performance. 
Alap: The introductory part of a Raag performance where the performer sets the mood of the Raag to 
build on. An Alap is not bound to any tempo and therefore no percussions are present in this part of 
the performance. 
Drone: A set of two notes which are played constantly on the Taanpura as an accompaniment to the 
performance. These may defer for each Raag. 
Bandish: An Alap is followed by a Bandish which sets the structure of the performance. The percussion 
starts to define the tempo of the performance and the lead performs solos or keeps improvising in 
this tempo. 
Lay: Tempo 
 
- Vilambit Lay: Slow tempo 
 
- Madhyam Lay: Medium Tempo 
 
- Drut Lay: Fast tempo 
Shruti: Pitch intervals smaller than a semitone. These are used for defining the tuning of a semitone. 
There are 22 Shrutis but the octave is divided in 12 notes always. Thus the purpose of a Shruti is tuning 
and they do not act exactly as quartertones. 
Murchhana: Analogous to a mode in the Western music theory, it is a mode in the context of a Raag 
and not a scale. 
Bhava: A state of an emotion. 
==PAGE:58==
Appendix 
50 
 
Sthayi-Bhava: The Dominant states of emotions. There are eight such states defined in the 
Natyasastra which are love, mirth, sorrow, anger, energy, terror, disgust and astonishment. 
Rasa: The sentiment felt due the experience of a combination of Dominant and other states of 
emotions.

Segmentation 
function [yframes,seg] = 
segmentation(y,Fs,segmentMs,overlapFraction,windowName) 
  
% INPUT 
% 
% segmentMs is the segment in miliseconds 
% overlapFraction is the overlap in fraction 
==PAGE:61==
Appendix 
53 
 
% windowName determines which window to use.  
    % windowName = 1 implies hanning window 
    % windowName = 2 implies hamming window 
    % windowName = 3 implies gaussian window 
    % windowName = 4 implies rectangular window 
     
% OUTPUT 
% 
% yframes is the segmented signal 
% seg is the segment length (number of samples) 
  
%calculate the segment length in samples 
seg = Fs*segmentMs; 
  
%determine the window to use 
if windowName == 1 
    window = hann(seg); 
end 
  
if windowName == 2 
    window = hamming(seg); 
end 
  
if windowName == 3 
    window = gausswin(seg); 
end 
  
if windowName == 4 
    window = rectwin(seg); 
end 
  
%calculate the overlap in samples 
overlap = overlapFraction*seg; 
  
%multiply the window to each segment 
tt = 0; 
for ii = 1:floor(length(y)/seg) 
     
    if (length(y)-ii)<seg 
        x = length(y)-ii; 
        window = window(1:x); 
    end 
     
    y(tt+1:seg+tt) = window.*y(tt+1:seg+tt); 
    tt = tt+seg; 
end 
  
%divide the input into frames 
yframes = buffer(y,round(seg),round(overlap)); 
  
end

% Map Minimum and Maximum Input Processing Function 
function y = mapminmax_apply(x,settings) 
y = bsxfun(@minus,x,settings.xoffset); 
y = bsxfun(@times,y,settings.gain); 
y = bsxfun(@plus,y,settings.ymin); 
end 
  
% Competitive Soft Transfer Function 
function a = softmax_apply(n,~) 
if isa(n,'gpuArray') 
    a = iSoftmaxApplyGPU(n); 
else 
    a = iSoftmaxApplyCPU(n); 
end 
end 
function a = iSoftmaxApplyCPU(n) 
nmax = max(n,[],1); 
n = bsxfun(@minus,n,nmax); 
numerator = exp(n); 
denominator = sum(numerator,1); 
denominator(denominator == 0) = 1;

a = bsxfun(@rdivide,numerator,denominator);

function a = iSoftmaxApplyGPU(n) 
nmax = max(n,[],1); 
numerator = arrayfun(@iSoftmaxApplyGPUHelper1,n,nmax); 
denominator = sum(numerator,1); 
a = arrayfun(@iSoftmaxApplyGPUHelper2,numerator,denominator); 
end 
function numerator = iSoftmaxApplyGPUHelper1(n,nmax) 
numerator = exp(n - nmax); 
end 
function a = iSoftmaxApplyGPUHelper2(numerator,denominator) 
if (denominator == 0) 
    a = numerator; 
else 
    a = numerator ./ denominator; 
end 
end 
  
% Sigmoid Symmetric Transfer Function 
function a = tansig_apply(n,~) 
a = 2 ./ (1 + exp(-2*n)) - 1; 
end
