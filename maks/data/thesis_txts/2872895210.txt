Abstract
This thesis investigates the development of a digital tool that supports mechanical en-
gineering design through interactive graph representations. Although computer-aided
design (CAD) continues to improve in usability and capability, its dominance in the de-
sign process has certain limitations, including a tendency toward design fixation and a
premature focus on detailed geometry. In response, this project explores a graph-based
approach where mechanical assemblies are modeled as nodes and edges, allowing de-
signers to work at a higher level of abstraction. Although the conceptual benefit of
graph-based design representations is recognized in design literature, existing tools are
either improvised or locked within complex systems engineering platforms not suited to
most design workflows. To address this gap, a prototype was developed using an Agile
process with continuous involvement from stakeholders. Commercialization aspects were
included in a deliberate effort to move beyond idealized methods and create a tool that
fits the realities of engineering work in practice. The final product complements current
mechanical design tasks and adds value through features such as automatic graph gen-
eration from CAD files and a modern, browser-based interface. The project concludes
with confirmed interest from industry for pilot studies and promising directions for aca-
demic collaboration, suggesting a foundation for further development and validation of
the approach.

Introduction
3D CAD (Computer-Aided Design) has become the dominant tool in mechanical engi-
neering design, offering powerful visualization capabilities, precise geometric modeling,
and enabling key downstream processes such as simulation and manufacturing prepa-
ration. CAD software providers are constantly evolving their tools with the promises
of increasing the productivity and competitiveness of their customers. Recent advance-
ments include, among others, cloud-based platforms (Onshape 2025a; Autodesk 2025b;
Dassault Systèmes 2025) and collaborative modeling environments (Onshape 2025b;
Autodesk 2025a; Dassault Systèmes 2024). As in almost any other field today, the engi-
neering design community is increasingly exploring the potential of AI. Companies such
as Autodesk and SolidWorks (Autodesk 2025c; SOLIDWORKS 2025) are embedding
generative design and AI-assisted functionality into their tools to extend the designer’s
capabilities.
Despite this progress, the thesis takes a different perspective and begins with the recog-
nition that a substantial part of engineering design is still not captured by CAD. At its
core, a CAD model is a geometric description of one or more components arranged in a
hierarchical assembly. In some cases, the model may also include part properties such
as material and colors, but higher level information, like how parts functionally relate to
other parts, are not present in the underlying data structure. This limitation has been
acknowledged by the Head of Autodesk Research, who describes it as one of the key
challenges for applying artificial intelligence to CAD. Since the intent behind a design
is not embedded in the model data itself, it becomes virtually impossible for a learning
algorithm to use it (K. D. Willis 2023). In contrast, 2D image data is not only widely
available but also often contains the meaning that machine learning models are trying to
learn. For example, one can find large datasets of animals labeled with “cat” or “dog”
and an algorithm can be trained to make the correct prediction. A CAD model, by
comparison, is a collection of parts with precisely defined shapes and dimensions, while
the whys behind those parts are either externally documented or exist only in the mind
of the designer.
From a design theory perspective, a CAD model can be seen as one out of many types
of design representations.
Working exclusively in CAD means operating on a single
abstraction level, which can negatively impact the design process, as will be explored
in the next chapter. Nevertheless, an increasing share of engineering design work takes
place in CAD environments (Atilola et al. 2016).
This thesis does not view the growing role of CAD as a decline in design practice, but
rather as a natural result of improved usability and significant advances in functionality.
Rather than opposing this development, it seeks to address aspects of design that CAD
has left unaddressed. These aspects include, but are not limited to, how parts relate
functionally and physically, how configurations vary between concepts and how design
architectures are evaluated early on.
To explore these aspects, the thesis investigates graph-based representations as a com-
plementary design tool.
Graphs have long been used in design theory and systems

Literature Review & Background
This review examines the role of design representations in mechanical engineering, first
focusing on the influence and limitations of CAD, and then, motivating graph represen-
tations as a complementary approach.
2.1
Design Representations
According to cognitive science, one way that humans solve large complex problems is
by breaking them down into multiple layers of abstraction (Shah and Wilson 1989). In
design, this is reflected in the designer’s use of various design representations (Figure 2.1).
These are visual tools that help designers explore, evaluate and communicate designs
at different levels of detail. Some examples include sketches, textual descriptions, DSM
matrices (Eppinger and Browning 2012), CAD models and technical drawings (Atilola
et al. 2016; Sarica et al. 2023). Design representations are at the essence of design
work, and the choice of representations greatly impacts the outcome of the design. A
study from MIT found that the original representation used in conceptualization can be
predicted with an accuracy of 48% better than random by other designers (Tsai and
Yang 2017). Additionally, the level of fidelity of the representation is a critical factor
influencing the generation of innovative ideas (Viswanathan and Linsey 2013).
Design stages vary in their levels of determination, with early phases being more open-
ended and exploratory, while later stages require increasing specificity (Howard et al.
2009). To support effective progress through these stages, designers should choose rep-
resentations that reflect these levels. Representations should neither impose premature
constraints nor hinder the communication of the current design state. The ideal de-
sign representation conveys the maximum amount of information while minimizing the
resources required to produce them (Macomber and Yang 2011). Research by Veisz
et al. (2012) supports this principle, suggesting that sketching is particularly effective
during early conceptual phases, while CAD tools offer greater eﬀiciency in later stages.
In practice, however, CAD has become the dominant tool across most of mechanical en-
gineering, often being used from the very beginning of the design process. The following
section examines this role and the influence it has on the design process.

2.2
Limitations of CAD in the Engineering Design
Process
Given the increasing reliance on CAD, several studies have already examined the impli-
cations it has on the design process. Especially, its impact has been studied in the early
stages of design, which are critical, since the decisions made here are said to make up
70-80% of the design life cycle costs (Saravi et al. 2008). A central concern is the risk
of design fixation, defined as “a state in which someone engaged in a design task under-
takes a restricted exploration of the design space due to an unconscious bias resulting
from prior experiences, knowledge or assumptions” (Crilly and Cardoso 2017). While
this definition emphasizes limitations arising from prior exposure, fixation can also be
reinforced by the very tools used during design, which has been shown to be true in the
case of CAD (Robertson and Radcliffe 2009).
CAD tools prompt the user to specify a level of geometric precision that can be in-
appropriate for early-stage design.
Their underlying structure is based on boundary
representations that mathematically define curves, faces, and vertices, whereas concep-
tual designs typically lack this level of specificity (Bernal et al. 2015). For instance,
even something as simple as designing a box requires the user to decide on exact wall
thicknesses from the outset, even if such details are not yet meaningful. As a result,
using CAD too early can constrain creative exploration. In their study, Robertson and
Radcliffe found that some designers circumvent this issue by using CAD systems more
loosely, for example by treating them as drafting tools without relying on parametric
constraints. While such workarounds are possible, they also noted that once a design is
modeled in CAD, designers tend to be less willing to make fundamental changes. Other
studies support this observation, suggesting that CAD models can create a sense of
completeness that discourages iteration, feedback, or reconsideration of earlier decisions
(Robertson and Radcliffe 2009; Isa et al. 2024; Fixson and Marion 2010; Yang 2005).
Returning to the earlier example, while the wall thickness of the box can certainly be
modified later, studies indicate that early decisions, once modeled in a CAD, are often
treated as more fixed than they need to be.
Some research has also claimed that CAD can be too constraining and slow during the
early phases of design. Macomber and Yang (2011) recommended that when selling
a concept, one should use sketches rather than CAD renderings, as sketches tend to
be more visually appealing and much quicker to produce. However, it is important to
consider that while the process of hand-sketching has not changed, CAD interfaces have
continued to improve in both usability and accessibility over the last decades. There
have been found indications of a generational divide in how designers perceive CAD’s
role in idea generation (Veisz et al. 2012). When asked if CAD should be used for idea
generation, experienced designers tend to advise against it, while novices tend to support
it. Although the study does not investigate whether these views shift with experience,
another possible explanation is that comfort with CAD may simply depend on training.
Increasingly, design students are using CAD software instead of hand-sketching (Atilola
et al. 2016).
Robertson and Radcliffe (2009) began their study with the assumption that the complex-
ity of CAD interfaces would constrain the designer. However, their findings suggested
the opposite. They introduced the concept of “positive circumscribed thinking,” where

the ease of adding features in CAD encourages designers to keep refining their models
in pursuit of perfection. Rather than constraining designers, the real issue becomes a
tendency toward over-designing. A way to understand this is through the distinction
between vertical transformations and lateral transformations. CAD works well in sup-
porting vertical transformations, where more detail is added to a single idea. In contrast,
it is less effective at supporting lateral transformations, i.e. moving from one design
idea to another. This was demonstrated in a study by Self et al. (2009), which found
that CAD was the least effective among various design tools evaluated when it came to
supporting lateral transformations.
Despite the limitations discussed above, this thesis argues that any attempt to exclude
CAD from early-stage design is inherently outdated and misaligned with the realities
of modern design practice.
Instead, the limitations of CAD should be addressed by
pairing it with tools that support creative exploration, for example, by enabling lateral
transformations. A recent study examined workflows in architectural education and found
that combining CAD with sketching can lead to more creative outcomes than using either
method alone (Shih and Sher 2021). Correspondingly, in a study of later-stage design
behavior, Snider (2014) show that switching between different types of representations
is associated with increased creativity.
Overall, this review shows that while CAD is effective for modeling parts in great detail,
it can also constrain exploration in earlier stages. Sketches and hybrid approaches help
maintain creativity and support exploration of the solution space. Nevertheless, there is
a clear shift towards digital workflows and an increasing reliance on CAD. The challenge
is not only to respond to the limitations of CAD, but to do so in a way that aligns with
how engineering work is evolving in practice.
2.3
Graph Representations
A graph can be simply defined as a data structure consisting of nodes that are connected
by lines known as edges (Figure 2.2). The findings of the last section can be used to
motivate graph-based representations in design. First, by focusing on high-level struc-
ture, graphs do not force designers to make premature and potentially fixating decisions
on geometry. Their lightweight nature allows a lateral exploration of alternative assem-
bly configurations without reworking full CAD models. Moreover, graphs can easily be
encoded and manipulated digitally, aligning with the broader shift toward computer-
aided workflows. Given these characteristics, it is worth exploring whether graph-based
representations can meaningfully support digital design processes.
Before developing a new graph-based design tool it is important to recognize that graph
representations are already used throughout engineering design. These include, but are
certainly not limited to, block diagrams, flowcharts, circuit graphs, knowledge graphs

Additionally, graph representations allow the use of graph grammars, which are systems
that automate design through the iterative application of predefined rewriting rules. Voss
et al. (2023) recently reviewed engineering applications that employ grammars.
In engineering design theory, several established graph-based methods are available.
Functional models decompose problems into specific tasks, clarifying the problem and
supporting the designer in the synthesis of embodied solutions. One example is the func-
tion structure described by Pahl and Beitz (1996) that maps the flow of material, energy
and signals with nodes representing functions. As the design progresses, each function
is divided into additional levels of sub-functions and the solution becomes increasingly
detailed. Another example is the function-means tree (Hansen and Andreasen 2002),
which hierarchically maps both functions and the means that realize them. In this model,
means are nodes that describe how to achieve the various functions. A means can be
anything from an entire subsystem, such as a cooling circuit that prevents overheating,
to a local feature of a single part, such as a high-friction surface finish that prevents
slipping.
The starting point for the representation used in this project is a graph where nodes
represent components and edges represent physical connections between parts. This
is sometimes referred to as a liaison graph, following the work of Bourjault (1984)
in assembly sequence planning. Alternative names include parts-connection diagrams
(Abell et al. 1991), assembly graphs (K. D. D. Willis et al. 2021), or contact graphs,
though definitions may vary across contexts (Wikipedia 2025). Representing mechanical
assemblies in this way supports analysis of product structure, kinematic constraints,
tolerance chains and more. Additional connection types can easily be added to support
more information than strictly physical contacts, such as critical alignments, functional
relationships etc. (Figure 2.3).
Expanding the analysis from the component-level perspective of mechanical assemblies
to broader systems places us within the domain of systems engineering. In Model-Based
Systems Engineering (MBSE), the industry standard representation is the Systems Mod-
eling Language (SysML) (Object Management Group 2025), which can also be seen as
a graph-based representation. In SysML, the assembly graph discussed before would
correspond to an internal block diagram. This is only one out of nine diagram types that

together capture both requirements, structure, behavior, and parametric relationships.
SysML integrates multiple perspectives of a system into a coherent graphical framework,
facilitating communication, coordination, and analysis across different engineering disci-
plines. Because of the level of detail it supports, SysML is used primarily in industries
that manage the largest and most complex systems engineering tasks, notably aerospace,
automotive, and defense (Sparx Systems Europe 2025). However, implementing MBSE
and SysML in practice can present notable challenges (Chami and Bruel 2018). For
example, a case from NASA’s Glenn Research Center described how adoption involved
a significant time investment, largely due to the diﬀiculty of learning SysML tools and
interpreting complex models (Parrott et al. 2016). While SysML is widely available, the
fact that even an organization like NASA encountered these challenges helps explain why
it has not seen widespread adoption in most companies as a graph-based tool to support
the development process.
Despite the existence of a range of alternative design representations, including graph-
based approaches, contemporary mechanical engineering practice remains centered around
CAD. This suggests that existing methods may not fully align with the workflows, pri-
orities, and needs of practitioners. This thesis takes that gap as a starting point and
explores how a graph-based representation can be shaped to better fit within current
design practice.
Note on literature search
Relevant literature for this chapter was identified through targeted database
searches and citation tracking. Initial searches were performed using DTU Findit
with search strings such as “creativity AND CAD AND (embodiment OR detailed
design)” and other combinations including words such as “product development”,
“fixation”, “representation” and “modality”.
Additional papers were identified
through citation tracking and using the graph-based discovery tool ResearchRab-

Methodology
The overall objective of this project was to develop a digital, graph-based design repre-
sentation to support the engineering design process and respond to limitations in current
CAD practices. In the chosen Design & Innovation approach, achieving this required not
only technical development, but also investigation of how such a representation could fit
into engineers’ workflows, address usability challenges, and explore its potential market
fit. An initial period was spent on literature review and planning to establish the problem
framing and plan the development process. This was followed by the development phase
itself structured around iterative sprints. After development, the remaining time was
allocated to documentation and final reporting (Figure 3.1).
3.1
Process Model
The process model for the development phase was shaped by Agile software development
(Beck et al. 2001). Although the project involved developing software, Agile was chosen
primarily because its principles aligned with the exploratory nature of the work. The
most prominently applied principles were:
• Focus on working software: Because graph representations could otherwise
appear too academic or intangible to users, relatively high-fidelity prototypes were
prioritized throughout the project to enable concrete and actionable feedback.
• Responding to change over following a fixed plan: The project faced un-
certainty in both the problem and solution space. While the literature provided
an initial direction, the relevant market application was not yet defined. On the
solution side, many technical methods still needed to be explored and learned. For
these reasons, flexibility was a key requirement for the process, to allow adjust-
ments as new opportunities or technical constraints emerged.
• Individuals and interactions over processes and tools: Prior experience with
graph representations created a risk of bias in evaluating the intuitiveness and use-
fulness of the concept. Continuous dialogue with users was therefore prioritized to
challenge assumptions and include external perspectives throughout development.

• Sprint Goal
Each sprint was guided by a defined sprint goal, providing an immediate objective

3.2
Testing Methods
As this project followed an exploratory, Agile development process, user testing was
conducted qualitatively with small participant groups. The goal was not statistical gen-
eralization, but to iteratively identify issues and inform development through continuous
user involvement.
Testing was conducted after each sprint. In total, nine testing sessions were completed,
each lasting at least an hour, often longer. An overview of the participants and applied
methods is given in Table 3.1. Each testing round involved two participants with similar
backgrounds. This made it possible to observe repeated issues while keeping the analysis
manageable. Although additional participants would have improved generalizability, the
extra post-processing time was deemed less valuable than allocating that time to other
tasks, such as development.
In all cases, the testing methods were qualitative, using structured interviews (subsec-
tion 3.2.1) and/or the Think-Aloud Protocol (subsection 3.2.2). It was also considered
whether to more use quantitative tests based on larger sample sizes, for example, surveys
or A/B-testing. While this might have led to more definitive conclusions, the choice to
prioritize qualitative methods was guided by Agile principles, which emphasize human
interaction. The flexibility of a qualitative approach was seen as important, as the goal
was not only to gather clear-cut answers, but also to refine how the product should
be communicated. Observing participants’ reactions and adjusting the questions ac-
cordingly was expected to provide insights into how to present and frame the product
effectively.
After each interview, data was extracted by re-listening to the recordings while taking
free-form notes that captured the points considered essential for the subsequent steps of
the project. In case of the the Think Aloud Protocol sessions, the interviewees’ screens
were also recorded to make it possible to review issues as they arose (Figure 3.3a). All
interview notes are collected in Appendix C.

3.3
Business Model
The business model perspective was included in the project to connect product-level
decisions with a broader strategic direction aimed at a potential market application. To
support this, the Lean Canvas framework developed by Maurya (2012) was applied. In
this context, the framework served as what Morris et al. (2005) describe as a “focus-
ing device tying strategic choices together,” helping to give an overview and highlight
what needed to be considered. In addition, the Software Business Model Framework
developed by Schief (2014) was applied to analyze existing business models and to aid
the understanding of software-specific considerations relevant for the project. The two
frameworks are presented here.
3.3.1
Lean Canvas
The Lean Canvas, developed by Maurya (2012) as an adaptation of the Business Model
Canvas (Osterwalder and Pigneur 2010), was chosen for this project. According to the
creator (Maurya 2015), it was designed as a more actionable version addressing the high
degree of uncertainty in early-stage startups
In this project, the Lean Canvas was not used to define a complete business model, but
to surface key open questions that could guide the following stages. As these questions
emerged, it became clear that further insights were needed beyond existing knowledge
of software business models. To support this, businesses with comparable products were
analyzed to help inform the next steps.
3.3.2
Software Business Model Framework
To support the analysis of existing businesses, a framework was sought that would draw
attention to aspects specific to software business models. These aspects might have
been overlooked when using a more generic, blank canvas approach.
The Software
Business Model Framework developed by Schief (2014) was selected for this purpose,
as it includes software-specific fields such as “software stack,” “maintenance model,”
“license model,” and several others, enabling a more detailed analysis (Table 3.2). The
results of the analysis are included in Appendix B.
In total, seven businesses were analyzed using the framework. Three of them, Miro,
RD8 Suite, and PowerPoint, had been identified during the interviews as alternative
solutions addressing similar needs to those targeted by the prototype. Cameo, Capella,
and Simulink, were selected as other examples of graph-based modeling tools within
engineering. In addition, the SysML standard was analyzed, even though it is not a
business, to explore the alternative strategy of developing a standard or open-source
solution rather than a purely commercial product.
The analysis involved reviewing each parameter based on publicly available information.
For each parameter, a value was selected from the available options in the framework, and
a brief note was added to explain the reasoning. After completing the framework tables,
two-dimensional plots were created to explore relationships between selected parameters.
The visual processing was exploratory and not intended as a formal statistical analysis.

3.4
Coding & Development
Approaching the coding work, a central trade-off was considered. On the one hand,
minimizing technical debt and ensuring a logical architecture was necessary to keep the
code maintainable. Otherwise, it would suddenly have become too hard to develop the
prototype. On the other hand, developing value-adding features at a high pace was key
to maximizing learning and the main point of the product in the first place.
In addressing this trade-off, development decisions prioritized maintaining momentum in
functionality while keeping the code-base suﬀiciently structured to support continuous
progress. While technical debt was accumulated to some extent, choices were made with
the limited time-frame of the Master’s project in mind, aiming to keep complexity at a
manageable level for the duration of the work.
Throughout the project, the code was actively commented, and a visual overview of the
software architecture was developed in each sprint, as seen on Figure 3.5. The overviews
are included in the worksheets in Appendix A. Technical consideration notes were added
directly on the worksheets during development. In many cases, these notes led directly
to PBIs that were added to the backlog.
The documentation of the pythonocc module by Paviot (2018) was frequently used as
a reference for code examples. In addition, GitHub Copilot was employed as a coding
assistant in various ways. It was used both for questions, for example, about appropriate
data structures: “suggest a data structure to store items copied to a clipboard”, and
for execution of automated edits: “remove all methods and functionality related to the
tableView class and delete the class”. The use of AI-assisted coding helped reduce the
time spent on repetitive or boilerplate code, allowing more attention to be directed
towards interaction design and architectural decisions.

4.2
The Azembra Application: Value Proposition
Azembra is a digital modeling environment developed to support high-level mechanical
engineering design through a structured yet lightweight representation. It enables users
to represent their designs as graphs, where nodes are components and edges are con-
nections representing either physical connections or abstract relationships. With this
representation, users can:
1. Reason about the assembly structure
By modeling their designs as graphs, users gain a clear visual overview of how
components are connected. Understanding these couplings early supports modular
thinking and helps reduce unnecessary rework, since changes to one part often
affect other connected components.
2. Work with existing CAD data
Recognizing that most engineering work is done in CAD, designers can import
their existing drafts or finished CAD models to generate graph representations.
As found in the literature study, complementary representations can help spark
creativity.
3. Systematically evaluate interfaces
With every connection visualized, the designer can systematically review each one
to ensure that the interfaces are appropriately designed. To aid the overview, the
interfaces can be named and further visualized with images and sketches.
4. Analyze robustness
By inspecting loops in the graph, designers get an indication of possible over-
constraints and tolerance chains. This task is relevant both in mechanical en-
gineering courses and in industry (referred to as “what-is-guiding-what” in the
interviews, subsection 5.1.1). Furthermore, the connections can be filtered based
on translational dimensions, which is key to identifying tolerance chains.
5. Design on a higher level of abstraction
The software enables conceptual work without requiring detailed geometric defi-
nitions. For example, instead of modeling a housing with a specific thickness and
shape, the designer can quickly draw a sketch or insert an indicative image. Work-
ing with a minimum level of detail allows the designer to eﬀiciently explore overall
configurations before choosing one to focus on. This supports lateral rather than
vertical exploration, as discussed in the literature review.
Existing graph-based approaches for high-level design tend to fall into two categories.
Some are improvised and unstructured, sometimes leading to conceptual design not being
facilitated at all. Others are heavy systems engineering tools that, while powerful, are
overly complex and not suited to the workflows of most mechanical engineering teams.
Through a digital, user experience-focused environment, Azembra aims to make the
above-mentioned design activities intuitive, ultimately supporting the development of
better engineering solutions.

Several features have been added to enrich the visual representation of the graph. For
example, by clicking a component, users can assign colors to indicate custom groupings
or categories. When selecting “More” on a new connection or “Edit Connection” on an
existing one, users can label the connection, define whether it represents a “physical” or
“abstract” relationship, and specify its kinematic directions. Connections can later be
filtered by direction, allowing engineers to focus on relationships along specific axes or
planes.
In most regards, the user interface follows the expected behavior of modern software. It
supports common shortcuts, such as ctrl+z/y for undo and redo, delete for removal of
elements, ctrl+c/v for copy and paste, holding ctrl for additive selection and holding alt
while dragging a component for duplication. By holding and dragging across the screen,
multi-selections can be made, which can the be copied and pasted into other tabs to
support the creation of design variations (Figure 4.7).

backend hosted on a Linux server. The two components communicate over HTTPS and
WebSockets, enabling interactive functionality such as file upload, graph visualization,
and real-time processing feedback.

Frontend
The frontend of the application is built with standard web technologies: HTML and

Backend
The backend is developed using Python with the Flask framework. For STEP file pro-
cessing, the pythonocc library is used, which allows parsing and analysis of geometry
from CAD files. During processing, NumPy is used for numerical operations and trimesh
is used for mesh-based operations. This enables basic inference detection and the con-
struction of the graphs.
Azembra Workspace File Format
To store complete workspaces, a custom JSON-based format called AWSP (Azembra
WorkSPace) is used. Each file contains graph data, including node positions, edges,
labels, and visual elements such as sketches and images. Images are stored in base64,
and node positions are saved as normalized coordinates to ensure layout consistency
across screens. Metadata like tab titles and the last active view is included, allowing
users to reload or share their work with all content and layout preserved.

potential future features on a scale from 1 to 5. The three responses varied considerably
and did not show any clear patterns. Given the very limited sample size, the ranking was
not used to guide feature prioritization but served as a small additional input illustrating
the differing needs across user groups. Ultimately, it was concluded that feature require-
ments would be better identified through future pilot projects, where functionality could
be developed and tested in connection to actual use cases.
The findings of this sprint resulted in the following implications for the project:
• Concrete points of critique in the landing page communication were imme-
diately addressed and are reflected in the currently online version (as seen in
Chapter 4).
• The tool was now seen as suﬀiciently functional to be applied in real de-
sign work by both students and professionals, leading to opportunities for
extended pilot use in both settings.
• The question of product maturity beyond core functionality was identified as
a critical factor for organizational adoption, highlighting a need to involve
relevant expertise to address aspects such as data security, reliability, and
maintainability.

Discussion
This chapter reflects on the findings and design outcomes of the project in relation to
the four main objectives, highlighting key insights, discussing limitations, and presenting
ideas for the future development of the tool.
6.1
Objective 1
Investigate the limitations of a CAD-first design process and the potential of graph
representations
The literature review highlights key limitations of a CAD-first design process. In partic-
ular, it points to the risk of design fixation due to premature decisions enforced by the
high fidelity of the representation. While CAD excels at refining a single design direction,
it offers limited support for lateral explorations, tends to discourage feedback, and can
lead to over-complex parts. In the interviews, engineers explained how diagrams drawn
in PowerPoint or on ad hoc drawings help them build the appropriate structure. The re-
liance on these improvised representations reinforces two assumptions from the literature
review. First, complementary graph representations can help address CAD’s limitations.
Second, no purpose-built tool currently supports this process. Despite the small sample
size, these findings align industry practices with the literature-based problem definition.
This justified the development of a graph-based tool. Beyond that, specific aspects of
the problem also informed the functionality. For example, the limited support for lateral
exploration motivated the introduction of a tab system that allows parallel variations to
be explored side by side. The challenge of constrained feedback inspired the idea of a
shared, collaborative workspace. Moreover, the creative benefit of using complementary
design representations shaped the vision of enabling seamless integration of CAD data
into the graph-based environment.
The focus on integration was also important due to limitations in the graph representation
itself. As noted, design representations should not impose premature constraints, as can
be the case with CAD, but they should not hinder communication of the current design
state either. While graphs offer flexibility by remaining abstract, this same quality can
make them less effective for conveying detailed design information. Sketches, images,
and colors were added to enrich the graph-based visualization, but they do not replace
the precision of a CAD model. Thus, the combination is essential.
In this project, relatively little attention was initially given to selecting the type of graph
that would form the basis of the representation. The decision to use a contact graph
was primarily informed by prior work and justified by a general openness to adjust the
representation based on future findings. However, there are important differences be-
tween a contact graph and other graphs, such as functional models or a process flow
diagrams. Thus, once the contact graph was adopted, fully switching to another type
would admittedly have been diﬀicult. The advantage of the contact graph was its com-
patibility with existing CAD data, allowing for automatic extraction. Starting with a
40
Development & Commercialization of Innovative Digital Design Tools
==PAGE:51==
contact graph, functional information could theoretically be added by the user after-
wards, whereas starting from a purely functional model would not allow for the same
level of automation. In that sense, contact graphs were a suitable starting point.
The project set out to create a separate, complementary representation rather than trying
to intervene directly in existing CAD systems. Alternatively, the work could have focused
more closely on the limitations of CAD as they occur in practice, for example by studying
how specific interaction patterns could be improved as they occur. Given the central role
of existing CAD in engineering workflows, there would have been a strong case for trying
to address the issues within the tools already in use. However, due to the diﬀiculty of
modifying proprietary platforms, the decision was made to build a self-contained tool.
That does not rule out future directions where the same ideas are brought closer to
existing workflows, for example through a plugin.
6.2
Objective 2
Design and implement an interactive graph-based tool as an alternative design
representation
The development followed an Agile methodology, structured through selective elements
of Scrum. This approach was chosen to allow for flexibility, given the exploratory nature
of the project, and with the recognition that the initial academic framing might not
resonate with industry stakeholders without a tangible prototype. This choice proved
beneficial, as it enabled the prototype to reach a relatively high level of fidelity within
the available time. This was instrumental in the successful demonstration leading to the
commitment to pilot studies.
The fact that the implementation progressed steadily throughout the entire thesis period
indicates that a reasonable balance was achieved between delivering user-facing function-
ality and limiting technical debt. For future development, more attention will be directed
toward code structure and maintainability. This is relevant in light of feedback from the
final interview, which emphasized the importance of product maturity as a prerequisite
for adoption in professional engineering environments.
Due to a long initial planning phase and a required documentation phase at the end, the
project included only four sprints. This limits the extent to which some of the chosen
methods may be evaluated. Whereas the usefulness of the Think-Aloud Protocol was
evident from the first usability test, the value of the Lean Canvas as a continuously
iterated working document cannot be fully assessed based on only two applications.
Several aspects of the prototype were consciously down-prioritized due to their technical
complexity but were not excluded from the long-term vision for the product. In particular,
the graph extraction algorithm remains an area requiring further development, as it
occasionally produces incorrect results when faced with atypical STEP file structures or
computational constraints. Given that this is not a computer science thesis and that
user value could still be meaningfully explored despite these limitations, a more advanced
implementation was considered beyond the scope of the project.
Another area deferred was the development of collaborative workspace functionality.

This was expected to require significant backend work as suggested in the interviews.
Furthermore, testing collaborative environments would have required larger group ses-

During the project, several architecture diagrams were developed. Although this was only

graph representations. They supported structural thinking, while the code itself served
as the specific implementation, similar to how CAD models represent detailed geometry.
By using the diagrams, it became possible to identify which parts of the system should
be separated and to gain an overview of areas for improvement. Whether this reflects
a personal bias or a more general design principle, it reinforces the potential of graph
representations as tools for structuring complex design problems.
6.3
Objective 3
Continuously evaluate the usefulness of the graph-based tool for mechanical engi-
neering design
Testing was done continuously at the end of each iterative sprint. The Think-Aloud
Protocol proved especially effective, as the observations could be immediately translated
into actionable improvements. This led to a significant increase in prototype quality
following Sprint 2. The combination of Agile development and Think-Aloud testing is
considered a strong candidate for the default approach in future iterations of the tool.
The structured interviews also contributed important insights, particularly when partici-
pants were allowed to digress beyond the planned questions. It was more productive to
treat the interview guide as a flexible starting point with follow-up questions going in to
relevant areas that had not been previously anticipated. For example, questions about
features like grammar systems or design-for-assembly were not especially engaging to
users in Sprint 1. On the other hand, when it became apparent that the participants
were already working with graph-based representations, this led to more meaningful
discussions that informed the direction of subsequent development.
While the focus on a few well-suited participants aligns well with the Agile development-
driven process, it also limits the ability to draw broader conclusions. A different strat-
egy, involving more participants and quantitative testing, could have provided greater
certainty in that regard. However, this would have required more extensive planning and
longer study periods, which would likely have slowed development. With a less developed
concept, it could have been harder to clearly communicate the core idea of the tool and
gather actionable insights.
For future work, with greater emphasis on commercialization, involving multiple pilot
studies or companies will be important. This is not only beneficial for improving product
quality but was also raised as a consideration in discussions with DTU Skylab. Demon-
strating interest from several industry sources will be essential in building a convincing
business case and securing funding.

Regarding the usefulness of the system itself, even with the small number of participants
interviewed, it was clear that its perceived value depended heavily on the individual and
their existing workflows. The senior mechanical engineer engaged with the graph-based
concept from the beginning, while the students initially found it diﬀicult to see how it
could fit into their way of working. However, as the prototype became more detailed,
one of the students expressed interest in using it for their own project. This suggests
that some level of utility was unlocked, even for users who did not already work with
similar representations.
The differences in perceived usefulness were also reflected in how participants responded
to the automatic graph generation feature. For the professional participants, who were
already accustomed to working with graphs, the feature initially created confusion. It
seemed to imply a fully automated workflow, which did not align with their expectations
of an interactive design tool. For the students, however, this automatic generation served
a different purpose. It made the abstract concept of graph-based design more concrete
and helped them understand how the visual structure related to the underlying model.
Nevertheless, even with help from the automatic graph generation, the abstract nature
of the representation could still pose a challenge for some users. Therefore, future work
should also investigate how the readability can be made clearer. For example, one could
imagine standard icons for certain components types, like fasteners, or nodes that reflect
the physical size of components.
Finally, it is worth considering what the ideal balance is between automated graph gener-
ation and a manual, interactive approach in terms of supporting the designer. Mechanical
engineers from industry noted that the value of the graphs lay in how the manual draw-
ing process supported their reasoning. If that is the case, greater emphasis should be
placed on the interactive aspect, even though a sophisticated, automatically generated
graph may offer high usability. This is a particularly relevant discussion in the context of
student projects, where the primary objective is learning. At the same time, this should
not be used as an argument against technological advancement. A useful analogy can
be drawn to the use of generative AI, both in education and in industry. While the
short-term benefits of such tools are clear, the longer-term consequences of increased
reliance on automated output are not yet fully understood.
6.4
Objective 4
Explore and discuss business considerations for positioning a graph-based design
tool in the engineering software market
The purpose of including the business aspect in the project was to guide the value
creation. While the academic arguments for the graph representations in general were
considered valid, it was also observed that most methods do not successfully transfer
to industry practices. For this reason, a market positioning was explored using meth-
ods such as the Lean Canvas and the Software Business Model Framework from Schief
(2014). The latter was used to analyze the strategies of existing companies to draw
inspiration from. In addition, external viewpoints from software business entrepreneurs
were included to help uncover blind spots and provide direct recommendations to the
project.

Within the time-frame of the project, the business model was not developed to a point
where detailed elements such as pricing or licensing could be meaningfully defined. In-
stead, efforts focused on clarifying the core value proposition and identifying early adopter
segments, which are necessary prerequisites for refining the business model later. Pilot
studies in educational and industrial settings represent a realistic next step for testing
value delivery, conditions for adoption and ultimately monetization paths.
One of conceptualizing the position of the product is as a way of turning informal and ad
hoc engineering tasks done by some engineers into a more structured and eﬀicient digital
process. A key challenge with this framing is that the tool does not only need to be sold
as a solution for a specific type of workflow. In many cases, the workflow itself would
need to be introduced and promoted to potential users. While some engineers, such
as the senior mechanical engineers interviewed during the project, already use similar
methods, it was clear that many of their colleagues do not. Their work tends to follow a
more of a CAD-first approach. For these users, both the underlying process and the tool
would need to be introduced and justified. In addition, for the engineers who already use
this kind of workflow, it has so far been possible to manage with informal or improvised
methods. As a result, their need for a dedicated tool may not feel urgent, and they may
expect a relatively low price in order to consider switching, which could challenge the
viability of the product.
A different way of framing the product builds on its similarity to systems engineering
tools. These tools are in demand in industry but often remain inaccessible to smaller
companies due to their complexity. Azembra could be positioned as a simplified and more
approachable version of such tools, aimed at less demanding engineering contexts. The
current graph structure already resembles the internal block diagram of SysML, and with
a few additional features, the tool could potentially evolve into a kind of “SysML light.”
Given the presence of a heavily regulated industry like medtech or pharma in Denmark,
this framing could point to a more promising market segment without requiring a major
shift in the underlying technology. The existing graph structure already aligns well with
needs related to traceability and system-level structuring. Furthermore, since the testing
in Sprint 4 indicated that the landing page did not clearly communicate what the tool
specifically does, drawing on more established terms such as systems engineering could
help users more quickly contextualize the product.
Another argument in favor of a systems engineering direction comes from a key insight
in Sprint 3. In one of the interviews, it was noted that retaining customers often depends

on controlling critical parts of their data. Currently, the type of data that the software
supports is often left in ad hoc formats like PowerPoint presentations. It has not been
validated at scale whether companies see a clear benefit in moving this kind of analysis
into a structured system. However, systems engineering practices involve organizing data
such as requirement specifications and traceability links. In fields like medtech, where
this level of structure is crucial, aligning the tool with such practices could strengthen its

A final consideration relates to how the product could be framed within the domain of
AI. As noted in the introduction, one of the key challenges in applying AI to engineering
design is that CAD data primarily captures geometry rather than design intent.
As
a result, current AI applications in engineering design remain limited.
For example,
the text-to-CAD tool developed by Zoo (Zoo Inc 2025) allows users to generate 3D
models from natural language descriptions. Although the functionality is impressive,
it still relies on the designer having fully conceptualized the part in their mind. The
benefit is, therefore, mainly to accelerate the modeling process, not the design process.
Furthermore, the output only consists of individual components, not assemblies.
The graph environment developed in this project may be able to capture the missing
layer of information by explicitly modeling relationships such as fixation, alignment, or
clearance etc.
Instead of interpreting two shapes simply placed near each other, an
AI system could begin to understand them as functionally connected components. This
could support future AI applications capable of producing full assemblies based on higher-
level requirements instead of purely geometric descriptions.

potential of AI, there could suddenly be a strong incentive to adopt tools like Azembra.

a step toward bringing engineering design into the age of AI

Conclusion
This thesis set out to explore how a digital, graph-based design tool can support the
engineering design process. As a starting point, several limitations of CAD-first workflows
were identified, most notably the risk of design fixation, which can inhibit lateral and
conceptual exploration of the solution space. Graph representations were proposed as a
complementary approach, provided they overcome current barriers such as the lack of
appropriate tools and the complexity of existing alternatives.
The development process was structured through sprints based on Agile principles. This
allowed for continuous refinement of both the tool itself and its strategic direction. User
testing was conducted with students and professionals from mechanical engineering and
software backgrounds. Some participants were later identified as potential early adopters
and expressed a willingness to contribute to future development. Notably, a verbal offer
was made to initiate a longer-term pilot study, and the prospect of academic collaboration
also appears promising.
From a business perspective, several strategic directions were explored during the project,
but the business case is still early in development. User testing and interviews suggest
that some practitioners currently rely on general-purpose tools such as PowerPoint to
model product structures. This points to a potential opportunity for a purpose-built
solution that supports high-level mechanical design in a more structured but accessible
way. Compared to full systems engineering platforms, which offer broad capabilities but
often come with high complexity, there appears to be space for a lightweight alternative
that better fits typical mechanical design workflows.
The prototype reached a functional and visually refined state. Still, several areas require
further development. These include improving the robustness of the automatic graph
generation feature, enabling real-time collaboration, and addressing organizational needs
such as data security and maintainability. Pilot studies in both educational and industrial
settings will be important to validate the tool’s value proposition and to further guide
the development.
The thesis contributes a technical solution that connects a design-theoretical problem
with a practical application relevant to real-world engineering workflows. Most design
work remains CAD-dominated, which is natural given the sophistication and usability of
modern software. While many design methods can help address the negative implications
of a CAD-first process, their adoption is often limited. The developed prototype attempts
to bridge this gap through a modern, user-friendly software tool with the overall aim of
enabling better product design.

Two different constraint checking tactics have been 
tried. Both use a rough bounding box filter to find 
candidate pairs followed by a specific constraint 
checking method.
a)
Boolean operation
Using the method
common = BRepAlgoAPI_Common(shape1, shape2)
to find the exact overlapping shape volume after 
each consecutive translation of the parts.
b)
Points sampling on meshes
Converting all parts to STL (meshes) and saving 
them locally. 
Sampling points from one component’s mesh surface 
and checking the signed distance to the other’s 
mesh surface after translation
Used the Python module, Trimesh.
In all test cases where method a) finished, the results 
were accurate.
However, for most complex assemblies, the algorithm took 
too long to be worth the wait (estimated multiple hours)
For all test cases, method b) finished in a few minutes 
max.
Some errors occurred in method b) due to insufficient 
sampling and components getting mixed up when reading the 
STEP files.
Method b) may be improved with more tactical sampling 
(e.g., corners or around bounding box intersections) and 
a more robust step file reader.
