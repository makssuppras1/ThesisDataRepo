Abstract 
During this project, the potential of three candidates was investigated for their suitability as 
indicators of the bacterial inactivation in raw milk following the application of a novel UV-C 
light-based technology, that is to replace the alkaline phosphatase activity-based method 
widely used as an indicator for thermal inactivation. The selected candidates were:  
• Loss of Vitamin C 
• Formation of lipid oxidation by-products 
• Formation of pyrimidine dimers 
The choice of candidates was based on a literature review on the state of UV-based 
technology in the dairy sector and reported experimental results on the chemical and 
biochemical changes in milk content caused by UV-exposure. Vitamin C loss was evaluated 
using a HPLC procedure, formation of lipid oxidation by-products by a thiobarbituric acid 
reactive substances (TBARS) assay, and formation of pyrimidine dimers by a combined qPCR 
and endonuclease procedure.  
A series of experiments were performed using pasteurised skimmed milk, and raw milk, 
using two UV-C light-based pasteurisation units provided by Lyras A/S; a full-scale and pilot-
scale unit, with the full-scale unit being tested on-site at the Thise dairy plant. The aim of 
the experiments was to evaluate the bacterial inactivation efficiency for the two units, and 
the effect of processing on the selected indicator candidates. Results for the inactivation of 
thermoresistant spores in pasteurised skimmed milk produced a 4.32 LOG10 reduction for a 
UV-dose ≥ 106.73 J/m2 at a flow rate of 2.33 m/s, proving the potential of the technology to 
extend shelf-life. A bacterial inactivation curve was established by processing raw milk using 
the pilot-scale unit for a series of UV-doses; 0.0, 19.8, 39.6, 48.0, 59.4, 79.2, 96.0, 144.0, and 
192.0 J/m2. A 3.15 LOG10 reduction in naturally occuring bacterial content was achieved for 
UV-doses ≥79.2 J/m2, with a calculated D-value of 17.13 J/m2 using first-order kinetics.  
Results for the UV-induced formation of lipid oxidation by-products and pyrimidine dimers 
came back inconclusive and require further refining of the used evaluation procedures. The 
loss of vitamin C as a function of increasing UV-dose showed a 62% decrease for UV-doses 
≥96.0 J/m2 and showed the most promise of the selected candidates as an indicator for the 
bacterial inactivation in raw milk for the novel UV-C technology used in this project.

1 Introduction  
A common trait applying to any industrial scale process is constant change, as technological 
advances both force and allow companies to produce goods in more efficient ways. These 
advances come through research efforts being put into the development and optimisation 
of existing or the creation of novel technologies, both a welcome opportunity to assess if a 
current state is the optimal1–3. 
This Master Thesis focuses on the latter of the two; the process of implementing a novel UV-
C light-based technology into the liquid foods processing industry with emphasis on its 
application in the Danish Dairy Sector.  
A collaboration between DTU National Food Institute and the company Lyras A/S was 
established, in order to investigate the possibility of introducing the novel technology 
possessed by Lyras A/S to the dairy sector, with focus on methods to validate the bacterial 
inactivation efficiency of their UV-C pasteurisation units. During the Thesis work, this 
resulted in on-site testing of a full-scale pasteurisation unit and small-scale experiments on a 
pilot unit at the Thise Dairy Plant in Roslev, and Lyras facilities in Aalborg, followed up by 
analytical experimental work performed at the DTU Food facilities.

1.1 Aims and Objectives 
The following briefly describes the background for this Master Thesis which looks at the 
emerging novel UV-C based pasteurisation technology in the dairy industry, with focus on 
the immediate impact on the Danish Dairy Sector from a processing point of view.  
The global dairy sector currently relies on heat-based Holder pasteurisation, one of the main 
attributors to its annual emission profile due to the energy intensive nature inherent to 
pasteurisation. As is true for every existing industrial sector, the search for the next 
replacement of an existing technology is ever present, with regards to efficiency, energy 
and/or raw material consumption, climate impact, just to name a few factors.  
 
A research effort has been put into replacing the traditional heat-based pasteurisation 
approach with alternative technologies. Amongst these are UV-C based solutions, which 
represents a less energy intensive pasteurisation technology. However, the technological 
gap has yet to be breached, in order for the technology to be implemented. Amongst the 
companies showing promise is the company Lyras, who offers and specializes in UVC-light 
based, cold pasteurisation technology, as their processing design is fully capable of 
achieving the same pasteurisation effect as thermal pasteurisation. 
 
Changing one main technology to another of course requires thorough analysis of how the 
production line will be affected, especially when handling food products. One key change 
when substituting heat with UV-C in the pasteurisation step is the loss of the Alkaline 
Phosphatase Test (ALP), which is utilized as a quality control to document efficient 
pasteurisation (i.e. the desired log-fold inactivation of bacteria) in parallel to heat- and flow-
logging. This is due to efficient UV-C doses not have the same inactivation effect on the ALP 
as heat pasteurisation does. As a result, a new quality control procedure, using a new 
indicator, to document efficient pasteurisation is needed to support the new technology.  
 
 
 
==PAGE:14==

This project aims to:  
 
• Investigate, through a literature review, the current state of the dairy sector, with 
regards to trends in practice and production, with focus on the potential utility of a 
UVC-light based pasteurisation procedure on the dairy sector.  
• Investigate, through a literature review, alternative methods for a quality control as 
an indicator to replace the current alkaline phosphatase-based procedure.  
• Perform experimental work on a Lyras UV-C pasteurisation unit, to investigate 
microbial inactivation and the validity of the suggested indicator methods.  
• Assess, based on findings in the literature review and experimental work, potential 
changes in the general dairy processing design, necessary for implementation a UV-C 
based pasteurisation procedure.

1.2 State of the Global Dairy Sector 
On a global scale, the dairy sector has seen continuous growth over the past decades, 
reaching an annual production of 887 mill ton in 2018, as reported by the International 
Farming Comparison Network (IFCN) Dairy Report of 2019. The regional distribution and 
exports for the past 5 years can be seen in Figure 1. Livestock ranges from region to region, 
with bovine, boffola, goat, and sheep being the main contributors to the annual production, 
with bovine milk contributing 82% of total milk produced [FAO; EFSA]. On average, an 
annual growth of 1.8-2.5% has been reported since the mid-1990’ies, and there is a 
projected growth of 177 mill tons for 2025.  
 
Figure 1. Annual production of milk and export of dairy per region, from [FAO, 2019]4. 
For a global industry the environmental impact is significant and contributes with an annual 
production of 2.7% of the total Greenhouse Gas (GHG) emissions, reported by [Gerber et al. 
in 2010]5, with an increase of 18% from 2005-2015 reported by the Food and Agriculture 
Organization (FAO). The latter relates this to the increase in global milk production of 30% 
during this 10-year period, e.g. establishment of new dairy plants, growth of individual dairy 
farms, increase in public demand. An estimated total of 1,711.8 mill tonnes CO2-equivalent 
GHG were emitted by the dairy sector, where production at dairy farm and processing at 
dairy plant levels listed as the two main contributors, with regional changes in the 
percentagewise distribution between the two. The growth trends and emission data 
illustrate why the industry receives a lot of attention, with regards to developing both the 
technology and procedures for dairy farms and plants.

1.3 Legislative Demands for Processing of Raw Milk 
For any food producing company, a set of legislative demands are in place to ensure the 
safety of the product consumer. For the Dairy Sector, this covers raw milk production at 
dairy farm level, all handling of raw milk, and processing of raw milk at the dairy plants. 
These regulations are the foundation of current industrial practice. From the Regulation of 
Hygiene (Hygiejnebekendtgørelsen)6 Chapter 5, § 11 it is (roughly translated) stated that: 
“for any production of dairy based goods, the raw milk supply must as a minimum be heat-
treated with a combination of heat and time corresponding to pasteurisation.”  
“§ 11. Rå mælk og rå mælkebestanddele, der indgår i fremstilling af mejeriprodukter 
og af andre fødevarer, skal som minimum varmebehandles ved en kombination af tid 
og temperatur, der svarer til pasteurisering, jf. dog stk. 3 og 4 samt § 12.” 
 
However, it is noted that this requirement for a specific time and temperature treatment 
demand can be overcome by receiving of a dispensation from the Danish Veterinary and 
Food Administration in Chapter 5, § 12: 
”§ 12. Fødevarestyrelsen kan dispensere fra kravet i § 11, stk. 1, såfremt 
virksomheden kan dokumentere, at forarbejdningen foregår under anvendelse af 
andre hygiejnemæssige foranstaltninger eller teknologier, der sikrer, at gældende 
hygiejnekriterier overholdes, og såfremt Fødevarestyrelsen vurderer, at disse 
foranstaltninger er hygiejnisk og sundhedsmæssigt forsvarlige.” 
 
The paragraph states that if presented with proper documentation, the introduction of 
alternate technologies is accepted if deemed safe by the Danish Veterinary and Food 
Administration. Herein lies the main hurdle for the Danish Dairy Sector, and presents the 
work needed to be done by companies developing novel technologies wanting to 
implement novel technologies in the processing of raw milk to produce dairy products, i.e. 
present documentation proving that;  
• the novel technology fulfils the required hygiene criteria equal to or better than 
current heat-based technology 
• the novel technology does not cause any unwanted changes in the quality of the 
product during processing of raw milk 
==PAGE:17==
17 
 
Currently, UV-based technologies are not implemented in the Danish Dairy Sector for 
pasteurisation purposes. So, in order to satisfy the regulatory demands, the first place to 
start is an analysis of how heat-based pasteurisation is implemented in the dairy sector 
today, as it is the performance of current equipment that any alternative technology must 
match to be considered a safe choice2,3,7,8. The complete chapters for the regulations on the 
production of raw milk-based food is presented in Appendix I. 
In an article published in 2016 by the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA)9, UV-based 
technology was deemed safe as an extended shelf-life (ESL) application for processing of 
milk, by evaluating the formation of the unwanted Vitamin D3, using a commercial UV-
processing unit (SurePure Turbulator) with an applied UV-dose of 1045 J/L. The assembled 
committee deemed the UV-based ESL application safe as the formation of vitamin D3 was 
insignificant, showing promise for future of UV-based technology in the dairy sector.

1.4.2 Standardisation Process 
Following the initial pasteurisation is standardisation, where the milk fat is skimmed, 
producing skimmed milk (0.1% fat) and cream (≈38% fat). The process is illustrated in Figure 
5. The standardisation device is based on a centrifugal mechanism, using the difference in 
density of the milk fat content to separate the two fractions. Standardisation occurs directly 
after the initial pasteurisation, as a temperature of 55-60 oC favourably changes the 
rheology of the milk for this process. Downstream, the high fat content cream is added to 
the skimmed milk, in order to obtain the commercial fat percentages, e.g. in Denmark four

1.4.3 Bactofucation Process 
Applying the same centrifugal mechanics as the standardisation unit, bactofuges can 
remove any bacterial spore content due to their size and density. As bacterial spores in 
general are heat resistant, this is an effective add-on for dairy processing, to further 
enhance the quality of the processed milk. The basic principle is the difference in density for 
bacterial spores and the harmless components of milk, with exception of some high-density 
casein micelles, which is separated along with the unwanted bacterial content. Again, this 
process is carried out at 55-60 oC for optimal operating conditions2,10–12,17. 
 
1.4.4 Validation of Bacterial Inactivation 
To evaluate the bacterial inactivation efficiency of heat-based pasteurisation, the standard 
method is the alkaline phosphatase (ALP) test of DS/EN ISO 11816-118. The method is a 
fluorometric determination of the alkaline phosphate activity, with results being evaluated 
in a positive/negative manner.  As seen in Figure 3 earlier, exposure to heat has a 
destructive effect on alkaline phosphatase, and here servers as an indirect indicator of the 
bacterial inactivation efficiency. In practice, samples are collected at random from the dairy 
plant processing line and analysed using this method. One disadvantage of using an indirect 
==PAGE:22==
22 
 
method such as this, is the lack of insight on how the actual pasteurisation efficiency, 
especially with regards to the heat-resistant microbial content in the processed milk. 
Another common validation method is traditional plate counts, where 1 mL of processed 
milk sample is inoculated in a bacterial growth-enhancing medium for 24-48 h at 37 oC, after 
which all visible colonies are counted, resulting in a calculated colony forming unit (CFU) per 
mL for the tested sample. Standard procedures for this approach is based on the 
“Compendium of Methods for the Microbiological examination of Foods”19. This is an 
example of a direct validation of the bacterial inactivation and has the advantage of being 
able to target different organisms, by changing the growth medium. It is however a slow- 
and time-consuming procedure, which presents a problem for a continuous processing of 
milk, as it potentially delays shipping of batches2,11,20.  
1.5 Establishing a Validation Method 
The main aim of this project is to propose a validation method for the bacterial inactivation 
for a UV-C light-based technology, as research shows the destructive effect of thermal 
pasteurisation is not transferable for UV-based solutions. Designing a procedure for 
determining the bacterial inactivation efficiency of a novel UV-C pasteurisation technology 
must take into consideration any relevant aspect related to the mechanism involved, if it is 
to be considered useful as standard industrial practice.  As both the regulatory demands and 
the standard evaluation method for the current heat-based pasteurisation processing has 
been explained, this allows for a small analysis of what such a procedure aims to do, and 
how it functions. For the ALP test, the thermal inactivation of bacteria and the decrease in 
alkaline phosphatase activity are held up against each other as a function of temperature, 
and since both display the response, the parallel relation is valid as a validation method. The 
same principle must be applied to a validation method for UV-based pasteurisation of raw 
milk; bacterial concentration as a function of increasing UV-dose must be established, and 
any compound displaying the same development can be considered an analogue indicator 
of bacterial inactivation. Furthermore, sufficient statistical data must be provided, to fully 
validate the application of the suggested validation method, if it is to be used as industrial 
practice across for the global dairy sector. The bar is set high for any potential candidate, 
which is why the simplicity of the ALP test is its strength. For UV-processing of raw milk, this 
means any naturally occurring compound, which is altered by the UV-light dose and present 
==PAGE:23==
23 
 
in stable and detectable concentrations in milk, would be a potential candidate and should 
be investigated. Additionally, the formation of UV-induced by-products are also of interest, 
as the validation method does not necessarily have to be of a destructive nature2,18,20–22.  
Listed below are some of the general criteria used when establishing a validation method. 
These can be used to evaluate and compare different suggested methods and present a 
basis for choosing between any relevant candidates2,3,11,12,21,22. 
 
• Repeatability  – The suggested method can be applied at all times 
• Reliability  
– Sensitivity of the validation method

– The required time for the results of the method to be returned 
• Difficulty

1.6.3 Summarizing the Raw Milk Content Analysis 
The data presented in the previous sections sum up the general composition of raw milk 
and will be used later in this project, when the current findings of UV-light based technology 
in the dairy sector are investigated through a literature review. Among the different milk 
components, within potential candidates reside, where especially the high protein and 
vitamin C content seem promising22,25,27–31.  
With regards to the fatty acid content, the unsaturated fatty acids could be of interest, due 
to the potential of lipid peroxidation occurring as a result of the UV exposure. The carbon-
carbon double-bonds of the unsaturated fatty acids are able to react with Reactive Oxygen 
Species (ROS), radicals formed by absorption of the photonic energy of the UV-light. This 
reaction can produce a secondary product, malondialdehyde (MDA, CH2(CHO)2), which is 
used as an organic marker for oxidative stress in analytical biochemistry. MDA is measurable 
due to its reaction with thiobarbituric acid (TBA), a recognized basis for determination 
assays for this type of oxidative stress. As a result, oxidative stress in the form of MDA 
production was selected as a potential candidate for evaluating bacterial inactivation as an 
indirect marker30,32–35.

1.7 Basic Principles of UV Based Technologies 
UV based technology has seen many applications since its germicidal effect was discovered, 
and represents a lower energy intensive, less harmful alternative for sterilization processes, 
compared to other traditional heat-based technologies, and equally important, an 
alternative to the usage of chemical agents. Widely utilized in the drinking water industry, 
due to its high efficiency and germicidal impact, rendering biological hazards harmless and 
providing a much-needed supply of clean drinking water in areas of need. 
In the liquid food processing industry, UV based technology fulfil several roles, depending 
on the application. [Koutchma, 2019]22 lists the applications in a transparent manner, as 
either safety, preservation, functionality, or chemicals and toxins destruction, presented in 
Figure 7 below. Naturally, a UV based application may fulfil multiple of these roles, but in 
the case of this Thesis, the food safety and preservation aspect is of most interest.

1.7.3 Inactivation Mechanism 
The mechanism for UV-C based bacterial inactivation lies in the photokinetic effect UV 
generated photons has on the DNA and RNA of the pathogenic organisms. On an atomic 
level, the atom-to-atom bonds are able to absorb the photonic energy and form new bonds. 
In the DNA double helix, consisting of the four different nucleotides (cytosine (C), guanin 
(G), adenine (A), and thymine (T)), inter-nucleotide bonds are able to form between 
adjacent pyrimidine-containing nucleotide pairs, i.e. cytosine and thymine in DNA, and uracil 
(U) in RNA, the thymine analogue in RNA. The most common pyrimidine dimer is the 
thymine-thymine pyrimidine dimer, but formation of T-C, C-C also occurs, with the reaction 
for T-T is shown in Figure 8 below22,36.  
 
The presence of the pyrimidine dimer prevents any DNA-replication mechanisms, as the 
DNA strain now contains a damaged site, or nick. This formation is reversible by 
endonuclease enzymatic pathways, but effectively renders any effected organism unable to 
replicate. It is notable, that the pyrimidine dimer contains a unique cyclobutane 
constellation, that is not common in organic chemistry, due to its energy-demanding state, 
i.e. the angles between the four carbon atoms are unfavourable, and will revert to its 
original or another, more energy favourable constellation if possible. As a result, exposure 
to light may revert this nick, a phenomenon known as photoreactivation. The energy 
required to alter these bonds on an atomic level is shown in Table 9 below, along with their 
corresponding wavelength36.

1.9 UV-C Light-based Applications in the Dairy Sector 
Currently, there is no reports of UV-C light-based applications being utilised in the Dairy 
Industry, but due to its clear potential as an alternative to traditional heat-based Holder 
pasteurisation it has received some attention in the research community. This can be 
explained by no commercial units being available at the moment, as well as the research 
effort investigating the mechanics involved when using the technology is still on-going. As a 
result, the technology can still be considered in its development phase, on the verge of 
achieving the same status in this particular aspect of liquid food processing as the traditional 
approaches. This chapter aims to highlight some of the work carried out by different 
research groups and analyse their results in order to obtain an understanding of the current 
state of UV-C light-based technology in the dairy sector. Any results on bacterial inactivation 
and other biochemical and chemical changes caused by the UV-treatment will also be noted, 
with regards to possible inactivation method candidates. 
Amongst the research groups investigating UV-C light-based solutions is Tatiana Koutchma, 
whose publication “Ultraviolet Light in Food Technology: Principles and Applications” from 
201922 present a great source of information on the topic. Her work covers, as the title 
suggests, the basic principles of UV technology and its application in the food processing 
industry, as well as current trends and technological developments. Due to the 
complications mentioned earlier related to the treatment of dairy products compared to 
more opaque liquid foods, the technological breakthroughs are far more present in other 
areas, as its technology is easier to integrate as i.e. experimental results and operational 
design follow expectations. This can also be attributed to the lesser extend of UV sensitive 
compounds being present in some of the other liquid foods, where dairy products contain 
UV-absorbing and -shielding compounds, as it is a more complex colloidal suspension. 
However, the research effort still provides interesting results, which showcase the potential 
of the technology. Of particular interest is the reported UV-dosage required to achieve a 
given log-reduction of bacterial content, and any additional effects on both sensory and 
chemical properties of the treated milk. 
==PAGE:39==

A key article by [Matak et al., 2005]33 describes a series of experiments investigating the 
reduction of Listeria monocytogenes in goat’s milk using a SurePure CiderPure 3500A, a unit 
designed for pasteurisation of cider, as implied by its name. A schematic of the reactor 
chamber can be seen in Figure 14 below and is built as a thin-film flow chamber, where the 
fluid passes through a 0.457 mm thick chamber around 8 UV emitting low-pressure mercury 
lamps, encased by a quartz sleeve. Turbulent flow dynamics are ensured by varying flow and 
calculating the responding Reynolds Number. They achieved a 5-LOG10 reduction with a UV-
C dosage of 15.8 mJ.cm-2 and were amongst the first to apply UV-C based technology on 
dairy products. The article is an extension of the Ph.D. thesis by Matak32, in which they also 
investigated if there were any chemical changes in the goat milk after exposure to the UV-C 
light, evaluating the oxidation of unsaturated fatty acids by measuring TBARS values, but 
these results were not included in the available article or Ph.D report [Matak, 2004]32. In 
conclusion, the design of the reactor presents difficulties with regards to achieving sufficient 
turbulence in the reactor, despite the achieved reduction in their experiments.

[Crook et al. 2015]45 investigated the bacterial inactivation of bovine milk inoculated with 
several different bacterial strains of one commercial unit, the SurePure Turbulator. The 
Turbulator is an example of a somewhat commercialised UV-C light-based pasteurisation 
unit, used by a number of different research groups, as it is a functional unit, allowing for a

standardisation of testing, instead of each research group having to custom build their own 
unit for their experiments. The Turbulator is an inside-out reactor, i.e. the UV emitting light 
source is at the centre of the reactor, with the liquid passing on the outside as a thin-film in 
a stainless-steel chamber, as seen in the schematic of the reactor presented below in Figure 
15. Four of these chambers make up the reactor. Their results showed how a 5-LOG10 
reduction of all pathogenic species is achievable using UV-C light-based technology, with a 
corresponding UV-C dosage ranging between 1,450-2,000 J/L and treatment time ranging 
from 247-329 seconds, as a result of multiple cycles through the Turbulator unit needed to 
achieve the effective UV-C dosage.

Using the next generation of SurePure Turbulator, the SurePure 40 Turbulator (SP-40), 
[Cilliers et al. 2014]25 performed a thorough study on the bacterial inactivation, biochemical, 
and sensory changes in bovine milk. This new iteration of the Turbulator consists of 40 
reactor chambers with low-pressure mercury lamps, compared to the 4 of the SP-4, and is 
able to effectively deliver a UV-C dosage of 430 mJ/cm2 for a retention time of less than 25 
seconds. For comparative reasons, experiments were carried out on raw, traditional Holder 
pasteurised (HTST), UV-C treated, and a combination of Holder and UV-C treated bovine 
milk, to investigate the effects of the different methods. The reported bacterial inactivation, 
obtained through aerobic plate count, is highest for the combined methods, with the UV-C 
and HTST treated samples displaying similar reductions of approximately 4-LOG10. Vitamins 
and other macromolecules remain unaffected by UV treatments, with the exception of a

35% decrease in cholesterol and a 67.24% increase in methionine sulfones. Lipid oxidation is 
also reported to having increased, and the missing results from [Matak et al., 2005]33 are 
mentioned as a reference to support this observation, where the increase in oxidation 
products are reported as twofold. Of particular interest from this study is the analysis of 
enzymatic activity, where qualitative determination of alkaline phosphatase and peroxidase 
are investigated. As mentioned earlier, alkaline phosphatase is the chosen compound for 
validating the bacterial inactivation of heat-based pasteurisation in the dairy sector, due to 
the destructive effect of heat on the enzyme. Results show, that the alkaline phosphatase in 
the UV treated milk was unaffected, where the HTST and UV-HTST treated samples 
displayed the expected destructive behaviour, i.e. inactivation kinetics.

proposes a method for evaluating different UV-reactor designs.

In addition to investigating the bacterial inactivation of E. coli and Bacillus cereus in both

operational conditions, evaluated by Reynolds Number, as is common practice. Their results 
show a clear relationship between an increasing Reynolds Number and bacterial 
inactivation, where it is especially clear how raw milk benefit from a more turbulent 
environment during processing, compared to skimmed milk. Naturally, due to the higher fat 
content of raw milk, it requires additional hydraulic work to achieve proper mixing, which 
must be compensated through reactor design and operational conditions. A >5-LOG10 
reduction of E. coli for a calculated UV-C dosage of 11.187 mJ.cm-2 was achieved only for the 
skimmed milk at Re > 532, with all other failing to reach this level of bacterial inactivation.

[Guneser et al., 2012]48 investigated the effects of UV-light on the water- and fat-soluble

a coiled Teflon tube around a Quartz sleeve containing 9 UV-emitting lamps. Milk was 
passed through the coil 1, 3, 5, and 7 times with each pass corresponding to a UV-dose of 
12.60 J/mL and 11.72 J/mL for cow and goat milk, respectively. Their results showed how 
vitamin C is the most UV-sensitive of the vitamins chosen for analysis, with a reported 98% 
loss after 5 passes in their reactor, where the other vitamins were less affected. They range 
the investigated vitamins according to UV-light sensitivity (high-low) as follows: C >E >A >B2.  
Despite the reported lack of effect of UV-treatment on ALP activity, the experimental results 
of [Innocente et al., 2014]49 showed a 94% reduction in ALP activity in raw milk using a 
pulsed light unit, while also achieving a 3.2 LOG reduction in bacterial content. The DK/EN 
ISO 11816-118 procedure was used to determine the ALP activity. However, during the UV-
treatment a temperature increase to 55oC was reported, which could contribute to the 
decrease in ALP activity, but the high decrease of 94% should not be possible at this 
temperature.

1.10 Conclusion of Literature Study 
The reported findings of the research groups investigating the UV-based technology as 
bacterial inactivation show the great potential of the technology, with the experimental 
setups used all achieving high bacterial inactivation results. The literature study revealed 
three main types of experimental setups: 
• Custom-built lab-scale units 
• Thin-film reactors 
• Coiled tube reactors 
All three types of design display the desired 5-LOG10 bacterial inactivation, but with different 
reported UV-doses. This speaks to the effect of reactor design on the inactivation efficiency, 
as mentioned earlier. The custom-built units are of lesser interest compared to the other 
two, but the results from research groups using these can still be usable. Thin-film and 
coiled tube reactors both aim to work around the difficulty in dosage delivery related to 
treating an opaque liquid such as milk, but design-wise present different solutions. The issue 
concerning thin-film reactors is the control of flow dynamics and handling the heat-
discharge of the UV-lamps. This is handled better by the coiled tube design, where the lamp 
and coil can be placed in a chamber with sufficient cooling.  
As for potential candidates for an indirect bacterial inactivation method, the MDA formation 
has potential, as it is a direct indicator of the UV-lights effect on unsaturated fatty acids. The 
same can be said for vitamin C, which displays the same destructive response to UV 
exposure as the traditional alkaline phosphatase test is based on. Therefore, these two were 
selected as candidates for further study for developing a bacterial inactivation method for 
the novel UV-C light-based technology of Lyras A/S. 
Candidates for bacterial inactivation method; 
• Loss of Vitamin C  
• Formation of lipid oxidation by-products 
• Formation of pyrimidine dimers

2.1 UV-C Pasteurisation Units 
Two different units were used in this study; a full- and pilot-scale unit. Both units were 
produced by Lyras A/S (Aalborg, Denmark) and used for all milk processing. Operational 
conditions and experimental work for each is presented in the following sections.  
2.1.1 Full-scale Unit 
The full-scale unit used in this project is the first unit produced by Lyras A/S for industrial 
use. This proto-type is their vision of a UV-C light-based pasteurisation unit. The reactor

inner diameter of Ø 6-7 mm. Inserted between coils and LPM lamps is a quartz plate, to 
remove any unwanted wavelengths. Each reactor is equipped with a cooling device, to 
eliminate any heat-transfer to the milk during processing.

2.3 Determination of Vitamin C Loss 
For the analysis of vitamin C loss as a result of UV-processing a HPLC/based procedure was 
used. This analysis was performed by laboratory staff at the DTU Food facilities, using an 
adapted method combining the suggested methods of [Kall et al. 1990]53 and [Esther et al. 
2013]54. The procedure includes both detection of vitamin C (ascorbic acid) and its oxidized 
form, L-Dehydroascorbic acid, by including a reduction step using Tris (2-carboxyethyl)-
phosphine hydrochloride (TCEP). Extraction is performed using meta-phosphoric and oxalic 
acids, while UPLC column separation is based on ion-pairing chromatography. Detection of 
the vitamin C peak is by UV-absorbance at 265 nm. Conversion of the detected area to 
concentration of vitamin C in the milk samples was based on a standard curve created with 
vitamin C concentrations ranging from 0.5-100 µg/mL.

2.4 Determination of MDA Formation

TBARS Assay Kit from (Cayman Chemicals, Ann Arbor, MI, USA). The assay is based on the 
formation of an adduct between MDA and TBA at acidic conditions after 1 h at 100 oC, 
measurable at 530-540 nm. The reaction scheme is illustrated in Figure 28 below.  
The kit was primarily designed to analyse the oxidative stress in plasma, serum, urine, tissue

2.5 Determination of Pyrimidine Dimer Formation 
For the determination of pyrimidine dimer formation as a function of increased UV-dose, a 
two-step procedure was established; a DNA isolation procedure was used, to obtain 
samples of the affected bacterial DNA, followed by a quantitative Polymerase Chain 
Reaction (qPCR) procedure.  
2.5.1 DNA Isolation Procedure 
For the DNA isolation a commercial DNeasy PowerSoil Assay kit (QIAGEN, Hilden, DE) was 
used. The kit uses a series of preparation steps: 
1. Pellet formation 
2. Lysis 
3. Inhibitor removal 
4. Binding of DNA to Silica spin-filter 
5. Ethanol wash 
6. Release of DNA from Silica spin-filter 
==PAGE:58==

Pellet formation is a centrifugal process, where the higher density of the bacterial content in 
the milk samples will result in a precipitate at the bottom of the liquid container. Excess 
supernatant is removed, and the pellet is processed following the PowerSoil Assay kit 
procedure. As mentioned earlier, centrifugal processing of milk will leave any milk fat 
content as a top layer, which was removed for all samples using sterile cotton swaps. The 
supernatant phase will stay cloudy (colloidal suspension of milk proteins), and may contain 
some bacterial content, relevant for the following qPCR analysis. To properly assess this, a 
comparative pre-experiment was conducted. Samples of 1 mL milk with and without milkfat 
(MF) with 100 µL or 200 µL of either 0.5 M hydrochloric acid (HCl) or 99% acetic acid (AcOH) 
added to cause coagulation were prepared, for comparison with pellet formation only by 
centrifugation for 10, 30, and 60 minutes. Results are shown in Table 16 below; no visible 
effect on pellet formation quality for the samples, all HCl containing samples produced 
pellets and clear supernatant, while samples containing AcOH produced a cloudy solution 
with fragmented coagulates. As a result, addition of 100 µL/mL 0.5 HCl to milk samples as a 
pre-DNA isolation was chosen for this procedure.

2.5.2 Endonuclease DNA Treatment 
To evaluate the difference in pyrimidine dimer formation for the UV-treated milk samples 
obtained from the pilot-scale unit experiments, samples of the isolated DNA was treated 
with T4 Endonuclease V (New England BioLabs, Ipswich, MA, USA). The chosen procedure 
was based on the assay proposed by reported effect of endonuclease on pyrimidine dimers 
by [Oguma et al., 2001]56, and the results from initial test performed by Research Assistant 
at DTU Food Hanne Aarslev Jensen. Sample preparation were as follows: 
1. 20 µL samples were prepared, containing:  
10 µL isolated DNA 
4 µL PDG Endonuclease solution 
2 µL 10X PDG buffer solution 
4 µL DNA purified H2O 
2. Samples were incubated for 30 minutes at 37.5 oC. 
The endonuclease is able to interact with any pyrimidine dimer sites in the isolated DNA, 
classified as endonuclease sensitive sites (ESS), and the expected effect should be a 
difference in the obtained qPCR Ct values and corresponding drop in the calculated bacterial 
content, if the treatment is successful.

3 Results and Discussion 
Presented in this following chapter are the results of the experimental work performed 
during this project, followed by a discussion and assessment of each of the selected 
candidates as an indicator for bacterial inactivation of the UV-based pasteurisation 
technology of Lyras A/S. 
3.1 Testing of Full-scale Unit on Pasteurised Skimmed Milk and Raw Milk at Thise 
For the experiments performed on-site at the Thise dairy plant using the full-scale 
pasteurisation unit, the results will be presented for the skimmed milk experiments first, 
followed by the experiments on raw milk, before these are compared. 
3.1.1 Experimental Results for Processing of Pasteurised Skimmed Milk  
As mentioned in earlier sections, two identical experiments were performed processing the 
same batch of pasteurised skimmed milk, provided by Thise. 
3.1.1.1 
Determination of Bacterial Content in Processed Pasteurised Skimmed Milk 
All results (with the exception of 2 of a total of 120) for the aerobic thermophile and 
mesophile spore count analysis showed concentrations below the lower detection limit of 
the analysis of 10 CFU/mL, meaning the initial content of bacterial spores was very low as a 
result of the heat treatment. Presented in Table 19 and 20 below are the results for the 
bacterial inactivation, measured as total viable count (TVC), and thermoresistant bacteria 
for both experiments. As seen in the tables, the initial TVC and thermoresistant counts were 
different for the two experiments, especially for the TBC content; 495 and 20,904 CFU/mL 
respectively. As the processed milk was obtained from the same batch of pasteurised 
skimmed milk, this illustrates the processing complications presented by this type of 
bacteria, and how they are able to grow during storage time at the dairy plant; despite 
being stored at 4-6oC a 1.63 LOG increase (from 2.69 to 4.32 LOG) in bacterial concentration 
occurred over-night. This however, allowed for testing the full-scale unit for a higher initial 
bacteria count.  
The impact of the processing flow rate and UV-dose on bacterial inactivation efficiency of 
the UV-treatment can be seen by the data. As expected, a higher flow rate (turbulent 
hydrodynamic conditions yields a higher residence time in effected layer in processed milk) 
==PAGE:62==
62 
 
results in a higher bacterial inactivation, compared to a lower flow rate (higher residence 
time and UV-irradiation exposure in reactor, but lower residence time in the effected layer), 
despite the similar UV-dose of 101.4 and 106.7 J/m2 for the lowest chosen flow of 1.2 m/s 
and two passes through the reactor at 2.3 m/s, respectively.  
Here, a complete reduction in the initial bacterial content for both TVC and TBC (2.69 and 
4.32 LOG reductions, respectively) for both experiments was achieved for 2 and 4 reactor-
passes, while the remaining processing conditions all leave some bacterial content in the 
processed milk. This illustrate the potential for the UV-based technology possessed by Lyras 
as a post-pasteurisation ESL application, as it effectively reduces the thermoresistant 
bacteria, such as Paenibacillus and Bacillus59, to a minimum. The TVC count for the 
pasteurised skimmed milk was effectively reduced below detection limits for all operational 
conditions for the first processed batch, while only 2 and 4 reactor-passes achieved this for 
the second, despite the high efficiency for removing the thermoresistant content.

Determination of Fat, Protein and Dry Matter

batches of pasteurised skimmed milk are presented in Table 21 and 22 below. For varying

holding tank to another, some of the water in the system after flushing may have been

these data are affected by the uncertainties in the obtained results.

3.2.4.2 
Assessment of Pyrimidine Dimer Formation as a Validation Method 
Based on the results, the combination of endonuclease treatment followed by qPCR analysis 
does not seem suitable as a bacterial inactivation method in its current state. As it was not 
possible to reproduce initial LOG(CFU/mL) values for the analysed samples based on the 
isolated DNA, the procedure would require further tuning regarding the steps involved. The 
endonuclease treatment did not produce results that corresponded to expectations and 
showed no clear relation between applied UV-dose and endonuclease sensitive DNA-sites. 
As a consequence, using pyrimidine dimer formation as a validation method does not seem 
viable in this current form, until further studies may provide insight in the involved in both 
the pyrimidine formation and endonuclease mechanics for raw milk.

4 Conclusion 
During this project, methods for validating the bacterial inactivation of a novel UV-C light-
based technology, and its potential application as an alternative to heat-based 
pasteurisation in the dairy sector was investigated. Based on a literature review of the 
current trends in the dairy sector, it was clear UV-based solutions have gained attention and 
traction the last couple of decades, with research efforts being put into understanding the 
essential mechanics for designing a UV-reactor suited for treating milk with equal efficiency 
as thermal inactivation. Studies show, that the benefits from this technology are many, and 
with proper reactor design it is possible to meet legislative demands of a 5 LOG10 reduction 
in bacterial content. Understanding of the hydrodynamic conditions during processing is 
essential for reactor design, in order to deliver the required UV-dose.  
Based on the reported effect of UV-processing on the composition of milk and formation of 
possible by-products, three candidates were chosen as possible candidates for indicators of 
the bacterial inactivation during processing of dairy products in a novel technology 
possessed by Lyras A/S. These candidates chosen were; the formation of lipid oxidation 
MDA by-products, loss of vitamin C content, and DNA-pyrimidine dimer formation. For each 
candidate, an analytical procedure was chosen and applied to untreated and UV-treated 
samples, i.e. raw milk samples processed by a full-scale and pilot-scale unit provided by 
Lyras A/S. A bacterial inactivation curve was established for raw milk processing using he 
pilot scale-unit and the following UV-doses: 0.0, 19.8, 39.6, 48.0, 59.4, 79.2, 96.0, 144.0, and 
192.0 J/m2. A 3.15 LOG10 reduction in bacterial content to the lower detection limit was 
achieved for UV-doses ≥79.2 J/m2. The experimental data was modelled using first-order 
kinetics with the GInaFIT Excel add-in software and used to calculate a D-value of 17.13 J/m2 
as calculated using first-order kinetics. 
Results showed inconsistent behaviour for the chosen procedures for evaluating both the 
formation of lipid oxidation MDA by-products and DNA-pyrimidine dimers and would need 
further investigation if they were to be considered as valid candidates. The analysis of 
vitamin C loss as a function of increasing UV-dose showed a decrease in vitamin C 
concentration of 62% for UV-doses ≥ 96.0 J/m2 and aligned well with results from the 
analysis of bacterial inactivation. As a result, loss of vitamin C during UV-processing 
==PAGE:86==
86 
 
represents a viable candidate as an indirect indicator for bacterial inactivation for the UV-
based pasteurisation technology of Lyras A/S.  
Studies into other possible applications for the Lyras A/S UV-based technology in the dairy 
sector showed potential as an extend shelf-life (ESL) application for pasteurised skimmed 
milk, where a 4.32 LOG(CFU/mL) reduction in thermoresistant spores was achieved for a 
UV-dose of 106.73 J/m2 at a flow velocity of 2.33 m/s. 
Experimental results for the usage of the standard alkaline phosphatase activity test 
confirmed the reported non-destructive effect of UV-processing on enzymatic content in 
raw milk, supported by the analysis of fat, protein and dry matter of the processed samples.

5 Further Experimental Work 
As a last entry in this project, it is necessary to reflect on how additional experimental work 
would benefit the observations made based on experimental results obtained during this 
project. The most obvious missing piece here is the lack of biodosimetric studies using raw 
milk inoculated with a selection of bacterial strains, to establish a benchmark profile for the 
UV-based technology used in this project. If combined with collimated beam experiments, a 
more complete understanding of the limitations of the Lyras A/S unit would be achievable. 
This is of particular interest, if the legislative demands of the dairy sector are to be met, and 
the needed dispensation for UV-based technologies as a safe alternative to heat-based 
pasteurisation is to be given. This procedure would need to be initiated, and a standardized 
evaluation procedure would have to be established. 
As for the proposed candidates as indicators of bacterial inactivation, each would need 
extensive experimental work, to establish the statistical data needed to confirm the 
observed trends, or, in the case of lipid oxidation and pyrimidine dimer formation, an in-
depth look at the mechanics involved in kinetics for both the formation and analysis of the 
two candidates. Additional determination of the loss of vitamin C from the samples 
collected during processing of raw milk would be optimal, to verify if the observed trend is 
not a unique observation, but a representative pattern. This could be confirmed by 
analysing the vitamin C loss on raw milk with an artificial, increased concentration, to see if 
the same lower threshold is achievable.  
In general, the possible amount of further work is great, and the available pilot-scale unit 
allows for a variety of experimental designs, to fully understand the mechanics for the 
applied UV-dose and allows to vary essential operational conditions such as flow velocity 
and reactor-retention time.

Chapters 2, 4, and 5 from the Regulation of Hygiene (Hygienjebekendtgørelsen)6, published by the

demands for production, handling, and processing of raw milk. 
Kapitel 2 
 
Rå mælk 
§ 4. Primærproducenter må kun markedsføre rå mælk direkte til den endelige forbruger, når 
besætningen er indplaceret i niveau 1 i den til enhver tid gældende bekendtgørelse om salmonella 
hos kvæg m.m. 
Stk. 2. Primærproducenter skal sikre, at rå mælk ikke markedsføres direkte til den endelige forbruger 
senere end 24 timer efter, at mælken er udmalket. 
Stk. 3. Primærproducenter skal sikre, at rå mælk, som ønskes afsat direkte til den endelige forbruger, 
nedkøles til højst 6 °C straks efter udmalkning. 
Stk. 4. Primærproducenter skal sikre, at rå mælk, som markedsføres direkte til den endelige 
forbruger, opfylder kriterierne for celletal, kimtal og antibiotikarester i hygiejneforordningen for 
animalske fødevarer, samt opfylder det mikrobiologiske kriterium for E. coli i bilag 1. 
Stk. 5. Primærproducenter skal sikre, at den i bilag 2 nævnte kontrol med rå mælk udføres, samt at 
den rå mælk hver måned undersøges for E. coli. 
Stk. 6. Hvis den i stk. 5 nævnte undersøgelse af E. coli viser resultater, som ikke overholder de 
fastsatte grænseværdier, skal primærproducenter ophøre med at markedsføre rå mælk direkte til 
den endelige forbruger, indtil der foreligger en prøve med et indhold af E. coli under 10 cfu/g. 
Stk. 7. Primærproducenter kan dog, jf. stk. 6, fortsætte den direkte markedsføring af rå mælk til den 
endelige forbruger, hvis indholdet af E. coli ikke overstiger 100 cfu/g, og de foregående 6 
undersøgelser ikke overstiger 10 cfu/g. Primærproducenter skal inden en uge efter modtagelsen af 
det utilfredsstillende resultat sikre, at der udtages en ny prøve til undersøgelse for E. coli.

Kapitel 4 
Automatiske malkesystemer 
§ 9. Primærproducenter, som anvender automatiske malkesystemer, hvor mælk eller kolostrum fra 
det enkelte dyr ikke undersøges for afvigende organoleptiske eller fysisk-kemiske egenskaber af den 
malkende, men ved en metode med lignende resultater, skal sikre, at 
1) de anvendte automatiske malkesystemer til enhver tid opfylder kravene i den seneste udgave af 
ISO-standarden 
om automatiske malkesystemer, eller 
2) der iværksættes supplerende egenkontrolprocedurer, som gennemføres, så primærproducenten 
sikrer lignende resultater. 
Kapitel 5 
Håndtering og markedsføring af mælk 
§ 10. Fødevarevirksomheder må ikke markedsføre rå mælk og rå fløde til direkte konsum. 
Stk. 2. Uanset stk. 1 må rå mælk markedsføres fra primærproducenter direkte til den endelige 
forbruger, jf. §§ 3 og 4. 
Stk. 3. Køkkener på landbrugsskoler og lignende, der har egen malkebesætning, og hvor personalet 
har et fagligt grundlag for malkning og håndtering af mælken i mælkerum og køkken, må uanset stk. 
1 anvende rå mælk af egen produktion på de betingelser, der fremgår af § 4 om markedsføring af rå 
mælk fra primærproducenter direkte til den endelige forbruger. 
Varmebehandling af mælk og mælkebestanddele 
§ 11. Rå mælk og rå mælkebestanddele, der indgår i fremstilling af mejeriprodukter og af andre 
fødevarer, skal som minimum varmebehandles ved en kombination af tid og temperatur, der svarer 
til pasteurisering, jf. dog stk. 3 og 4 samt § 12. 
Stk. 2. Fødevarer, som ikke er mejeriprodukter, og som er tilsat rå mælk eller rå mælkebestanddele, 
skal varmebehandles i henhold til § 25. 
Stk. 3. Rå mælk og rå mælkebestanddele til fremstilling af blåskimmelmodnet ost kan 
varmebehandles ved mindst 57 ºC i 15 sekunder eller ved anden kombination af tid og temperatur, 
der har en tilsvarende effekt.

Stk. 4. Stk. 1 kan fraviges for rå mælk og rå mælkebestanddele til fremstilling af andre oste på 
følgende betingelser: 
1) ostekoaglet skal have undergået en eftervarmebehandling ved mindst 50 ºC i 30 minutter eller 
ved anden kombination af tid og temperatur, der har en tilsvarende effekt, 
2) osten skal være lagret i mere end 60 dage inden markedsføring til direkte konsum, og 
3) den færdige ost skal indeholde mindre end 56 % vand i den fedtfri ostemasse. 
Stk. 5. Stk. 1 kan fraviges for rå mælk og rå mælkebestanddele, som anvendes i andre fødevarer end 
mejeriprodukter. I så fald anvendes bestemmelserne i § 25. 
§ 12. Fødevarestyrelsen kan dispensere fra kravet i § 11, stk. 1, såfremt virksomheden kan 
dokumentere, at forarbejdningen foregår under anvendelse af andre hygiejnemæssige 
foranstaltninger eller teknologier, der sikrer, at gældende hygiejnekriterier overholdes, og såfremt 
Fødevarestyrelsen vurderer, at disse foranstaltninger er hygiejnisk og sundhedsmæssigt forsvarlige.
