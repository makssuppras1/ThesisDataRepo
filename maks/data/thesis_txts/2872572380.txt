Abstract
This thesis examines how energy system modeling influences climate and energy poli­
cymaking in Denmark and the United States, two countries characterized by significant
differences in scale and political structure. Employing a comparative analysis framework,
the study investigates the interaction between institutional structures, stakeholder en­
gagement, and techno­economic modeling practices to understand how model outputs
translate into actionable policy decisions.
Using a mixed­methods approach consisting of institutional mapping, 13 semi­structured
expert interviews, and document analysis, this research contrasts Denmark’s centralized
and consensus­oriented energy planning framework with the decentralized and pluralistic
governance landscape of the United States. The analysis reveals distinct governance log­
ics: Denmark integrates the TIMES­DK model within a standardized regulatory structure,
facilitating straightforward translation from modeling outputs to legislative targets with lim­
ited and highly structured public contention. Conversely, the U.S. relies heavily on open
data, peer review, and coalition­building, leading to variable policy adoption influenced by
shifting political contexts.
Despite structural and procedural differences, both countries increasingly adopt hybrid
approaches combining expert­driven modeling with stakeholder deliberation. This con­
vergence highlights that transparency in assumptions and iterative feedback mechanisms
enhance policy credibility and effectiveness. Specifically, iterative feedback allows poli­
cymakers to validate modeling assumptions against stakeholder insights, improving the
relevance and applicability of model outputs. Furthermore, integrating structured stake­
holder dialogues fosters a broader acceptance of energy policy decisions by addressing
potential concerns early in the planning process.
The study highlights how national “civic epistemologies” influence model legitimacy. Den­
mark’s high institutional trust simplifies model­based policymaking but risks marginalizing
dissenting voices and outsiders within its seemingly insular energy governance commu­
nity. By contrast, the U.S. adversarial style exposes underlying assumptions but compli­
cates both consensus­building and trust. Recommendations for policymakers include:
1. Publish clear input assumptions and data sources alongside model outputs;
2. Institutionalize structured, more diverse stakeholder forums to avoid exclusionary ef­
fects; and
3. Integrate quantitative scenarios with qualitative narratives to connect numerical results
back to real­world concerns.
Policymakers should also remain cautious of consensus­driven exclusion, where an em­
phasis on agreement can inadvertently marginalize critical perspectives or alternative
views. Limitations identified include Denmark­centric interviews and constantly evolv­
ing policy contexts, suggesting future research should validate these findings in emerging
economies and at sub­national governance levels.

Introduction
This introductory chapter provides an overview of the key concepts and context that frame the
report. It begins with an explanation of the global energy transition and the purpose of energy
planning, highlighting the critical role of transitioning from fossil fuel­based systems to renewable
and low­carbon energy sources. The chapter then discusses the importance of evidence­based
policymaking, particularly in the context of long­term energy planning, where robust and data­
driven approaches are essential for sustainable decision­making. Next, the chapter examines how
energy modeling shapes policy and outlooks, focusing on how different countries integrate models
into policy design. This comparative perspective will center on Denmark and the United States,
as they represent contrasting approaches to energy modeling and governance. The introduction
also addresses the limitations and challenges of energy models, emphasizing the need for reflexive
modeling practices that incorporate diverse perspectives and systematically assess uncertainties.
The chapter concludes with the problem statement, research gap, and project aim, outlining the
specific research questions that guide this study. By understanding how institutional contexts,
expertise, and stakeholder engagement shape the effectiveness of energy models in Denmark
and the United States, the study aims to identify best practices for integrating energy models into
policymaking and develop guidelines for enhancing their credibility, transparency and practical
application.
1.1
Overview of the Energy Transition and Purpose of
Energy Planning
The energy transition is the global shift from fossil fuel­based energy systems to low­carbon and
renewable sources such as solar, wind, hydroelectric and bioenergy, aimed at reducing carbon
emissions in line with the urgent climate objectives as outlined in the Paris Agreement [90]. Ac­
cording to the EPA, energy­related greenhouse gas emissions constitute the majority of all anthro­
pogenic emissions. The adoption of renewable energy and energy efficiency measures is critical in
reducing greenhouse gas emissions and mitigating the impacts of climate change [53]. Achieving
this goal requires technological advancements, effective policy initiatives and enhanced energy
security.
The energy transition is inherently complex and relies on the coordination and collaboration of di­
verse stakeholders, including policymakers, energy experts, industry representatives, data providers,
energy modelers, and the wider public, whose acceptance is essential for success [23]. Neces­
sary infrastructure and economic changes must be strategically planned to ensure a stable, cost­
effective and equitable transformation of an energy system [47]. As energy demand continues to
rise, particularly in industrialized and rapidly developing economies, governments rely on energy
planning tools to model future scenarios, assess risks and guide policy decisions [10]. Energy
system planning is essential for a successful transition, as it integrates diverse stakeholder per­
spectives, combines qualitative narratives with quantitative energy modeling, and incorporates
techno­economic data inputs.
Energy system planning serves several critical functions:
1. Long­term policy development – Energy planning helps governments define long­term strate­
gies for decarbonization and energy security, ensuring resilience against future ecological
and environmental uncertainties [5].
2. Infrastructure and Investment Decisions – Large­scale investments in renewable energy
and grid infrastructure require cautious forecasting of energy demand, resource availability,
and technology costs [73].
3. Stakeholder Integration and Public Engagement – Effective planning integrates perspec­
tives from multiple stakeholders, ensuring that energy policies align with public interests
and economic priorities [47].

4. Energy models support decision­makers in assessing the feasibility of emerging technolo­
gies, such as energy storage, hydrogen production, and carbon capture and storage (CCS),
in achieving climate targets [94].
Given the increasing role of energy system models in shaping policy, it is critical to examine how
different institutional structures and stakeholder engagement approaches influence model effec­
tiveness and policy outcomes. This comparative analysis of Denmark and the U.S. will highlight
the role of energy modeling in shaping the future of energy transitions and explore how different

1.2
Research gap
Despite the critical role of energy system models in informing policy decisions and long­term plan­
ning, existing research predominantly focuses on their technical and quantitative aspects, often
overlooking the institutional and participatory aspects that shape their effectiveness and credibil­
ity. Current studies tend to emphasize the technical optimization of energy pathways while ne­
glecting how models are embedded within institutional contexts and how stakeholder involvement
impacts their implementation and acceptance [50]. This study addresses this gap by examining
how institutional contexts, expertise, and stakeholder engagement shape the effectiveness of en­
ergy system models in policy­making across two different institutional structures and approaches:
Denmark and the United States. Denmark’s energy modeling practices are characterized by struc­
tured stakeholder engagement and collaboration with academic institutions, while the U.S. exhibits
a more decentralized approach, leading to variability in model application and stakeholder involve­
ment. Understanding these differences is essential to enhance the integration of energy models
into decision­making and to develop more context­sensitive and participatory modeling practices.
Furthermore, existing research often fails to address how cultural and governance differences in­
fluence the perception and use of modeling outputs, which can significantly impact the credibility
and acceptance of policy recommendations [50]. By analyzing both technical practices and partic­
ipatory approaches, this study hopes to contribute to a more comprehensive understanding of how
energy models function as both technical and socio­political instruments in policy development.
1.3
Defining the Problem: Problem Statement
Energy systems modeling is a fundamental tool in modern energy policymaking, helping countries
navigate the path toward sustainable transitions. As countries aim to decarbonize their economies,
energy models become crucial tools for evaluating technology pathways, projecting emissions tra­
jectories, and estimating the socioeconomic impacts of policy measures. However, different coun­
tries employ diverse methodologies and governance frameworks for incorporating these models
into decision­making, reflecting their unique political, social and economic contexts.
Denmark has developed an integrated and participatory approach to energy systems modeling,
which incorporates structured stakeholder engagement into techno­economic modeling. This in­
tegrated framework aims to enhance model transparency and ensures that diverse perspectives
are incorporated into energy planning processes. For example, the TIMES­DK model supports
long­term strategic planning by modeling the complete Danish energy system, including power,
heat, industry, residential and transport sectors. The model’s collaborative development between
the Danish Energy Agency (DEA) and research institutions, such as the Technical University of
Denmark (DTU), exemplifies how academic and policy­making spheres can work synergistically
to build comprehensive, data­driven energy strategies. Additionally, Danish municipalities are in­
creasingly involved in energy planning at the local level. Despite having a structured approach,
there remain challenges in bridging the gap between sophisticated model outputs and practical
municipal planning needs. Because the models are highly complex, municipalities often depend
on external experts and consultants. This highlights the gap between advanced analyses and
everyday planning needs.
In contrast, the United States operates within a fragmented governance structure where multiple
federal and state agencies independently develop and utilize various modeling tools. This de­
centralized approach leads to heterogeneous modeling practices and a lack of standardization,

making it difficult to harmonize policy applications and achieve cohesive national strategies. For
example, the U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA) employs the National Energy Model­
ing System (NEMS) to project energy trends, to produce the annual Standard Scenarios Report
of the U.S. electricity sector and to inform policy. However, at the state level, models vary signif­
icantly in scope and methodology, creating potential inconsistencies in policy effectiveness and

The decentralized and market­driven approach in the U.S. also results in limited stakeholder en­
gagement compared to Denmark’s collaborative practices. While some federal reports, such as
the Annual Energy Outlook (AEO), are made publicly available, the complexity of modeling pro­
cesses often inhibits meaningful stakeholder involvement. This can reduce the perceived legiti­
macy of model­based policy recommendations and restrict their practical applicability. Moreover,
the interdisciplinary nature of energy models, spanning economic forecasting, engineering analy­
sis, and social considerations, adds complexity to their interpretation. Policymakers navigate un­
certainties, data limitations, and institutional barriers that influence model application in real­world
decision­making. As noted in Venturini et al. (2019), integrating qualitative social dimensions into
quantitative models remains a challenge, as models often fail to adequately capture behavioral
aspects and social acceptance of proposed technologies [94].
The methodological choices made during model construction can shape policy outcomes by em­
bedding implicit assumptions that influence decision­making. Deterministic models that do not
account for uncertainty may inadvertently create a false sense of precision and robustness. To re­
duce this risk, best practices in modeling include adopting reflexive modeling techniques, such as
Numeral Unit Spread Assessment Pedigree (NUSAP) and sensitivity auditing (SAUD), to critically
assess uncertainties and model assumptions [48].
This research compares Denmark’s structured and participatory modeling framework with the
U.S.’s decentralized and market­driven approach to identify best practices for integrating energy
models into policymaking for climate objectives and targets. By examining the strengths and limi­
tations of each approach, the study aims to develop guidelines that improve the credibility, trans­
parency, and practical application of energy modeling in various governance contexts.
1.4
Project Aim
Specifically, this study aims to compare how energy systems modeling informs policy design in
Denmark and the United States, focusing on three key aspects:
1. Integration of stakeholder input – How participatory processes influence model outcomes
and policy adoption.
2. Institutional contexts – How different governance structures shape the role and impact of
modeling.
3. Methodological approaches – The comparative effectiveness of techno­economic modeling
frameworks in each country.
1.5
Research Objectives and Questions
This research will further explore:
1. What are the long­term energy targets and planning frameworks of Denmark and the U.S.?
2. How do both countries integrate energy models into decision­making?
3. What institutional and cultural factors influence the use of energy models?
4. How do participatory and techno­economic approaches shape policy outcomes?
By addressing these questions, this study intends to contribute to the broader understanding of
how energy models can be leveraged more effectively in diverse governance settings to support
evidence­based and socially inclusive policymaking to build toward defined climate objectives.

Background
Building upon the foundational concepts and research objectives outlined in Chapter 1, this chapter
provides detailed contextual background and historical insights necessary to understand the role
of energy systems modeling within distinct governance contexts. It begins by defining evidence­
based policymaking and its critical role in long­term energy planning, before tracing the historical
evolution and methodological development of energy system models. Following this, the chapter
contrasts the distinct institutional contexts of Denmark and the United States, detailing how each
country’s political structure and governance culture influences energy modeling practices. Finally,
the chapter introduces the core energy modeling systems utilized in both nations, highlighting
key methodologies, data inputs, and their implications for policy outcomes, in order to set the
foundation for the subsequent theoretical and methodological analysis.
2.1
Evidence­Based Policymaking and Long­Term Planning
The global energy transition requires ambitious policies that support decarbonization, energy secu­
rity and economic sustainability. Evidence­based policymaking is vital as countries set long­term
targets to reduce greenhouse gas emissions. Comprehensive, data­driven approaches enable
policymakers to navigate the complexity of energy system transformations by balancing techno­
logical feasibility, economic viability and social acceptance. There is significant variability of model
integration into policy design between nations based on differences in governance structures, in­
stitutional settings, and stakeholder involvement at various stages.
Energy system models are widely used to explore, analyze, and plan energy futures, offering quan­
titative insights into potential policy pathways. These models inform decisions on technological
investments, energy mix transformations, and policy impacts under varying scenarios, allowing
governments to create informed and evidence­based strategies. In recent years, energy system
models have played a key role in informing policy decisions by providing insights into the potential
impacts of different energy pathways.
In Denmark, the TIMES­DK model is used to develop cost­effective strategies and evaluate long­
term scenarios for achieving carbon neutrality [5]. TIMES­DK is a technology­rich multi­sectoral
optimization model that supports long­term energy planning by evaluating socioeconomic optimal
pathways and sectoral interdependencies. It guides policymakers to assess trade­offs and syn­
ergies between various policy measures in planning for carbon neutrality [5]. The structured and
inclusive nature of energy modeling in Denmark is also evident in municipal planning contexts,
where strategic energy planning is conducted through collaboration between local governments,
district heating companies and academic partners [3].
In the U.S., energy system models such as the National Energy Modeling System (NEMS) and Re­
gional Energy Deployment System (ReEDS) are used to develop strategies and assess long­term
decarbonization scenarios. These models support planning by evaluating technological feasibility,
economic impacts, and policy outcomes, especially within a decentralized governance framework
[14] [31] [35]. Specifically, the National Energy Modeling System (NEMS) is a comprehensive,
economy­wide model used to project U.S. energy production, consumption, and pricing across
sectors under varying policy, economic, and regulatory scenarios. It supports long­term planning
by simulating the interactions between energy markets and macroeconomic conditions [1]. In con­
trast, the Regional Energy Deployment System (ReEDS) is a high­resolution, capacity expansion
model developed by NREL that focuses on the deployment of renewable energy technologies,
with detailed spatial and temporal representations of the electricity grid. ReEDS provides critical
insights into the feasibility, reliability, and cost­effectiveness of integrating high shares of variable
renewables while accounting for transmission constraints, resource variability, and policy incen­
tives [44]. Policymakers use these models as a tool to identify trade­offs and synergies, enabling
informed decisions toward a sustainable energy future.
Energy models face limitations. Models often make assumptions regarding technology adoption
rates, economic growth, and societal behavior, which may not align with real­world developments.

For example, deterministic thinking and rigid assumptions in UK energy modeling have led to over­
confidence and unexpected deviations from projected outcomes [48]. This highlights the need for
reflexive modeling practices that incorporate diverse perspectives. These practices should sys­
tematically assess uncertainties through methods like Numeral Unit Spread Assessment Pedigree
(NUSAP) and sensitivity auditing (SAUD) to enhance inclusivity and robustness [50].
Municipal energy planning further illustrates the challenge of integrating models into decision­
making processes. As highlighted by Amer et al. (2020), municipalities often lack the expertise
and resources to operate complex models, resulting in reliance on simpler CO2 calculation tools
or externizing analysis [3]. Addressing this gap requires the development of more accessible
and transparent modeling tools and stronger collaboration between local governments and key
stakeholders [3].
Given these challenges, energy modeling must continuously evolve to meet contemporary policy
making demands with public and stakeholder involvement. This requires fostering greater trans­
parency, improving stakeholder engagement, and incorporating diverse perspectives and uncer­
tainties into modeling practices. By enhancing the credibility and usability of energy models, it
may allow policymakers to make more informed decisions that account for the multifaceted nature
of the energy transition.
2.2
Evolution of Energy Systems Modeling
Energy systems modeling has evolved over the past half­century as a critical tool for understand­
ing and planning complex energy futures. The field emerged prominently in response to the 1970s
oil crises, which catalyzed the development of the first formal energy models aimed at reducing de­
pendence on imported oil and assessing economic impacts of energy policies [79]. Early models
often used linear programming to optimize energy supply and demand under various constraints.
A well­known example was the Brookhaven Energy System Optimization Model (BESOM), one
of the first comprehensive energy optimization models [79]. Building on such early efforts, the
International Energy Agency’s Energy Technology Systems Analysis Programme (ETSAP) intro­
duced the MARKAL model in the late 1970s, a dynamic linear programming model that provided
a bottom­up technology­rich representation of a country’s energy system [34]. MARKAL and its
successors (notably the TIMES model introduced in the late 1990s) enabled planners to examine
detailed technology pathways for energy supply and consumption, and were adopted by dozens of
countries as standardized tools for energy planning [34]. In parallel, other optimization models like
the Energy Flow Optimization Model (EFOM, the European Commission’s model) and the Model
for Energy Supply Strategy Alternatives and their General Environmental impact (MESSAGE, de­
veloped by IIASA) were developed in the 1980s, reflecting a growing international effort to create
robust frameworks for energy policy analysis [79]. These bottom­up models focused on techno­
logical options and least­cost solutions, complementing top­down macroeconomic models (e.g.
general equilibrium models) that treated the energy sector in broader economic terms [79].
Bottom­up models represent individual technologies and processes in high detail, optimizing sys­
tem performance from a technical and engineering perspective, while top­down models focus on
macroeconomic relationships and often use aggregated sectoral data to simulate economic im­
pacts of policy. These approaches offer complementary insights, and hybrid models increasingly
seek to combine their strengths.
Hybrid models combine the detailed technological representation of bottom­up models with the
macroeconomic scope of top­down approaches. This integration allows analysts to assess not
only the optimal deployment of specific technologies, but also their broader economic impacts
[7]. For instance, a hybrid approach may link an energy system optimization model such as the
TIMES­DK with a computable general equilibrium (CGE) model to evaluate how decarbonization
pathways affect GDP, employment or sectoral output. This coupling enables feedback loops: en­
ergy strategies inform economic indicators, which in turn influence energy demand or investment
timing. Hybrid models therefore have potential enhance realism and policy relevance by capturing
the two­way interaction between energy systems and the wider economy [7].
As concerns about energy prices and the economic effects of energy policy grew during the 1980s,

researchers began coupling detailed, technology­rich energy models with broader macroeconomic
frameworks, integrating bottom­up and top­down approaches.
By the late 1980s and into the 1990s, energy modeling expanded to address global environmen­
tal challenges, giving rise to integrated assessment models (IAMs). IAMs pair energy­economy
models with climate science, land use, and atmospheric modules to explore long­term scenarios
for greenhouse gas emissions and climate change.
Early IAMs, such as DICE (Dynamic Integrated Climate­Economy model) introduced by Nordhaus
and the Integrated Model to Assess the Global Environment (IMAGE) used in early Intergovern­
mental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) assessments, provided a means to evaluate how energy
and economic pathways translate into climate outcomes [92]. These models were instrumental
in framing the first global climate policy analyses; for example, integrated models helped develop
the emission scenarios featured in the IPCC’s initial reports around 1990 [92].
Over subsequent decades, IAMs such as MESSAGE, the Global Change Analysis Model (GCAM),
and AIM have become central to international climate negotiations by quantifying mitigation path­
ways, carbon budgets, and technology needs to meet targets such as the Paris Agreement objec­
tives [92]. The integration in IAMs signifies drawing from many disciplines (including economics,
energy engineering, climate science) to produce policy­relevant insights on issues ranging from
future emission trajectories to the cost of avoiding dangerous climate change [92].
Another pivotal development in the evolution of energy modeling has been the rise of scenario
planning as a framework for decision­making under uncertainty. In contrast to single­point fore­
casts, scenario planning presents multiple plausible futures to capture a range of uncertainties.
The approach was famously pioneered by Royal Dutch Shell in the 1970s: Shell’s scenario team,
drawing on methods developed by futurist Herman Kahn, prepared a set of divergent energy sce­
narios that enabled the company to anticipate and respond to the 1973 oil crisis [8]. This success
illustrated that scenarios can “modify the mental model of decision­makers” rather than precisely
predict the future [8]. Since then, scenario analysis has been widely adopted in energy policy
circles. Governments and organizations including the International Energy Agency (IEA) and the
IPCC routinely use scenarios (e.g. baseline vs. low­carbon futures) to stress­test policies and
explore the implications of uncertainties in fuel prices, technological breakthroughs, or climate ac­
tion. By the 2000s, scenario­based modeling had become standard practice for long­term energy
outlooks. For example, the IPCC’s Special Report on Emissions Scenarios (2000) and the more
recent Shared Socioeconomic Pathways involved coordinating many modeling teams to produce
harmonized scenario projections of energy, land and climate futures [54].
In summary, from simple linear programs to complex integrated frameworks, energy models have
continually advanced in scope and sophistication. This historical evolution from optimizing national
energy mixes to simulating global climate­nergy interactions laid the groundwork for the powerful
modeling tools used today in energy and climate policy planning.
2.3
Role of Energy Models in Decarbonization Strategies
Energy system models have emerged as critical tools in guiding countries towards decarboniza­
tion goals, playing a central role in shaping long­term strategies and informing policy decisions.
By providing quantitative, scenario­based insights, these models enable policymakers to explore
the feasibility, costs, and implications of different energy pathways under varying technological,
economic, and social conditions.
A pivotal aspect of energy modeling in decarbonization is its ability to handle uncertainty and
complexity through scenario planning. Rather than offering single­point forecasts, models such
as TIMES­DK in Denmark and NEMS in the U.S. provide a range of plausible futures. This ap­
proach captures uncertainties related to technological innovation, market dynamics, and policy
effectiveness, thereby supporting robust and resilient decision­making processes. For example,
the IEA and the IPCC routinely utilize scenario modeling to stress­test policies against potential
technological breakthroughs, shifts in fuel prices, and evolving climate policies [8].
In Denmark, energy modeling supports a highly structured, collaborative governance framework.

Denmark and the United States differ fundamentally in government structure and scale. Denmark

coalition governments that must negotiate with other parties to pass laws; as one analysis notes,
Danish legislation is usually approved by “overwhelming majorities” after cross­party bargaining
[71]. In fact, researchers observe a stable political system with a “strong consensus tradition” in

U.S. is a large federal republic with a presidential system. U.S. policymaking authority is decen­

formal veto points. For example, the U.S. Constitution’s separation of powers means policy out­

executive, and powerful state governments); this difference offers a valuable basis for comparison

stitutional federalism that disperses authority. Unlike Denmark, the presidency is separate from
Congress, and laws require approval in both houses of a bicameral legislature. States retain sig­
nificant policy power (e.g. in education, policing, elections). As a result, the U.S. system contains

2.4.2
Consensus vs Pluralist Policy Culture
Denmark’s political culture is highly consensus­oriented. Because coalitions are the norm, Dan­
ish policymaking often takes place through negotiation and expert­driven consultation rather than
direct conflict or confrontation. For example, Denmark maintains a tradition of corporatism and
stakeholder dialogue: government commissions and advisory councils regularly bring together
ministers, public servants, union leaders, business representatives, and experts to hammer out
policy (often behind the scenes) [61]. A study describes this “quiet politics” in Denmark as a form of
corporatist coordination, where selected labor and business leaders “broker and foster agreement
between different stakeholders” with the state [61]. Experts and civil servants have especially
strong roles: research finds that Danish political scientists and other specialists provide extensive
informal advice to officials and advisory bodies, basing recommendations on “sound scientific ev­
idence”. High public trust in institutions reinforces this style: Danes report comparatively high
confidence in government (e.g. 44% of Danes trust their national government which facilitates
acceptance of expert­guided, long­term policy agreements [59]).
In contrast, the U.S. embodies a pluralist, adversarial political culture.

Implications for Energy and Climate Policy
Denmark’s consensus democracy channels political competition into durable cross­party agree­

was concluded by parties representing 95% of the Folketing and set legally backed milestones,

In the United States, federalism and separation of powers create multiple institutional veto points;
authority over climate action is diffused across Congress, the presidency, states, and the courts,

through litigation and rule­making challenges rather than pre­legislative bargaining, raising trans­

2.4.4
Theoretical Foundations and Typologies
Lijphart’s well established consensus–majoritarian dichotomy offers a foundational explanation
for why otherwise similar democracies may yield different policy outcomes. Denmark illustrates a
classic consensus system characterized by power­sharing cabinets, proportional representation,
and a culture of negotiation. In contrast, the U.S. exemplifies majoritarian presidential governance
with limited incentives for cross­party compromise [46].
Building on this macro­typology, Katzenstein’s work introduces this macro typology for small Euro­
pean states, coining democratic corporatism to describe institutionalized cooperation among gov­
ernment, labor, and business. He argues that this corporatist model allows small open economies
like Denmark to balance social protection with market competitiveness, by embedding interest
mediation directly into policymaking structures [43].
At the meso­level, Richardson’s national policy­styles framework identifies durable, country­specific
“ways of doing policy” along two intersecting dimensions: (1) problem­solving approach­anticipatory
and forward­planning versus reactive and short­term; and (2) state­society relationship­consensual
bargaining with organized interests versus unilateral, impose­and­defend decision­making ([78]
drawing on Richardson et al., 1982). Mapping countries in this two­by­two space yields a spectrum
from consensual/anticipatory styles to adversarial/reactive styles. Empirical applications of the
Sustainable Policy Pathways framework show Denmark clustering at the consensual–anticipatory
pole, whereas the United States aligns with the adversarial­reactive pole, with knock­on effects for
policy coherence and institutional learning capacity [78]. Further reinforcing this distinction, Ka­
gan’s notion of adversarial legalism supplies the micro­institutional logic for the U.S. case, where
rule making and litigation create a punitive, court centered regulatory arena that raises compliance
costs and deters long­term infrastructure investment [42].
Taken together, these theories show that structural veto points, interest­group access rules, and
epistemic cultures co­evolve. In corporatist consensus regimes, expert commissions convert con­
tested goals into broadly accepted evidence, allowing ambitious climate targets to survive electoral
turnover. In pluralist adversarial regimes, the same evidence is refracted through partisan and liti­
gation lenses, producing incremental or reversible climate policy. Understanding these typological
logics is therefore essential for designing energy­system models and advisory institutions that are
politically “fit for purpose” in each country context.

The U.S. Modeling Systems: NEMS
National Energy Modeling System (NEMS) –
United States
The National Energy Modeling System (NEMS) is the flagship energy forecasting and policy anal­
ysis model used by the United States government. Developed and maintained by the U.S. Energy
Information Administration (EIA), NEMS is a large­scale energy­economy model that projects U.S.
energy production, consumption, technology deployment, and prices typically through mid­century
[86]. The model was first used for the EIA’s Annual Energy Outlook in 1994 and it has since be­
come a principal analytical tool for outlining America’s energy future under various scenarios [83].
NEMS produces annual projections by simulating the behavior of energy markets and consumers,
subject to a comprehensive set of assumptions about macroeconomic growth, resource availabil­
ity, technological change, and government policies [87]. Its fundamental approach is to achieve a
general equilibrium between energy supply and demand in each future year, meaning that energy

prices adjust within the model until markets (for coal, oil, natural gas, electricity, etc.) balance
[86]. Unlike a single monolithic program, NEMS is designed in a modular structure: it consists of
multiple interlinked components that each represent a segment of the energy system. These mod­
ules include separate representations of primary energy supply (oil and gas, coal, renewables),
energy conversion sectors (electric power generation and fuel refining), end­use demand sectors
(residential, commercial, industrial, transportation), as well as modules for international petroleum
markets, macroeconomic feedbacks, and emissions accounting [86]. An Integrating Module iter­

the electricity supply from generators meets the modeled electricity demand, and fuel prices are
such that all markets clear [86].

In practical terms, NEMS generates the U.S. government’s official Annual Energy Outlook (AEO)
each year, which includes a reference scenario and alternative cases (e.g. high economic growth,
low oil price, rapid technology improvement scenarios). These scenarios help policy­makers un­
derstand the range of possible futures and the impact of uncertainties. The model’s outputs cover
a wide array of metrics: energy production by fuel, consumption by sector, electricity generation
mix, energy prices, import/export levels, and CO2 emissions, to name a few [39]. Because NEMS
integrates economic activity with energy supply and demand, it can also evaluate policy propos­
als, such as emissions regulations, fuel taxes, or efficiency standards, by simulating how these
interventions would ripple through energy markets. Analysts use NEMS to answer questions like:
How would a carbon price affect coal vs. natural gas usage? or What might be the long­term oil
import dependency under different vehicle efficiency standards?
A key strength of NEMS is its detail and granularity. It represents regional differences within the
United States (with multiple regions for electricity and fuel markets) and includes a rich dataset of
technologies. For example, the electricity module contains numerous power plant types (natural
gas combined cycles, various renewables, coal with carbon capture, etc.) each characterized by
costs and performance; the transportation module simulates vehicle stock turnover with multiple
vehicle types (gasoline, electric, hybrid, etc.). Each year, the EIA updates these assumptions in
the model to reflect the latest data and technology trends, often documented in an Assumptions
report. The Office of Energy Analysis at EIA regularly uses NEMS for special analyses requested
by policymakers, “performing policy analyses requested by decision­makers in the White House,
the U.S. Congress, [and] offices within the Department of Energy,” as noted in EIA’s overview [88].
For instance, if Congress debates new fuel economy standards or renewable energy tax credits,
EIA may run side­cases with NEMS to estimate the impacts on energy use and emissions, thereby
informing the policy debate with quantitative evidence [14].

AEO Assumptions Report
The fundamental basis for federal energy forecasting and policy analysis in the U.S. is the Annual
Energy Outlook (AEO), an authoritative publication released annually by the U.S. EIA. The AEO
provides detailed, scenario­based projections of U.S. energy supply, demand, technology deploy­
ment, and emissions, with projections to 2050. These projections are generated using the NEMS,
a modular and dynamic partial equilibrium model [86]. To ensure transparency and replicability,
the EIA publishes an accompanying document titled the Assumptions Report, which outlines the
complete set of technical, economic, behavioral, and policy inputs used in each NEMS module

The Assumptions Report functions as the foundational input database for the NEMS model. It
documents the sources, logic, and parameter values that govern all sectors of the U.S. energy
economy. Similar to Denmark’s Technology Catalogues, the Assumptions Report provides stake­

constructed. While it does not offer exhaustive data on every energy technology, it defines the
baseline modeling assumptions that underpin all AEO scenarios, including techno­economic in­
puts for energy technologies, macroeconomic growth trajectories, resource availability, behavioral
elasticities and policy contexts [86].

Denmark’s Modeling System: TIMES­DK
TIMES­DK – Denmark’s Integrated Energy Sys­
tem Model
Denmark’s approach to energy modeling is exemplified by the TIMES­DK model, a comprehen­
sive, technology­rich framework tailored to the Danish energy system. TIMES­DK is built on the
TIMES model platform (The Integrated MARKAL­EFOM System), which is a widely used bottom­
up optimization model developed under the IEA­ETSAP initiative. Introduced in Denmark in the
2010s, TIMES­DK represents the first national model that captures Denmark’s entire energy sys­
tem, encompassing all sectors and fuels within one integrated framework (see Figure 2) [5]. It
is a linear programming model that computes cost­optimal path­ways for the energy system over
a long­term horizon (typically through 2050 or beyond), given inputs on technologies, resources,
demands, and policy targets. Fundamentally, TIMES­DK determines how to meet the projected
energy service demands (for electricity, heating, transportation, industry, etc.) at minimum total
system cost (or under other optimality criteria) while respecting technical and policy constraints
(such as emissions caps or renewable share targets) [5]. This optimal planning approach is espe­
cially useful for exploring decarbonization strategies: by running the model with a carbon­neutrality
constraint, analysts can see what the least­cost configuration of technologies and fuels is for Den­
mark to reach net­zero emissions by a certain year [5]. The model endogenously decides on
investments in new infrastructure (power plants, wind farms, boilers, electric vehicles, etc.) and

the operation of the energy system (fuel use, electricity dispatch, etc.) in each future time period
such that the overall discounted system cost is minimized while meeting demand and policy goals.

as offshore and onshore wind, solar PV and bioenergy plants; conventional power plants; a variety

Energy Agency publishes baseline projections (a ’frozen policy’ scenario) and a set of alternative
policy scenarios that explore how Denmark can achieve its climate and energy objectives; These
analyses are powered by TIMES­DK at their core [16]. The scenarios are then used by the Min­
istry of Climate, Energy and Utilities and by the Danish Climate Council to advise Parliament on
policy choices. Beyond central government, even municipalities and industry groups in Denmark
engage with scenario modeling. For example, some regional planners use smaller­scale models
consistent with the TIMES­DK assumptions for municipal energy plans, ensuring coherence from
local to national levels [16].
Methodologically, TIMES­DK is a perfect foresight optimization model, meaning it computes an
optimal solution over the entire time horizon in one go (assuming perfect knowledge of future
conditions within a scenario). This allows it to evaluate the timing of investments (build now vs.
later) by considering trade­offs over time (e.g., early investment in an expensive technology might
reduce future fuel costs or emissions penalties). However, it also means that the model’s output is
contingent on the input assumptions about future trends (fuel prices, technology costs, etc.) that
are input into it. To address uncertainties, analysts typically run multiple scenarios with differing
assumptions to see how the optimal strategy shifts, a form of scenario planning within the modeling
exercise. TIMES­DK has a modular structure similar to other TIMES models, which facilitates
linking it with other models. In particular, DEA has coupled TIMES­DK with a computable general
equilibrium (CGE) model of the Danish economy to assess broader economic impacts of energy
transitions [5]. In this setup, TIMES­DK provides the least­cost energy solution for a given climate
target, and the CGE model takes these results to evaluate the effects on GDP, public finances, or
sectoral economic outputs (and can feed back into TIMES­DK with adjusted demand projections).
This model coupling reflects Denmark’s integrated analytical approach, which ensures that energy
strategies are evaluated not only on technical feasibility and cost, but also on economic welfare
and competitiveness.
Overall, TIMES­DK serves as Denmark’s strategic planning engine for the green transition. It em­
bodies the country’s commitment to evidence­based policymaking and transparent analysis. In
line with Denmark’s participatory governance style, the assumptions and results of TIMES­DK ex­
ercises are often shared and discussed openly, for instance through public reports or stakeholder
workshops. This openness builds trust in the insight of the model. By providing a consistent plat­
form to test ’what if we do X or Y’, TIMES­DK has guided decisions such as the expansion of
offshore wind tenders, the electrification of railways and the role of biomass in the heating sector.
It continues to be updated to address new challenges (for example, the model is being enhanced
to better represent hourly variations for integrating high shares of wind and solar and to consider
emerging options like Power­to­X fuels). As Denmark raises its climate ambitions, the TIMES­DK
model remains at the heart of analyzing how these ambitions can be achieved through coordi­
nated, cost­effective measures across all energy systems [5].

Danish Energy Agency’s Technology Catalogues
Supporting the TIMES­DK model (and much of Denmark’s broader energy planning efforts) is a
robust foundation of data known as the Danish Energy Agency’s Technology Catalogues. These
catalogues are a set of reference documents that provide uniform, up­to­date data on the technical
and economic characteristics of a wide range of energy technologies. The DEA has been publish­
ing Technology Catalogues since the 1980s to ensure that all stakeholders and models work from
a consistent set of assumptions about technologies [19]. Initially introduced to support municipal
heat planning in the 1980s, the catalogs have expanded over time; as of 2025, they cover eight
key sectors of the energy system, including: (1) electricity and district heating generation, (2) in­
dividual heating plants, (3) renewable fuels, (4) carbon capture, transport and storage, (5) energy

senger transport [19]. So, if a planner or modeler in Denmark needs data on, say, offshore wind
turbines, electric boilers, hydrogen electrolyzers, or electric vehicles, the Technology Catalogues
are the default source. They provide technology­specific chapters that describe each technology
and present a standardized data sheet of parameters.

Each Technology Catalogue typically includes two main components [19]:
• Qualitative information: a narrative description of the technology, including how it works;
its current development status (commercial, developing, or experimental); its advantages
and challenges; environmental impacts; and examples of deployment. This section often
discusses the expected development trajectory of technology, such as upcoming innova­
tions or known constraints. For example, a wind turbine chapter might explain turbine sizes,
capacity factors in Danish wind conditions, and issues like visual impact or grid integra­
tion. This contextual information helps non­experts understand the role and maturity of the
technology.
• Quantitative data: a set of tables or data sheets presenting key technical and economic
metrics. These usually include the capacity (size of the unit), efficiency or performance (e.g.
heat rate, conversion efficiency), availability (capacity factor or up­time), lifetime (years),
capital cost (investment cost per unit capacity), operation and maintenance (O&M) costs,
fuel or input requirements, emissions factors, and projections of how these parameters are
expected to evolve in the future (often given for current, 2030, 2040, 2050 in five­year incre­
ments) [19]. The data are presented on a socio­economic basis, meaning costs are often
reported as normalized costs excluding taxes or subsidies, to allow neutral comparison of
technologies from a societal perspective. Wherever possible, the catalogues also provide
levelized cost of energy (LCoE) calculations using a uniform method, enabling consistent
comparison of the cost per MWh of, for instance, offshore wind versus a gas engine [19].
Importantly, the Technology Catalogues are produced through an open and collaborative process.
The Danish Energy Agency updates these catalogues regularly (many are updated every two
to three years) and involves a broad range of stakeholders in their development (see Figure 3).
Typically, an update will be led by DEA analysts or external consultants with expertise in the specific
technology area, who draft the chapters and propose data. Drafts are then circulated in public
consultation or presented in workshops where industry representatives, researchers, and other

government bodies can provide input (illustrated in Figure 3) [19]. This process ensures the data
reflect both the latest global knowledge and local Danish context. For instance, wind turbine costs
might be informed by both international reports (IEA, IRENA, industry outlooks) and the actual
cost experience from recent Danish offshore wind tenders. Through stakeholder feedback, the
data gain a degree of credibility and consensus, as they become commonly accepted reference
points for analyses (see Figure 3) [96] [19]. The DEA emphasizes that the primary objective of the
catalogues is to establish a uniform, commonly accepted dataset for energy planning [96] [20]. By
having all parties use the same assumptions, debates can focus on strategy rather than on whose
numbers are correct.
The Technology Catalogues cover technologies at different stages of maturity: existing options,
those under development, and even emerging concepts in the experimental phase (though em­
phasis is on the first two categories [19]). For each, the catalogues often provide multiple projection
years (e.g., current, mid­term, long­term) to indicate expected improvements like cost reductions
or efficiency gains, based on global learning curves or R&D targets. For example, the catalogue
might show that solar PV capital costs in 2020 are X €/kW and are expected to drop to Y €/kW by
2030 and Z €/kW by 2050, given trends. This allows models such as the TIMES­DK to incorporate
technological learning over time, which is critical for long­term scenario accuracy [20].
In Danish policy processes, the Technology Catalogue data have an official status. They are man­
dated as the baseline data for certain analyses. For example, Danish municipal heating projects
are required by law to use DEA catalogue costs in their feasibility studies unless they have more
specific documented data [19]. This underscores the role of the catalogues as the “economic
reference data” for energy planning in Denmark [76]. Beyond domestic use, Denmark has been
sharing this approach internationally: the DEA has helped partner countries (such as Vietnam and
Ukraine) develop their technology catalogues (Interviewee G), recognizing it as a best practice for
transparent data sharing in energy planning [19].
The Technology Catalogues are essential to the TIMES­DK model, supplying the input data for all
technologies represented. This ensures that the model’s optimization decisions rely on consistent
and authoritative assumptions. When TIMES­DK evaluates the future energy mix, for example,
it uses catalogue data to assess the cost and performance of each option, including wind, solar,
electric vehicles, and heat pumps, at each projected time step [19]. As a result, the scenarios
generated by the model derive their credibility from the authoritative nature of the underlying cat­
alogues. If new evidence emerges (for example, a faster­than­expected drop in battery costs or
a new carbon capture technology), the catalogues are updated and the changes propagate into
the next modeling round, thus updating policy insights. Additionally, because the catalogues en­
sure all models and analyses in Denmark use aligned parameters, TIMES­DK can be coupled or
compared with other models, such as the economic CGE model or sector­specific tools like power
grid simulations, without inconsistency. By aligning on a common set of techno­economic inputs,
analysts and policymakers draw on a consistent evidence base, which greatly enhances the co­
herence of policy advice. As a DEA brief notes, the catalogues have established a “shared point
of reference” for energy modeling in Denmark, allowing discussions to focus on analytical results
rather than differing input assumptions [19].
In summary, the Danish Energy Agency’s Technology Catalogues are a foundational element of
Denmark’s energy planning ecosystem. They exemplify a commitment to transparency, rigor, and
stakeholder engagement in the development of data for modeling. By providing comprehensive,
peer­reviewed source of technology data, the catalogues support tools like TIMES­DK in delivering
high­quality insights, ultimately enabling policymakers to make informed decisions on the country’s
path to a sustainable energy future [19]. The combination of advanced models and trusted data
sources has been instrumental in Denmark’s ability to construct long­term strategies, offering a
model of good practice that other countries are increasingly looking to emulate.

3
Theoretical Framework
This chapter provides the theoretical foundation for analyzing how energy systems modeling in­
forms policymaking in Denmark and the United States. In support of this study’s core aim of com­
paring institutional contexts and participatory approaches, the chapter synthesizes key theoretical
concepts relevant to energy scenarios, the role of evidence, the construction and contestation of
expertise, and modes of governance. The purpose of integrating these perspectives is to pro­
vide a framework for assessing how energy models both reflect and shape societal values, power
dynamics and institutional structures.
3.1
Purpose of Energy Scenarios and Planning
Energy scenarios serve as strategic tools for navigating uncertainty and complexity in long­term
planning. Scenarios are used to explore alternative policy pathways, assess robustness under
varying assumptions, and support deliberative processes around future energy transitions. They
combine both qualitative narrative development and quantitative modeling to provide a rich picture
of possible futures [49] [63] [94].
In In the Oxford Scenario Planning guide to design a scenario plan to support strategy and public
policy, the authors Ramirez and Wilkinson emphasize the dual nature of scenarios: explorative
narratives that enable stakeholder engagement, as well as predictive models that produce system­
level projections [63]. In practice, this is reflected in the work of Venturini showing how participatory
methods allow stakeholders to co­develop scenario inputs and link qualitative narratives with quan­
titative analysis [94]. McDowall reinforces this by framing scenarios as ’credible worlds’ or thought
experiments that aid learning by analogy instead of forecasting [48]. Göke adds that scenarios
shape expectations and institutional coordination by acting as performative instruments that align
diverse actors around imagined futures of the energy system [33].
While previous analyses have emphasized top­down macro­economic frameworks, Schubert (2022)[68]
offers a complementary bottom­up perspective with an open­source agent­based tool that simu­
lates sector­level investment decisions under varying policy regimes. By capturing the behavioral
dynamics of utilities and end users, his approach fills a gap in understanding how granular actors
respond to policy signals and underscores the need to assess political and social feasibility along­
side technical viability—arguing that scenario credibility improves when institutional alignment,
public acceptance, and governance context are explicitly considered during development[68].
In practice, this dual role of combining technical feasibility through modeling with political and so­
cial feasibility through stakeholder engagement is evident in the Danish TIMES­DK model, which
integrates participatory scenario­building with techno­economic optimization. Stakeholders gener­
ate narrative scenarios that are subsequently translated into quantifiable inputs for the TIMES­DK
model, which enables alignment between public values and policy recommendations.
3.2
Civic Epistemologies and Cultures of Evidence
Central to understanding the policy relevance of modeling is how societies define and legitimize
“evidence”. Civic epistemologies, “the institutionalized practices through which societies assess
knowledge claims”, was introduced by Jasanoff and is discussed in the context of climate gover­
nance by Miguel et al. 2022 [41] [52]. Building on this, McDowall develops a framework around
”cultures of evidence”, which identifies three core dimensions: (1) How evidence is understood:
from value­neutral objectivity to interpretative pluralism (2) How evidence is used: instrumentally,
symbolically, or as enlightenment (3) How evidence is judged to be useful: in terms of credibility,
salience, and legitimacy [49].
These frameworks highlight that different countries have their own standards and norms for what
is considered valid evidence in policymaking.

For instance, Jasanoff explains that the Netherlands legitimizes evidence primarily from indepen­
dent advisory bodies, such as the Planbureau voor de Leefomgeving (PBL), reflecting a delibera­
tive and corporatist political culture that values consensus and institutional independence [41] [48].
In contrast, Jasanoff describes the United Kingdom’s civic epistemology as emphasizing proce­
dural rigor and formal quality assurance, legitimizing evidence through transparent processes and
standardized methods rather than institutional independence [41]. Understanding these epistemic
cultures is essential for analyzing the interaction between models and policymaking.
This argument is echoed in work by Süsser, who advocates for transparent, user­oriented, and
reproducible open­source models to align modeling practices with democratic norms [74]. While
not tied to a specific national culture, this reflects an evolving civic epistemology where openness
and co­production increasingly define what counts as credible and legitimate evidence.
3.3
The Role of Expertise in Policymaking
The question of who counts as an ”expert” is central to energy modeling and policy. Expertise is
not only about possessing technical knowledge; it is also shaped by social context and embedded
within institutional structures that grant legitimacy to certain actors and forms of knowledge.
Building on this view, Collins and Evans argue that expertise must be seen as both technical
competence and socially recognized authority [13]. Similarly, Freeman emphasizes that expertise
is not fixed but negotiated in practice, emerging through interactions and relationships between
stakeholders [30]. Together, these perspectives challenge technocratic models that equate exper­
tise with objectivity or neutrality, highlighting instead the inherently political and constructed nature
of expert authority in policymaking.
Andersen and Silvast caution against the epistemic closure of technocratic governance, in which
expert­driven decision­making may marginalize public perspectives [4]. They advocate for a more
inclusive framing of expertise that acknowledges diverse forms of knowledge.
Participatory approaches to governance offer an alternative, aiming to balance the authority of
experts with stakeholder engagement. This redefinition of expertise opens the door for citizens,
NGOs, and industry actors to shape energy scenarios and challenge dominant assumptions em­
bedded in models.
3.4
Governance Models and Institutional Theories in Energy
Policymaking
Governance structures mediate how models, experts, and stakeholders interact in policymaking
(as illustrated in Figure 4). These structures range from technocratic systems dominated by formal
expertise to participatory models emphasizing stakeholder co­production.
Technocracy in energy emphasizes scientific and engineering knowledge as the basis for decision­
making. It is often operationalized through complex optimization models like TIMES or MARKAL.
However, critics such as Fischer and Stirling argue that technocratic approaches often treat polit­
ical decisions as if they were purely technical, thereby excluding public values and political con­
testation [28] [72] .
Participatory governance offers a contrasting approach. Venturini and McGookin document how
participatory methods, such as scenario co­design and iterative workshops, foster institutional
learning and democratic legitimacy [94] [50].
Scenario co­design is a participatory process where stakeholders can actively contribute energy
scenario development by shaping assumptions, narrative and policy options. Rather than being
subject to purely model results, participants help define the questions and parameters guiding the
scenarios, which enhances transparency, contextual relevance, and legitimacy [94] [50]. These
approaches aim to embed stakeholder narratives into the modeling process in order to enhance
both relevance and accountability.
Melin et al. 2024 expands on participatory governance by advocating for the use of deliberative
ethics assessments in energy scenario development [51]. They emphasize the need for reflective

dialogue rooted in energy justice and democratic norms to allow for ethical scrutiny of future path­
ways.
Hybrid governance models seek to integrate these approaches. Drawing on theories from Science
and Technology Studies (STS) and deliberative democracy, hybrid models institutionalize mecha­
nisms for interaction between modelers, policymakers and stakeholders (see Figure 4). Jasanoff
and Collins and Evans support this view, emphasizing that policy­relevant knowledge emerges
through co­production, not isolation [13]. Co­production is defined by “the simultaneous produc­
tion of knowledge and social order”, where each mutually shapes each other and evolves together
[41].

Methodologies such as Alcamo’s Story and Simulation (SAS) approach exemplify the integration
of participatory governance with quantitative modeling [2]. By combining stakeholder­developed
narrative storylines with formal simulation tools, the SAS method supports transparency, trace­
ability, and reflexivity in scenario planning.
Venturini and Fortes illustrate how this can be achieved in practice [94] [29]. Specifically, Ven­
turini describes a participatory framework in which stakeholders collaboratively construct scenario
narratives that are then translated into quantitative assumptions [94]. This iterative process in­
tends to ensure that societal values and contextual knowledge inform technical analysis, while
also allowing stakeholders to reflect on and refine their contributions from model feedback. Sim­
ilarly, Fortes demonstrates the use of the SAS approach in Portugal’s energy planning, where
qualitative narratives developed through workshops were systematically integrated into a TIMES
modeling platform. The resulting scenarios were both technically rigorous and socially grounded,
which reinforces the credibility and relevance of the outputs for policy decision­making.
However, this integration also presents inherent limitations. Translating qualitative narratives into
quantitative inputs often requires simplification or abstraction, which can distort or misrepresent
stakeholder intentions. McDowall highlights this risk and argues for reflexive modeling practices
that explicitly acknowledge such limitations and incorporate mechanisms for ongoing adjustment
and learning [48].

Conclusion
This theoretical framework provides the conceptual foundation that guides the analysis of partic­
ipatory energy modeling in policy design. It outlines (1) how energy scenarios function as both
strategic foresight tools and socio­technical constructs; (2) how cultures of evidence shape the
legitimacy of models; (3) how expertise is defined and contested; (4) and how governance struc­
tures mediate the translation of knowledge into policy. It also incorporates theoretical insights on
open modeling, political feasibility, and deliberative ethics to enhance the evaluative framework
for assessing the use of energy models in participatory and institutional contexts.
All together, these frameworks support a comparative analysis of Denmark and the U.S. by high­
lighting the institutional, epistemic, and participatory dimensions that condition how models are
used and contested in national energy policy contexts.

Limitation in Methodology
Due to time and inability to successfully get in communication with all initially identified experts, an
uneven distribution of a total of 13 interviews were held during the month of April 2025. This study
encountered several methodological limitations that shaped the scope and depth of the findings.

undermined a fair cross­country comparison and narrowed the range of perspectives, particularly
from U.S. industry stakeholders. Moreover, the limited interview counts in each country do not re­

Denmark – Institutionalized Scenario Planning and Policy Integration.

facilitate cross­sector insight.

7.2
Institutional and Cultural Contexts: Evidence and
Expertise in Denmark vs. the U.S.
The use of modeling evidence in policy is deeply influenced by institutional norms and broader
political culture, which Jasanoff refers to as ”civic epistemologies,” or the socially embedded prac­
tices by which societies evaluate and legitimize knowledge claims [41]. These epistemologies are
not universal. Denmark and the United States demonstrate sharply contrasting logics in how they
assign legitimacy, interpret technical evidence, and involve experts or lay stakeholders. Following
McDowall’s application of this concept to energy modeling and policy and informed by Andersen
and Silvast’s hybrid framework of technocracy and participatory foresight, this section explores
how civic epistemologies in Denmark and the U.S. shape the role of modeling in public policy [48]
[4].
Consensus­Seeking and Trust in Technical Expertise in Denmark
Denmark’s civic epistemology emphasizes consensus, institutional trust, and procedural trans­
parency. This fosters an environment in which modeling evidence, especially when produced by
public authorities, is regarded as a neutral and legitimate basis for policymaking. A senior govern­
ment advisor involved in national energy modeling noted that “the DEA and Ministry put their
assumptions up for review … people can respond if they feel something is off” (Intervie­
wee E), referring to the annual public hearings on the AF24 scenario assumptions. Though few
changes result from these consultations, the process embeds stakeholder voice and transparency
into the modeling system.
This aligns with Andersen and Silvast’s characterization of Denmark’s technology catalogues as
consensus­building tools [4]. These catalogues, which underpin the TIMES­DK model, codify
techno­economic inputs through expert­stakeholder review processes. According to the docu­
ment analysis, their structure creates a “level playing field” for all actors to challenge or endorse
key assumptions. A Green Power Denmark interviewee noted this iterative transparency: “If we
overshot a cost or missed something, it gets corrected each year” (Interviewee K).
The legitimacy of modeling in Denmark thus stems not only from expert authority but also from
recurring peer review and formal consultation. DEA workshops regularly include academic re­
searchers, industry analysts, and NGOs. As a researcher specializing in participatory modeling,
“there’s a lot of movement towards open­source models and data … The principle is having
peer­reviewed revision of the assumptions prior to publication” (interviewee G). This reflects
a reflexive technocracy, one that acknowledges expert authority but invites stakeholder oversight.
A senior consultant involved in local energy planning explained: “you have to have the whole
picture … and also the more detailed [models]. It’s hard to get people to have a stake if
they don’t see the relevance, so we try to localize results for them” (Interviewee F).

Limits of Consensus: Exclusion and Epistemic Closure in Denmark
Denmark’s energy governance is often lauded for its broad consensus­driven culture, with cross­
party political agreement on ambitious climate and energy targets [4]. This collaborative ethos
has advantages for social cohesion and policy stability. However, it also carries a risk of epistemic
closure, which is a narrowing of viewpoints that can suppress innovation, marginalize dissent­
ing voices, and entrench dominant framings. In practice, consensus­seeking processes in Dan­
ish energy transitions have at times excluded alternative perspectives and slowed transformative
change.
Formal Participation vs. Real Inclusion: Even though Denmark has institutionalized public
engagement in energy planning, critics note that these processes can become perfunctory. For
example, municipal wind turbine planning is “often praised for its participatory merits” yet is
“not without problems” [4]. Research on Danish wind projects found limited, top­down partic­
ipation requirements that “’decouple citizens’ perspectives while providing a space for economic
and strategic interests that is closed in its character” [50]. In such invited forums, project devel­
opers hold disproportionate power, while local citizens feel their input has minimal impact [12].
As a result, genuine dissent tends to move outside official channels: community members resort
to protests and self­organized campaigns (a ’claimed’ space of participation) to voice concerns
[12]. This dynamic suggests that consensus­oriented procedures, rather than diffusing conflict,
may simply gloss over it, leading to public backlash. In fact, the growing local opposition to wind
farms has been identified as a ’bottleneck’ in Danish planning, forcing authorities to rethink partic­
ipation to avoid delays [12]. In short, a ’good process’ on paper can become a ’great illusion’ if it
eliminates oppositional viewpoints, delays projects, and creates mistrust.
Stakeholder Engagement as “Therapeutic” Exercise: Denmark’s consensus tradition extends
to expert and stakeholder committees that inform energy policy. These forums aim to align di­
verse actors behind common assumptions. For instance, the Danish Energy Agency convenes
industry experts, academics, and officials to co­produce its energy Technology Catalogues, which
set agreed­upon cost and performance data for future energy technologies [4]. Such exercises do
build a shared evidence base (creating “a consensus on the projected costs and data” that serves
as a common reference point), and interviewees note this helps “build consensus in society” by
moving debates past quibbling over numbers [4]. However, this very uniformity can become a
liability. A Danish stakeholder cautioned that if the consensus assumptions are wrong, “and we
are all using that, [then] that is highly problematic. It puts a lot of obligation on the tech­
nology catalogue to actually be trustworthy” (Interviewee M). In other words, an agreed single
worldview can amplify collective blind spots, a hallmark of epistemic closure. Moreover, social
scientists observe that public engagement in such processes is sometimes treated as a procedu­
ral formality rather than a genuine opening of debate. Andersen and Silvast note the critique that
much participatory input is “merely a ‘therapeutic exercise’ … aimed at securing a commitment
from key stakeholders” rather than challenging the status quo [4]. When stakeholder workshops
serve mainly to simply endorse decisions or confer legitimacy, they risk excluding more radical or
marginalized ideas, effectively co­opting dissent instead of integrating it.
Lock­in of Dominant Framings: A consensus­driven approach tends to favor conventional techno­

economic framings of the energy transition, potentially at the expense of novel solutions. Danish
energy modeling and planning are heavily based on ’proper’ and ’transparent’ data and cost­benefit
analyses as neutral grounds for agreement. Although this technocratic consensus provides a com­
mon foundation, it can also narrow the imagination of policies. Studies have shown that standard
model datasets often clash with on­the­ground stakeholder insights, and modeled scenarios may

collectively assuming certain costs or outcomes, institutions start to bring about those outcomes,
reinforcing the initial frame and marginalizing the paths not taken [4]. This self­reinforcing loop
makes it difficult for outlier ideas (for instance, proposals from citizen groups or non­dominant
actors) to gain traction in the Danish energy discourse. In sum, a consensus culture can lock in
particular technologies and policies, slowing the exploration of innovative or divergent approaches.
Overall, these examples illustrate how Denmark’s admirable pursuit of consensus can uninten­
tionally produce exclusionary effects in its energy transition. When consensus­seeking becomes
an end in itself, it may suppress productive conflict and creativity, leading to groupthink, delayed
action, or the overlooking of social inequities. Recognizing this risk, recent analyses call for more
reflexive and pluralistic engagement. For instance, McGookin emphasizes that truly participa­

merely confirming pre­set assumptions [50]. In the Danish context, this means designing stake­

including voices and ideas that were previously excluded, Denmark can leverage its consensus­
oriented institutions while avoiding the closure that constrains innovation and slows the very tran­
sitions that consensus aspires to achieve.
Pluralism and Skepticism in the U.S. Evidence Culture
The United States presents a markedly different epistemic configuration. The civic epistemology is
more pluralistic and often adversarial. Rather than a single institution setting assumptions, multiple
bodies, including federal agencies (EIA, DOE), state commissions, NGOs and academic teams
compete to define credible modeling narratives. As a result, modeling in the U.S. is more often
negotiated in political and institutional arenas than deliberated in technocratic consensus.
In this context, McDowall’s dimension of credibility is prioritized: actors strive to demonstrate ana­
lytical rigor, but legitimacy and salience must often be earned through strategic engagement [48].
A senior academic and director of an energy and society institute emphasized the importance of
trust, recalling efforts to “open the code and data to all” (Interviewee K) and involve stakeholders
in scenario design to avoid perceptions of ideological bias. Similarly, a senior U.S. climate policy
expert (Interviewee I) noted that a major environmental NGO built influence not just by publishing
models, but by maintaining long­term relationships with policymakers and cultivating trust over
time.
Document analysis of the AEO2025 and NEMS confirms this culture of procedural rigor [87] [35].
The EIA’s methods are exhaustively documented, with public release of all key input tables and
modules. Yet, as noted in the AEO’s disclaimer, “projections are not statements of what will
happen”, but conditional forecasts based on assumptions [38]. Unlike Denmark’s AF24 assump­
tions, AEO inputs are not subject to a public comment hearing, nor are they designed to align
multiple agencies [18].
This decentralization reflects the fragmented institutional context. Modeling in U.S. policymaking
is often issue­specific: e.g., the U.S. Long­Term Strategy (LTS) was developed through inter­
agency coordination but without stakeholder review [82]. At the subnational level, New York and
California use modeling more directly in regulatory planning, but even there, stakeholder advocacy
often results in competing model outputs [57]. Senior U.S. climate policy expert and director of
an academic institute on energy and society explained: “it’s very hard to align time frames;
research is time­intensive, and policymaking often can’t wait” (Interviewee M).

Transparency and Open­Source Modeling: Cross­Context Reflections
In both countries, transparency has become a central theme, but it is enacted differently. Den­
mark emphasizes assumption transparency through public hearings and catalogue publication.
The core optimization engine (for example, TIMES­DK’s solver) remains closed to most users,
although efforts toward modular open­source tools are underway. By contrast, the U.S. modeling
community has embraced fully open platforms such as Temoa, a techno­economic energy­system
optimization framework published on GitHub.
A former head of a major US energy information agency and academic researcher (Interviewee
H) explained that treating modeling as a scientific endeavor requires transparent practices from
the ground up, not only sharing code, but also providing a clear layer of synthesis that maps
raw output onto real­world insights. This shift, from model as ’black­box’ to model as dialogue,
positions transparency as a means to build credibility and public trust, especially in adversarial
policy environments.
Document analysis confirms this contrast. The EIA publishes detailed input tables but retains dis­
cretion over model structure and parameter choices. In Denmark, by contrast, modeling assump­
tions are vetted through institutional review and stakeholder consultation, relying on procedural
legitimacy rather than fully reproducible code. These divergent practices reflect distinct civic epis­
temologies. Denmark privileges collective review and shared assumptions, while the U.S. stresses
reproducibility paired with interpretive pluralism. Together, they shape the way participatory mod­
eling approaches are designed and adopted, influencing inclusivity, transparency, and ultimately
the impact of energy scenarios. The following section explores these implications in depth.
7.3
Participatory Approaches, Techno­Economic Inputs, and
Stakeholder Acceptance
A key finding from interviews and document analysis is that who participates in modeling and how
their insights are incorporated affects both scenario outcomes and the credibility and acceptance of
those outcomes in policy circles. Transparent handling of techno­economic inputs (assumptions,
datasets, cost trajectories) emerges as foundational to effective energy policy design in multi­actor
governance systems.
Stakeholder Participation as a Driver of Legitimacy
Participatory modeling fosters mutual learning and strengthens legitimacy. In the U.S., a senior
academic and director of an energy­society institute (Interviewee K) described the AGREA project,
where an engineering university engaged environmentalists, utilities, regulators, and industry to
co­develop New England electric­sector scenarios. Stakeholders proposed policy options, the
model tested them, and consensus emerged when participants agreed the model was reasonable.
For example, when the model disproved a favored demand­only strategy, stakeholders accepted
the outcome and revised their recommendations accordingly.
This example reflects McDowall’s notion of scenarios as ’credible worlds’, shared mental models
that gain authority when stakeholders help build them, and aligns with Andersen and Silvast’s
concept of hybrid governance, in which legitimacy arises from structured expert input blended
with democratic deliberation [48] [4].
In Denmark, participatory mechanisms are more formalized. Annual Technology Catalogue work­
shops convene industry, academia, and NGOs to vet assumptions. As one researcher special­
izing in participatory modeling (Interviewee G) noted, attendees feel they can influence model
inputs, correcting overshoots or omissions. Although expert stakeholders dominate, these pro­
cesses support ongoing model adaptation. A regional energy consultant (Interviewee F) recalled

a Bornholm planning exercise in which lay participants could meaningfully express preferences,
increasing local support.

Open­Source Modeling and Stakeholder Engagement
Former head of a major U.S. energy information agency emphasized that modeling is not just about
computation but about interpretation: “There’s a very important layer of synthesis to map the
model results onto the real world … that’s where the insights come from” (Interviewee H).
This interpretive transparency was a recurring theme in interviews. A senior academic (Interviewee
K) and senior advisor at a national laboratory with decades of experience working with a major
U.S. federal energy agency (Interviewee J) both noted that models must be communicable to
build trust. This interviewed academic made his stakeholder models fully open: “The model has
to be open enough that you can inspect it … here’s the code, here are all the numbers”
(Interviewee K).
In Denmark, TIMES­DK and Balmorel are backed by open­assumption catalogues, and Balmorel
itself is open­source. In the United States, open­source platforms such as Temoa, whose com­
plete codebase and sample datasets are freely available on GitHub for anyone to replicate and
review, illustrate an expanding commitment to transparency. Former head of a major U.S. energy
information agency and academic researcher remarked, “If we want to treat modeling as sci­
ence, we need to be more transparent … we started from scratch” (Interviewee H), referring
to the open modeling movement. This approach mirrors McDowall’s emphasis on transparency
as a precondition for credibility in civic epistemologies with contested authority [48].
Openness alone does not guarantee broad lay participation; interviewees stressed that sharing
code primarily enables peer review by experts. As one senior academic analogized, “It’s like en­
cryption on the Internet … you trust it because experts have vetted it, even if you haven’t
read the code” (Interviewee K). To move beyond a purely technical transparency, deliberate en­
gagement through structured forums for interpretation and deliberation is essential. Designing
participatory processes that integrate diverse perspectives ensures that open models translate
into meaningful stakeholder involvement and yield more robust, widely supported energy scenar­
ios.
Techno­Economic Inputs: Transparency, Uncertainty, and Bias
Numerous interviewees emphasized that input assumptions, not just model structure, greatly
shape outputs. A policy officer working at the intersection of research and industry in a Euro­
pean Commission directorate remarked: “With a model like TIMES, the benefit is you see the
big picture … but to be frank, you can get whatever result you want; it’s a strongly political
tool” (Interviewee C). Inputs like CAPEX, fuel prices, and carbon costs can be selectively framed.
That’s why transparent input publication matters.
The European Commission’s approach, publishing a harmonized “technology assumptions file” for
all scenario projects, was praised by both a policy officer in a European Commission directorate
(Interviewee C) and a modeling researcher focused on participatory approaches (Interviewee G).
Similarly, the Danish catalogues, as our document analysis shows, “anchor techno­economic as­
sumptions in expert­reviewed public documents” [4]. Yet even with open assumptions, model
opacity remains a challenge. PRIMES, while using open inputs, is proprietary, creating what one
researcher called a “black­box despite transparency” (Interviewee C).
To mitigate this, a policy officer in a European Commission directorate (Interviewee C) highlighted
the Commission’s multi­model comparison method: comparing PRIMES, POTEnCIA, and others
to isolate robust trends. This “triangulation”, also visible in Denmark’s use of Balmorel and
TIMES­DK in parallel, boosts stakeholder trust in policy scenarios (Interviewee C).
Modelers must navigate the trade­off between analytical comprehensiveness and communicative
clarity. The same policy officer noted that excessive scenario proliferation can overwhelm: “Sen­

Acceptance and Policy Design Outcomes
Participation and transparency build institutional learning and trust. In New England, a collabora­
tive exercise led by Interviewee K shaped a utility plan that balanced supply and demand strategies
and was embraced by workshop participants due to the collaborative process. In Denmark, DEA­

Danish interviewee explained, “They trust the model results as a fair baseline for discussion,
even if they debate the policies derived from them” (Interviewee E).
Absent participation, models may be seen as imposed or biased. A researcher specializing in
participatory modeling referenced Vietnam’s planning, where stakeholder feedback was often ig­
nored: “What is useful [to the government] in those analyses is considered, and what is not
is scrapped” (Interviewee G). This selectivity undermines legitimacy and stakeholder alignment.
In Denmark, model iteration fosters adaptation. The annual revision of assumptions (e.g., EV
uptake, offshore costs) corrects misprojections and embeds institutional learning. A senior U.S.
climate policy expert and director of an academic institute on energy and society described similar
loops at a major environmental NGO: models informed advocacy, policy outcomes were mea­
sured, and new findings fed back into the next modeling cycle. These cycles require both humility
from modelers and responsiveness from policymakers.
It was observed that a strong participatory design can be used to enhance both technical rigor and
stakeholder relevance. Hybrid governance models, which combine technocratic modeling with
deliberative input, emerge as the most robust. As Andersen & Silvast argue, techno­economic
scenario planning should be both expert­informed and stakeholder­shaped [4].

uptake, more credible models, and adaptive learning systems.
7.4
Summary of Comparative Insights
This comparative analysis demonstrates how Denmark and the United States integrate energy
systems modeling into policymaking in divergent yet increasingly convergent ways, shaped by
distinct institutional configurations and civic epistemologies. Drawing on McDowall’s framework
and supported by document analysis and interviews, the findings reveal that Denmark exempli­
fies a centralized, expert­led system of modeling legitimacy, while the United States relies on
distributed, contested evidence cultures that require modeling efforts to build trust and align with
shifting political coalitions [48].
In Denmark, modeling is embedded into the structure of policymaking through legally mandated
instruments (e.g., the Energy Outlook, AF24 assumptions), overseen by public institutions like
the DEA. As interviewees emphasized, formal hearings, cross­ministerial review, and expert­
stakeholder workshops create a routine structure for integrating modeling insights. These mech­
anisms reflect what Andersen & Silvast call a ”reflexive technocracy”, where expert knowledge
is central but opened to scrutiny and correction via participatory structures [4]. The Danish civic
epistemology thus values legitimacy through consensus­based procedural trust, a norm institu­
tionalized in techno­economic catalogues and scenario design processes.
Conversely, the U.S. case displays a fragmented modeling landscape, with models like AEO/NEMS,
Net­Zero America, and state­level tools operating in parallel. As interviews and document analysis
revealed, U.S. modeling legitimacy is tied to transparency, reproducibility, and strategic relevance
[45] [35] [38]. Modeling credibility stems from institutional independence (e.g. EIA’s method­
ological transparency) and the open­source ethos promoted by academic efforts (e.g., Temoa, as
emphasized by former head of a major U.S. energy information agency (Interviewee H)). But le­
gitimacy must be negotiated: expert networks such as NGOs, national labs, and academics build
coalitions to insert evidence into policy discourse at the right moment. This highlights McDowall’s
38

notion of salience as conditional and context­dependent; model outputs are only influential if stake­
holders perceive them as timely and relevant [48].
Despite these structural differences, both countries converge on several key insights:
• Participation enhances legitimacy: In both Denmark and the U.S., stakeholder inclusion,
whether through formal hearings or collaborative modeling efforts, increased trust in model
results. Examples like the Bornholm regional planning in Denmark and the AGREA partic­
ipatory modeling in New England illustrate how stakeholder co­authorship of assumptions
fosters greater policy alignment.
• Transparency builds credibility: Both nations recognize that clear documentation of as­
sumptions and modeling methods is essential for stakeholder acceptance. In Denmark, this
takes the form of annually published Technology Catalogues and assumption files. In the
U.S., it’s realized through the open­source modeling movement and multi­model compar­
isons (e.g., LTS, Princeton Net­Zero America) [82] [45].
• Iterative modeling supports learning: Denmark’s annual update cycle institutionalizes
feedback from model outputs to policymaking. In the U.S., while more ad hoc, iterative
engagement (e.g., EDF’s policy loops or RGGI planning) creates pathways for continuous
learning, provided institutional memory and political receptivity exist [26] [64].
• Modeling is socially situated: Across both contexts, interviewees emphasized that mod­
eling is never just technical, it is shaped by who builds it, who uses it, and how it is commu­
nicated. As one senior academic (Interviewee K) and a researcher specializing in participa­
tory modeling (Interviewee G) stressed, openness to stakeholder critique and simplicity in
communicating uncertainty are essential to retaining model relevance.
Finally, both countries appear to be evolving toward hybrid models of governance. In Denmark,
experts increasingly recognize the limits of top­down scenario design and the need for broader
inclusion of municipal actors and lay publics. The DEA’s local­scale participatory efforts and on­
going stakeholder engagements indicate a shift toward more distributed modeling authority. In
the U.S., where modeling legitimacy must often be earned case­by­case, recent initiatives (e.g.,
DOE’s Justice40 tool, or state­level scenario planning processes with structured community input)
represent moves toward formalizing participatory structures [80].
As one expert in advising a governmental energy modeling lab summed up, ”it often becomes
messy, but that’s how we figure out better solutions – connecting those dots between the
siloed experts and the people impacted” (Interviewee E). That insight reflects the core message
of this chapter: technical rigor must be coupled with participatory deliberation to make energy
system models both actionable and just. Energy modeling is not merely about projecting futures,
it is about co­producing them with those whose futures are being modeled.
7.5
Limitations in Findings
The findings of this study reflect the specific temporal, institutional, and discursive contexts in which
the interviews and documents were analyzed. One major limitation is the inherently situated nature
of the insights: energy systems modeling practices and stakeholder engagement are evolving
rapidly, particularly in response to new policy developments and technological advances. The
findings presented should be seen as a snapshot in time, rather than a comprehensive account
of how practices have changed or will continue to evolve. As McDowall stresses, the use and
understanding of modeling are historically contingent and may shift over time or with political cycles
[48].
Furthermore, findings are shaped by the epistemic positioning of participants. Interviewees gen­
erally held senior or expert roles, which may bias the findings toward institutional perspectives on
participation and credibility. While some interviewees reflected critically on institutional limitations
or transparency gaps, lay or marginalized stakeholder perspectives are underrepresented. This
limitation is echoed by Andersen & Silvast, who caution that formal stakeholder processes may

Another limitation concerns the interpretive nature of scenario modeling itself. As shown in Ven­
turini’s work, translating qualitative stakeholder narratives into quantitative model inputs involves
significant abstraction and simplification [94]. These transformations were not always visible in
the documents reviewed or fully disclosed by interviewees. Therefore, while the analysis identifies
robust participatory mechanisms, especially in Denmark, it cannot fully assess how stakeholder
inputs were modified during the modeling translation process.
Finally, the study’s insights may not generalize beyond the cases examined. The civic episte­
mologies and institutional arrangements that legitimize modeling in Denmark and the U.S. are
highly context specific. While comparative insights are valuable, they should not be assumed to
directly transfer to other national contexts without adaptation. In this sense, the findings align
more closely with an exploratory and illustrative comparative approach rather than a prescriptive
or generalizable model.

Conclusions and Future Research
8.1
Summary of Key Findings
This work set out to investigate how energy systems modeling informs policy design in Denmark
and the United States, with specific attention to the role of institutional structures, stakeholder en­
gagement, and techno­economic modeling practices. Through a comparative methodology that
integrated institutional mapping, semi­structured interviews, and document analysis, several im­
portant findings emerged:
First, Denmark’s approach is characterized by a high degree of institutional integration, trans­
parency, and participatory foresight. The TIMES­DK model is embedded within formal govern­
ment planning processes, supported by standardized techno­economic assumptions, and shaped
by structured stakeholder consultation. In contrast, the U.S. employs a more decentralized and
pluralistic modeling system, where multiple agencies and non­governmental actors develop and
deploy models independently. Here, credibility is derived through transparency and peer review
rather than formalized consensus.
Second, the legitimacy and utility of modeling outputs are deeply shaped by national civic episte­
mologies. Denmark’s culture of consensus, institutional trust, and procedural transparency lends
credibility to its scenarios, while the U.S. model culture is more adversarial and strategically de­
ployed, requiring stakeholder networks to build influence and trust in model­based recommenda­
tions.
Finally, both countries increasingly reflect hybrid governance trends that blend expert authority
with deliberative stakeholder input. Despite differing institutional pathways, both cases reveal that
stakeholder participation, assumption transparency, and iterative model learning are essential to
ensuring that energy modeling supports credible and actionable policy.
8.2
Contributions, Policy Recommendations and Reflections
This research contributes to the growing field of participatory energy systems modeling by apply­
ing the ”cultures of evidence” framework in a cross­national comparative context [48]. It deepens
our understanding of how civic epistemologies and institutional design shape model uptake, credi­
bility, and policy salience. Methodologically, the integration of institutional mapping with document
analysis and interviews offers a replicable model for future comparative studies. The thesis also
adds to the literature by documenting how reflexive, hybrid models of technocratic and participa­
tory governance emerge and function in practice.
Across institutional context
1. Standardize Assumption Review Procedures: Establish formal, recurring procedures for
reviewing and updating techno­economic assumptions used in energy models. These pro­
cesses should involve not only technical experts but also representatives from public agen­
cies, industry, and civil society to ensure robustness and relevance.
2. Enhance Assumption Transparency: Increase transparency around modeling inputs and
structures by publishing open­access documentation, including cost and performance as­
sumptions, scenario logic, and model code. This supports trust, reproducibility, and external
critique, which is particularly important in contexts where institutional legitimacy may be con­
tested.
3. Institutionalize Co­Design in Scenario Development: Move beyond retrospective con­
sultation toward structured co­design processes in which stakeholders help shape the early
modeling agenda, define scenario boundaries, and reflect their priorities in the narratives.
This supports democratic legitimacy and mitigates the risk of epistemic closure.

4. Embed Iterative Learning: Treat modeling as an iterative, adaptive process rather than
a single instance exercise. Institutionalize feedback mechanisms that allow for adjustment
of assumptions and scenarios over time, particularly in response to new data, contested
inputs, or shifting societal goals.
United States Specific Recommendations:
1. Formalize Stakeholder Engagement Structures at the Federal Level: The U.S. currently
lacks consistent, institutionalized pathways for stakeholder input in federal modeling. Agen­
cies like the EPA or DOE should establish formal consultation frameworks to solicit input
from civil society, state governments, and underrepresented communities during the mod­
eling design phase.
2. Harmonize Modeling Assumptions Across Agencies: Decentralization in the U.S. mod­
eling ecosystem has led to inconsistent assumptions across different models and agencies.
Coordinating common baselines (for example, around technology costs or emissions fac­
tors) would improve comparability, reduce confusion, and facilitate cross­agency alignment
on policy pathways.
3. Support State and Local Modeling Capacity: States like California and New York are
leading in participatory scenario planning, but many jurisdictions lack resources. Federal
support (financial, technical, and institutional) can help local and state entities build modeling
capacity, especially for IRPs and climate action plans that integrate stakeholder voices.
4. Facilitate Interoperability and Data Sharing: Modeling tools used at federal and state lev­
els often remain siloed. Creating standardized platforms or APIs to enable data exchange
and interoperability between tools (e.g., NEMS, ReEDS, state­level IRPs) can reduce du­
plication and enhance coherence in national energy planning.
Denmark Specific Recommendations:
1. Extend Stakeholder Involvement Beyond Technical and Industry Domains: While Den­
mark excels in structured foresight and institutional trust, stakeholder engagement often
remains confined to technical or industry actors. Inclusion of municipalities, NGOs, and
public­interest groups, particularly in assumption­setting stages, would enhance legitimacy
and broaden scenario diversity.
2. Caution for Epistemic Closure in Technology Catalogues: The Technology Catalogues
are central to Danish modeling and planning, but their role as the official data source can
create barriers to alternative assumptions or contested knowledge. Introducing more flexible
update processes, open feedback rounds, or versioning options could address this risk.
3. Improve Local­National Coherence: While national planning is highly centralized, local
governments (e.g., in heat planning) often face challenges applying national modeling as­
sumptions to their contexts. Developing tools that translate catalogue data into localized
formats, or that support two­way learning between municipal and national levels, can bridge
this gap.
4. Strengthen Participatory Mechanisms in Export Contexts: As Denmark increasingly
supports other countries through catalog export and modeling assistance, care must be
taken not to impose Danish epistemologies inappropriately. Supporting co­production and
scenario pluralism abroad is crucial to ensure exported tools support just and locally legiti­
mate transitions.

Adapting Processes Across Contexts
The findings of this study emphasize that energy systems modeling is never purely technical; it is
shaped by civic epistemologies, institutional structures, and political cultures. As Denmark con­
tinues to serve as an international reference point for techno­economic modeling and stakeholder

inclusion, its tools and practices, particularly the Danish Energy Agency’s technology catalogues,
are increasingly exported as models for other countries.
Yet as recent collaborations with countries like Vietnam illustrate, exporting these catalogues en­
tails more than transferring data; it exports Denmark’s epistemic assumptions. These include a
high level of institutional trust, low power distance, and a well­established consensus culture. As
a researcher specializing in participatory energy modeling methods and stakeholder engagement
(Interviewee G) noted, these assumptions can clash with more hierarchical governance systems,
where participatory forums are symbolic or narrowly framed. For instance, in Vietnam’s central­

concerns like land use, integral to just transitions, are poorly captured by techno­economic mod­

Such experiences signal a key lesson for the future: modeling tools must be reflexively adapted to
the institutional and cultural contexts in which they are deployed. Without such adaptation, Den­
mark’s model risks reproducing technocratic closure rather than enabling plural and just energy
transitions.
Future research should explore:
1. Co­creation frameworks for international catalogue development that integrate local knowl­
edge and priorities.
2. Participatory interface innovations that allow broader stakeholder groups to explore model­
ing assumptions.
3. Scenario pluralism, moving beyond consensus­based forecasts toward narratives that ac­

Moreover, efforts should be made to institutionalize feedback loops from marginalized actors, en­

of participatory governance. These recommendations align with calls by Andersen and Silvast
(2023) and McDowall (2024) to reconceive modeling not as a universally transferable method­
ology, but as a socially situated practice that must negotiate legitimacy anew in each setting [4]
[48].
Ultimately, Denmark’s leadership in participatory techno­economic modeling carries a dual re­
sponsibility: to uphold the rigor and transparency of its modeling practices, and to remain open to
contestation, context, and co­creation in how these tools are shared globally.
In parallel, the U.S. case also offers instructive future directions for participatory modeling inno­
vation. While U.S. modeling is often more fragmented and institutionally dispersed, interviewees
noted promising developments in inclusive modeling at the subnational level. For example, state­
level efforts, such as those in California and New York, have begun to build participatory struc­
tures around scenario planning. An energy modeler and consultant for EU initiatives (Interviewee
D) highlighted the growing role of local climate offices and NGOs in shaping the assumptions
used in utility IRPs (Integrated Resource Plans), illustrating how bottom­up stakeholder influence
can offset federal­level fragmentation. These emergent practices demonstrate the potential of
decentralized modeling ecosystems to foster contextual and democratic participation when well­
supported.
Future research on the U.S. should examine how federal and state­level modeling interfaces can
be made more interoperable, and how civil society actors can gain greater access to influence
early­stage model design. Particular attention should be given to communities historically ex­
cluded from planning processes. This would ensure that participatory processes are not only
formalized, but meaningfully transformative.

Interview Guide
Baseline Questions (for all experts interviewed)
Background & Role
1. Can you describe your current role and how it relates to energy policy or modeling?
2. How have you engaged with energy system modeling or long­term energy planning in your
work?
Participation & Process
3. Have you ever participated in a stakeholder process involving energy models or scenario
development?
4. In your experience, what works well and what doesn’t in participatory modeling or scenario
planning?
Understanding & Use of Models
5. How do you understand the purpose of energy system models like TIMES­DK or similar
tools?
6. Do you find the results of such models useful or credible in your decision­making or analysis?
Expectations of Participation
7. What would meaningful participation in energy modeling processes look like to you?
8. What barriers exist to broader participation or influence on modeling processes and out­
comes?
Model Assumptions & Transparency
9. Do you feel model assumptions (e.g., on technology costs, policies, behavior) are transpar­
ent and open for scrutiny?
10. How do you think modelers should handle uncertainties, values, or political trade­offs?
Additional Questions for Energy Modelers & Data Analysts
Modeling Approach
1. What modeling approaches or tools do you typically use? How are stakeholders or policy
priorities incorporated?
2. How do you determine which parameters or assumptions to include or emphasize?
Dialogue with Non­Experts
3. How do you communicate complex model results to non­technical audiences?
4. What challenges do you face in making models participatory or transparent?
Reflections on Participatory Modeling
5. Have you ever revised a model based on stakeholder input? What changed as a result?
6. How do you navigate conflicts between technical coherence and stakeholder preferences?
Integration of Narratives
7. Have you used narrative or qualitative scenario inputs in your modeling work? If so, how
were they translated into model parameters?

2. Core Modeling Tools
United States – NEMS Documentation and Integration (EIA, 2023–2025)
• National Energy Modeling System: NEMS is an integrated model of the U.S. energy sys­
tem. Equilibrium­seeking partial CGE; modular architecture reinforces agency silos (oil,
electricity, transport). Epistemic strength: directional sensitivity to market prices. Weak­
ness: limited representation of deep­decarbonization tech (PtX, long­duration storage).
• Civic epistemologies: NEMS itself is not a policy document but is the engine behind AEO
and other DOE forecasts. The 2023 NEMS Overview is a publicly available technical report
that explains the model’s structure and data flows (EIA NEMS, 2023). This transparency fits
the American norm of open government data, even though most users do not engage with
the code. EIA publishes NEMS modules and describes how outputs connect, reinforcing
trust through openness.
• Governance models: NEMS is developed and maintained by DOE/EIA (with earlier ver­
sions by JHU/SAIC) as a government­owned, modular energy–economy model. The 2023
Overview describes NEMS as a “central database” linking separate sectoral modules for
fuels, electricity, industry, transport, etc. (each with its own specialists) (EIA NEMS, 2023).
The modular design allows combining bottom­up detail with macroeconomic feedback. Gov­
ernance is hierarchical: DOE sets goals, in­house modelers and contractor analysts update
modules.
• Role of expertise: The model development is led by technical experts (economists, engi­
neers, computer scientists). The documentation report emphasizes peer review and revi­
sion within the federal agency. However, modelers note that many policy stakeholders trust
NEMS outputs partly on the basis of EIA’s institutional neutrality. The recent NEMS mod­
ernization effort is guided by DOE/EIA experts who chose to rewrite legacy code in Python
to improve maintainability.
• Techno­economic inputs: NEMS takes as inputs the same kinds of data as AEO (e.g. re­
source estimates, technology cost trajectories, demand growth rates). The 2023 NEMS
overview details numerous input categories (fuel costs, technology efficiencies, demographic
projections, policy parameters). These inputs are stored in a central database and fed into
each module. The recent NEMS integration update (AEO2025 Model Development report)
replaced thousands of lines of old Fortran and shell scripts with open­source Python code.
This change does not alter the techno­economic assumptions but is intended to make them
easier to update and audit.

• Civic epistemologies: TIMES­DK is the Danish Energy Agency’s techno­economic model
of the national energy system. In Denmark’s consensus culture, it is publicly regarded as
a neutral, expert­driven tool to inform long­term planning. The model itself is not widely
debated by politicians or media; rather, its outputs (published in energy projections) receive
attention. The use of TIMES­DK fits the Danish expectation that evidence is crafted by
expert agencies but subject to consultation.
• Governance models: TIMES­DK operates within a hybrid planning framework. It was de­
veloped under the Energy Agreement and is embedded in the DEA, which also maintains a
separate general equilibrium model. The IntERACT approach “links a general equilibrium
model with a technical model (TIMES­DK)” [5]. This reflects a coordinated, institutionalized

3. Assumptions
United States – EIA Assumptions to the AEO
• Civic epistemologies: EIA frames AEO as a policy­neutral baseline: projections “are not
predictions of what will happen” but a reference point for incremental policy analysis. Cred­

and modular documentation are published, and every assumption cut­off date (1 Dec 2024

architecture (11 cases in 2025) signals uncertainty rather than authoritative forecasting.

the outlook must appear annually; methods are inspected ex­post by GAO audits, EIA’s
advisory council, and occasional National Academies reviews. Since 2024 the integration
code was migrated from Fortran/shell to Python and open­sourced, which effectively invites
community auditing but does not give outsiders edit rights on official runs.

internal. Stakeholder engagement is episodic: EIA solicits data corrections from agencies
(EPA emissions rules, IRS tax credit guidance) and industries, yet changes are at EIA’s
discretion; there is no negotiated comparable to Denmark’s AF24. Expertise hierarchy is
clear: federal statistical standards and reproducible code trump stakeholder preference;
dissenting views are encouraged to run their own “user NEMS” versions.

sector. For example, separate notes cover prices, wind power, hydrogen, etc. The key
inputs (e.g. number of offshore wind farms, datacenter loads, CO2 transport needs) are
based on specialized studies. There is limited explicit stakeholder “participation” beyond
the formal review period, but the assumptions reflect industry forecasts (e.g. planned wind
projects) and public policies (e.g. 2 GW Nordsee connector by 2036 as noted in summaries).
In short, recognized experts (DEA and partner consultants) provide the data, which are then
publicly documented for transparency.

on fuel and carbon prices, technology deployment (wind, solar, heat pumps, PtX, etc.), and
interconnections (DEA Forudsætningskatalog, 2024). For example, it explicitly includes
new offshore wind capacities, hydrogen production scales, and CO2 pipeline use as per
policy targets. These assumptions are the “inputs” for infrastructure models (carried out
by Energinet). They are drawn from the same Technology Catalogues used in DEA sce­

efforts.

is ensured by intense media coverage and the legal duty for ministers to respond.
• Governance models: The council sets its own agenda but follows a statutory calendar:
inventory review in spring, recommendations in February. Draft chapters are circulated to
relevant ministries for fact­check; final PDFs are published simultaneously in Danish and
English. Public hearings are not required but stakeholder workshops are held ad­hoc.
• Role of expertise: Nine professors span economics, engineering, agronomy and ecology,
supported by a 12 person administrative team. Modeling work relies on DEA datasets,
TIMES­DK runs at DTU and bespoke bottom­up analyses (e.g. livestock abatement curves).
External peer reviewers are credited in annexes.
• Technoeconomic inputs: GHG baselines come from Denmark’s national inventory. Cost
and capacity assumptions inherit from the Technology Catalogues and AF24; land­use num­
bers build on Aarhus University agro­ecology models. The 2025 report adds carbon­price
shadow modeling and DAC cost ranges taken from IEA 2024.
Synthesis: Both advise rather than decide, but the US LTS speaks to a global audience and gains
authority from diplomatic signaling, whereas the Danish reports function as a domestic account­
ability mechanism backed by statute.
6. Compliance & Monitoring Instruments
United States ­ EPA Climate Enforcement and Compliance Strategy (EPA OECA, 2023)
• Civic epistemologies: This EPA memorandum sets priorities for enforcement of climate­
related regulations, reflecting the Biden administration’s climate agenda. It is an internal
policy directive, not a research study. It signals that EPA’s enforcement culture now explicitly
includes climate objectives. Civic expectations in the U.S. allow such executive guidance,
but it has generated political pushback (as congressional reports show). The strategy is
based on climate science but translates it into legal/regulatory action, fitting an adversarial
U.S. policy environment where rule­of­law is paramount.
• Governance models: The Strategy is a top­down mandate from the EPA Assistant Adminis­
trator for Enforcement to all enforcement offices. It formalizes a technocratic approach: EPA
is instructed to “prioritize enforcement and compliance actions to mitigate climate change”
across all programs (U.S. Environmental Protection Agency [EPA], 2023). This creates a
unified internal governance model but does not involve external stakeholders in developing
it. It is a clear example of executive­driven environmental policy.
• Role of expertise: Technical experts in EPA’s Office of Enforcement interpret this mandate.
They are expected to integrate climate science (e.g. methane reduction) into enforcement
cases. The document itself cites interagency plans and EPA strategic goals. It names cli­
mate science reports and earlier EPA plans as its basis. Enforcement personnel will need
specialized training (“capacity­building”), so expertise is formalized via bureaucratic chan­
nels.

Denmark ­ NECP Denmark
• Civic epistemologies: The NECP is a compliance report to the EU Governance Regulation,
hence its authority stems from EU law rather than national debate. Transparency is ensured
by publication in Danish and English, but public engagement is limited.
• Governance models: Compiled by the Ministry of Climate, Energy and Utilities in consul­
tation with sector ministries, then notified to the Folketing’s Climate Committee before sub­
mission to Brussels. Revision every two years creates an iterative monitoring loop.
• Role of expertise: DEA analysts prepare the energy­system projections; emissions account­
ing comes from DCE/Aarhus University. Stakeholder input is channeled through a written
comment round managed by the ministry.
• Technoeconomic inputs: Baseline is DEA Outlook 2023 with Technology Catalogue costs;
policy tables list every adopted measure with quantified impact and funding.
7. Stakeholder Engagement Records
United States ­ DOE / NREL Workshops
• Civic epistemologies: Framed as community science exchanges that bolster transparency.
Attendance is free and sessions are recorded, enabling broad audit.
• Governance models: Not a formal rule­making process; instead a consultative forum where
lab staff present drafts and collect GitHub issues that feed the next ATB release or ReEDS
upgrade.
• Role of expertise: National­lab analysts lead, with panels from utilities, state regulators and
academia. Industry data submissions are vetted but remain voluntary.
• Techno­economic inputs: Pre­release cost curves, learning rates and performance bins are
presented for comment; resulting corrections log shows which figures were updated before
final publication.
Denmark ­ DEA Hearings & Technology Catalogue Workshops
• Civic epistemologies: Workshops embody Denmark’s norm of consensual expertise. Le­
gitimacy arises from open invitations and publication of minutes; credibility from on­record
exchange of data.
• Governance models: DEA schedules at least two catalogue sessions per edition (kickoff
scoping and draft review). Comments are logged, responses published and, where ac­
cepted, reflected in the final tables.
• Role of expertise: Participants include OEM engineers, utility planners, NGO analysts and
academics. DEA moderates and retains decision rights but must justify rejections of sub­
mitted evidence.
• Technoeconomic inputs: Workshop feedback has historically shifted key parameters (e.g.
electrolyzer CAPEX in 2023). Slides and spreadsheets are distributed in advance to maxi­
mize substantive input.
