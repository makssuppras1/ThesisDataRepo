Abstract
This project studies colour printing, using plasmonic metasurfaces, made using DUV
lithography, aiming at making a final device, made from noble materials such as sapphire
(Al2O3), gold, silver or platinum for decorational purposes. Most research in the field of
plasmonic colour printing, has been done using E-beam lithography. This project uses
DUV lithography, exploiting the advantage of faster printing og larger areas as opposed to
e-beam lithography known to be very slow. In total 3 process flows were designed,
fabricated and characterized using fabrication and characterization facilities at DTU
nanolab.
First process flow revealed bright, plasmonic colours after metalization using e-beam
evaporation, however most colours disappeared after lift off. Process flow optimization lead
to a secondary process flow accounting for the poor lift off and this process flow displayed a
successful lift off step, along with some low intensity colours. A single bright area was
observed on this device before lift off, but disappeared after lift off. The morphology of the
bright areas observed before lift off in Pf1 and Pf2, were studied using scanning electron
microscopy, leading to a third process flow. The third and final device displayed green,
blue and red/brown colours. These areas were characterized, using
microspectrophotometry, measuring reflectance spectra. The spectra was used to calculate
RGB colours and CIE colour coordinates. RGB colours looked similar to visually observed
colours, but darker. The CIE colour diagram revealed a similar tendency, as had been
observed visually, that different colours were obtained, but not bright, as they were close to
the center of the diagram.
A forth and fifth process flow were hypothesized, expanding the results found from Pf1, Pf2
and Pf3. These hypothesized, process flows would incorporate the use of noble metals.
During the fabrication steps of the different process flows in this project, many different
colours of different saturation were achieved, leaving much room for further research in the
==PAGE:5==
area of large scale plasmonic colour printing for decorational purposes, using DUV
lithography.

Introduction
"I would like to describe a field, in which little has been done, but in which an enormous
amount can be done in principle"
Such was the introductory words, by Richard Feynman in a lecture held in 1959 titled
"There is Plenty of Room at the Bottom". A seminar in which he envisioned writing the
entire 24 volumes of the Encyclopedia Britannica on a single pinhead, and the possibility of
manipulating individual molecules, even atoms, to create such new materials [7].
A few years later, in 1974, the term "nanotechnology" was introduced by japanese Professor
Norio Taniguchi, and the field has gained widespread recognition since the ’80s [2].
Today, nanotechnology has evolved into what Feynman envisioned: a rapidly advancing
field of science and engineering, focusing on mastering the manipulation of matter at
molecular and atomic scales.
Nanotechnology has led to tremendous technological advancements. For example, while
early computers once occupied entire rooms, today, nanotechnology enables billions of
transistors to be packed onto tiny chips, providing immense computational power that fits
in the palm of our hands. This has paved the way for innovations like smartphones,
computers, sensors, headphones, batteries, encryption technologies and solar cells, just to
name a few.
The future of nanotechnology holds even greater promise. Researchers across the world are
exploring its potential for optimizing batteries, medicinal technology, development of
quantum computers and unprecedented materials, such as metamaterials. Metamaterials
are a type of novel, exciting materials, that challenges conventional physics. By engineering
at the nano and micro scale, scientists are able to open up the possibility of achieving
effects on macro scales, that have never been seen before[14],[1].

2.1
Plasmonic metasurfaces colour printing
This thesis explores a branch of nanotechnology called "plasmonic metasurfaces". It
focuses specifically on fabricating colors for decorational purposes with plasmonic
metamaterials, using sapphire and noble metals.
In simple essence, this involves engineering materials with different electrical properties
(conductors, semiconductors, and dielectrics) in periodic patterns at the nanoscale. These
nano structures made from specifically chosen materials, engineered in specific dimensions,
will result in colours on a macroscopic scale, visible to the human eye.

This project, its fabrication and characterization processes has taken place entirely at the
cleanroom facility located at Danmarks Tekniske Universitet, Building 347 East, Ørsteds

advanced capabilities in nanoscale fabrication and characterization. With over 1350 square

2.2.1
Metamaterials
Metamaterials are type of artificially engineered materials, designed to have properties not
found in nature. These materials derive their unique properties from their structures, of
precisely arranged elements on a scale smaller than the wavelength of incident light
(sunlight for example).
Metamaterials can manipulate electromagnetic waves in unconventional ways, such as
bending light backward creating negative refractive index materials, which for example can
be utilized for creating tiny high resolution cameras.
The field of meta material research field is broad, and covers electromagnetics, acoustics,
and mechanical science, all aimed at controlling wave propagation and the behaviors of
electromagnetic waves, through controlling specific boundary conditions of the interfaces in
the material.
2.2.2
Plasmonics
Plasmonics focuses on the interaction between electromagnetic waves and free electrons at
the interface between a metal and a dielectric (for example air). When light strikes this
metal/dielectric interface, it can excite a collective oscillation of the free electrons in the
metal, resulting in a phenomena known as surface plasmon (SP’s). These surface plasmons
confine electromagnetic energy to regions much smaller than the wavelength of the incident
light, allowing for extreme localization and control of light at the nanoscale. This can be
technologically exploited to manipulate light in ways that traditional optical components
cannot achieve.
The effects of these surface plasmon resonances, are utilized in a wide range of
applications. For example, they enable enhanced sensing capabilities, as the localized
electromagnetic fields can dramatically increase the sensitivity to tiny changes in the
surrounding electric field environment, making them ideal for detecting chemicals or bio
molecules. Plasmonics is also fundamental in developing ultra-compact optical devices,
such as nanoscale wave guides, that can route light within tiny photonic circuits, enhancing
data transfer in optical communications. Additionally, plasmonic effects are harnessed in
areas like solar cells, and even in medical applications.
2.2.3
Plasmonic metamaterials
By combining the principles of metamaterials and plasmonics, as has been is done in this
project, interesting new materials can be made. The plasmon/meta material this synergy
enables engineering designs of materials with precisely tailored optical properties, capable
Page 3
==PAGE:12==
of producing vibrant, angle-independent colors at the nanoscale. In applications such as
plasmonic color printing, these materials allow for high-resolution color displays, paving
the way for innovations in imaging, security, and anti-counterfeiting technologies. The
fusion of these fields offers exciting opportunities to push the boundaries of traditional
optics and develop new functional materials with unprecedented control over light.
2.3
The field of plasmonic colour printing
Research in plasmonic colour printing covers areas ranging from advanced encryption
[[11][16][28]] using holography [12], or making surfaces that change color depending on gas
present at surface [16]] to polarization dependant plasmonic colour devices [[21]].
Apart from the security aspects, using plasmonic colour printing as an alternative to
traditionally used dye or pigment is also of big interest [[23][17][21][17][25][15]]
Plasmonic colour devices displaying wide gamut has been found [[21][17][25][15][25]],
however most studies are using a method called E-beam lithography; a very precise, yet
very slow lithography method. Large-scale production of plasmonic color surfaces, remains
a research area with much yet to be explored.

3.1
Pf1: tools, process & results
Figure 3
The first
step in the process flow involves deposition of an
aluminum back reflector layer, followed by a coating
of a dielectric layer of alumina. This deposition was
done using a method called E-beam evaporation.
Electron beam (E-beam) evaporation is a thin
film deposition technique used to deposit thin films
typically ranging from 10 to 1000 nanometers (1 micron). It is a form of physical vapor
deposition (PVD), where a target material (anode) is bombarded with an electron beam
generated from a tungsten filament under a high vacuum. This beam is directed and
controlled by a magnetic field. When the electron beam strikes the target, it causes the
target atoms to vaporize, which results in a condensation on the substrate/wafer positioned
at top of the chamber. The process results in a thin film covering the whole chamber and
wafers loaded inside the tool. The thickness of the film deposited, is measured and
calculated by use of a small quartz crystal positioned inside the chamber. Its resonant
frequency changes as function of material deposited on it.

3.1.2
Thin film deposition process and results
Wafers were loaded in sample holder, lid closed, chamber pumped down to about 5−6 Torr
and 100 nm of aluminum was deposited at a rate of 10 Å/s on the four 6-inch DSP wafers
at 45% power. Instead of venting the machine back to atmosphere, it was kept in a vacuum
for the subsequent deposition of 50 nm of Al2O3 at a set rate of 2 Å/s. The measured
deposition rate, by the crystal monitor was 1 Å/s at 5.7% power. An additional 5% oxygen
gas was introduced into the chamber during the Al2O3 deposition to ensure sufficient
oxygen for alumina formation.
The entire process, from loading to unloading including venting and pumping, took
approximately 1.5 hours
3.2
Lithography
Figure 5: lithography
Lithography is a nano/micro-fabrication
process, used to transfer precise
patterns onto a substrate, either by means to define
a final design or as part of multiple sequential steps.
A lithography process starts with applying
a thin layer of resist, a light-sensitive polymer
that serves as the foundation for pattern creation.
This is followed by a precise light exposure step,
where the resist is illuminated through a mask, with the desired pattern.
Photo resists are categorized into two overall types: positive and negative type resists.
For positive resist, the exposed areas become soluble in a developer, and are removed,
while in negative resist, the exposed areas harden and remain intact during development.
A developer is a solvent used as the last step in lithography, which will dissolve fragile
Page 7
==PAGE:16==
resist. The resulting pattern then serves as a guide for further processing steps and the.
3.2.1
DUV lithography
Figure 6: Schematic showing how three different ret-
icles (physical masks) of identical design, but differnet
orientations (switching 120 degrees about central axis for
each step) will make a pattern that results in hexagonal.
Figure borrowed from [[9]]
The type of lithography used for
this process step is called Deep UV lithography.
To do a desired Pattern transfer in DUV,
one must make a job file; a code which tell the
DUV lithography machine where to emit light
on the wafer and for how long (which will result
in a given dose) and a what focus. Only one
focus was used in this project, called focus 0.
The machine will emit light though a
so called reticle, a physical mask that will allow
light trough in certain areas, and not in others.
While the concept may sound
simple, its a complex process, as diffraction
of light though the reticles will change
the outcomes of lithography, and engineering
these reticles is a whole research field itself.
The reticles used will form a hexagonal pattern
on the final design af seen in figure (6) and the resulting pattern will be nanoscale (around
100-200 nm) pillar formations in resist structured in a hexagonal pattern as seen in figure 7.
Figure 7: left: mask primarily cov-
ers the areas marked with pink, result-
ing in a hexagonal pillar pattern made
in the resist, that can be used for fur-
ther processes (right)
To make the lithography pattern the machine
will go to an area described in the job file with 4 different
corners defined by extremely precise x and y coordinates.
It will use the reticle with pitches 340, 350 and 360 that each
consists of 3 different masks that will be illuminated stepwise.
The pitch is given by the distance from center of one
pillar to center of its neighboring pillar. So to achieve a pitch
of P340, the distance between the lines (center to center)
is 340 nm, for P350 distance is 350nm and for P360 360nm.

3.2.2
Dose sweep, pillar diameter
Different doses of light though the same reticles are known to result in different pillar
diameters [[9][18]] and such a dose sweeps is preferable in a design where colours are known
to change as a function of nanoscale topography differences. M. Gonzalez [9] found
structures using doses 70, 80, 90, 100, 110, 120 on a process flow similar to this. Based on
these results, a dose sweep combination was chosen to go from range 60, 70, 80, 90, 10, 120,
130 J/m2 in this project, involving additional 60 and 130 J/m2 dose areas, to expand area
and possibly results.
3.2.3
Job file
The job file, that works as the code with precise instructions for the DUV stepper, was
made on a separate computer with specific software installed working only with the DUV
stepper machine. Exact details of how this job file was made will not be discussed but all
information on doses, resicles and positions should be available to extract from this work.
Excel file containing coordinates used for job file creation are attached in appendix.
The resulting job file works as a code, that specifically tells the machine to use the 3
different reticles (each consisting og 3 areas, giving 9 total) in 3 different areas resulting in
3 different pitches (distance between pillars) as seen in 8. In each pitch there are 3 identical
areas, that can be used to compare final result and and evaluate reproducibility.

3.3
Process
Figure 9: DUV stepper located in DTU clean-
room. Light is yellow as blue light will expose resist
on substrates
Before lithography in the DUV stepper
tool, the 150mm(6”) double side polished (DSP)
wafers were covered with a 65 nm BARC resist coat
layer in a tool called "spin coater: Süss stepper",
with recipe "1201-DCH 150mm BARC 65nm",
with resist name DUV42S-6. It it crucial that
wafers are of the type DSP, as they are flatter and
more even than single side polished wafers (SSP).
This evenness is crucial for the lithography step
as an uneven surface will result in bad lithography.
BARC stands for Back Anti Reflective Coating and
is a layer that absorbs back reflected light resulting
in enhanced precision in light exposure on resist [13].
After BARC,
a layer of 360 nm DUV resist type M230Y was spin
coated on top of the BARC in the same machine
"Süss stepper", using recipe named "1301-DCH
150mm M230Y 360nm", with resist "KRF M230Y".
After resist deposition, one wafer at a time,
the four wafers were processed in the DUV machine.
An SD card containing the job file
was plugged into the machine, and the reticle named
“LAB11-HEXCROSS-340-350-360” was manually
loaded in the machine.
LAB11-HEXCROSS-340-350-360 is a negative type reticle, meaning it exposes light in the
white areas in figure (6). M230Y DUV resist is a positive type resist, and when exposed,
areas will be brittle, so after development resist should remain approximately on a periodic
hexagonal pattern as has been shown in fig (7).
After SD card with job file is inserted along with reticle and a resist covered wafer, the
lithography step can begin. It lasts approximately 15 minutes pr. wafer, much faster than
what could have been achieved with e-beam lithography.

3.5
Lift off
Figure 12: PF1 lift off step
After
metalization liftoff of resist pillars were attempted. Lift off
is a process of immersing a wafer with resist made structures,
having with exposed sidewalls, to dissolve the resist.
Literature
on lift of on such small (apx 200 nm wide) structures
could not be found, and haven’t been done before according
to DTU cleanroom lithography group. A similar lift off
process, with similar process flow, but larger structures, has
been done in another thesis [13] and were used as reference.
Wet bench 07 located in area 346/D-3 is a wet bench used specifically for lift off. It
consists of three wafer baths, one with a heated remover solvent, named 1165. Next to it a
IPA bath, and then a deionized water bath.
The 1165 can be used with or without ultrasound to either aid a liftoff process, or turned
off for a more gentle process.
[13] used 40C 1165 bath at 60 minutes with ultrasound turned on, then cleaned 5 minutes
in IPA and rinsed with water as a last step.
The metalized wafer from Pf1 was immersed in the 1165 remover bath at 40C for 10

4.1
Pf2: tools, process & results
4.1.1
BARC removal
Figure 17: barc etch
After lithography and development
BARC is exposed between resist nanopillars on device. This
step 17 aims to remove the BARC, exposing the alumina underneath.
The removal is done with a method called deep reactive iron etching
(DRIE) which is a type of plasma dry etching. It is directional with
a force perpendicular to wafer flat, resulting in anisotropic etching, as
opposed to wet etching that has isotropic etching properties. The main
way it works is inserting a wafer in a vacuum chamber where electrodes
will make a magnetic field so strong that it will tear particles apart
resulting in free ions, called a plasma. These electrically charged ions together with a gas,
can then with another field be drawn to the surface of the wafer crating volatiles in the
form of reaction gas materials that will evaporate away an get sucked out the chamber.
The tool used was a SPTS Advanced Deep Reactive Ion Etching (DRIE) Pegasus, with
serial number CP0170 and is located in Cleanroom section C-1. The tool used for the dry
etch removal og BARC is called in DTU nanolab and is located in section 346/C-1. It is
named "DRIE-Pegasus 4" in DTU nanolab.
Since BARC is a polymer and hence an organic material, only using O2 for etching is
possible [5].
The etching process will result in both resist nanopillars and BARC being etched, but since
BARC is 65 nm, then apx (360-65) 300 nm tall nanopillars will remain, leaving plenty of
sidewall exposed after metalization later on.
The BARC etch process is described below:
Everything done at 20 degrees Celsius inside the chamber.
First a 5 min TDEDC cleaning recipe with name "clean\05min TDESC clean high power"
was run in an empty champer.
Then a dummy wafer was loaded and given recipe "clean\20min chamber clean". Its a 20
minutes chamber clean, with an oxygen plasma at 20 degrees. Then dummy wafer was
unloaded and wafer from Pf2 was loaded and processed, using a standarc BARC etching
recipe called "standard2". Process parameters were coil power: 200 Watt, platen power: 20
Watt, O2 flow 40 sccm (square cubic centimeters pr minute), pressure: 10 mTorr, for 45
seconds.

4.6.1
Optimizing processflow
So far bright colours have been achieved in both Pf1 and Pf2. Some common traits can be
summarized to the following, referencing to other work:
Sidewall coverage: figure 11 from pf 1 shows surface morphology of areas with bright, high
chromatic colours. The structures observed may be characterized as metal nano spheres
close to each other. Bright plasmonic colours has been achieved with nanoscale holes or
spheres in similar sizes, resulting in bright colours on macroscopic scales in other work
[[10][8][22]]. This effect may be due to electric field enhancement of the nano spheres that
are located very close to each other.
Both figure (21) and figure (11) shows what can be approximated to what other work [[23]]
calls "hybrid" plasmonic metasurfaces, with a device consisting of both antennas (nano
discs encapsulated in dielectric material (air, alumna, fused silica)). Both figures show
metal layer in bottom and "antennas" of metal on top of resist.
These findings lead to a third process flow where the aim was to have a final device with
both nano antennas, nano holes (hybrid) and these layers being close together to test the
hypothesis of amorphous nano structures being close together.

Outlook
In the project two additional process flows were planned and approved, but could not be
carried out because of tools being out of use in the cleanroom. This section will give an
explanation of the main ideas of theese procesflows and will therefore also be the outlook
section.
As seen in fig 30 etched pillars in Pf3 were very small and hence the height difference
between bottom, hole layer and top antenna layer very small. Repeating Pf3 but with an
additional 20, 30, 40 or 50 nm ect depth would be interesting, in order to see if brighter
colours would appear. An additional wafer from Pf2 was still available, and doing an
additional 10 nm ecth (giving 40 nm total) into it for comparison with Pf3 optical results,
would have been interesting.
Since different colours were achieved in Pf 3, an additional process flow with silver
(backreflector and metasurface) was hypothesised. All processflows have shown colours
only in the first areas with doses 60, 70, 80, 90. Removing the last (100, 110, 120, 130) and
changing the dose in a new processflow to 50, 55, 60, 65, 70, 75, 80, 85, would be
interesting. Also a thicker layer of alumina 100-150 nm could deposited, in order to allow
for deeper etch and therefore higher pillars, broardening the possible outcomes. Deciding
this pillar height could be aided by a COMSOL simulation, measuring the theoretical
reflectance spectra of unit cells with different alumina pillar height and silver layer.

7
Conclusion
This project has studied fabrication of colours for decorational purposes, using plasmonic
metasurfaces. The aim has been to make a final device using only noble materials such as
sapphire Al2O3, gold, silver or platinum, with bright colours perceived by the human eye.
Noble metals would only be implemented in a process flow, that had proven to show
plasmonic colours on a final device. The project also used DUV rather than E-beam
lithography as most other research has done in within this field. DUV allows for much
faster production of plasmonic metasurface colours. Based on an extensive literature study,
a first process flow was made, using DUV for lithography, sapphire as dielectric material,
and aluminium as back reflector for creating the plasmonic meta surface.
Process flow 1 revealed bright, intense colours after metalization, however they were
removed after lift off. SEM inspection of these colourful areas before and after lift off
indicated that the aluminium did not adhere well to the BARC. Optimizing this process
flow lead to a second process flow, aiming to remove BARC entirely. Also a thinner layer of
aluminium would be deposited for the device/plasmonic metasurface layer (25 instead of
100 nm).
Process flow 2 demonstrated a very successful lift off process, but colours were not bright,
despite similar research, with silimar process flows, that found bright colours. This effect
could maybe be attributed to the aluminium still being left on corners of holes after lift off.
Process flow 1 and 2 revealed bright colours on areas before lift off. These areas would
both have metal on top of resist pillars, and in the bottom. These findings were used to
design a third process flow, aiming to make a final hybrid device with metal-nano antennas
both on top of alumina pillars and a bottom layer of metal with holes.
Process flow 3 used a dry etching technique, using resist as a mask to etch pillars directly
in the alumina. This process flow yielded low intensity plasmonic colours, but blue, green
and red/orange areas were achieved, and 4 areas were chosen for characterization using a
micro spectrophotometer. Reflectance as function of wavelength in the optical spectrum
data was gathered and used to calculate RGB colours, representative of colours perceived
by the human eye. These RGB colours were compared with colours captured by optical
photograph image and shown i a figure together. RGB colours and optical image colours
looked similar, however RGB colours were darker than the optical colours. Three
dimensional RGB colour coordinates were transformed to two dimensional CIE colour
coordinates and plotted in a CIE diagram. This diagram showed a tendency similar to
what had been observed visually: different colours (blue, green brown/orange) but low
chromaticity/brightness, meaning the colors were close to the center in the CIE diagram.
The original goal of the project was to achieve a device made from alumina and noble
metals (Ag/Au). This remained the goal of the project, however there was not enough time
to do an iteration with these metals. A vast range of different plasmonic colours with
Page 33
==PAGE:42==
varying intensities were observed and documented with optical photographs during the
project. A fourth and fifth process flow was proposed, for enhancement of colour intensity
and introduction of noble metals, leaving plenty of room for future research in the
possibilities of large scale plasmonic colours printing for decorational purposes using DUV.
