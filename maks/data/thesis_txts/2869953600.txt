Abstract
Motivation: The purpose of this study was to investigate the influence of thread and
point design on the withdrawal capacity of screws in timber. The study aimed to determine
whether the geometrical design of screws, particularly the point design, affects the with-
drawal strength parameter. Existing standards, such as Eurocode 5 [1, 2], does not consider
point design as a variable in design calculations despite some manufacturers claiming that
different point geometries enhance performance. This project aimed to develop a model
that incorporates the effects of screw thread and point design.
Methodology: The experimental evaluation of withdrawal capacity was conducted through
pull-tests, in accordance with DS/EN 1382:2016 [3]. A total of 198 tests were performed
on 18 different screws embedded in three timber types: Laminated Veneer Lumber (LVL),
Structural Timber (ST), and Glue-Laminated Timber (GLT). The primary objective of
testing in LVL was to assess the influence of thread design on withdrawal capacity, while
testing in ST and GLT aimed to determine the impact of point design.
Results: Approximately 200 screws were tested, exhibiting withdrawal capacities ranging
from 6 kN to 24 kN. The results formed the basis for model calibration, providing insights
into the performance of each screw specimen, timber specimen, manufacturer, and point
type. The withdrawal strength parameter generally increased with timber density, with a
stronger effect observed in ST and GLT compared to LVL. Conversely, larger diameters
reduced the withdrawal strength parameter, with a more pronounced effect in LVL. A
small influence of pitch was found, particularly in LVL, where a smaller pitch led to
higher withdrawal strength parameters. Additionally, certain point designs exhibited lower
withdrawal capacities indicating their potential impact on screw performance.
Conclusion: This study demonstrated the influence of point design and thread geometry
by calibrating models to predict withdrawal capacity. It was found that certain point
designs resulted in lower withdrawal capacities. The key influential parameters of thread
geometry were identified, and the model attempted to incorporate the effect of pitch in
estimating withdrawal capacity.
While the data collection method was effective in principle, challenges arose in utilizing
withdrawal data across the two models due to material differences and variations in screw
behavior among the three timber types.

Introcuction
An increase in use of timber as a structural building material emphasizes the need for
optimal timber connection. Optimal connections relies on accurate design models to
estimate load-carrying capacities of fasteners. Understanding the load-carrying capacity
of timber fasteners is crucial to ensure optimal and safe connections in timber structures.
Therefore, accurate calculations and verification of timber fasteners load-carrying capacities
are very important.
Screws are the most commonly used method for joining timber
structures. They are either used directly as 6-10mm screws or for fastening brackets using
smaller screws. The focus of this project is on the axial resistance of screw fasteners in
timber, specifically withdrawal resistance, and the influence from the screw point and
thread design on the withdrawal resistance. Screw points exhibit diverse geometrical
designs, varying significantly by screw type and manufacturer, ranging from traditional
threaded points to drill points.
The new Eurocode 5 (prEN 1995-1-1 2023[2]) stipulates that 1d is withdrawn from the
embedment depth the withdrawal load-bearing capacity, although several screw manufac-
turers claim that the geometrical design of their screws improves the withdrawal capacity.
This results in questions regarding whether the different geometrical designs of screws
point design affect the withdrawal capacity. Incorrect assessment of the effective point
length could lead to incorrect withdrawal capacities. Furthermore, screws exhibit a range
of designs for the thread of the screw, as DS/EN 14592:2022 [4] allows for different designs
for the thread. Parameters such as pitch, thread height, diameter and ratio between inner
and outer diameter can lead to different withdrawal capacities, which is why research
regarding the influence of the thread design influence is important.
This project will employ the test methods described in DS/EN 1382:2016 for assessing the
withdrawal capacity of timber fasteners. The aim of this project is to determine whether
different geometrical thread and point designs of screws influence the withdrawal capacity
and to determine how the point should be accounted for by determining an effective point
length for the various designs. Furthermore, the project aims to demonstrate that the
withdrawal capacity of the thread itself does not vary depending on the manufacturer.
Through literature review, experimental testing and data analysis, this project aims to
enhance the reliability of axial resistance assessments of 8mm and 10mm diameter screws.
Results from own tests will be compared to findings from the literature in order to evaluate
the withdrawal resistance of various types of screws. The data from this will establish
a model based on screw design parameters and timber density to assess the withdrawal
capacity, based on methods for statistical assessment of resistance as described in DS/EN
1990:2007 Annex D.8 [5]. The model will be based on different parameters such as effective
point length, screw diameter and timber density among other relevant parameters, which
is found influential. Five different manufacturers have been chosen, totaling 18 different
screws in the sizes 8 mm and 10 mm. Ten different point designs are tested ranging from
traditional point design to a drill point. The screws are tested in spruce timber with a
density between 400 kg/m3 to 500 kg/m3. The moisture content of the timber is aimed to
be around 12%.

Literature review
2.1
Background
This chapter provides a summary of the existing literature, studies, and theses relevant
to the withdrawal capacity of screws in timber. The primary focus is on the withdrawal
strength parameter of timber-screw connections and the various influencing factors, such as
thread geometry, screw point design, timber density, and moisture content. The reviewed
literature offers valuable insights into the mechanics of withdrawal failure and forms the
foundation for this study’s experimental and data analytical approach.
Sandhaas (2024) [6], using an extensive dataset comprising over 6000 observations, provides
critical insights into the influence of several parameters on the withdrawal strength param-
eter. Her research investigates the effects of screw thread geometry, screw point geometry,
and timber density, offering a comprehensive understanding of how these factors interact
with one another. Her findings are an important reference for analyzing timber-screw
connections in this study.
Brandner (2019) [7, 8] studied the performance and properties of self-tapping screws in
hardwood, examining parameters such as embedded length, the screw-axis-to-grain angle,
and the effects of pre-drilling. Although this study was conducted on hardwoods, its
findings contribute to understanding the mechanics of screw insertion angles and pre-
drilling. The understandings and methods used are applicable to a broader range of
timber types. Brandner’s observations serve as a valuable guide for evaluating the effect of
insertion techniques on withdrawal strength.
Pirnbacher (2009) [9] conducted a detailed investigation into various parameters influencing
the withdrawal strength parameter, including moisture content, screw diameter, thread
length, and pre-drilling. The study introduced multiple correction factors based on these
variables. Pirnbacher also proposed an approach for calculating the effective embedded
length of screws by accounting for the portion of the screw diameter withdrawn, which helps
normalize results for the withdrawal strength parameter. This work provides important
tools for analyzing withdrawal performance. The paper by Pirnbacher has been summarized
in Appendix C.
Hoelz (2022) [10] focused on the influence of common thread parameters, such as thread
height, flank distance, and thread angle, using customized test specimens. By isolating
these parameters, the study provides a deeper understanding of how specific thread design
characteristics impact the withdrawal performance of timber-screw connections. Hoelz’s
findings emphasize the importance of optimizing thread geometry to enhance withdrawal
capacity. The paper by Hoelz has been summarized in Appendix C.
Westermayr (2019, 2020) [11, 12] conducted experiments to investigate the withdrawal
strength of screws inserted both parallel and perpendicular to the grain in beech and spruce.
For screws inserted parallel to the grain in beech, the results indicated that the withdrawal
strength parameter was higher when the screw point was outside the specimen, showing
that the screw point plays a significant role in withdrawal strength. In further experiments,
Westermayr tested screws with the point removed. He did so by first inserting a screw 2-3
thread turns into the timber. He then removed the screw with a point and inserted the
screw with no point. He found that withdrawal strength was significantly higher when the

screw point was intact. These findings highlight the importance of screw point design in
timber connections and underscore the need for further investigation into this parameter.
Gerhards (1982) [13] and Hoffmeyer (1978) [14] examined the effects of moisture content
on the mechanical properties of timber. Both studies demonstrated that higher moisture
content negatively affects timber’s mechanical properties, including stiffness and strength.
This relationship implies that moisture content also significantly influences the withdrawal
capacity of timber-screw connections. These findings emphasize the need to control and
account for moisture content when evaluating the performance of timber connections.
David Hoffmeyer and Jørgen Munch-Andersen [15] investigate the withdrawal capacity
of screws in plywood panels. The study examines manufacturer dependent differences.
While most studies focus on solid wood, LVL, and other timber products, this study
focuses on plywood, which is a common structural material. The experimental program
includes over 500 individual tests covering 126 unique combinations of screws and plywood
panels. These results are utilized in the calibration of the statistical model to predict
the withdrawal capacity based on key material parameters. The success of the model
calibration is measured based on the minimization of Coefficient of Variance (CoV), which
is the same for this thesis.
2.2
Withdrawal failure
Withdrawal failure occurs when the axial force applied to a screw exceeds the resistance
offered by the timber member in which it is embedded. This failure mechanism can be
expressed as:
Fax > Fw(fw)
(2.1)
where Fax is the axial load applied to the screw, Fw is the withdrawal resistance, and fw
represents the withdrawal strength parameter.
When this condition is met, the screw is pulled out of the timber. Depending on factors such
as screw geometry, embedded depth, and timber density, the failure mode may transition
from a timber-screw withdrawal failure to a tensile failure of the screw itself. Tensile failure
occurs when the material strength of the screw is insufficient to withstand the applied
force. Studies suggest that an embedded depth of approximately 16d (16 times the screw
diameter) may lead to tensile failure of the screw [8, 16].
2.2.1
Withdrawal capacity
The withdrawal capacity refers to the maximum force that a timber-screw connection
can withstand before failure. This capacity is influenced by various factors, including
timber density, moisture content, screw geometry, diameter, embedded depth, the angle of
the screw axis relative to the grain, and timber composition. While predicting the exact
withdrawal capacity is challenging due to the interaction of these variables, estimations can
be made by focusing on the most influential parameters, such as screw diameter, embedded
depth, and density.
This thesis employs experimental data and statistical modeling to predict withdrawal
capacity and investigates the impact of screw point design on this critical property.
2.2.2
Withdrawal load
The withdrawal load refers to the axial load applied to the screw during testing. In this
study, the applied load, Fmax, corresponds to the withdrawal capacity of the screw. This
load results in the screw being withdrawn from the timber member. By recording the
applied load, the experimental results can be compared with the predictions from the

2.2.3
Withdrawal strength parameter
The withdrawal strength parameter, fw, represents the equivalent shear strength at the
interface between the screw and the timber. It is calculated using the following equation:
fw =
Fmax
d · lw · π
(2.2)
where Fmax is the maximum withdrawal load, d is the screw diameter, and lw is the
embedment depth. In order to normalize the effect of the embedded depth, it is possible to
use the effective embedment depth (lef), which has the point length withdrawn from the
embedded depth. Different approaches have been utilized to determine the effective length,
which are described later in this chapter.
The withdrawal strength parameter accounts for the surface area of contact between
the screw thread and the timber, providing a measure of the timber-screw connection’s
resistance to withdrawal forces. However, it is a conventional way of describing the stress
along the outer surface area of the screw, as the stress distribution is not considered
uniform. Therefore, it is referred to as a parameter rather than the exact withdrawal
strength of the screw. Some literature and standards use the cross-sectional area of the
screw as the reference area for dividing the maximum withdrawal load, which is calculated
using the following equation:
fax = Fmax

content, and the screw’s geometric design, can affect the withdrawal strength parameter.
It is possible to establish a model for predicting fw based on specific input variables, which
is further described in Chapter 3.

2.3.1
Diameter and thread design
The geometrical design of the screw thread plays an important role in the withdrawal
capacity of the screw. The shape of the thread is crucial because it significantly influences
the interlocking interaction between the thread and the timber fibers. Therefore, an
optimized screw thread can enhance the withdrawal capacity of the screw.
Furthermore, the diameter of the screw also has a significant impact on the withdrawal
capacity. It is, therefore, important to investigate the effect of screw diameter on the
withdrawal strength parameter.
Many studies have examined the influence of screw
diameter on the withdrawal strength parameter. Pirnbacher (2009) [9] found that screws
with smaller outer diameters exhibited a higher withdrawal strength parameter.
He
suggested the following correction factor for screw diameter, demonstrating that the
withdrawal strength parameter is negatively correlated with increasing screw diameter.
kdiam = 2.44 · d−0.428
(2.4)
Pirnbacher (2009) [9] also found that the relationship between inner and outer diameter did
not have an impact on the withdrawal strength parameter. Sandhaas (2024) [6] investigated

to 9 mm, or 9 to 11 mm), the withdrawal capacity of the screws increased on average by
12.0%. However, the withdrawal strength parameter of the screws decreased on average by

withdrawal strength parameter.

2.3.2
Screw point design
When disregarding the design of the point, it is evident that the point will still influence
the withdrawal capacity of the screw due to its cone shape. The cone shaped surface
area decreases as it approaches the tip, resulting in approximately half the surface area
compared to a cylindrical section. Furthermore, the geometrical features (threads), which
enable a strong bond between the timber and the screw, gradually diminish along the
point. An example of this is shown in Figure 2.2.
Few studies have specifically investigated the impact of screw point design on the withdrawal
strength parameter. Pirnbacher et al. [9] conducted one such study, examining the influence
of various parameters on withdrawal strength, including effective length and point geometry.
They tested solid Sitka spruce and glue-laminated timber using two types of screws from

consequently, the influence of the screw tip. Screw diameters ranged from 8 mm to 12 mm,
with thread lengths between 50 mm and 180 mm. Pirnbacher et al. determined the influence
of the point by analyzing the slope of a linear regression function derived from a plot of
the withdrawal strength parameter as a function of embedded depth. They concluded that
the effective length of a screw can be calculated using the following equation:
lef = lw −d · 1.17

pronounced effect when the screw is inserted parallel to the grain. While this study provides
a generalized factor for determining effective length, it does not address the diversity of
screw point designs, which is an important consideration given the variety of available
designs.
Contrary to Pirnbacher’s findings, Sandhaas [6] found that longer embedment depths did
not result in higher withdrawal strength parameters. However, she concluded that these
findings could be caused by statistical anomalies, as the dataset for longer withdrawal
depths was not as large as for shorter withdrawal depths. Furthermore, the variety of
screw types and timber densities in her dataset was larger, which may have contributed to
greater scatter in the results, as thread design was not considered.
Hübner [18] identified a screw point influence factor of 1.11 when testing three different
types of screws in hardwood. Although Hübner’s study focused on ash timber, it provides
valuable insights into point design effects. However, Hübner’s [18] tests only included three
different screw types, limiting the knowledge gained regarding multiple screw point designs.
Both Pirnbacher et al. and Hübner observed that the influence of the screw point on
effective length exceeded the traditional 1 · d factor commonly used in calculations.

different 8 mm screws with distinct point designs, rather than analyzing multiple screw
types as in her previous dataset. One of the screws had a normal point, while the other had
a drill point. She examined the withdrawal strength parameter across varying penetration

two approaches, one approach subtracted the point length, while the other considered the
full penetration length. The results showed that by subtracting the point length from the
penetration length, the withdrawal strength parameter became nearly constant, indicating
no influence of penetration length on the withdrawal parameter. The results from these

measuring the screw.

2.4
Influence of physical parameters on the withdrawal
capacity
2.4.1
Timber density
Timber density is a critical material property that significantly influences its structural
properties and behavior. Higher density results from a greater amount of cell wall material
within the total volume, which enhances the timber’s structural integrity. Numerous studies,
including those by Brandner (2019) [7, 8], Gehloff (2011) [16], and Gutknecht (2019) [19],
have demonstrated that timber density is one of the most influential factors affecting the
withdrawal capacity of timber-screw connections. These studies show that higher density
generally leads to a greater withdrawal strength parameter. Consequently, hardwoods,
which typically have higher densities than softwoods, exhibit superior withdrawal properties.
Westermayr (2020) [12] found in his study that the variation in timber density is lower
when measured as drill cores perpendicular to the grain compared to parallel to the grain.
This correlates with the increased withdrawal strength parameter observed when a screw
is inserted perpendicular to the grain, as higher density results in a higher withdrawal
strength parameter.
The new Eurocode proposal [2] introduces separate calculation methods for the withdrawal
strength parameter based on timber species, providing specific factors for softwood and
hardwood. This allows for more tailored calculations and offers designers greater flexibility
when selecting timber species for structural applications.
In Sandhaas’ thesis [6], she investigated the withdrawal strength parameter by differentiating
point designs across a range of densities. She did so to examine the impact of other
parameters on the withdrawal strength parameter, as a high degree of scatter was observed
in results when differentiating by insertion angle and density. However, she found that the
point type did not provide any further explanation for the amount of scatter in the results,
as the drill tip did not lead to lower withdrawal strength parameters. She noted that a
more thorough investigation of point geometry could not be carried out, as the geometry
was not fully defined. The results from her study can be found in Figure 2.4.

characteristics of a board that contribute to greater withdrawal strength.

As a hygroscopic material, timber absorbs moisture from its surroundings, which signif-
icantly affects its mechanical properties. Gerhards (1982) [13] investigated the effect of
moisture content on the mechanical properties of timber and found that most properties
improve as moisture content falls below the fiber saturation point (approximately 30% of
the mass). The fiber saturation point is reached when the cell walls are fully saturated, but
the cell cavities are empty. Below this point, timber begins to shrink. Figure 2.5 illustrates
the different stages of moisture content in timber.

At 6% moisture content, he found a 20% increase in modulus of elasticity perpendicular to

increases mechanical properties, the risk of timber splitting also rises as moisture content

The moisture content of timber significantly affects its mechanical properties, including
stiffness and strength. Consequently, moisture content also influences withdrawal capacity.
Pirnbacher et al. [9] investigated the relationship between moisture content and withdrawal
capacity in their 2009 study, examining the effects of moisture content on withdrawal
capacity. They tested four levels of timber moisture content (0%, 9%, 14%, and 19%) on

to the grain. Their findings indicated that samples with moisture content below 10% were
more prone to splitting, while samples with moisture content above 10% showed a slight
decrease in the withdrawal strength parameter. Based on these results, they suggested
that no correction is necessary for timber with moisture content between 8% and 12%.
However, for moisture content between 12% and 20%, they recommended a reduction in
the withdrawal strength parameter of 0.65% per percentage point increase in moisture
content above 12%. This project uses a climate chamber to achieve a moisture content

2.4.3
Screw-axis to grain angle
An important factor to consider when determining the withdrawal capacity of a timber-
screw connection is the angle at which the screw is inserted relative to the timber grain.
This angle can vary from perpendicular to parallel to the grain, corresponding to an
insertion angle between 0 and 90 degrees. Here, 0 degrees represents parallel to the grain,
and 90 degrees represents perpendicular to the grain. The angle relative to the grain has a
significant impact on the withdrawal capacity of the screw, and this behavior has been
observed in numerous studies, including those by Sandhaas and Brandner [6, 7, 8]. It is
well established that the withdrawal capacity is highest when the screw-axis-to-grain angle
is 90 degrees.
Brandner (2019) [7] suggests an inflection point at α = 30◦in his study on the performance
of axially loaded screws in hardwood. He found that when the screw-axis-to-grain angle
increased from α = 0◦to α = 30◦, the withdrawal capacity increased linearly. However,
when the angle changed from α = 30◦to α = 90◦, the withdrawal capacity did not change
significantly. Based on his observations, he proposed that fax,α = fax,90 for α ≥30◦,
and fax,0 ≤fax,α < fax,90 when α < 30◦. This implies that the withdrawal capacity can
be considered equal for screw-axis-to-grain angles of α ≥30◦and that corrections to
the withdrawal capacity should be made for angles α < 30◦, as also suggested in prEN
1995-1-1:2023 [2]. Brandner’s study [8] indicates that for each degree below α = 30◦, the
withdrawal strength parameter decreases by approximately 1%.
When a screw is inserted perpendicular to the grain, the screw threads cut across the fibers,
creating a stronger mechanical interlock and resulting in a higher withdrawal capacity.
Conversely, when screws are inserted parallel to the grain, the thread follows the fibers,
resulting in less friction and lower withdrawal capacity. In this study, only screws inserted
perpendicular to the grain are investigated. This approach ensures that the withdrawal
capacity of the screws is fully considered, eliminating the need for corrections in the model
due to angular insertion negatively affecting the withdrawal strength parameter.

Numerous studies have focused on the withdrawal capacity of screws, leading to the

tailored to predict the withdrawal capacity of self-tapping screws in different timber types,
with varying screw parameters such as diameter, embedded depth, or screw-axis-to-grain

Although many models exist for predicting withdrawal capacity, screw point geometry is
rarely a parameter in these models, with the exception of the study by Pirnbacher and
Brandner (2009) [9]. In this study, a correction factor for effective length was proposed by
subtracting the point length using klength = d·1.17. Sandhaas [6] has also examined various
input parameters for the design of timber fasteners. Her study included a preliminary
analysis of screw point design but she concluded that the data was insufficient to establish
clear findings regarding point geometry. However, she found that a point length of 1d was
sufficient for normalizing the effects of withdrawal length. While these models consider the
effective point length, they do not account for differences in point design, underscoring the
need for further investigation into screw point design and its effect on withdrawal capacity.

3.2.1
Choice of parameters
As shown above, the chosen model relies on parameters. The purpose of the parameters is,
first and foremost, to fit the model to the results obtained from the experiments. Initially,
the parameters are chosen arbitrarily with the aim of fitting the model to the results.
However, the parameters will also provide information regarding the performance of the
timber, screw, and point design.
Each screw and timber combination is associated with a specific set of k-parameters, whereas
the reference values apply to the entire dataset, meaning that only one reference withdrawal
strength parameter, density, and diameter can be chosen. The unique combination of
k-parameters for each data point will provide valuable insight into the performance of each
unique test result.
A higher value of the parameters kwood and kscrew will indicate that the performance of the
test is generally better, whereas a higher value of kpoint will suggest poorer performance of
the point design, as the kpoint value estimates the effective point length withdrawn from
the embedment depth. It is worth noting that it is generally accepted in the literature
and standards that a certain percentage of the diameter must be withdrawn from the
embedment depth.
Since there are ten point design types and eighteen different sets of screws, some screws will
share the same kpoint. This means that multiple kscrew parameters will be influenced by the
same kpoint, and ultimately, they will influence each other. This is visually demonstrated

3.3
Comparison between Eurocode 5 and project model
The model found in the Eurocode for calculating the withdrawal strength parameter relies
on empirical factors such as kscrew, kw, kρ, and kmat. These empirical factors provide
flexibility and adaptability for various materials and conditions. However, they are not
represented as physical properties, but rather as scaling factors for the calculation under
different conditions. The kscrew factor depends on the screw used and ranges from 6 to
10; however, the value 7.5 may be used for most screws. Using an exponent factor of
-0.33 for the screw diameter establishes a relationship between the withdrawal strength
parameter and diameter, where the withdrawal strength parameter is negatively correlated
with increasing diameter. However, the diameter loses its physical meaning, as no units are
used in the calculation. The parameters used in the calculation of the withdrawal strength
cause a lack of intuitive, physically-based rationale, which is necessary for a better and
deeper understanding of the application of the withdrawal strength parameter.
The model used in this thesis emphasizes the physical parameters by introducing reference
values. The model is based on measurable properties, which makes it more physically
understandable. The aim of using the reference values is to eliminate any parameter that
causes the model to lose its physical meaning. The reference values also allow for direct
scaling based on timber density and screw diameter. The introduction of kpoint allows for
the correction of embedment depth to account for the effective point length. The effective
screw length, however, retains its physical meaning. Even though the thesis utilizes the
factors kwood and kscrew, these are primarily used during the statistical analysis phase to
identify deviations and evaluate material- or product-specific influences. The ultimate goal
of the thesis model is to create a unified approach, with these factors converging to a value
of one, reflecting minimal deviation and ensuring the model remains grounded in physical
principles.
The thesis model’s reliance on physically-based reference values and measurable properties
offers a more robust, scientifically meaningful approach compared to the proposed standard
model.
The proposed standard model emphasizes adaptability but is less physically
meaningful. This makes the thesis model not only more transparent but also more reliable
for understanding the fundamental mechanics of withdrawal capacity in timber connections.

Methodology
5.1
Experimental design
5.1.1
Pull-test methodology
To determine the impact of screw point and thread design on withdrawal capacity, the
screws are subjected to a pull test. It is important to test as many combinations as possible
due to the interest in different parameters, such as density, screw thread geometry, and
timber type. Therefore, each screw is tested in the three timber types (LVL, ST, and GLT)
used in this thesis. The screws are subjected to a test in which they are inserted into LVL
with the point going all the way through, allowing for the determination of the maximum
allowable withdrawal force of the thread alone. Additionally, the screws are tested with
the screw point embedded in both ST and GLT. The test methods are in accordance with
DS/EN 1382:2016 [3]. According to this standard, the loading rate should be set so that
the time to reach the maximum force, Fmax, is within (60±15) seconds [3].
During each test, a load cell records the Fmax values and automatically stores them in
an Excel sheet for efficient tracking. Given the large number of tests, it is crucial to
document key information for each trial, including timber type, density, moisture content,
screw type, embedded depth, diameter, point design, and other relevant parameters.
This comprehensive data sheet will later be used for in-depth analysis, facilitating the
development of an accurate model to assess withdrawal capacity.
To determine the impact of screw point design on the withdrawal parameter, it is essential
to isolate the withdrawal capacity of the threaded portion of the screw. This is achieved
by conducting pull tests in a thin piece of timber, such as LVL, where the screw is inserted
completely through the material so that only the threaded part is in contact with the
timber. The maximum allowable withdrawal force is then measured. The objective is
to demonstrate that the withdrawal capacity of the threaded portion is independent of
the manufacturer and instead governed by the geometrical design of the screw, which is
analyzed and used for model calibration. Additionally, Hoelz et al. (2022) [10] describe how
parameters such as thread height, flank distance, and thread angle influence withdrawal
strength.
In contrast, withdrawal strength tests in ST and GLT are performed as pull tests where the
screw point remains embedded in the timber. This test approach accounts for the influence
of screw point geometry on withdrawal capacity, providing insight into how different screw
point designs affect withdrawal capacity. By comparing these results with the LVL tests,
the relative significance of thread influence versus screw point influence can be assessed by
incorporating the performance of the thread from the LVL experiments into the model for
ST and GLT.
The pull-test system used for the two experiments is shown in Figure 5.1. The test setup
consists of a chain and different braces. The system is capable of withstanding a force of
60 kN but has an allowable load of 20 kN due to a safety factor of 3. The system has been
verified in Appendix B.

5.3
Methodology for data analysis
The calibration of the models in this study aims to enhance the accuracy of predicting
the withdrawal capacity of screws in timber. The primary objective is to minimize the
coefficient of variation (CoV), thereby improving the consistency and reliability of the
prediction models, as described in Annex D of DS/EN 1990:2023 [5].
5.3.1
Selection of variables and calibration process
To account for variations in timber properties and screw performance, three key specimen
factors are introduced: kwood, kscrew, and kpoint.
These factors adjust the predicted
withdrawal capacity by accounting for the effects of timber, screws, and screw points on
withdrawal capacity. Initial reference values follow prEN 1995-1-1:2023 and are selected to
minimize the mean error. The exponents in the model are initially chosen to align with
the literature.
Model calibration is performed using Excel’s Solver function, which iteratively adjusts
the k-factors to achieve the lowest possible CoV. Since Solver does not utilize constraints,
Axial resistance of screws in timber
37
==PAGE:52==
calibration can sometimes yield unrealistic k-factors. To prevent this, a stepwise approach
is implemented to ensure that individual variables are adjusted systematically to optimize
the full model.
5.3.2
Development of calibration models
Three different model calibrations are created in order to achieve a better understanding
of the behavior of screws in timber.
Simple model
This baseline model assigns all k-factors a value of 1.0 to assess general trends in with-
drawal capacity. By applying uniform factors, the model provides an initial evaluation of
performance differences between timber types and screws, highlighting the need for further
refinement. The reference values are inspired by prEN 1995-1-1:2023 [2], as an initial step
for analyzing. These values are however optimized during the model calibration for the
next models.
Optimized model
A refined version of the model individually adjusts the k-factors based on observed devia-
tions. Separate models are created for LVL and ST together with GLT to account for their
different purposes. The LVL model investigates the thread influence, and the ST/GLT
model investigates the point influence. The adjustments to the k-factors aim to reduce the
CoV while ensuring that the parameters remain physically meaningful. The purpose of
these adjustments is to better represent the behavior of the screws and the influence of the
timber properties while still achieving a consistent and reliable model.
Simplified model
To evaluate the sensitivity of the models, a simplified version is created. The optimized
k-factors are rounded to multiples of 0.05 or 0.1. This allows for a sensitivity check when
modifying the model factors, followed by an analysis of the change in CoV. A slight increase
in CoV is acceptable if the simplifications provide further understanding of screw behavior
and contribute to a more uniform model.
5.3.3
Assessment of model accuracy
Each model undergoes evaluation by comparing predicted withdrawal capacities with
observed test values. Scatter plots and error plots assess the alignment of predictions with
measured results. Special focus is placed on identifying screws and timber specimens that
consistently deviate from expectations, which might indicate some influence from either
thread geometry, point design or material properties.
5.3.4
Challenges and considerations
A key challenge in the calibration process is the strong correlation between screw and point
type, making it difficult to separate their individual effects. The variability of ST/GLT
timber properties also contributes to higher CoV values compared to LVL. While refining
the k-factors improves model consistency, some deviations still exists, these are further
analyzed. Refinements made in the discussion may involve incorporating geometric screw
features into the model to better understand their influence on withdrawal capacity.

7.2
Model calibration for ST and GLT
The model calibration for ST and GLT introduces the k-factor for the point type, which
aids in understanding the performance of each point type. The kscrew (screw factor) and
kpoint (point factor) are strongly correlated, making it difficult to determine whether it is
the screw or point that is over- or underperforming. It is therefore beneficial to lock one of
the factors to a uniform value, which allows for a separate analysis, whereas a combined
analysis would make the thread and point influence interfere. It is also explored whether
any geometrical features of the screws could have an impact. If this can be determined, it
will be possible to create a more uniform optimal set of kscrew-factors, which allows for a
better analysis of the point type’s performance. This is further explored in Chapter 8.

8
Discussion
In this chapter, the influence of physical and geometrical parameters are investigated. The
physical parameter investigated is the influence of density. The geometrical parameters
investigated are the diameter, pitch, and thread height. In order to conduct a thorough
investigation of these parameters, a new model is introduced. The model is a basic model
with exponents of 0 in order to mitigate the influence of density and diameter. This
model is utilized for comparison with the simple, optimal model and observations from the
literature.
Furthermore, an alternative model is created in order to mitigate influences from geometrical
parameters, which potentially leads to a better representation of point influence. The
alternative model is based on the observations found in the first part of this chapter.
8.1
Influence of physical parameters on the withdrawal
capacity
As described in the literature review, many studies have found that the density of the
timber has a large influence on the withdrawal capacity [7, 8, 16, 19]. This is a general
acknowledgment in multiple studies and is utilized when calculating the withdrawal capacity
in accordance with the Eurocodes DS/EN1995-1-1:2007 and prEN 1995-1-1:2023 [1, 2],
where exponents for the densities are larger than one, indicating a non-linear correlation
between density and the withdrawal strength parameter.
To verify this hypothesis and find the influence of the density on this model, a data analysis
of the density and model can be made. In order to factor out the embedment depth and
diameter of the withdrawal test, the withdrawal strength parameter is used as the reference
for performance. Furthermore, the withdrawal strength parameters are independent of the

8.2.2
Influence of pitch and thread height on the withdrawal capacity
To gain a deeper understanding of thread performance in timber, the thread height and
pitch are analyzed using the same method as in the previous analyses. Both Sandhass [6]
and Hoelz [10] investigated the influence of pitch on withdrawal capacity. While Sandhass
found minimal or no correlation between pitch and withdrawal strength parameter, Hoelz
found that a smaller pitch and larger thread height had an influence on the withdrawal
strength parameter.
Since thread height, pitch, and diameter are related, developing a model that incorporates
all three geometric factors is challenging. Changes in one of the parameters often result
in changes in the others, making it difficult to isolate their individual effects. However, if
either of the geometric parameters—thread height or pitch—proves to have a dominant
influence on withdrawal capacity, it may be possible to refine the simple model to account
for that influence. If one of these geometric features has a more significant impact on
withdrawal capacity than the others, it could make it easier to create a simpler, more
accurate model.
If the main geometric effects are properly accounted for by the adjustment to the model,
the geometrical unknown influences would be diminished in the kscrew-factors, making
it, in principle, possible to isolate the effects of the point geometry by transferring the
kscrew-factors from the LVL model to the ST/GLT model. This would be successful as long
as it is assumed that the geometric influences stay consistent between the two materials. If
successful, this would be a significant step toward generalizing the influence of the point
type.
Furthermore, an optimization regarding identifying unknown influences alone in the
ST/GLT model could also enable the ability to isolate the point influences. The models
used for this analysis utilize the same set of exponents and reference values as previously
mentioned. Furthermore, the CoV of the models remains the same.

8.4.1
Alternative methods for finding the influence of the point design
Since thread and point performance and the influence of timber properties do not correlate
between the models, alternative methods for determining the impact of point design
may have been preferable. The experimental method used in this project did not yield
optimal results, highlighting the need for alternative approaches. This section describes
two experimental methods that would likely provide a more effective analysis.
Screws Inserted in Timber with the point not embedded
The first method for assessing the influence of the point type is similar to the approach
used in this project. However, instead of using LVL as the test specimen for screws with
the point not embedded in timber, using only ST and/or GLT would have likely produced
more reliable results. This approach would retain the same experimental setup but replace
LVL with ST or GLT, requiring thinner cuts of these materials, as the cross-section of the
specimen was approximately 200 mm.

estimate the effective length of the screw, as shown in Equations 8.3 and 8.4. Westermayr

If the withdrawal strength parameters obtained from the experiments are plotted against
embedment depth, a sloped trend line indicates that embedment depth influences the

quantifies the extent to which the embedment depth needs to be corrected, following the

will increase. However, after applying this correction, the values will align, causing the
trend line to become horizontal, since embedment depth is not linearly proportional to the
withdrawal capacity of the screw.

to quantify the influence of the screw point, demonstrating its effectiveness in assessing
embedment depth related influences on the withdrawal capacity. Similarly, Sandhaas [6]
utilized this method to determine the effective length of screws, subtracting the point length
from the embedment depth. However, rather than using the slope of a linear regression
function, she determined the effective length by directly measuring the screw, finding
an effective point length of 1d. This approach yielded similar results, leading to a more
horizontal withdrawal strength parameter curve, as described in Chapter 2. Sandhaas [6]
did, however, also find that plotting multiple types of screw and density combinations as a
function of the embedded depth did not yield an increasing withdrawal strength parameter.
She mentioned that this could be caused by statistical anomalies or that the many screws
and densities plotted could have been the reason for the negative slope.

9
Conclusion
This project aimed to investigate the influence of screw point and thread design on the
withdrawal capacity of screws in timber. A total of 18 different screws were tested across 11
timber specimens, resulting in 198 tests, two of which were identified as anomalies. Three
types of timber were used: Laminated Veneer Lumber (LVL), Structural Timber (ST), and
Glued-Laminated Timber (GLT). The LVL specimens were specifically used for tests where
the screw point was not embedded in the timber, isolating the effect of the thread design.
In total, three LVL specimens were tested, accounting for 54 tests. Four ST and four GLT
specimens were tested, each contributing 72 tests. All screws were subjected to pull-out
tests, and the results were analyzed to calibrate multiple models. The calibration process
incorporated screw-specific, point-specific, and timber-specific parameters to optimize the
model by minimizing the coefficient of variation (CoV).
9.1
Summary of key findings
9.1.1
Influence of diameter and density
The two original models were based on the parameters diameter and density. The diameter
did not have a significant influence on the withdrawal capacity in ST and GLT. Test
results and model calibration showed that the optimal cd value for the ST/GLT model
was approximately -0.2, indicating a smaller influence from diameter compared to values
reported in the literature. However, applying a value of -0.3, as suggested by the literature,
did not result in a significant change in CoV.
In contrast, diameter had a substantial influence in the LVL model, with an optimal cd value
of approximately -0.60. Here, diameter was the governing factor affecting the withdrawal
strength parameter, as larger diameters led to a significant decrease in withdrawal strength.
Model calibration and discussion revealed that density influenced the withdrawal capacity
in the ST and GLT models. The relationship between withdrawal strength and density
followed trends reported in the literature. However, the different ST and GLT specimens
exhibited significant performance variations despite having similar densities. This led to
considerable differences in the kwood factors, confirming the findings of J. Munch-Andersen
[20] regarding board-specific performance variations.
For screws in LVL, density had minimal influence on withdrawal capacity. No significant
change in CoV was observed when adjusting cρ in the model. Additionally, a correlation
between density and diameter was identified in the LVL experiment. An attempt was made
to correct this correlation by incorporating the weight loss due to screw removal into the
density calculation. However, this adjustment did not reduce the correlation. Nevertheless,
it did align the mean density of the samples more closely with the mean density of the
boards.
9.1.2
Influence of pitch
Pitch had minimal influence on the withdrawal capacity of screws in timber, as observed in
both models. While some correlation between pitch and the withdrawal strength parameter
was identified, its impact was not substantial, as optimizing for pitch did not result in a
significant change in CoV. However, pitch was incorporated into the alternative model to
minimize uncertainties related to the threaded portion of the screw, with the objective of
isolating and determining the influence of point design.

method did not provide an optimal design basis.

9.2
Contribution to the field
This study demonstrated that while traditional parameters for calculating withdrawal
capacity, such as diameter and density, remain dominant, screw point design should not be
entirely neglected. By accounting for differences in point type in design calculations, the
precision of withdrawal capacity predictions can be improved, enabling the selection of
optimal screws for specific connections.
Furthermore, this project highlighted that the mechanics of withdrawal failure vary de-
pending on the type of timber into which the screw is inserted. It was also found that
different parameters influence withdrawal capacity to varying degrees: diameter had a
significant impact in LVL, while density was the dominant factor in ST and GLT.
9.3
Future work
As discussed in Chapter 8, the method used in this study did not yield optimal results
for determining the influence of screw point design. Since timber properties and thread
performance could not be directly transferred between models, the chosen method was not
ideal. Alternative experimental methods for assessing screw point influence may have been
more effective.
The most optimal method would likely involve a combination of inserting screws without
embedding the point and testing multiple embedment depths in similar timber types.

This approach would produce a dataset containing information on both thread and point
influence at varying embedment depths, providing a stronger foundation for understanding
the impact of screw geometry. Additionally, it is expected that a more representative
effective point length could be estimated using this method.
A more carefully selected set of screw specimens would also have been beneficial, as some
point types were represented by only a single screw type and one diameter size. A more
comprehensive specimen selection could have improved the representation of point influence.
For a project of this size, selecting three or four distinct point types, each represented
by both 8 mm and 10 mm screws and sourced from different manufacturers, would have
provided a broader perspective on the interaction between point and thread design.

The model used to determine withdrawal capacity is based on Annex D in DS/EN 1990:2023
[5]. Due to the experimental nature of this thesis, data is collected and analyzed to derive
a suitable model. Annex D.8.2 in DS/EN 1990:2023 outlines a statistical procedure for
developing resistance models. The statistical determination of the resistance model is based
on data collected during the experimental phase, with a total of 198 tests conducted. The
tests and test combinations are further described in Chapter 5. According to the Annex,
the following assumptions must be met for the procedure to be applicable:
• The resistance function is a function of a number of independent variables X.
• A sufficient number of test results is available.
• All relevant geometrical and material properties are measured.
• There is no correlation (statistical dependence) between the variables in the resistance
function.
• All variables follow either a Normal or Log-Normal distribution.
Once these assumptions are satisfied, the following steps are followed to create the model
for determining the axial resistance of screws in timber inserted with a screw-axis to grain
angle of 90◦.

To analyze compatibility, the resistance function is assessed, and any scatter within the

with experimental data. Scatter can be reduced by:

• Modifying b and Vδ by dividing the total test population into appropriate subsets
where the influence of additional parameters can be considered constant.
As previously mentioned, many parameters can influence the withdrawal capacity of
screws, but not all are included in the current model. Testing whether including more
parameters improves accuracy is essential, as large variations in results may indicate
additional influencing factors. For instance, parameters such as the ratio between inner
and outer diameter and various thread geometrical parameters, as described in Hoelz et
al. [27], may affect withdrawal capacity. This underscores the importance of gathering
information about screw geometry to decide which parameters to include or exclude.

C.1.1
Introduction
Pirnbacher, Brandner, and Schickhofer [9] investigate key factors that influence the axial
withdrawal strength of self-tapping screws in timber. Recognizing the limits of current
standards (EN 1382:1999, EN 1995-1-1, DIN 1052), which focus primarily on density, diam-
eter, and length, the study aims to enhance understanding of additional parameters such as
moisture content, temperature, screw diameter, slenderness ratio, thread embedment, angle
between the screw axis and grain, and pre-drilling effects. This comprehensive approach
seeks to provide more nuanced insights applicable to real-world construction settings.
C.1.2
Scope and methodology
A rigorous test program involved 5,524 withdrawal tests on Sitka spruce (classified as
C24 for solid timber and GL28h for GLT). The tests were carried out in matched sample
groups, ensuring homogeneity in density by organizing specimens by weight. This enabled
direct comparisons across varied conditions in moisture, temperature, and screw geometries,
isolating each parameter’s effects on withdrawal resistance.
Each screw was tested in both parallel and perpendicular orientations relative to the timber
grain. Screws from WÜRTH (ASSY II, partially threaded) and SPAX-S were selected
for their ability to maintain effective length without introducing additional experimental
variables. Screws ranged from 8 mm to 12 mm in diameter, and tests were performed using
the Proceq Z-25FS and LIGNUM-UNI-275 rigs, allowing for maximum loads of 25 kN and
275 kN, respectively.

C.2
Summary of article 2: Influence of thread parameters
on the withdrawal capacity of timber screws
C.2.1
Introduction
Hoelz et al. [10] investigate how the shape and dimensions of screw threads affect the
withdrawal capacity, or grip strength, of timber screws. The study looks specifically at
four main features of the threads: height, spacing between ridges (pitch), the angle of the
thread as it wraps around the screw (lead angle), and the angle between each thread ridge.
The aim is to determine which configurations provide the best grip in timber, with tests
carefully set up to account for the timber’s grain direction.
C.2.2
Methodology
This study uses spruce timber of the C24 classification, with tests following the EN 1382
guidelines. Instead of commercial screws, custom “threaded test objects” allowed for
precise control over the shape of the threads. Each of the four thread features was tested
at two levels—thread height (1.0 mm and 1.48 mm), spacing (3.04 mm and 6.08 mm),
lead angle (6.8◦and 13.6◦), and ridge angle (30◦and 55◦). Tests were done on timber
in two orientations (RT and TL planes) relative to the timber grain, and an analysis
was conducted to see how each feature and combination of features impacted withdrawal
strength.
C.2.3
Key results and observations
• Thread height:
A greater thread height (1.48 mm) resulted in better withdrawal strength in both
timber orientations, with a 14% improvement in RT and a 19% increase in TL. These
results suggest that taller threads improve the screw’s hold in timber.
• Pitch:
Closer spacing between thread ridges (3.04 mm) increased grip strength, raising
capacity by about 9% in RT and 14% in TL. This closer spacing seems to improve
how well the threads connect with timber fibers.
• Thread angle:
A sharper thread angle (30◦) also improved withdrawal strength by about 4% in both
planes. The RT orientation showed a stronger effect, while TL showed a consistent
but less intense benefit, suggesting that sharper angles may help increase screw grip.
• Lead angle:
In RT, a higher lead angle (13.6◦) slightly improved grip (+4%), though no effect
was observed in TL. This indicates that lead angle may work differently depending
on the direction of the timber grain.

Interactions and statistical analysis
Further analysis showed some interactions between thread features that impact grip

• Thread angle and thread height:

sharper angle gave higher strength. This shows that balancing thread height with
the angle can help maximize the screw’s hold.
• Thread angle and lead angle:
A sharper thread angle with a smaller lead angle improved grip in RT but not in TL,
showing that timber grain orientation is important when optimizing these thread
features.
C.2.5
Conclusion
The study shows that thread height, spacing, and angle are important for optimizing screw
withdrawal capacity. The findings suggest that customized thread designs can improve the
interaction between screws and timber, which can be helpful for both design improvements
and calculations. The study suggests a combined model for both timber orientations (RT
and TL), since the effects of thread parameters were consistent across them, making it
simpler to design screws that work well regardless of timber grain direction.

Appendix F - Data acquisition
The data acquisition software used for the experiments was Instrons In-House software
”Bluehill Universal”. The software was connected to the load-cell which was connected to
the machine to record tensional forces. Before testing could begin the software required a
method to be created in order to achieve the correct tensional forces of Fmax at the time
of 60±15 seconds. The following segment describes the method configuration used for data
acquisition. The method creation tab can be seen in Figure F.1, with the different tabs
where different settings can be changed.
Figure F.1: Data Acquisition software [30]
The software is automatically set to record the time, tensional force and displacement. It
is therefore necessary to configure the software to record other information, which in the
case of this study was the maximum recorded force. The most important configuration tab
in the software is the ”Test control”. In this tab it is possible to control different criteria
for the test phases, pre-test, test and end of test.
”Pre-test”
The software will start record the measured values at a ”starting force” set by the user. It
is therefore possible first start recording the data when the timber is tightly placed in the
braces. This results in a cleaner data curve, due to the timber not moving when tightening
against the braces. The software is set to move the load cell at a rate of 2 mm pr. minute
until the load reaches a value of 0.2 kN, which is the starting point of the test.
144
Axial resistance of screws in timber
==PAGE:159==
”Test”
To achieve a load of Fmax at the time of 60±15 seconds a maximum force Fmax was
estimated for every screw, with the following equation:
Fmax = fw · d · π · lw
(F.1)
Where fw = 6.4 MPa.
After maximum force was found, it was possible to configure the machine to apply the load
Fmax pr. minute, which should result in most of the tests being around one minute, which
is in accordance with DS/EN 1382:2016 [3].
”End of test”
Since the screw does not provide much resistance once Fmax has been achieved it was chosen
at first to stop the test when the screw only provided 90% of Fmax in resistance. However,
this resulted in the software crashing, meaning that the software had to be restarted. This
setting was therefore changed to be 30% drop from Fmax in order to resolve the issue of
the software crashing. The curves derived from the test will therefore stop when 70% of
Fmax is reached
In the console tab of the software it is possible to arrange the live display of the software.
This allows the user to choose what is shown when the test has been started. It was chosen
that the displacement, force, time and maximum force was shown in the live display. Under
the console tab, there is also the setting for the specimen protection feature. It is very
important to set the specimen protection setting, and starting the feature before every time
a screw has been inserted in the hook. The specimen protection feature sets a maximum
allowable force when using the manual controller for the machine. This means that when
this feature has been started, the console will stop working when there is a force of above
0.1 kN, so the the screw is not suddenly pulled out of the timber if the user is not attentive.
The software/testing machine had some issues with the sensitivity/gain of the machine,
this resulted in the machines electromechanical system started shaking, which meant that
the data collected had an uneven curve for the displacement-force diagrams. This was
corrected by changing the gain to a lower setting.
