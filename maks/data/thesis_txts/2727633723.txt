Abstract
This master thesis mainly analyzes cyber security penetration testing tools and evalu-
ates their effectiveness, benefits, limitations, and usability. A diverse range of tools was
selected based on criteria such as functionality and popularity. The pentesting tools
were categorized and evaluated based on their strengths, weaknesses, cost, scanning
techniques, speed, supported protocols, interface, compatible operating systems, output
formats, extra features, and use diﬀiculty. Additionally, this thesis briefly introduces
penetration testing and the role of pen-testers in cybersecurity firms. It also covers the
most critical vulnerabilities and attacks currently in existence, along with some helpful
advice for mitigation. The objective of this thesis is to aid cybersecurity experts and
pen-testers in choosing the right tool for specific attacks and procedures during an as-
sessment while also familiarizing them with different techniques and tools available.
Search Keywords: Penetration test, pen-test, pen-testers, penetration testers, cy-
bersecurity, penetration testing tools

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In today’s technologically advanced world, where cyber threats constantly evolve and
cyber-attacks occur more often than before, individuals and organizations face notable
challenges in protecting their systems. Targeted attacks aimed at breaching or compro-
mising systems are common, with numerous attempts made every day. The intentions
behind them range from stealing sensitive data to achieving financial gain, fulfilling per-
sonal vendettas, and even for fun.
Thus, penetration testing has become necessary for organizations in order to secure
their systems and networks from malicious actors by identifying vulnerabilities and eval-
uating the effectiveness of their security controls.
Consequently, for the penetration
assessments to be successful, the right penetration tools need to be selected and the ap-
propriate tests to be made. However, choosing the proper tools for each testing scenario
can be tough for cybersecurity experts and beginners in the field, since there are many
tools with different features and abilities to choose from.
The objective of this thesis is to conduct an in-depth analysis of cyber security pen-
etration testing tools. This analysis will assess their characteristics, advantages, disad-
vantages, and ease of use by comparing their features. The aim is to assist pen-testers
in choosing the most suitable tool by providing a comprehensive guide.
This thesis provides an in-depth analysis of cyber security penetration testing tools.
The topic of penetration testing is examined in light of existing literature and research,
with different tools compared and evaluated in terms of their characteristics, strengths,
weaknesses, and ease of use by comparing their features. Additionally, in the first chap-
ters, there is a brief introduction to penetration testing, the steps involved in a typical
penetration test, the pen-testers roles and types, and some of the most commonly found
vulnerabilities that exist today. The aim is to assist pen-testers in choosing the most
suitable tool by providing a comprehensive guide.

Due to the massive number of cyber attacks, penetration testing consultants are needed
more than ever to conduct penetration tests on computer systems to discover vulner-
abilities and identify any weak spots within a system’s defenses that attackers might

2.1
Methodology
The following methodology was used to research and write about cyber security pen-
etration testing tools. As this is an industrial thesis, first, practical information was
gathered from the company regarding the necessary steps to be followed in an assign-
ment, the most competent tools to be used, and the attack methods used by pen-testers
to perform a real test case scenario.
Then, a thorough search was performed on the web to find scientific literature, publica-
tions, and books that address related topics to this and could provide valuable insights
for the thesis. In continuation of this step, research questions were formulated to focus
on the specific subject. The following research questions were included:
Research Questions
• What is penetration testing?
• What do penetration testers do?
• What are the stages, types, and approaches involved in pen testing?
• What types of attacks and vulnerabilities exist, and how can they be performed
and mitigated?
• What are the different categories of pentesting tools?
• What are the most widely known and most commonly used tools?
• What is the appropriate category for each tool?
• What are their advantages and disadvantages, and what additional features do
they offer?
• Are they compatible with all the operation systems?
• Are they open source?
• Are they easy to learn and use?
The next stage was to answer all the general questions before focusing solely on the tools.
Further, more research was made to investigate each tool specifically and find all their
features and characteristics in order to be compared. Unfortunately, not much scientific
material could be found on all the tools since most of them were open source and still
in construction. Thus, an additional investigation was made on their web pages to find
all the needed information.

CHAPTER 3
Penetration Testing
Penetration testing, also known as pen testing, is the process of simulating a cyberattack
on a computer system to uncover any vulnerabilities. A penetration test is beneficial for
a company to determine its protection and defense mechanisms against an actual cyber
attack. Furthermore, many compliance requirements now demand penetration testing,
which is why many businesses conduct penetration tests. [62]
This chapter emphasizes the importance of penetration testing, as it explains its mean-
ing, test stages, types, and approaches. Additionally, it describes the role of penetration
testers and their types.
3.1
Penetration testers role
The term pen-tester, which stands for penetration tester, refers to a cybersecurity profes-
sional who conducts controlled attacks on computer systems, networks, or applications
in order to discover and exploit vulnerabilities in their system’s security defenses before
the attackers do. [3]
Several stages are involved in the pentesting process, including reconnaissance, vulnera-
bility scanning, exploitation, and analysis following exploitation. During an assignment,
the pen-testers use different methods and tools to simulate realistic attacks in order to
identify security weaknesses and provide recommendations for resolving them.
After the assignment has been completed and potential vulnerabilities have been iden-
tified, pen-testers typically document their findings in a report that describes each vul-
nerability, the level of their risk (informational, low, medium, or high) in case they were
exploited, and recommendations for mitigating them. The report is then handed out to
the interested competent organization’s security team.

3.2
Pen-testing Stages
Penetration testing has become a key part of preventing modern-day cyber security
attacks. While objectives and types of penetration testing vary, there are several key
steps that need to be followed in the testing process. The different stages that a pen-
tester should follow during the test are as follows: [75], [30], [27], [64]
Figure 3.1: Pen-testing Stages.
1. Reconnaissance: As part of the project’s first stage, an agreement will be signed
that will serve as the basis for all projects and provide mutual legal protection.
Additionally, the agreement will indicate the specific engagement team, the exact
date, and the test duration. Furthermore, it will specify the escalation path, as
well as the scope, approach, and methodology agreed upon.
2. Scanning: Based on the information gathered during the reconnaissance stage,
the pen-tester can now plan how to perform the test by drafting a list with testing
tools, methodologies, and publicly available exploits that will be useful. Following
this, the pen-tester will scan the application for vulnerabilities using these tools.
3. Exploiting: Following the identification of vulnerabilities in the previous phase,
a pen-tester will attempt to verify the validity of these vulnerabilities by exploiting
them. This step will only be performed at the customer’s request and only after
having obtained formal authorization. The advantage of undertaking this partic-
ular step is that it allows for validating the existence of a given vulnerability. In
continuation of the previous task, the pen-tester will attempt to gain access to the
information contained within the protected network segments. If the attempt is

successful and access inside the system has been accomplished, the customer will
receive evidence of the penetration test.
4. Reporting: After conducting the security assessment and reviewing all the test
cases included in the scope of work, the assigned tester will present the client with a
written report. This report will detail any critical issues identified and recommend
solutions to address them, ensuring that the client is fully informed.
3.3
Types of Penetration Testing
Penetration testing is categorized into various types, depending on its scope and focus.
The commonly known types of penetration testing are network, web application, and
mobile application penetration testing.
Other types include wireless network, social
engineering, and physical security penetration testing.
Identifying vulnerabilities in
these areas can help organizations understand their overall security status and take
necessary measures to fix any identified weaknesses. [64] More analytically:
• Network Penetration Testing: By using this technique, a pen-tester can iden-
tify security vulnerabilities in the target organization’s network during its design,
implementation, and operation. In order to gain access to the target’s network,
the pen-tester analyses and checks various components such as modems, remote
access devices, and other connections. [64]
• Application Penetration Testing: In this type of testing, the pen tester targets
all the applications owned by the test, concentrating primarily on web applications,
as these remain comparatively more vulnerable to security threats. By highlighting
the risks posed by actual exploitable vulnerabilities, pen-testers aim to evaluate
the effectiveness of an application’s security controls.
In practice, a pen-tester
simulates the actions of a real attacker and targets web applications in order to
identify vulnerabilities that may lead to unauthorized access or the loss of data if
they are present. [64]
• Social Engineering: Social engineering involves auditing the workflow of the
target organization by using human interactions in order to gain access to details
about the target and its components, which are otherwise supposed to be kept
confidential. [64], [71]
• Physical Penetration Testing: During physical penetration testing, a pen tester
simulates a real-world threat by attempting to compromise physical barriers to
gain access to a company’s infrastructure, buildings, systems, or employees. It is
the primary objective of a physical penetration test to identify weaknesses and
vulnerabilities in physical controls (locks, barriers, cameras, or sensors) so that
they can be addressed rapidly. In order to strengthen the physical security posture,
proper mitigation should be implemented after identifying these weaknesses. [3]

3.4
Pen-testing Approaches
Figure 3.2: Black - White - Gray Box [37].
In the field of penetration testing, there are three types commonly used. These include:
[64], [30], [42]
White Box
In white box penetration testing, the tester has access to the entire system, includ-
ing credentials, source code, infrastructure maps, and everything needed to conduct the
attack. It involves simulating an attack from an internal perspective, with full knowledge
of the system being tested. This approach can help identify vulnerabilities that could
be exploited by insiders or for verifying the effectiveness of internal security controls.
Black Box
Black box penetration testing, or external penetration testing, involves simulating an
attack from an external perspective without prior knowledge of the system being tested.
This approach can be useful for identifying vulnerabilities that an external attacker
might exploit.
Gray Box
In gray box penetration testing, the tester obtains low-level credentials, network maps,
and logical flow charts of a network to simulate an attack with limited knowledge of
the system. In order to conduct the testing, a basic understanding of the application,

system, and network is required. This approach is advantageous, as some vulnerabilities
can only be discovered by inspecting the source code, while similar vulnerabilities may
go undetected by black box penetration testing.
Overall, penetration testing methods are chosen based on the specific objectives of each
assignment and the resources available to each security team. A successful penetration
test can provide significant insights into an organization’s security posture and pinpoint
potential vulnerabilities before they can be exploited by attackers.
3.5
Hackers by type
There are six category types of hackers, the white, black, gray, green, red, and blue hats,
each with its own characteristics, which are explained in detail below. [58], [65]
White Hat
White hat hackers are ethical hackers or cybersecurity professionals who use their hack-
ing skills to protect organizations. They perform hacking simulations from a defensive
perspective for organizations. As opposed to other types of hacking, this type of hacker
conducts its assessments exclusively with the company’s explicit permission for security
purposes. [56], [58], [65]
Black Hat
Hackers, referred to as Black Hats, are those who use their skills illegally or maliciously.
They breach or violate remote computers’ system integrity. After gaining unauthorized
access, black hat hackers can destroy vital data, deprive legitimate users of service, es-
calate user privileges, and cause problems for their targets. [58], [65]
Gray Hat
This type of hacker is somewhere between white and black hats and may act offensively
or defensively based on the situation. It is important to note that gray hat hackers
generally do not damage businesses; thus, they hack into things to see if it is possible
and then move on to another task or target. [58], [56], [65]
Green Hat
In the hacking community, green hat hackers are considered new to the field, with little
understanding of security mechanisms. However, they tend to be highly motivated to
learn and eager to advance in their position. A subset of green hat hackers are indi-
viduals known as script kiddies. These individuals utilize pre-existing scripts to attack
systems. This motivation can manifest itself in positive or negative methods depending
on the path they choose to take. Green hat hackers focus more on learning to hack
themselves and develop their tools. [46]

Red Hat
This red hat hacker is often a security professional who simulates attacks on an orga-
nization’s systems, applications, and networks in order to identify vulnerabilities and
weaknesses. The red team’s goal is to simulate the tactics, techniques, and procedures
of real-world attackers and to provide valuable feedback and insights to the organization
on how to improve its security posture. [48]
Blue Hat
The blue hats are a group of security professionals who defend an organization’s sys-
tems, applications, and networks from attacks. Blue teams work to detect and respond

CHAPTER 4
Vulnerabilities and Attacks
Cybersecurity contains two forms of attacks the passive and the active.
In passive
attacks, attackers seek to intercept data and sensitive information that flows through a
network. They do so discreetly, without altering or disrupting the system’s operation,
by eavesdropping on network traﬀic and monitoring user activities. Contrarily, active
attacks involve the same actions, but with one distinction, that attackers can manipulate
or disrupt the transmission process. [30] Below are described the most common attacks
and vulnerabilities, as well as their mitigation schemes.
4.0.1
Password Cracking
Password hacking is the most common method of gaining unauthorized access to a
system’s data and applications. Passwords are usually stored in an encrypted format
generated through a hash function.
Attackers can use brute force attacks or other
techniques to uncover hashed passwords. [51] Furthermore, individuals often use the
same password across multiple platforms, and they also tend to choose easy passwords,
so they can memorize them, such as important dates, places, etc., making it easier for
hackers to guess it and compromise multiple accounts simultaneously. [76] Organizations
can mitigate this risk by increasing password complexity, enforcing regular password
modifications, implementing multi-factor authentication, and educating their staff on
safe password practices. [31]
4.0.2
Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS)
A Distributed Denial of Service attack involves flooding a specific system or network
with many requests from numerous sources, causing it to be inaccessible to users. [51]
It may also attempt to disrupt connections between two machines, particular systems,
or individuals and prevent access to a service. [68]
More analytically, a DDoS attack overwhelms a target system with traﬀic originating
from a network of compromised computers, called botnet. The botnet’s controller then
sends a number of requests simultaneously to the target, which exhausts its resources
and leads to system failure. DDoS attacks can pose a significant threat to organizations,
especially those heavily reliant on their online services, as they can cause financial dam-
age and disrupt normal operations. In some cases, DDoS attacks are used as a diversion
tactic to conceal other types of cyber attacks. [59]

To be protected against this kind of attack, companies can take different measures, in-
cluding implementing network security tools like firewalls, intrusion detection systems,
and load balancers. Except from these, they can also do the following: [68]
• Filter Routers: By blocking and filtering packets entering and leaving the net-
work, it prevents attacks from neighboring networks and prevents the network
itself from becoming an unaware target.
• Disable IP Broadcasts: As a result of disabling IP broadcasts, host computers
can no longer be used as amplifiers for ICMP flood attacks and smurf attacks 1.
• Apply Security Patches: Security patches and techniques must be updated
regularly on host computers to prevent denial of service attacks.
• Disable Unused Services: Disabling network services that are no longer needed
or unused will prevent attacks.
• Perform Network Monitoring: By monitoring traﬀic patterns, a network can
determine when it is being attacked and take appropriate steps to defend itself; by
inspecting host systems, a host can also prevent itself from being used as a means
of attack.
4.0.3
Man-in-the-Middle (MITM)
This attack takes place when someone establishes a connection with a server or service,
allowing them to either passively listen to a communication between two parties or ac-
tively change the transmitted data. [31] Essentially, that intruder deceives both parties
into believing they are communicating directly with each other while someone else is in
the middle controlling the communication; that is what the actual attack’s name refers
to. [51]
An MITM attack can occur in two ways, either by the attacker creating fake networks
or by manipulating the connection between the victim and a legitimate network. The
first one is commonly used to target people using public WiFi, while the second method
is more complex, involving the attacker penetrating an unsecured connection between
the victim and themselves and creating a secured connection to the genuine network,
which is harder to detect. [31] Additionally, a MITM attack can also be in the form
of a phishing email that appears to be authentic and is sent to a user with a URL link
pointed toward the fake site instead of the actual one.

With this attack, hackers usually aim to steal confidential information like passwords,

4.0.4
Cross-Site Scripting (XSS)
An XSS attack occurs when malicious JavaScript lines of code are injected into an ap-
plication that is vulnerable. Cross-site scripting vulnerabilities enable an attacker to
compromise a user’s account and perform actions that they usually do. For example,
the intruder might be able to steal personal data or modify the content of the web page,
and in certain cases where the victim has privileged access rights, then the attacker can
control the whole platform or page and, in worst-case scenarios, even delete it. [49]
The XSS attacks can be categorized by their type as follows: [49] , [71]
• Reflected XSS: Non-persistent attacks, also known as Reflected XSS attacks,
happen when malicious scripts are inserted into HTTP query parameters on a web
page and reflect back from a web server to the victim’s browser. These links can
be sent through email messages or found on other websites, so when a user clicks
on them, the injected code travels to the vulnerable website, which reflects it back
to the user’s browser.
• Stored XSS: Persistent attacks, or Stored XSS, are the ones where the injected
scripts are stored on the target servers, such as in a database, and they are executed
in the user’s browser upon accessing the vulnerable page.
• DOM-based XSS: A DOM-based XSS (Document Object Model) attack is pos-
sible if the web application inserts data into the Document Object Model without
proper sanitization. Thus an attacker can manipulate this data to include harmful
scripts on the web page. Consequently, when these scripts are executed, they can
access various properties of the page and alter their values.
To prevent XSS attacks, it is recommended to take precautions such as sanitizing user
input, encoding output, and following secure coding practices.

4.0.5
Phishing
Phishing attacks intend to deceive individuals into providing sensitive information to
malicious actors, like passwords or other personal details, without realizing it. [51] These
attacks always involve fraudulent emails that appear to come from reputable sources like
banks or well-known websites and contain fake links that mimic real ones. [10] To protect
from these attacks, individuals should avoid opening suspicious emails and clicking on
enclosed links, and during online transactions, always check for the ”https” in the URL
and the secure sign on the browser status bar. Additionally, organizations should educate
their employees on recognizing phishing emails to prevent any harm. [7]
4.0.6
SQL Injection
Structured Query Language, commonly known as SQL, is a programming language used
to manage and manipulate database data; with SQL injection to be a dangerous security
vulnerability to web applications as it enables attackers to execute unauthorized SQL
queries on the database server, which can result in sensitive information being compro-
mised, modified, or even deleted. [50], [71]
According to CWE/SANS’s most recent Top 25 Most Dangerous Software Errors re-
port, SQL injection is considered a high-risk vulnerability, which has caused many data
breaches in recent years. [61] Moreover, another reason why it is extremely dangerous
is that an attacker can obtain a constant backdoor into an organization’s systems and
remain unnoticed for a long time.
4.0.7
OS Command Injection
OS Command Injection, or shell injection, occurs when an application fails to properly
sanitize user input, enabling an attacker to execute operating system commands.[2] This
attack can result in the unauthorized execution of commands, data theft, unauthorized
access, or even a total takeover of the targeted system. In order to prevent OS Command
Injection attacks, input validation and sanitization must be performed, and user-supplied
input should be limited when constructing operating system commands. [51]
4.0.8
Directory Traversal
Directory traversal, also referred to as path traversal, is a web application vulnerability
that provides an attacker with access to files and directories outside of the intended hi-
erarchy of the directory. Typically, this vulnerability occurs when the application fails
to validate user input properly, usually via file and resource names passed in URLs or
user-supplied parameters.

Network Scanning and Enumeration
Network scanning involves using various tools and techniques to discover live hosts, open
ports, and services running on hosts within a network. It helps to map the network’s
infrastructure, providing information about its architecture, IP addresses, and network
devices. [9]
Enumeration focuses on gathering more detailed information about the services and
systems discovered during the scanning phase. It involves probing the open ports and
services to identify their versions, configurations, and potential vulnerabilities. Enumer-
ation techniques include banner grabbing, service identification, user enumeration, and
directory enumeration.
The objective of network scanning and enumeration is to gather as much data as possible
regarding the target systems. This includes details such as usernames, shared resources,
system configurations, and potential entry points.

Nmap, also known as Network Mapper, is a freely available open-
source tool for conducting automated security audits and scanning
computer networks.[43] It is compatible with all the major opera-
tions systems and is designed to identify hosts and services on com-
puter networks by sending packets and analyzing their responses.
[63]
To gather information about target systems, Nmap uses both TCP and UDP scanning
methods to investigate targets, determine open ports, identify running services, and fin-
gerprint operating systems. [13]In addition to its flexibility and customization, Nmap
offers a wide range of options for users to customize their scans, including specifying
scan types, timing options, and target specifications. Security professionals are able to
adapt their scans to meet specific test requirements as a result of this flexibility. [5]
Furthermore, Nmap offers scripting capabilities through the Nmap Scripting Engine
(NSE). The Nmap Scripting Language (NSL) repository contains an extensive collec-
tion of pre-existing scripts as well as custom scripts that allow users to expand its
functionality. [66]

5.2
Vulnerability Assessment and Scanning
Vulnerability scanning utilizes automated tools to search for known vulnerabilities in the
target system or network. [5] These tools compare the system’s incorrect configurations,
weak encryption, default login credentials, installed software, and services to a database
of known vulnerabilities.
Vulnerability assessment involves performing an analysis of the identified vulnerabili-
ties. Each vulnerability is thoroughly evaluated during this process to determine its
severity and potential impact. This assessment may require manual verification, as well
as further testing and analysis to confirm the existence and level of danger posed by the
vulnerabilities.
The purpose of vulnerability assessment and scanning is to give organizations a bet-
ter understanding of their security status and pinpoint areas that require attention.
By recognizing weaknesses, pen-testers can provide more accurate recommendations for
reinforcing an organization’s systems and networks to improve its security.

networks, and applications for known vulnerabilities and poten-
tial security issues that attackers can exploit. It can identify
vulnerabilities in targets, like servers, network devices, operat-
ing systems, web applications, and databases. [5]
Further, Nessus has a database of known vulnerabilities and utilizes scanning techniques
like remote and local scanning to detect them. Then reports the identified vulnerabilities,
listed in order by their severity levels, and provides suggestions for fixing them. [35] It
also supports multiple compliance standards like PCI DSS that help organizations meet
regulatory requirements.[13]

Reverse engineering and binary analysis in pentesting involve dissecting and analyzing
software or firmware to understand its inner workings, vulnerabilities, and potential at-

Reverse engineering techniques can be applied to proprietary software, malware samples,
or embedded systems. Through reverse engineering, pen-testers can uncover valuable in-
formation, including encryption algorithms, protocols, authentication mechanisms, and
other hidden features.
Binary analysis focuses on analyzing binary code to understand its behavior, identify
security flaws, and assess potential risks. This can involve disassembling, decompiling,
or debugging the binary code.
Usually, pen-testers use these techniques to uncover
vulnerabilities and analyze the logic flow.

in hardware components, firmware, communication protocols, and overall security archi-
tecture. This type of testing includes analyzing firmware, evaluating wireless protocols,
and assessing physical security measures.
Organizations can enhance the security of their hardware and IoT deployments and
minimize the risk of unauthorized access, data breaches, or device manipulation by con-
ducting Hardware and IoT Testing.

Social Engineering
Social engineering is the act of deceiving individuals into performing actions or revealing
confidential information that can be useful for harmful purposes. pen-testers simulate
real-life social engineering scenarios by using human psychology, trust, and manipula-

Social engineering approaches can appear in multiple forms, with some of them being
phishing emails, phone calls, pretexting, impersonation, or physical interactions. The
overall objective of this assessment is to evaluate an organization’s exposure to attacks,
discover potential security weaknesses, and create employee awareness. [3]

Physical Security Testing
Physical Security Testing evaluates an organization’s physical security level by inspecting

Discussion and Future Work
Following the descriptions of each tool and the comparisons of their specific attributes
and characteristics in the preceding section, this chapter will delve deeper into their
details and the general results found, and also share my personal opinion on which tool
is more preferable when performing a penetration test.
Cybersecurity has an extensive study field that constantly evolves, containing various
pentesting tools and methodologies. Although every security firm uses its own set of
practices and tools, this thesis focused on identifying and analyzing the most commonly
used tools that are widely accepted.
The pentesting tools have been categorized previously based on their functions and
the type of test they can be used for. In this section are going to be discussed the cat-
egories of Network Scanning and Enumeration, Vulnerability Assessment and Scanning,
Web Application Testing, Wireless Network Testing, Password Cracking, Exploitation
and Frameworks, Reconnaissance and Information Gathering, Wireless Network Audit-
ing, Social Engineering, and Physical Security Testing. This categorization was done to
group almost equivalent tools used for similar purposes.
Yet, it is essential to point out that the choice of tools depends on the scope of the
penetration test, the specific objectives, and the network environment a pen-tester is
assessing.
Discussions
Network Scanning and Enumeration Tools
The first set includes the tools Nmap, Netcat, Unicornscan, and Masscan. For a penetra-
tion test, Nmap is generally the recommended choice because it provides a wide range of
scanning techniques, OS detection capabilities, version detection, scripting engine, and
many reporting options, and it is also very flexible and has a broad feature set. Netcat,
Unicornscan, and Masscan are also good choices, but they are more focused on specific
aspects of network scanning and may not offer the same level of complexity as Nmap.
However, depending on the penetration test’s requirements, combining these tools for
specific scanning tasks that require speed or scalability may be beneficial.

Vulnerability Assessment and Scanning Tools
In the second group are the tools Nessus, OpenVAS, Qualys Cloud Platform, and Nex-
pose.
From these tools, the Qualys Cloud Platform is recommended since it is an
open-source tool that offers a wide range of features and capabilities and provides many
advanced scanning techniques, comprehensive reporting, integration options, and addi-
tional security features. However, for organizations with more extensive requirements
and budget flexibility, Qualys Cloud Platform and Nessus commercial editions provide
a broader set of features.
Web Application Testing Tools
Here are the Burp Suite, OWASP ZAP, Nikto, and Acunetix. Personally, I would always
choose Burp Suite since it stands out as a top choice due to its extensive feature set, flex-
ibility, and widespread adoption in the industry. It also offers a powerful combination
of manual and automated testing abilities, it has a library with additional features that
can be easily added to the main suite, and it can run multiple functions simultaneously.
For example, it can automatically perform a vulnerability scan on a web page while
the pen-tester tries brute force attacks on the login credentials. Additionally, it scans
a web page for all the types of vulnerabilities and attacks and lists them based on their
risk in case of exposure from an attacker. The only drawback is its price; since it is an
expensive tool for an individual, it offers a free edition; however, it is not as good as
the commercial one. About the rest of the tools, OWASP ZAP is a strong candidate
as well, especially for those seeking a free and open-source tool supported by OWASP;
however, at first, it can be a bit challenging to understand how to use it. Nikto can be
useful for quick vulnerability assessments, and Acunetix, being a commercial tool, offers
automated scanning with a focus on web application vulnerabilities.
Wireless Network Testing Tools
In this category are the Aircrack-ng, Reaver, Kismet, and Wireshark. Regarding wireless
network testing, the tools presented can have different uses, and the choice of the right
tool depends on the tasks required to be performed in the penetration test. For example,
for Wi-Fi network security assessments, Aircrack-ng is a valuable tool for capturing and
analyzing network traﬀic to crack WEP and WPA/WPA2 keys. Kismet and Wireshark
are good options for monitoring and analyzing wireless networks. Kismet has many help-
ful features; it supports multiple wireless protocols, offers powerful visualization tools,
and provides advanced filtering and search options. Wireshark is more adaptable as a
tool and can be used in various situations, such as troubleshooting network problems,
examining security threats, and can capture wireless traﬀic and other types of network
traﬀic. Lastly, Reaver is mainly designed to exploit the WPS vulnerability, which nowa-
days has been reduced due to the security improvements in modern routers.

This group of tools in this category are John the Ripper, Hydra, Hashcat, and Medusa.
For this set of tools, the ones I find more useful are John the Ripper and Hashcat. The
reason is that if the assignment focuses on cracking offline password hashes, John the

Here are Maltego, theHarvester, Recon-ng, and Shodan. I recommend utilizing all of

Wireless Network Auditing Tools
The wireless network auditing category includes Fern WiFi Cracker, Wifite, and Ghost
Phisher. The first two are very similar tools, focused on assessing the security of WiFi
networks and cracking wireless encryption keys. When deciding between them, it is
important to consider that Wifite is an automated tool, making the process easier; how-
ever, it can be challenging for less experienced users with the command line, as it must
be executed from there. Ghost Phisher is more beneficial for social engineering and
phishing attacks since it can create fake access points and perform DNS spoofing.

testing, the penetration testers must maintain a level of transparency to avoid detection

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
Penetration testing is one of the most common approaches for testing the security of
organizations, systems, and networks, with penetration testing tools playing a signifi-
cant role in a security assessment. Moreover, with the variety of penetration testing
tools available in both open-source and commercial versions, it can be challenging for
pentesters to find the most suitable tool for each type of assessment. This master thesis
examined the cyber security penetration testing tools, aiming to analyze their capabili-
ties, strengths, and limitations.
Firstly, this thesis aims to inform the readers about this security field by briefly illustrat-
ing penetration testing and the role and types of pentesters. Secondly, it enumerates
the most common vulnerabilities and attacks that can be found when performing a pen-
test and give some insights on what way can be dangerous and some advice on how to
mitigate them and secure a system before it becomes a target.
Additionally, as the main focus is on the pentesting tools, it presents an extensive list of
tools divided into almost identical types of assessments that can be used. These tools
have been explored and compared based on their functionalities, advantages, and limita-
tions. The advantage of this thesis is that the tools have been divided into comparison
tables, making it easier for the pentester to locate their differences and choose the most
suitable tool. Lastly, this thesis concludes with the discussions chapter, written from my
perspective, and analyzes the results presented in the comparison tables in the previous
section.
This thesis serves as a helpful guide for both experienced and novice pentesters, pro-
viding insight into the tasks and tools of the profession and their respective functional-
ities. It can also serve as a helpful starting point and reference for those interested in
researching and developing new tools that will better address and handle threats more
eﬀiciently and adaptively.
