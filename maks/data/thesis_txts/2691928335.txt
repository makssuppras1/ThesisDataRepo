 Abstract 
As a high-performance light metal, aluminum is often used in various fields, especially 
in the transportation and aerospace industry to produce parts with good strength-to-
weight ratio. Meanwhile, metal additive manufacturing techniques, also known as 3D 
printing, are receiving most attention from researchers and industrialists and are 
developing at a rapid pace to cope with the current industrial demands to utilize the 
unmatched capabilities that these advanced techniques have to offer. Among the many 
aluminum alloys, AlSi10Mg is receiving particular attention in the context of 3D 
printing, as it is relatively easy to process by laser applications due to the near-eutectic 
composition.  
The manufacturing of AlSi10Mg parts via selective laser melting – one of the most 
common metal additive manufacturing techniques – offers the opportunity to further 
improve the material specific strength by producing a very fine microstructure, which, 
on the other hand, is typically affected by low ductility and presence of residual stresses. 
Heat treatments have the potential to overcome these limitations by leading as-printed 
materials to new types of microstructures, whose mechanical behavior is, however, 
largely unknown.  
This thesis investigates the microstructure and micro-mechanical behavior of heat-
treated AlSi10Mg samples produced via selective laser melting.  
Three different heat treatment conditions, annealing at 520℃ for 5h, annealing at 520℃ 
for 5h plus ageing, and annealing at 520℃ for 48h are examined. The resulting 
microstructures are observed with optical and scanning electron microscopy. The 520℃ 
48h sample is found to contain the largest Si particles, with size of up to 10 micrometers, 
and it is therefore chosen for further investigation based on X-ray tomography. Tensile 
testing combined with in-situ X-ray tomography is used to reveal the micro-
mechanisms of deformation and fracture. The main conclusions are: 
1. Solution heat treatment for as-printed SLM parts leads to a decrease in hardness. 
The subsequent artificial aging enhances the hardness to some extent, but not to 
the initial level of the as-printed microstructure. 
2. By increasing the solution heat treatment time from 5h to 48h, larger Si particles 
are obtained, which increase the hardness. Nevertheless, the hardness improvement 
is not as good as that of artificial aging. 
3. Fe rich needle like phase has been found in the heat-treated samples by SEM/EDS. 
4. Both Si particles and porosity have been successfully separated from CT scan 
data to build a 3D mapping of their morphology and distribution.  
5. The deformation of the porosity is larger and more obvious than that of Si 
particles. Soft porosity exhibit elongation in the direction of the load. Hard Si 
particles exhibit separation from the Al matrix under high load.

 1 Introduction  
o 1.1 Aluminum alloy AlSi10Mg 
Aluminum (Al) is a chemical element with atomic number 13. It is a kind of silvery, 
light weight, non-magnetic, non-sparking, malleable metal, with a density of 2.702 g/cc 
at 300K [1], which is lower than that of other common metallics alloys. In 1825, 
Aluminum was discovered by Danish physicist Hans Christian Ørsted. French chemist 
Henri Étienne Sainte-Claire Deville first used Aluminum at industrial production in 
1856. As a relatively young metal material, Aluminum is mainly used in the aviation 
industry, construction industry, container packaging industry, transportation industry, 
power and electronics industry, machinery manufacturing and petrochemical industries. 
Aluminum is used in a large amount and has a wide range, making it the second largest 
metal material after steel. 
As important lightweight structural materials, there are two classifications of 
Aluminum alloy, namely casting alloys and wrought alloys; both can further distinguish 
whether it can be heat treated or not.  The typical alloying elements are copper, 
magnesium, manganese, silicon, tin and zinc. Cast aluminum alloys have been largely 
used in the transportation and aerospace industries [2].  
In general, Al–Si-based alloys comprise more than 90% of all castings [3], as they 
exhibit sound castability, good weldability and excellent corrosion resistance [4]. The 
hypoeutectic alloy is near the eutectic composition (12.5% Si) which is responsible for 
the excellent casting properties [5] [6] [7]. Similarly, near-eutectic composition of Al 
and Si contributes to good weldability owing to small solidification range [8]. Due to 
their attractive combination of mechanical properties, high heat conductivity and low 
density, Al-Si alloys are found a large number of applications in aerospace, automotive, 
domestic industries and medical area [9].  
In addition, alloying magnesium to the Al-Si alloy enables the precipitation of Mg2Si 
phase in the natural or artificial ageing treatment, which will strengthen the matrix of 
Aluminum to a significant extent without compromising the other mechanical 
properties [10].  Minor additions of magnesium (0.3–0.5% are most beneficial) allow 
hardenability by natural or artificial ageing [6], while the alloys of AlSi10Mg 
containing 0.45 to 0.6 wt% Mg need to be hardened through the precipitation 
mechanism by applying a specific heat treatment [11]. 
Common aluminum alloy processing methods include rolling, extrusion, stretching and 
forging, etc. Aluminum alloy is also a good material for additive manufacturing.  
As a hypoeutectic alloy, AlSi10Mg has a narrow solidification temperature range, 
thereby reducing the susceptibility to thermal cracking during cooling [2] [12].

o 1.2 Selective laser melting 
Additive manufacturing (AM) also known as 3D-printing, is a key emerging technology 
that is challenging standard manufacturing methods [13].  This manufacturing 
technology integrates computer-aided design material processing with moulding 
technology. Generally, additive manufacturing is based on digital model files through 
software and numerical control systems, using special metal materials, non-metal 
materials and medical biological materials, through extrusion, sintering, melting, light 
curing, spraying, etc., layer by layer accumulation to fabricate parts [14][15]. In brief, 
it is through the digital increase of materials that fabricates parts, including steps as 
follows: 3D CAD model; layered sectioning; stacking up in layers; and nearly net 
forming parts.  
The biggest advantage of additive manufacturing over traditional machining is from 
scratch on flat substrates or add features to existing parts, which is suitable for 
manufacturing geometrically complex parts and saving materials since it uses only the 
material needed to fabricate the part of interest. In addition, AM has a series of new 
features that go beyond traditional technologies: 
 
Free forming without mould devices, along with reduction in the dependency on 
mechanical fixtures and additional production operations [16]. 
 
It can form almost infinite complex part structure. 
 
It is suitable for forming almost all kinds of materials. 
 
It is suitable for forming parts of almost all sizes. 
 
Manufacturing philosophy is dimensionality reduction and formulation principle, 
most suitable for the characteristics of the scientific method. 
There are promising functions as follows: 
 
Significantly accelerate the technological iteration time cycle of new product 
development through replacing a series of production processes by a single-step 
process [16]. 
 
Solve technical problems that are difficult to be solved with traditional 
technologies. 
 
Optimized design of support structure, such as significant lightweight of aircraft 
structural parts. One case is optimized A320 Nacelle FCD Hinge; a 60% weight 
saving was achieved through topology optimization [17]. 
 
Overall structure optimization through combining components within assemblies 
[16], like significantly lightening the satellite antenna (3D printed spacecraft 
antenna reduces weight by 95% from 100 parts to one, with manufacturing time 
reduction from 11 months to 2 months, and manufacturing cost reduction by 20%). 
 
Design of special functional structure [18] [19], such as optimization of 
hydrodynamic performance. 
 
Follow-up cooling die will bring revolutionary progress in die field.

3D printing is creating new approaches to advanced medical technology.

With the development of Additive Manufacturing (AM) technology, Aluminum alloy 
are receiving most attention from researchers and industrialists, for the reasons that can 
offer a good compromise between strength and density, along with being relatively 
inexpensive; positioning themselves to be used in applications where good performance 
and light-weighting are needed concurrently [16] [20].   
SLM is one of the most popular technique for processing Al alloys, as it can fabricate 
bulk and open-cell structures, such as porous or cellular Al, which is a strong candidate 
for crumple zones in automobiles due to its being a light-weight metal with good 
deformability [21]. SLM is also beneficial for processing hard-to-machine or form Al 
alloys. For example, the AA-6xxx series contains a large amount of hard intermetallics 
that make forming it challenging [22]. The microstructural enhancement is another 
advantage for SLM processing Al (cast alloys, especially). In the traditional way, cast 
alloys are strengthened through refining their microstructures during casting by adding 
chemical modifiers. Whereas the high cooling rates associated with SLM lead to 
microstructural refinement during manufacture [23] without altering the chemical 
composition. As such, SLM processing of castable Al alloys can cope with 
the demands for the fabrication of complex structures with refined microstructures [16]. 
SLM system composition 
There are several devices in a SLM system, including laser source, powder containers, 
delivering and layering apparatus, build platform, computer systems, and so on. Figure 
1.1 is the schematic diagram of a SLM system, including powder reservoir, powder 
feeder/dosing, wiper blade, overflow powder outlet, powder overflow reservoir, 
moveable laser beam, platform, and build plate. 
SLM process 
As the name implies, SLM process can selectively melt powder layers along the scan 
track to allow fusion to occur between tracks and consecutive layers, leading to a highly 
dense part [24]. 
The swift laser-powder interaction creates a small melt pool, which has high 
temperature and complex hydrodynamic flows. In order to reduce contamination and 
oxidation during manufacturing,  the SLM process should be carried out in a gas 
protective building environment and the building chamber needs to be pressurized 
with inert gas (e.g., argon, nitrogen) before the 3D printing starts [25].

 
The SLM process begins with an optimized 3D model from a computer aided

 
Orientate to the optimal build direction, which is generally parallel to the Z axis of

 
Generate support structures. 


 
Heat the build-plate to aid in lowering the thermal gradient [26] to avoid part 
curling [27].  
 
A layer of powder of a predefined thickness is spread onto the build-plate. 
 
A focused laser beam is fired onto the layer, moving in the x-y plane to selectively 
scan and melt the regions of interest. 
 
The build-plate is then lowered a distance equal to the predefined layer thickness 
to allow for the deposition of another layer, which is more commonly referred to 
as “recoating”. 
Repeating steps 6 to 8 to print the part layer by layer, i.e., stacks up in layers, until 
nearly net forming parts come into being. At the same time, the unmelted powder can 
be collected and sieved for future use.

SLM process parameters 
SLM process parameters can be divided into four related parts [28]: 
 
Laser-related: laser power, spot size, pulse duration, pulse frequency. 
 
Scan-related: scan speed, scan spacing, scan pattern. 
 
Powder-related: powder particle size, particle shape and distribution, powder bed 
density, layer thickness, material properties. 
 
Temperature-related: power bed temperature, powder feeder temperature, 
temperature uniformity.  
Laser power control is about how much energy deliver to material. Aluminum as a 
highly reflective material need high power to melt [29].  
Scan pattern is the design of laser path in one layer. Another parameter about scan is 
scan strategy like rotation between two layers. 
Powder related parameters are about the raw materials. They will be adjust accordingly 
based on different kinds of material and their properties. The size of the powder will 
affect the layer thickness and porosity. 
Temperature settings are one of the most important parameters. They are also related to 
the material properties. 
 
Design of SLM parts 
The design of SLM parts is much different from casting part. The shape and geometry 
of SLM parts can be more complicated. At the same time, it can save materials due to 
reducing non-loading structure which are usually designed for metal flow during 
casting.  
 
Materials for SLM 
SLM uses powder as feedstock. For now, the widely used feedstock are different kinds 
of alloys, including aluminum alloys [30], nickel alloys [31], steels [32], and titanium 
alloys [33]. 
Also, these alloys have been serving the industrial for a long time. The understanding 
of materials selection for SLM manufacturing is still missing. Most materials chosen 
for SLM are due to their original process ability, such as castability or weldability, 
suitable for SLM process. 
On the other hand, new materials or multi-materials, designed according to functional 
requirements, are of research interest.

New materials synthesized in situ by laser selective melting: it has specific advantages,

The feature of powder is crucial to the density and quality of the parts, for reasons that 
the morphology and granulometry of powder play an important role in flat, uniform

Therefore, metallic powders should possess small size distribution range and good

fabricated by atomization [35]. 
 
AlSi10Mg in SLM 
AlSi10Mg alloy is one of the first used Al alloy in SLM processing [36]. There are 
some difficulties using AlSi10Mg in SLM. Aluminum powder has high reflectivity to 
the laser beam, high thermal conductivity, relatively active, and easy to form an oxide 
film on the surface. These reasons lead to extensive porosity in SLM Al samples and 
the need of high energy densities. There are two types of porosity in SLM parts. The 
first one with small size usually severely microns, is gas pores or hydrogen porosity, 
denoted by Weingarten et al. [37]. The second type of porosity is a large hole of 
hundreds of microns in size, which looks like a flat disc. This kind of porosity has been 
attributed to the oxides in the parts during melting and solidification [38].  
Porosity can be a good mark for in-situ X-ray tomography. 
Spatter is also one of the common defects in the SLM AlSi10Mg. Studies have shown 
that splashing is mainly caused by lateral shielding airflow, fluctuations in the molten 
pool and recoil pressure [39]. Splashes falling on the powder will form larger metal 
particles, which will result in under-fusion and pore defects, which are detrimental to 
the tensile strength and fatigue performance of the components. In addition, splashes 
falling on the surface of the solidified layer will affect the next layer of powder, 
resulting in an uneven layer of powder, and even damage the squeegee.

Prashanth and Eckert attributed the porosity to the high solidification rates during the 
SLM process, the near eutectic composition, and the Si-Al equilibrium partition ratio 
less than one. Nevertheless, it is these reasons that make this material have a unique 
microstructure [40].  
The heat treatment method of AlSi10Mg commonly used in industry is T6. First step is 
solution heat treatment at over 500℃ for 6 hours. Then, water quench at room 
temperature. Finally, artificial aging at around 165℃ for 7 hours.  
Brandle et al. [41] built samples in three different directions and worked with T6 
condition for SLM AlSi10Mg: peak-hardened at 525℃ for 6 hours, at room temperature 
water quench, and artificial aging at165℃ for 7h. The results showed that the 
orientation of the microstructure caused by built directions in the melting pool was not 
obvious after heat treatment. 
Aboulkhair et al. [42] took T6-like procedure, i.e. 1 h solution heat treatment at 520℃ 
and artificial aging at 160℃ for 6h. Obvious changes in microstructure and mechanical 
properties could be observed before and after heat treatment. Both the Si particles and 
pores in the materials evolve during annealing. How these changes affect the 
mechanical properties have not been well understood yet.

o 1.3 X-ray tomography 
X-ray computed tomography (CT), also known as X-ray microtomography, can provide 
unrivalled information about the internal structure of materials non-destructively from 
the meters down to the tens of nanometers length scales. It exploits the penetrating 
power of X-rays to obtain a series of two-dimensional (2D) radiographs of the object 
viewed from many different directions. A computed reconstruction algorithm is then 
used to create a stack of cross-sectional slices from these 2D projections (radiographs) 
of the object. This process provides a digital 3D greyscale representation (often referred 
to as a tomogram) of the internal structure of the object, which can be quantitatively 
analyzed and virtually sliced in any direction or specific constituents can be digitally 
colour-coded, or rendered transparent, to visualize the 3D morphology [43]. 
For this reason, X-ray has been widely used in medical flaw detection and airport 
security. It can also be used on non-destructive testing of industrial products. 
The non-destructive aspect also opens up the possibility of studies monitoring the 
evolution of 3D structure, for example the manufacture and assembly of products 
[44] or product degradation in service [45].  
Several important factors need to be considered, including the object size, the features 
of interest and their composition, the allowable dose and the temporal resolution. The 
contrast recorded by each projection is dependent on the interaction of X-rays with 
matter. As a result, several contrast modes are possible that suit different types of 
imaging task. When an electromagnetic X-ray wave passes through an object, both the 
intensity and the phase change vary according to the absorption (attenuation) 
coefficients index [43].

o 1.4 Objectives and tasks 
The objectives of this study is to understand the relation between microstructure and 
mechanical properties as well as the micro-mechanical deformation mechanisms of 3D 
printed and heat-treated AlSi10Mg by means of cutting-edge 4D (x,y,z plus time) 
experiments based on X-rays. Specific tasks for reaching the goal include: 
 
i) 
To establish the relationships between the heat treatment parameters and the 
resulting microstructural features (like size and morphology of the Si 
particles and pores), 
ii) 
To characterize the mechanical properties of the sample by micro-hardness 
test, 
iii) 
To characterize the microstructural features using X-ray CT, 
iv) 
To perform tensile tests combined with in-situ X-ray tomography to reveal 
the micro-mechanisms of deformation and fracture of selected heat-treated 
microstructures.

 2 Methodology 
SLM AlSi10Mg samples were provided by Danish Technological Institute, DTI. One 
of the samples was cut into small parts for three different heat treatments and artificial 
aging conditions to find the parameters suitable for X-ray tomography. Another sample 
was sent to the workshop to be machined into tensile samples. Optical microscope 
images of the three samples were taken to find the one with the largest Si particles size. 
520℃ 48h annealing was chosen as the heat treatment parameter for the sample of in-
situ tensile test. At the same time, the as-printed sample and the three samples with 
different heat-treated conditions had been subjected to microhardness test and scanning 
electron microscopy analysis.

ProntoPress-20 Mounting press; RotoPol-22 Grinding/Polishing machine; FM-700

electron microscopy support by FORCE Technic.

Figure 3.21 Segmentation of Si particles

For the porosity, the setting of Interactive Thresholding 2 is different. The chosen blue 
part is everything in the sample except the porosity. After that, use Closing to create a

 4 Conclusions 
Selective laser melting is capable of producing the fully dense near the net-shape 
components, and has broad prospects and important significance for developing high-
performance and complex structural materials. 
AlSi10Mg, as a hypoeutectic casting alloy, is well known to have light weight, high 
strength, good weldability, hardenability and corrosion resistance. Hence, fine-
structured Aluminum parts are extensively used in prominent industrial fields, 
especially in aerospace areas. However, such opportunities are accompanied with great 
challenges, such as how to control over the microstructure of the SLM fabricated 
Aluminum alloys AlSi10Mg and the mechanical performance of the parts in an efficient 
and effective manner.  
This thesis works on the microstructure and micro-mechanical behavior of heat-treated 
AlSi10Mg samples fabricated via selective laser melting, by means of cutting-edge 4D 
experiments based on X-ray computer tomograph. And main conclusions have been 
drawn as below. 
 
1. Solution heat treatment for as-printed SLM parts leads to a hardness decrease. 
Artificial aging produces many small Si particles which rebound to some extent 
hardness but still less than the as-printed one. 
2. Solution heat treatment time from 5h to 48h lets the Si particles grow. Larger Si 
particles contributes to the hardness increase at the same time. Nevertheless, the 
improvement in SLM parts’ hardness is not as good as that of artificial aging. 
3. Fe rich needle like phase has been found in the heat-treated samples by SEM/EDS. 
4. Both Si particles and porosity have been successfully separated from CT scan data 
to build a 3D mapping of the morphology and distribution. 
5. The deformation of the porosity is larger and more obvious than that of Si particles. 
Soft porosity exhibit elongation in the direction of the load. Hard Si particles 
exhibit separation from the Al matrix under high load.

 5 Future works 
The 4D in situ deformation data are very rich, containing micromechanical deformation 
information. It can be further analyzed using for example digital volume correlation 
method to analyze the local strain field, and then correlated that to the local 
microstructure. This type of analysis will be important for understanding the 
relationship between the microstructure and mechanical properties of heat-treated SLM 
AlSi10Mg. 
Another possible direction is to convert the X-ray tomography data into a digital matrix 
and use this as a basis for computer simulation analysis. A comparison between the 
simulated and the experimentally determined deformation strain field will be critical 
for developing advanced modeling.
