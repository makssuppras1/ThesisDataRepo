1. Project description 
 
The purpose of this project was to get a pilot scale trickle bed reactor (Synferon) fully operational. The Synferon 
was a biomethanation unit using syngas as a substrate. This entailed troubleshooting, acquiring know-how on 
the equipment and development of documents describing the equipment e.g. a safety assessment and a manual.  
When the Synferon was fully operational, experiments were performed to evaluate its performance. These 
experiments, when relevant, were compared with a laboratory scale trickle bed reactor at similar operational 
conditions. The primary target of this project was to estimate the maximum methanogenic capacity of the 
Synferon and if supplying it with raw syngas affected its performance. In addition, the effect of the liquid 
recirculation rate on syngas biomethanation was assessed, along with its economic impacts with respect to 
power consumption. The decrease the operational cost was also studied by examining the performance of 
Synferon without medium addition. 
For the laboratory scale reactor, previous research activities had already been performed, and thus the 
performance of the system capabilities was already known. Instead, the liquid recirculation was evaluated to 
examine if the effect on the laboratory reactor would be similar to the pilot reactor.

2. Abstract 
The theme of this thesis was thermophilic biomethanation of syngas using trickle bed reactors. The initial part 
was getting a pilot scale biomethanation reactor (5L) operational and doing commissioning on it. The pilot reactor 
was an upscaling of a laboratory reactor (0.18L). Besides this, experiments were also performed on both reactors. 
The first experiment conducted was assessing the pilot reactor capability to convert syngas into CH4, which was 
done by varying the empty bed retention time (EBRT). The reactor was able to maintain > 98% conversion of 
both H2 and CO at EBRT of 36 min with a productivity of 4.4 mL/Lbed/min (96.5% of the theoretical maximum). 
The lowest EBRT evaluated was 20 min. At this condition the reactor converted 97% of the H2 and 76% of the CO, 
giving a productivity of 7.3 mL/Lbed/min (90.2%). In comparison the laboratory reactor, had a conversion of 96% 
H2 and 87% CO and a productivity of 2.2 mL/Lbed/min (82%) at an EBRT of 60 min. The difference in efficiency was 
primarily attributed to the nozzle used for liquid distribution that was placed on the top of the pilot trickle bed 
column, but other factors such as pH control and height per diameter (H/D) ratio might also have played a part. 
The liquid recirculation rate (QR-L/Lbed/min) was evaluated on both systems. The QR’s evaluated were 1.2, 0.9 and 
0.6 for the laboratory- and 0.3, 0.2 and 0.1 for the pilot reactor. The nozzle placed in the pilot reactor enabled it 
to operate under lower QR. In the laboratory reactor, decreasing the QR directly correlated with decreasing 
performance, reducing the conversions to 94% for H2 and 82.4% for CO and the productivity to 2.1 mL/Lbed/min 
(78%) at a QR of 0.6. It was concluded this was a result of insufficient wetting of the bed. The pilot reactor 
displayed a slight decrease at the initial reduction of OR, followed by an increase in performance at the lowest 
QR. Giving conversions of 99% H2 and 73% CO and a productivity of 7.5 mL/Lbed/min (92.2%) at a QR of 0.1. Two 
explanations were proposed, one being a shift in metabolism due to unforeseen incidents, though the VFA profile 
or production supported this. The second was that foam negatively affected the hydrogenotrophs as no foam 
was seen while operating at QR of 0.1. 
To evaluate the necessity for liquid medium, it was turned off for 8 days, at an EBRT of 60 min. During this period 
the total concentration of VFAs increased from 1.7 to 3 g/L, primarily due to an increase in acetic acid making it 
the dominant VFA. This, along with low concentrations of nutrients, is probably what caused the significant 
decrease in the conversion of H2 to 33%. As it had not yet stabilised when the experiment was terminated, it 
could potentially have dropped further. The activity of the carboxydotrophs seemed to be stable at around 90%. 
During this period productivity dropped by 54%. However, this shows that at least the carboxydotrophs were 
able to reuse the nutrients supplied from the initial medium. 
Finally, the pilot reactor was also evaluated at Dansk Gasteknisk Center (DGC), where it was connected to a 
gasifier supplying it with raw syngas produced from the gasification of wood pellets. Complications with the 
methanation catalyst, caused the experimental work to be cut short and only EBRTs of 89, 60 and 35 minutes 
were examined. The highest reactor productivity was reported at an EBRT of 60 min, being 3 mL/Lbed/min (101%), 
while 100% was achieved at all EBRTs for both H2 and CO. It should be noted that the flow measurement was 
done using a flowmeter and was not accurate as the flow fluctuated. The primary result from this experiment 
was that using raw syngas, did not seem to affect the efficiency of the reactor at low to moderate EBRTs.

3. Abstract (Danish) 
Projektet omhandlede termofil biometanisering af syngas ved brug af trickle bed reaktorer. Den første del af 
projektet var klargøring af pilot skala biometaniserings reaktoren (5L) og få den operationel. Pilot skala reaktoren

Det første eksperiment udført var at estimere pilot reaktorens evne til at konvertere syngas til CH4, hvilket blev 
gjort ved at variere gassens opholds tid (EBRT). Reaktoren kunne opretholde en konversion over 98% af både H2 
og CO ved en EBRT på 36 min resulterende i en produktivitet på 4,4 mL/Lbed/min (96,5% af det teoretiske mulige). 
Den laveste EBRT undersøgt var 20 min. Under disse betingelser konverterede reaktoren 97% H2 og 76% CO, med 
en produktivitet på 7,3 mL/Lbed/min (92,2%), til sammenligning havde laboratorie reaktoren en konversion på 
96% H2 og 87% CO og en produktivitet på 2.2 mL/Lbed/min (82%) ved en EBRT på 60 min. Forskellen i effektivitet 
blev antaget til at være dyssen in toppen af pilot trickle bed kolonnen, men andre faktorer har muligvis også 
spillet en rolle. 
Væske recirkulationshastigheden (QR-L/Lbed/min) blev evalueret på begge systemer. QR’erne evaluerede var 
1,2, 0,9 og 0,6 for laboratorie- og 0,3, 0,2 og 0,1 for pilot reaktoren. Dyssen i pilot reaktoren gjorde det muligt at 
opererer ved lavere QR. I laboratorie reaktoren, korrelerede lavere QR direkte med ydeevnen og mindskede 
konversionen af H2 til 94%, CO til 82,4% og produktiviteten til 2,1 mL/Lbed/min (78%) ved en QR på 0,6. Det blev 
konkluderet, at det var resultatet af utilstrækkelig befugtning. Pilot reaktoren viste et mindre fald i ydeevne ved 
første reduktion af QR, efterfulgt at øget ydeevne ved yderligere reduktion af QR. Dette gav en konversion på 
99% H2 og 73% CO og en produktivitet på 7,5 mL/Lbed/min (92%) ved en QR på 0,1. To forklaringer blev foreslået, 
én at metabolismen havde ændret sig grundet nogle uforudsete hændelser, dette var dog ikke understøttet af 
VFA profilen eller VFA produktionen. Den anden forklaring var at skum påvirkede hydrogenotropherne negativt, 
siden intet skum blev observeret ved en QR på 0,1. 
For at evaluere nødvendigheden af flydende medium, blev det slukket i 8 dage, ved en EBRT på 60 min. Under 
denne periode steg VFA-koncentrationen fra 1,7 til 3 g/L, primært grundet højere eddikesyre koncentrationer, 
hvilket gjorde den til den dominante VFA. Dette i samspil med lave nærings koncentrationer, er sandsynligvis 
hvad der forårsagede det betydelige fald i konversionen af H2 til 33%. Da den ikke var stabil endnu, da 
eksperimentet blev stoppet, er det sandsynligt at konversionen ville være faldet yderligere. Aktiviteten af 
carboxydotroph’erne var stabil omkring 90%. Under denne periode faldt produktiviteten med 54%. Dette viser 
at som minimum for carboxydotroph’erne, er det muligt at genbruge næringsstofferne fra mediet. 
Sidst blev pilot reaktoren også testet på Dansk Gasteknisk Center (DGC), hvor den blev forbundet til en forgasser 
som leverede rå syngas, produceret fra forgasning af træpiller. Komplikationer med metaniseringskatalysatoren 
forkortede det eksperimentelle arbejde og kun EBRT på 86, 60 og 35 min blev derfor testet. Den højeste 
produktivitet fundet, var ved en EBRT på 60 min, på 3 mL/Lbed/min (101%), ved 100% konversion af både H2 og 
CO. Det bør nævnes at flowmålingerne, blev lavet at et flowmeter og var ikke præcise fordi flowet fluktuerede. 
Det primære resultat fra dette eksperiment var, at brugen af rå syngas ikke så ud til at påvirke reaktoren ved lave 
til moderate EBRT.

7. Introduction 
This section aims to give a general introduction to biofuels. It covers the importance of biofuels, general concepts 
used, what syngas is along with the requirements for injecting gas into the natural gas grid in Denmark. This gives 
the background for understanding biofuels and the motivation for this project. 
7.1 Fuel demand 
Globally the demand for energy and chemicals is increasing, partially due to an increase in the population [1]. 
Many products such as energy but also plastics, pharmaceuticals and fertilizers among others are produced using 
fossil fuels [2]. This leads to a worldwide increase in the demand for oil, coal and natural gas, which are often 
derived from fossil fuels [3]. In 2011 approximately 85% of the energy produced was produced from fossil fuels 
[3]. The U.S. Energy Information Administration has predicted that the world’s energy consumption will increase 
by 28% from 2015 to 2040 [4]. The current and predicted utilization of different fuels can be seen in Figure 7.1. 
Figure 7.1 shows an increase in all energy sources, except coal. This indicates an overall increase in energy 
consumption, making renewable energy more important in the effort to decrease CO2 emissions. Due to the 
increase in fossil fuel consumption, it is expected that the current supply of oil and gas will only last 40-60 years 
and coal 150 years [5]. 
 
 Figure 7.1 – World energy consumption by energy source (1990-2040) - Figure reused from [4] 
Besides being in limited supply, burning fossil fuels is also associated with emitting greenhouse gases (GHG) [3] 
and increasing the global CO2 level. The increase in CO2 is closely related to the increase in global warming [2], 
[6]. The increase in CO2 also leads to more CO2 being dissolved in the oceans, which decreases their pH. The 
decrease in pH of the world oceans changes their ecosystems and contributes to a loss in biodiversity [7]. Other 
GHG, such as methane (CH4) also contribute to the global rise in temperature. However, CO2 is the most common, 
accounting for 81% of the total GHG in 2016 in the US [8]. Therefore, it is imperative to find viable alternatives 
to fossil fuels such as solar-, water-, wind- and biomass-derived energy. Viable alternatives can reduce the

dependency on fossil fuels leading to fewer GHG being released. One such alternative is biofuels due to their 
similarity to fossil fuels. Biofuels can due to this similarity substitute fossil fuels and thus decrease the use without 
requiring large investments in changing the infrastructure [2].  
The advantage of biomass compared to the other renewable energy sources is its liquid or gaseous form, which 
makes it easy to storage. The challenge with wind-, water- and solar energy is they directly produce electricity 
and do so independently of consumption. Thus, a high amount of electricity can be produced without being 
consumed wasting it unless it is stored, unfortunately this is currently difficult to do efficiently [3]. One possibility 
is to combine biofuels with solar/water/wind power. This can be done by using excess electricity to produce e.g. 
hydrogen from water. The hydrogen can be combusted, as it is a fuel or used by some of the biofuels production 
technologies [9]. Another advantage is biofuels are not as geographically dependent since biomass like trees 
grows in most parts of the world. Whereas, solar/water/wind requires a certain amount of either wind, water 
currents or sunlight to be effective [3].  
Theoretically, the biomass used for biofuels can be both stored and combusted without any changes to it. 
However, the calorific values (CV) of the initial state of the biomass is low. CV is a measure of the energy pr. 
volume, meaning storage and combusting of low CV products requires more space, thus making it inconvenient. 
By increasing the CV through the conversion of the material, it becomes possible to directly substitute fossil fuel-
derived products such as natural gas, diesel and gasoline, with the biofuel equivalent. This approach only requires 
a few alterations to existing infrastructure [3]. The substitution is possible because both products combust at 
high temperatures and are very similar in composition. This substitution is already done to some degree by some 
countries, where bioethanol and biodiesel are blended into the gasoline or diesel [3].  
The similarities between biofuels and fossil fuels mean the combustion of biofuels also produces CO2. However, 
biofuels are considered renewable due to the carbon cycle. The CO2 released from the combustion of biofuels is 
the same amount used to regrow the combusted biomass through the photosynthesis process occurring in 
plants, giving a net CO2 release of zero. Technically this is also the case for fossil fuels. However, the time it takes 
to produce is so long that it cannot be considered a closed system [3]. 
The carbon cycle is an important parameter when producing biofuels, especially from energy crops (crops grown 
for the sole purpose of making biofuels). If not managed correctly fuel, can be consumed faster than the crops 
are regrown, making it necessary to obtain the biomass for fuel elsewhere. The extra biomass obtained and not 
regrown would release a surplus of CO2 to the atmosphere [3]. Even when the carbon cycle is managed correctly, 
biofuels are not completely CO2 neutral. Energy is still used for production and distribution, which releases CO2 
but significantly less compared to fossil fuels [3]. 
Biofuels also has the advantage that it can be derived from several different biological materials. The advantages 
lay within, most geographical locations have some of these materials available, unlike fossil fuels. These materials 
can be items like wood, sewage sludge, aquatic plants and biological waste. It should be noted that due to the 
difference in these materials the pre-treatment procedure of the biomass is highly dependent on the type and 
the sought product [3]. Because of the different pre-treatment procedure required it might not be possible to 
easily change the feedstock in the process.

Another advantage is biofuels can be produced from waste, which also means it can reduce or make use of waste 
produced in other industries. Having the option to use waste products gives a relative low-cost resource, which 
can then be refined into a much more expensive product [3]. The currently prevailing issue is biofuels are still 
not economically competitive with fossil fuels, mainly because of the cost, availability and sustainability of the 
feedstocks [6]. This issue occurs because biofuels e.g. bioethanol is often produced from food cops meaning high-
quality agricultural land is necessary for the growth of the feedstock. Further adding to the cost is the growth 
time of the crops, which is high [10]. 
 
7.2 Biofuels in general 
Biofuels are normally divided into four groups, based on the production technology and how the biomass used 
as feedstock is obtained. The groups categorized as first-, second-, third- and fourth generation biofuel [3], [10].  
First-generation biofuels are synthesised from edible products such as wheat, sugar, starch, and vegetable oil. 
Common first-generation biofuels products are different bioalcohols such as biomethanol and bioethanol, 
vegetable oils and biodiesel [3], [10]. The most common one being biodiesel, which is produced through 
transesterification of the oil extracted from the biomass. The main principle is chemically reacting the oil or fat 
with an alcohol, which requires a catalyst. As earlier mentioned, this substance can easily be mixed with regular 
diesel, only requiring few changes to the combustion engine. This approach does benefit the CO2 balance and 
domestic energy security is increased since there will be a lower dependency on oil [2].  
The key issue with first-generation biofuels is whether it is moral to use agricultural land to grow crops used for 
fuel, instead of food. The primary argument for this is using agricultural land or products for fuel production 
increases the price of basic food commodities. Another issue is the decrease in biodiversity caused by the 
increase in agricultural land necessary for production [2]. Because agricultural land is used to produce feedstock, 
the price is high making it difficult to produce cost-competitive to petroleum-based fuels. However, in some 
cases, it is cost-competitive due to the increase in the price of petroleum-based fuels, which to some degree can 
compensate for the high feedstock price [2]. 
Second-generation does not require agricultural land, because they can be produced from feedstocks such as 
wood and waste products from agricultural production (such as wheat straw) [3], [2]. Because they can be 
produced from waste and wood, they are not in direct competition with food and should hypothetically not 
increase food prices. However, the number of waste products from agricultural production is not enough to 
satisfy the demand for fuel. Though it can still help satisfy a portion of the fuel demand, and thus be part of the 
solution along with other fuel alternatives. Feedstocks such as wood and some of the waste types are normally 
referred to as lignocellulosic feedstock because they contain a high amount of lignin and cellulose. [3], [2]. Figure 
7.2 gives an overview of the more common processes and production routes for second-generation biofuels.  
==PAGE:13==

To process the lignocellulosic biomass, biochemical and thermochemical methods are used. Thermochemical 
processes such as pyrolysis and gasification have the advantage of converting almost all the organic compounds 
compared to the biochemical route [2]. The main issue when using the biochemical routes with lignocellulosic 
material is, it is difficult for enzymes and microorganisms to digest. This is due to the high content of lignin and 
the crystallinity of the cellulose. To overcome these barriers, it is necessary to pre-treat lignocellulosic materials 
and making them more amorphous, making it easier for enzymes and microorganisms to attack and degrade the 
material [11 - chapter 3]. The need for pre-treatment is the current main drawback since this often requires 
much energy and makes it difficult to produce these fuels cost-effectively. Making fuel produced from a second-
generation feedstock more expensive compared to petroleum-based fuels. It is anticipated that when the 
technology comes of age, it will become the most cost-effective way to produce fuel since waste can be used as 
feedstock. Using waste is cheap compared to other biomass and can to some degree compensate for the 
expensive pre-treatment [2].  
Third- and fourth-generation is defined as algae-derived biomass. As this is not an edible crop, nor do they require 
to be cultivated on agricultural land and are they are fast growing. Because of the high growth rate, the potential 
of the algae is high due to the possibility of having large productions [3], [7]. Like second-generation biofuels 
thermochemical and/or biochemical methods must be used for the conversion into fuel. Again, this is a significant 
cost to the production. The difference between third and fourth generation lies in the algae’s, which are 
genetically modified in fourth-generation. Modifying the algae can increase the yield achieved by the process 
[3]. Besides this, the production methods are the same as for the other types of feedstock [7].  
The biogas obtained from the Synferon would be considered second-generation biogas. It uses gasification where 
non-edible crops, e.g. wood pellets, can be converted to syngas. However, instead of the traditional way of using 
Fischer-Tropsch synthesis is uses a biological conversion of the syngas into CH4.

7.3 Natural- and Biogas in Denmark  
Methane (CH4) both in the form of fossil fuel, natural gas or as renewable biogas has the potential to reduce GHG 
emissions if used as a replacement for other larger hydrocarbons. Because CH4 has the highest number of 
hydrogens pr. carbon it has higher energy content than larger hydrocarbons. The high energy content in CH4 
results in less CO2 being emitted when using CH4 as less is needed for the same amount of energy. Making it 
more favourable regarding GHG emissions and easier to store. Generally, the larger the hydrocarbon the larger 
is the GHG emission from combusting that compound [12], [13]. Therefore, shifting current used fossil fuels to 
methane (where possible) could help reduce GHG emissions. Another advantage of using methane compared to 
other hydrocarbons is the presence of other pollutants. CH4 does not in itself contain sulphur and nitric groups, 
which can be present in other hydrocarbons. Therefore, using CH4 can also contribute to lower SOX and NOX 
emissions in e.g. cars [13]. 
The main difference between natural- and biogas is the CH4 content. Natural gas is almost entirely CH4 whereas 
biogas has a relatively high content of CO2. Biogas is defined by the Danish government as a gas produced by 
anaerobic digestion of organic material such as manure, sludge, energy crops or organic waste [14]. In contrast, 
being a fossil fuel, natural gas is found underground and is in a finite amount. Due to the inherent difference in 
the gas types, the different requirement also applies to them such as the calorific value and the Wobbe Index.  
The calorific value (CV), is defined in Denmark as the heat released during combustion of one unit of gas (MJ/m3) 
under constant pressure when supplied the gas for the combustion has a temperature of 25°C [14]. The CV is 
separated into a higher and lower value. During combustion, water is formed, which is the basis for the 
difference. For the lower CV, the energy content in the water vapour is not considered, thus giving a lower energy 
content. The energy in the water vapour is considered in the higher CV [14]. 
The other parameter is the Wobbe Index. Like the CV, it is split into an upper- and lower Wobbe index and is 
measured in MJ/m3. The Wobbe Index is defined as the CV, divided by the square root of the relative density of 
the gas. The Wobbe Index is used to describe the interchangeability of gasses or fuels [14]. As such two gases 
with the same Wobbe Index will release the same amount of energy when combusted under the same conditions 
and can normally be substituted with one another. 
Biogas can also be used in the natural- [15] and city gas grid [16]. Thus, making it possible to replace fossil natural 
gas with a renewable alternative. However, to do so it is imperative that both the CV and Wobbe Index of the 
biogas is the same as natural gas. Thus, the CO2 content of the biogas must be lowered. If the biogas is to be 
supplied into the natural gas grid it requires an Upper Wobbe Index between 50.76 and 55.8 MJ/m3. The gas 
should also have a relative density between 0.555 and 0.7 kg/m3 [14]. On the other hand, if it is to be supplied 
into the city gas grid upper Wobbe Index is required to be between 23.6 and 26.2 MJ/m3 [14]. Normally city gas 
consists of a mixture of biogas and natural gas. However, a maximal amount of 43% of the energy in city gas can 
come from biogas (assuming a composition of 63% CH4 and 37% CO2) [14]. Several other regulations are also in 
place to ensure the quality of the gas is high enough not to damage the respective gas grids. They include 
regulation of the number of impurities allowed in the gas, such as ammonia, siloxanes and sulphur compounds 
[14].

7.3.1 
Syngas 
Syngas is a useful gas and is short for synthetic natural gas. It is defined this way to distinguish it from 
conventional natural gas [3]. Syngas primarily consists of CO, CO2, H2, CH4, N2, tar, water and some trace gases, 
which are depending on operational parameters and the feedstock from which it is derived. However, lots of 
contaminants are usually present in the gas such as alkalis, sulphur, unreacted tars and inorganic solids [3], [17]. 
These contaminants often cause problems in the downstream process as corrosion, clogging and deactivation of 
catalysts. Due to these issues syngas normally requires cleaning, thus a suitable cleaning strategy for the syngas 
is necessary [3].  
The calorific content of syngas can be up to 28 MJ/Nm3 [3]. Assuming a density of 0.8 kg/m3 (45% H2, 20% CO, 
25% CO2 and 10% CH4 at 1 ATM and 20°C) the upper Wobbe Index is 31.3 MJ/m3. This is much lower than the 
required values, so besides the toxic nature and other pollutants, syngas does not have the required heat content 
for any of the Danish gas grids. This emphasises syngas’s need for treatment such as methanation to increase 
the CV and Wobbe Index to an acceptable level. However, it can still be directly combusted to produce heat 
and/or electricity. 
Syngas can also be referred to as producer gas, this is depending on the gas mixture. The term producer gas 
normally refers to the gas used as a fuel for power generation and has a lower H2 content. Whereas syngas also 
can be used to make chemical intermediates [2]. The products syngas can be used for includes ammonia, CH4, 
bio-hydrogen and bio-methanol [3]. It is possible to convert producer gas into syngas by increasing the H2 amount 
in the gas, this can e.g. be done through the WGS reaction (see Equation 8.8 in section 8.2.4) [2], or by adding 
produced H2 from e.g. electrolysis of water.

8. Theory 
This section covers the necessary theory for understanding of the production of syngas, syngas biomethanation 
and trickle bed reactors.  
8.1 Trickle bed columns 
Trickle bed columns (TBC) are three-phase reactors meaning they have both a liquid, gas and a solid phase. They 
are also known as fixed-bed multi/three-phase reactors. TBC are often operated continuously, giving high 
productivity making them suitable for bulk production. This is an advantage in the petrochemical industry where 
they are frequently used, however, they have the potential to be employed in other industries as well. They are 
often used to produce various liquids and/or gas products. The solid phase is a fixed bed composed of a catalyst 
or a packing material, normally with a high surface area per volume. They are relatively simple in design and are 
composed of the filled column, a distributor in the top and a gas inlet either at the top or bottom. Depending on 
the applications and the design they might also recycle the liquid- and/or gas phase [18 - Chapter 1].  
Conventionally the solid phase is randomly packed within the column, but in some cases, using a structured 
packing is preferred. Non-random packing is normally used at low catalyst loading or poor mechanical strength 
of the catalyst. Non-random packing has the advantage of giving a lower pressure drop over the column. The 
primary purpose of the solid phase is usually, to increase the surface area between the gas and liquid phase, 
along with catalysing the process. [18 - Chapter 1].  
In TBC the liquid phase flows in the direction of gravity, as a liquid film over the solid phase. It enters the reactor 
at the top and is usually distributed in the column through a suitable mechanism [18 - Chapter 1]. The distribution 
mechanism is often a nozzle, but alternatives exist. If the liquid is not properly distributed, the reactor 
performance will decrease, due to dry areas and/or areas with too high liquid flow for optimal mass transfer. In 
larger reactors, it might be necessary to redistribute the liquid at some point in the reactor. As the liquid flows 
down through the reactor some paths might emerge, leaving part of the reactor dry. This can have a similar effect 
as insufficient distribution in the top, thus making it favourable to redistribute the liquid [18 - Chapter 1]. One 
issue with TBCs is controlling the flow regime within the reactor. Additionally, the flow regime within the reactor 
might also change depending on the position within the reactor. Part of this is often due to the randomness of 
the packing material. This gives it a non-uniform distribution within the column, which affects the liquid flow. 
[18 - Chapter 1]. The complex flow regimes within a TBC are often simplified into four patterns but are often a 
combination. 
Figure 8.1 shows the principle of the four different regimes (trickle-, pulse-, spray- and bubble regime). The flow 
regime which exists within the TBC is primarily dependent on the gas and liquid flow but column dimensions and 
the properties of the packing material (such as size and shape) also affects this. This makes it difficult to estimate 
which flow regimes exist in the TBC at different liquid flowrates, without empirically examining the configuration 
[18 - Chapter 2].

The trickle flow regime is normally associated with low liquid- and moderate gas flowrates. Giving a small 
interaction between the phases. In this regime, the liquid flows as a thin film over the solid phase. The trickle 
flow regime is associated with a relatively low pressure drop and low liquid/gas capacity due to the low flowrates. 
The heat and mass transfer at the trickle flow regime is lower, compared to other flow regimes. The lower mass 
and heat transfer can in large part be attributed to an incomplete wetting of the solid phase. This leaves part of 
the solid phase dry and unable to facilitate interaction between the liquid and gas phase [18 - Chapter 2].  
Pulse flow regime occurs under moderate gas and liquid flows, this increases the interaction between the phases 
compared with trickle flow. Under pulse flow, the formation of alternating gas/liquid enriched zones occurs due 
to blockage of local flow paths for the gas. In these zones, due to the high wetting of the solid phase, the area 
for mass and heat transfer is larger thus giving a higher transfer. However, because of this, the pressure drop in 
the column is also higher, compared to trickle flow [18 - Chapter 2]. Most industrial trickle flow reactors are 
operated in the border region between the trickle- and pulse flow regime [18 - Chapter 2]. 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
8. Theory - Trickle bed columns 
[17] 
 
The spray regime emerges at a low liquid flowrate and high gas flowrate. Under these conditions, the column is 
filled with gas and only small droplets of liquid appear. This gives low liquid holdup since the liquid can move 
freely and is not limited by liquid blockages. The spray regime is favourable when the gas phase is the limiting 
element for mass transfer. However, due to the low amount of liquid, it might get saturated quickly.  Another 
issue is that the solid phase will not be completely wetted due to the limited amount of liquid in the system [18 
- Chapter 2]. 
In the bubbling flow regime, most of the column is filled with liquid, and the gas is dispersed through the column 
as bubbles. Operating the column this way leads to a high pressure drop and high liquid holdup times. This is an 
issue since it can cause back mixing. On the positive side in the regime the wetting of the solid phase is complete. 
This results in relatively high heat and mass transfer rates and is suitable for operations where the liquid phase 
is limiting the mass and heat transfer [18 - Chapter 2]. 
The gas flow is normally co-current in industrial setups, this means the gas and liquid both flow in the same 
direction. For trickle bed reactors this is in the gravitational direction. Only in some specific cases are counter-
current operation used. If counter-current gas flow is used, the gas will enter the bottom of the column and exit 
in the top [18 - Chapter 2 and 3]. The advantage of counter-current over co-current is its ability to circumvent 
the equilibrium limitations in reactions such as hydrocracking [19].  
When the flows are in the opposite direction of one another, concentrations will be low in the top and high in 
the bottom of the column for both the gas and liquid phase. Because the concentration is at its highest in both 
the gas and liquid phase in the bottom some mass transfer can still occur from the gas to the liquid phase. During 
co-current flow, the gas phase has a lower concentration in bottom, but the liquid phase still has a high 
concentration, making mass transfer more difficult. However, during counter-current, the risk of flooding the 
column is far higher, compared to co-current, which is why it is rarely used [20]. 
Trickle bed columns are not the only three-phase reactors. Several other types of reactors have also been 
developed, each with different advantages and disadvantages. Some examples of other reactors are stirred-, 
bubble column- and gas-lift reactors.  
Stirred tank reactors are commonly used in industrial-scale biological fermentations. Depending on the 
construction they can be run both as batch and continues, such as a chemostat reactor. They are composed of a 
large vessel or tank, equipped with an impeller to ensure mixing, and a sparger at the bottom to increase the 
mass transfer of the gas. The concept is distributing gas by the sparger and the impeller increases the distribution 
and decreases the bubble size [21]. Stirred tanks reactors have good mixing capabilities, scale-up characteristics 
and many degrees of freedom (such as impeller type, speed, gas flow rate and the dimensions of the tank) [22], 
[18 - Chapter 1], [23]. However, the mass transfer between the gas and liquid phase is low when fermenting 
syngas. The mass transfer is often increased by an increase in impeller speed, this breaks and decreases the 
bubble size giving a larger surface area for the mass transfer. The limitation of increasing the impeller speed is 
the increase is power consumption, which can be quite high on a large scale. Increasing the impeller speed also 
increases the risk of damaging and breaking the biomass. For these reasons, this is often not economically 
feasible to use stirred tank reactors for syngas fermentation [22], [23].

[18] 
 
Another alternative for syngas fermentation is bubble column reactors. A bubble column reactor consists of a 
large tank filled with liquid a gas inlet or sparger at the bottom. The bubble column reactors can contain packing 
material, but it is not necessary. Bubble column reactors have no mechanical mixing, instead, the mixing is done 
by the gas flow which requires less energy. Airlift reactors work by the same principle as bubble column reactors. 
However, gas is confined to the centre of the reactor giving a different flow pattern of the liquid [21]. The 
advantages of these types of reactors are the ability to manage high solid concentrations and their high 
mass/heat transfer rates. The mass transfer of these systems, dependent upon the bubble size. The disadvantage 
is the high back mixing which occurs in these reactors. The back mixing will often decrease the conversion and 
increase the formation of by-products. Also, a high gas flow is required, since operation with a low flow increases 
the risk of insufficient mixing [18 - Chapter 1], [23].  
 
8.1.1 
Trickle bed reactors for biomethanation 
The principle of using a TBC for biomethanation is biological growth and in the form of biofilm on the packing 
material. Here the biomass is immobilized, making them conceptually equivalent to a catalyst. The 
immobilization of the microbes also decreases the risk of washout, which can be an issue in e.g. unpacked bubble 
column reactors [23]. Previous research has also shown significantly better productivity in TBCs compared to 
bubble column reactors, which has been attributed to the immobilization of the microbes [24]. Washout can be 
a large issue since the microbes are anaerobic and thus grow slower compared to aerobic microbes [23]. The 
syngas used is as the carbon source for the microorganisms, and the liquid contains other necessary nutrients 
and is recycled. Traditionally, one of the main issues with syngas fermentation is the low mass transfer rate of H2 
and CO caused by the low solubility of the gas compounds in the water-based medium [23]. However, the 
concept of using a TBC is to decrease the diffusion path of H2, CO and CO2 due to the thin liquid film over the 
packing material. The shorter diffusion path increases the driving force giving a better mass transfer, as gas 
species are quickly consumed thus not saturating the liquid phase. A thicker liquid film will, for this reason, result 
in a decreased mass transfer compared to a thin film [25], thus suggesting a thin film is advantageous. One 
limitation of syngas fermentation is that CO and H2 have different solubility and diffusion coefficient [9]. The 
different solubility and diffusion coefficient can lead to un-optimal stoichiometry between CO and H2 in the liquid 
phase, despite being optimal in the gas phase. 
 
8.2 Production of SYN gas 
Syngas is produced through gasification, which is a thermochemical transformation of solid or solid-like material 
into gas. Gasification is done by reacting the biomass at high temperatures often with steam, air or oxygen. The 
total stoichiometric amount of oxygen available for the reaction must be less than what is required for full 
combustion, if not everything will be combusted, in which case no syngas will be produced [10]. The advantage 
of using oxygen compared to air is that no nitrogen is added. The nitrogen is inert and therefore dilutes the gas 
which reduces the CV, making this undesirable [26].

8.2.1 
Drying 
Drying is the initial step in the gasification process, where most of the water is removed from the biomass. Having 
dry biomass is a necessity for the pyrolysis process to take place. The amount of heat required for this part of 
the drying process is directly proportional to the moisture content of the biomass. Having relative dry biomass, 
therefore, reduces the energy needed for this step [27]. The heat required for this part of the process can often 
be generated from partial oxidation, which is the exothermic part of the gasification. The drying process takes 
place until the biomass is approximately 120-150°C, at this temperature most of the water should have 
evaporated [3], [17], [27].

8.3 Syngas biomethanation 
8.3.1 
Biomethanation 
Biomethanation is the biological conversion of a substrate into CH4 and can be done by a mixed microbial 
consortium (MMC). The leading technology within biomethanation is anaerobic digestion (see section 8.4.1). 
However, it has a low conversion efficiency because some compounds like lignocellulosic biomass are difficult 
for the micro-organisms to digest. By first gasifying the biomass to syngas, the biomass is converted to a substrate 
that is easy to digest for some organisms. This increases the overall conversion efficiency because more of the 
material will be converted [23]. Converting the syngas into CH4 increases CV of the gas and removes toxic CO, 
making it easier to use and store the gas. However, converting the syngas to CH4 is only viable if the syngas is not 
utilized immediately after production, because energy is used for the conversion decreasing the overall energy 
yield [32]. 
Biomethanation of syngas requires different microbes within the consortium. Giving the possibility of multiple 
conversion pathways of the syngas into CH4 [23]. Some of the microbes within the consortium do not produce 
CH4. Instead, they produce various compounds such as volatile fatty acids (VFA). VFAs can be used by other 
microbes in the consortium to e.g. produce CH4, creating a symbiotic relationship between the different species 
of microbes. The use of MMC is different from the more common fermentation approach, which consists of only 
one microorganism. The disadvantage of using an MMC for fermentation is the difference in optimum conditions 
for the microbes. Because each microbe has a different optimal condition, optimal conditions cannot be achieved 
for all the organisms simultaneously, thus some will work under sub-optimal conditions. The conditions include 
parameters such as gas composition, temperature, pressure and pH [23]. The conditions affect which pathways 
are most favourable for CH4 production and can thus affect the conversion, reaction rate, by-product formation 
etc [33]. As an example, the formation of CH4 under mesophilic (30-40°C) conditions will primarily be through 
the formation of acetate. Whereas under thermophilic (50-65°C) conditions, CO will be converted to H2 and CO2 
(Equation 8.20), which is then converted into CH4 (Equation 8.16/Equation 8.17) [33]. 
The concept of using biomethanation for the conversion of syngas in CH4 is using CO and CO2 from the syngas, 
the sole carbon source for the MMC and only nutrients must be added. Oxygen is toxic to the microbes in the 
MMC, so the process is anaerobic. In the MMC, the microbes responsible for the biomethanation can be 
categorised into of four groups; acetogens, methanogens, sulfate-reducers and hydrogenogens [23]. In the 
consortium, CH4 can be produced directly from the conversion of CO by carboxydotrophic methanogens or 
H2/CO2 by hydrogenotrophic methanogens. Another option is that the syngas is converted into methanogenic 
precursors like acetate and formate, which are then subsequently converted into CH4 through a variety of 
reactions [23]. The methanogens can only use a few substrates such as CO2, methyl groups and acetate. 
Therefore, larger compounds such as VFAs must first be processed into some of the smaller compounds before 
the methanogens can use the carbon [34]. CO can also be directly used as a substrate but only by two species of 
methanogens [34]. The overall pathways from CO and H2/CO2 can be seen in Figure 8.4.

Carboxydotrophic methanation 
The other option for directly converting the syngas to CH4 is through carboxydotrophic methanation. This is done 
with the enzymes CODH and hydrogenase, and CO acts as an electron donor [38]. The overall reaction to produce 
CH4 is shown in Equation 8.18. Initially, all four moles of CO are oxidized into CO2, then one mole of CO2 is reduced 
to CH4 using hydrogen. Hydrogen is an intermediate product in this process, meaning it is available for the 
reduction of CO2 [34].

pathways are more dominant [33]. Both M. barkeri and M thermoautotrophicus grow slowly on CO and their 
growth is limited at high levels of CO [39 Chapter 3.5].

8.3.10 Advantages and disadvantages of syngas fermentation 
The gasification before prior to the fermentation is advantageous because many types of biomass can be used if 
they are dry enough. This is possible is due to the combination of thermochemical and biochemical conversion. 
Syngas does not contain any complex substrates such as long carbon chains and can thus be consumed more 
easily [41], [23]. Whereas using e.g. lignocellulosic biomass from trees, extensive pre-treatment is required to 
break down the structure, to make it easier to digest for the microbes [25], [23]. The pre-treatment is often 
==PAGE:31==
8. Theory - Competing technologies 
[30] 
 
expensive. By first gasifying the biomass and subsequently fermenting it, this can be circumvented. The easy 
access to the carbon from the syngas gives a high chemical efficiency for the methanation. As an example, the 
chemical efficiency of wood gasification followed by methanation can be up to 65%. For anaerobic digestion, this 
is only 20-41% [5]. The difference in chemical efficiency can in large part be contributed to the easy access to the 
carbon source [42]. 
Using biomass to convert syngas into CH4 has some advantages compared to the traditional way of using a 
catalyst. The biomass used for the conversion is cheaper compared to the catalyst and has a higher tolerance to 
impurities, which often causes deactivation of catalysts. Also, the operation conditions for the fermentation are 
milder, meaning the operation can be done at a lower temperature and pressure [23], [43]. The microbial 
“catalysts” also have the advantage of being more flexible with respect to the H2/CO ratio compared to the 
chemical catalysts. The flexibility of the biomass reduces the need for cleaning and optimizing the gas before 
use, thus reducing operational costs [23].  The selectivity of the microbes is also much higher than their chemical 
counterpart. Allows for higher conversion of the available carbon (assuming enough H2) because it is not limited 
by a thermodynamical equilibrium. Giving syngas biomethanation the possibility of being an overall more 
environmentally friendly process compared with the chemical catalysed process [43], [23].  
The primary limiting parameter of syngas biomethanation is the low solubility and mass transfer of both H2 and 
CO. The low solubility gives limiting the amount of substrate for the microbes, thus low cell growth and 
concentration. The microbes used in the biomethanation process are strictly anaerobic, which are associated 
with longer growth rate and lower productivity. Making it difficult to produce high volumetric flows of CH4 [23].  
 
8.4 Competing technologies 
8.4.1 
Anaerobic digestion 
Currently, biogas in Denmark is primarily being produced through anaerobic digestion of organic material such 
as waste or manure. It is used in several applications such as the production of electricity or heat in industrial 
processes. The biogas can, after cleaning and/or upgrading be supplied into the gas grid [15]. The main source of 
material for this process comes from the agricultural sector in the form of manure.  In short, anaerobic digestion 
is a biological process where biomass is broken down by bacteria in an oxygen- or nearly oxygen-free 
environment. One of the products from this process is CH4 [44]. Biogas can be produced from most kinds of 
biomasses by using anaerobic digestion. By utilizing waste products such as animal manure and slurries, it is 
possible to reduce the amount of GHG produced by e.g. livestock [45]. The biogas produced through anaerobic 
digestion approximately contains 60-70% methane and 30-40% CO2 depending on operational conditions [2]. 
The process can be done under various conditions such as psychrophilic (10-30°C), mesophilic (30-40°C) and 
thermophilic (50-60°C) conditions, all with different advantages and disadvantages [46]. 
Biogas production through anaerobic digestion can be classified into 4 steps. The steps are hydrolysis, 
acidogenesis, acetogenesis and methanogenesis. Acetogenesis is not always considered as a separate step, but 
occasionally as a part of acidogenesis. Figure 8.5 shows an overview of anaerobic digestion [46]. 
==PAGE:32==

[31] 
 
 
Figure 8.5 – Overview of anaerobic digestion – Figure taken from Zhang et al. (2014) [46] 
The first step is hydrolysis and is normally considered the rate-limiting step [46]. For this reason, chemical 
compounds are often used in this step to reduce digestion time, which also results in a higher CH4 yield [44]. 
During hydrolysis, insoluble organic matter is broken down into smaller molecules. Lipids are converted to fatty 
acids, polysaccharides such as cellulose are broken down to monosaccharides, the proteins are converted to 
amino acids and the nucleic acids are converted to purines and pyrimidines [44].  
In the second stage, acidogenesis, acetogenic bacteria converts the fatty acids, monomers and amino acids into 
simple organic acids such as acetic acid, ethanol, propionic acid, butyric acid and other VFA’s [44].  
The third stage consists of breaking down the VFA’s formed in the second step into acetate, H2 and CO2, which is 
converted into CH4 in the fourth step. However, it is possible for several of the previous substrates to also be 
directly converted into CH4 by some methanogens [46]. The reactions taking place in this process are similar to 
the reactions taking place when fermenting syngas, which is explained in more detail in section 8.3. 
The gas produced through this process besides CO2 also contains several trace gasses such as H2, hydrogen 
sulphide, and ammonia. These gases can both inhibit the anaerobic growth but also cause corrosion in pipelines 
and the plant [44]. Another issue is the low chemical efficiency which is only between 20-40% [5]. However, the 
low chemical conversion is compensated by the low energy consumption of the process. The energy 
consumption is low due to the process taking place in large tanks with limited mixing. The low energy 
consumption of the process is the reason it is still feasible even with low chemical efficiency. 
 
8.4.2 
Fischer–Tropsch synthesis 
Syngas along with natural gas, oil and coal can be used to produce hydrocarbon products through the Fischer-
Tropsch synthesis (FTS). Products from FTS are often liquids, this is an advantage as the product can substitute 
diesel and petroleum. The liquid hydrocarbons produced in FTS are normally produced in three steps. Feed to 
syngas, then syngas to syncrude (synthetic crude oil) and finally syncrude to the refined product. The FTS covers 
the syngas to syncrude part of the process [32]. The feed to syngas is covered in section 8.2. The syncrude to 
product part of the process upgrades the product from the FTS by e.g. removing light compounds and

be required if directly combusted [6]. If the syngas used is derived from waste and/or biomass it is possible to

The operating conditions for FTS are normally pressures between 15 and 40 bars [6], [47] and temperatures from

The composition of the product from the FTS is not only dependent on the operational conditions, but also the 
syngas composition and the catalyst. Thus, by changing one or more of these it is possible to control the products 
and ratio between them. Generally, the product selectivity follows the thermodynamic order for what is most 
favourable (alkanes > alkenes > alcohols) [32]. The most commonly used catalysts are cobalt and iron. The iron 
catalyst has an advantage at low H2/CO ratios because it increases the WGS reaction, but it has a short lifetime. 
The cobalt catalyst gives a higher conversion rate and gives more linearly chained hydrocarbons but requires a 
higher quality of syngas [47], [6].  
Generally, the stoichiometric ratio of H2/CO should be approximately 2 because the main component of the 
hydrocarbons is –CH2–. However, as mentioned when using an iron catalyst this ratio can be as low as 0.7 [47]. 
In the case of a low ratio and an iron catalyst, some of the excess CO reacts with water which yields CO2 and H2 
increasing the H2/CO ratio. At too high ratios some H2 will remain unreacted.  
Like every other process, this FTS also has some drawbacks. One of the main challenges is the effort to avoid 
deactivating the catalysts. The catalysts are highly sensitive to impurities such as sulphur, which are often present 
in syngas. Another limitation is the fixed H2/CO ratio required when e.g. using a Co catalyst. Keeping this specific 
ratio of H2/CO is estimated to account for around 60 - 70% of the operational cost making it a significant expense 
[49]. The energy efficiency of using biomass for FTS is estimated to be around 33 - 50% [6]. Also, the catalysts are 
not very selective, which can result in different by-products that must be removed afterwards [49].

10.1 Operating parameters 
10.1.1 Hydraulic retention time 
The HRT is a parameter describing the time it takes to replace all the liquid in the system. A high HRT will decrease 
the nutrients available for the microbes since less medium will be added to the system. Over time a high HRT will 
also cause more VFAs to accumulate in the system as these are not washed out and can inhibit the microbes. A 
lower HRT will increase the nutrients; yet, this might wash out more of the microbes.  
The HRT is calculated based on the amount of replaced liquid and the total volume of the system. It is assumed 
that the liquid level in the system is always constant at 4.11 L for the pilot plant and 0.17 L in the laboratory scale. 
The pumps adding medium to the systems are controlled by a timer activating the pump at specified times for a 
specified duration. If a different HRT is desired the number of activations or the length of the activation can be 
changed, changing how much medium is added (Qmedium) this is also shown in Equation 10.1. Another option is 
to change the speed of the peristaltic pump (vpump) as this would also change Qmedium.

The recirculation ensures that the medium and thus the nutrients are supplied to the biofilm. A higher QR 
increases the liquid available for mass transfer and nutrients available for the biofilm. The downside to a high QR 
is that the mechanical shear stress from the liquid on the biofilm, which can break or damage the biofilm. Another

described in section 8.1.  
An economic consideration regarding the QR is the power consumption of the recirculation pump, which 
increases with the QR. Considering this, the increase in revenue achieved by increasing the QR must be higher 
than the expense for the pump. If it is not, then increasing the QR is not feasible.

10.1.4 Gas composition and pressure 
Increase in the pressure presses more gas into the liquid phase, thus increasing the mass transfer. Despite this, 
the pressure has been known to decrease methane yields when increased from 1 to 2.5 atm when using syngas, 
presumably due to an increase in the partial pressure of CO [23]. However, studies only using CO2 and H2 have 
reported increasing efficiency by increasing the pressure up to 9 bar, meaning the optimal pressure is dependent 
on the gas composition [51]. Thus, for syngas with low CO content higher total pressures could increase reactor 
performance. 
The effect of the partial pressure was not assessed on this system, so the effect is not known. However, it is 
reasonable, to assume that the partial pressure will have a similar effect on the TBC biomethanation, as on other 
processes containing similar microbes. As an example, Sim et al. [52] reported the highest cell growth at a partial 
pressure of CO at 2.02 atm, not the highest consumption or product formation. Instead, the highest product 
formation (acetic acid) and CO consumption were observed at a partial pressure of 1.55 atm. They attributed 
this to cell lysis at high CO concentrations. Regarding biomethanation increasing the partial pressure of CO has a 
partial inhibitory effect. The methane production rate has been reported to be optimal at a CO partial pressure 
of 0.2 atm, under mesophilic conditions [53], [23]. 
Like CO, the partial pressure of H2 also impacts the MMC. The effect of H2 and CO2 on the microbes is however 
lower. This is probably because it is not toxic, thus does not inhibit growth at higher partial pressures. It can, 
however, change the composition of microbes in the consortium. At higher partial pressures of H2 a reduction of 
the hydrogenase enzyme has been reported, probably due to saturation of the enzyme. This makes it likely that 
the hydrogenotrophic methanogens in the MMC also gives lower conversions at high H2 partial pressures [23].  
Regarding the inhibitory effects of some of the gas species on some of the microbes, the TBC has an advantage. 
Because the gas travels through the column and is continuously consumed the partial pressures of the gas species 
changes throughout the column. This allows microbes less inhibited by CO or H2 to thrive in the top of the column 
reducing the partial pressure of these. The more affected microbes can then grow further down the column, 
where the partial pressure is lower.  
Another important parameter of the gas is the content of impurities. Impurities such as tars, NOx, HCN, H2S, and 
NH3 are often found in industrial syngas. Some of these impurities have been found to inhibit some of the 
enzymes in acetogenic bacteria [23]. The limited research in on the impurities does show, that at lower impurity 
concentrations most of the microbes are unaffected. Acetoclastic methanogens appear to be the most sensitive 
group but at high concentrations, all the microbes are affected. Reducing the impurity content to an acceptable 
level is therefore required to ensure that MMC works optimally. 
 
10.1.5 pH 
The pH influences the metabolism and bioenergetics of the microbes by changing the electrochemical gradient 
over the cell membrane, changing the favoured pathway. The acetogenic microbes can grow at both acidic and 
alkaline conditions and are usually more resistant. Hydrogenogenic generally prefers a neutral pH of around 7. 
The optimum pH for the methanogens has been reported to be between 6.8 and 8.5 [34]. Despite the acetogenic

[42] 
 
microbes’ capabilities to grow at a large pH interval, the pH still affects the pathway used [23]. To favour the 
more vulnerable microbes, the pH in biomethanation processes is normally kept between 7 and 7.6. This is not 
the optimal condition for most of the microbes but is a compromise between the different acceptable conditions. 
The production of acetate is also increased at lower pH, but the consumption of substrate is normally unaltered. 
Operating at lower pH is therefore associated higher production of acetate and other VFAs. Even at neutral pH 
VFAs are produced, dictating that some form of pH control is necessary to keep this process parameter within 
the desired value [23]. In the laboratory scale reactor, the pH was controlled by a high buffering capacity of the 
medium, whereas in the pilot reactor used the pH control system. 
 
10.1.6 Temperature 
The temperature has a large influence on the MMC as it influences, the pathway for CH4 production. At 
mesophilic conditions, the production of CH4 is favoured through acetate production, which is subsequently 
converted into CH4. Under thermophilic conditions, it is more likely that the production of CH4 is through the 
conversion of H2. Conrad R. et al. 1990 [54] suggested that this could also be a result of the H2 producing reaction, 
becoming more exergonic at higher temperatures.  
A comparison was done by Guiot et all. 2011 [56] finding a productivity of 5.6 mmol/g VSS (volatile suspended 
solid)/day at 60°C. Whereas under mesophilic conditions (35°C) the reported productivity was only 1 mmol/g 
VSS/d. Despite them not using a TBC, the effect of the temperature is likely to be the same since similar microbes 
are used in this thesis favouring higher temperature.  
Further Lemmer and Ullrich (2018) [55] reported an increase in CH4 content in the outlet gas by increasing the 
temperature of a TBC from 40°C to 55°C. This increased the CH4 from 88.29 vol% to 94.99 vol% using CO2 and H2, 
meaning an increase in temperature when using TBR could increase reactor performance. The reason for the 
better performance at higher temperatures was attributed to the faster growth rate of the thermophilic 
methanogens compared with the mesophilic. The increase in growth rate is 2-3 times higher for the thermophilic 
methanogens [55]. Additionally, because of the higher metabolism, the mass transfer is also increased because 
lower concentrations will be present in the liquid due to consumption, increasing the difference in concentration 
[55]. Further increases in temperature might be advantageous in the Synferon reactor as Guneratnam et al. 
(2017) [57] reported an increase in productivity in an ex-situ batch setup by increasing the temperature from 
55°C to 65°C. 
Increasing the temperature lowers the solubility of the gas, adding to the already limiting factor of mass transfer 
between the two phases [23]. In this thesis, the temperature was kept at 60°C and the effect was not examined. 
This was done because experiments prior to this thesis had been performed at 60°C and 37°C (only laboratory 
reactor), thus 60°C was selected as the operational temperature.

11.1 Chromatography 
Chromatography is a method used to separate different molecules. The basic principle for all chromatographic 
methods is, that the substance analysed is suspended, in a mobile phase and retained by the stationary phase. 
The sample analysed passes through the stationary phase at different speeds. This difference in retention time, 
in the column, is then detected and enables the classification of the different compounds. The best separation is 
usually best achieved close to equilibrium conditions for the species analysed [58]. 
 
11.1.1 Gas chromatography overview (GC) 
Gas chromatography (GC) is used to determine the gas composition and uses an inert gas as a carrier to transport 
the sample so it can be analysed. The most common gasses used for this is hydrogen, nitrogen, argon and helium. 
However, gases such as helium or argon can also be used. The properties of the carrier gas can affect the results, 
the carrier gas should, therefore, be chosen depending on the properties of the gas/liquid examined [58]. The 
basic principle for a GC is shown in Figure 11.1. 
 
Figure 11.1 – Principle of GC – Figure reused from [59] 
As shown in Figure 11.1 GC’s are having an inlet where the gas/liquid sample is injected. If the samples are liquid 
the GC needs to be equipped with a vaporizer so the sample can be carried as a gas. The sample is then 
transported by the carrier gas to the column, where it is separated. The separation is dependent upon the 
properties of the column and the sample. The columns are normally grouped into packed and capillary columns 
[58]. The packed columns are filled with small coated particles and the capillary columns have several small 
tubes, which are coated on the inside. The coating is used as the stationary phase. After the separation, the gas 
is carried through a detector, which then detects the different gasses and different amounts. Several different 
types of detectors are available depending on sensitivity (lowest amount detectable) and selectivity (separation

[44] 
 
of strong responses). The data from the detector is then used to make the chromatogram, which gives the 
composition of the gas/liquid in the sample [58].  
The different species elute at different times from the columns, giving different peaks corresponding to the 
different species in the gas mixture. The amount of each compound can then be found by either using the height 
or width of the peaks [58] using calibration curves (see section 19.1.1). 
The system used for this thesis was an SRI 8610C Gas Chromatograph, using helium as a carrier gas. Two packed 
columns were used one 6’x1/8” Molsieve 13x column and a 6’x1/8” silica gel column. The columns were 
connected in series. The column temperature at the measurements was 3 minutes at 65°C, followed by an 
increase of 10°C pr. minute until 95°C was reached. Afterwards, the temperature increase was 24°C pr. minute 
up to 140°C.  
The GC was connected to a PC running Peaksimple, which could determine the molar fractions of the gas, in the 
form of peaks. Peaksimple can do this based on the height or area of the peaks. In this thesis, the area was used 
for the calibration curves. The GC was set up to detect peaks for H2, O2, N2, CH4, CO and CO2. In the case of both 
oxygen and nitrogen, it distinguished between remnants from the loop and what was from the syringe. This 
created two peaks for both nitrogen and oxygen. The peaks from the loop were disregarded, however, the 
oxygen peak from the syringe was used to calculate the amount of nitrogen that came from the air. This was 
necessary to calculate the mass balance based on nitrogen as explained in section 12.2.1. The calibration curves 
and the integration of the curves is explained in Appendix C section 19.1.1 and 19.1.2. 
 
11.1.2 Gas sampling 
The sampling from both the pilot- and laboratory scale was done from sampling ports (see section 9.2 and 9.3). 
The syringe was flushed a minimum of 5 times while in the sampling port to ensure all the gas had been 
exchanged with gas from the reactor. The sample size was approximately 0.1 mL, but because the samples were 
taken manually, small variations did occur. After taking the sample, it was injected as quickly as possible into the 
GC. The time between the sample and injection varied since the setups and the GC was initially placed in different 
buildings, thus increasing oxygen in the early samples. For the latter samples, the GC was in the same building, 
thus decreasing the oxygen. 
 
11.1.3 High pressure liquid chromatography overview (HPLC) 
The carrier in HPLC is a liquid and it operates under high pressure, it must be inert to the sample and the coating 
in the column [58]. The high pressure in the column is necessary to push the liquid through the stationary phase, 
HPLC has a high resolution allowing one to separate similar compounds [60]. 
==PAGE:46==
 
 
11. Analytical methods - Chromatography 
[45] 
 
 
Figure 11.2 – Principle of an HPLC – figure reused from [61] 
An overview of how an HPLC device works is shown in Figure 11.2. The solvent used has a high impact on the 
HPLC column and will depend on the separation type used and the sample. Due to the sensitivity of the column, 
it is important that the solvent contains no particles, and has no bobbles [60]. 
The pumps function is to transport the solvent to the injector, and later through the column while maintaining 
high pressure. The function of the injector is to deliver the sample to the solvent, it should ensure the sample 
reaches the solvent as tiny droplets [60]. Several methods of injecting the samples are used in HPLC, the simplest 
ones are on-column and stop-flow injectors, where the sample is delivered through a syringe. The difference 
between the two is the stop-flow injector, which stops the pump before the sample is added [60]. The other type 
of injector is valve injectors. In valve injectors, the sample is delivered to a pressurized column with no 
interruptions in the flow. The sample is then deposited into an external loop when the valve is turned, the sample 
is transferred into the mobile phase and passes through the column [60]. This means the sample deployed in the 
column is always the same, which gives a high reproducibility, this type of system can also be automated [60]. 
The species in the sample eluates at different speeds from the column, reaching the detector separately. Several 
types of detectors are used but the most common once are refractive index and photometric detectors [60].  
HPLC is a versatile measurement technique, which can detect many types of compounds such as carbohydrates, 
acids, amino acids, peptides, and proteins among others. This can be done because many of the components in 
the HPLC can be selected so it fits the purpose (such as solvent, column and the detector) [60].   
 
11.1.4 Operation 
The HCPL used was of the brand Shimadzu and equipped with an Aminex HPX-87H column as a stationary phase. 
The mobile phase used for the system was 12 millimolar H2SO4 at a flow rate of 0.6 mL/min. The column was 
operated at 63°C. Master students at DTU are not allowed to use the HPLC, meaning the measurements 
themselves were done by either a PhD student or research assistant. The samples were prepared from the liquid 
samples also used for OD and pH measurements. The samples were prepared by taking 1.5 mL of the liquid, then 
transferring it to either a 2- or 1.5 mL Eppendorf tube. The Eppendorf tube was then centrifuged for 10 minutes 
at 10,000 RPM, to remove most of the cell debris. After centrifugation, 970 microliters were transferred to a 
clean Eppendorf tube and mixed with 30 microliters 10% w/w H2SO4. Using a syringe, the sample was transferred 
through a filter to the HPLC vial. The filtering ensured that no remnant cell debris entered the HPLC. Afterwards, 
the HPLC samples were stored in a freezer until they were analysed.

11.2 Optical density (OD) 
The optical density (OD) was used as a proxy for the number of cells in the pilot reactor, and in the effluent for 
the laboratory scale. The general principle behind measuring OD is that light passes through the sample at a 
specific wavelength. Some of the light is slowed down by the sample, thus a difference is measured. The slower 
the light travels through the sample the higher the OD. The wavelength used was 600 nm, which is a common 
wavelength for determining biological material. The spectrophotometer used was a DR 3900 made by Hach 
Lange. Before measuring the sample, the spectrophotometer was zeroed empty. The measured OD thus included 
the cuvette, medium, and cells. It would have been possible to either zero the spectrophotometer with either 
medium or water in a cuvette. This was not done since the wavelength primarily determines biomass, thus 
zeroing with water or medium would not significantly impact the measurements. Also, the primary interest in 
the OD was for comparison between different conditions and the reactors. Making the interesting parameter 
the difference in OD and not the exact value. The precision of the spectrophotometer decreased if the OD was 
above 0.7. To ensure that measurements were taken in the correct range, both an undiluted and a diluted OD 
were measured. The diluted OD was diluted in the ratio 1:5 and is denoted OD5. For the undiluted sample, 0.9 
mL was transferred to a cuvette and measured. The diluted sample was made by mixing 0.8 mL water with 0.2 
mL sample in an Eppendorf tube, before transferring the 1 mL to a cuvette and measuring it. The cuvettes used 
were made from polystyrene and had a volume of 1.5 mL. 
The pH was measured by a pH-probe attached to a PHM210 pH meter from MeterLab (Radiometer). The pH was 
usually measured first to ensure enough liquid in the flacon tube to submerge the probe. Before measuring, the 
probe was cleaned with water and dried off with paper. The measurement was taken directly in the falcon tube 
containing the sample. The pH for the pilot reactor was continuously measured by the probe in the system. 
However, to check and compare with the laboratory reactor the pH was also measured in the sample by the 
same probe at the laboratory reactor sample. 
A significant difference was detected between the probe in the pilot reactor and the probe in the lab. This was 
despite that both probes had been calibrated and were both accurate. This difference in pH between the probes 
was primarily attributed to the difference in temperature. The probe in the setup measured the pH at 60°C, 
whereas the sample was measured at room temperature (approximately 20°C). This difference in temperature 
could affect the probes, especially the pilot plant probe since it was calibrated at 20°C. 
 
11.2.1 Liquid sampling  
Liquid samples were taken from both the pilot- and laboratory reactors, in order to analyse the HPLC, check the 
OD and pH. The samples were normally taken at approximately 9:30, however, deviations did occur. The 
laboratory reactor added and removed liquid at this time. Thus, sampling at this time did not interfere with the 
HRT and the samples could be directly taken from the effluent exiting. The liquid sampling from the pilot reactor 
was taken from the liquid sampling port since the mechanism for removal of liquid was a level controller. The 
samples from the pilot reactor were still taken at approximately 9:30 so the liquid samples could be analysed at 
the same time. However, the samples taken at other times did not interfere with the HRT such as for the 
laboratory reactor.

11.3 Definition of steady state 
The steady-state definition used for this thesis was not a true steady state. A system is normally in a steady state 
when parameter within the system is completely constant, in all phases. To achieve this, parameters should 
normally have been unchanged in at least three cycles. For the liquid state, this means the steady state is 
achieved after three HRT’s. Given the default, HRT used in the system is 8 days it would take 24 days to get a 
steady state. For the gas phase, this criterion was easier to achieve since the highest EBRT was approximately 6 
hours meaning the steady state could be achieved after 18 hours max. 
Instead of waiting for a true steady state a quasi-steady state was defined. Because the primary interest was the 
gas phase, the defined steady state was based on the gas phase. The definition used was: Over the course of a 
minimum of 3 days, the maximum and minimum value measured in the gas composition, must not deviate 
more than 1%, as an absolute value. The composition is measured in percent, as a result, the absolute value is 
also a percent.  
No steady state was defined for the liquid phase. The decision was made due to the vast number of parameters 
affecting the liquid phase, and that change in e.g. OD and pH did not seem to significantly affect the gas 
composition. The pH would probably affect the gas phase if it had variated more, but it was relatively stable and 
did not change more than 0.5 for all the experiments. The steady state for the liquid phase also depends on how 
fresh the medium was, meaning it was constantly changing. Besides this a constant concentration of all the VFA’s, 
the OD and pH all had to be stable to have a steady state in the liquid phase. However, OD, pH and the 
concentrations of VFA were monitored, meaning large deviations in the state of the liquid were known. Given 
large deviations, in the liquid state, the duration of the measurement was extended to ensure this did not affect 
the gas phase.

12. Calculations 
This section explains the necessary parameters they were calculated in the thesis. The result of these calculations 
is presented as results. Many of the calculations are also based on assumptions, these assumptions are also 
explained in this section. The calculations themselves can be found in Appendix D under “Calculations” or “DCG”. 
12.1 Assumptions 
12.1.1 Steady state 
The calculations are done assuming a steady state. This means system parameters does not change, though small 
oscillations are tolerated. This assumption is based on the operational time of the system, meaning the system 
is left with specific conditions for a period until the gas exit composition and temperature is stable, pH and OD is 
also considered but not required. This assumption allows for other assumptions and some simplified equations 
such as the mass balance. The assumption can be considered valid, because, the system is left with the same 
operating conditions until most parameters in the system are stable, indicating very little or no change. The 
definition of steady state used in this thesis is presented in section 11.3. 
 
12.1.2 The liquid is saturated with gas 
Throughout the calculations, it is also assumed that the liquid is always saturated with the gas. This means that 
no extra gas will be dissolved in the liquid. Thus, the components from the gas do not exit the system with the 
effluent since the gas is already saturated and cannot dissolve more of the components. This is reasonable to 
assume since the liquid, for the most part, has a relatively high HRT meaning only a little new liquid must be 
saturated. Both temperature and pressure support this as the pressure is low and the temperature is slightly 
elevated, both reducing the saturation point. Despite the parameters lowering the solubility and low HRT, it is 
not completely accurate as some gas will be dissolved due to the replenishment of the liquid.  
It should be noted that the medium is stored at room temperature under nitrogen. Because less N2 can be 
dissolved at higher temperatures some N2 might be released from the medium due to oversaturation. This 
increases the N2 in the outflow leading to an overestimation of the outlet flow. 
 
12.1.3 N2 is inert 
It is assumed that N2 is completely inert, meaning it will not participate in any chemical reactions. This is relatively 
realistic, given that is has a triple bond between the two nitrogen molecules making it very stable. Thus, no N2 
should be lost due to reactions, giving that all the N2 entering the system also exits makes it possible to do a mass 
balance based on N2.

12.1.4 The pressure is 1 atm 
Throughout the calculations, it is assumed that the pressure is 1 atm (0.9869 bar) everywhere in the reactor. This 
assumption primarily affects the calculations regarding the ideal gas equation. Due to the system configuration 
in the pilot plant, some pressure will build up. The maximal overpressure is approximately 0.0065 bar making 
this assumption reasonable. 
 
12.1.5 Temperature 
The temperature affects the GC measurements because the gas cools down which decreases the volume. 
Meaning if the samples are taken at 60°C, once the gas cools down, the sample size will be less than the 0.1 mL, 
which is the initial sample size. This should not affect the measurement itself since the values are normalized 
thus accounting for small variations in volume. The outlet temperature of the gas is also not precisely known and 
is also different between the two reactors.  
In the pilot reactor, the sample is taken from a sampling port going into the LR, whereas in the laboratory scale 
the samples are taken close to the gas outlet. This means the gas sample from the pilot reactor is probably 
warmer but probably well below 60°C. It was therefore assumed that the sampling port in the pilot reactor was 
cold enough to cool the gas sample down to room temperature (25°C). The same was assumed for the laboratory 
reactor. The latter assumption is probably more accurate. This temperature is also used in the ideal gas equation.

13.1 Startup of the pilot reactor 
13.1.1 Inoculation 
The pilot plant was inoculated 21 of March 2019 with an active mixed microbial consortium from the laboratory 
reactor, which had been grown for several weeks as a small batch. Before the addition of medium, the column 
was washed with warm water to remove dirt and other impurities. Afterwards, the column was flushed with N2 
to remove the oxygen. Before the inoculum was added 3.5 L of medium was added, allowing the system pumps 
to operate. The medium initially used was the Pilot scale 50% medium, see Table 9.1 in section 9.1.1 for 
composition. To saturate the added medium with the gas components the system was left with syngas on. Due 
to the CO2 dissolving the pH of the medium dropped, the inoculum was added after the pH had stabilized. During 
the inoculation, the recirculation speed was 1.5 L/min and the mixing pump was on. The inoculum had a volume 
of 0.46 bringing the total volume up to 3.96 L. Due to the continues addition of liquid of 0.5 L/day the working 
volume of 4.1L would be reached in less than a day. The inoculum was added by extracting it from the flask with 
a syringe and pushing it into the reactor through the liquid sampling port. 
After inoculation, both a liquid and a gas sample were taken from the reactor, to examine the initial conditions 
in the reactor. The OD was 0.118 (OD5 0.062) and a pH of 7.13. The initial outflow gas composition was 44.4% 
H2, 14.68% N2, 0% CH4, 21.8% CO and 19.15% CO2. This data can also be seen in Figure 13.1 and Table 13.1 and 
is numbered as day 0. 
In the initial phase of the system, another MFC was used than the one described in section 9.3. The MFC used 
during this phase was of the same brand and type but was only able to deliver flows up to 100 mL/min, as it was 
initially thought to be enough. The MFC was changed on the 30 of April to the one capable of delivering gas flows 
up to 500 mL/min and is described in section 9.3.

13.1.3 Reactor breakdown 
Friday the 12 of April (day 22) in the evening, it was suspected that someone changed the settings of the reactor 
by accident. This resulted in the change was that the pH setpoint changed to 0, adding approximately 0.5 L of 
nitric acid (HNO3). This event was discovered Monday the 15 of April (day 25). The pH data from the reactor 
showed that pH had been below 6 and had slowly increased over the weekend due to the addition of medium. 
Furthermore, the acid pump had been on, even after no acid was left in the container, which meant air was 
pumped into the system. The large decrease in pH for the long duration seemed to have killed the microbes. To 
confirm this both liquid and gas samples were taken. These gas measurements are shown in Figure 13.4.  
 
Figure 13.4 – Gas data after the breakdown of the reactor 
Figure 13.4 shows no production of CH4 is occurring in this period. The gas composition for H2 and CO is also 
slightly off, especially on day 25 where H2 is over 50% and CO is under 15%. On day 25 it is possible that some 
residual gas was left in the reactor giving high H2 and lower CO readings. It seems unlikely that this change in gas 
composition on day 25 was caused by microbial activity because no CH4 or CO2 had been produced. For day 26-
32 the gas composition was relatively stable and within what is expected if no reaction is occurring. The only 
issue was that N2 appears to be a few percentages lower than expected. This can be explained by the GC being 
placed in a different building at this time. Transporting the sample between the buildings, increased the risk of 
air contaminating the sample, and likely affected the accuracy of the measurements by the high amounts of air 
in the samples. It also means that during this time the conversion of H2 and some days CO was negative. Liquid 
samples were only taken three days, this data can be found in Table 1.5 in Appendix A and Appendix D under 
“OD”. The OD trend was decreasing throughout the period. 
During the period with no conversion, it was attempted to restart the microbes in the reactor. In the hope that 
some of the biofilm had survived, most of the old medium was removed through the liquid sampling port and 
fresh medium were added. This should reduce the possible by-products, which might have been formed. Also, 
the liquid volume was replaced several times after that to try and wash out the products formed. Several times 
in the period from the 15 to the 24 of April small 10-20 mL of effluent was transferred to the pilot reactor in the 
hope that this would help the reactor recover. As can be seen from Figure 13.4 this did not reactivate the 
microbes or get new microbes to start growing.

The inability to start the reactor, even after the liquid volume had been changed several times suggested that 
some compounds, that were toxic to the microbes were present. The high number of compounds present in the 
medium made it possible for the nitric acid to form many different products such as nitrates from reactions either 
with VFA’s or other compounds in the medium. Besides lowering the pH, it is also possible that these nitrates 
were toxic to the microbes. If the formed nitrates were toxic to the microbes, it would explain why it was 
impossible for the microbes to grow even if they were only present in low concentration. The possible toxicity of 
the nitrates suggests that nitric acid might not be the best option for pH control for this MMC. Unfortunately, 
hydrochloric acid (HCl) has a corroding effect on the steel used, so this was also not a viable option. Instead, the 
solution became to turn off the acid by changing the maximal pumping speed to 0%, meaning it was not possible 
for the pH control to decrease the pH. However, over time the system would itself be able to correct this by the 
addition of new medium and due to the acids produced by the microbes. Also, the only way that the pH in the 
system could increase was if too much base was added, which due to VFA formation would correct itself over 
time. Therefore, the disabling the acid seemed the better option. 
 
13.1.4 Re-inoculation 
The system had to be moved to Dansk Gasteknisk Center (DGC) at some point, so to ensure that the reactor was 
operational at that point the system was re-inoculated. The re-inoculation had to be done sometime before 
moving the reactor since it was vital that the biofilm was strong enough to withstand the moving. During the 
moving of the reactor, it would be without syngas supply and recirculation for several hours, meaning the biofilm 
will not get any of the nutrient necessary.  
On April 24 the reactor was opened, and all the medium was removed. The system was then washed four times 
with water. During the wash, the pumps were running, and the heating was set to 70°C to ensure everything was 
dissolved. After the fourth wash, the removed water was completely clear, and it was assumed that the reactor 
was clean enough for inoculation. The re-inoculation was done with the inoculum prepared right after the 
discovery that no conversion took place in the reactor, with microbes from the laboratory reactor. This inoculum 
had only been grown for 9 days, and thus was not as strong as the previous inoculation. 
After inoculation, the capacity of the reactor was briefly evaluated at syngas flows of 110, 140 and 200 mL/min. 
The primary purpose of this period was also to make the reactor ready to move and not to generate data. For a 
flow rate of 200 mL/min, the data suggested that this was too much for the reactor. During this period of 8 days, 
the CO content in the exit gas increased from 7.33% to 9.64% and was still increasing. The same was the case for 
H2 which increased from 1.3% at day 48 to 2% at day 53. The increase even after 8 days suggests that the system 
had difficulty adapting to the high flow rate, however, it could also be because the MMC was not fully developed 
yet.

As expected, Figure 13.5 clearly shows that at lower EBRT the volumetric flow out of the reactor increases, 
relative to the inflow. The content of both H2 and CO increases at lower EBRT by a lower conversion. The increase 
in the outflow composition H2 increases from 0.49% to 2.53%, whereas the increase in CO was higher, increasing 
from 0.67% to 8.73% (see Table 13.2).  The decrease in conversion at lower EBRT was caused by the gas flow 
through the reactor is exceeding the MMC capacity for conversion, thus lower conversion is achieved. The fact 
that H2 was less affected than CO, indicates that the limitation of the MMC is caused by carboxydotrophs. This is 
further supported by the change in consumption of CO2, which increases because less CO2 is produced from e.g. 
the WGS, carboxydotrophic acetogenesis/methanation and various VFA production pathways.  
The relative content of CH4 is also decreased due to the lower conversion of the H2/CO. However, the content of 
CH4 was between 29.26% and 26.23%, which is close to the maximum of 29.55%. To increase the CH4 production 
further additional H2 would have to be supplied to the reactor, this would also decrease the content of CO2 since 
the carbon would primarily come from CO2. The highest decrease in CH4 content happens between the EBRT of 
25 and 20 minutes, this decrease is larger than the decrease seen between the EBRT of 35 and 25 minutes. This 
relatively large drop in the production of CH4 suggests that decreasing the EBRT further would accelerate the 
decrease in yields. Depending on the application of the final gas, this low conversion might cause issues since the 
quality of the gas is lower, however, decreasing the EBRT is at the expense of capacity of the reactor.  
Figure 13.5 also shows a negative conversion of CO2, meaning CO2 is produced at higher EBRT. It is the result of 
high conversion through carboxydotrophic hydrogenogenesis producing more CO2 than what can be used by the 
hydrogenotrophic methanogenesis as explained in section 12.2.5.  
Figure 13.6 shows the electron yields obtained during the testing of EBRT. The CH4 Ye− is for all EBRT high and is 
increasing with decreasing EBRT to the point where it is around 100%. This is not likely since some electrons also 
go to VFA production and to sustain the microbes. The most probable cause for the electron yield exceeding 
100% is inaccuracies in the inflow measurements, especially from the MFC. This inaccuracy might increase at 
higher flowrates. However, since carbon and nutrient recycling was proven (see section 13.6), it is possible 
additional carbon came from recycling allowing for temporary yields above 100%. The high CH4 Ye− also leads to 
a negative Undefined Ye−. As the Undefined Ye− is defined as the electrons neither used for VFAs or CH4. However, 
this is not possible as the undefined Ye− primarily is the electrons used to maintain and produce the biomass, as 
of such it is not possible to be negative for EBRT of 25 and 20 minutes.

expense of decreasing quality of the gas. 
Like the CH4 Ye−, the carbon fixed to CH4 is also positively correlated with the EBRT. The most reasonable 
explanation for the increase in the carbon fixed to CH4 is likely the same as for the CH4 Ye−. In principle, these 
two values should be the same, however, because some of the CO2 from the syngas is dissolved in the medium 
less carbon exits the system, decreasing the carbon fixed to CH4. This also explains why the carbon fixed to CH4 
increases more at lower EBRT than the electron yield. CO2 is constantly being dissolved to saturate the liquid 
phase. However, because the gas flow is higher, relative to the gas flow a smaller amount of CO2 will be dissolved, 
thus increasing the carbon fixed to CH4. 
The carbon yield is between 34.90% and 32.57%, this is the amount of carbon used to produce CH4 regardless of 
the total uptake of carbon. The carbon yield correlates slightly negatively with decreasing EBRT, suggesting that 
less carbon is used at lower EBRT. This is expected since the conversion of H2 and CO is decreasing, which is the 
primary factor affecting the carbon yield. The carbon yield is limited by the amount of hydrogen, and by extension 
also CO through the WGS. Because the conversion of both H2 and CO drops at lower EBRT, percentage of H2 used 
for CH4 is also lower, thus less carbon is used for CH4, giving a smaller carbon yield. Due to the stoichiometry of 
the gas, the maximal amount of carbon that can be used is 36%, giving that the process can be improved much 
by an increase in available H2.

13.2.4 Summation 
The EBRT has a large effect on the performance of the reactor. As expected, a lower EBRT points towards higher 
CH4 productivity and higher concentrations of VFAs in the liquid phase. Between EBRT of 25 and 20 minutes, the 
decrease in conversion intensifies and it would probably decrease faster at even lower EBRT. However, it is 
possible that the CH4 productivity would still increase, as a decrease in EBRT does not seem to affect rate increase 
of the productivity. The productivity would most likely also begin to decrease at lower EBRT either due to the 
accumulation of VFAs and/or accumulation of CO. The accumulation of CO is likely since the conversion 
decreased at lower EBRT. This is also reasonable since the MMC has less time to convert the gas, thus a smaller 
percentage is converted. Like the conversion decrease, the carbon yield also starts to decrease between EBRTs 
of 20 min and 25 min, further suggesting that an EBRT of 20 min is close to the limit of the reactor. This is indicated 
in Figure 13.9, where to conversion of H2 is clearly still linear, whereas CO seems to be decreasing and is close to 
reaching a plateau. Meaning the reactor is close to having reached its capacity for CO conversion.

One possibility is that the low QR supplies insufficient nutrient to the microbes in the TBC. This seems unlikely 
since the pilot reactor works at much lower QR (0.3) and performs better. Another possibility is that the low

wetting leaves some areas of the bed dry and inactive. This does not seem to affect carbon fixed to CH4 as the 
same relative amount of carbon goes to CH4, however overall less carbon is converted.

mass transfer. It is worth noticing, that the decrease in performance is not as distinct between QR values of 1.2

results such as productivity. Also, decreasing the QR below 0.6 does not seem feasible as the decrease in 
performance seems to be intensifying below QR’s of 0.6, meaning that small decrease in QR, could give large 
decreases in productivity and conversion.

13.4.3 Discussion 
The results presented in Figure 13.14, Figure 13.15 and Table 13.5 all indicated that operating at a QR of 0.1 is 
favourable to both a QR of 0.2 and 0.3. This seems counterintuitive as the opposite happened in the laboratory 
reactor (section 13.3). One explanation could be that the liquid film becomes so thin, that the biofilm can almost 
instantly convert the substrate, meaning the mass transfer is not affected by a higher concentration of CO and 
H2 in the medium. However, if this was the case a higher conversion would also have been expected at a QR of 
0.2 compared to 0.1, which was not seen making this explanation unlikely. 
Another possible explanation regards the formation of foam in the reactor. During operation with a QR of 0.1, no 
foam was observed in the foam trap. Also, the liquid in the system seemed to have increased, based on the speed 
of the mixing pump, which increased compared to QR’s of 0.1 and 0.2. It is possible that the formation of foam 
had a negative impact on the hydrogenotrophic microbes, as their activity increased when the foam disappeared. 
This can be verified by operating the reactor at a higher QR with antifoam and seeing if the efficiency of the 
microbes at higher QR increases. However, before doing this, the effect of antifoam should be assessed, to ensure 
that the antifoam does not impact the performance of the microbes. Another possibility would be to change the 
nozzle so the pressure drop remains the same, thus foam should occur, making it possible to measure.

attributed to the fact that no foam was formed at this condition. The other likely reason was some unexpected 
incidents occurred which could have impacted the metabolism. However, the VFA profile was unaltered 
suggesting that the used pathways remained the same but fewer VFAs were produced. Besides the better 
performance at QR = 0.1, a brief economic evaluation of the power usage also showed, that large amounts of

13.5.3 Discussion 
The most obvious reason why the pilot scale reactor works better is due to the nozzle placed in the top. The 
nozzle distributes better the liquid compared to the glass tube in the laboratory reactor. The nozzle sprays the 
liquid over the packing material in small droplets, which compared to compact liquid flow, increases greatly the 
surface area of the liquid. The larger surface area of the liquid increases the mass transfer, which in turn makes 
more substrate available for the microbes. This increases the efficiency of the reactor, thus allowing for lower 
EBRT for the pilot reactor. This also allows for operating the pilot reactor under different flow regimes. In the 
laboratory reactor, the liquid enters the reactor through a glass tube, this means large parts of the column will 
not be wetted unless high QR is used. This means the laboratory scale reactor does not function well at lower QR 
when compared with the pilot reactor, which can also be seen from the sections 13.3 (laboratory) and 13.4 
(pilot). The high QR will cause the column to contain a lot of liquid, thus likely being between pulse and bubble 
flow (see section 8.1). Because the pilot plant was not transparent, it was impossible to determine the flow 
regime. Based on the QR compared to the laboratory reactor, it is likely that the pilot reactor was evaluated at 
spray-, trickle- and perhaps also pulse flow during the different QR evaluated. The QR evaluated for the laboratory 
reactor can be seen in 13.3 and the pilot reactor can be seen in 13.4. The increased efficiency of the pilot reactor 
indicates that operating at very high liquid recirculation might not improve performance such as in the bubble 
flow regime.  
Another key difference between the reactors is the H/D relationship. The pilot reactor is 27.5 times larger than 
the laboratory reactor. However, the geometrical dimensions differ between the two setups, making them 
difficult to directly compare. The geometrical differences between the two reactors can affect the flow and thus 
the efficiency of the reactor even at the same EBRT. Because there also is the difference of the nozzle between 
the systems, it is difficult to say precisely how the H/D affects the system. It is, likely that lower QR can be used 
at a high H/D because a smaller cross-section area of packing material must be covered by the liquid. However, 
even though the whole area is wetted, the supply of medium should still be enough to provide the MMC with 
the necessary nutrients. Meaning there is a lower limit to the recirculation even if both mass transfer and wetting 
of the bed are sufficient.  
Lastly, the pilot reactor has a large headspace above the nozzle, unlike the laboratory reactor where this is 
relatively small. It is difficult to say if and how this affects the performance of the reactors as many other

parameters were different and probably with greater effect. One possible effect of this, in the heating of the 
syngas, before it reaches the bed and liquid, which decreases the solubility and mass transfer, potentially 
decreasing the reactor performance. On the other hand, heating the gas prior to contact with the bed helps 
maintain the correct temperature and does not cool the bed on contact, which can increase the reactor 
performance. Further, some backmixing with gas from the upper layers of the bed and new can also occur. 
The laboratory reactor VFA profile consists mainly of acetic acid, whereas the pilot reactor consists primarily of 
propionic acid (see Appendix A Figure 17.4, Table 17.22 and Table 17.23). This suggests that some of the 
metabolic pathways employed in the MMC differ from one another when comparing the reactors. The use of 
different pathways in the reactors is most likely a result of the slightly different operating conditions between 
the reactors, such as pH control, QR, the nozzle and the H/D ratio. Especially the pH control is likely to influence 
the metabolism of the MMC, as the addition of base might affect the microbes. Interestingly, the laboratory 
reactor produces fewer acids compared to the pilot reactor, even at the same relative gas inflow rates. It is 
unlikely that the low concentrations of VFAs have an inhibitory effect in the laboratory reactor, meaning the 
lower performance cannot be accredited to inhibition from VFAs. 
 
13.5.4 Summarisation 
When comparing the laboratory and pilot reactors at similar EBRT Figure 13.20 clearly shows the pilot reactor 
achieves better results than the laboratory reactor. The difference in conversion between the pilot- and 
laboratory reactors is large at an EBRT of 60, the pilot converts everything. Whereas at EBRT 20 for the difference 
was smaller.  
The differences in the performance of the pilot and laboratory reactor are most probably a consequence of the 
employed liquid distribution mechanism in each case. Therefore, upscaling the reactor further might not yield in 
similar improvements in the performance since most of this can be attributed to the nozzle. Another parameter 
that likely also has a large impact on the performance is the H/D relation, which can influence the flow patterns 
in the reactor. Few other differences might also have contributed to the better performance such as the QR, 
which also changes the internal flow in the reactor, and the VFA profile. The most probable reason for the 
difference in the VFA profile is the pH control, however, it is possible that the differing in the other parameters 
also affected this. Also, due to the low concentration of VFAs, it is unlikely that they were responsible for the 
poorer performance of the laboratory reactor.

13.6 Deprivation of liquid medium in the pilot reactor 
13.6.1 Background 
It was decided to deprive the MMC in the pilot reactor of liquid medium, hence no nutrients would be added. 
This was done to examine how the MMC would react under nutrient limiting conditions and if they were capable 
of nutrient recycling. The medium was thus disconnected, and no new medium was added, forcing the microbes 
to sustain themselves on what was already present. The experiment was conducted at an EBRT of 60 minutes 
(Qv,in = 100 mL/min) and a QR of 0.1 L/Lbed/min (0.5 L/min). The low recirculation was used to avoid foam, which 
could exit the reactor potentially leading to a decrease in the absolute number of nutrients in the reactor. 
This was inspired by Savvas et al. (2017) [64], who had successfully operated an MMC biomethanation reactor 
for 180 days without the addition of liquid medium. They used a bubble column reactor with a volume of 1.5L. 
The gas inlet was placed right next to the recirculation pump in the bottom of the reactor, thus gas and liquid 
were mixed while being recirculated to the top of the reactor. It was performed under mesophilic conditions 
(37°C), with a gas consisting of only CO2 and H2. The pH in the reactor was regulated through the addition of CO2 
forming HCO3- decreasing the pH. The inoculum they used anaerobically digested sewage sludge and was filtered 
before they use, no buffering agents of nutrients were added to the inoculum or in any other part of their 
experiment.  
They reported achieving a 100% conversion into CH4 at an inflow rate of 60 L/Lreactor/day. Furthermore, they 
performed q-PCR on the MMC, showing that only 7% of the microbes were methanogens, out the methanogens 
3.1% were acetoclastic methanogens the rest were hydrogenotrophic methanogens.  
It should be noted that during these experiments the location of the peaks in the GC had. This led to an 
underestimation of CO2 in the gas. The underestimation of CO2 caused the other gas species to be overestimated, 
especially CH4. This should be considered when reviewing the results. It should also be mentioned that based on 
Figure 13.21 to Figure 13.23, the system does not reach anything resembling a steady state and should have 
been prolonged. The experiments were discontinued due to a faulty cylinder also containing oxygen.

whereas the carboxydotrophic microbes appear to be less affected. It is unlikely that any nutrients remained 
after 8 days of operation without addition. This seemingly proves that at least the carboxydotrophs are capable 
of nutrient recycling or benefitting from scavengers capable of this in the consortium. The hydrogenotrophic did 
not fare as well, their conversion decreased throughout the period and did not stabilize. This is in contrasts to 
the findings of Savvas et al. (2017) [64], who primarily had of hydrogenotrophic methanogens, which were able 
to survive and produce CH4 under similar conditions. One explanation is the CO in gas, as it is not fully converted, 
it can dissolve and saturate the liquid over time. CO is inhibitory to the hydrogenotrophic microbes making it 
possible that in increased amount of CO in the liquid, could be responsible for the poor conversion. However, 
this is not likely as a similar trend would have been seen during the EBRT experiments (section 13.2) where the 
conversion of CO was even lower but the conversion of H2 was high. 
A more likely reason is inhibition of the hydrogenotrophs by the high concentration of VFAs and the shift towards 
acetic acid instead of propionic acid. The hydrogenotrophic microbes are slightly more sensitive to such changes 
compared to the carboxydotrophs. Therefore, they are affected more, which in turn also further reduces their 
productivity. 
The last possibility is that the carboxydotrophs can outcompete the hydrogenotrophs for the available and 
presumably scarce nutrients, thus hindering growth of the hydrogenotrophs. This is not unlikely as the 
carboxydotrophs are generally more resilient. It is possible that this could be overcome, by using a medium with 
higher concentrations of nutrients. Many of the available nutrients are probably part of the life and death cycle 
within the consortium. Thus, increasing the concentration could also increase the free available nutrients 
benefitting the hydrogenotrophs.  
The decrease in hydrogenotrophic activity is likely caused by a combination of the above-mentioned reasons, as 
they can also affect one another. One way to solve this in an industrial unit would be to have two reactors, this 
would likely be the case anyway due to the large volumes that need to be processed. The initial unit could then 
house the carboxydotrophs converting the CO. The second unit the hydrogenotrophs, where a different VFA 
profile could be in place and they would not have to compete with the carboxydotrophs for the nutrients. 
This experiment shows that it might not be necessary to add liquid medium to the process. Seeing as both the 
hydrogenotrophs and carboxydotrophs were affected, it is possible that adding small amounts of medium could 
==PAGE:90==
 
 
13. Results and discussions - Deprivation of liquid 
medium in the pilot reactor 
[89] 
 
be beneficial, keeping up productivity and still reducing operational costs. Another possibility is that only a few 
of the nutrients with the largest influences the MMC, meaning some compounds may be omitted, also reducing 
costs. No matter what medium addition cannot be completely avoided, as water is produced in the process 
leading do dilution of the nutrients over time.  
 
13.6.4 Summation 
In short, these results show that it was possible to keep the carboxydotrophs alive and operation in the reactor. 
However, the hydrogenotrophs did not seem to thrive, unlike reported by Savvas et al. (2017) [64]. The lacking 
growth of the hydrogenotrophs was assumed to be linked to the shift in VFAs since the MMC began to produce 
large amounts of acetic acid. This production of acetic acid likely inhibited the hydrogenotrophs thus causing the 
conversion of H2 to decrease to 33%. Further, it was also possible that the carboxydotrophs could outcompete 
the hydrogenotrophs for the nutrients and inhibit them this way. The carboxydotrophs were also affected, 
however, much less. Their conversion only drooped to around 90% and was stable for several days. The decrease 
in conversion of primarily H2 caused the productivity to decrease with 53% from 3.2 to 1.7 mL/Lbed/min and it 
was still decreasing when the experiment was stopped. 
The results presented show great potential for reducing the cost of medium, however, omitting medium does 
decrease performance. Meaning a suitable compromise between the medium, its composition and productivity 
should be reached. Further research could include removal of unnecessary components from the medium, only 
adding small amounts of medium or increasing the initial concentration of nutrients.

13.7.1 Background for the experiment 
Syngas produced from a gasifier often contains several trace compounds some of which are associated with 
inhibitory effects on the MMC (see section 8.3). The syngas used for the other experiments were supplied in 
pressurized cylinders and very pure, thus containing no impurities. To evaluate the system with raw syngas, the 
system was, therefore, moved to DGC where it was possible to supply the reactor with syngas directly from a 
gasifier. The primary goal was to assess if the microbes could use/survive with the raw syngas and if/how it would 
affect the reactor performance. The system was placed at DGC from 17 of May and returned to DTU on the 11 
of June. 
 
13.7.2 Setup 
The setup at DGC was slightly different compared to DTU. The two setups differed in three ways; the gas 
composition, the method for controlling the gas inflow, and the separation system for the gas/liquid system. 
The gas was supplied from a gasifier and inevitably this would change the gas composition. Gas samples were 
taken in gasbags and brought to DTU to be analysed as described in section 11.1 (DGC also had a GC which they 
used, but the results in this thesis are based on the GC at DTU). The gasifier was feed with wood pellets and using 
CO2 to push them into the reactor, giving a relatively high content of CO2 in the gas. After the gasifier, a tar-
cracker and a methanation catalyst were placed. They had the function of breaking down larger hydrocarbons 
and converting them to CH4. The gas from DGC did not contain any nitrogen making it impossible to determine 
the volumetric outflow using the nitrogen balance. The measured gas compositions are shown in Table 13.9. As 
it can be seen in Table 13.9, sometime between day 69 and 74 one of the chemical catalysts in the tar cracker 
was deactivated leading to a decrease in CH4 production. At day 77 the catalyst responsible for the WGS shift 
was almost completely deactivated and almost no CH4 was present in the inlet gas.

should not affect the performance of the reactor.

13.7.4 Foam formation 
High amounts of foam were created during the time the reactor was placed at DGC. The high amount of foam 
created problems for the liquid/gas separation system as the foam is on top of the liquid can exit the system 
even if too little liquid is present in the system. Because the foam contains liquid the large removal of foam over 
time can remove more liquid from the system than what is added. This makes it impossible to know the amount 
of liquid in the system, thus the HRT is not known. Another issue caused by the decreasing liquid level was at 
two times the recirculation pump stopped working. The best explanation for this is that the liquid level got so 
low there was no pressure on the exit side of the pump, causing the pump to give an error and stop pumping. 
After this happened the first time approximately 3 L of the medium was added before liquid, not foam, was seen 
exiting the system. This suggests that much more liquid was removed in the form of foam than was added as 
medium and that the liquid level in the system had for a long duration been much below the expected. This 
makes it difficult to conclude anything based on the liquid state of the system because the system was “diluted” 
with the medium in the attempt to correct the liquid level several times. 
Antifoam could have been added to the system to resolve this issue, but this was not done. The reason for this 
decision was that it is difficult to say how the antifoam would affect the system. It is possible that the antifoam 
would affect both the stability of the biofilm or cause some form of inhibition on the MMC. To ensure that the 
data gathered could be compared with cylinder syngas it was deemed best not to add an antifoaming agent. 
Foam formation was also experienced at the system configuration used at DTU but on a much smaller scale. It 
was difficult to explain the exact reason for this difference in the formation of foam. One possibility was the gas 
composition and impurities associated with the gasifier gas. It is possible this changed the metabolism, increasing 
the creation of compounds which causes the medium to foam more. Based on Figure 13.28 this does not seem 
to be the case since the concentration of VFAs are lower compared to other times where this was not an issue. 
Figure 13.28 can, however, not be used as a conclusion due to the frequent dilution with medium and because 
it is possible that the foam causing compounds were proteins and not VFAs.  
Another possible explanation is the difference between the liquid/gas separation systems used. The pipe 
allowing the liquid to escape the LR in the DTU setup was much longer compared to DGC. It is possible this allows 
for some of the foam to break before it completely removed from the system. Allowing broken foam, which turns 
into a liquid, to run back down into the LR reducing the amount of liquid removed. It is difficult to know what 
exactly caused the foam to appear in the system, without conducting experiments to determine this. Most likely 
it is a combination of the mentioned possibilities and possible also other parameters.

It is difficult to conclude much besides that the MMC can achieve full conversion at low to medium flow rates.

14. Conclusion 
In this thesis, it was demonstrated that the upscaling of the lab-scale trickle bed reactor (0.18L) to pilot-scale (5L) 
significantly improved the efficiency of the biomethanation process. At an empty bed residence time (EBRT) of 1 
hour, the laboratory setup was unable to convert the electron donors and had a relative productivity (p%) of 82%. 
Under these same conditions the pilot reactor converted all H2 and CO and its relative productivity was 98%, as 
some of the substrates are still used for maintenance/growth of biomass and VFAs. 
The lowest EBRT evaluated in the pilot reactor was 20 minutes. Under this condition, the pilot reactor still had a 
relative productivity of 90%, however, it was unable to achieve full conversion. At an EBRT of 20 min, the pilot 
reactor still contained 2.5% H2 and 8.7% CO in the effluent gas, which corresponded to a conversion of 97% for 
H2 and 76% for CO. The relatively better performance of the pilot reactor can be attributed to the improved mass 
transfer in the trickle bed column induced a) by the introduction of the liquid in the form of micro-droplets with 
a nozzle and b) by the higher height per diameter (H/D) ratio compared to the laboratory reactor. Another 
interesting difference between the two systems was their VFA production as the pilot reactor mainly produced 
propionic acid, whereas the laboratory reactor mainly had acetic acid, this difference was primarily attributed to 
the pH control in the pilot reactor. 
The maximal CH4 productivity during the EBRT assessment was found in the pilot to be 7.3 mL/Lbed/min (p% = 
90%) at a syngas inflow rate of 250 mL/min (EBRT of 20min). Further increase of the syngas inflow rate would 
result in even higher CH4 productivity at a cost of the conversion efficiency of H2 and CO. At this flowrate it also 
seems as the capacity for carboxydotrophic conversion in the reactor was close to reaching a threshold.  
Suggesting a further increase in the gas flow, might not increase the molar conversion of CO much. The 
productivity of syngas fermentation in the Synferon was similar to biomethanation studies only using H2 and CO2, 
indicating that this could be a viable green technology in the future. 
The effect of the liquid recirculation rate was also assessed on both the laboratory- and pilot reactor, at EBRTs 
of 60 and 20 minutes respectively, however, they yielded opposite results. In the laboratory reactor liquid 
recirculations (QR – L/Lbed/min) of 1.2, 0.9 and 0.6 were assessed. The results obtained showed a relative minor 
decrease in performance from 1.2 to 0.9 with a decrease in productivity from 2.21 (p% = 82%) to 2.2 mL/Lbed/min 
(p% = 81%) and only a slight decrease of the conversion of H2 and CO. However, the decrease in QR from 0.9 to 
0.6 further decreased the productivity to 2.12 mL/Lbed/min (p% = 78%) and decreased the conversion of CO by 
4.5%. The conversion of H2 was decreased by less than 1%. This suggests the carboxydotrophs were more 
vulnerable to this change. The decrease in performance was attributed to insufficient wetting of the bed. This 
conclusion was based on the fact that the pilot reactor was assessed at QR of 0.3, 0.2 and 0.1, thus had 
significantly lower liquid recirculation and operated without issues.  
The low QR in the pilot reactor was possible due to the nozzle giving a better distribution of the liquid, making it 
unnecessary to have as recirculation in the column to sufficiently wet it. In the pilot plant, it was found that 
decreasing the QR from 0.3 to 0.2 slightly decreased its performance. The productivity was reduced from 7.33 
(p% = 90%) to 7.25 mL/Lbed/min (p% = 89%). The conversion of H2 and CO decreased from 96.9% to 96.0% and 
75.7% to 72.3% respectively. Unexpectedly decreasing the QR to 0.1 increased the performance of the reactor. 
==PAGE:101==
14. Conclusion - Experiments at Dansk Gasteknisk Center 
[100] 
 
Under this condition, the productivity increased to 7.49 ml/Lbed/min (p% = 92%) and the conversion of H2 
increased to 98.9% and CO to 73.0%, meaning the effect of this was greatest on the hydrogenotrophs. The 
increase in performance at the low QR was reasoned to either be caused by large reduction in foam, or due to 
some unforeseen occurrences which could have changed the metabolism. However, nothing in the OD or VFA 
profile indicated a change in metabolism except that fewer VFAs were produced. From an economic standpoint, 
this increases the viability of the process as the recirculation uses a significant amount of energy. As no decrease 
in performance was seen, there is no drawback associated with this decrease of QR. 
It was also attempted to deprive the MMC of liquid medium, inspired by Savvas et al. (2017) [64]. This caused 
the conversion of CO to decrease to around 90% at an EBRT of 60 min. It was stable from day 3 to day 8, where 
the experiment was terminated. The conversion of H2 decreased already after one day, this decrease was still 
ongoing at day 8, and the conversion had decreased to 33% from the initial 100%. This gave a total decrease in 
the CH4 productivity of 52%. The large decrease in hydrogenotrophic activity was probably caused by two factors. 
One the large increase in the concentration of VFA, which increased from 1.7 to 3 g/L. Acetic acid was the primary 
contributor to this increase and ended up being the dominant VFA, however, propionic acid also slightly 
increased, the rest of the VFAs were relatively stable. The second being that carboxydotrophs under nutrient 
limiting conditions could outcompete the hydrogenotrophs for the available nutrients. However, these results 
still show that nutrient recycling is possible for the carboxydotrophs, since it is unlikely that any nutrients are left 
free in the medium after 8 days of operation. It is suspected that nutrient recycling is possible for 
hydrogenotrophs as well but requires slightly different conditions. 
The pilot Synferon was also assessed at Dansk Gasteknisk Center (DGC), where it was supplied with raw syngas 
produced from the gasification of wood pellets instead of high purity laboratory syngas. This was done to assess 
the effect of possible impurities on the reactor performance. Due to unforeseen circumstances, a catalyst in the 
gasifier setup was deactivated resulting in a varying inlet gas composition. The reactor was, however, able to 
convert all the supplied substrate at the tested syngas flowrates (EBRT’s of 80 min, 60 min and 35 min). It should 
be noted that the inflow was measured using flow meters and not a mass flow controller (MFC), making the 
measurements slightly inaccurate, hence also the reported results.  
Also due to the absence of N2 in the exit gas, a mass balance could not be made, meaning the outflow was 
estimated based on a volume reduction of the gas correlated to the conversion. The highest productivity of the 
reactor reported was 3 mL/Lbed/min (101%) at an EBRT of 60 min. These measurements were conducted while 
the methanation catalyst did not work properly, meaning the highest number of mols of the substrate had to be 
converted under these conditions. No EBRTs lower than 35 minutes were assessed as the gasifier broke and the 
experiments were discontinued. The results obtained before the breakdown of the gasifier still showed that the 
reactor was not affected using raw syngas, further indicating that the Synferon could be viable on an industrial 
scale.

15. Future perspective 
To fully achieve an industrial potential, additional work with the Synferon is required as much can still be 
examined and optimized. For instance, a temperature of 65°C or 55°C might yield better results compared to 
60°C. This should be done in combination with the evaluation of the optimal pressure, as both pressure and 
temperature impact the solubility of, especially, H2 and CO. This is significantly important regarding CO as it has 
inhibitory effects on the MMC. However, its conversion is at the same time often limited by poor solubility, thus 
finding the optimum temperature and pressure can help resolve this. Especially considering that higher pressures 
have been reported to increase productivity when using only CO2 and H2 as a substrate [48]. 
The current strategy of continuously distributing the liquid medium through a nozzle should also be evaluated 
as it would be a significant expense at an industrial scale. Besides, by lowering the liquid recirculation rate even 
further, it is also possible to operate the circulation at timed intervals, only having liquid recirculation for e.g. 1 
minute every 15 minutes. Before this can be done, it is necessary to evaluate for how long the setup can be 
without recirculation before the performance is significantly impacted.  
Based on this, the time intervals and length of the intervals can be determined, effectively reducing the QR. It 
might also be possible to reduce the pressure drop over the distribution nozzle by finding another distribution 
mechanism, such as e.g. a distribution tray in the top. Both these suggestions would likely decrease the 
performance of the reactor to a certain extent, thus the economic benefits of doing so should be greater 
compared to the performance.  
In the system used it is also possible to vary the H/D ratio to examine its effect by either adding or removing part 
of the packing material. This could be used as a reference if further upscaling with a higher H/D ratio can operate 
at a lower relative liquid recirculation, as it would be easier to keep the bed wet. This could be done by increasing 
the amount of packing material (the volume of the bed) and operating at the same liquid recirculation. This 
would be akin to operating at a lower QR, hence this could have similar beneficial economic effects on the reactor. 
No optimization of the medium used in this process had been done, making this a large opportunity for 
optimization of the process. Finding the exact composition and vital nutrients for the best reactor performance 
could make the process more viable as some nutrients added might not be necessary while others might be 
available in abundance. Further adding to this are the results showing carbon recycling for carboxydotrophic 
microbes. This indicating that this might also be possible for hydrogenotrophic microbes but should be examined 
more thoroughly. As recycling of the nutrients is possible it is likely that changing the initial concentration of 
nutrients could be beneficial as more nutrients could be available. This could potentially limit the negative effect 
on productivity associated with not adding liquid medium. Another possibility based on nutrient recycling is to 
classify the nutrients having the largest impact on performance and only adding them. This could also 
significantly decrease cost, probably with a lower impact on performance compared to some of the other 
suggestions. 
Another interesting possibility based on the results shown, would be to attempt operation of two reactors in 
series. As indicated both the hydrogenotrophs did not thrive without addition of liquid medium, as was reported 
by Savvas et al. (2017) [64]. Thus, operating two reactors in series would allow carboxydotrophic conversion in 
==PAGE:103==
15. Future perspective - Experiments at Dansk Gasteknisk Center 
[102] 
 
the first, effectively removing the CO. The second reactor could then be operated with only CO2 and H2 closer 
mimicking the conditions reported by Savvas et al. This is also realistic at an industrial scale as several 
biomethanation units are likely required to achieve the necessary productivity, however, they would likely be 
operated in parallel.  
Other interesting but more difficult experiments due to the practicality of them, would be to change the gas flow 
from co-current to counter current. Changing the flow to go in the opposite direction could yield better 
performance due to the concentration gradients. Also, this could indicate if it is possible to do without the issues 
normally associated with doing so, or if creative solutions are needed to operate this system as counter current.  
Since the Synferon is only a part of the overall process, research should also go into finding suitable feedstock 
for the gasification, and potential production of additional H2 for addition. All in all, there is a lot of potential 
improvements to the current system that can be done, to make it more appealing to use as a future source of 
renewable energy.

19.1.1 Calibration curves 
Initially standard vials with various gas compositions containing known molar compositions. This was necessary 
to determine the relationship between the molar compositions and the area under curves. The different vials 
contained more than one gas, so multiple gases could be measured at the same time. Each vial was measured 
twice, giving average values. The determined averages values of the areas for the specific gasses were used to 
make correlations between the area and the composition. To do this, multiple compositions of the same gas had 
to be evaluated, each correlating a specific area with the percent of the gas in the sample. The relation between 
the composition and the area was linear, giving a linear regression for each gas. Due to the correlation of area to 
composition, the sample size had to be precisely the same size. If the samples are not the same size, the 
regressions will be inaccurate. These regressions are presented in Table 19.1. In Table 19.1, y is the molar 
composition and x is the area given by Peaksimple. The samples were taken using a gas-tight 1 mL syringe, the 
samples themselves were 0.1 mL.

19.1.2 Integration of GC curves 
The column was cleaned 28/5-30/5 due to dirt interfering with the measurements. The issue was that the peaks 
for CH4 and CO were overlapping each other, making it difficult to distinguish between them. After the cleaning 
procedure, the position of the peaks moved, this only made a difference for CH4 and CO, which were not 
overlapping afterwards. The integration procedure of the peaks, therefore, changed after the cleaning, thus two

19.1.3 Calculations for H2 necessary to achieve 100% CH4 
For 100% conversion, the required hydrogen content was also calculated, this was also based on a 1 mol 
calculation. This gave the theoretical best gas composition, which could yield 100% methane (assuming no 
nitrogen in the gas). These calculations were based on the cylinder gas composition. If based on the DGC gasifier 
gas it would be slightly different due to the difference in CO/CO2 ratio. The H2/carbon ratio was calculated via
