Abstract
A method for estimating the stiﬀness and damping of a structural connection is devel-
oped. The application is for structures that are ﬁxed at one end and free at the other
e.g. aircraft wings, towers, balconies, and more. The idea is to measure only the ﬁrst
few natural frequencies of the structure and use these to estimate the stiﬀness of the
structural connection. The relation between boundary stiﬀness and natural frequen-
cies is analytically modelled and experimentally measured. Poor correlation is seen
for very stiﬀboundaries, but as soon as the boundary softens it aﬀects the natural
frequencies straight away. This is used to estimate the changes in boundary stiﬀness.
Simulations yield high accuracy estimations for a very broad range of boundary stiﬀ-
ness. Experimental estimation encounters issues when it must distinguish between
rotational and translational stiﬀness changes.
When the entire boundary softens,
experimental estimation of boundary parameters is very successful. The estimation
method can used to evaluate the structural health of boundaries.

1
Introduction
Many structural components are mounted at one end face and are free at all other
faces. This is for instance the case for a wing of an aircraft or of a wind turbine,
balconies, masts, micro electro-mechanical vibrating chemical sensors, bridge decks or
wind turbine towers. Structural health monitoring of these constructions is necessary
as the consequence of failure is very harmful.
All of these structures are ideally fully clamped at one end. However, in reality
clamped boundaries are never inﬁnitely stiﬀand can be considered as elastic supports.
Through the use of measured vibrations, the aim is to determine the stiﬀness and
damping of the clamped boundary. This is a type of parameter estimation problem,
where known vibration response is used to determine the parameters of the model [1].
Wear and minor damage of a clamped boundary might be detected by the bound-
ary stiﬀness decreasing compared with the initial structure without faults. Alterna-
tively, if non-linear parts of the spring stiﬀness change or the damping in the boundary
increases these might also be indications of forthcoming failure of the boundary. This
project intends to develop methods for determining the boundary stiﬀness. These
methods can then later be used to carry out studies of speciﬁc structures and deter-
mine which boundary parameters best indicate upcoming failure or necessary main-
tenance.
1.1
Why is it important?
For the case of an oﬀshore wind turbine, 81% of these in Europe are mounted on
monopiles [2]. Cyclic loads from the turbine make the soil progressively yield and
changes its stiﬀness. This results in the ﬁrst natural frequency of the whole oﬀshore
wind turbine structure changing after initial installation. An example is given in [3]
where the ﬁrst natural frequency of initially 1.30 Hz dropped to 1.14 Hz after three
months.
For wind turbines designed with the ﬁrst natural frequency between an
upper bound of the rotor frequency and lower bound of the blade passing frequency,
unplanned resonance might occur and have a signiﬁcant contribution to fatigue [4].

1.2. What is the gap in knowledge?
The soil-tower interaction represents an elastic boundary that, just after installa-
tion, has a certain stiﬀness. This stiﬀness decreases as the loads on the blades and
tower of the turbine forces the soil to compress and pushes the sand particles away
from the foundation. An easy and reliable method of monitoring this boundary stiﬀ-
ness is sought, and implementing the use of a parameter estimation method based on
measured vibrations, could make this possible.
Reliability issues with wind turbines are frequent, and four to ﬁve issues a year
are common even for newly installed turbines [5]. Of all these issues, roughly half
are mechanical. In [6,7] structural health monitoring of turbine blades is attempted
through statistical correlations between three failure types and natural frequencies and
mode shapes. These damages are modelled as reduced stiﬀness at the blade mounting
in a ﬁnite element model. A typical example is when a pitch blade bearing is starting
to fail, the angle of attack for that blade will be slightly diﬀerent from the two others,
resulting in asymmetric loads. This causes an increase in fatigue damage and can be
avoided if it is detected by a structural health monitoring system, that then adjusts
the two other blades to minimise the asymmetric loading, until the turbine can be
serviced by a technician.
Estimation of boundary stiﬀness is also used for bridges, rods in old cathedrals,
micro electro-mechanical systems, bolt joints and machine interfaces [8–13].

Estimation of beam boundary stiﬀness from vibration measurements has been done
by [17] for a pinned-pinned micro electro-mechanical beam with rotational springs
at both ends.
Here the lowest natural frequencies and mode shapes are used to
determine the stiﬀness of the torsional springs, the systems residual stress and the
beam’s curvature. This is done through the use of a nonlinear least squares regression
not further speciﬁed. A pinned-pinned beam model with rotational springs is also
used by [18] but with tension added. Here, expressions are derived for the rotational
spring stiﬀnesses as a function of the natural frequencies and tension of the beam.
This is applied in practice in [9] for maintenance of the Milan Cathedral.
Reference [19] investigates a beam in tension with translational and rotational
springs at both ends. An algorithm is developed and the ten ﬁrst natural frequencies
are used to determine the tension and boundary stiﬀness. Often it is not easy to obtain
measurements of the ﬁrst ten natural frequencies and, therefore, [12] successfully
developed a method where measurement of only the ﬁrst few natural frequencies are
needed. This is possible as two sets of measurements are obtained, one with and one
without an added mass at one end. However, these models are for a beam, with both
ends mounted to translational and rotational springs.
1.3
Problem statement
The main question of this project is: How can the change in stiﬀness parameters of a
clamped boundary supporting a structure be estimated with measured vibrations?
From this problem statement, several questions follow:
• What is the relation between natural frequencies and boundary stiﬀness?
• How can the boundary stiﬀness of an experimental setup be varied and con-
trolled?
• In which range can the boundary stiﬀness successfully be estimated from mea-
surement of the lowest three natural frequencies?
• How can the boundary damping of a clamped boundary be estimated?

1.4. Report overview
1.4
Report overview
The strategy of this project is ﬁrst to determine the natural frequencies of beam
models with gradually improved representation of an experimental model. In other
words, the direct problem is ﬁrst analysed. This is used to map the relation between
the boundary parameters and the natural frequencies and mode shapes of the model.
Once these relations are understood, the parameter estimation problem is investigated.
This is carried out for both numerical tests and experimental tests. Here the aim is
to determine the robustness of the estimated boundary stiﬀness from the developed
method and thus understand in which range of stiﬀness the method is applicable.
The report ﬁrst introduces the background knowledge of parameter estimation and
dynamics of continuous beams in chapter 2. Chapter 3 contains analytical deriva-
tions of the natural frequencies and mode shapes for diﬀerent models. This is initially
performed for simpliﬁed models with only a rotational spring and only a transverse
spring, followed by more complex models with combinations of springs and added
point masses. Chapter 4 describes the experimental setup and, thereafter, results
of the experiments are presented in chapter 5. This is followed by the direct model
results, which are then compared to the experimental results. Then parameter es-
timation of the boundary stiﬀness is carried out on the basis of the experimental
measurements and numerical simulations. Lastly, conclusions are presented in chap-
ter 6.

Mathematical modelling and analysis
This chapter introduces the direct analysis of the transverse vibrations of a cantilever
beam with rigid, semi-rigid and elastic boundary. Initially, simple models are intro-

3.1

transverse vibrations and under the assumption that the beam is slender. A slender

beam height should be 20 or greater. This means that shear deformations and rotary
inertia can be neglected. Longitudinal and torsional deformations are assumed small

4.3
Measurement equipment
For the dynamic measurements of the vibrating beam, a uni-axial accelerometer is
used. Speciﬁcally, a Brüel & Kjær 4397 accelerometer [26]. Measurements are made
for the beam in a free-free state and a hammer with a force transducer is used to give
the beam an impulse load. The impact hammer is a Brüel & Kjær type 8206 [27].
For the natural frequency and damping ratio measurements where the beam is
mounted in the test rig, a pendulum with a force transducer is used.
The force
transducer is a Brüel & Kjær type 8230 [28]. For both the hammer and the pendulum
a rubber tip is used. This results in a relatively long impact time, but reduces the
possibility of double hits and excites a high enough frequency range.
For the static measurements a photoelectric sensor is used. Speciﬁcally, the Omron
ZX-LD30 [29]. The force is applied through the use of a spring with marked readouts
for the force in Newtons.

5.3
Simulated parameter estimation
In order to investigate the range in which it is possible to estimate the boundary
stiﬀness parameters, a simulation is carried out. In appendix A.2.2 the MATLAB
scripts are provided. The procedure is described in subsection 4.6.1 and is repeated
for 40 diﬀerent initial values of kr and 40 diﬀerent initial values of kt. These initial
values are forgotten and kr,est and kt,est are then estimated purely on the data of
the ﬁrst three natural frequencies. This yields estimated rotational and translational
boundary stiﬀness kr,est and kt,est as of Figures 5.18 and 5.19.
Figure 5.18: Simulation of rotational boundary stiﬀness estimate with 1000 samples. m1 =
m2 = J1 = J2 = 0. lower bound for estimate: 10−4. Upper bound for estimate: 109.
If the estimation worked faultlessly, the ﬁgures would be two completely even
planes, where only changes in kr would change the estimated stiﬀness kr,est.
For
the vast majority of the initial combinations of kr and kt it is indeed seen that the
estimated rotational stiﬀness kr,est only changes along the kr axis in Figure 5.18.
This is also the case for the translational boundary stiﬀness estimation of Figure
5.19. This illustrates nearly the most important attribute of the estimation tool. It
should be able to estimate the relative change in rotational and translational boundary
stiﬀness and not necessarily the absolute values.

increases. This same area of poor estimation is also seen in Figure 5.19 for trans-

also true. To improve the estimates in this region, mode shapes could be measured
and included in the estimation procedure. This way, the estimation tool will easier
distinguish between which of the boundary stiﬀness parameters should be high and
which one should be low.

6
Conclusion
6.1
Summary
The simple analytical model of a cantilever beam is extended by making the boundary
elastic. This is done in several stages where each extension is veriﬁed. The elastic
boundary is split into rotational and translational movement, each with a spring and
damper. The analytical models show that boundary stiﬀness aﬀects the natural fre-
quencies. However, when the boundary stiﬀness is signiﬁcantly larger than the beam
stiﬀness, small changes in the boundary stiﬀness do not aﬀect the natural frequencies.
A test rig, with the intention of providing an adjustable elastic boundary, is de-
signed and manufactured. This is used to perform experiments with an aluminium
beam for diﬀerent elastic boundary stiﬀnesses.
Next, a boundary parameter esti-
mation method is developed.
This uses a non-linear least squares approach with
measured natural frequencies and damping ratios.
Through simulation it is shown that the estimation method can estimate boundary
stiﬀness for a very broad range. Generally for very high stiﬀnesses, the natural fre-
quencies do not change and therefore the estimation method has the greatest amount
of error for very stiﬀboundaries.
The intention of the experimental measurements is only to measure the transverse
natural frequencies.
However, through mode shape measurements it is seen that
natural frequencies that are not purely transverse, are also measured. This results in
a mismatch between the third analytical and measured natural frequencies. Therefore,
for the majority of the experimental boundary estimates, only two natural frequencies
are used.
Parameter estimation is carried out for two diﬀerent data inputs; simulated mea-
surements with a 5 % normally distributed error and experimentally measured natural
frequencies and damping ratios.
The simulated parameter estimation is very successful, as it works in a very broad
range of boundary stiﬀness. It can detect small changes in boundary stiﬀness, which
is necessary in order to use the estimates as structural health indicators.
69
==PAGE:82==
6.3. Perspective
The boundary estimation with experimental data is also successful at estimating
the changes in stiﬀness. Particularly, simultaneous changes of rotational and transla-
tional stiﬀness are very well estimated. The absolute values of the boundary stiﬀness
estimates are in some cases not good when compared with the statically measured.
However, for the majority of the cases, estimates are less than 50% oﬀthe statically
measured stiﬀnesses.
Damping estimates strongly depends on the stiﬀness estimates. As a result, issues
with the stiﬀness estimates are carried over to the damping estimates. However, the
quality of the damping estimates are diﬃcult to evaluate because of a lack of reference
values. Generally, boundary damping is seen to drop with lowered stiﬀness, however,
clear correlations are not identiﬁed.
6.2
Discussion
The aim of the project is to investigate the usability of an analytical model of cantilever
beams for boundary parameter estimation. The model is not for use in one speciﬁc
application but must be useful for any given clamped-free structure.
One issue that arises with the measured frequencies is the need for measuring the
mode shapes. This is because mode shapes that are not purely transverse, easily
get jumbled into measurements. This results in a mismatch between analysis and
measurements, which the estimation method can not handle.
To improve the estimations, experimental measurements must be improved. They
must only return the transverse natural frequencies of the beam. Alternatively, the
model must be made three dimensional. However, this contradicts the wish for a
simple estimation method that can be used in many applications.
The estimation method struggles with changes in only rotational or translational
stiﬀness. It is therefore hard to identify which boundary parameter is dropping in
value when the boundary softens. The method struggles to identify wether rotational
or translational stiﬀness is reduced, because the input for the estimation contains no
information about the mode shapes.
6.3
Perspective
The next step to take is to measure the mode shapes for several setups in a broader
frequency range. Then, more natural frequencies can be used as input for the estima-
tion tool. This is expected to yield improved estimates, as right now, only the two
ﬁrst natural frequencies are used.
A future big step would be to initiate collaboration with a wind turbine operator.
Data of soil properties and natural frequencies of the turbine could be gathered, which
could then be used to investigate and improve the predictions of the analytical model.
In turn, this would improve the boundary estimation and prove its value to the turbine
operator.
From here the boundary stiﬀness values that will require structures to be serviced
and repaired should be identiﬁed. This should then be used to set threshold values for

any components are damaged.
