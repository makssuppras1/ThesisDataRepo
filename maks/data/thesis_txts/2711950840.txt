Abstract
Streamer discharges are thin ionized cold plasma channels.
They are the initialisers for lightning
leaders, arcs, sparks and transient luminous events. The purpose of this research is to implement a
particle model to describe negative streamer propagation using the Monte Carlo code. Models that
were used to run this theory are the Monte Carlo Collision model, and Particle-in-Cell model, and a
Poisson solver to calculate the electric field from the overall domain potential. The model used 10000
electrons and ions as a test case and tested the speed and particle update scheme. The result was a
3D visualization of the charge density at different iterations. This code was built with the premise of
speed and accuracy, given that particle models are more accurate than fluid models when it comes to
track individual particles. The code can be expanded to use more particle species and collision types,
but was made with the concept of future expansion. The electric field is consistently updating after
each iteration. The model was used on DTUs High-Performance-Cluster with the possibility of using
multiple GPUs to solve the Poisson equation and improve the run time.

1
Introduction
Streamers are thin ionized cold plasma channels, depending on it being a negative or positive streamer
changes the behavior and propagation and evolution of the streamer. The negative streamers propagate
against the electric field, the direction of the electron drift during their movement they collide with
the ambient gas molecules.
Depending on their collision type, elastic, inelastic and ionization the
properties of the free electrons changes. During ionization collision, the electron can transfer enough
energy to knock lose an electron thus creating a chain reaction known as an electron avalanche. The
positive streamer propagate with the electric field, this requires a supplement of free electrons granted
by photoionization or background ionization.
During collision the electrons will scatter at different angles.
Tracking individual electrons using
simulations requires a lot of computation power, however depending on the research different simulation
models can be used.
1.1
Events and models
Streamers are the initialiser of lightning leaders, arcs, sparks and transient luminous events (TLEs).
They have many applications within processing and medicine. Streamer modeling and the understand-
ing of streamer discharge is computationally challenging. Most models have been fluid models that
track the electron and ion density. In order to get a better understanding of the streamer discharge and
the electron avalanche, particle models are better suited. Particle models track the individual electrons
within the domain, it updates the individual electron parameter, calculates the density contribution,
and collision type for each electron. Due to the amount of computation power needed, particle models
are not able to follow a real amount of electrons, but the accuracy and resolution of the simulation is
better. Using fluid models gives rise to stochastic errors since it is not able to calculate the collision
type for each particle. This paper will therefore focus on the implementation of a particle model to
describe negative streamer propagation.
1.2
Model Criteria and Outlook
For a particle model the ions are considered heavy and therefore stationary. This results in a space
charge electric field between the free moving electrons and the ions. The space charge field contributes
to the ambient electric field, thus changing the acceleration of each particle depending on their location
within the domain and the amount of particles, all having their own local electric field. The model will
be made for 3D simulations of electrons, but with the ability of having more species added to it in the
future. The Poisson solver only works with a 3D grid that has equal side lengths with fixed boundary
conditions. The x and y plane will have an electric field of zero at the edges. The z plane has a fixed
potential at the edges, 0 for z=0 and for z=max the ϕ = E0/L where L is the length of the domain
and E0 is the background electric field.

Theory
The following sections contain the underlying theory for the methods chosen later in the paper, start
out by describer the a streamer and define the two models used to simulate streamer discharge before
discussing the use of a particle update method, the collision types, and the use of a Poisson solver.
2.1
Plasma Events and Models
Different events and applications within plasma physics have streamers as the precursor and there
are different types of plasma models depending on the field of research and the equations needed to
simulation the propagation of streamers within an ambient gas. Most common models are the particle
models and the fluid models.
2.1.1
Streamers
Streamers are fast growing ionized channels going through an ambient gas. There are three criteria for
the formation of streamers, free electrons, an ambient field to accelerate the electrons and an ambient
gas for the electrons to collide with. The propagation of the streamers can be both negative and
positive. A negative streamer discharge is where the enhanced electric field at the tip propagate in the
opposite same direction of the ambient electric field due to the electrons being accelerated towards the
head of the streamer. In order for the propagating electrons to cause an electron avalanche the electric
field needs to be above the breakdown field1[Christoph Köhn NeubertChristoph Köhn Neubert2019]
such that there are more ionization events over attachment event. Ionization events are when free elec-
trons collide with the ambient gas, ionizing it, and creating new electrons. Attachment events happen
when the electrons attach themselves to the molecules thereby losing electrons. For positive streamer
discharge, the enhanced field tip at the bottom is due to electron from outside being attracted to the tip,
however this requires photoionization where molecules gets ionized by the photons energy when collid-
ing with molecules. Despite the fact that positive streamers propagate against the electron drift velocity
(with the electric field lines), in air the positive streamers appear more easily than negative streamers
and their propagation speed is higher than negative streamer[Wormeester, Pancheshnyi, Luque, Nijdam EbertWormeester
2.1.2
Particle Models
Most plasma simulations regarding particles and streamers are done using a particle-in-cell (PIC) and
Monte Carlo collision (MCC) model. The benefit of the PIC/MCC model is that they are able to track
each particle individually, this includes their velocity, position, and collision as well as their position
within the grid. This is important if the desire is to keep track of each electron and their individual
contribution to the density and electric field calculations. The disadvantage of particle models is the
computation power needed to run simulations with a lot of particles.[Chao LiChao Li] More particles
means more memory needed to store the information regarding each individual particle.
Particle
models can only simulate the early stages of streamers such as the avalanche phase if it uses real
particle numbers. At later streamers stages, the PIC/MCC models need to introduce super-particles,
which is multiple particles having their attributes added together into a single particle.
Creating
super-particles lowers the resolution of the model and introduces numerical and stochastic errors. The
numerical error becomes apparent if the model includes particle heating due to their energy, since
super-particles will have an increased energy and density. When calculating the collisions for each
particles, the model encounters a stochastic error when calculating the collision for a super-particle
given that it is built up of many individual particles, which statistically all would have different collision
outcomes.

2.1.3
Fluid Models
Compared to the particle model, the fluid model can not track individually.
The fluid model ap-
proximates electron as part of a density patch that it tracks. By calculating the continuous density
difference, it can calculate the increase or decrease of particles and track the progression of the streamer
as a whole. The fluid model is computationally more efficient than the particle model but also less
accurate on a particle scale level.[Chao LiChao Li]

2.3
Collisions
Free electrons will start moving when in the presence of an electric field. When the free electrons are in
an ambient gas they will collide with the gas molecules in different ways, depending on the properties
of the electrons and ambient gas molecules. As the electrons accelerate they build up energy which

during the collisions gets transferred.
There are different types of collisions.
Elastic collision are
collision where the kinetic energy and momentum is conserved. When the electron collides with the
air molecule the energy is shared between the two.
Inelastic collision doesn’t conserve its kinetic energy. Depending on the collision type with nitrogen
or oxygen atoms, the loss in energy changes. Collisions with nitrogen and oxygen atoms excite them
to certain energy levels depending on the inelastic collision, whether it is due to electronic excitations,
rotations or vibrations2.
Ionisation collision is where the electron energy transfer, upon collision, is high enough to free an
electron from an atom. Ionisation collisions are necessary for a patch of electrons to accumulate more
propagating electrons, thus reaching a streamer stage which are the cause for electron avalanches.
2.4
Poisson
Initially the electron and ions have the same position, therefore the net charge is zero. As the electrons
move away from the stationary ions a space charge field starts to form and affects the electric field.
The Poisson solver calculates the potential difference, from the charge density, which can be converted
into the electric field for each Cartesian vector, used to update the velocity of the free electrons.

3.2
Workspace and Important Parameters
It is important that the 3D workspace has equal sides, as the Poisson solver calculates the potential in
a cube. The upside to increasing the size of the domain is the increased resolution as the size of each
grid cell gets smaller. Given the small and quick evolution of streamers the size of the grid has been
chosen to be around 2.5cm. Therefore, if you increase the grid size you have to decrease the size of
each grid cell. The disadvantage to increasing the domain is the memory. As each array will use more
memory when the domain increases.
The Poisson solver is the part of the model that takes the longest to run. Depending on the fine tuning
required for the simulation, the number of iterations and the convergence criteria can be changed.

update regarding position, velocity, and collision. The first time step is restricted using the Courant

3.4
How to Run
In order to run the code, a few things has to be defined in both the moduleglobal.cpp and the main.cpp.
Initially the background electric field E0 has to be set a long with the amount of electrons and ions.
Next, is the domain size and grid size (gridDelta). The basis of the model is that the domain length
is about 2.5cm, it is not a criteria for it to work though, the domain and grid size can be changed to
improve either computation speed by lowering the domain size and increasing the grid size, or make

species number (0 for electrons, 1 for ions). The code has been run using the DTU High Performance

run by typing MP_NUM_THREADS=<num_threads> ./main[RydahlRydahl].

Results and Discussion
The following section compares the analytical models with simulation results and discusses the particle
charge density results from the simulation. The results simulated using the model had 10000 electrons
and ions and without a gas mixture but using the cross section for N2 The model tracks the particles in
a 3D grid separated in grid cells with an equal distance between. The size of the grid is defined by the
amount of grid cells, which when multiplied by the distance between gives the length. Each grid used
in the simulation consists of three array. The domain for this simulation was 300·300·300 which equals
an array size of 27,000,000. In the early stages of the model, the output was ASCII outputs which
requires a lot of computation power. Instead of printing in ASCII the grid output containing density,
potential, and electric field information became VTK files. VTK files can be visualized in ParaView,
however, ParaView also has its limitations. By choosing a domain with each side having 300+ grid
cell the application would crash. Despite lowering the run time and overcoming the computational
bottleneck the model was unable to have a bigger domain.

Poisson Solver
The Poisson solver was validated using the case problem
u(x, y, z) = sin(x)sin(y)sin(z)
in a domain with u = zero on the horizontal planes with a gradient that are non-zero in the vertical
plane. The right hand side of equation 16.
f(x, y, z) = −3sin(x)sin(y)sin(z)
By testing this case in Matlab against the Poisson solver made in the programming language C and
then visualized in ParaView 5.9.0. it showed that the solver converges towards the same values as the
true solution calculated using Matlab[RydahlRydahl].

5
Conclusion and Outlook
The aim of the project was to create a particle-in-cell Monte Carlo code, which could be used to simulate
electron avalanches and streamer discharges in air on a supercomputer. The code has the potential of
working as a electron avalanche simulator. Currently, it only has two types of collisions elastic and
inelastic, but it has the potential of having more added. The code is a functioning PIC/MCC code,
that creates and updates particles their parameters and tracks them individually compared to a fluid
model which follows the density. The disadvantage to the model is the resolution is restricted due to
the size of the domain. Increasing the size grants computation problems in the form of the time it
takes to print ASCII and the visualization program ParaView crashing due to the large domain.
The model can be expanded and certain aspects should be changed in order to improve the simulations.
The starting particle patch should be a Gaussian distributed patch, but is instead a random position
with max radius of 3·∆x from origin. When calculating the the collision index the model does not take
account for the altitude. The air density should get smaller as altitude increases. The model should
also have a proper air gas mixture when determining the cross section for the collision index, instead
of only using N2. After the model gets the ionization collisions it needs to, a way of re-sampling the
amount of electrons once the avalanche begins.
