Abstract
Flow meters play a very important role wherever there is a requirement of flow
measurement. Over the years, different types of flow meters have been developed and
currently the Coriolis flow meter is said to be the most accurate among all of them.
Coriolis flow meters are those flow meters which makes use of the Coriolis principle to
accurately measure the mass flow rate. These flow meters are also capable of measuring
density very accurately which in turn helps in calculating the volumetric flow rates.
Flow meter is an essential part of a ship fuel system. It helps in monitoring the
amount of fuel consumed, inventory control and throttle management. For a company
like TORM, which operates a lot of vessels to transport both oil products and dry cargo
to many places around the world, accurate flow meter readings are very important
because many financial decisions like hull cleaning, refueling the storage tank etc are
taken based on these readings and the Specific Fuel Oil Consumption Value (SFOC).
SFOC is amount of fuel required to produce 1kW power by the shaft.
TORM has provided data of six vessels. A basic procedure for data analysis was
followed. During the descriptive analysis, it was found that two vessels out of the six
vessels had to be excluded from the data analysis because the quality of the data was
very bad and one vessel among them was considered as the benchmark vessel for any
comparisons because the quality of the data was really good. It was also found that
the flow meter reading were not trustable in any of the vessels and therefore, TORM
decided to base their decision on the shaft power data as these readings were more
trustable.
Many methods were applied and it was found that certain methods were not
applicable to this case or did not produce any major results. These methods are not
included in this report. But by using methods like mean shift and process capability
index, trends in the data were found out in the benchmark vessel and compared with
the other three vessels to see if they followed the same trend or not. The accuracy of the
flow meter was checked using SFOC as the parameter. The ideal SFOC was supposed
to be around 200kg/kWh and therefore, the limits were ± 15kg/kWh. At the end of the
data analysis, it was found that the flow meter data are always accurate and reliable
when the readings are above 1200kg/h (or shaft power generated is above 6000kW) so
as to produce an SFOC value within or close to the range.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Shipping is considered as one of the most fuel efficient forms of transport.
As per
January 1st 2016, there are over 50,000 ships in the world [1]. Therefore, a proper track
of the fuel consumption as well as the disposal of the related waste should be kept as it
accounts for millions of dollars each year. An accurate and reliable monitoring system
for the ships is thus very important and opens great opportunities to determine how
efficiently the vessels are running.
The KPIs of the shipping companies are based on the fuel consumption, or the
power generated or the Specific Fuel Oil Consumption (SFOC). A lot of decisions
such as cleaning of the hull, refueling of the tank etc are taken by the company based
on these readings and therefore, it is important that these readings are accurate and
reliable. Flow meters are used to monitor the fuel consumed by the main engine and
the auxiliary engine (generators). In this thesis, the focus is on the flow meters which
monitor the fuel consumption by the main engine.
Based on a variation analysis of available flow meter data from different vessels,
this project therefore investigates the accuracy and reliability of individual flow meter
readings.
1.1
Case study company: TORM
TORM is a pure product shipping company that owns and operates in two main
segments: tanker and bulk carriers. Tanker segment transports refined oil products and
the bulk segment transports dry cargo commodities (grain, coal etc).
Four main goals of TORM are [2]:
1. Putting customers first to achieve full customer reach and flexibility in order to
outperform available earnings benchmarks.
2. Ensuring quality in everything for example by improving technical and commercial
tradability and delivering optimized processes across the entire value chain.
3. Acting responsibly by maintaining the highest safety, environment and CSR
standards.
4. Operating in a cost-efficient manner.
==PAGE:18==
2
1 Introduction
One of the aspects which will help TORM operate in a cost-efficient manner is by proper
fuel management.
1.1.1
Why is fuel management important?
The main goals of fuel management is to reduce the fuel consumption, increase the
operational efficiency and and improve the fleet management. This is done by constantly
measuring, monitoring and reporting the fuel consumed in the vessels.
A correct
measurement of fuel is needed because:
1. Fuel Monitoring: When there is a fuel monitoring system onboard, it will help
the captain and crew members to keep a track of the fuel consumption of the main
and the auxiliary engine from the control room. It will also help them to see the
amount of fuel left in the tank. Such constant monitoring of fuel will help the crew
to make decisions that impacts the fuel consumption and efficiency positively.
2. Inventory Control: The accuracy of measuring the fuel tank manually is low
and takes time.
Therefore, fuel tanks need to be installed with sensors for a
continuously monitoring the fuel level in the tanks. Also, by installing flow meters
on the transfer lines, the consumption of the fuel can be tracked and logged often.
3. Accounting: To avoid fuel thefts, it is important to monitor the fuel transferred
onboard and couple it with the fuel consumed by the main and auxiliary engines.
In order to do this, flow meter should be installed in every transfer line on the ship.
By comparing this data with the burn rates, it can be checked whether the fuel
is secretly transferred off the vessel or not. Also, the costs to ship and container
rates can also be set according to the fuel consumption of the engines.
4. Throttle Management: Fuel usage is mainly controlled by how much throttle
the engine should produce. The amount of fuel consumed is not always reduced by
lowering the engine speed. There are other factors other than speed of the engine
which affect the fuel consumed by the vessel. They are wind, current, condition of
the hull, load and the condition of the propulsion system.

Marine engines are designed to burn heavy residual oil which is the final by-product after

Important components in a ship fuel system are:
1. Fuel oil service tank: There are usually two fuel oil service tanks on board, one
for heavy fuel oil and the other for diesel fuel oil. The fuel oil is used for both
propulsion and for generator systems. Each tank should have a capacity to operate
for at least eight hours, at sea, at maximum continuous rating of the propulsion
plant and/or generating plant associated with the tank [4].
2. Quick closing valve: Quick closing valve is an emergency valve used to cut-off the
fuel flow from the service tank. This can be done by simple pull wire mechanism
or hydraulically.
3. Suction filter, supply pumps and pressure control valves: Suction filters,
made from fine metal gauze, removes particles which could damage the supply
pumps. These filters can be cleaned and reused. Supply pump raises the pressure
to 7 bar. A pressure control valve is used to keep this discharge pressure constant.
If the pressure valve develops a fault, a manual bypass can be used.
4. Backflushing filter: The pumped fuel passes through a backflushing filter (25
micron) [3]. These filter have the ability to clean themselves, i.e. when the filter

clogs, a pressure sensor initiates backflushing so that the filter clean themselves.
A fuel oil drain tank is used to collect this backflushed oil with sediments where
it can be recovered and purified [3].
5. Fuel flow meter: Flow meter is used to monitor the quantity of fuel used.
6. Fuel oil buffer tank or mixing column: In this tank or mixing column, the
fuel which passes through the flow meter is mixed with the fuel returned from the
engine.
7. Fuel oil circulating pump: This pump is used to pump the fuel oil by raising
the pressure to 8 bar.
8. Heater and viscosity control: Heaters are used to raise the temperature of the
fuel. A viscotherm is used to help maintain the optimum viscosity required for
injection by controlling the heater.
9. Final filter:
The final filter ensures that fuel oil is free from sediments or
contaminants which can damage fuel pumps, injectors and cylinder lining.
10. Engine fuel pump and injectors: From the inlet rail, fuel enter the fuel pump
(one pre cylinder) which delivers fuel at high pressure (250+ bar) to the fuel
injector [3]. A return rail is attached with the fuel pump so as to return the excess
fuel back to the buffer tank.
11. Pressure valve on return line: The pressure valve is used to reduce the pressure
to the required value before it is sent to the buffer tank.
2.2
Flow meters
A device used to measure the flow rate or quantity of fluid flowing through a pipe is called
flow meter. They are also referred by other names such as flow gauge, flow indicator,
liquid meter etc.
There are mainly three types of flow meter:
1. Positive Displacement or Volumetric flow meter: Positive displacement
flow meter or volumetric flow meter are devices which measures flow by passing
a volume of fluid with each revolution. The flow rate is measured in volume per
unit time (e.g. m3/s). In these flow meters, the internally moving components are
locked hydraulically with the volume of fluid moving through the flow meter. The
advantage of such a construction is that the flow meter can measure intermittent
flows, very low flow rates and liquids of almost any viscosity.
The output signal of a volumetric flow meter is directly related to the volume of
fluid passing through the flow meter. The types of flow meter which fall in the
category of volumteric flow meters are bi-rotor types (gear, oval gear, helical gear),
rotating disc, reciprocating piston type and oscillating or rotary piston type flow
==PAGE:22==
6

meters. Figure 2.3 shows a schematic diagram of a rotating positive displacement
flow meter.
Figure 2.3: Rotating positive displacement flow meter [5]
2. Mass flowmeter: Mass flow meters are devices which directly measures the mass
of the fluid flowing through the flow meter. The flow rate is measured in mass
per unit time (e.g. kg/s). The output signal is directly related to the mass of the
fluid passing through the flow meter. There are two types of flow meters which fall
in this category: Thermal and Coriolis mass flow meters. An advantage of using
mass flow meter is that the mass or weight of the fluid does not vary depending
on the temperature or pressure. Figure 2.4 shows a Coriolis mass flow meter.
Figure 2.4: Coriolis Mass Flow Meter [6]
3. Velocity flow meter: These flow meters measures the velocity of the fluid passing
through the flow meter. The flow rate is measured in length per unit time (e.g.
m/s). There are various types of velocity flow meter:
• Electromagnetic
• Ultrasonic
• Turbine, propeller and paddle wheel or Pelton wheel

• Vortex shedding and Sonar
• Target and vane
• Variable area and rotameter
• Orifice plate, open channel, flow nozzle, laminar, Venturi and Pitot tube
There are no flow meters which are suitable for all the types of applications. It is
important to know the flow specifications in order to select a particular flow meter for
the application. Things to know before selecting a flow meter are:
• What type of fluid is measured, gas or liquid?
• What is the minimum and maximum flow rate?
• What is the required accuracy?
• Temperature and viscosity of the fluid.
• Compatibility of fluid with materials of the construction.
• Maximum pressure and the allowable pressure drop.
• Is the location where the meter will be installed hazardous?
• Is it a continuous or intermittent fluid flow?
• What is the type of output signal required?
Because of the high accuracy of Coriolis mass flow meter and it’s independence
on temperature and pressure, Coriolis flow meter are substituting other types of flow
meters in the ship fuel systems.
Maritime companies can incur huge losses because
of the wrong readings from a flow meter. The amount of fuel consumed on board is
directly related to the overall performance. The better the performance, the lower will
be the fuel consumed. An offset of 1% on a vessel consuming an average or 100 tons a
day equals an addition requirement of 365 tons a year. For instance, if the marine fuel
oil price is US$ 200/t, then the company spends extra US$ 73,000 [7].
In this thesis, the study is focused on Yokogawa RCCS36 Coriolis mass flowmeter
which is used on board in the fuel system of the TORM ships.
2.3
Coriolis Flow Meter
Coriolis flow meters are those meters which makes use of the principle of Coriolis force.
These flow meters directly measure both mass flow rate and density of the fluid passing
through the instrument with high accuracy. Re-calibration is not required for these flow
meters to handle different fluid or when process conditions change [6].

2.3.1
History
Over the past years, Coriolis flow meter has been one of the most important areas of
development in flow measurement. These developments are because of the advances in
theoretical modelling, signal processing, computing, facilities for testing and acceptance
by various industries.
The first flow meter which appeared to make use of the Coriolis principle was
described by Kollsman and the patent was published on 8 July 1952. The flow meter
made use of a motor to drive the impeller into rotation with interaction with the
incoming fluid flow and a separate rotor was used to sense force or torque imparted
by the accelerated flow. Figure 2.5 shows the flow meter described by Kollsman.
Figure 2.5: Flow meter as described by Kollsman [8]
In another patent published on 6 January 1953, Pearson [8] described a flow meter
with a single flow tube in a coil shape where oscillation is the optimal means for mass
flow measurement. Figure 2.6 shows the flow meter Pearson described.
==PAGE:25==
2.3 Coriolis Flow Meter

Figure 2.6: Flow meter as described by Pearson [8]
In 1967, Sipin described two designs for flow meter. In the first design, motor was
used to oscillate a tube and a strain gauges or a torque motor was used to obtain the
flow measurement. In the second design, an electromagnetic driver was used to oscillate
the tube and the motion was detected by two magnetic pick-up sensors. It was noticed
that the inlet bellow will tend to lag the vibratory motion and the outlet bellow tends
to lead the vibratory motion. Smith [8] developed a Coriolis flow meter which is similar
to the modern day Coriolis flow meter. A simple theoretical model was used to relate
the mass flow rate, Q, to the time delay between inlet and outlet motion sensors, ∆t.
Figure 2.7 shows the U-shaped flow meter used by Smith.
Figure 2.7: U-shaped coriolis flow meter [8]

2.8. They were arranged in the shape of a tuning fork. The advantage of using two
identical vibrating loops is that much less power is needed to oscillate the two tubes at
their natural frequency.
Figure 2.8: Coriolis flow meter with two U-shaped tubes [8]
In 1985, Smith and Cage [8] described a flow meter where two U-shaped tubes formed
two parallel flow paths (Figure 2.9) unlike the other two U-shaped tube flow meter which
used single flow path. The advantages of using twin flow path were achieving higher flow
rate with better sensitivity, dynamically balanced pair, better decoupling due to floating
reference to each other, reduced overall meter size and less pressure loss.

In 1986, Mizerak used two straight parallel tubes instead of two U-shaped tubes.
He noticed that the twin tube design helps to reduce the common node vibration noise
and thus helps in improving the performance. Also, the advantages of using a straight
parallel tubes were ease of assembly, compact in size, ease of installation, easy to scale
up or down in size and less density sensitivity.
The model of Coriolis flow meter used by Smith and Cage (Figure 2.9) is the one
which is closest to the modern-day Coriolis flow meter.
2.4
Basic operating principle
The two U-shaped tubes are oscillated at their resonant frequency, which is often called
as the drive or working frequency. When there is no flow through the tubes, the inlet
and outlet portions oscillate in phase with each other. But when there is a flow, this
equilibrium in the oscillation is disturbed because of the Coriolis force. The Coriolis
force tends to decelerate the movement of the oscillating tube, whereas at the outlet, the
Coriolis force tends to accelerate movement, and thus the tubes oscillate at a frequency
known as Coriolis frequency. Figure 2.10 shows a cross-sectional view of a tube in a
Coriolis flow meter.
==PAGE:28==

Figure 2.10: Cross-sectional view of the tube in a Coriolis flow meter [8]
The general equation used to calculate the mass flow rate is:
qm = K × (td −to) [8]
(2.2)
where, K is the flow calibration factor related to the sensitivity of flow measurement
which is dependent on both structural properties (e.g.
Young’s modulus) and fluid
properties, td is the time delay when there is a flow and to is the time delay when there
is no flow.
Flow sensor (or flow transducer) and flow converter (or flow transmitter) are the
two main components of modern Coriolis flow meter.
Flow sensor is an assembly
which consists of flow tube (one, two or four flow tubes), drive system, motion sensors,
additional sensors and supporting structure and the flow converter is an electronic
control system which receives and processes signals from the flow sensor, provides drive
to the flow sensor and gives an output of the measured variables [8]

Performance of a Coriolis flow meter consists of two terms: a base accuracy and a zero

stability (or zero offset) is the measure of meter’s sensitivity, which is normally specified
either by a mass flow rate value or a percentage of a known nominal value [8].
Addition of the base accuracy and zero stability (zs) gives the maximum allowable
errors or maximum permissible errors [8], which results in a smooth (C1 continuity1)

2.6
Factors that influence a Coriolis Flow Meter
During the development of the Coriolis flow meters, various factors that influences the
performance of Coriolis flow meters were found out. The effects of these factors are
usually minimized by either changing the tube design or by special installations [10].
Below are the factors that influence the Coriolis flow meter:
1. Temperature:
The mass flow reading can be affected by the changing
temperatures of the fluid and housing which affects the elastic properties of the
Coriolis mass flow meters. Temperature change can also affect the zero offset and
the performance of the electronic components of the flow meter to some degree.
This drift in the electronic components will usually lead to changes in the zero
offset of the flow meter. Special electronic design can be used to minimize these
effects.
==PAGE:30==
14
2 Literature Study
2. In-Line Pressure: A slight deformation of the tube because of the change in the
in-line pressure can influence the stiffness and the stress conditions of the system
which in turn affects the reading of the flow meter. The manufacturer of the flow
meter states the influence of the in-line pressure in the operation manual and can
be corrected for. The in-line pressure is as low as 0.5% for 100 bar for an optimized
design of a Coriolis flow meter [10].
3. Vibration: Vibration is usually caused by a neighbouring device, pumping system
or could be a flow-induced vibration. External vibrations are typically below 180
Hz. Measurement error can only occur if the external vibration is close to the
working frequency or Coriolis frequency of the Coriolis flow meter. Therefore, it is
preferred to have a Coriolis flow meter with high frequencies so that they are less
sensitive to the external vibrations [10]. Using flexible piping or vibrations isolating
pipe supports can also reduce the effect of external vibrations. It was found by
Clark and Cheessewright [10] that these errors in Coriolis flow meter are caused
due to the failure of the phase measurement algorithm and using high-performance
filtering techniques, accuracy of the Coriolis flow meter measurement can be
maintained even when there are external vibrations or pulsating flow.
4. Corrosion and Erosion: Wall thickness of the tube can deteriorate because
of both corrosion and erosion and this affects the stiffness of the tube leading
to incorrect mass flow rate readings.
Corrosion can be reduced by choosing a
Coriolis flow meter with appropriate tube material and erosion can be reduced by
maintaining low flow rates.
5. Humidity: The components of the Coriolis flow meter are sealed in a housing
and therefore are isolated from the atmospheric conditions. However, if there is a
damage in the seal or the housing, the humidity causes condensation on the tubes
and the electronic components leading to corrosion and failure in the components.
6. Fluid Velocity: Fluid velocity can cause a minor effect in the accuracy of the flow
meter reading and does not necessarily require any correction. But the Coriolis
flow meter can account for this minor effect if the velocity of the fluid is known.
7. Flow Profile Effects: The effect of velocity profile is generally very small. But,
the presence of a secondary fluid velocity field at low Reynolds number flow tends
to make the Coriolis flow meter under-read.
8. Reynolds Number: As Reynolds number reduces, error in the flow rate reading
increases.
This is mainly because of the oscillatory secondary flow affects the
sensitivity of the flow meter. Also, when the flow changes from laminar to turbulent
(Reynolds number range 1000 - 5000), the sensitivity of the Coriolis flow meter
changes slightly (0.1% - 0.5%) depending upon the tube geometry [10].
9. Bubble Effect: The presence of bubbles allows “splashing” inside the tube, which
generates noise and changes the energy needed for the tube vibration. This causes
==PAGE:31==
2.7 Advantages of Coriolis flow meter
15
error in the flow meter reading and large cavities can lead to a complete failure of
the flow meter [11].
10. Acoustic or Compressibility Effect:
The compressibility effect can cause
an under-reading in the Coriolis flow meter because the effective mass which is
oscillating in the tube is less than the true mass which reduces the sensitivity.
From the fundamental physics, the stiffness because of the compressibility of the
fluid can introduce a resonance at a frequency higher than the drive frequency.
The magnitude of this high frequency is c/d, where c is the speed of the sound and
d is the internal diameter of the tube. Therefore, the compressibility effect will be
noticeable when there is a high drive frequency or when the compressibility-related
frequency is low. The main reason for this effect is the higher speed of fluid than
the tube unlike the ideal case where both move at the same speed.
11. Multi-phase flow: Multi-phase, especially the presence of air in fluid, can cause
error in the Coriolis flow meter reading. This error is caused because of the relative
movement or decoupling of the phases. Other effects of aeration are the bubble
effect, compressibility effect and non-symmetric damping effect [8].
12. Entrained Particles: The entrained particles generate a force on the oscillating
fluid which causes phase decoupling of the fluid and error in the flow meter
measurement [12].
2.7
Advantages of Coriolis flow meter
1. One of the main advantages of Coriolis flow meter is that it can measure mass flow
rate and density directly.
2. Coriolis flow meters are extremely accurate.
3. It can provide multi-variable outputs (flow rate, density and temperature) and
therefore, there are less instruments which should be installed, calibrated and
maintained.
4. These flow meters are bi-directional.
5. They have large turndown ratios.
This is advantageous for batch reactions,
multi-product fiscal transfer and process start-up applications.
6. Because there are no moving parts, these flow meters need less maintenance,
servicing and calibration and thereby enhancing safety.
7. Enhance installation safety as fewer pipe breaks are required to install these flow
meters.

2.8
Limitations of Coriolis flow meter
1. When compared to other types of flow meters, Coriolis flow meter are more
expensive (capital costs) and prices increases with size.
2. Available only in limited size ranges.
3. Cannot be used for liquids when there is significant amount of gas content.
Presence of gas content can cause huge error in the readings.

Types of data analysis can be described as follows:
1. Descriptive: In this type of analysis, the data is summarized based on single
set of data without any further interpretation of the data. It could also tell the
analyst about the three main characteristics of the data i.e. it helps understanding
the distribution of the data, the central tendency and the dispersion of the data.
The distribution gives an information such as the percentage of data above or
below a particular value. The central tendency tells about the mean, median and
mode of the data and dispersion gives an idea of how the values are spread around
the central tendency.
2. Exploratory: Here, the data is explored to find some correlations or trends
between the variables so as to generate some ideas. Mostly, graphical techniques
are used to explore the data more so as to get get better information about the
basic trend of the data, identify the important variables and detect noise in the
data. The graphical techniques generally consists of plotting the raw data as it
is, statistical plots like mean plots of standard deviation plots and using multiple
plots per page to help increase the chances of pattern recognition.

3. Inferential: In an inferential type of data analysis, it is checked whether the
results found in exploratory type data analysis holds for the data in the future.
One of the main questions to ask during an inferential data analysis is whether
the distribution is normal or non-normal.
If the distribution follows a normal
distribution, then parametric tests are implemented and if the distribution follows
a non-normal distribution, non-parametric tests are implemented.
4. Predictive: The inferential data analysis is used to find an inference on the data
as a whole while the predictive uses a subset of the data to predict the effect
on another measurement or variable. Here, techniques like machine learning and
predictive modelling are used to along with the current and old data to make
predictions about the future events.
5. Causal: Causal data analysis is used to find the average effects between the
different variables.
Causal data analysis can be carried out either through
experimentation or statistical research.
A variation is one variable which is
assumed to cause variation in the other variable is observed and then change
in the other variable is measured.
6. Mechanistic: Unlike causal data analysis, mechanistic is a more deterministic
approach i.e. it seeks to find how a change in one variable will always produce a
specific change in the other variable.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion And Discussions
From the descriptive and exploratory data analysis, it found out that the quality of the
data for two out of the six vessels (Vessels 3 and 5) were not good i.e. Vessel 3 had a
lot of readings where shaft power is less than zero and Vessel 5 had a lot of readings
were the flow meter reading was zero but there was shaft power reading. At the same
time, Vessel 1 was assigned to be the benchmark vessel for any comparisons because it
has the least amount of errors in the data.
It was also found out that the flow meter data were not most trustable data. This
was found out because of some unnecessary peaks seen in the flow meter data (Figure
4.3, 4.4, 4.5 and 4.6) which did not reflect in the shaft power data. This was verified
by plotting GPS data along with the flow meter and shaft power and it was seen that
the GPS data and shaft power data were in sync where as the peaks seen in flow meter
data were not reflected in GPS data.
Accuracy of flow meter checked by using mean shift. Mean shift of SFOC helped
in showing that SFOC value are close or within the limits at higher flow rates. Also,
by using mean shift and process capability index, it was found out that the data were
reliable only for flow rates above 1200kg/h or shaft power greater than 6000kW. The
reason of data being not accurate and reliable when the engine is just started or engine
is being stopped could be because of the vibration of the engine during these conditions.
Based on the descriptive and exploratory analysis, TORM has decided to base their
decision making on the readings from the torque meter instead as the torque meter has
a much less volatile behaviour and accuracy is high.
