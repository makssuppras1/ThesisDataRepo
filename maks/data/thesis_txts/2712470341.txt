Abstract
DMI and other national and international meteorological institutions use numerical weather
prediction (NWP) models to predict precipitation. However, these models tend to misplace or
even miss especially local and intense precipitation, which can create flash-floods. During the
last decades, research on increasing grid resolution of NWP models are investigated as these
models might improve the forecast.
This study compares the predictive skill of two deterministic NWP models for extreme rain-
fall events in Denmark, excluding Bornholm. The two models, an operational NWP model,
NEA, and a high-resolution experimental NWP model, DK750, have been compared on 7 hours
forecast horizon. Respectively, these NWP models differ by their grid resolution of 2.5km and
0.75km. This near-nowcasting horizon is important for meteorologists when considering making
a warning of a possible extreme precipitation event to the public and to agencies who can react.
This study aims to investigate the potential of the high-resolution experimental NWP model
for predicting extreme rainfall. This is done by handling, processing, and interpreting NWP
data provided by DMI. The processing includes using traditional and neighborhood verifica-
tion tools to assess forecast differences compared to radar quantitative precipitation estimates
(QPE). The interpretation especially involves the NWP model’ roles in the operational cloud-
burst warning system.
Results indicate an improved forecast for most extreme rainfall events investigated using DK750.
Across fifteen events, DK750 appears to improve the representation of location, peak intensity,
and deformation of precipitation with substantial visual improvement for the latter two. The
Fractions Skill Score (FSS) improvement is on average 15% (± 13%) across intensities of 1-20
mm
hr and scales of 2.5-320 km. The largest improvement when using DK750 is for lower scales
and intensities. General improvements are also seen when removing bias, where DK750 appears
to be relative best for events with a lower wet area ratio, while the reverse is true for NEA.
DK750, unlike NEA, tends to underestimate structure and amplitude, which was seen using
the object-based verification score structure, amplitude, and location (SAL).
There is a potential for using DK750 in the operational cloudburst warning system, where
the most considerable potential is seen for smaller and more spread precipitation cells. NEA
appears to be more constant in skill across all lead times, suggesting that the largest potential
for using DK750 is within a nowcast horizon. Focus on the future should be to improve spatial
skills further and improve forecasts of small and intense precipitation cells.

Introduction
Accurately predicting precipitation is an important service for society, businesses, and individ-
uals. Extreme rainfall like cloudburst and severe rain events can inflict material and financial
losses, which is the case when these events form over heavily populated areas. For example, a
cloudburst event over the Copenhagen area on the 2nd of July 2011 caused damage of more than
6 billion DKK for insurance companies. The high cost can partly be assigned to the forecast,
as an earlier warning could have been made (Jensen et al., 2015). By improving the forecast
accuracy of these extreme events, material and financial loss can be mitigated by improved
real-time control of urban drainage systems, and flash-flood mitigation tools used needs time
before effective use (Beredskabsstyrelsen (2018),Jensen et al. (2015)). As climate predictions
suggest that there will be more significant frequent extreme rainfall in a warming climate, this
could increase the value of accurate forecasts (Rasmussen (2016),Bauer et al. (2015)).
Numerical weather prediction (NWP) models are used operationally to predict precipitation in
the future by simulating atmospheric processes. This simulation happens by discretizing the
atmosphere in a three-dimensional grid within a given area called the domain. Atmospheric
processes like turbulence and radiation have different characteristic scales (Bauer et al., 2015).
Convective clouds, which may result in suddenly appearing precipitation due to the lifting of
warm air (B¨ulow, 2015), is a phenomenon that exists within a scale of a few kilometers down
to even less than a kilometer. Following the recent decades’ research, decreasing the size of
individual grid cells can produce more realistic forecasts as there are ultimately more grid cells
in the domain to simulate numerous low-scale phenomenons (Chan et al., 2018). The grid cell
size is known as the grid resolution, divided into a horizontal (latitude and longitude) and a
vertical grid resolution. The current scale of horizontal grid resolution investigated is a few
kilometers down to a few hundred meters. Models with a horizontal grid resolution of less than
a kilometer are popularly known as sub-kilometer NWP models (Gilleland et al. (2016),Ito et al.
(2017)). In this project, these are regarded as high-resolution, though the term high-resolution
is changing as models become constantly finer.
The difference between sub-kilometer NWP models and reality is heavily investigated. For
example, Fukui and Murata (2021) found that reducing the horizontal grid size down to 0.5 km
approached a precipitation distribution similar to radar rainfall estimates and could therefore
give a better representation of deep moist convection (DMC). However, indications of stagnat-
ing improvements are seen when reducing the horizontal grid size below the kilometer scale.
For example, Ito et al. (2017) found that despite implicitly including low-scale atmospheric
turbulence, the rainfall forecast was not improved noticeably. This might indicate that other

complications and challenges may follow by using an increased model resolution, which might
not be worth the cost and extra computational power. It is important to assess forecast accu-
racy to disentangle the mixed results found in literature.
Analyzing forecast accuracy can be done with a wide range of verification tools to verify the
model’s skill against ground truth. Looking at previous research, high-resolution NWPs are
often verified using neighborhood-based methods when the horizontal resolution is only a few
kilometers. These methods allow predictions to be slightly misplaced, removing the issue of
being punished for being wrong at two locations close to each other, known as the ”double
penalty” problem (Singhai et al., 2020).
The fractional skill score (FSS) is a highly used
neighborhood-based method which smooths precipitation clouds. Among others, this method
is used by Ito et al. (2017), and Jensen et al. (2015). Another neighborhood-based approach
uses an object-based approach, where the features of identified precipitation clouds are com-
pared. This can, for example, be done by using the Structure, Amplitude, and Location (SAL)
skill score (Zick, 2020). These and many other verification methods are available.
Focus in the coming years should be to assess whether NWP models in high-resolution res-
olution are the approach to more accurate forecasts. This project is set to aid the increase
in research regarding high-resolution NWP models. This is done by assessing and comparing
two different two NWP models on extreme precipitation events. (1) The current operational
NWP model used by DMI in their routine forecast, called NEA (North Europe Domain A),
covers the whole of Scandinavia and a larger part of the North Atlantic with a 2.5km grid size.
(2) An experimental high-resolution NWP setup, named DK750 hereon, covers only Denmark
and nearby water at a 750m grid spacing. The latter is not in use yet as the model is much
more computational demanding, but it is thought that it could improve the forecast of extreme
rainfall events. The two NWP model are compared on the first 7 hours of forecast - mainly
within the horizon of a so-called nowcast (< 6h). The investigation is based on comparing
rainfall estimated by DMIs radar network.

Purpose
This project aims to investigate the potential of using DK750 in DMI’s operational precipita-
tion forecast with NEA as current input. This potential is assessed by interpreting differences
in forecast skill. Other studies present their finding: High-resolution NWP models have the
potential to provide more reliable forecasts, especially for precipitation. Based on this, DK750
could increase forecast skill by improving simulations of small scales phenomena that can in-
duce extreme rainfall. The overall objective is to analyze and interpret differences in forecast
skill between DK750 and NEA on extreme rainfall events using appropriate verification tools
found in meteorological literature.
The central question to be answered in this thesis is:
• How do high-resolution NWP models compare to the operational forecast system in terms
of forecast skill on extreme rainfall events, including cloudbursts and severe rain?
Which can be divided into the following:
• How do numerical weather models forecast precipitation, and why might increased grid
resolution improve the forecast performance?
• Which verification methods and skill scores are appropriate for evaluating the performance
of high-resolution NWP?
• Does increased grid resolution in DMI’s operational NWP improve the subjective realism
of the simulated precipitation fields?
• How does high-resolution NWP affect the predictive skill of DMI’s nowcast in terms of
intensity and spatial scales of extreme rainfall events?
• For which types of precipitation events can DMI benefit from using high-resolution NWP,
and what should be the improvement focus in the future?
• What can the operational cloudburst warning system gain when using a high-resolution
NWP model, and what could be done differently?

Background Theory
This section will describe the relevant technologies and concepts used to fulfill the purpose. This
involves knowledge about how rainfall is detected using radar technology in Section 2.1, what
forecasting is, and specifically how NWP products estimate rainfall in the future in Section
2.2. Afterward, different tools for verifying forecasts will be introduced in Section 2.3. Finally,
different rainfall types will be introduced in Section 2.4 before going into detail on how DMI
sends out warnings before potential extreme precipitation events in Section 2.5.
2.1
DMIs radar network
A weather radar is a remote sensing tool frequently used by weather services worldwide for
monitoring precipitation. Its popularity is mainly due to its high spatial and temporal resolu-
tion and which can quickly provide precipitation estimates (Sokol et al., 2021). Weather radars
work by transmitting microwave pulses into the atmosphere, and since precipitation backscat-
ters this signal, the radar detects the reflectivity of the precipitation (Nielsen et al., 2014).
Different radar products, which all detect reflectivity, are produced by DMI. DMI operates
five C-band radars across Denmark. The C-band radars, referring to the frequency that the
radar transmits (Sokol et al., 2021), are placed in strategic locations to get measurements across
Denmark. For a C-band radar, reflectivity is measured at a range of up to 240 km. Quantitative
Precipitation Estimate (QPE) is a measure of the range limit before the quality of the data
starts to decrease because of attenuation of the signal. However, a reasonable QPE for C-band
radars is in range of 100-150 km (He et al. (2011),Sokol et al. (2021)).
The radar product used in this project is a composite made from all five radars. This product is
known as a composite pseudo-Constant Altitude Plan Position Indicator (PCAPPI), meaning
it produces one layer of reflectivity data. The radar sends pulses into the atmosphere at nine
different elevations ranging from 0.5-15 degrees. The recording that comes back from these
scans can project a reflectivity map in about 1 km height (Schleiss et al., 2020). An illustration
showing this process, produced by (Nielsen et al., 2014), can be seen in Appendix A. In some
places, the radar measurements overlap. Here, the maximum reflectivity value is chosen (Sokol
et al., 2021). The end result is a data file with a reflectivity field, e.g the whole domain, which
consist of individual reflectivity cells.

2.2
Forecasting and numerical weather predictions
Weather forecasting is the method of estimating the future atmospheric state at a given time.
There are many techniques to do so, and one of them is to use NWPs. The two NWP mod-
els investigated in this study predict future weather by simulating physical processes in the
atmosphere. This is done by using Navier-Stokes equations, the ideal gas law, and the first
law of thermodynamics. This system of equations allows NWP to forecast the evolution of
the atmosphere. Together, these equations are descriptors of changes in atmospheric processes
(e.g. wind, temperature etc.) due to global and local forcings (Bauer et al. (2015),Haupt et al.
(2017)). The atmospheric processes are approximated by spatial and temporal discretization.
Processes on a smaller scale than the discretization is the so-called unresolved scale of motion.
NWP models cannot resolve all processes, either due to the smallest processes being in the scale
of centimeters or simply due to unknown analytical representation. These unresolved processes,
which still have important interactions with resolved processes, need to be accounted for. Here,
conceptual equations, known as parameterizations, are used. The more processes that are re-
solved due to smaller discretizations, the more computing power is required by NWP models.
Some of the largest supercomputers in the world are already used for this matter (Bauer et al.
(2015),Haupt et al. (2017),Pedersen (2021)).
2.2.1
Types of NWP
In general, there exists mainly two types of NWP models that differ by spatial size - (1) global
models that extend to the entire earth and (2) local area models (LAMs) of a given regional
extent of finer resolution compared to the global models (Pedersen, 2021). The NWP models
used in this project are LAMs. However, many NWP systems exist with different configurations

2.2.2
Producing a forecast
The atmosphere is made up of many processes on different scales, where non-linear and tur-
bulent processes altogether create atmospheric chaos. Perfect forecasts of atmospheric states
are therefore impossible (Clark et al., 2009). However, to get as close to reality as possible,
the models need to be fed with high-quality observation. These observations, ranging from
rain gauge -, radar -, airplane -, and satellite data, can be used to assimilate model starting
values. In order to make sure that these data are of high quality, the various data types are
decoded and processed in order to be useful (Danish Meteorological Institute (2015),Gilleland
et al. (2016)).
The NWP models utilize the observational data by forcing the previous forecast closer to
the observations within that time frame. This analysis of the model and data relationship is
commonly assimilated within time windows and can range between several hours (Rabier and
Liu, 2003). This analysis and model correction is altogether called data assimilation (DA).
The DA is performed continuously to ensure that the model is close to reality.
This con-
tinuous update can minimize the overall error of the forecast, as small errors in the initial state
can quickly propagate. Therefore, a technique called variational DA is used. This technique fits
the model to the analysis by minimizing a cost function and is often done using observations
in three dimensions of space, known as three-dimensional variational DA (3D-var). Because of
this forcing, the model is not in stability at the time of forecast. There is a period before the
model reaches a statistical equilibrium due to the forcing of the correction. This time frame is
known as the model spin-up period. (Kealy, 2019). Since there is no time component in 3D-var,
observations in the time window are assumed to be valid at the time of the analysis (Rabier
and Liu, 2003). To minimize error further, it is possible to run multiple forecasts with varying
initial conditions, making the overall forecast probabilistic (Haupt et al. (2017), Rabier and
Liu (2003)).. However, this project will solely focus on using DA of the maximum probability
state - meaning a deterministic forecast.
The DA technique is different for the two NWP models investigated and therefore plays a
role in their overall difference. The DA window for NEA is 3 hours from -1h30min to 1h30min
relative to the nominal time of analysis, while it is 2 hours for DK750 from -1h to 1h relative to
the nominal time of analysis. However, due to a cut-off time, which shrinks the time the model

2.3
Forecast verification
Once the actual weather event is done, reality can be compared to the forecast. There are
various ways to quantify the skill of a model, but there is not one method that can give the
Page 7
==PAGE:17==
Master Thesis

complete picture (Sass and Yang, 2012). The method of assessing model skill is called verifica-
tion and is a metric for the closeness of a forecast compared to observations.
Traditional verification methods of a forecast include comparing each point in a grid matched
with reality (Zick, 2020). However, many models experience issues with heavy local rainfall, and
even rain gauges have difficulty catching all events, thus making the verification troublesome.
Also, though the visual inspection of the NWP output looks similar to observations, it can
be slightly shifted. Therefore, several high-resolution model investigations use a neighborhood
verification approach on the model forecast instead of having an exact match. This means
that it is possible to make a forecast that is close to being correct without being unreasonably
punished for a slight misplacement (Ebert, 2008).
There are many methods to limit the misplacement effect. Gilleland et al. (2009) lists four
ways to perform spatial verification that the majority of neighbor verification methods fit into.

2.4
Rainfall types
Rain forms primarily from three different weather mechanisms, which are all similar by an
upward lift of air where moisture condenses and then falls as rain. These are (1) convective
systems that produce rain from a vertical lift of a warmer air parcel. (2) Frontal systems, which
are the effects of cold and warm air coming into contact and subsequently pushing the warm
air upwards. (3) Rainfall generated from the orographic effect, where high topography forces
the air upwards.
The particular interest in convective systems lies in the sudden mechanisms behind the ver-
tical lift, producing local, intense, and short-lived precipitation. The same mechanism is also
what makes convective rainfall difficult to predict (B¨ulow, 2015). However, the other types of
precipitation-inducing weather events can also produce intense rainfall, usually over a longer
period. These scenarios are also relevant to forecasting accurately.
Convective precipitation usually happens when an air parcel near the surface is warmed. The
difference in temperature compared to the surrounding air creates a vertical lift. When the rise
is quick, and the moisture content is high, the moistue in the air parcel can produce intense
rainfall when cooled. The phenomenon is mainly seen during the summer period and is typi-
cally a local event and rarely lasts for longer than one hour (B¨ulow, 2015).
A warm front can produce intense rainfall if the warm air quickly rises over colder air. Con-
versely, a cold front can also produce rain as it pushes the warm air upwards but is usually
slower. As a result, the intensity of the rain is lower in a cold front, but the duration is longer
Page 9
==PAGE:19==
Master Thesis
Technical University of Denmark
and can sometimes produce precipitation for longer than a day. Suppose the duration of pre-
cipitation ranges between an hour to a day. In that case, it is suggested that the event is a mix
of precipitation types, which is usually the case for most precipitation (B¨ulow, 2015). Some
precipitation can also happen in the central part of Jutland from the orographic effect. Here
a ridge of higher altitude affects the overall mean precipitation of the area. This area has the
highest overall mean precipitation in Denmark, with around 800-1000 mm per year (B¨ulow,
2015).
2.5
DMIs’ cloudburst warning system
Extreme rainfall events, in this project limited to cloudbursts and severe rain, can produce
flash floods which can have financial and social costs if neither local authorities nor citizens
know beforehand. Therefore, DMI sends out a risk message up to 36 hours before a potential
event. When the event gets closer, and the risk of an extreme rainfall event is still high, this can
result in a cloudburst warning. This information is processed by meteorologists on duty who
interprets the forecast from global NWP models days in advance. If a forecast shows signs of
extreme precipitation, the forecast will be carefully investigated and assessed as it comes closer.
When the possible event is within days, NEA and its probabilistic counterpart, COMEPS, is
also used to gain better information and stronger confidence in what might happen. Since it is
known that the NWP models have trouble, especially with the location of precipitation several
hours into the future, the risk and potential warning sent out is within a band of potential dis-
placement. When adequate information indicates an extreme rainfall event, the risk or warning
is made. Other tools are also used to understand why the models produce these high-intensity
precipitation cells, as assessing why the modes predict these weather events gives the best un-
derstanding. This can, for example, be convective available potential energy (CAPE) forecast
tool. CAPE is a measure of thunderstorm likelihood due to the estimating the humidity in the
different layers of the atmosphere and thereby giving indications on upwards updraft of poten-
tial air parcels Adams and Souza (2009),(personal communication, L. Henriksen at DMI). This
investigations, in combination, is what can potentially lead to a warning.
Suppose there is confidence in an extreme rainfall event approaching. In that case, warnings
are sent out to the public on various platforms, as well as closely communicated to The Danish
Emergency Management Agency, beredskabsstyrelsen, who can act locally on these warnings.
This can, for example, be to reduce the impact of an extreme rainfall event. If sewers overflow,
there can be implications on traffic or water needs to be pumped away from an area at risk
(Beredskabsstyrelsen, 2018). Therefore, it is important to effectively predict and warn society
against cloudbursts and other heavy precipitation events. It is expected that the amount of pre-

4.6
Subjective verification
Subjective verification has been done by comparing the chosen extreme rainfall event from the
radar QPE and the two NWP models. This subjective verification approach has been done
by comparing model performances on six categories regarding location, structure, amplitude,
and deformation features. These has been chosen to reflect important categories for a useful
forecast and has been made by looking on individual lead time across all events. These have
also been classified dependent on date, time of forecast, as well as lead time. Lead times where
the radar QPE shows clear indications of cloudburst or frontal precipitation have also been
classified.
The subjective verification is always relative to the radar QPE. For some categories, ”None”,
”Miss”, ”Misplaced”, or ”OK” will be used to assess relative skill. Across all categories, ”None”
means that both the NWP model and the radar data do not depict any rainfall. These are,
therefore, empty precipitation fiels. ”Miss” means that a specific category of the precipitation
cells in the NWP model is off compared to the radar QPE that it could be regarded as non-
useful. ”Misplaced” means that the specific cells are is somewhat close, and ”OK” means that
it is a useful forecast for that category.

• Location of peak intensity. Forecasts are evaluated on their ability to locate peak intensity.

Many studies, including Gofa et al. (2018), Mittermaier et al. (2013), and Roberts and Lean

Overall, DK750 appears to produce a more realistic forecast on more extreme rainfall events

Conclusion
This thesis aims to compare the forecasting skill for extreme rainfall events of two models with
different horizontal grid resolutions. The comparison is done by: (1) Subjective verification
by comparing model forecast to radar QPE. (2) Objective verification, where a combination of
different skill scores are used to assess the models’ forecasting skill quantitatively.
NWP models forecast rainfall by simulating atmospheric processes and assimilating to spe-
cific data sources in the analysis. During warmer months, convective precipitation cells can
form and produce short-lived, intense precipitation in local areas. When the model grid size is
coarse, intense local precipitation is often not predicted, making it harder to warn local areas.
Therefore, high-resolution NWP models might approximate reality to a greater extent since
the grid size reduction increases the amount of resolved atmospheric motions.
To assess the quality of a forecast in hindsight, verification tools can be exploited to mimic
desired forecast quality by users and meteorologists. Forecast skill can be assessed using tra-
ditional verification measures like subjective verification. In this study, subjective verification
has been done, where six categories of precipitation cell properties have been compared to
radar QPE. Using other traditional verification methods like point-verification might punish
high-resolution models unreasonably known as the ”double penalty” issue. Therefore, neigh-
borhood verification scores might serve as a more fair comparison for model predictions. Using
complementary skill scores can indicate model skills regarding placement, intensity, and struc-
ture, appropriate for assessing total forecast skill. Based on these considerations, the objective
methods applied in this study include FSS and SAL.
Subjective verification has been used to interpret the visual difference between the two NWP
models across fifteen chosen extreme precipitation events. These are compared to the radar
QPE on various categories related to the entire domain and the peak intensity precipitation
cell. The most noticeable results are listed below.
• Results suggest that DK750 mainly improves forecast quality on the location of peak
intensity and the deformation with a subjective improvement of 74% and 72% of forecasts
classified as reasonable, respectively. The improvement using DK750 is mainly seen when
precipitation cells are small and spread.
• NEA is found to give a forecast closest to the radar QPE on amplitude and size of the
precipitation cells with 22% and 16% of subjective improvement. The difference is highest
during frontal precipitation systems, though the number of events used in the comparison
Page 54
==PAGE:64==
Master Thesis
Technical University of Denmark
is limited to give any robust conclusion.
Neighborhood verification methods, FSS and SAL, have been used to compare the models.
Combining a filtering and a feature-based verification method gives a broader perspective on
differences in the predictive skill.
• DK750 primarily improves FSS. The most considerable improvements are seen for low
scale and low intensities, while the models are more similar on larger scales. The largest
relative FSS increase was 0.1 with a mean relative improvement of 15% (± 13% std) when
using DK750.
• When removing bias, DK750 generally shows a lower minimum scale above FSSuniform
across percentiles with a mean improvement of 22km (± 32km in std) across all events
using the 99th percentile. 2/15 events are in favor of NEA.
• SAL indicates that DK750 leans toward underestimating both structure and amplitude,
while the reverse is true for NEA. DK750 is generally closer to optimum values for all
components. The location component shows good scores for both models, limiting this
component’s usefulness.
Based on the results, perspectives can be made on the models’ role in future extreme rainfall
forecasts. The perspectives are limited to usefulness dependent on precipitation characteristics
and the potential for using them in a cloudburst warning system. The findings of this study
suggest the following:
• When considering quantitative and subjective analysis, NEA could remain useful for large-
scale organized precipitation events, while DK750 firmly improves prediction on smaller
scaled events with more spread, but depends on what needs to be captured well.
• Some events with local and extreme precipitation have been difficult to forecast by both
models. Therefore, the improvement in the future lies in increasing the quality of pre-
dicting these very local and extreme precipitation events as that would be of most value.
• Considering the cloudburst warning system, DK750 improves spatial usefulness, both
subjectively and objectively. However, the skill remains low for high intensities but with
signs of improvement. The usefulness of DK750 might be limited to a nowcasting horizon,
as several results indicate a quicker decrease in skill over time than NEA. Therefore, the
optimum might be to use these models complementary.

Further Investigations
This section, first of all, serves the purpose of reflecting on what could have been done differently
in hindsight. This reflection has been limited to the three aspects below.
• When meteorologists receive the first indications of a possible extreme precipitation event,
NEA is used up to two days before it happens. This study indicates that while DK750
improves important precipitation aspects on a nowcasting horizon, NEA might still be
valuable for short-range forecasts. However, exactly when the improvements of DK750
disappear as lead times increases could have been investigated.
• This study has used neighborhood verification methods but has been limited to the spa-
tial extent. For further investigations, a temporal neighborhood could be implemented
to investigate potential timing issues, which would also be valuable for both users and
meteorologists. This information could also allow model producers to tweak the models.
• The subjetive verification could have given more skill to overestimations than underesti-
mations but has not been done. This weighted skill would have improved subjective skill
for NEA.
Further, this section also serves to reflect what could be investigated after this thesis. This has
also been limited to the three aspects below.
• Having fifteen days to verify forecast differences limits the conclusion’s validity. Having
more data for different seasons could improve the overall robustness of the differences and
should be done before taking a decision.
• The use of complementary models to get better cloudbursts warnings could be done. As

valuable to investigate.
• To evaluate if a model is worth being implemented or not socio-economic considerations

Provided by X. Yang from DMI on the 19/3/2022 by email and was accepted to be used in the thesis. 
“The equation sets for HARMONIE model are traditionally differentiated on rotated lamber 
projection. It is also possible to run the model on a polar stereographic projection. To run the 
model on a rotated lat-lon coordinate shall also be possible but it require some recoding, which 
has never been done. 
 
 
Reprojecting a model output may cause inprecision and loss of details, thus in principle one 
shall either avoid or to choose resolution that is close to the original resolution. At DMI we 
choose to do projection to rotated lat/lon in order to maintain product continuity, but it is not 
entirely necessary. 
 
The reprojection of the model from one coordinate to another is quite artificial and there is no 
strict 1 to 1 correspondence. For the DK750 domain, the original gridmesh 
is 800x720. When reprojecting to rotated latlong, it was necessary to use even number in 
degrees, (eg. 0,007 degree instead of 0.00698 degree), hence some sacrifice in resolution was 
given, which resulted a 
gridmesh of 700x630 after 
the reprojection.

color).

domain must always be 
smaller than the original, 
but it can be as small as a 
cutout.” (X. Yang at DMI)
