SUMMARY
Due to digitalization, the electricity demand from data centres (DCs) is expected to double every
five years [1]. Denmark alone is expected to host several large-scale DCs, whose demand for
electricity in 2040 may reach 33% of 2017 national electricity consumption [1]. However, prac-
tically all electricity consumed in a data centre is transformed into heat that must be removed
to keep the data centre running.
The expected high share of electricity demand from DC implies also a huge amount of heat
waste generated, and thus an opportunity of benefiting from integrating this heat into the net-
work. Increasing recovery of data centre’s waste heat is likely to benefit society by lowering the
heating supply costs, decarbonizing the supply chain and providing additional revenue to DC
owners.
Recent studies suggest various ways in which waste heat can be utilized, such as nearby build-
ings heating or, on a larger scale, integrating DC’s heat waste into the district heating network.
Despite the high potential, only a few projects are developing this concept, indicating that tech-
nical, financing and current regulatory framework conditions make them unprofitable.
This thesis aims to quantify the private-economic feasibility of waste-heat recovery for data cen-
tres by identifying the parameters and conditions crucial for the project’s revenue. To do so, in
first place, an optimization model in GAMS is to be developed. This model optimizes the opera-
tion of the heat-recovery system, considering technical constraints, operating signals, and heat
delivery conditions. Then, to calculate the private economic feasibility of the waste-heat recov-
ery process, the outcome of the optimization model is utilized, testing the results under different
scenarios attending to energy prices, financing and regulatory conditions, hence providing a
sensitivity analysis. As a final step, the study compares the feasibility of the Danish case with
other DC hubs in the European territory. Therefore, the model is tested under country-specific
policy initiatives, building scenarios and testing the sensitivity of the business case.
This thesis sheds light on the main factors impeding waste-heat recovery in Denmark and Eu-
rope, identifying which policy tools yield the best outcome for the waste-heat recovery systems.

RESUMÉ (DANISH)
På grund af digitalisering forventes elefterspørgslen fra datacentre (DC’er) at fordobles hvert
femte år [1]. Danmark alene forventes at være vært for flere store datacentre (DC’er), hvis
efterspørgsel efter elektricitet i 2040 kan nå op på 33% af det nationale elforbrug i 2017 [1].
Men stort set al elektricitet, der forbruges i et datacenter, omdannes til varme, der skal fjernes
for at holde datacentret kørende.
Den forventede høje andel af elefterspørgslen fra DC indebærer også en stor mængde varme-
spild, der genereres, og dermed en mulighed for at drage fordel af at integrere denne varme
i nettet. Øget genvinding af datacentres spildvarme vil sandsynligvis gavne samfundet ved at
sænke varmeforsyningsomkostningerne, dekarbonisere forsyningskæden og give yderligere in-
dtægter til DC-ejere.
Nyere undersøgelser peger på forskellige måder, hvorpå spildvarme kan udnyttes, såsom nærli-
ggende bygninger opvarmning eller i større skala at integrere DC’er varmeaffald i fjernvarmenet-
tet. På trods af det høje potentiale er det kun få projekter, der udvikler dette koncept, hvilket
indikerer, at tekniske, finansieringsmæssige og nuværende regulatoriske rammebetingelser gør
dem urentable.
Denne afhandling har til formål at kvantificere den privatøkonomiske gennemførlighed af spild-
varmegenvinding til datacentre ved at identificere de parametre og betingelser, der er afgørende
for projektets omsætning. For at gøre det skal der i første omgang udvikles en optimeringsmodel
i GAMS. Denne model optimerer driften af varmegenvindingssystemet under hensyntagen til
tekniske begrænsninger, driftssignaler og varmeleveringsbetingelser. Derefter, til at beregne
den privatøkonomiske gennemførlighed af spildvarmegenvindingsprocessen, anvendes resul-
tatet af optimeringsmodellen, der tester resultaterne under forskellige scenarier med hensyn
til energipriser, finansiering og regulatoriske forhold, hvilket giver en følsomhedsanalyse. Som
et sidste trin sammenligner undersøgelsen gennemførligheden af den danske case med andre
DC-hubs i det europæiske territorium. Derfor testes modellen under landespecifikke politiske
initiativer, opbygning af scenarier og test af følsomheden af business casen.
Denne afhandling belyser de vigtigste faktorer, der hindrer spildvarmegenvinding i Danmark
og Europa, og identificerer, hvilke politiske værktøjer der giver det bedste resultat for spild-
varmegenvindingssystemerne.

Introduction
Recognizing that climate change represents an urgent threat to societies and the planet, the
Paris Agreement establishes the goal of keeping global warming well below 2°C and pursuing
efforts to limit the increase to 1.5°C [2]. To achieve this reduction, the European Union (EU)
has set itself a long-term goal to reduce greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions by 80–95% before
the year 2050, compared to the 1990 levels [2].
With 50% of the EU energy demand used for heating in buildings and industrial processes,
renewables have made a significant contribution to the decarbonization of this sector. Between
2004 and 2016, the share of renewables in the heating and cooling sector nearly doubled from
10.3% to 19.1% and provided 99.3 million tonnes of oil equivalent (Mtoe) [3]. Solid biomass
remains the largest contributor, accounting for 80% of renewable energy production, with its
capacity growing from 1.8 Mtoe in 2004 to 78.8 Mtoe in 2016 [3]. Heat pumps (HP) have also
seen substantial growth, increasing more than fivefold from 1.8 Mtoe in 2004 to 9.9 Mtoe in
2016 and constituting 9.9% of renewable heat production [3]. While absolute heat production
levels have remained relatively stable since 2000, the share of renewables in the heating sector
has risen from 11% in 2005 to 15% in 2010 and further to 19% in 2015 [3].
At the same time, the potential for utilizing waste heat from industry and electricity production
is the same magnitude as the total heating demand of all buildings in Europe [4]. This indicates
the possibility of reducing imported and consumed energy, decarbonization the energy sector,
and enhancing energy security [4].
In addition to the manufacturing and power plant industries, the growth and digitization of the
technology sector have created new sources of waste heat for use in district heating, including
data centers.
In 2015, the Information Technology (IT) sector in Denmark accounted for 0.5% of the country’s
total electricity consumption [5]. Projections by Energinet, considering the potential growth of
data centers through to 2040, suggest that this proportion could significantly escalate, ranging
from 8% to 33% [6]. However, it’s critical to note that nearly all electricity consumed by the data
center industry is transformed into heat. This heat generation necessitates cooling systems
to maintain operational standards within these facilities. The increasing prevalence of data
centers posits this industry as a potential contributor to the district heating (DH) network, aiding
the broader decarbonization objective.
1.1
Overview of current challenges in the heat sector
The World Energy Outlook 2022 [7] identifies several significant challenges in the European
heat sector’s ongoing decarbonization transition context.
The first challenge identified is the pressing need for infrastructure modernization [7]. Many
existing heating systems across Europe have grown outdated and inefficient, demanding an
urgent upgrade with modern, energy-efficient technologies.
The second hurdle is synchronizing heat supply with its demand, which exhibits both sea-
sonal and daily fluctuations [7]. A relatively constant supply is crucial for maintaining the seam-
less functioning of heating systems, mandating the invention and deployment of dynamic and
innovative solutions.

The economic implications of sustainable heating solutions present another obstacle in this
complex situation. The considerable initial investment required for these solutions raises af-
fordability issues, especially among lower-income households [7]. Thus, a crucial aspect of this
transition lies in balancing between implementing sustainable practices and ensuring they are
economically accessible.
The regulatory and policy landscape introduces additional complexity. Occasional discrepan-
cies between EU and national regulations may complicate the transition towards sustainable
heating [7]. Consequently, a detailed approach becomes necessary, one that carefully aligns
these policies to ensure continuous progress toward greener heating solutions.
The path towards a sustainable and decarbonized European heat sector is replete with obsta-
cles such as infrastructure modernization, aligning supply and demand, maintaining affordabil-
ity, and streamlining regulations. Despite these considerable challenges, the significance of
achieving a sustainable and efficient heat sector cannot be overstated.
1.2
Waste heat recovery as a solution
The industrial sector, which accounts for 39% of global energy-related carbon emissions, needs
energy efficiency improvements of 3% annually to reach Net Zero Emissions by 2050 Scenario
[8]. Yet, current rates hover around just 1% annually, demonstrating the urgent need for effi-
ciency solutions.
Waste heat recovery (WHR) represents a significant opportunity in this landscape. The EU’s
heavy industrial sites generate over 267 TWh of waste heat annually - more than the total heat
produced by Germany, Poland, and Sweden combined in 2021 [8]. Even utilizing waste heat
sources above 95°C and within 10 km of existing district heating infrastructure yields a potential
of 64 TWh, equivalent to 12% of the energy supplied to the EU district heating infrastructure
each year [8].

Heavy industries, including cement, chemicals, and steel, account for nearly 60% of global in-

The waste heat produced, often at high temperatures, is a valuable energy source that can be

potential cost savings. This vital step brings us closer to a sustainable and resilient heating
sector.
1.3
Research question
The principal research question this thesis seeks to address is: ”What is the impact of different
policies and incentives on the business case for the reuse of waste heat from data centers
and its integration into district heating networks?”
In the wake of an extensive literature review, presented in section 2, it has become evident that
while no explicit legal barriers are preventing the utilization of waste heat from data centers,
there is a significant absence of supportive policies and incentives to foster such practices.
Hence, this thesis will probe the business implications for data centers, investigating how vary-
ing policies might influence their operations and surmount their distinct challenges. The intent is
not only to quantify the effects of these policies but also to facilitate the decision-making process
in adopting waste heat recovery technologies.
1.4
Main contribution
This thesis develops a model to assess the impact of different policies on the feasibility of imple-
menting waste heat reuse systems in data centers. The model simulates the cooling operations
of a data center and incorporates the option to enhance the quality of waste heat and sell it to
the grid for generating revenues.
By applying carefully selected policies, identified through an extensive policy review, the study
evaluates their influence on the operational and financial aspects of the data center’s business
case.
The primary objective is to identify strategies that increase the attractiveness of investing in
waste heat reuse systems from the perspective of data center owners. Moreover, the study aims
to address the regulatory gap related to waste heat integration into district heating systems.

2.1
Denmark’s District Heating Policy
Government policy significantly influences the decarbonization of the heat supply and the in-
corporation of new heat sources into the district heating system.
District heating systems naturally offer flexibility, serving as effective platforms for integrating
evolving policy initiatives. As changes occur in the energy landscape and as technology ad-
vances, it’s necessary for policies to evolve in order to stay relevant and effective. This flexibil-
ity is essential to crafting a robust, forward-looking energy strategy. Denmark exemplifies this
adaptability, with its continual policy evolution guiding the nation successfully through changes
in energy dynamics over the years.
Denmark’s journey since 1976 is a good example of the transformative power of policy initiatives.
Following the 1973-1974 energy crisis, Denmark prioritized fuel independence and increased
energy efficiency.
Between 1976 and 1979, Denmark established the Danish Energy Agency and initiated its first
long-term energy plan [10], setting the stage for a strategic approach to heating that optimizes
available energy sources. This first plan marked the birth of public heat planning, resulting in a
robust heating network that continues to deliver substantial benefits today.
In 1979, the first Danish Heat Supply Act was released [10], assigning local authorities the
responsibility to approve new heating supply projects based on the principle of the highest so-
cioeconomic benefit. The act also promoted the use of Combined Heat and Power (CHP) plants
when feasible and ensured fairness in the prices paid by consumers [10].
Denmark’s ambitious commitment to reduce greenhouse gas emissions by 70% by 2030 and
achieve 100% climate neutrality by 2050 necessitates a transformative shift in the heating sec-
tor. Heat Plan Denmark 2021 [11] was developed with this objective in mind. The plan sets out
a clear road map for the transition, detailing the potential for energy savings in buildings and
expansion of the district heating network to supply between 63-70% of the total heat demand
[11].
Furthermore, the Heat Plan emphasizes the need for strategic planning at the municipal level
to consider the potential for district heating expansion and identify suitable heat sources. This
would involve adopting an energy-efficient approach outside district heating areas, favoring
individual heat pumps supplemented by solar thermal energy. To meet the defined targets,
strategic planning and an enabling political framework are essential.
In 2022, the Danish Government responded to these evolving needs by introducing a new bill to
regulate the price of waste heat sold to district heating networks [12]. The regulation is guided
by three core principles: providing a reasonable financial incentive for waste heat utilization;
supporting final consumers in obtaining heat at the lowest possible cost, in line with Danish
district heating’s least-cost principles; and safeguarding against heat production becoming the
primary activity of waste-heat producers, a scenario also known as ’false waste heat.’
The costs of a district heating system are heavily dependent on economies of scale, both for
heat-producing plants and network expenses [10]. Thorough and precise planning is crucial in
ensuring consumer safety, social benefits, and stable investment conditions for a technology
that plays a critical role in the green transition [10]. The existing monopoly nature of the district

2.2
Review of Data Centers and their potential impact in District
Heating and Cooling networks
Numerous studies have evaluated the potential impact of integrating DCs into district heating
networks and the broader heating sector. These studies have assessed the economic benefits,
limitations, and challenges associated with such integration.
Petrović et al. [14]. conducted a comprehensive study on the benefits of utilizing waste heat
from DCs.
The study found that waste heat from DCs can have a significant positive impact on the overall
energy system. In four out of five scenarios analyzed, waste heat utilization was maximum,
demonstrating the economic advantages of recovering waste heat from DCs. Results suggest
that by 2040, waste heat recovery could contribute up to 27% of Denmark’s district heating
In their study, Petrović et al. primarily focus on the impacts of waste heat reuse systems in DCs
as contributors to district heating, specifically examining their effects on the energy system.
However, their perspective overlooks the crucial business implications for DC owners, failing to
consider the essential economic parameters required for comprehensive analysis. This omis-
sion stems from the perception that DCs operate outside the system boundary of the energy
system, being introduced exogenously for business purposes. Consequently, this overlooks
the significant economic considerations and potential challenges that DCs may face when im-
plementing waste heat recovery strategies. As a result, there remains a gap in understanding
the specific business implications for DCs in this context.
An economic analysis of these positive impacts is presented in the study by G.F. Davies et al.
[15]. The authors analyzed potential waste heat sources from data centers in the context of the
heating requirements of various districts in London.
Their findings indicated that a 3.5 MW data center could generate substantial savings, includ-
ing over 4000 tonnes of CO2eq and nearly £1 million per year, even without considering the
Domestic Renewable Heat Incentive (DRHI) . The DRHI is a government financial incentive de-
signed to support renewable heat usage, reduce carbon emissions, and achieve UK renewable
energy goals. It provides quarterly payments over seven years to eligible households that have
installed renewable heating systems, regardless of their gas grid connection [16]. This incentive
alone has the potential to provide an additional financial boost of £0.7 million annually.
It is indeed crucial to acknowledge that the study conducted by G.F. Davies et al. makes certain

In addition to the economic considerations, the waste heat integration from data centers into

Schmidt et al. [18] offer a comprehensive overview of the hurdles of integrating waste heat
from data centers into district heating networks. These challenges encompass technical, finan-
cial, legislative, and regulatory aspects. One significant technical issue highlighted in the study
is the temporal and qualitative mismatch between the waste heat generated by data centers
and the heat demand of the district heating networks. This mismatch, referred to as ’intermit-
tence,’ can occur on an hourly, daily, or seasonal basis. During the summer, this intermittence
creates a competitive environment among various renewable heat sources, known as the ’heat
merit order.’ Furthermore, the unstable nature of the waste heat supply can lead to control
complications within the network.
Schmidt et al. highlight the potential lack of interest or expertise among ’waste heat owners’
in supplying waste heat to district heating networks. It is important to note that waste heat
utilization is typically not their core business or primary area of expertise. The perceived prof-
itability of selling waste heat often does not justify the associated costs, including investments,
allocation of human resources, and potential impacts on their primary activities [18]. Thus, it
is crucial to consider and understand the perspectives of waste heat owners, particularly data
center owners, in the analysis.
Concluding their findings, Schmidt et al. identified a lack of specific regulatory restrictions hin-
dering the supply of waste heat into DH networks. Bilateral agreements between the DH net-
work operator/utility and the waste heat owner predominantly govern the dynamics of waste
heat supply. Despite this relative regulatory freedom, the study underscores a noticeable ab-
sence of active promotion and incentives for waste heat integration into the DH network. This
situation suggests an untapped opportunity for policy development to encourage and expedite
this integration process.
Building upon the insights from Schmidt et al. [18], Fontaine and Rocher argue that waste
heat recovery cannot be identified within the current sectoral organization of the energy field,
unlike other energy sources [19]. Fontaine and Rocher pinpoint that WHR does not yet have a
clear representation or a defined network of supporters within the current organization of energy
sectors [19].
Given the inherently cross-sectoral nature of WHR, Fontaine and Rocher underscore the fact

unique considerations. The success of each case depends significantly on the specific heat
source characteristics and the dynamics of local heat demand [19]. Therefore, they recommend
public policy instruments that consider local effects and encompass technological, market, fi-
nancial, legal, and spatial factors. These policies should facilitate the pairing of heat supply and
demand, identifying and addressing local challenges to achieve appropiate solutions [19].

2.3.1
Pricing regimes
The way of setting DHC prices varies between countries, depending on the regulatory frame-
work and whether the sector operates on a profit or non-profit basis [20].
Consumer prices can be regulated or liberalized, with or without price control [20]. This
control can be either ’ex-ante’, needing approval before application, or ’ex-post’, reviewed after
the fact to check their reasonableness.
The ex-post scheme, in the context of district heating, establishes prices based on actual costs
incurred over a specific period, typically the previous year. This approach contrasts with ex-ante
pricing, where prices are set beforehand based on anticipated costs [23].
In price regulation, two primary concepts may be recognized among the focal nations investi-
gated in [20]:
In countries like Finland, Germany, and Sweden, DH prices are fully liberalized and decided
by the market. Regulatory intervention can initiate to mitigate potential price abuse if there
are suspicions of producers setting disproportionately high fares [20]. For added transparency,
producers need to make public their pricing structures and annual reports. Notably, in Sweden
many DH producers are municipally owned, which further helps prevent monopolistic behavior.

Meanwhile, in Bulgaria, Denmark, Lithuania, Poland, Slovakia, and the Netherlands prices are
regulated by law. Here, the authorities check prices before or after setting them. In this ap-
proach, regulatory authorities review prices either prior to their implementation (ex-ante) or after
(ex-post), following a pre-established methodology. The calculated prices, typically determined
for a year or longer, are flexible and can be modified if influencing factors such as gas prices,
CO2 prices, or inflation rates fluctuate.
In particular, Denmark enforces a non-profit rule [20]. This rule mandates that DH operations
are predominantly managed by consumer cooperatives or municipally owned companies, con-
tributing to the maintenance of low prices.
France adopts a unique approach, integrating price control indirectly into the DH supply conces-
sion tendering process [20]. Here, companies negotiate prices with local authorities during the
tender procedure, and these prices become part of the concession agreement. The Nether-
lands, on the other hand, initially capped DH prices at the level of alternative heating options
but has since shifted to a more flexible system to accommodate climate goals.
Adding to the price regulation, the pricing models determine the final price of the heat in the
DH. These pricing models vary significantly across countries [20].
Across liberalized markets, such as those found in the Netherlands and Norway, market forces
principally form the prices [20]. However, these countries have introduced a price cap, known
as the ’No-More-Than-Alternative’ approach, which links the maximum price to a reference fuel
(gas in the Netherlands and electricity in Norway).
On the contrary, countries with explicit price regulation typically employ the cost-plus method
[20], whereby prices are determined by adding a specific mark-up to the unit cost. This unit cost
encompasses all the expenses a DH supplier incurs or forecasts over a given period, including
fuel prices, investment and operational costs for infrastructure maintenance and development,
concession charges, and metering.

However, it is essential to note that a pure cost-plus approach does not automatically incentivize

2.3.2
Connection and access to DHC networks
DHC systems are natural monopolies [20]. A natural monopoly exists whenever, due to high
fixed costs and low marginal costs, it is cheaper if only one company and not several competing
companies supply the market.
To prevent natural monopolists from abusing their market dominance, the markets concerned
require a minimum level of regulation. In particular, this applies to network operations. Typically,
the regulation core is the connection and usage conditions of the infrastructure [20].
The Renewable Energy Directive II calls on the Member States to increase the share of renew-
able energies in the grid-based heating and cooling supply, opening up two ways of doing it
[24]:
• Implementing measures aimed at increasing the share of Renewable Energies (RES) in
heating and cooling networks by 1 % per year.
• By granting producers of renewable heat/cold or waste heat access to the grid Third Party
Access (TPA) .
TPA refers to a legal and regulatory structure that ensures all qualifying parties have equal
access to infrastructure networks, such as DHC networks [20]. It allows producers of renewable
energy or waste heat to deliver their heat to the grid. As stipulated in the Renewable Energy
Directive II, the TPA provision presents an opportunity for district cooling systems to connect with
local DHC networks, thus facilitating an increase in renewable energy within these networks.
TPA regulation presents varied implementations across countries [20]. In the Czech Republic
and Slovakia, mandatory TPA only applies to heat derived from renewable energy and waste
heat sources [25]. Meanwhile, in Sweden, it applies to by-product heat, and in Estonia, TPA
arises from competitive bidding processes initiated when additional network capacity is needed.
However, access can still be denied due to technical or economic hurdles.
Economic constraints, particularly those associated with heat generation costs, can also affect
the efficiency of TPA. For instance, in Lithuania, grid operators must purchase heat from third-
party producers when the costs are competitive.
Regulations in Latvia, Poland, Slovakia, and the Czech Republic illustrate difficulties new gen-
eration sources encounter when compared cost-wise with existing plants [25], often leading to
TPA implementation only when it’s necessary to install new-generation capacity.
Even though TPA regulation allows waste heat producers to transfer their heat to the net-
work, shifting to such frameworks has financial implications, especially for data centers
bound to incur significant investments. Thus, balancing these transition costs against the po-
tential revenue from selling waste heat is crucial for achieving feasibility. Consequently, the
specific economic and regulatory conditions within each DHC system can determine
whether data centers can feasibly serve as renewable heat sources.

4.1
Method
4.1.1
District Heating Networks
District heating network’s heat is frequently generated in a Combined Heat and Power plant
[35]. CHP facilities have numerous configurations and can operate using diverse fuels, encom-
passing natural gas, coal, oil, and biomass. The most popular form of CHP plant generates
energy by passing hot steam via turbines. The heat is then transferred to the cold district heat-
ing water line during the condensation process and, rather than being squandered, is delivered
to the surroundings via the district heating network.
However, because this common solution is based on a centralized method of producing thermal
energy, it is not the most energy efficient. A distributed heat system, in particular, results in
more efficient energy dispatch. At the moment, a dispersed heat source may be linked to a
DHC network primarily in three ways [35]:
a) Return to supply (R/S): Water is extracted from the return line, heated to the desired
temperature, and then returned to the supply line. This is the most advantageous feed-in
system since it does not impact the return line temperature and can transmit the same
power without increasing pumping usage.
b) Return to return (R/R): Water is extracted from the return line, heated at a higher tem-
perature, and then fed back again to the return line. This feed-in approach minimizes
the central unit’s heating peak output. Furthermore, by installing it near the heat produc-
tion area, heat losses and pump electric consumption are reduced in comparison to other
configurations.
c) Supply to supply (S/S): Water is extracted from the supply line heated to the desired
temperature, and then returned back to the supply line.
The most beneficial system is the return/supply (R/S) solution since it influences the DH system
the least, turning data centers into distributed heat supply units [35], as figure 4.1 displays.

After servers, cooling represents the second largest source of power consumption in DCs, with
the cooling operation being critical for their proper functioning [40]. The cooling energy required
in a DC, also known as waste heat, fluctuates depending on the cooling technology utilized. In
the most efficient DCs, cooling energy may comprise about 10% of the total energy consump-
tion, in contrast to nearly 45% in air-cooled DCs [40].
There are two categories of data center cooling systems: air-cooled and liquid-cooled. In air-
cooled DCs, waste heat can generally be captured between 25 °C and 35 °C, while liquid cooling
would allow capturing the waste heat at 50–60 °C [38]. Liquid cooling systems permit waste heat
capture closer to the central processing units (CPUs) and other components, where operating
temperatures are high. As liquid provides a more effective heat transfer medium than air, the
circulating liquid’s temperature can be higher. This advantage translates to a higher quality of
collected waste heat [38].
With a close interdependence, the level of IT equipment utilization determines the cooling load.
As IT activity fluctuates, the cooling load remains relatively stable at approximately 80% of total
installed capacity [35].

4.1.3
Cooling technologies
Cooling can occur by free cooling, mechanical refrigeration (electric chiller and heat pumps),
or a combination of both, which is the most common method. Since mechanical refrigeration
consumes high amounts of energy, free cooling is typically more profitable when expelling the
heat load from the DC [38].
Integrating heat pumps into the cooling system presents an opportunity to waste heat harness-
ing, reusing it for applications that necessitate higher temperatures. This potential forms a
fundamental part of energy-efficient solutions addressing the extensive energy consumption of
data centers.
Typically, waste heat from hot aisles is collected and directed to the ventilation system or reintro-
duced into the Computer Room Air Conditioning (CRAC) [38], as shown in figure 4.3. Following
this collection, the waste heat often passes through multiple stages, including an evaporator, a
condenser, and a heat pump, before reusing, for example, for district heating purposes .
Figure 4.3: Configuration for a waste heat recovery system for a remote air-cooled data center,
which utilizes waste heat in DH [38].
Waste heat utilization methods are diverse and span various scales. On the one hand, small-
scale and location-specific solutions, such as heating swimming pools or self-heating facilities,
typically entail lower initial investments when compared to large-scale endeavors.
However, it is fundamental to consider that the immediate proximity of heat demand is a sig-
nificant factor in determining the feasibility of these smaller solutions. In cases where heat
utilization is required further away, a connection to a DH network becomes advantageous due
to its capacity to transport heat over longer distances.
4.1.3.1
Free cooling
Free cooling is an energy-efficient cooling method widely implemented in data centers. It utilizes
external ambient air, leveraging lower outdoor temperatures to cool the IT equipment without
needing mechanical refrigeration systems [38]. The DC capitalizes on the cooling capacity of
ambient air, seawater, or the ground by using chilled water for cooling purposes.

4.1.3.2
Electric chiller
Typically, the electric chiller is employed to meet the cooling requirements that cannot be sat-
isfied through free cooling methods, particularly in cases where a heat pump system is not
implemented.
The electric chiller is crucial in the cooling infrastructure of data centers. It extracts heat from
the water loop using a refrigerant, which evaporates the water and dissipates the heat into the
atmosphere.
However, careful attention must be given to the electricity consumption associated with the
chiller, as it often represents a significant portion of the total energy usage in data centers and
can consequently impact their overall energy efficiency.

8
Discussion
The analysis of investment aid scenarios, those involving Heat Pumps and Networks, reveals
that while these policies improve the feasibility and attractiveness of the investment, they do
not significantly affect the heat pump operation. This low operation impact suggests that while
such policies can encourage initial investment, additional measures may be necessary for
the optimal utilization of these systems.
Notably, the yield parameter indicates that the most effective measures are those based on
the operational side of the system. Policies focusing on operation performance exhibit
better indicators than those centered on investment. The two scenarios that incorporate
performance support display the highest yield parameters, exceeding the value of one. These
values imply that these scenarios’ implementation results in a net monetary gain.
Analyzing the data center’s cooling operations confirms the vital role of free-cooling technology.
This importance is mainly due to electricity price volatility affecting directly waste heat recov-
ery costs. High electricity prices can make heating and selling to the district heating grid less
profitable, especially in colder months. Therefore, free cooling is a critical, cost-effective al-
ternative during these peak periods, effectively buffering against electricity price spikes.
The feasibility of the business case, even in a no-policy scenario, is supported by a slightly
positive NPV. However, the high discount rate applied to the investment introduces a disparity
between the Payback Time and Net Present Value indicators. This high discount rate reflects
the uncertainty faced by data center owners. These results highlight the need for metrics be-
yond standard NPV and PBT for a more nuanced analysis. The necessity becomes apparent
when considering the PBT indicator. Although all scenarios show relatively low PBT, suggest-
ing a profitable business case, the Capacity and Yield factors vary significantly across policies,
providing a better overview of the policy impact in the system operation.
A further key challenge identified is the seasonal discrepancy in heat demand. Various sources
highlight the importance of addressing this seasonal fluctuation to enhance the economic via-
bility of waste heat recovery systems. Using the chiller during summer, when heat prices are
low due to diminished district heating demand, underscores this point. Managing this seasonal
variance is crucial for the waste heat recovery systems optimal performance and guaranteeing
their long-term economic feasibility.
The literature review highlights that, even with the presence of investment aid, a funding gap
for certain technologies remains [32],[22]. If operational support is lacking, the burden of these
additional costs may fall on heat consumers. This highlights the importance of comprehensive
policies that tackle both initial investments and recurring operational expenses.
The sensitivity analysis uncovers how the system responds to slight modifications in the level
of support. The fixed price sensitivity study shows that even minimal price fluctuations can
considerably affect the economic assessment of the investment. From the break-even
point, changes of only 3% produce a high increase of up to €50,000 in the NPV value. This
finding emphasizes the critical influence of heating prices on the viability of the investment and
underscores the necessity for a more uniform pricing structure year-round. Such consistent
pricing can mitigate seasonal mismatches and foster a positive NPV for the investment.
The concept of an appropriate and optimal level of support arises when considering the Heat
Pump operating and HP investment sensitivities. From the data center’s perspective, a higher

8.1
Limitations
This research presents a number of limitations that must be considered while interpreting its
findings:
• The economic model in this study, while comprehensive, carries an inherent level of un-
certainty due largely to potential inaccuracies or limitations in the data available. Con-
sequently, this research primarily offers a comparative analysis between the ’WHR: no
policies Scenario’ and various policy scenarios, rather than an in-depth economic analy-
sis of the investment itself.
• Currently, few policies address the direct link between waste heat sources and district
heating networks. Indirect measures, such as CO2 quotas, are in place; however, fully
understanding their implementation and impact would require a broader scope that en-
compasses the entirety of the DH portfolio.
• Although strategies to manage seasonal variations—like implementing heat storage or
batteries—exist, they fall outside the scope of this study as the model does not include
any storage technologies.
• The heat pricing methodology presents a constraint that influences the precision and relia-
bility of the analysis. The estimated heat prices used in this study derive from two previous
studies and additional modeling work developed within this research, which can potentially
affect the accuracy of the data.
• The DH operator’s role omission limits the analysis, specifically concerning heat availabil-
ity and acceptance from the DH networks. The choice of a small DC (500kW) sidesteps
the potential issue of DH availability. However, a broader analysis might underscore how
DH availability can influence the feasibility of the business case, especially in relation to
constraints on revenue opportunities.
• Finally, the study does not delve into any contractual elements, which restricts the ex-
amination of potential contractual frameworks. As a result, aspects such as mandated
minimum operation hours for heat delivery to consumers, and the subsequent impact on
the business, are not factored into the analysis
8.2
Further research
• Future research could explore policies targeting the implementation of storage solutions
to address the temporal mismatch issue. This study could provide valuable insights into
alternative ways to balance supply and demand and enhance system efficiency.
• This study focused on connections to the network. Subsequent research could broaden
this perspective to include local heat demands beyond the DH network. This approach
could reveal additional opportunities for waste heat utilization, enhancing the overall eco-
nomic and environmental benefits of waste heat recovery by expanding the sinking port-
folio and increasing heat pump utilization.

Conclusion
The study’s research question, ”What is the impact of different policies and incentives
on the business case for the reuse of waste heat from DCs and its integration into dis-
trict heating networks?”, was addressed by constructing a Waste Heat Recovery model and
testing various policy scenarios. This model clarified how different schemes targeting prices,
implementation incentives, operational support, and efficiency influence the business model for
data centers, extending the analysis beyond mere investments to encapsulate the yield from
each policy.
Even in the absence of policies, the base scenario presented a viable business case. How-
ever, the findings highlight the crucial role of support mechanisms in successfully integrating
waste heat reuse systems into DCs. The results suggest that policies should primarily address
operational aspects, especially heat and electricity prices.
The sensitivity analysis, particularly in the context of the Ceiling Price scheme, underscored
the importance of stable, favorable heat prices for incentivizing DCs’ business engagement.
Moreover, the Operating Aid policy’s effectiveness showcased its potential to address electricity
prices effectively, thereby generating substantial revenue for investments aimed at reducing
electricity costs. These results emphasize the necessity for tailored policies that account for the
unique operational challenges and economic factors faced by data centers, thereby fostering
sustainable and financially viable waste heat recovery initiatives.
Transitioning to Low-Temperature District Heating can yield significant benefits in terms of ef-
ficiency and cost reductions. The results underscore the profound impact this transition could
have.
However, implementing these new working temperatures requires a comprehensive
adaptation from all the actors involved in District Heating, ranging from producers to house-
holds
CAPEX aid scenarios also enhance the investment appeal. Given the concern of high up-
front costs for DC owners, alleviating this burden could stimulate waste heat harnessing. Both
CAPEX scenarios show a significant increase in NPV compared to the policy-free scenario, indi-
cating that these policies facilitate investment but do not significantly impact the WHR system’s
long-term operation. This fact underpins the need for a combined approach that integrates both
investment and operational support.
Moreover, and extracted from the policy and literature review, DC owners must deepen their
understanding of waste heat recovery systems. Currently, the knowledge gap exacerbates in-
vestment uncertainty, creating challenges for convincing DC owners to invest in these systems.
However, the promotion of investment certainty calls for simplifying the business case analysis.
The current scarcity of sufficient and accurate data complicates a comprehensive analysis and
increase uncertainty in the investor’s side. Enhancing transparency in heating prices and un-
derstanding the business trade-offs can elucidate the scenario for potential investors, thereby
encouraging investment in waste heat recovery systems.
In conclusion, alongside formulating tailored policies, improving transparency in data and
industry practices is pivotal in promoting sustainable investment in this waste heat re-
covery area.
