Abstract
As distributed energy resources (DER), particularly household rooftop photovoltaic (PV)
systems, continue to proliferate in low-voltage (LV) electricity networks, new socio-economic
challenges are emerging alongside environmental and economic benefits which have
been generally overlooked.
Some of these challenges emerge as a consequence of
active DER curtailments from system operators to manage network constraints raising
fairness-related concerns about how burdens and benefits are shared among prosumers.
This work investigates the fairness implications of various DER curtailment strategies
in LV networks. Using a simplified feeder model, several operational policies are eval-
uated including: the current status quo, efficiency-oriented approaches, and proposed
community-based solutions. Fairness is assessed through multiple principles to capture
diverse perspectives and is quantified using both a classic and linear Quality of Service
(QoS) or Jain’s index, offering upper and lower bounds for fairness evaluation.
The analysis reveals that while future operational control strategies (such as dynamic op-
erating envelopes and Volt-VAr control) can improve efficiency, their fairness outcomes
are highly dependent on the chosen fairness principle and network conditions. Community-
based policies are shown to deliver more balanced outcomes, achieving fairer distribution
of curtailments with only marginal efficiency drawbacks especially when including reactive
power management.
Overall, this work demonstrates the importance of integrating fairness into DER control
policy design and provides tools to assess the trade-offs between system efficiency and
fairness in the operation of emerging LV distribution systems.

Introduction
In the energy sector, significant efforts are being made to deliver innovative solutions aim-
ing at reaching a decarbonized system by integrating new technologies. Traditionally, the
word integrate mentioned is used to describe how these technologies can be introduced
into current power systems without jeopardizing their operation in terms of well established
standards such as system adequacy or reliability, security and, more recently, resilience.
While these standards ensure the integrity of the system’s operation and the continuous
supply of demand, they overlook other factors which are now gaining relevance. Specif-
ically, new socio-economic aspects, are being included in the broader discussion which
should not be undermined. Addressing them is key to promote the general public’s sup-
port and engagement in the energy transition.
Some of the socio-economic challenges emerging are rooted in the concept of fairness.
While solutions are designed to offer environmental and economic benefits to society,
they often come at the cost of adverse local impacts. In high voltage (HV) systems, some
of these impacts have become evident through local community opposition to the devel-
opment of new transmission infrastructure and wind farms [1][2]. A big part of these new
technologies, however, are emerging in the form of distributed energy resources (DER).
These are generally smaller sized assets located at the edge of the grid in medium or low
voltage (MV/LV) networks.
The rising adoption of DER is presenting both opportunities and challenges which are
re-shaping the operation of current power systems. Prosumers (consumers with local
electricity production) are benefiting from greater autonomy, direct access to clean energy
and compensation for participation in energy markets or system services. On the other
hand, transmission and distribution system operators (TSOs/DSOs) are facing challenges
related with the operation and control of these technologies to comply with the previously
mentioned grid performance standards.
In some countries like Australia, this has led system operators to directly intervene in the
operation of DER by actively curtailing them due to network constraints. To enhance their
controllability and visibility, new solutions are being studied to manage network constraints
with new operational solutions. However, what are the results of these solutions in terms of
fairness? And, fundamentally, what is fairness in this context and how can it be measured?
Furthermore, what are the impacts of considering fairness in overall system efficiency?
This work aims at answering these questions regarding fairness in DER curtailments in
the context of household rooftop photovoltaic (PV) operation in low voltage electricity net-
works. To explore this, a toy model LV feeder is used to illustrate the effect of different
by-design curtailment strategies on system efficiency and fairness. Moreover, fairness
is evaluated under different fairness principles to reflect diverse perspectives and mea-
sured according to the quality of service (QoS) also known as Jain’s index. Alongside the
classic QoS formulation, a linear version is proposed which provides a more conservative
assessment, effectively serving as a lower bound for fairness comparisons. Lastly, the
curtailments strategies assessed include the status quo, future efficiency-oriented strate-
gies, and proposed community-based solutions that enhance both system efficiency while
incorporating fairness principles.

Objectives
The objectives of this thesis are therefore to:
1. Analyze the impact of non-community curtailment policies (status quo, dynamic
operating envelopes and Volt-VAr) on system efficiency and fairness assessment
based on two fairness metrics: classic quality of service (QoS) and a proposed lin-
ear QoS.

3. Conduct sensitivity trade-off analysis between system efficiency and fairness of
community policies through Pareto frontiers.
4. Examine individual exports over all operational policies.

Problem Description and Background
2.1
DER context
The energy transition is bringing forward the integration of new technologies across the
whole sector. In the electricity sector, this is being materialized, in part, by the replace-
ment of conventional fossil fueled power plants with renewable energy sources (RES).
These RES are appearing not only as large scale utility generation plants in transmis-
sion networks but also as smaller non-scheduled distributed energy resources (DER) in
mid-low voltage networks. This is something already noticeable in countries like Australia
where currently, one in three houses have rooftop solar photovoltaic (PV) installed in their
homes, representing around 12% of the generation supply mix (more than gas, hydro and
utility/grid solar). As seen in figure 2.1 below, the capacity is expected to increase four-fold
by 2050.
Figure 2.1: Historical and forecasted installed capacities in Australia’s National Electricity
Market (NEM). Adapted from [3].
The rapid uptake of end-consumers owned DER such as small-scale solar, electric vehi-
cles (EVs) and batteries located at the edge of the electricity network presents significant
challenges and opportunities for the current technical operations, business models and
industry structure [4]. Recently, in Australia, DER are more commonly referred as con-
sumer energy resources (CER).
2.2
DER Challenges and Opportunities
In regards to the challenges and opportunities of the technical operations, as mentioned
before, DER are not scheduled. In other words, these resources are located behind-the-
meter on the consumer’s side and are therefore non-visible, non-dispatchable and non-
controllable by system operators. This can lead to inefficient outcomes while possessing

network security risks. One of the causes of inefficiencies are network constraints such
as over voltages where system operators are forced to require increased power manage-
ment and curtailment of DER to maintain system security and reliability. Some regions
in Australia such as South Australia are already experiencing curtailments controlled by
their transmission system operator (TSO) the Australian Energy Market Operator (AEMO)
through active network management (ANM) to ensure grid stability in case of over voltages
in local distribution networks [5][6]. Furthermore, this has made distribution system oper-
ators (DSOs) arrange fixed exports limits which depending on the region are set to 5kW
per phase regardless of network location or set between 0-5kW per phase depending on
the available local hosting capacity. These static export limits reduce network efficiency
as well as customer benefits and the supply of clean renewable energy. Some solutions
being explored to reduce DER curtailments while managing network constraints are the
following:
• Dynamic network pricing (DNP): cost-reflective network charges (i.e. price sig-
nalling) that reflects local network constraints to manage the available hosting ca-
pacity. This is already being trialed by DSOs such as Ausgrid through project Edith
[7].
• Dynamic operating envelopes (DOEs): flexible meter-level time-varying upper/lower
limits of the exports/imports of DERs dependent to network constraints. The design
and integration of DOEs was studied through project Edge [8] and trialed by DSOs
Ausnet and SA Power Networks in the Flexible Exports for Solar PV Trial [9].
• Volt-VAr and Volt-Watt control: passive voltage management solution through an
inverter control setting that alters the active and reactive power setpoint to manage
and alleviate voltage constraints. These control settings are already required and
being mandated through standards such as AS4777 [10].
2.3
Problem Definition
It is important to note that the above measures are categorized as non-network solutions
meaning that these solutions do not involve network upgrades and are services procured
from DER, often providing a cost-effective alternative to network reinforcements by re-
ducing and deferring investments. Non-network solutions aim to control the operation or
available hosting capacity of the network in space and time through indirect (e.g. price
signalling) or direct (e.g. DOEs and Volt-VAr/Watt control) methods to maximize overall
system efficiency. In other words, the objective is to allocate the resources so as to min-
imize curtailments therefore making the most out of the existing infrastructure and DER
capacity. However, focusing solely on efficiency can lead to large asymmetries in the
capacity allocation of end-user’s DER which are location dependent often leading DER
located at more sensitive parts of the network to be more penalized [11][12]. In short, it
can lead to unfair situations. Following, a toy example of a LV feeder network is used to
showcase the impacts of network constraints on prosumers (consumers with local elec-
tricity production) allocated capacity based on their network location.
Consider a low-voltage (LV) feeder such as the one depicted in figure 2.2 with three pro-
sumers: A, B, C. At the head of the feeder (HoF) is a MV-LV transformer and each of the
households has a rooftop PV installed with equal capacities. In a sunny day with low loads
where the PV panels are exporting electricity to the feeder, the voltage levels will increase
along the feeder as displayed below. The reason for this can be explained simply through
Ohm’s law which shows that there has to be a voltage drop for current (and power) to flow

In light of the above, concerns regarding fairness on the capacity or resource allocations
of the different methods arise. Solutions being studied in order to mitigate the technical
challenges of integrating DER in current power systems are leading to other new social-
economic challenges that are undermined. Therefore, end-users perceived notions of
fairness are required to be considered and assessed along with the rest of technical as-
pects in order to design operating policies (or non-network solutions) that share the burden
of network constraints in a fair manner while continuing to incentivize DER uptake from
end-users.

2.4
Energy Communities as a Solution
In this context, energy communities (ECs) could bring forth a framework to manage the
challenges of increasing DER integration in LV networks. In Europe, through the Internal
Electricity Market Directive 2019/944 as part of the EU’s Clean Energy Package, energy
communities are already recognized as a legal entity (named as Citizen Energy Com-
munities) that can provide a cost-efficient way to meet citizens’ need and expectations
regarding energy sources, services and local participation [16]. It stipulates that mem-
ber’s engagement must be driven by the environmental, social and economic benefits
that the community can bring to its members and society. Countries such as Denmark
are introducing the regulatory framework to support the development of such communities
highlighting their potential to coordinate flexibility from residential electricity users that are
close to each other, for instance connected to the same LV feeder [17].
Given this, energy communities can organize citizen-driven energy actions to access more
economic value from DER while maintaining a common perception of fairness agreed
collectively by its members. Some of the different mechanisms unlocked through energy
communities to deliver environmental, economic and social benefits are displayed in figure
2.6 below.

when determining the communities objective. Fairness, however, can have very sub-

considering the specific individual context of each member is not trivial. However, by con-

3
Literature Review of Fairness
The concept of fairness in resource allocation problems has been under scrutiny for sev-
eral millennia across diverse disciplines ranging from philosophy and economic theory to
engineering. Within engineering, several domains such as telecommunication networks,
healthcare, disaster management have required fairness as a key criteria. Given that this
thesis is oriented in applications relevant to energy systems, first a general overview of
the fundamental fairness principles in general economic and game theory will be intro-
duced. Even though there is no single principle that is universally accepted, they serve
as the foundation for many of the approaches discussed in the second part.
3.1
Fairness in Economic and Game Theory
For centuries, justice theories originating from ancient philosophers have shaped our cur-
rent understandings and perceptions of fairness. One of these oldest theories is Aristo-
tle’s principle of distributive justice in which he stated: ”Equals should be treated equally,
and unequals unequally, in proportion to the relevant similarities and differences.” This
principle asserts that the resource allocation between people should be in proportion to
their differences. Under the influence of theories such as this, several economic theo-
ries emerged during the 19th and 20th centuries which form the bases of most fairness
schemes today.
One approach is the classical utilitarianism (efficiency) which dictates an allocation of
resources that maximizes the sum of utilities. However, this approach has received a lot of
criticisms since the utility of certain individuals might be greatly reduced in order to provide
an overall general benefit. When deciding on a fair allocation scheme, the allocations are
typically decided directly among the stakeholders through a bargaining process. This
is at the core of game theory which develops solution concepts for negotiating in such
situations.
3.1.1
Nash Bargaining Solution: Proportional Fairness
John Nash was the first to model bargaining through his seminal paper [22] in 1950 us-
ing the framework of game theory proposed a few years earlier by Von Neumman and
Morgenstern [23]. Nash’s model considers a bargaining problem where two agents (i.e.
those bargaining - also called players or claimants) try to maximize their utilities. He pro-
posed an axiomatic approach which under a number of assumptions, shows that there
exists a unique solution satisfying those axioms. The solution is quite simple and given
by maximizing the product of the claimants utilities gains over the disagreement point:

a utility better than the one received in the NBS. In other words, a player cannot
receive a better utility without making another one worse off [26].
3. Invariance to Affine Transformations - INV: the bargaining solution does not change
if the utility functions (and disagreement point) are scaled by an affine transforma-
tion. A player’s preferences resented by a utility function ui is also represented by
any utility function vi = aiui + bi where the coefficients are real numbers and ai is
positive since multiplying by a negative numbers would flip the utility function there-
fore changing the preferences. Since both summarize the same information, the
NBS should yield the same underlying outcome if some players utility function is
changed from ui to an affine transformation vi. However, the payoff at any given
outcome q changes from ui(q) to aiui(q) + bi - just as changing from Celsius to
Fahrenheit temperature changes the boiling temperature of water from 100 to 212
[27].
4. Independence of Irrelevant Alternatives - IIA: if the set of possible utilities U is
contained in a larger set of utilities S, either the bargaining solution of both sets
should be identical or it selects one of the new alternatives of the larger set S. In
other words, given the larger set S, if the players agree on an outcome included
in the smaller set U, then the players agree on the same outcome when only the
smaller set U is available. Hence, the remaining alternatives in the larger set S are
irrelevant.

3.2
Fairness Applied to the Energy Sector
In the existing energy systems literature, numerous approaches have been proposed to
handle fairness via aggregators, communities or other forms of decentralized or central-
ized control. As previously, mentioned some approaches follow an ex-post redistribution
policy that compensates participants in accordance with fairness considerations; others
a by-design principle where fairness notions are embedded in the operational or finan-
cial dynamics of the model; and others ad hoc or heuristic methods where fairness is
evaluated posteriorly via different metrics.
With respect to ex-post approaches, many of these have been designed for situations
without network constraints. For example, [39][40][41] coordinate DERs aiming to im-

==PAGE:22==
prove the overall financial benefits of participants while individual payoffs are based on
their individual contribution through, for example, their Shapley value. However, under
network constraints, some prosumers will be more affected than others, leading to differ-
ent operational limitations and, consequently, varying impacts on their economic returns.
In other words, network-constraints act as an externality affecting differently individual
DER’s operations which is not considered in the Shapley payoff policy. This is also exem-
plified in [42], where game theoretic approaches determine the allocations of a shared-
energy problem in an aggregator scheme. Here, acceptability constraints ensure that
each prosumer receives at least as much benefit as in independent operation. However,
this criterion overlooks the impact of network constraints, which may have initially affected
prosumers unequally, leading to potentially unfair allocations. Therefore, when including
the effects of network constraints on individual DER’s, these methods may prove to be
inadequate.
Relative to by-design methods, some articles do include network-constraints in their stud-
ies and provide different ways of internalizing by-design them as constraints or in the ob-
jective function. In [43], a fair share of household PV curtailments due to voltage limits is
proposed based on individual self consumption rates (SCR). This is the ratio between the
part of the PV generation directly transferred to the load and the total PV generation. Pro-
sumers with lower values of SCR experience higher curtailments than those with higher
SCR. Therefore, in this case, fairness is directly associated with self-sufficiency only. In
paper [11], fair PV curtailments are considered proportional to their available PV capacity
without considering household demand. On the other hand, [44] proposes community
owned assets to manage network constraints where benefits redistributed to individual
households are proportional to their instantaneous demand. In [45] two fair mechanisms
for benefit allocations are considered: proportional benefits relative to their share of de-
mand and equal benefits irrespective of individual demands. Other fairness principles on
curtailments are introduced in [46][47] such as dynamic uniform curtailments where pro-
sumers are curtailed to ensure equal exports among those exporting above a threshold.
Some other fairness notions introduced in the formulation of the problem can be seen, for
example, in [48] which follows the Rawlsian principle by maximizing the worst off through
a maximin objective. The paper [49] introduces a community energy sharing framework
in which resource allocations are based on participants’ initial energy costs or bills prior to
forming the community. While this approach aims to reflect each member’s historical con-
tribution, it may result in unfair outcomes—particularly if some prosumers faced network
constraints that inflated their costs before joining the community. In such cases, these
participants might be systematically disadvantaged despite their limited control over past
conditions.
Lastly, regarding approaches with a posterior fairness evaluation through different met-
rics, [50] proposes a heuristic Volt-VAr-Watt control strategy for PV curtailment aimed at
managing voltage constraints. The approach defines fair curtailments as being propor-
tional to the available PV capacity, with fairness evaluated retrospectively using Jain’s
index. While the method yields fair results in feeders with high (i.e. 100%) PV penetra-
tion, it performs poorly in terms of fairness in feeders with lower (i.e. 50%) PV penetration.
Moreover, no analysis is performed on the loss of efficiency as a result of the fairness-
aware control strategy. Lastly, in [51], a multi-objective formulation which includes a cost
minimization and a community goal term is presented. However, the community goals
(such as autonomy or penalizing maximum importer) are independent of fairness princi-
ples which are only assessed retrospectively through different metrics without affecting
any reallocation of resources.

4.2
Including Fairness in Community Policies
A community can be managed along two complementary dimensions: operational and/or
financial. These correspond to two common approaches: by-design and ex-post redis-
tribution. The operation dimension controls the physical operation of the communities
assets (i.e. household assets or community level shared assets) to decide on the set-
point of their net exports or imports while considering fairness. This is achieved by em-
bedding fairness considerations in the objective or constraints of the community model.
The financial operation does not change the communities operations and relies on an ex-
post redistribution of the communities earnings or savings to the individual members that
conform it according to an agreed-upon rule.

4.2.1
Financial and Operational Policies Implications
Both operational and financial policies have different implications to the individual pro-
sumers in a community and to the community as a whole. Typically including fairness
notions in the operation of a community comes at a price of loss in efficiency since the
problem is more constrained. In [34], the bounds on the loss of efficiency due to different
operational schemes including proportional and minimax fairness approaches is com-
pared. An advantage of operational policies is that it does not rely on money transfers
between the members which may be undesirable for a couple of reasons. First, money
transfer through financial redistribution adds complexity by requiring the design of a fair
and transparent allocation framework. This includes decisions such as how to account for
the diverse retail tariffs of individual members, or whether to adopt a single, uniform re-
tailer for the entire community. Secondly, this financial redistribution in the community can
incur extra costs corresponding to its management. Therefore, the economic earnings or
savings of each member is directly related to the individual operation of their household
as occurs currently in present day households.
However, one of the advantages of including both operational and financial aspects in
a community is that it can solve for any unfairness arising among its members due to
externalities such as network constraints while allowing the community to operate at its
most efficient setpoint. This can be translated to a larger overall benefit - a ’bigger pie’ -
for the community through its optimized operation. However, the distribution - pie portions
- depends on the redistribution policies introduced. The next section introduces the set of
policies selected for the case study presented.
4.2.2
Policies Selected
This thesis focuses on the by-design approach concerning the operation of the communi-
ties DER. Therefore, they are implemented during the communities operation as opposed
to after the operation and collection of the total community earnings or savings. It is im-
portant to mention that all policies included in this section are in terms of the curtailed
export power of the households. This refers to the portion of power that households are
capable of exporting but are unable to due to network constraints. In order to maintain
simplicity in the management of the community, the policies selected are:
1. Equal allocation. All prosumers are curtailed equally regardless of their installed
rooftop PV nameplate or available net exports capacity. Therefore, all households
are curtailed the same amount.
2. Proportional allocation. Prosumers are curtailed proportionally to:
(a) Available net exports capacity. This is the residual power capacity in the house-
hold resulting from the rooftop PV generation after the load consumption is sub-
tracted. Hence, the amount of curtailed power is proportional to the available
capacity.
(b) Installed capacity. This is the nameplate rated capacity of the installed rooftop
PV. Thus, the amount of curtailed power is proportional to the installed capacity.
Furthermore, all of these options are evaluated against the current status quo in which
prosumers assets operate individually with given fixed export limits. It is worth highlighting
that the analyses included in this thesis are done in relation to the power export curtail-
ment values allocated to the prosumers rather than economic benefits or utilities. This is
done to simplify the evaluation and ensure consistency across both the operational and
financial policies considered. Importantly, this should not compromise the validity of the
economic analysis since allocated power exports are directly proportional to economic

4.6
Efficiency Vs. Fairness: Pareto Frontier and PoF
When considering only operational fairness policies, one valuable analysis is to study
the impact of the such policies in the overall efficiency and hence curtailments of the
community as a whole. As a general rule already discussed in 4.2.1, when communities
are operated considering a fairness policy as opposed to solely maximizing efficiency, the
overall benefits of the community are decreased but more widely distributed among the
community. This trade-off can be explored by computing the Pareto frontier.

Until now, only two objectives (namely efficiency and a given fairness policy) have been
presented as part of a communities operational policy. This trade-off analysis is fully
reliant on all community members uniformly agreeing on a specific operational fairness
policy. In practice, though, different members could have different preferences on which
policy to adopt. For example, some might prefer proportionally allocated curtailments
while others might prefer equal curtailments. In this case, the multi-objective optimization
con be expanded to include the different preferences or objectives of the members.
Finding the Pareto frontier is increasingly difficult as the number of policies considered
increases. Additionally, drawing clear conclusions about how these policies affect one
another and the overall efficiency can become complex and ambiguous. One way to
find a solution which integrates the different policy preferences that may exist among
members is by expanding the NBS solution presented in 3.1.1 to an asymmetric NBS.

but expands to include different (asymmetric) weights to the different utilities considered.

agents [61][27]. In this case, the individual utilities of agents are substituted by the fairness

Results and Discussion
This results chapter begins with an overview of curtailment strategies aiming to highlight
their necessity in managing network constraints. The analysis contrasts scenarios with
no curtailment or passive strategies (e.g., Volt-VAr control) against those involving ac-
tive curtailment methods (such as Volt-VAr with active power curtailment, DOEs, and fair
curtailment policies). The impact of these policies on feeder total exports (efficiency),
maximum voltages and fairness is evaluated.
In the remaining sections, the proposed policies with active power curtailment to manage
network constraints are analyzed through a toy feeder model. First the current status quo
(fixed export limits with active power curtailments) and future efficiency-oriented opera-
tional policies (DOEs and Volt-VAr with active power curtailments) are analyzed in relation
to their efficiency and fairness implications. These policies aim at increasing total DER
output while managing network constraints and therefore are categorized as efficiency
policies. Following this, the impact on system efficiency of the different fairness policies
proposed (with and without reactive power management) is examined. Additionally, the
sensitivity analysis of efficiency versus fairness is presented through their Pareto fron-
tiers. Lastly, the overall impact of all these policies on prosumers curtailments/exports is
shown.
It is important to mention that the results shown consider scenarios with no line con-
gestion caused be thermal constraints. In other words, in the study conducted, voltage
constraints limits are met before thermal limits. This is done to streamline the analyses
as voltage-related curtailments in LV feeders are significantly more common than thermal
constraints [62]. The last section however offers an analyses on the impact of thermal
limits on prosumers exports.
5.1
Overview of Curtailments Strategies For Managing
Voltage Constraints
As discussed in 4.2.1, when handling network constraints, there is a loss of efficiency due
to curtailments to prevent violating them. In countries like Australia, DSOs have started
to implement fixed export limits for DER which introduce drastic inefficiencies in hopes of
managing network constraints. However, some solutions are already being considered to
unlock additional existing capacity available from DER in MV/LV networks while managing
network constraints. Some of these, such as DOEs, are currently under evaluation, while
others, like passive Volt-VAr control, are already integrated into newly manufactured in-
verters in some countries (i.e. Australia and New Zealand). Despite this, these solutions
overlook potential fairness issues caused by their curtailment strategies.

voltage and equal available net export capacities among the DER in the feeder. Figure 5.1

Recommendations
Following the discussion of the results, this section aims to offer a clear set of recom-
mendations to the different stakeholders concerned with fairness in low voltage networks
due to network constraints. First, the set of stakeholders are presented in relation to their
roles and responsibilities. Thereafter, the recommendations derived from the study are
outlined highlighting what stakeholders they involve and what actions they need to take.
In total, there are four stakeholders engaging in the recommendations presented:
1. Regulator. Decides and designs the rules by which distribution networks, ECs and
foreseeable markets should operate in. Hence, any operational policy concerning
fairness should be first approved by the regulator in order for it to be implemented.
2. DSO.Operates the distribution network ensuring reliable and secure delivery of elec-
tricity to all connected customers. Under network constraints, the DSO plays a cen-
tral role in managing grid capacity, planning reinforcements, and implementing op-
erational measures such as flexibility services or curtailment. Moreover, the DSO is
responsible of the interaction of the EC with the remaining distribution network.
3. Community Manager (CM): Operates the community level feeder ensuring that fair-
ness principles decided upon the communities members are followed. Moreover,
the community manager is responsible of the interactions of the different prosumer’s
technologies through smart devices with information and communication technolo-
gies (ICT).
4. Prosumer: End user owning consumption and generation technologies directly af-
fected by the state of the network and the operational policies approved by the reg-
ulator and/or community.

cases fail at managing network constraints (such as Volt-VAr, see figure 5.1 in results), the
following recommendations are outlined in table 6.1 to consider fairness while operating
the network securely inside its limits.

The first recommendation aims to highlight the importance of including other socio-economic
factors when assessing solutions to manage network constraints. This can lead to a
holistic assessment where prosumer’s interests are considered and balanced alongside
technical and socio-economic aspects. In this report, only non-network solutions are con-
sidered. For example, the impact of utilizing reactive power to enhance more efficient and
fair outcomes is demonstrated through the proposed reactive power management of ECs.
However network solutions related with grid reinforcements such as installing online tap
changing transformers (OLTCs), static VAr compensators (SVCs) or battery energy stor-
age systems (BESS) should be jointly assessed with the non-network solutions presented
to find the optimal solution.
The second recommendation outlines the importance of maximizing economic efficiency
while ensuring fairness standards are met. Depending on the feeder’s characteristics
and the penetration of DERs on the overall MV/LV network, the impact of solutions on
efficiency and fairness can have varied outcomes. In cases where high efficient solutions
severely compromises fairness, network solutions should be provided.
The third recommendation aims at establishing clear categories of approaches that can
be utilized to integrate fairness in chosen solutions. Each type of approach may have
several solutions. For example, in this report, several solutions are presented (i.e. pro-
portional and equal curtailments) under the by-design approach. If ex-post is preferred,
redistribution is necessary whereas for market/heuristic based approaches, posterior fair-
ness evaluation should be performed. In both cases, their fairness outcome should be
assessed to ensure they meet the desired objectives.
The fourth recommendation emphasizes the need for fairness metrics to clearly and intu-
itively reflect the specific fairness outcomes being targeted. Although fairness is inherently
complex, subjective, and difficult to quantify, well-designed metrics can effectively capture
its subtle and nuanced aspects. In practice, fairness metrics can give distinct outcomes
when used posteriorly to evaluate individual outcomes among prosumers. For by-design
approaches, where fairness is explicitly embedded in the solution, the outcome is fully
fair and remains consistent regardless of the metric chosen. In contrast, when assess-
ing solutions that do not explicitly account for fairness, the results can vary significantly
depending on the metric applied.
The fifth recommendation stresses the importance of having detailed knowledge on the
feeder being examined. Characteristics such as the R/X estimated of the cables, trans-
former capacity and range of voltage levels at the HoF are key determinants for under-
standing the impact and guiding the design of proposed solutions. For example feeders
with low R/X ratios may be able to manage voltage constraints only with fair community
policies under reactive power management.
The last recommendation proposes that local network constraints be managed through
small localized organizations such as ECs. This approach offers the advantage that fair-
ness principles can be more easily defined and agreed upon within smaller groups of
prosumers (such as those connected to the same feeder) who share similar interests
and conditions. Moreover, decisions made at this community level can be implemented
through the CM, who embeds these agreed-upon fairness rules directly into the control
strategies of the prosumers DERs. This localized management fosters greater trans-
parency, engagement, and alignment with the member’s values.

7
Conclusion
With the increasing deployment of DER in current LV electricity networks, new measures
are being explored to manage their interaction with the rest of the grid. Decisions on
these measures typically favor solutions that make the most out of the networks available
capacity (i.e. efficiency oriented). While this criteria seems reasonable, it overlooks is-
sues caused by externalities or other context-specific issues such as network constraints.
There are two ways to handle these externalities: (i) eliminating them or (ii) internalizing
them. The former could result in costly measures related to upgrading existing infrastruc-
ture in the system referred to as network solutions while the latter looks into introducing
new control measures to operationally manage network-constraints also known as non-
network solutions.
This work focuses on the second of these approaches and how internalizing these exter-
nalities through by-design or operational policies impacts household DER curtailments.
Moving beyond the usual focus on overall system efficiency, it introduces new social and
economic challenges that arise from policies aimed at improving efficiency and delivering
broader environmental and economic benefits. In particular, this work highlights the con-
cept of fairness, exploring how it can be defined and evaluated based on different fairness
principles within the context of forming an energy community.
First, it has been highlighted how the different metrics used to quantify fairness can in-
fluence the general perception of how fair an outcome is. After exploring the statistics
captured in the QoS or Jain’s index formulation, it has been shown how this metric can
lead to rather optimistic (upper bound) perceptions of fairness in a resource allocation
problem. To address this, a linear QoS metric has been introduced and proposed as a
more conservative alternative, serving as a lower-bound reference for fairness evaluation.
Following, the status quo has been evaluated with respect to system efficiency and fair-
ness according to different perceptions considered. With current fixed export limits, the
available capacity in the network is being under utilized leading not only to inefficient
but also unfair outcomes, especially under equal curtailments considerations.
Future
efficiency-oriented DOEs and Volt-VAr control strategies have been evaluated against
current practices, demonstrating substantial gains in efficiency. Fairness outcomes, how-
ever, are more nuanced. Under perceptions based on proportional curtailments, results
vary across scenarios and metrics, whereas under equal curtailment assumptions, fair-
ness shows noticeable improvements. It is worth noting that high R/X ratios along the
feeder can limit the effectiveness of Volt-VAr control, which may require increased reliance
on active power through direct curtailments or Volt-Watt control, potentially impacting both
efficiency and fairness.
To address these challenges, community-based operationally fair policies have been pro-
posed aimed at delivering fair operational solutions while assessing their impact on over-
all system efficiency. The trade-off analysis through Pareto curves show that under most
HoF voltage scenarios, fair outcomes can be reached with a small reduction in overall
efficiency. Furthermore, these policies are compared over the future efficiency-oriented
policies and the benefits of reactive power management are assessed. While no reactive
power management yields better results in terms of fairness, overall efficiency levels are
decreased relative to DOEs and Volt-VAr control. However, by including reactive power
management, efficiencies result considerably better while maintaining fair outcomes.

In response to binding network thermal constraints, the results illustrate how prosumers
located at the end of feeder can be further penalized, specially under Volt-VAr control.
In this case, reactive power management under fair community policies is ineffective in
terms of efficiency gains but fair outcomes can still be reached by redistributing the exports
according to fair principles.
Furthermore, with the case study presented, the following recommendations have been
derived to handle fairness concerns in DER rich LV networks arising because of increasing
curtailments due to network constraints:
1. Incorporate both economic and socio-economic factors, including fairness, into cost–
benefit analyses of network and non-network solutions for managing constraints.
2. Perform sensitivity analyses to understand the trade-offs between economic effi-
ciency and fairness in policy design.
3. Clearly define the fairness integration approach used (by-design, ex-post, or mar-
ket/heuristic) when proposing solutions.
4. Select fairness metrics that intuitively reflect the intended fairness concepts and
assess their impacts on individual prosumer outcomes.
5. Conduct pre-assessments of feeder characteristics to inform the design and evalu-
ation of constraint management solutions.
6. Manage local network constraints through fair, locally-driven approaches enabled
by aggregated schemes like ECs with agreed-upon rules.
Lastly, additional future work could be oriented to:
1. Include other DER technologies such as BESS and how they can interact with the
rest of DER to reach the most cost-effective and fair solutions. While this work pro-
poses reactive power management to manage network constraints, BESS can do
this through their flexible operation and unlock revenue streams for system services.
2. Study the socio-economic and environmental benefits of community managed and
owned DER versus individually owned but community managed DER. This work
only considers individually owned rooftop PV assets. However, community-owned
assets could lead to similar results with lower individual investment costs due to
economies of scale.
3. Study ex-post solutions and how these can virtually mitigate the effects of network-
constraints through financial redistribution.
Given that this work focuses on by-
design or operational policies, such ex-post solutions can offer complementary tools
to compensate for residual unfair outcomes.
4. Extend studies and sensitivities to different type of networks since different feeder
characteristics such as length, lifetime, underground/overhead cables and R/X ra-
tios will have different effects on the outcomes of operational policies.
5. Design and implement control for reactive power management of DER and under-
take case study with real LV network. By leveraging smart-meter data and informa-
tion and communication technologies (ICT), distributed assets can be coordinated
to set their active and reactive (P-Q) outputs to achieve the most fair and efficient
outcomes. This can be tested on a real network to validate the results of this work
and assess its practicality.

merely installed in power systems making the most out of the system’s assets and infras-
tructure in the most efficient and fair manner. In doing so, both societal and individual
interests are aligned, fostering wider acceptance and engagement with the energy tran-
