ABSTRACT
IX
Abstract
The aim of this thesis is to evaluate the feasibility of integrating a heat pump into
a biogas upgrading system compared with conventional heat production methods,
such as straw or gas boilers. Two heat pump applications were appraised:(1) inte-
grating a Vapour Compression Heat Pump (VCHP) to recover waste heat, with a
boiler as main heat supply and integrating a Hybrid Compression Absorption Heat
Pump (HACHP) to supply the entire heat demand.
The proposed methodology compares the feasibility of the cases of study using
an economic model, based on the working conditions of the biogas upgrading plant.
The working conditions were previously calculated in a thermodynamic model, im-
plemented in Aspen.
A validation performed on the Aspen model showed good
overall concurrency with measured data from Madsen Bioenergi biogas plant. The
largest deviation was found in the heat recovered into the reactors, showing a dis-
crepancy of 5.7%.
The optimal fuel for the boiler was assessed, both when it works alone and to-
gether with the VCHP. Straw was found as the optimal fuel with savings potential of
3.3% and 19%, compared to the natural gas and the biogas respectively in nominal
conditions.
The base case and the two heat pump applications where compared to establish
the optimal solution for diﬀerent working conditions. In the nominal conditions,
using a straw boiler was found optimal. However, with an electricity price reduction
of 13%, utilizing HACHP becomes optimal. Besides, with lower CO2 concentration
in the biogas, the reduction needed in the electricity price decreases; being required
only 4% when the CO2 concentration is 26%. Using VCHP with a straw boiler,
was found less economically competitive than the other cases of study for all the
conditions analyzed.
Aside of assessing the best available technology, a sensitivity analysis was per-
formed to address the main drivers of NPV for the three cases of study. Two scenarios
were considered:(1) actual biomethane price in Denmark and (2) biomethane price
equal to natural gas. In the ﬁrst scenario, biomethane price was found as the most
inﬂuencing factor. Moreover, since the rest of the parameters have minor inﬂuence
over the NPV, the three cases of study can be considered robust business cases. In
the second scenario, biomethane price is still the most sensitive factor. However, the
uncertainly of the business cases increases, since the NPV becomes more sensitive
to the parameters evaluated, especially in the HACHP.

1.1
Motivation
The growing energy demand, the limited amount of fossil fuels and the global con-
cern about the greenhouse gas emissions and the pollution, are one of the main
concerns and subjects of discussion among the world’s political delegates. Denmark
is the ﬁrst country to propose a speciﬁc plan for achieving a fossil fuel free energy
and transportation sector by 2050. In order to face these challenges, new types of
energy fuels and energy savings are needed.
The biogas industry represents one of these new types of energy fuels, being able
to fulﬁll most of these targets. The composition of the biogas is mainly methane
and carbon dioxide. There are several ways of utilizing biogas as heating, electricity
production, transport fuel or energy storage. Despite combustion of biogas produces
carbon dioxide (CO2), it comes from the CO2 accumulated along the life time of
the plant matter, which it would have been emitted anyway without the energy
utilization. Hence, this gas is considered CO2 neutral since it does not add CO2 to
the greenhouse emissions. Moreover, any fossil fuel consumption replaced by biogas
will lower the CO2 emissions.
Additionally, biogas can perform an important role in the electricity market.
Since the introduction of variable renewable energy sources, such as wind and solar
energy, will increase the variability and uncertainty of the electricity production;
the need of power system ﬂexibility will be substantial. Being the biogas a suitable
solution due to the energy storage capacity of the CH4 [1].
Depending on the end use, diﬀerent biogas treatments are necessary. For some
applications, where it is required to have a high energy content, e.g.
as vehicle
fuel, energy storage or grid injection, the biogas needs to be upgraded. The energy
content of the biogas is in direct proportion on the methane content; therefore, by
removing the carbon dioxide the energy content of the gas increases.
This project is developed in collaboration with Ammongas AS, a company spe-
cialized in biogas upgrading by using chemical scrubbing system. Mono ethanolamine
(MEA) is the solvent used for the adsorption of the carbon dioxide.

1.2. PROBLEM STATEMENT

1.2
Problem statement
The aim of this thesis is to evaluate the integration of a heat pump (HP) into a
biogas upgrading system (BUS). The aim is narrowed further to investigate the po-
tential of heat pumps regarding high temperature heat applications in a BUS, and
to establish whether the HP is a feasible technology compared with the conventional
heat production methods, already integrated into the BUS (e.g. gas or straw boilers).
Two potential HP applications are analyzed, waste heat upgrading and high
temperature heat generation. Moreover, a thermodynamic model is developed to
simulate the BUS and the two HP applications. The purpose of the model is to out-
line the performance of the BUS in diﬀerent working conditions. The model will be
validated against experimental data obtained from a BUS implemented in Madsen
Bioenergi biogas plant (Skive, Denmark).
An economic model is developed to assess the feasibility of the HP compared
with conventional heat sources (e.g. gas and straw boilers), based on the perfor-
mances obtained from the thermodynamic model.
Primary objectives
• To perform a heat balance into the biogas upgrading plant.
• To develop a thermodynamic model capable of simulating a biogas upgrading
plant and the two heat pump applications.
• To verify and to validate the thermodynamic model against experimental data.
• To develop an economic model capable of assessing the feasibility of the heat
pump applications in diﬀerent scenarios.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
1.3
Biogas technology
Biogas production consist of the anaerobic digestion of biodegradable raw material.
It is based on microbial activity in oxygen-free conditions (anaerobic), resulting in
two-end products: energy rich biogas, mainly methane and carbon dioxide and a
nutrient-rich digestion residue (digestate) [2].
1.3.1
Anaerobic degradation
During the anaerobic degradation process, diﬀerent microbial consortia degrade the
raw material in four steps:
• Hydrolysis: During the hydrolysis, the polymers (carbohydrates, proteins and
lipids) are degraded into monomers by hydrolytic enzymes. This process is
highly dependent on the surface area of the raw material. Hence, the higher
surface area, the more eﬃcient the process is [3].Hydrolysis is often the rate-
limiting step in the anaerobic digestion, being some pre-treatments, such as
macerations, used to accelerate it [4].
• Acidogenesis: Once the raw materials are degraded into monomers, the acido-
genic bacteria transforms the long chain fatty acids (LCFA), such as alcohols,
simple sugars and amino acids into volatile fatty acids (VFA) [5]. The type of
VFA obtained depends on the operational conditions, raw materials and mi-
crobial activity. Besides, one product from the acidogenesis is the ammonium-
nitrogen (NH4+-N), remarkable due to its toxicity and therefore it has to be
removed in a post-treatment.
• Acetogenesis: During the acetogenesis the VFAs are degraded into acetate,
hydrogen and carbon dioxide.
• Methanogenesis: The methane-producing microbes (methanogens) convert the
acetate and hydrogen into methane, carbon dioxide and small traces of other
gaseous compounds. Approximately 70% of the methane is produced from
acetates (acetoclastic methanogens) and the 30 % remaining from hydrogen
(hydrogenotropic methanogens) [6].

1.3.2
Factors aﬀecting anaerobic eﬃciency
There are several factors aﬀecting the global biogas production eﬃciency, deﬁning
eﬃciency in terms of methane concentration [9]. The most important factors are
listed following.
• pH: The acidity level is especially important for hydrolysis and methanogene-
sis. However, the optimal pH value for hydrolysis is 6.0, while for methanogen-
esis varies from 6.0 to 8.0 depending on the methanogens microbes involved in
the process. Thus, a compromised solution is needed. Moreover, pH values do
not only aﬀect the anaerobic digestion directly though the microbes but also
indirectly via the chemical equilibrium of the possible ammonia formed.
• Temperature: Temperature inﬂuences the growth and survival of the microbes
in charge of the anaerobic digestion [8]. The chemical and enzymatic reactions
depends on the temperature; being accelerated when the temperature rises.
This statement is only true up to certain temperature values, further than
these values the microbes may be irreversible damaged. The optimal temper-
ature in the anaerobic reaction vary with the type of microbe. Psychrophilic
and psychrotolerant microbes grow up with temperatures from 0-20 C. How-
ever, psychrophilic microbes have limited applications, such as biogas plants
with wastewaters with high amounts of soluble organic compounds. Mesophilic
microbes have their operation temperature at 30-40 C and thermophilic at
55-60 C. These two types of microbes are the most common when digesting
heterogonous raw material. To keep these temperatures in the digestion, the
reactor has to be heated, being the main heat expense in the biogas plant.
• Inhibition: Ammoniﬁcation of organic nitrogen compounds produces ammo-
nium nitrogen, which can form ammonia (NH3). Ammonia is toxic to microbes
at high concentrations; therefore since the amount of ammonia changes with
the temperature and the pH, it becomes a limiting factor in the biogas pro-
duction.
• Mixing and hydraulic time of retention (HRT): Homogeneous quality in the
digester is important for an eﬃcient biogas production. Moreover, the agita-
tion does not only guaranty homogeneity, but also allows the bubbles of gas
to release the digested mass into the biogas collector system. The Hydraulic
time of retention depends on the reactor size and the volume of daily feed.
The longer the HRT, the higher is the amount of organic material degraded
and therefore the higher biogas is produced. The HRT varies within 14-50
days, depending on the type and amount of raw materials.
• Raw materials: The ﬁnal methane concentration in the biogas stream depends
on the raw materials content of carbohydrates, fats and proteins. In theory,

1.3.3
Technological solutions for biogas plants
Despite biogas generation is based on anaerobic digestion, the actual large central-
ized scale biogas plants consist of diﬀerent processing units. These units are classiﬁed
into three categories; pre-treatments, digesters and post- treatments.
• Pre-treatments: Diﬀerent pre-treatments can be applied before the biogas re-
actor in order to increase the performance of the biogas production and ensure
the quality of the digestate [10].
– Size reduction: Smaller particles in the raw material imply a higher sur-
face area for the hydrolytic enzymes to dissolve them into more soluble
components, improving substantially the performance [11]. The smaller
particles are also easier to feed into the digester and they ensure the
homogeneity of the mixture since they are less prone to ﬂoat in the re-
actors. Therefore, many raw materials are macerated or chopped before
the digestion stage.
– Thermal treatment: Hygienisation or sterilization may be used as a pre-
treatment to biogas processes, especially in cases where the raw materials
are potential hygienic risky [12]. These thermal treatments suppose the
main heat expense in the pre-treatments stages.
• Digesters: In the digester takes place the anaerobic digestion of the raw ma-
terials. There are many diﬀerent types of digesters, however only continuous
stirred-tank reactor (CSTR) is explained since is the most common type. The
CSTR consist of an above-ground tank with a ﬁxed roof. The biogas is col-
lected from the top of the tank and is pumped to a diﬀerent gas holder. The
digestate is removed from the bottom of the tank. The anaerobic digestion
requires a constant temperature, being the largest heat expense of the biogas
plant the heat demanded by the reactor. Since homogeneity is also needed
in the biodegradable material, an agitator is installed into the reactor. An

1.4
Heat pump technology
A heat pump is a refrigeration device used to move heat from a low-temperature
level to a higher temperature level.
Heat pumps draw heat from a heat source
(input heat), to a sink at a higher temperature (output heat), through a closed pro-
cess [18]. Depending on the energy source for the heat pumps, they can be classiﬁed
into compression heat pumps (using electricity), absorption heat pumps (using heat)
or compression-absorption heat pumps (hybrid).
An important advantage regarding heat pumps is the capacity to produce both
heating and cooling. Hence, the “product” of a heat pump can be both heating and
cooling at the same time.
Heat pumps are utilized for industrial processes (both for heat supply and waste
heat recovery), individual space heating and district heat production.
In this speciﬁc study, the heat pump will be used for waste heat recovery and
heat supply in a biogas upgrading plant.
Waste heat recovery and heat supply
needs 86 and 110 ◦C respectively, therefore only high temperature heat pumps will
be considered.

1.4.2
Vapour compression heat pumps for high temperature
applications
The vapour compression heat pumps are the simplest type of heat pump. The work-
ing principle is based on the reversed Rankine cycle. Many modiﬁcations can be
done to the simple cycle to improve the eﬃciency. However, they are out of the
scope of this thesis.
The main components of a compression heat pump are: condenser, evaporator,
compressor and expansion valve. In the close cycle, a refrigerant is used to transfer
the heat from the heat source to the heat sink. In the evaporator, the refrigerant
vaporized due to the absorption of heat from the heat source. From the evaporator,
the refrigerant is compressed to increase the temperature and pressure in order to
change state to saturated vapor. In the condenser, the refrigerant released the heat
to a lower temperature sink until this condensate. Finally, the expansion valve leads
the refrigerant to the initial state by reducing its pressure. The result is a liquid-
vapor mixture phase which enters again into the evaporator and close the cycle. An
example of a standard VCHP is presented in ﬁgure 1.6
Figure 1.6: Compression heat pump [18].
The vapour compression heat pumps are a viable option for heat recovery of
low grade waste heat, where a heat source of 0-50◦C should be upgraded to up to
90 ◦C [19]. There are many refrigerants available for this range of temperatures,
but the two more commonly used are ammonia and carbon dioxide, since they are
natural refrigerants with no ODP and no or low GWP.
Both refrigerants can upgrade water up to 90 C. However, the optimal election
depends on the sink inlet temperatures. For inlet temperatures below 20 C, carbon

1.4.3
Hybrid compression adsorption heat pump
Ammonia-water hybrid compression-absorption heat pump (HACHP) is a new type
of high temperature industrial heat pump. HACHP allows to supply temperatures
up to 111 C by using standard 28 bar refrigeration components [20]. What makes
this technology suitable for industrial heat supply and recovery of waste heat at
high temperature. A summary of the working principle of the HACHP is presented
in ﬁgure 1.7
Figure 1.7: (a) Components from HACHP, (b) HACHP process sketched in temper-
ature [20].
The working principle of the HACHP is as follows. The working ﬂuid is a water-
ammonia mixture, which is a liquid-vapour mixture in the outlet of the desorber.
The liquid has lean concentration of ammonia, while the gas phase is mainly am-
monia with some traces of water. Before the compression stage, the liquid and the
gas phases are separated and sent to the pump and compressor respectively.
In the compressor, the gas increases its pressure and temperature before entering
the gas cooler, where the temperature decreases while releasing heat to the sink.

In the pump, the lean liquid is compressed, nevertheless since liquid phase is
almost incompressible, the temperature increase over the pump is small. Therefore,
the liquid needs to be reheated in the internal heat exchanger to reduce the entropy
generation when mixing with the vapour phase.
Before entering the absorber, the vapour and liquid phases are mixed in an adia-
batic process. When thermodynamic equilibrium is reached, the absorption begins.
In this step, the ammonia is absorbed in the liquid solution while releasing heat to
the sink.
After the absorption, the mixture is subcooled in the internal heat exchanger
and expanded into the throttling valve before feeding the desorber. In the desorber
the vapour phase is generated by transferring heat from the source [21].
The ﬁrst advantage of the HACHP compared with compression heat pumps is
the lower vapour pressure, since the refrigerant used is not a pure volatile compo-
nent. This implies that HACHP can supply higher temperatures by using standard
refrigeration components.
A further advantage of the HACHP is the non-isothermal process of absorption
compared with the isothermal process of condensation in the vapour compression
heat pump. Therefore, the HACHP cycle approach better the Lorenz cycle, dealing
with a higher COP due to the reduction of the entropy generation by heat transfer
over a ﬁnite temperature diﬀerence.
Theoretically, the maximum discharge temperature is 111 ◦C with standard 28
bar components. However, with special high-pressure compressors, temperatures
of 129◦C (50 bar) and 147◦C (120 bar) are achievable. Limiting the compressor
discharge temperature to 180◦C to ensure the thermal stability of the lubricating
oil and the thermal stress of the compressor materials [20].

Methods
A methodology was developed to address the problem statement and fulﬁll the pri-
mary objectives of this thesis.
Before developing the methodology, a pinch analysis of the biogas plant with
the biogas upgrading system was carried out to verify the technical feasibility of the
cases of study 1 or 2.
The methodology performs a private economic feasibility analysis of a biogas
plant with a biogas upgrading system, in diﬀerent technical and economic scenarios.
This methodology is shown in ﬁgure 2.1.
The ﬁrst step was to create a model able to calculate the heat expenses in the
biogas plant and the biogas upgrading system.
The heat expenses in a biogas plant mainly depend on the amount of raw ma-
terial processed and the type of bacteria used. Usually, these two parameters do
not change in the biogas plant normal operation. Therefore, a constant heat con-

2.1
Pinch analysis
The aim of the pinch analysis was to appraise the technical feasibility of the cases
of study proposed. Whether the system has no need of external cooling or heating,
the approach of using heat pumps is not technically praised anymore.
The pinch analysis has two parts: (1) the maximum heat recovery calculation
and (2) the design of the optimal heat exchanger network. The second part is out
of the scope of this thesis since it implies a total redesign of the biogas plant layout.
To accomplish the pinch analysis, the methodology proposed by Rokni.M [32]
was followed. The ﬁrst step was to identify the streams to include in the analysis.
Only the process streams were considered, not the utilities.
Once the streams were deﬁned, the temperature intervals needed to be establish.

interval was between the largest and the second largest temperature. The next was

amount of heat needed was calculated by using equation 2.1

2.2
Private economic feasibility analysis
The objective of the private economic feasibility analysis was to calculate key eco-
nomic evaluation parameters, such as Net Present Value (NPV) , Internal Rate of
Return (IRR) and Payback Time (PB), using three diﬀerent inputs types: Finan-
cially, technically and analytically based inputs, for the three cases of study.
• Financially based inputs: Investment, maintenance and fuel costs, in addition
to the ﬁnancial parameters required, such as discount rate, inﬂation, tax rate
etc.
• Technically based inputs: The type of fuel used in the boiler. There are three
options; biogas, natural gas and straw.
• Analytically based inputs: The heat balances of the biogas plant and the biogas
upgrading system. They were necessary to assess the operational cost of the
system.
In order to perform the analysis, some assumptions were required. The assump-
tions are listed below:
• The biogas plant was assumed to run 8760 hours per years, not considering
the maintenance time of the installation.
• The lifetime of biogas plant and biogas upgrading system was assumed to be
20 years.
• The discount, inﬂation and tax rates were ﬁxed to 4.25%, 1.9% and 22%
respectively. Based on assumptions by Danish Energy agency and Skat [24].
• Electricity and natural gas prices correspond to market cost for industrial
customers in Denmark in year 2016, according to eurostats [25]. They were
assumed ﬁxed during the lifetime of the project, only changing with the inﬂa-
tion.
• Biomethane and straw price, were the prices corresponding to market cost in
Denmark in year 2016, data obtained from the biogas plant.

3.4
Fuel optimization
The object of this section is to establish the optimal fuel (biogas, natural gas and
straw) for each case of study, in diﬀerent technical and economic scenarios, by using
the methodology developed.
In the technical scenarios, the inﬂuence of heat consumption over the fuel choice
is analyzed. The heat consumption is varied by changing the CO2 concentration in
the inlet biogas, and therefore the heat consumed by the biogas upgrading system.
The CO2 range evaluated varies from 30% to 45%, since it is the range of CO2
typically found in biogas.
In the economic scenarios, the cash ﬂows are modiﬁed by varying the biomethane
price, to appraise their inﬂuence over the fuel election. Three scenarios are proposed:
(1) in the ﬁrst one, so called low price, the biomethane price is assumed to be equal
to the natural gas. (2) In the second scenario, the actual biomethane price in Den-
mark is considered and (3) in the third one, the price linearly decreases from the
actual price to the natural gas price along the lifetime of the project.
The values were chosen to simulate extreme biomethane scenarios. In the ﬁrst
scenario it is simulated the situation where the biomethane price has no government
support, this is currently the most common situation in the European countries
and the worst case scenario. In the second scenario, the current situation regarding
biomethane price is Denmark is analyzed. Finally, in the third scenario, a transition
from the actual biomethane prices until the natural gas prices is assumed, simulating
possible future for biomethane prices in Denmark.

Chapter 4
Discussion
The economic feasibility of integrating a heat pump into the biogas upgrading system
compared with the conventional heat production methods was assess in this thesis.
The integration of HACHP for supplying the heat consumption of the biogas plant
has shown a high potential; since it has been pointed as optimal solution in some
speciﬁc conditions. However, the integration of a VCHP for waste heat recovery has
shown the lowest proﬁtability in all the working domain evaluated. Moreover, the
optimal conﬁguration for the current working conditions of the biogas plant is using
a conventional boiler with straw as fuel.
As previously stated, before developing the methodology, it was appraised the
technical feasibility of the cases of study, by doing a pinch analysis. The pinch anal-
ysis showed that the minimum external heat required by the biogas plant was 895
kW, in the nominal conditions, which is the heat needed in the stripper to regenerate
the MEA. Therefore, this fact outline the importance of analyzing new absorption
solvents less heat demanding, since the minimum total heat consumption of the bio-
gas plant will reduce proportionally. The minimum external cooling calculated was
74 kW, for the 300 kW actually required. Implying that the existing heat exchang-
ers grid for recuperation is not optimal and promoting the use of external active
components like heat pumps. Therefore, since both cases of study proposed the
use of heat pumps across the pinch point and they are able to provide the required
temperature, they are considered technically feasible solutions.
A methodology was developed to carry out the analysis. However, as for any
deterministic model, the simpliﬁcations leads to uncertainly in the results. For this
reason it is important to analyze the limitations and consequences of the elements
used in this methodology.
The two models developed in the methodology are based on the experimental
measurements made in the biogas plant. Therefore, the uncertainly of the measure-
ments performed inﬂuence directly the results obtained from the methodology. As
the number of measurements was limited specially in the biogas upgrading facility,
some uncertainly needs to be considered in the results.
A thermodynamic model was constructed in Aspen with the purpose of simu-

conclusion that the results obtained from the Aspen model were acceptably reliable,
hence deeming the model ﬁt for application.
Regarding the economic model, several assumptions were needed to simplify the
calculations and ensure the robustest of the model. The investment, capital and
maintenance cost were obtained from veriﬁed sources, in addition to the ﬁnancial
parameters such as discount rate, tax rate, inﬂation etc. Related to this, it is im-
portant to remark that all the cost were assumed constant along the lifetime of the
project, specially unlikely assumption for the electricity, gas natural and biomethane
price. However, the lack of consensual forecasts for electricity and natural prices
justify this decision. Concerning the biomethane price, it is not so much market
dependent but it depends on the governments support, making it even more unpre-
dictable. On the other hand, it is propose as future work, to analyze the electricity
and natural gas forecasts in order to include them into the economic model and thus
have a more realistic approach.

capital cost of using straw, it seems to be cheaper fuel than the natural gas since

increasing until 5.5% when the CO2 in the biogas is 45%. It is remarkable the strong
NPV dependency on to the biomethane price, but nevertheless it does no inﬂuence
the election of the optimal fuel.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
The feasibility of integrating a heat pump into a biogas upgrading system, compared
with traditional heat supply sources, has been addressed in this thesis. Two heat
pump applications has been considered: (1) VCHP for recovering waste heat and
(2) HACHP for supplying the heat consumption of the biogas plant. A preliminary
pinch analysis was performed outlining the technical potential of the solutions pro-
posed.
A methodology was developed to carry out the analysis. It consists of an econom-
ical model calculating ﬁnancial evaluation parameters, based on the heat expenses
of the biogas plant and the biogas upgrading system. The heat consumption of the
biogas part was calculated with a simple heat balance. However, a thermodynamic
model in Aspen was constructed to calculate the heat expenses in the biogas up-
grading system. The model was validated against experimental data measured in
Madsen Bioenergi biogas plant.
The methodology was applied to assess the optimal fuel for each case of study,
in diﬀerent working and economic conditions. In the case of study 2, electricity
is used as fuel since all the heat consumption is provided by the HACHP. In the
base case and case of study 1, straw was established as optimal fuel; resulting in a
NPV increase of approximately 3.3% and 19%, compared to natural gas and biogas
respectively in nominal conditions.
Moreover, the best technology solution was outlined for diﬀerent working con-
ditions. It was found that in nominal conditions, using straw boiler is the optimal
solution. However, with a reduction of a 13% in the electricity price, using HACHP
to supply the heat demand is more proﬁtable. Besides, with lower the CO2 concen-
tration in the biogas, the reduction needed in the electricity price is lower; being
required only 4% when the CO2 concentration is 26%. Additionally, it was found
that the biomethane price has not inﬂuence over the optimal technology election.
Finally, using VCHP for waste heat recovery was found less economically competi-
tive than the other cases of study for all the conditions analyzed.
The main drivers of NPV were identiﬁed in a sensitivity analysis. Two scenarios
were considered: (1) actual biomethane price and (2) biomethane price equal to nat-

ural gas. For the ﬁrst scenario, biomethane price was found as the most inﬂuencing
factor. Moreover, since the rest of the parameters have minor inﬂuence over the
NPV, the three cases of study can be considered robust business cases. However in
the second scenario, even though the biomethane is still the most inﬂuencing factor,

The integration of HACHP for supplying the heat of the biogas plant, has been
