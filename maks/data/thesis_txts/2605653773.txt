Abstract
Objective
Herlev Hospital was recently the first clinic in the world to implement a novel com-
mercial solution for online adaptive radiotherapy based on cone beam computed tomog-
raphy (CBCT) driven by artificial intelligence (AI). During an AI driven deformable
image registration, the system generates a so called synthetic CT from the daily CBCT
for dose calculation and optimize plan adapted to the anatomy of the day. However,
the deformable image registration and the resulting synthetic CT inherently has uncer-
tainties both geometrically but also in the assigned Hounsfield units (HU). To minimize
these uncertainties, this project aims at assessing the feasibility of direct use of CBCT
for dose calculation in AI-driven CBCT-based oART.
Material and Methods
The study was divided into three parts: 1. Determining HU-relative electron density
(RED) calibration curves for a range of CT and clinical CBCT modes: An electron
density phantom was scanned for a range of CT and clinical CBCT modes. The clin-
ical CBCT were reconstructed with a dedicated algorithm including motion artifact
reduction and called improved CBCT images. The HU-RED calibration curves were
constructed for each scan protocol and compared. 2. Phantom study: Dose calculation
was done on a verification Alderson phantom in order to investigate the CBCT dose
accuracy based on the acquired HU-RED calibration curves in first part. Results were
evaluated by gamma analysis. 3. Patient study: Dose calculation was done on CT, clin-
ical CBCT and improved CBCTs of fourteen patients based on the acquired HU-RED
calibration curves in first part. Results were evaluated by dose volume histogram (DVH)
and gamma analysis.
Results
1. Calibration curves for CT, clinical CBCTs and improved CBCTs were well matched
in soft tissue area but were further apart in the bone area due to more scatter. 2. Hav-
ing no ED change in phantom, the dose distributions between pCT and CBCTs were in
100 percent agreement as expected. 3. Dosimetric evaluations, dose profile and DVH
comparisons of CT and CBCTs were found to be in agreement with one another. In
addition, gamma analysis results showed acceptable passing rates of superior to 96.3%
for 3% /3mm dose-difference (DD) and distance-to-agreement (DTA) criteria using 20%

Conclusion
It is concluded that CBCT can be used for direct dose calculation in AI-driven CBCT-
based oART but some restrictions like limited field of view and image quality of CBCT
images require further investigation for more patients.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Radiotherapy (RT) is one of the most important modalities in oncology to cure cancer.
Concentrating the radiation dose in the tumor and avoiding organs at risk (OARs) is
of outmost importance for the success of the treatment. However, the positions and
shape of organs of tissues is changing during the course of treatment. Online adaptive
RT where the treatment is adapted to the anatomy of the patient directly prior to the
actual dose delivery, is the most advanced approach to overcome this problem.
Herlev Hospital was recently the first clinic in the world to implement a novel commer-
cial solution for online adaptive radiotherapy based on cone beam computed tomography
driven by artificial intelligence (AI). During an AI driven deformable image registration,
the system generates a so called synthetic CT from the daily CBCT for dose calculation
and optimized plan adapted to the anatomy of the day. However, the deformable image
registration and the resulting synthetic CT inherently has uncertainties both geometri-
cally but also in the assigned Hounsfield units (HU). One approach to minimize these
uncertainties could be to instead rely on the CBCT for direct dose calculation.
In order to assess the feasibility of using CBCT directly in online adaptive radiotherapy,
this study aims to:
• Determine calibration curves (mass and electron density vs HU) for a range of
clinical CBCT modes
• Perform treatment planning and full dose calculations using an advanced dose
calculation algorithm (Acuros) on CBCT images acquired for both phantoms and
patients – to be assessed quantitatively compared to corresponding calculations of
reference CT scans
• Quantify clinical CBCT data in terms of artifacts (qualitatively) to later be corre-
lated to dose accuracy
• Investigate quality of re-reconstructed CBCTs using improved reconstruction algo-
rithms
• Compare dose calculated on the synthetic CT created during adaptive radiotherapy
with direct dose calculation on CBCT

CHAPTER 2
Background
Cancer has continuously remained one of the two leading causes of death in Denmark
over the last decade, and was responsible for 15342 deaths in 2018 [24]. Along other
treatment modalities for cancer such as surgery and chemotherapy, radiotherapy is a
fundamental pillar in multidisciplinary treatment of many cancer types. It is a non-
invasive effective treatment for both curative and palliative purpose and about half of
all cancer patients in Denmark receive radiotherapy at some point of their treatment
[11], [19].
External beam radiation and internal radiation therapy are two main radiotherapy types.
This study is based on external beam radiotherapy which is further explained in the fol-
lowing sections of this chapter.
2.1
External beam radiotherapy
External beam RT treatment (EBRT) involves linear accelarators (LINACs) that gener-
ate high energy ionizing radiation to destroy the DNA of cancerous cells. The aim of RT
is to destroy the tumor by targeting it with high doses of radiation while keeping the doses
to organs at risk (OARs) to a minimal level [3]. For this aim and depending on tumor
type as well as location of the cancer, different methods of EBRT are performed; con-
formal radiotherapy, intensity-modulated radiotherapy (IMRT), volumetric-modulated
arc radiotherapy (VMAT), image guided radiotherapy (IGRT) and proton beam therapy
[34].
Conformal radiotherapy shapes the radiation beams to precisely fit around the tumor
area. For this purpose, the LINAC is equipped with a multileaf collimator (MLC) with
thin leaves of tungsten. These leaves can move independently and form a beam shape
that conforms the dose precisely around the tumor or target volume. IMRT is a type of
conformal radiotherapy, where each radiation beam is split up into more small beamlets
that can variate the intensity of radiation so that complex dose patterns which minimize
dose to OARs and ensure complete dose coverage of the tumor can be delivered. VMAT
is a type of IMRT, where the radiation source contiguously rotates in an arc around the
patient making the delivery time shorter and reduce the used overall radiation dose.
In order to understand the EBRT treatment delivery, some fundamentals need to be

2.2
Fundamentals of EBRT
To deliver an EBRT treatment, the patient undergoes one or several imaging procedures,
mainly computed tomography. These images are used to delineate all anatomical vol-
umes relevant to high precision radiotherapy. Next, these segmented images are used to
generate a personal treatment plan for the patient. The aim of the treatment planning
process is to establish the optimal clinically desired dose distribution and its technical
delivery.
2.2.1
Image acquisition and Volume Delineation
A crucial step in RT is the localization of the tumor volume. This is done by acquiring
a CT scan (and in many cases additional magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) or/and
positron emission tomography (PET)-CT are acquired to provide a better soft-tissue
contrast) of the patient. This CT scan referred to planning CT (pCT) will be used for
contouring three main tumor volumes and OARs adjacent to the tumor. This process is
called volume delineation. The purpose of volume delineation or contouring is to make
sure that the tumor can be precisely targeted with the prescribed dose and other healthy
tissues are spared from unnecessary irradiation.
At Herlev Hospital for treatments with the Ethos linear accelerator (Varian Medical
Systems, CA, USA), the volume delineation is performed by a radiation oncologist on
pCT images, later in the adaptive course, an AI-based algorithm generates the delin-
eation on CBCT images for on-couch adaption and it can be corrected manually by an
operator. The most important anatomical volumes employed in RT are listed below.
Gross tumor volume (GTV) - is the gross tumor that is visible by either eye or
palpation through clinical examination or imaging techniques (CT scan, MRI, PET).
The whole GTV should therefore receive an adequate dose as determined by the treat-
ment planning process. This volume is shown as red color in figure 2.1 for a rectum
patient [21], [6], [28].
Clinical Target Volume (CTV) - is the volume including the GTV and the tis-
sue around that is affected due to sub-clinical disease spread which needs to be treated
[2]. This volume is delineated as the pink contour in figure 2.1.

Planning Target Volume (PTV) - includes CTV with anisotropic margins to con-
sider possible geometric uncertainties in beam alignment, patient positioning, organ
motion, and organ deformation [2], [26]. This volume is shown as blue color in figure
2.1.
OARs - Organs at risk are those healthy tissue or organs around the target volume
with important functions in body. Extra irradiation of these structures can result in se-
rious irreversible damage, therefore the dose delivered to these organs must be restricted
and stay within clinically defined dose limits and dose volume constraints [17]. OARs
can be seen as yellow and green color in figure 2.1 which represent bladder and bowel
bag respectively.

2.2.2
Treatment Planning
After volume delineation, treatment planning is carried out in a treatment planning sys-
tem (TPS). The TPS facilitates a virtual simulation of the upcoming treatment of the
patient with the aim of finding the dose delivery parameters which warrant an optimal
clinical treatment outcome.
This includes, simulation of the beam geometry and arrangement, dose delivery and
dose assessment and optimization of the plan. Dosimetrists or medical physicists use
the TPS to define the beam shape and intensity by arranging a number of beam fields
with different angles and different intensities based on the selected delivery technique
(such as IMRT or VMAT) and the pre-delineated volumes. Accordingly, the simulation
system determines the prescribed radiation dose in Monitor units (MU) to be delivered
by the treatment machine. The dose distribution is calculated to asses the quality of
the plan by considering the delivered dose mainly to PTV and OARs. This evaluation
is done in terms of DVH which will be discussed in section 2.2.

Once the treatment plan is ready, it must be verified before the actual dose delivery
to the patient.
This is called patient specific quality assurance (PSQA) and is per-
formed to minimize the likelihood of systematic errors and eventually provide a safe and
effective treatment.
2.2.3
Conventional radiotherapy workflow
In a conventional RT workflow, after image acquisition and contouring and once the
treatment plan is ready, the quality assurance of the plan will be done. This process
is called PSQA. In conventional RT, this verification is performed by transferring the
treatment plan to a quality assurance (QA) phantom and the dose is recalculated and
compared to measured data. The planned and delivered dose distribution will be com-
pared by a quantitative method called gamma evaluation which will be explained in
section 2.2.
Finally, at the day of treatment, the therapist will correct the target position by taking
a CBCT of the patient in treatment position directly prior to treatment. This procedure
aims to identify geometrical changes of the patient anatomy which may have occurred
since the pCT images were acquired. A crucial spatial parameter is the location of the
tumor target. Its position correction will be automatically done by matching the recent
CBCT with the pCT. After this step, the plan is uploaded to the treatment machine and
the treatment can be delivered [39], [4]. This approach is called IGRT which enables
the possibility for adaptive radiotherapy.

2.3
IGRT and Adaptive radiotherapy
IGRT is a method using any imaging technologies such as CT, CBCT and MRI to detect
the patient anatomy directly prior to or even during the treatment sessions. Modern
medical linear accelerators for IGRT are equipped with on-board imaging devices with
these either 2D or 3D imaging capabilities. During a treatment session, patient images
are used to derive and adjust the radiation beam to the actual location of the radiation
target and the OARs at time of therapy [29], [8].
Morphological changes in patient, and tumor anatomy during a typical several weeks
treatment course of radiotherapy does not always make pCT based plans reliable for the
treatment day. Using IGRT, it is possible to re-plan according to the anatomy of the
patient at the day of treatment. This process is called adaptive radiotherapy (ART),
which can be applied in an offline setting in between treatment fractions or online to
adjust the treatment plan within each single fraction.
IGRT can be applied for all modes of advanced high precision RT like IMRT, proton
beam therapy, stereotactic radiosurgery, stereotactic body radiotherapy (SBRT), and
VMAT. Typically, offline ART is applied to the majority of these modalities, while rou-
tine use of online ART has been limited due to the cumbersome workflow for adaptation
(including re-delineation and re-planning on the anatomy of the day) usually requiring
one to two days of work in the offline setting
2.3.1
Online Adaptive Radiotherapy
As defined in the previous section, in ART the treatment plan is updated based on the
anatomy of the patient at any stage during the course of treatment. This is enabled by
in-room imaging (CBCT, MRI or CT on-rails) which images are co-registered to the pCT
for re-delineation of targets and OARs, and subsequent re-calculation or re-optimization
of the treatment plan taking detected anatomical changes into account.
ART can be performed at three timescales: offline between treatment fractions, on-
line immediately prior to beam delivery, and in real time during a treatment session
[16]. Online ART (oART) is defined as re-optimization of the treatment plan immedi-
ately prior to beam delivery with the patient on the couch, assessing the need for ART,
re-planning and performing QA within minutes. The short timescale of these steps for
oART are made possible by semi or full automated integrated tools such as automated
re-planning algorithms for re-contouring, plan optimization and quality assurance. The
advantage of online ART is that geometrical changes of the patient anatomy can be
accounted for directly before the treatment is delivered. Consequently uncertainties of
the tumour location and shape are reduced which can be potentially exploited by the

2.3.2
Ethos Al-driven Online Adaptive Radiotherapy
A novel solution for CBCT-based oART was introduced with the launch of the AI-driven
Ethos TM therapy solution in 2019 which delivers an entire adaptive treatment in a typ-
ical 15-minute timeslot [35].
Ethos is a CBCT-integrated LINAC with a closed bore, equipped with double stacked
MLC, with no secondary jaws and a field size of 28 cm x 28 cm. The machine has a 6
MV flattening filter free beam including pre-configured beam data and a dose rate of up
to 800 MU/min [35].
Ethos system uses an AI based algorithm to automatically contour pre-defined struc-
tures in the closest proximity to the target which influence the shape of the target.
These structures are called influencers and drive the propagation of the target and the
OARs from the pCT to the CBCT of the day. A synthetic CT adapted to the anatomy
of the day is thereby generated and used for dose calculation with full re-optimization
on the anatomy of the day [35].

2.3.3
Ethos Workflow
The Al-driven online adaptive treatment workflow is broken down into three main steps:
1. Initial offline planning - where the clinicians can choose between some templates to
create clinical goals (target dose and OAR constraints) and select the diagnostic imaging
needed for contouring. At Herlev Hospital, the physician delineates the targets and the
OARs in Eclipse TPS which are then imported into Ethos. The system converts the
clinical goals into optimization objectives to drive the automated so-called intelligent
optimization engine which generates a set of IMRT and VMAT plans with fixed system-
defined beam configurations. These plans will be compared against the defined clinical
goals and the primary reference plan is selected [35].
2.
The on-couch adaptation - where clinicians have the choice of adapting a pa-
tient´s plan in order to more accurately deliver the physician’s intent. It starts with
CBCT acquisition with a length up to 38.5 cm and as fast as 17 seconds. This CBCT
will be the basis for on-couch adaption. Once CBCT image quality and patient geometry
has been approved, the AI segmentation technology automatically contours influencers
in 15-30 seconds. Then a structure guided or elastic deformation algorithm begins prop-
agating the target structures from the pCT. Once the target contour is accepted, the
system automatically generates two plans for review; scheduled plan (scheduled plan is

2.4
Imaging modalities
Diagnostic kVCT- and therapy integrated CBCT images provide the key patient infor-
mation to facilitate the oART approach of ETHOS therapy. CBCT IGRT is used for
monitoring anatomical deformations and organ/tumor localization to improve the clini-
cal outcome of RT [33]. This section provides a short review on CT and CBCT imaging
modalities.
2.4.1
Computed Tomography
CT is an image modality that generates images of slices of the body. It consists of
fan-shaped x-ray source and detectors (one or more multiple ring detector arrays) which
are mounted opposite each other on a gantry. The multiple ring detector arrays is used
to obtain full body scan and a high image resolution. [5], [10].
CT scanner measures the x-ray attenuation (µ) along a line between an x-ray source
and detectors. Therefore, the detectors register the exit beam intensity along that line.
Image reconstruction of a 2D cross section requires to collect the measurements along
all lines within the cross section [22]. Along the lines, there are pixels representing the µ
related to the voxels in the anatomic slices (figure 2.7) [10]. Different algorithms can be
used to reconstruct the data to 3D image. Filter back projection and iterative algorithms
are the most common ones.
Figure 2.7: Illustration of CT [25].
2.4.2
Cone Beam CT
CBCT has a cone-shaped beam produced by kV X-ray source and a detector which are
rotating around the region of interest of the patient. CBCT covers the entire field of
view (FOV) with only one rotation of the gantry unlike CT incorporating the FOV by
several slices with helical movement [31].
The different beam shape in CBCT results in a larger FOV compared to CT. This causes
large amounts of scattering yielding lower image quality and CT number accuracy [33].
Another difference between CT and CBCT is that CBCT uses high spatial resolution
flat panel detector which is more sensitive to small motions and causes artefacts [10].

2.4.3
Dose calculation and Calibration
Dose calculation in treatment planning is based on converting HUs into RED at a certain
point in patient’s body [37], [18]. The HUs are not unique for a given material and are
dependent on the scanner and on the scanning protocols [10]. The relationship between
RED and HU must therefore be measured for each scanner used for treatment planning
[37].
Precise treatment planning and dose calculation depends on the accuracy of Hounsfield
Units (HU) which is computed by µ at each pixel relative to µwater [22]:

2.5
Evaluation
Evaluation of the treatment plan is a mandatory step for QA of RT process in aspects
of verification of dose constrains (within PTV and OARs) and comparison between the
measured and delivered dose before any treatment delivery. DVH and gamma evaluation

2.5.1
Dose volume histogram
A 3D treatment plan has a vast amount of dose information which makes dose evaluation
and comparison a complicated task. As a solution, Dose Volume Histograms are intro-
duced to summarize this vast information into a 2D graph that are easier to interpret.
There are two types of DVHs, differential and cumulative. The differential DVH is a
graph that represents the relative or absolute volume receiving a given dose, whereas
the cumulative DVH (cDVH) represents the relative or absolute volume receiving greater
than or equal to that given dose. The cDVH is more common in treatment planning
systems and is broadly used as a dose evaluation tool [7], [32], [23].
DVHs are a central quantity for the optimization of treatments and for comparing their
quality. For the PTV, the dose prescription is often performed as the medium dose.
Furthermore, quantities like VD defined as the volume V of an organ which receives at
least a dose D, are used to score the quality of a treatment plan. For instance V95 of
the PTV characterizes the dose coverage of the target. A set of similar parameters are
defined for OARS. The clinically relevant values of VD are organ specific and will be
used for the comparison of clinical dose distributions in chapter 6 of this thesis.
Treatment plan verification using dose volume histograms (DVHs) enables straight com-
parison of the measured data from the verification process with the calculated ones from
the TPS. However, DVHs do not provide spatial information and therefore the location
of over or under dose regions in DVHs can not be determined. This is due to the fact
that integral histograms mask errors in small volumes [15], [10].
A precise comparison including both spacial information and dose difference between
the reference dose distribution and the evaluated dose distribution can be done with
gamma evaluation which is explained in the following section.
2.5.2
Gamma Evaluation
Information concerning the space domain is given by distance-to-agreement (DTA) which
is defined as the distance between a dose point in the reference distribution and the most
adjacent point in the evaluated dose distribution that appears to have the same dose
value or at least a dose value within a tolerance limit [12].
The gamma evaluation is a method combining dose difference (∆D2(rR, rE) and distance-
to-agreement (DTA) generating a dimensionless metric γ to compare the reference and
evaluated dose distribution providing both spatial and dose difference information.

2.6 CBCT image quality

2.6
CBCT image quality
Image quality represents how accurate the image is compared to its original object. It
is important to have a high quality image in order to calculate the dose distribution di-
rectly on CBCT. Some factors such as x-ray source, the geometry of the imaging system,
the image formation at the detector and the object being scanned can cause artifacts.
As a consequence, noise and artifacts reduce the image quality. Some of the common
artifacts in kV CBCT are metal artifacts, motion, ring artifacts and beam hardening [38].
Metal artifact - is due to metal implants (e.g. prostheses) in patients’ body. The
mean beam energy increases as passing through the metal. This is due to high photo-
electric cross section which is the result of high Z material. Therefore, a dark area at
the center of metal can be seen. for patients with double prostheses, the metal artifacts
appear between the metals as dark streaks [38].
Motion artifact - happens by the movement of the scanned object e.g. lung (respira-
tion) and rectum (gas). This artifact appears as blurring or dark and bright streaks [38].
Ring artifact - is due to flat panel detector e.g.
miscalibrated detector or manu-
facturing defects. The artifact appears as a bright ring which is due to increase in pixel
value or dark ring which is the result of pixel value reduction[38].
Beam hardening - is the result of increasing the mean energy of the beam which
is attenuated less by penetrating the object. The attenuation of the lower energy x-rays
are stronger than the higher energy x-rays due to photoelectric effect. Therefore, the
lower energies are absorbed as the beam penetrates the object [38].

There are different articles about CBCT-based dose calculation in adaptive radiotherapy
and its accuracy evaluation. The four most relevant of these articles are described in
this chapter. None of these studies include AI-driven online adaptive radiotherapy but
have been useful for this research in particular with respect to establishing calibration
curves and dose calculations.
The first study by Y. RONG et al, investigates the feasibility of CBCT based dose
calculation in adaptive radiotherapy using site-specific HU-RED calibration curves. Fo-
cus of the study is on sites including head, lungs, and lower body (abdomen/pelvis).
The CBCT scans were performed using Varian Trilogy™system and dose compari-
son between CT and CBCT image-based plans in phantom studies, showed that the
site-specific calibration curve method results in 2 percent dose agreement. This paper
concludes that CBCT imaging can be a feasible option for dose calculation in adaptive
radiotherapy if site-specific calibration is applied [30].
The second study which was done at Herlev hospital evaluates, CBCT based dose calcu-
lation in adaptive radiotherapy with focus on head and neck (HN) and cervix patients
using site-specific calibration curve method for CBCT dose calculation as in the first
study by Y. RONG et al. The CBCT scans were performed using Varian Clinac iX sys-
tem. This study shows that CBCT can be used for dose calculation on HN patients with
a 0.3 percent maximum dose difference (between CT and CBCT), Further investigation
was, however required for cervix patients [10].
The third study focuses on CBCT based dose calculation in ART for prostate patients
and aims at a solution to correct HU data in the CBCT images with a segmentation
method. This segmentation method relies upon the operator dividing the CBCT data
into a set of volumes where the variation in the relationship between pixel values and
HUs is small. For this purpose a Multilevel threshold algorithm (MLT) is used to cat-
egorise pixel values in the CBCT images into segments of homogeneous HU and the
CBCT numbers are converted to CT numbers. The CBCT scans were performed using
Elekta Synergy X-ray volumetric imaging system. The patient study showed that using
three material segments, air, water and bone will result in a reliable dose difference less

A recent study by Thing et al, investigates the accuracy of dose calculations performed

4.4
Discussion
The CT, clinical CBCT and improved CBCT calibration curves are well matched in soft
tissue area but are further apart in the bone area where photoelectric effect dominates
and due to less scatter correction of CBCT compared to the fan beam imaging. In
pelvis large mode, with greater photon energies, this uncertainty in high density area is
reduced as attenuation decreases.
Figure 4.4 shows the CT and CBCT based calibration curves derived from a Varian

and CBCT calibration curves in both studies, it is observed that current derived calibra-
tion curves were significantly in better agreement. This confirms a better CBCT image
quality in Ethos.
From the calibration curves, it is expected that dose calculation based on CT, clini-
cal CBCT and improved CBCTs will be in good agreement with inaccuracies mostly in
high density tissue areas. This will be studied in the following chapters where phantom
and patient based dose calculations with the derived calibration curves are investigated.

6.3
Discussion
Recalculated doses on pCT, clinical CBCT and improved CBCT of all patients were
evaluated by DVH parameters and gamma analysis. The clinical study showed a sta-
tistically close PTV coverage for pCT, clinical CBCT and the improved CBCTs of all
patients. Furthermore, mean doses to PTV were compared between the four studied
images. Results demonstrated an acceptable agreement between pCT, clinical CBCT
and improved CBCT for all patients, however mean dose to PTV based on improved
CBCTs were closer to pCT.
Gamma evaluation was carried out on the dose calculated on the CBCT images from
the first fraction for all patients, using the CT based dose calculation as reference. With
one exception (patient 6), the global gamma (normalized to DMAX) with 3% /3 mm
criteria and 20% dose threshold yielded passing rates > 96.3 which is higher than the
clinically applied 95% passing rate tolerence.
Median gamma passing rates, confirmed that both clinical and improved CBCTs were re-
liable for dose calculation, however dose distributions calculated on the improved CBCTs
were closer to the pCT. This was also expected from the well matched calibration curves.
Paired Wilcoxon ranksum test illustrated that there was no significant difference between
the two median gamma passing rate.
The superior results for the improved CBCTs, both by DVH and gamma evaluation,
indicate that motion artifact reduction in the improved CBCTs seem to result in a more
reliable dose calculation. However, this has to be investigated for more patients.

CHAPTER 7
Image quality
Accuracy of CBCT based dose calculation is in accordance with the image quality. Any
artifact present in the CBCT images deteroiate the HU definition and thus reduces
the dose accuracy compared to values obtained from the CT images. One of the most
common artifacts that hinder direct dose planning on iteratively reconstructed CBCT
images are the motion artifacts caused by breathing of the patient. Varian has therefore
developed a new reconstruction algorithm (currently for research purposes only) that
aims at reducing the motion artifact.
7.1
Materials and Methods
The CBCT images of 14 patients were investigated to assess the amount of image quality
improvement achieved with the new reconstruction algorithm.
The data was imported to Matlab and assessed by a medical physicist at Herlev hospital
by presenting clinical and improved images side-by-side in an in-house developed GUI .
Both clinical and improved CBCT images were in random orders and the observer could
score the potential image quality superiority from 1 (very little) to 5 (a lot).
7.2
Results and discussion
The visual image quality comparison between clinical CBCT and improved CBCT of 4
patients is discussed in this section. The other 10 patients are in appendix due to very
little quality improvement. In all cases, the left images are clinical CBCTs and the right
images are improved CBCTs.
Patient 2
Patient 2 is a palliative patient with double hip prosthesis. Figure 7.1 shows improved
CBCT has a higher HU in prosthesis due to more trust to scatter profile, and it does
not limit the amount of scatter signal to subtract for certain structures. Therefore, in
general the bone and metal values have higher HU values in improved CBCT.

Patient 11, 12 and 14
Patients 11, 12 and 14 have motion artifact in clinical CBCT images (visible as bright
high density streaks close to the body outline) whereas this artifact is reduced in im-
proved CBCTs. Removing these motion artifacts helps to visualize more details in the
internal anatomy of the patients. However, it could be observed that additional mass is
instead present outside of the body.
The problem occurs due to the nature of producing a 3D image out of a 4D object
as the object is moving during image acquisition. Therefore, it is a trade off between
preferring one type of artifact versus other types. There is not a single snapshot of
patient at a certain time and it is several projections made of patient at different states.
Therefore, the image reconstruction algorithms try to explain all visible mass in the pro-
jections with something. In other word, the smear outside of body is the mass observed
in projections but it does not fit inside of the patient due to patient movement.
This type of artifact did not affect the dose calculation by using the same plan and
the same structure set, as the current TPS only takes mass included in the defined body
outline into account during dose calculation. Further investigation should be done in
the future to find out whether this artifact is detected as part of the body and include
it to dose calculation or disregard this high density outside body.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
The results of the study showed that direct dose calculation on CBCT is possible in
Al-driven oART when using site specific HU-RED calibration curve.
However some
restrictions such as limited field of view and image quality in CBCT images should be
further investigated for larger groups of patients. The dosimetric volume comparisons
between CT and clinical CBCT and artifact improved CBCT were in good agreement.
No significant differences were observed when comparing dose distributions on pCT and
both CBCTs but artifact improved CBCTs yielded a more accurate dose calculation.
