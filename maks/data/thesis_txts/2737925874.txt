Abstract
The study aims to determine whether there is a di↵erence in perception of the indoor environment between
neurotypical and those with Autism Spectrum Condition (ASC), primarily in relation to the visual and
thermal comfort domains. Although, people with ASC can be hypo- or hypersensitive to all sensory modalities,
few have investigated this altered perception in relation to the indoor environment. For this reason, current
indoor environmental quality standards may not account for autistic individuals’ unique perceptions since
they are derived from studies using the average neurotypical population.
This study utilized two experimental approaches involving autonomous adult participants: (1) a controlled
oﬃce-like environment at DTU and (2) a ﬁeld study in private homes. Both studies used comfort questionnaires
and measurements of the indoor climatic conditions to monitor the perception of the indoor climate.
In the controlled environment, an autistic group (n = 17) and a neurotypical control group (n = 20)
were exposed for 2 hours to three distinct thermal conditions (Predicted Mean Votes - PMV of -1, 0, and
+1, respectively) as well as two variations of visual environments (with and without view-out), totaling 6
experimental combinations. The color temperature of a personal light was altered between a white, blue, and
yellow color, and participants could request changes to the illuminance, during all experiments. Furthermore,
participants had the opportunity to alter their clothing after 30 minutes. Answers to comfort questionnaires
(given 7 times) and performance tests (given 3 times) were compared between the two groups, and results
were cross-checked with ﬁndings from the ﬁeld study (n = 13) in order to investigate if they were conﬁrmed
in everyday life.
The ﬁeld studies lasted one year, with participation ranging from 1 to 12 months, based on individual choice.
Participants answered comfort questionnaires once a week on average during the experiment.
In the controlled environment experiment, autistic participants perceived warm and cold (less evident)
thermal conditions di↵erently, with signiﬁcant di↵erences in thermal sensation, satisfaction, and acceptability.
As a result, they felt warmer/cooler and more dissatisﬁed than the controls. The ﬁeld study supported the
tendency to feel warmer than predicted by PMV. Results from the controlled environment experiment found
that thermal satisfaction and sensation were positively inﬂuenced by access to a view, and the same was
found for visual satisfaction, though the latter was not conﬁrmed via the ﬁeld studies. ASC participants were
signiﬁcantly more dissatisﬁed with the visual environment under blue light. Multidomain analysis showed
that the ASC group had stronger and more frequent correlations in inter-domain satisfaction as well as a
correlation between visual and thermal satisfaction when having a view-out.
In the controlled environments adaptive opportunities were utilized more by the ASC group both in regards
to lighting and clothing, and the ﬁeld study also reﬂected the use of adaptable solutions in the visual
environment. This conﬁrmed the need for ﬂexible design in the indoor environment when used by autistic
people.
The performance improvement of ASC participants stagnated for the last test, contrary to the
controls, and performance was not signiﬁcantly inﬂuenced by the visual and thermal exposures.
The ﬁndings indicate that PMV inadequately predicts the thermal sensation of autistic individuals, calling
for further exploration of the thermal sensation of people on the autism spectrum and the reasons behind.
Additionally, key aspects in relation to visual satisfaction and view-out should also be precisely identiﬁed in
order to correctly feed into building design guidelines.

1
Introduction
Individuals perceive the indoor climate di↵erently; what constitutes a perfect environment for one person
might not be ideal for another [Wang et al., 2018]. It is widely recognized that individuals with autism are
hyper- or hyposensitive to their surroundings [APA, 2022], leading to the speculation that they are likely
more sensitive to the indoor climate than neurotypical individuals (people without a neurological diagnosis).
Given people spend more than 90% of their time indoors, it is crucial to maintain a healthy and comfortable
indoor environment [Katsoyiannis and Cincinelli, 2019].
1.1
What is autism?
Autism spectrum condition (referred to as ASC)1 is a lifelong, pervasive, neurodevelopmental condition
that typically begins presenting in early childhood, from age 2 and up. Diagnosis can, however, happen
at any stage in life.
It a↵ects communication skills and behavior, leading to social communication and
interaction challenges, repetitive behaviors or interests, and hyper- or hyposensitivity to sensory stimuli
[NIMH, 2023, APA, 2022, Autism Speaks (A), nd].
Autism being a spectrum means that there is not one singular way that ASC presents itself; it varies in
severity, encompasses a diverse range of symptoms, and has a unique presentation in each individual, further
inﬂuenced by comorbidities [Masi et al., 2017].
Along with ASC oftentimes comes other comorbidities, with 70% of autistic children found to have at least
one comorbid condition, and 41% having two or more [Simono↵et al., 2008]. The comorbidities can be both
medical and psychiatric, and some of the typical comorbidities are sleep disturbance, depression, ADHD,
anxiety, and intellectual disability [Masi et al., 2017].
The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM-5), which is written by the American
Psychiatric Association (APA), is a widely used psychiatric manual that deﬁnes the diagnostic criteria of
ASC. DSM-5 handles the diversity in ASC by deﬁning it as a singular diagnosis based on multiple dimensions
and further categorizes the severity of autism into three groups [APA, 2022, Rosen et al., 2021]:
• Level 1: Requiring support
• Level 2: Requiring substantial support
• Level 3: Requiring very substantial support
A new criterion was introduced with the updated version of DSM [APA, 2022], so the guideline now includes
sensory sensitivity as a core criterion. Therefore, this aspect is no longer viewed as a secondary characteristic:
with 90% of autistic estimated to have an atypical sensory experience, it is crucial to understand the
implications of this processing di↵erence properly [Robertson and Baron-Cohen, 2017].

1.2.1
Gender di↵erences
More men are diagnosed with autism compared to women. It is speculated that the actual gender distribution
of autism may not match statistics, especially for the adult age group of 20 years and older. Meanwhile,
more females are getting diagnosed in the 15-20 age group. Women with an average or above-average IQ
appear to be better at adapting socially and may not exhibit the typical autistic symptoms that would
prompt a diagnosis. Women also have di↵erent coping strategies than men, allowing them to camouﬂage
their symptoms better, which could contribute to the lower diagnosis rates among women [HBS Economics,
2023]. Additionally, studies have shown that the symptoms of autism in adults may di↵er between men
and women. Currently, diagnostic criteria are based primarily on the standard symptoms observed in men
[Halladay et al., 2015, Lai et al., 2011].
It is suspected that camouﬂaging autism symptoms may lead to other mental health challenges as individuals
use considerable energy trying to ﬁt in. These challenges can include stress, anxiety, and depression. Studies
have indicated that men are more prone to depression associated with camouﬂaging than women.
This
di↵erence in vulnerability may stem from women’s greater adaptability to gender-related social experiences
[Lai et al., 2017].
Furthermore, a review revealed a gender imbalance in studies regarding sensory perception of individuals with
ASC. The review found that only 31% of the examined studies, solely involving ASC participants, included
more than 33% females. In studies involving control groups, 49% of studies had more than 33% female
participants [Zaniboni and Toftum, 2023]. This gender imbalance in research is signiﬁcant to acknowledge,
as gender, age, and other factors have been shown to inﬂuence thermal comfort [Wang et al., 2018].
1.3
Research on autism concerning indoor comfort
Research concerning the indoor environment in relation to ASC has increased in recent years. In a scoping
review, 57 out of 74 studies were found to be from 2012 or later [Zaniboni and Toftum, 2023], showcasing
that this ﬁeld of study still could be in its infancy. Additionally, despite this inﬂux in research, signiﬁcant
gaps and shortages of knowledge remain within the ﬁeld.
The review investigated the extent of current knowledge regarding the indoor environment and sensory stimuli
in relation to ASC. The goal was to determine how many existing studies were based on a comfort and well-
being framework and to identify areas for future research to address existing knowledge gaps. The review
revealed that most studies focused on children, with a smaller portion addressing sensory stimuli in adults
(⇠33% of the studies). This is problematic since ﬁndings regarding children might not be directly applicable
page 2 of 148
==PAGE:15==
to adults due to di↵erences in physiology, behavior, and mental processes [Larcher, 2017]. Therefore, studies
must also be conducted on an adult age group to avoid generalization.
Furthermore, the most represented indoor environment was educational institutions (n = 34), followed by
studies describing general conditions (n = 21), and living spaces/houses (n = 17). Only 6 studies focused
on workspaces [Zaniboni and Toftum, 2023]. This indicates a lack of understanding of the environmental
inﬂuence on autistic individuals in di↵erent settings outside of schools, which again mostly pertains to
children.
Due to the nature of ASC, perception and well-being cannot be assumed to be similar across
di↵erent environments. Furthermore, di↵erences in tasks, sensorial stimuli, as well as social context possibly
create distinctly di↵erent environments, which all need to be understood. Autistic people have been found
to have a high unemployment rate, with statistics for the UK ﬁnding autistic individuals to have the lowest
employment rate among disabled [Oﬃce for National Statistics, 2021]. This adds to the need to understand
work environments better, speciﬁcally to ensure that the oﬃce environment is not what is keeping autistic
individuals o↵the job market.

The distribution of study types found in [Zaniboni and Toftum, 2023] is illustrated in Figure 1. Most studies
involved questionnaires or interviews, with 20 out of 32 of these studies utilizing proxy respondents. Proxy
respondents in ASC studies are people other than the autistic individual, such as family members, professional
caregivers, teachers, or psychologists, who answer on behalf of the autistic individual. A potential issue with
proxy respondents is that they may not accurately interpret the signals and expressions of autistic individuals,
leading to potentially inaccurate responses. Additionally, the quality of responses may vary depending on
the indoor comfort category; for example, discomfort due to rapid changes in sound may be more noticeable
than discomfort from the thermal environment.
Although the majority of the studies involved questionnaires and interviews, few studies pertained to indoor
environment engineering, and most lacked the use of comfort questionnaires and simultaneous environmental
measurements. This area, therefore, needs further exploration, particularly given the low number of studies
deﬁning thresholds within the indoor comfort domains.
Additionally, out of the four studies deﬁning
thresholds, only one was based on an experimental setup, while the others relied on literature from outside
the indoor environment ﬁeld or on assumptions [Zaniboni and Toftum, 2023].
Within the four comfort domains (indoor air quality - IAQ, visual, acoustics, and thermal), the acoustic
domain was found to be the one with the most research (n = 58), with visual coming in second (n = 49), and

1.4
Standards
The widely used standard, ISO 7730, as well as other standards, are primarily based upon Fanger’s PMV
model to determine thermal sensation [DS/EN/ISO 7730, 2005]. This model is based upon studies including
approximately 1300 mainly college-aged people, which inﬂuence the kind of people the model ﬁts. The model
only applies to wider age groups with corrections, and studies of neurodivergent people are lacking [Van Hoof,
2008]. In general, most research that standards are based on uses students as test people since these studies
usually occur in universities across the world. Buildings are, therefore, primarily built to ﬁt neurotypicals.
For people with autism, living in neurotypically-design conditions could intensify and add extra to their daily
life struggles [Nagib and Williams, 2017].
A UN Sustainable Development Goal is to reduce social and economic inequality by improving inclusion for,
e.g., people with disabilities [UN, nd], and this could be implemented through proper design of the built
environment.
When adding up the fact that autism is a lifelong, pervasive condition inﬂuencing an increasing part of the
population, it is evident that these individuals should be taken into account in the building design. Therefore,
new studies in controlled environments need to be conducted in order to adequately understand the autistic
experience so as to suit standards for autistic individuals better. The adaptation of the built environment to
be more autism-friendly not only enhances the spaces for autistic individuals but can also potentially improve
the experience for everyone [Shell, 2016]. This is the case since environmental conditions, based on standards,
are also uncomfortable for certain neurotypical people. By modifying the criteria to be more inclusive to the
ASC population, these criteria could beneﬁt neurotypicals bothered by the indoor environment. This could
be the case if the design conditions found to suit the ASC population lie within those of the neurotypical
population, resulting in narrower design criteria. Therefore, by beneﬁting a minority, it is possible to create
inclusive spaces that serve the majority.
To evaluate a building’s performance in terms of the four indoor comfort categories, DS/CEN/TR 16798
(2019) is generally used. The standard contains threshold values for various parameters depending on the
type of building and the environmental indoor quality category for design (IEQcat) [DS/CEN/TR 16798-2,
2019]. The IEQ categories consist of four tiers depending on the level of expectation of the building, with IEQI
being the highest and IEQIV being the lowest. Category IV should only be used for short-term occupancy,
while the three others can be used for normal occupancy and provide an acceptable indoor environment.
Category I is stated to be for occupants with special needs and could, therefore, imply that it could be suitable
for autistic people, but since these values, as stated previously, are not based on tests on a neurodivergent
group, their feasibility for this group is not known.

Research questions
Based on the shortcomings in current research regarding ASC and the indoor climate, this study aims to
provide new knowledge on how people with autism perceive indoor environment exposures. The study was
based on two established experimental setups, consisting of a ﬁeld study and a controlled experimental setup.
• How do individuals with ASC perceive the exposures from the thermal and visual indoor environment
domains?
• What actions do ASC individuals take to enhance their well-being?
– For a controlled environment study, how do the actions di↵er from those of a control group?
– What actions do individuals do in their homes?
• What are the multi-domain e↵ects, if any, between the indoor environment domains?
• How do prolonged conditions inﬂuence individuals with ASC regarding performance as well as symptom
intensity?

2
Methodology
This project has been based on quantitative data from two studies: one referred to as Controlled Environments
(from here on called CE) and the other is referred to as Field Studies (from here on called FS), which
only involved autistic people. CE took place at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) in a setup
consisting of oﬃce-like rooms, where participants with ASC and a control group were exposed to di↵erent
environmental conditions while answering questionnaires and completing performance tests. Environmental
and physiological measurements were additionally carried out to monitor room conditions and participants’
physiological responses. In between these activities, participants could do sedentary work of their choosing.
The ﬁeld studies took place in individuals with ASC’s own homes, where measuring equipment was installed,
to track their indoor environment, while they occasionally answered comfort questionnaires.
2.1
Controlled Environments
The CE study was set up to compare the comfort of individuals with ASC against a control group in a
controlled setting to determine possible di↵erences in environment perception between the two groups. The
experiment was designed so that the visual and thermal conditions were controlled variables.
IAQ and
acoustics were measured but not parameters included in the experimental design. The experiments were
conducted from October to December 2023 and again from January to February 2024.
2.1.1
Recruitment
Two groups were recruited: A group of autonomous, adult, autistic people and a control group of adult,
non-autistic people.
Recruitment took place during the autumn of 2023 and was conducted as part of
another master’s thesis project. The recruitment consisted of posters that were set up at DTU as well as the
surrounding dormitories. Furthermore, there were made posts to Facebook groups for autistic people, and
the SPS network (Specialpædagogisk støtte/Special Educational Support) at DTU was used to promote the
project to their contact pool. The recruited controls did not know what the purpose of the study was, only
that it was concerning indoor climate and holistic well-being. They did not know about the autistic case
group in order for the knowledge not to inﬂuence their answers and actions in the study. All participants had
to ﬁll out the Autism-Spectrum Quotient (AQ) test. It is a self-administered test consisting of 50 questions,
and the test score evaluates the number of autistic traits in adults with average intelligence [Baron-Cohen
et al., 2001]. An AQ cut-o↵score of 26 points has been found to correctly classify the greatest number of
autistic individuals in a clinical trial [Woodbury-Smith et al., 2005]. A cut-o↵value of 26 traits or more was
therefore used to ﬁlter out potential autistic controls, as not to skew the results of the controls. Participants
in the CE study were compensated equally for their participation at an hourly rate, with payments made
after the ﬁnalization of all sessions. The payment did not rely on how well the participants did in the studies
so as not to inﬂuence the study. It was set to suﬃciently acknowledge participants’ time and e↵ort without
overly encouraging participation solely due to a monetary gain.
2.1.2
The setup
The CE study used three identical rooms, simulating an oﬃce setup, each with a unique air temperature
setting, ranging over a warm, neutral, and cold environment. The Warm Room was designed to have a
temperature of 26.5◦C, the Neutral Room 22.5◦C, and the Cold Room 18.5◦C. The values were selected to
achieve PMV = +1, PMV = 0, and PMV = −1, respectively. The temperature was kept constant by
page 6 of 148
==PAGE:19==
the use of a local heat pump in each room. The rooms had windows so that sessions could be run with
and without daylight and view-out. During sessions with daylight, the ceiling light was switched o↵; these
were referred to as natural light (NL) sessions. For sessions without daylight and view-out, the ceiling light
was turned on, and these were referred to as artiﬁcial light (AL) sessions. This made for six experimental
combinations, which are illustrated in Figure 2.
Figure 2: Overview of the variations in room temperature and lighting across the sessions.
The experimental setup for each room is sketched in Figure 3. Each room had space for three participants
and one researcher during a session, which lasted ⇠2 hours. Each participant’s desk was equipped with a
personal desk lamp, which was positioned 0.5 m above the table and pointing directly down at the start of
the session. The personal light was equipped with a smart bulb, which allowed the light to be turned up or
down in intensity and gave color-temperature control, all via an app. The luminous ﬂux of each bulb was
adjusted right before a session to attain an illuminance of 550lx on each desk. 550lx was used, based on 500lx
being the design value, deﬁned in [DS/EN 12464-1, 2021]. An additional 50lx was added precautionarily
for light adjustments and variations. Throughout the session, the color of the light was changed, each time
ensuring that the illuminance was set back to 550lx. During the session, the participants could perform
actions to achieve more comfortable conditions by adjusting their clothing level, as well as the positioning
and/or the intensity of the personal light. The changes to the lighting could be done at any given time, but
the adjustment of the clothing level was not permitted during the ﬁrst half hour of the experiment to allow
for adaptation to the thermal conditions. Afterward, participants could regulate their clothing to feel more
comfortable.
Several measuring instruments were used during the sessions in order to monitor the conditions.
The
placement of long-term measuring devices is noted in Figure 3. A more in-depth description of the measurement
equipment is provided in section 2.4 Equipment. Photos from the rooms can be seen in Figure 4 and Figure 5.

according to DS 17037:2018, see Table 2 [DS/EN 17037, 2021]. All rooms had a window-to-ﬂoor ratio of

The sessions followed a ﬁxed structure, which was managed by a researcher who was present in the room
during the entire session. To minimize deviance from the structure, a checklist was constructed with prompts

be things such as noises, weather changes, activities of the participants, the participants’ emotional state,

The log of the participants’ actions
regarding lighting and clothing was then summarized to determine any di↵erences in actions between the two
groups.

2.1.4
Performance test
A performance test was administered three times during each session, see Figure 6, to be able to assess the
participants’ performance under prolonged exposures. The test used was a so-called D2 test, which is a
self-paced pen-and-paper test that quantiﬁes the attention and concentration of the participant. The test
consisted of rows of d”s and p”s along with marks at each letter placed either above, below, or both above
and below the letter, with 0-2 marks in either position. The targets that should be crossed out consisted
of d”s with two total markings, giving three target variations. The targets were placed among distractors,
which consisted of d”s with 1, 3, or 4 markings and p”s with 1 or 2 markings.
A test example piece can be seen in Figure 8. There were 14 rows in total with 47 characters in each row, and
the participant worked from left to right over each line, crossing out the targets [Brickenkamp, 2002, Steinborn
et al., 2018]. 15 seconds were given for completing each line, totaling a test time of 3.5 minutes [W¨uhr and
W¨uhr, 2023]. The researcher managed time using a stopwatch, who instructed the participants when to go
to the next line. The test sheet had two sides, consisting of a front with instructions, which initially faced
the participants. When instructed by the researcher, they ﬂipped the page and then ﬁlled in the ﬁrst row.
The whole test can be seen in Appendix D.
The test scores were assessed based on concentration performance (KY), which is a test metric quantifying the
performance speed and accuracy [Niu et al., 2013], with high reliability and the oﬃcial measure of performance
[Brickenkamp, 2002].KY was calculated for each row as the total processed characters subtracted the errors
[Brickenkamp, 2002]. The sum of the row scores gave an overall test score. If the researcher mistakenly
gave more than 2 extra seconds, the test was discarded. Tests that had been wrongly ﬂipped and, therefore,
performed upside down were also discarded. Participants went through a trial row before their ﬁrst session.
The test was kept the same for all the tests in each session (see Figure 6) and across sessions. Consequently,
practice beneﬁts were inevitable, but this was not considered in the analysis.
Figure 8: Example piece from the D2 test that the participants were given on the front of the test paper.

ASC. During the measurement period, the participants regularly ﬁlled in comfort questionnaires. The scope
was to measure the IEQ parameters in a room that the individual preferred and in another room that the

2.2.2
Weekly questionnaire
Participants were asked to complete a comfort questionnaire at least twice per week but no more than once a
day. Otherwise, the response rate was left up to them. Participants who desired it received a weekly reminder
containing a link to the online questionnaire and their personal login.
The weekly comfort questionnaire included questions regarding their current satisfaction, acceptability, and
preference in relation to each indoor environment domain, as well as thermal sensation. They were also
asked to answer reasons for dissatisfaction, if not satisﬁed, for each comfort domain as well as report their
sleep quality. All of these questions were identical to the ones used in CE; however, in the FS, the questions
regarding reasons for dissatisfaction only appeared if they had answered Dissatisﬁed or Just dissatisﬁed within
the speciﬁc domain. Furthermore, participants were asked to indicate in which room the questionnaire was
answered in and if they had had to do any actions to improve their well-being. Possible actions were given
as a multiple-choice list, with the option ”Other” for additional answers. The weekly FS questionnaire can
be found in Appendix G.
2.2.3
Feedback report
Upon completing the measuring period, each participant could request to get the results of the measurements
that had taken place in their home. All participants were interested in this. Individual feedback reports were
written, summing up the measured data as well as an overview of the questionnaire replies. All reports
were written in Danish, since all participants were Danish, and they were written in a language ﬁtting
people without an academic background.
Explanations of the terms they might not have been familiar
with (illuminance and CO2-concentration) were added, as well as an introductory text of the meaning and
structure of the report. An example of one of the feedback reports can be found in Appendix H.
The report was sectioned into the IEQ categories, and duration plots were made for air temperature,
relative humidity (RH), CO2-concentration, and sound level.
For the visual domain, a summary of the
spot measurements on the ﬂoor was constructed with minimum, maximum, and mean illuminance for each
room and light setup (with and without curtains and artiﬁcial light). Data was shown for a typical week
and a critical week. This was done to give the participants an overview of their indoor climate without
overwhelming them with data. The critical week was chosen based on the data points that, when compared
to recommended limit values found in standards, exceeded these limits the most.
The threshold values
were also printed on the plots given to the participants, indicated by a horizontal, colored line. For sound
measurements, a scale with everyday noise sources was given to relate the sound strength to that of known
sources. This was done since no regulations apply to sound levels in private homes. For the participants who
were part of the experiment for multiple seasons, the measured data was further split into seasons. Their
answers to the questionnaire were divided into the heating season (from the 1st of October to the 30th of
April [Varmekonsulenterne Aps, nd]) and the period outside of this. Bar charts showed the distribution of
answers for satisfaction, preference, and thermal sensation. Comments were made both to the measured data
and the questionnaire answers.

2.3
Ethical concerns and consent
Care must be taken when working with human test participants and their data. All individuals in both
studies were given details about the experiments before consenting to participate, ﬁlled out a consent form,
and were notiﬁed that they at any given point could withdraw their consent to participate.
The ASC group in the CE study was given some leeway when conducting the experiments by including the
possibility of a break on their request. This was done to minimize the potential strain on the individuals and
avoid meltdowns.
The inﬂuence and distress caused by the experiments on the ASC population were by an ethical committee
determined to be potentially stressful to the participants, and screening of the participants was mandated,
since they were seen as vulnerable.
They were, therefore, screened through four questions concerning
thoughts, plans, or actions regarding self-harm or suicide within the last six months. If they could agree to
any of the questions they were excluded from the studies. The preliminary questionnaire that the participants
were given can be found in Appendix I.
In both experiments, to preserve their personal information, the participants were given anonymous codes
consisting of letters, characterizing the experiment, a three-digit number, and a letter signifying their gender.
An example of this is FS014M, which signiﬁed that the participant was in the ﬁeld studies, number 014,
and male. Any personal information of the participants was either physically locked up or in encrypted,
password-secured digital documents. Both studies were approved by the Scientiﬁc Ethics Committees for the
Capital Region (H-22068454).

2.6
Calculation of PMV
The TSV of the participants in FS was compared to that predicted by PMV to see how much their sensation
deviated from projected. PMV was calculated based on the measurement data for RH and air temperature,
taken in a 30-minute timespan before the questionnaire was answered, as well as clothing insulation at
the time of answering. Air temperature and operative temperature were assumed equal, and airspeed was
assumed to be 0.1 m/s. The clothing insulation was the result of the summation of the individual pieces’ clo
value. The values for the clothing types used can be found in Appendix B (Tables 24 - 25). The PMV was
calculated using the pythermalcomfort module in Python [Tartarini and Schiavon, 2020].

2.7.1
Mann-Whitney U test
The Mann-Whitney U test was used to assess the di↵erence between two samples, i.e. the ASC- and control
groups’ questionnaire answers. The test was conducted for their satisfaction, acceptability, and preference for
the thermal and visual domains, as well as TSV for the thermal domain. The null hypothesis was that there
were not any di↵erences between the two groups, meaning that the medians of the two populations were the
same. If there was a signiﬁcant di↵erence between the groups, the hypothesis could be rejected. Since the
test was performed on questionnaire data, with the same limited possibilities of input, ties did occur in the
pools. Due to these ties, the analysis was done in IBM SPSS statistics, since the program corrects for ties,
by assigning a correction to cases with ties [IBM SPSS Statistics, 2020].

Summary of results
The results showed a di↵erence in sensation between the two groups in the Cold (AL) and Warm Room, with
the ASCs feeling cooler/warmer than the controls. The same tendency was apparent for thermal satisfaction
and acceptability, with the greatest mean di↵erence in the Warm Room.
Thermal satisfaction was also
inﬂuenced by access to view-out. The ASC group was more dissatisﬁed due to feeling warm on extremities
and less dissatisﬁed due to a warm sensation on the chest than the controls.
Both groups took action
regarding clothing, however, the ASC group did it to a greater extent than the controls in the Neutral and
Warm Room.
The largest di↵erences in visual satisfaction occurred in the Cold Room, and the ASC group was also found to
be more dissatisﬁed when there was blue light than the controls for all thermal conditions. Visual satisfaction
was generally higher with a view-out, which was more apparent for the controls than the ASC. The ASC
group was less bothered by Too low daylight than controls were. The ASC also took more action to change
the lighting conditions. This was done more in the Warm and Neutral Room and more in AL conditions
compared to NL.
For the multi-domain e↵ects in regards to inter-domain satisfaction, signiﬁcant correlations were mainly
observed in the Warm and Cold Rooms for both groups, as well as in NL conditions.

There were no remarkable di↵erences in performance between rooms and settings, but there was a signiﬁcant

4
Discussion
Few studies, coupling answers from comfort questionnaires with quantitative measurements of the environmental
conditions, have been made for people on the spectrum. Since they can be hypo- or hypersensitive to sensorial
stimuli, it was investigated how this inﬂuenced the perception regarding certain thermal and visual exposures.
Di↵erences in perception may also be reﬂected in the actions that people on the spectrum do to achieve well-
being as well as symptom intensity and performance. Furthermore, this may be inﬂuenced by multi-domain
e↵ects. These aspects were therefore studied to get a better understanding of how individuals with ASC
may di↵er from the neurotypical population in regards to the experience of the built environment, ultimately
ensuring inclusivity in building design.
4.1
Perception of the indoor environment
4.1.1
Thermal environment
When studying the CE and FS results, di↵erences in the perception of the thermal environment were found
when comparing the autistic population to the neurotypical one. This was evident for thermal sensation in
CE in the warm conditions, where the ASC group had a signiﬁcantly di↵erent thermal sensation than the
control group. Their satisfaction and acceptability scores were signiﬁcantly di↵erent as well. One thing is
that they felt warmer, but their satisfaction scores also reﬂected that they were more dissatisﬁed with being
in the Warm Room than the control group. This was found even though the ASC group had a slightly lower
clothing insulation level when answering, which, in neurotypicals, would result in a cooler thermal sensation.
No signiﬁcant di↵erences were found for the cold conditions in CE when only ﬁltering at room level. However,
the ASC group was more dissatisﬁed, but not at a signiﬁcant level.
The p-values were, however, near the 5% signiﬁcance level, and results may have di↵ered with a larger sample
size, and these results should be investigated further. However, looking closer at the di↵erent conditions,
there were signiﬁcant di↵erences in thermal sensation, satisfaction, and acceptability between the ASC and
control groups for the Cold Room - AL. A similar thing was seen in the Warm Room, where thermal sensation
was signiﬁcantly di↵erent between the groups, but only for the AL scenario. Access to view-out therefore
contributed to the ASC group having similar thermal comfort as the control group for the same thermal
exposures, since this tendency was only seen for NL.
The reason for the di↵erence in signiﬁcance and thermal satisfaction, when ﬁltering at room level and when
considering AL and NL, could be related to interactions between the visual and thermal domains. Thermal
and visual satisfaction were both higher in rooms with NL for the ASC group, and the positive e↵ects
experienced in NL conditions, were hypothesized to positively a↵ect the thermal satisfaction, when thermal
conditions di↵ered from neutrality. Interaction between the exposures in the visual and thermal domains is
further discussed in subsection 4.3 Multi-domain e↵ects.
These ﬁndings overall suggest that the ASC group feels warmer than the neurotypical when exposed to warm
condtions and su↵ers more when in that state. These ﬁndings in CE were supported by those in FS, where a
comparison of PMV and TSV found that the test group felt warmer than predicted by PMV. Additionally,
the participants in FS were more satisﬁed during the heating season than the non-heating season, which
could be linked to the higher air temperatures in the non-heating period than the heating period. This adds
to the fact that the ASC group was less satisﬁed in warmer conditions. The di↵erence in thermal perception
compared to neurotypicals was therefore found not only in an artiﬁcial environment but in an everyday
environment and for di↵erent age groups as well.
page 65 of 148
==PAGE:78==
Based on these ﬁndings, PMV cannot suﬃciently predict the sensation of autistic individuals when in warm
conditions, and possibly not in cold conditions either.
The primary reasons for dissatisfaction were the same for the autistic group in CE as for those in FS during
the non-heating season.
There was an inversion in trends between the autistic and control group, with
controls mostly reporting dissatisfaction due to having a warm chest, with this being less reported in the
ASC group, but more reports were made of dissatisfaction due to extremities and the head feeling colder
than the controls. This may indicate a di↵erent feeling or perception of the temperature between the two
groups. The same trend with di↵erence in reported discomfort at the chest was present for the Cold Room in
CE, but without any remarkable di↵erence for the extremities. The di↵erence in perception and feeling may
only relate to the head and chest or be temperature-dependent. This could mean that the higher number of
responses of feeling warm on the extremities could be due to hypersensitivity, which is a hallmark trade of
ASC [APA, 2022].
Lack of control, was for the Warm Room in CE as well as the non-heating season in FS, a frequent trigger
of dissatisfaction. In FS, this could be due to a lack of resources to cool the living spaces during summer
and, therefore, feeling less in control of the thermal environment. In order to achieve more control of the
thermal environment, ﬂexible heating and cooling systems may be preferred in building design. The need
to control the environment is recommended in several studies [Zaniboni and Toftum, 2023]. Lack of control
being a trigger of dissatisfaction in CE and FS, therefore aligns with the previous ﬁndings. The importance
of ﬂexibility in design and the ability to regulate the environment is further discussed in subsection 4.2
Environmental adaptation for comfort.
Previous studies have investigated the inﬂuence of the thermal domain on autistic individuals, but no
remarkable ﬁndings in relation to ASC individuals have been found. In a study of self-reported discomfort,
temperature was found to be the only IEQ factor with equal mean discomfort to that of a control group,
both at home and at the workplace [Noble et al., 2018]. If looking at the self-reported sensitivity to the
four domains, thermal sensitivity did not greatly di↵er between the ASC and control group in CE. This
aligns with the self-reported thermal discomfort in [Noble et al., 2018]. If instead exposed to speciﬁc thermal
conditions, as done in CE, a di↵erence was noticed.
It might also be the case that other IEQ factors are more problematic than temperature, and therefore it is
not reported as uncomfortable as other areas.
Additionally, [Shell, 2016] concluded that ”there is no evidence relating thermal comfort to ASD ”(ASD is
synonymous with ASC). The notion that ASC individuals felt both warmer and colder than a control group
contradicts the statement of [Shell, 2016].
From this study, it is not clear why it is that the ASCs seemingly perceive the thermal conditions di↵erently.
It is therefore worth looking further into the reasons why; whether it is related to di↵erences in metabolism,
vasoconstriction, or brain sensorial processing, or something else.
4.1.2
Visual environment
When comparing visual satisfaction based on NL and AL conditions, the ASC group had higher mean scores,
regardless of light color, in NL compared to AL conditions. This was even more apparent when observing the
results of visual satisfaction for Q2(2), where white light was used (Figure 31), and where thermal exposure
and adaptation were more uniform than results with various light colors. The access to outdoor green views
can, therefore, be thought to increase visual satisfaction. This ﬁnding is not evident when matching with
the results of the FS. Here, there seemed to be no clear evidence of correlation between visual satisfaction
page 66 of 148
==PAGE:79==
and access to view-out. However, several unknown/uncontrolled factors inﬂuencing visual satisfaction rating
in the FS were not taken into account. These factors include illuminance (both from artiﬁcial and natural
light), view-out quality, and view of greenery, to mention a few. Therefore, visual satisfaction in FS cannot
solely be attributed to view-out. Therefore, the ﬁndings in FS were not enough to deny the ﬁndings in CE.
Problems with glare were in CE not speciﬁc to the NL scenario, and were not as frequent as in FS. The lack
of problematic glare in CE could be due to the position of the rooms, facing northwest and partially shielded
by trees, as well as the sun in the winter period only being in direct view for short periods of time. With
the participants’ homes in FS having other orientations and the period of study lasting for the whole year,
problems with glare could be more rightfully depicted in FS.
[Noble et al., 2018] identiﬁed that natural light caused the lowest level of avoidance and low levels of discomfort
when compared to 11 other IEQ factors. This supports the use of NL further. Autistic individuals might
often have a di↵erent circadian rhythm [Zaniboni and Toftum, 2023], and a more regular circadian rhythm
can be promoted with suﬃcient daylight exposure. This is also well aligned with the fact that they seem to
experience better visual satisfaction in scenarios with a view out, and further stress the importance of view-
out. On the other hand, glare and direct lighting have been found to be disruptive in four studies, while other
studies recommend the use of clerestory lights or indirect light since the use of eye-level windows can cause
distraction [Black et al., 2022]. Recommendations regarding natural light are therefore not straightforward
since its inﬂuence is multi-faceted. Nevertheless, the ﬁndings of this study support the importance of ensuring
daylight.
4.2
Environmental adaptation for comfort
The decrease in clothing insulation was greater for the Warm Room even though a similar share of participants
decreased their clothing level, meaning that the ASCs decreased their clothing insulation more substantially
than the controls when lowering the clothing insulation. The greater decrease in clo for the Warm Room
aligns with the fact that the ASC participants felt signiﬁcantly warmer than the control group in the Warm
Room and more dissatisﬁed, and therefore had a greater urge to decrease clothing.
Under neutral conditions, the ASC group was more prone to taking o↵clothes than the controls.
It is
worth noting that the ASCs felt mostly thermally neutral in the questionnaire before clothing change was
allowed, and only a small portion of the responses reﬂected dissatisfaction. It is therefore suspected that
some participants could prefer to be slightly cooler in order to be satisﬁed.
In the Cold Room, the controls increased their clothing more than the autistic participants, underlining the
fact that the ASC might feel more comfortable in colder conditions. However, compared to their satisfaction
scores, the ASC was not necessarily thermally satisﬁed in the Cold Room (see Figure 33). It is speculated
that some ASC participants might not have had additional clothes that they felt comfortable wearing inside
due to peculiarities in clothing preferences [Kyriacou et al., 2023], and therefore did not change clothing.
In FS, changing of clothing was also one of the most used actions when disregarding window operation, in
relation to thermal adaptation.
Some precautions should, however, be taken regarding the calculated clothing insulation, as these contained
some generalizations. Throughout the CE participants noted down the kind of clothing they were wearing,
and these were additionally cross-checked by the researchers. In spite of this, there still was some uncertainty,
especially in estimating the thickness of the sweaters and jackets that the participants typically wore at the
beginning of the sessions.
Regarding thermal comfort, the design recommendations from [Shell, 2016] instructed on using operable
page 67 of 148
==PAGE:80==
windows and providing thermostats for occupant control. According to FS, the implementation of operable
windows seems to be a beneﬁcial suggestion since this was a highly executed action. On the contrary, the
regulation of the thermostat in day-to-day life was found to be limited in the FS. This could, however, be
due to the monetary costs of using energy and the fact that several participants lived with their families, so
they had to take into account the comfort of all the people living in the same dwelling.
Overall the autistic group dimmed the light more than the controls. However, for the Cold Room, noticeably
fewer autistic participants decreased the light compared to the two other rooms. Why there was this drop was
unknown, especially when coupled with the ﬁnding of the ASC group being more visually dissatisﬁed in the
Cold Room. However, there seemed to be no di↵erence in the elements of dissatisfaction that would cause this
di↵erence in actions. However, it is possible that the lack of observable di↵erences in dissatisfaction elements
was due to participants adjusting the lighting, thereby mitigating their dissatisfaction. Findings revealed
that the ASC group was more dissatisﬁed with the blue light, but there were no signs that they adjusted
the blue light more than the other colors. It could be suspected that the autistic participants could prefer
slightly darker conditions since they more actively dimmed the light. However, this was not supported by the
answers regarding visual preference. The ASCs also dimmed the light more under AL than NL conditions.
This could be due to them being more bothered by the artiﬁcial light.
The position change of the lamp was done almost exclusively for the white light. The reasons for the positional
change were unknown. It was speculated that it was an act to avoid directly looking at the bulb, which was
not fully shielded by the lampshade, or a way of increasing or decreasing the illuminance manually without
interacting with the researchers. This positional change occurred primarily with white light. It could be
linked to the fact that white was the ﬁrst color used, and the lamp was not repositioned to the start position
when changing the light color.
In FS, various methods were used by participants to regulate the visual environment. In the non-heating
period, rolling down of shading and decreasing of lighting were done to equal extents. This could be due to
too much daylight or glare, as reported as unsatisfactory elements in Figure 48. The many actions executed
in regard to the visual environment, both in CE and FS, highlight the recommendations set by [Shell, 2016]
to provide lighting controls and to focus on ﬂexibility.
4.3
Multi-domain e↵ects
From Figure 31, it was seen that there was a lower satisfaction for IAQ for both controls and ASCs in the
Warm Room compared to the other thermal conditions. A signiﬁcant correlation was also found for AL and
NL conditions in the Warm Room across the participant groups. From measurements, the Warm Room did
not have higher CO2-concentration but contrarily had a lower concentration compared to the other rooms.
They could also have been negatively inﬂuenced by the relative humidity, since the air was in some sessions
dry. However, it is most likely due to a decrease in perceived air quality with increasing air temperatures
[Fang et al., 1998].
Based on the Spearman test, there was found to be a strong, signiﬁcant correlation for the ASC between the
thermal and visual satisfaction in the Cold and Warm Rooms when given a view out. This correlation was
not seen in the control group. The correlations for this relationship were also weaker in the same rooms in
AL for the ASC group, with no correlation in the Cold Room and correlation of moderate strength in the
Warm Room . This was further cemented by the relationship between the view-out condition and thermal
satisfaction, resulting in higher thermal satisfaction in the Warm and Cold Room with NL compared to AL
for the ASC group (and not remarkably for the controls). Additionally, view-out also positively inﬂuenced
page 68 of 148
==PAGE:81==
visual satisfaction. Summing up these ﬁndings, view-out may help better cope with otherwise unsatisfactory
thermal conditions.
There were found to be correlations between the thermal and visual domains when ﬁltering for light color
and experimental conditions. These were mainly found for the blue and yellow light and few for the white
light (for both groups).
There were no remarkable di↵erences between the ASC and the control group,
and even though there were several strong correlations, it is diﬃcult to say what the causes behind this
were. The shift from few correlations for the white to more with blue/yellow light may be due to di↵erences
in exposure time.
Adding to this, at these points in time, they had had the opportunity to alter their
clothing. Consequently, some participants may have changed their clothing immediately before answering
the questionnaire, potentially a↵ecting their ability to perceive the current temperature accurately. Further
research into what the reasons behind this correlation could be interesting to investigate.
Further exploring the interaction between the thermal and visual exposures, there was a decrease in visual
satisfaction in the Cold Room when using yellow and blue light for both groups, with the ASC group having
the greatest decrease in mean satisfaction.
This could be linked with thermal satisfaction since thermal
satisfaction was also seen to be lower in the Cold Room at the same conditions (see Figure 33).
There
was, therefore, thought to be an interdependency in thermal and visual satisfaction in the Cold Room. It
is important to notice that the thermal satisfaction depicted in the ﬁgure was extracted from questionnaires
later in the session than those analyzed for thermal perception and discussed in subsection 4.1.1 Thermal
environment.
Cross inﬂuence between e.g.
the visual and auditory domains have previously been found, as well as
peculiarities in inter-sensory interaction [Zaniboni and Toftum, 2023].
Therefore, potential inter-sensory
interaction between the visual and thermal domains is plausible and could be an area for further investigation.
4.4
Prolonged conditions
Regarding the development of symptoms, ﬁndings showed that the ASC generally felt the symptoms more
intensely than the controls, and overall feeling and headache were worse for AL than NL. Since this was
the case for most symptoms and the di↵erence was present for the ﬁrst questionnaire, this was not thought
to be due to the experimental conditions. This may, however, be due to the ASC group being inﬂuenced
by more strains in their daily life due to sensitivity. It could also be attributed to the higher prevalence of
co-morbidities, which could contribute to the stronger symptoms. There were small variations in tendencies
over time between the two groups, but of little di↵erence and a relatively high standard deviation. Tendencies
in symptom intensity and symptom development for the thermal and visual exposures were therefore found
to be too unsure to draw any clear conclusions from.
Analysis of performance scores showed that, as time passed in the sessions, the performance of the autistic
people improved signiﬁcantly from T1 to T2 for all conditions, except in the Warm Room under AL. The
controls, on the contrary, improved their score from T1 to T2 for all experimental setups, as well as for T2
to T3. The development of the symptoms did not show that they felt better as time went on, and this is
probably not the reason for the improvement from T1 to T2; it is, therefore, rather a result of practice e↵ects
[W¨uhr and W¨uhr, 2023]. The light color was white during T1 and either blue or yellow for T2 and T3.
Di↵erences in performance between T1 and T2/T3 could, therefore, be due to di↵erences in light color. Due
to randomization in color for T2 and T3, comparison between these can be assumed to be una↵ected by light
color.
The ASC group generally had a lower performance score than the control group, but the di↵erence was only
page 69 of 148
==PAGE:82==
found signiﬁcant for T3 in the Cold Room - AL. The general di↵erence in performance scores is speculated
to be attributed to ASC participants typically scoring lower than neurotypicals, possibly due to being in a
foreign environment or the inﬂuence of comorbidities.
The ASC group seemed to perform better in NL conditions than in AL conditions. This aligns with other
ﬁndings in the study, with higher visual and thermal satisfaction under NL. Yet, the di↵erence in performance
was only signiﬁcant for the ASC group for T3. The e↵ect of the exposure could take time to build up and
develop, which could explain why there was no signiﬁcant di↵erence between the AL and NL conditions for
the two previous tests. Therefore, the inﬂuence of NL and performance needs to be further investigated.
The performance test did showcase some di↵erence in the mean and median scores, especially for tests in AL
in the Warm and Cold Rooms, due to skewness and outliers in the datasets. The primary reasons for the
outliers were assumed to be due to errors made by the participants when ﬁlling in the tests. These errors
consisted of e.g. skipping lines, misunderstanding the conﬁguration of the targets, and initially ﬂipping the
paper wrongly. The e↵ect of outliers on the mean performance scores could have a↵ected the statistical
analysis.
4.5
Limitations of the study
4.5.1
Representativeness of the participant groups
As mentioned in subsections Recruitment 2.1.1 and 2.2.1, the participants were recruited at DTU through
posters as well as at nearby dormitories, through the SPS network (Socialpædagoisk Støtte/Special Educational
Support) at DTU, and through Facebook groups. The Facebook groups speciﬁcally targeted autistic commu-
nities, aiming at recruiting individuals with ASC. For CE, there was a lack of diversity in age, with most
participants (neurotypical as well as neurodivergent) being under the age of 30 and, therefore, not fully
representing the adult population. Participation in CE required commitment to six sessions of two hours
each, totaling 12 hours, all scheduled during the daytime.
This schedule was considered challenging for
individuals working full-time or living away from campus to accommodate. The controls for the CE were
furthermore only recruited through posters at DTU, while participation in the ASC group (for CE) was
attributed to the SPS network. Both of these recruitment paths likely contributed to the lack of age diversity
in each group.
As regards the FS, recruitment primarily occurred through Facebook groups.
In today’s context, active
Facebook users were suspected to be outside of the normal student age range, a↵ecting the age distribution of
participants. The time needed to participate in the FS was more limited than for CE, with no transportation
time needed for participants as well as no need to schedule several hours at set times. This was thought
to contribute to the more diverse age distribution in FS. Nevertheless, there were fewer participants in FS,
although there had been two separate recruitment rounds. This was possibly due to the measurements feeling
invasive to people’s private space, since it was conducted in private homes. Additionally, the participants did
not receive any compensation for participating, which may have made participation less appealing.
For both studies, there was an overrepresentation of women in the ASC groups, having more women than
men. This contradicts statistics indicating that more men are diagnosed than women [HBS Economics, 2023].
Based on this, the ﬁndings of the study could, therefore, be skewed compared to the demographics of the
ASC sample. However, the di↵erence in perception of autistic people in regards to the indoor climate had
never been studied before, and no comparison of results can therefore be made.
The ASC participants in both CE and FS had several comorbidities, contrary to the controls. Comorbidities,
page 70 of 148
==PAGE:83==
as well as other parameters such as age and gender, inﬂuence sensory perception [Zaniboni and Toftum, 2023]
and may, therefore, also have inﬂuenced the ﬁndings of this study.
The CE study consisted of 17 ASC and 20 control participants. The rather small participant groups resulted
in relatively small sample sizes for statistical analysis. This might have had an inﬂuence on the statistical
results. Several results were close to the 5% signiﬁcance level, and results might have been found to be
signiﬁcant with a greater sample size. Finally, a bigger sample size could also minimize the inﬂuence of errors
and irregularities.
4.5.2
Controlled Environments
Initially, participants were limited to one session a day, but as the ﬁnalization of the experiment got closer,
participants in both groups were allowed to have multiple sessions a day in order to accommodate the
participants’ schedules. This was permitted since the ASC group tolerated the experiments well, and they
did not seem too tiring or stressful for them. Within the ASC group, there were 16 instances of people
having multiple sessions per day and 20 for the controls. Some of these sessions were taken back to back,
with a break in between. This meant that some participants might have been more tired and less attentive
for their second session, but since it was unknown what participants did before sessions, this could also
apply to participants only doing one session a day. Some participants were seen to ﬁll out questionnaires
in a mechanized manner, possibly lacking awareness of their current well-being. This could skew the data,
although participant judgment was generally relied upon. It can also be speculated that there could have
been a ripple e↵ect between participants when there were multiple participants in a session. For instance,
when reminding them about the possibility of changing the light, the rest of the room became more aware of
their own settings if one person made changes. It was, therefore, very important for the researcher to remind
all groups equally about their possible actions.
There were found to be smaller di↵erences between the rooms regarding illuminance, CO2-concentration, RH,
and airspeed. Additionally, there were di↵erences in airspeed and sound level between sessions with AL and
NL. This could have inﬂuenced results since the environmental conditions were not fully consistent between
rooms and sessions in the same room. However, di↵erences were to be expected since the experiments were
not conducted in climate chambers but in oﬃces set up for the experiments, with little control of the air and
sound levels in the rooms.
4.5.3
Field Studies
The placement of equipment was not ideal in some instances because participants did not wish to be able
to see the equipment since it was a stress factor for them. The equipment also needed to be placed where
there was no risk of knocking it over or taking up valuable space for daily use. For a few participants, the
equipment was either placed in a shelving unit or directly on a windowsill, which would have had some e↵ect
on the measurements by possibly being in direct sunlight, in areas with no light, or in areas with poor mixing
of the air opposed to other places in the room. This was, however, mitigated as much as possible.
The environmental conditions of the rooms were known based on long-term measurements, but it was
unknown if the participants had been in the conditions long enough to be adapted to them. Therefore,
the conditions the participants had been exposed to before answering the questionnaire were uncertain to
some extent. This concern also entails the clothing reported by the participants since this relied on the
accuracy of the participants themselves.
The comparison of PMV and thermal sensation for FS should,
therefore, not be interpreted in an absolute manner; rather, the results were used to study trends.

4.5.4
Potential improvements of experiments and future research potential
The aim was for participants to answer the weekly questionnaire twice a week. In reality, the average response
rate was around once per week. To improve this, questionnaire reminders should have been sent out twice
a week (they were sent once a week) and through an automated system so that the reminders would come
consistently each week instead of being manually sent. Reminder messages may also have been more e↵ective
if sent via text message rather than email.
At the conclusion of the experiment, the questionnaire interface was found to be hard to navigate due to
formatting errors. This could have led to potentially wrongful answers or omission of individual questions.
This lack of user-friendliness could also have been a factor in the lack of answers.
The CE experiment was placed close to light-rail work, meaning sessions were bothered by construction noises,
which participants, both ASC and controls, complained about. It would be interesting to further investigate
the acoustic e↵ect on ASC people with an experiment where it would be easier to control the sound level,
since almost all ASCs saw themselves as sensitive to this domain compared to the controls. Additional studies
resembling the ones in CE but placed in quieter surroundings could be beneﬁcial in investigating the possible
e↵ects of the acoustic domain. Furthermore, studies with fewer variables could be of interest to make it easier
to pinpoint what causes correlations between domains. This could contribute to a better understanding of
what inﬂuences multi-domain e↵ects, which is crucial since people on the spectrum may experience more
multi-domain e↵ects than neurotypicals, as suggested in this study.
Studies further investigating the mechanisms behind the perception of the ASC individuals would also be
relevant, to uncover why they feel warmer than neurotypicals. By fully understanding the mechanism behind
it, it may be possible to better account for the altered thermal sensation in building design.

5
Conclusion
The purpose of this study was to investigate whether people with Autism Spectrum Condition (ASC) perceive
the indoor environment di↵erently than neurotypical people. This was done through two types of studies:
Controlled Environments (CE) and Field Studies (FS). Participants were given comfort questionnaires in
both studies and the indoor environment was measured.
The conducted analysis and interpretation of results from the two studies can be summarized as follows:
• In total, autistic individuals might sense the indoor environment di↵erently than neurotypicals and
than what is predicted by PMV. The ASC group had a signiﬁcantly warmer thermal sensation when
exposed to warm conditions compared to neurotypicals in CE. Warmer thermal sensation was also
found when comparing TSV and PMV in FS. Autistic individuals also felt signiﬁcantly cooler and less
satisﬁed in cold conditions without access to view-out. Elements for dissatisfaction were found to be
more frequently reported by the ASC group, suggesting a higher sensitivity to uncomfortable elements.
The ASC group tended to be more dissatisﬁed than neurotypicals due to warm feeling at extremities
and head. Contrarily, the ASC group was less triggered by a warm sensation at the chest. These
ﬁndings were found both in mock-up oﬃces and private homes and across a wide age range, from young
adults to middle-aged people.
• Regarding the visual domain, the ASC participants were found to be signiﬁcantly more dissatisﬁed
than neurotypicals when exposed to blue light. Moreover, access to a natural view positively inﬂuenced
visual satisfaction in the CE experiments. However, real-life scenarios (FS) were less conclusive, and
elements linked with natural light, such as glare, could have inﬂuenced visual satisfaction.
• The ASC participants utilized the adaptive opportunities more in CE, both in regard to lighting
conditions and clothing adaptation compared to the control group.
Active adaptation of the light
was also found in the ﬁeld studies, underlining the importance of ﬂexible lighting options.
• There was a possible interaction between the visual and thermal domain, with natural light (NL)
improving thermal satisfaction. There was a statistical correlation, ranging from moderate to strong,
between the visual and thermal domains in 3 out of the 6 experimental setups for the ASC in CE.
Most signiﬁcant correlations of inter-domain satisfaction were found to be in NL for cold and warm
conditions and were stronger and more frequent.
• The ASC group reported more intense symptoms than the neurotypical group, but there were otherwise
no remarkable di↵erences in changes in symptom intensity over time between the two groups. Contrary
to the controls, the ASC group did not improve their performance in the last test of the sessions in
CE. Furthermore, the thermal and visual exposures did not signiﬁcantly inﬂuence the performance of
either the ASC or the control groups.
These ﬁndings underline the importance of ﬂexible design, allowing ASC individuals to control the environment
to their speciﬁc needs. PMV is suspected not to be an adequate tool to describe the thermal sensation of the
autistic population, and IEQ design regarding thermal sensation should be rethought to be more inclusive.
The inﬂuence of view-out should be studied further to properly determine what aspects of the view-out are
inﬂuential in achieving visual satisfaction concerning individuals with ASC. Furthermore, research uncovering
the mechanisms resulting in a warmer thermal sensation should be done.

Dear participant, 
This questionnaire regards the well-being indoors of people on the autism spectrum.  
People on the autism spectrum deserve more attention to their needs for indoor comfort and well-being. 
Due to their sensitivity to stimuli from the indoor environment (i.e., hyper-sensitivity or hypo-sensitivity), 
they might have particular needs in terms of thermal, visual, indoor air quality and acoustic environments. 
Nevertheless, standards and guidelines typically address average neurotypical populations, and little 
knowledge is present in the literature about how the indoor environment affects the well-being of people 
on the spectrum, especially from the indoor environment and comfort engineering perspective. Moreover, 
some literature suggests that optimal indoor conditions for people on the spectrum might be more pleasant 
for neurotypical people as well. This knowledge gap has motivated SENSEwellbeing, in which we aim to 
perform an extensive study on indoor well-being of people on the spectrum. 
For these reasons, we kindly ask you to answer the following questions.

sensitive data will always be presented to the public in an aggregated form.

Your contribution is very important for the well-being of people, both neuroatypical and neurotypical!

Sammenligning med standarder: 
Den anbefalede maksimal temperatur for boliger i sommermånederne er 26°C og minimum 20°C om 
vinteren jf. DS/EN 16798. Generelt ligger temperaturen indenfor det anbefalede område for boliger. I 
sommermånederne er der en enkelt uge, hvor der bliver målt over den anbefalede max værdi på 26°C. En 
så lille overskridelse er dog ikke problematisk, og man kan i nogen tilfælde designe beboelse til at have en 
maksimal øvre grænse på 27°C. For enkelte perioder i vintermånederne, er det minimumsgrænsen på 20°C, 
der derimod bliver overskredet. Temperaturen i efteråret befinder sig gennem hele måleperioden indenfor 
grænserne. Stuen er for det meste varmere end køkkenet, med størst forskel imellem rummene i 
sommermånederne.

Belysningsstyrke 
Belysningsstyrke er den samlede lysstrøm, der rammer en overflade. Det er et mål for, hvor meget af det 
indfaldne lys, der oplyser en overflade. Det måles i ”lux” [lx]. 
Belysningsstyrken i rummene er målt ved punktmålinger rundt omkring i de to udvalgte rum, som kan ses 
opsummeret i tabellen nedenfor. Målingerne er foretaget på gulvet, og vil være påvirket af møbler, som vil 
afskærme for lyset målt. 
Målingerne er foretaget på en overskyet dag.  
 
 
• 
Sammenligning med standarder: 
Bygningsreglementet foreskriver at beboelsesrum og køkkener skal have en dagslystilgang, så de er 
tilstrækkeligt belyste. Dette indebærer at der er mere end 300lx i mindst halvdelen af rummet i halvdelen af 
tiden. Dog vurderes den nødvendige belysningsstyrke også efter din egen præference, og der er derfor intet 
galt i at have en belysningsstyrke under grænsen, så længe du selv er tilfreds med forholdene. 
 Belysningsstyrken i både stuen og køkkenet er mørkere end anbefalet.

Sammenligning med standarder 
Ved en CO2 koncentration på over 2000ppm kan der opleves symptomer såsom hovedpine og søvnighed. Et 
for højt CO2 niveau er i sig selv ikke farligt, men indikerer dårlig ventilation samt mangel på frisk luft. 
Nybyggede huse designes til at være under 1200ppm (DS/EN16798). Der er i flere perioder målt værdier 
over 2000ppm for stuen. De resterende målinger topper næsten dagligt omkring 2000ppm. Stuen har 
desuden en højere CO2 koncentration end for køkkenet. Der er kun målinger for CO2 koncentrationen i 
efteråret og vinter for køkkenet.  
Den relative luftfugtighed bør være indenfor intervallet 30%-60%. Den relative luftfugtighed ses at være lidt 
højere eller tæt på den øvre grænse om sommeren og for dele af efteråret. Vinterperioden befinder sig 
indenfor de anbefalede værdier.

Projektnavn: SENSEwellbeing 
Baggrundsinformation 
 
Kære deltager, 
Dette spørgeskema indgår i en undersøgelse af, hvordan indeklima opleves af personer med autisme. 
Spørgeskemaet anvendes til at indsamle baggrundsinformation om deltagerne i undersøgelsen. 
På grund af anderledes følsomhed over for stimuli fra omgivelserne (f.eks. hypersensitivitet eller 
hyposensitivitet), kan personer på autismespektret have særlige behov med hensyn til temperatur, visuelle, 
atmosfæriske og akustiske påvirkninger. Ikke desto mindre bygger standarder og retningslinjer for indeklima 
typisk på gennemsnitlige, neurotypiske populationer, og der findes kun meget begrænset viden om, hvordan 
indeklimaet påvirker komfort og velvære for personer på spektret. En del af den eksisterende viden indikerer, 
at et indeklima der er optimalt for personer på spektret, også kan være mere behageligt for neurotypiske 
personer. 
Den manglende viden har motiveret SENSEwellbeing, som er en omfattende undersøgelse af indeklima og 
velvære hos personer på spektret. 
Som en del af undersøgelsen vil vi gerne bede dig om at besvare de følgende spørgsmål så præcist som muligt. 
GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation, EU Regulation 2016/679) regler og etiske principper altid blive 
overholdt. Data vil blive pseudonymiseret og følsomme data vil i henhold til DTUs procedurer for 
datahåndtering blive krypteret og slettet to år efter projektets afslutning. Kun forskere tilknyttet projektet vil 
have adgang til data og følsomme data vil kun blive offentliggjort i aggregeret form. Al behandling og analyse 
af data vil overholde databeskyttelsesloven og databeskyttelsesforordningen. 
 
Din deltagelse er vigtig for at forstå sammenhænge mellem indeklima og velvære, for både personer med og 
uden en autismediagnose. 
Tak for at du vil deltage.
