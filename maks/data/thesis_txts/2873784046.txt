Abstract
The global shift towards renewable energy is transforming power systems into complex,
converter­dominated grids, introducing significant stability challenges that traditional anal­
ysis tools struggle to address. This thesis details the development and validation of a
Python­based toolbox designed for the modeling, simulation, and stability analysis of
modern power systems. The toolbox employs a modular, hierarchical architecture, allow­
ing complex components like grid­following and grid­forming converters to be assembled
from a library of interchangeable subcomponents. A key methodological contribution is
the transformation of the system’s inherent differential­algebraic equations into a compu­
tationally manageable ordinary differential equation framework, enabling both non­linear
time­domain simulation and linearized small­signal analysis.
To verify its accuracy, the toolbox was benchmarked against the industry­standard soft­
ware PSCAD. The validation process demonstrated a high degree of accuracy across
multiple test cases. Static load flow calculations matched the benchmark with negligi­
ble error. Dynamic simulations of multi­converter systems, including GFL­GFL and GFL­
GFM configurations, successfully replicated the transient and steady­state behavior in
response to power reference step changes. Furthermore, the toolbox’s linearization pro­
cedure was validated, showing excellent agreement between the linearized model’s dy­
namic response and the non­linear PSCAD results.
This work successfully delivers a validated toolbox that provides researchers and engi­
neers with a flexible and powerful platform for analyzing converter­driven dynamics. As
such, it represents a valuable contribution to the tools required to navigate the stability
challenges of the ongoing transition to a renewable­based energy future.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
The global energy landscape is undergoing a profound transformation, driven by a surge
in electricity demand and efforts to push toward decarbonization. According to the Global
Energy Review published in March 2025, global energy demand experienced a signifi­
cant increase of 2.2% in 2024, surpassing the average growth rate [1]. This surge was
primarily driven by the power sector, where electricity demand rose due to several factors:
rising global temperatures necessitating greater cooling demand [2], expanding industrial
electricity consumptions, and a notable uptick in computing power requirements. A report

Theoretical Background
2.1
Power System Stability
Power system stability is defined as the ability of a power system to maintain a state of
operating equilibrium under normal conditions and to regain an acceptable equilibrium
following a disturbance [12]. According to a recent IEEE Task Force report, this long­
standing definition remains valid and applicable to modern systems with high penetrations
of converter­based technologies [13]. Traditionally, this concept is classified into rotor an­
gle stability, frequency stability, and voltage stability, each rooted in the physical behavior
of synchronous machines that have long dominated power systems [12, 14].
In traditional power systems, synchronous generators provide inherent inertia and damp­
ing through their rotating masses.
This direct electromechanical coupling to the grid
means that any frequency deviation triggers a natural response: the generator’s rota­
tional speed adjusts, injecting or absorbing energy to stabilize the system. This mecha­
nism is critical for frequency stability and overall system resilience. However, renewable
energy sources like wind and solar, interfaced via power electronic converters, disrupt
this dynamic. These converters control power flow electronically, decoupling the gener­
ation source from the grid’s frequency. As a result, the system loses this natural inertial
response, increasing its vulnerability to frequency swings and disturbances [15]. This dis­
placement of synchronous machines also reduces their contribution to short­circuit cur­
rents and can negatively impact the rotor angle stability of the remaining generators in the
systems [13].
To address this, grid codes worldwide have introduced requirements for synthetic inertia,
where converter­based resources are designed to mimic the frequency response of syn­
chronous machines. For instance, Hydro­Quebec in Canada mandates synthetic inertia
from wind turbines [16], while EirGrid in Ireland has set minimum rotational kinetic energy
levels and updated grid codes to handle a Rate of Change of Frequency (RoCoF) of 1
Hz/s over 500 ms, up from 0.5 Hz/s. These adaptations highlight the enduring relevance
of traditional stability concepts, even as power systems evolve [17, 18].
The inclusion of power electronic converters introduces converter­driven stability chal­
lenges distinct from traditional electromechanical phenomena. As illustrated by analysis
from the National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL) and shown in Figure 2.1, the
dynamics of converter­based resources are governed by fast­acting electronic controls
operating across a wide range of frequencies, fundamentally different from the slower
dynamics of synchronous machines [19].
Figure 2.1 highlights these key converter controls, such as phase­locked loops (PLLs) for
grid synchronization, inner current controllers, DC­link voltage controllers, and pulse­width
modulation (PWM) switching schemes. While essential for operation, the interactions
between these fast control loops, and between the converters and the grid, can lead to
new forms of instability, including high­frequency oscillations and harmonic resonance.
To formally address these emerging issues, the traditional stability classification has been
extended with two new categories: Resonance Stability and Converter­driven Stability.

2.1.1
Resonance Stability
This category encompasses instabilities arising from the periodic exchange of energy
between power system components in an oscillatory manner. If the system has insuffi­
cient damping, these oscillations can grow, leading to large voltage, current, or torque
magnitudes that can threaten equipment. Resonance stability is divided into two distinct
phenomena:
Torsional Resonance
This involves an interaction between the electrical grid and the mechanical shaft sys­
tems of turbine­generators. It includes classic Subsynchronous Resonance (SSR), where
series compensation resonates with the turbine­generator shaft, and Device­Dependent
Subsynchronous Oscillations (DDSSO), which arise from interactions with fast­acting power
electronic devices [20].
Electrical Resonance
This is a purely electrical phenomenon, independent of mechanical dynamics. The most
prominent example is Subsynchronous Control Interaction (SSCI), which has been ob­
served in wind farms with Double­Fed Induction Generators (DFIGs). SSCI is fundamen­
tally caused by the Induction Generator Effect, where the converter controls introduce
negative damping that destabilizes an electrical resonance between the generator and a
series­compensated network, leading to large and damaging current oscillations [21].
2.1.2
Converter­Driven Stability
This category includes instabilities caused directly by the control systems within power
electronic converters interacting with other power system components. The wide timescale
of these controls can lead to oscillations over a broad frequency range. This class is di­
vided into two subclasses based on the speed of the interacting controls.
Fast­Interaction Stability
This involves high­frequency oscillations, typically in the range of hundreds of hertz to
several kilohertz. It is driven by the fast dynamics of inner­current control loops (as shown
in Figure 2.1) interacting with network components like filters or feeder capacitors. This
phenomenon is also sometimes referred to as harmonic instability [13].

Slow­Interaction Stability
This involves low­frequency oscillations that arise from slower outer control loops. These
controls can interact with the electromechanical dynamics of synchronous generators or
other slow­response components of the power system. This type of instability is a signifi­
cant concern for CIGs connected to weak grids, where low short­circuit ratios can desta­
bilize other controllers [22].
The stability challenges just described, from high­frequency resonance to slow control
interactions, are all rooted in the behavior of power electronic converters. Therefore,
before these issues can be analyzed, it is essential to understand how these converters
operate and are modeled. The following sections provide this background, beginning with
the fundamental converter types that define today’s power systems.
2.2
Converter­Dominated Power Systems
The functionality of a power converter in renewable energy systems is conversion of en­
ergy from one stage to another stage, with the highest possible efficiency, lowest cost and
optimal performance [23]. Examples of this are DC­AC converters that can invert the DC
output from PV modules or wind turbine systems, and AC­DC converters can be used to
charge a battery to store excess power in the system. [24].
In converter­dominated power systems, two primary types of converters have emerged
as widely used in modern grids. Grid­following (GFL) and grid­forming (GFM) converters
[25]. GFL converters represent the conventional or first­generation approach to converter­
based generation and remain the most common type currently deployed in power systems
[26].
Figure 2.2: (a) Simple GFL representation, (b) simple GFM representation.
2.2.1
Grid­Following Converters
GFL converters operate by controlling their output current and are typically conceptualized
or modeled as controlled current sources [27]. This implies that they ”follow” the voltage
signal already imposed by the main grid. Under no­load conditions, a GFL converter
requires a reference angle for current injection, contrasting with GFM converters which
can provide a reference voltage for loads and other units [25]. Figure 2.2a shows the
general representation of a GFL converter as a controlled current source with a parallel
impedance.
An integral part of GFL control is the previously mentioned PLL. The PLL’s essential func­
tion is to measure or estimate the frequency and phase of the voltage at the Point of
Common Coupling (PCC), which is the interface to the grid [27]. In most implementa­
tions, a synchronous reference frame PLL (SRF­PLL) is employed due to its robustness
and established structure [28]. The PLL obtains the reference angular speed (ωref) and

phase angle (θref) by regulating the quadrature (q­axis) component of its input voltage
typically via a proportional­integral (PI) controller [27]. This θref is then crucial for dq to
abc transformations within the converter’s internal control system. The overall control of
GFL converters typically occurs in the dq0 reference frame, comprising outer control loops
for active and reactive power references and inner control loops for current regulation [26].
2.2.2
Grid­Forming Converters
GFM control is widely recognized as a promising and essential solution for the future of
power grids. GFM converters are controlled to establish both the angle and amplitude
references for the converter’s modulated voltage [26]. They are often modeled as con­
trolled voltage sources behind an impedance (Figure 2.2b). This inherent voltage source
behavior allows them to contribute to grid stability by ensuring local voltage stiffness [27].
A key feature of GFM converters is that they do not necessarily rely on PLLs for grid syn­
chronization during normal operation [25]. Instead, they can synchronize with the grid
based on their output active power, a concept termed ”power­based synchronization”.
Their primary objective is to form the voltage magnitude and phase angle at the PCC
which makes them particularly effective for isolated or ”islanded” modes of operation [29].
In such islanded scenarios, GFM converters are able to supply the load entirely by them­
selves.
A significant advantage of GFM converters is their ability to introduce inertial response to
the system [30]. The true inertial response is defined as an instantaneous power response
proportional to the RoCoF, occurring within one cycle or less. While synthetic inertia can
offer a similar idea, its response time might exceed one cycle [29].
The internal operation of GFM converters is built upon control architectures that emulate
the behavior of synchronous machines. A general GFM control structure typically com­
prises outer and inner control loops with dynamics ordered from slow to fast. Typically, a
Droop Control scheme is adopted to establish a relationship where the converter’s output
frequency (ω) adjusts based on the difference between active setpoint (P ∗) and measured
power (P), and output voltage adjusts based on reactive power (Q) differences. The an­
gle is then obtained by integrating the frequency. This type of control scheme is effective
and typically does not require an additional synchronization unit during normal operation.

2.3.1
Grid­Following Model
The standard GFL control architecture, shown on the left in Figure 2.4, builds directly upon
the SRF­PLL. The PLL provides the angle to the cascaded control loops. An outer power
control loop uses PI controllers to regulate the measured active and reactive power to their
respective setpoints, generating the reference currents in the rotational reference frame.
These references are then fed to an inner current control loop, which is responsible for
generating the final voltage commands for the converter, thereby controlling the injected
current.
The conventional SRF­PLL performs well under balanced and ideal grid conditions, but its
performance can degrade significantly in the presence of harmonics, unbalance, or other
disturbances. Several advanced PLL architectures have therefore been proposed to im­
prove synchronization under non­ideal grid conditions. These approaches, as extensively
reviewed by Golestan, Guerrero, and Vasquez, primarily focus on enhancing disturbance
rejection capabilities [32].

Advanced GFL Control Schemes
One common approach is the moving average filter, which incorporates MAFs either in­
side the control loop or as a pre­filtering state. This greatly improves the rejection of dis­
turbances but can introduce delays that slow down the PLL’s dynamic response. A more
sophisticated technique is the Multiple Synchronous Reference Frame (MSRF)­PLL. This
method utilizes additional reference frames rotating at specific harmonic frequencies to
selectively filter harmonic components from the voltage signal, leading to a much cleaner
synchronization signal. Other advanced methods include those based on notch filters
(NFs) and second­order generalized integrators (SOGIs), each offering different trade­
offs between filtering capability, dynamic response and computational complexity.
2.3.2
Grid­Forming Model
In contrast, GFM modeling strategies are designed to operate as controlled voltage sources.
As seen on the right in Figure 2.4, the power control block directly outputs a desired volt­
age magnitude (V ) and phase angle (ϕ). These are then passed to a voltage control loop
that synthesizes the final converter output voltage, effectively forming the local grid volt­
age. The two most common conventional approaches to implement this logic are droop
control and the virtual synchronous machine (VSM) [25]. Droop control is the simplest
method, where the converter’s output frequency is ”drooped” in proportion to its active
power output, and its voltage is drooped in relation to its reactive power output. The more
advanced VSM model explicitly emulates the swing equation of a synchronous generator
to provide virtual inertia and damping to the system.
Advanced GFM Control Schemes
Beyond these conventional methods, several other GFM control structures have been de­
veloped. The Power Synchronization Control (PSC) was one of the first control structures
designed for grid­connected converters that mimics the power synchronization mecha­
nism of a synchronous machine, thus overcoming the need for a dedicated PLL for syn­
chronization [33]. A more recent approach is the Virtual Oscillator Control (VOC). This
is a nonlinear control strategy that models the converter to reproduce the dynamics of a
weakly nonlinear limit­cycle oscillator, such as a Van der Pol oscillator [34]. A key charac­
teristic of VOC is that it allows multiple converters to synchronize with each other based on
instantaneous time­domain signals without needing any communication. The control can
be designed to be dispatchable, allowing for the regulation of active and reactive power.
These diverse control structures, each with its own set of differential equations, form the
basis for the detailed system models used for stability analysis. While a wide variety of
advanced control schemes exist, this thesis focuses on implementing and validating the
foundational models of GFL and GFM converters. This approach allows for a rigorous
verification of the core modeling framework and the system assembly procedures of the
developed toolbox, without the added complexity of comparing state­of­the­art control
variations.
To analyze the behavior of these dynamic systems in a systematic and efficient manner,
it is necessary to organize their governing equations into a standardized mathematical
framework. This is achieved by using the state­space representation, which provides a
powerful and universal method for describing and analyzing dynamic systems.
2.4
State­Space Representation of Dynamic Systems
The dynamic models of power system components, as described in the previous section,
result in a set of coupled ordinary differential equations. State­space representation is

a suitable method used in modern control theory that describes a system using a set of
first­order differential equations for the internal state variables, combined with an algebraic
equation that defines the output variables [35]. This framework is central to this thesis,
as the developed toolbox will represent every dynamic component and the overall power
system using this formulation.
A system in state­space is described by three types of vector variables:
• The State Vector x(t): The state of a system is defined as the minimum set of
variables whose values at an initial time, along with the system inputs, are sufficient
to completely determine the future behavior of the system. These variables, known
as state variables, are often related to the energy storage elements in the system,
like capacitors and inductors. The number of state variables defines the order of the
system [12].
• The Input Vector u(t): These are independent external signals that act as outside
influences to the system’s dynamic behavior. For a power converter, inputs could
include power setpoints or the grid voltage at its terminals.
• The Output Vector y(t): These are the system variables that can be measured or
are of particular interest for analysis. An output may be a state variable itself or an
algebraic function of the state and input variables [35].
A power system is an inherently nonlinear dynamic system. The relationship between its
state variables (x) and control inputs (u) can be generally expressed as:
˙x = f(x, u)
y = g(x, u)
Where f and g are vectors of nonlinear functions. The system reaches a steady­state
when ˙x = 0, meaning f(x, u) = 0.
2.4.1
Linearization for Small­Signal Analysis
To study the stability of the system when it is subjected to small disturbances around an
equilibrium point, a method that linearizes the system can be used [12]. For a system op­
erating at an equilibrium (x0, u0), and neglecting higher­order terms, the system dynamics
for small deviations (∆x, ∆u, ∆y) can be described by a set of linear, time­invariant (LTI)
equations:
∆˙x = A∆x + B∆u
∆y = C∆x + D∆u
Here, the matrices A, B, C and D are the Jacobian matrices of the functions f and g
evaluated at the equilibrium point.
• A is the (n × n) state matrix, which contains the eigenvalues that determine the
stability of the system.
• B is the (n × m) input matrix, which determines how the inputs affect the states.
• C is the (p × n) output matrix, specifying how outputs are derived from the states.

• D is the (p × m) feedthrough matrix, representing the direct influence of inputs on

This linearized state­space model is a powerful basis for analyzing the system’s dynamic
modes and stability characteristics. However, this model is only an approximation. Since
the system is linearized around a specific equilibrium point, the model is only valid for
disturbances that do not significantly deviate the system from that point. This is known
as local or small­signal stability. For larger disturbances, the neglected nonlinear effects
become significant, and this method cannot accurately predict the system’s true behavior.
The primary purpose of this linearization is to obtain the state matrix A, whose mathe­
matical properties, specifically its eigenvalues, determine the small­signal stability of the
system. While a detailed study of all possible analysis techniques is beyond the scope of
this work, the ability to generate the linearized state­space matrices is the essential first
step. The toolbox developed in this thesis successfully implements this complete mod­
eling and linearization pipeline, providing the necessary foundation for any subsequent
stability assessment.
2.5
Power Flow and Dynamic Simulation
While the state­space models describe the dynamic behavior of individual components, a
power system analysis must begin from a stable, steady­state condition. The process of
finding this steady­state is known as load flow analysis, with the goal of determining the
voltage magnitude and phase angle at every bus in the network, as well as the active and
reactive power flowing through each component.
This analysis solves a system of nonlinear algebraic equations that represent the power
balance at each bus, governed by Kirchhoff’s Laws. The solution to these equations
provides the system’s equilibrium point (x0, u0), which plays a critical role for two reasons:
1. It serves as the linearization point for small­signal stability analysis. The accuracy of
the linearized state matrix A is entirely dependent on the accuracy of this calculated
equilibrium.
2. It provides the initial conditions for time­domain dynamic simulations. To accurately
simulate the system’s response to a disturbance, the simulation must start from a
valid and stable steady state.
Once the power flow is solved and the equilibrium point is known, both small­signal and
large­signal stability can be investigated. Specifically, large­signal stability refers to per­
forming a full, nonlinear time­domain simulation to observe the system’s response to sig­
nificant events like faults or large changes in generation or load. To facilitate these analy­
ses, the toolbox developed in this thesis implements methodologies for both small­signal
and large­signal stability assessments.

3.1
The PowerNetLab Simulation Framework
The core of PowerNetLab is organized into several key modules, each addressing a spe­
cific aspect of power system analysis:
• Network Definition (network module): The foundational module is responsible for
the static representation of the user­defined power grid. Users can define buses,
transmission lines, transformers, generators and loads. Herein also lies the code
responsible for assembling the system’s admittance matrix.
• Load Flow (loadflow module): Building upon the network model, this module im­
plements algorithms, such as the Newton­Raphson method, to solve the power flow
problem, determined steady­state voltages, angles, and power flows across the sys­
tem. It provides the initial operating condition for dynamic simulations.
• Dynamic Modeling (model.dynamicmodeling module): This module addresses the
time­domain behavior of power system components. It allows for the simulation and
analysing of dynamic models, which are represented by a set of SymPy expressions.
The simulation engine handles ODE systems using the scipy.integrate.solve_ivp
to solve the system of differential equations. This module houses robust and general­
purpose capabilites that is intended to be used in the toolbox.
• Small­Signal Stability (smallsignal module): This focuses on small­signal stabil­
ity analysis. It provides tools to create symbolic state­space models for individual
network components and can systematically combine component models into an
overall system model. It can then use the aggregated model to assess stability
through eigenvalue analysis and frequency response calculations.

The design of the toolbox is underpinned by two core principles: modularity and scalability.
These principles ensure the toolbox can accommodate diverse power system configura­

The toolbox employs a modular architecture to streamline the development, testing, and

3.3
Component and Subcomponent Modeling
The network and component modeling framework is designed to create modular, self­
contained systems that reflect the hierarchical and interoperable nature of a network.
Each component encapsulates a set of subcomponents, which are smaller, integrated
units maintaining a layered architecture.
A subcomponent is defined as a lower­level dynamic module inside a higher­level com­
ponent. More precisely, each subcomponent
• Maintains its own state vector
• Produces a set of output signals
• Receives a specific input vector
By assembling multiple subcomponents into a single component model Ci,j, we can re­
flect the layered structure of real hardware (e.g., inner current loop, outer voltage loop,
filters, etc.). This decomposition drastically simplifies testing and supports swapping con­
trol loops without rewriting the entire device model.

Modeling Electrical Systems with ODEs vs DAEs
ODEs describe system dynamics with equations involving purely time­derivatives of state
variables and no direct algebraic constraints. In contrast, DAEs include both differential
equations and algebraic equations that must be satisfied at all times [36]. Specifically in
electrical circuit and power system modeling, DAEs naturally arise because Kirchhoff’s
laws impose instantaneous algebraic constraints on voltages and currents, alongside any
dynamic equations. This way of formulating the equations effectively couples the de­
vice dynamics to the power flow constraints. This results in a more accurate structure­
preserving model of the system [37].

Conversely, an ODE model of the same system requires eliminating the algebraic con­
straints. Common modeling for system’s control and estimation is based on ODE models,
partly due to limitations on system theory for DAEs [37]. In typical use cases the formula­
tion of an ODE model from a DAE model is done by either neglecting algebraic constraints
or by decoupling the state variables from the algebraic constraints. Such ODE models
can simplify analysis but at the cost of instantaneous network balances.
Algebraic Constraints and DAE Index
In a DAE, the algebraic constraints are equations with no time­derivatives that ties vari­
ables together at each instant. These constraints fundamentally affect the solution prop­
erties of the problem. The DAE initial value problem may fail to have a solution unless
the initial conditions satisfy all algebraic constraints and their time­derivatives. These are
also called hidden constraints, and giving a consistent initial value is know to be a complex
task [38].
In simpler terms, the system’s state must always satisfy the algebraic constraints ­ it must
stay on a specific surface within the state­space. If these constraints aren’t met, no valid
solution can be found. This leads to the concept of index in DAE theory. By differentiating
the algebraic constraints, the DAE model can be reduced to an ODE form ­ and the amount
of times one must differentiate said constraints is referred to as the differentiation index
[36]. The higher the index, the more complex DAE model and intuitively how far the model
is from being an ODE.
The ability to convert a DAE model to an ODE model does not come without its disad­
vantages that one should be aware of. In the mathematical procedure of reducing a DAE
to an ODE, which will not be covered in detail in this thesis, the DAE is reduced to its
underlying ODE. However, solving the underlying ODE a numerical phenomenon called
”numerical drift” might occur. This drift is when the solution drifts away from the solution­
surface, and results in the solution no longer satisfying the algebraic constraints. In other
words, the numerical solution to the DAE will always be a solution to the underlying ODE,
but the numerical solution to the underlying ODE will not always be a solution to the cor­
responding DAE [39]. This problem is well studied and can be traced back to a famous
paper from 1982 by Petzold aptly titled ”Differential/Algebraic Equations are not ODE’s”
[40].

The DAE to ODE Transformation: A Foundational Design

The following section will try to elaborate on the explored methods to establish an ODE

The first strategy uses linearization to transform the DAE model into a linear ODE sys­

mented and evaluated to determine the final approach for the toolbox.

3.5.2
Approach 2: DAE to ODE Conversion for Nonlinear Simulation
While linearization is powerful for stability analysis, nonlinear time­domain simulation re­
quires a different approach that preserves the system’s inherent nonlinearities. The chal­
lenge lies in the algebraic constraints imposed by the network KCL equations. The follow­
ing methods convert these algebraic constraints into differential equations by strategically
introducing or relocating capacitive elements, thereby creating a pure (though often nu­
merically stiff) ODE system.
Method 2.1: Introducing Artificial Nodal Capacitance
Another solution explored in this thesis is the inclusion of a dynamic element to artificially
convert the system from a DAE formulation to an ODE formulation. It is formally recog­
nized in numerical analysis that DAEs can be transformed into ODEs by adding small
energy­storage elements. In fact, methods such as the dummy derivative technique in­
troduce negligible dynamics to reduce the index of the system [49].
Further, Lalgudi and Swaminathan [50] acknowledged that adding fictitious capacitances
and inductances can enable explicit simulation methods, provided the elements are suf­
ficiently small to avoid materially altering system behavior.
Well­established simulation tools, such as 20­sim, also recommend breaking algebraic
loops by introducing parasitic energy storage elements. However, it is noteworthy that
such additions tend to introduce large poles into the system dynamics, resulting in a stiffer
system and longer simulation times [51]. The term ”stiff system” refers directly to the nu­
merical stability of the set of differential equations representing the system. This is math­
ematically measured by calculating the ratio of the largest time constant to the smallest,
being proportional to the capacitance. Therefore, by introducing a small capacitance at
the bus, the gap between time constants of the system can potentially be much bigger
[12].

3.6
Power Flow Design
Having defined our network as a graph­based DAE/ODE system in Section 3.4, we now
turn to computing its steady­state operating point via load flow calculations. Unlike the
time­domain formulation, which uses differential equations to capture dynamics, load flow
solves only the algebraic power balance at each bus. This step decouples static initial­
ization from the subsequent dynamic simulation capabilities of the toolbox explained in
Section 3.7, providing the voltages and angles that serve as starting conditions.
Load flow calculations employs numerical techniques such as the Newton­Raphson method,
Gauss­Seidel method or fast decoupled load flow (FDLF) method [12]. These methods
all work to directly solve the algebraic equations governing power balance, which are
crucial to establish operating points to serve as initial conditions for subsequent dynamic
analysis.
The Gauss­Seidel method is simple and reliable but has slow convergence for larger
systems or heavily loaded conditions. The Newton­Raphson method offers much more
rapid convergence and is particularly effective for large systems requiring precise solu­
tions. Finally, the FDLF method provides a computationally efficient approximation of the
Newton­Raphson approach, making it suitable in situations where computation speed is
valued higher that accuracy.
The toolbox implements the Newton­Raphson method exclusively. Using the graph­based
assembly outlined earlier G(V, E), the toolbox implements the Newton­Raphson algorithm
purely as an algebraic procedure on the static network. A brief explanation on how this is
carried out can be seen below.

much larger time steps and much quicker computation times, making BDF a far better
option for converter­dominated systems with time constants varying in many orders of

4.4
Grid­following Converter
Unlike the simpler components such as the transmission line, transformer and static load
described in the preceding sections, the GFL converter is a complex, modular component
composed of several interacting subcomponents. To align the design with the hierarchical
modeling technique of the toolbox, the composite GFL model will have to be decomposed
into it’s subcomponents.
As established in the theoretical background, GFLs fundamentally operate as controlled
current sources, meaning they inject a specified amount of current into the grid, thereby
”following” a voltage signal from the grid. This aligns well with how the toolbox operates,
as every component is characterized by the current being injected into the connecting
node.
The operation of a GFL converter can be formulated by a few control loops, each modeled
as its own separate subcomponent. These loops typically comprises three interconnected
control layers: the PLL for synchronization, outer power control loops to manage active
and reactive power, and inner current control loops for precise current injection.
Figure 4.3 illustrates the control architecture for the GFL implemented in this thesis. The
diagram is adapted from the GFL control schematic presented by July et al. [56]. and
has been simplified to emphasize the key functional blocks. Figure 4.3a provides a high­
level overview of the control architecture of the GFL, while 4.3b details the structure of the
PLL responsible for grid synchronization. This model serves as the foundational design
chosen for the toolbox.

Grid­forming Converter
In contrast to the GFL architecture, the GFM converter operates as a controlled voltage
source, actively regulating the voltage and frequency at its connection point. The GFM
converter does not rely on a PLL for synchronization, instead it dictates the local grid
angle and frequency based on its internal control schemes, governed by a droop control
as implemented in this toolbox.
The GFM model implemented in the toolbox is a simplified yet functional representation
designed to capture these core behaviors. Following the toolbox’s hierarchical design,
the composite GFM component is decomposed into its essential subcomponents.

4.6
Modular Design Benefits and Subcomponent Flexibility
In the previous sections, the implementation approach for both simple algebraic com­
ponents (lines, transformers, loads) and complex dynamic components has been es­
tablished through consistent state­space representation. The decomposition of the GFL
converter into individual subcomponents ­ Power Controller, Current Controller, PLL and
GFLcore ­ demonstrates the toolbox’s modular architecture. Similarly, the GFM converter
was decomposed into its Droop Controller and GFMcore subcomponents. This structured
approach for both converter types reveals several key advantages for power system mod­
eling mentioned earlier in this thesis.
Independent Development and Modification
The modular design enables independent development and modifications of subcompo­
nents without affecting the broader system architecture. Each subcomponent operates
as a self­contained unit with clearly defined inputs, outputs and internal dynamics. This
isolation verifies the earlier claims that:
• Logic modifications can be implemented within a subcomponent without requiring
changes to other parts of the system.
• Performance improvements or bug fixes in one subcomponent automatically prop­
agate to all systems utilizing that component.
• Algorithm variations can be tested by simply swapping subcomponent implementa­
tions.
For example, if research identifies an improved PLL design with better disturbance rejec­
tion, the new algorithm can be implemented as a separate PLL subcomponent. As long as
the new PLL maintains the same input/output interface, it can be directly substituted into

The assemble() function orchestrates the creation of the unified state­space model by
combining subcomponent models and applying internal connections. During assembly,
the internal connections in internal_connections are resolved as symbolic substitutions,
effectively removing internally connected signals from the external input list and replacing
them with the appropriate output expressions from source subcomponents. In general
terms, the assemble() function does the following:
• Aggregation: Collect states, dynamics, outputs, inputs, and parameters from all
subcomponents.
• Naming: Apply the systematic naming transformations to ensure uniqueness.
• Connection: Resolve internal connections through symbolic substitution.
• Compilation: Generate the final unified state­space representation of the compo­
nent.
This function is central to the toolbox’s operation. Therefore, the following section will
provide a detailed explanation of how a component is assembled in code.
Phase 1: Aggregation and Collection
The assembly process begins by systematically collecting all mathematical elements from
the individual subcomponents. Each subcomponent contributes its states (x), state deriva­
tives (˙x), outputs (y), inputs (u), and parameters (p) to their respective aggregate collec­
tions. During this phase, duplicate elimination is performed for inputs and parameters to
prevent redundancy, while states and their derivatives are concatenated to preserve the
complete system dynamics.
This aggregation creates preliminary system­wide matrices that represent the mathemat­
ical foundation of the composite component. However, at this stage, all symbolic names
remain in their original, generic form as defined within each subcomponent.

The resolution of the internal connections represents the most complex aspect of the
assembly process. This phase handles the transformation of connections stored in the
internal_connections into actual mathematical substitutions that eliminate internal sig­

subcomponent. The source output expression, which may be a complex mathematical
relationship involving states and inputs, is then substituted for every occurrence of the
destination input symbol throughout the system equations.
The substitution of symbolic variables effectively ”wires” the subcomponents together at
the mathematical level. Internal signals that were previously treated as external inputs
to individual subcomponents are eliminated from the composite component’s external
interface, as they are now satisfied by internal connections.

load flow results are easily accessible at each node in the system, making it straight­

5.4
Newton­Raphson Method Implementation
This section details the practical implementation of the NR algorithm outlined in Chapter
3 section 3.6. The algorithm is implemented as a core method within the Networkx class
and designed to work with the MultiGraph data structure.
The first step is the systematic construction of the bus admittance matrix Y_bus. The
Networkx class contains a dedicated method _build_ybus which constructs this matrix
by traversing the MultiGraph structure representing the power system topology.
Given the network formulation as G(V, E) where V represents the set of buses and E
represents the set of connecting elements, the algorithm proceeds as follows:
1. Matrix Initialization and Indexing: The method first creates a zero­initialized com­
plex matrix of dimensions n × n, where n is the number of buses V in the system. A
bus indexing dictionary maps each bus identifier to its corresponding matrix index,
ensuring consistent access to matrix elements.

2. Series Component Processing: For each edge representing a series component
(transmission lines or transformers), the algorithm determines the component type

get_series_admittance(...)

as capacitive, its capacitance value is converted to admittance using Yshunt = jωC.

element in the admittance bus.

A good toolbox needs a good user interface. Even if everything works, it’s not very useful
if people can’t easily use the functionalities within the toolbox. A well­made user interface
lets engineers and researchers use all the toolbox’s features without needing to deeply
understand how the code works or how different parts connect.

of making this toolbox was to hide this complexity. This lets users set up, adjust, and
connect system parts easily. This way, users can focus on analyzing the power system
itself, instead of getting stuck on how the software is built.

PSCAD is an industry­standard software package known for simulating and analyzing

Validation 3: Linearization
The final and critical validation of the toolbox’s capabilities is to assess the correctness of
the linearization procedure. While the time­domain simulations, as demonstrated in the
previous sections, are essential for observing dynamic behavior during large transients,
they do not provide the same grade of small­signal stability analysis. The primary goal of
this validation is therefore to confirm that the symbolic, non­linear system model can be
accurately transformed into a linear state­space representation.
The functionality being tested is encapsulated within the linearize_model(eval_point)
method of the DynamicSimulator class, as detailed back in Section 3.5.1. This method
performs the symbolic differentiation of the ODEs and algebraic equations to compute
the state­space matrices (A, B, C, D), which describe the system’s behavior for small
deviations around a specific operating point.
For consistency, this validation uses the same dual GFL converter model from the pre­
ceding dynamic tests, seen in Figure 6.3. This information is also presented in Appendix
E. The system is first initialized to the same steady­state operating point validated in Ta­
ble 6.5, which is crucial as the accuracy of the linearized model is entirely dependent
on the accuracy of the equilibrium point. The evaluation point of the linearization is con­
structed by the toolbox as a dictionary containing the steady­state values for all symbolic
variables in the system, including the dynamic states, inputs, outputs and parameters.
These values are determined from the load flow solution and the initial conditions of each
component model.
One noteworthy difference in the configuration file, even though it is the exact same sys­
tem, is that line susceptance is no longer lumped to adjacent nodes. That means all nodes
are non­capacitive, and the system is no longer a ODE but instead a DAE.

Future Work
This thesis laid out the foundation for a useful Python toolbox to investigate power sys­
tems. In order to achieve the main goals of the thesis, only a few select components/sub­
components were added to the toolbox. This was to establish a functional concept of the
toolbox, leaving out room for future improvement. A key priority for future work should be
to address the specific modeling limitations identified during validation. For instance, the
GFM converter model, while dynamically stable, exhibited a persistent reactive power off­
set compared to the PSCAD benchmark and an incorrect initial voltage calculation when
serving as the system’s slack bus. Resolving these discrepancies is crucial for ensuring
high quality results. This effort would be part of a bigger expansion of the component
library, which would also include the addition of more advanced component models, such
as different converter control strategies, other renewable energy sources, and energy
storage systems.
While this thesis prioritized the development of core modeling and simulation function­
alities, features for comprehensive small­signal analysis were outside its scope. Future
work could integrate a thorough participation factor analysis to quantitatively link system
modes to physical state variables. Additionally, the toolbox’s modular design would fa­
cilitate the implementation of parameter sweep capabilities for control tuning, involving
iterative solving and simulation. These additions, though not part of the current develop­
ment, are well­accommodated by the toolbox’s architecture.
Another thing that was thought of for the toolbox was some addition of a graphical user
interface (GUI), or more advanced, interactive plotting tools to further enhance the tool­
box’s accessibility. The toolbox’s complexity could be significantly increased, but without
a proper user interface and result presentation, the toolbox would not stand out amongst
other similar programs.
Furthermore, certain aspects of the toolbox’s current implementation, while functional for
this initial iteration, could benefit from refactoring. These design choices, initially made to
facilitate rapid prototyping and achieve core objectives, may present challenges for long­
term maintainability and commercial scaling. The following suggestions highlight areas
for future restructuring that would enhance the toolbox’s robustness and extensibility.
7.1
Refactoring the String­Based Naming System
A core aspect of the current implementation is the use of string­based naming convention
to ensure that all symbolic variables (states, parameters, and inputs) are unique across the
system. This is handled by programmatically constructing names based on the convention
outlined earlier in the thesis.
While functional, this approach was observed to be fragile. The system’s integrity relies on
every part of the toolbox adhering to the exact same string format, making the code sensi­
tive to typos and very tedious to modify without having to manually update the naming logic
in multiple locations. An example of this is the prepare_linearization_eval_point()
function, which ended up containing some hardcoded checks for specific parameter names
to ensure correct mapping.
Development of a Python­Based Toolbox for Small­Signal Modeling and Stability Analysis of
Converter­Dominated Power Systems
89
==PAGE:104==
A future overhaul could replace the string­based system with something that relies on
direct object references. This could be made as a centralized SystemRegistry object,
making the toolbox more robust and less error­prone to missmatches in naming.
7.2
Decoupling Component Logic from System Builders
Another big design choice made in the toolbox was that the code would automatically
assemble a component from its subcomponents. In the end, the component is being as­
sembled from a manually defined template pattern that always connects certain subcom­
ponents to other subcomponents, no matter what specific version of that subcomponent
it is. This ended up working quite well for cases where components are always made with
the same amount of subcomponents, but comes with undefined behavior if any subcom­
ponent is left out.
To improve modularity, a recommendation would be to move the assembly logic from the
builder into the composite component class itself and rethink how internal connections
are being automatically made, being able to handle every component composition. This
would also allow the builder to remain generic, adhering to the philosophy of keeping all
toolbox elements contained and modular.
7.3
Better Integration with PowerNetLab
A major part of how the DyMo simulates systems comes down to a rather large amount
of manually written code to define recordings, setup plots, introduce system events etc.
These features are remnants of the previous PowerNetLab functionalities, and provided
a very good way to test the toolbox from the very beginning of the thesis. However, this
is cumbersome to setup and having to define your output recordings the way it’s currently
being done is not a very user­friendly experience. Something that would make the toolbox
even easier to use would be to extend the builders, and the configuration­based design,
to also automatically generate a text file to allow the user to define plots, system events
and recordings. This would further simplify the work­flow and make for a fantastic user
experience.

Conclusion
The global energy transition towards renewable resources is fundamentally reshaping
the landscape of electrical power systems, giving rise to converter­dominated grids with
unique stability challenges not present in conventional, synchronous machine­based net­
works. This paradigm shift has created a pressing need for accessible, flexible, and open­
source modeling tools that can accurately capture the complex dynamics of power elec­
tronic converters. This thesis was undertaken to address this need by contributing to the
development of such a tool.
The overarching goal of this project was to advance the development of a Python­based
toolbox for the modeling, simulation, and stability analysis of modern, converter domi­
nated power systems. A primary focus was the creation of a standardized procedure for
modularly composing system models, where complex components are built from a library
of interchangeable subcomponents. This objective was successfully realized through the
implementation of a three­layered architecture encompassing a network, component, and
subcomponent layer. This provides a flexible and extensible foundation for future devel­
opment.
Careful validation was essential to verify the correctness of this architecture. Through
systematic benchmarking against the industry­standard tool PSCAD, the toolbox’s design
was proven to be not only feasible but highly effective. The validation process confirmed
that the toolbox can produce results with a high degree of accuracy for static load flow,
non­linear time­domain dynamics, and linearized small­signal analysis. While a limitation
was identified in the GFM converter’s behavior, the overall results conclusively demon­
strated the successful implementation of the core functionalities, thereby achieving the
central aims of this thesis.
The toolbox was made usable through a unique and accessible configuration­driven in­
terface. This interface sought to separate the user from the complexity of composing
systems, making it possible to define, connect and work with systems in only a few lines
of code. Although still with a lot of room to improve, what was achieved in this work proved
functionality of the chosen method, and paved the way for further development.

Toolbox User Guide
This guide provides step­by­step instructions to help you get started with the toolbox. The
toolbox is built on the PowerNetLab framework, and this guide covers all the necessary
steps to interface with its core simulation and plotting functions. By following the hands­on
example, you’ll gain the foundational knowledge to begin using the toolbox.
F.1
Setting up the environment
Before you can run a simulation, you must configure your system to recognize the tool­
box’s code directory. Because the toolbox is built as a custom Python package, you need
to add its location to your system’s path. This allows Python to find and import the nec­
essary modules.
Follow these steps to set up the environment variable correctly.
Step 1: Set the Environment Variable
You need to tell your operating system where the PowerNetLab project directory is located.
The command below is for Windows users and should be run in the PowerShell terminal:
1
[Environment]::SetEnvironmentVariable('POWERNETLAB_PATH', 'C:\path\to\
PowerNetLab', 'User')
Listing F.1: Setting the POWERNETLAB_PATH Environment Variable
Make sure to replace the path with the actual, absolute path to the project folder on your
computer.
Step 2: Restart Your IDE
For the changes to the environment variable to take effect, you must close and reopen
your IDE.
Step 3: Add the Path in Your Python Script
Finally, in the beginning of any Python script you write to run a simulation, you must
include the following code. This dynamically adds the toolbox path to your script’s session,
ensuring that the import statement works correctly.
1
import os
2
import sys
3
4
# Get the environment variable you just set
5
powernetlab_path = os.environ.get('POWERNETLAB_PATH')

if powernetlab_path:
9

11
