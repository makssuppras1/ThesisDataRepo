Abstract 
This thesis concentrates on the optimization of façades by measuring their energy 
performance under high impactful and probable scenarios. More specifically, six future 
scenarios influencing the design of components are investigated, including climate change (A2 
2020, 2050 & 2080) and building typology (apartment) change. These different scenarios are 
simulated in IDA ICE software under Danish climate, after creating a baseline office building 
that is used for comparison. In total 1152 design iterations were tested, each one with six 
parameter variations. This includes variations in terms of the number of glazings, WWR, 
window shading control strategies and external wall insulation width. The research aims of this 
project are to identify which component sets are the most optimal and flexible for each scenario 
and if we prepare our envelope for future scenarios in the present time, how much extra energy 
will we spend in the meantime and what are the implications of this decision.  
General results showed that the different scenarios significantly alter the energy frame of 
the building, as heating demand tends to minimize (in the office scenarios) and cooling demand 
is significantly increased. Parametric runs demonstrated that 700mm insulation along with 57% 
WWR and solar shading strategy came up with the best results for the office's baseline, 2020 
and 2050 scenarios and slim/medium insulation (300&500mm) and 57% WWR for the 2080 
case. For the typology change scenarios, 300 & 500mm insulation width along with 32% WWR 
created the lowest energy results. Also, Run-10012/10016/10020 component sets are the most 
flexible for the office scenarios, Run-10060/10064/10068 for the typology conversion cases 
and for all scenarios combined Run-10012/10016/10020 has the best results according to the 
Energy Flexibility Index. Since the office buildings consumed 12.3 kWh/year of excess energy 
than the best components would have consumed, then preparing our buildings with the most 
flexible components is considered reasonable because the excess energy spent is not 
significantly higher and this little extra energy is outweighed by the other benefits of flexibility 
(less cost, less environmental impact etc). Overall, it is concluded that using optimization tools 
that take into account future changes can be highly beneficial for the overall performance across 
the whole life span of the building.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Problem statement & Motivation 
The building and construction sector remains one of the highest greenhouse gas emitting 
fields globally; despite the imperative to decarbonize the industry by 2050 to meet the Paris 
Agreement targets [1] according to EU Commission, it still contributes about 40% of the total 
energy and over 33% of the energy-related CO2 emissions [2]. 
In general, buildings are constructed to serve a particular purpose and are either torn down 
or extensively renovated once they become redundant [3].  In some countries, even relatively 
young buildings (15-30 years) are demolished when they do not fulfil their users or 
performance needs. Both of these practices though, present certain drawbacks as extensive 
building renovations have significant expenses and material consumption [4]. Moreover, 
constructing new buildings is considerably more harmful to the climate compared to 
renovation. This is because new construction involves a higher volume of materials. Research 
[5] indicates that new construction can be up to fifty times more detrimental to the climate than 
renovation, provided that the building can be used for the same purpose [6]. 
Given that a large portion of EU buildings is over 50 years old and lack energy-efficient 
upgrades, refurbishments are necessary. The majority of these buildings fall short of current 
energy standards due to poor insulation and outdated systems. To address this, renovations may 
involve substantial changes to the building fabric, highlighting the importance of designing 
flexible buildings, which is their ability to adapt to the new context without having to make 
significant fabric changes or spend a lot of money [7]. 
With regards to the climate, according to the Fifth Assessment Report (AR5) of the 
Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), global warming is undeniable and has led 
to extensive effects on both the environment and human society [8]. Globally, the most recent 
report from the World Meteorological Organization confirms that 2016 was the hottest year on 
record, with temperatures rising by an unexpected 1.1°C above pre-industrial levels and 0.06°C 
higher than the previous record set in 2015. As GHG concentrations keep increasing, we can 
anticipate future climate changes that will surpass those already observed and attributed to 
human activities [9]. The anticipated rise in air temperature will extend the duration during 
which shading is needed and promote space cooling as a major challenge in the building 
industry. Therefore, design solutions aimed at reducing buildings' cooling demands, such as 
passive cooling strategies, will become critically important [10]. Moreover, a system designed 
to provide self-shading during the original cooling season while allowing solar radiation into 
the building during the heating season, would become less effective as the climate warms. 
Likewise, a static shading device optimized for current conditions might fail to offer adequate 
shading under changing climate scenarios [11].

Since building performance is strongly interconnected to weather conditions, there is a 
bidirectional relationship between building performance and climate change. In this context, 
implementing appropriate building design strategies that consider the changes to the external 
and internal environment in the future and provide flexibility, should be prioritized [12].  
Besides that, due to the growing urban population and rapid urbanization, projections 
suggest that in the next 40 years, the world will need to construct more urban capacity than it 
has in the previous four thousand years. This expansion will occur in order to accommodate 
the rising proportion of the global population living in urban areas, expected to increase from 
55% in 2018 (around 4.2 billion people) to 68% (6 billion people) by 2050 [13][14]. 
However, just adding more housing to address the urbanization issue isn't sufficient if those 
homes quickly become obsolete. The solution to the supply and demand problem lies in 
creating buildings that are flexible enough to accommodate new demands on the built 
environment, including challenges like climate change, shifting demographics, emerging 
technological requirements, an ageing population and changing work patterns such as remote 
work and online learning [15].  
In response to this, the European Union has implemented various policy measures like the 
EU Taxonomy, that encourage circular economy (CE) principles and evolve the building 
regulations in the construction environment in order to achieve a zero-emission and fully 
decarbonized building stock by 2050 [16].

1.2 Scope 
This study aims to optimize the performance of façades under highly probable and impactful 
future changes, such as climate change (A2 2020, 2050 & 2080 scenarios) and building 
typology conversion under Danish climatic conditions in Kastrup, Copenhagen. Subsequently, 
the impact of various façade components on energy (heating and cooling demand) and lighting 
consumption is investigated. The typology conversion concerns the transformation from office 
to apartment and the time frame of the investigation starts from 2009 (present time) until 2100. 
Furthermore, the data are obtained from the results of the IDA ICE parametric runs in kWh. 
Afterwards, the study intends to measure the flexibility of the components based on the 
comparison with the baseline model by utilizing a method published in 2023 by Carlucci F. et 
al. called “Energy flexibility of building systems in future scenarios”. 
It is important to mention here that this research thesis does not examine flexible facades 
under the advanced technology spectrum (phase change materials, kinetic shading systems, 
adaptive insulation etc.) but rather concentrates on the scope of purposefully designing them 
to be flexible by installing specific components that can adapt to functional or environmental 
changes in the future (e.g. a building that later can be easily refurbished into another typology 
and/or constructing it with components that are capable to perform under higher temperatures 
etc.). 
 
1.3 Research questions and objectives 
This research thesis will try to answer the following questions: 
• 
How do the changes in the weather and building typology influence the energy frame and 
selection of facade components?  
• 
Which façade components are the optimal solutions for each future scenario? 
• Which of these solutions is the most flexible for future scenarios? 
• Should we prepare or not for future scenarios in the built environment and why is this 
decision considered good or bad?  
• 
If we prepare for the worst-case future scenario how much extra energy will we spend for 
that by installing flexible components? 
2. Theoretical background & Literature review 
2.1 Definition of building flexibility and main principles 
“A sustainable building is not one that must last forever, but one that can easily adapt to 
change.” – Peter Graham, Environment Design Guide, 2006, Royal Institute of Australian 
Architects 
Different definitions of flexibility are present in the research literature, varying based on 
building type and the focus of each study. This difference is especially evident when specific 
attributes or elements of buildings are under investigation. Terms like versatility, convertibility, 
and adaptability are commonly used to describe flexibility, all highlighting the capacity to 
accommodate change. These definitions clarify the importance of flexibility in design and 
advocate for its integration into building practices [17].  
At the construction industry level, a building is deemed flexible if its structure or 
components can accommodate climatic, cultural, social and economic changes (context) in 
both the short and long term. These adjustments can be made by the user or owner of the

building without the need for extensive demolition or reconstruction, providing value in the 
long run [18]. 
Schmidt III and Austin, argue that many researchers refer to building flexibility and 
adaptability interchangeably without having a clear distinction and often in abstract contexts. 
They believe that this distinction only adds to the confusion of the subject and for that reason 
they conceive these two attributes as the same [19]. Even so, the general perception of 
flexibility in the literature is a way to achieve a certain level of adaptation [20].

2.3 Historical facts 
Rybczynski (1987) notes that in Europe, the concept of rooms dedicated to specific uses 
emerged only in the last three centuries. Traditional vernacular housing adapted spaces to 
address various needs, ranging from single multi-functional rooms for family rituals to clusters 
of individual huts surrounding a courtyard, offering flexibility. Similarly, traditional Japanese 
houses were designed for adaptability, with rooms easily converted for different purposes, 
utilizing tatami mats and sliding walls for modular layouts. Moreover, the traditional home 
contains no load-bearing walls, enabling easy changes to the post and beam [23]. 
In modern times, three main factors have driven the development of flexible housing. Firstly, 
in the 1920s, European social housing programs necessitated mass housing solutions, 
prompting architects to design flexibly to accommodate varying needs despite reduced space 
standards. Secondly, from the 1930s onwards, there was a belief in the potential of 
prefabrication and emerging technologies to provide mass housing, with flexibility seen as 
inherent in industrialized building methods. Thirdly, the emphasis on user participation in the 
1960s and 1970s reignited interest in flexible housing to offer users more choice and 
involvement in their living spaces [15].  
 
2.3.1 First episode – Modernity and the minimal dwelling 
After World War I, European nations faced an unprecedented need for urban housing, 
especially for the working class. To meet this demand economically, space standards were 
significantly lowered and became regulated by legislation, capturing the attention of designers 
across Europe. With less space available, architects focused on maximizing efficiency and 
flexibility in design. This resulted in the development of new housing plan types, many of 
which incorporated flexible elements. Internal variability of dwellings emerged as a crucial 
aspect of this effort, driven by the goal of making minimum-sized apartments as comfortable 
and cheap as possible [15].

2.3.2 Second episode – The Industrialization of Housing 
The second episode is primarily influenced by technological advancements, particularly the 
adoption of industrialized methods for housing provision. As technical capabilities expanded 
and housing demand surged in the early 20th century, interest in standardized housing 
production grew. Architects, faced with the post-World War I housing crisis, began devising 
designs for residential dwellings suitable for mass production through industrial prefabrication. 
Starting in 1914, Le Corbusier who was a strong advocate of factory production, envisioned 
projects like Maison Domino (1914), Maison Citrohan (1922), and later Maisons Loucheur 
(1928) for assembly line manufacturing. More specifically for Maison Domino, plan libre 
provides maximum flexibility for both the user and architect in designing living spaces by 
entirely separating the structure from other elements. Without the need for walls to serve as 
structural supports, they can be placed anywhere according to functional needs or spatial design 
ideas [19]. Standardized components allowed for future adaptability, enabling easy 
replacement or addition of elements. Flexibility in many prefabricated systems relies on 
components that can be arranged in potentially infinite configurations, providing designers 
with significant planning advantages [15].  
2.3.3 Third episode – Participation and user choice 
In 1961, N. Jahn Habraken introduced the concept of separating building elements into 
"Support" and "Infill." He proposed that in residential construction and design, the 'support' or 
base building should be distinct from the 'infill' or interior fit-out. The support structure, 
according to Habraken, enables the construction, modification, and removal of dwellings 
independently. Supports are permanent and part of the public domain, while infill belongs to 
individual owners/users and is changeable, aiming to prioritize user participation and freedom 
of choice [15].  
Open Buildings seeks to cater to user’s preferences by offering the flexibility needed for the 
adaptation of individual units over time. The design should facilitate the installation or 
modification of sub-systems with minimal interface problems, thus reshaping ownership, 
design responsibilities, and building engineering while promoting participation, co-creation, 
and user autonomy [24]. However, this approach may limit flexibility and adaptability at the 
infill level only [25].  
In summary, Open Building encompasses various related ideas concerning the construction 
of the built environment, with Habraken using five levels of physical systems to analyze 
different projects [25].  
 
2.4 Shearing layers theory 
An important approach to flexible construction involves conceiving the building not as a 
single entity, but as comprised of distinct layers. This concept recognizes that building elements 
have varying lifespans and should be constructed distinctly. The term was founded by Frank 
Duffy, who emphasized evaluating buildings not only in material terms but also in terms of 
time [26]. Duffy, focusing primarily on the office sector, introduces the notion of 'sets' to 
describe the regular changes in furniture and equipment within buildings. He argues that this 
reconceptualization of buildings alters our understanding of initial and ongoing costs. Building 
upon Duffy's idea, Brand (1994) conceives these layers as a series of 'shearing' layers that 
evolve at different rates [27]. The more interconnected the layers, the greater the challenge and 
expense of adaptation, suggesting that design should prioritize slow-changing or hard to change

7 
 
components, such as structure restrains the facade, which in turn influences the services. Blyth 
and Worthington (2001) expanded Duffy's model by incorporating additional layers such as 
skin, site, and systems [28].

2.5 Flexibility metrics and assessment 
Assessing the flexibility of a building or its components is crucial for decision-making in 
the construction industry, in the process towards adaptable projects. There are several metrics 
in the literature which assess the flexibility/adaptability of a building. Most of them were 
conceived in the last fifteen years and have a weighing score (DGNB, FLEX 4.0, AdaptSTAR 
etc). In general, there are two main categories regarding flexibility assessment. The first one 
examines the transformation potential (change its function through adaptive reuse like 
AdaptSTAR) and the other category examines the transformation capacity (its ability to 
incorporate future changes like FLEX 4.0) [29]. For this project, a relatively new method will 
be applied for the measuring of flexibility in components.  
Carlucci et al. (2023) created a new approach to consider future changes by developing a 
flexibility metric [30]. The metric can be used to compare the flexibility of different building 
components with regard to the future scenarios analysed. In short, the assessment includes 
potential future scenarios that strongly affect the chosen evaluation parameter and are likely to 
happen. It simulates all possible combinations of these scenarios for all components that need 
to be compared, using a baseline scenario for normalization. This allows testing the 
performance spread independently from each component's general performance. Flexibility is 
then classified into six categories based on the statistical distribution of performance across all

8 
 
simulated scenarios, with Class 1 being the most flexible and Class 6 the least. The goal is to 
visualize how results vary around zero. Furthermore, by using the average of the normalized 
data, a single number index is achieved for comparison. While the paper utilized the metric to 
assess the energy performance of a dynamic internal curtain in the paper, in theory, it can be 
applied to various performance measures and components [31].

Generally, the aforementioned flexibility metric calculates the spread of the different 
scenario simulation results, indicating the stability of component performance under diverse 
scenarios. This evaluation is independent of the overall performance of individual components. 
For instance, even if a component consumes significant energy, if its consumption remains 
consistent across all scenarios, it will be considered highly flexible. Thus, flexibility should be 
considered alongside other metrics to assess various design solutions, as is common practice 
in most quality assessment tools. For example, even though a design is highly flexible, it may 
use much more energy than another solution, and thus have a worse performance overall [31].  
2.6 Flexibility and sustainability 
A flexible building can save energy and money by minimizing refurbishments, operational 
costs, construction materials, and pollution while providing greater comfort to users [20]. 
Additionally, flexible construction extends a building's useful life by enabling changes while 
retaining a significant part of the building, particularly the structural parts. This contributes to 
sustainability by avoiding premature demolition and preserving material value and embodied 
energy, thereby reducing the need for new material production. This approach regarding 
building structure holds promise for reducing CO2 emissions throughout the building's 
lifecycle. Studies indicate that extending a building's lifespan from 50 to 100 or 150 years can 
result in significant reductions in CO2 emissions and embodied energy, with potential 
reductions of up to 16% after 100 years and 29% after 150 years [32].  
Moreover, it has the potential to significantly alter the typology and use of both new and 
existing buildings. Andersen and Negendahl demonstrated through an analysis of demolition 
data from over 100,000 buildings that building typology has a substantial impact on their 
average lifespan. For instance, new office buildings typically last 41 years, whereas 
multifamily housing lasts about 168 years. Therefore, a building designed for flexible use could 
theoretically endure at least as long as the most durable typology it encompasses. Extending 
the lifespan of buildings has a major impact on life cycle effects, such as global warming 
potential and overall life cycle costs [32]. For these reasons, the concept of altering building 
typology is a key focus of this thesis [30].

2.7.1 Aspects that influence flexibility 
Israelsson and Hansson (2009) [34] identified key factors contributing to flexibility and 
examined their influence on adaptability. They categorized these factors into two groups: hard 
aspects, which encompass material standards, production, and installations directly related to 
the building, and soft aspects, including awareness, finance, and future planning, which are not 
directly tied to the physical structure but are deemed more crucial. 
Cellucci and Di Sivo (2015) emphasized the strategies that are essential for achieving 
building flexibility and categorized flexibility design into four domains: user, functional, 
physical, and procedural (Table 4) [35]. 
Živković and Jovanović (2012) analyzed flat projects to distinguish between modifiable and 
unmodifiable components, recognizing the impact of design on achieving an optimal solution. 
Unmodifiable parts include access (communications), supply elements (installations), 
envelope, building structure (columns, beams, slabs etc) and location, while changeable parts 
are spatial attributes with ample interpretation (Table 3). Factors affecting flexibility were 
deduced from flat analyses, including interior space freedom, multifunctionality, and room size 
adjustments. Various layout arrangements were presented to optimize design, with orientation 
and access per façade influencing adequacy [36].

2.7.2 Flexibility attributes of crucial importance 
The most important things to consider before designing a flexible building are the site, 
height and structure, as they are difficult or impossible to change after its construction. There 
are also other building systems, such as facade or roof components that can be modified after 
some time, but usually, it is a costly process, which means that they should be carefully 
designed beforehand to be more flexible. Another important factor to take into account is that 
the building should be constructed in different layers according to Brand’s theory. The 
systems/layers should be separable and not interact with each other because this would 
significantly increase the time, cost and difficulty of the renovations in the future. Modular 
construction, standardized or demountable components (e.g. demountable facades) can also 
assist towards the increase of convertibility and adjustability of the building's components [19]. 
For these reasons, the scope of this thesis project focuses on the building façade. 
Arguably, one of the most important sustainability factors is the longevity of the buildings. 
Bearing in mind Brand’s theory and the Open Buildings Movement, flexibility can be 
conceived as a major contributor to the increase of buildings’ lifespan. While no data on this 
statement are directly provided, the reasoning for this hypothesis is built upon an assumption, 
that the more flexible a structure is, the better it can accommodate future changes. In that sense, 
the most important flexibility attributes are the ones that can increase the total lifespan of the 
parts that are harder or slower to change. Some strategies towards this approach are briefly 
stated in Table 5. These can be the over-dimensioning of the total space (floor area) and height, 
maximising structural load and technical installation capacity and creating adequate daylight 
conditions for different building uses [18].

2.7.3 Case study 
Next21 is an innovative 18-unit housing project initiated by Osaka Gas Company in 
collaboration with the Next21 planning team. It integrates the Two Step Supply System and the 
Century Housing System, incorporating advanced building technologies and several key 
sustainability experiments, such as energy conservation, recycling, and the creation of urban 
green spaces. Notably, it is the first OB project where a single architect designed the structural 
framework, while various architects designed the individual units. 
Each unit’s layout, both interior and exterior, was freely designed within a coordinated 
system for element positioning. The generous floor-to-floor height allowed for utility 
distribution space above ceilings and under raised floors, enabling ducts and pipes to be routed 
==PAGE:20==
 
 
13 
 
independently of structural elements. Main beams were designed with reduced depth at the 
midspan, allowing ducts and pipes to pass over without using sleeves. 
The building's frame (skeleton), exterior cladding, interior finishes, and mechanical systems 
(infill) were designed following CHS principles, treating them as independent subsystems with 
different cycles for repair, upgrade, and replacement. The design of the 18 units began after the 
skeleton design and continued during its construction. The units and their mechanical systems 
were designed before the base building’s mechanical system. Mechanical services at all levels 
were later installed by a single contractor. 
Next21 was constructed as a unified entity but designed to allow its various subsystems to 
be adjusted independently. To test this, one fourth-floor unit underwent substantial renovation 
with all work conducted from within the unit using hanging scaffolding, minimizing disruption 
to neighbouring residents. A significant percentage of the removed materials, particularly from 
the facade, were successfully repurposed. The project continues to explore new methods for 
urban housing and experimental infill systems to accommodate diverse lifestyles while 
reducing energy consumption [37].

2.8 Optimization 
Optimization is a technique for identifying the best scenario that achieves the highest 
performance within specific constraints and variables. In the context of Building Performance 
Simulation (BPS), however, optimization typically refers to an automated process relying 
entirely on numerical simulation and mathematical optimization [38]. By combining BPS with 
optimization methods, one can develop a process to choose the optimal solutions from a range 
of alternatives for a given design problem, based on a set of performance criteria [39]. 
Building performance optimization (BPO) typically involves addressing multiple 
conflicting objectives, making it a multi-objective optimization problem. Two common 
methods exist to tackle this issue. The first method involves normalizing and summing the 
values of each objective with assigned weighting factors, thereby converting the multi-
objective problem into a mono-objective one [40][41]. This approach works well when the 
optimization goals are clear and the weighting factors are appropriately chosen, even though it 
does not explicitly show the relationship between objectives. The second method is the Pareto-
dominance approach, which generates a set of nondominant optimal solutions known as the 
Pareto frontier. Decision-makers can then select the most suitable solution from this set. This 
approach is considered more practical in building research and is therefore the most widely 
used method in BPO studies [42][43][44]. 
 
2.9 Optimization of façade components for future climate scenarios  
Carclucci et al. (2023) examined the flexibility and behaviour of a high-performance 
dynamic internal curtain of a pavilion located in the Danish climate [30]. This was done by 
forming a matrix of possible future scenarios, with the most prominent being climate change. 
Several different control strategies for the curtain were created to compare which is the best 
for future contextual or environmental changes. As mentioned before, this study also proposed 
a new method for flexibility assessment of components, the Energy Flexibility Index (EFI). 
The findings emphasize that the optimized control strategy (C3) achieves greater energy 
consumption reduction compared to the other strategies analyzed (C1, C2) across all scenarios. 
This confirms that a well-controlled responsive envelope can adapt effectively in both 
==PAGE:22==
 
 
15 
 
short/mid-term scenarios (days and seasons) and long-term scenarios (decades), enhancing 
energy performance throughout the building's lifespan. 
Glassman et al. (2013) optimized various facade variables (window-to-wall ratio, absence 
of shading, fixed overhang width, and insulation thickness) to identify the best solution for 
achieving a favourable energy balance [11]. The study further examined how this optimized 
solution might evolve under future climate scenarios for the years 2020, 2050, and 2080 as well 
as long-term periods (2010-2080). The optimization was defined in two ways: the lowest cost 
solution over a ten-year period and the solution that generated the least carbon emissions over 
the same period. The results showed that cost optimization tended to implement energy-
conserving measures more widely as energy prices increased, even if the climate moderated 
energy use from the current baseline. In contrast, carbon optimization showed less variation 
but was more directly indicative of climate changes. Generally, carbon-optimized solutions 
resulted in lower annual energy use than cost-optimized ones. Over longer periods, cost and 
carbon optimizations began to converge, especially as energy prices increased. Long-term 
optimizations justified larger initial investments in facade elements due to aggregated savings 
over time. For instance, window-to-wall ratios were generally larger, as the long-term benefits 
of daylighting outweighed the initial costs. Carbon optimizations required higher initial 
investments than cost optimizations. While some of the costs were offset by annual energy 
savings, the full return on investment was not always achieved within a ten-year period. 
Costanzo et al. (2022) examined three passive cooling solutions—external roller shade, 
electrochromic glazing, and internally ventilated shading—to reduce cooling demand under 
future climate conditions projected for 2070 and 2080, using various climate morphing 
techniques [10]. The simulations, conducted in IDA ICE for an office building in a Swedish 
climate, revealed an increase in cooling demand from 3 to 24 kWh/m² and a 20-50% reduction 
in heating usage over the next 50-60 years. The different climate morphing methods highlighted 
inherent uncertainties in future evaluations, despite showing similar weather patterns. 
Enhancements in solar and optical properties led to a 10-16% reduction in cooling and 
ventilation usage. Electrochromic technology exhibited the lowest cooling demand, with a 
reduction of up to 24%, while internally ventilated shading outperformed the other solutions 
with a 4-9% lower annual energy consumption and the lowest life cycle cost (LCC).  
Zou et al. (2021) performed a multi objective optimization of building variables considering 
the effects of long-term climate change (RCP 4.5 & 8.5) in a hot and humid area in China. The 
results argue that optimizing the building performance with future climate change in mind 
significantly increases outcomes compared to optimization based solely on historical TMY 
data. Specifically, winter discomfort hours decrease by 7.4% under RCP 4.5 and 13.3% under 
RCP 8.5 when future climate changes are considered. This demonstrates that ignoring climate 
change in building performance optimization can lead to considerable uncertainties. The 
proposed method effectively improves building performance in a changing climate [12]. 
3. Research methodology 
This research thesis will investigate the energy performance of building façade components 
in whole-year simulations under future scenarios, such as climate change and building typology 
conversion. The parameters are adjusted to test the scenarios separately (climate change 
scenarios only) or both of them occurring at the same time (climate change and building 
conversion). Several façade components of different characteristics will be implemented in the 
simulation program that represent different design choices, as well as renovation considerations 
==PAGE:23==
 
 
16 
 
for the future. The Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) to be measured and compared are the 
yearly heating, cooling and lighting demand in kWh.  
Initially, a baseline model of an office is developed to examine how different design 
variations affect energy usage. After that, parametric runs will be conducted to examine which 
components present the best performance results for each scenario. In the end, the flexibility 
of the most optimal facade components will be measured, and the results will be compared and 
ranked to the designated flexibility class according to their NECV distribution around zero. 
Each design variation is given a classification based on its energy performance using the 
baseline scenario as a denominator for comparison. After the parametric runs, the most flexible 
and inflexible design solutions are specified and compared relative to their classification. 
Lastly, the tendencies of the design solutions will be discussed. 
 
3.1 Software 
This study is conducted on IDA ICE 5.0, which is a building simulation software from 
EQUA. IDA ICE software is validated by numerous official institutions and organizations like 
ASHRAE 140, 2004, CEN Standard EN 15255 and 15265, 2007, CEN Standard EN 1379 [45]. 
Its main function is to perform dynamic simulations that can predict and analyze the 
performance of the buildings. IDA ICE creates a mathematical model where a simultaneous 
dynamic simulation of air flow and heat transfer is provided. Apart from the simulations, the 
users can create custom control macros for different components and the program can perform 
multi-objective or mono-objective optimizations via parametric runs.  
3.2 Boundary conditions 
The simulation will examine the building model without taking into account any contextual 
changes outside or around the model, for example, new building construction, demolitions, 
planting of trees etc. These changes would have slightly affected the KPIs of the simulations, 
but they will be ignored, as they are not in the scope of this project.  
3.3 Weather file & Location 
The location of the simulation takes place in Kastrup, Copenhagen, Denmark. It has 
temperate coastal climate characteristics with cool to cold winters and mild summers. The 
latitude of the location is 55,61 N and the longitude 12,65 E. Because of Denmark's northern 
location there are rather small temperature differences between summer and winter and low to 
medium access to daylight and solar radiation on an annual basis.  
The software must load a weather file in EPW format, which contains hourly weather 
information for a typical year at the chosen location. This format is text-based and uses comma-
separated data. The data includes parameters that identify the specific location, such as the 
name, data source, latitude, longitude, time zone, elevation, peak design conditions, holidays, 
and daylight-saving period. It also includes meteorological variables such as dry bulb and dew 
point temperatures, relative humidity, station pressure, various types of solar radiation (global, 
extraterrestrial, horizontal infrared, direct, and diffuse), illuminance, wind direction and speed, 
and sky cover. All data is provided in SI units. 
In this work two types of weather files are used for the simulations: i) the weather files 
available in the IDA ICE database for the “current climate” simulations and ii) the “climate 
change” weather files for the “future climate” simulations [46].

3.4 Scenario Analysis 
3.4.1 Climate change 
The climate change scenario is examined using anticipated weather files for the years 2020, 
2050 and 2080. These future weather files were created with the 'Climate Change World 
Weather Generator' tool developed by the University of Southampton. This tool transforms a 
typical meteorological year (TMY) baseline climate file, which contains the present hourly 
data, into EPW files with mean monthly values for the specified future years, incorporating the 
expected impacts of climate change.  More specifically, the time slices from which the files are 
produced are the following: 2010-2039 (‘2020s’), 2040-2069 (‘2050s’) and 2070-2099 
(‘2080s’) relative to the baseline period (1961-1990) [47] [9]. The morphing process is based 
on the HadCM3 global circulation model, utilized in IPCC assessment reports. The IPCC's 
Special Report on Emissions Scenarios (SRES) identifies six climate change scenario families: 
A1Fl, A1B, A1T, A2, B1, and B2. For this study, the weather file morpher uses the medium-
high emissions A2 scenario by default. It is a “business as usual” scenario with rapid population 
growth, high energy consumption and regionally oriented economic growth [11]. The baseline 
scenario weather file is assumed to be on the year 2009, one year before the first period which 
the morphing procedure used to produce the weather files.

3.4.2 Conversion of typology 
The continuous influx of people over the years towards the cities will require several 
building typologies to be transformed into houses or apartment complexes in the future 
(adaptive reuse). The reuse of buildings is gaining popularity as it is viewed as one of the most 
promising strategies in circular construction practices. Alterations in building use [48] can 
affect the building energy behaviour. For that reason, the second scenario will examine the 
conversion of the office zone to a small studio apartment. 
It is evident that the two typology variations have different occupant densities. BR18 [49] 
states that 20 m² is the minimum gross area for an apartment per person and for the office case 
EN16798-1 [50] suggests 10-15 m²/person but a more realistic value is between 5-7 m² per 
person. 
The change in building typology is simulated by altering the occupancy, internal gains and 
schedule of the zones. More specifically, it is assumed that the occupant will stay in the house 
after working hours from 17:30 until 07:30 the next morning. Activity levels will be changed 
during these hours, from resting to sleeping. During the weekend, it is assumed that the user 
remains in the house all day long. More details about the changes in internal gains and 
occupancy are presented in unit 3.8.

3.5 Building model: Geometry & Orientation 
The building model represents a simplified rectangular office building which is located on 
the outskirts of Copenhagen. It consists of six small office zones (three on each side) and a 
hallway in the middle. Each office zone has a typical-sized window with no external shading 
system and also a flat roof geometry is assumed. In the hallway, there is an entrance door on 
the western side which is assumed to remain closed and a small window on the other side of it. 
The zones (Fig. 14) form a three-person office (5m × 4m × 2.7m) measuring 20 m², located on 
the south and northern sides of the building. The total area of the building is 152.8 m². 
According to Raftery et al. (2011), combining multiple small zones into larger ones is 
disadvantageous, as it results in less accurate simulations. This is because it can ignore the fact 
that a zone may have a heating requirement while the zone next to it has a cooling requirement. 
Furthermore, it prevents the creation of precise user-profiles and internal load calculations, 
which significantly affect the energy demands and indoor climate of a zone [51].

Similarly, solar shading and regulation of façade transmission is one of the most important 
design decisions of flexible buildings as the cooling demand is projected to increase in the next 
decades. By managing solar gains, shading systems directly impact a building's energy balance, 
utilizing passive solar energy while preventing indoor overheating. Shading systems also 
control natural daylight, reduce glare, and enable views to outside. Their dynamic nature allows 
designers to adapt to changing sunlight conditions, avoiding compromises in window-to-wall 
ratios typical of conventional facades [53][54]. 
Fixed shading systems, however, have limited ability to respond to daily or seasonal 
environmental changes and may perform poorly if the operating conditions shift. Non-flexible

facades that maximize transparency can lead to occupant adjustments due to glare or 
overheating, ultimately reducing long-term comfort and energy savings [55]. Therefore, a 
conventional window might be enough to allow daylight into a space, but providing more 
natural light into the deep dimensions of the space requires more advanced design alternatives 
[56]. 
The shading system for all the variations is a horizontal type Venetian blind with 45-degree 
slat angle and 100mm spacing between each slat. Generic aluminium with thermal conductivity 
λ of 159 W/(mK) and longwave surface emissivity of 0.9 has been used as slat material. 
The Venetian blind is designed to be drawn under specific control strategies, such as when 
the solar radiation level exceeds 100 W/m². Furthermore, for the office case, Schedule 1 draws 
the blinds only during the period from the 1st of May to the 30th of September and within 
working hours (08:00-17:00h). Regarding the apartment case, the blinds are programmed to 
operate within the same month period but only during 10:00-19:00h, when there is typically 
higher outside temperature and more overheating. Number 1.0 in the Schedules represents a 
drawn blind, while 0 represents a blind which is not drawn. Also, since the cooling period of

cooling demand. These different shading control strategies are either simulated separately or

3.10 Ideal heater and cooler  
Ideal heaters and coolers in a model are not tied to any specific physical location or type of 
installation system. They are defined solely by their capability to deliver the required heating 
or cooling to reach the designated temperature setpoints [45]. Consequently, the calculated 
heating and cooling energy represents the demand assuming that it can be fulfilled, without 
specifying how this should be achieved. For example, the required cooling could be supplied 
through a combination of mechanical cooling methods. Furthermore, no air handling unit 
(AHU) or domestic hot water are considered in this study to make the comparison process 
easier. 
There are twelve ideal loads systems in total in the building, six ideal heating and six ideal 
cooling systems, which means that there are two systems in each zone. The ideal heater and 
cooling capacity of the zone were set to 5000W to have enough capacity to fulfil the setpoints. 
They are both electrical systems with 1.0 (heating) and 3.0 (cooling) COP respectively. Last 
but not least, the heating set point of the system for the whole year was set to 21 C and 24.5 C 
for the cooling setpoint, so that it is activated just before it reaches the overheating threshold 
of 25 C. When the office is not occupied, the heating setpoint is lowered to 18.0C in order to 
conserve heating energy (night setback) and the same applies to the apartment when it is not 
occupied.

An important part of the scope of this project is to investigate how the aforementioned future

 In the next section, the energy consumption resulting from various chosen scenarios is

The absence of the shading system in the baseline model presents some heat gain benefits 
at least for the winter, since Denmark has a long heating period, thus reducing the overall 
heating consumption. The heating demand between the apartment and the office baseline of the 
current year varies for the most part. Even though the apartment is occupied for more hours 
than the office, more people operate in the office case and the activity levels of the occupants 
are different. To be more specific, in the apartment case the occupant is assumed to be in rest

variation. The cooling results in 2020, 2050 and 2080 office cases are significantly increased

considerably important factor. In comparison with the baseline model, in 2020, 2050 and 2080

heating demand is steadily decreasing over the different climate change variations, except in 
the apartment case where it significantly decreases after the 2020 time slice period.

scenarios. It is only reduced by 28% in the typology change scenario, perhaps due to the lower 
power consumption of the lamps (10W instead of 40W) and due to the lower dimmable daylight 
sensor setpoint (200 lux). 
 From the presented diagrams, it can be concluded that the cooling and heating consumption 
of a building can change drastically under different future conditions. As also mentioned in the 
introduction, it is evident that cooling demand could become one of the highest influential 
factors in the future design of buildings. Likewise, the typology change scenario will likely be 
the most influential factor regarding heating consumption.

of having a dominant heating period throughout the year is to minimize the usage of heating 
systems. On the contrary, the mean air temperature and solar radiation in the furthest time slice

large window area combined with a high solar heat gain suggests that utilizing natural heating 
can be advantageous.

the only exception being the shading control strategy, where the most effective strategy is the

prevalent in the apartment case because it has higher internal gains and thus there is a bigger 
need for thermal dissipation while at the same time keeping the house as warm as possible 
during the winter.

4.4 Flexibility assessment 
The approach is inspired by a method developed by K. Negendahl, F. Carlucci and F. Fiorito, 
which proposes a technique to measure the energy flexibility of a shading system. The 
theoretical framework of the developed method is outlined in Chapter 2.5. 
Since this project deals with two key performance indicators that are often in conflict with 
each other, then the optimization process needs to find a balance that provides an acceptable 
compromise between the two. For example, strategies to reduce cooling load (e.g., increasing 
insulation) might inadvertently increase the heating load, and vice versa. 
To investigate and plot the most efficient designs a Pareto frontier is drawn where possible. 
A Pareto frontier allows the engineer to restrict attention to the set of efficient choices which 
are uniformly satisfactory. In the scenario diagrams where the heating demand (y-axis) is close 
to 0, no Paretto frontier can be formed, thus the solutions on the x-axis (cooling load) that are 
located the closest to the intersection with y-axis are the best. As mentioned before, one bullet 
plot can represent the consumption of more than one design solution. 
Three design solutions that appear most commonly across the best results are selected for a 
comparison of their flexibility. The designs are selected due to their performance illuminated 
in Figures 24-27 and Table 21.

modifications are necessary due to the limited number of different scenarios [30]. In this case,

are conceived as 2020:RCP4.5, 2050:RCP6.0 and 2080:RCP8.5. At the same time, typology 
conversion is considered an independent scenario as well. This provides us with 6+1 different 
scenarios that enable us to compute one Energy Flexibility Index per combination of design 
solutions. After this clarification, Equation 1 is used for all scenarios of the three component 
sets.  
Results are presented in Tables 25-27. In general, ECV values near zero indicate similar 
energy consumption across all analyzed scenarios. Conversely, values significantly different 
from zero, whether positive or negative, indicate scenarios that can greatly affect energy 
consumption. 
In the Energy Flexibility paper [30], it was stated that the ECV should be further normalized 
to allow for reliable comparison in case the current scenario is more efficient than a reference 
one. Thus, a weighting factor w was proposed by using a reference technology. In the same 
paper, the façade component was a dynamic shading curtain, hence, a model without curtains 
was used as the reference. However, instead of examining one component only, in this thesis 
component sets are examined which are vital for the function of the building and no building 
model can be conceived without them. Consequently, the weighting factor w is excluded. 
The Energy Flexibility Index of a control strategy k (EFIk) is defined as the opposite of the 
mean of the NECVk and it is reliable provided that it represents the original data. Equation 3 
is used for this part.

typical design solution of previous decades and it was used as the denominator for the 
calculation of ECV. Across the four box plots, it is noted that the baseline design shows higher 
minimum and maximum values, as well as a different interquartile range (IQR). This difference 
is believed to be due to the use of 300 mm insulation, 32% WWR and no shading in their 
design, which is a configuration with no stable performance in climate or typology scenarios, 
while the developed design solutions employ the same or higher insulation levels and optimized 
shading macro strategies to adapt to higher solar intensity. Additionally, the median of the 
baseline design is close to zero and its IQ3 surpasses zero, which means that the baseline design 
belongs to a lower flexibility class (Class 3). 
 
4.6 Calculation of excess energy for future-proof preparation 
As already mentioned before, the components with the most stable performance under 
different scenarios are considered flexible. If we want to prepare the current office envelopes 
with the best-performing components for future scenarios, then the components of the most 
flexible parametric run (Run-10012/10016/10020) should be installed from 2009 until the last 
year of the examined period. Afterwards, the component sets with the best performance from 
each period are selected. Then, to make a viable comparison, the sum of the best design results 
from each time slice is subtracted from the sum of the results of the most flexible components 
(Equations 4 & 5). This way, the excess energy (between the best and most flexible 
components) required for a building to be prepared for future scenarios will be measured. 
 The aforementioned method measures the difference considering the “ideal renovation 
case”, meaning that every 30 years the owner is assumed to renovate the façade by installing 
the best components for each period (just before each period’s first year). This is also in 
==PAGE:54==
 
 
47 
 
accordance with Shearing Layers theory, which states that the envelopes are typically renovated 
(or demounted and replaced) every 25 to 50 years. By contrast, the most flexible option is 
installed one time in the present (2009) and remains in the building until the end of the last 
examined period (2100).

Equations 4 & 5 were used to calculate the excess energy. As depicted in the calculations 
between the most flexible and the best component sets from each period, no significant energy 
amount will be consumed if we prepare our buildings for future changes. More specifically, for 
the 2020 office period the excess energy accounts for 422 kWh, 492 kWh and 207 kWh of 
extra energy for the 2050 and 2080 periods respectively. Overall, the total difference in energy 
consumption for 91 years of building use is 1121 kWh, so the average excess energy per year 
is 12.3 kWh. Consequently, it is a logical decision to prepare the building in the present time 
for future scenarios, as the excess energy between the most flexible and the best components 
does not present high values. Additionally, the project did not measure the extra energy for the 
mounting/dismounting of the best façade configurations every 30 years, which would have 
added more energy demand for the “ideal renovation scenario”. 
For the apartment case, the most flexible and best component set from all time slices 
happens to be the same, meaning that the parametric runs and the flexibility assessment showed 
that this design solution is the most energy efficient and performs well across the four 
designated scenarios. This is by far the most beneficial case, if someone strives to design an 
adaptable building with minimum environmental impact. 
 As a result, we can say that using the most flexible components against the best ones is a 
strategy in which the benefits outweigh the costs, let alone when these components happen to 
be the same. Also, to make things even clearer, worth remembering are the general 
environmental and cost benefits of flexible building components because the building owners 
won't have to spend money upgrading their façade every 30 years but keep the same flexible 
components from the beginning until the end of the examined periods. 
 
4.7 Uncertainties regarding model, parameters, scenarios and flexibility 
assessment 
An important factor influencing these results is the design of small heating zones (20 m²) 
that simulate cubicle offices. Subsequently, these zones are converted into small studio 
apartments, resulting in a low area per inhabitant ratio (20 m²/inhabitant). This ratio is notably 
small for Danish housing standards and typically applies only to student or social housing. If 
the heating zones were larger, providing more space for each inhabitant, the results might have 
been different. 
The model parameters were selected to generate representative results that could be 
beneficial for the design of future buildings. Firstly, this project examined cubicle offices as a 
baseline due to their feasible simulation modelling. Nevertheless, it is mostly an outdated office 
typology in northern European countries, so it is unlikely that this internal layout will be 
preserved for the next 90 years [60]. Over the last two decades, more and more companies have 
operated in open layout offices, so this design choice could have been another model variation 
for future scenarios. 
Nearly all building parameters impact energy consumption to a certain extent. Consequently, 
determining which parameters to prioritize is a major topic of discussion. Numerous factors, 
including roof materials and material properties, significantly influence annual heating and 
cooling demands. The results also suggest that including aspects such as material heat capacity, 
solar absorptance, slat angle, and orientation could have been relevant. This simulation could 
==PAGE:58==
 
 
51 
 
have included more design iterations but the time frame of the thesis as well as the computer 
processing capacity were limited. 
The choice of scenarios was based on their relative impact and probability of change. The 
probability of change is difficult to predict and is tied to many uncertainties. Therefore, the 
boundary conditions of each future simulation should be carefully examined. This project’s 
scenario matrix didn't provide the required number of different scenarios for the flexibility 
assessment to produce accurate and reliable results. Therefore, certain modifications were 
created that may distort the general overview of results. The alteration of climate is both 
impactful and likely to occur in the next decades, however, a scenario such as building typology 
change is not so certain, as the rapid urbanization tackling is primarily a political decision 
which can also be controlled with other measures (e.g. decentralization of economic activity 
away from major cities). 
Moreover, a major uncertainty in the employed method stems from its overall simplification. 
Identifying clear patterns and trends can be challenging when numerous interrelated factors are 
involved. Often, these different factors form bi or multi-directional relationships with other 
factors and scenarios. 
 Finally, most of the codependences remain unclear. The complexity of energy consumption 
makes it uncertain to draw any definitive conclusions from just five design parameters. 
Ultimately, this investigation simplifies a highly complex problem, but it can depict clearly the 
tendencies and the “bigger picture” of things. 
 
4.8 Limitations 
This study did not use realistic occupancy scenarios, a strategy which could affect the results 
up to a point. For example, office employees usually go to meeting rooms, toilets or to other 
places during their day, so their internal gains fluctuate. The same applies to the apartment user, 
as only the “employee” case was simulated, even though there are other highly popular 
inhabitant categories such as elderly people who typically remain all day long in their homes, 
thereby radiating more bodily heat. Also, the simulations did not take into account the presence 
of bathroom areas both in offices and apartments. Their equipment gains as well as different 
internal wall layouts could have impacted the energy consumption results. For the apartment 
scenario, the fridge consumption was not calculated due to difficulties in creating a realistic 
heat gain schedule (Carnot cycle).  
When examining the heating and cooling demand of a building, it would also have been 
productive to include thermal comfort diagrams to see the various temperatures throughout the 
year. This issue was tackled with the fact that very high heating and cooling capacities were 
integrated into the ideal loads system so that they were always capable of keeping the 
temperature within the comfortable set-point range. In addition, there is a high probability in 
the next decades that the energy mix for Denmark will change [61], but this scenario was not 
taken into account. Additionally, since the excess energy was calculated assuming that the 
façade is renovated every 30 years using the DfD technique, the calculation did not include the 
energy required for disassembling and assembling the new facade component. This would have 
provided an even clearer perspective on what strategy is truly beneficial.  
Moreover, when the excess energy between the most flexible and the best components from 
each time slice was calculated, it was assumed that the whole façade was replaced after 30 
years. Admittedly, this renovation type cannot be performed in every building and it is possible 
==PAGE:59==
 
 
52 
 
only under the premise of demountable façades, a construction solution which is generally used 
in flexible buildings and is part of the Design for Disassembly (DfD) technique [62]. 
Regarding the climate change weather files, the A2 scenario was formed after the (AR3) 
2001 IPCC, which does not include the latest data or predictions for the future of the climate. 
Weather files with the (AR6) 2023 data could have produced more accurate results. In addition, 
weather files with more moderate climate change effects should have been also used because 
examining only the extreme A2 scenario does not allow us to comprehend the full spectrum of 
flexible components across all possible scenarios. Finding out the most flexible façade 
components across all climate change levels (low, medium and high impact) would have shown 
very interesting results. Overall, during the project, obtaining reliable and free-of-charge future 
climate weather files was a difficult and time-consuming process. 
In addition, in this study, weather files from the international weather for energy calculation 
(IWEC2) database are considered for the baseline scenario. The IWEC2 database has been 
derived from measured weather data from 1999 to 2011 [63], which is a different timeframe 
than HadCM3 (1961-1990). According to Jentsch [64], this means that morphed weather files 
created using this EPW data are expected to overestimate the effect of climate change for the 
given location, hence the cooling demand results might be presented slightly higher and the 
heating slightly lower. 
5. Conclusion & Future work  
This study aimed mainly to optimize façades for specific future scenarios and calculate the 
excess energy consumption between the most flexible and best components, if we prepare the 
buildings in the present for the future. Since the A2 scenario depicts the “business as usual” 
case, in which GHG emissions continue to rise exponentially throughout the next years, it was 
shown that the need for cooling will be significantly increased in the next decades and heating 
demand will be minimized, especially if the building fulfils certain passive design criteria. As 
a result, climate change which is present in all scenarios, will affect both heating and cooling 
demand. In terms of the building typology change, heating demand is significantly increased 
in the first two time slices, but then it decreases as the climate becomes warmer. 
 The findings indicate that the components that most affect the results are the insulation 
width and window-to-wall ratio (WWR). All of the climate change office cases typically obtain 
the best results with 700mm insulation and 57% WWR, with only one exception in the furthest 
(2080) time slice, which requires 300mm insulation. On the other hand, all of the typology 
climate change scenarios perform the best with 300mm & 500mm thickness insulation and 
32% WWR. In conclusion, this study contributes to our understanding of façade components 
concerning future scenarios and provides a foundation for future research in energy-related 
building flexibility. 
Since the office buildings consumed 12.3 kWh/year of excess energy than the best 
components would have consumed, then preparing our buildings with the most flexible 
components is considered reasonable because the excess energy spent is not significantly high, 
and this small extra energy is outweighed by the other benefits of flexibility (less cost, less 
environmental impact etc). On the other hand, in the apartment cases, the best and the most 
flexible component sets coincide, which is deemed as the most beneficial solution if someone 
wants to create a low-energy building with a long lifespan. Overall, it would be highly 
beneficial for the AEC industry to implement regulations that require the energy simulation of 
==PAGE:60==
 
 
53 
 
the buildings under future scenarios, due to their extensive life span and high probability of 
change in the future. 
The topic of this thesis could be improved and expanded in many ways. More future 
scenarios and parameters could be included, and each variation could be investigated separately 
to achieve more independent and clear results. Furthermore, it could be interesting to create an 
analysis of how each parameter affects one another. For example, another highly probable 
future scenario could present the reduction of electrical equipment gains or the homeworking/e-
learning occupancy schedule scenario, which is a highly relevant case in our time. More output 
data could be optimized also, for instance, daylighting, thermal comfort and CO2 emissions for 
each façade component. For further future research also, the roof and slab parts of the building 
could be set as adiabatic, so that the effect of the façade on energy consumption could be better 
understood and measured. 
Since construction technology is constantly being developed and the time frame of the 
simulations expands to 90 years from the present time, new technologies could be implemented 
in the simulations such as electrochromic windows, adaptive insulation, efficient HVAC 
equipment, phase change materials and so on.
