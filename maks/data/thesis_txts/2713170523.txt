Abstract
The cocktail party effect is a phenomenon of selective hearing in an audiologically deôÙ
manding situation. It was first proposed by Cherry in 1953 [1] and described as a human
ability to selective auditory attention and sound source recognition in noisy situations.
Scientists in hearing care industry aim to minimize the influence of this effect on hearing
impaired people.
The thesis explores possibilities of using wearable eyeôÙtracking glasses to study human
behaviour and building situation aware systems. The eyeball data is utilized to perform
online eye movement metrics analysis based on object detection and tracking algorithms.
Additionally, previous work has been enhanced with face recognition and testing enviôÙ
ronment, to evaluate system limitations and test future modules. All components are
accessible and controlled in Graphical User Interface (GUI), which allows running custom
experiments. The application is, to the best of the authorãs knowledge, the only solution
allowing realôÙtime scene analysis with the use of the Tobii eyeôÙtracking glasses.
This thesis consists of the introduction in Chapter 1, where motivation and project objecôÙ
tives are described. Then, the chosen methodology is introduced in Chapter 2 and the
implementation is explained in Chapter 3. All modules are tested against set requirements
in Chapter 4. Finally, the author summarizes the project in Chapter 5 and elaborates about
further development possibilities in Chapter 6.

Introduction
In this chapter, the motivation and its background are presented. In addition, objective
and research questions are formulated.
1.1
Motivation
The cocktail party effect is the phenomenon of selective hearing in an audiologically deôÙ
manding conditions. It was first proposed by Cherry in 1953 [1] and describes the human
ability to selective auditory attention and sound source recognition in noisy situations. This
complex problem has received considerable attention from scientists from different areas
such as physiology, neurobiology, cognitive psychology, and computer science to underôÙ
stand it deeper. The basics of this phenomenon lie in spatial hearing. This is a process
of masking auditory sound inputs which are in different locations. This ability decreases
when sound sources are in the same location. A healthy person also uses visual stimulus
of speaker face to improve their hearing capability [2].
However, noisy auditory scenes can be demanding for hearing impaired and deaf people
using hearing assistive devices. According to World Health Organisation (WHO), hearing
loss affects 1.5 billion people globally and this number could rise to 2.5 billion in 2030.
700 million out of 2.5 billion people are predicted to require hearing rehabilitation. Whatãs
more, over 1 billion people in age of 12 to 35 are in risk for hearing loss because of
the recreational exposure to loud sounds [3]. To meet one of the United Nations (UN)
Sustainable Development Goals, especially ãGoal 3 ã Good health and WellôÙBeingã [4],
researchers and engineers have taken action to secure hearing health. The hearing aids
industry faces challenges of making hearing impaired people experience better and inôÙ
crease their quality of life.
Two important pieces are underôÙdeveloped in todayãs hearing assistive devices (HAD),
which are limiting their effectiveness:
1. online situational awareness (SI) targeting various aspects of the scene which the
user is occupying, e.g. locations and other metadata of sound sources and the
scene contextëƒ
2. intention awareness (IA) aiming to understand the user behaviour in various condiôÙ
tions, and it can be used to control the hearing aid (HA) optimally to satisfy the user
needs.
The form factor of inôÙ and aroundôÙtheôÙear devices is limited in terms of power supply,
computational resources, sensing location, and geometrical configuration. It is therefore
important to study alternative configurations, such as glasses, which could serve as ideôÙ
alized hearing assistive devices with room for multiple microphone arrays, eye tracking,
pupillometry sensors, internal measurement units (IMU), magnetometers, optical camôÙ
eras, and more. Such a platform could work as an additional sensor package connect
with standard HAs and used in difficult listening scenarios (DLS). Furthermore, there is
an increasing wish for more ecologically valid testing in HA research to better understand,
personalize, and improve the technology for realôÙlife usage.
To this end, Eriksholm Research Centre (ERH) utilizes eyeôÙtracking glasses Tobii Pro
Glasses 2 also enabled with a scene camera, microphone, and inertial sensors. With a
codebase from previous projects at LinkûÑping University (LiU) and experiment data from

Objective
In this master thesis project, the author wants to utilize the knowledge gained during his

deliver a set of tools for audioôÙvisual scene analysis. New tools and modules for scene
and eyeôÙtracking data analysis were embedded into the existing system developed by LiU

1.3
Problem statement
Considering the main objective of the project, the following research questions were
raised:
1. What modules are the most relevant for the audioôÙvisual scene analysis using eyeôÙ
tracking glasses?
In human perception studies, understanding the scene and human reactions is vital.
Therefore, the goal is to fuse object detection with eyeôÙtracking glasses data and
obtain measures regarding audioôÙvisual scene. Moreover, from the perspective of
the cocktail party problem, it is important to recognize people on the scene.
2. How to improve the work of researchers?
There is a need for the openôÙsource analyser, which allows running different onôÙ
line and offline tests. The experiments should be easily customizable in terms of
parameters tuning and modules selection.
3. How to make application scalable and its further development possible?
The application should be developed using ObjectôÙOriented Programming (OOP)
paradigm and well documented. Modules should stay as independent as possible.
4. How to evaluate and test current and future features?
The application should contain a robust test bench for already existing modules,
such as fiducial markers based system. In addition, evaluation of the performance
of each component should be accessible to make the optimization process easier.

2.2
Eye movements classification
The human eyeãs ability to spatial and temporal sampling determines how people perceive
a scene. It highly depends on a personãs visual acuity during quick eye movements. The
human sense of sight has developed a set of movements for efficient and meaningful
scanning of the areas of interest. A few useful terms and metrics related to eyeôÙphysiology
and attendance are established in the community [8] [9]:
ãÂ Gaze points ã eyes position in the time point. Tobii Pro Glasses 2 sample gaze
points with a frequency of approximately 50 Hz. In this case, each gaze point is
separated by 20 ms in the period.
ãÂ Fixations ã moments in which eyes stop scanning the object and hold a central foveal
vision in place. At this moment, the brain collects more descriptive data about the
object of interest. Fixations consist of multiple gaze points, and a number of those
gaze points determine fixationsã duration. Fixations are not directly measurable.
They are an output of the mathematical algorithm calculating fixations sequence.
Fixations typically last for 60 ã 600 ms, however, longer fixations are observed.
They consist of slow movements used for aligning with the target. The example of
identified fixations can be seen as the high position on the first plot in Figure 2.3.

ãÂ Saccades ã rapid movements used to change the fovea from one object of interest
to another. The image on the retina is typically not clear during saccades. Both eyes

are preôÙplanned by the brain and take 100 ôÙ 1000 ms depending on the task. The

2.4
ArUco markers
The importance of fiducial markers in computer vision domain is constantly rising. They
are typically black and white markers put on the scene, used as a point of reference. They
are used for multiple purposes, e.g. image scaling determination, object poses estimaôÙ
tion, localization and projection of augmented reality objects. There is a vast selection of
different markers. Among the most popular are ARToolKit, ARTag, AprilTag, ArUco, and
QR.
In this project, ArUco [11] will be used because it is the fastest and most robust solution
[12]. Moreover, ArUco is widely recognized in research community and there is developed
dedicated OpenCV class supporting them. In this thesis, ArUco markers are used to
create a universal test bench for distance estimation algorithms and tracking landmarks
of the audiological scene.
An ArUco marker is a synthetic square marker, see Figure 2.5 for an example. It consists
of an inner binary matrix encoding identifier (ID) and wide black border. Inner binary
codification is not only used of identification, but also error detection and correction. On
the other side, black border helps in the fast marker detection. Markers can have different
sizes, which is determined by the resolution of the internal matrix. For instance, 16 bits
marker has 4x4 matrix. Binary codification of the marker also allows unequivocal marker
corners identification. It is used in marker detection on frame as well as in determination
of its original rotation. [13]
A set of markers are stored in the dictionary which is specific for the application. The
dictionary contains binary codifications of each marker. Dictionary size and marker size
are the main parameters of each dictionary:
ãÂ Dictionary size ã the number of markers in this dictionary.
ãÂ Marker size ã the number of bits used to create each marker.

Face recognition
This module is used to recognize faces of the people on the scene, as it can be seen in
Figure 2.6. It is based on the Python library called ãface_recognitionã [16]. It allows assoôÙ

new face features with existing one. The module also allows adding new faces to the
directory.
Face recognition is a complex matter which is derived to four isolated problems:
1. Finding faces on the frame ã based on the Histogram of Oriented Gradients (HOG)
method [17]. In this method, a picture is converted to greyscale. Later, for each
pixel, a gradient of brightness compared to surrounding pixels is found. Then the
frame is divided into 16 x 16 pixels batches in which we find which major gradient
direction (up, up left, left, etc.) is most frequent. The most frequent directions for
each batch represent this batch. Finally, the face is found by comparing frame HOG
patterns with preôÙgenerated HOG face patterns based on the face images dataset.
2. Finding pose and projecting face ã once the face is detected, the pose is unknown.
To find it face landmarks estimation algorithm is used. It calculates 68 specific points,
namely an outer edge of the eyes, eyebrows, a bottom chin, a nose edge, and an
inner and outer edge of lips. It is done by the preôÙtrained machine learning algorithm
based on the HELEN database [18]. Using affine transformations ôÙ rotating and
scaling, the face is transformed to the centre position facing straight.
3. Finding unique features of the face ôÙ the system takes 128 measurements of each
face, soôÙcalled embedding. The algorithm which picks embedding is trained by takôÙ
ing 3 pictures at a time, 2 different pictures of the same known person, and one
picture of a different person. The deep neural network is tweaked to generate meaôÙ
surements which for the picture of the same person are close and further for the

[19].
4. Compare found face features with already known face features from the database
ôÙ in this step, a linear SVM classifier is used. Classification is based on a linear
combination of feature values presented to the feature vector [20].

Design summary
This chapter has explained the design and implementation of modules developed during
the scope of this project. Those tools are meant to advance and support future behavioural
research in eyeôÙtracking and hearing industry community. As it was stated before, there
was a great focus on modularization of the blocks that then can be reused in the future.
At this moment three main capabilities of the system can be distinguished:
ãÂ Gaze data analyser, which involves gaze metrics module, object detection, and obôÙ
ject tracking.
ãÂ EKFôÙSLAM, which estimates pose of the glasses wearerãs head and target faces on
the scene. It requires face mesh, EKFôÙSLAM, face recognition and tracking modôÙ
ules.
ãÂ Evaluation test bench. It is based on ArUco modules used for evaluation and trackôÙ
ing of ArUco markers on the scene. In addition, application and modules perforôÙ
mance is measured.
In addition, developed modules meet one of the main objectives ôÙ making researchers
work easier. There is a possibility of creating different experiment cases which can be
easily customizable by running different modules on online and offline data. In addition,
there is a possibility of online application performance evaluation. It helps developers to
check feasibility of their implementations.
The application is modularized, which allows users to expand the analyser framework.
Each module can be reused and initialized several times if there is a need. It creates an
opportunity for testing different experiments and modules setups simultaneously. MoreôÙ
over, object references were created for objects which are entirely or partially depended.
It simplifies class methods and increases code readability. All classes and their attributes
and methods are described and commented to help future developers ramping up into
the development process.
Lastly, the application is easy to set up on every computer. It only requires installing
the Conda, or another Python environment based on the prepared configuration file, and
installing external modules as it is explained in README file.

4.6
Results summary
In this chapter, the results of numerous tests have been shown. Their main purpose was
to prove the robustness of the system under different circumstances and ability to work
on live eyeôÙtracking data. In addition, limitations of the system have been presented. The
results are covered in this section.
Firstly, the app is a feasible solution for working on the recorded and live data on different
experiment setups. The average latency of each module is low, with the longest time
reaching 40 ms for FHD object detection. Keeping processing time low for each module
allows realôÙtime processing, achieving the rate from 9 FPS for FHD processing up to 30
FPS for SD. The problem with timeôÙconsuming plot drawing which can last over 150 ms
is solved by setting its refresh rate. If the application is working on the recorded data,
which is not a timeôÙcritical case, the app user can decide to update plots every frame.
In the case of online test, plots are updated less frequently to minimize the influence of
Matplotlib plot update latency. Moreover, it has been shown that the app performance
stays stable during the experiment. It is crucial to obtain meaningful and correct data.
Secondly, thanks to the stable and robust performance of the app run on online and ofôÙ
fline data, the gaze analysis results are similar for the same or similar data in the same
environment for different resolutions and data types. The most important eyeôÙtracking
detector ã the fixation detector ã works identically for both types of data. Differences for
other eyeôÙtracking metrics are minor and are caused by object detection. Although results
may vary from test to test, the outcome of human analysis of the results should stay the
Learning audioôÙvisual scenes with eyeôÙtracking glasses
51
==PAGE:64==
same. Researchers can easily distinguish patterns in the behaviour of the respondents.
In order to evaluate and develop future modules, ArUco test bench has been introduced.
It has been shown that it provides robust marker position estimation in range of almost 5
m for low resolution up to 7 m for high resolution. Moreover, the error margin does not
exceed 5 cm and markers can be detected when they are tilted up to 70ô¯in relation to
camera origin, as it was also proved in another research [39].
In order to create distinctive matches between ArUco markers and faces on the scene,
face recognition is embedded into the face mesh module. Even though, the identification
is not always correct, the solution is relatively lightweight, which is the top priority for realôÙ
time system. Moreover, most of the incorrect recognitions are registered with new ID,
instead of not correct existing ID. Thanks to that, the system is reliable in the sense of not
confusing faces.
Lastly, target faces position estimation has been evaluated using ArUco test bench. The
association of the markers with detected faces on the scene allows to dynamically track
the target face and compare results, when assuming ArUco position as a ground truth. It
has been proved that it is a robust tool to estimate the position of the faces in range up to
2 meters. However, detection must take place in a range lower than 1 meter. The addiôÙ
tional constraint is the velocity of the faces on the scene. The system does not recognize
faces, which change their position quickly ã run/jump from side to side. From the eyeôÙ
tracking research and cocktail party problem perspective, the closest people who attend
the conversation are the most important in terms of analysis. Therefore, the EKFôÙSLAM
solution is assumed to be the sufficient solution for this problem.
Combining the result of the different test, there is a conclusion that the best resolution
to work on online data is HD. It is the best trade off good input frame quality and good
performance. It is the most stable experiment setting which can bring the most meaningôÙ
ful results. Although object detection and face mesh modules perform well on the good
quality video, the quality of the video from the Tobii frontal camera can be low for two
reasons. Firstly, the exposure control problems often appear, as it can be seen in Figure
4.17. Camera exposure control corrects brightness of the device. Moreover, the camera
has a low maximum dynamic range, as shown in Figure 4.18. Dynamic range specifies
the contrast ratio between the lightest and the darkest colour which the camera can capôÙ
ture. It reduces face and markers detection abilities on many frames and decreases the
accuracy of the object detection.

5.1
Research questions
1. What modules are the most relevant for the audioôÙvisual scene analysis using eyeôÙ

In eyeôÙtracking research, there are a lot of factors which can help in understanding
human behaviours. The System must have modules which increase its situational
awareness to bring vital information for the researcher. Therefore, object detection
and tracking were embedded. They allow distinguishing specific AOI on the scene
and track their position from frame to frame. It makes the system aware of the
objects which appear on the scene and their movements in relation to the glasses
wearer. Those modules are coupled with the eyeôÙmovement analysis system. It is
created to help to understand glasses wearer intention awareness. Basic fixation
and saccade detector in connection with object detection and tracking system are
used to map and measure eyeôÙmovements toward different objects. Basing on this
information, researchers can argue which objects play a key role on the scene.
Lastly, previous developed face mesh and EKFôÙSLAM modules are advanced with
face recognition module. It not only brings the information not only about the position
of the people on the scene but also identifies them. It helps to understand how a
specific person influences the behaviour of the glass wearer.
Those modules combined bring a complex set of tools which reduce researchersã
work on data acquisition and labelling. In addition, they bring many important metrics
used in the eyeôÙtracking research. The application also makes it possible to analyse
online data, which was less feasible with tools available nowadays.
2. How to improve the work of researchers?
First of all, the reduction of time spent on the data labelling is the most burning probôÙ
lem in time optimization. As it was stated in the first question, using the analyser
developed for this project eliminates this time. Secondly, the ability to run differôÙ
ent tests on different data types and environmental settings might help in getting
into feasible solutions during research work. Test results must be evaluated on the
builtôÙin figures and visualized on the frame. Finally, as it was mentioned in the first
question, the possibility of analysis of online data can bring a lot of new possibilities
for eyeôÙtracking research and allow building new systems based on it. All those
features are built into the application.
3. How to make the application scalable and its further development possible?
Application is modularized, allowing future engineers to develop it. Each module has
a simple interface with defined input and output data. It allows extending existing
modules with new features and reusing them for different purposes. Moreover, the
program is written using OOP paradigm, so each module class can be expended
with new methods. It allows making system more configurable and complex. In
addition, different modules can be initialized multiple times. Thanks to that, existing
module can be easily utilized in multiple places.

1. RealôÙtime system capability is constrained to 9ôÙ21 FPS on average, depending on
the input frame resolution.
2. RealôÙtime performance depends on the modules chosen to run.
3. Abilities of eye movement analyser are constrained by maximum Tobii glasses freôÙ

4. Several eye movement metrics rely on object detection, whose accuracy is limited.

5. Object detection accuracy is limited. Object tracking works best if the tracked objects
are relatively stable.
6. Face recognition has limited accuracy.
7. 10 x 10 cm ArUco maximal distance perception is limited from 5 m to 7 m, depending

8. EKFôÙSLAM target faces estimation works in the range up to 3 m. EKFôÙSLAM module
is not robust for quick movements of the faces on the scene.

Further work
In this section, the author presents possible future development paths for this project.
6.1
GUI
It can be improved with a multiôÙtab user interface, which would increases app readability
and user experience. Tabs can be used not only for metrics displaying, but also to give
app users better access to configuration of separate modules. Moreover, a time bar could
be added for recorded data mode to allow the app user to jump between events during
the experiment. It would also allow replaying and focusing on important parts of the exôÙ
periment. Higher configurability would also bring the possibility of creating custom plots
focused on specific object classes and IDs.
Secondly, app performance would be improved by multiple threads, e.g. each module
has a separate thread. Additionally, the app can be embedded with a multiprocessing
feature, which allows running multiple tests on the same data at the same time. Thanks
to that, researchers could compare different experiments setups on one screen.
6.2
Object detection
The object detection module contains object tracking. This module can be improved by
adding object recognition for selected classes, the same as for face recognition. It could
advance data analysis with more unique information about objects on the scene. In adôÙ
dition, the object detection neural network could be trained on a customized dataset user
for specific purposes to allow the user to play with different options and choose the most
suitable model for the experiment. It would limit the number of irrelevant detections and
simplify the analysis.
Moreover, two new modules could be developed based on the data from object detection.
The first module would be similar to the automated subtitles generator based on machine
learning. It would describe the scene depending on visible objects. It would increase
situational awareness of the system. The second might be MonoSLAM for estimation of
the position of other objects on the scene. For that, the ORB algorithm could be used. It
would bring more descriptive analysis of the scene.
6.3
Audiological solution
The application backend could be used for either HA parameter tweaking or output audio
signal modulation. Its purpose would be to adjust soôÙcalled beamforming parameters
depending on the environment where HA user currently is located. Beamforming is used
for controlling the directionality of the received signal. Beamforming parameter specifies
the width of audio signal that the hearing aid processes to the user ear. ML solution could
change the input audio signal by separating voices from the background noise, and then
amplify separated channels in a desired way. However, it is an area of research for many
scientists, and there are no feasible solutions today.
