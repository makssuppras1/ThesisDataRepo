Abstract
This thesis establishes a framework for using a Wiener filter as a linear adaptive filter when
determining the phase noise of an optical signal. The Wiener filter is applied on a noisy signal
generated by a numerical simulation of a coherent detection setup. The input signal is a noisy
signal with a phase noise generated by a Wiener process.
The coherent detection is a Heterodyne setup. This generates a beat signal which is also subject
to detector noise. The phase noise is extracted using the Hilbert transform of the beat signal.
The phase noise is analyzed using the power spectral density. It is the signal to noise ratio, not
the noise floor of detectors, that impacts the phase noise.
A Wiener filter is implemented and a physical understanding of the weights is presented. A
hyperparameter is introduced in the Wiener filter and a logarithmic dependency between the
signal power and hyperparameter is observed. The Wiener filter has been optimized for the power
spectral density of the phase noise.
For further work this thesis proposes looking at the root mean square error of the phase
fluctuations and comparing it to the quantum limit.

Introduction
Narrow-linewidth lasers with low phase noise and a large coherence length are a necessity as a
high spectral purity light source in many applications such as gravitational wave detection [1, 2],
optical atomic clocks [3], lidar [4], coherent optical communication [5], and distributed optical fiber
sensing [6]. The development and need for these ultra-narrow-linewidth lasers puts a requirement
on highly accurate measurement techniques for determining the laser linewidth [7].
Many different methods for measuring the laser linewidth have been developed over the years.
The linewidth is typically determined by either calculating the linewidth directly from the power
spectral density or by the indirect relation between the linewidth and the phase noise. Calcu-
lating the linewidth directly from the power spectral density make use of an optical spectrum
analyzers. The resolution of commercial optical spectrum analyzers is typically in the GHz range
[8]. Determining the linewidth by measuring the phase noise is more difficult, but can yield a
much higher resolution especially with modern adaptive filtering techniques [9].
Measuring the phase noise can be done using coherent detection.
Coherent detection can be
done by either homodyne or heterodyne techniques [10, 11].
The homodyne and heterodyne
techniques are distinguished by the frequency difference between the interfered optical signals,
the beat frequency, where the signals in homodyne detection have the same frequency, i.e. having
no beat frequency. Coherent detection uses another laser, called a local oscillator, to interfere
with. Phase noise measurements can also be done by interfering the signal with itself, potentially
with a frequency shift. These techniques are called self-homodyne and -heterodyne techniques.
Homodyne detection is prone to low frequency self noise from the electrical spectrum analyzer.
To overcome this problem the heterodyne detection techniques can be used. This is done by
shifting the beat signal to a frequency far away from dc either by modulating the laser with a RF
signal or using a reference laser with a frequency different from the test laser. Self-heterodyne has
the advantages of high spectral resolution and simplicity over traditional linewidth measurement
methods such a coherent detection [11].
The resolution of optical detectors is limited by noise, which can be mitigated by using filtering
methods. A well known and researched filtering method is the Kalman filter [12]. The Kalman
filter has multiple applications, for example, in military, biomedicine and other robotic systems.
The Kalman filter was developed in 1960 by Rodulf E. Kalman as an algorithm for the estimation
of nonobservable variables using observable variables subjected to noise. The Kalman filter is a
linear filter and is widely used for many problems and has, therefore, also been extended to solve
nonlinear problems. The extended Kalman filter makes use of Taylor’s approximation to convert
the nonlinear problem into a linear problem [12]. The Kalman filter is, however, a complicated
and extensive filtering technique. Another filter is the Wiener filter. This filter has interesting
properties such as its simplicity and possibly also its speed.
The Wiener filter estimates the
desired outcome of a signal by filtering the measured noisy signal. The Wiener filter is a statistical
approach which relies on optimizing the error function in some statistical sense, for example, by
minimizing the mean squared error. The optimal solution to the Wiener filter can simply be found
by solving a linear set of equations where the number of equation corresponds to the length of
the impulse response [13].
In this thesis a simple heterodyne detection scheme is investigated. A signal laser is combined with

State of the Art
The aim of this section is to discuss different measurement techniques for measuring the linewidth
of a laser. Each section will give a short introduction to the method followed by some results of
the method. The advantage and disadvantages of each method is presented.
In general, two different approaches for measuring the laser linewidth are used:
1. Calculating the laser linewidth directly from the power spectral density (PSD).
2. Deducing the linewidth indirectly using the relation between the phase noise and the laser
linewidth.
To obtain the PSD it is necessary to beat the optical signal. Using two lasers with a Lorentzian
lineshape function the lineshape function of the beat signal is also Lorentzian [14]
g(ν) =
∆ν
2π [(ν −νb)2 + (∆ν/2)2],
(1)
where ∆ν = νt + νr, νt is the linewidth of the test laser, νr is the linewidth of the reference laser,
and νb = ±|νt −νr| is beat frequency. Typically a reference laser is chosen with a linewidth that
fit in one of two cases. In the first case, the linewidth of the reference laser is chosen to be close
to or equal to the linewidth of the test laser νr ≈νt. This means the linewidth of the beat signal
is twice the linewidth of the test laser. In the second case, the linewidth of the reference laser is
chosen to be much smaller than the test laser. This means the linewidth of the reference laser
can be neglected in the calculation of the linewidth of the beat signal.
One method uses an additional laser to generate the reference laser beam to beat with the test
laser. Here a photodetector receives the mixed signal and transmits it to an electrical spectrum
analyzer (ESA). Using a reference laser with the same linewidth as the test laser it is possible to
measure any linewidth. The difficult part is to measure and calibrate the reference laser. Using
a reference laser with negligible linewidth compared to the test laser it is possible to meet the
requirement for measuring the linewidth of most lasers. However, it is extremely difficult or in
some cases not possible to measure the linewidth of ultra-narrow-linewidth lasers. Furthermore,
there is a strict requirement on the stability of the experimental setup which increases the difficulty
and cost of the testing.

Using the Stokes wave of a Brillouin laser as beat notes an additional reference laser is no need
for measuring the linewidth. This work by dividing the test laser into two beams using a coupler.
One beam is transmitted into a fiber ring cavity to form the Brillouin laser, while the other beam
couples with the backward propagating first order Stokes wave from the Brillouin laser.
The
coupled signal is measured in a photodetector where it beats. The beat signal is then analyzed
using an ESA. The linewidth of the first-order Stokes beam is typically much smaller than the
linewidth of the test laser itself. The linewidth of the beat notes can thus be approximated as
the linewidth of the test laser, ∆ν ≈νt. The frequency shift in optical fibers is typically around
10 GHz [17] which is also the frequency difference νb between the Stokes wave and the test laser.
Therefore, a large frequency shift will result in a center frequency of the beat signal that exceeds
the measurement range of the ESA. The scheme is thus most suited for measuring a linewidth
in the range 1-500 kHz [18]. In 1994, Boschung et al. [19] used a 100 kHz linewidth laser as an
incident laser into the fiber ring cavity resulting in a 3.84 Hz linewidth Brillouin laser.
The delayed self-Homodyne interferometric detection scheme is based on the unbalanced Mach-
Zehnder interferometer.
The test laser thereby interferes with a delayed version of itself and
fluctuation of the phase is converted into fluctuation intensity. This method has the advantage
of a wide measurement range, simple structure, and low optical transmission loss. One of the
disadvantages is the center frequency of the beat note is 0 Hz which results in low-frequency
noise. In 1998 Ludvigsen et al. [20] modified a self-homodyne setup by using a phase modulator
in the delay fiber to average out the dependence on the mean phase difference. Using a delay line
of 71.7 m with a coherent length of 100 m they measured a linewidth of 460 kHz.
Delayed self-Heterodyne interferometry overcomes the problem of low-frequency noise by using
an acousto-optic modulator to shift the center frequency of the beat note to higher frequencies.
The delayed self-Heterodyne method must be delayed more than five times the coherence time
for accurate measurements [21].
The increase in the length of the optical fiber increases the
attenuation of the optical signal transmitted through the fiber. Increasing the power of the test
laser will result in Brillouin scattering, which travels in the opposite direction of the laser. This
increases the transmission loss and may disturb the signal so the photodetector is unable to
measure the signal. In 1986 Richter [22] used delayed self-Heterodyne interferometry to derive
the power spectral density function of the beat notes. They concluded that the result would be
more accurate for delay times longer than the coherence time of the test laser. In 2015 Chen et al.
[7] proposed a fitting scheme based on the Voigt profile. They did this to offset the influence of
the 1/f frequency noise. The Voigt function is the convolution of the Lorentzian lineshape of the
laser and a Gaussian function. Fitting the data with this function effectively filters out the 1/f
frequency noise. In 2019 He et al. [23] used a short-delay self-Heterodyne interferometry scheme
to measure a linewidth of 2.5 kHz. This was done by splitting the beam using a 3 dB coupler.
In one of the arms, an acousto-optic modulation imposed a 55 MHz frequency shift. The second
arm was a 5.02 km long single-mode fiber resulting in a retardation of 25.2 µs. The signal was
then combined using another 3 dB coupler. They estimated the linewidth by demodulating the
coherent envelope of the short-delay self-Heterodyne interference spectrum resulting in a linewidth
of 2.53 kHz.

Chapter 1
Coherent Detectors
Coherent detection relies on the coherent combination of an optical signal with a continuous-wave
optical field. This way it is possible to not only rely on an intensity modulated scheme with direct
detection. With coherent detection it becomes possible to transmit information by modulating the
phase or frequency of the optical carrier, and detect the signal by using Heterodyne or Homodyne
detection techniques [24].
In the past, the main motivation for coherent detection was due to the optical gain of the mea-
sured signal. Today several techniques and technologies provide the optical gain needed. Now
the reasoning for coherent detectors is their application as an ultra-narrow wavelength division
multiplex (WDM) optical filter. The coherent detector will only respond to wavelengths close
to that of the local oscillator hence the WDM filtering capabilities. Furthermore, if the local
oscillator is tunable the WDM filter becomes tunable [25].
This chapter will introduce the concept of coherent detection, then explain the two different
techniques Homodyne and Heterodyne detection, and, lastly, a brief introduction to signal to
noise ratio for this type of detection.
1.1
Concept
The concept of coherent detection consist of combining the optical signal coherently with a
continuous-wave (CW) optical field after which the combined signal is detected [24], as shown
in figure 1.1. The CW field is generated with a narrow linewidth laser locally at the receiver, and
is denoted as the local oscillator (LO).

Chapter 2
Statistical Analysis
The Wiener filter is used to give a better estimate of the phase noise of the signal laser when the
system is subject to detector noise. The Wiener filter is chosen over other linear adaptive filters
due to its simplicity. Before describing the theory behind the Wiener filter, it is important to
understand the statistical tools that will be used.
To introduce the notation and language used to describe the Wiener filter, the chapter starts by
giving a partial characterization of a discrete-time stochastic process. The chapter then gives a
short description of Power spectral density as it is the tool used for analyzing the phase noise.
Moreover, the chapter will give a short introduction to autoregressive models and in doing so
introduce the transfer function and z-transforms.
Lastly, the chapter will describe the basic
theory behind the Wiener filter used in this thesis.

3.3
Phase Noise
The phase noise of the signal laser has so far just been treated as a function of time. In order
to simulate the phase noise, one must know how to model it. The minimum fluctuations of the
phase noise are dictated by the amplified spontaneous emission in the laser cavity and can be
approximated by a Wiener process. A Wiener process is a real-valued continuous-time stochastic
process that starts at zero and has independent increments, i.e. for t > 0 the future increments
are independent of the past values. Each increment is drawn from a normal distribution with 0

sum of all the former elements including the element itself. The length of the list is, therefore,
not altered in the process. The algorithm then outputs the random walk which functions as the
true phase noise of the signal laser.

signal laser.

Conclusion
The goal of this project is to formulate and establish a framework for using a Wiener filter as a
linear adaptive filter when determining the phase noise of an optical signal. The project describes
an cost function and how it can be used to train the weights of the Wiener filter. A Wiener filter
will be implement and the effect of the Wiener filter will be present using the power spectral
density of the phase noise.
A Heterodyne coherent detection scheme has been implemented as a measurement technique for
the noisy measurement of the phase noise of the signal laser. The Heterodyne detection scheme
include simulations of shot noise and thermal noise of the detectors.
The phase noise of the
simulation has been determined using the Hilbert transform of the balanced current between the
two detectors. However, using the Hilbert transform on a signal with phase noise resulted in a
discontinuity of the power spectral density of the phase noise. The phase noise has, therefore, been
approximated in the low phase noise power limit. The power spectral density of the phase noise has
been investigated for laser linewidth, power of the local oscillator, and detector bandwidth. The
investigation concludes that the relative change in the power spectral density when the detectors
are subject to noise depends on the signal to noise ratio.
The minimization of the mean square error cost function is described. This description leads to
the Wiener-Hopf equations which determines an optimal solution for the weights of the Wiener
filter by using the correlation matrix and the cross-correlation vector. It is from this equation a
mathematical description of the weights of the Wiener filter is obtained.
The Wiener filter is implemented with the weights determined by the solution to the Wiener-
Hopf equations. However, it is shown that these weights are not optimized for this setup and
a hyperparameter is introduced. This hyperparameter controls the decay of the weight function
and, thereby, controls if the phase noise at time n is determined by the phase noise far away, i.e.
with large lag, n −l, or close to (in time) the phase noise being estimated. The hyperparameter
is investigated for the laser linewidth, detector bandwidth, and signal power. The value of the
hyperparameter only depends on the signal power. The hyperparameter is calculated for different
value of the signal power and there is a logarithmic dependency between the signal power and the
hyperparameter.
For further work a method for comparing the root mean squared error of the phase noise to the
quantum limit is proposed. Figure 4.7 illustrates this comparison but because the root mean
squared error of the noisy phase measurement, ϕtan−1, is not parallel to the quantum limit for
high signal powers, this figure is believed to not be complete. However, assuming this figure is
correct it can be concluded that the Wiener filter is able to reach the quantum limit resulting in
a high resolution of the phase noise measurement.
