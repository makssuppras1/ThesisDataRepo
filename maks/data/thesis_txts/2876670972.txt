Abstract
The growing penetration of wind power plants (WPPs) and other inverter-based resources
(IBRs) increases the need for accurate Electromagnetic Transient (EMT) models to sup-
port reliable and stable system operation. While existing validation frameworks are well
established for Grid-Following (GFL) wind turbines (WTs), Grid-Forming (GFM) technol-
ogy introduces new functionalities and it lacks standardized validation procedures. This
thesis, investigates the applicability of existing GFL validation practices and evaluates
emerging GFM specific testing methods to support robust EMT model validation of WTs
equipped with GFM control.
A review of current standards, grid code requirements and industry practices highlights
which GFL validation procedures may remain applicable to GFM and identifies function-
alities that may require adapted or new methodologies. Time-domain EMT simulation
studies in PSCAD show that controlled signal injections (phase jumps and RoCoF) pro-
vide a suitable and practical method of characterizing key GFM functionalities, also en-
abling field-deployable tests that can serve as reliable validation sources for further studies
in other environments. Small-signal voltage injections reproduced the expected reactive
current behaviour, though active power responses differed due to inherent differences be-
tween back-emf signal injections and external voltage perturbations. Frequency domain
studies, including positive sequence impedance scans and Jacobian transfer functions,
assessed short-time voltage source property but also revealed high sensitivity to control
architecture and tuning, underscoring the need for further methodological assessment
than the presented in this work before these tests can be used for model validation.
The thesis also evaluates the suitability of playback validation for GFM EMT models of
WTs. Playback remains effective at unit level and accurately reproduces FRT and island-
ing behaviour. However, sensitivity analyses show that realistic tolerances in grid, test
equipment, connection cables, as well as in WT-side components such as transformers,
can introduce deviations of several percent. These findings reinforce playback as the pre-
ferred method for WT-level validation at the product design phase, while full-grid setups
may remain necessary for system-level studies.
Overall, the results indicate that a hybrid validation framework is needed: existing GFL
WT practices can be reused for shared functionalities, while GFM WT specific capabili-
ties require complementary testing methods that enable consistent and reliable validation
EMT models.

Introduction
The global energy transition, together with national and international decarbonization
targets, is driving a rapid transformation of power systems. To meet these objectives,
Renewable Energy Sources (RES) are being rapidly deployed and increasingly integrated
into both transmission and distribution networks. Wind energy, in particular, has experi-
enced strong growth, with installed capacity in Europe expected to reach 441 GW by
2030, as shown in Figure 1.1 [1]. In 2024, Denmark generated more than 50% of its
electricity from wind, and at least seven other European countries met over one-quarter
of their demand from wind generation [2]. Such penetration levels underline the central
role of wind energy in present and future low-carbon power systems.
Figure 1.1: Evolution of cumulative wind power capacity in Europe and outlook up to 2030
[1]
However, this large-scale integration of RES interfaced through power electronics intro-
duces system behaviours fundamentally different from those of grids dominated by large,
centralized synchronous generators. Challenges include reduced system inertia, weaker
grid strength, and complex control interactions across grid devices [3]. These issues af-
fect grid dynamics, protection schemes, and overall system stability. In fact, an IEEE Task
Force formally recognized converter-driven and resonance stability as new categories of
power system stability, emphasizing the need for updated analytical frameworks and ad-
vanced simulation tools in converter-dominated grids [4].
Under these conditions, grid connection studies have become essential for assessing the
behaviour of Wind Power Plants (WPP)s under different conditions and ensuring com-
pliance with Transmission System Operator (TSO) requirements before commissioning,
i.e. conection of the plant to the power grid. The accuracy of these studies directly de-
pends on the fidelity, level of detail and suitability of the electrical models used, which
range from time-domain dynamic models to frequency-domain and steady-state models.
Models which fail to replicate the system’s behaviour may lead to incorrect conclusions
from simulation studies, interoperability issues among grid users, or undesired protection
actions, as evidenced by several real-world disturbances [5, 6].
Within this context of increasingly converter-dominated grids, Grid-Forming (GFM) has

gies, such as Battery Energy Storage System (BESS), are already commercially available

tional challenges arise from the interaction with the mechanical system. As shown in

1.1
Motivation
Grid connection assessment studies of WPPs rely on simulation models capable of repro-
ducing their electrical behaviour under different operating conditions. Among the available
modeling approaches, time-domain models are essential for accurately representing the
electrical response of WTs and WPPs as a function of time. Model fidelity is ensured
through validation of the model against measurements from actual WTs or WPPs sub-
jected to the same conditions, either during the testing phase or real network events.
Standardized methodologies exist for validating Root-Mean Square (RMS) models, and
although experience with Electromagnetic Transient (EMT) model validation is increas-
ing, the standardization of EMT validation procedures is still ongoing. Besides, existing
validation practices were originally designed for WTs operating under conventional Grid-
Following (GFL) control. Since these methods are well established and widely applied,
it is necessary to assess to what extent they remain suitable for GFM WTs and WPPs,
and to identify where adaptations or new procedures are required. Extending current
standards to incorporate the specific validation needs of GFM technology is essential to
ensure the maturity and reliability of WTs equipped with GFM control.
Despite the need for validated GFM WT models to conduct simulation studies, there is still
no consensus within the industry on how to test the specific capabilities of these resources.
Several proposals focus on simulation-based functionality testing, yet the suitability of ex-
isting GFL testing setups for GFM technology remains under discussion. These gaps
underscore the need for a coherent testing framework that supports functionality, per-
formance, and capability testing, as well as model validation of GFM WTs and WPPs,
given that testing constitutes the primary source of reference measurements for model
validation.

1.2
Grid-Following vs Grid-Forming
Nowadays, most converters in WTs operate in GFL mode, also referred to as current con-
trol. In this scheme, grid synchronization is achieved through a Phase-Locked-Loop (PLL)
that estimates the grid frequency. The converter “follows” the grid dynamics and injects
active and reactive currents according to local voltage and frequency measurements. GFL
converters cannot operate in electrical islands without an external voltage source and can-
not provide services such as black-start. A simplified representation of a GFL converter
is shown in Figure 1.3.
In contrast, GFM WTs behave as controlled voltage sources, setting voltage and fre-
quency locally. In the short-transient time frame, they maintain an approximately constant
internal voltage phasor (commonly referred to as virtual back-emf voltage), effectively act-
ing as a low-bandwidth voltage source. They can self-synchronise without a PLL and are
capable of operating both grid-connected and in electrical islands. Their key feature is
the inherent capability to deliver fast voltage and frequency stabilizing actions right after a
grid disturbance. A simplified representation of a GFM converter is shown in Figure 1.4.

In modern power systems, grid stability will need to be guaranteed even under scenarios
where IBRs supply 100% of the generation. Consequently, future IBRs must provide all
grid-support capabilities traditionally delivered by synchronous machines to ensure stable
and reliable system operation. Within this context, critical challenges such as reduced
system inertia and grid strength must be addressed [11].
It is well known that GFL IBRs can lead to instabilities under weak-grid conditions due to
their reliance on voltage measurements through the PLL [12]. In addition, the replacement
of synchronous generators with GFL units reduces the system’s inherent voltage and fre-
quency regulation capability, making the control over AC voltage fluctuations increasingly
difficult [11].
To overcome these limitations, GFM technology has emerged as a promising alternative
to conventional GFL control, being eventually capable of describing the dynamic response
of electromechanical synchronous generators, which is particularly valuable when GFM
converters are required to provide virtual inertial response during transient grid-frequency
deviations [13]. However, GFM IBRs are inherently limited in their ability to deliver such
a response due to their restricted overload capability and for some technologies, the lim-
ited or non-existent headroom available in their generation. This is especially true for
Photovoltaic (PV) plants and WPPs, which typically operate close to their maximum power
point to maximize revenue. As a result, their inertial and active power response to phase
jumps can be significantly constrained, particularly in situations where an increase in ac-
tive power injection would be required. However, it is generally accepted that these gen-
eration units with none or very limited inherent energy storage are required to provide only

negative active power changes. In the same way, these units are not required to provide
an active power response if that involves consuming active power from the grid [14].
1.3
Methodology
The practical part of this thesis employs EMT models which capture all the relevant dy-
namics of power systems dominated by IBRs. This approach enables the representation
of the switching behaviour of WT converters, as well as the analysis of potential reso-
nances, instabilities under weak-grid conditions, and the response to unbalanced faults,
i.e. phenomena that cannot be fully represented using RMS models. The WT model is
therefore implemented in PSCAD v5.0.2 using a generic converter control representation.
This generic design provides full and easy access to the control architecture and allows
for modifications to carry out a wide range of studies. However, this also inherently limits
the degrees of freedom available to shape a robust control response under diverse oper-
ating conditions. Furthermore, because the model is not optimally tuned, it may display
good performance in certain scenarios while exhibiting a more compromised response in
others.
The studies focus on a Type IV WT topology, which is mainly used in offshore applications.
It includes a Permanent Magnet Synchronous Generator (PMSG) interfaced through a full-
size Voltage Source Converter (VSC) topology. A full-switching representation of both the
machine-side and grid-side converters is implemented, together with a DC link equipped
with a capacitor and a chopper to dissipate excess energy during power imbalances due
to grid events. More detailed descriptions of the WT and converter models, including the
control architecture and analytical derivations, are provided in chapter 3.
Besides, a Fault-Ride Through (FRT) manufacturer container model and a real test center
grid is used to emulate field testing conditions of WTs and to investigate the suitability of
playback validation methodology in the GFM case.
In chapter 4, where specific testing methods for assessing GFM capabilities are investi-
gated, the frequency-domain test cases are carried out using the open-source Immittance
Measurement ToolBox (IMTB) tool developed by Energinet [15]. The underlying mathe-
matical formulations are presented when necessary to support the interpretation of the
results.

The primary objective of this thesis is to investigate the model validation procedures that
can be employed to verify and guarantee the accuracy of WT models equipped with GFM
control. The work focuses on assessing whether the well-established validation practices
used for GFL WTs remain applicable in the GFM case, and on evaluating the suitability
of newly proposed methods for testing, and subsequently validating, GFM capabilities.

used as “validation sources”. The work also analyzes recent proposals for testing
and validating GFM WT models, with the aim of contributing to the development of
a structured framework on practices for the testing and validation of WTs equipped
with GFM control.

2.1
Introduction
As IBR such as WPPs become increasingly dominant in power systems, their accurate
modeling in simulation studies becomes essential to assess grid connection compliance,
i.e., their performance across a wide range of operating conditions and grid disturbances.
Traditionally, TSOs have relied on RMS models for these studies [16]; however, with the
growing penetration of IBRs, EMT models are increasingly required to capture fast control
and protection dynamics that are not adequately represented by RMS-based approaches.
This position is supported by a several real-life system events that could not be satisfac-
torily reproduced with RMS models, but were accurately replicated using EMT simulation
softwares [5, 6, 17, 18].
WT simulation models are developed by OEMs during the WT design phase. These
models are later used by developers to conduct WPP-level design studies and are utilized
throughout the commissioning and operation of the WPP to verify grid compliance. The
WT models aim to replicate their electrical behaviour in the time domain, including detailed
representations of electrical components and control systems. The level of accuracy of
the model depends on the study domain considered, but uncertainties are minimized when
the model is based on the actual control source code. In fact, OEM models commonly
implement converter and WT controllers via embedded Dynamic Link Libraries (DLL)s,
enabling high-fidelity simulations. In addition to validated WT models, WPP models must
include detailed representations of other components such as cables, transformers, surge
arresters, Static Synchronous Compensator (STATCOM), etc.
Grid codes, along with national and international standards, define the modeling require-
ments, including test plans, validation procedures, and model accuracy criteria. As illus-
trated in Figure 2.1, validation is carried out at different stages during the life cycle to
guarantee the validity of the models:
• WT model validation: Conducted by comparing the model response to measure-
ments obtained from field or test bench testing. It is non-site-specific, as field testing
does not necessarily take place at the WPP installation site but rather at dedicated
facilities such as Østerild [19], which are available to the OEMs.
• WPP model validation: Performed during the commissioning of the WPP at the
site where the plant is installed. However, the scope of the validation is limited to
the test cases that can be conducted without causing significant perturbations to the
Investigating Validation Methodologies for EMT Models of Grid-Forming Wind Turbines
7
==PAGE:21==
grid. These tests usually refer to set-point changes and frequency control response
of the WPPs.
• WPP model validation during lifetime: Post-commissioning validation based on
real system events is essential for continuously assessing plant compliance. Given
the continuous updates to parameters, control software, as well as changes in the
grid characteristics and equipment hardware, this process can be highly complex
[20].
Traditionally, WT and WPP model validation has relied on comparing simulated results
with measurements from full-scale field testing for various test cases, according to IEC
61400-21-1 [21] (for WTs) and 21-2 [22] (for WPPs). Validation methodologies and pro-
cedures included in IEC 61400-27-2 [23] are widely accepted between OEMs, developers
and TSOs to validate RMS models of WTs and WPPs. Besides, ongoing standardization
efforts under IEC 61400-27-4: ”Structure and validation procedure of Electromagnetic
Transients (EMT) models” aim to define procedures and methodologies for validation of
EMT models.
Nevertheless, the decreasing time-to-market for new WT developments and increasingly
rigorous grid compliance requirements imposed by TSOs to developers, force OEMs to
deliver validated models at early stages, so that developers can start earlier their design
and compliance studies [24]. This creates more challenges for testing solely on a field
full-scale WTG and thus the industry is also adopting earlier-stage validation procedures
based on Components & Subsystems (C&S) testing according to IEC 61400-21-4 [25],
which offer significant advantages in cost, testing time, flexibility and controllability of test-
ing conditions.

2.2
Wind Turbine Functionalities Classification
Current WPPs consist of WTs operating under GFL-control. WTs’ converter controller
includes a fast inner current control loop to regulate the output currents, and a phase-
lock-loop (PLL) to synchronize the WT to the grid. Therefore, the GFL WT relies on the
existing grid to which it is connected and behaves as a current source in the transient time
frame [26]. Although this control methodology has been optimized through robust control
schemes and advanced tuning techniques, it has limitations, especially under weak-grid
conditions [12] and may not be sufficient to ensure grid stability as the share of IBRs in-
creases. GFM technology is proposed as a potential solution to overcome GFL limitations
[27, 28], and is expected to be adopted by future IBRs, including WTs. The draft amend-
ment [29] to the EU Directive 2016/631 [30] stipulates that Power Park Module (PPM)s,
such as WPPs, shall be capable of providing GFM capability at the connection point by
2029, highlighting the voltage source behaviour behind an internal impedance as require-
ment for the individual units.
The core functionality of GFM control is the behaviour as a voltage source behind an
impedance. GFM IBRs set a voltage and frequency at their terminals, which allows them
to operate on an electrical island or when connected to a grid. The voltage phasor re-
mains constant in the transient and sub-transient time frames, contributing inherently to
voltage and frequency support. Most functional requirements typically associated with
GFM units, such as inherent voltage-jump reactive power or phase-jump active power,
are nothing but direct manifestations of their capability to hold the internal voltage phasor
relatively constant immediately after a disturbance [31]. Besides, GFM WTs can be de-
veloped to cover further functionalities such as system black-start, being able to energize
transformers, cables and substations.
In the last years, different TSOs, expert groups and other entities have started to define
requirements on GFM functionalities, mainly targeting IBRs, independent of generation
source or BESS, as shown in Figure 1.2. To these contributions, the latest ENTSO-E
report on technical requirements must be added [14]. A review of different sources on
GFM functionality requirements is presented in [32], where their applicability to WTs is
assessed and functionalities are classified into core functionalities and those that can
also be achieved by GFL WTs.
Building on these functionalities, they are analyzed from the perspective of model valida-
tion for WTs, and their classification is presented in Table 2.1, with the aim of bridging the
current gap and addressing the lack of established requirements for validation strategies

GFL control. These include voltage-source behaviour behind an impedance, islanded op-

2.3.2
Grid Codes
Grid codes, elaborated by TSOs based on standards, industry experience, and feedback
from OEMs and developers, define the national technical requirements for grid compliance
of WTs and WPPs. These documents include validation procedures, and modeling and
accuracy requirements. While most of the grid codes require validated RMS and EMT
models, notable differences remain across countries in terms of validation methodologies,
scope, and error tolerances. Table 2.3 summarizes the test cases required by technical
associations and TSOs in Europe, Asia, the U.S., and Australia. Although these grid
codes generally address validation for IBRs without distinguishing between technologies,
some are more specific –for example, VDE-AR-N 4131 targets HVDC-connected PPMs.
Energinet, VDE, and REE, directly reference international standards such as IEC 61400-
27-2 or FGW TG4 to define their validation procedures and permissible deviations –
particularly for set-point change scenarios and the different stages of FRT events, includ-
ing thresholds for positive and negative sequence components. Others, such as NGESO
and AEMO [60], define their own transient tolerances, typically expressed as a percent-
age of samples that must remain within specified error margins. KEPCO specifies the
same tolerances for transients and steady-state conditions. ERCOT, on the other hand,
requires validation only for EMT models, followed by a benchmark with the RMS model to
ensure the validity of the latter. Tennet NL states the same steady and transient tolerances
for RMS and EMT models.

2.4
Validation Testing
Traditionally, WT model validation has been conducted through field testing using the
full-scale WTs with all their mechanical and electrical components. Figure 2.5 shows
the electrical setup including the FRT test equipment and the external grid to which the
WT connects. The FRT container allows for UVRT and OVRT testing by inserting pro-
grammable shunt impedances, which define the magnitude of the voltage deviation. Field
testing is crucial for assessing the impact of the actual mechanical system and various
components of a WT, alongside the converter and the electrical system. It offers the high-
est fidelity and reliability, but it presents limitations in terms of flexibility, controllability, and
16

==PAGE:30==
the scope of the test cases that can be conducted. Weather conditions, repeated test due
to design modifications and software or parameter updates can delay and prolong vali-
dation campaigns. Besides, certain tests such as single-phase-to-ground faults or phase
jumps, are often impractical or unsafe to conduct on-site [61], reducing the scope of val-
idation. However, these cases are among the most common events happening in the
grid, thus the importance and need to validate that the model can capture correctly the
WT response in such scenarios.

To address these challenges, the wind industry is increasingly adopting C&S testing us-
ing mechanical-, power-, or control-hardware in the loop (mHiL, PHiL, and CHiL), which
focuses on testing only the most relevant parts of the WT. These practices are standard-
ized in IEC 61400-21-4, which defines test benches of varying complexity –from nacelle
to converter and auxiliaries’ test benches– and their applicability to different test cases,
as well as the transferability of results to the full-scale WT testing. Figure 2.6 illustrates a
Converter Test Rig (CTR), which is a PHiL setup in which the WT is represented by its con-
verter hardware, while real-time simulators emulate the mechanical dynamics –including
generator, rotor, structural, and aerodynamic behavior– which should be previously val-
idated for the intended range of dynamic conditions. Similarly, real-time simulators em-
ulate the grid (commonly referred to in the literature as grid emulators), enabling tests
under well-defined grid conditions and variation of grid parameters, generation of com-
plex fault conditions, assessment of response to frequency variations, phase jumps, etc.
The performance and fidelity of such setup is demonstrated in [63].
This approach facilitates early model validation by removing dependencies on uncontrol-
lable external factors such as wind speed or weather conditions. The controlled test en-
vironment allows assessment of voltage and frequency capabilities, harmonics, reactive
power capabilities at various voltages, RoCoF and phase-jumps, and ancillary services
[64]. Growing industrial experience supports the use of these test benches for grid com-
pliance assessment and model validation. In [24], a validation strategy combining field
and CTR measurements is proposed, showing that CTR can enable earlier and more ex-
tensive validation. However, the authors emphasize that the proposed strategy does not
eliminate the need for field testing, but complement it to achieve a more robust and timely
validation approach.
Additionally, Real-Time Simulation (RTS) is emerging as a promising solution to address
several of the outlined challenges. RTS Hardware in the Loop (HiL) setups allow for val-
idation of actual turbine and converter control hardware, while electrical and mechanical

Guidelines on GFM testing and validation remain limited, mainly due to the lack of ex-

2.5
Validation Methodologies
Validation methodologies used to compare simulation models with test measurements are
detailed in IEC 61400-27-2 for RMS models. These are widely adopted across grid codes
and standards, and include two main approaches:
• Playback approach: The WT or WPP model is validated by replaying test voltage
measurements from high-speed recorders into the simulation software. Measure-
ments from field or HiL tests (typically voltage) are injected through an ideal voltage
source, while model outputs (usually active/reactive power or current) are compared
against the corresponding measured values. Figure 2.5 and Figure 2.6 indicate the
playback locations: voltage measurements are injected at MP2 and comparison is
performed at MP1. Thus, this approach isolates the specific unit or plant of inter-
est, which could refer to a single WT, a feeder of WT, or the entire WPP. Playback
is commonly used as a first validation step due to its simple setup, independency
to the external grid, effectiveness for model debugging, and applicability to a wide
20
Investigating Validation Methodologies for EMT Models of Grid-Forming Wind Turbines
==PAGE:34==
range of scenarios (as long as measurements are available). However, it does not
allow to account for interactions with the grid and nearby active devices.
• Full-grid approach: The WT or WPP model is integrated into a full system simu-
lation, including the grid and test equipment, where the same event is reproduced
in the simulation environment. Although this approach is more complex and time-
consuming to set up, it enables validation of all control loops and potential inter-
actions with the grid. However, it introduces additional modeling uncertainty due
to the need to represent components outside the scope of validation (grid and test
equipment models).
Table 2.6 gathers the advantages and disadvantages of both validation methodologies.
OEMs have widely adopted the use of playback, since the scope is on the accuracy of the
model itself. Its application is demonstrated in [61], alongside the full-grid approach, for
WT model validation under unbalanced faults, and in [75], where it is used to validate a
solar farm model against real scenarios. In [76], playback is applied across multiple WT
validation stages, from test benches to full-scale WT level, including life-cycle validation
of WPPs during operation, highlighting challenges related to measurement availability
and location. This latter validation stage is essential for validating model accuracy un-
der disturbances that cannot reasonably be tested during the commissioning phase and
for maintaining model accuracy over time following software updates, parameter adjust-
ments, or even during regular operation, as aging may introduce slight deviations between
the model and the actual plant. Therefore, playback remains as the easiest and most ef-
fective method for indivudual WT and WPP model validation, while full-grid approach can
be used at a later stage to replicate real world events when the WPP is under operation
and the interest relies on system dynamics [20].

2.6
Discussion
This chapter of the thesis emphasizes the importance of building upon established GFL
validation practices to develop a robust framework for GFM WTs model validation. As
discussed in section 2.2, several functionalities are common to both GFL and GFM con-
trol methods and can be validated using current GFL validation strategies. Leveraging
these existing methods allows OEMs to continue using familiar setups, such as the field
test configuration shown in Figure 2.5, which would include only the FRT equipment and
external grid connection. Such setup is sufficient to validate a significant subset of GFM
functionalities shared with GFL, as summarized in Table 2.1. These include FRT validation
via shunt impedance insertion, active and reactive power/voltage controllability through
setpoint changes, and imbalance withstand, for example by enabling/disabling negative
sequence control. While frequency variation testing in this setup may seem challeng-
ing due to the fixed grid frequency, it remains feasible by injecting frequency deviations or
phase jumps into the internal voltage phasor reference of the GFM WT converter controller
[77], as demonstrated in chapter 4 fo the thesis. Combined with the playback validation
methodology introduced in section 2.5, these procedures should be suitable and remain
applicable for validating the electrical models of GFM WTs.
Besides, validation of the simulation model against the full-scale WT response to such
tests (FRT, RoCoF, phase jumps, etc.) in its natural environment, also enables further
analysis in test rigs and offline simulation tools. It represents an important step in realizing
the benefits of CTR testing discussed in this section and enables the use of aero-elastic
simulations to characterize the stochastic response of WTs. The latter further allows engi-
neering choices on how to average across multiple WTs to represent the WPP response
at the Point of Interconnection (PoI) in electrical simulation studies. Note that this is rel-
evant since aggregating accurate and validated models do not guarantee an accurate
response at the WPP’s PoI, especially when wind conditions and mechanical dynamics
come into play, e.g. during inertial response at maximum available power.
Regarding validation of the GFM’s core functionality of behaving as a voltage source be-
hind an impedance, VDE-FNN and ENTSO-E proposals introduce a fictitious islanding
setup requiring switchable load banks. However, this approach is often impractical for
large-scale WTs due to the high power ratings involved and the limited availability and
high cost of large load banks. Moreover, the proposed abnormal grid condition may be
representative only in certain scenarios, such as microgrids or small islanded systems,
but it may seen rather unrealistic for bulk power systems, where it is unlikely that every
single GFM WT could be individually disconnected from all the other GFM resources and
synchronous generators [33]. This scenario is commonly referred to in grid codes as the
22
Investigating Validation Methodologies for EMT Models of Grid-Forming Wind Turbines
==PAGE:36==
“loss of the last synchronous generator” [8, 14, 66, 67, 69, 78]. Some of these references
define the case for assessing voltage source behavior, while others also use it to evaluate
the islanding capability of the GFM unit.
However, voltage source behaviour behind an impedance is the ability of the WT to main-
tain the voltage phasor constant in the transient and sub-transient time frames, contribut-
ing inherently to voltage and frequency support. It is important to note that this property
of GFM relates to the small-signal behaviour of the GFM control. Thus, validation of this
core functionality could be conducted through small-signal response to phase jumps and
voltage disturbances –achievable either through internal control perturbations or via ex-
ternal disturbances at the PoC. The use of large loads and fictitious islanding setups
may add none or a very marginal improvement in the validation process of this function-
ality. Additionally, alternative testing methodologies to verify voltage source behaviour
behind an impedance of GFM units have been proposed [79, 7, 33], which consist on ob-
taining impedance measurements and frequency scans of the reactive and active power
response to voltage and phase perturbations at the grid voltage. Impedance of a GFM
WT presents a lower value around the fundamental frequency due to its voltage source
behaviour and its magnitude over a range of frequencies could be used for model valida-
tion. The reactive and active power frequency responses involve obtaining the transfer
functions from active power to phase angle (or frequency), and reactive power to voltage
magnitude.
Moreover, the use of the same ficticious islanding test case introduced before to assess
islanding capability may not be representative in the context of a bulk power system.
This is because it is unlikely that a single GFM WT would operate in islanded conditions
without the presence of other active devices. In other words, this capability should be
demonstrated at the plant or cluster level, where it must be shown that the combination of
WTs and other active devices can coexist within an electrical island and jointly stabilize
voltage and frequency. Conducting such a test on a full-scale setup would require sub-
stantial effort; therefore, alternative approaches, such as real-time control replicas as per
IEC 61400-21-5, could be considered.
Another topic that remains under discussion is the applicability and transferability of re-
sults from WT test benches. C&S testing seems to be key enabler for obtaining timely
validation of models and achieving robust validation strategies complementing field test-
ing. However, this testing approach relies on novel real-time models capable of repre-
senting the missing parts, including the mechanical system. The influence of the latter
on the electrical response of a GFM WT is more significant than in GFL-equipped WTs.
This is because the inherent functionalities of GFM control –such as the requirement for
a fast active power response similar to that of an ideal voltage source with a reactor– may
induce additional mechanical stresses in the drive train as compared to the WT operating
with GFL control. Therefore, even though recent sources suggest that the same equip-
ment used for FRT tests in GFL converters can also be used to assess GFM properties
[14], it is necessary to identify cases in which the mechanical response contributes signif-
icantly and to validate the test bench against field measurements to ensure an accurate
representation of the missing components.
For example, voltage source behaviour behind an impedance relates to the bandwidth of
the GFM control loop. This means that the property is given from the implemented con-
trol in combination with the electrical system between the converter terminals and the grid
interface point, whereas the WT mechanical system, the environmental conditions such
as wind, or the fast protective converter responses will not have any significant influence.

==PAGE:37==
Thus, this property could be validated and assessed in a HiL setup through small-signal
disturbances (disturbances small enough to avoid pushing the controller outside its linear
operating range), or even assessed in a SiL environment with the actual converter con-
trol code embedded. In the same way, the mentioned testing procedures of impedance
spectroscopy to determine the resonance behaviour and passivity of the GFM WT with
respect to controller interactions, could also be carried out in these setups due to their
small-signal nature.
In general, steady-state performance cases, impedance characteristics, reactive power
or voltage control, and harmonics depend almost entirely on the control of the grid-side
converter and can be validated, if required, using measurements from HiL setups.
Regarding other GFM functionalities such as inertial response, it could be consider as a
converter control implementation if energy provision is low. However, when the inertia
energy provision is higher, it influences the mechanical system of the WT, the WT control,
as well as its operating point. Other cases such as FRT scenarios have similar influence
in the mechanical side as GFL control, what may hint that the same test benches as
specified in IEC 61400-21-4 can be used.
2.7
Conclusion
This chapter presented an overview of the validation procedures for electrical models of
wind turbines (WTs) and wind power plants (WPPs). The requirements and guidelines
from different validation sources such as transmission system operators (TSOs) and the
main national and international standardization bodies are presented, including the WT
and WPP test cases to be validated, model accuracy metrics, and validation methodolo-
gies. Testing setups used for model validation of WTs, such as field testing, component
and subsystem (C&S) test benches and real-time control replicas have been introduced,
while referring to the specifications from grid codes and standards. Validation methodolo-
gies from IEC 61400-27-2 have been presented and their adoption and discussion among
validation sources and industry experience has been included. The section distinguishes
between established validation practices for grid-following (GFL) WTs and WPPs and the
emerging approaches for grid-forming (GFM) units, which are still under development.
Arguments have been made for maintaining existing GFL validation procedures for GFM,
when applicable. In addition, recent proposals for GFM tetsing and validation have been
critically analyzed, considering practical implementation challenges.

3.1.2
Generator-Side Converter Control
The machine-side converter operates in GFL mode, regulating the DC-link voltage and
the reactive power on the generator side. Synchronization is achieved through a PLL,
which aligns the d-axis of the converter’s dq reference frame with the controlled voltage.
The PLL provides the phase angle used to transform voltages and currents from the abc
to the dq frame.
The control scheme follows a cascaded structure. The outer d-axis loop regulates the DC-
link voltage, while the q-axis loop controls the generator-side reactive power. Their outputs
serve as current references for the inner current control loop. Decoupling feedforward
terms are included in the current loop to compensate for the reactor’s inductive reactance.
Limits are enforced both on the magnitude of the current references and on the reference
voltages supplied to the PWM modulation.

3.1.3
Grid-Side Converter Control
The grid-side converter can operate in either GFL or GFM mode. Since this thesis focus on
GFM operation, only the GFM control structure is described here. For reference, the GFL
control scheme is similar to that shown in Figure 3.3, but implemented through sequence
decomposition control.
An schematic of the grid-side converter control is shown in Figure 3.4. Many GFM con-
trol architectures exist in the literature, with some studies comparing their performance
under various grid conditions and disturbances [83, 84, 85], with [86] focusing specifically
in Offshore Wind Power Plant (OWPP). Such comparisons are non-trivial, as they de-
pend strongly on tuning choices, considered prerequisites and tests conditions, which fall
outside the scope of this project.
In this work, a virtual-admittance-based GFM scheme is used, usually know as Virtual
Synchronous Machine (VSM) [13]. This approach is simpler to implement compared to
cascaded voltage–current loops, whose tuning becomes challenging when ensuring sta-
ble operation in parallel with other units. Conversely, including an explicit current controller
provides a straightforward mechanism for reference current limitation, protecting the con-
verter hardware by preventing overcurrent conditions. Sequence decomposition control is
not implemented and as a result, the converter naturally responds to negative-sequence
components during unbalanced events, producing 100 Hz oscillations in the controlled
dq-frame voltages and currents. This behaviour is consistent with that of a synchronous
generator (which GFM aims to mimic) and leads the converter to inject some negative-
sequence current (or impose negative-sequence voltage) provided the current controller
is sufficiently fast.

3.1.3.2
Outer Voltage - Reactive Power control loop
The difference between the reference voltage Vref and the measured voltage Vmeas at the
PoC is processed through a proportional gain Kvq. The resulting signal is then combined
with the reactive power error and passed through a PI controller, which provides the d−axis
voltage reference (virtual back-emf voltage) to the subsequent inner-loop controls, as
seen in Figure 3.4.
The inclusion of the Kvq droop, which links voltage and reactive power, is particularly
advantageous under strong grid conditions where the voltage is stiff and difficult for the
converter to regulate. Without this droop, the controller might continuously add voltage
error in an attempt to track the voltage set-point, eventually driving the converter into its
current limits. Introducing Kvq prevents this by relaxing the tightness of voltage control,
thereby improving robustness and enhancing interoperability with other devices.

3.1.3.5
Current Control Loop
The inner current controller requires a relatively high bandwidth so that, from the per-
spective of the outer loops, the inverter behaves as a controlled voltage source behind
an impedance. The current controller is implemented using PI regulators in the dq frame
and includes standard feedforward decoupling terms.
Since a grid-forming converter must provide fast support during grid transients (such as
inertial response or stabilizing voltage and frequency) the current controller must ensure
low settling time, strong disturbance rejection, and robustness.

4
Testing for Validation of Grid-Forming
Specific Functionalities
This chapter investigates testing methodologies for evaluating GFM functionalities. Both
time-domain and frequency-domain studies are carried out to evaluate if the tests can ef-
fectively characterize voltage source behaviour and inertial response, and provide mean-
ingful measurements for validating EMT models of GFM WTs. The objective is to identify
test methods that can be applied not only in simulation but also transferred to full-scale
field testing and test bench setups such as PHiL and CHiL. The analysis accounts for
the known limitations of WT EMT models in accounting for the mechanical dynamics, as
stated in section 1.4.
4.1
Voltage Source Behaviour
As discussed in chapter 2, the core functionality of GFM resources is their ability to be-
have as a voltage source behind an impedance. Most functional requirements typically
associated with GFM units, such as inherent voltage jump reactive power or phase jump
active power, are therefore direct manifestations of their capability to maintain a nearly
constant internal voltage phasor immediately after a disturbance, i.e., to behave as a near
ideal voltage source behind an internal impedance in the short-time frame [31].
Time-domain and frequency-domain testing methodologies proposed in the literature to
characterize this behaviour were briefly introduced in section 2.6. The aim of this chapter
is to assess their suitability when applied in an EMT environment and to evaluate their po-
tential extrapolation to field testing and test bench setups, which could serve as ”validation
sources” for verifying the accuracy of EMT models.
It is important to emphasize that this section does not aim to establish specific test condi-
tions or prescribe technical requirements. Instead, the objective is to evaluate whether a
given test configuration may provide meaningful insight into the intended GFM function-
ality or performance to be tested and validated.
4.1.1
Time Domain Studies
In section 2.6, it was argued that time-domain testing of voltage source behaviour should
rely on small-signal voltage and phase jump perturbations rather than on fictitious island-
ing setups. This section evaluates the WT’s response to such perturbations, with a par-
ticular focus on whether test signal injections can serve as an equivalent alternative for
performing these tests. This is especially relevant for phase-jump experiments, which
may seem difficult to carry out in field testing environments. In practice, a phase jump
can only be produced by bypassing or inserting the series impedance of the FRT con-
tainer, thereby changing the impedance seen by the WT, but without any precise control
over the perturbation magnitude. Test signal injection, by contrast, could enable con-
trolled phase perturbations and is being increasingly recognized by grid codes such as
VDE [58] and ENTSO-E [14]. Demonstrating the equivalence between injected signals
and real grid perturbations would allow these tests to be performed directly in the WT’s
natural operating environment (field), thereby improving confidence in test benches and
EMT model validation.

signal injection. Besides, the DC-link voltage response is also included as a proof of the
small-signal nature of the test, which would have limited or none impact on the mechanical
dynamics.
These results demonstrate that the WT response to a phase jump perturbation can fea-
sibly be obtained in a field testing setup, provided that the controller grants access for
signal injection. As discussed earlier, this enables the acquisition of valuable measure-
ment data from the actual full-scale WT, which can subsequently support model validation
and further investigations in PHiL, CHiL, or SiL environments.
Regarding the EMT model validation against this test, it is unclear which accuracy metrics
could be used. Potential metrics could include the peak active current, the rise time of
the response, the settling time, or the damping of the oscillations (if any). However, defin-
ing such metrics is challenging without an expected response, as these characteristics
strongly depend on the specific technology and grid conditions. A definition of suitable
validation metrics therefore lies beyond the scope of this thesis.

4.1.2
Frequency Domain Studies
In section 2.6, frequency-domain methods were introduced as an alternative way to test
and verify the voltage-source behaviour of GFM resources. These methods include posi-
tive sequence impedance scans and the evaluation of the P/δ and Q/V transfer functions.
Their main purpose is to complement the time-domain studies presented earlier by pro-
viding additional metrics to quantify voltage source behaviour and enabling the analysis of
the GFM response across a range of perturbation frequencies. Frequency-domain anal-
ysis also allows a clearer distinction between fast and slow dynamic responses, which
can be difficult to separate in time-domain tests, and these methods are increasingly rec-
ognized by TSOs [66, 71, 93]. Although such techniques can also support stability and
damping assessments (for example, using passivity criterion) these aspects fall outside
the scope of this thesis.
Frequency scans can be carried out in CHiL or PHiL setups. In the latter, small-signal
perturbations can be injected using grid emulators [94, 25]. Besides, some works have
shown the use of these testing methods as source for model validation [73]. In this work,
frequency-domain studies are performed in PSCAD, using the Single Machine Infinite
Bus (SMIB) system shown in Figure 4.7, which includes the open-source IMTB toolbox
developed by Energinet [15]. The tool enables both SISO and MIMO impedance scans
in various reference frames and provides the frequency response of the Jacobian matrix,
which is further analyzed in subsubsection 4.1.2.2.

4.2
Synthetic Inertial Response - Signal Injection Test
Synthetic inertia is another capability commonly included in GFM technical specifications.
As noted in section 1.2, GFM resources can emulate the inertial response of synchronous
machines through virtual inertia; however, this is only feasible for certain GFM architec-
tures, typically referred to as synchronous power converters or VSMs [13].
Although synthetic inertia is usually required at WPP level, WT level tests are typically
proposed to characterize the inertial response of individual GFM units. Once the WT
models are validated using these tests, the aggregated inertia contribution of the entire
WPP can be assessed through simulation studies [14].
In this section, the focus is on assessing the suitability of the RoCoF signal injection test,
as proposed by ENTSO-E [14] and visually illustrated in Figure 4.12. Similar to the previ-
ously discussed phase jump test, the RoCoF perturbation produces a temporary deviation
between the grid voltage phasor and the virtual back-emf voltage phasor, relative to the
steady-state angle δ defined by the active power setpoint. The equivalence between both
tests arises because a deviation produced by the acceleration of the grid phasor (per-
turbation case) can be reproduced by decelerating the back-emf voltage phasor (signal
injection case), and vice versa. The simulated RoCoF is intentionally small to highlight
that, for limited inertia energy content, the response is primarily governed by the control
implementation, whereas higher inertia levels would more strongly excite the mechanical
dynamics. Consequently, such tests can be readily performed in PHiL or CHiL setups.

4.3
Conclusion
This chapter evaluated emerging testing methodologies for characterizing voltage-source
behaviour and synthetic inertia of GFM WTs, with the aim of determining whether these
tests can provide meaningful validation data for EMT model validation and be transferred
to field or HiL environments.
Regarding voltage source behaviour, time-domain studies showed that phase jump in-
jections replicate grid phase perturbations, confirming that controlled signal injection can
serve as a practical alternative to conduct these tests in the field. Small-signal voltage
steps also exhibited an equivalent reactive current response, although active power devi-
ations differed due to the intrinsic discrepancy between increasing the back-emf voltage
(injection) and decreasing the grid voltage (disturbance). These limitations are inherent
but do not prevent the use of voltage injections for validating short-time reactive current
behaviour against voltage jumps.
Frequency-domain studies demonstrated that GFM units exhibit the expected low impedance
at 50 Hz and negative gain in the P/δ and Q/V transfer functions within the short-time
window where GFMs should inherently react to voltage and phase deviations. However,
the response is highly sensitive to the control implementation, particularly the current
controller and virtual admittance, meaning that the simplified representation of a “volt-
age source behind a reactance” requires caution when frequency-domain analyses are
conducted.
For synthetic inertia, the RoCoF injection test produced an active current response con-
sistent with analytical expectations and equivalent to applying the disturbance at the PoC.
The negligible impact on the DC-link voltage further suggests that small inertial response
testing is already feasible in PHiL and CHiL environments.
Overall, the studies indicate that test signal injections is a viable and representative method
for field testing and model validation of key GFM functionalities, especially for phase and
Investigating Validation Methodologies for EMT Models of Grid-Forming Wind Turbines

RoCoF perturbations. Impedance- and Jacobian-based frequency-domain tests may pro-
vide additional insight, but their interpretation is strongly dependent on control design and
requires further investigation and knowledge than what is included here before being used
as model validation methods.

Playback as Validation Methodology for
Grid-Forming Wind Turbines
Validation methodologies refer to the procedures used in simulation to assess the ac-
curacy of a model by comparing its response against measurements obtained from the
actual system. This chapter focuses on evaluating the suitability of the playback valida-
tion methodology for GFM WT models in an EMT environment, describing the studies
performed and the assumptions involved. Playback has been extensively applied in the
validation of synchronous machines and IBR models equipped with GFL control, including
WTs and WPPs. Therefore, as highlighted in chapter 2, it is of significant interest to de-
termine whether this methodology remains applicable to GFM technology. If so, existing
industry and academic practices could serve as a foundation for developing a structured
validation framework for GFM WTs and WPPs.
5.1
Playback Validation Methodology
The assessment of the applicability of playback methodology to the case of a WT equipped
with GFM control is conducted without the use of real measurements taken from high-
speed recorders from WT testing or grid real disturbances. Instead, two simulation se-
tups have been proposed to emulate the scenario of model validation through field testing
during the design phase of a new WT product. The first setup, considers the WT model
connected to the FRT container test equipment and the public grid at Østerild test center,
whose topologies were described concisely in subsection 3.2.2 and 3.3. In this setup,
the instantaneous three phase voltage values are recorded at the electrical points MP1
and MP2. The MP2 measurements are reproduced as ideal single-phase voltage sources
in the second setup, to which the WT model is connected, as shown in Figure 5.1. The
measurements taken at MP1 are used for obtaining the sequence components of the volt-
age, current and powers, which are used for validation between both models, according
to FGW [34] standard and several grid codes.
Throughout this section, following the terminology introduced in section 2.5, the setup
that replicates the field-testing environment is referred to as the ”full-grid approach”, as it
includes a model of the WT, the grid, and the testing equipment. In contrast, the setup
used to study the playback methodology is referred to as the ”playback approach”, since
it emulates the validation process using measurement data obtained from the full-system
topology.
The FRT container is configured to apply both three-phase and two-phase-to-ground
faults. Measurements at MP2 were recorded in .out files using PSCAD output chan-
nels. Initially, the sampling rate was set equal to the simulation time step (10 µs), which
resulted in a perfect match between the full-grid and playback setups when replaying the
measurements at MP2 in the playback setup. This behaviour was expected, as the WT
model (identical in both configurations) experiences exactly the same voltage waveform
at every simulation step.
However, such a high sampling rate is not representative of real measurement devices,
which typically operate in the range of 10–40 kHz. Therefore, the sampling rate was
subsequently reduced to 10 kHz (100 µs). Under these conditions, some deviation be-
tween full-grid and playback responses was expected, since the WT model runs at 10 µs

5.2
Discussion and Conclusion
This section shows that playback remains a valid model validation methodology when
moving from GFL to GFM control. Its applicability has been demonstrated for both FRT
and islanding scenarios. Although other cases such as phase jumps, RoCoF, voltage
steps, or active/reactive power controllability were not analyzed here, playback is ex-
pected to perform equally well in those situations. In fact, playback is particularly useful
for accurate replication of grid effective impedance and conditions, which may vary over
time and strongly influence the response of a GFM WT.
It must be emphasized, however, that the studies conducted in this chapter compare the
model against itself (model-to-model comparison). In a real validation process, the model
response is compared against field measurements, which involve additional sources of
uncertainty: impedance tolerances, frequency-dependent behaviour of equipments, measurement-
device accuracy and resolution limitations, transformer saturation effects, data post-processing,
etc. All of these factors are difficult to model accurately or may affect the performance of

==PAGE:67==
the EMT model in simulation. Moreover, while the same WT model is used in both setups
here, real validation compares the model against the actual WT. If the model does not ac-
curately represent the physical turbine, the replayed waveform may no longer reflect the
correct grid conditions for that model, potentially increasing the mismatch during playback
validation [97].
Finally, the analysis performed here focuses exclusively on validation during full-scale WT
testing at dedicated test centers. The complexity may be lower when validating the model
against test benches, which eliminate uncontrollable factors that are difficult to model in
simulation environments, thereby providing a more easily replicable setup.
Ultimately, when moving to validation during operation, challenges such as accounting for
controller interactions or interoperability issues may be encountered when considering the
use of playback for model validation at that stage. However, these aspects fall outside
the scope of this thesis.

6
Sensitivity Studies on Model Validation
Methodologies
As pointed out in subsection 3.2.2, the field testing setup includes cables and transform-
ers between the WT, the FRT container, and the external grid. These components, as
well as the FRT container, inevitably exhibit manufacturing tolerances, and their actual
electrical parameters may differ from the nominal values typically used when modeling in
EMT simulation. In practice, uncertainties in cable impedance, transformer parameters
and saturation, frequency-dependent behaviours or temperature effects can significantly
influence the results of the model validation process [20].
To evaluate the impact of such modeling uncertainty, two fictitious scenarios are consid-
ered in this section. These scenarios assess how deviations in component parameters
affect the outcome of both validation methodologies introduced in section 2.5, namely the
full-grid and playback methodologies.
1. Sensitivity in the full-grid methodology: Impedance tolerances between −10%
and +10% are introduced in the FRT container and grid model. This scenario il-
lustrates how deviations in the modeled test equipment and grid can affect the WT
model validation outcome compared to the reference case (0% tolerance) which is
considered to include the true value of the passive components.
2. Sensitivity in the playback methodology: A tolerance is applied to the WT trans-
former impedance when simulating the field testing setup (“full-grid approach” in
Figure 5.1). The resulting measurements are then used in a playback setup where
the nominal transformer value (0%) is assumed instead. This scenario represents
the realistic situation in which the true transformer impedance differs slightly from
the manufacturer’s specified value, which is the one usually considered as a first
approach in EMT modeling.
6.1
Sensitivity in Full-Grid Validation Methodology
Since the combination of tolerances in the different passive components of the FRT con-
tainer, cables and the external grid can be many and random, just a couple of repre-
sentative cases are shown here: tolerances effect on the impedance to fault of the FRT
container, and a ”worst-case” scenario of same tolerances in all passive components up to
the connection to the external grid (i.e. cables, step up 33kV/150kV transformer and T-line
model, according to subsection 3.2.2). Note, that the effect of these results could be thus
”accumulative” and eventually lead to a bigger mismatch compared to the actual system,
which in this case is assumed to be represented when tolerance is 0%. In both cases, a
three-phase-to-ground fault was simulated, thus only positive sequence components are
shown in Figure 6.1 and 6.2. Note that all the variables of the plots are measured at the
LV terminal of the WT, i.e. where the WT controls power and voltage.
The results in Figure 6.1 show the expected influence of the FRT container impedance
tolerance on the fault response, with no influence on the steady state response. When the
fault impedance is reduced (–10%), the retained voltage at the WT terminals decreases,
making the fault more severe; the opposite occurs for a positive tolerance deviation. As
a direct consequence of the lower terminal voltage, both active power and active cur-
rent decrease with respect to the nominal (0%) case and the reaction in reactive current

the +10% impedance case, where higher retained voltage yields higher active power and

Sensitivity in Playback Validation Methodology
In this study case, a tolerance of +7.5% is applied to the transformer impedance of the
WT unit in the fiel testing setup, or “full-grid approach” in Figure 5.1. The resulting three-

transformer impedance is kept at its nominal value. The error e(t) = yplayback(t)−yfull grid(t)

rates and grid strengths, as introduced in the previous section. Since the event is a three-
phase-to-ground fault, positive sequence quantities are presented.
During steady state operation, the voltage deviation between both setups remains rather
small, as the tolerance is applied to the transformer, which is located to the right of the
measurement point of the WT and towards the grid. The WT regulates the voltage but
not tightly, since a droop with reactive power exist in the control (see Figure 3.4). Due to
the regulated voltage not differing significantly between the setups (Upos close to 0 pu),
and the voltage at the MV side of the transformer being the same in both setups (the play-
back setup reproduces the MV measurements obtained in the full-grid setup), the reactive
power increases in the case of lower transformer impedance, that is, the playback case.
This explains the positive delta observed in reactive current and power during the steady
state pre-fault and post-fault time windows. The same positive deviation in reactive power
explains the negative delta in voltage (droop implementation). In the same steady-state
periods, active power remains nearly unchanged in both setups because the transformer
resistance is relatively small. Any deviations in active current are therefore explained by
the small voltage deviations seen which result in essentially the same active power.
During the FRT response, the voltage deviation between both setups becomes larger.
This is consistent with the fact that both cases impose the same retained voltage at the
fault location, but the lower transformer impedance in the playback setup leads to a lower
LV voltage in the playback case, making the error (yplayback(t)−yfull grid(t)) more negative.

6.3
Conclusions
This section assessed how tolerances in the passive components of the field testing
setup influence the outcome of model validation studies. The results show that typical
impedance tolerances in the grid, test equipment, or transformer can introduce deviations
up to approximately −2.5% to +2.5% in voltage, currents and powers. These deviations
are due solely to parameter tolerances; additional uncertainties such as aging, unmodeled
frequency dependencies, or nonlinearities may further increase the discrepancy between
the simulated and real behaviour of these elements.
Because the objective of the validation process is to assess the accuracy of the WT model
(not the surrounding grid or test equipment) playback is generally the recommended ap-
proach during the model design and validation phase, as it removes uncertainty from
external components. However, for system level studies, playback may not always be
suitable, since relevant interactions involving multiple grid active devices cannot be repro-
duced through replayed voltages. For this reason, as suggested in [97], accuracy should
ideally be verified using playback for component-level or unit-level model validation, and
using full-grid simulations when system-level interactions are of interest. Ultimately, en-
gineering judgment and experience remain essential, as there are examples of cases
where playback validation of WPPs has achieved satisfactory alignment with real fault
events[76].
Regarding inherent tolerances WT unit passive components, such as in transformers,
these cannot be avoided. In practice, steady-state power-flow adjustments may be needed
to tune the transformer impedance of the model to match the measured operating points
before performing any FRT-based validation. This mismatches can be identify in simula-
tion results as an offset in the reactive power response.

Conclusions & Future Work
The main objective of this thesis was to investigate and assess validation methodologies
for EMT models of Grid-Forming (GFM) Wind Turbines (WTs). In chapter 2, the work
began by reviewing the current validation practices applied to Grid-Following (GFL) con-
trol. This review covered the requirements set by standards and grid codes regarding
the test cases and functionalities to be validated, the testing setups used as “validation
sources”, and the two main validation methodologies employed in industry: playback and
full-grid. In addition, the most recent proposals on GFM testing and model validation
were critically analyzed with two aims: first, to examine the extent to which established
GFL testing setups and validation practices remain applicable to GFM technology; and
second, to identify those cases that are specific to GFM and therefore require new test-
ing and validation methods. FRT capability, active/reactive power and voltage control or
reactive power capability may require similar validation procedures, while complete vali-
dation framework for GFM resources must also incorporate additional methods tailored to
their specific capabilities, such as voltage-source behaviour, islanding or synthetic inertial
response. A summary of these discussions is presented in Table 7.1, which classifies the
main test cases for which model validation may be required, together with the applicable
testing setups and validation methodologies.
It is expected that this table will continue to evolve and expand as more industry expe-
rience is gained. A strong emphasis is placed on identifying which parts of the WT are
relevant for each specific test and to what extent they influence the overall response.
While field testing remains the most reliable setup for model validation, it is limited in
scope, repeatability and flexibility. For this reason, alternative setups that combine a re-
duced physical representation of the WT with high-fidelity real-time models of the missing
components may offer a promising path to accelerate the validation process, broaden the
range of validation cases, and increase testing flexibility.
In chapter 4, time-domain studies demonstrated that test-signal injection is a practical ap-
proach for evaluating key GFM functionalities. Phase jump and RoCoF injections closely
reproduced the responses obtained under actual grid perturbations or disturbances, sup-
porting their suitability for field testing, where they can serve as a source of high-quality
validation measurements for further investigations in HiL and SiL environments. Small-
signal voltage injections produced reactive current responses equivalent to those obtained
from grid-based perturbations, although differences in active power behaviour highlighted
the intrinsic distinction between modifying the internal back-emf voltage of the converter
and applying an external voltage dip in the grid.
Frequency-domain studies included positive sequence impedance scans and Jacobian
transfer functions aimed at assessing voltage source behaviour. The impedance results
confirmed expected GFM characteristics, such as reduced impedance around the funda-
mental frequency, while the negative gains observed in the P/δ and Q/V transfer func-
tions over the short-time frame frequency range further illustrated the inherent voltage and
frequency stabilising behaviour of GFM units. However, these tests proved highly sensi-
tive to control implementation and tuning, particularly to the inclusion of current control and
virtual admittance loops. Consequently, although frequency-domain methods are valu-
able diagnostic tools and allow for easier quantification of the voltage source behaviour
property, their use for model validation may require further methodological development
and experience.

65
==PAGE:79==
The thesis also evaluated the suitability of the playback methodology for GFM model vali-
dation. The results confirmed that playback remains applicable to GFM WTs, successfully
reproducing the behaviour observed in both FRT and islanding scenarios. Nevertheless,
further studies based on real field measurements are needed to fully support this con-
clusion, particularly considering challenges such as weather variability, component toler-
ances, nonlinearities, or frequency dependent effects. Moreover, the adoption of playback
in EMT environments generally exposes a mismatch between the measurement sampling
rate and the simulation time step. This issue was observed to become more pronounced
in weak-grid conditions and ongoing work under IEC 61400-27-4 is expected to address
part of this gap by advancing the standardization of validation practices for EMT model
validation.
Finally, the study on modeling uncertainty through passive component tolerances showed
that deviations of up to ±5% between edge scenarios can be expected when tolerances
in the grid and test equipment are considered. These findings reinforce that playback is
generally the preferred method for WT-level model validation, as it eliminates uncertain-
ties associated with the external grid and test equipment and ensures that the validation
focuses on the WT itself. In addition, inherent mismatches in WT components, such as
transformer impedance tolerances, may require preliminary parameter fitting to enable a
fair comparison between model and measurements. Conversely, although not covered
in the simulation studies, full-grid setups may remain necessary when studying system-
level interactions that cannot be reproduced through playback, particularly in cases where
using real measurements is difficult or the interest lies in capturing grid-dynamic phenom-
ena.
While this work has primarily focused on testing and validation methodologies, accuracy
metrics must also be defined to quantitatively assess model performance against mea-
surements from the actual WT or against reduced representations used in test benches.
Recent proposals from VDE were briefly described in chapter 2. Some of these metrics
may already be suitable for adoption, whereas others relate to the expected GFM re-
sponse against a given disturbance, something that remains highly dependent on both
specific technology and grid conditions. As a consequence, the definition of expected
responses and associated tolerances remains under discussion between the different in-
dustry stakeholders.

Abstract
The rapid growth of large-scale wind power plants (WPPs) and other inverter-based resources (IBRs) is significantly transform-
ing and impacting power system design and operation, introducing new complexities and challenges. In this context, high-fidelity
models that replicate the electrical behaviour of the actual plant are critical to ensuring secure and stable grid operation, increasing
the demand for validation of electrical models against the actual component. Although validation techniques for grid-following
(GFL) wind turbines (WTs) are well established, grid-forming (GFM) technology introduces functionalities that current valida-
tion practices may not fully address. Therefore, it is necessary to assess to what extent GFL validation techniques are suitable to
validate GFM technology. This paper reviews the requirements of grid codes, standards, and expert groups on validating WT and
WPP simulation models, outlines the WT test setups used in practice, and presents the validation methodologies currently used
to assess the accuracy of models. The paper finally discusses the few recent proposals on the validation of GFM WT models and
aims to bridge the methodological gap between existing validation practices and the validation of emerging GFM capabilities,
offering a foundational perspective for original equipment manufacturers (OEMs), transmission system operators (TSOs) and
industry working groups to shape future validation frameworks for GFM controls in WTs.
1
Introduction
As inverter-based resources (IBRs) such as wind power
plants (WPPs) become increasingly dominant in power sys-
tems, their accurate modeling in simulation studies becomes
essential to assess their performance across a wide range of
operating conditions and disturbances.
Wind turbine (WT) simulation models are developed by
original equipment manufacturers (OEMs) during the WT
development phase and are utilized throughout the design,
commissioning, and operation of WPPs. These models aim to
replicate the electrical behaviour of WTs in the time domain,
including detailed representations of electrical components
and control systems. Converter and WT controllers are com-
monly implemented via embedded Dynamic Link Libraries
(DLLs), enabling high-fidelity simulation with the actual con-
trol behaviour [1]. In addition to WT models, WPP models
must include detailed representations of other components such
as cables, transformers, surge arresters, etc.
Grid codes, along with national and international standards,
define the modeling requirements, including test plans, valida-
tion procedures, and model accuracy criteria. As illustrated in
Fig. 1, validation is carried out at different stages during the
life cycle to guarantee the validity of the models.
Traditionally, WT and WPP model validation has relied
on comparing simulated results with measurements from full-
scale field testing for various test cases, according to IEC
61400-21-1 [2] and 21-2 [3]. Validation methodologies and
procedures included in IEC 61400-27-2 [4] are widely accepted
between OEMs, developers and transmission system operators
(TSOs) to validate Root-Mean Square (RMS) models of WTs
and WPPs. Besides, ongoing standardization efforts under IEC
61400-27-4 aim to define validation procedures for Electro-
magnetic Transient (EMT) models. Nevertheless, the decreas-
ing time-to-market for new WT developments and increasingly
rigorous grid compliance requirements, force OEMs to deliver
validated models at early stages [5]. Thus, the industry is also
adopting earlier-stage validation methods based on components
and subsystems (C&S) testing according to IEC 61400-21-4
[6], which offer significant advantages in cost, testing time,
flexibility and controllability of testing conditions.
On the other hand, with the ongoing transition towards
a converter-dominated power system, TSOs are increasingly
requesting new control functionalities and ancillary services
from WPPs, such as grid-forming (GFM) capability. However,
standardized requirements for testing and model validation of
emerging GFM controls remain under development. Given that
well established practices and experience exist for existing WT
control methods such as grid-following (GFL), it is essential

to evaluate to what extent those established validation method-
ologies remain applicable to GFM WTs and WPPs. Expertise

This paper presents a review of existing validation require-

national grid codes, while also addressing emerging proposals

the validation methodologies standardized in IEC 61400-27-2,
including their adoption across national grid codes. Finally, a
discussion is made on recent GFM model validation propos-
als, challenges and recommendations for pushing towards a
robust, reliable and effective model validation strategy for WTs
equipped with GFM control.

Wind Turbine Functionalities
Current WPPs consist of WTs operating under GFL-control.
WTs’ converter controller includes a fast inner current con-
trol loop to regulate the output currents, and a phase-lock-loop
(PLL) to synchronize the WT to the grid. Therefore, the WT
behaves as a current source in the transient time frame [7].
Although this methodology has been optimized through robust
control schemes and advanced tuning techniques, it has limita-
tions, especially under weak-grid conditions [8] and may not be
sufficient to ensure grid stability as the share of IBRs increases.
GFM technology is proposed as a potential solution to over-
come GFL limitations [9] [10], and is expected to be adopted
by future IBRs, including WTs. The draft amendment [11]
to the EU Directive 2016/631 [12] stipulates that power park
modules (PPMs), such as WPPs, shall be capable of providing
GFM capability at the connection point by 2029, highlighting
the voltage source behaviour behind an internal impedance as
requirement for the individual units.
The core functionality of GFM control is the behaviour as a
voltage source behind an impedance. GFM IBRs set a voltage
and frequency at their terminals, which allows them to operate
on an electrical island or when connected to a grid. The volt-
age phasor remains constant in the transient and sub-transient
time frames, contributing inherently to voltage and frequency
support. Besides, GFM WTs can be developed to cover further
functionalities such as damping active power and eventually
system black-start, being able to energize transformers, cables
and substations.
In the last years, different TSOs, expert groups and other
entities have started to define requirements on GFM functional-
ities, mainly targeting IBRs, independent of generation source
or battery energy storage systems (BESS). A review of differ-
ent sources on GFM functionality requirements is presented in
[13], where their applicability to WTs is assessed and classi-
fied into core functionalities and those that can also be achieved
by GFL WTs. Building on this, these functionalities have been
considered from the perspective of electrical model validation
of WTs, and their classification is presented in Table 1, with
the aim of bridging the current gap and addressing the lack
of established requirements for validation strategies of GFM
WTs. This classification seeks to identify functionalities that
are common to both GFL and GFM modes, and that can be
tested and validated using the same approaches already applied
to GFL, which will be discussed in detail in later sections.
Even if the control implementations differ –such as inertia pro-
vision being "fast" in GFM and slow-synthetic in GFL, both
of them fulfill the same functionality and the associated test-
ing methods and validation procedures from GFL WTs may
remain applicable to GFM WTs. Another example is validation
against Fault-Ride-Through (FRT) tests, where both control
modes could be tested in the field or in test rigs using the same
equipment and validated with the same approaches outlined in
Section 4 and 5.

3
Validation Requirements Review
The process of model validation involves multiple stakehold-
ers. Standards outline general strategies and methodologies for
conducting validation, which are widely adopted by TSOs and
national authorities. However, these entities often supplement
the standards with additional national or grid-specific require-
ments. Developers and OEMs must comply with the validation
requirements set out in grid codes issued by TSOs. These
requirements typically specify the required model types, val-
idation test cases, delivery timelines, and acceptable accuracy
tolerances. Besides, in some countries, such as Spain and Ger-
many, certification bodies also play a key role in the process.
When unit certificates are required by national regulations, cer-
tification bodies review the model validation reports –or, in
some cases, the raw test data and the model itself– to assess its
compliance with the validation criteria and issue a model cer-
tificate. Thus, OEMs and developers must carefully optimize
their validation strategies while ensuring compliance with the
diverse requirements of the different markets they target, what
entails demanding validation campaigns.
3.1 Standards
Standards play a key role defining validation methodologies
and measures to assess simulation model accuracy. Table 2
summarizes the required test cases to be validated accord-
ing to standards, categorized by WTs and WPPs. Although
not explicitly stated, it can be inferred from the requirements
that those generally apply to GFL control. IEC 61400-27-2
focuses specifically on WT and WPP model validation, while
the remaining standards address IBRs more generally. ENTSO-
E specifications are also included due to their strong influence
on national regulations.
3.1.1 IEC 61400-27-2: addresses the validation of WT and
WPP electrical models in accordance with IEC 61400-21-1
and 21-2, respectively. However, the validation procedure for
WPPs is not extensive. The standard applies only to RMS mod-
els and defines time windows (pre-fault, fault, and post-fault)
and error metrics (maximum, mean, and mean absolute errors)
for assessing model accuracy in FRT cases. Additionally, step-
response criteria are specified for control performance test
cases. These quantitative measures are widely adopted by other
standards and national grid codes, which typically define their
own threshold values.

according to FGW TG3 [20]. As per FGW TG8 [21], it requires
a model certificate to be issued by an authorized certification
body. Validation test cases in Table 2 includes reactive power
control, where optional control modes – Q(U) or Q(P) con-
trol – must be validated if they are measured and are within
the certification scope. The standard also requires validation of
unit protection settings and comparison of harmonics’ magni-
tude and impedance with the EMT model. Tolerances for FRT

3.3 Validation Requirements on Grid-Forming Units
Guidelines for model validation specific to GFM IBRs
remain very limited. The need for extensive field testing clashes
with the lack of established grid codes by TSOs on GFM func-
tionalities and testing, who in turn depend on further field
experience to develop their requirements [38]. As a result, reg-
ulatory frameworks and industry practices for GFM remain
in early development. Existing guidance is mostly generic
to IBRs, with limited focus on GFM WTs, whereas BESS
have more advanced test and validation procedures. Validation
requirements are predominantly defined at the generating unit
level. Table 4 summarizes GFM model validation cases from
different sources, some specific to BESS and others general to
IBRs, PPMs, or HVDC systems.
Validation efforts for GFM units focus on core function-
alities such as voltage source behaviour, continuous voltage
support, and active power synchronization. A significant step
towards establishing GFM model validation requirements has
been taken in Germany, where VDE-FNN has recently pro-
posed a market-based introduction of GFM technology. The
FNN notice [39] provides the technical foundation for an
incentive system aimed at procuring GFM capabilities, placing
simulation model validation at the center of the certification
process, and specifying the validation test cases in Table 4.
Model accuracy requirements in this notice are defined
through different tolerances, depending on the nature of each
test. These include pre-event, event and post-event tolerances
for assessing the accuracy of the model’s response. Some test
cases –such as those for primary frequency control, inertia
provision, and attenuation of frequency-power oscillations–
include specific tolerances on the frequency and damping char-
acteristics of transient oscillations. Others, such as over/under
VRT, voltage setpoint change or response to small voltage
perturbations at the point of connection (PoC), focus on set-
tling time and model response accuracy of the reactive cur-
rent. A noteworthy case is the phase jump ride-through test,
which includes asymmetric tolerances depending on the direc-
tion of the phase jump—distinguishing between offered and
non-offered directions. Ultimately, some test cases –RoCoF
robustness and fast OVRT protection– do not define explicit
numerical tolerances but instead focus on verifying correct
model implementation. Other sources in Table 4 do not specify
tolerances.

magnitude of the voltage deviation. While field testing offers
the highest fidelity and reliability, it presents limitations in
terms of flexibility, controllability, and the scope of the test
cases that can be conducted. Weather conditions, repeated test
due to design modifications and software or parameter updates
can delay and prolong validation campaigns. Besides, certain
tests, such as single-phase-to-ground faults or phase jumps, are
often impractical or unsafe to conduct on-site [42], reducing the
scope of validation. However, these cases are among the most
common events happening in the grid, thus the importance and
need to validate that the model can capture correctly the WT
response in such scenarios.
To address these challenges, the wind industry is increas-
ingly adopting C&S testing using mechanical-, power-, or
control-hardware in the loop (mHiL, PHiL, and CHiL), which
focuses on testing only the most relevant parts of the WT.
These practices are standardized in IEC 61400-21-4 [6], which
defines test benches of varying complexity –from nacelle to
converter and auxiliaries’ test benches– and their applicabil-
ity to different test cases, as well as the transferability of
results to full-scale WT testing. Fig. 3 illustrates a converter
test rig (CTR), where the WT is represented by its converter
hardware, while real-time simulators emulate the mechani-
cal dynamics (previously validated for the applied range of
dynamic conditions) and the grid, making it easy to apply volt-
age dips, frequency variations, and other grid disturbances. The
performance and fidelity of this setup are demonstrated in [43].
This approach facilitates early model validation by removing
dependencies on uncontrollable external factors such as wind
speed or weather conditions. The controlled test environment
allows assessment of voltage and frequency capabilities, har-
monics, reactive power control at various voltages, and GFM
functionalities [44]. Growing industrial experience supports
the use of these test benches for grid compliance assessment
and model validation. In [5], a validation strategy combining
field and CTR measurements is proposed, showing that CTR
can enable earlier and more extensive validation. However, the
authors emphasize that the proposed strategy does not elimi-
nate the need for field testing, but complement it to achieve a
more robust and timely validation approach.
Additionally, real-time simulation (RTS) is emerging as a
promising solution to address several of the outlined chal-
lenges. RTS HiL setups allow for validation of actual control
hardware, while software in the loop (SiL) offers a similar
scope by embedding the control software directly into the
simulator, reducing hardware requirements. These approaches
support early validation of electrical models of WTs. In addi-
tion, they broaden the verification scope for WPPs, where
commissioning field tests are typically limited to set point
changes and post-commissioning validation relies on the occur-
rence of real grid events. Ongoing work under IEC 61400-21-5
aims to standardize these setups for both WTs and WPPs.

Guidelines on GFM testing and validation remain limited,
mainly due to the lack of experience. Several TSOs have pro-

GFM cores capabilities of BESS or more generically IBRs,
relying on accurate and validated models [46], [47], [48],
[49], [50]. Besides, experience exists with testing GFM BESS
resources at PHiL test benches [38], [51]. Concerning WTs,
[52] carries out the testing of a Type III WT equipped with
GFM control in a PHiL electrical generator test bench, includ-

from ENTSO-E [45] and the VDE-FNN notice [39], with
ongoing work by CENELEC (CLC/prTS 50744-1) to further

the use of test benches. ENTSO-E outlines test cases to verify
GFM functionalities through field testing, simulations, or test
benches –depending on the TSO’s requirements. Similarly, the
VDE-FNN notice provides validation guidance and mandates
field testing of GFM WTs with all associated components,
while also acknowledging the future role of M-HiL, P-HiL, and
C-HiL test rigs, which are not yet included. Both documents
propose comparable setups as the one seen in Fig. 2, involving
load banks and define two main test configurations: fictitious
islanding and synchronous operation. The fictitious island-
ing configuration is used for validation of the voltage source
behaviour and primary frequency reserve provision according
to VDE-FNN. The test involves transitioning of the WT opera-
tion from grid-connected to islanded mode, supplying a defined
local load via a switchable load bank. Model validation is based
on comparing the model response to the WT’s ability to stabi-
lize after the transition, focusing on oscillations’ frequency and
damping during of the transient. The synchronous operation
setup is used to evaluate performance under conditions such
as phase jumps, FRT, voltage controllability, RoCoF response,
and the attenuation of frequency-power oscillations. Some of
these tests can be conducted either by manipulating the inter-
nal controls of the GFM unit (e.g., injecting an angle offset into
the internal voltage phasor) or by adjusting external grid con-
ditions at the point of connection using grid emulators or FRT
equipment.
5
Model Validation Methodologies
Validation methodologies used to compare simulation mod-
els with test measurements are detailed in IEC 61400-27-2 for
RMS models. These are widely adopted across grid codes and
standards, and include two main approaches:
•
Playback approach: The WT or WPP model is validated
by replaying test voltage measurements into the simulation.
Measurements from field or HiL tests (typically voltage) are
injected through an ideal voltage source, while model out-
puts (usually active/reactive power or current) are compared
against the corresponding measured values. Fig. 2 and Fig. 3
indicate the playback locations: voltage measurements are
injected at MP2 and comparison is performed at MP1. Play-
back is commonly used as a first validation step due to
its simple setup, independency to the external grid, effec-
tiveness for model debugging, and applicability to a wide
range of scenarios (as long as measurements are available).
However, it does not allow for interactions with the grid.
•
Full-grid approach: The WT or WPP model is integrated
into a full system simulation, including the grid and test
equipment, where the same event is reproduced in the
simulation environment. Although this approach is more
complex and time-consuming to set up, it enables validation
of all control loops and potential interactions with the grid.
However, it introduces additional modeling uncertainty due
to the need to represent components outside the scope of
validation –grid and test equipment models.
OEMs have widely adopted the use of playback, since the
scope is on the accuracy of the model itself. Its application is
demonstrated in [42], alongside the full-grid approach, for WT
model validation under unbalanced faults, and in [54], where it
is used to validate a solar farm against real scenarios. In [55],
playback is applied across multiple WT validation stages –from
test benches to full-scale level– including life-cycle validation
of WPPs during operation, highlighting challenges related to
measurement availability and location. This latter validation
stage is key for maintaining model accuracy along time follow-
ing software updates, parameter adjustments, or even regular
operation, as aging may introduce slight deviations between the
model and the actual plant.
Regarding adoption in standards and grid codes, IEC 61400-
27-2 permits playback in RMS models for all test cases. FGW
TG4 particularly recommends its use for EMT model valida-
tion, to map the frequency of the disturbance precisely. EG
ISSM recommends playback for FRT cases but declares that it
may not be suitable for weak grid conditions due to its inability
to represent interactions with the grid. In IEEE 2800.2/D3.2,
the proposed simulation setup for validation is based on a full
system representation, with no mention of playback for unit
model validation. However, it strongly recommends playback
for post-commissioning validation of IBR plant models, since
it is a simple way to account for the contribution of nearby
active devices and effective grid impedance while not depend-
ing on TSOs models, which may be unavailable because of
confidentially matters with nearby grid users.
Regarding GFM unit validation methodologies, Fingrid
requires playback-based validation of BESS models using post-
commissioning disturbances. In contrast, VDE-FNN mandates
replication of the full test setup in the simulation environment,
thus requiring the full-grid approach.
6
Discussion
This paper emphasizes the importance of building upon
established GFL validation practices to develop a robust frame-
work for GFM model validation. As discussed in Section 2,
several functionalities are common to both GFL and GFM
control methods and can be validated using current GFL val-
idation strategies. Leveraging these existing methods allows
OEMs to continue using familiar setups, such as the field test
configuration shown in Fig. 2, which would include only the
FRT equipment and external grid connection. Such setup is
sufficient to validate a significant subset of GFM function-
alities shared with GFL, as summarized in Table 1. These
include FRT validation via shunt impedance insertion, active
and reactive power controllability through setpoint changes,
and imbalance withstand, for example by enabling/disabling
negative sequence control. While frequency variation testing
7
==PAGE:100==
24th Wind & Solar Integration Workshop | Berlin, Germany | 07-10 October 2025
in the field may seem challenging due to the fixed grid fre-
quency, it remains feasible by injecting frequency deviations
or phase jumps into the internal voltage phasor reference of the
GFM WT controller [56]. Combined with the playback valida-
tion methodology introduced in Section 5, these procedures are
suitable for validating the GFM WT electrical model.
Validation of the model against the full-scale WT response
to such tests in its natural environment, also enables further
analysis in test rigs and offline simulation tools. Furthermore,
it represents an important step in realizing the benefits of CTR
testing discussed in this paper and enables the use of aeroelas-
tic simulations to characterize the stochastic response of WTs.
The latter further allows engineering choices on how to aver-
age across multiple WTs to represent the WPP response at the
point of interconnection in electrical simulation studies.
Regarding validation of voltage source behaviour behind an
impedance GFM core functionality, VDE-FNN and ENTSO-
E proposals introduce a fictitious islanding setup requiring
switchable load banks. However, this approach is often imprac-
tical for large-scale WTs due to the high power ratings involved
and the limited availability and high cost of large load banks.
Voltage source behaviour behind an impedance is the abil-
ity of the WT to maintain the voltage phasor constant in the
transient and sub-transient time frames, contributing inherently
to voltage and frequency support. It is important to note that
this property of GFM relates to the small-signal behaviour of
the GFM control. Thus, validation of this functionality could
be conducted through small-signal response to phase jumps
and voltage disturbances –achievable either through internal
control perturbations or via external disturbances at the point
of connection. The use of large loads and fictitious islanding
setups may therefore add none or a very marginal improve-
ment in the validation process. Additionally, alternative testing
methodologies to verify voltage source behaviour behind an
impedance of GFM units have been proposed in [38] and [57],
which consist on obtaining impedance measurements and fre-
quency scans of the reactive and active power response to
voltage and phase perturbations at the grid voltage. Impedance
of a GFM WT presents a lower value around the fundamental
frequency due to its voltage source behaviour and its mag-
nitude could be used for model validation. The reactive and
active power frequency responses involve obtaining the trans-
fer functions from active power to phase angle, and reactive
power to voltage magnitude. From the active power frequency
response, primary frequency response and inertia constants can
be obtained and validated.
At the same time, the applicability and transferability of
results from WT test benches remains under discussion. C&S
testing seems to be key enabler for obtaining timely validation
of models and achieving robust validation strategies comple-
menting field testing. However, it relies on novel real-time
models capable of representing the missing parts, especially the
mechanical system, whose influence in the electrical response
is greater than in GFL-equipped WTs. Thus, an exercise must
be done to identify those cases that involve a contribution
from the mechanical response, and validation of the test bench
against field measurements.
For example, voltage source behaviour behind an impedance
relates to the bandwidth of the GFM control loop. This means
that the property is given from the implemented control in
combination with the electrical system between the converter
terminals and the grid interface point, whereas the WT mechan-
ical system, the environmental conditions such as wind, or
the fast protective converter responses will not have any sig-
nificant influence. Thus, this property could be validated and
assessed in a HiL setup through small-signal disturbances (dis-
turbances small enough to avoid pushing the controller outside
its linear operating range), or even assessed in SiL with the
actual converter control code. In the same way, previously men-
tioned testing procedures involving frequency scans could also
be carried out in these setups due to their small-signal nature.
In general, steady-state performance cases (such as small
voltage magnitude or angle jumps), impedance characteris-
tics, reactive power or voltage control, and harmonics depend
almost entirely on the control of the grid-side converter and can
be validated using measurements from HiL setups.
Regarding other GFM functionalities such as inertial
response, it could be consider as a converter control imple-
mentation if energy provision is low. However, when the inertia
energy provision is higher, it influences the mechanical system
of the WT, the WT control, as well as its operating point.
Other cases such as FRT scenarios have similar influence in
the mechanical side as GFL control, what may hint that the
same test benches as specified in IEC 61400-21-4 can be used.
7
Conclusion
This paper presents an overview of the validation procedures
for electrical models of wind turbines (WTs) and wind power
plants (WPPs). The requirements and guidelines from differ-
ent validation sources such as transmission system operators
(TSOs) and the main national and international standardiza-
tion bodies are presented, including the WT and WPP test
cases to be validated, model accuracy metrics, and validation
methodologies. Testing setups used for model validation, such
as field testing, component and subsystem (C&S) test benches
and real-time control replicas have been introduced, while
referring to the specifications from grid codes and standards.
Validation methodologies from IEC 61400-27-2 have been pre-
sented and their adoption and discussion among validation
sources and industry experience has been included. The paper
distinguishes between established validation practices for grid-
following (GFL) WTs and WPPs and the emerging approaches
for grid-forming (GFM) units, which are still under devel-
opment. Arguments have been made for maintaining existing
GFL validation procedures for GFM, when applicable.In addi-
tion, recent proposals for GFM validation have been critically
analyzed from an original equipment manufacturer (OEM)
perspective, considering practical implementation challenges.
This paper seeks to bridge the gap between established
GFL validation practices and current uncertainties in GFM WT
model validation, providing input toward the standardization of
validation practices essential for the development and maturity
of GFM technology.
