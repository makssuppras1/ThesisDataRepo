Abstract
Prostate cancer is the second most prevalent cancer in men in the world, with diagnosis
typically requiring a prostate biopsy. In Denmark alone, around 9.000 prostate biopsies are
carried out each year, where about half of them confirm presence of cancer. The procedure is
performed using a transrectal ultrasound probe in hospitals. However, the use of a transrectal
probe negatively impacts patient comfort, which provides a potential for improvement of the
patient experience.
This report presents the results from a master’s thesis project. The goal with the project was
two-fold; Firstly, to reduce the complexity of the procedure for the physician, with the intent
of increasing procedure efficiency, to ease the pressure on the healthcare system. Secondly,
to provide a more comfortable patient experience to reduce some of the psychological
stress associated with the current procedure, and remove an entry barrier for men to get
diagnosed.
A sociotechnical analysis of prostate cancer detection and transperineal ultrasound scanning
viability was done through a literature study. The current procedure was analyzed through
observational studies and interviews with physicians at three hospitals in Denmark. A catalog
of eight different challenges was made, to highlight the most essential problems. The problem
was defined in a problem statement together with specifications for the future procedure.
A Morphology diagram was created and the development work was focused through a
co-design process together with the company supervisors. Three distinct concepts that
focused on the equipment, were developed and validated with three physicians. Further idea
generation and detailing of a spring mechanism for keeping constant patient contact was done.
A prototype of the spring mechanism was built using 3D print and tested in an experiment,
to validate its effectiveness and for future improvements.
The result of the project is made up of two parts. Firstly, it consists of a catalog of
challenges with the currently used rectal-guided transperineal prostate biopsy procedure.
Challenges stem mainly from observation of targeted MR-US fusion biopsies at three hospitals
in Denmark.
Secondly, the result consist of a proposal for a new prostate biopsy procedure, that utilizes
transperineal ultrasound scanning, without compromising physician and patient needs. Two
different solution proposals are presented, that both offer a plan for which procedural steps to
perform as well as a recommendation for the required equipment and which functions it should
fulfill. The difference between the two solutions, lies in the readiness of the technological
aspects incorporated in the equipment.

Biopsy Procedure Terminology
In the literature the terminology used to describe prostate biopsies is usually centered around
which procedure is used to take the biopsy samples: transrectal ultrasound (TRUS) or
transperineal ultrasound (TPUS). This description conveys the implicit assumption that the
probe being used for the biopsy always is an endocavity probe that scans through the rectal
wall. Due to the nature of this thesis being about contesting this assumption, it is appropriate
to adjust the terminology used when describing the prostate biopsy procedure.
For this project when describing a prostate biopsy procedure the ultrasound scanning method
will be specified using RG (rectal-guided) or PG (perineum-guided). Additionally, the method
for tissue sampling will be specified using TRPB (transrectal prostate biopsy) or TPPB
(transperinial prostate biopsy). For example, the procedure normally described as TRUS
biopsy uses transrectal ultrasound scanning and takes samples transrectally. In this report it
will be written as ’RG-TRPB’. This is done to ensure a clear distinction between the different
biopsy procedures while reading.

Introduction
Problem Description
With the project we want to address the following:
1. What challenges exist with current prostate biopsy procedures?
2. Is it possible to gain an adequate ultrasound (US) image of the prostate that can be
used to perform prostate biopsies, by scanning transperineally?
3. What would a perineal-guided transperineal-prostate-biopsy procedure (PG-TPPB) look
like?
4. And what changes to the equipment would be necessary to perform the new procedure?
This report strives to answer these questions with a variety of means. Question 1 is intended
to identify whether there are other aspects of the current procedure that could be improved.
It will be addressed in observational studies at three Danish hospitals, as well as in a literature
study. Question 2 represents the initial question that the project has sprung from. It will be
answered through an experiment with the question as inspiration for the hypothesis. With
question 3 we want to create a new procedure that improves upon the findings from question
1 and 2. This will be done through a concept development process and concept validation
with users. Question 4 is intended as the final development step of the project, followed by a
hand-over of a new procedure solution to the project’s collaborative partner (BK Medical).
Because the timeline of the project is limited, the finishing touches will be focused on one
select part of the final solution, which requires special design attention. This part will go
through a rapid ideation and prototyping process, concluding in an experiment to test its
functionality.
Problem Background
This project is focused on the diagnostic process for the second most prevalent cancer type
in men worldwide (1.47 million cases in 2022), prostate cancer (PCa). Geographically this
cancer type is most widespread in countries and areas with a high Human Development Index
(HDI), with the USA and Nothern Europe at the top of incidence rates. [Bray et al. 2024]
Incidentally, the treatment in the places that have the highest incidence rates is also where
the treatment is most advanced and has the highest survival rate.
Today, the process of diagnosing PCa is heavily dependent on three procedures: Prostate-
specific antigen (PSA) test, a Digital Rectal Examination (DRE), and a Prostate biopsy.
For many years, the biopsy procedure has been performed with a method called transrectal
(through the rectum) ultrasound-guided biopsy. This involves using a probe with a cylindrical
shaft and a US array at its end, and a so called biopsy gun, that can extract tiny strings
1
==PAGE:12==
of tissue with a long needle. The biopsy is performed by first inserting the probe into the
patient’s rectum. Then a biopsy gun needle is inserted via a needle guide in the probe,
through the rectal wall. Guided by the US-image, a number of tissue samples are taken
from the patient’s prostate. These tissue samples are then used to assess the presence of
carcinogenesis (cancer growth).
The process of diagnosing PCa has evolved over the years, notably with the introduction of
the PSA test in the 1980’s, and more recently with the use of magnetic resonance imaging
(MRI). In unison with US, this method enables the performance of targeted biopsies which
subsequently decreases the amount of tissue samples necessary to come to a diagnosis. In
recent years, another breakthrough in the procedure is winning ground in the EU and the USA.
Instead of taking the tissue samples transrectally the alternative method takes the samples
transperineally (through the perineum). This method avoids going through the rectum and
therefore does not have the same risk of infection [Tu et al. 2019]. Additionally, it does
not require administering of antibiotics which, due to rise in antimicrobial resistance (AMR)
[Commission, Health, and Safety 2023][Agency 2023] is considered a positive.
With the new biopsy procedure the US-image continues to be gained via a transrectal (TR)
probe. Though TR scanning in medical terminology is not invasive, as it is not gaining
access to the inside of the body via incision of puncturing of the skin, it is tied with varying
degrees of negative psychological and physical effects in a part of the men going through the
procedure [Oliffe 2004]. The purpose of this project is to determine whether it is possible to
change the method for gaining the US-image for a prostate biopsy, from scanning via the
rectum to scanning on the perineum. This could result in a less invasive patient experience,
and thereby potentially alleviate some of the stress associated with it.
Project Boundaries
To keep the work relevant to the project goal and for us to stay within the predetermined
time frame, some boundaries have been defined. The project will mainly focus on the part
of the diagnostic procedure for PCa related to the biopsy. This means that the following
biopsy results and possible cancer treatment will not be included in the main work of the
project. Additionally, it has been decided that, though the project will follow the practices
and philosophy of the Double Diamond model, the final recommendation will rely on US in
some capacity, regardless of the findings during the project. This is done in respect of BK
Medical, being a company that has their main expertise in this field.

2.1
Project Framework & Approach
Due to the origin of the project, stemming from a real user-need, it was deemed appropriate
to apply a problem-driven design approach to figure out the underlying issues with the status
quo, before focusing on a solution.
The Double Diamond Model
Before the project start, it was decided that the Double Diamond model would serve as the
design framework for the project. This model has been useful in previous projects and is
highly familiar to us, which is a benefit when entering a new domain. On Figure 1 the Double
Diamond model can be seen.
Figure 1: The Double Diamond model.
The Double Diamond model provides an innovation and design process that is useful when
developing solutions for people. It is structured in two diamonds, the first covering the
problem-space with the goal of ’designing the right thing’, whereas the next diamond, covering
the solution-space, is about ’designing things right’. [Design Council 2024]
The diamonds can be divided into four distinct phases: Discover, Define, Develop, and Deliver.
Each diamond consists of a diverging and converging thinking phase, where the Discover
and Develop phases are divergent in nature and Define and Deliver are convergent. The
goal of the diverging phases is to open up the problem/solution space by collecting a wide
variety of data and widening the knowledge base, whereas in the converging phases it is about
synthesizing the data to narrow down and decide on a focus. [Design Council 2024]
3
==PAGE:14==
The four phases are described in a bit more detail here:
Discover: The first phase is about Exploring the problem rather than assuming what is
wrong. The divergent nature of this phase calls for widening the knowledge base surrounding
the problem. Talking to and spending time with people who are affected by the issue is a
crucial part here, to really understand the pain points and shortcomings of the status quo.
The output is usually a huge amount of data pointing in different directions.
Define: Based on the data collected up until now, it is time for Scoping down the focus to
what the remainder of the project should focus on. The data should be synthesized into a
more defined and clear problem-space. The convergent thinking here points towards finding
patterns in the data and incorporating the patterns into an overall challenge, defined in a
different way than the original problem. The output of this phase would be a clear problem
definition, and a requirements specification for the solution to be created.
Develop: Having a clearer and knowledge-founded understanding of the problem it is now
time for Developing potential solutions for solving or minimizing it. The divergent nature
of this phase, should encourage designers to give different answers to the clearly defined
problem, inspired from other fields or domains, and maybe co-developing the ideas with a
range of different people. The output is usually some ideas or concepts that have been
iterated to a certain fidelity.
Deliver: In the final phase it is time for Refining the solution by testing the ideas and
scrapping the ones that do not work, improve, or combine them into new concepts for
validation. This is exactly the converging thinking of this phase, that multiple ideas should be
tested to find the one that best solves the problem. The output here is a final recommendation,
at some level of fidelity, and an outline on why it is the best option to go forward with.
[Design Council 2024]
As can be seen on Figure 1 the Double Diamond model is not a waterfall model with a
linear sequential flow, instead it allows for jumping between phases in the diamonds. It
might be necessary to loop back to an earlier phase in order to advance with the overall
process. However, when passing the middle with the problem definition done, it marks the
end of the problem-space and going back to the previous diamond should not be needed if
done properly.
User-Centered Design Approach
Since the problem surrounds people, and more specifically the well-being of people, a
user-centered approach would be ideal for bringing forward a user-friendly solution. The
user-centered approach is crucial, since the procedure is being performed on people by people.
So, focusing on the user and patient needs is critical to reach the best possible solution.
4
==PAGE:15==
[IxDF 2024] A visual representation of the User-Centered Design process can be seen on
Figure 2.
Figure 2: Visual representation of the User-Centered Design process [IxDF 2024].
To keep the user-centered design focus, it is important to include users in different steps
along the way. Users contain invaluable insights, that we as designers do not necessarily
posses, therefore they should be included in the process. This can e.g. be for prioritizing or
confirming our findings or even help develop different ideas. However, it is also important to
keep them out of some decision as they might not always know what they need. So, even
though we base a lot of the decisions on user data and interactions, it is our job as designers
to navigate the information and make qualified decisions.
A high similarity between the Double Diamond model and the user-centered design approach
is seen. The four steps in user-centered approach all refer to their own respective phase in the
Double Diamond model. The user-centered approach might be seen as a bit more iterative
and adds the perspective of, did we get to the right use-context and requirements, are we
missing something, or does it need to be altered a bit? When striving for the best solution,
these are important questions to ask yourself, when progressing in the development.

2.3
Research
The research of the report has been conducted with a methodological triangulation approach,
using two different research methods (literature study and observational study) done separately
from each other, with the goal of answering a set of research questions, as seen in Table 1.
This approach has been chosen to enhance the validity of the answers to the questions, as
they have been gathered independently, and from two distinct sources: the literature study
relying on scientific research, and the observational study relying on observing real world
scenarios and interviewing specialists.

Issues with RG-TRPB
While RG-TRPB has been the world standard procedure recommended for prostate biopsies
since the 1990s the implications of using it has been put under scrutiny [Emiliozzi et al. 2003].
The critique is pointing towards five factors that ultimately puts the use of the procedure
into question:
Infection risk: With the procedure comes the risk of post-biopsy infection. The infection is
caused by fecal bacteria entering the bloodstream through the puncture points in the rectal
wall, where the tissue samples are gathered through. [Tu et al. 2019] The infection risk
persists despite the use of antibiotics to prevent it, with the incidence rate having increased
over the years [Heidenreich, Bastian, et al. 2014]. This is possibly tied to the higher rate of
AMR, which is caused by the widespread use of antibiotic drugs such as fluoroquinolones.
[Loeb et al. 2012][Tu et al. 2019]
Use of antibiotics: Because the procedure puts the patient at risk of infection, and possibly
hospitalization, it is necessary to administer antibiotics for the procedure. Due to the
documented increase in AMR [Cuevas et al. 2011] the use of antibiotics in general has
become controversial, causing an estimated 35.000 deaths in the EU every year. In response
to this, the European Medicines Agency (EMA) has issued guidelines for more stringent use
of antibiotics e.g. fluoroquinolone to combat this development. [Commission, Health, and
Safety 2023][Agency 2023] This is hoped to make the use of procedures where these drugs
are required less attractive.
Prostate accessibility: The RG-TRPB procedure uses a setup where the biopsy gun and the
probe is connected via a needle guide in the probe. This makes operating the biopsy gun and
probe a somewhat elemental task that can be performed without much training. However,
the setup also makes the maneuverability of the biopsy needle less flexible which means that
some areas of the prostate, specifically anteriorly placed areas, are harder to gather samples
from. [Emiliozzi et al. 2003][Tu et al. 2019]
1
2
==PAGE:23==
Inferior detection rate: With the introduction of MR-scanning prior to the biopsy, it is
possible to identify suspicious lesions within the prostate that can be sampled directly via
a targeted biopsy. This detection method has been added as a recommended procedure
from the European Association of Urology (EAU) [Philip et al. 2024] helping cement it as a
standard in the field. Using MR-guided biopsies with the RG-TPPB procedure has meant
the detection effectiveness of clinically significant prostate cancer (csPCa) has increased.
Comparing the two, RG-TPPB has surpassed RG-TRPB (86% vs. 73%) [Philip et al. 2024].
Without the use of an MR-scan before the procedure the two methods were concluded to be
equal in detection effectiveness [Hara et al. 2008].
Patient experience: For a patient, the prostate biopsy is connected to different emotional
impact before, during and after the procedure. The time between the scheduling of the
procedure and its execution is for some patients, defined by an increasing feeling of anxiety
and uncertainty. This can be caused by uncertainty about the procedure itself and possible
pain and discomfort during it. The procedure can also cause a feeling of embarrassment,
as there exists a type of stigmatization around the nature of it, specifically about the use
of the TR probe and the necessity for anal penetration to scan during the procedure. For
many patients, this aspect of the procedure is connected to a sense of vulnerability and
loss of control. During the procedure, patients experience discomfort and varying degrees
of pain from the needle penetration. The level of pain is often a surprise to the patient as
physicians beforehand reassure them that it will likely only be a bit uncomfortable. Following
the procedure, the patient experience is defined by anxiety about the test results, as well as
possible post procedure pain that can last multiple weeks. [Oliffe 2004]
Issues with RG-TPPB
Sampling the tissue through the perineum as an alternative to through the rectum, has been a
suggested method for prostate biopsies in well over a decade [Emiliozzi et al. 2003]. However,
despite having been mentioned in the EAU guidelines since 2011 [Heidenreich, Bellmunt, et al.
2011], the procedure has not been able to gain much ground in practice until recently. The
reason for this seem to be affected by several factors:
Technical requirements: Performing RG-TPPB, the physician is required to operate the US
probe and the biopsy gun as two independent devices. This can be challenging as the biopsy
gun needle and the probe need to be held parallel to each other in order to use the US-image
as an accurate reference [Emiliozzi et al. 2003]. Compared to RG-TRPB where the needle of
the biopsy gun and the US probe is connected to each other, this separated setup is more
demanding to use and therefore requires more skilled personnel to operate.
1
3
==PAGE:24==
Equipment requirements: Because the perineum area is more sensitive compared to the
rectal wall, it is necessary to administer local anesthetics to mitigate the discomfort for
the patient before a RG-TPPB. The anesthetic is injected into the perineum area as well
as around the prostate. In general, this is not necessary when performing RG-TRPB and
therefore adds some complexity and duration to the procedure. [Bass et al. 2017]
In order to reap the most benefits from performing the RG-TPPB MR-US fusion needs to
be applied. This enables the use of MR-guided targeted biopsies where an MR-scanning
gets fused with the real-time US-image, in order to extract samples from specific regions
of interest (ROI). However, MR-US fusion requires a so-called fusion-platform in order to
utilize it, with its own proprietary hardware and software needing to be procured. [O’connor
et al. 2021] These factors will inevitably make the RG-TPPB procedure more costly to offer
compared to RG-TRPB.
Patient experience: Most points from the RG-TRPB regarding psychological and physical
perspectives from patients holds true for this method as well. Although, taking the tissue
samples by inserting the biopsy gun through the perineum has been shown to slightly increase
the discomfort of patients during procedure [Bass et al. 2017]. The increase occurs despite
the patients having been injected with local anesthetics beforehand. The registered pain
has been measured only slightly higher than with the RG-TRPB procedure. Using a visual
analogue pain score (VAS) spanning from 0 (no pain) to 10 (worst pain possible), the pain
has been measured to 2.93 for RG-TPPB and 2.67 for RG-TRPB [Kubo et al. 2009].
Diagnostic effectiveness: While RG-TPPB statistically seems to be more effective at diag-
nosing csPCa than RG-TRPB [Philip et al. 2024] the detection rate is close to equal when
performing a +12-core biopsy with a systematic layout [Hara et al. 2008]. In this procedure,
the physician uses a fixed layout of the prostate divided into a set number of areas (normally
between 12-24), from which a single tissue sample is gathered. This procedure does not
utilize MRI and therefore, lacks the benefits provided by this e.g., targeted biopsy using
MR-US fusion. Due to the additional requirements for PG-TPPB compared to RG-TRPB,
an equal detection rate is not desirable.
Alternative Detection Methods
Alternative US: Different methods for US guidance during biopsy procedures have been
experimented with.
Transabdominal: A transabdominal approach to US guidance has the same disadvantage as
TP guidance, of having the probe scan the prostate from a further distance than TR. In
earlier tests of this method, it has been deemed too difficult to get sufficient visualization of
the prostate with transabdominal scanning, due to the placement of the pubic bone obscuring
the prostate [Shinohara et al. 2003].
1
4
==PAGE:25==
However, recent studies have shown new interest in the approach, utilizing dynamic contrast-
enhanced US. The technique uses, so called, microbubbles that act as an US contrast agent
(UCA), injected into the patient intravenously. As tumors often have higher blood flow
compared to the surrounding tissue, the contrast agent will help highlight ROI in a subsequent
US-scan. Another change with this procedure compared to a rectal scan is that the patient is
asked not to void before the procedure. This is because a partially filled bladder will help
mitigate attenuation created by the space in an empty bladder. [Mischi et al. 2015]
The method seems to be able to create US-scans comparable to a TR scan, but its cancer
detection ability is not yet uncovered.
Transurethral: Scanning through the urethra is another alternative that has been explored,
though only vaguely. It has been used for cases where TR scanning is not a possibility, e.g.
for patients without a rectum. For the procedure, a resectoscope is used as guidance for a US
probe supposedly allowing for imaging of the prostate with a quality good enough for volume
measurements, and a systematic TPPB. [Seaman et al. 1996] The technique however, has
not been given much interest in recent years showing in a lack of research.
PSMA-PET/CT: This method is used mostly for patients that already have been diagnosed
with PCa, and are being monitored for any development, specifically if the cancer has
metastasized. Prostate-specific membrane antigen (PSMA) is a protein that can be found
on PCa cells. Using a tracer that binds to this protein allows for the visualization of the
presence of PCa cells in a patients body via a PET- or CT-scan. PSMA is however, also
found in other parts of the body so the scan is not a definite answer to whether a patient has
metastasis. [Lisney et al. 2022]
In-bore MRI biopsy: Another alternative to the US-guided approach is using MRI as guidance
during a biopsy. This method is called In-bore and it allows for performing targeted prostate
biopsies, combining the results of a multiparametric MRI (mpMRI) over live MRI-scan images.
This method has been tested with both TR and transgluteal sampling methods, meaning
that it can be used in patients without rectal access. [Gurgitano et al. 2020][Bera et al.
2024]
3.2.2
Transperineal Prostate Scanning Viability
TP Scanning for Different Purposes
TP scanning with US can not be considered a completely novel or unheard of approach to
scanning the prostate, but has seen limited studying. However, RG-TRPB remains the most
suitable and widely used method. One of the reasons for this is due to the flexibility (size and
economics) of US systems, allowing for large-scale and field studies. [Griffiths et al. 2007]
Another reason is the historical aspect of the prostate diagnostic procedure, since US was the
1
5
==PAGE:26==
main component, the best possible image resolution was required to provide the best possible
image to the physician. For this, low depth, and high frequency probes would be required.
This meant that the probe should be as close to the prostate as possible, which is through
the rectum.
Abdominoperineal Resection
For people undergone abdominoperineal resection (due to e.g. colorectal cancer, ulcerative
colitis and familial polyposis coli), the TR approach is simply not possible, due to the removal
of the rectum. A scientific article from the Journal of Urology from 2003, reviewed records
of 28 patients from two institutions. Patients had a history of abdominoperineal resection
and had all undergone a PG-TPPB for evaluating elevated PSA-levels. The method involved
a catheter through the urethra into the bladder, assisting in localizing the prostate, avoiding
the urethra with the needle and filling the bladder to get a better view of the prostate. A
TR probe with end-fire setting applied at 6 or 5 MHz was used for the US guidance which
would provide a coronal (a plane that divides the human body into dorsal back and ventral
front sections) and sagittal (a plane that divides the human body into a left and a right)
view of the prostate gland. 6-12 random biopsies were taken, with the needle directed in
the sagittal plane of the prostate. The conclusion from this study was, that PG-TPPB can
provide accurate tissue diagnosis. But, compared with TR scanning the image quality is poor.
PG-TPPB can be used for template biopsies, but may not be adequate for lesion targeted
biopsies. Do note that these conclusions are from 2003. [Shinohara et al. 2003]

Prostate Volume Measuring
Another study was done in 2007 to evaluate TP scanning to measure total and central
prostate volume compared with the standard TR scanning method. Here 290 men without
known prostate disease were measured with both methods at a single session by the same
experienced sonographer. For TR scanning a high frequency 7.5-5 MHz biplanar sector
TR probe was used and for TP scanning a medium frequency 5-3.5 MHz transabdominal
mechanical sector probe with a footprint of 3.5 cm. For each method total and central
prostate volume was calculated by the ellipsoidal rule from the sonographic measurements of
the 3 maximum diameters of the relevant region. The conclusion from this study, was that
TP scanning provides an accurate and less invasive alternative, but with a higher technical
failure rate than TR scanning, especially for central prostate volume. The authors concluded
1
7
==PAGE:28==
that their TP scanning method was not robust enough for measuring central prostate volume
and would require further technical refinement. There was a correlation between failed
measurements and weight/obesity, so for men with more fat, it resulted in more failed
measurements. Each participant were also asked about their preferences between methods
afterwards, and in all instances, TP scanning was deemed more acceptable than TR scanning.
Additionally it was concluded that the TR probe is a major barrier for at least younger men
without prostate disease to participate in a study. Another important point from the authors
is that the requirements of sterilization of an TR probe also slows down and hinders the
portability of the procedure. Furthermore, sterilization is expensive due to the downtime of
the probe. [Griffiths et al. 2007]
Cancer Detection Rate
A large study carried out in 2024, had the objective of comparing the prostate cancer
detection rate (PCDR) of PG-TPPB versus RG-TRPB. Here, a total of 111 patients went
through the PG-TPPB, and 188 underwent the RG-TRPB approach. 11 core systematic
biopsies were performed with both methods. A convex array 5-2 MHz probe was used for
PG-TPPB and a TR 9-5 MHz probe for RG-TRPB. The conclusion from this study, was that
PCDR for PG-TPPB and RG-TRPB, was 33.3% and 39.9% respectively. Taking PSA-levels
into consideration, there was no significant difference in the PCDR. The postoperative
complications of TPPB was significantly lower than that of TRPB. Futhermore, the PG-
TPPB used simpler equipment and did not require enema administration (inserting fluid, gel,
into the rectum). Images from this study showed two sets of hands required for holding and
taking the biopsies with PG-TPPB. [Xiao et al. 2024]
Radiotherapy
In radiotherapy, the application of 4D (able to monitor movement in 3D) real-time TP
scanning has been studied to track the movement of the prostate, to prevent tumor targeted
areas from being missed or normal tissue being overexposed. The Clarity Ultrasound System
(Elekta, Sweden) enabled the use of TP 4D scanning with automated US scanning technology.
Here, it was concluded, that the US system with high frequency was able to provide high
resolution images that can be used for radiotherapy. [Li et al. 2020]
In relation to radiotherapy, another scientific paper provides how-to guidelines for how to
position a TP probe in radiotherapy, pitfalls and lessons learned. The equipment is the same
as used in the before mentioned study, the Elekta solution, which differs a bit from the
handheld devices previously covered. Here there were two pitfalls, one being sub-optimal
image quality of the prostate gland and the other, excessive TP probe pressure. Quite a few
aspects can result in poor image quality, this being poor contact with the skin, inappropriate
angle and height placement of the probe and presence of rectal gas and inadequately filled
1

bladder. The excessive probe pressure can result in an ineffective scan path due to compressed
perineal region. With fusion (CT in this case), too much pressure might make the contouring
challenging. So, adequate training and user experience is required to apply TP scanning
successfully. [Pang et al. 2021]

is below 15 years the patient might not live long enough to experience the benefits of

ng/mL [AUA 2023].

in both the EAU and AUA guidelines, likely be put on a screening program or active
surveillance, which both includes PSA-tests every few years.

Sociotechnical Implications of Current Practice
The current practice for prostate biopsies by the EAU and AUA vary slightly, but generally
agree on what methods to use for the procedure.
This includes PSA-test, where the
AUA generally favor using PSA-screening as an early detection tool, which the EAU have
been reluctant to impose due to concerns of overdiagnosis and overtreatment [Wei et al.
2023][Cornford et al. 2024]. Arguments both for and against PSA-screening exist, with the
ones against raising concerns of overdiagnosing and overtreatment of patients that have PCa,
but never in their lifetime would know they had it or be affected by it. Because the side
effects include impotence and urinary incontinence, conditions that are potentially life altering,
it seems warranted to be cautious about PSA-screening. On the other hand, screening can
lead to an increase in early detection of PCa, and possibly an increase in diagnosis of local

lesions. The European Randomized Study of Screening for Prostate Cancer (ERSPC) is a
study of 182.160 men across eight European countries with the objective of uncovering the
effect of screening for PCa. Its 16-year follow-up results leans significantly towards the use
of screening, showing a 20% lower mortality rate in the screening group compared to the
control group. [Hugosson et al. 2019]
Though both the AUA and EAU guidelines now recommend the use of the RG-TPPB
procedure, RG-TRPB is still widely in use, making up an estimated 90% of biopsies in the
USA. This is likely caused by different factors, namely the lack of physicians that have the
required training to perform the procedure. Furthermore, the insurance industry in the USA
provide the same pay for an RG-TPPB as for an RG-TRPB despite the former requiring
additional training and equipment. This can work against the adoption rate of the procedure
and slow the transition. [Shoag et al. 2020]
The continued practice of the RG-TRPB procedure, comes with implications. In the USA
around 5-7% of patients gain an infection post-procedure, and 1-3% require hospitalization.
The infection rate has been fairly stable in recent years. [Shoag et al. 2020] Compared
to Denmark, where the transition to RG-TPPB is well underway, the hospitalization rate
between the first year the procedure began broad implementation, 2022 (13%) and 2023
(59%) the hospitalization rate was halved from 4% to 2% [RKKP 2024].
To combat the infection risk from an RG-TRPB procedure, antibiotics are administered to the
patient. As mentioned earlier AMR is on the rise, causing an estimated 1.27 million deaths
globally in 2019 [WHO 2023], which could rise to as much as 10 million by 2050 [Murray
et al. 2022]. In this context, large scale initiatives towards minimizing the use of antibiotics
such as Global Action Plan on AMR have been created. Therefore, it seems illogical from a
sociotechnical perspective, to continue the use of a procedure that puts patients at risk of
infection, and contributes to increased AMR, when an alternative exists.

From Innovation to Approval
Drivers of Innovation and Development in US
Coming up with a new and innovative products is not always an easy task for companies. One
reason for this, is due to limitations of developing new knowledge internally in the firm. To
access new knowledge, most companies have to collaborate with external sources. [Chatterji
and Fabrizio 2014]
Physicians are one of the most important external resources, since they are out in the field
using the existing tools every day. In that process, they see what is working and what is not
working, which might result in them coming up with new ways or tools for a specific purpose.
Having this real-life knowledge, provides companies with knowledge that might not be present
2
2
==PAGE:33==
inside a company. Since they have to use the tools every day, they can be very motivated to
improve the existing tools. [Chatterji and Fabrizio 2014]
A study from 2017 highlights the advantage of engaging physicians in part time positions as
expert user in innovation roles or as advisors when developing new solutions. Having one foot
placed in practice, and attending medical conferences and one in development, makes them
able to support innovation activities well. A lot more than if they are full time employees at
the company. However, they are much better at producing new ideas for a specific purpose,
than being in the selection stage later on. Their firmly established knowledge in their field,
can make them blind to more novel opportunities. [Katila et al. 2017]
Another important external resource are hospitals, in particular, university or research hospitals.
They are, among others, big providers of healthcare services, adopters and users of new
technology, and potential developers of new processes. Overall, they contain a lot of medical
knowledge. The hospitals contribute to new idea generation by learning through clinical
practice and research. This results in identifying problems and potential solutions, which
is often put into collaboration with universities and companies. During the development
phase, the hospitals provide their clinical knowledge and they can be involved in testing and
documentation of effectiveness, safety and efficiency. [Thune and Mina 2016]
University-industry collaboration is also a driver for new innovative products. Evidence suggest
that multidisciplinary research has become an integral part of medical innovation. The
development of new and more advanced devices generally depends on the transfer of scientific
and technological advances outside of medicine, such as engineering and physics. An example
of this is that MRI had its origin from an experimental physicist exploring the structure of
the atom. It is not only knowledge transfer from the scientific community that inspire new
products, the medical industry also rely on technological advances generated in the industrial
world. An example of this, is that US originated from submarine warfare. So, medical
device innovation involves many different fields of study, both from within an academic and
industrial context, to work together. It relies on new developments in areas like electronics,
optics, computers, and materials science to develop new advanced technologies for healthcare.
[Gelijns and Rosenberg 1995]
For any novel solution or incremental improvement to be a reality it requires a lot of
collaborations between multiple people. The product needs to be continuously refined through
testing and delivered to customers in accordance with a business model. Specifically in the
healthcare industry the process requires major capital investment, regulatory approvals and
collaboration of individuals with diverse areas of expertise. [Bertrand et al. 2021]

Medical Device Development and Approval
In Europe medical devices are categorized into four categories, namely Class I, IIa, IIb and III.
These depends on the type of contact with the patient, their clinical purpose and the risks of
serious adverse events inherent in their use. More or less the same is applicable in the USA,
where they have three categories, I, II and III. The devices undergo regulatory approval via
submission to a notified body (an independent commercial organization), which is responsible
for regulating the device approval in the EU. The main requirement, is ’that the device is
safe and that it performs in a manner consistent with the manufacturer’s intended use’. In
the USA, devices are submitted to the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for approval.
[Valerio et al. 2016]
Before starting a medical device development project it is necessary to decide which market
the product is to be sold. Different countries use different approval processes and criteria,
and before the product is available for use in clinics and procedures, the device must be
approved by national public health systems as well. The approval process in the EU and
USA require mostly the same deliverables, however a lot of work should be done upfront to
figure out, which type of submission will be required for the specific product. The FDA e.g.
both have the 510(k) and Premarket Approval (PMA) pathways, which has very different
requirements for the deliverables and can be very expensive if not chosen correctly. [Valerio
et al. 2016]
A survey study from 2010 with more than 200 medical technology companies, found that
bringing a low- to moderate-risk 510(k) product from concept to clearance was approximately
$31 mil. with around $24 mil. linked to FDA dependent and related activities. For a
higher-risk PMA product, the avg. total cost was approximately $94 mil. with $75 mil. spent
on FDA activities. Time between submission and clearance should also be considered. The
average time for 510(k) submission were 10 months, whereas for PMA it was 54 months.
Comparing this with EU approval, the timeline in EU is significantly quicker with 7 months
for 510(k) equivalent devices and 11 months for PMA equivalent devices. [Makeower, Meer,
and Denend 2010]
Due to all of the required deliveries to prove safety and effectiveness of the medical device, a
development process or model is needed to structure the work. A simplified development
process can be seen on Figure 10. During development the risk management or usability
process must be followed, which are detailed in ISO standards. The process often involves
phases, checkpoints, milestones and reviews. If milestones and development phases are
designed in accordance with the FDA design control requirements, then two advantages
are gained: legal requirements are being followed and best practices are being followed.
Furthermore, a requirement by the FDA is that the product development process description

shall be provided for submission and shall be exactly followed. Deviations between process
description and execution should be avoided, but if necessary the process should be adjusted
to match actual practice and the changes should be justified. The development process is
quite comprehensive and differs between software and physical products, this will not be
further described here. [Müllner and Klinger 2023]

Medical Procedure Development
A definition of a clinical or medical procedure is:"any practice of a health practitioner
that involves a combination of special skills or abilities and may require drugs, device,
or both" [Gelijns 1989]. Development of procedures can be divided into two categories,
radical/breakthrough innovations and incremental innovations. The radical innovations often
arise in academic or academic-associated centers, where professional resources and support
systems are available. Incremental innovations is a less formal process and involves numerous
physicians refining and modifying an existing procedure in their every day clinical practice.
[Gelijns 1989]
The development process of procedures are different from development of drugs or devices,
in that no formal governmental regulatory system exists for the development of clinical
procedures. The development has mainly been placed in the hands of physicians and the
trust relationship between patients and physicians. Evaluation of the procedures therefore
depends heavily on professional self-regulation e.g. through peer review and institutional
review boards (IRBs). Here the different categories come into play again, since the radical
innovations generally will be subject to IRBs, whereas the incremental innovation often is not
required to go through IRBs due to it only being an incremental change compared to the
current practice. [Gelijns 1989]
2
5
==PAGE:36==
The current review process for procedures in the USA happens during the application for a
Current Procedural Terminology (CPT) code. This code is created by the American Medical
Association (AMA) and is used by all providers and payers for medical record keeping and
billing in USA. To get a CPT code, the applicants must meet at least one of three criteria:
"a) support by at least one CPT/HCPAC advisor representing practitioners who would use
the procedure, b) support by peer-reviewed literature, and c) an IRB approved protocol of a
clinical study, an ongoing U.S. clinical trial, or other evidence of evolving clinical utilization".
[Dranove et al. 2022] This means that there is an extensive range of research methods used
to assess the efficacy and safety of new procedures. [Dranove et al. 2022]
In the EU, there is not a direct equivalent to the AMA, though the EAU does act as a
regulator in the sense of creating guidelines for the urology field [EAU 2024]. Through a
yearly report published by the association, updates and new findings from scientific studies of
the prior year are discussed. These reports give clear and scientifically supported guidance
on best practice, which has been reviewed with a number of other organizations, such as
European Society for Radiotherapy & Oncology (ESTRO) and European Society of Urogenital
Radiology (ESUR). [Cornford et al. 2024]

3.4
Research Discussion
As mentioned both in the literature study and the observational study the prostate biopsy
procedure does have a certain amount of stigmatization connected to it. A significant part
of this comes from the use of the TR probe, and the fact that it needs to be placed in
the rectum during the procedure. The psychological aspect of this is substantial as the
area of the rectum tend to be considered very vulnerable to many men. Having this area
of privacy invaded by people that are more or less strangers can incidentally be a major
break of boundaries. The discomfort of this part of the procedure may mainly be caused by
psychological factors, affected by gender and societal norms. Physicians and assistant nurses
at the procedure will try to mitigate the anxiety that causes a patient to tense up, but this
can be difficult to succeed at, and remains a significant part of the anxiety that patients
experience prior to, and during the procedure.
A key part of making the patient feel comfortable is the communication provided by the
physicians and assistant nurse in the time up to the procedure, but also during and after it.
As seen in the literature study and the observational study, hospitals approach this in different
ways. AUH utilize physician-patient consultations prior to the procedure, to inform about the
process followed up with a pamphlet and possibly a phone call at a later point. Adding to this,
Herlev Hospital provides video material to the patient that covers the most important parts
of the procedure, which the patient can watch from home. During the procedure it is crucial
to keep the patient in the loop about what is happening and what is going to happen next
3
4
==PAGE:45==
e.g., the assistant nurse demonstrating the sound that the biopsy gun makes when activated.
This helps the patient be mentally prepared for it and avoid an unpleasant surprise that could
otherwise result in unnecessary tension. Following the procedure, effort is put on making
receiving the test results a safe experience. Hence, why the physicians recommend having
a consultation at the hospital where the patient can bring a person as support, instead of
taking the conversation over a phone call. Though the communication about the procedure
varies between hospitals it is clear that it is an important part of the procedure that can have
both positive and negative impact on the patient experience.
Another aspect of the current procedure that is important to consider for any type of
development of US procedures, is the resolution. If the possibility of an alternative scanning
point for a prostate biopsy should be considered it is necessary to know how good the
resolution should be to be viable, as increasing the distance to the subject will result in less
contrast and thereby less visibility on the US-image. To help address the issue of resolution
there exists technology designed to enhance the visibility on a US-scan such as mpMRI that
is already in use. It helps in highlighting possible lesions on the US-image and enabling the
physician to perform targeted biopsies. Additionally, as mentioned in the observational study,
an experimental use of PSMA-PET testing combined with MRI is in use at AUH. This method
is meant to combine the tracking capability of PSMA-PET tesing with the limited focus of
MRI, in order to locate lesions in patients, that does not show up on ordinary MRI, but have
high PSA-levels.
One can ask themselves why procedures that use less invasive measures for scanning are not
being used more widespread, like the perineum scanning occasionally performed at Herlev
Hospital and AUH for prostate biopsies. Here it is important to take into account that the
procedure does not officially exist in guidelines, and no dedicated equipment for it exists
either. Hence, physicians that perform the procedure need to be willing to experiment with
alternatives, like the convex probe used at AUH.
In order for TP scanning to become the new standard, we see a few obstacles. The main
challenge would be to prove that using PG-TPPB would result in the same or better PCDR
as RG-TPPB. This would most likely require a clinical study, to prove its effectiveness and
safety. The study would also serve as material for an IRB, in order to get the new procedure
a billing code and become the new recommended procedure in guidelines.
Even a well tested and documented procedure can take a long time to implement into a
healthcare system. The reason for this is multi-faceted, but could include a country’s economic
capacity as well as the structure of its healthcare system. A market-driven profit-based
healthcare system could be quick to implement new technology especially if there is financial
incentive, such as higher profits from using novel procedures. On the other hand, a universal
3
5
==PAGE:46==
healthcare system might be slower at starting implementation of new technology due to
demands of equity and budgetary limitations. However, when implemented it can quickly
spread to the entire system. Other factors, such as additional staff training and purchase of
new equipment might also impact the speed at which a new procedure can be adopted.
Finally, its worth noting that the data that has been recorded in the observational study is
susceptible to biases, notably Observer bias, and the Hawthorne effect. The Observer bias
is related to the observing party’s interpretation of the events they are recording. As one
observer can interpret an observation in one way, and a second observer could interpret it
differently. The Hawthorne effect relates to the phenomenon that an observed party will
change their behavior if they are aware of the fact that they are being observed.
Both biases are inherent to Observational studies and can never be completely removed.
They can however, be addressed in order to mitigate their potential damaging effects on the
study’s results. For this observational study a few things have been done to work against the
effects of the aforementioned biases. Firstly, two observers with individual recording of data
have been used for all observation sessions. These recordings have then been assessed and
combined by both observers to agree on a final interpretation of the event. This reduces the
risk of misinterpretation of observations that can happen when only one observer is used.
Furthermore, observations that evoked uncertainty in the observers were elaborated on as
questions for the physician at a post procedure interview.

Study Results
In the results the findings from the literature study and observational study are used to answer
the research questions from Table 1.
1. Current Practices for Prostate Biopsy
a. How are prostate biopsies performed in other countries? (USA, EU, China)
Performing a biopsy procedure in the USA, EU, or China is quite similar to each other. Two
procedures are used in general: RG-TRPB and RG-TPPB, with the former being in the
process of getting phased out due to higher risk of post procedure infection. In the USA
and EU, where the incidence rate for PCa is several times larger than China, the focus and
handling of the disease have been prevalent for years. The handling of the cancer diagnosis in
the USA and EU, has been very reliant on screening strategies to get people tested, based on
age, ethnicity, genetics, etc. This has translated into a relatively low MIR, when compared
with China, who has not had the same focus on PCa in the past.
3

b. What issues does the current procedures experience? (RG-TRPB & RG-TPPB)
The two procedures in use today are quite different from each other in terms of how they are
performed. The issues experienced with them are therefore mostly unique to the individual
procedure. However, they do share one issue, namely patient experience. The use of the
rectal US probe negatively impacts the experience of the procedure in both methods. For
the RG-TRPB procedure the main issue are the inherent infection risk caused by sampling
the prostate tissue through the rectal wall. Begotten from this issue is the administering
of antibiotics to mitigate infection risk, which is problematic due to the rise of AMR. The
main issue of the RG-TPPB procedure concerns the increased complexity of performing the
procedure, which has resulted in increased procedure duration, and fewer physicians able to
perform it.
c. Are there alternative methods that fulfill the same demands as current procedures?
Alternatives to the current prostate biopsy procedures do exist. Some are in use and some have
only been experimented with. For Alternative US methods, transabdominal and transurethral
US have been tested in experimental settings, but neither have gotten much traction besides
a few studies. TP scanning remains the main alternative to TR scanning, which has gotten
significant attention both in scientific studies and in practice. Alternative diagnosis methods
are being used, such as PSMA-PET/CT for tracking the spread of PCa, and In-bore MRI
biopsy, which switches the US-guided approach with a live MRI-scan.
2. TP Prostate Scanning Viability
a. What are the benefits and drawbacks of TP scanning compared to TR scanning?
Scanning the prostate transperineally does have distinct drawbacks compared with rectal
scanning. The biggest of these is the loss of image quality due to the increased distance to
the scan subject. This drawback can be difficult to adjust for in probe design as the distance
from perineum to prostate can vary from patient to patient, for example due to a thicker
layer of fat at the perineum from a higher body weight. Additionally, scanning on the prostate
removes the inherent probe stability that comes with placing the probe in the rectum, which
is going to make positioning of the probe more complex. Perineum scanning does also come
with benefits, with the most significant one being a less uncomfortable patient experience.
Patients having undergone both rectal scans and perineum scans report preferring the latter.
This scanning method also enables patients who has undergone abdominoperineal resection
to go through a prostate biopsy, which was not possible with TR scanning. Beside these
benefits, the PCDR of perineum scanning looks to be comparable to rectal scanning, when
factoring in PSA-levels.

b. Is it viable to scan the prostate transperineally & achieve ’sufficient’ image quality?
Scanning the prostate through the perineum does result in a decrease in scan quality, compared
with the use of a rectal probe though, with the PCDR potential being on a similar level. The
outline of the prostate and urethra is visible on the US-image, however lesions can not been
seen. With MR-US fusion being standard practice in guidelines, additional guidance, which is
independent from probe scan quality, is enabled. Based on this it seems like TP scanning of
the prostate is a viable method for prostate biopsies.
3. Sociotechnical Aspect of PCa Detection
a. What does the complete user journey look like for a prostate biopsy patient?
The user journey of the individual patient, from first test till biopsy, can vary based on
circumstances like risk group, age, region, and physician assessment. Though in general
it will have the following steps: 1. Initial test: The patient is recommended a PSA test.
2. Initial risk assessment: Based on the PSA test the patient is put on a type of surveillance
if the result is low-risk. If the result is intermediate- or high-risk they move forward into the
process. 3. Stratifying risk assessment: Is the risk intermediate, an assessment will be done
based on the patients condition. Here they can either be pushed further into the process,
or put on surveillance. 4. Decisions for further action: If they are pushed forward they will
likely get an mpMRI-scan to locate sign of lesions. If the physician deems a biopsy necessary
this scan will lay ground to the MR-US fusion in the biopsy. 5. Biopsy: A biopsy is performed.
It can either be targeted or systematic dependent on whether or not an MRI-scan has been
performed prior.
b. How is the sociotechnical and economical landscape with the existing procedures in
the EU vs. USA
In both the EU and the USA the transition towards RG-TPPB is underway. However, slow
implementation in the USA means that their post procedure infection rate has not seen a
decrease in the same way as a European country like Denmark, where the transition is close
to completion. The continued use of the RG-TRPB procedure also has negative implications
in its use of antibiotics. With the rise in AMR being a cause of over a million deaths yearly
on a global scale, WHO is advocating for minimizing the use of this type of drug.
4. From Innovation to Approval
a. Who drives innovation and development in US?
As our world becomes gradually more and more complex, multidisciplinary involvement is a
necessity for driving innovation with new technology. This is also the case in the medical field.
The development of new medical solutions often require collaboration with a mix between a
company and different external knowledge resources. For US companies, scientific transfer
3
8
==PAGE:49==
of knowledge through universities provide advancements in new technologies that can lay
ground to new innovations. Combining this with knowledge from physicians and hospitals
gives a real-life context, through experiences in their daily work, that can be applied to new
technologies to drive innovation.
b. What does it take to get a medical device on the market?
The process of getting medical devices approved goes through different institutions depending
on the country or region. The overall process however, can be generalized. The device is
categorized based on its desired application, which determines the guidelines it will have to
comply with to be able to sell commercially in that region. During development of the device,
the producer will have a set of control requirements they will need to uphold to get approval
from the respective institutions. These are wise to incorporate into the development phases
of the device, in order to synchronize milestones with requirement deadlines and ease the
compliance process.
c. What does it take to implement a new procedure in the real world?
There is no governmental regulatory body that dictates medical procedures within either the
USA or the EU. In the EU, guidelines within different medical fields are created by independent
associations which will consist of members specialized within their respective fields. These
members review scientific progress on a regular basis and updates the guidelines accordingly.
In the field of urology, this association is the EAU. The guidelines, however, are not law and
can differ to local guidelines on a national, and even hospital level. In the USA the AMA is
able to create a CPT code used for billing patients through the healthcare system. Getting
this code requires some type of approval from practitioners, for instance via clinical studies.
The code is essential in the USA, as physicians will lack an incentive to use a procedure if
there are barriers to bill for it.
The best way to implement a new procedure will therefore be to approach physicians and
collaborate with them to conduct a clinical study to test the effect of a given procedure. The
goal is then to get the attention of the right medical associations with the scientific journal
that comes from the study.

Breakdowns Identified for Perineum Scanning
The below breakdowns have come to light through talks with our BK Medical supervisors, and
represents challenges specifically in relation to TP scanning. The challenges also stem from
the observational study at AUH, when talking about their experience with the few PG-TPPB
they have performed.
5
1
==PAGE:62==
16. Work interface overlap: When changing the scanning method to the perineum from the
rectum, it results in taking up more space on the perineum skin, as both the needle entries
and the probe array is placed on the perineum. This might result in the two interfaces directly
interfering with each other, complicating the workflow of using one of the tools. Synergies
might exist between the probe and needles, when having the same contact surface. However,
for the tools not to negatively impact each other during the procedure, a solution must be
found which does not compromise the user workflow or patient safety.
17. Scan-plane perspective difference: Scanning on the perineum with current single or
bi-plane technology, will provide sagittal and coronal plane images of the body. Physicians
are used to seeing the sagittal and transverse plane images of the body, with a TR probe.
This means that physicians will have to learn these new images for guiding the needle. But
also that the current MRI workflow and fusion software, will have to change to utilize the
new plane. Furthermore, scanning on the perineum will provide another perspective of the
prostate. Scanning through the rectum made sure the rectal wall was parallel with the image,
now the rectum is visible and depicted differently than it used to. This might be a challenge
for physicians to get used to.
18. Decreased image quality: Due to the increased distance between the scan surface and
the prostate when scanning TP versus TR, a decrease in image resolution will be seen. This
is because of limitations of the US technology, with reflection, refraction and scattering of US
waves resulting in fewer waves returning back to the probe array in deeper tissue. This results
in an image with fewer details, which is making it more challenging to view hyperechoic and
hypoechoic areas on the image (which might indicate ROIs). At this point it is unknown, how
much of a drop in image resolution will be seen and if optimization of the image resolution
can mitigate the challenge. Physicians are used to the high image quality of the TR probe,
so whether the decrease in image quality is a problem, remains to be discovered.

4.3
Problem Specification
The final part of this phase is to define the problem into a single direction, that highlights
the actual problem. This provides us with a well defined focus, that is crucial when entering
the solution space. The purpose of this section is to scope down the focus into a concrete
problem statement, and develop a direction for the next phase, with a set of requirements
and a design brief.
4.3.1
Problem Statement
The Problem
The RG-TPPB procedure has been developed by fitting it to existing technology used in
its predecessor, RG-TRPB. Consequently incorporation of physician and patient needs and
perspectives have been neglected. The result is a procedure that has higher patient safety,
but fails to address the psychological side effects that negatively impact the experience of a
significant number of the procedure’s patients. Furthermore, the complexity of performing the
procedure has risen, increasing the user’s required technical expertise, resulting in cognitive
overload. It has thereby shrunken the pool of physicians able to perform the procedure.
5
7
==PAGE:68==
Problem Context
The implications of needing more skilled and specialized physicians than previously is that
the cost of performing the procedure will increase due to higher salaries for the specialized
work, as well as costs used to train new personnel. Having less physicians able to perform a
procedure will also increase the number of patients each physician will need to see, forcing
them to either increase work hours or down prioritize another work task. Either way, additional
pressure will be put on either the individual physician or onto the hospital, and thereby the
healthcare system as a whole. See the problem context in Figure 25.
Figure 25: Problem context.
The pre-procedure anxiety might create a barrier for men with prostate issues to get a
diagnosis, potentially resulting in avoidable PCa development and subsequent preventable
deaths. This should not be another reason for men not to get their health issues checked,
which is already an acknowledged issue today.
Root Cause
The complexity of performing the RG-TPPB procedure comes from the separation of the
US-probe and biopsy gun. This has made it necessary to use free-hand when taking the
biopsies, a method that is significantly more complicated to perform. This is, in part due to
the biopsy needle and US-probe scan-plane not being fixed in relation to each other, meaning
the physician has to constantly align the two with each other throughout the procedure.
The meaning behind separating the US-probe and biopsy gun from each other, is to take
the biopsies transperineally. This effectively removes the risk of sepsis that taking them
transrectally has, hence increasing patient safety. Why the US-scanning method was not also
changed to be TP scanning is unknown. It might be due to technical reasons, specifically
that scanning transrectally is the method that gives the closest and clearest US-image of the
prostate. Additionally, TR scanning is not putting the patient at any health risk. Changing
5
8
==PAGE:69==
it does not increase patient safety, which was the main motivation behind changing to TP
biopsies in the first place.
A notable number of men getting a prostate biopsy report being affected psychologically
negatively by the procedure, caused in part by the procedure breaking personal boundaries.
Firstly, by putting them in a vulnerable position, partly naked in front of strangers. Furthermore,
by having to insert the US-probe into the rectum during the procedure. Though these
measures are not necessarily ’harsh’ in a medical context, the acts of the procedure intrude on
complex societal norms of masculinity that many men intrinsically hold. This likely introduces
unnecessary mental stress on affected patients, that potentially could be avoided.
Ideal Outcome
The prospect of getting a prostate biopsy should not be cause for worry and neither should it
have a traumatic effect on a patient afterwards. Ideally, men that are being referred for a
prostate biopsy should not be contemplating compromising their health because of worries
about how the procedure is performed. Additionally, a prostate biopsy should not be so
complex to perform that it limits the number of physicians that can do it, to a degree that
puts them under high pressure and prevents them from doing other tasks.

Goals
The overall project goal is to make a complete procedure package, built from physician and

2. Making a procedure that is less invasive to the patient, with the purpose of mitigating

Target Market & Audience
The most important audience is BK Medical, as they are the first stakeholder that need to be

With the project there are of course certain limitations that we should address in order to

• The time span of the thesis project is restricted to a five month period. This will

• A big part of the solution’s effectiveness will be dependent on the quality of the US-scan

5.1
TP Scan Experiment
Since the beginning of the project, the extra scan depth associated with TP scanning had
been a concern. Furthermore, at our visit to AUH, we got confirmation from the physician
that image quality was an issue when scanning transperineally. Therefore, we wanted to
perform an experiment, which could help us clarify the obtainable image quality with currently
available US probes. The experiment has also been documented in Worksheet R1.
Purpose
The purpose of this experiment is to test whether US scanning of the prostate via the
perineum is viable for doing prostate biopsies. Two factors contribute to this assessment, the
first being how good of a resolution is obtainable, and secondly how much of the prostate will
be visible in the scan-image.
Hypothesis
This is the hypothesis for the experiment:
The current technology will be able to provide an image that clearly shows the
outline of the prostate and urethra.
The reason for choosing the outline of the prostate and urethra, is due to our assessment,
that these should be enough to be able to perform targeted fusion biopsies. The requirement
for US to be able to show lesions with hyperechoic and hypoechoic areas is a relic from before
MRI became the primary means of doing risk stratification.

supervisors helped us chose which probes to use in the experiment, due to their expertise
within the area. A technical comparison of the probes can be seen in Table 2.

Reflections
In relation to performing the experiment, a few reflections have come up:
• We have done the subjective evaluation of the images. However, we are not experts in
this area.
• It is unknown how comparable our experiment setup is to the human body. On a real
human body, the pelvic bone might create shadows, that limits the view of the prostate,
as US can not penetrate bone. US waves hitting the bone, will create scattering, which
might result in lower image resolution. Furthermore, the internal materials of the
phantom might be a bit too uniform and clear compared to real tissue. However, this is
the closest we could get, due to restrictions on testing on human subjects.
• The images captured with the 9081 probe lacked contrast, and were not very good.
This might be because the 9081 probe was a prototype, so its performance might not
be equal to the real product on the market.
• When comparing the control TR image with the TP images, it can be seen that the
orientation and perspective is fundamentally different. This is further exaggerated by
the fact, that the chicken breast was not a rectangular piece. This can be seen as
it is thinner on the right side compared to the left side of the images. Rotating the
US-image 90° or 180° might be needed to help physicians with the spatial understanding
of the image. In order to make it easier to translate their hands movement into the
needle path.
Furthermore, testing the 9024 (see Figure 31) with the bi-plane arrays was an interesting
test even though it did not outperform the 9032 and 9062 in image quality. On Figure 32 a
dual side-by-side view, can be seen captured with the 9024 probe.

The hypothesis is supported. Some of the probes used in the experiment provided a clear

curved array of the 9062 provided a high detail level that was able to depict hyperechoic and
hypoechoic areas inside the prostate.

5.3.1
Weighted Objectives Method
To evaluate the concepts in a less subjective manner, we wanted to incorporate the Weighted
Objectives Method (WOM). This method makes us reflect on which key performance indicator
(KPI) are most important for the end product and more specifically the end users. It lets us
reflect on how each concept score in relation to each KPI and how the concepts compare
to each other. Finally by multiplying the weighted KPIs with scores given to each concept,
a total weighted score is calculated. The method is not considered completely objective,
however it lets us reflect on why a certain concept is scoring high or low on certain KPIs.
Selecting KPIs
The overall procedure specification consists of a lot of KPIs for the final solution. However,
not all KPIs are equal in relation to their importance. To keep it at a manageable level, only
a few were selected. The most essential requirements and criteria were carefully chosen by
reviewing the importance of all in relation to each other. Most of the chosen KPIs were
criteria, as these can be fulfilled to varying degrees, whereas the requirements are more
of a binary nature, either being fulfilled or not. But, two of the selected KPIs were also
requirements, as these could also be fulfilled to a varying degree. The six selected KPIs can
be seen in Table 3.

Solution Proposals
6.2.1
Tomorrow’s Solution: Analogue Needle Guide
This solution is the proposal for a procedure that could be implemented within the foreseeable
future (see Figure 43). See it in larger format in Worksheet E1. The intention has been to
select sub-solutions that already exist in some capacity within the medical domain.
Tomorrow’s solution has been configured to be able to be developed and implemented into
the healthcare system relatively quickly. Additionally, the solution is made to be familiar to
physicians practicing the RG-TPPB procedure. The motivation for having a solution like
this is based on the need for more practitioners, that are able to perform a prostate biopsy.
This solution address this by lowering the complexity, and thereby the cognitive overload,
of the procedure. It does this using a needle guide, a probe fixation arm, and a number of
visual information elements on the US-scanner that aid the physician in their decision making.
Furthermore, it decreases the need for having additional personnel performing supporting
tasks such as manual highlighting of areas where biopsies have been taken, and wiping off
biopsy samples. Finally, it also improves the patient comfort by scanning transperineally.
Figure 43: Drawing of Tomorrow’s solution with an analogue needle guide.
Implementing this solution will result in faster training of new practitioners, while still allowing
existing ones to continue performing biopsies with minimal on-boarding necessary.

6.2.2
The Future’s Solution: Digital Needle Guide
Whereas Tomorrow’s solution has been made to quickly address the problems identified with
the current biopsy procedure, the Future’s solution is able to take things further. Here,
the intention has been to configure the solution that we believe best address the issues of
complexity and discomfort from the RG-TPPB procedure (see Figure 44). See the image in
larger format in Worksheet E1.
With these heightened intentions also comes a higher ambition level. The goal of this solution
is to provide the same aiding tools as Tomorrow’s solution, while also improving on them. The
main aspect of this is a lowered level of procedure complexity, which this solution addresses in
four areas (See Figure 44). The biggest one of these is the use of a matrix probe instead
of a rotating probe array. With it, the physician will be able position the probe once at
the beginning of the procedure, and then let go of it completely for the remainder of the
procedure. This will be possible by placing a sensor on the co-axial needle, synced to the
EMF generator, that tracks its position and direction in space. With it, the matrix probe can
be synced to the co-axial needle, and create a 2D US-image that corresponds to the position
and direction of the needle, and thereby guide the physician on how they should position the
biopsy gun within the co-axial needle to reach the intended ROI. With the matrix probe, the

US system will have a 3D model of the prostate and its immediate surroundings, during the
entire procedure. This will allow the system to track how well the ROIs are being covered
throughout the procedure, and provide a recommendation on how many samples to take

The Future’s solution is evidently more advanced than Tomorrow’s solution, hence its name.

6.2.4
Koelis Trinity
During our research in the Discover phase, an alternative US system came to our attention,
Koelis Trinity. This system is interesting as it does not use an electro magnetic field (EMF)
generator to track the probe and create the MR-US fusion. Instead it uses a TR probe with
a mechanical array, that can make a sweep covering the full prostate and generate a 3D
volume of it. Besides not needing an EMF generator, the Koelis Trinity is also not affected
by patient movement. It achieves this partly with the aid of a contour painted on the sweep
by the physician, and image recognition.
8
9
==PAGE:100==
To get a feel of how this system fared in real life, we visited Herlev Hospital once again, as
they have used the system for several years. Our takeaway from the visit was quite positive.
The physician was very comfortable with the system, as he has used it for multiple years, but
it was also clear that the system worked well. The biopsies only took around 20-25 minutes
to perform, each with four samples taken. The sweep function allowed the physician to save
a US-image right after a sample had been taken, and afterwards mark the needle location in
the image. This subsequently marked that location on the 3D volume of the prostate. This
gave a good post procedure overview on how well the samples had covered an ROI.
In relation to the two solution proposals, observing the Koelis system in action gave realizations
on some of the chosen sub-solutions:
• Image recognition is a key part of the functionality of the Koelis Trinity system, as it
allows for a simple procedure setup, that is also resistant to patient movements. This
cements our confidence in the viability of using this technology for the solutions.
• Contrary to the physicians we have seen working with BK Medical systems, the physician
using Koelis used a needle guide for the sampling procedure. Like with the BK Medical
systems, a needle guide is not necessary, but through observation it seemed like it
eased the process of aiming the biopsy gun needle for each new sample. Also here,
our confidence in the use of a needle guide in Tomorrow’s solution grew, especially in
relation to lowering procedure complexity.
• Finally, the Koelis system allows the physician to move the highlighting of biopsy sample
areas until after the procedure. Removing a task from the middle of the procedure
lowers the cognitive overload of the physician, and thereby some procedure complexity.
This assures us that automating this process will be beneficial, as it will have the same
effect on procedure complexity, while also giving the physician a continuous overview of
sample coverage during the procedure.
In conclusion, the observation of the Koelis Trinity system was useful at providing insight into
the functionality of another prostate biopsy system. Furthermore, it confirmed our choices of
some of the sub-solutions that have been selected for the two solution proposals.

6.3.1
Constant Skin Contact Device
Research was done to figure out if something like this already existed, but nothing was found.
This meant that we had to develop it from scratch. The starting point was creating a specific
set of requirements for this device.
Requirements & Criteria
The requirements and criteria, can be seen in Appendix 3. As this device does not already
exist, they mainly represent our reflection of what is needed of such a device, based on our
knowledge of the procedure.
Most of the requirements and criteria are not very measurable, since they were impossible to
quantify at this point. However, they provide a framework for brainstorming a solution.
9
1
==PAGE:102==
Idea Generation
First, different mechanical principles for keeping this constant skin contact was brainstormed.
Here two principles were found. Either a wearable solution for the patient, which would
follow the patient movements. Or a pressure device, which would be part of the arm used for
holding the probe. The wearable device was quickly scrapped, as too many issues presented
itself with this solution. This included the challenge of fitting it to different sized humans,
while still having skin contact and also the issue of how to reuse this solution for several
patients. So, the pressure mechanism was chosen as the mechanical principle.
To obtain this pressure, the device would need a spring or something similar. The spring would
be compressed a bit during the setup of the probe, so that it would be able to decompress, if
the patient would move slightly away from the probe. Furthermore, it was believed that the
spring would also allow for small movements in the directions perpendicular to the spring.
This would also be necessary for the device to fully follow patient movements. It was decided
to explore the use of a spring further.
As part of the process of coming up with a design for the spring mechanism, some drawings
were done. Drawings can be seen in Figure 45. 45A shows the initial design idea. 45B shows
our detailed design of the mechanism and how it can be attached to other elements. This
design utilized washers with an indent, to attach the spring in both ends with a bolt and nut.
45C shows the final version, which based on a talk with student workshop employees were
simplified. The springs were simply mounted around a cylinder with the same diameter as the
internal diameter of the spring and kept in place by adding a ’lip’ to the outer barrel.
Figure 45: Sketches of the spring mechanism.
As some interdependence exists between the spring mechanism and other elements of the
system. It was necessary to detail these elements and their interfaces. As the probe layout
with the biopsy gun and how it should interact with the patient and physician were unknown,
this had to be detailed.

options during the procedure, 46D & E were scrapped, as it will be challenging to insert the
needle into the needle guide as the handle is blocking the view. The handle of 46C might

The spring mechanism interface with both probe, stabilizing arm, and biopsy gun. This

Doing these different drawings of the placement of elements in relation to each other made

should be perpendicular to the array surface in a straight line. If the spring mechanism is

6.4.3
Testing the Prototype
The purpose of designing and producing a prototype is to test a hypothesis. The hypothesis
for this prototype concerns the functional principle of the spring mechanism.
Hypothesis
The spring mechanism is able to maintain a connection between the surface of
the probe and the imaging phantom, to provide a consistent US-image when the
imaging phantom is being moved around.
Setup & Execution
To test the hypothesis a setup was put together which can be seen in Figure 57. A prostate
imaging phantom, along with a bkActive scanner, was used to test whether the prototype’s
spring mechanism is able to fulfill the hypothesis. A surgical positioning arm, FlexArm, from
Mediflex was used to fix the position of the prototype in place. The prototype’s probe holder
was fitted with a 9024 bi-plane probe as it was designed for.

Figure 57: Left: Closeup of the probe positioned up against the phantom |
Right: Setup with scanner showing live US-image from the 9024 probe.
To execute the test, the imaging phantom was positioned up against the 9024 probe to
get an initial scan-image of the prostate in both the sagittal and coronal view, as seen in
Figure 57. The imaging phantom was then slowly moved around while monitoring the spring
mechanism and the US-image on the scanner.
Figure 58: Moving the phantom

Results
From the test a number of things can be concluded. The
functional principle of the mechanism works. The setup
allows the imaging phantom to move without negatively
impacting the scan-image as seen in Figure 58. The spring
mechanism allows the probe itself to move, while the rest
of the handle is fixed in place with the Mediflex arm.
The setup had some aspects that worked less ideally:
• The springs used in the prototype are not ideal, but
were also chosen from a very limited selection. The
spring constant of the current spring setup is too
stiff. The force required to compress it deforms the
imaging phantom visibly in the scan-image. This
is not desirable in terms of patient comfort, or in
ease of use for the physician. It also increases the
requirements for the arm that holds the probe, as it
should be completely rigid during procedure.
9
9
==PAGE:110==
• The rigidity of the Mediflex arm and the Gorilla Pod is insufficient at sustaining a fixed
position for the probe, when force is exerted on it.
• The spring mechanism is prone to lock itself in a slanted position. This seems to be
caused by the top plate being caught on the edge of the lip on the outer barrel.
So, is the hypothesis confirmed or should it be discarded? Looking at the results we conclude
that the prototype is able to fulfill the hypothesis, though as described above, there are things
to improve upon. Most significantly, a future prototype should be tested on a human.
As can be seen in the prototype sketches, the prototype was initially supposed to be compatible
with both solution proposals. After building and testing it, however, concerns about the
mechanism’s usability in Tomorrow’s solution arose. The mechanism allows the probe to
move independently of the movement of the probe’s user. This could compromise the usability
of the probe overall, as it would remove some of the control the user normally has of the
probe. The mechanism is likely most appropriate for situations in which the user is not going
to interact continuously with the probe throughout the procedure.
Based on this realization, the mechanism is only a part of the Future’s solution, and not
Tomorrow’s solution.
6.5
The Solution
As no concerns or significant changes were proposed by our company supervisors at the
Milestone 4 meeting, the final solution adapts the two solutions from the solution proposals
in Section 6.2. The Future’s and Tomorrow’s solution represents the best possible solutions
for changing to TP scanning for MR-US targeted prostate biopsies.
The benefit of Tomorrow’s solution is the relatively shorter time to market, since it utilizes
a needle guide which is already used for other needle guidance applications, and also an
easier approach for new users to learn. The benefit of the Future’s solution, lies in the added
flexibility and ability to reach more complex areas of the prostate. However, we estimate it
will take longer to develop due to the maturity of the technology. These considerations will
need to be included, when deciding on which route to take.
As further detailing of the solutions, we have made a Journey Map for each solution. The
Journey Map includes a detailed description of the individual steps for the physician to take
and which equipment is needed. Here, it is assumed that the equipments used, are the ones
we have designed for each of the solutions. The Journey Map can be found in Worksheet
E2. The most defining steps and their benefit compared to the current procedure will be
described in more detail below:

Step 8 - Apply deeper anesthetics through needle guide
To apply the deeper anesthetic around the prostate and in the tissue the needle travels
through, the needle guide is used. The syringe, containing the anesthetic, is inserted into one
of the holes in the needle guide. The US-image is used to guide the needle while emptying
the syringe.
This step provides the benefit of utilizing the needle guide. The needle guide makes sure
the needle is always visible in the plane, which makes the needle guidance a lot easier. The
physician can focus on putting the anesthetization in the right spots, rather than also having
to make sure the needle is visible in the plane.
Step 9 - Create MR-US fusion
This step is similar to what is being done today. The physician will spend some time to fuse
the MR-images, with the US-images. It is done by taking a sweep with the probe and then
marking the outline of the prostate. The 3D volume created from the sweep, is ’scrolled’
through to check that the outline matches in all parallel planes. When the physician is
satisfied with the outline, the fusion is created.
The step differs in the remainder of the procedure as it uses an image recognition algorithm to
make sure the MR- and US-images stay aligned throughout the procedure, even with patient

on the scanner is used to select the appropriate hole to use on the needle guide. The co-axial

To provide flexibility, the co-axial needle is only pushed in a few millimeters. This allows for

need for manually aligning the scan plane with the needle. This removes one of the factors

7.1
Reflections on Results
Changing the Focus
The project was initiated with the purpose of making the patient experience better, by
changing to TP scanning. This is what we have explored and made a solution for, how it will
be technically possible. An ideal approach for taking the patient perspective could have been
to interview a lot of patients before and after the procedure, to find more challenges and root
causes related to them. However, after our first biopsy observation it was quickly realized that
a lot of challenges were associated with the current procedure from a physician perspective.
Also, to make a new procedure allowing for TP scanning, the physician perspective was deemed
the most important as they are the users of the solution. Not including the challenges with
the current procedure, could have resulted in us developing an unusable solution. Realizing
this made us pivot the focus a bit to focus more on the physician needs, as the biggest
potential was seen here. A focus on the patient needs is still included, but do not take the
focus in our project.
Addressing Challenges
During the Develop and Deliver phase, it can be argued that we addressed all eight challenges
located in the problem catalog. Some of them to a higher detail level than others. The
solution paths proposed for each challenge, served more as inspiration for how to dig into the
challenge, and was not followed strictly. Overall the problem catalog provided a structured
framework for us to work with in the remaining parts of the project.
Challenge 1 was addressed via the TP Scan Experiment in Section 5.1, where it was concluded
that it is possible to scan transperineally and achieve sufficient image quality. Challenge 2, 3,
5 & 8 were all connected to the equipment used, and has been addressed in the Morphology
parameters in section 5.2.1. Improved sub-solutions have been proposed for each of the
parameters and the new solutions utilize these, resulting in solutions which address the four
before mentioned challenges. Challenge 4 was addressed as part of the user validation in
Section 5.3.3 with questions related to this challenge. Here, it was concluded that it would
just require some getting used to, before the new perspective would be the normal. Lastly,
challenge 6 & 7 have been addressed by changing the procedure to use a less invasive scanning
method for the patient. These two challenges are not addressed in more detail, due to the
pivoting of focus to the physician instead.
Addressing the Procedure Specification
With our final solution we believe we have addressed most of the requirements and criteria
in the procedure specification (Worksheet S1). All requirements and criteria related to the
1
0
7
==PAGE:118==
physician and patient needs have been fulfilled, which is deemed most critical to address, as
these were left out in development of the RG-TPPB procedure. Some requirements and
criteria connected to effectiveness and safety, such as PCDR of this new procedure and
whether the medical device will be approved by regulatory bodies, is not possible to determine
at this point in time. However, we are confident it will be possible to achieve down the road.
It is just a matter of time or dependent on the money being put into development.
Looking back at our procedure specification, it could have made sense to split it into two
separate specifications. One that was focused on what we are able to affect within the
time of the project and our knowledge & competencies. And another one, which would be
more external factors, that we have learned about through the literature study and deemed
important, but that we are not able to affect directly or assess whether they have been
fulfilled or not. However, this is a reflection in hindsight, and was not something we were
able to commit to at the time of creating the specifications.
Discarding EM Technology
Our final solution utilizes an image recognition algorithm to keep a continuous fuse of the
US and MR-image. This is in our opinion the best way of tracking the prostate movement
during the procedure, as it looks directly at the prostate. The challenges observed with using
EM technology for fusing, introduces too many sources of error which can result in poor
procedure accuracy. BK Medical has already invested in EM tracking for this purpose, but
our recommendation will be to develop the image recognition algorithm for better accuracy
and a better user experience. One aspect to keep in mind here, is that Koelis already seems
to do something like this. It will require some research to figure out how to achieve this
without infringing any patents or intellectual property.

Conclusion
Sprung from the ambition of improving the patient experience during prostate biopsies, the
project scope has expanded to improve the procedure in general, focusing especially on the
physician experience.
With this project we wanted to address the following:
1. What challenges exist with current prostate biopsy procedures?
2. Is it possible to gain an adequate US image of the prostate that can be used to perform
prostate biopsies, by scanning transperineally?
3. What would a PG-TPPB procedure look like?
4. And what changes to the equipment would be necessary to perform the new procedure?
Addressing these four points, spanned the entire timeline of the project, and required a variety
of methods to answer.
With the literature study and especially the observational study it was possible to define a
pretty detailed frame for the state of prostate biopsy procedures in Denmark. The data
acquired in these studies, allowed us to identify eight challenges that cover the issues with the
current procedures, and the barriers a new procedure would have to overcome. Besides the
problems with the patient that initiated the project, these challenges highlighted substantial
problems with the current procedure’s complexity level. With these findings it became clear
that the potential positive impact of the project would be increased by pivoting towards
a holistic view of improving the procedure, and change the main focus to the physician.
From the data analysis it was concluded that most of the original problem with the patient
experience, could be addressed with the change of scan location, while the issues with the
physician experience would require a lot more work.
With this pivot we, of course, did not abandon the original project ambition, and continued work
to uncover the viability of improving the comfort for the patient by scanning transperineally.
This was most significantly done with an experiment that, despite not being performed on
a real person, gave valuable insight into probe and probe-fixture requirements for a TP
probe.
The overview gathered in the first half of the project was used as a reference for generating
ideas for a new procedure. Challenges laid ground to the parameters that framed the
functionality of the new procedure, in order to ensure they would be addressed. Through a
stakeholder involved process including: ideation, concept generation, and subsequent workshop
and user validation, a set of solution proposals were synthesized. As stated many times
1
1
1
==PAGE:122==
already in the report, the involvement of stakeholders in the development of solutions is
integral to its changes for success. This was also apparent during this part of the project,
as the contributions from BK Medical compared with the contribution from the physicians,
highlighted different agendas, one representing the commercial interests of the solution,
and the other representing the functional interests. Having to balance these perspectives
without direct input and collaboration with these stakeholders would have been impossible,
and inevitably would have resulted in a less confident solution.
With a defined framework for a new procedure, the next thing to do is implementation. Easier
said than done, as despite a lot of effort has been put into creating the right solution for
the user’s needs, there is still a change in perspective when going from paper to a physical
object. For this reason, it was important for us to be able to start this building process as it
undoubtedly puts things into a new light. This is also true when working with stakeholders,
as the conversations become a lot more concrete when you have a physical prototype as a
boundary object. Though we do not have the time to build the solutions in their entirety, due
to time limitations, getting the process started correctly, is just as important.
The main contribution of this project is its results, which are twofold. Firstly, it provides a
thorough analysis of current practice for prostate biopsies in Denmark, and the challenges
with it. Secondly, it gives a concrete framework for a new procedure that address and improve

Project Title
Development of Less Invasive Prostate Solution.
Project Description
The thesis project aims to develop a novel diagnostic procedure for the prostate that avoids
transrectal biopsy, complete with recommendations for probe design. In doing so a feasibility
study will explore the viability of using ultrasound scanning of the prostate through the
perineum using probe technology from BK Medical. Feasibility of performing free-hand
prostate biopsies will be investigated and image quality from perineum scanning will be
assessed. Additionally, the project delves into the potential societal and economic implications
of implementing such a procedure.

will serve as the structural basis for the project. It will culminate in a report detailing
findings, recommendations, and will be followed up with an oral defense. The project will be
conducted in collaboration with BK Medical, a subsidiary of GE Healthcare, and involves
close collaboration with medical professionals to ensure the development of a practical and
effective solution.

Project Title
Development of Less Invasive Prostate Diagnostic Solution.
Project Description
Current prostate biopsy procedures use a transrectal ultrasound probe to obtain images
for needle guidance. For patients, the probe is connected with psychologically negative
connotations that impact them before and after the procedure. The thesis project aims to
develop a novel diagnostic procedure for prostate biopsies that avoid the use of the transrectal
probe. A large part of the project will involve a detailed investigation of the status quo, to
locate the underlying critical root causes of the initial problem, before going into a solution
oriented design phase. In doing so the viability of using ultrasound scanning of the prostate
through the perineum will be explored.
The Double Diamond design process methodology will serve as the structural basis for the
project. It will culminate in a report detailing findings, recommendations, and will be followed
up with an oral defense. The project will be conducted in collaboration with BK Medical, a
subsidiary of GE Healthcare, and involves close collaboration with medical professionals to
ensure the development of a practical and effective solution.
