Abstract
The continuous increase in global energy consumption, particularly within the building
sector, necessitates innovative approaches for enhancing energy eﬀiciency and achieving
sustainability goals. This master thesis addresses this challenge by focusing on optimizing
energy management through the development of advanced forecasting models for space
heating (SH) and domestic hot water (DHW) demand in buildings. The research aims
to improve prediction accuracy and reliability, thereby facilitating more eﬀicient building
energy management systems (BEMS) in the context of a Zero-Emission Building (ZEB)
in Trondheim, Norway, and a residential building in Nordhavn, Copenhagen.
Utilizing datasets from the Zero-Emission Building (ZEB) laboratory and the Heat Booster
Substation (HBS) providing the residential building, this research incorporates external
weather data to enrich predictive capabilities. The methodology involves thorough pre-
processing, data analysis, and feature engineering to ensure the models receive feature in-
formation leading to the best possible predictions while remaining compatible with model
requirements. The primary models evaluated include Seasonal Auto-Regressive Integrated
Moving Average with eXogenous regressors (SARIMAX), Long Short-Term Memory net-
works (LSTM), Gated Recurrent Units (GRU), Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN),
and Temporal Fusion Transformers (TFT). These models aimed to predict demand in
both contexts for 24 and 48 hours ahead.
Comprehensive analysis of the two demand time series reveals different patterns that, when
incorporated correctly, can help the models predict distinct occasions. Key influencing
factors such as outdoor temperature, hour of the day, and day of the week are as important
as lagged versions of the time series itself to reflect ongoing weekly and daily patterns.
Results indicate that while traditional models like SARIMAX perform adequately, espe-
cially in the case of DHW demand prediction, advanced neural network models demon-
strate superior accuracy, particularly in capturing non-linear relationships and temporal
patterns in the space heating context. The LSTM and GRU models show robust perfor-
mance in both space heating and DHW demand forecasting, with the CNN and hybrid
models also yielding competitive results. The TFT model, despite its potential for han-
dling temporal data, falls short in accuracy compared to other neural network models,
highlighting the need for further refinement and contextual feature integration.
However, the study also acknowledges certain limitations, including the computational
complexity and runtime issues, as well as lower interpretability of advanced machine learn-
ing models compared to traditional methods. Additionally, the accuracy of forecasts was
influenced by the quality of weather data used as input features.
In conclusion, this thesis successfully achieves its objectives of analyzing SH and DHW
demand patterns, developing and evaluating advanced forecasting models, and improving
prediction accuracy for both SH and DHW demands. The insights and models developed in
this research contribute to the optimization of energy management in buildings, supporting
the broader goal of achieving sustainable and zero-emission buildings. Future research
should explore hybrid models combining traditional and machine learning approaches,
further investigate additional contextual features for model enhancement, and implement
real-time applications to validate model effectiveness in practical settings.

1.1
Background
The continuous growth of global population and economic development have led to a surge
in energy consumption. As one of the main consuming sectors, buildings consume 40% of
global primary energy and contribute in excess of 30% CO2 emissions. The potential to
save energy by systematic building management is known to be significant and estimates
range from 5% to 30% [1]. Therefore managing the energy and other needs in buildings
eﬀiciently and intelligently can have considerable benefits.
These energy saving opportunities, through a systematic building energy management,
have led to a number of studies conducted worldwide to improve building energy eﬀiciency:
on the designs and construction of building envelopes; technical and economic analysis
of energy-eﬀicient measures for the renovation of existing buildings; control of lighting
systems and the control of heating, ventilation and air conditioning (HVAC) installations.
A significant proportion of the increase in energy use was due to the spread of the HVAC
installations in response to the growing demand for better thermal comfort within the
built environment. In general, in developed countries HVAC is the largest energy end-
use, accounting for about half of the total energy consumption in buildings, especially
non-domestic buildings [2].
To reduce this impact, a building energy management system (BEMS) can be an effec-
tive solution. It is a sophisticated method to monitor and control the building’s energy
needs, reducing building heat and energy consumption while maintaining occupant com-
fort.
BEMSs control HVAC and lighting systems in buildings; more specifically, they
control HVAC’s primary components such as air handling units (AHUs), chillers, and
heating elements [3].
It changes a conventional building into an energy-aware environment that enables auto-
mated real-time building management and operations for controlling various aspects of
smart buildings, providing greater energy savings on building appliances and enhancing
indoor comfort [4].
For BEMS, scheduling control, tariff and load control; and smart
home/environment are the three main functions for uplifting the energy-saving effect of
BEMS. Through feedback of human intention, energy savings can be further increased [5].
The general scheme of a BEMS is simply a predictive and optimization series of inputs
from the external data (e.g., weather), sensors (e.g., temperature, lighting, and energy
usage levels), and information from occupants inside. The BEMS algorithms will process
these parameters, resulting in outputs as commands for the actuator to recognize the
conditions of both the systems and the occupants.
Building energy flexibility has been identified as a critical resource in this context that
should be utilised in Demand response scenarios. Demand response refers to balancing

the demand on power grids by encouraging customers to shift electricity demand to times
when electricity is more plentiful or other demand is lower, typically through prices or
monetary incentives. In reality, by modifying HVAC system operations, buildings can
give energy flexibility by utilising their properties, such as thermal inertia, electrical and

Heat demand forecasts can serve as an input (next to other information) for the controller

storage, reducing electricity usage or costs for electricity needed for heating or both.
Using different machine learning algorithms based on historical heating demand, together
with relevant features such as meteorological information, indoor environmental infor-
mation and occupancy-related data, human intention could be predicted and potentially
cause a higher eﬀiciency for the overall heating process [7], [8], [9], [10], [11]. Therefore
prediction of heat demand is the main objective of this master thesis project. The evalu-
ation of the prediction models includes a comparison of their performance in two distinct
contexts: domestic hot water (DHW) demand at a residential building and space heating
(SH) demand at a research building. Both pieces of information are given as time series.
The research aims to contribute to the field by addressing the shortcomings of existing
forecasting models and providing insights into improved models for optimizing heating
management in buildings connected to a district heating system (DHS).

1.1.2
Residential building in Nordhavn, Copenhagen
The investigation around domestic hot water demand takes place for a residential building
in the district Nordhavn of Copenhagen. A heat booster substation (HBS) is located in
the area for a multifamily building for operation in the Ultra Low Temperature District
Heating (ULTDH) system. A schematic of the HBS system is displayed in figure 1.3. A
heat pump (HP) is boosting the district heat (DH) supply temperature up to a level where
domestic hot water can be produced, but optionally also boost the temperature for the
heating system. A DHW system delivers hot water to fixtures used by people at the sink,
shower, tub and any other appliance where water may contact humans. They typically
work by delivering hot water through a centralized storage tank separate from water that
may be used for heating.
ULTDH aims to minimize network thermal losses and increase the energy eﬀiciency of
the total energy chain through the utilization of lower supply temperatures. Moreover,
it provides an opportunity to significantly increase the penetration of RES in the DH
system by acting as a flexible load of the power system through electrically driven HPs
and Thermal Energy Storage (TES) devices [15].

Literature Review
To put my research into perspective, which will help to show a gap of knowledge, that
my research attempts to fill and to use findings and knowledge from previous investi-
gations or studies in the field of forecasting, this literature review is carried out. This
offers the opportunity to collect proven methods from similar contexts for time series data
analysis, feature selection and prediction model architecture that will be worth using and
investigating in the field of building heat demand forecasting.
Before applying any kind of model to a given dataset, the data needs to be preprocessed
and analysed.
Despite differences between the techniques that will be applied in the
following chapters, Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) is a preliminary step that must be
done, regardless of the model architecture. It is focused on analyzing and visualizing data
to summarize their main characteristics and extract relevant information. This process is
of considerable importance in the field of data science, as it establishes the groundwork
for the following step of feature engineering. Which involves creating, transforming, and
extracting features from the dataset, thereby enhancing the model’s performance and
eﬀicacy. The book ”Forecasting: Principles and Practice” [16] provides information on
conducting such analysis on time series data.
The research conducted in the paper Heating load predictions using the static neural
networks method” aims to identify the critical weather parameters that should be incor-
porated into the model creation process, given the significant influence of outdoor weather
conditions on heating load. The building used for investigation in this study is a 9-storey
apartment building in Seoul.
Feed-forward, back-propagation neural network methods
are developed to estimate heating load using dry bulb temperature, dew point temper-
ature, global horizontal radiation, direct normal radiation and wind speed as the input
variables. After an analysis of variance, the conclusion is that dry bulb temperature and
wind speed significantly affect the heating load. The other parameters turned out to not
have a significant effect. The dry bulb temperature had the most significant impact [17].
Another paper ”Impacts of data preprocessing and selection on energy consumption pre-
diction model of HVAC systems based on deep learning” examines the effects of various
data processing methods and feature selection techniques on predicting HVAC energy
consumption using data-driven models. It suggests using inputs such as raw 1-day his-
torical energy consumption, next-day dry-bulb temperature and dew-point temperature,
holiday type, and day type for accurate day-ahead HVAC energy consumption predic-
tions. The study highlights that a larger training set generally leads to higher accuracy
because it encompasses a wider range of energy-use patterns, allowing prediction models
to learn more comprehensive mapping relationships. Additionally, the paper notes that
while data smoothing can enhance the accuracy of prediction models compared to using
smoothed data, it can significantly reduce prediction performance when raw data is used
as a reference. Therefore, data smoothing should be applied cautiously and is not always
recommended [18].
The paper ”Parametric Time-Series Modelling of London Smart Meter Data for Short-
Term Demand Forecasting” employs two statistical time-series models, the Seasonal Auto-
Regressive Integrated Moving Average (SARIMA) and SARIMA with exogenous inputs
(SARIMAX), to forecast half-hourly energy demand and daily peak demand over a week at
8
==PAGE:14==
half-hourly intervals. These models were tuned and tested on both aggregate half-hourly
data and individual meter data from the London smart-meter dataset. Cross-validation
was performed across different seasons to evaluate the models’ robustness with varying
training data sizes and temperature conditions. The SARIMA model showed superior
performance in consistently forecasting daily demand peaks. However, the SARIMAX
model demonstrated overall higher accuracy, albeit at a higher computational cost, due
to the inclusion of the exogenous temperature variable, which accounts for demand pro-
file volatility caused by temperature fluctuations. Consequently, the SARIMAX model
provided a better fit [9].
In predictive energy management, control is executed by applying a predictive model to
measured data to forecast the most optimal control strategy that can be performed. The
paper ”Machine learning-based energy use prediction for the smart building energy man-
agement system” sums up various studies in the building sector that have extensively
explored ML development, particularly in energy, building performance, and occupant
comfort. These studies demonstrate the eﬀicacy of ML models in predicting energy per-
formance, retrofitting scenarios, and HVAC control systems for buildings. Notably, ML
methods like Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM), Convolutional Neural Network (CNN),
Gradient-Boosted Regression Trees (GBRT), and deep reinforcement learning (RL) algo-
rithms have been successfully applied, offering faster computation times and improved
eﬀiciency compared to traditional simulation tools. The research suggests that a smart
building energy management system (SBEMS) utilizing ML algorithms can bridge knowl-
edge gaps, enhance operational eﬀiciency, and contribute to the widespread adoption of
smart building solutions by empowering stakeholders with data-driven decision-making
capabilities. Overall, the integration of ML-based SBEMS is expected to showcase the
multifaceted benefits of smart buildings, ultimately reducing environmental impact and
promoting informed decision-making in building management [8].
The study ”An Energy Consumption Prediction Method for HVAC Systems Using Energy
Storage Based on Time Series Shifting and Deep Learning” investigates peak shaving and
valley filling in the context of energy consumption of oﬀice building energy-storage HVAC
systems. The research emphasizes that deep learning algorithms, such as the Gated Re-
curring Unit (GRU) model, excel in identifying temporal patterns and dependencies in
time-series data. This capability allows GRU models to effectively capture long-term de-
pendencies and temporal dynamics, yielding superior results in time-series predictions.
Conversely, traditional models like Support Vector Regression (SVR) and Random Forest
(RF) exhibit limited sensitivity to time-series patterns, resulting in less significant im-
provements in prediction performance. The GRU model’s proficiency in capturing complex
nonlinear relationships proves advantageous for predicting energy consumption patterns,
which are often influenced by such characteristics. In comparison, SVR and RF models
struggle to model these nonlinear relationships effectively, leading to lower prediction ac-
curacy. The study further demonstrates that applying time-series shifting enhances the
prediction performance of the GRU model significantly [10].
The paper Deep neural network based demand side short term load forecasting proposes
the use of deep neural network (DNN)-based load forecasting models, applied to an em-
pirical load database on the demand side. These DNN models are trained using individual
customers’ electricity consumption data alongside regional meteorological elements. The
results demonstrate that DNNs provide more accurate and robust predictions compared
to other forecasting models, such as the double seasonal Holt-Winters (DSHW) model, the
autoregressive integrated moving average (ARIMA) model, and shallow neural network
9
==PAGE:15==
(SNN) models [11].
Given the critical importance of short-term forecasting in heating energy management,
Salehi et al. [7] evaluated six prevalent deep-learning algorithms, including both single
and hybrid models, for predicting energy load as heat demand density. The single model
architectures assessed were LSTM, Recurrent Neural Network (RNN), and Convolutional
Neural Network (CNN), with hybrid models created from these bases. The results in-
dicated that hybrid models using CNN were the overall best performers, irrespective of
being multivariate or univariate. However, the prediction accuracy of the best-performing
multivariate hybrid models declined faster per hour compared to univariate models. These
findings suggest that multivariate models are more suitable for early time step predictions,
while univariate models perform better at later time steps. Combining these models can
thus enhance accuracy across various time steps, offering a comprehensive tool for energy
management.
The study also employed backward and forward feature selection tech-
niques, identifying the most significant features as dry bulb temperature, direct normal
radiation, day of the week, snow depth, occupancy schedule, and heating demand in the
previous hour. Among these, dry bulb temperature consistently appeared in all feature
sets, underscoring its crucial role given the significant impact of outdoor temperatures on
heating energy demand in cold climates. Furthermore, the research demonstrated that
multivariate models were more effective in forecasting heat demand density than univari-
ate approaches, highlighting the importance of incorporating multiple variables and their
interdependencies in predictive modelling. The study concluded that while hybrid models
generally outperformed single models, and multivariate models outperformed univariate
ones, the superiority of hybrid and multivariate models was marginal.
The review ”A Comprehensive Review of the Applications of Machine Learning for HVAC”
provides a thorough summary of recent literature on the use of machine learning for
optimizing, controlling, and detecting faults in HVAC systems. The review highlights the
widespread use of deep neural networks (DNNs) in recent years, noting their power and
versatility.
DNNs can achieve high accuracy and apply to a range of tasks, including
supervised learning, unsupervised learning and time series or sequence problems using
recurrent neural networks (RNNs). Convolutional or dense layers are particularly useful
for feature extraction to improve classification, especially with non-linearly separable data
sets.
However, the paper also discusses the limitations of DNNs.
These include the
extremely large number of parameters, which increases the risk of overfitting, the diﬀiculty
in achieving stable solutions due to high model variance, the challenge of finding optimal
architectures given the vast search space, and the significant time required to design and
validate DNNs [19].
The part Neural Networks and Deep Learning from the book ”Hands-on machine learn-
ing with scikit-learn, keras, and tensorflow” provides information about the fundamental
components of neural networks, including neurons, layers, and activation functions. It
progresses to training a neural network using TensorFlow, a prominent deep-learning li-
brary. It also delves into more advanced neural network architectures, such as CNNs and
RNNs for sequential data. Additionally, it covers essential techniques like hyperparameter
tuning, which are critical for developing effective deep-learning models [20].
The paper ”Short-Term Electricity Load Forecasting Based on Temporal Fusion Trans-
former Model” addresses the limitations of traditional forecasting methods like AR, ARIMA,
and SARIMA, which have been widely used for short-term electricity load forecasting. Re-
cent advances have shown that artificial and deep neural networks significantly improve

forecasting accuracy over these traditional statistical models.
The study introduces a
novel recurrent neural network model, the Temporal Fusion Transformer (TFT), to fore-
cast short-term electricity load in Hanoi city. The TFT combines the advantages of LSTM
networks and the self-attention mechanism to enhance performance. In addition to histor-
ical load data, the model incorporates temperature, humidity, and time-related features
such as calendar month, lunar month, day of the week, hour of the day, and holidays.
The forecast results from the TFT model are compared with those from traditional sta-
tistical models and well-known RNN models. The comparison shows that the TFT model
outperforms other methods in both Mean Absolute Error (MAE) and Mean Absolute
Percentage Error (MAPE) criteria. The study highlights the effectiveness of deep learn-

interpretability for short-term electricity load forecasting [21].
The paper ”Temporal Fusion Transformer for Thermal Load Prediction in District Heating
and Cooling Networks” investigates the effectiveness of the Temporal Fusion Transformer
(TFT) in forecasting thermal loads for district heating and cooling systems. It begins
by summarizing the differences between TFT and other machine-learning methods, then
details the process of training a machine-learning model for thermal load forecasting using
TFT. The study utilizes eight years of hourly operating data from the district heating
network in Ulm, Germany, to train and evaluate the TFT model. The method was ap-
plied to produce 72-hour heating load forecasts for three different district heating grids in
Ulm. The use of TFT demonstrated potential for significant improvements in thermal load
forecasting. Benchmarking results show that TFT outperforms other machine learning-
based forecasting approaches for district heating and cooling networks. Predictions were
analyzed across multiple time frames and heating networks, with all data, including sta-
tistically optimized point weather forecasts, pre-processed before training and validation.
The TFT model, predicting 72 hours in advance, achieved better Mean Absolute Errors
(MAEs) and Mean Absolute Percentage Errors (MAPEs) compared to three other machine
learning methods evaluated in previous studies. Notably, the TFT model showed signif-
icant improvements in forecast accuracy during the spring and fall seasons, highlighting
its robustness and effectiveness for thermal load forecasting in various scenarios [22].
The literature reviewed highlights the critical importance of data preprocessing, feature
selection, and model architecture in enhancing the accuracy and eﬀiciency of load fore-

Studies such as ”Heating load predictions using the static neural networks method” under-

and wind speed being crucial for heating load estimation.
Advanced machine learning and deep learning models, including Long Short-Term Mem-
ory (LSTM), Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN), and Temporal Fusion Transformer

like ARIMA and SARIMA. For instance, the TFT model, as shown in studies on short-term
electricity load forecasting and thermal load prediction, effectively combines historical load
data with weather and temporal features, achieving lower Mean Absolute Errors (MAEs)
and Mean Absolute Percentage Errors (MAPEs) compared to other models.
Moreover, the review emphasizes the importance of hybrid models and multivariate ap-

Hybrid models, particularly those integrating CNNs, have shown enhanced
performance in predicting energy loads across different time steps, highlighting the ad-

The research goals of this thesis aim to fill gaps identified in the current literature on heat
demand forecasting, particularly in the context of space heating and domestic hot water
demand in oﬀice and residential buildings.
By continuing and adapting the collected information on EDA, analyzing the space heating
demand at the ZEB intends to uncover patterns, understand behaviours, and derive useful
features. This foundational analysis will address the current lack of detailed studies on SH
demand patterns in oﬀice buildings, providing valuable insights that can enhance feature
engineering for predictive models. The given literature provides insights into significant
features for similar use cases, which should be adapted in this research.
Developing and evaluating various forecasting models for SH demand at the ZEB aims to
explore advanced machine-learning techniques, such as LSTM, CNN, GRU and TFT tai-
lored to the characteristics of oﬀice and residential buildings. Existing research provides a
foundation of model architectures that have proven performance in similar contexts, which
can guide this research with its own model architecture creation. This effort will contribute
to the broader understanding of how modern forecasting methods can be optimized for
energy-eﬀicient structures and how much better they potentially perform compared to a

itations of existing SARIMA models, this research will develop and evaluate forecasting
models for DHW demand at the HBS. By overcoming issues related to data availability
and irregular consumption patterns during holidays and weekends, this study will enhance

Comparing the performance of various forecasting models in the contexts of DHW at a
residential building and SH at a research building will provide a comprehensive evaluation
of their applicability and effectiveness. This comparative analysis will fill a gap in the
literature by providing empirical evidence on the relative strengths and weaknesses of
different models across diverse scenarios.
By achieving these goals, this research aims to improve forecasting accuracy and reliability
for both SH and DHW demands, addressing significant limitations in existing models
and contributing valuable knowledge to the field of energy management in sustainable
buildings.

4.1
Pearson and Spearman correlation
Using the Pearson correlation coeﬀicient between a potential feature and a target variable
is beneficial for several reasons when determining if the feature is significantly helpful
for prediction in a model. The Pearson correlation coeﬀicient measures the strength and
direction of the linear relationship between two variables. A high positive or negative
correlation with the target variable indicates a strong linear relationship, suggesting that
changes in the feature are associated with predictable changes in the target variable,
making it a potentially valuable predictor. Also a correlation measure can help to identify
possible connections between a error time series from forecast weather data to actual
weather data and the error time series from predictions to actual values for the target
variable.
Features with higher absolute correlation values can be considered more important and
relevant for predicting the target variable. This helps in selecting the most significant
features and reducing the dimensionality of the dataset, improving model performance
and reducing overfitting. Identifying features with significant correlations can also simplify
the model-building process by providing a clear starting point for feature engineering and
prioritizing which features to focus on.
However, Pearson correlation has limitations. It only measures linear relationships and
will not effectively capture non-linear relationships. It is sensitive to outliers, which can
distort the perceived strength of the relationship, and it assumes that the data is nor-
mally distributed, which may not always be the case. Despite these limitations, Pearson
correlation remains a valuable tool for initial feature evaluation and selection [26].

The Spearman correlation coeﬀicient offers several advantages that make it a valuable
complement to the Pearson correlation in analyzing the relationships between variables.
Unlike Pearson correlation, which measures linear relationships, Spearman correlation
assesses the strength and direction of monotonic relationships, capturing both linear and
certain types of non-linear associations.
It is less sensitive to outliers because it is based on the ranks of the data rather than the
raw values, providing a more robust measure in the presence of extreme values. As a non-
parametric measure, Spearman correlation does not assume any specific data distribution,
making it versatile and applicable to a wider range of datasets.
Using both Pearson and Spearman correlations together can enrich the analysis by re-
vealing both linear and monotonic relationships, offering a comprehensive understanding
of the data’s underlying patterns and guiding better feature selection and model devel-
opment decisions. If both correlations are high, it suggests a strong linear relationship.
If Spearman is high but Pearson is low, it indicates a strong monotonic but non-linear
relationship [27].

Evaluation metrics RMSE and MAE
To be able to evaluate and compare the different prediction model architectures with
respect to their predictions, common metrics need to be implemented.
Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) is a commonly used metric for evaluating the accuracy
of time series forecasting models. It is the square root of the Mean Squared Error (MSE),
and it measures the average difference between the predicted and actual values, taking
into account the squared differences to emphasize larger errors. Applying the square root
expresses the result in the original magnitude of the variables. One of the main advantages

of RMSE is that it penalizes large errors due to squaring, making it particularly useful
when large errors are undesirable. Additionally, it is easily interpretable as it expresses
the error in the same units as the original data. However, RMSE is sensitive to outliers
and can be skewed by extreme values.
Furthermore, RMSE does not provide insights
into the direction or pattern of forecast errors, nor does it distinguish between over- and
under-predictions [29].
Mean Absolute Error (MAE) measures the average size of the error between predicted and
actual values in a time series dataset. It is calculated by taking the absolute difference
between the predicted and actual values and averaging them. One of the main advantages
of MAE is its simplicity and ease of interpretation, as the mean error is expressed in the
same units as the original data. Additionally, MAE is less sensitive to outliers compared
to other error metrics such as MSE. However, MAE has some disadvantages, it does not
distinguish between overestimation and underestimation, and it does not provide infor-
mation about the direction or magnitude of individual errors. Furthermore, depending on
the specific problem, it may be seen as a disadvantage that MAE does not penalize wrong
predictions as much as MSE [29].

4.4
SARIMAX model
The SARIMAX model (Seasonal Auto-Regressive Integrated Moving Average with eXoge-
nous regressors) is an extension of the ARIMA model that incorporates seasonality and
exogenous variables to improve forecasting accuracy. This model is particularly useful for
time series data that exhibit seasonal patterns and are influenced by external factors. It
is therefore very suitable as a first true prediction model and forms another benchmark to
compare the neural network models against.
The purpose of the model is to fit temporal structures within the time series data to predict
future values. The autoregression part of the model measures the dependency with itself
through lagged values. Differences are implemented to make the data patterns stationary.
Moving average helps condense and bring out significant features from the data.
The pre-processed datasets are scaled to remove impact from features with different mag-
nitudes. After that, they are split into training and test sets. The SARIMAX models are
trained on the training sets and their predictions are measured against the test sets after
inverse scaling the results.
The SARIMAX model can be described by the notation:
SARIMAX(p, d, q)(P, D, Q, s)
with the inclusion of exogenous variables. Here’s a breakdown of the components:
Non-seasonal components:
• p: The order of the autoregressive (AR) part.
• d: The degree of differencing involved.
• q: The order of the moving average (MA) part.
Seasonal components:
• P: The order of the seasonal autoregressive part.
• D: The degree of seasonal differencing.
• Q: The order of the seasonal moving average part.
• s: The length of the seasonal cycle.
Exogenous variables (X): Additional independent variables that are expected to influ-
ence the dependent variable.
The parameter p denotes how much lagged information (until which timestep in the past)
is taken into account for the autoregression. Parameter d corresponds to how often the

Building a SARIMAX model is carried out with the following consecutive steps [30]:
1. Stationarity Check
A stationary time series is one whose statistical properties do not depend on the time at
which the series is observed assuming it has constant mean, variance, and autocorrelation
over time. Ensuring stationarity is critical because many time series models, including
SARIMAX, assume the series is stationary [31]. Tests for stationarity are the following:
• Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) Test: This test aims to reject the null hy-
pothesis that the time series has a unit root (i.e., it is non-stationary). If the p-value
is less than the significance level 0.05, the series is considered stationary.
• Kwiatkowski-Phillips-Schmidt-Shin (KPSS) Test: This test’s null hypothesis
is that the series is stationary around a constant. If the p-value is greater than the
significance level 0.05, the series is considered stationary.
If the series is non-stationary, apply differencing to remove trends and seasonality:
• Non-seasonal differencing (d): Subtracts the previous observation from the cur-
rent observation.
• Seasonal differencing (D): Subtracts the observation from the same season in the
previous cycle.
Finding the hyperparameters d and D, it needs to be investigated how often the data
needs to be differenced to be stationary to prevent incorrect bias while training the model.

4.5.1
Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)
Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) is a type of recurrent neural network (RNN) architec-
ture that is particularly well-suited for time series forecasting and other sequence learning
tasks. The core component of LSTM is the memory cell. Memory cells are designed to
maintain information over long periods of time. They do this by explicitly controlling how
information is added, updated, and forgotten. LSTM networks use gates to regulate the
flow of information into and out of the memory cells. There are three main types of gates:
• Input Gate: Controls the extent to which new information flows into the memory
cell.
• Forget Gate: Decides what information to discard from the memory cell.
• Output Gate: Determines what information is output from the memory cell.
The cell state is a key component that runs through the entire chain of the network, with
minor linear interactions. The LSTM can remove or add information to the cell state,
carefully regulated by the gates.
LSTM networks use three types of gates to manage information flow. The input gate
employs an activation function to determine which input values should update the memory
state, followed by another activation layer to generate new candidate values. The forget
gate uses an activation function to decide what information to discard from the cell state.
The output gate filters the cell state with an activation function and then scales the output
using another activation function to a specific range.
LSTMs excel at managing long-term dependencies by overcoming the vanishing gradient
problem that impairs traditional RNNs. Their memory cell and gating mechanism enable
them to retain information over extended periods. This selective memory capability allows
LSTMs to remember and forget information as needed, which is crucial for time series data
where past information varies in importance.
43
==PAGE:49==
4.5. NEURAL NETWORKS
Designed to handle sequential data, LSTMs consider both current input and previous
states, making them highly effective for forecasting tasks. They are adept at capturing
temporal dependencies, essential for understanding short-term patterns and long-term
trends in time series forecasting. Moreover, LSTMs demonstrate robustness to noise by
selectively forgetting irrelevant information and can adapt to different time scales, making
them versatile for various types of time series data, whether daily, weekly, or monthly [33].
4.5.2
Gated Recurring Unit (GRU)
Gated Recurrent Units (GRUs) are a type of RNN architecture, similar to LSTM networks
but with a simplified structure. They manage the flow of information using two main
gates: the reset gate and the update gate. The reset gate controls how much of the past
information to forget, while the update gate regulates how much of the new information
to pass to the current state. Unlike LSTMs, GRUs combine the hidden state and the cell
state into a single entity, simplifying their architecture.
Typically, GRUs use sigmoid and tanh activation functions to model complex, non-linear
relationships in the data, but also other activation functions are tested for performance.
The reset gate applies an activation function to decide which part of the previous hidden
state to discard before applying new input. The update gate also uses this activation
function to balance the previous hidden state with the new candidate state, determining
how much of the past state should be carried forward. The candidate hidden state is
computed using the reset gate and current input, processed through another activation
function, and then combined with the previous hidden state based on the update gate’s
output.
GRUs are notable for their simplicity. They use fewer gates and combine the cell state
and hidden state into one, making them computationally more eﬀicient and faster to
train compared to LSTMs. Despite their simplicity, GRUs effectively capture long-term
dependencies in data, a crucial aspect for tasks like time series forecasting. Their fewer
gates and combined state also make GRUs less memory-intensive, beneficial for large
datasets or models.
The update gate in GRUs allows for selective memory, enabling the network to retain or
discard information as needed. This capability is essential for time series data, where not
all past information is equally important. GRUs handle sequential data by considering
both current input and previous states, making them effective for forecasting tasks. They
can understand both short-term patterns and long-term trends, critical for accurate time
series forecasting.
GRUs are robust to noise in time series data due to their ability to selectively forget
irrelevant information. They can adapt to various time scales in the data, making them
versatile for different types of time series, whether daily, weekly, or monthly.
In comparison to LSTMs, GRUs are simpler, using fewer gates and no separate cell state,
leading to faster training times and lower computational costs. While both GRUs and
LSTMs often perform similarly on many tasks, GRUs might be preferred for simpler
models or when computational eﬀiciency is a priority. GRUs are generally more memory-
eﬀicient due to their simpler architecture, making them suitable for tasks with limited com-
putational resources. Although LSTMs might capture more intricate temporal dynamics
in some datasets due to their more complex structure, GRUs often achieve comparable
performance with less complexity [34],[35].

4.5. NEURAL NETWORKS
4.5.3
Convolutional Neural Network (CNN)
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) are a type of deep learning model primarily used
for processing grid-like data, such as images.
However, their architecture can also be
adapted for time series forecasting.
The core component of CNNs is the convolutional layers, which apply filters (kernels) to
the input data to extract features. Each filter captures a different aspect of the input, such
as edges in images or patterns in time series data. After convolution, activation functions
introduce non-linearity into the model, allowing it to learn complex patterns.
Pooling layers reduce the spatial dimension of the data, helping to decrease computational
complexity and control overfitting. Max pooling, the most common type, takes the max-
imum value from a set of neighboring pixels. In the later stages of the network, fully
connected layers integrate features learned by convolutional and pooling layers to make
predictions, similar to traditional neural networks. Before passing data to fully connected
layers, the multidimensional output of convolutional layers is flattened into a single vector.
CNNs use local receptive fields through convolutional layers, meaning each neuron is con-
nected to only a small region of the input data. This helps in capturing spatial hierarchies
and patterns. Parameter sharing occurs because filters are shared across the entire in-
put space, drastically reducing the number of parameters compared to fully connected
networks. This makes CNNs more eﬀicient and less prone to overfitting.
CNNs are adept at automatically extracting important features from raw data. In time
series forecasting, they can identify temporal patterns such as trends and seasonal effects
even without manual feature engineering. The use of convolutional layers allows CNNs to
capture local patterns in the time series data, which is particularly useful for identifying
short-term patterns. They can handle multivariate time series data eﬀiciently, learning to
extract features from multiple input channels simultaneously.
Pooling layers aid in reducing the dimensionality of the data, making the network faster
and less prone to overfitting, while still preserving essential information. Moreover, CNNs
can be combined with other models like LSTMs or GRUs to capture temporal (long-term)
dependencies. This hybrid approach can lead to more accurate time series forecasting
models [36].
4.5.4
Temporal Fusion Transformer (TFT)
The Temporal Fusion Transformer (TFT) is a sophisticated neural network architecture
designed specifically for time series forecasting. It combines several advanced techniques
to handle both short-term and long-term dependencies in time series data.
Input Embedding Layer: Converts categorical and continuous inputs into a unified rep-
resentation. Variable Selection Network: Dynamically selects relevant variables at each
time step. Gating Mechanism: Controls the flow of information through the network to
prevent overfitting and manage complexity. LSTM Encoder-Decoder: Captures sequen-
tial dependencies in the data. Static Covariate Encoder: Integrates static features that
do not change over time. Temporal Self-Attention Layer: Models long-term dependencies
by attending to different time steps. Instance Normalization: Normalizes input data to
stabilize training and improve performance. One of TFT’s standout features is its dy-
namic variable selection network, which dynamically selects the most relevant variables at
45
==PAGE:51==
4.5. NEURAL NETWORKS
each time step. This ensures the model focuses on the most important features, enhancing
forecast accuracy and interpretability.
TFT is designed for multi-horizon forecasting, meaning it can predict multiple future
time steps simultaneously. This capability is particularly useful in practical applications
where forecasts for different time periods are needed. The architecture effectively handles
missing data through its gating mechanism and attention layers, making it robust in
real-world scenarios where data might be incomplete or irregular.
The temporal self-
attention layer allows TFT to model long-term dependencies by attending to various time
steps, enhancing its ability to capture long-range patterns and trends in the data. By
incorporating a static covariate encoder, TFT integrates static features with dynamic time
series data, providing a comprehensive understanding of the data. The combination of
dynamic variable selection and self-attention mechanisms makes TFT highly accurate and
interpretable, as it identifies and focuses on the most relevant features and dependencies in
the data. TFT’s ability to handle missing data and integrate static and dynamic features
makes it robust and versatile in various real-world forecasting scenarios [37].
As can be seen, neural networks, including LSTM, GRU, CNN, and TFT, offer powerful
methods for time series prediction, particularly when additional features are involved.
Each method has its strengths, with LSTM and GRU excelling in capturing sequential
dependencies, CNNs in extracting local patterns, and TFTs in handling complex temporal
relationships.

The training and test set follow the splits regarding time as mentioned at the beginning

Since the SARIMAX model creates lags and moving averages within itself, these haven’t

Predictions for the next 24 hours and 48 hours are created through a sliding window going
through the whole testset in steps of 48 hours. This means that after the first prediction
for 1st and 2nd of February 2023, the model includes these days now in the training set
and predicts for the next two following days. SARIMAX is heavily relying on the starting
position from the forecast and for such variable data as the heat energy consumption it
can’t forecast long into the future.

SARIMAX model
The training and test set follow the splits regarding time as mentioned at the beginning of
the section and consist of consumption as the target variable and of outside temperature
and solar radiation as the exogenous variables from the Open-Meteo actual weather data
API. Since the SARIMAX model creates lags and moving averages within itself, these
haven’t been introduced into the dataset.
Predictions for the next 24 hours and 48 hours are created through a sliding window going
through the whole testset in steps of 14 days, to keep inference time moderate, because of
runtime issues. This means that after the first prediction for 18th and 19th of December
2019, the model includes these days now in the training set as well as an additional 12 days
and predicts for the next two following days after the window moved 14 days further from
the beginning. Error metrics are calculated for this whole test set time frame. Next to
that the models are visualised for the exemplary week and a holiday period as mentioned
before.
To identify hyperparameters for the SARIMAX model, differencing of the time series with
autocorrelation and partial autocorrelation analysis is being carried out.
The differencing approach in figure 5.36 successfully reduces autocorrelation, making the
series more suitable for ARIMA modeling. However, the residual spikes indicate potential
seasonality that might require additional modeling techniques or seasonal differencing to
address fully. The pattern in the ACF and PACF in figure 5.37 indicates that a first
and seasonal differencing (168 lags) is appropriate for making the series stationary. The
absence of significant autocorrelation after lag 1 in the PACF confirms the appropriateness
of the differencing approach.

6
Conclusion
This research aimed to analyze space heating demand at the Zero-Emission Building and
domestic hot water demand at the Heat Booster Substation, develop and evaluate forecast-
ing models for SH and DHW demand, and improve these models to address the limitations
of previous approaches. The following key objectives were achieved:
Analysis of SH demand at ZEB:
The analysis of space heating demand at the ZEB revealed distinct patterns and behaviors
in energy consumption, reflecting the use of an oﬀice, where people come in the morning
and leave in the afternoon. By examining the data, significant features such as tempera-
ture, time of day, and day of the week were identified as influential factors in SH demand.
This understanding provided a foundation for developing more accurate forecasting mod-
els.
Analysis of DHW demand at HBS:
The analysis of domestic hot water demand was carried out in a similar way as the analysis
for space heating demand. A different consumption pattern could be identified, reflecting
the use of residents in peak periods before and after work, creating a bathtub-like demand
curve.
Key conclusions were similar to the analysis for the other context, consistent
weekly patterns suggest using weekly features derived from lagged or exogenous variables,
differences between weekdays and weekends imply the need for categorical features to
distinguish between normal days and weekends, features representing different seasons are
needed, derived from calendar data or temperature records and features representing the
hour of the day to capture consumption peaks and bottoms will be necessary.
Development and evaluation of forecasting models for SH demand:
Various forecasting models were developed and evaluated for predicting SH demand 24
and 48 hours ahead.
The models included traditional approaches like SARIMAX and
machine learning-based methods such as LSTM, GRU, CNN, and hybrid models. The
results showed that neural network models generally outperformed traditional methods,
particularly in capturing complex temporal dependencies and variability in the data. For
instance, the LSTM model demonstrated superior performance with lower RMSE and
MAE values compared to SARIMAX, across both 24 hour and 48 hour forecasts. The error
analysis for weather forecast errors compared with the errors of the different prediction
time series came to the conclusion that errors in the weather forecast seemed to not have an
impact on the prediction results, through different behaviour of errors at different times of
the day next to low correlation. The error analysis showed also that high errors appeared
in the model predictions at times where there was high actual demand.
Development and evaluation of forecasting models for DHW demand:
Similar to SH demand, forecasting models for DHW demand at the Heat Booster Substa-
tion were developed and assessed. Previous models faced limitations due to their depen-
dency on recent data and poor performance during irregular consumption patterns like
holidays. The introduction of machine learning models addressed these issues, providing
possibilities for models with less recent data needed. The best neural network models yet
still were facing issues in predicting irregular patterns such as public holidays. Hybrid
models were showing promising approaches to tackle these issues. Over the whole testing
time frame the implemented SARIMAX and neural network models still outperformed
101
==PAGE:107==
the baseline model and two distinct SARIMAX models for working day and weekend
prediction shows promising results at least for regular patterns.
Comparison of forecasting models:
The performance comparison of different forecasting models highlighted the strengths and
weaknesses of each approach in the contexts of SH at ZEB and DHW at HBS. Neural
network models consistently provided better performance metrics, with lower RMSE and
MAE values, indicating their robustness in handling complex and irregular consumption
patterns, especially in the case of space heating demand prediction. In domestic hot water
consumption prediction the neural network models did not stand out as much, so that
improved SARIMAX models together with higher computational power could be leading
in performance in this context. The TFT model did not live up to the expectations of
accurate predictions that could keep up in performance with the other neural network
models. The predictions were mainly far off struggling to capture dynamic patterns in
the data. Reasons for that could be too few data, wrong implementation and not enough
tuned hyperparameters.
Limitations:
One major limitation encountered was the computational complexity. The advanced ma-
chine learning models employed required significant computational resources and time for
both training and prediction. This high demand for resources often made the process
lengthy and intensive and also lead to moving one with less extensive models.
Another significant challenge was model interpretability.
While the machine learning
models provided higher accuracy, their complexity rendered them less interpretable com-
pared to traditional models like SARIMAX. This lack of interpretability made it diﬀicult
to understand the underlying mechanics and justify the model’s predictions.
Additionally, the models struggled with predicting sudden spikes or drops in energy de-
mand. This limitation posed a challenge in accurately forecasting abrupt changes in energy
consumption, which are critical for eﬀicient energy management.
In the context of DHW demand prediction at the HBS, there could be an issue of overfitting
to the training data. This was particularly evident for the data in the year 2019, which was
used for training, where the overall energy consumption was higher compared to 2020, the
year used for testing the model. This discrepancy could have led to the model being overly
tailored to the training data, resulting in less accurate predictions for future periods.
Suggestions for further research:
Suggestions for further research include exploring the development of hybrid models that
combine the strengths of different machine learning approaches to balance accuracy. Addi-
tionally, further investigation into additional contextual features, such as occupancy data,
could enhance model performance giving deeper insights for the model into the variability
of behaviour. Implementing and testing these models in a real-time building energy man-
agement system could provide practical insights into their effectiveness and scalability,
which is already carried out at the ZEB research laboratory.
In conclusion, this research successfully achieved its objectives by developing and evaluat-
ing advanced forecasting models that improve the accuracy and reliability of SH and DHW
demand predictions. These models offer promising solutions for optimizing energy man-
agement in buildings, contributing to the broader goal of achieving zero-emission buildings
and sustainable energy systems.
