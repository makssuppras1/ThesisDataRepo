Abstract
The study of Hemiwicking phenomena plays a crucial role in advancing the under-
standing of liquid transport in micro- and nanostructured surfaces, with applications
spanning energy harvesting, thermal management, and microfluidic devices. This thesis
investigates the behavior of Hemiwicking on nanostructured fused silica surfaces, fab-
ricated and characterized through advanced lithographic and etching techniques. By
controlling structural parameters such as nanopillar height, diameter, and aspect ratio,
the research explores their influence on the Hemiwicking length and dynamics under
varying flow rates and UV exposure conditions.
A modular experimental setup is employed to record and measure Hemiwicking pro-
gression in real time. The raw data is analyzed using a combination of direct mea-
surements and analytical curve fitting, employing a composite function that models
the transient growth and stabilization of the liquid front.
Three convergence met-
rics—numerical derivatives, root mean square error (RMSE), and cumulative moving
average (CMA)—are applied to ensure reliable quantification of the Hemiwicking length.
The final values are computed as weighted averages, with uncertainties quantified using
a blended error approach that incorporates maximum deviation, weighted variance, and
the standard deviation of the means.
The fabrication process utilizes UV lithography, inductively coupled plasma etching, and
atomic layer deposition to create high-precision nanostructures. Nanopillar dimensions
are characterized using scanning electron microscopy (SEM), with error propagation
techniques ensuring robust uncertainty estimation. Systematic investigations reveal the
critical role of nanopillar geometry and etching parameters in dictating the Hemiwicking
behavior. Flow rate and UV exposure further modulate the liquid transport, offering
insights into the interplay of surface geometry and fluid dynamics.
This work provides a way to reproducibly extract the Hemiwiking length from the fabri-
cated samples and demonstrates that it is possible to fabricate sample with nanopillars
on fused silica coated in TiO2 that demonstrates the light induced hydrophilicity of a
metal-oxide thin film.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Introduction
The fabrication of surfaces with engineered roughness is a critical area of study, with
applications ranging from microfluidics to photocatalysis. This thesis explores the fab-
rication of fused silica chips with arrays of nanopillars to simulate rough surfaces and
examine the spread of liquid along the surface, quantified as the hemiwicking length. The
ability of such surfaces to manipulate liquid transport is fundamental to both scientific
and industrial processes, including lab-on-chip technologies and advanced coatings.
The hemiwicking length is formally defined as the maximum distance a liquid can
spread over a textured or patterned surface without the assistance of external forces,
purely driven by capillary action. It quantifies how far a liquid can travel along a tex-
tured surface while maintaining contact with specific surface features (e.g., nanopillars
or microgrooves). This parameter is crucial for understanding the physics of liquid-solid
interactions, surface tension, and wetting phenomena.
Capillary forces, the primary driver of hemiwicking, arise from the interplay between
a liquid, a solid surface, and the surrounding environment, governed predominantly by
surface tension. These forces are responsible for phenomena such as liquid climbing
up a straw, spreading on a solid surface, or transport through porous materials. In the
context of textured surfaces, the arrangement and dimensions of features like nanopillars
dictate the balance between capillary and gravitational forces, ultimately controlling the
extent of liquid spreading.
The study draws inspiration from recent work on photocatalytic hydrophilicity in metal-
oxide thin films, where the wettability of surfaces can be manipulated through UV light
exposure. As demonstrated by Deshpande et al. in their investigation of TiO2 and
ZnO thin films, UV-induced excitation of electron-hole pairs activates surface groups
that modify the contact angle of liquids. This phenomenon highlights the importance of
surface morphology, material properties, and precise fabrication techniques in influencing
liquid-surface interactions [1].
In this context, nanopillar arrays serve as an analog to natural and synthetic rough
surfaces, enabling controlled studies of liquid spreading. By systematically varying the
dimensions and density of the nanopillars, this work aims to develop a deeper under-
standing of the parameters governing hemiwicking length.
The results are not only
relevant to fundamental physics but also offer design principles for applications requir-
ing precise fluid control on textured surfaces.

This section details the theory needed to for understanding the fabrication process is

2.1
Theory on lithography
Lithography is a microfabrication technique used to transfer patterns onto a substrate,
forming the basis for creating intricate structures even at the nanoscale. It involves
coating a substrate with a light-sensitive material, or resist, which is then selectively
exposed to light through a mask. The exposed areas of the resist undergo chemical
changes, allowing for selective removal of material and ultimately revealing a desired
pattern on the substrate.
If the resist is positive, the exposed area vanishes when
developed, whereas for a negative resist, the unexposed areas are removed. The exposure
process thus transfers a pattern onto a photosensitive film, creating a latent image that
becomes visible upon development.
After exposure, the wafer undergoes a development process to reveal the patterned
structure on the resist. In this step, the wafer is immersed in or sprayed with a developer
solution, which selectively dissolves either the exposed or unexposed regions, depending
on whether the resist is positive or negative. For a positive resist, the developer removes
the exposed regions, leaving behind a pattern that corresponds to the mask. In contrast,
with a negative resist, the unexposed areas are removed, creating the inverse pattern.
Development conditions, including time and solution concentration, are optimized to
ensure precise pattern transfer and uniformity across the wafer, as well as to maintain
the resist profile for subsequent processing steps.
Proper development is crucial for
achieving high fidelity in the patterned features and preparing the wafer for etching or
deposition.
2.1.1
Deep ultraviolet (DUV) lithography
To achieve the highest possible resolution, electron beam (e-beam) lithography is com-
monly used. However, this technique can be slower than photolithography. In cases
where high-throughput and precision are needed, deep ultraviolet (DUV) lithography
offers an efficient alternative and is an industry standard. Here, we apply the methods
of DUV exposure with off-axis illumination as detailed in [2].
4

DUV lithography, operating with a 248 nm wavelength KrF excimer laser in the Canon
FPA-3000 EX4 Stepperool (see subsection 2.4.2), is particularly effective for achieving
high resolution. By employing a triple-exposure technique with off-axis illumination,
DUV lithography enables enhanced pattern fidelity and resolution beyond conventional
single-exposure methods.
This approach leverages multiple exposures with 60° rota-

2.1.4
Implementation of Triple Exposure Technique
The triple exposure method utilizes multiple exposure passes with mask adjustments to
create complex patterns beyond the standard resolution limits of a DUV stepper tool.
For hexagonal arrays, the mask is rotated by 60◦between each exposure, aligning each
exposure with a unique dipole orientation. This sequential exposure aligns each feature
with the orientation of the dipole source, enhancing resolution and image fidelity. For
example, three exposures are aligned so that the pattern from each exposure contributes
to a hexagonal array. This method significantly improving sidewall angles and image
contrast in the resist layer, details of which can be seen in [3].
2.1.5
Role of Assist Features and Enhanced Contrast
To further improve image fidelity, assist features (AFs) are incorporated into the mask
design. These transparent lines, although not printed in the final pattern, modulate the
exposure dose and sharpen the critical dimensions. With the inclusion of AFs, resist
image contrast (RIC) nearly doubles, enhancing lithographic quality and facilitating
accurate pattern transfer even at pitches nearing half the wavelength of the DUV tool
[4].
2.1.6
Fabrication Results and Application in Large-Scale Nanopat-
terning
The combination of OAI, triple exposure, and AFs yields critical dimensions below the
specified limit of DUV stepper tools, achieving nanopillars with pitches as fine as 300 nm.
The periodic nanopillar arrays enhance light-matter interactions, making them suitable
for examining the photo light induced hydrophilicity of metal-oxide thin films like TiO2.
The hexagonal array configuration, achievable through these techniques, aligns well with
large-scale roll-to-roll processes for industrial replication [3].
The use of triple exposure combined with dipole OAI in DUV lithography demonstrates
a powerful method for creating high-resolution nanopatterns. This approach allows the
realization of CDs beyond traditional limits, enabling applications across various fields
requiring precise nanoscale patterning.

Theory on dry etching
In dry etching, plasma etching is used to achieve the precision and resolution needed for
nanometer-scale structures. Plasma can be generated by heating a gas to temperatures
between 4,000 K and 20,000 K, which is too hot for our application. Therefore, the
plasma is generated electrically at temperatures ranging from 300 K to 1,000 K. The
advantage of this approach lies in the increased reactivity resulting from the acceleration
of electrons into the gas.
The parameters of the plasma are tunable via coils and
electrodes. The substrate is etched simultaneously, and at low pressure, the ion and
radical beam will be well collimated due to the long mean free path (mfp) of the ions
and radicals. mfp is λ =

cross-section.
A long mfp ensures that they reach the substrate without losing momentum through
collisions with the gas.

The parameters of the resulting etch that s are: Uniformity, both across the individual

or anisotropic: isotropic etching has a uniform etch rate in all directions (typically wet
etching in chemical solution in the 1970’s), while anisotropic etching is stronger in one
direction; it is a directional process. Anisotropic etching is the goal in this project to
create nanopillers simulating a rough surface.
The etch rate refers to the amount of material etched per unit time. In some cases,
a mask is needed to etch the substrate and leave protected areas where the material
remains, forming the required structures, which is the case in this project. In these
cases, the etch rate of the substrate and the mask become important. The ratio of these
rates is called selectivity; high selectivity means that the etch process removes much
more of the target material compared to the mask etc.. The mask itself is discussed
in 2.1 on lithography. In essence, the process involves exposing open areas to etching,
where material is gradually removed, while the areas covered by the mask are protected.
Chemical etching can occur in both wet and gaseous phases, and is typically isotropic,
meaning the etch rate is the same both laterally and vertically. However, some etches
are direction-dependent, often due to the crystal orientation of the material. In such
cases, structures can be etched primarily along specific crystal planes.
Physical etching involves materials that need to be redeposited, and as a result, plasma
etching is often used to combine both chemical and physical etching processes.
There are several parameters associated with the etch that can be used to characterize
the process. These include: etch depth, sidewall profile, roughness, uniformity,
and others.
There are 4 states of matter; solid, liquid, gas and ionized gas or plasma! In plasma
etching we use cold electrically driven plasma or weakly ionized plasma; meaning at
most, on average, only one in every ten thousand atoms are ionized. In the clean room
we often works with these in dry etching.
2.2.1
Dry Etching for Nanopillar Fabrication
Dry etching, particularly using Inductively Coupled Plasma (ICP) and Deep Reactive
Ion Etching (DRIE), plays a critical role in the fabrication of nanoscale structures,
including the nanopillers used to simulate rough surfaces in the current study. These
nanopillers, produced on fused silica (Quartz) substrates, are intended to investigate
Hemiwiking length in later experiments. This section outlines the dry etching process
and its associated challenges, with a focus on the etch rate, selectivity, and dimensional
control of the nanopillers.
2.2.2
Etching Process Overview
The dry etching process utilized in this work involves the use of ICP and DRIE to
pattern fused silica wafers that are first coated with a chrome mask, followed by the
deposition of a BARC (Bottom Anti-Reflective Coating) layer and a photoresist to define
8
==PAGE:18==
the nanopillar pattern.
Chrome was selected as the etch mask material due to its
excellent etch resistance in the presence of aggressive etching gases and plasmas.
In the ICP process, the material is etched by a combination of ion bombardment and
chemical reactions, where the ions are generated by a high-frequency power source.
The ICP method is known for its high etch rates and anisotropic etching, which allows
for better control over the etching directionality, critical when creating precise vertical
nanopillars. The DRIE process, a more specialized form of ICP, is used to achieve deep
etching with high aspect ratios, ensuring that the nanopillars reach the required depth
without significant lateral etching.
2.2.3
Etch Rate and Selectivity
The etch rate of fused silica during ICP and DRIE is influenced by various factors,
including the gas chemistry, pressure, and power settings. For this study, the etch rate
is of primary concern since achieving consistent etching depth and profile is crucial to
obtaining the desired nanopillar dimensions.
The selectivity between the etch mask (chrome) and the substrate (fused silica) is an-
other critical factor. Chrome, with its relatively high etch resistance compared to fused
silica, allows for precise pattern transfer and protection during etching. A high selec-
tivity ratio between the chrome mask and silica ensures that the mask remains intact
while the underlying material is etched away, preventing the mask from being removed
prematurely and enabling the accurate fabrication of the nanopillars. This selectivity
is particularly important when using BARC and resist layers, which can influence the
overall etch rate and uniformity.
2.2.4
Etch Uniformity and Aspect Ratio
Achieving uniform etching across the entire wafer surface is essential, as any variations
can result in inconsistencies in pillar height and diameter. Uniformity is influenced by
several parameters, such as the plasma density, gas flow rates, and chamber pressure.
Since the chamber used for etching in this study was also employed for other research,
residual materials from previous etching processes can also affect the etch rates of the
material and thus also uniformity. These residuals may interact with the etching plasma,
causing non-uniform etching rates across the wafer, or lead to contamination that im-
pacts the final device quality.
The aspect ratio, defined as the ratio of pillar height to diameter, is another important
factor in nanopillar fabrication. Achieving high aspect ratios is challenging, particularly
when etching deep structures.
The DRIE process is designed to achieve high aspect
ratios, but careful control of etching time and chamber conditions is needed to avoid
problems such as microloading effects, where regions with more exposed resist or mask
material etch at different rates than those covered by the resist.
2.2.5
Process Optimization
The dry etching process must be carefully optimized to produce nanopillars with precise
diameters and consistent heights. Etching time plays a significant role in controlling
pillar height, while maintaining selectivity between the chrome mask and fused silica
9
==PAGE:19==
ensures that the features are well-defined without premature mask degradation. Etch-
ing time must be adjusted based on the desired pillar depth and the etch rate, and
residual effects must be monitored, especially when the etching chamber has been used
for multiple processes. These residuals can introduce variations that compromise the
final pillar quality.
Moreover, achieving uniform pillar diameters is equally critical, as any deviation can
impact the Hemiwiking length measurements, which rely on highly accurate and repro-
ducible pillar features. Variations in etching time, pressure, and power are all monitored
and adjusted to ensure the final nanopillars meet the required specifications for subse-
quent experiments.
2.2.6
Conclusion
Dry etching, particularly via ICP and DRIE techniques, enables the fabrication of pre-
cise nanopillars on fused silica wafers, essential for the investigations in this thesis.
The key challenges in the process—etch rate, selectivity, etch uniformity, and aspect
ratio control—must be carefully addressed to ensure that the final nanopillars meet
the dimensional requirements for the Hemiwiking experiments. Ongoing optimization
of process parameters, alongside attention to the etching environment and chamber
cleanliness, ensures consistent and high-quality results, crucial for the success of the
subsequent experiments.

Deposition Techniques: Theory and Mechanisms
This section discusses the underlying theory and operational mechanisms of Physical
Vapor Deposition (PVD) and Atomic Layer Deposition (ALD) processes, with a focus
on the machines used at DTU Nanolab for thin-film deposition. These processes are
essential for achieving uniform coatings on substrates with high precision and control
over film thickness which is applied in this thesis.
2.3.2
Physical Vapor Deposition (PVD) with chrome
PVD is an atomistic deposition process where material is vaporized from a solid source
(often called a target) and then transported through a vacuum to condense on the
substrate, forming a thin film. In this work, chrome is sputtered on both sides of a 6-
inch fused silica wafer using the Cluster-Based Multi-Chamber High Vacuum Sputtering
Deposition System (hereafter referred to as ”PC1”) at DTU Nanolab.
The PVD process in the PC1 machine involves generating a plasma by bombarding
argon atoms against the Cr target. The energetic argon ions eject chrome atoms from
the target, which then deposit on the wafer in a line-of-sight manner. This method
enables high purity films and is suitable for materials like metals, which do not react
with the background gases in the vacuum environment.
The PVD process operates in an ultra-high vacuum, typically around 10−6 to 10−7 Torr,
which minimizes contamination and ensures film purity. The machine’s vacuum system
comprises a roughing pump and a turbo pump, with a cryogenic pump to trap residual
gases. These layers of pumps progressively reduce the chamber pressure, creating the

2.3.3
Atomic Layer Deposition (ALD)
ALD is a chemical vapor deposition technique characterized by its cyclic and self-limiting
nature, which allows for the deposition of thin films with atomic layer precision. In
this thesis, TiO2 is deposited using the ALD Picosun R200 system at DTU Nanolab,
operating at 300°C. This temperature ensures the stabilization of the TiO2 in the anatase
crystalline phase, a property critical for applications in UV-activated wettability.
2.3.3.1
ALD Process Mechanism
The ALD process in the Picosun R200 follows a four-step cycle to achieve monolayer
deposition as seen on fig. 2.5. The steps are:
• 1.
**Precursor A Pulse**: The first precursor, TiCl4, is introduced into the
chamber, where it adsorbs onto the surface of the substrate, binding to reactive
sites.
• 2. **Purge**: An inert gas, typically nitrogen, purges any unreacted precursor
and by-products from the chamber, preventing gas-phase reactions.
• 3. **Precursor B Pulse**: The second precursor, H2O, is introduced and reacts
with the adsorbed layer of TiCl4, resulting in a thin layer of TiO2 on the substrate.
• 4. **Purge**: Another inert gas purge removes any remaining unreacted H2O
and reaction by-products. See fig. 2.4.
This cycle repeats until the desired thickness is achieved. The process is self-limiting
because each cycle deposits only a single atomic layer, leading to exceptional uniformity
and precise control over the film thickness. The cyclic nature of ALD makes it especially
well-suited for coating high-aspect-ratio nanostructures. At the beginning of the process
there is an induction phase before the deposition rate becomes linear in number of cycles.

UV-Induced Surface Effects and Applications in Hemiwicking Flow
The TiO2 deposited in the anatase phase exhibits photocatalytic properties, making it

This functionality is explored in detail in the results and discussion sections of the second
half of this thesis, where UV light interaction with TiO2 is correlated with observed
wettability changes and flow dynamics.

2.4.4
Machines
Etching Process for Fused Silica Wafers with Chromium Layers and Nanopil-
lar Formation
The etching process described in this work is designed to create nanopillars on fused
silica wafers, each coated with a chromium (Cr) layer on both sides. One side of the
wafer is additionally coated with a Bottom Anti-Reflective Coating (BARC) and KRF
M230Y photoresist.
Following UV exposure and development, etching is performed
using an Inductively Coupled Plasma (ICP) metal etcher. Precise control over the ICP
machine’s parameters and setup is essential to achieve the high fidelity and aspect ratio
required for nanopillar fabrication.
2.4.4.1
ICP Etching Machine
The ICP system, henceforth referred to as SPTS ICP Metal Etcher operates through
plasma generation induced by an electromagnetic field from an RF-powered coil. This
inductively coupled setup has two independent RF generators, one for the coil and
another for the platen, enabling distinct control over plasma density and ion acceleration.
2.4.4.1.1
Plasma Generation and Machine Setup
The SPTS ICP Metal
Etcherachine comprises a vacuum chamber where high-density plasma is generated and
maintained through inductive coupling. Key components include:
• RF Coil: Surrounding the chamber, the RF coil generates the primary magnetic
field necessary for sustaining high-density plasma, with plasma density typically
around n = 1011 cm−3.
• Dual Power Generators: The independent RF generators allow decoupled con-
trol: the coil generator governs plasma density, while the platen generator ad-
justs ion bombardment energy, enabling anisotropic etching for high-aspect-ratio
nanopillars.
• Cooling Mechanism: The platen is cooled, typically using helium or oil, ensuring
the temperature remains stable during the etch process, preserving the MY230
resist and chromium layer integrity.
• Mass Flow Controllers (MFCs): These devices precisely regulate the gas flow
into the chamber, maintaining optimal etching conditions for both chromium and
fused silica.
Etching Recipe and Control Parameters
• Gas Composition: Careful control over reactive gases (e.g., SF6 and CHF3 for
silicon dioxide or chromium etching) enables anisotropic etching while minimizing
sidewall roughness.
19
==PAGE:29==
• RF Power for Coil and Platen: RF power to the coil controls plasma density,
while the platen power impacts ion acceleration towards the wafer, adjusting the
physical etch rate independently of plasma density.
• Pressure and Cooling: Chamber pressure and platen cooling are adjusted to
limit isotropic etching, which is essential for retaining the verticality of nanopillar
sidewalls.
Comparison with Previous Etching Methods: Deep Reactive Ion Etching
Pegasus 4 (Etching of silicon based dielectrics on 150 mm wafers)
Earlier in the project, a different plasma etcher, referred to here as Deep Reactive Ion
Etching Pegasus 4 (Etching of silicon based dielectrics on 150 mm wafers) was used.
However, due to significant changes in the chamber conditions over time, it became
challenging to reproduce earlier etching results consistently. This led to the adoption
of SPTS ICP Metal Etcher which offered better control and repeatability due to its
independent dual-generator setup.
Preparatory Plasma Vapor Deposition (PVD)
Although not directly part of the etching phase, understanding the PVD processes used
for chromium layer deposition contextualizes the overall workflow:
• Chrome Deposition: Sputtering or evaporation in PVD ensures uniform chromium
layers on both wafer sides, providing a stable base for nanopillar etching.
• BARC and MY230 Resist Coating: Applied only on the etching-designated
wafer side, these coatings are patterned post-UV exposure, serving as essential
masking materials for the nanopillar structure definition.
This ICP-based etching process, combined with preparatory PVD, facilitates precise
nanopillar fabrication on fused silica wafers, leveraging advanced plasma control and
optimized materials layering.
Deposition Techniques for Thin Film Fabrication
This section discusses the two primary deposition techniques employed in this work for
thin film formation: Physical Vapor Deposition (PVD) and Atomic Layer Deposition
(ALD). Both techniques serve distinct purposes, with PVD primarily used for metallic
and conductive layers, while ALD provides excellent step coverage and thickness control,
especially useful for conformal coatings.
Physical Vapor Deposition (PVD)
Physical Vapor Deposition is a process used to deposit thin films by vaporizing a solid
material in a high-vacuum environment and allowing the vapor to condense on the
substrate. Key methods of PVD include:
• Thermal Evaporation: In this technique, a resistive heater is used to heat
the source material until it vaporizes.
It is ideal for materials with moderate
20
==PAGE:30==
vapor pressures. At DTU Nanolab, materials such as Al, Ag, Cr, Ge, and Au are
deposited by thermal evaporation. However, it is limited in material choice due to
the difficulty in vaporizing high-melting-point substances.
• E-beam Evaporation: An electron beam heats the material in a crucible, en-
abling the deposition of high-melting-point materials like Ti, Pt, and W. E-beam
evaporation is useful for achieving uniform and precise film thickness and is often
used for metals, oxides, and semiconductors.
• Sputtering: Sputter deposition involves bombarding a target material with en-
ergetic ions (typically Ar ions), causing atoms to be ejected from the target and
deposited on the substrate. Types of sputtering include DC, RF, magnetron, and
HiPIMS (High Power Impulse Magnetron Sputtering). This method allows the
deposition of almost any material, including compounds and alloys, and provides
improved step coverage compared to evaporation methods.
PVD processes at DTU Nanolab include both line-of-sight deposition and conformal
coating methods, depending on the specific requirements of the application. The primary
applications of PVD include metal contacts, optical layers, and conductive films.
Atomic Layer Deposition (ALD)
Atomic Layer Deposition is a chemical process used to create thin films with atomic
precision. ALD relies on sequential, self-limiting chemical reactions, providing excellent
control over film thickness and conformal coverage, even on complex or high-aspect-ratio
surfaces; though the purge-time has to be changed for more complex structures, e.g. the
aspect ratio exceeds 1.2. ALD processes are conducted at relatively low temperatures,
making them suitable for a wide range of substrate materials.
• Thermal ALD: This process relies on thermally activated surface reactions be-
tween gaseous precursors. Each precursor is introduced separately, with purging
steps in between, allowing for layer-by-layer growth. Thermal ALD is commonly
used for oxides, nitrides, and some metal films.
• Plasma-Enhanced ALD (PEALD): In PEALD, plasma is used as an addi-
tional energy source to activate the precursor reactions. This allows for the de-
position of materials at lower temperatures than conventional ALD and enables
deposition on substrates that are sensitive to higher temperatures. PEALD is par-
ticularly useful for applications requiring highly conformal coatings with precise
control over film composition and density.
The conformal nature of ALD makes it particularly advantageous for depositing films
over complex topographies, ensuring uniform coverage even on deep trenches or nanopil-
lar structures. ALD is frequently employed for dielectric layers, barrier films, and pas-
sivation layers in semiconductor and MEMS devices. [1]. In this project we deal with
thermal ALD with precursors TiCl4 and H2O. ”Titanium(IV) isopropoxide (TTIP),
Tetrakis(dimethylamido)titanium (TDMAT), and TiCl4 are the most popular”. [8] The
temperature is set to 300 degrees and the purging gas is nitrogen.

2.5.1.1
Diameter method
Firstly, the SEM image is acquired.
Key considerations include setting the working
distance and determining whether the material is conductive. For non-conductive mate-
rials, such as glass, it is advisable to use VPSE mode, as these materials are dielectrics.
Begin by locating the edge of a chip, then use it as a reference to zoom in gradually
while adjusting the focus until the periodicity of the pillar structures becomes visible.
Once the structures are discernible, move to the center of the chip to avoid edge effects,
and zoom in to a high magnification. To estimate measurement errors, it is beneficial
to include multiple pillars within a single image and calculate the standard deviation of
the measured parameters. Maintaining consistent magnification across images ensures
uniform scaling, which simplifies subsequent processing.
In fig. 2.10, this method is
demonstrated visually. The image in fig. 2.10a illustrates nanopillars exposed with a
dose of 82.5
J
m2 , where the malformed structure of the pillars is apparent. Disregarding
this issue, the following steps outline the image processing procedure:
• Open the image with all the excess information in ImageJ (see 2.10a).
• Zoom in on the scale bar, draw a line across it, then navigate to Analyze →Set
Scale in ImageJ, and specify the distance corresponding to the drawn line. Check
the global box, and reopen the image without the extra information (see 2.10b).
• Navigate to Analyze →Set Measurements... and ensure the parameters re-
quired for measurement are selected, and that the correct image is being analyzed.
• Press Ctrl + Shift + T to initiate thresholding.
Pixels within the threshold
range (defined by the lower and upper threshold values) will be highlighted in red.
• Use the selection tool to highlight the desired structure, then press Ctrl + T to
add it to the ROI Manager. Ensure that the label and show all boxes options are
enabled, and confirm that all fully in-image structures are selected.
• Select all entries in the ROI Manager and press Measure.
• In the results pop-up, navigate to Results →Summarize to obtain the data for
analysis.
• The mean value is recorded as the average diameter, while the standard deviation
represents the measurement error.
• Finally, use the Read and Write Excel plug-in in the Plug-in section of ImageJ
to generate an Excel file containing the data. This method avoids potential data
corruption that can occur with copy-pasting. Other programs may also be used
as alternatives.

3.1

3.1.1
Contact Angle and Wettability
The contact angle θ is a fundamental parameter in surface science, quantifying how a
liquid interacts with a solid surface. It is defined as the angle formed between the tangent
to the liquid–vapor interface and the solid surface at the point of contact. This angle
provides insight into the wettability of the surface and is determined by the balance of
interfacial forces between the liquid, solid, and vapor phases.
Figure 3.1: Illustration of the contact angle θ formed at the interface of a liquid droplet
on a solid surface surrounded by vapor.
38
==PAGE:48==
3.1.1.1
Wettability Classification
The contact angle is widely used to classify the wetting behavior of a surface:
• High Contact Angle (θ > 90◦): Indicates poor wettability, where the liquid
forms droplets on the surface. This behavior is typical of hydrophobic materials.
• Low Contact Angle (θ < 90◦): Signifies good wettability, where the liquid
spreads across the surface. This behavior is characteristic of hydrophilic materials.
3.1.1.2
Governing Relationships
The equilibrium contact angle for a static system is governed by the Young equation:
cos θ = γSV −γSL
γLV
,
(3.1)
where:
• γSV : Surface tension of the solid-vapor interface [mN/m],
• γSL: Surface tension of the solid-liquid interface [mN/m],
• γLV : Surface tension of the liquid-vapor interface [mN/m].
This relationship highlights how surface energies drive the wetting behavior. The Young
equation assumes a flat, chemically homogeneous surface under equilibrium conditions,
which may require extensions or corrections for rough or chemically heterogeneous sur-
faces.
3.1.1.3
Applications of Contact Angles
The contact angle is critical in a variety of applications, including:
• Coating Technologies: Ensuring even spreading of liquids for paints or protec-
tive layers.
• Adhesion Processes: Understanding the interaction of adhesives with surfaces.
• Water-Repellent Surfaces: Designing surfaces that resist water by increasing
the contact angle.
• Photocatalytic Surfaces: Utilizing UV-induced surface reactions, such as those
on TiO2, to dynamically alter the contact angle and promote hydrophilic behavior.
The contact angle is central to studies of photocatalytic surfaces, such as UV-illuminated
TiO2, where changes in surface hydrophilicity dynamically alter the contact angle. This
makes it an essential parameter for understanding surface wettability in the context of
electron-hole dynamics. For more details on the concept of contact angles and their
implications, refer to Wetting [12] and Contact Angle [13].
The next section explores how the Cassie–Baxter model extends the concept of contact
angles to chemically heterogeneous surfaces and its relevance to photocatalytic studies.

3.1.2
Cassie–Baxter Model and Its Relevance to Hole Concentration
The Cassie–Baxter model provides a theoretical framework for understanding the wet-
tability of chemically heterogeneous surfaces. It describes the contact angle θ of a liquid
on a composite surface in terms of its surface fractions:
cos θ = f cos θ0 + (1 −f) cos θ1,
(3.2)
where f is the fraction of the surface area corresponding to a surface chemistry with an
intrinsic contact angle θ0, and 1 −f corresponds to a second surface chemistry with an
intrinsic contact angle θ1, which is demonstrated on fig. 3.2.
When a TiO2 surface is illuminated with ultraviolet (UV) light, electron–hole pairs are
generated within the material. The holes diffuse to the surface and activate inactive
surface groups, changing the surface chemistry. This transformation shifts the intrinsic
contact angle from θ0 to θ1, thus dynamically altering the wettability of the surface as
described by Eq. (3.2).
The rate of change in the contact angle depends on the surface activation kinetics,
which are influenced by the photo-induced carrier concentration p(x, t) at the surface.
This relationship is significant because it establishes a link between the semiconductor’s
hole concentration and its wettability properties, enabling indirect measurements of
photo-generated hole diffusion lengths and recombination rates through contact angle
experiments.
The relevance of the Cassie–Baxter model to this study lies in its ability to describe
wettability changes as a function of the activated surface area, which in turn depends
on the minority carrier dynamics derived in the preceding sections. Incorporating this
understanding, we can relate the contact angle dynamics to the solutions for p(x, t)
derived from the drift-diffusion equations.

3.1.3.4
Physical Plausibility of the Solution
The derived solution for the hole concentration p(x) is grounded in the principles of
semiconductor physics and consistent with experimental observations of photocatalytic
systems, particularly at metal oxide-semiconductor interfaces. This section evaluates
the solution’s plausibility based on physical boundary conditions, system constraints,
and expected trends.
3.1.3.4.1
Agreement with Boundary Conditions
The solution respects the
flux boundary conditions at x = 0 and x = l, which model surface recombination via
Robin boundary conditions. The interplay of surface recombination velocities (vf, vb)
and diffusion effects (Dp, L) produces a concentration profile that aligns with known
physical phenomena:
• At x = 0, a strong surface recombination rate (vf →∞) reduces p(0), whereas
weak recombination (vf →0) results in minimal flux and nearly reflective behavior.
• At x = l, the back boundary introduces additional recombination effects, control-
ling the gradient of p(x) and the system’s overall carrier dynamics.
3.1.3.4.2
Diffusion-Dominated Behavior
For large diffusion lengths (L), the
concentration p(x) shows a slower spatial decay, as expected for systems with efficient
carrier transport.
Conversely, smaller L values lead to sharper gradients, reflecting
increased recombination within the bulk. These trends are consistent with the physical
role of L =
p
Dpτr in characterizing minority carrier dynamics.
3.1.3.4.3
Experimental Parameters and Trends
The model incorporates
generation rates (g0) and thermal recombination lifetimes (τr) consistent with reported
experimental conditions for metal oxides such as TiO2. The dependence of p0 on the
recombination velocities vf, vb and layer thickness l mirrors experimental trends observed
in studies of UV-induced wettability on TiO2/SiO2 systems [1]. Additionally:
• Infinite surface recombination (vb →∞) produces the expected rapid decay to
zero carrier concentration at the boundary, highlighting effective carrier loss.
45
==PAGE:55==
• Minimal recombination (vb = 0) retains high carrier concentrations, as surface
recombination effects are suppressed.
3.1.3.4.4
Implications for Photocatalytic Systems
The solution highlights
key parameters influencing the effectiveness of photocatalytic processes:
• The balance between carrier generation (g0) and recombination (vf, vb, τr) deter-
mines the availability of active carriers at the surface, directly impacting reaction
rates.
• The interplay of thickness (l) and diffusion length (L) identifies optimal material
configurations for enhanced photocatalytic efficiency.
The next step involves linking these theoretical insights to experimental measurements
of contact angle dynamics, as described by the Cassie–Baxter model. With an under-
standing of the reaction kinetics, we now turn to the absorption properties that govern
the light interaction with the thin-film structure.

3.2.2
Measurements and Data Processing
The Hemiwiking lengths were obtained through a combination of direct measurements
and analytical fitting techniques, using a modular code that processes data from Excel
files. The filenames follow the format:
Material_Dosage_etchTime_FilmThickness_ExposureTime_UvLightPower_FlowTime_FlowRate_data_s
Direct Measurements: An initial estimate of the Hemiwiking length for each sample
was obtained directly from the final frame of the recorded experiment. These values
provide a baseline for further analysis.
Analytical Fitting and Convergence: The raw Hemiwiking lengths were analyzed
using the following steps:
• Curve Fitting: Differences between the front and back radii over time (∆L) were
modeled using a composite function:
∆L(t) = A · tB −C · e−D·t + E
where:
– A · tB: Captures early-time growth following a power-law behavior.
– −C · e−D·t: Describes stabilization behavior through exponential decay.
– E: Represents the steady-state offset.
This functional form ensures the model can accommodate both transient and
steady-state dynamics of the Hemiwiking process.
• Convergence Methods: To ensure reliable ∆L values, three convergence metrics
were applied:
– Numerical Derivatives: Stabilization points were identified when the deriva-
tive of ∆L, d(∆L)
dt
, approached zero.
52
==PAGE:62==
– Root Mean Square Error (RMSE): Plateaus were detected by tracking the
RMSE within a sliding window over time.
– Cumulative Moving Average (CMA): Stable trends in ∆L were identified by
computing a cumulative moving average over time.
• Weighted Average and Blended Error: After determining stabilization points
from the convergence methods, the final Hemiwiking length, ∆Lfinal, was computed
as a weighted average:
∆Lfinal =
P

P
i wi
where wi are weights assigned to the methods: wndc (numerical derivatives), wrmse
(RMSE), and wcma (CMA). These weights were selected to balance sensitivity and
noise rejection.
• Uncertainty Estimation: The uncertainty in the final Hemiwiking length was
quantified using a blended error metric, σblended, which incorporates:
– Maximum Deviation (σmax-dev): The largest deviation between the re-
sults of the convergence methods.
– Combined Variance (Weighted Std, σcombined-var): Variance weighted
by per-method uncertainties, including the variability of ∆L around the sta-
bilization points.
– Standard Deviation of Means (σstd-means): The standard deviation of
the mean ∆L values obtained from the convergence methods.
These contributions were combined as:
σblended = σmax-dev + σcombined-var + σstd-means

The time

convergence parameters such as derivative thresholds (dt), RMSE thresholds (rmset),
and CMA thresholds (cmat) were optimized on a per-dataset basis. When no dataset-
specific parameters were provided, default values (dt = 0.00001, rmset = 0.001, cmat =
0.001) were used.
This process minimizes noise and outlier influence, yielding robust ∆L estimates with
quantified uncertainties. The results from these analyses are visualized in the following
section, where plots of the Hemiwiking lengths and their associated uncertainties are
