Abstract
Zostera Marina species, commonly known as eelgrass or seagrass, are vital towards maintaining
the carbon cycle and play an important part towards the ecological balance of temperate coastal
habitats. In order to meet with the climate change goals of keeping global warming under 2◦,
CO2 emissions must be removed from the atmosphere. Eelgrass act as carbon sinks and are as
important as rainforests, storing about 10% of the ocean’s carbon each year. However, eelgrass
habitats have been declining at alarming rates of about 7% each year and have decreased by 75%
of its total coverage in Denmark alone in the past few decades. Eelgrass conservation requires
eﬀicient mapping, surveying and re-seeding of meadows. This is where underwater vehicles such
as ROVs and AUVs, which have seen an increase in usage for recent years, come into the picture.
The autonomous capabilities of industrial ROVs largely relies on a monocular/stereos camera
with IMU readings, which can be a challenge when operating in murky underwater conditions.
The implementation of autonomous mission plans such as surveying eelgrasses requires good
pose-estimation of the ROV and 3D mapping of the surroundings. The pose-estimation needs
to be accurate and one way to reduce uncertainty is to have data redundancy.
The fusion
of acoustic and visual odometry estimates with IMU pose-estimations can be hugely beneficial
towards obtaining error-free pose estimations. This thesis project analyses different sensor fusion
methods, and performs a mapping based opto-acoustic fusion using the Kabsch algorithm, which
can be used to combine sonar and camera readings. The project also achieves sensor fusion with
a monocular camera and 2D sonar information, and therefore approaches the task with some
unique solutions to approximate 3D information. The project also studies underwater feature
extraction techniques and proposes methods to segment and detect eelgrass images from ROV
imagery using traditional and computationally inexpensive machine learning techniques. An
image segmentation inspired k-means clustering is used to generate a visual bag of words and
the images are classified using a k-nearest neighbour algorithm. Finally, the project will discuss
some contemporary methods which could be built on top of this platform and achieve opto-
acoustic odometry. These objectives are intended to move towards the eventual goal of achieving
autonomous operations using underwater vehicles, with minimal operator intervention.

Introduction
1.1
Background
The first couple of decades of the 21st century have seen a critically rising trend of global warming and
greenhouse emissions. There is a strong need to address social and environmental challenges to ensure
the safety of our planet and the future generations of humans and other species. There is a drive to
go sustainable and reduce emissions in order to meet certain requirements before there are irreversible
changes to the environment, such as, rising water levels.
This being said, a number of restoration
attempts are being made and they must be reinforced with the help of engineering. The UN has laid
out the sustainable development goals (SDGs) which is intended to change our way of thinking towards
a sustainable future. With the rapid takeover of industry 4.0, robotics and AI are at the forefront of
development and it is imperative that more research effort is put into tackling environmental problems
and achieving goals such as the Paris Climate Agreement.
Greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions are one of the primary causes of increasing global temperatures while
deforestation is exacerbating the situation. Amongst the GHG, CO2 pollution is driving global warming,
with about 34 billion tonnes being generated each year. To offset this, a carbon credit system was initiated
to restrict countries and large-scale carbon emitters by requiring them to buy carbon emission credits.
To further develop this idea, a carbon offset system is being enforced on companies and countries to
offset their CO2 pollution by a number of methods such as opting for sustainable energy options, filtering
out their atmospheric emissions and restoring areas of forests and underwater habitats,. ([1]. One such
important underwater ecosystem comprises of eelgrass (the Zostera genus).
Eelgrass habitats grow in temperate zones around the globe. They play a number of important functions
in underwater ecosystems such as providing foraging areas to fish, food for turtles and preventing coastal
erosion. They can also have the unique property to trap CO2 and convert it into organic carbon matter
and function as invaluable carbon sinks. Detection and restoration of these meadows is an excellent way to
offset CO2 emissions not only because they are highly effective at sequestrating carbon but also due to the
vast possibilities of sustainable engineering at sea. This greatly helps counter global greenhouse emissions,
with carbon dioxide being one of the chief contributors. However, eelgrass percentages have massively
declined in the past decades due to habitat loss resulting from human practices such as fishing, agriculture
& urbanisation, and increasing water turbidity & temperatures. Based on a number of eelgrass studies,
there has been a drastic depletion in eelgrass coverage around coastal seas in comparison to historical
estimates. Holmer’s studies [2] reveal a 7% decline in eelgrass habitats on an annual basis.
This thesis project aligns with the UN SDGs 11,13 and 14, and aims to find a way to address the issue of
climate change from an engineering perspective. This is done by providing a platform which can assist in
surveying and quantifying the distribution of Zostera along the Danish coastline. The biggest challenge in
the restoration of these meadows remains the diﬀiculty in surveying and obtaining eelgrass coverage data.
The operating cost of sending out trawlers and boats to perform surveys is high and is limited by lack
of human resources and weather. Therefore, automating the entire process would make data gathering
campaigns cheaper and less dependent on experienced divers & operators, with relatively simpler post-
processing of acquired data, which is also a tedious process. UAS (Unoccupied Aerial Systems) and other
remote sensing methods which use satellites perform well but are sensitive to wind ,cloud coverage icy
sheets & tidal activity, and need to be complemented with autonomous underwater systems. A study
by Roelfsema et al [3] recommends the use of AUVs in order to improve accuracy and reproducibility of
data collected using remote sensing techniques. The answer to this problem lies in the usage of fleets of
autonomous underwater vehicles such as ROVs (Remotely Operated Vehicles) and AUVs (Autonomous
Underwater Vehicles).
Due to the challenging weather and lighting conditions out at sea, data collection has to be robust and
multiple sensors are required to complement each other. For example, an optical sensor is a short range
sensor due to scattering of light because of underwater particles and objects. In comparison, an acoustic
sensor has a much longer range but provides less information and is hard to interpret. Baseline systems,
which provide global positioning estimates, are also unreliable (especially with increasing depth) due to

1.2
Motivation
The motivation behind this thesis is to explore and improve on the capabilities of computer vision and
autonomous underwater vehicles in sub-sea applications. As mentioned above, there is a need to survey
and conserve aquatic ecosystems because they play an important part in carbon storage, food cycles and
oxygen generation.A number of measures are being put into place to restore eelgrass meadows but this
must be aided by robust and eﬀicient underwater surveying and mapping methods. One such method
is use of swarms of autonomous vehicles, which can be eﬀicient at surveying areas without the need for
large-scale operations with big vessels or experienced divers. However, they remain yet, in a nascent stage
of operation and apart from upgrades in their design and operating costs, their autonomous capabilities
must be built further. This is especially crucial to plan underwater missions with the challenges in data
acquisition in the murky underwater conditions. It also significantly harder achieving a good fix on the
pose of a robot underwater, with the inaccuracies in IMU/INS sensors over time. Acoustic positioning
sensors perform well but with an increase in accuracy (use of long baseline over short baseline for example)
comes with increasing operating costs and logistical problems. It is also diﬀicult to work with optical
sensors, with loss of colour, fogging of images and short range of operation. Acoustic sensors such as
sonars are very accurate and are the holy grail of underwater depth estimation and reconstruction, and
must be fused with these other sensors in an eﬀicient manner.
This means setting up a robust and
autonomous platform which can obtain and process information from multiple sensors. This is where
sensor fusion comes into the picture. There is also a need for better feature extraction in the underwater
environment due to a significant lack of underwater features and poor visibility.
This renders tasks
such as odometry and mapping significantly harder, which are vital to autonomous missions. This calls
for better underwater image segmentation methods, which can be run to perform tasks like odometry,
in a computationally eﬀicient manner. Due to the diﬀiculty in logistics and conditions, there is also
a significantly smaller availability of underwater datasets as opposed to objects on land, such as the
Imagenet.
Therefore, the responsibility lies on the scientific community to generate more large-scale
datasets, of eelgrass for example, for marine research and development.
1.3
Problem Statement
This thesis project addresses the requirement for more autonomy on-board autonomous underwater vehi-
cles for operations of surveying and navigation. The project tackles this from the perspective of eelgrass
conservation and making improvements in the blue economy. It is the need of the hour to work towards
conservation of underwater habitats, which are invaluable towards achieving our sustainable goals, in the
same vein as terrestrial habitats like rain forests. Underwater vehicles such as ROVs and AUVs are finding
an increased use in such applications but lack in their autonomous capabilities due to the challenges of
operation at sea, such as strong waves and turbid conditions. This calls for development of the design and
autonomous capabilities of an ROV, which can permit large-scale autonomous operations. This thesis
aims to understand the challenges of ROV operation, the limitations posed by different sensors and the
huge benefits of fusion of these modalities to improve sensing capabilities. It also addresses the challenge
of underwater feature extraction and presents methods to improve them.

2.1
Eelgrass studies
Zostera species (eelgrass) are a family of perennial flowering plants, found in shallow, fully submerged
coastal ecosystems. They are usually found in clusters known as meadows in sandy or muddy seabottoms
& estuaries, in temperate zones around the world. They are relatively easy to identify as they have pointy
green leaves upto about 3 feet long and grow in clusters. According to an eelgrass fact sheet [4], eelgrass are
as important as tropical rainforests and rich farmlands in terms of their ecological significance. Eelgrass
perform a number of important functions, including providing food to animals like endangered turtles,
migratory birds & sea urchins, mitigating ocean acidification, recycling millions of nutrients annually,
to even providing a habitat for and feeding algae, small vertebrates & bacteria. Their most important
function, however, is that they can trap carbon dioxide and sequester it into organic carbon matter which
helps bring down global CO2 emission levels.
The study [5] showed that CO2 emissions since the early 2000s have gone up by about 40-45% due
to increasing population and incomes, and will continue to rise in the years to come till energy usage
structures continue to evolve sustainably. The ocean absorbs about a third of the CO2 emissions and
coastal habitats sequester these into carbon significantly faster than forests and farmlands [6].
The
carbon dioxide absorbed by the ocean and its sequestration by coastal habitats is collectively known as
the ’blue carbon’ initiative. It is important to note that while these ecosystems are protected, they retain
organic carbon. However, if they are destroyed, they could release upto 1.02 billion tons of CO2 into
the atmosphere annually which amounts to about 19% of emissions from tropical deforestration. For
comparison, the global CO2 emissions for 2020 were recorded at about 34.81 billion tons. About 30% of
the historical eelgrass coverage has been lost already and seagrass ecosystems are highly threatened [7] .
A study performed by Orth et al.,2010 [8] studies the eelgrass abundance in the Chesapeake Bay and
surrounding areas, which has seen constantly declining percentages of eelgrass, in an estuary which is
heavily dependant on eelgrass for balancing the nitrogen cycle. However, the eelgrass habitats suffer from
increasing average water temperatures and deterioration of the ecosystem, since the early years of the
21st century. Eelgrass restoration techniques are recommended to ensure long-term survival of eelgrass,
which includes water protection and eelgrass transplantation by seeding. A great proportion of these
tasks such as water column profiling and seeding of the meadows can be performed far more eﬀiciently
by underwater drones.
A study modelled the coastal areas around Denmark [9] by applying a spatial GIS approach combining
information on six key eelgrass requirements- (light availability, water temperature, salinity, frequency of
low oxygen concentration, wave exposure, and sediment type). The model revealed a potential eelgrass
coverage area of 2204 km2 compared to historical estimates of around 7000 km2. This means that there
is huge potential to expand the eelgrass distribution. Furthermore, validation of the model with ground
truth surveys over a 110 km2 stretch revealed only about 67% results matched with in-situ observations.
This leaves out a huge requirement for drones in eelgrass surveying and monitoring for such in-situ tasks.
2.2
Sensors used in the sea
There is a wide plethora of sensors available for underwater applications. The three main types of sensors
used for mapping and localisation are- optical sensors, acoustic sensors and inertial sensors. There are
also other sensors such as pressure sensors , pH sensors and dissolved oxygen (DO) sensors, CTD sensors
and turbidity sensors to name a few. These are used to understand important ocean parameters and to
create water column profiles. An IMU consisting of a 3 axis accelerometer along with 3 axis gyrocopes
and magnetometers is typically used in underwater robots. In other cases, something known as an AHRS
(Attitude and Heading Reference Sensor) is used which provides heading information in the form of roll,
pitch, yaw angles and the magnetic north from the earth’s magnetic field. It also consists an onboard
CPU which is used to embed a state filter such as an extended kalman filter (EKF) , which is used
to calculate the estimation matrix. This typically consists of three angular velocities and quaternion

2.2.1
Acoustic sensors
Acoustic sensors are work on the principle of time of flight of acoustic waves (sound waves).
Some
commonly used acoustic systems are sonars and baseline systems. Sonars are of many types depending
on their configuration and they allow for mapping and bathymetry. Since the 15th century, when Leonardo
Da Vinci discovered the relative ease with which sound waves could travel through water, sonars have
found countless applications. All ships possess an echosounder to calculate the depth and profile the
seabed. They are preferred in subsea ranging and mapping over optical sensors because light gets scattered
underwater. Some common examples of acoustic sensors are- forward looking sonars (FLS), multibeam
sonars (MBS) , sidescan sonars (SSS), baseline systems, DVLs (Doppler velocity logs) etc.
Forward
looking sonars are most commonly used on autonomous vehicles for obstacle avoidance, homing, docking,
mapping etc. [11]. Sonars work by emitting a sound wave, known as a ping, which strikes objects, seabed,
vegetation etc, and returns to the device. Based on the time of flight and intensity of the reflected wave,
the sonar returns information of the range, hardness and azimuth angle of the targeted object.

Eelgrasses in particular are highly visible on a sonar due to the air filled sacs, and return a high intensity

2.3.1
Opto-acoustic fusion
Sensor fusion, from a robotics perspective, involves combining information from multiple sensors in order
to perform more accurate mapping and navigation. Usually, high resolution sensors like cameras are
combined with the INS (Inertial Navigation System) on board to obtain good estimates of pose and
odometry of the system. Visual-inertial odometry and V-Slam have been the subject of intense studies in
recent years to improve autonomy of vehicles, with different levels of autonomy now being achieved with
assisted-drive and self-driving cars. These systems are more often than not combined with a radar or
laser scanner in order better mapping of the objects and obstacles in the world. From a mapping point of
view, the sensor information is combined to perform 3d scene reconstruction and create 3d point clouds
of objects around the system. This can then, for example, be combined with localisation and odometry
techniques to obtain reliable pose graph estimates of the system, and safely perform obstacle avoidance
and navigation.
The same problem is applied to underwater applications but with a greater challenge due to the distinct
lack of landmarks and satellite positioning.
Furthermore, in the underwater environment where the
visibility is low due to turbidity, optical ranging and scanning sensors such as radars and lidars are not
an option. There are a number of works on photo-mosaicking and 3-d mapping of the seafloor using
downward looking stereo camera set-ups. However, they do not function beyond a few metres due to
scattering and dispersion. Therefore, the only advisable solution is to fuse this data with a better range
sensor. Acoustic sensors perform very well in underwater applications and have been used for decades
in ship navigation for ranging and obstacle avoidance. A common reconnaissance procedure involves
performing coarse-to-fine resolution based surveys using multiple sensors starting with acoustic and then
optical) and stitching these images together to form seabed maps and 3d point clouds. Therefore, this
section will review relevant research in opto-acoustic fusion. Ferreira et al., 2016 [28] describes some
of the sensor fusion techniques being currently implemented. Some of the common challenges faced in
acoustic sensor fusion tend to be around the interpretation of sonar data and difference in projection.
Denoising acoustic images is another challenging aspect of the pipeline because of all the noise presented
by reflections, floating objects, stray noise and sonar movement.

2.4
Feature extraction
Feature extraction is the one of the most fundamental aspects of computer vision and robotics. There
is a plethora of literature and research on land based feature extraction methods.
Image processing
and feature extraction is at the heart of a number of industries ranging from medical imagery, factory
and pipeline inspection, robot and autonomous vehicle vision, text recognition etc. However, there has
been relatively less development in underwater feature extraction methods whether it be due to lack
of underwater sensors, data quality or noise due to weather/turbidity. Another challenge is posed by
limitations in size and power of an ROV or an AUV. Even the largest and better equipped AUVs have
dive cycles of less than 15 hours on average and also have electrical power usage constraints to house
a powerful computer such as a jetson for deeper operations. This somewhat restricts the use of more
complex DNN based feature extraction to shallow water operations. In the case of ROVs, which are
connected (and sometimes powered) by a tether, data processing can be performed on a computer on
shore. However, there is a limit to the extent of processing time there can be before data is transmitted
back to the ROV via an ethernet cable.
There are a number of classical feature extraction techniques based on image processing which are widely
used. It is also possible to extract the pixel RGB values from images and use them to classify images.
They are often used as inputs into deep learning models, but require large datasets and distinct features.
Image feature extraction techniques usually involve some preprocessing steps in order to remove noise,
outliers and segment images. Some morphological operations such as image erosion & dilation, defog-
ging, contrasting and increasing saturation are performed to improve image quality. This is followed by
a thresholding operation which can be used very effectively to extract objects based on colour or pixel
intensities. Commonly, images which are usually of the RGB format are also converted to HSV (hue,

saturation, value) format or grayscale to extract features. Grayscaling of images is a valuable preprocess-
ing step for image gradient based detectors. From an underwater context, these image processing steps
are even more important due to the poor quality of images. Some traditional detectors such as Harris
corner detection use a sliding gaussian window to detect corners. The corners can be calculated based
on the eigen values of the derivative matrix of the pixel intensities.

There have been developments on this with the shi-tomasi corner detector, sobel corners and then edge
detection methods such as canny edge detection, for example, which works on computing intensity gradi-
ents and hysteresis thresholding. These days, faster and more robust algorithms such as BRIEF, FAST,
ORB, SIFT, SURF etc are used which are scale and rotation invariant. They are very useful for classifi-
cation, matching and bag of words representation of image features as they output a descriptors vector.
The descriptors are vectors which contain information of the extracted ’keypoints’ and these may be used
for matching and classification tasks. Papers by Karami et al. [42] and Tareen et al. [43] compare the
performance of different feature extractors for image matching and registration respectively.
While traditional image processing methods are heavily relied on for feature detection, machine learning
and deep learning based approaches are taking over with the high accuracy in detection and classification.
With deep learning, the step of feature extraction was deemed unnecessary with the end-to-end learning
approach. With end-to-end learning, a deep neural network can predict presence or lack of objects and
features in images without the need to extract features and match them. This can be done by anotation
and creation of a large number of datasets with different training conditions associated to each training
batch. While this improves the accuracy in classification of images based on ’learning the features’ of
each class of image, the process requires large amounts of data to avoid over-fitting and avoid the local
maxima problem. Traditional feature extractors also excel over DL approaches when it comes to specific
tasks such as scene creation, motion estimation and capture, image stitching etc [44]. Deep learning
set-ups also come at high computational costs and may often be deemed as over-complicating a problem.
Tranfer learning can be used to use DNNs such as VGG and Resnet to extract feature vectors from
images. The two approaches can also be combined such as extracting SIFT or SURF features from videos
and annotating large datasets of descriptors for classification problems. The paper [45] is a survey of
deep learning methods such as CNNs and MLPs to extract feature vectors from images.
Spears et al.,14, cite-[46], used a videoray with a monocular camera and a blueview P900-45 sonar sensor
(attached at the same inclination as the camera to ease the calibration process) to detect features from
under-ice images. After finding a model to transform points between both sensor frames, their goal is
to extract features from the sonar images and map them onto the camera images. They perform some
morphological operations to remove noise and image segmentation around the object/region of interest.
They proceed to use the detected features from the video and sonar feeds to match them with features from
a known database (feature matching). To obtain feature matches, they employ a well known technique
known as HOG (Histogram of Oriented Gradients). The histogram descriptors consist of magnitudes and
orientations of image gradients and these extracted descriptors can be matched with those from other

2.4.1
Underwater feature extraction
Underwater feature extraction is a tricky problem where there has to be a trade-off between computational
cost and robustness in picking up features over noise. The most commonly used underwater feature
extractor used for applications of odometry and V-SLAM is the Harris corner detector followed by the
shi-tomasi detector, owing to their computational speeds and accuracy in detecting corners. There have to
also be a number of denoising and morphological operations to remove gaussian noise from these images.
SIFT, SURF and similar feature detectors perform well but come at more than twice the computational
cost, which heavily slows down the odometry pipeline, on what are computationally weak industrial
ROVs. Another commonly used image processing technique is the Lucas-Kanade algorithm, which is a
sparse optical flow technique used to estimate the relative motion of features in successive images. There
are also deep learning models which perform fairly well and are discussed in the following text, but require
large-scale training datasets.
In an informative paper by [50], the authors compared the pros and cons of an optical and acoustic
sensor and set up a low-cost opto-acoustic platform. The platform was built on a UCAT robot and
made to detect and track a diver, performing both acoustic and visual tracking of the diver and then a
combined tracker using a kalman filter. For image feature extraction, they chose down-scaled version of
the tiny YOLOv3 deep network, which performs well on a constrained embedded system. The network
was trained on a total of 3000 manually annotated images consisting of the diver, which were scaled up
to 8000 images by adding scale and rotation variations. The acoustic tracking is performed by receving
pings from hydrophones set up on the diver’s suit. The distance is calculated by time of flight and the
heading orientation is calculated as the yaw quaternion relative to the robot, based on the phase shift of
the pings.
In (Liu et al., 2020) [51], they perform scale adaptive image matching of acoustic and optical images based
on gaussian spatial features and a cross-correlation filter. They perform several denoising, contrasting
and other image processing techniques to improve quality of the images. After defogging and performing
morphological operations, they perform feature extraction. They find a unique way to extract spatial
features using cross-correlation instead of extracting image features which are easily affected by noise,
blurry edges, fogging etc. They obtained better matching results in comparsion to other methods (92%)
and the occassions they do not get successful matches are due to noisy features.
[52] entails a review of feature extraction and classification techniques of seagrass based on different kinds
of imagery (acoustic, camera, spectral, satellite etc) and [53] surveyed on deep learning in underwater

as gradient filtering and keypoint thresholding. In a relevant paper by Raine et al. 2020 [55], the authors

4
Methodology
The goal of the project is to achieve sensor fusion between the optical and acoustic sensor present on
board the Bluerov 2 Heavy and improve on the autonomous capabilities of the vehicle. The other aim is
to perform eelgrass feature extraction onboard an ROV and detection on either of the sensor modalities.
To achieve this, multiple experiments were carried out with the ROV along the seashore as well as in the
testing pool.
4.1
Experiment phase 1
The first experiment phase was carried out at the seashore in Skovshoved, Denmark on the Øresund sea.
Danish coastal waters are known for eelgrass habitats and Thurøbund bay in the south funen archipelago
holds the world record for most carbon storage in the world.
Despite the 80-90% loss of habitat of
eelgrass in the region, the Danish coastline continues to be a promising asset towards eelgrass studies and
preservation.
There are a number of challenges to collection of eelgrass data, one of which is the impact of the weather
on the sea. The short Danish summer remains the golden period for any oceanographic activity, and data
collection was a challenge with the onset of fall. This meant any data collection had to be done on the
shore, rather than out at sea in order to find calmer waters. A data collection campaign in September
proved to be vital, as the weather deteriorated rapidly. Despite finding a relatively sunny day on the
calendar, the sea was quite turbid with below-average visibility owing to cloud cover and strong waves.
There was also a consideration to get data with a team of divers but for the purpose of the sensor fusion
challenge, data had to be captured from both sensor modalities at a fixed tranformation from each other.
This meant that using the ROV to perform a survery was the optimal solution.
For the data collection planning, transects were performed with the ROV at the constant depth until the
eelgrass could be detected. Once the eelgrass was detected, different angles of the eelgrass were captured
by yaw and strafing operations. The control of the ROV proved to be challenging, but a good few minutes
of eelgrass data was captured, before the thrusters of the ROV got entangled in seaweed. The waterlinked
GPS was set up at the end of the jetty, immersed in the water in a known configuration. The placement
of the GPS should be at a higher altitude than the ROV’s mission, which in this case wasn’t ideal because
of the shallow operating conditions.
4.2
Experiment phase 2
The initial experiment was performed with the consideration that a number of different sensor fusion
techniques such as point cloud registration, iterative point cloud algorithms based 3D matching, correcting
pose graphs with sonar corrections etc which would require data from both sensors with good point
features for registration. This, however, proved challenging with the nature of data and therefore, it was
decided to proceed with an extrinsic calibration (more in the following sections). Following this, another
experiment phase was carried out in the test pool. Due consideration was given to selection of a well
defined object that could be easily detected and extracted from both sensor modalities.
After a few tests with trial and error, a red aluminium can was deemed the best possible available object.
Red was chosen despite being easily absorbed underwater due to the high contrast from the surrounding
colours. The aluminium can was easily detectable in the sonar as a bright blob too, owing to the low
acoustic absorption of metals. In order to obtain an accurate transformation between the sensors, it was
important to record the object of interest at the same position in both modalities. To achieve this, the
sensors were synchronised temporally and the time stamp information from both the recordings were
matched. The object was recorded in different positions relative to the sensors to obtain the best possible
registration of points.

4.4.2
Image Denoising
After undistorting the images, the next step is to perform image denoising, defogging and contrasting. A
number of filtering operations were performed to improve the image quality. Underwater images suffer
from high fogging, loss of saturation and colour because of absorption by water, and more so with greater
depth.
The first denoising step is to smooth the image using a 2d median filter. The image smoothing is good
to remove small quantities of noise in a local neighborhood of pixels. A typical way is convolution of a
kernel window of defined size over the image. It is good to remove ’salt and pepper’ noise while preserving
edges within the image.
This is followed by a image defogging/dehazing method applied based on the paper [57] to remove haze
and constant white noise over the image in order to drastically improve visibility. The final operation is
to improve the saturation of the image to make specific colours more visible in the image. This was done
by converting the images into HSV (hue, saturation, value) format and using the inRange() function
to create a colour mask and apply it on the desired colour channel of the original image. To better
understand the eelgrass images and the required threshold patterns, a histogram of the RGB values was
plotted. Based on the histogram, it is easy to see that the background has constant values of blue and
green and the darker eelgrass backgrounds are characterised by high red values. 4.4

4.4.3
Feature extraction
The feature extraction process for the project involved processing images from both sensors, performing
thresholding operations and extracting eelgrass information. The first step of feature extraction was
performed on the red aluminium can for the external calibration process.
For the aluminium can, the important step was to extract the pixel value of the centroid of the can.
The first approach was to use simple and fast extraction algorithms such as harris corners and the
goodFeaturesToTrack algorithm on OpenCV. However, there were also unnecessary background features
getting tracked along with it. Therefore, the images were thresholded based on colours. The images were
converted to HSV format and a mask was created for the red colour of the aluminium can. Due to
discontinuities along the edges, the background was subtracted to allow easy object detection.
The
thresholded hsv image was passed through a canny edge detector and the contours were identified. Once
the edges were successfully detected, the moments of the largest contour were calculated and used to find
the centroid of the object.

4.5
Sensor fusion/Calibration
Sensor fusion, which is at the core of this project, will be discussed and the different fusion algorithms
that were considered. The aim of the fusion is to fuse the extracted information from camera and sonar
sensors, and establish a transformation to express 3D points from both sensors in world coordinates.
Two significant challenges during the fusion process were- a) The lack of depth information in the optical
sensor due to the lack of stereo vision. b) The loss of elevation angle information from the 3D sonar
because of the missing raw data. Different approaches were considered and some assumptions were made
to find the best possible fusion solution.
4.5.1
Fusion problem
The purpose of the opto-acoustic sensor fusion is three fold-
1. To allow better interpretation of the sonar imaging. This would require coverting the optical 3D
points Po to acoustic 3D points by means of a direct transformation from the optical frame to
the acoustic frame. This would then be followed by mapping the points on the sonar image and
performing 3D point matching or reconstruction.

3. To predict to position of objects of interests or obstacles with the acoustic sensor and then subse-
quently get within optical range and track or survey it.

from the sensors, some unique solutions are explored.
An approach to estimating depth from motion analysis was considered initially to solve the problem
of missing stereo vision. An approach defined in the paper [59] talks about estimating 3D positions of
objects from 2D acoustic images. This involves extracting the position of the object in successive images,
with well timestamped attitude and heading information of the system. The 3D position is estimated
as lying within a ’circumference’ with readings from successive image frames. This is also a common
approach in monocular SLAM, but the depth estimates are often lacking scale, which is a problem in
the unbounded underwater environment. This technique also requires a well defined baseline distance
between the frames to produce consistent depth readings. The challenge applying this technique during
the project was the inaccuracy creeping in due to inconsistent baseline measurements due to sway of the
ROV and the imprecise readings from the acoustic positioning system in the pool.

Approximations of elevation (δ)
The sonar images for the corresponding timestamps were processed and the range (ρ) and azimuth
(α) were calculated. Due to missing elevation information, different values of delta were considered to
calculate the sonar coordinates. This means that δ values were approximated based on a certain range
of values for each segment of the image. The images were segmented based some threshold x and y pixel
values and the optimal delta value for each reading was tuned this way based on the smallest amount of
error in the point cloud registration. The Oculus m750D also has a minimum elevation angle resolution
of 10 degrees due to the size of the aperture. Furthermore, with a majority of the calibration readings
were taken at the centre of the camera vertically which meant that the positioning of the can with respect
to the sonar is about 0.13m above. This could equate to an approximation of about 5 degrees for the
elevation angle. Based on these considerations, the elevation angle was manually tuned in order to find
the best fitting transform with the smallest error.

4.7
Machine learning
With the detection of eelgrass and extraction of eelgrass features from the sensor modalities, the next task
is detecting the presence or absence of eelgrass and only processing frames which contain eelgrass. This
would mean detection of eelgrass in the image frame, as sonar feature extraction is not suitable enough
for confirming presence of eelgrass due to lot of noisy features. With a high amount of sonar images,
it would be possible to perform classification using deep networks to obtain better classification results
of sonar imagery. The same applies to the optical images, which are being considered within the time
and scope of this project. Some deep learning algorithms have already been fairly successful at detecting
eelgrass and can be referred to in the literature review section.
Another important consideration for this project is that the machine learning algorithm is designed to
function within the real-time pipeline of the ROV and processing power constraints must be considered.
OpenCV and deep networks introduce heavy delays into processing of data and returning to the ROV
which is limited to communication via an ethernet VDSL protocol over a tether with 4 twisted pairs. This
will be a challenge when considering developing odometry and SLAM algorithms for the ROV. Therefore,
to find some improvements in the amount of data required to be processed for time-consuming iterative
processes such as bundle adjustment will find huge computational benefits. Therefore, rejection of certain
frames in odometry or mapping tasks, such as eelgrass mapping using trained ML models can be useful.

5.1
Sensor fusion
The sensor fusion task between optical and acoustic sensors was studied and contemporary research was
analysed, following which an opto-acoustic platform was set up successfully. A number of different fusion
methodologies have been successful at achieving this result, and based on the constraints of the project
set-up and data, a few approaches were selected out. A subsequent data collection campaign was carried
out in the testing pool to obtain 3D point clouds in both sensor modalities. An extrinsic calibration
between the sensor modalities was performed and a rigid transformation obtained. The uniqueness of
the solution obtained during this project is the implementation of an accurate transformation with a
monocular camera with a relatively small calibration dataset. Different methods were considered, and
the problem was solved using the Kabsch algorithm. The noise in the transformation came from the
elevation angle (δ predominantly.
This was iteratively minimised by tuning the value and reducing
the RMSE error. The final RMSE error calculated was found to be 6.97(in mm), which was reliably
accurate. There is another small depth error in mapping points between the two modalities due to the
minor inaccuracies in the camera depth estimation, which can be noticed in the mapping of the points on
the aluminium can. The extrinsic calibration is used to map points from sonar to camera images, which
can help identify areas of interest and understand sonar data better. The range data from the sonar is
also available in conjunction with the camera information, which has far greater accuracy than camera
depth estimates.
The sensor fusion was used to map certain extracted points from the sonar image to the image frame. To
test the accuracy of the mapping, some manually extracted points of the aluminium can from the sonar
image were reprojected onto the image frame as seen in A.6. The eelgrass mapping on the image frame

as many noisy features as possible. The reprojection is reliably accurate within a few pixels , with the
only discrepancies arising from what was expected- the inaccuracies arising from the depth estimations of
the monocular camera which results in some loss of depth reconstuction. This issue could be minimised
with access to raw data and stereo camera integration and is discussed in more depth in future work.

6
Discussion
This section shall conclude and discuss the methods and results obtained throughout this project. In
conclusion, the project was accomplished as planned, with the limitations of the project outlined and
future ideas presented.
The sensor fusion task, which was the primary goal of the project, was accomplished successfully. The
transformation between the optical and acoustic modalities was calibrated. The secondary goal of the
project was to perform feature extraction and detection on the collected eelgrass data. The eelgrass
meadows were detected successfully, with the application of traditional machine learning methods such
as k-means clustering and nearest neighbour classification. A good overview of literature has also been
provided to study the contemporary research in the fields of marine robotics and computer vision. Finally,
a future work study has been performed in the following sections to discuss some of the potential ideas
and outcomes that this project presents.
6.1
Project execution
The project was executed with a planned timeline and research objectives. These objectives have been
accomplished to the planned milestones to a satisfactory level. The project also consisted of two planned
data acquisition experiments with the Bluerov2 Heavy. The first collection campaign occurred in Septem-
ber near the harbour in Skovshoved, Denmark, where planned transects of the eelgrass meadow were
performed. Despite picking the best available date in terms of weather and wind, the data collection
campaign was halted midway due to some issues with the motors due to the turbulent conditions at
sea. Nonetheless, a reasonable dataset of eelgrass was obtained with decent lighting conditions. This
prompted the initial process of data investigation and preparation.
Based on the preliminary data study and literature review, it was decided to perform another data
collection campaign, but this time in the testing pool. This was done with the specific approach for
fusion that was formulated. The camera calibration data was also obtained in the testing pool, using a
checkerboard pattern. The fusion algorithm was readied, but owing to some initial challenges in extraction
of data, the timeline for the fusion process was pushed back a bit. Once these were resolved, a sensor
fusion transformation was calculated. This permitted enough time for achieving the secondary machine
learning and eelgrass detection goal. Therefore, the project achieved well within the deadlines, and sets
up an exciting platform for future opto-acoustic fused odometry and mapping pipelines. A tertiary data
collection campaign would have been beneficial for the machine learning task, but was limited by the
January weather conditions of the Danish coastline for ROV operation. Another conclusion to be drawn
from this would be to perform such a study with due consideration given to the adverse effects of the
Winter weather to ROV operation.
Overall, despite some challenges, the project was completed to address all research objectives, theoretically
and experimentally.
6.2
Sensor fusion
The sensor fusion task, was completed and a transformation between the optical and acoustic sensors was
successfully calculated. A study of different sensor fusion methods was performed. Some of the contem-
porary approaches involve calculating a rigid transformation between the sensors, iterative point cloud
based refinement approaches between point clouds or pose-graph correction based on acoustic measure-
ments. These methods perform iterative refinement to minimise noise or achieve better noise covariance
estimates. This process is multiplied in the underwater problem due to more than average gaussian noise
due to image fogging. These methods also utilise a stereo camera set-up and have accurate 3D point
clouds with small reprojection error. Some of these methods also have access to raw acoustic data and
generate accurate acoustic point clouds. They, therefore, establish a fairly accurate 3D correspondence
between the point clouds and perform minimisation on the image pixel noise.
The project uses a monocular camera, and therefore to produce a 3D reconstruction would require fairly
accurate estimates of the depth. This problem was approached by taking inputs from an image triangu-

lation based depth calculation algorithm. The depth estimations were matched up with the Pythagorean
approximation of the sonar range readings to confirm their veracity. Due to some uncertainty in these
values, it was decided it would be best to also minimise the image noise. Therefore, iterative point cloud
based matching of eelgrass from both modalities was discarded as a possibility. The correspondence prob-
lem also had to be considered and therefore the problem was redefined to finding a rigid transformation
between both point clouds. Therefore, a data collection for a well-defined object in both modalities was
performed to apply the triangulation. This was also done in such a way that both sensor recordings were
temporally synchronised. The 3D points in the acoustic sensor were also approximated based on some
considerations 4.5.2 and refined to minimise the RMSE in the calibration.
The transformation was obtained, utilising the Kabsch algorithm. Some points corresponding to the
object of interest were extracted from the sonar image and mapped onto the camera image. The mapping
is accurate up to a few pixels. The mean standard deviation error is 6.97mm.
6.2.1
Limitations
Despite the small standard deviation from the ground-truth readings, the mapping lacks in fully accurate
depth reconstruction due to the approximations made with the depth of the monocular camera. The
uncertainty creeping in is presumably represented by small error in rotational angles of the transformation.
This can be overcome by incorporating a stereo set-up and obtaining precise 3D reconstructions.
Another limitation to the reconstruction process after fusion at the moment is the sonar feature extraction.
While reprojecting sonar 3D points on the camera frame, features from the sonar were excluded based
on discarding features based on their pixel values. Also, some noisy points have snuck into the mapping
process around the eelgrass which have to be discarded by outlier detection methods.
6.3
Seagrass detection
The seagrass detection aspect of the project was also completed to a satisfactory result with a 73.5%
accuracy in classification of eelgrass clusters from ROV images.
In the first approach, images from the Deep seagrass are denoised by using dehazing methods and then
pre-processed using HSV and otsu thresholding methods. The k-means clustering process is tested using
different feature extractors and they are compared on their computational speeds and clustering accuracy.
Based on this analysis, ORB features performs best and was chosen as the preferred extractor for the
classification process.
The second eelgrass detection approach involved an image segmentation process using clustering. This
was achieved with a relatively small dataset of about 30-40 images, which is a good indicator of its
performance. There have been some works on image segmentation using fuzzy algorithms which have
found good accuracy and this project draws inspiration from them and applies a k-means based segmen-
tation to test the performance on eelgrass. The eelgrass images from the ROV are used in this feature
detection process and the extracted descriptors are fed into a k-means clustering algorithm. The visual
bag of words technique is a powerful tool which are created by appending the cluster centroids. The
test and train images are then used to create histograms using these visual words. The test histograms
are matched with the labelled train histograms using a 1-nearest neighbour classifier and are classified
accordingly. The visual words method works much better than regular classification of images based on
colour histograms, for example.
6.3.1
Limitations
Based on the performance of the model and the data size, and sets a good precedent for image segmen-
tation and classification in eelgrass data. Even though it outperforms some simpler models by far,it is
not ideal for eelgrass classification for marine research. There can be improvements made to improve
the accuracy which could make this approach promising for eelgrass reconstruction and mapping for
oceanographic surveying missions.

being fully retained. This will also thereby allow for accurate pose-estimation of the ROV. The task of
performing motion estimation, requires accurately matched 3D point clouds. Minimising reprojection
error in depth tends to be one of the computationally expensive pieces of the jigsaw puzzle, as does
the bundle adjustment process, which are iterative processes meant to reduce noise and uncertainty in
pose-estimation. Fusing highly accurate 3D points or range information from the sonar as reference to
this problem can be hugely important to improve data redundancy and obtain a ground-truth, especially
in depth estimates. In fact, acoustic odometry could be used to perform ’egomotion’ of its own, with the
reprojection of 3D acoustic point clouds. Better processing of sonar data onboard ROVs can have the
same impact Lidars have in achieving autonomous navigation on land. This also proves hugely useful for
ROVs because INS systems are prone to errors over large distances and wheel encoders cannot be used
on underwater vehicles.
Seagrass detection- The project approaches the eelgrass detection problem from a bag of words based
image segmentation process.
It is computationally more eﬀicient than bulky deep learning processes
and can perform well within the constraints of an ROV. The performance of the pipeline can be vastly
improved with larger datasets for creating the training histograms and visual words. Using weighted
visual words or a fuzzy model to represent the visual words as described in some research works such as

huge datasets that are relevant to ROV imagery and will still require segmenting of eelgrass before they
can be fed into a DL model such as [61].
6.4.1
Opto-acoustic odometry
Visual-inertial odometry is a common term for odometry which combines information deduced from image
sequences with the estimates from the internal sensors. Nister et al.,2004 [62] have a popular work in
which they estimate motion of a mono/stereo camera set-up using a feature tracker of the video inputs.
They use a Harris corner detector with minimal amounts of local saturation and thresholding in case
of excessive points being detected so that the process doesn’t slow down. They then perform feature
matching of points between successive frames of points which are within a certain distance (disparity)
from one another. The matched points are triangulated into point clouds and a preemptive RANSAC
algorithm is used to obtain the pose of the camera set-up. A 3-point algorithm is used as a ’hyothesis
generator’ and iterative refinement (bundle adjustment) is performed based on the re-projection error.
The preemptive RANSAC algorithm for ego-motion estimation is introduced by Nister in the paper-
[63]. Nister also introduces the well-known 5-point algorithm where the ego-motion can be estimated by
relative camera views of 5 different correspondences.
The paper ’Fast semi-direct monocular visual odometry’ by Forster et al. [64] describes a method of
eliminating costly feature extraction and computing 3D points from image pixel intensities. They used
Opto-acoustic sensor fusion
39
==PAGE:48==
probabilistic mapping method to remove outliers and a sparse model-based method for aligning successive
image frames. Scaramuzza et al., 2011 [65] is another great paper on visual odometry which provides a
history of visual odometry. There is also a comparison of different VO methods based on local feature
extraction based methods or global appearance based methods, which tend to be far more expensive
computationally. The paper discusses different motion estimation scenarios between 2D-2D, 3D-3D and
3D-2D point correspondences from successive images. The motion estimation, and odometry estimates,
are obtained from the calculation of translation and rotation matrices between these point clouds. [66]
From a maritime perspective, odometry is more commonly known as dead-reckoning and is used to track
the position and heading of a vehicle at sea over an elapsed time-period. Ego-motion estimation is used to
find the pose of the camera by calculating the translation and rotation from the previous pose. One such
paper [67] performs large-scale underwater 3D reconstruction and predict the state of an AUV using a
Viewpoint Augmented Navigation (VAN) algorithm and loop-closure is implemented in the 3D mapping
process from the stereo images. The 3D triangulation is done using a midpoint method described in
the informative book about multiple view geometry- [68]. This is followed by calculating the translation
and rotation matrices between the image frames and feeding the information into an EIF filter for pose
estimation. Loop closure is an important aspect of localisation, as it greatly minimises pose uncertainty,
and [69] and [70] tackle the underwater loop-closure problem with very unique solutions.
In order for visual-odometry to provide relevant pose estimates, it needs to be fast and computationally
cheap, especially on vehicles such as ROVs and AUVs. This means the two time-consuming steps of
feature extraction and bundle adjustment need to be eﬀicient. A relevant paper by [71] performs a low-
cost and fast visual odometry and mapping process on an ROV. They speed up the stereo-matching
process and employ computationally fast feature extractors such as Harris corners and Lucas-Kanade.
They also use a single-layer neural net to model the uncertainty in VO and perform semi-global bundle
adjustment to speed up the algorithm. Finally, [72] provides a good detailed overview of the VIO process.

6.5
Conclusion
To conclude, the thesis project has been successful in obtaining a transformation between the optical
and acoustic senor and mapping the sonar points in the image frame. The fused data can be used to
perform monocular odometry or monocular SLAM with sonar range corrections. The eelgrass features
were successfully extracted from camera images and segmented from the background using a clustering
based visual bag of words approach. This goes to show that image segmentation based approach be used
to survey and map eelgrass coverage as well as be used in computationally demanding tasks such as visual
inertial odometry.
Conservation of eelgrass can be take major steps if robotics can be implemented to the extent of semi
or fully autonomous swarms of AUVs which can survey and track large areas far more eﬀiciently. The
payload of an ROV can also be chosen specifically for the task, where it is to monitor the percentages
of certain elements or profile water columns. Other tasks such as gas pipeline crack detection, ship hull
maintenance etc which are crucial tasks requiring teams of expert divers or manually operated ROV
missions can be performed with eﬀicient autonomous mission plans. The next step towards achieving
such autonomy is arming ROVs and AUVs with sensor data redundancies to achieve accurate odometry,
localisation and mapping. The possibilities at sea with these remote vehicles are endless.
