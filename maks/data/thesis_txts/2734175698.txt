Abstract
Observations with The International Gamma Ray Astrophysics Laboratory, INTEGRAL, are coming
to an end in 2024. The mission is one of ESA´s large missions with a focus on discovering the properties
of the universe in X-ray and Gamma-ray wavelengths. As is customary when a mission ends a legacy
archive, here in the form of a catalog of the X-ray sources, is to be produced by ESA. Present project
is the start for this process covers the initial five years, 2003-2007, of the INTEGRAL mission, focusing
on the performance of its X-ray units, JEM-X.
JEM-X has detected 90 sources in the first five years and we will present some of the brightest and most
active sources. Their activities will be showcased through single pointing images and combined images
known as mosaics. The project concerns X-ray sources in X-ray binaries singular X-ray sources like
the Crab nebula, investigated through their count rate, detection significance, and Hardness-Intensity
diagram (HID). We will be discuss the different processes and situations producing the various X-rays
measured by the instrument. The sources will be show cased in two distinct energy bands, soft (3-10)
KeV and hard (10-25 KeV).
JEM-X has in its lifespan detected several thousand X-ray bursts, including the initial five years of
the mission. A script has been developed, based n the light curves of JEM-X, capable of detecting and
reporting potential X-ray bursts from a given source, providing the time of the burst, the count rate
of the peak and the corresponding Signal to Noise ratio.

The Integral Mission
The Integral mission is an ESA mission that was launched on the 17th of October 2002. The Integral
Mission is the first space mission to measure light from distant stars in different wavelengths, the
wavelengths being X-ray, Gamma-ray and Optical [1]. The primary target of the mission is violent
explosions of stars emitting gamma-rays as they perform super novae, as well as the X-ray observation
of Black Holes, Neutron Stars and the afterglow of Gamma-ray bursts. The mission has been scheduled
to end in 2024 and for a planetary re-entry in 2029 [1].
INTEGRAL flies in a highly elliptical orbit around earth[2]. The time it takes to make one lap around
Earth, here called a revolution, takes up to three days in space, for which the Satellite is turned off
when it is closest to the planet. Because of the elliptical orbit the satellite is only 13000 kilometers
from the surface at its lowest point and 153000 kilometers at its highest [2]. Since 2015 however these
heights have been shrinking due to the planned decommission in 2029 [2].
INTEGRAL looks into high energy phenomena in Space, namely X-rays and Gamma Rays. INTE-
GRAL utilizes three different instruments to measure the photons from these high energy events. This
project has been made through the usage of the X-ray instrument JEM-X, however the main instru-
ments of the INTEGRAL mission is the Gamma ray instruments SPI and IBIS. SPI is capable of
measuring in between 18 KeV - 8 MeV. with an angular resolution of 2.5 degrees [3]. IBIS works in
between 15 KeV to 10 MeV with an angular resolution of 12 arc minutes[4].
2.1
JEM-X
The Joint European Monitor for X-rays is the X-ray instrument onboard the INTEGRAL satellite.
The instrument works in conjunction with the main Gamma ray instrument measuring the energy
and position of high energy photons with a 3 arc minute resolution [2]. The instrument is capable of
measuring photons with energies ranging in between 3 to 35 KeV. The instrument works through the
usage of a two JEM-X units, each containing a coded mask and detector working in unison of one
another[5].
Figure 1 shows the insides of a singular Jem-X unit. A Jem-X unit consists of a coded mask and a
detector co alligned with the other instruments onboard Integral [5]. The unit consists of a coded mask
and a gas chamber detector located 3.4 m apart from one another.
2
==PAGE:4==
Figur 1: Overview of an JEM-X Unit [5]
The detector consists of a Micro strip Gas chamber Strip. The gas chamber of the detector contains a
gas mixture which consists of 90 pct. Xenon and 10 pct. Methane at a constant pressure of 1.5 Bar [5].
A X-ray photon passes through the coded mask and lands on the detector. The Photon interacts with
the gas chamber through photo electric absorption. The interaction of gas to the high energy photon
begins a cascade effect of photo electric absorption as the gas molecule emits energy, hitting a new
molecule starting the process over. The total amount of energy emitted during this cascade is tied to
the energy of the photon.
Figur 2: overview of JEM-X science capability [2]
The coded mask is based on a hexagonal uniformly redundant array [9]. Jem-X pattern consists of
22501 elements and only 25 pct. is open area [9]. The Coded mask is designed that only photons who
pass through the wholes of the mask will make it through to the detector. X-ray photons come from
all over the night sky and is a struggle to find the location of the photon. Having a low transparency
allows to detect the direction of the photon coming in. This way it is possible to locate the source of
the X-ray. Jem-x is capable of detecting sources of about 5 degrees of axis, sources further away than
this should be looked at with uncertainties.

2.2
X-ray Astronomy
X-ray Astronomy have been practiced since the 1960’s. Looking at the electromagnetic spectrum, we
know that X-ray photons are some of the shortest wavelength photons out there, and hence some of
the most energetic. Astronomers are restricted however, as all X-ray telescopes need to be in orbit
around the planet. This is due to the fact that is it impossible to detect X-rays on the surface as X-ray
photons are absorbed by Earths atmosphere.
Figur 4: Atmospheric interaction with different wavelengths [14]
As can be seen in figure 4 X-rays do not pass through to the surface of the planet, and for the atmosp-
here to have little to no interaction with the measurements of X-rays a telescope needs to be in orbit
around 193 KM at a minimum.
There are three primary processes producing X-rays. Blackbody radiation, Thermal Brehmstrahlung
and Synchroton Radiation [14].
Blackbody Radiation
Blackbody radiation is the surface radiation of a fully absorbing surface which is tied to the tempera-
ture of the surface. It is tied to the cooling of the surface and the higher the temperature the higher
the photon energies [14]. Most stellar objects have an surface temperature of about 2500K to 40000K,
however to emit X-rays through Blackbody radiation, you need a temperature in the 106K. A neutron
star newly formed from a super nova have this temperature and can potentially emit X-rays through
Blackbody radiation. This Blackbody radiation dominates in the 0.1-1 KeV energy range.
Thermal Brehmstrahlung
Thermal Brehmstahlung, as the name suggests, is dependent on the temperature. At temoeratures
surpassing 105K atoms are ionized and gas consists of positive protons and negative ions [14]. When
5
==PAGE:7==
an electron passes by or moves close to an positive ion, its trajectory is changed by strong electric
forces. This accelerates the electron and it emits electromagnetic radiation which is what we here call
Brehmstrahlung. The higher the temperature the higher the energy there is emitted during such a
process. For the energy to be emitted in X-rays, the temperature needs to exceeded 106K. At 106K
besides Brehmstahlung there will also be some line emission from different elements. This is primarily
in low energy of X-rays, i.e. in between 0.1-3 KeV. At temperatures higher than 5 · 107K, the thermal
emission comes primarily from thermal brehmstrahlung[14] .
Figur 5: Spectral forms of different thermal processes [14]
Synchroton Radiation
Synchroton Radiation is a kind of brehmstrahlung [14]. Synchroton radiation occurs when a electron
is in the viscinity of a exstremely strong magnetic field. This magnetic field changes the trajectory of
the electron. This changes the velocity vector of the electron and it emits radiation in X-rays [14]. This
radiation is not tied to the temperature of the surface bur rather to the energy of the electron, the
strength of the magnetic field and the direction of the motion relative to the field [14]. Neutron Stars
have these immense magnetic fields surrounding them which is capable of accelerating these electrons
to such extremes they will emit X-rays through synchroton radiation.
LMXB and HMXB
X-ray sources are found, for the most part, in binary systems together with a companion star. These
we will call X-ray binaries. The X-ray binaries exist in two different versions, a Low mass X-ray binary
(LMXB) and the High Mass X-ray Binary (HMXB). In a low mass X-ray Binary system the compact
object, Neutron star or Black Hole is accompanied by a main sequence star. This companion star
begins to shed its outer layers of gas and begins to form what is known as a Roche Loop overflow [14].
The Roche Loop is the region surrounding a star where material/Matter is gravitationally bound to
the star. A large star or a main sequence star pushes out a large amount of matter as it burns its fuel
and over time will be capable of filling its Roche Loop [14]. The matter that moves beyond the Roche
Loop is no longer tied to the original star. In the case of a LMXB the material is transferred from
the original star to the compact object forming an accretion disk [14]. This accretion disk is a mass
transfer from the companion to the compact, and in this disk, temperatures will rise to the 106 −107K,
emitting X-rays by thermal brehmstrahlung or by blackbody radiation. In the case of a Neutron star
the matter from the accretion disk can form layers on the surface, which over time, will explode and
perform an explosion known as a X-ray burst

==PAGE:8==
Figur 6: Depiction of accretion [6]
Figure 6 is a depiction of an accretion disk in a LMXB system. The matter from the companion star
moves to the neutron star and forms a disk surrounding the neutron star. The temperature of the disk
reach high temperatures through radiation and friction within the disk. These temperatures reach the
threshold for thermally emitted X-rays. The flow of matter have an impact on the energy of the disk,
where a high and unstable flow will have a higher temperature than a slow and more stable flow.
HMXB are binary systems where the compact object, most likely a neutron star, is in orbit surro-
unding a massive star. The companion star in these kind of systems is usually 8-10 Solar masses or
more[14]. A neutron star is at max three solar masses. In these kind of systems, the compact object
moves in orbit around the Massive Star. The companion star is a massive unstable star and is inca-
pable of holding onto its outer layers. The star proceedes to continuously shed its outer layers, through
stellar winds, sending out a vast amount of gas to the area surrounding it [14]. The Neutron star is
moving in an elliptical orbit around the big star and will on occasion pass through the stellar winds of
the star. When it passes through the winds, the star takes on matter through Bondi-Hoyle accretion
[14]. During these kinds of situations, the intensity of the neutron star usually increases, but for the
most part, the neutron star is not interacting with the winds, being too far away from its companion.
HMXB are unable to perform X-ray bursts.
Neutron Stars and Black Holes are also found in singular systems star systems where they are the
sole remnant. These we define as Super Novae Remnants and tend to be some of the more stable and
linear X-ray sources.

Performance of the Mission
Since 2002. INTEGRAL have been steadily flying around the planet making observations of X-ray
sources around the galaxy. The mission have been able to discover and analyse a lot of X-ray sources,
primarily in the higher end of the energy range, i.e. above 10 KeV. Before INTEGRAL there were
not many missions who had missions who had instruments capable of working with a good resolution
above 10 KeV.

2.3.1
Observational Constraints
INTEGRAL and in essence JEM-X can view any point in the sky [5]. However due to constraints
tied to different instruments, some parts of the sky is never in the field of view of JEM-X. These
constraints are to protect the Gamma ray instruments onboard INTEGRAL or limitations set by the
orbit. Targets which is never in the field of view is The Moon, the Sun and Earth.
The satellite is aligned during its operational span to be +/- 40 degrees from the sun which implies
that sources who are closer than 50 degrees to the sun is not viewable by the mission[5]. Because of
this constraint there will be months where certain X.ray sources are never detected due to them being
too close to the sun.
Another constraint for the JEM-X instrument is the star trackers. The star trackers of the spacecraft
is co-aligned with the instruments onboard INTEGRAL. In order to protect the star tracker, INTE-
GRAL will always be at minimum 15 degrees away from the moon [5]

2.5
JEM-X Products
INTEGRAL generates data when it is either keeping a stable position or moving to a new area. These
data are called science window, scw, and any operations during the observations is stored in the scw
[9]. It is from these scw that we get the different products available from JEM-X. JEM-X saves the
results in the form of .fits files from which we can generate the Light curves or Images of the source
being analyzed..
As was stated earlier, JEM-X is capable of detecting X-ray photons with energies ranging from 3 KeV
to 35 KeV. This energy range can be split up in different energy bands. It is possible to have as many
as possible, but here we will define two, Soft and Hard. Soft X-rays, who is the most dominant, have
an energy range of 3 KeV to 10 KeV. Hard X-rays have an energy range of 10 KeV to 25 KeV. We do
not use the full 35 KeV as there is a loss of sensitivity past 25 KeV. The data contained in the ScW’s
are split in the energy bands defined by the user and can be read through the usage of the software.
Jem-X can work with up to 15 Energy bands, but it is advised not to go beyond 4.
2.5.1
Images
As Figure 8 shows, the IMA step uses the data of a ScW to make images of the sky with the sources
in view of the instrument. The image produced will show the region of the sky the instrument was
aligned to and report sources based off the ScW. The exposure time of a source in a singular ScW is
sufficient for a strong X-ray source to be detected by jem-X. For the weaker sources this may not be
the case. A weaker source may require longer exposure time for jem-X to detect it.
IMA2 produces an combined image of different images called an Mosaic. The mosaic will be based
off an observation group. An observation group is an user defined grouping of different ScW’s. The
exposure time of sources in a mosaic will be longer and will show some of the weaker sources not visible
earlier in a single ScW image. Figure 11 shows an example of a Mosaic of the Galactic Bulge region
made by Jem-X.

The structure of the mosaic pictures is dependent on the observational pattern of the used method
during the scw’s. The most popular standard picture of JEM-X comes in the form of a 5 x 5 Hexagonal
Picture. This kind of pattern consists of one detection focused on the target of the observation and 24
other observations surrounding it. For each observation the instruments moves about 2 degrees. This
method is largely used for vast regions of space, such as the galactic center or galactic bulge, as due
to the nature of the observation pattern a singular source will not be in focus for all of the pictures.
Another well used picture creation method is a hexagonal picture of 7 different pictures. These are
larger, with one picture having a width of 11.7 degrees [5] , In this form, there is 1 picture focusing on
the target source and six surrounding, where all 6 will overlap the target. The measured flux is based
of the average of these different pictures in the different energy bands. The reason for these picture
creation methods is due to constraints related to the Gamma ray instrument SPI and IBIS [5].

Light Curves
The Light curves produced by Jem-x shows the count rate of a source over a period of time. The data
for the light curves are based off the images and can help explore the properties of an X-ray source.
Neutron Stars in Low Mass X-ray binaries can produce what is known as a X-ray burst. A X-ray burst
is a thermonuclear explosion happening on the surface of the Neutron Star.
Figur 12: X-ray Burst seen with Lcurve
Figure 12 is an Light curve produced by JEM-X unit 1 for Revolution 371 scw 32 of the X-ray Source
HETE J1900.1-2455. The rapid changes during a X-ray burst is usually short, a couple of seconds, the
light from a singular scw is sufficient during a X-ray burst.

Jem-X Catalogue
As was said earlier, INTEGRAL is coming to an end in 2024 followed by planetary re-entry in 2029.
ESA and the team behind maintaining the INTEGRAL mission will be constructing a legacy archive
of the mission in the form of a catalog of the entire mission during its entire duration from 2002 to
2024. For the creation of this catalogue, a series of scripts have been made in order to extract the
desired information. For the catalogue, it is desired to keep the source id, name, coordinates, detection
significance, the flux in soft and hard X-rays and all the error bars for each column.
A catalog of JEM-X for the first five years of INTEGRAL flight have been constructed, corresponding
to a period from January 1st 2003 to December 30, 2007. Revolution wise, this is equivalent to Rev 27
to Rev 636.
The observations have been grouped into monthly observations. Using a monthly observation schedule
moves the 609 revolutions into 72 tables of data containing the information of approximately 9 Revo-
lutions each.
Figur 13: Data of an obs res .fits file for the JEM-X observations of January 2003
The information about the sources discovered during the JEM-X analysis for January 2003 is stored
in a obs res .fits file. The obs res .fits file contains the flux, coordinates and names of the sources di-
scovered in the scw’s. The Flux of of the observations is tied to the column named Flux. Each of the
columns containing the ”Plot”lines are files containing a table of more data points. Figure 14 shows
the table of the Flux column.

4.2
Hardness Intensity Diagram
A common technique to analyse X-ray sources is by constructing what is known as a Hardness Intensity
Diagram.
H = F10−25KeV [mCrab]
F3−10KeV [mCrab]
(12)
I = F3−10KeV [mCrab] + F10−25KeV [mCrab]
(13)
Where F is the flux of the energy band. The hardness Intensity diagram is a method used for longer
period of time, observations of X-ray sources. The diagram will reveal information about what kind of
system we are dealing with. The method tells about the interactions within the systems in focus. Each
kind of system will each form their own kind of HI diagram, with a neutron star in a LMXB being
able to form two different patterns.
32
==PAGE:34==
4.2.1
The Crab
The first HI diagram to be shown is that of the crab nebula. The Crab is a bright Neutron star in
a singular system. The neutron star is a Super Nova remnant and does not have any accretion from
another companion star.
Figur 27: HI Diagram for the Crab
As can be seen from Figure 27 the combined intensity of the crab is rather stable at around 108 Co-
unts/s, besides a single points below 88 counts/s. Its hardness is moving from 0.29 to 0.32. There is
no significant change in either intensity or in hardness.
The Crab nebula is a singular source and does not have an accretion disk. an Accretion disks or stellar
winds are the primary factors which increases or decreases Hardness/Intensity.
The little to no significant changes in the HI-Diagrams shows an lack of an accretion disk and corre-
sponds to that of a single neutron star who does not have a companion star.
33
==PAGE:35==
4.2.2
4U 1820-303
4U 1820-303 is one of the more exotic X-ray sources. The system is an X-ray binary containing a
neutron star. Its companion however is not a large main sequence star but rather a white dwarf. This
system is called a Compact X-ray Binary (CXB).
Figur 28: HI diagram of 4U 1820 - 303
A neutron star in a CXB acts much akin to a LMXB. The white dwarf orbits around the Neutron star
and emits matter which is trapped by the neutron star and forms an accretion disk surrounding it.
The white dwarf is emitting matter from its surface by sending out gravitational waves.
The spectrum of the source is predominantly hard, with the hardness primarily around 0.8 and even
exceeding 1. We do not have enough data to point out what king of source it is, however, the shape
of the HI diagram suggests that of an Atoll source. An atoll source is one of the two forms a Neutron
star in a LMCB can form in such a diagram. The Atoll shape moves from a Low High intensity / low
hardness to a Low Intensity/High Hardness state and paints out what looks like a C. or atoll. In here

4.2.3
Vela X-1
As can be seen in figure 5 Vela X-1 is a HMXB containing a massive star and a Neutron Star. The
Neutron star is moving in an elliptical orbit surrounding a massive star who is shedding a large amount
of matter through stellar winds.
Figur 29: HI Diagram of Vela X-1
As we can see on the HI Diagram, Vela X-1 is predominantly a hard X-ray source. For most of its
orbit, the compact object is not interacting with its companions excess gas. Unlike in an LMXB there
is no Roche Loop overflow and for the most part, X-ray generation is tied to Synchroton Radiation
due to the immense magnetic field of the neutron star and Thermal Brehmstrahlung.
As the neutron star moves in its elliptical orbit around the giant star however, it is bound to pass close
by its companion. During this period the neutron star interact with the stellar winds of the companion,
by moving through the stellar winds. In this period, accretion onto the neutron star occurs through
Bondi-Hoyle accretion [14]. During this period the intensity of the neutron star increases significantly
and its hardness softens, due to the fact that the X-rays generated by accretion effects are primarily
in the soft energy band.
We can see this increase in flux and fall in hardness, which could suggest that Vela X-1 passed through
the stellar winds of its companion star during that observation. However there is not enough measu-
rements within the period to make this assessment.

GX 3+1 is a neutron star in an Low mass X-ray Binary. The system is located in the Galactic Center.

Figur 31: GX 3+1 HID
GX 3+1 is known as an Atoll source. Atoll sources unlike the Z-sources, do not form a letter but rather
form a C or banana shape. This is a regular development for a low intensity neutron star in LMXB. a
Neutron star in a LMXB system will move from a soft intense state to a hard not to intense state. This
change is tied to changes in the accretion disk. The flow of matter is not consistent and on occasion the
disk will move in closer to the neutron star.When there is a high flow, or the accretion disk is close to
the compact object, the X-ray is stronger and dominated by the soft photons from the accretion disk,
but when the flow is low, or the accretion disk is further away from the compact object the intensity
is low and the synchroton radiation and thermal brehmstrahlung is more prominent. The shape of GX
3+1 suggests a C shape, akin to an Atoll source.

GRS1915+105 is a LMXB who is containing a Black Hole as its compact object. For a Black Hole in

Light Curves
Light curves is a JEM-X product showcasing the count rate of a source on a seconds based timescale.
We can identify further aspects about a X-ray source by looking at its light curve. X-ray bursts are
only visible through JEM-X by looking at the Light curve of an X-ray source.
Figure 34 shows the light curve of HETE J1900.1-2455 from scw 32 rev 371. HETE J1900.1-2455 is a
neutron star in a LMXB and from the light curve we can see it has performed an X-ray burst. X-ray
bursts are interesting to astronomers for a plethora of reasons.
Firstly, it is only a neutron star in a LMXB who can perform them, and to find one, is a certain
guarantee of what kind of system we are looking at.
Secondly during a X-ray burst, it can be assumed, the flux from the source comes directly from the
neutron star, not the accretion disk, making analysis of the compact object itself possible.
Figur 34: X-ray burst from HETE J1900.1-2455, Rev 371
The light curve shows the usual development of a regular X-ray burst. During the burst, the count
rate sky rise, which can be seen in figure 34. The incredible rise is then followed by an exponential
decay. The decay in count rate brings the flux back down to the same level as pre-burst. Most of the
bursts have a decay t of just a few seconds. it is however possible to see a burst lasting minutes, even
10’s of minutes. These we call Long Bursts, and their longevity is down to the thickness of the layer
when it explodes. The measurement of a Light curve in JEM-X is in Counts/100cm2/s.

Burst Detection
When looking for a burst in a Light curve, we expect to see a significant change in the count rate and
the Signal to Noise Ratio. As can be seen in Figure 34 The burst went to about 350 Counts/sec, from
an regular 40 counts/sec.

N > 3
(14)
There needs to be a significant change in the count rate to count in a burst. To detect and find these
bursts, we look at the Signal to Noise ratio and at least 3 σ.
There is an immense increase in the signal during an X-ray burst. The error increases as well but not
nearly as quick as the signal. When a burst occurs, the ratio between signal and noise increases, and
when it exceeds 3, there is a potential for a X-ray burst.
The next step, if the first criteria has been meet, is then to find the σ-value. The sigma value is the
standard deviation of the noise. We need to look at the light curve without the burst to find this value.
To do this we delete the area around the burst from the light curve and from there finds the standard
deviation. We then compare the signal to the sigma value. Once again we look for a ratio of greater
than 3.
S
3σ > 3
(15)
If this second condition is true, then we can consider a burst to have happened. We have had a signi-
ficant difference in the signal to noise, followed by a major detection significance.

6
Discussion
The activity of various X-ray source have been presented and a catalog of various X-ray sources pre-
sented. Due to the very nature of X-ray sources, having a monthly based average have its limitations.
X-ray sources in XrB have daily changes tied to them, raising or lowering their intensity and/or hard-
ness ratio. Taking the average of an entire month you will miss these daily changes in the activity of
the XrB system.
The mosaics were grouped together based off their observation Target. This means that the images
from various revolution were combined into the same picture, even if there was a whole revolution or
more in between them. In this period a X-ray source can move from one state to another, which will
not be seen by JEM-X or in the analysis of that specific mosaic.
JEM-X have an operational field of view of about 5 degrees. However, detection’s who are off axis
by more than five degrees are still included in the final product of the program used to convert a
obs res.fits file into the catalog.fits file. Further work on this project could automatically remove or
analyze points who are off-axis by more than five degrees and remove them from the final file. further
more, errors who have been detected as new sources also remain in the catalog file and should be
removed.
The X-ray burst detecting tool is capable of finding and reporting the time, detection significance
and flux of any detected X-ray burst. It is possible, however, that errors occur in Light curves and a
fake point is created with a detection significance value in the thousands of Sigma. Usually these fake
measurements are alone and could be removed from the programs end product. It is also possible for
errors to form in the shape of a X-ray burst, with signals who meet the criteria of an X-ray burst,
meaning for weak sources, and bursts, the user should still look to the plot of the Light curve itself if
there is an X-ray burst or not.
7
Conclusion
We have constructed and presented a catalogue of the 90 bright JEM-X have detected from 2003 to
2007. The catalogue is constructed from sources based off single pointing images. The catalogue shows
the sources in two different energy bands, soft (3-10 KeV) and hard (10-25 KeV). To be able to perform
this task, a program was created to draw out the needed info from JEM-X data files into catalogue
files containing the info required, namely Source ID, Position, Detection Significance, Energy bands
and error bars.
Several images from different pointing were combined into Mosaics and their properties analyzed.
Through these mosaics, HI diagrams were produced, showcasing the different properties of Compact
objects in different various systems, like High and Low mass X-ray binaries as well as in a singular
system. From these we can see various changes in their activity and hardness, changes tied to the
accretion disk surrounding the compact object.
A program to detect and report the activity of X-ray bursts from a Light curve given by a source
have been created. The program utilizes the changes in Signal to Noise ratio and reports the time,
Maximum flux and Signal to Noise of the burst.
