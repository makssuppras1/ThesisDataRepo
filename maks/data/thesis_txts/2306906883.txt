SUMMARY 
 
The design of a solvent-based separation process together with solvent selection is usually obtained through 
a trial and error approach (heuristics, mathematical programming, and/or hybrid). However, can the design 
be obtained without any trial and error? It is possible by first determining the process design for a desired 
(target) solvent and then finding a solvent that matches the desired solvent properties. This decomposition of 
the design problem is feasible because the process design is based on the properties of the system and does 
not need the identities of the chemicals involved. On the other hand, a unique solvent-azeotropic mixture 
behavior cannot be obtained for all homogeneous azeotropic mixtures. This means that the azeotropic 
mixtures need to be classified first and for each class, the optimal solvent behavior and the corresponding a 
priori process design determined. Also, can the solvent-based separation problem further improved in terms 
of energy consumption for the same separation specification? By using the diving-force concept (1), it is 
ensured that for the a priori design, the extraction and recovery columns operate at the maximum driving 
force, which translates into the design of a more sustainable energy efficient process. 
A systematic method is developed to design extractive distillation columns for homogeneous azeotropic 
mixtures. The method is presented by a step-by-step approach using a series of procedures and additional 
tools. The main features of the method are: classification of the solvent-azeotropic mixture behavior; finding 
solvents that match the design targets; and a database containing a priori process design based on driving 
force from which designs of other problems can be extrapolated or interpolated. For each class of azeotropic 
mixture in the database, design parameters correlations are available. Therefore, for a separation involving a 
known azeotrope, the optimal solvent and process design are obtained with minimal calculations. For an 
azeotropic mixture not available in the database, simply identifying the class of the azeotropic mixture, all 
other necessary details can be extrapolated or interpolated from the available data.  
The method is generic and able to directly determine the best solvent and the most optimal design parameters 
for the separation of homogeneous azeotropic mixtures available in the database. It allows safe 
extrapolations for azeotropic mixtures not covered in the database but that match the classified behavior. 
Until the final verification stage (rigorous simulation), the method hardly needs any calculations and is able 
to provide more sustainable and near optimal simultaneous selection of solvents and its corresponding 
optimal process design. The savings is not only in energy and material but also time to operate and solve 
problems.

1. MOTIVATION AND OBJECTIVES 
 
Azeotropic mixtures pose challenging problems owing to difficulties in separating them into pure products. 
These mixtures are present in many chemical processes. Due to the importance of separating them into pure 
products through energy efficient and environmentally acceptable techniques, new studies on sustainable 
separation process designs are of interest. 
Pressure-swing distillation is a typical example of a process that is able to separate azeotropic mixtures 
without adding a solvent to achieve the separation. An important condition for its operational feasibility is 
that the azeotrope must be pressure sensitive over a moderate change in pressure (2). However, if the 
azeotrope does not satisfy this condition or the process is energy intensive and/or environmentally unfriendly 
then a promising alternative is a solvent-based separation process (3).  
The most widely used technique for solvent-based separation of azeotropic mixtures is extractive distillation. 
This kind of distillation is more complex than conventional distillation operations as a minimum of two 
columns, distillation boundaries, and solvent selection need to be considered. Therefore, during the design 
stage, several parameters have to be considered. These parameters are the solvent selection, solvent flow 
rate, solvent feed location, column configurations, etc. 
The complexity of the process design for this kind of mixtures benefits the presence of problems in the 
design and operation stages. The process design together with solvent selection is usually obtained through a 
trial and error approach (heuristics, mathematical programming, and/or hybrid). However, can the design be 
obtained without any trial and error? And can the solvent-based separation problem further improved in 
terms of energy consumption for the same separation specification? 
This MSc thesis aims to develop a method where the design of extractive distillation processes for separation 
of homogeneous azeotropic mixtures for a fixed solvent-mixture behavior results to be the most optimal one, 
reducing the amount of solvent used and minimizing the energy consumption of the columns by operating at 
the maximum driving force. This type of design leads to more energy efficient processes because the largest 
driving force is used together with appropriate solvent amount. The method produces results that may be 
used as very good initial estimates for the rigorous-model-based simulations.  
Having an azeotropic mixture and a desired solvent behavior, the method is able to look for the 
corresponding optimal solvent that matches the process design. For this case, a simultaneous tailor-made 
solvent and process design could be very easily achieved. The conceptual basis for this method is that

solvent properties have a direct and unique influence on the separation process behavior, for example, 
separation feasibility is defined by the distillation boundary (corresponding to the ternary system of solvent 
and the azeotropic mixture); energy consumption is influenced by solvent capacity and solvent pure

If no solvent is matching the desired solvent behavior, the method can perform a process design for a given

The method will not only find the best solvent or the most optimal process design for an azeotropic mixture, 
it will ease the way of solving separation design problems and it will reduce the time to solve those 
problems.

2. THEORETICAL BACKGROUND 
 
2.1 
AZEOTROPIC MIXTURES 
 
Azeotropic mixtures are nonideal mixtures that present deviations from Raoult´s law, that is, the vapor or 
liquid composition of a mixture is not only a function of the system temperature and pressure but also the 
activity coefficient (𝛾𝑖). This nonideality of the mixture is measured by the activity coefficient of the mixture 
varies depending on temperature and composition (4).  
For ideal mixtures, the activity coefficient is equal to 1,  (𝛾𝑖= 1) . However, nonideal mixtures like 
azeotropes can present either positive  (𝛾𝑖> 1) or negative  (𝛾𝑖< 1) deviations from Raoult´s law. These 
deviations correspond respectively to minimum or maximum-boiling points at some composition that appear 
in temperature-composition (T-x-y) phase diagrams (4). In pressure-composition (P-x-y) phase diagrams, the 
minimum-boiling azeotropes are equivalent to maximum-pressure azeotrope and the maximum-boiling ones 
are equivalent to minimum-pressure azeotropes. 
 At this maxima or minima point, the vapor and liquid compositions are the same for azeotropic mixtures 
and, therefore, the relative volatility (𝛼𝐴𝐵) is equal to 1 and the separation by simple distillation is not 
possible (2). 
Partially or non-miscible mixtures tend to be nonideal mixtures, so they are more likely to form azeotropes. 
Mixtures with liquid phase immiscibility present more than one liquid phases, so azeotropes from these 
mixtures are called heterogeneous and azeotropes with only one liquid phase are called homogenous (4).  
The heterogeneous azeotropes can only exist as minimum-boiling point azeotropes because very positive 
deviations of Raoult´s law (𝛾𝑖≫1) are a requisite for liquid phase immiscibility (4). 
Taking into account everything mentioned above, the classification of the different types of azeotropes is as 
follows: 
 Minimum-boiling or maximum-pressure homogeneous azeotropes  
This kind of azeotrope mixtures is the most common one (4). These mixtures present positive deviations of 
Raoult´s law and one liquid phase. Examples of them are Ethanol/Water, Benzene/Cyclohexane, Acetic 
acid/n-N-Heptane and Methanol/Benzene. Figure 1 shows the Methanol/Benzene system.

AZEOTROPIC SEPARATION TECHNIQUES 
 
The separation into pure products of azeotropic mixtures is a challenging mission in petrochemical and 
chemical industries. They cannot be separated by simple distillation so most methods for distilling these 
mixtures include the addition of a third chemical or different column pressures that ease the separation (4).

based separation can be attractive

 Extractive distillation 
Extractive distillation is commonly used in the industry to separate azeotropic, close-boiling and low relative 
volatility mixtures. The separation is possible thanks to a miscible, higher boiling, liquid separating agent 
called, solvent (4). The selection of the solvent is a very important task when designing extractive distillation

affinities to incite an increase in relative volatility (𝛼𝐴𝐵) of the compounds so that the separation can be 
feasible and economical (2). 
Additional factors need to be considered in order to choose the right solvent: availability, cost, corrosivity, 
vapor pressure, thermal stability, heat of vaporization, reactivity, toxicity, infinite-dilution activity 
coefficient, ease of solvent recovery for recycle and the non-existence of azeotropes with the other 
compounds (2).

 Salt-effect distillation 
The salt-effect distillation is similar to the extractive distillation. The difference is that instead of using a

The salts are fed to the column as a solid or melt in the reflux, so the salt must be soluble on it. The salt is

volatility but also present several disadvantages like corrosion, increasing of foaming and crystallization in

relative volatility (4) (2).

2.3 
DISTILLATION COLUMNS DESIGN 
 
The design of distillation columns to separate azeotropic mixtures consists of several steps (6): 
 Mixture analysis 
A good starting point for the design of distillation columns is to analyze the azeotropic mixture, including 
pure components and mixture properties. This analysis will give information about what kind of separation is 
possible. 
 Possible separation techniques 
For azeotropic mixtures, the conventional distillation is not possible if two products are desired. Possible 
solutions to this problem are described in previous section 2.2. 
 Solvent identification  
The solvent to separate an azeotropic mixture can be found in a solvent database or with the help of a 
computer tool. Given a set of desired solvent properties, the computer tool is able to find the compounds 
with these properties.  
A more wide explanation of how to identify the best solvent to separate an azeotropic mixture is presented in 
section 2.3.1. 
 Flowsheet configuration 
Once the solvent is known, the distillation boundary is calculated from the ternary diagram. The flowsheet 
configuration depends on the position of the distillation boundary. It will give information about how many 
columns are needed and which compound is the distillate and which one leaves the column at the bottom. 
 Design parameters 
The design parameters are the reflux ratio, number of stages, feeding stage location, solvent flow rate and 
heat duties for reboiler and condenser. These design parameters are calculated to achieve already determined 
design targets that are recovery and purity of the products. The driving force method (1), explained in section 
2.3.3, is used for this calculation. 
The correct selection of the solvent also plays a very important role in the design because the better the 
solvent, the lower solvent flow rate and the lower the energy consumption necessary to achieve the desired 
separation.

This thesis is focused on extractive distillation of homogeneous azeotropes, so the design of extractive 
distillation columns is presented in this section. 
2.3.1 APPROACH TO SOLVENT SELECTION 
A very important step in the design of extractive distillation columns is identifying an effective solvent to 
perform the separation of the azeotropic mixture. 
A key criterion for the selection is the boiling point of the solvent. The solvents can be then classified as 
heavy (𝑇𝑏,𝑆> 𝑇𝑏,𝐵> 𝑇𝑏,𝐴 ) , intermediate (𝑇𝑏,𝐵> 𝑇𝑏,𝑆> 𝑇𝑏,𝐴 )  or light (𝑇𝑏,𝐵> 𝑇𝑏,𝐴> 𝑇𝑏,𝑆 ) . The most 
common solvents in the literature are the heavy solvents but they are not used in cases with heat-sensitivity 
compounds or when the main product to be separated is the major and the higher-boiling azeotropic 
compound (7). 
In simple distillation of non-azeotropic mixtures, the low-boiling compound is obtained as distillate and the 
high-boiling compound leaves the column at the bottom. However, the situation can be different for 
distillation processes with solvents like extractive distillation. Which compound is recovered as distillate or 
at the bottom depends on how the solvent interacts with the compounds of the azeotropic mixture. This is 
due to the fact that the solvent changes the activity coefficients and the volatility of the compounds (8). 
This means that for some azeotropic systems, the high-boiling compound can be obtained at the top and the 
low-boiling and the solvent at the bottom because the interaction force between the low-boiling compound 
and the solvent is larger than that of high-boiling compound and the solvent (9). An example of this kind of 
system is Ethanol/Benzene with Hexylene glycol as solvent.  
The solvent selection process is usually performed in several steps (4) (2). Figure 10 presents the different 
steps and represents the number of analyzed candidates per step: 
- 
Step 1: Selection of the functional groups that can be combined to generate a set of candidate 
solvents for the desired separation. Constraints: No toxic or expensive, geographically available (it 
can be a compound of the process), noncorrosive to the materials of the column. 
- 
Step 2: Analysis of the generated candidates based on the specified property and mixture constraints; 
No azeotrope formation with any compound of the azeotropic mixture, non-reactive with the 
compounds of the azeotropic mixture, low latent heat of vaporization, selectivity and capacity of the 
solvent. 
- 
Step 3: Detailed vapor-liquid equilibrium analysis for the most favorable candidates for selection of 
the best candidate using VLE, T-x-y phase diagrams and ternary plots from ICAS. Constraints: Easy 
separation from compound B in the recovery column, desired column configuration.

3. METHOD 
3.1 
CONCEPT 
 
This chapter presents a description of the design problem for extractive distillation columns to separate the 
components of binary homogeneous azeotropic mixtures. This method is based on the driving force concept 
and on the solvent-azeotropic mixture behavior.   
By using the driving force concept, it is ensured that for the process design, the extraction and recovery 
columns operate at the maximum driving force, which translates into the design of a more sustainable energy 
efficient process (1).  
The solvent-azeotropic mixture behavior is defined by the distillation boundary for a homogeneous 
azeotropic mixture. The solvent properties have a direct and unique influence on the separation process 
behavior. The solvent capacity and pure solvent properties influence the energy consumption of the process, 
the solvent selectivity and solvent environmental properties influence the waste generation and 
environmental impact and the separation feasibility is defined by the distillation boundary in the ternary plot. 
The distillation boundary, from the azeotrope (located on the binary vertex of the two compounds that 
constitute the azeotrope with compound 1 not removed by the solvent) to the compound 2-Solvent line, 
divides the ternary diagram into two distillation regions, above and below the distillation boundary (See 
Figure 14). The region below corresponds to the extractive column and can produce a distillate with nearly 
pure compound 1. The region above corresponds to the recovery column and can produce a distillate with 
nearly pure compound 2, while the bottom product is mainly the solvent (2).

3.2 
PROBLEM FORMULATION 
 
The starting point of the reverse design problem is a homogeneous azeotropic mixture with an azeotropic 
composition between 30-70%mol, desired driving force of the system, and compound 2 and solvent, and a 
desired distillation boundary. The method formulates and solves two sub-problems:  
 Sub-problem 1 
Given, a homogeneous azeotropic mixture and a priori process design; find the best (optimal) solvent that 
satisfies the process design in terms of solvent-mixture properties. 
 Sub-problem 2 
Given, a homogeneous azeotropic mixture and a solvent that does not match the a priori design; find the 
optimal energy efficient extractive distillation design from a priori determined design correlations. 
In order to solve the reverse design problem, a method is developed using computer-aided tools and a priori 
determined design correlations.

4. APPLICATION OF THE PROPOSED 
METHOD TO CASE STUDIES  
 
The method explained before is applied to three study cases. The first study case shows a case where the sub-
problem 1 is solved, the a priori design is given and the optimal solvent to match this design is found. The 
studied system is Methanol/Diisopropyl ether. The second study case solves the sub-problem 2, where the a 
priori design is given and optimal solvent to match this design is not found. Then, the method will determine 
the most optimal process design from the a priori determined design correlations. The system studied in the 
second case is Formic acid/1,2-Dichloroethane. The third case studies the extrapolation of the method for 
azeotropic mixtures not included in the database. The studied system is chosen to be Methanol/n-Heptane 
considering that another mixture form the same azeotropic group is in the database, Methanol/Hexane.

5. CONCLUSIONS AND FUTURE 
WORK 
5.1 
SUMMARY OF ACHIEVEMENTS 
The problem of design of extractive distillation processes for separation of homogeneous azeotropic mixtures 
has been studied in this MSc thesis with the objective of developing a systematic method for the solution of 
this problem. The following has been developed: 
- 
Classification of the solvent-azeotropic mixture behavior. 
- 
Database containing a priori process design from which designs of other similar problems can be 
generated. 
- 
A series of correlations for the a priori process design based on the driving force have been 
developed. 
- 
A workflow representing the method in a comprehensive step-by-step manner and specifying the 
procedures and tools used in each step are presented. 
- 
A series of procedures that helped to generate the data for the tools of the method are also given. 
The developed method is able to directly determine the best solvent for a given azeotropic mixture and a 
solvent behavior, and it is also able to find very good initial estimations for the process design in terms of 
solvent flow rate, driving force, number of stages and energy required for the separation of the classes of 
homogeneous azeotropic mixtures available in the database.  
The method is generic so allows safe extrapolations for azeotropic mixtures not covered in the database but 
that match the classified behavior. This is possible because the method is based on the properties of the 
system and does not need the identities of the chemicals. 
The method has been tested for a wide range of homogeneous azeotropes and the a priori design was 
generated with correlations for the solvent behavior and design parameters: solvent flow rate, driving force, 
number of stages, feeding stage location and reboiler duty of the columns. 
Three case studies were carried out, two of them solving the two proposed sub-problems and one of them to 
evaluate the performance of the method for azeotropic mixtures not available in the database. These case 
==PAGE:106==
Chapter 5. Conclusions and Future Work 
105 
 
studies proved the good performance of the method and that the design parameters estimated by the method 
are a very good initial point for rigorous simulation, very similar to the final design.  
Until the final verification stage (rigorous simulation), the method hardly needs any calculations and is able 
to provide more sustainable and near optimal simultaneous selection of solvents and its corresponding 
optimal process design due to the driving force concept. 
The savings is not only in energy and material but also time to operate and solve problems. 
 
5.2 
REMAINING CHALLENGES AND FUTURE WORK 
 
There are several remaining challenges and future work that can be done to achieve a more flexible and 
optimized systematic method. For that, the following future works were proposed: 
- 
Find new solvents for those mixtures in the database with more than 85%mol of solvent composition 
in the ternary plot. 
- 
Carry out rigorous simulation of the 10 systems remaining from Procedure 4. 
- 
Extension of the database to correlate the High-Low and High-High classes. 
- 
Extension of the database so the method could be used for other types of azeotropes or 
configurations. 
- 
Carry out a case study for a homogeneous azeotrope outside the composition range to study the 
extrapolation capacity of the method.
