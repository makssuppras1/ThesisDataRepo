Abstract
Floating oÔ¨Äshore wind turbines (FOWT) have the potential to make wind energy the ma-
jor global supplier of clean energy. As FOWT are approaching commercialization phase, the
widely known negative damping problem undermines their aeroservoelastic stability. The nega-
tive damping problem is more important around rated wind speed, where the thrust curve slope
with respect to wind speed is the largest. This thesis investigates the implementation of peak
shaving on FOWT as a method to reduce the thrust slope around rated wind speed and there-
fore reduce the pitch instability of the turbine. The controller designs most commonly suggested
to avoid negative damping are: (i) a classic onshore controller in which the pitch PI controller
frequency is reduced lower than the platform pitch frequency, also called de-tuned, and (ii) a
Ô¨āoating controller with an additional nacelle feedback loop in the pitch controller, a method
called parallel compensation. Peak shaving is applied to both these controllers and its eÔ¨Äect
on the turbine stability is assessed. Firstly, a review of key concepts regarding FOWT, wind
turbine control and aeroelastic stability is given, the controllers used in this investigation are
presented and the metric used to assess stability is deÔ¨Āned. Then, the frequencies of the IEA 15
MW reference wind turbine mounted on the UMaine semisubmersible are determined and tower
resonance problems are tackled. Next, the model is simulated with the ROSCO and the DTU
WEC controllers in HAWC2 and its performance is assessed. Finally, peak shaving is applied
to the de-tuned and to the nacelle feedback controller designs using ROSCO. According to the
deÔ¨Ānition of stability chosen, peak shaving does not improve the stability of the system. On the
one side, it reduces the loads and displacements in the fore-aft direction of the turbine; on the
other, the rotor speed tracking is poorer which increases the shaft torsion fatigue load.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This chapter introduces and contextualizes this research project. Firstly, a review of the
key aspects of Ô¨āoating oÔ¨Äshore wind turbines, wind turbine control system and aeroservoelastic
stability is presented. Next, the major challenge of Ô¨āoating oÔ¨Äshore wind turbines is presented,
which sets the ground for the motivation of this thesis.
1.1
Theoretical background
1.1.1
Floating oÔ¨Äshore wind turbines
In the current years, Ô¨āoating oÔ¨Äshore wind turbines (FOWT) are becoming an attractive
alternative to Ô¨Āxed-bottom turbines. One of the FOWT major advantages is that they can be
installed at water depths larger than 50 m, where Ô¨Āxed-bottom solutions become economically
unfeasible [1]. This makes it possible for FOWT to harness a very good wind resource, with high
mean winds and low turbulence intensity. Furthermore, other advantages include low environ-
mental and visual impacts, more space availability and less sensitivity to site-speciÔ¨Āc constraints
(such as water depth) [2].
One of the characterizing properties of Ô¨āoating platforms is static stability, which allows for
the FOWT to counteract the overturning moments due to external loads. The restoring force
(FRest) can be expressed by the sum of three contributions [3], as reported in Equation 1.1:
(i) the waterplane area contribution (FWP), that depends on the area of the Ô¨āoater that is in
contact with the water; (ii) the ballast contribution (FB), that is a function of the distance
between the center of gravity and the center of buoyancy of the platform; (iii) the mooring
system contribution (Fmoor). The Ô¨āoaters are classiÔ¨Āed depending on which of the three terms

1.1.1.2
Aerodynamic loads
The aerodynamic loads on a wind turbine are modelled by the Unsteady Blade Element
Momentum (BEM) theory. The Ô¨Ārst aerodynamic model for a wind turbine is attributed to Betz
[11], who described the rotor as a disc and formulated the aerodynamics with a 1-dimensional
momentum conservation theory. This model was further developed by Glauert [12], who extended
it correlating the aerodynamics of the rotor with its geometry and with the geometrical properties
of the blades, such as chord, twist and airfoil characteristics. Glauert‚Äôs model deÔ¨Āned the Blade
Element Momentum algorithm, which is able to compute the steady-state aerodynamic loads
and power for a wind turbine.
A realistic wind inÔ¨āow, besides the mean wind speed, is characterized by turbulence, wind
shear and the tower eÔ¨Äect; other engineering models have therefore been added to Glauert‚Äôs to
account for the unsteady, stochastic wind. The Unsteady BEM includes eÔ¨Äects such as dynamic
stall, tip and hub losses, dynamic inÔ¨āow and wake corrections to model the aerodynamics of the
turbine [13]. The reader is redirected to [14] for further details; the models used in this project
are presented in section 2.2.
The main trade-oÔ¨Äto energy production is the thrust load, the aerodynamic force that acts
perpendicularly to the rotor. The thrust has the steady-state characteristic curve displayed in
Figure 1.3: below rated wind speed, it increases with the square of the wind velocity; above rated
wind speed, the blades are pitched in order to limit the power production and the thrust load
decreases. As a consequence, the thrust has a peak at rated wind speed and its derivative with
respect to wind speed changes of sign from below- to above-rated wind speed. The importance
of this feature of the aerodynamic thrust is further discussed in the following section.

1.1.3
Stability of wind turbines
Aeroservoelasticity of wind turbines refers to the study of the interaction between the inertia
forces, elastic forces and aerodynamic forces to which the Ô¨āexible structure is subject to, and
the control system [27, 28]. All these elements are strongly coupled together. When the Ô¨āexible
structure is subject to aerodynamic, inertial and gravitational forces, it deforms and vibrates.
Its deformation and vibration velocity have an impact on the aerodynamic loads in turn. The
aerodynamics also determine the action from the control system, which, in order to achieve its
objectives, loads and moves the structure to modify the aerodynamics. In FOWT, the hydrody-
namics also play an important role in the motion of the system. From an aeroservoelastic point
of view, there are two common issues that need to be addressed in both design and operation
phases of a wind turbine: resonance and instability. If not addressed properly, both these issues
can lead to catastrophic failure of the wind turbine.
Resonance is a situation where the natural frequency (or eigenfrequency) of a mode of the
structure is close to or identical to an external forcing frequency [27]. The natural frequency
of a system is the frequency at which that system oscillates after an initial disturbance has
moved it from its equilibrium position (Figure 1.7) [29]. When resonance occurs in a mode of
a structure, the structure undergoes severe vibrations that lead to very high loads on the struc-
ture and eventually to its failure. Floating wind turbines are designed to avoid the structure‚Äôs
natural frequencies overlapping with environmental and operational frequencies. Environmental
frequencies are for instance wave frequencies, and the Ô¨āoating platform is usually designed with
frequencies that are outside the waves frequency range. An important operational frequency is
the rotor frequency indicated with 1P and its harmonics, that vary with wind speed; of particular
interest is the 3P frequency, or blade passing frequency, that might interact with the lowest tower

Instability, on the other hand, refers to a situation with negative damping. Damping is
the measure of how the oscillations of a system decay in time (Figure 1.7), i.e., how the system
dissipates the energy that the initial input has introduced [29]. If a mode has a negative damping,
when an external force excites the system it adds energy to the mode which start to oscillate with
increasing amplitude [27]. The mode is therefore unstable. If a mode is positively damped, when
an external force excites the system, the mode responds with bounded oscillations. However,
positively-low-damped modes have to be considered carefully, since a mode with little damping
that is forced would produce oscillation diÔ¨Écult to die out.
Damping is not easy to determine, and for a wind turbine is highly aÔ¨Äected by the aerody-
namic loads and, therefore, the operating conditions of the turbine. FOWT, in particular, are
characterized by the fore-aft motion instability when they are regulated with a classic onshore
controller; this is discussed further in section 1.2.
Stability and resonance are nevertheless connected: if the natural frequency of a highly-
damped mode is excited, the mode would respond with contained oscillations thanks to the
energy dissipated by the high damping; a low-damped mode subject to resonance would undergo
higher oscillation instead, that would hardly decay. In order to properly assess the stability of

1.2
Challenges of FOWT
The pitch control above-rated region can be critical for stability of turbines due to the fact
that the aerodynamics and the structural dynamics of the turbine may interact signiÔ¨Ācantly and
produce undesired eÔ¨Äects [20]. In particular, it is of interest the interaction between the thrust
and the tower motion of the turbine, especially for Ô¨āoating wind turbines.
During operation of the turbine, the tower oscillates backwards and forwards subjected to
external loads; this motion changes the relative wind velocity seen by the rotor so that, when
the tower is moving forwards, the relative wind velocity increases, while the opposite is true
when the tower moves backwards. Above rated wind speed, the pitch controller is regulating
the generator speed and reacts to the change in relative wind velocity due to tower motion; in
particular, when the tower moves forwards, the relative velocity increases and the pitch controller
pitches the blades to limit the generator speed while also decreasing the thrust (see Figure 1.3:
the slope of the thrust curve above rated wind speed is negative, meaning that for an increase
in wind speed, the thrust decreases). Because the thrust decreases, the aerodynamic damping
provided by the thrust also decreases, which enhances the forward tower motion; the opposite
happens when the tower moves backwards, and the pitch controller reacts to a decreased wind
speed causing the thrust to increase, which enhances the backwards tower motion. This eÔ¨Äect is
known as negative damping [10, 25], and can cause serious problems to the turbine stability if
not properly addressed.
The negative damping problem is a phenomenon that is present in both Ô¨Āxed-bottom and
Ô¨āoating wind turbines. For Ô¨Āxed-bottom turbines, the solution is decreasing the reaction time of
the pitch controller by setting its natural frequency lower than the Ô¨Ārst natural frequency of the
system (which is commonly the Ô¨Ārst tower fore-aft mode) [25]; tuned this way, the pitch controller
still has a satisfactory performance and the turbine is well regulated. If the same onshore pitch
controller is applied on a Ô¨āoating wind turbine, instabilities occur as observed in [10]: in a wind
ramp simulation, the FOWT was having severe instability issues after reaching rated wind speed,
for which the tower started to move back and forth with increasing displacement amplitude; the
rotor speed was also having big oscillations as well as the pitch angle, which the controller was

enhanced by the controller frequency interfering with platform modes, in particular, with the

Multiple solutions to the negative damping problem of the pitch mode have been suggested.
A common approach is to decrease the natural frequency of the pitch controller lower than the
platform‚Äôs pitch frequency [10, 26, 32]. Although it solves the problem and the pitch instability
is avoided, the pitch controller results to be very slow to react to changes in wind speed, causing
big variations in the rotational speed of the rotor. Fleming et al. [26] also studied the integration
of the nacelle velocity feedback in the controller, described in [25] as well, an approach called
"parallel compensation"; it succeeded in achieving stability and load reduction in the tower,
but also showed over-speed and large rotor speed variations.
Fischer [33] suggested feeding
back the nacelle velocity to the generator torque. Jonkman [32] and Larsen and Hanson [10]
also investigated active stall control as a solution to negative damping, but did not lead to a
satisfactory improvement. Individual blade pitch control for reducing pitch motions has been
investigated by Namik and Stol [34], which showed promising results.
Finally, Lackner and
Rotea [35] suggested a non-control solution where a tuned mass damper (TMD) is included in
the nacelle of the turbine; however, such solution would imply the addition of the TMD on the
nacelle, which could increase costs.
The control strategy also plays an important role in the negative damping instability. While
for onshore wind turbines it is preferred to regulate the generator speed above rated maintaining
constant power, such strategy results in high over-speeding of the rotor for FOWT. In case of
FOWT, it is more common to use the constant torque strategy, which seems to limit the variation
in rotor speed [23].

2.2
Time-domain aeroelastic simulations
The Horizontal Axis Wind turbine simulation Code 2nd generation (HAWC2) [42] is an
aeroelastic simulation tool used to compute the response of wind turbines in time-domain. It
is a commercially available tool developed by DTU Wind Energy, that is able to simulate very
complex systems and wind turbine designs thanks to its Ô¨āexible formulation. HAWC2 comes
in the form of an executable and is run specifying an input Ô¨Āle that contains all the necessary
information to model the turbine. It is composed by several modules that model the environment
in which the turbine is simulated, the loads applied on it, the structural dynamics and the control
system. Its full functionalities and the modelling options are described in [13, 42]; in the following
paragraphs only the models used in this thesis are outlined.
The environment in which the turbine is immersed in is described by the wind and water
Ô¨Āelds. The wind Ô¨Āeld is generated by the build-in Mann turbulence generator that models a
fully coherent 3D-turbulence Ô¨Āeld; wind shear is modelled with a power-law proÔ¨Āle, where the
shear exponent is speciÔ¨Āed; the tower shadow eÔ¨Äect is included and modelled as a potential Ô¨āow
where the shadow source is the tower. The water Ô¨Āeld is modelled specifying the water density,
mean water level, mud level and the water kinematics. Waves are computed through the use of
a Dynamic Link Library (DLL), which reads a pre-written input Ô¨Āle with the waves speciÔ¨Ācs.
The waves speciÔ¨Ācs are given by metocean data (subsection 2.2.2).
The aerodynamic model of HAWC2 is based on the Blade Element Momentum (BEM)
theory, enhanced with other models to include additional eÔ¨Äects. Among others: unsteady aero-
dynamics are included through the dynamic inÔ¨āow model; dynamic stall is modelled with a
modiÔ¨Āed Beddoes-Leishmann model [43] which includes the Theodorsen Theory; Prandtl‚Äôs tip-
loss correction [12] is included to account for a Ô¨Ānite number of blades; large blade deÔ¨āections are
taken into consideration since they inÔ¨āuence the eÔ¨Äective rotor area ultimately aÔ¨Äecting aerody-
namic forces on the rotor; shear eÔ¨Äect and skew inÔ¨āow are modelled to account for misaligned
inÔ¨āow wind.
The turbine structural part is based on a multibody formulation: the turbine is divided
into main bodies with a speciÔ¨Āc coordinate system and composed of linear Timoshenko beam
elements. The main bodies are connected to each other by constraints that impose a limitation
on the relative motion of the bodies. The beam elements assume small deÔ¨āections, therefore the
blades, that might undergo large deÔ¨āections and rotations, are modelled as a set of sub-bodies
that can have non-linear deformations between them. The connection between the turbine and
the support substructure (Ô¨āoater and mooring system) happens thanks to an external DLL; the
Ô¨āoater is constrained to the tower bottom and the mooring system is constrained to the Ô¨āoater.

Aeroservoelastic stabiliy
The FOWT model used in this investigation has been designed so that the natural frequen-
cies of its components do not interact with known external conditions, for instance the waves
frequency range and the rotor rotational frequency. However, the HAWC2 model of the IEA-
15-240-RWT is fairly new and, in order to verify the correct implementation of the model, and
to ensure that the design does not have any relevant resonance issues, the frequencies of the
system are identiÔ¨Āed. Two approaches are used to identify the turbine‚Äôs frequencies: the Ô¨Ārst
is a frequency-domain approach, for which the aeroelastic stability tool HAWCStab2 is used;
the second method is via time-domain simulations with HAWC2, in which an exciting force is
applied to the turbine bodies of interest and the time-series response is analysed. The methods
are presented in subsection 2.4.1.
Furthermore, in order to assess the aeroservoelastic stability of the IEA-15-240-RWT taking
into account the dynamic response of the turbine to realistic inÔ¨āow conditions regulated by a
control system, a stability metric inspired by the Lyapunov stability is deÔ¨Āned.
The metric
depends on the identiÔ¨Āed characteristic states of the above-rated control loop and assesses the
boundedness of the states. The stability of the system is not proven analytically, instead, the
metric captures a global aeroservoelastic performance of the system, and in this sense, it classiÔ¨Āes
the aeroservoelastic stability of the system regulated by diÔ¨Äerent controller designs. In this way,
the controller designs can be evaluated in terms of which one gives states more bounded. Details
on the metric are given in subsection 2.4.2.

2.4.1
Determination of system frequencies
2.4.1.1
Frequency-domain identiÔ¨Ācation
HAWCStab2 is a frequency-domain aeroelastic simulation tool developed by DTU Wind
Energy that is able to compute aeroservoelastic stability of a wind turbine; it is based on the
earlier version HAWCStab [55], with the modiÔ¨Ācation that HAWCStab2 uses a co-rotational ap-
proach and sets up the eigenvalue problem for a deÔ¨āected state. The aerodynamics are modelled
via a Blade Element Momentum algorithm coupled with unsteady aerodynamics based on the
Leishman-Beddoes model. The inÔ¨āow is assumed uniform, and the blades identical, therefore,
the Coleman transformation is used to linearize the equations of motion around the equilibrium
point, that are needed to set up the eigenvalue problem. The eigenvalue problem is then solved
and frequencies, damping ratios and mode shapes are determined for the entire wind turbine
structure.
In the latest version of HAWCStab2, the tool is developed to handle oÔ¨Äshore Ô¨āoating struc-
tures using super elements, that are calculated by HAWC2 [56]. A super element is a reduced-
order model of a set of elements speciÔ¨Āed by the user, that includes the non linear dynamics,
substrate structure interaction and hydrodynamic interaction of the set of elements. For this
investigation, HAWC2 is used to generate a super element that includes the Ô¨āoater and the
mooring system.
The superelement is then included in HAWCStab2 and constrained to the
bottom of the tower. Modal analysis can therefore be performed on the turbine including the
Ô¨āoating substructure.
HAWCStab2 can include the controller equations and perform aeroservoelastic modal anal-
ysis. However, as of today the controller equations in HAWCStab2 do not include the nacelle
feedback loop for FOWT, and the aeroservoelastic stability of FOWT is limited to an onshore
controller design. Therefore, only aeroelastic modal analysis is performed.
When aerodynamic forces and the controller dynamics are considered in the determination
of the turbine‚Äôs modes, the frequencies and damping ratios become a function of wind speed.
The representation of the eigenfrequencies as a function of wind speed is called the Campbell
diagram. Campbell diagrams are computed with HAWCStab2 in this thesis.
2.4.1.2
Time-domain identiÔ¨Ācation
Time-domain identiÔ¨Ācation of system‚Äôs frequencies relies on exciting a DOF of the system at
one particular point on the structure with a force. The force can have characteristic magnitude
and frequency content, depending on the eigenfrequency of the mode to excite. Two types of
forces are used: single-step and doublet.
A single-step force is a force that is active for a certain amount of time on the body and
35
==PAGE:54==

then it is turned oÔ¨Ä, leaving the body free to oscillate with its natural frequency. It is suited for
modes with very low eigenfrequencies, as for example the ones of the Ô¨āoater.
The doublet is a double-step force and it is suited for modes with eigenfrequencies that are
in the range of turbine tower frequencies.
Single-step force: freedecays
In order to calculate the natural frequencies of the Ô¨āoater, a single-step force is applied on the
the 6 Ô¨āoater‚Äôs DOFs: surge, sway, heave, roll, pitch, yaw. Single-step forces are good for exciting
modes with low natural frequencies in time-domain [57]. The eigenfrequency identiÔ¨Ācation is then
performed in frequency-domain, from the Power Spectral Density (PSD) of the displacement of

Stability metric
The proposed stability metric aims at representing how much the solutions of the system
are distanced from their equilibrium states. It is inspired by the Lyapunov deÔ¨Ānition of stability
and the Lyapunov function, brieÔ¨āy presented in subsection 1.1.3. Lyapunov deÔ¨Ānes a system
stable if the states of the system start close to their equilibrium point and remain close to their
equilibrium point forever [31]. The ‚Äúcloseness‚ÄĚ of the solutions of the system to their equilibrium
is represented by a ball whose boundaries deÔ¨Āne the bounds of the system‚Äôs solutions. This idea
is expanded into: if the boundary of the solutions of a Ô¨Ārst system is smaller than the boundary
of the solutions of a second system, then we can say that the Ô¨Ārst system is more stable than the
second one - i.e., its solutions are contained in a smaller ball. For wind turbines, for instance,
this can be thought of in terms of rotor speed: if a controller design gives a smaller convergence
radius of the rotor speed than a second controller design, then the Ô¨Ārst one produces a system
whose rotor speed is more bounded. The concept can be extended to many states, Ô¨Ānally deÔ¨Āning
a holistic metric that characterizes the boundaries of the solution of the wind turbine through
the main states governing the system.

model of the wind turbine includes the dynamics of the drivetrain, the pitch actuator dynamics

In a wind turbine controller, the anti-windup is a strategy implemented to enhance the
responsiveness of the integral term of the pitch controller when the turbulent wind increases from
below to above rated. If the turbulent wind drops below rated for several seconds and the pitch
angle is kept at optimal value, the rotor speed error would be negative for some time, therefore,
also the integral on the error would be negative. When the rotor speed increases to rated, the
proportional term would become 0 or positive almost instantaneously but, without anti-windup,
it would take a long time for the integral term to become positive again, and contribute to the
pitch angle. In order to prevent this, the anti-windup scheme of the DTU WEC sets the integral
term at each time step equal to the reference pitch minus the proportional term at that time
step, so that the integral term of the pitch controller for the next time step is calculated as [54]:

Conclusion
A damping force is calibrated to carry out simulations in HAWC2 with the IEA-15-240-RWT
mounted on the UMaine semisub, so that the initial transient motions are removed within the

Since the frequencies determined via freedecay tests in HAWC2 correspond to those reported
in the UMaine platform technical report [6], the model is implemented correctly in HAWC2.
HAWCStab2 and the super element implementation show not to accurately compute the dy-
namics of the system: the frequencies of the Ô¨āoater are not in agreement with the ones reported
in the UMaine platform technical report [6] and the frequencies of the tower are not in agree-
ment with the ones determined via doublet force. The investigation of the tower frequencies via
doublet force highlights resonance issues in the tower designed in the UMaine platform technical
report [6] with the 3P rotor rotational frequency. To remove resonance, the tower is stiÔ¨Äened
by increasing the Young‚Äôs modulus in the structural deÔ¨Ānition, and the lowest frequencies are
shifted towards higher values.
The tuning procedure of the DTU WEC and of the ROSCO shows a discrepancy in the
pitch angles calculated for the CP -maximizing minimum pitch saturation at low wind speeds
because the BEM code used by the tuning procedure of ROSCO to compute the CP surface does
not include torsion deformation of the blades. Furthermore, the tuning procedure from ROSCO
computes the gain scheduling for the pitch PI controller by the gradient of the CP surface, and
results in more aggressive gains that turn positive for high pitch angles, which would result in a
control action opposite than expected.
The thrust computed by ROSCO with peak shaving implemented does not achieve the
set reduction in peak shaving percentage partially because the BEM code used by ROSCO to
generate the CT surface does not include torsion.

Chapter 4. Conclusion
The estimated wind speed of ROSCO used to select the minimum pitch angle in the pitch
saturation routine cannot capture properly the rotor averaged wind speed at low winds. As a
consequence, the power production decreases and the thrust force increases.
The Ô¨Ālters used in the controller, such as the rotor speed Ô¨Ālter, have a high impact on the
responsiveness of the controllers. ROSCO implements a low-pass Ô¨Ālter on the rotor speed with
cut-oÔ¨Äfrequency lower than DTU WEC. As a consequence, the ROSCO‚Äôs rotor speed tracking
is slightly worsen, and the DTU WEC generator torque and pitch signals have a higher variation
that ultimately weights on the shaft torsion and pitch bearing fatigue.
When the pitch angle is not close to optimal pitch and the wind speed decreases, the anti-
windup scheme of DTU WEC gives an oÔ¨Äset in the pitch angle that delays the saturation of the
pitch. This causes deceleration of the rotor and high variation of the generator torque, which
reÔ¨āect in increased shaft torsion fatigue.
The Lyapunov factor is a holistic measure able to capture the main dynamics of the system
by assessing the boundedness of the solutions of the selected states. It is up to the designer
to eventually chose what are the states that are most critical in the turbine design, and weight
them accordingly. However, the type of assessment that has been done with the deÔ¨Ānition given
in this thesis is only qualitative, as the P matrix used to deÔ¨Āne LF has been selected based
on simulation results. A more robust approach is needed and, therefore, a linearized model of
a Ô¨āoating wind turbine is necessary. The linearized model can serve as a basis to construct
the Lyapunov function on more solid grounds and can then be used to assess stability in time
domain, appropriately deÔ¨Āning the system states.
According to the stability metric deÔ¨Āned in this thesis, peak shaving applied on the nacelle
feedback and on the de-tuned controllers for the Ô¨āoating IEA-15-240-RWT does not help enhance
the stability of the model. The beneÔ¨Āts form the reduction in fore-aft motion are counteracted
by an increase of rotor speed variation. The former results in lower fatigue of the tower-base
fore-aft and lower oscillations in the platform pitch and surge, the latter in an increased fatigue
of the shaft torsion. In addition, the sub-optimal operation implies a power production loss that
aÔ¨Äects the AEP in turn.
No signiÔ¨Ācant improvement is registered at rated wind speed from the parallel compensation
term in the pitch controller. The tuning used for the de-tuned controller and for the nacelle
feedback controller produce similar Lyapunov factor: the enhanced rotor speed tracking with the
nacelle feedback controller is balanced by the reduced pitch angle and platform pitch oscillations
from the de-tuned controller.
Overall, one important conclusion of this work is that Ô¨āoating wind turbines have to be
designed in a holistic manner, in particular taking into account the control system as a design
parameter. The tower design can be inÔ¨āuenced to high extent by the controller, in terms of the

the controller design.
Future work
HAWCStab2 could be enhanced to include gravity and the nacelle feedback controller. In-
cluding gravity could increase HAWCStab2‚Äôs accuracy in calculating the frequencies of the sys-
tem. Including the nacelle feedback controller would allow to exploit HAWCStab2‚Äôs ability to
compute aeroservoelastic stability for FOWT with a FOWT controller. The generation of the
super element should be further veriÔ¨Āed, in particular, the computation of the mooring system
dynamics, since the yaw mode has not been accurately determined.
To the author‚Äôs knowledge, a tower re-design for the UMaine support structure has recently
been provided on the GitHub repository of the IEA-15-240-RWT. The tower re-design has higher
mass than the original one, therefore the frequencies should have increased. Further veriÔ¨Ācation
is however needed. The platform integrity should be veriÔ¨Āed and the hydrodynamic Ô¨Āles from
Wamit should be adjusted to the new design.
To formulate a more robust deÔ¨Ānition of the Lyapunov stability to be used in time-domain
simulations to assess FOWT stability, a linearized model of a Ô¨āoating wind turbine is recom-
mended.
The linearized model of a FOWT can be used in HAWCStab2 as well to perform
eigenvalue analysis and aeroservoelastic stability, without the need to generate and include a
super element.
Further investigation on the wind speed estimator of ROSCO is recommended. The im-
plementation of a wind speed estimator in DTU WEC is as well recommended, since nacelle
anemometers are not always available and might result in higher measurement uncertainties af-
fecting the turbine performance. Further investigation can be made in order to assess if there
is an optimal cut-oÔ¨Äfrequency for the low-pass Ô¨Ālter on the rotor speed that results in a good
rotor speed tracking while minimizing the loads on components.
According to the comparison between the results from the de-tuned controller and the nacelle
feedback controller, it would be interesting to investigate the possibility of gain scheduling for the
nacelle feedback proportional gain. It might help reduce responsiveness of the controller around
rated wind speed, while increasing the controller action at higher winds.
A careful assessment on materials, components and maintenance costs that are saved versus
AEP production loss should be done in order to appropriately assess whether peak shaving is a
valuable strategy.
Finally, it would be interesting to test peak shaving on other controller designs and on other
platform models, to see if the conclusions of this thesis can be extended to more controller designs
and FOWT models.
