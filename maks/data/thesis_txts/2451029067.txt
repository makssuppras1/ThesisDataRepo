Abstract
Acoustical characterization of porous type absorbers has for many years been given through a
normal incidence surface impedance or Sabine’s absorption coeﬃcient. Both measured quantities
can however be diﬃcult to relate to the actual absorbing properties of a mounted absorber. As an
alternative the non-acoustical parameter the ﬂow resistivity is proposed as a characterization param-
eter. Through the ﬂow resistivity the characteristic impedance, surface impedance and ultimately the
random incidence absorption coeﬃcient may be calculated. In this study an investigation and com-
parison of the prediction accuracy and related uncertainties of an impedance tube conversion method
and 3 diﬀerent Sabine absorption coeﬃcient methods used to inversely estimate the ﬂow resistivity
of porous type absorbers is presented. The analysis of the uncertainty of the estimated parameters
are conducted within a Bayesian framework. The presented work is based on measurements on 5
diﬀerent Ecophon® industry standard porous type absorbers. All methods are tested based on either
Delany & Bazley’s, Miki’s, Komatsu’s or Allard & Champoux’s model. The ﬁndings show that the
proposed impedance tube conversion method yields results within the inter-sample deviation with a
standard deviation of less than 10% in the majority of tested cases for a characteristic impedance
calculated using either Miki’s or Allard & Champoux’ model. The Sabine absorption conversion
methods showed mixed results, however for a absorber mounted with small or none backing air cav-
ity, the predictions were very close to or within the inter-sample deviation for the majority of tested
cases. Meaningful estimation for other mounting conditions was also found and the discrepancies
werediscussed to be related to the violation of Thomasson’s size correction.

Introduction
Since the end of the 19th century scientists around the world have been studying the behaviour of sound
in rooms and how to control it. Today, with a very active modern society with a still growing population
together with an increasing use of machines and devices, the need of noise control is more important
than ever.
Noise control in rooms have until just recently exclusively been through the use of diﬀerent sound
absorbing elements. The most common absorber type used in room acoustics is the porous type ab-
sorbers such as mineral wool elements. To choose the optimal absorber and mounting condition for a
given acoustical problem is a highly complicated matter. The alterations of the sound ﬁeld caused by
the installation of an absorber is a combination of both the distribution of sound energy incidence on
the absorber, the propagation of the sound inside the absorber and the mounting condition. There exist
a wide range of diﬀerent products which acoustically behaves very diﬀerently, however a very general
problem has always been how to acoustically characterize these products. The industry standard for
acoustical characterization has for many years been through the use of either impedance tube measure-
ments according to ISO 10534-2 or reverberation chamber measurements according to ISO 354. Both
of these methods rely on various assumptions making the measured quantities hard to relate to the
actual physical installed absorber, or even to the fundamentals of acoustics. The main reasons that an
acoustical characterisation of a porous type absorber is diﬃcult is simply related to the fact that the
sound propagation inside an absorber is physically very complicated and involves a vast amount of non-
acoustic parameters. For an acoustic characterization to be practically useful it needs to be very simple
such as through a single parameter like the Sabine absorption coeﬃcient. A meaningful alternative to
the classical acoustical characterization could be given through the non-acoustical parameter, the ﬂow
resistivity. The ﬂow resistivity is a very important parameter as it is used as input parameter to the well
known and commonly used empirical based equivalent ﬂuid models such as Delany & Bazley’s model.
Knowing the ﬂow resistivity the characteristic impedance of a given absorber can be calculated by the
empirical models and by knowing the mounting condition the surface impedance can be calculated.
From the surface impedance the absorbing properties can directly be calculated and as opposed to the
Sabine absorption coeﬃcient without loosing the phase information.
In this project various methods of converting the conventional impedance tube measurements or
measured Sabine absorption coeﬃcients into ﬂow resistivity has been analyzed and compared. The main
idea behind the conversion methods, is to make a model which as accurately as possible represents the
measured quantities while being a function of the ﬂow resistivity. The models includes the fundamental
theory which the measured quantities are based on together with the known violations of the assump-
tions. By formulating a cost function as the diﬀerence between the measured quantities and the model
output, a best ﬁtting ﬂow resistivity value can be found by minimizing the cost function. An extended
comparison between the conversion models of the inherent uncertainties has been conducted utilizing a
Bayesian framework. The main research question in this project is which conversion methods yield the
most accurate and certain estimates of the ﬂow resistivity and to understand under which conditions
the diﬀerent methods can be used.

A note on extended and local reaction
In many cases of sound propagation in absorbers, the speed of the transmitted wave will be much smaller
than in air. In such a case the transmitted angle will be close to zero for most incidence angle. The
calculation may be simpliﬁed by assuming that all incidence angles results in a transmitted angle of
zero. If this approximation is used on a given impedance layer, the layer is said to be locally reacting.
The opposite case where all angles are used is called extended reaction. Extended reaction gives a more
physical correct description of the system and it has been shown that for a porous absorber backed by
a air-cavity, extended reaction yields better agreement with measurements than local reaction [7][14].

2.3
Sound propagation in a porous media
The propagation of sound in a porous media is a very complex subject which have been investigated
heavily for many years. The complexity of the problem will be dependent on how detailed a description
of the absorber is needed. The simplest models are the empirical equivalent ﬂuid models, which models
the absorber as a homogeneous and isotropic equivalent ﬂuid. These models are based on regression of
measured data of a large range specimens and does often only depend on a single physical variable. Since
these models describes the average behavior of a range absorbers within a limited frequency range, these
models will often show noticeable deviations from measurements. However, due to their simplicity they
are often used to give a reasonable order of magnitude of the characteristic impedances. Other more
elaborate models will phenomenologically try to describe the propagation inside the material based on
the interaction between the air and material through viscous and thermal dissipation. These models
are based on an averaged description of the geometry of the pores inside the absorber and often on
an assumption of a rigid frame. A rigid frame means that the solid part of the material will not be
excited by the propagating sound. This assumptions is valid if the frequency of interest is much higher
than the so called phase decoupling frequency [1], which refers to the frequency where the forces in the
frame are comparable in magnitude to the forces in liquid phase (the air). The most complex and often
most precise models are based on Biot’s theory which includes the elastic behavior of both the frame
and the material it self through a diphasic model [3]. A general problem regarding the models is that
the more complex the model gets, the more physical input parameters are needed. It can often can be
hard or impossible to measure these input parameters, which have led to many simpliﬁed versions of the
diﬀerent models using various assumptions. In this project, only the single variable empirical models
and the simpliﬁed Allard and Champoux model [2] have been used due to their simplicity.

Due to the complexity of the structure of a porous absorber it may be diﬃcult to fully characterize.

In probability theory marginalization is a very important tool which is used to integrate or sum

the measurements as closely as possible, including as many of the known violations of the assumptions

4.1
Deﬁnition of errors
A very fundamental aspect of an optimization tasks is the deﬁnition of errors. As mentioned in section 4
the errors can either be deﬁned using the L1 −norm or the L2 −norm as shown in equation 102 and 103.
It is obvious that the two deﬁnition can lead to diﬀerent results of the optimization. If the L1 −norm
is applied, all magnitudes of errors will be weighted equally, where the L2 −norm deﬁnition will weight
large errors much higher due to the squaring of the diﬀerence. In [15] and [16] the cost function was
based on the use of the L1 −norm. In this project a main focus will be on the L2 −norm deﬁnition to
comply with the inherent deﬁnition of the Gaussian distribution and its standard deviation used within
the Bayesian framework. A complete overview will however also be given in the appendix based on the
L1 −norm.

5.3.1
Bayesian inference
In the following ﬁgure 20, the ﬂow resistivity calculated from the measured values in table 2 is plotted
together with the MAP and the standard deviation of the posterior distribution. The posterior distri-
bution has been formulated using equation 99 and evaluated at 1000 values linearly spaced in the range
σopt/4 to 2σopt, where σopt is the optimized ﬂow resisitivty. The expected range of the ﬂow resistivity
used for assigning the prior distribution was set to 1000 to 300000 N s m−4. The prior distribution of the
standard deviation has been assigned a Jeﬀrey’s prior with a possible range of values of STD(e(σopt))/2
to STD(e(σopt)) · 2 where STD(e(σopt)) is the standard deviation of the diﬀerence between the mea-
surements and the model ﬁt using the optimized ﬂow resistivity. This choice of range assumes that the
vast majority of possible values of the unknown standard deviation will be around the actual observed
standard deviation. During the implementation of the model, various range was tested showing that a
larger range would not change anything noticeable.

Discussion
In the following section the results presented in section 5 will be discussed and suggestions for future
work will be addressed.
6.1
Impedance tube conversion
The implementation of the impedance tube conversion method has shown very promising estimations of
the ﬂow resistivity. For estimations based on either Miki’s or Allard & Champoux’ model it was shown
in ﬁgure 20 that it was possible to achieve values of the ﬂow resistivity within the inter-sample deviation
for the vast majority of absorbers under test. By comparing ﬁgure 70 and 73 in appendix C3 it can be
observed how the model using a cost function using the L2 −norm clearly yields estimations closer to
the measured values compared to the L1 −norm. In ﬁgure 70 it can also be observed how the methods
performs comparable to the Matelys® RokCell® method. The cost functions presented in ﬁgure 17
indicates that no cases of non-uniqueness was observed, i.e only a global minimum was observable for
both the real and the imaginary parts. The initial value used as starting guess for the fminsearch
was examined during implementation indicating that the method was very robust to guesses far from
the actual best estimate, which also indicates a clear global minimum. An eﬀect of the cost function
being a sum of a real and imaginary part can also be observed in ﬁgure 17. A shift of the position of the
optimized ﬂow resistivity from the minimum of the imaginary part to the minimum of the real part for
increasing ﬂow resistivity values. This eﬀect can be explained by observing the magnitude of the surface
impedance in ﬁgure 42. For low frequencies the magnitude of the imaginary part is much larger than
the real part, meaning that the minimization of the cost function will favor a better ﬁt for the imaginary
part. By comparing the cost function and the model ﬁt for e.g. Industry Modus S and Focus Ds in
ﬁgure 17 and 18 it may be seen how the alignment of the minimum of the cost function for the real and
the imaginary parts is directly related to model ﬁt. The miss alignment of the minima indicates that
the model cannot give a perfect ﬁt for the measured data, as opposed to the simulated case presented
in ﬁgure 12 and 12. It is of course expected to observe discrepancies between the model and the data
since the method is based on empirical models which are formulated to describe the average behaviour
of absorbers. A discrepancy between the model and the data which can be observed for all absorbers is
the dip in both the real and the imaginary part of the measured surface impedance at between 700 to
2000 Hz. This dip may be the result of the elastic frame of the material, which none of the empirical
models have included. During the measurements of the surface impedance at Ecophon® a model ﬁt
based on various elastic frame models was examined through a Matelys® program called RokCell®. It
was noted that through variations of variables such as the bulk modulus of the material, a ﬁt which
successfully included the dip was indeed possible. It is interesting to observe how the minima of the
real and imaginary part of the cost function for e.g Master Rigid E 1 is highly separated, resulting in
a model ﬁt which shows signiﬁcant discrepancies compared to the measured data, while the estimate of
the ﬂow resistivity as presented in ﬁgure 20 still are very close to the measured value. In ﬁgure 20 and
21 and in table 3 the relative STD of the posterior distribution for the method based on the diﬀerent
model can be noted as being below 10% in all cases. It may also be noted that both Miki and Allard
& Champoux models performs very similar regarding uncertainties. The observable robustness of the
method could be explained by the simplicity of both the measurement and the model.
Beside from the already noted discrepancies caused by basing the method on empirical models
which does not include the eﬀect of a elastic frame, some other error sources may be predicted. By this
method it is assumed that the quantity measured using the impedance tube and the theoretical surface
impedance are equal, however as was mentioned in section 2.4.1 many of the fundamental assumptions
associated with the impedance tube measurement can in practice be very hard to fulﬁll.
In [9] a
comparison of the performance of impedance tube measurements on the same samples in individual
laboratories was presented. The study highlights a high (10 - 20%) inter-laboratory dispersion in the

6.2
Sabine absorption coeﬃcient conversion
The implementation and comparison of the Sabine absorption coeﬃcient conversion methods based on
various empirical models has shown widespread results. The cost functions presented in ﬁgure 22 to 24
or in appendix D1 shows how a global minimum exists within the range of interest in the majority of
the cases. However in a few cases as for αsize based on Miki’s model used on absorber 1 or 2 a case
of non-uniqueness may be observed. This eﬀect may be related to the circles of constant absorption
as illustrated in ﬁgure 2. The absolute value of the denominator in equation 47 leads to an inﬁnite
pairs of real and imaginary parts of the surface impedance which gives the same absorption coeﬃcient.
However since the real and imaginary parts of the surface impedance are generated in pairs by the given
empirical model as a function of frequency and ﬂow resistivity, the non-uniqueness will seldom happen.
Another eﬀect which could lead to multiple minima could be the negative real part generated by the
Delany & Bazley model below the valid frequency range. It is important to be aware of the possibility
of a non uniqueness in the cost function, when a initial guess is given to the fminsearch algorithm.
The robustness of the method in terms of ﬁnding the best ﬁtting minimum for a given initial value will
be dependent on the measurement and the choice of model. It may not be possible to know beforehand
if a non-uniqueness may exists, however judged from the cost functions in ﬁgure 22 to 24, the possibility
of hitting the wrong minimum seams very small if a moderate initial guess is given. The ﬁts presented
in ﬁgure 25 shows how a very reasonable ﬁt was achieved for all absorbers and mounting conditions.
The majority of the discrepancies between the measured absorption coeﬃcient and the model ﬁt can
be observed for the mounting conditions with larger backing air cavities. Observing ﬁgure 26 to 29 it
may be noted how all methods and model combinations yields results either within of very close to the
inter-sample deviation for all absorbers having a small or no backing air cavity. A re plot of ﬁgure 94
where only the smallest backing cavities are shown beneath. It may be observed how all models have

A reason for the increased discrepancy at mounting conditions with backing air cavities may be
related to the formulation of αsize. Thomasson’s size correction used to formulate αsize assumes a ﬂush
mounted absorber or a plane region, which is locally reacting. Both of these assumptions will be increas-
ingly violated for increasing depths of the total system, i.e thickness of the absorber plus the backing air
cavity. By applying the theory outside the assumptions, the radiation impedance will no longer match
the actual physical phenomena measured. The optimization will compensate for the wrongly calculated
radiation impedance through the surface impedance by either over or under estimating the ﬂow resis-
tivity. This eﬀect is very noticeable when comparing the model ﬁts and estimated ﬂow resistivity of
αsize and αsize&room. In the majority of the cases αsize performs better in terms of estimations accuracy
compared to αsize&room even though the only diﬀerence between the models is that αsize&room has a free
scale factor included as a optimization parameter. The free scale parameter will obviously only make the
model ﬁt as good as αsize or better, which also can be observed for e.g absorber 3 in ﬁgure 25, however
the accuracy of the estimated ﬂow resistivy gets decreased. This clearly indicates how the model does
not match the measurements accurately. A reason why all methods performs very comparable in terms
of estimation accuracy if only the smallest backing cavities are considered, may be related to the eﬀects
that αsize&room and αsize&diff are formulated to express. Since both room eﬀect compensations factor
are included to model the violations of the diﬀuse sound ﬁeld assumption both factors are expected to
be of increasing importance as the backing cavity depths increases. When the size of the total system
increases, the inﬂuence on the diﬀuseness of the sound ﬁeld may also increase leading to a increased
need of a compensation factor.
Other sources of errors could be related to the assumption of plane waves used both in Thomasson’s
size correction and in the calculation of the surface impedance. It could also be argued that the frequency
dependency included in αsize&diff by the second term in equation 110 is based on a average behaviour
which therefor cannot be expected to perfectly match the measured conditions.

6.3
Future work
To improve the impedance conversion method the mentioned error sources needs to be taken into account
when the model is formulated. One general observation is the dip in the measured surface impedance
caused by the elastic frame of the material, which the current model does not include. By replacing
the equivalent ﬂuid models with more advanced elastic frame models such as the Biot’s model this dip
may be included in the model and thereby a better estimation may be given. However the Biot’s model
requires many more parameters which either needs to assumed or also estimated through a optimization
procedure, which ultimately may cause a decrease in accuracy or an increase in uncertainty.
The errors caused by the imperfections of the cutting and mounting of the specimen may possibly be
included in a similar fashion as the frequency dependency of the room factor was included in αsize&diff.
By examining round robin tests a average behaviour could possibly be extracted and included in the
model, however it may also very likely be impossible, since these eﬀects could be random to a extend
where no useful information could be retrieved.
Regarding the Sabine absorption coeﬃcient conversion methods, the most critical feature which
needs to be addressed in future work is on the calculation of an eﬀective radiation impedance for non-
ﬂush mounted absorbers. An FEM approach could possibly be used to calculate an eﬀective radiation
impedance which could substitute Thomasson’s size correction for larger backing air cavities. An ad-
ditional correction factor could also be examined by observing the discrepancies between the estimated
ﬂow resistivity and the measured ﬂow resistivity for increasing backing air cavities. The results of such
an approach could however be hard to relate to the physical behaviour, but a better overall estimation
may be achieved.
At the current state, it may be hard to improve on the frequency dependency of the room factor.
It is obvious that a formulation based on the average behavior inherently will induce errors. A more
precise approach would be to apply a separate room factor for the given reverberation chamber. However
as there to the authors knowledge exist no general characterisation of the diﬀuseness of a room, this
may be impossible. A approach based on applying a weighting function based on the sound intensity
distributions as a function of incidence angle was proposed in [13] and further in [15]. The method was
not tested in this study as data on the exact measurement setup and room geometry was not available.
n interesting new direction could be to extend the method to use on other materials such as foams
or textiles, as much research recently has been put into the investigation of the absorbing properties of
such materials.[8] The inclusion of other materials could naturally be done through the substitution of
the empirical models with appropriate propagation models.

Conclusion
This study investigates and compares the prediction accuracy and uncertainties of an impedance tube
conversion method and 3 diﬀerent Sabine absorption coeﬃcient methods used to inversely estimate the
ﬂow resistivity of porous type absorbers, within a Bayesian framework. The presented work is based
on measurements on 5 diﬀerent Ecophon® industry standard porous type absorbers. The impedance
tube conversion method based on either Miki’s or Allard & Champoux’ model has shown estimation
within the inter-sample deviation for the majority of the tested cased. Estimations based on Miki’s
or Allard & Champoux’ model have shown a similar standard deviation of below 10% for all tested
cases. A noticeable diﬀerence between the model ﬁt and the measured data was observed as a dip in the
measured surface impedance, which the model did not include. An expansion of the model to include
this dip based on a elastic frame model was proposed.
The comparison of the Sabine absorption conversion methods have shown widespread results. For
absorbers mounted with a small or none backing air cavity, the majority of the method and model
combinations produced comparable estimation with comparable uncertainties. For increasing depths of
backing air cavities the performance in terms of both accuracy and standard deviation decreased for all
methods. The reason for this decrease of performance was discussed as being related to the violation
of the assumptions of Thomasson’s size correction. As an indication of overall method performance, if
the methods was used on various backing conditions, the number of estimations within the inter-sample
deviation and the sum of the standard deviation was compared. For such an indication either αsize
or αsize&diff together with Delany Bazley’s model can be concluded as the most accurate and certain
estimation.
For both methods an extensive comparison of the eﬀect of diﬀerent error deﬁnition has additionally
been presented in the appendix, however to be able to analyze the uncertainties within a Bayesian
framework, only the L2 −norm deﬁnition has been presented and discussed in the main study.
Diﬀerent approaches for further improvements and expansions of the methods has been given.
All conversion methods was found to give meaningful estimations of the ﬂow resistivites under various
conditions.
