Abstract
Today, more than 80 % of the world’s energy consumption is based on fossil resources. In addition, the growing popula-
tion, especially from developing countries, fuels the future global energy consumption trend. This constant increase leads
to climate change caused by anthropogenic Greenhouse Gas (GHG) emissions.
A reduction in the emissions of GHGs, especially CO2 mitigation, is one of the top priority challenges in the EU. There-
fore, biogenic Carbon Capture and Utilisation (CCU) and thermochemically converted biomass coupled with electrolysis
are gaining interest among the solutions to mitigate future emissions. Methanol is one of the chemicals that can be pro-
duced and is considered one of the ideal fuels for the shipping industry. Methanol has become a signiﬁcant player in the
future fuel strategy for the Danish shipping company Maersk as a critical part of their net zero climate goal that they aim
to reach by 2040. This makes methanol and its production from biogenic carbon a current, intriguing topic in climate
change mitigation.
In this master thesis, four different renewable technologies for making methanol based on biogenic carbon as a feedstock
were investigated. A focus on methanol as a shipping fuel was chosen where Maersk has been the case project. The
technologies were assessed both technically and economically. This was done by simulating the four different synthesis
reactions in Aspen Plus and building feasibility models based on company-speciﬁc data and from literature.
The simulations successfully achieved fuel-grade methanol production with the following syngas and hydrogen to methanol
conversion efﬁciencies:
• Gasiﬁcation achieved 82% syngas and hydrogen to methanol conversion
• Pyrolysis achieved 81% syngas and hydrogen to methanol conversion
• Electric Steam Methane Reforming (E-SMR) achieved 79% syngas and hydrogen to methanol conversion
• CO2-to-methanol achieved 87% CO2 and hydrogen to methanol conversion
The calculated levelized cost of methanol was found to be the following:
• 1328 euro/ton for gasiﬁcation
• 898 euro/ton for pyrolysis
• 998 euro/ton for E-SMR
• 1310 euro/ton for CO2-to-methanol
The prices are signiﬁcantly higher than the current listed market price for fossil methanol at 917 euro/ton [64] with an
energy density 1.95 times higher than methanol.
The CO2 abatement cost was calculated to be:
• 547 euro/ton for gasiﬁcation
• 177 euro/ton for pyrolysis
• 332 euro/ton for E-SMR
• 556 euro/ton for CO2-to-methanol
The calculated abatement per carbon input was determined to be:

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
Ever since the industrial revolution, a signiﬁcant link between human development and the ever-
increasing consumption of fossil energy resources has been evident. More than 80% of the energy
consumption is based on fossil resources [57]. Meanwhile, demand for products and fuels currently
produced from fossil raw materials is increasing [57]. The total global energy consumption has in-
creased by more than 50% since 1991 [57]. A growing population coupled with growing consumption
for products and services will accelerate the global energy consumption.
This overuse of fossil fuels has led to the disruption of the carbon cycle balance, causing climate
change with the forecast of reaching a 1.5 °C increase already by 2034 with the pace GHG’s are
emitted today [16].
Furthermore, global CO2 emissions related to anthropogenic activities reached 36.4 GtCO2 in 2021,
and the highest-ever ppm of CO2 in the atmosphere was measured to be 420 ppm [14]. Climate
change and global warming will further aggregate with devastating and harmful consequences for all
species and humankind if these emissions are not mitigated [16].
This necessitates the green transition of our society, where the energy generation and transportation
sectors are the most signiﬁcant contributors to the increase of GHGs [40], making these sectors the
most critical and urgent sectors to change.
Replacing power generation capacity with Variable Renewable Energy Sources (VRES) can signif-
icantly reduce emissions in the energy production sector. However, a signiﬁcant challenge is that a
certain degree of ﬂexible power plant production will still be necessary to produce electricity when
the VRES cannot meet demand [40].
Power-to-X (PtX) technologies, where ”X” can represent gases and liquid fuels, which provide poten-
tial energy storage, can be a part of the solution in a VRES system. With these technologies, surplus
green power can be converted into different forms of energy carriers.
Methanol can be produced from hydrogen from electrolysis and bio-resources or CO2. It is considered
a primary compound in the chemical industry as it can be utilized in various processes. Due to its
high energy content, it ﬁts nicely as an energy carrier, while its compatibility with liquid transportation
fuels makes it suitable for fuel blending.
PtX has a great potential to drastically reduce the transport sector’s emissions, especially heavy-duty
transport such as ships, airplanes, and trucks that cannot be easily electriﬁed directly.
This master thesis will focus on the shipping industry, responsible for 2.9% of global GHG emis-
sions.[15]
The project’s focus was inspired by the news in the Summer of 2021 when Maersk announced that
they would use green e-methanol to reach carbon-zero by 2040, with the ﬁrst ships sailing entirely
on methanol already by 2023 [38]. The fact that Maersk will rely on green electricity to make green
fuels through the PtX solution is a signiﬁcant transition for the shipping industry.
This master thesis will therefore investigate four different technologies for sustainable methanol pro-
duction incorporating power-to-X, including the opportunities and barriers for the various technolo-
gies.

The objective of this master thesis is to investigate four different methanol production technologies

Methanol
This chapter describes the properties of methanol, its applications as a marine fuel, and its current and
forecasted demand. It is followed by a detailed summary of conventional methanol production.
Methanol, CH3OH, is a versatile chemical compound that can serve as a hydrogen and energy carrier,
fuel, and a building block for the chemical industry. Methanol is a water-soluble, colorless liquid with
a mild alcoholic odor and is also known by the names methyl and wood alcohol.
Methanol is mainly produced from natural gas by ﬁrst steam reforming to convert natural gas to
syngas, then methanol synthesis from the CO, CO2, and H2 in the syngas.
3.1
Application of Methanol as a Marine Fuel
Methanol has several advantages as a sustainable marine fuel compared to other renewable energy
carriers, including hydrogen, LNG, ammonia, and batteries [33]. Therefore, Maersk has decided to
focus on methanol and not different types of fuels for their ships. The properties of methanol as a fuel
will be listed in this section, as well as the reasons for choosing methanol instead of other solutions
such as hydrogen, Liquid Natural Gas (LNG), ammonia, and batteries. Methanol’s advantages and
disadvantages compared to fossil marine fuel, VLSFO, will also be described.
First, methanol has an energy content of 19.9 MJ/kg LHV and a volumetric energy density of 16
GJ/m3, see Table 1. Thus, methanol is about half of the volumetric energy density of gasoline and
diesel, but about three times the volumetric energy density of compressed hydrogen (at 700 bar) and
twice that of liquid H2 [33].
Another signiﬁcant advantage of the production of methanol compared to other fuels is that methanol
can be produced from many different feedstocks, including residual feedstocks, and methanol pro-
duction is often connected with fewer energy losses compared to other biofuels [73]. In addition,
methanol is degradable in seawater, so there is no risk of marine pollution when using methanol as
fuel[74].

pressure [33], and the fuel is therefore also volatile and expensive to use.
Still, it must be mentioned that ammonia has some advantages that methanol does not have. First,
since ammonia, NH3, does not contain carbon, it does not require biogenic carbon. According to the
Danish Climate Council, this is an advantage, as there will be a lack of sustainable, biogenic carbon
to use for fuel production [38]. Furthermore, methanol still has CO2 emissions from the combustion
of the fuel, which ammonia does not have.
Compared with VLSFO, methanol also has some challenges. The most critical challenges are the
higher cost of sustainably produced methanol and methanol’s lower energy density. Methanol is
furthermore toxic to humans and can be lethal if ingested [61].
Moreover, methanol is corrosive to some metals and incompatible with some materials. This means
that some engine modiﬁcations need to be done before implementing the methanol as a fuel. Still,
these are minor modiﬁcations compared to, for example, using H2 as a fuel [33].

3.3
Production of Methanol
In this Section, the history of methanol and methanol production from natural gas will be covered.
Robert Boyle discovered methanol in the 17th century via wood distillation, which continued to be
the production method until the beginning of the 20th century when Sabatier introduced a synthetic
way via reacting CO and H2 [7]. The process is based on a synthetic gas which is a mixture of CO,
H2, and CO2, produced originally via coal gasiﬁcation. The reaction took place over a ZnO/Cr2O3
catalyst at high pressure and temperature (250-350 bar and 300-400 °C). The process was highly
inefﬁcient but improved over the years by changing the process conditions, the catalyst and obtaining
a cleaner syngas production. Syngas can now be produced from a variety of carbon-based materials,
from coal to biomass, where the most common feedstock globally is natural gas. Currently, methanol
is produced via synthesis processes operating at pressures of 50–100 bar and temperatures of 200–300
°C [7]. The conventional production of methanol consists of three main steps: Syngas production,
catalytic conversion of syngas, and distillation of raw methanol.
Syngas is produced from natural gas by Steam-methane-reforming (SMR) following the reaction
shown in Equation 1.
CH4 +H2O −−⇀
↽−−CO+3H2
(1)
As the SMR is endothermic, it requires high temperatures of 700-1100 °C. At temperatures below
700 °C, the methane cracks into coke and hydrogen instead of carbon monoxide and hydrogen. The
coke deactivates the catalyst and plugs the equipment.
Figure 3: Overview of the process from fossil natural gas, methane, to methanol [55].

An overview of the process from methane to methanol can be seen from the Figure 3 showing a

Methane, the reactant, is obtained from natural gas. First, the natural gas is compressed to approxi-
mately 10-20 bar as the SMR runs best at high pressures. Hydrogen is added and sulphur is removed.
The heavier hydrocarbons contained in the natural gas feed are steam reformed into methane in the
pre-reformer to prevent the formation of coke in the main SMR feed and in the preheat coil and at the
catalyst tube inlet.
The pre-reformer is an adiabatic, catalytic reactor and its main function is to increase the overall
plant reliability and to reduce the steam-to-carbon ratio. Reducing the steam-to-carbon ratio saves
energy and allows the increase the pre-heat temperature and thereby also the temperature to the main
reformer, which is beneﬁcial in terms of reduced radiant duty and cost of the primary reformer. Fur-
thermore, the steam reforming reaction partially takes place in the pre-reformer which further de-
creases the load of the main reformer. In the pre-reformer, the reactor is not heated anymore once
the equilibrium has been set. To heat up the pre-reformer to push forward the strongly endothermic
SMR reaction, fossil fuel is combusted, which is why oxygen is added to the reaction. An additional
oxygen input can also be seen for the secondary reformer.
In the main SMR, the catalytic reactors are usually 10-12 meters long with a diameter of 12 cm [76].
The catalytic gas of steam and methane needs to be heated to temperatures between 700-1000 °C to
avoid coke formation as described above. The SMR catalysts are pills of 16x11 mm size based on
nickel and alumina. The reactors are heated from outside the cylindrical reactor from either the sides
or from above. One of the main challenges of the SMR reactor is the heat transfer through the tube
walls [76].

The conventional method to achieve sufﬁcient H2 in the H2/CO2 ratio in the syngas is by using the
Water-gas-shift reaction (WGS) reaction (Equation 2) followed by removal of the produced CO2. The
CO2 is typically emitted to the atmosphere.
CO+H2O −−⇀
↽−−CO2 +H2,
∆H = −41.21kJ/mole
(2)
The WGS reaction, Equation 2, is exothermic and does not depend on pressure.
Once a syngas with the desired hydrogen content is obtained, the syngas is catalytically converted to
methanol by the following reversible reactions:
CO+2H2 −−→CH3OH,
∆H = −90.77kJ/mole
(3)
CO2 +3H2 −−→CH3OH+H2O,
∆H = −49.16kJ/mole
(4)
The above reactions, equation 3, and equation 4, are exothermic and the reactions therefore prefer
low temperature and, due to Le Chatelier’s principle, high-pressure conditions.
The catalysts used for the syngas to methanol synthesis are based on variations of Cu/ZnO/Al2O or
Cu/ZnO/Cr2O3, where both produce methanol with a selectivity rate above 99.9% [29]. The catalysts
can be used at relatively low pressures which has made methanol synthesis more economical. The
copper part of the catalyst is the main active site of the catalyst, zinc oxide has a stabilizing effect
while alumina or chromia is used for preventing sintering and also stabilizing effects [50].
The lifetime of the catalysts depends on the conditions of operation and the impurities of the syngas.
In most cases, the catalyst gets deactivated due to poisoning or sintering which leads to increased
operating costs. Poisoning stems from the impurities in the syngas such as chlorine, sulphur at levels
above 5 parts per million (ppm), iron and nickel carbonyls while sintering is caused by increasing
temperatures above 270 °C. The average lifetime of catalysts is 2-5 years [7].
The methanol synthesis takes place at 2-300 °C at 50-100 bar. As the reaction from syngas to methanol
is exothermal, the methanol synthesis reactor needs to be cooled. If too high temperature is reached
within the catalyst bed, the conversion will be lowered as by-production will increase and the catalyst
will start sintering with subsequent lost of activity.
Therefore, the synthesis to methanol reactor has to be designed in a way so that hot-spots in the bed
can be avoided. Current designs for methanol synthesis reactors are mostly ﬁxed-bed gas phase re-
actors. Fluidized-bed gas phase liquid phase and membrane-based reactor technologies are emerging
[7].

4.1.3
Pyrolysis
Pyrolysis is a thermal decomposition process at high temperatures of carbonaceous materials in the
absence of air. The high temperatures and lack of air make the chemical bonds of the biomass crack
and form molecules with a lower molecular weight [54]. The word pyrolysis is coined from the
Greek-derived elements pyro ”ﬁre” and lysis ”separating”.
Depending on the solid residence time and heating rate, biomass can be divided into three main types:
ﬂash pyrolysis, fast pyrolysis and slow pyrolysis.
Flash pyrolysis and fast pyrolysis processes are often used in systems with focus on bio-oil production.
These processes typically involve much higher heating rates and shorter residence times. In ﬂash
pyrolysis, the heating rates are extremely high at around more than 1000 °C/s and short residence
times of less than 0.5 s resulting in very high bio-oil yields which can get up to 75-80 wt%. In fast
pyrolysis the heating rate is typically 10-200 °C/s with residence times around 0.5-10 s, but typically
less than 2 s [54].
Slow pyrolysis typically takes place at less than 20 °C/min and retention times of more than 15
minutes. The slow pyrolysis maximizes the yield of biochar [54]. It is the slow pyrolysis process that
is used by the Skyclean technology by Stiesdal.
The larger the amount of biochar produced, the more of the heating value of the original biomass
feedstock remains in this solid residue.
Therefore, processes that seek to optimize bio-fuel typically seek to minimize biochar production.
Thus, the most commonly proposed routes from biomass to fuels are via gasiﬁcation and fast pyrolysis
[62].
However, it has been shown that biochar has signiﬁcant potential to simultaneously improve soil
fertility, while reducing atmospheric GHG concentrations by sequestering carbon if the biochar is
added to agricultural soils.
Co-production of biochar with bioenergy can thereby provide a greater climate change mitigation
impact than biofuel production alone, while it also provides the co-beneﬁt of increased agricultural
productivity of poor soils [62]. Therefore, pyrolysis is considered as well in this project.
Some of the environmental and climatic beneﬁts from biochar can be seen from Figure 10.
Pyrolysis can be seen as an overall CO2 reducing technology due to its carbon capture and storage via
biochar. According to Eurochar [28], 80% of the carbon in biochar is stable and thereby stored for
between 500-1000 years, and can thereby be deﬁned as carbon capture and storage.
Even though biochar may improve the sustainability measure of the biofuel production through its
positive effects on soil health, a market and consequently a value of biochar has not yet been generally
established [62]. Typically, the production of a biochar co-product increases the cost of bio-fuel
production as it decreases the bio-fuel yield. However, a revenue could be obtained from selling
biochar as it can be used as a product such as activated carbon, or as an electrode material, black
pigment, and others.
In Figure 11 the overall process of the carbon cycle in pyrolysis can be seen. Here it can be seen
how CO2 from the atmosphere via photosynthesis is bound to biomass from agriculture or forestry.

Advantages, disadvantages and barriers for pyrolysis
One of the major advantages of the pyrolysis technology is its ability to be versatile both in feedstocks
and the different types of products that it can produce. Pyrolysis is often designed for conversion of

• Difﬁculties in handling, storage, transport and collection
• High water content yielding a low energy density
• Bulky and inhomogenous, so that ﬂexible and resource-consuming handling is necessary
• Very unstable and difﬁcult to contain and generates local odor and dust problems
In Denmark, biomass types like these are generally residual woods, agricultural by-products and

biogas slurry.
Other types of secondary organic materials that could be used for pyrolysis is municipal by-products
such as organic waste, used textiles, grass and cuttings from road and park maintenance and sewage
sludge [54]. Furthermore, typical industrial by-products and residues could be used such as residues
from breweries, food packaging, food retail and food preparation could be used.
The fact that pyrolysis can be used to convert these otherwise waste products into a fuel has a great
positive impact both environmentally, economically and from a resource political point of view [54].

4.1.4
Gasiﬁcation
Gasiﬁcation is a thermochemical process that converts biomass into gases by reacting the feedstock
material at high temperatures (typically above 700 °C), with partial oxidation, by controlling the
amount of oxygen and steam present in the reaction. Thereby, gasiﬁcation can be characterised as
a partial, under-stoiciometric combustion [54]. Oxygen is the oxidation agent is typically 99-99.5%
pure oxygen. The gas mixture as a product is a syngas, mainly consisting of hydrogen (H2) and
carbon monoxide (CO), as well as CO2 and water (H2O). Syngas impurities are ash, char, tars sulfur
and light hydrocarbons such as methane. Trace elements such as ammonia, metals and halides are
also formed [54].

5.2.1
Syngas to Methanol Model: Pyrolysis, Gasiﬁcation and E-SMR
The syngas to methanol model is largely based on the article by Lasse R. Clausen from 2015 that
covers the maximizing of fuel production for gasiﬁcation by integrating hydrogen from electrolysis
to the syngas [13].
The syngas stream is compressed to 30 bar and mixed with a hydrogen stream of pressure 30 bar.
This pressure of 30 bar is due to the fact that AEC outputs hydrogen at 30 bar, to minimize costly gas
compression. As according to [13], the resulting stream is further compressed to 85 bar and mixed
with recycled gas from downstream in the process. Compression is done in a series of compressors
instead of all at once so that the temperature of the streams do not exceed 200°C. The streams are
cooled to 30 °C between compressors.
After the last compressor, the reactor input stream - which is a mix of syngas, hydrogen, and recycled
gas - is preheated to 230 °C, then fed into a reactor of temperature 260 °C with a pressure drop of 4.3
bar. The output from the reactor is cooled to 40 °C, then the gas and liquid phases are separated in
a ﬂash separator. The liquid phase consists mostly of methanol, but also CO2 and water (and small
amounts of other compounds). 99.9% of the CO2 in the liquid phase is removed using a separator,
and the remaining liquid without CO2, mainly a mixture of water and methanol, is sent to distillation.
The removed CO2 is recycled to be used as input material for methanol synthesis to obtain a better
carbon-conversion rate. The gas phase, which consists mostly of hydrogen, but also CO and CO2 (and
small amounts of other compounds), is separated so that 99% is recycled. The reason for the recycle
ratio not being higher than 99% is that impurities from the syngas such as N2 and CH4 accumulate
too much in the process.

seen in the Appendix section D, 8.4.

CO2 to Methanol Model
In the CO2 to methanol model, the input streams are pure CO2 and pure H2. These streams are mixed,
heated, and fed to an adiabatic reactor, meaning that the heat output from the reactor is stored in the
output gas stream (as opposed to a cooled reactor in the syngas to methanol model). The model is
largely based on Cui et. al. [17], with some simpliﬁcations.
CO2 of pressure 35 bar and 25 °C is mixed with hydrogen of the same pressure and temperature [17].
The mixture of hydrogen and CO2 is heated to 220 °C, with a pressure drop of 4 bar, then fed into

Carbon conversion of the four technologies
One of the main reasons why hydrogen is added to the syngas from electrolysis is to obtain a higher
carbon efﬁciency, resulting in a better usage of the biomass. The CCE is calculated on a mass basis
by how much of the carbon contained in the syngas is converted to methanol. An example of how
the CCE is calculated stochiometrically can be found in the Appendix B in Section 8.2. All syngas to
methanol CCE’s are based on Aspen modeling.
In the Aspen model for the E-SMR technology in the appendix B Section 8.2, a mass ﬂow of 12
kg C/hr from the syngas is converted to 11.36 kg C/hr in the methanol. This means that the syngas
to methanol CCE is at 94.29%. The biogas to syngas carbon conversion shown in the appendix B
is at 100% based on the Aspen modeling with 40% CO2 and 60% CH4 and no impurities such as
from sulphur and nitrogen. If looking at the biomass to methanol carbon conversion, the overall CCE
decreases quite signiﬁcantly. Just as the energy conversion of biomass to biogas is at 55% according
to [20], the mass conversion from dry biomass to biogas is at around 50% [5], and it is therefore
assumed that the mass conversion of carbon from biomass to biogas is at 50% as well. From a mass
conversion efﬁciency of carbon from biomass to biogas to syngas to methanol, the overall CCE thus
becomes 47.14%. Thereby, only 47.14% of the carbon in the biomass ends up in the methanol.
The CCE of CO2 syngas to methanol is 97%. It is assumed that the CO2 is a by-product of biogas
production to the gas-grid. With this assumption, the CCE loss from biomass to biogas is not included,
and the overall CCE is therefore at 97%. This assumption is backed up by the fact that DEA strongly
relies on biogas to the gas-grid as being a part of the green transition strategy [5], making CO2 from
biogas plants an available by-product.

If the biomass to CO2 was considered in the model, then the CCE would decrease substantially due to
the 50% CCE from biomass to biogas and the carbon wt% in the biogas assuming 40% mole fraction
CO2 and 60% mole fraction CH4. Converting mole to mass and comparing the carbon content in the
CH4 and CO2, only 40% of that carbon would be in the CO2 fraction. With these efﬁciencies included
the overall CCE for CO2 would become only 19.4%.
One could argue that this is a more reasonable assumption to make, since the CCE from biomass-to-
methanol is the case for the other technologies where the biomass to syngas conversion efﬁciency is
included.
Still, it makes sense to see the CO2-case as quite different from the three others because the biogas-
plants today already run with CO2 as a by-product. This not not the case for the other technologies, as
the biomass-to-syngas plant would have to be built and use the biomass solely to produce methanol.
One could argue that pyrolysis also has a signiﬁcant production of bio-char, and that the syngas was
therefore a by-product of the biochar production. However, this argument does not hold yet, as bio-
char currently does not have any economic value, and therefore this assumption for pyrolysis has not
been made.
The biomass to syngas CCE for gasiﬁcation is based on the article of Lasse R. Clausen [13]. 56.7
kg wet biomass (50% water content) with a carbon content of 47.2% is feeded combined with re-
circulation of two CO2-ﬂows from the distillation and CO2 feeded to the gasiﬁer. These carbon ﬂows
produces a syngas of 40 kg with a carbon content of 36% wt. resulting in an assumed CCE of 100%.
The high CCE assumed is conﬁrmed by experimental measurements and is theoretically possible due
to the very high temperatures of the gasiﬁcation. The syngas to methanol CCE is 98%. Thereby the
overall CCE is 98%.
The CCE for pyrolysis from biomass to syngas is based on Aspen modeling results given by Lasse R.
Clausen, which can be seen in appendix A, 8.1. Here, 1.38 kg biomass with a carbon content of 39%
wt is converted to 0.95 kg syngas with a carbon content of 32.7% wt resulting in a CCE of 57%. The
syngas to methanol CCE is based on the Aspen modeling and is found to be 95.2%. Thus, the overall
CCE is 56%.

Discussion
Discussion about gasiﬁcation and pyrolysis
It is assumed that no energy is needed for the compressors in both systems. This assumption is based
on the model in [13], where the difference between the total electricity input and the electricity output
of the system is equal to the electricity needed for electrolysis, meaning that the electricity needed for
other things such as compressors could be obtained from the system (here, the system encompasses
everything from the raw biomass input to methanol distillation). This is a gasiﬁcation model, but it
was deemed reasonable to apply the same assumption to the pyrolysis system.
Discussion about the E-SMR results

According to Topsøe themselves, the pilot plant by Foulum has a capacity of 10-40 kg methanol/hour
and will be able to convert 1 Nm3 biogas to 1 kg methanol [78]. The energy content of 1 Nm3 bio
natural gas is 22 MJ while the energy content of methanol is 23 MJ Higher Heating Value (HHV),
meaning a biogas to methanol energy conversion efﬁciency of 104%. However, another source from
Topsøe shows plant results where 28.8 MW biogas and 27.9 MW electricity produces 37.8 MW
methanol. This results in an overall energy efﬁciency of 66.84% [6], and if excluding added electricity
like in [78], a biogas to methanol efﬁciency of 131% . These results from [6] are from a biogas
conference last year about the Foulum pilot plant too. From the results, it seems that Topsoe does not
prioritize to optimize their energy consumption for the plant by, for instance, circulating heat from
coolers to increase the temperature of the E-SMR reactor and synthesis reactor. On the other hand it
seems that they assume that it will be possible to use the excess heat to district heating. They therefore
account all excess heat as potential district heating, summing to 9 MW. Furthermore a purge export
of 1.5 MW is included. If these to ﬁgures were included the overall efﬁciency would instead be of
85%.
The results of the E-SMR model were achieved with several assumptions, as it was not possible to
obtain information about the technology from Topsøe. One of the assumptions is that the biogas
entering the E-SMR reactor to produce syngas is pure methane and CO2 (60% methane and 40%
CO2). This is based on ﬁgures from a presentation from Topsøe [49], but it might not be completely
exact, as there can be some impurities from the biomass in the biogas. However, it seems like a better
assumption than guessing what those impurities could be and in what amount they are present.
Additionally, the operating conditions of the E-SMR step are not available. The pressure of 1 bar
was chosen as increasing the pressure while keeping other parameters constant in the Aspen model
did not increase the production of CO and H2. The temperature was chosen to be 860 °C rather
arbitrarily: literature values on conventional SMR range from around 750 to 1000 degrees, and vary-
ing the temperature in Aspen between these values showed higher methanol production with lower
SMR temperature, keeping all other parameters constant. However, the temperature should not be
too low as the reaction is endothermic and needs high temperature to occur. The temperature of 860
degrees was chosen because, at this temperature, the mole ﬂow of methanol increased with 1% for a
10 degrees decrease in temperature.
Disucssion about the CO2-to-methanol results
The demonstration plant is built to produce 300.000 L/year. No ofﬁcial numbers for the efﬁciency for
the plant can be found, but from the article [52] it can found that approximately 55% of the electrical
energy ends up in methanol, and that 190 kg hydrogen and 1.4 tons of CO2 becomes 1 ton of methanol.
The CO2 to methanol model was inspired by the Aspen model made by researchers from ReIntergate
[17], but with some simpliﬁcations. Namely, the methanol reactor was modelled as a REquil reactor
with two allowed equations, the CO2 to methanol equation and the reverse water-gas shift reaction,
with a temperature approach to equilibrium of 15 degrees. In the model by Cui et. al., it is a RPlug
reactor, and it’s speciﬁcations are much more detailed, especially regarding catalysis. Therefore, the
model may be oversimpliﬁed compared to the real CO2 to methanol process.

6
Economic Analysis
In this section feasibility assessments and techno-economic assessments are conducted for the four
different technologies. The objective of the section is to ﬁnd and compare the technologies’ produc-
tion costs, abatement costs and their potentials.
6.1
Indicators of Interest
6.1.1
LCOE
The LCOE is an indicator of the total net present cost of producing an energy carrier, relative to the
amount produced.
The LCOE is a good indicator of cost-effectiveness, because it can be calculated without assumptions
about the price of methanol, as is the case when assessing the Net Present Value (NPV) or payback
period. The LCOE is a good indicator for comparing different technologies, without the framework
conditions having an impact on the assessment [51].
The LCOE is calculated by ﬁrst calculating the NPV of total costs of the investments required to
produce the energy carrier, then annualising the NPV and dividing by the yearly production. Here,
the investments required to produce methanol are everything in the system described above, from the
offshore wind farm to the methanol reactor.
The NPV is calculated by taking the sum of total costs - investment and operational - and applying a
discount factor (also called Weighted Average Cost of Capital, WACC) to account for the time value
of money. Here, the WACC is set to be 8% [35] In addition, inﬂation is set to 4% [35]. The lifetime
of the system is given by the minimum of the lifetimes of all components of the system. In all four
systems, it is assumed to be 25 years [19].
Production capacity was assumed to be constant during the operational years.
6.1.2
CO2 Abatement Cost
Abatement cost calculations of the four technologies
A main advantage of biomass- and CO2-based methanol production is the reduction in the overall
CO2-eq GHG-emissions.
A complete Life-cycle assessment (LCA) where all the environmental and climate impacts are taken
into consideration will not be conducted in this project.
Conducting an LCA will depend on a large number of parameters including the nature of the feed-
stocks, by-product generation, processes applied, how the product is used and this is out of the scope
for this project.
Still, calculated estimations and abatement ﬁgures will be included as the climate beneﬁts from sus-

needed. Making a rough estimation on the GHG emission reductions from the four technologies will

The CO2 emissions from transportation will not be included in the assessment. This is due to the
uncertainty about how long the actual distances of the biomasses would be transported. Furthermore,

The marginal abatement cost of CO2 is the cost of reducing CO2 in euro/ton CO2-equivalent and
it is a measure to compare different technologies with each other to assess which technology is the
cheapest to implement in order to mitigate climate change. The CO2-reductions from the technologies
are compared to a business-as-usual benchmark of fossil maritime fuel. It is assumed that 1 MJ of
sustainably produced methanol will be able to substitute 1 MJ of fossil maritime fuel.
Another set of scenarios have been made for the CO2 abatement costs so that the ﬂuctuation of VLSFO
is also considered and a potential CO2-tax.
Therefore, beside the base case, three scenarios are made:
• Half price of VLSFO (458.5 euro/ton)
• A CO2-tax of 200 euro/ton-eq CO2
• A combined half-price VLSFO and CO2 tax
The CO2 tax of 200 euro/ton-eq CO2 is based on the Danish Climate Council’s climate mitigation
recommendations to the Danish government [37].
Abatement potential per ton C input
To accommodate the uncertainty of the LCOE’s, an analysis of CO2 abatement per ton carbon input

For gasiﬁcation, pyrolysis and E-SMR the abatement potential from 1 ton of C will be based on C-

Data for Calculating the CO2 Abatement Cost
The CO2-equivalent abatement cost is calculated for all four technologies. According to a life cycle
assessment made for the International Maritime Organization of maritime fuels from 2016, maritime
fuel oil has a CO2-equivalent impact of 3.21 g per g of fuel [41]. The price of maritime fuel with low
66 of 94
==PAGE:72==
Master Thesis
6
ECONOMIC ANALYSIS
sulphur-content VLSFO ﬂuctuates and is currently at 917 euro/ton [64]. This is the average world
price, at some harbors the price is higher and at some harbors it is at a lower price. The LHV of
VLSFO is assumed to be the same as Heavy fuel oil (HFO) with 39 MJ/ton found from the website
The Engineering Toobox [70].
Methanol has an energy level of just 19.9 MJ/ton on a LHV-basis, approximately just half of the
energy content of maritime fuel. This means that 1.95 ton methanol is needed to substitute 1 ton of
maritime fuel. If it was assumed that the sustainable methanol had a net-zero impact, it would mean
that 1.95 ton of sustainable produced methanol would be able to abate 3.21 ton CO2.
However, all four technologies have variations of emissions of CO2, and these numbers will be esti-
mated and taken into consideration to assess the net carbon abatement from substituting fossil mar-
itime fuel with sustainable methanol.
CO2 emissions from green hydrogen from AEC
According to the European standards, green hydrogen needs to be produced from renewable energy
sources and have a carbon footprint that is below 36.4 g CO2eq/MJ LHV. H2 has a LHV of 120
MJ/kg [70], and thereby the climate impact should be below 4,368 g CO2eq/kg H2 [71].
In this project, H2 is assumed to have a climate impact from its electricity consumption from wind
turbines, the electrolyzer, and the H2 storage.
The CO2-eq impact from the electrolysis plant itself excluding electricity is approximately 138 g
CO2/kg H2 output [39]. The electricity consumption for the electrolysis plant is assumed to be off-
shore wind energy. According to an LCA-study of wind energy in Denmark from 2016 [3], off-shore
wind has a CO2-eq impact of approximately 11 g CO2-eq/kWh. With an LHV energy content of 120
MJ/kg for H2 and an energy conversion efﬁciency of 66.5% for AEC, the electrolyzer needs 50.12
kWh/kg H2. The electricity demand increases due to the efﬁciency losses from transporting hydrogen
from the energy island via pipes (99% efﬁciency) and storage cavern (99%), resulting in an electricity
consumption of 51.14 kWh/kg H2. Thereby, the CO-eq impact of 562.57 g CO2-eq/kg H2 from the
electricity consumption. Overall with the electrolysis plant installation + electricity consumption +
H2 storage, a total emission of 707 g CO2/kg H2 is assumed.
With a climate impact of 0.707 g CO2 it can thus be concluded that the H2 used in this report is indeed
green H2 according to EU legislation with the rule of maximum 4,368 g CO2/kg H2 to be deﬁned as
green.
CO2 sequestration from biochar
The pyrolysis plant size for this project of 20 MW biomass input and 41% of this energy is assumed
to be biochar. This energy balance assumption is based on pyrolysis ﬂow sheet from Lasse Clausen
that modelled the entire pyrolysis process in Aspen based on the SkyClean technology. This means
that 8.3 MW biochar is produced for a 20 MW plant. Biochar has a LHV of 23.29 MJ/kg, meaning
that for every second 0.3547 kg of biochar is produced, resulting in a yearly output of 11,186 ton
biochar.
The biochar consists of 64% carbon, and a part of this carbon can be seen as carbon capture and
storage, and all of the biochar can be seen as a nutritious input to the soil, where especially poor soils

Discussion of CAPEX
In this master thesis it has been an objective to do an economic analysis on the four methanol produc-
tion technologies in order to estimate an LCOE for each of them.
In order to do so, gathering of economic data has been necessary. Initially it was the plan to ﬁnd
estimates of the CAPEX from either the different suppliers of the technology or from at least the
same source. This has been a goal due to the fact that the calculated total investment of a plant is an
uncertain value with uncertainty being as much as 30% of the total investment [2]. Therefore, using
different sources for estimating CAPEX should be avoided if wanting to compare the technologies.
However, this has not been possible, and therefore, as described, the CAPEX of the gasiﬁcation and
CO2-to-methanol reactor and methanol-synthesis plant are from [19] while the E-SMR and pyrolysis
technology-speciﬁc plants and methanol synthesis plants have been estimated from other sources.
The problem with using different sources for estimating the total CAPEX becomes highly evident in
Figure 35. Here, it can be seen how the the technology-speciﬁc capital + methanol plant has a cost of
6-7 Meuro/MW for gasiﬁcation and CO2-to-methanol, while it is only 1-2 Meuro/MW for the E-SMR
and pyrolysis technologies.
The difference in costs could potentially be that these pyrolysis E-SMR are just at a lower cost. This
is however not assumed to be the case for multiple reasons. Firstly, the E-SMR operates at conditions
similar to gasiﬁcation in terms of high temperature conversion of feedstock to syngas. Furthermore,
the pyrolysis plant has a low conversion efﬁciency due to the production of biochar, which should
theoretically increase the cost/MW methanol produced.
The CAPEX of the gasiﬁcation and methanol reactor from DEA seems very high compared to the
CAPEX for the other technologies. This could be because the DEA assumes that the installation cost
is higher than other sources do. Indeed, 25% of the CAPEX from DEA is for installation and 75%
is for equipment, while it is unclear whether CAPEX data from other sources take installation into
account and, if so, how much of the CAPEX is related to installation (for the methanol reactor data
used in pyrolysis and E-SMR systems, an installation factor of 1.1 is assumed in Equation 21, but this
factor is multiplied on a cost which could or could not include installation). Nevertheless, removing
installation cost from the CAPEX of the gasiﬁcation and methanol reactor, which means removing
25%, only lowers the LCOE of the gasiﬁcation technology to 1225 euro/ton methanol instead of 1328,
as can be seen in Table 53 in Appendix C 8.3. This is still a high LCOE compared to the LCOE for
pyrolysis, 898 euro/ton methanol, and E-SMR, 997 euro/ton methanol. It would however become
cheaper than CO2-to-methanol, which has a calculated LCOE of 1310 euro/ton methanol.
The economic model includes wind turbines and an alkaline electrolyzer, when it could have been
made simpler by considering green hydrogen to be a feedstock with a ﬁxed price, like wood pellets,
straw and biogas. It is therefore relevant to note that, based on the model, the LCOE of hydrogen
is 629 Eurp/ton or 31.6 euro/GJ (this is calculated by removing the costs of methanol synthesis and
gasiﬁcation, pyrolysis or E-SMR from the respective models, as well as the cost of feedstock. If hy-
drogen storage and transport costs are also removed, then the LCOE is 594 euro/ton or 29.8 euro/GJ).

Discussion of CO2-abatement
It has been an objective of this master thesis to estimate what climate beneﬁts would be related to using
renewable methanol instead of VLSFO. It was decided not to use an LCA from a literature study
because this study might be based on a different scenarios with different assumptions. Instead, as
mentioned in Section 6.1.2, it has been the goal to estimate the climate impact of renewable methanol,
but not conducting a full LCA. In this Section the uncertainties of not conducting a full LCA will
be discussed. The main consequence is that the methanol seems ”greener” than it actually is. This
is due to the fact that several of the climate impacts from the production processes are not included,
see Section 6.2.3. For instance, the climate impact of the installment and equipment used and the
transport of the methanol from the production plant to the harbour have not been included.
In the Methanol Outlook-report [33] it has been found that compared to conventional fuels, renewable
methanol offers carbon reduction beneﬁts ranging from 65% to 95%. These CO2-eq reductions have
been among the highest for alternative fuels that can replace gasoline [33]. In comparison, the CO2-
reductions for this project were found to be of approximately 95% and even 146% for pyrolysis due
to accounting for the biochar too.
Thus, if 1 ton of VLSFO is substituted by green methanol, a CO2 reduction of 95-146% is achieved.
If all climate impacts from the production of methanol had been included in this report, the reduction
per ton VLSFO substitution by renewable methanol would decrease, and likely be more in the 65%
to 95% range as in [33]. The climate reduction from renewable methanol would be more realistic.
This would also mean that the abatement calculations in euro/ton CO2-eq reduction and ton CO2
reduction/ton C for this report would change and become more realistic. For future work a more
thorough climate analysis would be beneﬁcial to obtain a more realistic result of the climate beneﬁts
of renewable methanol.

CONCLUSION
7
Conclusion
The main objectives of this master thesis have been to make a technical and economic assessment of
the four sustainable methanol production technologies E-SMR, CO2-to-methanol, gasiﬁcation, and
pyrolysis.
All four technologies have been coupled with hydrogen from alkaline electrolysis cells, AEC, to
obtain optimal CO/H2 - and CO2/H2-ratios. Technical descriptions of the four technologies and hy-
drogen production from electrolysis have been presented. It is assumed that the E-SMR utilizes raw
biogas, CO2-to-methanol utilizes CO2 captured from biogas plants, gasiﬁcation uses wood pellets,
and pyrolysis utilizes wheat straw and residual manure ﬁbers. In addition, an assessment of the resid-
ual biomass availability in a current Danish context has been conducted. The evaluation shows that
gasiﬁcation and pyrolysis have the most considerable methanol production potentials compared to the
CO2-to-methanol and E-SMR technologies.
The energy efﬁciencies of the technologies range from 45 to 61%. The most efﬁcient technology from
feedstock and electricity to methanol is E-SMR with 70%; however, this is much lower (45%) when
taking the conversion of biomass to biogas into account. The production of biogas is not a parameter
that can be optimized as it is out of the scope of the system considered in this thesis. Gasiﬁcation is
the most energy-efﬁcient technology, from biomass and electricity to methanol, with 61%. CO2-to-
methanol has an efﬁciency of 56%, which is assumed to be the complete efﬁciency as the input CO2
is a byproduct that would otherwise be emitted. Therefore the biomass to CO2 efﬁciency is not taken
into account (also, CO2 does not have an energy content, so this would not be possible). Finally, the
pyrolysis technology has a relatively low efﬁciency of 46%, but this is because the byproduct of the
pyrolysis step, biochar, has a value in the sense that it can be used for carbon capture and storage. The
energy going to the biochar should not be considered an energy loss. In terms of carbon conversion
efﬁciency, the syngas (or CO2) to methanol efﬁciencies range from 94 to 98%. Still, the overall
carbon conversion efﬁciency is much lower for E-SMR and pyrolysis - 47% and 55%, respectively.
For pyrolysis, this is again because of the production of biochar.
The Levelised Costs of Energy of methanol range from 655 to 1328 euro/ton when considering all the
scenarios, including the pyrolysis scenario using free residual manure ﬁbers with the assumption that
transport is free. However, the pyrolysis scenario using straw gives a higher LCOE (898 euro/ton)
but is still at a lower cost than the other technologies. As mentioned in Sections 6.4 and 6.5, the high
LCOE of the gasiﬁcation and CO2-to-methanol systems (1328 euro/ton and 1310 euro/ton respec-
tively) can be attributed to very high CAPEX from the Danish Energy Agency (or too low CAPEX
from other sources for the technologies), and therefore it is problematic to conclude much from the
LCOE of the technologies.
The abatement costs of the technologies range from 76.40-557.58 euro/ton CO2 in the base-case
scenario. Pyrolysis using residual manure ﬁbers with no transport included has the lowest cost, while
CO2-to-methanol has the highest cost. These abatement cost results are quite uncertain due to the
uncertainty of LCOE. The abatement of CO2 in ton CO2 abated per ton carbon input has therefore
also been calculated. Here, it can be concluded that gasiﬁcation has the highest reduction, 4.16 ton
CO2/ton carbon input. CO2-to-methanol and pyrolysis abate 3.86 ton CO2 and 3.52 ton CO2 per ton
of carbon input, respectively. The E-SMR technolgy has the lowest reduction of 2.01 ton CO2/ton
carbon. This is due to the biomass to biogas conversion mass-based carbon conversion efﬁciency of
50%.
