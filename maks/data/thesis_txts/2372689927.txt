ABSTRACT 
Urbanisation is taking place side by side with rampant motorization around the world, especially in 
cities from developing economies. If urbanisation increasingly continues to pressure the city space, 
the spread of motorized vehicles and their correlated road-infrastructure system requires a greater 
share of urban space. The rising scarcity of space in the city also reinforces potential distributional 
inequities among the urban population. This project will consider urban passenger transport under 
the spatial lens, and analyze the urban space used by transport today. This is undertaken from both 
the angle of a fair space allocation between transport-modes – and thus traveller categories – and 
an efficient one, by quantifying the space used by transport in the city. It will mainly investigate 
whether future transport planning strategies could enable a more sustainable transport space use. 
The project will elaborate a spatial assessment methodology to measure the spatial footprint of 
urban transport systems. 
The spatial assessment conceptualized suggests five indicators: three direct indicators – the space 
allocated to transport, the space consumed by travellers, the road occupancy ratios – and two 
derived ones – the value of the land used by transport and the ecological footprint. The analysis 
takes Rajkot, India as a case-study city, for which a Low-carbon Comprehensive Mobility Plan (LCMP) 
has been designed, and applies the examination to both the base-year and the forecasted transport 
evolution strategies. 
The operationalization shows that the space used by transport can become more efficient and 
distributed in a fairer manner if the transport system engages in the low-carbon development 
scenarios suggested by the LCMP. The base-year study identifies several weaknesses in the spatial 
organization of transport, both in terms of efficiency and fair distribution. The economic valuation of 
the different scenarios showcases the high price of the value of land that would be consumed by 
transports if their development would follow the past motorization trends. The ecological transport 
footprint displays that beyond the visible urban infrastructures, each scenario would need a vast 
portion of forestry land to sequester the associated CO2 emissions, with however important 
differences given the strategy adopted. 
Some of the indicators are presented to relevant stakeholders and their pertinence and utilization 
framework is discussed.

A. INTRODUCTION 
 
I. 
Problem statement and research questions 
Roads account for a major share of the urban space. From collector roads to urban highways, 
through arteries and parking lots, motorized modes rely on a wide infrastructure network. If noise 
and air pollution are well-known damages resulting from car-based transport systems, the vastness 
of the space consumed by motorized vehicles has seldom been questioned. With rapid urbanization 
around the world, city space is nonetheless increasingly pressured and steadily scarcer.  
This context asks to avoid wasting urban land and start considering transport planning with spatial 
efficiency concerns. Granting space for instance to a parking lot has a direct spatial cost, and an 
indirect one as it is done at the expense of another space use with possibly higher social benefits. 
Besides the cost of a potential space misuse, scarcity also implies reinforced distributional issues. Be 
that of the space allocated to different modes or used by different user categories, the quest for 
fairness of the land-use by transport in the city will be a lingering theme of the spatial assessment 
that this project aims at leading. Urban transport will be examined within the frame of its space-use 
impacts, a process coined spatial assessment, with both efficiency and fairness preoccupations, and 
in line with overarching sustainable transport development goals. 
A spatial assessment of transport systems does not intend to provide a comprehensive framework 
for sustainability, but rather present an innovative approach that highlights specific points at the 
crossroad of transport networks and sustainable development. Space-use in transport is involved in 
several fundamental sustainability issues, and raises very practical questions: how do we use the 
available space in the wisest manner? What is a sustainable space use for transports? Those broad 
interrogations will be investigated through two sub-questions. First, the current space used by 
transport in the city will be questioned, as to whether it is sustainably, and in particular efficiently 
and fairly organised. The second sub-question will ask if future transport development scenarios can 
make it more sustainable. 
The first step is to understand how space is used for transportation: how much, where and for 
whom. The discussion will show that there is no easy answer to describe the “good” use of space for 
transport.  The spatial lens may prove more fertile when used in a comparative manner: comparison 
between the spaces used and supplied, between modes, geographical regions, and transport 
functions (circulation or parking, as primary and subsidiary functions). The second step focuses on 
evaluating the spatial footprint of different transport strategies. It targets a higher-level kind of 
comparison, with a greater focus on implementation and thus entails decision-making and political 
implications. Both sub-questions could be examined separately, but the first supports the second 
and the overall operationalization framework made the scenario comparison propitious. The two 
investigations require foremost a spatial assessment methodology, which is a major outcome of the 
present report. The underlying technical research question is thus: how to measure the spatial 
footprint used by urban transport systems? 
==PAGE:16==
This thesis aims thus at proposing a methodology for a spatial assessment, and applying it to an 
Indian case-study city where particular developmental and urban transport challenges are to be 
found. India gathers the densest cities in the world (Demographia, 2017), as well as the most 
polluted ones (World Health Organization, 2016). The case-study will be based on Rajkot, Gujarat, 
India, where several urban transport development scenarios have been designed to serve the United 
Nations Environment Programme’s project on Low Carbon Transport in India. The previous 
problematics will hence be transferred to this precise context, and will enquire: 
1. What is the space used, both consumed by and allocated to transport in Rajkot today? 
o Is it sustainable, and namely fair and efficient? 
2. How does it change in the different transport development scenarios? 
Additionally, as a technical backbone question: how to measure the space used, both consumed by 
and allocated to transport in Rajkot? 
 
II. 
Methodology 
In an attempt to answer the questions presented in the previous section, this thesis will be 
structured around building an indicator-set. The goal is to account for spatial issues, including the 
space used by urban passenger transport in different transport development scenarios. As 
underlined by Cornet & Gudmundsson (2015), indicators for sustainable transport are defined and 
framed by three functions: conceptualization, operationalization and utilization. This thesis has 
shown that the indicator construction is an iterative process. In theory, the conceptualization and 
utilization framework should be as clear as possible before starting the operationalization. In 
practice though, the analysis lead during operationalization feeds both the conceptual and utilization 
sections. For the purpose of clarity and concision, this report will only present the outcomes at the 
final stage of the process, eluding the detailed account of the iterative process involved mainly in the 
two first phases (conceptualization and operationalization). The description will focus on the 
indicator-set built with the timeframe allocated to its creation. 
The conceptualization part will describe more in depth why the spatial assessment is relevant for 
sustainable transports, particularly in the local context of the case-study, and include a literature 
review of previous spatial assessments. An operationalization of the indicator-set selected will be 
undertaken thanks to the data available from the outcome of the United Nations Environment 
Programme’s project on Low Carbon Transport in India. The operationalization will hence answer the 
technical question regarding the process of setting up a spatial assessment. The combination of the 
conceptualization and operationalization will enable to have quantitative indicators yielding partial 
conclusions to the two research questions defined. The utilization section will present the indicators 
to stakeholders and discuss their feedbacks regarding the conceptualization of the indicators and 
their operationalization. The utilization part plays an important role in validating the indicator-
making procedure introduced and understanding its contribution. 
In terms of the three chapters’ size and content, the scale will be clearly tipped in favour of the 
operationalization. The operationalization section solves the technical question of how to conduct a 
spatial assessment. Through the indicator’s results, it will also participate in answering the 
==PAGE:17==
conceptual thread interrogations regarding the characterization of the space used for transport. The 
conceptualization gives a theoretical ground to the operationalization, and the utilization reviews 
both processes by including stakeholder’s input. 
Analyses on the Indian context are a result of the discussion with the external supervisors, well 
aware of the local issues. 
 
III. 
Delimitation and scope 
The broad scope and originality of the research question opens the ground for numerous analyses, 
even more so when considering transport as a system and its multiple linkages with development 
and sustainability. The complexity of this work has thus been in organising a sound delimitation for 
the analysis, as both the topic (spatial analysis of urban passenger transportation in the context of 
sustainable development) and the methodology (iterative indicator building) are in essence prolific. 
The delimitation and vocabulary will thus be clarified in this introduction. 
First, it is fundamental to note that the study follows on from the Low-carbon Comprehensive 
Mobility Plans developed by UNEP for Indian cities. The object of this project is the same as in 
LCMPs: urban, passenger transport for Indian cities. The case study which provides the foundations 
for this thesis is a study on Rajkot, one of the cities for which the LCMP methodology has been 
applied. The analysis will thus be very context-specific. However, the indicator-set construction 
methodology can be applied to different geographical contexts, and generic analysis on urban 
transport space use will also result from the indicator building process. 
Second, the scope is the one of the LCMPs, namely “Low-carbon” development. It is important to 
note that low-carbon development is less holistic than sustainable development, but potentially 
more quantifiable. I will nevertheless argue that the spatial assessment, as elaborated in this report, 
is a platform towards a more comprehensive assessment of urban transport in the context of 
sustainable urban transport than merely “low-carbon” development. This is as key distinction, as a 
spatial assessment can be conducted separately from a carbon emission perspective. As will be 
discussed later in this thesis, it maybe even should be independent of the carbon impact 
measurements. Moreover, the overall approach of UNEP is aligned with the sustainable 
development fostered by the United Nation. Even if UNEP have been appointed as the watchmen of 
the environmental aspects of the sustainable development, they target carbon emissions reduction 
while promoting sustainable development. The Low Carbon Transport in India project is a 5-year 
project funded by the German government’s International Climate Initiative and implemented by 
the UNEP Transport Unit in Kenya, UNEP DTU Partnership in Denmark and partners in India. Its 
purpose is to “build institutional capacity in urban areas, with the goal of improving mobility for the 
local population, lowering CO2 emissions, and creating what was known as “smart cities” (UNEP, 
2010). Its scope and purpose thus goes beyond solely low-carbon development, and wants to 
achieve inclusive and sustainable mobility. 
We need here to define, thirdly, the overall framework in which the indicator making takes place, 
which is one of sustainable development. The sustainable development definition selected for this 
==PAGE:18==
study is the one adopted by the United Nations, and with a founding definition elaborated in the 
Brundtland Report from the United Nations World Commission on Environment and Development 
(WCED) in 1987: “Sustainable development is development that meets the needs of the present 
without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs. It contains within it 
two key concepts: 
- 
the concept of 'needs', in particular the essential needs of the world's poor, to which 
overriding priority should be given; and  
- 
the idea of limitations imposed by the state of technology and social organization on the 
environment's ability to meet present and future needs.” (WCED, 1987).  
This definition has been followed by the development of millennial goals, and in 2015 seventeen 
sustainable development goals were established. The spatial assessment is strongly linked to goal 
11, “Make cities and human settlements inclusive, safe, resilient and sustainable”. 
 
IV. 
Purpose and contribution 
The spatial assessment aims at providing a support document for future decision-making in 
transportation projects. It establishes a methodology aimed at being reproducible in cities where 
sufficient data is collected and where a basic transport model has been developed. As indicator 
building is an iterative process that must involve stakeholders, the methodology and its results will 
be presented to planners and decision-makers and their feedback will be used to suggest 
recalibration of the design of the indicator aligned with the stakeholder’s agendas. 
This projects falls within a holistic approach of sustainability, as it lies in-between transport planning 
and sustainable development. Through the spatial assessment, it will be shown that transport 
planning, an area traditionally delegated to the technical spheres, nevertheless plays a structural 
role in social equity, which is a priority for democracies. The original spatial approach presents key 
developmental levers in line with the millennium development goals  
Moreover, since “what gets measured gets done”1, the quantitative approach of indicator-building 
can serve as a measurement tool to understand how sustainable or fair a specific transport system is 
in spatial terms, and the political implications this may have. 
 
V. 
Outline of the thesis 
The report will first present the conceptualization part of structuring the indicator-set, answering 
namely the questions of why a spatial assessment is relevant for sustainable transport and 
emphasize its importance in the context of urban transport in India. The methodology will be 
described in the second chapter, followed by the application to the case study of Rajkot and the 
interpretation of the results given by the indicator. The final chapter will elaborate on the utilization 
framework of the indicator-set, namely thanks to several interviews with local stakeholders.

B. SPATIAL ASSESSMENT, SUSTAINABLE TRANSPORT, 
AND URBAN TRANSPORT IN INDIA  
Conceptualisation and literature review 
 
I. 
Approach 
Land-use and transport planning have considerable impacts on people, the environment, and the 
economy. Economic effects of transports can be either direct and measurable, such as the value of 
land allocated to transport, and indirect and less easily quantifiable, such as the global economic 
attractiveness of a place generated by the efficiency of its transport system and the liveability of the 
urban environment it creates (Banister & Berechman, 2001). Transport systems are powerful actors 
of change (Dimitriou, Wright, & Ward, 2015), especially through their link with urban design, which 
is a decisive driver in the economic development of a country (Sen, 2013). A broad list of the 
transport land-use impacts can be found a report by T. Litman (2016) entitled Evaluating 
Transportation land-use impacts. This section aims at merging the sustainable transport theory and 
the transport land-use analysis to clarify the relevance of the latter for the former. 
It will present first the link between the spatial assessment of transport and the sustainable 
transport theory, taking as a sustainability framework the sustainable passenger transport 
framework elaborated by Holden et al. (2013). It will later summarize the very few works done on 
spatial assessment of transport so far, namely a study by Gössling et al. (2016) of the transport space 
supply in Fribourg, Germany, and a more extensive spatial assessment of the Parisian region by 
Héran & Ravalet (2008). The selection of the indicator set, as the natural continuation of the 
conceptualization, will however only be revealed in section B, subsection I (page 41), entitled 
“Indicator set definition”. As urban passenger transport in India is the context of the assessment, the 
last chapter of the present section will introduce the local urban transport challenges in India, and 
more specifically in the case-study city of Rajkot, Gujarat, and underline the specific pertinence of a 
spatial assessment accordingly. 
 
II. 
Why is a spatial assessment relevant for sustainable transport? 
Spatial assessment in the sustainable passenger transport framework 
In Sustainable passenger transport: Back to Brundtland, Holden et al. (2013) introduce a definition of 
sustainable development based on four equality important dimensions derived from the Brundtland 
Report. Sustainable development should safeguard long-term ecological sustainability, satisfy basic 
transport needs, promote intra and inter-generational equity (Holden et al., 2013). Parallel to the 
sustainable development arena, they introduce a sustainable passenger transport arena based on 
four fundamental dimensions: sustainable passenger transport activities must not threaten long-
term ecological sustainability, sustainable passenger transport should satisfy basic transport needs, 
promote intra-generational transport equity as well as intergenerational transport equity. This 
strong sustainability perspective on passenger transport will serve as a structure to analyse how the 
==PAGE:20==
spatial assessment method provides insightful sustainability assessment elements of urban transport 
systems. 
 
1. The ecological footprint of transport 
The Ecological Footprint is the indicator suggested by Holden et al., (2013) to evaluate how long-
term ecological sustainability is safeguarded. The Ecological Footprint stands for the area required 
to produce the resources people consume, the area occupied by infrastructure, and the area of 
forest required for sequestering CO2 not absorbed by the ocean (WWF International, 2012). In terms 
of passenger transport, the ecological footprint is made up of three elements:  
- 
the forestry land area required to sequester all the CO2 direct emissions,  
- 
the energy and forest land area associated to energy and materials used in the production, 
construction and maintenance of transportation infrastructures,  
- 
the land used for road infrastructure (de Nazelle et al., 2012) 
An analysis of the space used by urban transport infrastructure, if coupled with the two other 
components mentioned gives an approximation of the ecological footprint of a given urban 
transport network. Since data on the ecological footprint for transport is not readily available, 
Holden et. al recommend using energy consumption for passenger transport. The ecological 
footprint is a strong sustainability indicator, and a spatial assessment furthers the requirements 
established by Holden et. al by going beyond the energy consumption indicator suggested up to the 
core definition of sustainability. The Ecological Footprint tracks indeed directly the Earth’s bio 
capacity and regenerative capacity, which makes it the ideal indicator for safeguarding long-term 
ecological sustainability. 
 
2. The road space: safety and basic transport needs 
Urban public space is a contested space (Gössling et al., 2016). The arrival of cars in the public space 
changed the balance between modes on street. Its high speed represents a danger for other modes, 
and the development of individual cars as the main transport mode transformed the equilibrium of 
traditional streets into roads suited to accommodate the automobile as the dominant mode.  The 
cohabitation of modes with different speeds on road provokes safety issues. Safety issues imperil 
the purpose of transportation itself whose aim to allow people to satisfy their needs, and in a 
sustainability perspective their “basic needs”.  
A good space allocation for transport should be one that secures the integrity of all travellers. A lack 
of appropriate infrastructures for slower modes increases tensions between modes that need to 
share the same road space, putting thus vulnerable modes in jeopardy. Safe transportation goes 
hand in hand with adequate infrastructure supply, i.e. tailored in an adequate level of service able to 
satisfy transportation needs. If the demand for transport space is higher than the supply, the space 
gap will inevitably increase dangers on road. 
 
 
==PAGE:21==
3. The transport space distribution: intra-generational equity 
Space distribution has been pointed as one of the main dimensions where urban transport injustice 
is apparent (Gössling, 2017). In his article Urban transport justice, Gössling (2017) states that 
“contemporary transport systems are characterized by injustice, as they tend to favour and 
prioritize motorized transport, accepting that considerable environmental and social burdens are 
put on more sustainable forms of transportation, other traffic participants and society as a whole.” 
Intra-generational inequity is manifested in urban transport in unequal exposure to traffic risks, 
pollutants and distribution of space. Taking the case of Sao Paulo, Vasconcellos (2011) shows that 
the consumption of road space is a function of income and social status, with wealthier families 
consuming ten times as much road space as low-income families. This is caused by a higher use of 
cars in the uppermost income parts of the population, correlated with higher mobility (more trips 
travelled and to further distances). Be it spatial or related to other hurdles, the cost of individual 
motorized vehicles is unevenly distributed among the population. Society at large, users and non-
users bear its cost, “creating additional burdens on the poor” (Pendakur, 2011). Income and social 
disparities are very large in developing countries, and poverty a persistent trouble, making the 
equity perspective on transport systems of great relevance. 
The distinction between equity and equality needs also to be underlined. Equality stands for the 
state of being equal in right, status and opportunities (Oxford Dictionary, 2017); whereas equity 
requires that everybody should get what they need, regardless of their personal, physical, mental or 
social circumstances (Vasconcellos, 2011). Equity may thus infer a preferential treatment of 
transport modes that are “environmentally friendly and socially just” (Gössling, 2017). Intra 
generational equity takes into account individual differences among individuals (age, gender, 
income, physical conditions) and their correlated ability to use different transport modes. In spatial 
terms, aiming for equity asks for drastic changes in car-based transport systems as car is the highest 
space consumer mode per capita (Vasconcellos, 2011) and the most polluting one. 
Safety and tension on road space between modes is also related to intra-generational equity as 
vulnerable modes are mostly used by young travellers, old travellers and women (Law, 1999), a 
trend especially significant in India (Ahmad & Puppim de Oliveira, 2016). 
 
4. The economic value of transport space: nature’s intrinsic value and 
inter-generational equity 
Preserving nature’s intrinsic value is mentioned as a secondary dimension by Holden et al. It is 
nevertheless tightly knit to inter-generational equity, as one generation is dependent on the 
inheritance left by the previous one. There are thus here two points to consider: the scarcity of 
space, and the long-term implications of transport space allocation strategies. 
Space is per se a limited resource on the earth, hardly renewable, that requires a careful stewardship 
in dense urban areas where land is especially limited. Making good use of space is in a way part of 
preserving nature’s intrinsic value, and as such preserving it for future generations. In a paper 
entitled Urban sustainable transport indicators for global comparison, minimizing the use of land is 
stated as an intrinsic definition of sustainable transportation systems (Haghshenas & Vaziri, 2012). 
“The consumption of scare urban space by private cars will remain a crucial negative point in the 
==PAGE:22==
sustainable city” (Vlastos, Barbopoulos, & Milakis, 2003), all the more so as, on the contrary to air 
pollution that can be alleviated thanks to technological innovations, the urban space will remain the 
scarce resource in the sustainable city of the future. 
Transports are a fundamental activity that undeniably requires space. One knows nevertheless that 
infrastructure choices can lead to long-term lock-ins, and the vicious circle of car dependency. 
Adapting the road supply as the car traffic increases gives birth to induced traffic which in turn 
generates urban sprawl (Dimitriou, 2006). Figure 1 (T. Litman, 2016) shows the causal chain linking 
the rise of car ownership with urban sprawl. Increasing car ownership leads to a car-oriented 
transport planning, stigmatizing and reducing the use of other modes, fostering the path towards 
suburbanization, a car-based land use planning provoking urban sprawl and in turn increasing again 
car ownership. Intergenerational equity requires carefully ponder the transport land-use impacts 
and potential lock-ins associated.

Calculating the land-use cost associated with transport networks showcases the value of urban 
space and underlines its worth. Land has an economic value, and its use in transportation can either 
represent a cost for society or a benefit. In this regards, Shoup (2005) has estimated that providing 
free car parking represents a public subsidy in the profit of car drivers that would amount to 
US$127-374 billion per year. On the contrary, Copenhagen’s investments in biking infrastructures 
has been deemed as a socially beneficial investment for Denmark’s national welfare (Gössling, 
2017).

III. 
What is concretely a spatial assessment for transport?  
Literature review of spatial assessments 
 
1. Spatial assessment of transport networks in the literature 
The transport-related space assessment has not yet been extensively studied. 
The major work found is a French case-study on the Parisian region by Héran & Ravalet (2008). This 
study approaches the spatial assessment with a space demand and supply logic. The space supply is 
simply the space dedicated to the transport network in the city, and the space demand is the space 
used by travellers in their trips. The first varies very little with time – only when new infrastructures 
are built – and the second changes greatly with speed and time, being at its maximum during peak 
hours and its minimum at night time. Héran & Ravalet therefore resort to a space-time indicator, in 
m2.hour, able to measure the time and space used by moving vehicle. The space-time indicator had 
been introduced by Marchand (1984), an engineer for the RATP, in an attempt to compare the 
spatial efficiency of different modes as well as to monetize the space consumption. He ends up with 
an estimation of the utilization cost of transport infrastructure by different modes for the same trip 
length. The main cost components taken are the infrastructure construction costs and the land-use 
values. 
The space supply calculation relies on an easier methodology and has been implemented in a few 
cities from the western world. Apel (2000) namely did a comparative study on the structure of the 
cities of Oldenburg, Germany; Delft, The Netherlands, and Denver, USA. More recently, a case-study 
==PAGE:24==
of Fribourg, Germany has been implemented by Gössling et al. (2016) with an emphasis on the 
differences in space allocation within modes and the fairness issues implied, as well as a historicist 
approach on how transport infrastructures have been shaped together with the city’s development. 
The work undertaken by Héran & Ravalet will be more precisely presented in this section, as the 
overall framework of their methodology is very close to the one elaborated in Rajkot’s spatial 
assessment. 
The method developed by Gössling et al. will also serve as a comparative framework of this project, 
and both the methodology and the main outcomes of Fribourg’s case-study will thus also be 
introduced.

Parking space-time demand 
The parking times have been established based on travel diaries from households surveys, taking

Travel diaries specified departure and arrival time, as well as origin and destination locations. This 
enables to compute the distance travelled and then the corresponding speed, which is done for

The space consumed by a moving vehicle is a function of speed, and Héran & Ravalet use the

design of the road type required to accommodate vehicles of different speeds (median in-between 
lanes, emergency lanes, etc.). The final formulation for the dynamic surface is given in equation (1) 
and its formulation steps are detailed in Appendices.

IV. 
Why is a spatial assessment relevant especially in the context of 
urban transport in India? 
 
Urban transport is at the core of the environmental and developmental challenge India is facing. This 
chapter will give background information concerning urban transport in India, and then present the 
city of Rajkot, which has been chosen as a case-study for this project. In a third section, the Low-
Carbon Comprehensive Mobility Plans will be introduced more specifically. 
 
1. Motorization and economic development 
Transport and the economy are intertwined, making transport a key factor in a country’s 
development. Mobility is universally acknowledged as one of the most important prerequisites to 
economic growth and improved standard of living (Pendakur, 2011). Several studies have shown that 
as income increases, mobility - defined as passenger kilometres travelled – increases (Schafer & 
Victor, 2000). Vehicle ownership is positively correlated to income increase as well (T. A. Litman, 
2013), and there is a positive linear correlation between GDP per capita and motorization per capita 
mobility (Dhar & Shukla, 2014). Banister & Berechman (2001) argue though that there is no rational 
argument that would make the positive relationship between transport and economic growth a 
necessity. They advocate for decoupling mobility and economic growth, based on efficiency and 
environmental reasons. 
The rampant motorization is accompanied in India by several economic and environmental 
concerns. On an economic level, as the country’s oil resources heavily rely on importations, 
increased motorization impairs its financial stability (Dhar & Shukla, 2014). Local pollution is another 
serious impediment of motorization, and one third of the cities the most polluted globally are India 
==PAGE:32==
cities (World Health Organization, 2014). Besides local pollution, CO2 emissions have a global effect 
on climate change. Transport accounts for 14% of energy-related CO2 emissions (Indian Network for 
Climate Change Assessment, Ministry of Environment and forest, & Government of India, 2010) and 
urban mobility has therefore been identified as a major CO2 mitigation wedge in India (Dhar & 
Shukla, 2014).  
Car owners represent the wealthier share of the population, and pedestrians and cyclists are walking 
or cycling by default, as those modes are the only affordable. However, transport infrastructure 
conditions are not adequate for those captive users who face severe safety issues, which can 
prevent them to travel at all. In 2011, pedestrians and cyclists represent half of the mode share in 
Rajkot (Munshi et al., 2014). The size of the “captive users” category is significant, and may foresee a 
corresponding noteworthy latent demand for transport. Transport strategies focused around NMT 
and PT development would thus benefit a large share of the population, expectedly larger than the 
current number of pedestrians and cyclists, hence impacting positively mobility and accessibility of 
the urban populations. An increased accessibility of a large population share, if combined with 
economic externalities such as the availability of high quality labour force and a lively local economy 
can also be a major lever of economic development (Banister & Berechman, 2001). Increasing the 
level of service for this category of travellers would thus be of two main positive outcomes: 
- 
Unlocking the mobility needs of captive travellers and thereby answering a latent transport 
demand with all the economic impacts involved 
- 
Reducing the environmental pressure caused by the necessary use of motorized transports 
 
It is in the context of high environmental pressure that LCMPs have been introduced. The spatial 
assessment is a co-benefit analysis that will help understand the spatial impacts of the different 
transport scenarios. A sustainable transport development in India is one that would empower the 
current captive users as well as contributing to low-carbon development, and the spatial 
investigation intends to make the case for it. 
 
The equity question is fundamental underlying question especially in India, where the current 
captive users, i.e. pedestrians and cyclists are in majority young people, females, and less affluent 
socio-economic groups, as will be presented for Rajkot’s case. The spatial assessment will tackle the 
equity question by analysing how the space for transport is distributed among modes, and thus 
who the transport space consumers are. 
Last but not least, the spatial analysis takes an important signification when one is reminded that 
Indian cities are particularly dense cities. Half of the 20 densest cities in the world are Indian cities 
(Demographia, 2017). This makes space in general a scarce resource in the city, and as stated earlier 
if technology thrives to provide innovative solution for cleaner energy sources, suggestions for a 
wiser space use have received less attention (Vlastos et al., 2003).  Environmental impacts of 
individual motorized transportation are well known, but spatial consequences less so, even though 
cars and motor wheelers consume a considerable amount of space in the city, namely parking. The 
scarcity of urban space calls for its optimal use, and that is why the efficiency framework has been 
==PAGE:33==
selected for the spatial analysis. The goal of the efficiency spatial analysis is to determine which 
transport development strategy is the least space consuming.

3. The Low-carbon Comprehensive Mobility Plans 
The low-carbon Comprehensive Mobility Plans are the outcome of a three-year project funded by 
the International Climate Initiative of the German Government and in line with India’s National 
Action Plan on Climate Change established in 2008. Its purpose is to complete the Comprehensive 
Mobility Plans (CMPs) designed by the Indian Ministry of Urban Development (Ministry of Urban 
Development and Asian Development Bank, 2008) which have been unable to provide guidelines for 
sustainable transport strategies (TERI, 2011), both in terms of social and environmental

applications” of the data collected by furthering the analysis beyond the strict CO2 emissions

V. 
Conceptualization synthesis 
A spatial assessment of transport, not pretending to be a comprehensive sustainability assessment, 
nevertheless provides insights on decisive points at the intersection of transport and sustainable 
development. A sustainable passenger transport system should safeguard long-term ecological 
sustainability, satisfy basic transport needs, promote intra and inter-generational equity (Holden et 
al., 2013). Based on this sustainable transport definition, four transport space use impacts can be 
derived.  Firstly, the transport spatial assessment should keep watch of the global transport 
ecological footprint. Secondly, account for a space supply that would enable safe travels for all 
modes. Thirdly, promote an equitable space distribution. Finally it should foster an efficient use of 
urban space, mindful of its worth and scarcity thus preventing entering a long-term car dependency 
urban planning pattern.  
Two notable spatial assessments have been undertaken in the previous transport literature. One a 
work on Paris, France, by Héran & Ravalet (2008) which tackles both the space supplied for transport 
in the city and the space consumed by travellers, and the other a study of Freiburg, Germany, by 
Gössling et al. (2016) who assesses the transport space supplied for different modes.  If Héran & 
==PAGE:39==
Ravalet’s work is mostly an economic analysis aimed at assessing the efficient transport space 
organisation, Gössling et al. adopt a transport justice approach and resort to an urbanistic 
disucussion on the historibal development of the different neighbourhoods to explain how the space 
has been distributed among modes. 
For Paris, an overall value of 27% of the urban space allocated to transport is found, and for 
Freiburg, an average value of 19% on the four quarters studied. In both cities, the majority of the 
transport space supply is dedicated to roads and public parking. Measuring the transport space 
consumed has been done through the use of a space-time indicator in Paris, revealing that the vast 
majority of the transport space-time consumed corresponds to a parking time. Parking occupancy 
ratios are high (71%) in Paris, whereas road and footpath occupancy ratios are much lower, 
respectively 4.5% and 0.2%, even if Paris is considered as both a congested and walking city. This is 
mostly due to the fact that infrastructure supply is continuously available on the overall transport 
network, whereas transport space demand is concentrated on specific road segments and organized 
in brief traffic peak times. On the contrary, the parking space demand is temporally more 
homogenous, as individual vehicles spend most of the time parking. 
The urban transport development in India is characterized by a lingering increase in individual 
motorized vehicle ownership, which causes environmental, social, and economic burdens. 
Motorization is accompanied by a rise in CO2 emissions, with global consequences on climate 
changes, and local pollution implications. Moreover, current transport infrastructure organisation is 
threatening the safety of vulnerable modes, whom are mostly captive users as they resort to 
walking or cycling by default. As India relies on importing oil, motorization is also specifically 
jeopardizing its financial stability. In this context, Low-carbon Comprehensive Mobility Plans (LCMPs) 
have been designed for several Indian cities, as the outcome of a larger project on Low-Carbon 
Transport for India undertaken by the United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP) and 
implemented by UNEP DTU Partnership in Denmark and several partners in India. LCMPs suggest 
and compare four urban transport development strategies: business as usual (BAU scenario), land-
use improvements (LU), provision of non-motorized transport infrastructures (NMT) and public 
transport facilities (PT). 
The spatial assessment is a co-benefit analysis that will help understand the spatial impacts of the 
different transport scenarios. A sustainable transport development in India is one that would 
empower the current captive users as well as contributing to low-carbon development, and the 
spatial investigation intends to make the case for it by:  
- 
Analysing how the space for transport is distributed among modes 
- 
Determining which transport development strategy is the least space consuming.

3) Methodology limitations 
The operationalization process for the road space supply is rather approximate as it is based on 
average road widths data multiplied with the number of lanes. The actual data of on-ground surveys 
used for the LCMP regarding road width proved not precise enough to be used as such, and the 
methodology resorted therefore more to a modelling approach to calculate the space allocated to 
roads.  
The results are certainly less precise than the operational mode selected by Gössling et. al (2016), 
who focused on smaller geographical areas and accounted for a detailed record of all the transport 
infrastructure available. The methodology chosen for Rajkot is closer in essence to the city-wide 
assessment implemented by Héran & Ravalet (2008) which based its space supply evaluation on the 
road-type repertory and their speed limit, assigning an average road width to each road category. 
The methodology selected is however precise enough for the fairness and efficiency scope 
adopted. 
Besides the space supply, an investigation of the space demand for different transport 
developments needs to be completed to back up the previous recommendations and smartly hone 
the spatial assessment. 
 
2. Space demand 
1) Operationalization methodology 
i. 
Principle and data used 
A four-stage model has been designed to forecast the traffic in the LCMP scenarios for 2031. The 
main data available from this model are OD matrixes with the daily traffic flows for motor vehicles, 
cars, auto rickshaws, walking, cycling and public transport for 2011, BAU 2031 and the three 
alternative transport scenarios (LU, NM, PT). The traffic flows are in daily passenger trips and the 
origin and destinations are the different TAZ. 
A travel diary has collected the daily traveling behaviours of a sample of Rajkot’s inhabitants. The 
sample size is of 11100 individuals. 
==PAGE:56==
The road network includes actual speed of cars on road, resulting from on-ground speed surveys. 
Outputs from the transport model have been the primary data source, with some inputs from the 
travel diary on on-ground speed surveys when needed to build the specific chosen indicators. 
The methodology for the space-time consumption distinguishes the time spent traveling, called on-
road time, and the time when vehicles are standing still, designated as parking time. The analysis is 
undertaken by mode, by ward and by scenario. The detailed methodology is explained in 
Appendices.  
ii. 
Space-time used on road 
The general indicator required to estimate the space used on road is a space-time indicator that will 
be declined by transport function (parking / on-road), by mode and by ward. 
The OD matrix served as a basis to run the fourth stage of the model again (traffic assignment) to 
get the localisation of the flows on road. A simple assignment methodology was chosen, taking the 
traveling time as a proxy for travel cost. Moreover, the traffic assignment was done following an “or-
all-nothing” methodology, meaning that all traffic was assigned to the shortest paths, as if free flow 
speed was available on each road. In reality, flow and speed are correlated since the road has a 
limited capacity, and the shortest path will thus differ given the assigned flow. More accurate 
modelling methods have been developed in the transport model field (iterative models with 
equilibrium states) but for the sake of this spatial assessment, an all-or-nothing assignment was 
deemed precise enough to describe the space used by urban passenger transportation activities. 
Results have been aggregated by wards, which makes up for the imprecision of an all-or-nothing 
traffic assignment. When free flow speed is altered by too high a vehicle road occupancy, travellers 
tend indeed to choose as a second shortest path a new road in the close neighbourhood. One can 
then assume that the space used on this new path would remain in the same ward.

3) Methodology limitations 
The methodology developed has some precision limitations on several levels: 
i. 
Space-time used on-road 
a. Effective space used on-road and vehicle speed 
The average speed over the network has been taken to calculate the average mean effective space 
consumed by each vehicle based on the study realized by N. Y. Cao and K. Sano (2012). 
First, taking the average speed over the network is rather a generic approach, which may raise some 
accuracy issues when calculating the time spent on road, as the time spent travelling has been 
defined as the distance divided by the average speed 
Second, the effective space taken on road is also significantly dependant on the speed of the 
vehicle type considered, and using an average speed for the network does not take those differences 
into account. Figure 30 illustrates the relationship between the speed and the space used, as defined 
by N. Y. Cao and K. Sano. 
==PAGE:69==
 
Figure 30: Mean effective space used on road as a function of speed – based on (N. Y. Cao, K. Sano, 2012) 
Taking the average speed over the network may have occasioned imprecisions geographically, but as 
results are either aggregated on the ward or city level, one can hope  for the geographical 
aggregation to make up for local imprecisions. 
However, the choice has been made to stick with the effective space calculation suggested by N. Y. 
Cao, K. Sano, as it builds on a South Asian city context (Hanoi, Vietnam), characterized by a mixed 
traffic flow with a high presence of motorcycles. The very low space used by motorcycles (in blue on 
Figure 30), lower than the space used for cyclists (green curve), is nevertheless questionable, as 
bicycles are in average a little smaller than motorcycles. This trend will be coounterbalanced in the 
next chapter on occupancy ratios by applying Héran & Ravalet’s (2008)  space use parameters and 
comparing the obtained the results. 
b. Time on-road calculation 
To calculate the time on-road, the average speed on Rajkot has been taken, which is, as mentioned, 
a first source of inaccuracy. 
Moreover, no congestion phenomenon has been taken into account, which can play a major role on 
the time spent on-road. In this regard, Héran & Ravalet’s methodology prove more rigorous since 
the travel dairies include congestion phenomenon. 
Thirdly, the distance travelled has been defined as the centre of the origin traffic analysis zone 
(TAZ) to the centre of destination TAZ. In their methodology, Héran & Ravalet insert detour 
coefficients to have a stricter estimation of the distance travelled than merely the distance as the 
crow flies. They assessed the real distance travelled to be 15 to 25% higher than the sheer origin-
destination stretch. 
==PAGE:70==
ii. 
Space-time used parking 
a. Parking time based on commuting travel patterns 
The commuting pattern that has been chosen to ease the calculation may not be the most precise 
assessment. Basing the spatial assessment on a large-scale travel diary as has been done by Héran & 
Ravalet enable to avoid the commuting modelling. The trips made are indeed not all pendular 
home-work round trips, but may be of other nature, such as educational or shopping trips. This 
explains why the parking duration found at the primary destination are lower than a working day (6 
hours for motorcycles and bicycles, 8 hours for cars).It is however likely that people do more than 
one round-trip per day, which would slightly alter the parking time model. 
b. Parking fleet size for cars, motor-cycles and bicycles 
One of the major shortcoming of the space consumption methodology is that only vehicle traveling 
have been counted in the number of vehicle parking. It has been assumed that if people would 
possess an individual motorized vehicle, they would also use it for their daily travel. Bicycle 
ownership is less of an issue as they are comparatively to motorized vehicles minor space-time 
consumers. This assumption relating to vehicle ownership and vehicle usage would currently hold 
rather accurate in the current Indian context, but a more meticulous study on parking could allow 
deciding whether there are significantly more vehicles present in the city than vehicle traveling. This 
is especially true as people may own both a car and a motor cycle. What the scenarios concern, even 
if people shift from motorized vehicle to NMT or PT, they may still own a car that is standing parking. 
This consideration has been eluded from the model, and may increase the parking space-time 
consumed. It is interesting nevertheless to observe that the space-time consumed – and pkm 
travelled - by motorized vehicles in 2011 and in the 2031 PT scenario is expected to be of a 
comparable size. This suggests a PT scenario capturing mainly new urban dwellers by deterring 
them to acquire a private motorized vehicle, and opt rather for NMT or PT modes, as well as 
empowering base-year pedestrians and cyclists who would otherwise have been required to 
acquire a private motorized vehicle, or interested in doing so as their means would have allowed 
it. If this assumption proves true, the fleet size for cars and motor-cycles would not increase 
significantly. On the contrary, the BAU scenario implies a necessary increase in motorized vehicle 
use and ownership, with the important spatial repercussions described in this assessment that it 
would entail. The spatial assessment showed therefore two important points: there is a possibility 
to optimize the space required for transport in the city, and neglecting the opportunity would have 
major spatial inefficiencies and unfairness implications. 
c. Parking localization for buses and auto rickshaws 
The forecasted location for bus hangars being yet unknown, the choice of allocated the parking 
space to the major nodes is also questionable. Similarly, the assumption made regarding the 
distribution of the parking for auto rickshaws relies on the hypothesis that the 2011 repartition will 
remain for the 2031 scenarios. Be that as it may, given the size of the fleet, these estimations do 
not play a major role in shaping the outcomes.

iii. 
General limitations 
a. Parameters 
Occupancy ratios 
The vehicle occupancy ratios taken for Rajkot are the one suggested by the literature for

occupancy ratios raised by 20%. The two latter configurations proved to respectively increase the 
average road space used by 15% and decrease it by 15%. The first modifications did not significantly 
change the results. The assessment does no pretend to provide absolute values; the sensitivity of 
the model can therefore be considered reliable enough to lead a comparative examination, as has 
been done in the interpretation. 
b. Methodology conceptualisation 
Forecasting assumptions in LCMPs:  mode-choice and infrastructure supply 
The overall model used in the spatial assessment takes ground in the transport model build for 
Rajkot’s LCMP. Numerous assumptions have been made regarding the population growth forecast, 
land-use transitions, vehicle ownership, and mode-choices namely. Discussing the accuracy of 
assumptions made in the LCMP transport model goes over the framework of this study. An 
importance point to underline nevertheless is the relationship between mode-choice and 
infrastructure supply, which in the LCMP has been defined thanks to a stated preference survey. The 
surveys asked which mode the interviewee would choose if new transport infrastructures were 
provided such as NMT or PT infrastructure. The answers have been used to forecast future mode-
choice distributions. The reliability of the methodology used for mode-choice projections is 
questionable, but there is no forecasting without assumptions, and stated preference surveys are a 
==PAGE:72==
traditional projection technique. State-of-the-art stated preference surveys have shown good 
agreement with reality (Tillema, 2007). The accuracy could be improved by including benchmark 
cases where infrastructures have been supplied and changes in mode share observed.  
Space consumption VS space demand 
The space demand calculated merely fell back on the scenario elaborated in the LCMPs. The term 
“demand” may not be the most appropriate, as what has truly been assessed is “the space that will 
be used in the LCMPs scenario”. There may be a transport demand not captured by the LCMPs 
scenarios, namely still a latent demand for transport of people having yet basic travelling needs 
unmet, even in the most comprehensive LCMP. Mobility of poorer people leaving in the outskirt of 
the city or elderly mobility may be examples of latent transport demand, which would result in 
transport latent space demand as well. 
All or nothing assignment 
The geographical distribution of the space consumed relies on an “all-or-nothing” traffic flow 
assignment, which is less accurate than iterative assignments often adopted in traffic models. The 
“all-or-nothing” assignment stage implemented does not include capacity constraints and thus 
potential congestion problems. As this study does not focus on a traffic flow analysis, the precision 
level of the results provided by an “all-or-nothing” assignment can still be considered suitable. 
Eluding freight 
The scope has been set on urban passenger transport, both in the present study and in the 
literature. Freight transportation is also a space consuming activity, and further studies could try to 
assess its footprint. 
 
Now that the spatial investigation has tackled both the transport space demand and the transport 
space supply, a combined examination through road occupancy ratios can be addressed.  
 
3. Occupancy ratios 
1) Operationalization methodology 
As no information is provided for the space supply in the future scenarios, the road occupancy 
analysis will only be completed for the base year 2011. The comparative framework will thus not 
lean on scenario comparison anymore but the discussion of the results will be held in the light of 
Paris’ case-study by Héran & Ravalet (2008) . 
Road occupancy ratios can be calculated for 2011, when both space demand and space supply are 
available. The road occupancy is defined as the ratio of the space demand on the space supply. A 
high occupancy ratio stands for a high demand divided by a low supply, and vice-versa. As reminded 
on Table 24, the two indicators chosen for the road occupancy ratios will be analysed by mode and 
by ward. Since the transport space supply is made of footpaths, roads and public parking lots, the 
analysis by mode will focus on footpaths occupancy ratios, i.e. the space used by pedestrians divided 
by the space provided for pedestrians, and the roadway occupancy ratios, i.e. the space used by all 
modes excluding pedestrians divided by the road space available. The parking occupancy ratios have 
==PAGE:73==
not been chosen as indicators for this study as the parking situation would require more data and a 
more precise analysis, including namely private parking and parking in buildings that play a major 
role in the parking organisation. Moreover, the infrastructure inventories realized have counted 
almost as much space used for informal parking on road as space dedicated to formal parking lots, 
which already is a major indicator for the lack of parking spaces.

4. Synthesis 
1) Direct indicators results and methodology 
The space supply analysis pinpointed some infrastructural deficiencies, both in the nature of the 
infrastructure and their spatial distribution. The difficulty of conducting a transport space supply as 
==PAGE:83==
what has been done in Gössling et al. ( 2016) lies in the infrastructure categorization. The mode-
based infrastructures type definition suggested by the literature is inapplicable to Rajkot’s case, 
where there is little infrastructure diversity. The real infrastructural issue is found in the road-type 
categorization, between metalled and unmettaled road, as well as in the road-use, with multiple 
encroachment-types on road, such as hawkers and unauthorized parking. The space supply 
methodology would therefore require some adjustment to better fit local challenges. It nevertheless 
underlines important trends with regards to fairness and efficiency. It leads indeed to conclude that 
as of 2011, formal transport space allocation is mono-categorical based on roads, and following an 
all-or-nothing geographical distribution, with areas where several type of infrastructures are 
provided, and other where no proper transport space can be seen. The transport space supply 
analysis as implemented also invites practical recommendation by locating the neediest areas. 
The methodology could be made more robust as broad assumptions have been considered regarding 
the road width. Moreover, the interpretative framework proves sensitive as all the benchmark cities 
portray very different contextual environments. If the results were to be interpreted independently 
of the indicator-set or other larger comparative framework, the space supply indicator would benefit 
from a thinner conceptualization work on the optimal urban space that should be allocated to 
transport. 
 
The space demand analysis proves more fitting to the overall delimitation and scope, and more 
sensible in the comparative investigation than the space supply examination. 
It enables conclusions on which scenario type is the most space-efficient and the fairest, namely the 
PT scenario. The indicator also clearly demonstrated the decisive role of parking in spatial efficiency. 
It has been beneficial in highlighting as well that the scenarios are accompanied by a shift of the 
mode categories currently using the majority of the on-road space towards NMT and PT modes. The 
space-time consumption analysis disclosed which modes were the more space efficient (buses) and 
which were the least ones (individual cars). The geographical repartition of the space released would 
concern the whole city in the PT scenario. It allowed thus to conclude that the space consumed by 
transports can be made fairer and more efficient if the transport strategies shift from BAU to LCMPs. 
In terms of methodology, the indicator building process is based on a detailed and precise model. 
However, it relies also on the transport model built for the LCMP and the related forecasting 
methodology which implies some uncertainties. Some of the parameters choices can be discussed as 
well. The dynamic use of space definition for example varies greatly in the literature and involves 
methodological choices as to whether considering the theoretical space required for vehicle (Héran 
& Ravalet’s approach) or the empirical one (the present methodology). The overall space 
consumption calculation methodology could have been made more precise in some of the 
parameters, resorting to the average speed or the average parking time per mode for example is a 
broad approximation. Héran & Ravalet’s evaluation is more precise on this point as it is shaped by 
travel diary data, contrary to the present methodology relying on a transport model. The strength of 
the transport model methodology though is to go beyond the current transport patterns by 
including space consumption forecasts. 
 
==PAGE:84==
The occupancy ratio adds another layer to the previous analysis. The comparison with Paris makes 
the scale of pedestrian infrastructural needs plain, and showcases how the current transport 
network favors motorized vehicle by granting those a higher level of service and accomodating the 
demand with preciser geographical care than for pedestrian. 
What the occupancy ratio building methodology regards, as a ratio of the demand and the supply it 
also combines the methodological limitations of both indicators. However, it provides ample ground 
to lead an analysis correlating demand and supply and shedding light on specific gaps. The 
interpretation is nevertheless still rather difficult as only one study of its kind has been conducted 
previously, and within a very different context. The comparative framework used for the 
interpretation has thus been useful in getting a better grasp on what the space-time occupancy ratio 
entails, knowing for example that contrary to what can be expected, even in congested cities road 
occupancy ratios are very low. Be that as it may, a robust comparison ask for the same indicator-
building methodology, namely in terms of dynamic space definition. The attention paid to the 
different contexts enabled to nuance the interpretation of the value found, but road occupancy 
ratio, as an average daily value, did not turn out very compelling in its ability to answering the 
research question regarding space use efficiency. The fairness wedge was better encompassed 
thanks to the comparison of occupancy ratios of footpath and roadways.

First, the nature of the assessment differs, as for Freiburg only the transport space supply is 
considered, and for Rajkot and Paris both the space supplied and consumed by transport is included. 
Space demand estimation calls for the use of a space-time indicator. 
Then, the geographical scope, and thus the precision level vary. Gössling et al. focus on a 
meticulous analysis of the different spaces supplied for transport in selected areas, accounting in 
total for 9km2. Héran & Ravalet’s scope is wider, as the analysis is extended to almost 2000 km2 and 
divided into Paris ant its two suburban regions. For Rajkot , an area of 98 km2 is considered, with the 
precision scale and geographical comparison based on 23 wards. The first study present a very 
accurate description of the space supply in the city, whereas the two others resort to a methodology 
closer to a modeling procedure, with the aim not only of comparing the supply between the 
different areas defined but moreover to analize it combined with the space demand.  
The general on-road space categories are thus also more importantly developed in Gössling et al. 
methodology. The area distinction for Rajkot raised local issues as the transport infrastructures 
categories and spatial distinction are of a different kind, more concerned about the presence or 
absence of proper roads, and the road encroachement issues. The space supply inventory for Rajkot 
and Freiburg only included outdoor public parkings, whereas Héran & Ravalet made a clear 
distinction between the different parking categories, comprising both public and private parking. 
For the space demand analysis, the methodology developed for Rajkot included public transport and 
auto rickshaws, whereas the methodology elaborated for Paris focused on motorized two-wheeler, 
car, cyclists and pedestrians. The space demand assessment has majoritarily been based on an 
==PAGE:87==
extrapolation of a household surveys in Paris, including detailed travel diaries. Rajkot’s transport 
space demand evaluation relies on the output of a four-stage transport model elaborated for the 
LCMPs. The four-stage model uses household surveys as input and implements a traffic forecast 
calculation for four transport development strategies. The methodology elaborated for Rajkot may 
thus contain some imprecisions due to the traffic projections estimation, whose Paris’ case are 
exempt from. However, the trade-off allows future space consumption predictions, which have 
applicable policy support implications. 
The combined space supply and demand investigation is the most detailed in Héran & Ravalet’s 
work, which calculates occupancy ratios also for parkings. Occupancy ratios have also been 
estimated for Rajkot, but their interpretation suffers from the nature of the infrastructure supply’s 
imprecision. 
All in all,  Gössling et al. opt for an urbanistic approach and provide a detailed inquiry of the features 
and singularities of the different transport spaces, and quantify their supply. Space distribution 
fairness is a major concern in their framework. Héran & Ravalet resort to an economic approach by 
associating space supply and space demand in occupancy ratios. The geographical scope is wide, and 
Héran & Ravalet aim at decealing spatial inefficiencies on a network-level. The quest for an efficient 
spatial design of transport system is framing their study. The analysis elaborated for Rajkot 
attempted to emcompass the two previous scopes of spatial justice and efficiency, and building 
over it predictions for LCMPs performances in terms of space distribution fairness and economic 
efficiency. 
The strength of the spatial assessment undertaken for Rajkot is to include forecasts for future 
transport development strategies, and to reach to environmental concerns as well by incorporating 
low-carbon considerations – indirectly in the direct spatial assessment thourgh LCMP scenarios, and 
more obviously thanks to the derived indicators that will be developped in the next section. 
Reducing carbon emissions resulting from urban transport has indeed been the primary purpose of 
the LCMP, and the indirect indicators will expand on the relationship between space used for 
transport and CO2 emissions, as well as sharpening the economic approach by a direct monetary 
evaluation of the space consumed by transports. 
 
III. 
Derived indicators: methodology, results and interpretation 
In this section, the two selected indirect indicators will be presented: the economic assessment of 
the space used in the different scenarios, and the ecological footprint of transport, which combined 
the size of the transport network in the city (physical footprint) and the land required to absorb 
transport-related CO2 emissions (energy footprint). The two derived indicator are an application and 
an extension of the spatial assessment elaborated through the direct indicator in the previous 
section. If the direct spatial assessment was more of an analytical nature, the derived indicators may 
rather target decision makers who are sensitive to quantitative framework tackling budget allocation 
and CO2 emissions. 
==PAGE:88==
The two indicators will be described first introducing the operationalization methodology and 
secondly the results and interpretation, to then discuss the methodology limitations. The section will 
end with a synthesis of the strengths and weaknesses of the derived indicators. 
 
1. Economic valuation of the different spatial scenarios 
The first derived indicator chosen is the economic value of the land used by urban passenger 
transport in each scenario. Granting a monetary value to the space used by transport transposes the 
spatial assessment into an economic assessment approach, which can prove helpful in policy design 
and space regulation schemes. 
1) Operationalization methodology 
The economic valuation of the land used for transport in the different scenarios bases its estimation 
on the average rent value for housing in Rajkot as introduced in a technical study entitled Land use-
Transport Integration for Sustainable Urbanism (LUTISU) (Adhvaryu, 2016). The report estimates the 
average dwelling unit size at 56 m1 and with an average monthly rent of around 6800 Indian Rupees 
(INR) which defines a land price of 121 INR per square meters. 
The monetary value of land has been taken as the rental housing value for 2011. 
For the purpose of this economic assessment, the land values for the 2031 scenarios are calculated 
in the 2011 rent value, which serves as a nominal price. It does not take into account the inflation 
phenomenon, neither the differentiation of the land-use (housing and commercial land have 
different values for example), neither the future land-use changes nor the geographical 
differentiations in rent values. The assessment offers thus a basic conversion methodology from the 
spatial value into a monetary term, and excludes land-use prices variations, whether geographical or 
temporal. The space time indicator is given in km2.day, which is converted into m2.month to match 
the monthly rental price per square meter unit, expressed in INR per m2 per month. The figure 
obtained is hence a purely monetary term, described in INR, or million INR.

It is important however to note that only the space consumed by transport has been transferred into 
a monetary term, saying nothing about the space supplied for transport. This raises the complex 
enquiry of the interplay between transport space used and transport space supply. Without opening 
too broad a discussion, one can nevertheless note two points. First, as described previously, in the 
space-time assessment methodology, road occupancy ratios are very low, and much more space is 
required than used. The value of the space dedicated to transport is thus in all scenario higher than 
the value of the space consumed by transports. Secondly, what the comparison between scenarios 
regards, the differences between land-use costs is still likely to remain significant. As described in 
the spatial assessment, the space consumption concerns mainly the parking function, and the 
scenario changes would therefore mainly affect the space-used parking, and in a smaller scale the 
size of the on-road space use (the on-road spatial changes are more of a mode-distributional nature 
than a quantitative one). The parking space used and supplied are undeniably closer in size than the 
road space-use and supply given the fact that vehicles spent the majority of the time parking. This is 
shown by a parking occupancy ratio that will always be much higher than a road occupancy ratio, as 
described by Héran & Ravalet (2008), who found a parking occupancy ratio of 71% in Paris compared 
to a road occupancy ratio of 4.5%. If the space consumed by transport is expected to increase 
importantly, it would also require the provision of wider transport infrastructure, be that parking 
infrastructures or roads, which paves the way for urban sprawl and automobile dependency in the 
long term (Wegener & Fürst, 1999) with the vicious circle of induced traffic, or to risk congestion and 
parking disorganization, with all its economic and environmental burdens. On the contrary, if the 
transport space demand is projected to be lower, the supply can be adjusted accordingly. 
 
Moreover, taking the average rental value for the whole city does not account for differences 
between wards, even though rental values may significantly vary between wards, and the space 
consumed by transport changes significantly as well between wards. An economic valuation by ward 
could spot the areas where the value of the land used by transport is especially high, which is likely 
to be in the wealthier areas, where both a high space for transport is consumed, and high rental 
values are to be found. The rental value geographical distribution may also change by scenario. 
 
Finally, an economic assessment as such should include other components, such as the economic 
impacts of accessibility changes, travel time changes, and socio and environmental criteria. 
 
2. Transport ecological footprint 
The second derived indicator is the ecological space used by transport in each scenario. The spatial 
assessment here goes beyond the infrastructural considerations by integrating the CO2 emissions 
impacts and their own spatial footprint. As described in the methodology, both the direct CO2 
emissions issued from fuel consumption and the indirect CO2 emissions required to construct and 
maintain transport infrastructures are considered in the transport energy footprint. The transport 
ecological footprint is defined by the sum of the transport energy footprint and physical footprint, 
which is the transport space supply. 
 
==PAGE:91==
1) Operationalization methodology 
The ecological footprint of transport is calculated taking into account three elements: 
- 
the “physical footprint”, which is the total space allocated to transport in the city,  
- 
the forestry land required to absorb the transport-related direct CO2 emissions 
- 
the forestry land required to absorb the transport-related indirect CO2 emissions, namely 
the road construction and maintenance (de Nazelle et al., 2012) 
The two last elements are making up to what can be called the “energy footprint” (Chi & Stone, 
2005). 
The “physical footprint” has been calculated in the space supply estimation, and the “energy 
footprint” is calculated based on the model introduced for the LCMPs. The model built to calculate 
direct CO2 emissions for the LCMPs is based on the fuel consumed per vehicle type and per scenario. 
The fuel consumed is calculated thanks to the average fuel efficiency of the vehicle fleet, the vehicle 
fuel mix, and the yearly kilometres travelled per vehicle category and vehicle type. Combined with 
the CO2 emissions factors for different fossil fuels, the fuel consumed leads gives the direct CO2 
emissions per vehicle type and per scenario. 
Wackernagel & Rees (1996) estimated that the indirect carbon emissions for road construction and 
maintenance are equivalent to 45% of the total annual fuel consumed for vehicle travel. Hence, to 
get the combined direct and indirect CO2 emissions, one can multiply the direct CO2 emissions per 
vehicle type and per scenario with the suggested road construction and maintenance adjustment 
factor of 1.45. 
The energy footprint is then calculated dividing the total CO2 emissions by the annual absorption 
capacity of forestry land for the region. The most relevant data found for the absorption capacity of 
the forest land in Gujarat is a value estimated by Rizvi et al. (2016) for the Junagarh district, located 
50 km south of Rajkot. The study established a yearly value of net carbon sequestered of 1.5 tons of 
carbon per hectare, which is equivalent to 550 tons of CO2 per km
2.

both intra-generational, as it is forecasted to exceed the absorbing capacity of the area and may 
thus affect other region which do not directly benefit from Rajkot’s urban transport system, and 
intergenerational, as the CO2 emission excess may provoke long-term irreversible impacts on the 
global ecosystem. Including the transport ecological footprint in the spatial assessment

3) Methodology limitations 
The methodology suffers from some parameters imprecisions. 
First, as all the forecasting indicators developed in this project, it relies on the future mobility 
estimation provided by the transport model, with all the uncertainties that the model entails 
regarding both the modelling methodology and the projection assumptions. 
Another parametrical shortcoming is to have resorted to a global estimation for the road 
construction and maintenance adjustment factor. Since the share of unmettled roads is high in 
Rajkot, it is likely that this value may be lower than the one suggested by Wackernagel & Rees (1996) 
which evaluates road construction and maintenance to be equivalent to 45% of the total annual fuel 
consumed. The forestry land absorption capacity taken is the one approximated by Rizvi et al. 
(2016) for a small local forest area, and the real sequestration capacity of the Rajkot district forest 
may differ. The same study assessed another Gujarat district, Dahod, to have a forestry area with a 
very different absorption capacity. Forest in the Dahod district would indeed have an annual 
sequestration potential of 6.26 tons of carbon per hectare (Rizvi et al., 2016), compared to 1.5 tons 
per hectare, which is the net carbon sequestration capacity estimated for Junagarh, and located 50 
km south of Rajkot. Forestry absorption capacities, besides the biomass and soil carbon 
sequestration potential, depend on the forest exploitation level. In India, carbon sequestration 
potential is evaluated to range between 1.5 to 3.5 tons of carbon per hectare in small holding 
agroforestry systems (Dhyani, Newaj, & Sharma, 2009). 
The fuel mix has also been taken constant for the projection year 2031 even though it will probably 
vary. Similarly, the average vehicle fuel efficiency will certainly increase in the coming years. A more 
precise scenario has been elaborated in Rajkot’s LCMP, the technology scenario, which describes the 
fuel changes with greater details and that could also have proved relevant in calculating the 
transport ecological footprint. For homogeneity concerns, it has however been chosen to limit the 
analysis to the scenario used previously. 
All in all, the calculation of the transport ecological footprint reveals nevertheless compelling in 
unveiling the spatial wedge of CO2 emissions, especially its important scale in comparison with the 
space visibly used by roads in the city. In this approach, reducing CO2 emissions appears therefore to 
be another way of optimizing the global space used by urban transports. 
 
3. Synthesis 
Rather than providing a high accuracy level, the extension of the spatial assessment to derived 
indicators intends to apply the spatial assessment in more traditional frameworks such as the 
ecological footprint and the economic evaluation. Bridging the gap between the novelty of the 
spatial assessment per se and the usual indicator-based planning methodologies aims at improving 
the relevance of the indicators in a decision-making utilization perspective. 
The economic valuation of the different scenarios showed that the PT scenario would enable to 
decrease by half the expected increase of value of the land spent by transports. It highlighted the 
monetary differences of the space used by individual motorized vehicles and other transport modes, 
==PAGE:96==
showing that the value of land that individual motorized vehicles would consume in a BAU scenario 
would be very high. 
The economic analysis permitted to assign a land-use price to each scenario. The main limitations of 
this approach are the hidden geographical disparities between wards and the very basic 
methodology chosen to translate land-use in a monetary term. A proper economic assessment must 
have included more impacts than solely land-use costs. The economic assessment also invited to re-
consider the relationship between space used by transport and space supplied, to better apprehend 
whether the value of the space consumed by transport is significantly different from the space 
supplied for transport or not. On this point, if one notes the importance of parking in the space 
consumed by transports, one also realizes that the considerable spatial expansion of the transport 
system forecasted to occur in the BAU scenario would happen regardless of the road-space that 
would be supplied. The direct land-use costs associated to the 2031 BAU scenario would therefore 
anyhow be very high. 
The calculation of the transport ecological footprint proved to be a convincing indicator to disclose 
the space required to absorb transport-related CO2 emissions. It namely underlined how insignificant 
the space allocated to transport in the city is, when compared to the space necessary to sequester 
the CO2 emissions the system would provoke. The transport activity would nearly reach the natural 
absorbing capacity of the regional area for the base year 2011 and exceed it in all the scenarios 
considered. The PT scenario would nevertheless reduce the ecological footprint forecasted increase 
by 80%. The transport ecological footprint examination raised both spatial efficiency concerns and 
imperative inter and intra generational fairness issues. 
The overall methodology relies on several questionable parametrical assumptions. First, as almost 
all the other indicators, the results are shaped by the output of the transport model elaborated for 
the LCMP, with all the forecasting technique uncertainties involved. Resorting to a global average 
value for the road construction and maintenance adjustment factor, and a very local one for the 
forestry absorption capacity may involve inaccuracies, be that infrastructural or botanical. Taking a 
constant fuel mix and fuel efficiency for the projected year 2031 has clear accuracy shortcomings, 
and to make up for these limitations, an assessment based on the LCMP technological scenario 
would have revealed a sound extension. 
 
IV. 
Operationalization synthesis 
A sustainable transport perspective tied with the transport spatial assessment approach prompted 
the selection of both direct and derived indicators. The chosen indicator-set consisted in five 
indicators: the transport space supply, space demand and occupancy ratios – direct indicators –, the 
economic value of land used by transport and the transport ecological footprint – indirect indicators. 
The space supply disclosed a transport network quasi mono-categorical based on roads, and 
organized in an all-or-nothing spatial distribution logic. The space demand analysis revealed that the 
space consumed by transports can be made fairer and more efficient if the transport strategies shift 
from BAU to LCMPs, by decreasing the overall space consumed in the LCMPs scenario and improving 
the modal space-use repartition balance. Since parking plays a critical role in the space-time 
==PAGE:97==
consumption level, space-time efficiency and fairness enhancements would mainly occur by shifting 
from high space-time parking consuming modes (individual motorized vehicles) to lower ones (NMT 
and PT modes). Occupancy ratios made the scale of pedestrian infrastructural needs manifest, and 
showcased how the current transport network favors motorized vehcile. 
Even if some parametrical choices for the transport space consumption can be discussed, and if its 
results depend also on the reliability of the tranport model designed for the LCMPs, the results and 
their interpretation through several comparative scopes gave rise to numerous findings. The space 
supply analysis outcome proved instructive as well, but would have largely benefited from a prior 
normative conceptualization work on the features of a good transport space organization. 
Occupancy ratios allowed drawing some conclusions, with an overall more convicing argumentation 
for the fairness wedge than the efficiency one. 
Two literature sources have served as central references for the direct indicators: Gössling et al. 
(2016), who endorsed an urbanistic approach with great concern for space distribution fairness, and 
Héran & Ravalet (2008), who opted for an economic approach by estimating the transport space 
supply and space demand and measuring one to another through occupancy ratios. The spatial 
analysis lead for Rajkot’s case combined both frameworks and included forecasts for future 
transport development strategies. 
The economic valuation of the different scenarios demonstrated that the PT scenario would be the 
least costly in terms of value of land used. As the assessment only focused on the land consumed, it 
invited to reponder the difference of scales between the land consumed by transport and the land 
allocated. Since the space consumed is mostly a parking space, the direct land-use costs associated 
to the 2031 BAU scenario would be very high irrespective of the cost of the land allocated to the 
road network. 
The ecological footprint of transport exposed the critical amount of space that would be required to 
sequester CO2 emissions provoked by transport. The transport footprint would exceed the natural 
absorption capacity of the area in all the scenarios considered. The PT scenario would nevertheless 
fundamentally reduce the ecological footprint forecasted. This particular indicator raised both 
spatial efficiency concerns and imperative inter and intra generational fairness issues. 
The two derived indicators rely on some parametrical assumptions, but opened essential discussions 
on the societal and environmental cost of different transport systems.

III. 
How could the feedback be included to reshape and further the 
analysis?  
Opening to policy applications 
The value of land used by transport monetary valuation and the extension to ecological space used 
by transports, the two derived indicators developed previously go in the same sense as what has 
been suggested by stakeholders to make the spatial assessment more applicable. The economic and 
environmental perspectives could be developed further to answer fundamental research questions 
in land use and sustainable transport planning such as:  
- 
Can we establish a direct link between carbon emission and space allocated to urban 
transport?, or  
- 
How to assess the cost of the space used for transport?  
The ecological footprint and the land valuation through rental values are a first step in this direction, 
but those two wedges could be deepened. The economic wedge could be extended to the 
assessment of the overall social cost of the space used by the least space efficient vehicles (cars and 
motorized two-wheelers, especially because of their parking time), to try and define an optimal 
space consumption level for those vehicles. Héran & Ravalet (2008) opened this discussion as well. 
In a policy application perspective, the economic analysis proves helpful when seeking funds for 
transport infrastructure projects for NMT and PT. Parking taxing schemes for example could be an 
economic incentive that could serve demand management purposes and simultaneously be a 
financial resource for the opening of NMT or PT projects. Parking is the main space-time consumer, 
and promoting modes that need a limited parking space, as well as modes that do not need a long 
time parking such as public modes, would be a major lever to improve the transport space-use 
efficiency. Road-pricing schemes could also be fostered as space demand management measures. 
The spatial approach also invites to consider transport space consumption regulation by sheer 
transport space and infrastructure allocation. As underlined by one of the stakeholder, a major 
specific strength of the spatial assessment is to manifest the “cascading effect” of space use 
regulations: provisioning specific infrastructures such as NMT-dedicated spaces enables to cut back 
the proliferating individual motorized vehicle ownership trend by reducing the safety pressure 
feeding it,  and thus decreasing the otherwise inevitable transport space demand increase. 
Regulating space use and implementing travel demand management targeting individual motorized 
vehicles in developing countries during the early stages of their transport system development will 
enable to get “more efficient” systems with “fewer associated problems” (T. A. Litman, 2013). 
Focusing on local specificities is promising in targeting practical issues and supporting local policy 
applications. Rajkot faces currently two major problems regarding its road space: encroachments 
and unauthorized parking on roadways. The space-time consumption of these activities could be a 
good indicator to quantify their impact. 
 
Besides the analysis provided by the stakeholders, a general awareness of the scarcity of space and 
the associated equity concerns would be profitable to transport project appraisals. The spatial 
assessment has shown that the existing transport infrastructures favour the motorized vehicle 
==PAGE:104==
owners, who represent the wealthier range of the population. This bias raises a two-folded equity 
issue: neglecting a category of users, and moreover the one with the lower opportunities. Xavier 
Godard (2011), in a book section entitled Poverty and urban mobility: diagnosis toward a new 
understanding suggests that, for each transport action that aims at supporting poverty alleviation, 
the assessment should not only consider the poor who would benefit from it and those who would 
not, but moreover the non-poor who benefit. If their share is too high, a reverse redistribution effect 
could indeed occur. A spatial equity assessment could enlighten such an appraisal process by 
identifying the transport space consumers. 
 
IV. 
Synthesis of the utilization and discussion  
To frame the utilization of the assessment undertaken, several stakeholder interviews have been 
lead. Four stakeholders from four different backgrounds have been interviewed: city planning, 
academia and advocacy work. Four indicators have been presented on the description of the space 
demand for transports, and the discussions have been framed around two questions: the relevance 
of the spatial assessment for sustainable development of transport, and their ability to help 
alleviating implementation barriers.  
The feedbacks received underlined specific strengths of the indicators, but also deemed the overall 
analysis too theoretical to be directly suited for targeted advocacy works. Stakeholders made several 
suggestions to make the indicators more catching, in line with their specific profile and approach to 
sustainable transport development. The recommendations included bringing the scope down to 
specific local issues, integrating monetary consideration to the spatial assessment as well as linking it 
with the major concern of air pollution. This opens up to possible further research and policy 
applications. 
The overall stakeholder involvement process has nevertheless been rather limited, as only four 
people have been interviewed. The interpretation drawn may significantly be shaped by the 
stakeholder’s individual point of view. Guest (2006) maintains yet that information saturation is 
reached between six and twelve interviews, so four may already contain a fair share of information. 
The utilization provided interesting new knowledge inputs, such as the current prevalent concern 
about transport and air pollution in India. It can be assumed that some learning took also place on 
the stakeholder’s side. The new knowledge generated may be more important in the framework of a 
communicative planning (Innes, 1998) than the quantitative evidence conveyed. The 
communicative planning theory consider the purpose of indicators not only of informing about a 
specific topic, but moreover influencing “by becoming embedded in understandings, practices and 
institutions” (Innes, 1998).

E. GENERAL DISCUSSION 
 
I. 
Two remaining questions 
1. Is low carbon low space? 
A spatial assessment based on Low-carbon Comprehensive Mobility Plans requires clarification on 
the relationship between low-carbon transport plans and spatially efficient transport systems. The 
methodology suggested in this work is largely dependent on the scenarios envisioned for the LCMPs, 
which included the development of spatial efficient modes as transport means. The land-use 
concern has well been taken into account in the LCMPs design, which has been proven by the spatial 
assessment showing an overall reduction of transport space consumption. The comprehensive 
approach of the LCMP links the low-carbon approach with a broader sustainability framework. If one 
considers a low-carbon transport development principally relying on fuel technology changes or the 
introduction of electrical vehicles, the link between low-carbon and low-space would not have been 
as straightforward as what has been induced by the spatial assessment. Low-carbon mobility plans 
which do not consider their comprehensive inclusion in sustainable development, would therefore 
risk promoting transport space distribution injustices. In the current global transport paradigm, 
low-space-time consuming transport means are also low-carbon. In contrast to that, low-carbon is 
not necessarily low-space or fair in terms of space used. The transport spatial equity framework as 
developed in the spatial assessment is hence a strong approach fostering low-carbon transport 
modes, spatial efficiency and equity. 
 
2. How to characterize the link between space demand and space 
supply? 
The core of the spatial assessment has been the space consumption of different scenarios. 
Occupancy ratios have only been calculated for the base year in which space supply data were 
available. Even if LCMPs involve the opening of footpaths, cycle tracks and public transport network, 
which are transport spatial measures, a more detailed analysis on the correlated space allocation 
changes would prove relevant in assessing the overall spatial efficiency and spatial justice. The 
spatial analysis revealed that LCMPs would use both less space and that the space consumed by the 
different modes would prove fairer. These results hold true. Some bias may however remain in the 
transport space supply. The transport space supplied is the result of a deliberate planning process, 
and should therefore be expected to have taken efficiency and fairness concerns into account.  
The transport space consumed on the contrary is influenced by a broad range of factors, including 
the transport space supply as well as mobility patterns, and mode affordability namely. This has 
been shown by the analysis of the geographical repartition of pedestrians in Rajkot, which revealed 
to be independent of the footpath geographical supply distribution. The spatial performance of a 
scenario in terms of space consumed does not necessarily imply a high performing space supply 
scheme.  
==PAGE:106==
The analysis of the space supplied for transport may thus be more compelling in the transport 
justice framework. Since development and economic growth do not filter through the whole society 
spontaneously (Godard, 2011), planners and decision makers should champion equitable planning 
proactively. This is especially important as transport is a quasi-public good, meaning that it requires 
decision making to be initiated  not on the demand but on the supply side of the market(Sclar & 
Touber, 2011). 
The visible space demanded by travellers, which in this report is referred to as “space consumed”, is 
mainly a function of the mode choice, speed and mobility patterns. The space supplied for transport 
affects mode-choices. In the Indian context, safety is often a major mode-choice determinant, and 
dedicating transport space for NMT modes for example would therefore also play an important role 
in shaping mode-choice decisions. 
The discussion of the relationship between space demand and space supply would need to include 
the generalized cost of transport and a closer examination of the different mode-choice 
constituents. Here a few points can still be highlighted: 
On one hand, when analysing the space supply, some things can be said about the space consumed: 
For NMT modes: 
- 
A large share of space dedicated does not necessarily imply a large share of space used. This 
will be discussed in a later point, “infrastructure by itself is not a solution”. Increasing the 
share of NMT space can yet influence the number of travellers deciding to resort to walking 
or cycling, especially in context where travellers avoid doing so for safety reasons such as 
India. 
- 
A low space supply does not indicate a low space consumed, as shown for Rajkot where 
higher space can be consumed by pedestrians in areas with proportionally low space 
allocated to them. However, the case of Rajkot, with a high share of pedestrians in places 
with a low share of footpath, showcases the fact that the concerned NMT travellers are 
captive users, who would resort to a safer transport mode, as soon as they could afford it. A 
low space supplied to NMT modes would hence foster motorization in this context. 
For individual motorized modes: 
- 
A generous space supply may not be correlated in the short term to a high space consumed, 
but in the medium and long-term may indeed lead to a greater space consumed due to the 
induced traffic phenomenon and the self-reinforcing cycle of automobile dependency and 
sprawl described by Wegener & Fürst (1999). 
- 
A low space supply stipulates low space consumption. Contrary to non-motorized modes, 
motorized vehicles can hardly circulate without a proper road network, as roads are part of 
the technical system encompassing individual motorized transportation. Decreasing the 
space allocated to motorized vehicles may provoke congestion and reduce the overall 
individual motorized modes’ traffic. Congestion can however only be considered as an 
acceptable demand management method in a multi-modal context, where alternative 
modes than the ones locked in road congestion (individual motorized vehicles) are available.

This discussion has only considered the traffic space used and supplied, excluding parking. If road 
space is the main transport space supply in Rajkot, parking has however been identifies as the major 
space consuming transport function.  The low parking space supply in Rajkot has not prevented an 
increase in motorization, and is not forecasted to do so when elaborating future transport scenarios. 
The parking space consumption is a direct result of vehicle ownership, and is little influenced by 
public parking space supply. Motorization is likely to be more influenced by road provision than 
parking space allocation. The parking issue is yet the most spatially costly, especially when, as in 
Rajkot it results in unyielding unauthorized parking practices. Parking disorganization, besides 
impeding the traffic when vehicles are parked on road, extends the spatial cost of parking by 
creating unusable road lane segments. 
 
II. 
Improvement suggestions 
1. Focusing on travellers rather than modes 
The spatial assessment has measured the difference in space consumed and supplied by modes. The 
relationship between modes and traveller’s socio-economic profile has been introduced in the 
background chapter presenting Rajkot with the specific mode-shares by income class, gender and 
age. Instead of focusing the examination on modes, an analysis of the space consumed and the 
space supplied held directly by the mentioned categories could have reinforced the spatial justice 
argument. The transport model elaborated for the LCMP has shaped the mode choice utilities 
functions on different traveller socio-economic profiles, segregated namely by gender and income 
segments, which would enable to operationalize an assessment directly by traveller types. 
A “people centred” examination targets more straightforwardly the fairness argument than a 
traffic-based approach. This is especially important in a development context, where poverty 
alleviation and safety matters are at stake in every planning decision, including transport planning, 
demanding hence that “people not vehicle should be the focus of the evaluation” (Dimitriou & 
Gakenheimer, 2011). This has also been reaffirmed in India’s INDC (Intended Nationally Determined 
Contribution), stating that “Urban transport policy will encourage moving people rather than 
vehicles”. 
 
2. Involving more stakeholders 
One of the main limitations of the overall indicator building process is the restricted attention 
granted to the utilization framework, involving four stakeholders only. Interacting with stakeholders 
==PAGE:109==
allows better seizing issues at stake, clarifying the scope and improving the assessment methodology 
accordingly. This process is critical for indicators aiming at describing complex phenomenon, such as 
a transport spatial assessment. The indicator conceptualization would also have been enhanced by 
stakeholders’ inputs, with a twofold benefit: tailoring a more context-specific problematic and 
potentially nurturing stakeholder’s concern for the matter. 
 
III. 
So what does a spatial assessment bring out? 
1. An ambitious scope, with planning tool outcomes 
The methodology elaborated for the spatial assessment is of an innovative kind, as it is based on a 
space-time indicator which is not widely spread in the international transport literature yet. It 
permitted to combine an efficiency perspective with a fairness examination, in an overall 
environmental framework of low-carbon transport planning. The broadness of the issues at stakes 
gave birth to a hybrid approach. The methodology had recourse to the transport modelling 
techniques, the conceptualization to the sustainable development theory, and with overarching 
economic and spatial justice objectives, the scope turned out rather fertile, although ambitious. 
Novelty is costly, and some interpretations proved delicate, such as the road occupancy ratios. Some 
central concepts would also have gained from more explicit definitions, such as the nature of a fair, 
or an efficient urban space allocation. The transport activity requests consuming urban space, and a 
more detailed description of the essential features of a suitable or sustainable transport space 
allocation could have helped narrowing the overall analysis. Instead of plainly defining fairness and 
efficiency in space allocation, a comparative scope has been adopted in the indicator interpretation 
avoiding simultaneously the in-depth investigation that a normative discussion would have imposed. 
The parametrical uncertainties can easier be located as they mainly originate from the assumptions 
used in the LCMP forecasting methodology. 
If one wants to consider transport equity, there may be clearer indicators, such as accessibility, 
being the first-rate pointer for social equity (Caubel, 2004). Accessibility is “the extent to which 
land-use and transport systems enable (groups of) individuals to reach activities or destination by 
means of a (combination of) transport mode(s)” (Van Wee, 2016), and may therefore be an optimal 
indicator in the context of developing countries. As its definition involves a critical land-use 
component, transport space used and distribution is tightly knit to accessibility (Geurs & Van Wee, 
2004), and increasing the relationship between the spatial assessment and its accessibility impacts 
could thus have turned out a pertinent angle as well. In terms of efficiency, the congestion or travel 
time approach may have proven more compelling. Also, low-carbon is not necessarily low-space. 
The spatial assessment may cover too large a scope, which could explain why it has not yet been 
extensively addressed in the literature. All things considered, it overall aims at a holistic assessment. 
Besides policy-support applications, the spatial assessment, and especially space consumption 
forecasts, can serve as a planning tool. Knowing where the needs are conjectured, is a key element 
for transport planning; be it in a traditional “predict and provide” perspective or for a more strategic 
comprehensive space use planning that would be mindful of space distribution issues. 
 
==PAGE:110==
2. Still, “infrastructure by itself is not a solution” 
Moving towards sustainable transports cannot be restrained to a pure infrastructural solution, even 
though infrastructures play an important role. In a case study of Nairobi entitled “Obstacle for non-
motorized transport in developing countries”, Becker (2011) identifies several important 
prerequisites to move towards sustainable transport systems in developing countries, independent 
from the footpath or cycle track provision. Stating “Infrastructure by itself is not a solution”, Becker 
refers specifically to large-scale public awareness and education programs in order to create a 
“positive attitude” towards NMT. NMT is indeed often considered as a sign of backwardness, which 
would not commensurate to the development goals and aspiration of most of the policy-makers and 
professionals in cities of the developing world (Pendakur, 2011). There is moreover a low awareness 
of the accessibility needs of low-income travellers, who constitute the majority of the society. To the 
contrary, a rising interest in technological advancement takes place, which is often deemed as an 
above par developmental approach. The new technologies solution appeal has been apparent in the 
interview lead with the former RMC head city engineer, who suggested resorting to shaded cycle 
tracks with rooftop solar panels. Combining NMT infrastructures with smart technologies could 
prove an attractive option for the prevailing technology-based developmental aims. 
Becker (2011) further mentions other measures such as instituting high-quality infrastructural design 
standards for all transport infrastructures, and capacity-building for planners who lack knowledge 
about NMT infrastructure design. Strengthening lobbying organizations for NMT and reforming the 
overall driver’s education system to establish safer traffic environments are regarded as other 
cornerstone actions. 
Finally, accessibility is never merely infrastructural, as dimensions such as transport costs or 
cognitive accessibility (e.g. for illiterate persons) can constitute substantial obstacles to the urban 
poor especially (Godard, 2011). 
 
IV. 
General discussion synthesis 
As low carbon is neither necessarily low-space nor fair-space, this research has contributed to 
creating a stronger framework for low-carbon mobility plans. Beyond carbon emission 
considerations, the spatial assessment of LCMPs also engages in a spatial efficiency and equity 
evaluation. 
One of the main difficulties in such an assessment abides in describing the relationship between 
transport space supplied and transport space used. A study of the generalized cost of transport 
would help better understanding how space supply affects mode-choices and thus the space 
consumed by transport. The relationships that can be drawn from this analysis are a low space 
supply for individual motorized transport modes leading to eventually decreasing demand, and a 
high space consumed by individual motorized vehicles revealing to some extent an also necessary 
high space supply. Be that as it may, the transport space supply is an important lever impacting 
mode-choice and consequentially the transport space used. This is to be linked to the fact that road 
provision encourages resorting to individual motorized modes, that in turn will reveal high space-
time consumers, especially in terms of parking, irrespective of the parking space supply. 
==PAGE:111==
Two simple improvements to the present assessment can be suggested: involving more 
stakeholders at different stages of the indicator-building, and conducting a people-centred, rather 
than mode-centred, analysis. 
The spatial assessment is an original assessment methodology yielding to multiple findings and that 
can serve as a planning tool. However, the overall approach would have benefited from a deeper 
conceptualization work around the nature of the optimal transport space allocation and distribution 
among travellers. 
Lastly, providing proper infrastructures for sustainable transport is not sufficient to transition to 
sustainable transport systems. A comprehensive set of measures would include other components 
which target accessibility, as well as strategies to restore NMTs’ image in an era when technically 
complex solutions are favoured.

F. CONCLUSION AND FURTHER RESEARCH 
 
Problematic and scope 
The urbanization trends accompanied by rampant motorization strains the urban space. 
Urbanization and motorization are happening at a fast pace in cities from developing economies. 
The rising space scarcity in urban areas requires examining the spatial efficiency of urban transport 
systems, as well as the fairness of the transport space distribution among modes and traveller 
categories. Besides being often overlooked, the spectre of combined motorization and urbanization 
trends does indeed not prospect any improvements in the transport space repartition between 
users. 
The spatial assessment has therefore taken a closer look on the spatial impacts of urban passenger 
transport, elaborating on how to integrate the transport spatial assessment in the sustainable 
transport theory. A sustainable perspective on the land-use by the transport system forged the 
selection of indicators that aim at evaluating the fairness and the efficiency of the space used by 
transports, both currently and in potential future transport developments. The spatial assessment 
has been implemented on the case-study city of Rajkot, India, for which several Low-carbon 
Comprehensive Mobility Plans have been designed. Two essential questions shaped the spatial 
assessment: “Is the space used by transport currently efficient, and fairly distributed?”, and “How 
would LCMPs affect transport space use in terms of fairness and efficiency?” 
 
Methodology summary 
To answer these questions, the transport spatial assessment built on three pillars: the 
conceptualizations, which lead to crafting of an indicator-set, the operationalization, which 
implemented it, and the utilization, which attempted to determine its applications potential. If in 
theory the three parts could be conducted separately - ideally settling the conceptualization and the 
utilization framework before the operationalization - , the indicator-building exercise has shown 
recurrent interactions between the three sections. As the operationalization phase repeatedly gave 
rise to questions on the nature of the indicated results, the overall scope has been re-adjusted 
several times. Unfamiliar with the spatial analysis undertaken by Héran & Ravalet, (2008), it was first 
expected for example that the space demanded by travellers would exceed the road space supplied, 
and could possibly lead to identify demand/supply gaps. The calculation results showed that the 
space consumed by travellers is however always much lower than the road space available, and 
resorting to occupancy ratio proved a better approach than gap identification. The 
operationalization also nurtured the conceptualization and challenged the utilization perspectives. 
Since indicator-building processes are not the focus of this work, the iterative feedback loops have 
however not been lengthily described. 
 
==PAGE:114==
Finding synthesis 
The main results of the conceptualization, operationalization and utilization sections will be 
summarized, as well as the content of the final discussion. 
Conceptualization 
The spatial lens sheds light on specific decisive points at the intersection of transport and sustainable 
development. 
In line with the sustainable passenger transport paradigm established by Holden, Linnerud, & 
Banister (2013), a spatial assessment should keep watch of the transport ecological footprint, and 
account for a space supply that would enable safe travels for all modes, as a prerequisite to satisfy 
basic transport needs. Moreover, intra-generational equity calls for an equitable space distribution, 
and inter-generational equity for an efficient use of the urban space, aware of its scarcity and 
proactively preventing long-term car dependency patterns. 
The rapid motorization which characterizes general urban transport development in India leads to 
environmental, social and economic impacts. The current transport infrastructure organisation is 
threatening the safety of vulnerable mode users, who mostly resort to walking or cycling by default, 
for affordability concerns. A closer look at the distribution of mode shares by income, gender and 
age reveals that motorization increases with household income, and that among NMT users, mostly 
women are to be found, as well as the younger and older population groups. The imbalance of mode 
repartition within the different population categories prompts resorting to a spatial justice scope. 
Rajkot’s LCMP introduces four comprehensive urban transport development strategies: business as 
usual (BAU scenario), land-use improvements (LU), provision of non-motorized transport 
infrastructures (NMT) and public transport facilities (PT). A sustainable transport development in 
India is one that would empower the current captive users as well as contribute to a low-carbon 
development. The spatial investigation is a co-benefit analysis of the LCMP which intends to analyse 
how the space for transport is distributed among modes and determine which transport 
development strategy is the least space consuming. 
 
Operationalization 
The indicator-set selected consists of five indicators: the transport space supply, space demand and 
occupancy ratios – direct indicators –, the economic value of land used by transport and the 
transport ecological footprint – indirect indicators. 
Two literature sources have served as central references for the direct indicators: Héran & Ravalet 
(2008), who conducted an economic analysis aimed at assessing the efficiency of the transport space 
organisation in Paris, and Gössling et al. (2016) who adopted a transport justice approach by 
comparing the space supplied for different transport modes in Freiburg, Germany. The spatial 
analysis lead for Rajkot’s case encompassed elements of both frameworks, and included forecasts 
for future transport development strategies. 
 
==PAGE:115==
The transport space supply in Rajkot disclosed a quasi mono-categorical network based on roads, 
and regulated by an all-or-nothing spatial distribution. The space demand analysis revealed that the 
space consumed by transports can be made fairer and more efficient if the transport strategies shift 
from BAU to LCMPs. LCMP would contain the projected transport space consumption expansion, 
and improve the balance of the transport space used repartition by mode. Since parking plays a 
critical role in the space-time consumption level, space-time efficiency and fairness enhancements 
would mainly occur by shifting from high space-time parking consuming modes (individual 
motorized vehicles) to lower ones (NMT and PT modes). Occupancy ratios made the extent of 
pedestrian infrastructural needs apparent, and showcased how the current transport network favors 
motorized vehcile. 
Certain parametrical choices for the transport space consumption can be discussed, and the overall 
assessment largely relies on the robustness of the tranport model designed for the LCMPs. 
Interpreting the space consumption results through several comparative scopes gave nevertheless 
rise to well-founded findings. Although the space supply analysis lead to instructive outcomes, those 
could be strenghtened by a preliminary reflection on the features of a commendable transport space 
organization. The occupancy ratios proved strong indicators for the fairness framework. 
 
The economic valuation of the different scenarios demonstrated that the PT scenario would be the 
least costly one in terms of value of land used. As the assessment only focused on the land 
consumed, it invited to examine the correlation between the land consumed by transport and the 
land allocated. Since the space consumed is mostly parking space, the direct land-use costs 
associated to the 2031 BAU scenario would be very high and thus to some extend irrespective of the 
land dedicated to the road network. 
The ecological footprint of transport exposed the critical amount of space that would be required to 
sequester the direct CO2 emissions from fossil fuel combustion. The transport ecological footprint 
would exceed the natural absorption capacity of Rajkot’s district in all considered scenarios. The PT 
scenario however would fundamentally reduce the ecological footprint. This particular indicator 
raised both spatial efficiency concerns as well as imperative inter- and intra-generational fairness 
issues. 
The two derived indicators rely on a few parametrical assumptions, but opened essential discussions 
on the societal and environmental cost of different transport systems. The economic valuation of 
land use by transport resorts to a very basic value of land calculation. A broader economic 
assessment methodology would certainly prove more representative an indicator for evaluating the 
cost of the space dedicated to transport. A more accurate picture of the implications of the 
transport space used by travellers for the transport space actually allocated to transport in the city 
would facilitate a better interpretation of the constructed indicator. The transport ecological 
footprint approach compellingly questioned urban transport systems’ spatial efficiency and 
pinpointed important inter- and intra -generational fairness issues. 
 
 
==PAGE:116==
Utilization 
The pertinence and utilization potential of the spatial assessment has been discussed with several 
stakeholders. Interviews have been lead with Rajkot’s former head city engineer, a project associate 
from WRI, a representative of a regional NGO advocating for sustainable development, and a 
professor in Transport Modelling from CEPT University, Ahmedabad. The interviews focused on the 
relevance of the direct indicators chosen for the spatial assessment, and the role they could play in 
helping alleviating implementation barriers. 
Stakeholders underlined specific strengths of the transport space consumption indicators. The 
analytical approach has been appreciated, as well as the ability of the space-time indicator to 
illustrate the causality between infrastructure provision for less space consuming modes and 
reduction of the overall space used by transports. The current shape of the indicators would yet 
intertwine too many concepts to fit a targeted advocacy work. Recommendations to bolster the 
applications potential included reducing the scope to specific local issues, integrating monetary 
consideration to the spatial assessment as well as linking it with the major concern of air pollution. 
 
Discussion 
Two important questions regarding the application of a spatial assessment to LCMP remain. First, 
low carbon transport planning is not necessarily low-space. In low-carbon mobility plans relying 
mainly on technological solutions, the transport space distribution injustices and efficiency concerns 
related to a car-based transport planning would persist. The transport spatial equity framework as 
developed in the spatial assessment is hence a strong approach that, alongside fostering low-carbon 
transport modes, promotes spatial efficiency and equity. Secondly, one of the major challenge in the 
spatial assessment resides in the relationship between transport space supplied and transport space 
used. Only two configurations allow concluding on an equivocal causal relationship: a low supply for 
individual motorized transport modes leading to eventually lowering the demand, and a high space 
consumed by individual motorized vehicles revealing to some extent an also necessary high space 
supply. The lingering practice of unauthorized parking in the city suggests also that the lack of 
parking space does not deter resorting to individual motorized transport modes.  
Two simple improvements to the present assessment can be suggested: involving more stakeholders 
at different stages of the indicator-building process, and conducting a people-centred, rather than 
mode-centred, analysis. 
 
All in all the spatial assessment is an original assessment methodology yielding to multiple findings 
and that can serve as a planning tool. A comprehensive body of measure to support the transition 
towards sustainable transport systems would unmistakably jointly include non-infrastructural 
actions. 
 
 
 
==PAGE:117==
Further research 
The final discussion suggested several future research directions. 
A major point for further research would be to investigate whether an optimal transport space 
allocation can be defined. Transportation activities will always use some space in the city. An optimal 
level however would be neither too low nor too high, and would minimize its overall social cost. A 
too low transportation space for NMT modes could for instance provoke safety issues, as has been 
identified in Rajkot. Similarly, a too high transportation space for motorized vehicles could 
potentially encourage urban sprawl and motorization dependency with the related social costs and 
dependency patterns, as is to be seen in car-based city planning. An imbalance in the space 
dedicated to transport would be detrimental to the overall accessibility, and hinder the basic 
transport purpose in both cases. Héran & Ravalet (2008) attempted to characterize the cost of the 
space dedicated to cars in the city by dividing it into two separate components: damages costs and 
protection costs. The damages costs represent the social cost of granting too high a space to 
motorized transports. This includes the cost of the trips that would be done by cars rather than NMT 
modes as a result of distances travelled being too long. Travels based on individual motorized 
vehicles are more expensive than other modes as they include among other a high vehicle cost and 
high environmental costs. Damages costs would also include the cost of a decrease in mobility for 
non-car users. Parallel to the damage costs, Héran & Ravalet introduce “protection costs”, which 
cover the cost of the measures that would be needed to cope with the increase in space used by cars 
in the city. Protection costs would include the required new infrastructures, such as aboveground 
parking and underground PT, and devices necessary to protect other modes such as speed limitation 
systems and barriers. 
Adjoining efficiency concerns, the quest for the optimal space used by transport would also need to 
be extended to the spatial transport justice sphere. Spatial justice in transport is a rich ground for 
further investigations. This would include determining the nature of a fair (or unfair) spatial 
organization of transport, and elaborating on the meaning and significance of equity in the space 
dedicated to transport. Rajkot’s case study highlighted injustices in space transport repartition 
mainly among population categories but also raised the questions of the legacy left to future 
generations concerning spatial organization.  
Another important future research area would be to examine the relationship between mode-choice 
and infrastructure supply. The investigation would namely focus on how transport space distribution 
affects mode-choice and expand on the different parameters making up the generalized cost of 
transport. This could for example delve into the role played by each infrastructure type in 
encouraging travellers to use it. Parking for example may be less visible in the city than road 
networks and moving vehicles, which may prompt to forget its spatial-temporal cost. This trend has 
been suggested in Rajkot’s case by the unauthorized parking practices, whose spatial cost is very 
high and yet does not prevent motorization to follow an increasing track. An analysis on how the 
physical space taken in the city affects the symbolical / cognitive space of travellers, and thus 
incentives specific mode-choices could be lead. This could be especially relevant in understanding 
which role transport space allocation plays in helping alleviating poverty, or on the contrary reaffirm 
the stronghold of the wealthier classes. The overall perspective for such a work could be to explain 
the impact of transport space supply and distribution on accessibility, as has been introduced by 
Geurs & Van Wee (2004). 
==PAGE:118==
Besides this conceptualization work, a whole range of applications of the developed methodology 
could be envisioned. Conducting the spatial assessment for several cities with different transport 
systems could prove helpful to consolidate the current findings and to better understand the 
mechanisms accounting for road occupancy ratios values and transport space distribution features. 
Basic travel diaries methodology or four-stage transport model enable to conduct spatial 
assessments. The reliability of the operationalization for future scenarios depends on the car 
ownership changes, perhaps even more than mode changes, as parking is the higher space-time 
consuming function. Integrating vehicle ownership may fill an important methodological gap of this 
study. Performing several study of the kind could also feed the normative discussion suggested as a 
first part of the future research.  
Regarding the utilization, to which extend can indicators of spatial transport justice make a 
difference in the present entrenched and dominant socio-technical system, where habits, culture, 
technology, knowledge, and even institutional arrangements and organisations support such 
inefficient space provision? Future research could investigate on the influence of the information 
provided by the indicators. In a “communicative planning” (Innes, 1998) perspective, would this new 
spatial assessment give birth to a type of learning (by all agents, planners, users, service providers..) 
that would in turn foster a transition to sustainable transports ? 
Overall, the integration of a standardization of spatial equity and efficiency assessments in project 
appraisal procedures could also be considered. This would involve a review of what a spatial 
assessment should consist of and how it could be combined with the traditional decision-making 
tools. Such a study would require a more elaborate design of the conceptual part beforehand, and 
implementing several assessments to strengthen the theory and its applications.

Space-time consumption methodology description – Supplement 1 
Space-time used on road for Rajkot 
a. Methodology summary 
The general indicator required to estimate the space used on road is a space-time indicator that will 
be declined by transport function (parking / on-road), by mode and by ward. 
The OD matrix served as a basis to run the fourth stage of the model again (traffic assignment) to 
get the localisation of the flows on road. A simple assignment methodology was chosen, taking the 
traveling time as a proxy for travel cost. Moreover, the traffic assignment was done following an “or-
all-nothing” methodology, meaning that all traffic was assigned to the shortest paths, as if free flow 
speed was available on each road. In reality, flow and speed are correlated since the road has a 
limited capacity, and the shortest path will thus differ given the assigned flow. More accurate 
modelling methods have been developed in the transport model field (iterative models with 
equilibrium states) but for the sake of this spatial assessment, an all-or-nothing assignment was 
deemed precise enough to describe the space used by urban passenger transportation activities. 
Results have been aggregated by wards, which makes up for the imprecision of an all-or-nothing 
traffic assignment. When free flow speed is altered by too high a vehicle road occupancy, travellers 
tend indeed to choose as a second shortest path a new road in the close neighbourhood. One can 
then assume that the space used on this new path would remain in the same ward.

Space-time consumption methodology description – Supplement 2 
 Space-time used parking for Rajkot 
a. Methodology summary 
The space used parking has been chosen in this methodology as the vehicle size. Pedestrians 
obviously do not require any parking. For other modes, average vehicle sizes have been taken from 
the literature. Subsection b details the average vehicle size used. 
The time parking will also vary with vehicle class, and the parking time determination methodology 
will be explained in subsections c for cars, motorcycles and bicycles, d for auto rickshaws and e for 
public transports.  
The first vehicle category (cars, motorcycles and bicycles), the number of vehicle leaving and 
entering a ward is derived from the transport model. This has the advantage of combining the 
number of vehicle and their localization. The vehicle fleet size for this category is thus directly taken 
as an output of the transport model, even for the parking time. This approximation will be discussed 
later in the report, as the total number of vehicle parking may indeed be higher than the fleet size 
made up of the vehicle traveling.  
For the auto rickshaws and the public transport, the vehicle fleet size is given respectively by an 
adjusted vehicle registration dataset and the PT quantitative need assessed in Rajkot’s LCMP. For 
auto rickshaws, the parking localization relies on a 2011 vehicle ownership survey, whose 
geographical distribution is assumed to remain for the 2031 scenarios. The parking localization of 
buses is based on the forecasted location of major PT nodes.

d. Auto rickshaws 
Time parking and fleet size 
Auto rickshaws serve as a substitute for taxis or missing public transport and a same vehicle is in 
operation for several hours per day. Daily operation time for auto rickshaws we not per se available 
in the data sets, and the operation time used was taken by a study done for Mumbai (Shlaes & Mani, 
2013). Table 48 summarizes the parameters used. 
The number of auto rickshaws for year 2011 is given by the vehicle registration data covering the 
new vehicle registered over the period 2004-2011. It was combined with the data from a petrol 
pump survey realized in 2011 in the LCMP project, to account for survival rates of vehicles over the 
years. The petrol pump survey includes the age and the type of vehicle tanking up, which enables to 
establish the ratio of different vehicle types with their age and then determine the survival rates of 
vehicles over the seven-year period prior to the survey. This gives the number of vehicles for year 
2011. The number of vehicle projected for 2031 BAU and the different scenario have been 
forecasted adjusting the number of vehicle to the number of trip in each scenario. 
==PAGE:133==
Space consumed parking on a ward 
Auto rickshaws are owned by rickshaws drivers. A vehicle survey done for 2704 households for the 
LCMP project established the vehicle ownerships. Thirty one auto rickshaw owners were found, 
repartitioned in 12 wards. This repartition was used to allocate the parking space consumed by auto 
rickshaws on different wards.
