Abstract
Reverberation is a physical propriety people are encountered with every day, al­
though most of them rarely think about it. As the sound perception in different
places is altered due to reverberation, it can influence people emotions and feel­
ings. In this research focus is on digital artificial reverberation which is used as
a tool during various tasks in audio post production. What is more, aim is to pro­
vide more natural perception of reverberation, concerning human listening with
two ears. That acoustical concept is called binaural listening and it depends on
physiognomy of people, as every person has different head related transfer func­
tion (HRTF). Both Matlab and C++ programming methods were used in order to
achieve software reverberation based on feedback delay network (FDN). FDN
reverberation proved as a light processing power solution, which can be easily
manipulated in a real time processing to achieve desired effect, while inclusion of
binaural information improved perceived sound quality.
Keywords: reverberation, binaural listening, feedback delay network, real
time audio processing

1
Introduction
Artificial reverberation is a tool that achieved a well known effect of a sound per­
ception, caused by sound waves reflections, to be executable by Digital Signal
Processing (later referred as DSP). Nevertheless, the study of room modeling,
based on sound waves reflections, has been an active area of research since
the early 20th century. Wallace Clement Sabine was among the pioneers in this
field and conducted groundbreaking studies on room acoustics, particularly fo­
cusing on reverberation [1]. Reverberation is a crucial characteristic of sound as
it carries acoustic information and plays a defining role in sound quality. It also
contributes to the perception of space. Initially, analog methods were employed
to create reverberation effects, and instruments like the Hammond Organ incor­
porated a spring reverberator, which is still commonly used in guitar amplifiers.
Analog spring reverb is based on system which consists of a set of coiled metallic
springs, a transducer and a pickup. However, with the advent of digital technol­
ogy in the 1980s, analog techniques have gradually been replaced, and currently,
digital technology predominates in this domain. First digital units were produced
by company called Lexicon in 1971 with a size of approximately ”5U” where U
stands for ”rack unit” which is standard hardware size with 45cm width, used in
audio industry. Hardware units were extensively used in the following years but
in the current state of the art, software versions which run on a personal com­
puter or external audio mixer, are dominant. There are software implementations
which try to emulate a hardware units or even analog mechanical systems like
pre­described spring reverbs, but also implementations of completely new desired
sounds.
1.1
Motivation and Goal
Reverberation effects aim to simulate the reflections that occur within a room or
concert hall, thereby capturing how the acoustic environment influences the per­
ception of a sound source. Typically, reverberation plugins do not take into ac­
count the way sound is perceived by a listener, as they do not consider the effects
of the listener’s torso, head, and pinnas [2]. The main objective of this project
is to explore whether a reverberation plugin that incorporates the spatialization
of early reflections through a Head­Related Transfer Function (later referred as
HRTF) can enhance the quality of the sound image perceived by listener. HRTF
is a mathematical representation of how the sound is filtered by the human head
and ears before it reaches the eardrums. It describes the changes in the sound’s
frequency content, phase, and intensity caused by the physical features and ori­
entation of an individual’s head, torso, and ears [3]. What is more, the goal is to
achieve long lasting reverberation effect that can be executed in real time without
high CPU (central processing unit) usage.
As a DTU student enrolled in ”Engineering Acoustics” master programme the dig­
italization of acoustics research can be seen as a goal. During my previous and

common listeners. It can be noted that my previous experience with program­

1.2
Project overview
This thesis elaborate on digital artificial reverberation algorithms based on feed­
back delay network (later referred as FDN) DSP technique. Two different algo­
rithms have been implemented, both using the same FDN, but one has additional
information for early reflections based on HRTF [4]. This research project is com­
menced by thoroughly studying and reviewing concepts related to reverberation.
Additionally, a comprehensive review of existing literature and current research
was conducted to establish the current state of the field. The feedback delay
network technique and the Head­Related Transfer Function were specifically ex­
amined. Once a solid understanding of these concepts was acquired, the devel­
opment of reverberation plugins began by implementing fundamental digital signal
processing techniques, such as digital filters, to construct the foundational com­
ponents of the FDN algorithm. Algorithm coding has been done using MATLAB
where was possible to plot the data and research carefully proprieties of audio
changes. Moreover, MATLAB prototypes were than coded into Xcode integrated
development environment using C++ and JUCE libraries. Transition to C++ was
crucial in order to build real­time audio processing plugin which can be loaded into
digital audio workstation (later referred as DAW) in order to process audio through
the system with less than 10ms latency between input and the output.

2.1
What is Reverberation
Reverberation is the perception of spaciousness in sound, and different acoustic
environments, such as concert halls [5], forests [6], city streets [7], or mountain
ranges [8], each have their own unique reverberation characteristics. It is a con­
stant presence in our daily lives and holds significance in both synthesized music
and audio recordings, as it is often preferred for most sounds. Musicians are well
aware of how room acoustics can impact sound. For example, playing a musical
piece in two different halls can result in completely different experiences due to
variations in reverberation characteristics. As a result, the acoustic environment
may influence the performance, requiring adjustments in tempo and dynamics to
suit the specific reverberation [9]. Music without reverberation can sound dry and
lifeless [10], whereas excessive reverberation can make a performance muddy
and unintelligible.
Essentially, reverberation is a collection of numerous reflections caused by sound
waves colliding with surfaces, dispersing the sound and enriching it through the
overlapping of these reflections [9]. These reflections contribute to the ”color” or
timbre of the sound, altering its tonal qualities. The extent of reverberation de­
pends on factors such as the volume and dimensions of the space, as well as the
type, shape, and number of surfaces present. When sound is produced, it travels
via two paths: the direct path, which is the shortest route from the sound source
to the listener, and the indirect path, consisting of multiple delayed and attenuated
copies of the original sound that take longer routes by reflecting off walls, ceilings,
floors, and objects [11]. As a sound wave propagates in all directions, it undergoes
various transformations such as absorption, reflection, delay, and attenuation due
to interactions with the surrounding air, surfaces, and objects. The amplitude of
each reflection diminishes as the distance traveled increases, following an inverse
relationship. Additionally, the frequency content of each reflection is altered due
to the directivity of the sound source and the material characteristics of the reflect­
ing surfaces. It must be noted that higher frequencies are much more directional
compared to the lower frequency spectrum which propagate in all directions. The
combination of the direct sound and these reflections creates what is commonly
known as reverberation. The category of indirect sound can be further divided into
two components: early reflections and late reverberation. Early reflections occur
on a relatively short timescale, shortly after the direct sound, and are typically not
perceived separately by human hearing. Instead, they are integrated with the di­
rect sound, contributing to the overall perception of the sound environment. While
the direct path of sound provides information about the direction and position of
the sound source, early reflections play a crucial role in conveying information
about the shape, size, composition of reflecting surfaces, and contribute to the
perception of sound color. Following the early reflections, a subsequent set of
reflections, known as late reflections, reaches the listener. These reflections are
3
==PAGE:11==
characterized by a high density, random distribution, and typically exhibit expo­
nential decay, giving rise to diffuse reverberation. The diffuse sound field is a con­
cept in acoustics where sound waves are evenly distributed in all directions and
angles. The late reflections offer additional cues about the size of the room and
the distance of the sound source. The period between the occurrence of early
reflections and late reflections is referred to as the mixing time. Reverberation
time, often represented as RT60 or T20, measures the time it takes for the sound
pressure level or intensity to decrease by 60 dB. It depends on factors such as
the volume of the room and the properties of its reflective surfaces.
RT60 = 0.164 ∗V /A.
(2.1)
In the given context, the room volume is denoted as V , measured in cubic me­
ters, and the total absorption of the surfaces within the room is represented as
A, measured in metric sabins [12]. Typically, smaller rooms tend to have shorter
reverberation times compared to larger rooms, as sound waves travel shorter dis­
tances. However, factors such as acoustic treatment and other variables can
influence the reverberation characteristics. For instance, concert halls typically
have reverberation times ranging from approximately 1.5 to 2 seconds. Highly
reverberant environments, such as cathedrals, may have reverberation times ex­
ceeding 3 seconds, while damped studio enivorment often aim for 0.4 seconds
reverberation time.
When a sound wave reflects off a surface, some of its energy is lost, and different
materials absorb acoustic energy to varying degrees. Hard and solid surfaces
are efficient sound reflectors, while soft surfaces are highly absorbent. Additional
measurements associated with the perception of reverberation include the fre­
quency dependence of the reverberation time, the time delay between the arrival
of the direct sound and the early reflections, and the rate at which echo density
builds up. Lower frequencies tend to resonate for a longer time before fading,
which is greatly influenced by room natural frequencies, called room modes, in­
fluenced by room dimensions.
The time gap between the direct sound and early reflections can vary significantly
depending on the acoustic environment. Delays greater than 50 milliseconds can
lead to distinct echoes, while delays shorter than 5 milliseconds can create the
perception of a small space for the listener. In most well designed halls, the first
delay falls within the range of 10 to 20 milliseconds. The speed at which echoes
reach the listener is influenced by the volume of the room and is approximately
proportional to the square root of the room’s volume. In smaller spaces, there is
a rapid accumulation of echo density. Figure 2.1 shows sound power amplitude
decay over time.

2.2
Artificial Reverberation
Throughout the latter half of the 20th century, extensive research was conducted
on various techniques and methods to simulate natural reverberation. Engineers
aimed to develop electronic devices capable of replicating the long­term propa­
gation of sound in enclosed spaces. Whether in software or hardware form, a
reverberator can be conceptualized as a filter that attempts to mimic the impulse
response of the simulated environment. The presence of natural room or hall am­
bience is a vital aspect of recorded music, and having control over the parameters
that define the characteristics of reverberation grants sound engineers the ability
to shape the perception of spaciousness. However, it was not until the 1920s with
the advent of artificial reverberation for music broadcasting and recording that this
became possible [13]. Since then, numerous methods and techniques have been
proposed, including:
• Chamber reverberation
• Spring reverberation
• Plate reverberation
• Tape delays
• Digital reverberation
The practice of close miking and working in a damped studio environment often
resulted in a dry sound lacking the desired concert hall acoustics for music per­
formances. Also sound engineers liked to have option to chose what elements
of music needs added reverberation, which is a common desire even today. To
address this, an early technique involved the use of specially constructed echo
chambers. In this setup, the dry recorded signal was sent through a loudspeaker
while multiple microphones were strategically placed to capture the artificially re­
verberated sound. The dry and processed sounds were then combined, allowing
5
==PAGE:13==
recordings made in a small, absorbent studio to emulate the ambiance of a concert
hall [14].
Various electromechanical reverberation devices were developed, such as tape
delays [27], spring reverberation [28], and plate reverberation [29]. While these
devices and techniques produced high­quality reverberation, they were limited to
use in sound recording studios, were not easily portable, and exhibited variations
between units.
Due to these limitations and the significance of reverberation in recorded music,
digital artificial reverberators were created. These devices aimed to reproduce
reverberation effects in a more flexible and convenient manner. In recent times,
artificial reverberation has found a new application in virtual environments, where
the simulation of room acoustics plays a crucial role in creating a convincing im­
mersive experience [10].
2.3
Digital Reverberation
Digital reverberation is the most common used artificial reverberation in 21st cen­
tury. The reason is in its flexibility, affordable price and satisfactory results. Ac­
cording to [13], reverberation algorithms can be classified into three distinct cate­
gories:
• convolution based reverberation
• delay network
• computational acoustic
Convolutional methods involve the convolution of the input signal with a recorded
or estimated impulse response of an acoustic space. This method is accurate in
including acoustical properties of a room to the input signal but is computationally
intensive process which is not perfectly suited for a real time processing. On the
other hand, delay networks rely on the utilization of comb and allpass filters. The
input signal is delayed, filtered, and then fed back through a series of paths. These
two categories of reverberation algorithms are frequently employed to achieve
specific perceptual or artistic effects. Delay networks are the main interest of this
thesis.
In contrast, computational acoustic methods focus on simulating the propagation
of acoustic energy within modeled geometries. Methods based on wave equa­
tion such as Finite elements method are commonly used for frequencies below
Schroeder frequency (2.2) while ray based methods are used for higher frequency
simulation. These algorithms are primarily used in scenarios related to acoustic
design and analysis, where the goal is to accurately simulate and evaluate acous­
tic properties.

2.4
Delay Network Methods
The concept of generating artificial reverberation using digital signal processing
was initially introduced by Schroeder, notable acoustician already mentioned in
last section, in the early 1960s [15]. Schroeder came out with a reverberator
design that relied on comb and allpass filters. These filters are regarded as fun­
damental components in digital audio signal processing systems and are widely
utilized for creating reverberation effects.
2.4.1
Comb Filter
There exist two fundamental types of comb filters, namely feedforward and feed­
back, both of which can be considered computational representations of echoes.
Feedforward Comb Filter
A feedforward comb filter can be represented by a delay line where the input is
forwarded to the output, illustrated as follows:
Figure 2.2: Schematic of feedforward comb filter [2]
The difference equation describing the behavior of a feedback comb filter is as
follows:
y(n) = b0x(n) + bMx(n −M)
(2.3)
where,
• y(n) represents the output signal of the comb filter at time index n
• x(n) represents the input signal to the comb filter at time index n
• b0 is the feedforward coefficient or gain applied to the current input signal
x(n)
• bM is the feedforward coefficient or gain applied to the delayed input signal
x(n−M), where M represents the delay in samples
Feedforward comb filter can be explained as a simple discrete delay.
Feedback Comb Filter
In a feedback comb filter, a delay line output is fed back to the input.
7
==PAGE:15==
Figure 2.3: Schematic of feedback comb filter [2]
The difference equation can be written as:
y(n) = b0 ∗x(n) −aM ∗y(n −M)
(2.4)
This specific filter can be considered as a computational representation of a series
of echoes, which decay exponentially and are evenly spaced in time. To ensure
stability, the magnitude of coefficient aM must be less than 1. If the magnitude of
coefficient aM is greater than or equal to 1, each echo in the feedback comb filter
will be louder than the previous one. This will result in an unending and escalating
series of echoes.
2.4.2
Allpass filter
The allpass filter is a crucial component in digital audio signal processing systems.
It got its name from the fact that it allows all frequencies to pass through, as its
frequency response is consistent across all frequencies, resulting in a gain of 1.
This filter is widely employed in artificial reverberation and digital effects applica­
tions. An allpass filter can be conceptualized as a combination of a feedforward
comb filter and a feedback comb filter, where the feedforward coefficient is the
negative of the feedback coefficient.
Figure 2.4: Schematic of allpass comb filter [2]
The difference equation that describes an allpass filter is as follows:
y(n) = b0x(n) + x(n −M) −aM ∗y(n −M)
(2.5)
Most of the DSP reverberation are based on allpass filter which was first intro­
duced by Manfred Schroeder and Ben Logan in early 1960s.

2.4.3
Feedback delay network
The concept of Feedback Delay Network (FDN) was initially presented by Gerzon
[16], who introduced an ”orthogonal matrix feedback reverberation unit.” While
individual feedback comb filters were recognized as a computational model for
simulating echoes, they produced unsatisfactory quality reverberation when used
independently. However, when multiple filters were interconnected and cross­
coupled, they could produce more pleasing results [2]. The FDN structure for
artificial reverberation is built upon interconnected delay lines forming a feedback
loop, facilitated by a matrix. The basic feedback delay network structure is shown
in Figure 2.5 with corresponding equation shown in Equation 2.6
Figure 2.5: Classic feedback delay network [2]
Feedback delay network difference equation:
y(n) = x(n −m) + g ∗y(n −m)
(2.6)
Where, x(n −m) is delayed input, g is a gain coefficient of feedback line and
y(n −m) is previous sample output fed to feedback line.
The ”mixing matrix” enhances diffusion by dispersing energy across the N chan­
nels, effectively amplifying the density of the later stages of reverberation. Jot
and Chaigne [17] introduced an enhanced FDN algorithm that offers a systematic
approach, enabling significant flexibility in adjusting the reverberation time across
various frequency bands due to added filter in feedback delay network.
2.5
Spatial Hearing
When a sound wave reaches the ears of a listener, the auditory system pro­
cesses various information about the surrounding environment. This process­
ing enables the listener to determine the location, distance, and spatial extent of
sound sources, as well as certain characteristics of the rooms. The auditory sys­
tem utilizes different cues, including time and level differences between the ears
and spectral information, to locate sound sources. By comparing the information
received by both ears, humans can perceive their surroundings with considerable
9
==PAGE:17==
clarity. Consequently, audio engineers can artificially simulate these listening ca­
pabilities to process almost any sound source and create a model or simulation
of a real scenario.
Understanding how the body filters sound is crucial for reproducing binaural sound.
As mentioned earlier, sounds travel from a source to the listener and undergo sig­
nificant modifications due to the environment. The physical and geometric prop­
erties of rooms affect the sound signals reaching the listener’s ears, resulting in
reverberation. Three­dimensional sound plays a vital role in virtual reality sys­
tems, enhancing the immersive experience.
2.5.1
Head­Related Transfer Function
When a sound signal reaches both ears, it undergoes changes compared to the
original signal and also differs between the ears. The transfer function that de­
scribes the relationship between a sound source and the final sound in ear canals
is known as the Head­related Transfer Function (HRTF). In acoustics, the HRTF
characterizes how a specific ear (left or right) receives sound from a particular
point in space. The HRTF is dependent on the direction of the sound source rela­
tive to the listener, resulting in temporal and spectral differences between the left
and right ear canals.
Since the ears are located on different sides of the skull, the arrival times of
the sound signal vary depending on the direction. The skull creates an acous­
tic shadow, most noticeable at frequencies above 2 kHz, which affects the ear
farthest from the sound source. Frequencies below 800 Hz are not significantly
affected by this shadowing effect. Additionally, other parts of the body such as
the torso, shoulders, and pinnae also influence the sound. Therefore, it is well­
known that different body parts modify the spectrum of the sound that reaches
the eardrums. These modifications are captured by the HRTF. The HRTF ex­
hibits complex variations in relation to azimuth, elevation, range, and frequency,
and it varies from person to person.
2.5.2
Duda and Brown’s HRTF model
A widely recognized HRTF model has been proposed by Brown and Duda [4]. This
model serves as a straightforward, efficient and effective example for synthesiz­
ing binaural sound from a monaural source. It consists of separate modules that
simulate not only horizontal localization but also vertical effects and externaliza­
tion. Furthermore, the model parameters can be adjusted to match the individual
characteristics of a particular listener.
Each component of the model corresponds to significant structural elements of the
body and the external environment. Consequently, this model provides a highly
simplified representation of complex phenomena. Duda and Brown’s intention
was not to precisely replicate physical processes but rather to offer the simplest
customizable system capable of generating a strong impression of all spatial di­
mensions. As illustrated in Figure 2.6 , a monaural input is fed into the head and
shoulder model, as well as the room model. The outputs of the head and shoulder
model are combined and then passed through the pinna model, which produces
elevation effects. Finally, the output of the room model is added to introduce range

3.1.1
Early reflections
Early reflections are short delayed input signals which are commonly realized with
single channel allpass filter shown in Figure 2.4. Instead, in this project, input
signal is immediately copied to additional channels to have eight chunks of same
audio samples. An audio chunk is commonly referred as a buffer. If we delay
each channel with different amount of samples, input and output will still have the
same energy. Because the processing is preserving energy it can be manifested
as multichannel allpass. The same is valid if we perform matrix multiplication
between multichannel audio buffer and a Hadamard matrix. The delay lengths for
early reflections are random values between zero and 20ms. Delays in seconds
need to be converted to delay in samples which is done by:
Ds = Dt ∗fs
(3.1)
where Ds is delay in samples, Dt delay in seconds and fs is sample rate (44100Hz
in this project) In order to make good diffusion of the input signal, after matrix
multiplication the output is again fed to delay lines. With five iterations of these
”delay­hadamard” processing a good sounding diffusion is achieved.
3.1.2
Hadamard matrix
A Hadamard matrix is a square matrix whose entries are either +1 or ­1, and where
any two distinct rows or columns are orthogonal. In other words, the dot product
of any two rows or columns is zero, except when they are the same. When an
audio buffer is multiplied with Hadamard matrix it spreads audio content between
channels, making more delays and denser output while preserving total energy of
the input. Hadamard matrix with dimensions of 8x8 is shown in Figure 3.3. While
using Hadamard in Matlab is straightforward with the function ”hadamard(8)”, each
element of a matrix was statically declared during C++ implementation as shown

3.1.3
Late reflections
Late reflections are achieved with the main part of reverberation algorithm, with a
feedback delay network. A feedback delay network (FDN) is a type of digital audio
processing algorithm commonly used in artificial reverberation effects. It is based
on the concept of creating a network of delay lines with feedback connections to
generate a reverberant sound.
In a feedback delay network, an input audio signal is fed into multiple delay lines of
varying lengths. Each delay line introduces a specific delay time, typically corre­
sponding to different reflections in a reverberant space. The delayed signals from
these lines are then intermixed together by matrix multiplication with Hadamard
matrix and fed back into the delay lines with certain feedback coefficients called
”feedback gain” which is responsible for system stability.
The feedback in an FDN is what creates the persistence of the reverberation over
time. By carefully choosing the delay lengths and feedback coefficients, the net­
work can simulate the reflections and decay of sound in various acoustic environ­
ments, such as concert halls, rooms or even extremely long reverberation which
are non existent in the real life.
One of the advantages of feedback delay networks is their ability to produce dense
and diffuse reverberation, which can create a sense of spaciousness in audio
recordings. FDNs are often implemented using digital signal processing tech­
niques, making them suitable for real­time applications in audio production and
music synthesis.
Because of frequent case of ”metallic” sounding output a DSP low pass filter (3.2)
is implemented after each delay line before matrix multiplication. In my testing it
resulted in more natural output and possibility to use higher feedback gains which
led to longer and more stable reverberation. The usage of a low pass filter can
also be expected because of natural higher air absorption in the higher frequency
range.
y(n) = αx(n) + (1 −α)y(n −1)
(3.2)
where, lower values of α means lower cutoff frequency of a filter.

in the lossless reverberator prototype. Since it is advantageous to change delay
line lengths in a real time, it is recommended to choose each delay line length Mi
as an integer power of a distinct prime number pi:
Mi = pmi

cending order to facilitate their management and manipulation. Furthermore, the
prime numbers can be utilized in their natural order when selecting the desired
delay­line lengths.

3.2.1
Xcode
Xcode is an integrated development environment (IDE) developed by Apple for
macOS. It is the primary IDE used for developing software for Apple platforms,
including macOS, iOS, iPadOS, watchOS, and tvOS. Xcode provides a compre­
hensive set of tools, compilers, and frameworks to streamline the development
process for various devices. With its integration with JUCE audio plugins can be
exported in VST, VST3, Audio Units(AU) formats which can than be imported into
”Digital Audio Workstation” (DAW) of choice in order to process audio in a real
time. It was used as IDE for building the desired audio plugin.

3.2.2
JUCE
JUCE (Jules’ Utility Class Extensions) is a C++ framework and library primarily
used for developing audio applications, plugins, and interactive software. It was
created by Julian Storer and is known for its versatility, cross­platform compatibil­
ity, and extensive set of features.
It offers a wide range of functionalities, including audio processing, GUI devel­
opment, handling MIDI input/output, managing various audio file formats, imple­
menting audio effects and synthesizers, etc.
One of the key strengths of JUCE is its cross­platform nature. It supports multiple
operating systems, including Windows, macOS, Linux, iOS, and Android, allowing
developers to write code once and deploy it on various platforms without major
modifications.
JUCE is widely used in the audio and music industry as it provides a high­level
abstraction for audio­related tasks, allowing developers to focus on the application
logic rather than low­level implementation details.
In this project, the most JUCE class was AudioBuffer class which helped with
straightforward manipulation of the audio buffers.
3.2.3
Buffer size
One of the main point of concern in real time audio programming is a buffer size
which is declared by the DAW. Ableton Live was a DAW used in my testing and
in its preferences buffer size can be set to 32, 64, 128, 256, 512, 1048 or 2024
samples. If the buffer size is 128 samples, every new 128 samples of incoming
audio will be saved in 128 samples buffer which than can be processed and trans­
ferred to output buffer of the same size. If there is smaller buffer size time latency
between input and the output of a audio will be shorter but at cost of higher usage
of computing power by CPU.
Figure 3.6: Reported input and output latency of the audio system with the chosen

3.2.4
Circular buffer
A programming concept which was used throughout the project was circular buffer.
A circular buffer is a data structure that is used to efficiently manage a fixed­size
buffer. It is a linear data structure where the elements are stored in a continuous
manner. However, unlike a regular linear buffer, a circular buffer has a circular or
wrap­around behavior.
20
==PAGE:28==
When new elements are added to the buffer, they are placed in a circular manner,
starting from the beginning of the buffer again if the end is reached. This wrap­
around behavior allows for efficient utilization of the available memory without
having to shift or reallocate elements.
Circular buffers are commonly used in scenarios where a fixed­size buffer is re­
quired and the oldest data needs to be overwritten when the buffer is full. They
are frequently used in real­time systems, audio processing, and many other ap­
plications that involve buffering and continuous data streams.
3.2.5
Real time compute attributes
When it comes to real­time processing of VST (Virtual Studio Technology) plugins,
the computer’s CPU plays a crucial role. The CPU’s performance determines
how many and how complex VST plugins one can run simultaneously, without
experiencing audio dropouts or latency issues.
Real­time processing refers to the ability to process audio in real­time without
noticeable delays. VST plugins are software instruments or effects that run within
a DAW and process audio signals. In this project all the the testing was done
using VST3 format of plugins. It is the newest edition of VST type.
To ensure smooth real­time processing of VST plugins, it’s important to consider
the following factors related to the CPU:
• CPU Speed: The clock speed of the CPU, measured in GHz, determines
how fast it can execute instructions. A higher clock speed generally allows
for faster processing of audio data.
• Number of Cores: Modern CPUs often have multiple cores, which can han­
dle multiple tasks simultaneously. DAWs can distribute the processing load
of VST plugins across these cores, improving performance. Higher core
counts are beneficial when running complex projects with many plugins.
• CPU Architecture: Different CPU architectures can have varying perfor­
mance characteristics, even at similar clock speeds. Newer CPU architec­
tures often provide better performance per clock cycle, leading to improved
real­time processing capabilities.
• Cache Size: The CPU’s cache is a small and fast memory integrated into
the processor. A larger cache can help reduce memory access times and
improve performance, especially when processing large amounts of data.
• Multithreading: Some plugins and DAWs support multithreading, which means
they can distribute the plugin’s processing load across multiple cores effi­
ciently. It’s beneficial to have a CPU that supports multithreading to take
advantage of this feature.
• Power Management: Ensure that your CPU’s power management settings
are configured appropriately for real­time audio processing. Some power­
saving features, such as CPU throttling, may reduce performance when un­
der heavy load. Adjusting power settings or using high­performance power
plans can help prevent CPU slowdowns.
21
==PAGE:29==
When working with real­time processing of VST plugins, it’s essential to have
a capable CPU that meets the demands of your specific workflow. Consider
factors such as the number and complexity of plugins you intend to use
simultaneously, as well as the overall complexity of your projects.

Discussion and Results
4.1
Processing chain objective results
In this test a dry clap sound is fed to the processing chain in order to observe
changes. The data would be plotted as a spectrogram and as a waveform repre­
sentation in time domain.
A spectrogram is a visual representation of the spectrum of signal’s frequencies as
it varies over time. It is a two­dimensional plot where the x­axis represents time,
the y­axis represents frequency, and the color intensity represents the magnitude
or power of the frequencies at each point in time.
The spectrogram is obtained by dividing the signal into short overlapping seg­
ments and applying the Fourier Transform to each segment. This results in a time­
frequency representation of the signal, where the variations in frequency content
over time are visualized.
Each spectrogram rendering depends on few parameters:
• Window size ­ The window size determines the length of each analysis win­
dow in samples. It controls the trade­off between time and frequency res­
olution. A longer window provides better frequency resolution but poorer
time resolution. Conversely, a shorter window gives better time resolution
but poorer frequency resolution.
• Overlap ­ The overlap specifies the number of samples by which consecu­
tive windows overlap. It helps to reduce artifacts and improve the temporal
resolution of the spectrogram. Common choices for overlap are 50% (half
the window size) or 75% (three­quarters of the window size).
• FFT Length ­ The FFT (Fast Fourier Transform) length determines the num­
ber of frequency bins in the spectrogram. It is typically set equal to the
window size to achieve the best frequency resolution. A higher FFT length
results in more frequency bins and finer frequency resolution.
• Window Function ­ The window function shapes the analysis window to re­
duce spectral leakage. Commonly used window functions include the Hann
window, Hamming window, and Blackman window. The choice of window
function depends on the specific analysis requirements and trade­offs be­
tween main lobe width and sidelobe attenuation.
All the spectrograms shown in this report are made with Hamming window of 1024
samples, overlap of 512 samples and 1024 FFT points.

4.1.4
Head shadow
Another concept which should be considered is how the head shadow and pinna
model alters the audio signal. The diffused clap signal was first sent to head
shadow model as shown in Figure 3.2. For the case of this testing, azimuth angle
was set to 90° which indicate that direction of the audio signal is directly to the
right ear. That means that the left ear should have decent amount of head shadow
effects. By looking at the spectrograms shown in Figure 4.4 it can be seen that
left channel contains less energy comparing to right channel. What is more, the
biggest difference is in higher frequency ranges while the frequency below 100Hz
are almost identical. The reason is that the wavelength of of lower frequencies is
much longer than head radius so that both ears are perceiving the same, while
higher frequencies with short wavelength compared to the head radius are blocked
by head skull and torso.

4.2
Influence of the plugin parameters
The VST3 plugin that has been made have four parameters: delay length, feed­
back gain, feedback alpha and dry/wet gain.
• Delay length ­ this parameter gives control over delay length of first channel
while the other channels delays are connected with the first channel through
declared multipliers to be mutually prime between each other. Values are
manipulated from 0 to 50000 samples.
• Feedback gain ­ controls the gain at which the feedback channels are summed
with the input samples in FDN 3.4. Values are manipulated from 0 to 1, but
highest values are only achievable with a lot of filtering addition (low feed­
back alpha) as otherwise the processing would be come unstable.
• Feedback alpha ­ controls the amount of low pass filtering applied after every
delay line. Values are manipulated from 0 to 1.
• Dry/wet gain ­ controls the amount of dry unprocessed signal compared to
wet processed signal. If the parameter is set to zero, no signal alteration
is happening, while if the parameter is set to 1, only processed reverberant
signal is produced. Values are manipulated from 0 to 1.

Another test has been done to investigate a common parameter in acoustics, re­
verberation time. A Dirac delta function has been put through basic processing
algorithm (3.1) to receive an impulse response. The Dirac delta function, often
denoted as δ(t), is a mathematical function that is defined as an idealized impulse
or spike at t = 0 and zero everywhere else. It is a generalized function or distri­
bution, primarily used in mathematical analysis, signal processing, and physics.
An impulse response refers to the response of a system or a device to an input
signal that is an impulse or Dirac delta function. It provides valuable information
about the system’s characteristics, such as its frequency response, time­domain
behavior, and stability. It is particularly useful in analyzing linear time­invariant
(LTI) systems, where the response to any input can be obtained by convolving
the input signal with the impulse response.
The impulse response of a system at two of stability limits is shown in Figure 4.6.
The system plotted in Figure 4.6a has feedback gain = 0.5, feedback alpha =
0.54 and first channel delay length is 2048 samples, while a system shown in
Figure 4.6b has feedback gain = 0.4, feedback alpha = 0.82 and the same delay
length. By looking at the impulse response, it is possible to declare reverberation
time. As noted in section 2.1, reverberation time RT60 is a period of time that it
takes a sound to decay by 60dB. Common way of declaring RT60 is to look at
the time needed for 30dB decay, denoted by T30, and than multiply it with two, to
approximate 60dB decay time. This method is common because of noise floor
levels in real room scenarios. In this system, there are no real room microphone
measurements so there is nonexistant noise floor level, but T30 can be used as
the decay is smooth and linear which leads to valid approximation. The RT60 of a
plotted impulse responses are 24.5 and 599.78 seconds, which is extremely high.
Achieving huge reverberation time was a desired feature of this project work.

Subjective Testing
4.3.1
Preparation of a subjective test
Testing the sound quality of two audio plugins is a complex task due to various
factors such as different algorithms, equipment, subjective experiences, test pro­
cedures, and designs. Therefore, determining which plugin sounds better is not
a straightforward process. Additionally, it can be argued that in an audio post­
production scenario, users choose plugins for different purposes. Nevertheless,
it is valuable to design an experiment and analyze the data for potential insights.
Prior to proceeding with the experiment design, online research was done. An
intriguing guide by ITU (International Telecommunication Union), which outlines
general methods for the subjective assessment of sound quality. This guide is
based on ITU­R Recommendation BS.1116, which focuses on assessing minor
impairments in audio systems, including multichannel sound systems. Although
these recommendations were not strictly followed as they primarily address small
impairments in telecommunications and are not specifically tailored to audio post­
production effects. Nonetheless, the guide has been highly beneficial for testing
plugin implementations. The guide began by defining audio quality as an attribute
encompassing all aspects of the sound being assessed. This includes, but is not
limited to, elements like timbre, transparency, stereophonic imaging, spatial pre­
sentation, reverberance, echoes, harmonic distortions, quantization noise, pops,
clicks, and background noise. The main attributes, as outlined in Appendix A of
the thesis, served as the foundation for the test design. These attributes were
further divided into sub­attributes, which were used as questions during the ex­
periment. Given the specificity of these sub­attributes, a document was prepared
with definitions to assist the participants in comprehending and responding to the
questions accurately.
A test with two listening sessions in which participants were instructed to listen
the two audio samples and subsequently respond to several questions regard­
ing sound quality was presented. The participants consisted of five friends who
should be regarded as non­experts and five musicians which are familiar with au­
dio post production. The used audio samples were recorded by me, on an open
35
==PAGE:43==
air concert of jazz trio. The 18 seconds long audio recordings were cut. The mu­
sic was played by a band consisting of electric guitar, drums and double bass. To
mimic their position on a stage, a guitar was processed thru advance algorithm
shown in 3.2 with an azimuth angle of 270° which corresponds to source direct to
left ear, drums with an azimuth angle of 0° which represent central position and
double bass with and angle of 90°. These three audio files were than mixed to­
gether using Ableton Live to form one audio file presented in listening test. The
second audio file was prepared in the same manner but individual files were pro­
cessed through basic algorithm shown in 3.1, therefore, there was not possibility
of changing the azimuth angle. It can be noted the the drums were recorded with
3 microphones but the signal was summed to mono (1 channel) content before
processing. Throughout the test, the order of the two audio samples was random­
ized, and a set of eight questions were presented. All participants used the same
headphones (AKG 271 MKII). They were instructed to evaluate various sound
quality attributes using a 5­grade Likert scale, which is recommended by [21] and
detailed in Table 4.3. Audio samples presented in subjective test are included as

5
Conclusion
In this thesis, two audio processing effects has been presented, discussed and
analyzed. It has been shown that adding additional processing regarding early
reflections, inspired by binaural listening, can have beneficial influence on sound
perception. Furthermore, the type of subjective test that has been run is quite
trivial and can not be taken as deciding factor, but having additional options for
spatial localization during audio post production was a plus for all expert subjec­
tive test participants. Moreover, it was surprising to achieve such a flexible and
decent quality reverberation plugin with only multichannel feedback delay network
containing low pass filters and Hadamard mixing matrix. It has proven to be the
most straightforward delay network artificial reverberation design for achieve good
quality at low computing cost.
5.1
Future work
The main additional work is to implement whole processing of presented audio
algorithm in C++/JUCE framework. What is more, changing delay length param­
eter in real time produces momentary audible glitches which should definitely be
fixed for a more serious usage of a plugin. Although current processing power
consumption is not bad, with more prudent C++ coding processing power con­
sumption can be reduced. Finally, the current plugin version uses generic GUI.
An unique graphical work would definitely be beneficial for first impression if the
product would be available for commercial market.

ITU attributes
The list of main attributes, sub­attributes and definitions used during the experi­
ment:
SPATIAL IMPRESSION The performance appears to take place in an appropriate
spatial environment.
• Homogenity of the spatial sound: The subjective impression that the sound
space is a homogeneous whole.
STEREO IMPRESSION The sound image appears to have the correct and ap­
propriate direction distribution of sound sources.
• Directional balance: The subjective impression that the sound sources within
the sound image are placed in a way which makes the entire image bal­
anced.
• Location accuracy: The subjective impression that all sound sources are ac­
curately positioned in the sound image.
• Sound image width: The subjective impression of an appropriate width of
the sound stage in the stereo sound field.
TRANSPARENCY All details of performance can be clearly perceived.
• Sound source definition: The subjective impression that different instru­
ments or voices sounding simultaneously can be identified and distinguished.
TIMBRE ­ SOUND COLOURING Accurate portrayal of the different sound char­
acteristics of sound source(s).
• Sound colour: The subjective impression of an appropriate sound for each
source including all its characteristic harmonic elements.
FREEDOM FROM NOISE AND DISTORTIONS
• Absence of various disturbing phenomena such as electrical, acoustic noise,
public noise, bit errors, distortions, etc.
MAIN IMPRESSION/APPRECIATION
• The subjective impression/appreciation of the recordings
