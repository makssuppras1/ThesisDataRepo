Abstract
Gradient descent and its variants haves long been the go-to method for training neural
network model parameters. Large bodies of work in machine learning, numerical analy-
sis, and statistics have considered gradient descent to be a discretization of a continuous
gradient flow, and used backwards error analysis to derive equations which minimize the
local error of this integrator. The modified terms of the resulting flow are considered to
be the implicit gradient regularizer. However, little attempt to generalize this method to
gradient descent with momentum has been made. We provide two modified equations
for gradient descent with momentum via backwards error analysis. First, we consider
momentum gradient descent as a splitting method on a dissipative Hamiltonian system,
and provide novel derivation of the resulting modified equation. We then show that the
implicit effects of momentum are analogous to those in gradient descent, but in the phase
space of the system. Second, we provide a modified equation via backwards error anla-
ysis on multistep methods. We compare our first results to the ad-hoc modified equation
of Ghosh, by comparing both trajectories to momentum gradient descent. Furthermore,
we evaluate the effects of explicitly modifying the systems, when training a deep MLP to
classify the MNIST dataset, and measuring standard performance benchmarks including
the ‚Äôflatness‚Äô of the minima found.

Introduction
Imagine that your are on the top of a mountain, and you are trying to find the fastest way
down. You have access to your phone, which gives you your longitudinal and latitudinal
coordinates x = (x1, x2), and you know that the height of the mountain is given by h(x).
Which path should you take to descent the mountain as fast as possible?
Intuitively, how fast you move on the mountain is measured by how similar the change of
your coordinates is to the slope of the mountain. To move down the mountain as efficiently
as possible, you must make this value as negative as possible, and so you should walk
in the direction opposite to which the mountain is steepest. In other words, your most
efficient path follows the trajectory

(1.0.1)
This is gradient flow, and each you step you take is a step of gradient descent.
Such movement will allow you to arrive at the bottom of the mountain in a slow, but steady,
fashion, assuming that the mountain is well-behaved. But according to your rule, you
might consider yourself at the bottom of the mountain if you fall into a small hole or valley.
Or, you might move very slowly on a shallow part of the mountain, since your rule does
not tell how you fast to move.
Now suppose a heavy ball rolls down the mountain. It is an inanimate object, unable to
control itself against the force of gravityÕĺ it will roll out of small holes, and barrel through
valleys after rolling off a steep parts of the mountain. So what is it‚Äôs rule for getting down
the mountain? Whereas you move directly in the direction of steepest descent, at each
step, the ball starts moving in the direction of steepest descent. That is, the slope of the
mountain changes its momentum‚Äďits position then changes according to its momentum.
But, the ball is also under the force of friction, and so will eventually stop moving. Calling
the momentum of the ball v, and the coefficient of friction ő≥, the movement of the ball is

The second answer requires a slight change of perspective, utilizing the fact that we have
more information about the ball‚Äôs state. That is, when modelling such movement, we
consider how both its position and velocity change. The space this happens in is called
phase space.
Phase space has some special physical and geometrical properties. It is a classical result
from physics that each point in phase space corresponds to the energy of the system it is
describing. This energy is called the Hamiltonian of the system, and trajectories in phase
space describe how the energy of the system evolves through time.
From a geometrical point of view, the space no longer corresponds to a physical space,
and so it makes little sense to measure distances or angles in this space. Therefore,
we use an alternative measure, which measures volumes, and the orientation of these
volumes. This way, we can measure both the differences of both the position and mo-
mentum of to states in phase space. This measure is the symplectic 2-form of the space,
and the study of such kinds of spaces is called symplectic geometry.
With all of this in mind, we are ready to begin our thesis.
1.0.1
A More Technical Introduction
Gradient descent, as described above, is the basis for essentially all learning algorithms
in modern machine learning. Adding momentum has been shown to improve both the
convergence rate when estimating model parameters [1],[2], and the generalization capa-
bilities of modern neural network architectures [3]. Using momentum is almost a necessity
when working with modern neural network architectures.
Virtually every gradient algorithm seeks to minimize the cost function E of a learner. The
function E is parameterized by őł, the parameters of the learner, and measures in some
way or the other the accuracy of the learner. Additionally, in every such algorithm, a small
step size h must be defined.
In the case of gradient descent (GD), recent work has used backwards error analysis
(BEA) to find the modified trajectory induced by the finite step-size h [4, 5]. The new
dynamics of this trajectory are called implicit gradient regularization (IGR), and are in-
terpreted as penalizing the ‚Ąď2 norm of the cost function gradient ||‚ąáőłE(őł)||2. This work
furthermore shows that the addition of this regularizer pushes the trajectory towards flatter
minima of the cost function.
However, the case of gradient descent with momentum (GDM) is more complicated, be-
cause we can choose which space we work in. Or, alternatively, we can chose neither,
and construct the dynamics induced by GDM ad-hoc. This is the approach taken by
Ghosh, et.al. [6], which is the only other work we could find which studies the implicit
regularization due to momentum (IMR).
We consider gradient descent with momentum to be a symplectic integrator‚Äďan integra-
tor which preserves the symplectic properties of phase space‚Äďon the equations of mo-
tion (1.0.2) for the ball we considered above. Note, however, that these dynamics are
equivalent to (1.0.3), which in physics describes the motion of a forced-damped harmonic
oscillator.
Therefore, for the remainder of this thesis, we describe the movement of the ball as
damped harmonic oscillation, and make no differentiation between this movement and
the movement of our ball down our mountain. We justify this by noting that the energy of
our ball and the energy of damped harmonic oscillator are equivalent in phase space. For
the ball to stop moving, it must reach the bottom of the mountain (or a particularly deep

hole in the mountain), and stop moving, which implies that it stops accelerating. The
damped harmonic oscillator stops moving for the same reasons, except that the potential
force influencing it is generally not gravity. Furthermore, since we treat these systems as
equivalent in phase space, we treat GDM as an energy minimization problem, and use
this language going forward.
Such a shift of interpretation provides a sound theoretical basis for performing BEA.
Damped harmonic oscillation have long been considered the underlying dynamics of GDM
[1], and GDM can be reached by constructing the appropriate integrator. In physics and
geometry, damped harmonic oscillation has long been studied as a dissipative Hamilto-
nian system [7]Õĺ a smaller body of work connects such a framework to momentum algo-
rithms (see [8]). However, to the best of our knowledge, this is the first attempt to consider
GDM as a splitting method on such a system, derive an induced modified equation from
that method, and study its impact explicitly in phase space.
Furthermore, in researching this thesis, we have not found a cohesive, theoretical frame-
work for using backwards error analysis, to describe why such a small modification to the
algorithm has so big an impact. This is in part due to how, under many interpretations,
we are now considering global properties of the algorithm‚Äôs path on the loss landscape,
while backwards error analysis works with local truncation error. Similarly, studying the
geometric properties of GDM is still a niche topic, although the theory of geometric nu-
merical integration is well established. We will discuss more of this later. Therefore, we
proceed through this chapter as follows: first, we apply BEA directly to (4.0.4) and arrive
at a modified equation directly. In the section after, we consider GDM as a conformal
Hamiltonian system, and derive a modified equation elegantly through splitting methods.
Thesis Layout
The layout of this paper is as follows. First, for the convenience of the reader, we pro-
vide a brief overview of the background material needed to understand the results of this
thesis. Similarly, we summarize the foundational results of this paper, produced by Bar-
ret and Dherin, which use BEA applied to gradient descent, both in its deterministic and
stochastic versions. Additionally, we will provide a novel interpretation of the modified
terms, interpreting them in terms of energy-minimization, leading to new insights about
the geometric properties of the modified equation.
Next, we will do the same with momentum-gradient descent (MGD). Other literature has
presented modified equations for MGD as high-definition equations, and as piece-wise
differentiable systems. However, we restrain ourselves to BEA, and extend the novel
derivation we provide, to find our modified equations. To the best of our knowledge, these
results have not been produced in the literature before.
Finally, we will compare our results to the other similar result, found by Ghosh, et.al., which
we mentioned above. We study the modified terms both theoretically and empirically, by
defining them explicitly in the loss function used to train neural networks.

Neural Networks as Discrete Dynamical Systems
Much recent study has gone in to studying neural networks as discrete dynamical sys-

perspective, it can be shown that parameter updates can be performed without backpro-

NNs can be studied via their continuous counterparts. Such a study has recently born
fruit specifically in the study of recurrent neural networks (RNNs) [14].

which we interpret as the implicit regularizing effect.
The theory of BEA is rich, and although one may consider it as a direct calculation, in this
paper we consider it as taking into account higher order Lie derivatives of the system being
approximated. We explore this connection in the appendix, while using it to help interpret
our main results. For more information about error analysis in numerical integration, we
refer the reader to [21, 22].

Summary
To end this section, we reiterate the main train of though which applies each of the topics
above to the rest of this thesis, and why we find them beneficial to both the derivation of
our results and their interpretations.
1. Although subtle, it is beneficial to re-frame gradient algorithms in a physical context
as an energy minimization problem, instead of loss minimization problem. These
two interpretations are mathematically equivalent, and make no difference to the
purely formal study of this paper. However, we have found that the physicist‚Äôs mind-
set has allowed us to understand the effects of implicit regularization more deeply.
Indeed, if the purpose of the thesis is to understand the effects of implicit regulariza-
tion, we find it natural to contextualize the problem in a setting, of which the effects
can be seen physically.
Similarly, such a contextualization allows us to borrow profound results from tradi-
tionally unrelated fields, including geometric considerations which make our results
easier to interpret.
2. We find it beneficial to expand our understanding of backwards error analysis, be-
yond that of a computational tool. Perhaps facetiously, we find that this point re-
quires little explanation. Relating BEA to the structure of the dynamics being an-
alyzed is critical to the entire goal of the the thesis, which is understanding of the
dynamics at hand. Relating it to Lie algebra further allows us to interpret the IR
effects geometrically, providing potential bridges between our work and other work
treating neural networks as geometrical objects.
3. Finally, we find it beneficial to understand GDM using somewhat more exotic math-
ematical object, mainly as a conformal symplectic integrator. Although the analysis
of GDM as such in this thesis is admittedly somewhat shallow, we nevertheless be-
lieve that it puts our results on stronger theoretical footing than previous results. As
mathematicians, we must hope that such a foundation provides a solid ground upon
which our work can be expanded.

5
Literature Comparison and Results
In this chapter, we take the results of the last chapter, and compare them to existing
literature on implicit regularization due to momentum. Specifically, we have found one
paper [6] which applies a method similar to backwards error analysis to GDM, to arrive
at a piece-wise first-order ODE, which they claim represents the continuous dynamics of
GDM. We will argue that our results are better on three fronts:
1. First, we will challenge the assumptions they make, in order to arrive at their modified
equation. That is, we will argue that our approach is the more natural mathematical
setting in which to treat the problem. Specifically, we challenge the assumption that
BEA cannot be performed because GDM is a global algorithm.
2. Second, we will provide experimental results which indicate the validity of our ap-
proach. We do so in two suites of experiments: first, we study the setting of a toy
optimization problem solved via GDM, and show that our solution behaves better
on long time frames. Next, we examine the implicit effects explicitly, by training a
simple MLP with cross-entropy loss to classify the MNIST dataset.
This comparison and the subsequent results are treated in the order in which they are
shown above. Additionally, in appendix (D) we provide a heuristic argument about the
performance differences of both our models, but leave proving or disproving our hunch
as further research.

5.2
Challenging the Approach of Ghosh, et.al
Now, we challenge the two assumptions above, in the order they are presented.
5.2.1
The Global Nature of GDM
Ghosh, et.al. are direct about the fact that they view GDM as a global algorithm, and, as
such, BEA cannot be applied. However, this only holds if we wish to modify only őł. As we
argued in the last chapter, it is more natural to consider the algorithm as updating points
(v, őł) in phase space, where the algorithm is a local update.
The difference between their approach and ours is essentially a difference in interpreta-
tion, considering a dynamical system for őł to be the movement of őł as a curve őł(t) on
the loss manifold (őł, E(őł)). It is entirely right to view (5.1.1) as a global algorithm, as mo-
mentum changes the dynamics of the algorithm by keeping a short-term (still non-local)
memory of the position. However, in phase space, BEA is easily applied, and provides
the interpretation that momentum implicitly modifies both the position and momentum v
of őł.
Indeed, the phase-space interpretation of GDM is exactly what allows us to view it as a
local algorithm. That is, if both the position and momentum of the curve őł(t) are known
at time t, we have complete information about its dynamics, and its trajectory can be
updated locally. It is then completely natural to consider the effects of a finite step-size on
the momentum of the curve.
In essence, both interpretations would be valid, if the results showed similar convergence
and stability. But, as we see in our experiments, our results are more stable over step-
sizes and momentum terms, and find flatter minima‚Äďthe conventional way of measuring
the effects of implicit regularization.

Experimental Setup

produce the averaged results of three runs.
Remark 5.3.1. A more rigorous experimental analysis would train over many more than
50 epochs, and average over more than 3 runs. We chose these values as a tradeoff
between training time, and the number of parameters we were able to train over. We
chose to test a wider suite of parameter values at the cost of depth.
However, although not produced here, we did test with more epochs (up to 300), and more
runs (up to 10), and found the results to be about the same. The MLP interpolates the
data after approximately 20-25 epochs, and that the runs are quite consistent with each
other. This justifies our low choice of both.

6
Conclusion
For the reader‚Äôs convenience, we summarize the objective of this thesis below.
In this thesis, we have derived a modified equation for the continuous dynamics of gradient
descent of momentum, induced by GDM. We are not the first to do so. However, to the
best of our knowledge, we are the first to produce the modified equation, using BEA for
splitting methods, and to interpret the results as implicit regularization in phase space. In
this way, we have shown that the modified equation is completely analogous to the story
understood in previous literature, when we broaden our information about the energy of
the trajectory to include its momentum. If we have been successful, the reader will see
that our interpretation is more mathematically sensible than the infant results existing on
momentum methods.
Furthermore, we have shown that our derived modified equation performs better than the
attempt by Ghosh, across multiple metrics which by now have become standard bench-
marks. We believe that this further confirms our interpretation.
We hope that this thesis is able to provide a deeper mathematical understanding of neural
networks. We believe that such an understanding is paramount to future research in
machine learning and artificial intelligence.
6.1
Further Research
We see two avenues for further research based on the results of this thesis. First, we
wish to extend this method to Nesterov‚Äôs accelerated momentum, but were unable to do
so in this thesis. Furthermore, we wish to explicitly study the regularizers for stochastic
gradient descent. The starting point for such an analysis developed by Barrett and Dherin
is provided in the appendix, for the reader‚Äôs convenience. Finally, a more rigorous mathe-
matical treatment of the convergence rates of all methods considered would be beneficial
to our analysis. We provide a pseudo-rigorous analysis of these convergences in the
appendix.
Second, our rigorous theoretical treatment opens many avenues for considering the mod-
ified equations derived by us, and by Barrett and Dherin. For example, considering the
implicit effects in more general context of energy minimization opens connections to geo-
metric complexity, which has been shown to act as a proxy for all different sorts of regu-
larization, and to predict the generalization ability of DNNs [34]. In future work, we would
also like to study these results in the explicit contexts of, for example, Hamiltonian descent
and kernal methods.
