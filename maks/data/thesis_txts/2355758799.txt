Abstract  
Type 1 diabetes (T1D) is an autoimmune disease, caused by a selective immune-mediated destruction of the 
insulin producing β-cells, resulting in complete loss of insulin production. The proinflammatory cytokines 
interleukin-1 (IL-1) β, tumor necrosis factor (TNF)- α and interferon (IFN)- γ, released from activated 
immune cells, are cytotoxic to β-cells and act as key mediators of β-cell damage and death. These cytokines 
induce β-cell apoptosis by increasing the expression of pro-apoptotic and endoplasmic reticulum (ER) stress-
related genes. It has been demonstrated that the N-methyl-D-aspartate receptor (NMDAR) is expressed in β-
cells. NMDARs act as Ca2+ channels and require binding of ligands and depolarization of the membrane for 
activation. It has been demonstrated that inhibition of NMDARs reduces cytokine-induced β-cell death, 
however, the underlying protective effect of NMDAR inhibition remains to be clarified. The purpose of this 
project was to characterize the protective effect of NMDAR inhibition on cytokine-induced β-cell apoptosis 
and to investigate the underlying molecular mechanisms.  
The expression of NMDARs in insulin-secreting insulinoma INS-1E cells has been confirmed in this study. 
As expected, inhibition of NMDARs by the antagonist MK-801 reduced cytokine-induced apoptosis in INS-
1E cells. However, this effect was specific for apoptosis and not observed when both apoptotic and necrotic 
cell death was measured. The inhibition of NMDARs increased chronic insulin release in cytokine-exposed 
INS-1E cells. Nonetheless, NMDAR inhibition reduced glucose-stimulated insulin secretion (GSIS). NMDAR 
inhibition had no effect on cytokine-induced phosphorylation of extracellular signal-regulated kinase (ERK) 
or c-jun N-terminal kinase (JNK), which are members of the mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) family 
that are activated by IL-1β and contribute to β-cell failure. Further, NMDAR inhibition neither affected nitric 
oxide (NO) production and inducible nitric oxide synthase (iNOS) expression that is involved in cytokine-
induced ER stress in β-cells. The expression of the ER stress related protein CCAT/enhancer-binding protein 
homologous protein (CHOP) was neither affected by NMDAR inhibition. NMDAR inhibition had no effect 
on the intrinsic apoptosis pathway regulators Bcl-2 and Bax, or the chemokine Cxcl10. However, the 
expression of the pro-apoptotic gene Bim was downregulated in cytokine-induced cells when NMDARs were 
inhibited. 
Taken together, the obtained data do supports that NMDAR inhibition reduces cytokine-induced apoptosis. 
However, the mechanism by which NMDAR inhibition protects against cytokine-induced apoptosis in β-cells 
remains unknown.

Dansk Resumé 
Type 1 diabetes (T1D) er en autoimmun sygdom, der skyldes selektiv ødelæggelse af de insulinproducerende 
β-celler, hvilket resulterer i ophør af insulinproduktionen. De proinflammatoriske cytokiner interleukin-1 
(IL1) β, tumor nekrose faktor (TNF)-α og interferon (IFN)-γ, der frigives fra aktiverede immunceller, er 
cytotoksiske for β-celler og derfor spiller en væsentlig rolle i β-celle ødelæggelse og død. IL-1β, TNF-α og 
IFN-γ inducerer β-celle apoptose ved, at øge ekspressionen af pro-apoptotiske gener samt endoplasmatisk 
retikulum (ER) stress-relaterede gener. Det er blevet påvist at  N-methyl-D-aspartat (NMDA) receptorer 
udtrykkes på β-celler. NMDA receptorer fungere som Ca2+ kanaler, der aktiveres ved binding af ligander samt 
depolarisering af membranen. Inhibering af NMDA receptorer har vist at reducere cytokin-induceret β-celle 
død, men mekanismen bag denne beskyttende effekt kendes endnu ikke. Formålet med dette projekt var 
derfor, at karakterisere den beskyttende effekt af NMDA receptor inhibering af cytokin-induceret β-celle 
apoptose samt undersøge de molekylære mekanismer bag dette.     
I dette projekt blev ekspressionen af NMDA receptorer bekræftet i insulin-udskillende insulinoma INS-1E 
celler. Inhibering af NMDA receptorer via. antagonisten MK-801 viste desuden mindre cytokin-induceret 
apoptose. Denne beskyttende effekt blev kun observeret under apoptose og ikke når både apoptose og nekrose 
celledød blev målt. NMDA receptor inhibering viste øget kronisk insulin udskillelse fra cytokin-udsatte INS-
1E celler. NMDA receptor inhibering resulterede dog i mindre glukose-stimuleret insulin sekretion (GSIS). 
Yderligere, havde NMDA receptor inhibering ingen påvirkning på cytokin-induceret fosforylering af 
ekstracellulær signalreguleret kinase (ERK) eller c-Jun N-terminal kinase (JNK), som er medlemmerer af 
mitogen aktiveret protein kinase (MAPK) familien, der bliver aktiveret af IL-1β og bidrager til β-celle 
destruktion. Endvidere havde NMDA receptor inhibering ingen effekt på inducerbar nitrogenoxid syntase 
(iNOS) og nitrogenoxid (NO), som er involveret i cytokin-induceret ER stress i β-celler. Yderligere havde 
NMDA receptor inhibering ingen effekt på det ER stress-relateret protein CCAT/forøger-binding protein 
homolog protein (CHOP). NMDA receptor inhibering havde ingen virkning på de intrinsiske apoptose-
regulerede gener Bcl-2 og Bax eller på chemokin genet Cxcl10. Ekspressionen af det pro-apoptotiske gen Bim 
var derudover nedreguleret i cytokin-udsatte celler under NMDA receptor inhibering.  
Det kan således konkluderes, at data understøtter at NMDA receptor inhibering reducerer cytokin-induceret 
apoptose. Hvorimod mekanismen bag den beskyttende effekt af NMDA receptor inhibering af cytokin-
induceret β-celle apoptose forbliver uklar.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Introduction to type 1 diabetes 
The pancreatic islets are composed of three main endocrine cell types- β, α and δ cells that secrete insulin, 
glucagon and somatostatin, respectively. The secretion of these hormones are important for preserving the 
glucose homeostasis during fasting and feeding [1]. Insulin is a particularly important regulator of the glucose 
metabolism, by stimulating glucose uptake and storage in muscle, fat and liver. When insulin secretion is 
absent or reduced, due to impaired β-cell function, the blood glucose level increases, known as 
hyperglycaemia, which is characteristic of diabetes [2]. 
   
There are two major types of diabetes: type 1 diabetes (T1D) and type 2 diabetes (T2D). T2D is the most 
prevalent form and is characterized by insulin resistance and an insufficient compensation of more insulin 
secretion. T1D is characterized by an autoimmune-mediated destruction of the β-cells, resulting in complete 
loss of insulin production [3]. In this project the focus is on T1D.  
 
Today, more than 415 million people worldwide suffer from either T1D or T2D and this number is expected 
to increase. In Denmark, is it estimated that >400.000 people suffer from diabetes [4] of which T1D constitute 
10% of the total cases. This number is believed to increase to over 600.000 in 2025 (www.diabetes.dk).   
 
T1D is caused by an autoimmune reaction, where the immune system destroy the insulin-producing β-cells 
[2]. Patients diagnosed with T1D have 70% - 80% of their β-cells eliminated [5]. If untreated, diabetes is 
lethal and daily injections with insulin are necessary to maintain a normal blood glucose level [6]. However, 
despite insulin treatment, patients suffering from diabetes are still at risk of developing serious health 
complications, including kidney failure, heart diseases, stroke, and blindness [7]. 
 
The aetiology of T1D is not completely understood. It is believed, though, that T1D is the result of a 
combination of genetic susceptibility and environmental factors. Gene studies have identified multiple genes 
that are associated with the development of T1D, including human leukocyte antigen (HLA), insulin gene and 
cytotoxic T lymphocyte antigen 4 (CTLA-4). Epidemiological data has further indicated several 
environmental risk factors such as, viral infections e.g enteroviruses, cow’s milk, wheat proteins and bacteria 
[7]. 
 
Even though extensive research has been done to identify the genes and environmental risk factors in T1D, it 
has not resulted in discovering the exact cause of T1D.

1.2 Pathogenesis of T1D 
The pathogenesis of T1D has been extensively studied in animal models such as, the Non obese diabetic 
(NOD) mouse and the Biobreeding (BB) rat, both of which spontaneously develop a T1D phenotype. 
However, since there are significant differences between the in vivo models and human disease, a great 
amount of information on etiology and pathogenesis, at the human level, is still wanted [8]. Nevertheless, is it 
accepted that a cell-mediated immune response is the cause of β-cell destruction in T1D, in this process, 
proinflammatory cytokines are believed to play an important role.  
 
In T1D, pancreatic islets are invaded by antigen presenting cells (APCs), cytotoxic CD8+ T-cells, CD4+ T-
cells and autoantibody-producing B-cells [9], as part of an inflammatory reaction, known as insulitis [10]. 
These immune cells are activated and contribute to the production of nitric oxide (NO) and proinflammatory 
cytokines such as, interleukin-1 (IL-1) β, tumor necrosis factor (TNF)- α and interferon (IFN)- γ.  
 
One proposed model of the pathogenesis of T1D involves an “initiation” and “execution” phase [11]. In the 
“initiation” phase, initial β-cell damage could be mediated through toll-like receptors (TLRs) [12], which are 
an important family of pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs). TLRs are localized on macrophages 
and other immune cells and they are able to detect components associated with microbial pathogens [13]. 
TLR3 is present on β-cells and recognizes viral double-stranded (ds) RNA during viral infection [14]. TLR3 
expression triggers the release of proinflammatory cytokines, which will cause activation of innate immune 
cells [13]. Immune cells seek to eliminate the viral infection and cause apoptosis of the virus-infected cells. In 
genetically susceptible individuals, these attempts to eradicate the virus, might induce progressive 
inflammation and prolonged β-cell loss, thus causing the development of T1D [12]. In the “execution” phase, 
CD8+ T cells recognize autoantigens, displayed on major histocompatibility complex (MHC) class I 
molecules, located on the surface of β-cells [10]. This recognition will lead to β-cell killing through the 
Fas/Fas-ligand signalling or secretion of perforins and granzymes [1].  
 
The detection of β-cell antigens will initiate the adaptive immune system. In the islets APCs will take up and 
process β-cell antigens, which activates APCs to secrete IL-1β and TNF-α, along with induction of co-
stimulatory signals [15]. This stimulates migration to the draining pancreatic lymph nodes [10], where APCs 
will present β-cell antigens on MHC class II to mature naïve T-cells [15]. This leads to proliferation and 
differentiation of naïve T-helper (TH) 0 CD4+ T-cells into effector TH1 CD4+ T cells [1]. CD4+ T-cells are 
responsible for IFN-γ production [16]. IFN-γ increases the expression of MHC class II molecules on APCs 
and further induces IL-1β and TNF-α secretion. In addition, IFN-γ secretion leads to recruitment and 
activation of additional immune cells and thus amplify the inflammatory infiltration [15]. CD8+ T-cells 
recognizes β-cell antigens, presented by the β-cell on MHC class I molecules, and mediate β-cell apoptosis 
through the secretion of perforin and granzymes and/or Fas/Fas-ligand signalling [1]. In addition, CD4+ T-

The adaptive immune response involves production of autoantibodies that recognize the epitope on several

1.3 Cytokine-signalling in β-cells 
β-cells are susceptible to the cytotoxic effects of inflammatory cytokines [5][17]. Inflammatory cytokines 
induce numerous pro-apoptotic genes and endoplasmic reticulum (ER) stress-related genes [21], where the 
enzyme inducible NO synthase (iNOS) is a major gene target of signal transducer and activator of 
transcription 1 (STAT1) and nuclear factor κ-light-chain-enhancer of activated B cells (NF-κB) [22][23]. 
iNOS is involved in the production of NO, synthesized from arginine and O2 [24] and responsible for 
cytokine-induced ER stress in T1D [25]. In addition, cytokines increase Fas expression and MHC class 1 
expression on β-cells [19][20] and cause expression of several cytokines and chemokines [26][27] that further 
contribute to β-cell killing, through autocrine actions and chemoattraction of immune cells [28]. It has been 
demonstrated that IL-1β, TNF-α and IFN-γ alone have limited effects on β-cell, but a combination of these 
cytokines plays an important role for β-cell death and dysfunction [29]. It is therefore believed that IL-1β, 
TNF-α and IFN-γ act in synergy to trigger β-cell failure [28]. The combination of IFN-γ and IL-1β signalling 
can potentiate death in insulin-secreting insulinoma INS-1 cells and in rat islets [30]. Consequently, in this 
project TNF-α was not used to induce β-cell apoptosis and the TNF-α intracellular signalling pathway is not 
described. 
 
1.3.1 IL-1β signalling  
IL-1β and IL-1α belongs to the IL-1 family and are the two agonistic ligands of the family [31]. Both IL-1β 
and IL-1α bind to IL-1 receptor (IL-1R) 1 and induce the same signalling response [32]. In addition, the IL-1R 
antagonist (IL-1Ra) belongs to the IL-1 family, which function as a competitive inhibitor and prevents IL-1β 
and IL-1α from binding to IL-1R1. A second receptor, IL-1R2 also binds IL-1, but lacks the toll-IL receptor 
(TIR) domain in the cytoplasmic region and therefore acts as a decoy receptor that cannot induce an 
intracellular signalling [33].  
 
IL-1β signalling involves the activation of the transcription factor NF-κB and the mitogen activated protein 
kinases (MAPKs) such as, c-jun N-terminal kinase (JNK), p38 and extracellular signal-regulated kinase 
(ERK) [21]. Once, IL-1β has bound to IL-1R1 it undergoes a conformational change that facilitates 
recruitment of IL-1R accessory protein (IL-1RAcP) [17]. This allows binding of the adaptor protein myeloid 
differentiation factor 88 (MyD88) and IL-1R-associated kinase (IRAK) 4. At the IL-1R1 TIR domain, a 
trimeric complex is composed of MyD88 and IRAK4 [34]. A Toll-interacting protein (Tollip) and IRAK1 
complex is recruited via the MyD88 death domain, which causes IRAK1 phosphorylation by IRAK4 that 
allows recruitment of TNF-receptor-associated factor (TRAF) 6. TRAF6 and IRAK1 dissociates as a 
complex, translocates to the membrane and interact with a preformed complex, consisting of transforming 
growth factor β-activated kinase 1 (TAK1), TAK1 binding protein (TAB) 1 and TAB2 [35]. IRAK1 is 
ubiquitinated and degraded at the membrane [36] and the TRAF6/TAK1/TAB1/TAB2 complex translocates to 
the cytosol and associates with the ubiquitin ligases Uev1A and Ubc13 [35]. TRAF6, Uev1A and Ubc13 act

inhibitor of nuclear factor κB (IκB) kinases (IKK)-α and –β [37] that in combination with IΚΚ-γ constitutes 
the IKK complex, named NF-κB essential modulator (NEMO) [13]. Activated IKK phosphorylates IκB and 
promote K48-linked polyubiquitination of IκB, which target the protein to undergo degradation by the 
proteasomes [34]. IkB degradation allows the release of NF-κB from IκB, which enables NF-κB to translocate 
to the nucleus. In the nucleus, NF-κB can initiate gene transcription of various NF-κB-dependent genes [35]. 
Normally, NF-κB activation stimulates cell survival [38], but in β-cells NF-κB activation mainly has a pro-
apoptotic effect [39][23]. 
 
Further, TAK1 can activate upstream MAPK kinases (MAP2Ks) to trigger phosphorylation and activation of 
MAPK pathways [35]. Upstream activators of JNK and p38 are MAP2K4 (MKK4) and MAP2K7 (MKK7) 
and MAP2K3 (MKK3) and MAP2K6 (MKK6), respectively. ERK is, however, activated by the 
Ras/Raf/MAP2K1/2 (MEK1/2) cascade [40]. Activated JNK, p38 and ERK can subsequently phosphorylate 
the transcription factors c-jun, Elk-1, and activating transcription factor (ATF) 2 to induce gene transcription 
[35]. It has been demonstrated that IL-1β induces phosphorylation of JNK and inhibition of JNK activity has 
shown to prevent IL-1β induced β-cell apoptosis [41]. JNK may also cause β-cell apoptosis, through the 
activation of the pro-apoptotic B-cell lymphoma 2 (BCL-2) family member BIM [42]. It has further been 
shown that inhibition of ERK and p38 reduces IL-1β-stimulated islet NO synthesis [43], indicating that ERK 
and p38 contributes to β-cell apoptosis through the IL-1β signalling.

1.3.2 IFN-γ signalling 
IFNs has an important role in the innate and adaptive immune response and is mainly produced by natural 
killer (NK) cells and T-cells [44]. They are classified into two types, type I (IFN-α and IFN-β) and type II 
(IFN-γ) and are divided based on structural and functional differences [45]. Various cells secrete type I IFNs 
in low doses, but in response to specific stimuli the secretion of type I IFNs is increased [46].  IFN-γ is the 
only type II IFN and is more restrictively expressed [45].

1.4 Apoptotic pathways in β-cells 
Cytokine-induced β-cell death is primarily mediated through apoptosis [17][48]. Two signalling pathways 
lead to apoptosis, the extrinsic and the intrinsic pathway. The extrinsic pathway is triggered by activation of 
death receptors of the TNF receptor family e.g. the cell-surface receptor Fas [49]. The Fas-ligand on one cell, 
binds to Fas on another cell, which causes Fas receptor trimerization [50]. The complex will then bind Fas-
associated death domain (FADD) and initiate the formation of the death-inducing signalling complex (DISC). 
Subsequently, FADD recruits pro-caspase 8 that undergoes autocatalytic cleavage to form the active subunit 
[51]. This will initiate a caspase cascade, which involves cleavage and activation of caspase 3, 6 and 7 [49]. 
The activated caspases will thus cleave vital cellular substrates and induce apoptosis [52]. In addition, caspase 
8 can cleave BID and generate its active truncated pro-apoptotic form BID (tBID) that can bind to BCL-2, on 
the mitochondrial outer membrane and activate the intrinsic apoptotic pathway [49].  
 
The intrinsic pathway is mainly initiated by DNA damage and cytotoxic stress. The intrinsic pathway is 
regulated by conserved BCL-2 family of proteins that contain the pro-apoptotic and anti-apoptotic BCL-2 
family members [49]. All members of the BCL-2 family share homology [52], by having two to four 
conserved regions termed BCL-2 homology domains (BH1-4 domains). The pro-apoptotic BCL-2 family 
members include two subgroups that consist of BAX and BAK and the BH3-only proteins BAD, BIM, BIK, 
HRK, NOXA, and PUMA [49]. The BCL-2 family is mainly located in the mitochondrial outer membrane, 
where they interact with each other, to regulate mitochondrial outer membrane permeabilization (MOMP). 
BAX and BAK reside in the outer mitochondrial membrane and cytosol, respectively [28] and are necessary 
for mitochondria-induced apoptosis [52]. Activation of BAX and BAK lead to the loss of mitochondrial 
transmembrane potential (MTP), which is an early and irreversible step, leading to activation of the intrinsic 
apoptotic pathway [28]. In healthy cells, BAX and BAK are tightly bound to anti-apoptotic BCL-2 proteins 
e.g. BCL-2 or BCL-xl, thus blocking BAK and BAX homooligomerization and formation of pores in the outer 
mitochondrial membrane. The BH3-only proteins such as, BIM can directly bind to BAD and BAX, which 
causes them to form pores and induce MOMP. Further, BAD can activate BAK and BAX by binding and 
inhibiting anti-apoptotic BCL-2 proteins [52]. Upon dissipation of MTP and pore formation, cytochrome c is 
released from the mitochondria into the cytoplasm [28] and binds to apoptosis protease-activating factor-1 
(Apaf-1) that causes assembly of the apoptosome. The apoptosome binds and activates pro-caspase 9 [52] that 
lead to activation of caspase 3 by proteolytic cleavage [28]. Caspase 3 cleaves cellular proteins that eventually 
lead to cell death. The formation of BAX/BAK complexes additionally cause the secretion of apoptosis-
inducing factor (AIF), endonuclease G, second mitochondria-derived activator of caspases/direct inhibitor of 
apoptosis-binding protein with low pl (SMAC/DIABLO) and serine protease Omi/HtrA2 from the 
mitochondria [53]. SMAC/DIABLO and Omi/HtrA2 can bind and prevent inhibitor of apoptosis proteins 
(IAPs), from inhibiting the caspases in the cytosol and thus contribute to the apoptotic process [54].

1.5 ER stress in β-cells 
The ER is responsible for a number of important cellular functions [6], including folding, modification and 
export of newly synthesized proteins [55]. The ER is further important for the regulation of cell activities 
through Ca2+ signalling [6]. The Ca2+ storage, within the ER is maintained by the sarcoplasmic/ER Ca2+ 
ATPase (SERCA) pump [56] that is responsible for transporting Ca2+ to the lumen of the ER [55]. Ca2+ is 
released from the ER via the inositol 1,4,5-triphosphate receptors (InsP3R) or ryanodine receptors (RyR)-
regulated Ca2+ channels [28]. Insulin production and secretion is dependent on how ER can operate [6]. An 
imbalance between the demand for protein synthesis and the capacity to handle it, leads to the accumulation of 
misfolded or unfolded proteins, causing ER stress [57]. Depletion of Ca2+ from the ER reduces protein 
==PAGE:20==
10 
 
1. Introduction 
!
folding, due to impaired functional activities of Ca2+-dependent chaperone proteins [58]. These chaperones are 
responsible for proper folding and modification of newly synthesized proteins [59].  
 
The initial response to protein misfolding is induction of the unfolded protein response (UPR). The aim of the 
UPR is to restore the protein folding capacity of the ER [59]. This can be achieved by, decreasing mRNA 
translation of misfolded proteins, increasing translation of ER chaperones and degradation of misfolded 
proteins, through ER stress-associated degradation (ERAD) [55]. Although, if the stress is prolonged and 
misfolded proteins continue to accumulate, the UPR will trigger apoptosis to eliminate the cell [59]. The ER 
expresses high levels of UPR stress markers, including protein kinase RNA-like ER kinase (PERK), inositol-
requiring enzyme 1 (Ire1) and ATF6 [57] that activates the ER stress sensors, ATF4, X-box binding protein 1 
(XBP1) and CCAT/enhancer-binding protein homologous protein (CHOP), respectively [55]. The chaperone 
binding immunoglobulin protein (BIP) binds to the three sensors, but detaches during ER stress to support 
folding of proteins [57]. Detachment of BIP leads to activation of the stress sensors [28]. Cytokine-induced 
ER stress in β-cells is mediated through induction of iNOS in an NF-κB and STAT1 dependent manner 
[22][23]. NO inhibits the SERCA pump that leads to ER Ca2+ depletion and ER stress [25][60]. Ca2+ depletion 
leads to high cytosolic Ca2+, causing activation of JNK, p38 and ERK [61][62]. In case of prolonged ER stress 
JNK, CHOP, and proapoptotic BCL-2 family members are activated, which lead to β-cell apoptosis [63]. 
CHOP also has a proapoptotic role since CHOP-deficient mouse islets were protected against NO-mediated 
apoptosis [60].  
!
1.6 N-methyl-D-aspartate receptor 
The N-methyl-D-aspartate receptor (NMDAR) is a subclass of ionotropic glutamate receptors and functions as 
a Ca2+ channel, which plays an essential role in synaptic transmission in the central nervous system (CNS). 
NMDARs have a heterotetramic complex composed of tree different subunits GluN1, GluN2 and GluN3 [64]. 
The Grin1 gene encodes the GluN1 subunit. The GluN2 subunit exist in four types (A-D) and the GluN3 
subunit exist in two types (A-B) and are encoded by Grin2A-D and Grin3A-B, respectively [65]. The GluNs 
subunits bind as tetramers, typically composed of GluN1-GluN2 assemblies [66], where it has been shown that 
the GluN1 subunit is essential for the formation of a functional NMDAR in neurons [67]. The role of GluN3 
subunit is less clarified. The subunits are highly related in amino acid sequence and each subunit consist of an 
amino-terminal domain (ATD), a ligand-binding domain (LBD) on the extracellular site, a transmembrane 
domain (TMD) that spans the membrane and defines the ion channel pore and an intracellular carboxy-
terminal domain (CTD) [64]. NMDARs are ligand-gated [68], meaning that the receptors require binding of 
two ligands, glutamate to the GluN2 subunit and glycine to the GluN1 subunit [65]. Further, the receptors are 
voltage-gated i.e. active by a depolarized membrane potential [65]. Within the ion channel there is an Mg2+ 
binding site that occupies the channel pore under a resting membrane potential, thus blocking ion flow through

which further causes depolarization of the cell membrane and influx of Ca2+ [64].

Prolonged activation of NMDARs leads to neuronal injury or cell death under excessive amounts of 
extracellular glutamate concentration and neuronal cell depolarization- a process known as excitotoxicity [70]. 
Glutamate-induced excitotoxicity is involved in many CNS disorders e.g stroke, Alzheimer’s disease, and 
Parkinson’s disease [68]. Dizocilpine (MK-801) and dextromethorphan (DXM) is noncompetitive antagonists 
of NMDARs and have been used to block the function of NMDARs [71][72]. MK-801 is often used in 
experimental studies but not clinically, due to severe side effects [73]. Conversely, DXM, which is a clinical 
over-the-counter drug, is converted into its active metabolite dextrorphan (DXO) in the body that act as a 
highly potent antagonist [72]. 
NMADRs serve as a major drug target for the treatment of several diseases and many potential NMDAR 
blockers have already been investigated [65]. However, little is still known about the role of NMDARs in 
pancreatic islets and how they may affect β-cell function. 
!
1.6.1 The role of NMDARs in β-cells 
NMDARs have been shown to be expressed in pancreatic β-cells [74][75][76][77][78], where they have a 
modulatory effect on glucose-stimulated insulin secretion (GSIS) and islet cell survival [76]. Marquard et al., 
2015 proposed a mechanism for the function of NMDARs in GSIS. Uptake of glucose causes an increase in 
intracellular ATP in β-cells, leading to inhibition of KATP channels and thereby reducing K+ efflux, causing an 
increase of K+ in the cytosol [56]. To maintain the resting membrane potential, cells have a low intracellular 
concentration of Na+ ions and a high concentration of K+ ion, which lead to an electrical potential difference 
between the inside and outside of the cell. This is maintained by the Na+/K+ ATPase pump that pumps 3 Na+ 
out of the cell and transport 2 K+ into the cell. Hence, the intracellular space is negatively charged, whereas 
the extracellular space is positively charged. Closure of KATP channels will thus change the electrical charge 
==PAGE:22==
12 
 
1. Introduction 
!
and the intracellular space will become positive and thereby depolarize the membrane [79][80]. A depolarized 
cell membrane activates voltage-dependent Ca2+ channels (VDCCs) and allows an influx of Ca2+ into the cell. 
An increase in the intracellular Ca2+ concentration initiates the insulin secretory process [56]. A depolarized 
membrane, along with glutamate and glycine binding activates NMDARs, which further increases the 
intracellular concentration of Ca2+. This will promote activation of small conductance Ca2+ activated K+ 4 
(SK4) channels and KATP channels. The SK4 channel is an ion channel that allows efflux of K+ and is may 
activated due to an increase in intracellular Ca2+ caused by the NMDAR [76][81]. In neurons the KATP channel 
has shown to be activated by the NO production, which is an outcome of activated NMDARs [82][83].  
As mentioned, the role of NMDARs remains unclear in β-cells. Marquard et al., 2015 demonstrated that 
NMDARs inhibit GSIS and islet cell survival in β-cells. They suggested that the negative effect on GSIS is 
the result of activation of SK4 channels and KATP channels that cause repolarization of the membrane, leading 
to VDCC inhibition. Inhibition of NMDARs with the antagonist MK-801 caused sustained membrane 
depolarization and prolonged VDCC activation. Similarly, Grin1 knockdown in INS-1E cells was associated 
with higher GSIS. As inflammatory cytokines are an important factor in the pathogenesis of T1D and possibly 
T2D, they investigated whether MK-801 and DXM had a protective effect on cytokine-induced β-cell death. 
They demonstrated that both MK-801 and DXM decreased cytokine-induced cell death in mouse and human 
islets. Further, DXM increased serum insulin levels and improved glucose tolerance in T2D patients after oral 
glucose intake. The study concluded that pancreatic NMDARs may serve as a drug target for the treatment of 
T1D and T2D. However, additional studies are necessary to verify these results.

2. Rationale, hypothesis and aim of study 
2.1 Rationale  
Proinflammatory cytokines are believed to play an essential role in the development of T1D. These cytokines 
cause β-cell dysfunction and apoptosis by inducing ER stress and by altering the expression of BCL-2 family 
genes [30][84][85]. NMDARs are expressed in β-cells and inhibition of NMDARs reduces cytokine-induced 
islet β-cell death [76]. However, the underlying mechanisms of the protective effect of NMDAR inhibition 
remain to be clarified. In particular, the effects of NMDAR blockage should be examined in a β-cell model 
system.  
A better understanding, of the protective effect of NMDAR inhibition on cytokine-induced β-cell apoptosis, 
may eventually lead to new therapeutic strategies to prevent β-cell apoptosis in T1D. 
 
2.2 Hypothesis 
It was hypothesized that NMDARs are expressed in INS-1E cells and transcriptionally regulated by exposure 
to IL-1β and IFN-γ. Further, it was hypothesized that inhibition of NMDARs via MK-801 protects INS-1E 
cells from cytokine-induced apoptosis, due to inhibition of pro-apoptotic BCL-2 genes and cytokine-induced 
signalling pathways.  
 
2.3 Aim 
The overall aim of this project was to characterize the protective effect of NMDAR inhibition on cytokine-
induced β-cell apoptosis and to investigate the underlying molecular mechanisms. The specific aims were: 
1. To confirm that NMDAR expression in INS-1E cells and examine if cytokines change the expression 
of NMDARs 
2. To examine the effect of NMDAR inhibition by MK-801 on cytokine-induced INS-1E cell death   
3. To test the effect of NMDAR inhibition by MK-801 on cytokine-regulated signalling pathways 
4. To investigate the effect of NMDAR inhibition by MK-801 on the expression of pro- and anti-
apoptotic BCL-2 genes

3.6 NMDAR inhibition partially restores chronic insulin secretion in cytokine-exposed 
INS-1E cells 
As mentioned in the background section, studies done by Marquard et al., 2015 revealed that inhibition of 
NMDARs with MK-801, in INS-1E cells, enhanced GSIS. To validate the effect of NMDAR inhibition on 
insulin secretion the amount of accumulated insulin, in the culture medium, was measured. Insulin secretion 
was measured in INS-1E cells pretreated with 10µM or 20µM MK-801 for 1h, followed by exposure to 
150pg/ml IL-1β and 5ng/ml IFN-γ for 18h. As shown in figure 11, secretion of insulin was significantly 
increased in 20µM MK-801-treated cells, where the induction of insulin was increased with 80.22%, 
compared to IL-1β and IFN-γ-stimulated cells. This indicates that MK-801 partially reverses the cytotoxic 
effect of cytokines on insulin secretion. As expected, stimulation with IL-1β and IFN-γ correlated with a 
decrease in insulin secretion, compared to unstimulated cells, however, the decrease was not significant. 
Further, was it illustrated that the insulin secretion was not increased in unstimulated cells when NMDARs 
were inhibited.

3.8 JNK and ERK phosphorylation are not effected by NMDAR inhibition in INS-1E 
cells 
As mentioned previously, JNK and ERK are known key mediators of IL-1β signalling [35] and inhibition of 
JNK and ERK have been shown to reduce cytokine-induced β-cell apoptosis [41][43] To investigate if JNK 
and/or ERK are influenced by NMDAR inhibition, active phosphorylated ERK1/2 and JNK1/2 were detected 
by Western blotting. INS-1E cells were pretreated with 10µM MK-801 for 1h, followed by exposure to 
150pg/ml IL-1β and 5ng/ml IFN-γ for 0.5h and 3h. The protein level of phosphorylated JNK1/2 was, 
however, only detected after 0.5h. These incubation times were used, since JNK and ERK are upstream 
targets and induced early in the IL-1β pathway. Further, GAPDH was used as a control to ensure equal 
loading of proteins. As seen in figure 13, MK-801 did not significantly affect the content of neither 
phosphorylated JNK1/2 nor ERK1/2. These data suggests that NMDAR inhibition does not affect ERK or 
JNK activity. However, surprisingly, the level of phosphorylated ERK1/2 was nearly the same between 
unstimulated cells (+/- 10µM MK-801) and IL-1β and IFN-γ-stimulated samples (+/- 10µM MK-801). The 
high level of glucose (11mM) and Fetal bovine serum (FBS) (10%), in the culture medium, may explain the 
high level of basal ERK1/2 phosphorylation. It has been shown that ERK is activated in β-cells in response to 
glucose. Glucose causes Ca2+ influx, which stimulates activation of ERK through MAPK signalling [88]. 
Further, the culture medium contains many growth factors that may also cause activation of ERK [89].

4. Discussion 
Inflammatory cytokines are cytotoxic to β-cells [5][17]. It has recently been suggested that inhibition of 
NMDARs protect against cytokine-induced cell death and may provide a novel way to enhance pancreatic β-
cell survival and function [76]. Investigation, of the protective effect of NMDAR inhibition on cytokine-
induced β-cell apoptosis, could eventually provide an important target to prevent β-cell apoptosis in T1D. The 
underlying mechanisms, of the protective effects of NMDAR inhibition remain to be clarified. Consequently, 
the purpose of this project was to define the mechanisms, underlying the effects of NMDAR inhibition on 
cytokine-induced β-cell apoptosis.    
 
This project has confirmed the expression of the NMDAR subunit GluN1 in INS-1E cells 
[74][75][76][77][78]. In INS-1E cells the expression of GluN1 was relative low with Ct values 33 and the 
expression was not regulated by IL-1β and IFN-γ. Consistent with Marquard et al., 2015, it was observed that 
inhibition of NMDARs by MK-801 resulted in significantly lower cytokine-induced β-cell apoptosis, 
confirming that NMDAR inhibition has a protective effect on β-cells. However, this effect was specific for 
apoptosis and not observed when both apoptotic and necrotic cell death was measured. It was further 
examined if MK-801 treatment caused cell death through necrosis, by examining the production of NO. 
However, the inhibition of NMDARs did not alter the level of NO.  
 
NMDAR inhibition with MK-801 has recently been shown to increase GSIS in INS-1E cells and mouse islets, 
without having effects on basal insulin release [76]. It was possible to demonstrate that long-term exposure to 
20uM MK-801 and cytokines (18h), significantly increased chronic insulin secretion, comparable to cytokines 
alone, which further indicates that NMDAR inhibition has a protective effect on cytokine cytotoxicity. 
Inconsistently, however, it was not possible to show that NMDAR inhibition had a positive effect on GSIS. 
Instead, treatment with MK-801 tended to have an inhibitory effect on GSIS, whereas basal insulin release 
was unaltered. However, these data were not significant and were only based on three experiments with big 
variations. In order to get robust results, additional experiments needs to be performed. In addition, it can be 
questioned, if the increase in chronic insulin secretion was due to NMDAR inhibition, or because more cell 
death was measured in MK-801-treated cells, resulting in more insulin release, from dying dead cells. 
Interestingly, a study conducted on rat insulinoma BRIN-BD11 cells showed that short-term exposure (20min)  
to MK-801 did not alter GSIS, whereas incubation with MK-801 for 18h consistently decreased GSIS [78]. 
This suggests that MK-801 treatment possess distinctive effects that may be dependent on the use of β-cell 
system and incubation conditions. Marquard et al., 2015 observations correspond to another study, which 
have established that NMDAR activation possess a negative effect on GSIS [78]. On the other hand, the exact 
role of NMDAR function on insulin secretion in insulin-secreting β-cells, is conflicting, with inconsistent 
effects observed [74][75][77][78]. However, the results in this project indicate that NMDAR inhibition 
potentially possess favourable effects on β-cell survival.

4. Discussion 
 
29 
 
ERK phosphorylation and activation is triggered by elevated Ca2+ levels [62][88]. In this project ERK 
phosphorylation, in response to MK-801 treatment, was not observed, suggesting that MK-801 did not alter 
Ca2+ signalling. This is inconsistent with Marquard et al., 2015, which demonstrated that NMDAR inhibition 
increase cytosolic Ca2+ concentrations in mouse islets. However, other studies are also conflicting with this. A 
study on BRIN-BD11 cells demonstrated that NMDAR inhibition by MK-801, did not exert any effect on 
Ca2+ nor did NMDAR activation [78]. However, a study on rat islets showed that exposure to NMDARs, 
caused an increase of cytosolic Ca2+ in response to 20mM glucose and that this induction was inhibited by 
MK-801 [77]. Further, a study on mouse insulinoma MIN6 cells demonstrated that NMDAR stimulation, 
resulted in increase of cytosolic Ca2+, which where suppressed by the addition of MK-801 [75]. This suggests 
that NMDARs may function by increasing cytosolic Ca2+. In this project MK-801 treatment for 3h in 
unstimulated cells showed a trend towards lower ERK activation, supporting that inhibition of NMDARs may 
decrease the intracellular cytosol Ca2+ level. These diverse results between studies may be explained by 
variations in experimental design and/or the use of different β-cell models. Altogether, these studies suggest 
that further experiments are needed to clarify the role of NMDARs on Ca2+ signalling, ERK activation and in 
insulin secretion.  
 
Neurons and β-cells share many features and the expression of multiple genes are defined in both neurons and 
β-cells [94]. The application of NMDARs have shown to induce membrane depolarization in MIN6 cells [75], 
which is a known property of NMDAR activation in neurons [64][65][66][68]. This suggests that NMDARs, 
expressed in β-cells are similar to those in neurons. Since the exact role of NMDAR function on β-cells is 
largely unexplored, studies conducted on other cell models, focusing on gene regulation by NMDARs, will be 
discussed.  
 
The activation of NMDARs by glutamate and the following influx of Ca2+, have shown to be a critical 
mediator of excitotoxic neuronal death [65]. A study on adult rats showed that injection with NMDA 
upregulated Fas-ligand and TNF-receptor 1 (TNF-R1) in retinal neurons [95]. The Fas-ligand is involved in 
the Fas/Fas-ligand interaction and target cells to undergo apoptosis [50]. Further, NMDAR stimulation caused 
upregulation of TNF-R1 [95], a cell-surface receptor for TNF-α [51]. TNF-α is involved in the pathogenesis 
of T1D and has been shown to inhibit GSIS [96] and cause β-cell death in synergy with IL-1β and IFN-γ [29]. 
It has further been demonstrated that the expression of caspase 3 was markedly increased in retinal neurons 
after injection with NMDA in adult rats [97]. As mentioned, caspase 3 is an important mediator of apoptosis 
and is responsible for the cellular cleavage of proteins in the intrinsic and extrinsic pathway [49]. 
Interestingly, the activity of caspase 3 was highly blocked by MK-801 in cerebrocortical neurons [98]. In this 
project it was observed that the activities of caspase 3/7 was significantly inhibited in MK-801-treated INS-1E 
cells, which supports that NMDARs play a role in caspase 3-induced apoptosis in β-cells.

4. Discussion 
!
iNOS and CHOP are ER stress-related proteins that have shown to be induced by NMDAR stimulation. A 
study on rats, which were chronically injected with NMDA, showed that the protein and mRNA levels of 
iNOS were elevated in the frontal cortex [99]. Results from this project showed that the protein and mRNA 
level of iNOS were not influenced by MK-801. In β-cells, NO, which is the product of iNOS expression [24] 
causes Ca2+ depletion of ER through inhibition of the SERCA pump, causing ER stress [25][60]. ER stress has 
further been shown to activate NF-κB by the degradation of IκB [100]. Although, NF-κB is involved in iNOS 
activation [22] and that ER stress induces NF-κB activation, ER stress, surprisingly does not increase iNOS 
expression in INS-1E cells [100]. It has further been shown that NMDARs induces Chop gene expression in 
retinal neurons of rats and mouse [95][101]. This could suggest that inhibition of NMDARs may lead to 
reduction of CHOP expression. However, results from this project showed that inhibition of NMDARs with 
MK-801 did not affect the mRNA or protein level of CHOP in cytokine-stimulated INS-1E cells.  
 
Apoptosis has been associated with NMDAR-induced neuronal death. It has been demonstrated that injection 
of NMDA to rats, decreased the protein and mRNA expression of the anti-apoptotic protein BCL-2 and 
increased the expression of the pro-apoptotic protein BAD and BAX in the frontal cortex [102]. However, 
result from this project showed that the mRNA level of Bcl-2 was unaltered in MK-801-treated INS-1E cells, 
compared to cytokine-stimulated cells. In addition, the increase of BAD and BAX expression, after 
stimulation with NMDA, may indicate that NMDAR inhibition with MK-801 leads to reduced expression of 
these genes. Conversely, in this project the mRNA level of Bax was unchanged after MK-801 treatment, 
relative to cytokine-stimulated cells.  
 
It has further been shown that NMDAR overactivation induces JNK phosphorylation in retinal neurons in rats 
[103] and BIM protein expression in cortical neurons in mouse [104]. In this project JNK phosphorylation, in 
response to cytokine exposure, was unaltered by NMDAR inhibition, however, the mRNA level of Bim was 
significantly decreased in MK-801-treated cells after 6h incubation. This could indicate that NMDAR 
inhibition may affect BIM expression in cytokine-exposed β-cells. As mentioned, CXCL10 is a chemokine. A 
study conducted on mouse brain cells demonstrated that CXCL10 gene and protein expression was strongly 
increased in response to NMDA treatment [105]. However, results from this study showed that the gene 
expression of Cxcl10 was unaltered when NMDARs were inhibited, compared to cytokine-stimulated cells. 
These results demonstrate that NMDARs can cause up- or downregulation of various apoptosis-associated 
genes in neurons and potentially in β-cells. However, further studies are needed to understand the direct role 
of NMDAR in apoptosis- regulated genes in β-cells.    
 
In conclusion, results from this project clarify that the NMDAR subunit GluN1 is expressed in INS-1E cells. 
Further, inhibition of NMDARs by MK-801 decreased cytokine-induced INS-1E cell apoptosis, indicating 
that NMDAR inhibition has a beneficial effect on β-cell survival. The reduction of apoptosis observed during 
NMDAR inhibition by MK-801, opens a new window for further understanding how NMDARs regulate

5. Conclusion and perspectives 
!
5. Conclusion and perspectives  
The aim of this thesis was to characterize the protective effect of NMDAR inhibition on cytokine-induced β-
cell apoptosis and to investigate the underlying molecular mechanisms. In the present study, the expression of 
the NMDAR subunit GluN1 in INS-1E cell has been confirmed, with no regulation by IL-1β and IFN-γ. 
Further, NMDAR inhibition by MK-801 resulted in reduced cytokine-induced β-cell apoptosis, however, this 
effect was specific for apoptosis and not observed when both apoptotic and necrotic cell death was measured. 
NO production correlates with necrotic cell death, however, the inhibition of NMDARs did not alter the level 
of cytokine-induced NO. In addition, NMDAR inhibition increased the insulin release in cytokine-exposed 
INS-1E cells further suggesting that NMDAR inhibition has protective effects on cytokine cytotoxicity. 
Nonetheless, NMDAR inhibition alone resulted in reduced GSIS. NMDAR inhibition had no effect on 
cytokine-induced phosphorylation of ERK and JNK or on cytokine-induced iNOS and CHOP expression. 
Further, NMDAR inhibition had no effect on Bcl-2, Bax and Cxcl10 expression but cytokine-induced 
expression of Bim was downregulated, indicating that NMDAR inhibition may affect cytokine-induced 
apoptosis via BIM in β-cells. 
The results indicate that NMDAR inhibition has a beneficial effect on β-cell survival, however, further studies 
are needed to understand the role of NMDARs in more detail for β-cell function and apoptosis.   
 
In future studies, other cell models e.g primary cells, should be used. Primary cells are more physiological 
compared to INS-1E cells, which were used in this study. It should further be mentioned that there was not 
included a “control” that could verify the expression of inhibition of NMDARs by MK-801. In future studies, 
other methods should be used to inhibit the NMDAR. Besides using selective inhibition, gene deletion of 
Grin1 could be performed or Grin1 knockdown by using siRNA. In addition, the DXM inhibitor could be 
included, which is already an approved clinical drug. To confirm the presence of GluN2 and GluN3 in β-cells, 
the expression of these subunits should further be assessed. The protein level should additionally be 
determined, since the level of mRNA does not necessarily correlate to the protein level. The level of 
intracellular Ca2+ could be measured directly by using e.g. the fluorescent dye Fura-2. Further, in vivo studies 
may be performed using animal models. The effect of NMDAR inhibition or activation could be tested in e.g. 
NOD mice. By using animal models the results would have clinical relevance.

6.1 INS-1E cell line 
In this project experiments were performed on insulin-secreting INS-1E cells- a rat β-cell line. This cell line is 
widely used in diabetic research in order to study the molecular events underlying β-cell function and 
dysfunction [106]. β-cell lines have many characteristic features like primary pancreatic β-cells such as, 
glucose responsiveness and high insulin content [107]. The parental cell line, INS-1 was derived from a rat 
insulinoma, induced by x-ray irradiation. An important factor in INS-1 proliferation is the toxin β-
mercaptoethanol that is vital in the culture media for cell proliferation [107]. Although, after additional 
passages the INS-1 cell line developed unstable differentiation, which is observed as inconsistencies during 
laboratory practice. A research group were able to isolate the INS-1E cell line from the parental cell line. INS-
1E has a stable differentiated β-cell phenotype in up to 100 passages and has higher insulin content, compared 
to the original cell line [108].  
 
6.2 Methods for measuring β-cell dysfunction and apoptosis 
6.2.1 Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay  
To detect cell death, the Cell death detection ELISAPLUS kit (Roche Diagnostics) was used. The enzyme-
linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) is a sandwich assay and is based on the specific recognition of 
cytoplasmic mono- and oligonucleosomes, by the use of monoclonal antibodies (mAb). The process of 
apoptosis causes activation of endonucleases [109]. Endonucleases cleave the dsDNA to mono- and 
oligonucleosomes fragments, which will diffuse into the cytoplasm. Since nucleosomes are complexes of 
DNA and histones [109], the assay uses mAb against DNA and histones. The immunoreagent contains a 
mixture of capture anti-histone-biotin antibody and detection anti-DNA-peroxidase (POD) antibody. The anti-
histone-biotin antibody binds to the histone components of the nucleosome and captures the immunocomplex 
to the streptavidin-coated plate, via the streptavidin-biotin interaction. The anti-DNA-POD antibody binds to 
the DNA component of the nucleosome. 2,2'-azino-bis(3-ethylbenzothiazoline-6-sulphonic acid) (ABTS) 
function as a substrate for the conjugated POD enzyme and enzymatic cleavage of ATBS generates a green 
soluble product. The absorbance of this product is proportional to cell death. The ABTS stop solution will 
stop the ABTS substrate reaction and prevent high absorbance values.

6.2.1.1 QuantiFluor dsDNA system kit 
To analyse the ELISA data, is it necessary to determine the concentration of dsDNA that is used to correct for 
variations of cells in each well. To measure dsDNA, the QuantiFluor dsDNA system kit (Promega) was used. 
The method is based on a fluorescent dsDNA-binding dye that is proportional to the concentration of dsDNA. 
The concentration of dsDNA was calculated using a dsDNA standard curve. 
 
6.2.2 Caspase Assay 
To measure apoptotic cell death, the Caspase-Glo 3/7 assay (Promega) was used. The method is a 
homogeneous luminescent assay, which measures the activities of caspase 3 and 7. The method uses a 
luminogenic substrate that contains a tetrapeptide sequence DEVD, coupled to an aminoluciferin compound. 
Upon caspase activation, the luminogenic substrate is cleaved at the DEVD sequence and aminoluciferin is 
detached. Aminoluciferin is a substrate for luciferase and the reaction produces a luminescent signal that is 
proportional to the amount of active caspases.   
 
6.2.3 NO production 
To measure NO production, the Griess reagent system kit (Promega) was used. NO was detected by 
measuring nitrite in the culture medium and is a stable and non-volatile breakdown product of NO. The 
method is based on a chemical reaction, where nitrite reacts with sulfanilamide under acidic conditions and

and develops a purple color. The absorbance of the end product is proportional to the concentration of NO 
[110]. The production of NO was calculated using a nitrite standard curve that subsequently was normalized

6.2.4 Insulin secretion 
To measure the content of insulin in the culture medium, the Insulin ELISA kit (Mercodia) was used. The 
method is based on a sandwich technique that uses mAb specific for insulin. Microplates coated with anti-
insulin antibodies captures the insulin and anti-insulin antibodies conjugated with POD, detect the amount of 
insulin. 3,3’,5,5’-Tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) is a substrate for POD and by enzymatic cleavage, a blue 
colouring of the solution is generated. High concentrations of insulin will result in more conversion of the 
substrate. The reaction is stopped by the addition of a stop reagent, which is an acidic solution that turns the 
TMB yellow. The absorbance of the colour change is proportional to the amount insulin and the concentration 
was calculated using a standard curve. The amount of insulin was normalized to dsDNA, determined by the 
QuantiFluor dsDNA system kit (Promega).      
 
6.2.5 GSIS 
To measure insulin in basal state and at a high level of glucose, 2mM and 20mM glucose concentrations were 
used to evaluate GSIS. To measure GSIS, cells were incubated with glucose-free culture medium, following 
incubation with Krebs-Ringer bicarbonate 4-(2-hydroxyethyl)-1-piperazineethanesulfonic acid (HEPES) 
buffer. These steps were performed to ensure that the concentration of glucose was reset to 0mM and that only 
glucose-stimulated insulin was measured. Afterwards, cells were preincubated with Krebs-Ringer bicarbonate 
HEPES buffer supplemented with 2mM and 20mM glucose, respectively. The supernatants were collected for 
determination of insulin secretion that was assessed as described above. The level of secreted insulin was 
normalized to dsDNA, determined by the QuantiFluor dsDNA system kit (Promega).

6.3.2 Sodium dodecyl sulphate-polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis  
Proteins are separated by Sodium dodecyl sulphate-polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE) that uses 
gel electrophoresis to separate proteins based on their molecular weights. In this project prefabricated 
polyacrylamide gels were used to separate the proteins during gel electrophoresis.  Initially, the proteins were

that binds proteins and produces a negatively charged complex [111]. The protein mass is therefore the only 
determining factor of the migration rate of proteins, thus smaller proteins will migrate faster through the gel 
than larger proteins. 
 
6.3.3 Western blotting 
Western blotting analysis uses specific antibodies to detect specific proteins in a mixture of proteins. In this 
method the separated proteins were transferred onto a nitrocellulose membrane. This allows the specific 
antibodies to bind and detect the target protein. The pore size of the membrane can vary and was selected 
based on the size of the target protein. Initially, the proteins were transferred from the gel to the membrane, 
through electrical blotting that uses an electrical current to pull the proteins onto the membrane [111]. After 
the transfer step, the membrane was stained with ponceau to allow visualization of all proteins. The membrane 
was then incubated and blocked with milk, to prevent antibodies from binding non-specifically to the 
membrane [112]. Additional washing steps were performed and the membrane was incubated with primary 
antibody. Subsequently, the membrane was incubated with secondary antibody. The primary antibody was 
specific for the target protein and binds through the antigen binding fragments and the secondary antibody 
recognizes the species-specific fragment crystallizable region (Fc) region on the primary antibody [13]. The 
secondary antibody was additionally conjugated with the enzyme horseradish peroxidase (HRP). To develop 
chemiluminescence substrates from GE healthcare were added, which contains hydrogenperoxid and luminol. 
HRP oxidizes hydrogenperoxid into hydrogen and superoxide and the generated superoxide reacts and 
oxidizes luminol. The product of this reaction produces light that was detected by a camera that develops the 
light into a digital image.

6.4 Real-time polymerase chain reaction  
6.4.1 RNA extraction 
RNA was isolated using the RNeasy mini kit (Qiagen). The method uses the binding of RNA to silica-gel 
membranes by the high-speed of a mini-centrifuge. The RLT lysisbuffer contains guanidine thiocyanate that 
causes cell disruption and inactivation of RNases. Addition of ethanol promotes the binding of RNA to the 
silica-gel membrane and the solution can be transferred to a spin column, where centrifugation will force the 
RNA to bind to the membrane. The additional washing step and centrifugation removed the contaminants that 
were bound to the membrane. RNA was eluted in RNase-free water by centrifugation and collected in RNA 
eppendorf tubes. The quantity and purity of RNA was measured by a Nanodrop spectrophotometer.  
 
6.4.2 Reverse transcription  
Reverse transcription (RT) uses the retroviral enzyme, reverse transcriptase to transcribe single stranded (ss) 
RNA into complementary DNA (cDNA). To perform the two-step cDNA synthesis, the iScript cDNA 
synthesis kit (Bio-Rad) was used. Random primers were used to initiate the cDNA synthesis by annealing to 
the ssRNA at the 3’ end that cause cDNA to extend towards the 5’ end, via the RNA-dependent DNA 
polymerase activity of reverse transcriptase. The enzyme uses free nucleotides as a substrate, to generate the 
cDNA. The RT reaction was additionally primed with oligo(dT) primers that solely anneal to the 
polyadenylated 3’ tail of the mRNA. The mixture of random primers and oligo(dT) primers in the RT reaction 
allows binding to all RNA present in the sample [113] thus maximizing the amount of RNA that can be 
analyzed [114]. This ensures that the target template was present when the quantitative polymerase chain 
reaction (qPCR) was performed [115]. It was necessary to convert the RNA into cDNA, since the DNA 
polymerase, used in the qPCR reaction, cannot utilize RNA as a template.  
 
6.4.3 Polymerase chain reaction  
Polymerase chain reaction (PCR) uses a specific ssDNA as template, to synthesize a new complementary 
strand, using the thermostable DNA polymerase, Taq polymerase. In principle, PCR is a method that 
amplifies a specific DNA sequence in a mixture of DNA. One PCR cycle follows three steps: denaturation, 
annealing and elongation, where the temperature differs between these steps [113]. In the first step, samples 
are heated, which initiate DNA denaturation and separation of dsDNA into ssDNA [116]. By decreasing the 
temperature, forward and reverse primers will specifically anneal to their complementary sequence on the 
template strand. Again, the temperature is raised and the Taq polymerase will make a complete copy of the 
ssDNA template, starting from the 3´end of the primer and extend a new complementary strand in 5’ to 3’ 
direction by adding nucleotides to the primer [117]. The progression of the PCR, will continue to repeat these

causing inefficient amplification. Further, the primers are no longer in excess and the target DNA will begin 
to compete for the primers. Consequently, the PCR is less accurate in the plateau phase [113].

6.4.5 Comparative Ct method 
Every sample yields a Ct value that is used to quantify target genes [115]. The Ct value is dependent on the 
quantity of starting material present in the sample- the higher the amount of target DNA in the starting 
material, the faster a significant increase in fluorescent signal develops, and a low Ct value is produced [118]. 
The comparative Ct method was used to quantify the changes in gene expression as a relative fold difference 
between experimental samples and control samples [118]. By using this method it was assumed that the 
amplification efficiency of the PCR run was 100 % [120]. First, Ct values of the target gene were normalized 
by calculating the difference between Ct values of the target gene and the housekeeping gene (ΔCt).  The 
difference in the ΔCt values between the experimental samples and control samples was then calculated 
(ΔΔCt). Since, Ct values are based on a logarithmic-2-scale, is it necessary to convert to normal scale. The 
expression fold change between experimental samples and control samples was then calculated by 2(-ΔΔCt).

8. Appendix – materials and methods  
8.1 Cell culture 
INS-1E cells were grown in culture flasks and cultured in RPMI (Roswell park memorial institute) 1640 with 
glutamax. The medium was supplemented with 10% FBS, 50µM β-mercaptoethanol and 100U/L penicillin-
streptomycin. The cells were incubated at 37°C in a humidified 5% CO2 atmosphere.   
 
For every experiment, cells were passaged and seeded in proper plates, according to experimental setups. Cell 
passage was performed when there was 80% or higher confluence.  Two days after, cells were exposed to 
cytokines, to initiate cytokine-induced apoptosis. Dependent on the method, a mixture of IL-1β and IFN-γ was 
used, varying in concentrations and incubation times. Every week new prewarmed medium was added to the 
cell culture. The morphology of cells was routinely tested before and during the data acquisition.     
 
8.1.1 Passaging of cells 
1. Wash cells in 1 x Hank buffered salt solution (HBSS) without Mg2+ and Ca2+ in order to retain the 
activity of trypsin 
2. Add trypsin to remove cells from the culture flask surface     
3. Incubate for 3min at 37°C  
4. Loose the cells from the surface by slapping on the flask. Add medium to stop the trypsin reaction   
5. Transfer the cells to a 50ml tube and centrifuge at 1000 rounds per minute (rpm) for 5min  
6. Remove medium and resuspend in medium 
7. Count the cells in a cell counter    
8. Seed out in new flask and add proper amount of medium 
 
Small cell flask (T25):  
(1) 10ml HBSS 
(2) 1ml trypsin 
(4) 10ml medium 
(6) Resuspend in 5ml medium, add 5ml medium 
(8) 1.5 – 2 million cells in 10ml 
  
Medium cell flask (T75):  
(1) 20ml HBSS 
(2) 2ml trypsin 
(4) 20ml medium 
(6) Resuspend in 5ml medium, add 15ml medium 
(8) 3 million cells in 20ml 
 
Large cell flask (T75):  
(1) 30ml HBSS 
(2) 3ml trypsin 
(4) 30ml medium 
(6) Resuspend in 5ml medium, add 25ml medium 
(8) 7 million cells in 30ml

8.3.3 Western blotting 
1. To samples, adjusted for protein concentration add 7µl LDS buffer and 2µl DTT 
2. Boil the samples for 10min at 90°C, spin down and load on gel 
3. 2µl Magic Marker and 7µl Benchmarker are also loaded 
4. Run the gel in 1 x 3-(N-morpholino)propanesulfonic acid (MOPS) running buffer or 1 x 2-(N-
morpholino)ethanesulfonic acid (MES) running buffer at 165V, 150mA in 45min (MOPS buffer) or 
35min (MES buffer) 
5. After run, open the cassette with a gel knife and remove the top plate 
6. Soak blotting pads, filter papers and nitrocellulose membrane in 1 x transfer buffer 
7. Place a pre-soaked filter paper on top of the gel 
8. Turn the plate over and remove the bottom plate with a gel knife 
9. Place the pre-soaked nitrocellulose membrane on the gel 
10. Place another pre-soaked filter paper on top of the membrane and remove air bubbles with a stripette 
11. Place a pre-soaked blotting pad into the cathode core of the blot module followed by the 
gel/membrane assembly   
12. Transfer another pre-soaked blotting pad and place the anode core on top 
13. Hold the blot module firmly and slide it into the gel tank  
14. Fill the blot module with 1 x transfer buffer until the gel/membrane assembly is covered 
15. Fill the outer chamber with water to dispel heat generated during the run     
16. Run the gel at 10V and 150mA for 1 hour and 10min 
17. After run, transfer the membrane to a box and wash in 1 x Tris-buffered Saline (TBS) for 5min 
18. Stain with ponceau for 5min, wash in cold water and develop in LAS Image Quant 
19. Add blocking buffer for 1h 
20. Wash 3 x 5min in TBS-Tween-20 (TBST) 
21. Add primary antibody and incubate at 4°C overnight 
22. Wash in 3 x 5min in TBST and add secondary antibody for 1h 
23. Wash in 3 x 5min TBST and develop image

8.4 qPCR  
8.4.1 RNA extraction  
The work should be done sterile and on ice. 
 
1. Add 175µl/well RLT lysisbuffer and incubate 20min on ice 
2. Add 175µl/well 70% ethanol and transfer to RNeasy spin column 
3. Centrifuge for 15sec at 10.000rpm 
4. Discard the flow through and add 700µl RW1 buffer 
5. Centrifuge for 15sec at 10.000rpm 
6. Transfer the RNeasy spin column to new collection tubes 
7. Add 500µl RPE buffer and centrifuge for 15sec at 10.000rpm 
8. Discard the flow through and add 500µl RPE buffer 
9. Centrifuge for 2min at 14.000rpm 
10. Transfer the RNeasy spin column to a sterile eppendorph tube and centrifuge for 1min at 10.000rpm 
11. Transfer the RNeasy spin column to a sterile eppendorph tube and add 30µl RNase-free water 
12. Centrifuge for 1min at 10.000rpm 
13. Measure the purity and quantity on Nanodrop
