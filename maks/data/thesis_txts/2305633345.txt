Abstract
Creating rosters for employees occur in many parts of the public sector. A
speciﬁc area of interest in this context is creating rosters for nurses at hospit-
als. In this thesis Slagelse Emergency department is analyzed and the various
stakeholders involved in the roster creating process are described. The prob-
lem is to ensure that the interests of every stakeholder are met. A Dantzig-
Wolfe decomposition model is proposed as a solution to the problem, where
the stakeholders are the nurses (employees), Region Sjælland (the employer)
and the patients. To solve the problem a new approach for implementing the
rules set by the collective agreements are proposed. This method includes a
graph which takes the rules of the collective agreement into account and solves
the problem to satisfaction.
The problem is based on real data for 72 nurses, who must be allocated
to 18 tasks each day. Tests show that it is possible to generate good feasible
solutions for a one week period with a total running time of approximately
15 minutes. The tests also show that it is possible to theoretically save up to
160000 DKK each week, which is a reduction of approximately 23 % compared
to the current costs.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Creating rosters for employees is an essential task for many parts of the public
sector: police, public transport, and health care. Other examples are shops,
factories, and airplane crew scheduling. Creating good rosters is a very del-
icate process as many stakeholders have to be satisﬁed when creating the
rosters: The employer has certain interests e.g. saving money. These interests
are often not shared by the employees, as this will result in harder work sched-
ules. Another stakeholder is the customer/patient who must feel comfortable
and safe while being serviced. The roster which is created for the employees
must therefore balance the interests between every stakeholder in order to be
perceived as a good schedule.
In this master thesis the focus will be on the health care sector of Denmark.
More speciﬁcally the focus will be on using mathematical modeling to create
good rosters for the nurses at the emergency department at Slagelse Hospital,
which is operated by Region Sjælland. In operations research literature, this
problem is known as the nurse rostering problem and is regarded as a
NP-hard problem.
1.1
Problem Statement
The Danish government has reduced the budget for the healthcare sector in
Denmark, therefore Region Sjælland is eager to ﬁnd new and better ways of
using their resources.
Region Sjælland believes that there is room for im-
provement in the work schedules for the nurses at the emergency department
at Slagelse Hospital, thus reducing their expenses. At the moment, roster gen-
eration is done by hand by a planning nurse. This task can be overwhelming
because there are complex work time rules and many regulations in the col-
lective agreements which must be met. Furthermore the planner has to ensure
that nurses cover speciﬁc tasks during the day (answering the telephone etc.)
in order to create a high level of service for the patient. Since there are so
many rules and interests which must be taken into account, it poses a great

challenge for the human planner to ﬁnd rosters which balance the interests of
all stakeholders.
The planning nurse at the Slagelse Emergency department ﬁnds it hard
to create rosters where the tasks are always covered. Another issue is that
the realized rosters cause unbalanced work schedules, where some nurses work
much less than stated in their contracts, while other nurses work overtime in
the same time period. This is very expensive for Region Sjælland since they
pay the nurses a ﬁxed sum of money each month, depending on the amount
of hours stated in their contract e.g. 32 hours. So if a nurse works less than
32 hours, Region Sjælland pays for a product they do not receive, and if some
work more than 32 hours, Region Sjælland is forced to pay for overtime work.
The assessment of whether a nurse has worked more or less than stated in
their contract is based on the length of the normperiod. The normperiod at
Slagelse Emergency department is 16 weeks. So the rules are that a nurse
whose contract says 32 hours each week, should work 32· 16 = 512 hours dur-
ing the normperiod. If the nurse works more hours, this counts as overtime
and if she works less Region Sjælland still has to pay the nurse for 512 hours.
Another aspect of the problem is that working at certain times of the week or
day (e.g. at night) is more expensive, thus the amount of nurses working at
night and during the weekend should be kept to a minimum.
This thesis will seek to answer whether it is possible to create rosters so
that the nurses work as close to the amount of hours stated in their contract
while meeting the collective agreement rules and work time regulations, and
also reducing the expenses used for wage at certain times of the day/week.
Furthermore the daily tasks for the nurses (telephone etc.) should be covered
at all times of the day, if possible. The rosters created for the nurses should
be as general as possible. The solution which is proposed in this thesis should
take the unbalanced work schedules into account.
To support the work of this thesis, “The Danish National Audit Oﬃce” has
recently published a report [24] which states that there is room for improving
the roster quality at the hospitals in Denmark. In the report, the problem of
unbalanced work schedules is speciﬁcally stated as a problem, which requires
attention from planners.
1.2
Stakeholders
In this thesis there are three stakeholders: The nurses (employees), Region
Sjælland (the employer), and the patients.
==PAGE:9==
1.2. STAKEHOLDERS
3
Nurses
The nurses are part of a union which represents the nurses and negotiate with
the employer on their behalf. Region Sjælland and the union have agreed
on certain work time rules and collective agreements, which must be met
when creating rosters for the nurses.
The collective agreement constitutes
the majority of the mandatory rules which are covered in this thesis. These
rules are speciﬁed in [1, 2, 3, 4, 5]. Furthermore, the Danish government has
some regulations concerning the scheduling of work time in order to protect
employees. This is known as “The Working Environment Act” [8]
In order to make the work ﬁt ordinary family life styles and reduce the
planning complexity, the nurses would like to work in blocks (e.g. 7:30-15:45,
15:30-23:45).
Region Sjælland
The employer (Region Sjaelland) wishes to make sure that the amount of
work done by a nurse is close to what is stated in their contracts e.g. (32
hours/week). This will create a better usage of the resources, save money and
solve the problem of unbalanced work schedules.
Patients
The nurses should be available as often as possible. This is divided into groups
of daily tasks (D), afternoon tasks (A) and night tasks (N). If all tasks are
covered, this leads to a high level of service. The occurrence of “holes” in the
task planning (e.g. answering the telephone) will lead to a lower service level.
Hence the tasks should always be fulﬁlled.

The price of the shifts varies during the week due to the regulations in the

• Working between 18:00 and 23:00 is paid out with a bonus of 27% to

• Working between 23:00 and 6:00 is paid out with a bonus of 32.5% to

• Working between Saturday 6:00 and Sunday 24:00 is paid out with a

Soft Constraints
Weekly work
To determine whether the nurses work more (overtime) or less
than stated in the contracts, the collective agreement states that this should
be based on the amount of work done in a certain period. The Emergency
Department has decided to ﬁx this period to 16 weeks. This is called the
“normperiod”.
So if a nurse’s contract states that he/she should work 37
hours/week, they should work 16·37 = 592 hours during the normperiod.
A nurse working less than 37 hours will have the amount of working hours
reduced proportionally. The amount of work can be 10 hours in one week, and
40 in another. There is no restriction forcing it to be exactly 37 hours/week.
However, the hard constraints should of course always be respected.
The
amount of work done should be as close to e.g. 592 hours for a full-time nurse
(37 · 16 = 592).
Hard constraints
These constraints are speciﬁed in the collective agreements and the special
agreements between the nurses and Region Sjælland. [1, 2, 3, 4, 5].
Length of shift
The length of the shift must have a length between 5 and
12 hours. This is easily satisﬁed as the tasks assigned to nurses will always be
in blocks of 8 or 8.25 hours, as described in table 2.1.
11-hour work rule
The 11-hour work rule states that in a period of 24
hours, the employee must have at least 11-hours of rest.
In practice this
means that there must be at least 11 hours between each task assigned to the
same nurse.
Weekly Day Oﬀ
Within each 7 days, the employee must have a weekly day
oﬀ1. This period can last anywhere between 55 and 64 hours. E.g. Working
Monday 8:00 to 16:00, and then meet again at work Thursday 8:00 gives
8+24+24+8 = 64 hours.
It is possible to divide the day oﬀinto two periods of at least 35 hours
each. e.g. The employee can work Monday 8:00 to 16:00, then meet again at
work at Wednesday 8:00. This gives 8+24+8=40 hours of resting time. The
employee can then have Thursday oﬀ, and then meet again Friday at 8:00. In
this way, the employee has two oﬀperiods of at least 35 hours.
It is also possible to combine more weekly days oﬀ, by adding 24 hours to
the already existing day oﬀ. E.g. work Monday 8:00 to 16:00 and then meet
Friday at 8:00 (8+24+24+24 +8 = 88 hours oﬀ).

2.4
Problem Restriction
In order to maintain a decent workload during the work of this thesis and
maintain a relevant focus area, it has been necessary to omit certain aspects
of the problem:
Originally, the intention was to create rosters for every work group at the
Emergency Department (nurses, doctors, social- and health service assistants
etc.).
It has been decided to focus on the nurses.
The reason for this is
that each profession has diﬀerent work time rules and independent collective
agreements. Including all agreement rules for every profession into a single
mathematical model would be very time consuming and would most likely
make the problem impossible to solve. Another reason is that the nurses con-
stitute the majority of the employees (94 out of 158 employees) and therefore
are the most relevant profession to create rosters for. This ensures that the
project will reﬂect the majority of the planning while keeping the complexity
of the mathematical model at a reasonable level. The 94 nurses are distributed

Chapter 3
Literature Review
In this section the various solution approaches used in the literature for solving
the nurse rostering problem will be described.
For a complete overview of the various solution methods applied to the
rostering problem until the year 2004, see E. Burke et. al. [11]. E. Burke
et. al. give references to papers involving Mathematical Programming, Me-
taheuristics, AI- methods and Goal Programming. E. Burke et al. concludes
that there is a huge gap between the requirements of modern hospitals and the
nurse rostering approaches used in the literature. There is a need to address
the complexity and requirements of real world hospitals.
Ivo Blochliger [10] gives a detailed introduction to the nurse rostering
problem, describing constraints and cost and proposes a general model which
can be used to model the roster problem. Ivo Blochliger [10] does not solve
the problem, but oﬀer hints to solving the problem, either by using a linear
programming model or a heuristic algorithm. However, Ivo Blochliger reminds
the reader that a linear model will not be very practical, as human behavior
is very hard to model linearly.
A more concrete approach using heuristics is presented by E. Burke and
Peter Cowling [12].
The authors use a combination of Tabu search and a
Memetic/Evolutionary algorithm to solve the problem. The authors conclude
that the quality of the automatic generated schedules is much higher than if
they are created manually.
An interesting approach is given by E. Burke and T. Curtois [13] who use
regular expressions to solve the problem and propose a general model. The
model uses branch and price which proves to be very eﬀective.
Many articles use the dislike/like of particular shifts as the cost. [20, 21, 22]
are examples of such approaches. In the literature a good quality roster is
usually a roster where the preferences of the staﬀis highly weighted. In his
thesis Poulsen [22] uses the shift preferences of the staﬀto model the problem
and create good rosters.
To solve the rostering problem Poulsen [22] solves a 3 phase problem, where

minutes for a 4 week schedule.

Since the column generation and decom-

Mason et al. [21] propose a set covering model in which all nurses are

model must be changed accordingly.
The approach of using the like/dislike of the shifts as the cost (as in [20,

when creating the objective function.

Chapter 4
Modeling Approach
The proposed procedure is a Dantzig-Wolfe decomposition method. The prob-
lem is divided into two parts: A relaxed master problem and many sub-
problems. Each column in the master problem represents a roster for a nurse,
which is based on the optimal paths in the graph. When the relaxed mas-
ter problem is solved, the dual variables for the current solution are used as
weights on the edges of the directed graphs.
The graphs follows the hard
constraint rules of the 11-hour working rule and the oﬀday requirements. In
other words: The graphs make sure that the collective agreements are ful-
ﬁlled. The shortest path problem from source to sink for the graph is solved
by using a label-setting algorithm which ﬁnds the lowest cost of the routes
through the graph. The graphs are multi-criteria and directed acyclic graphs.
The Dantzig-Wolfe decomposition is a well known method in operations re-
search and has been proven successful in other contexts, e.g. in aircraft crew
scheduling. The sub-problem can handle the complex rules set by the collect-
ive agreement, while the master problem is used to ﬁnd the dual values which
are used on the edges of the sub-problems.
To meet the requirements set by the stakeholders, the master problem will
have an objective function which must be minimized. The objective function
will be the sum of the cost (in DKK) of all the rosters/columns created. This is
in the interest of Region Sjælland, since they would like to minimize the total
cost for the rosters. Meeting the requirements set by the nurses (the collective
agreement) is done in the graphs, where the 11-hour work rule and oﬀday
requirements are met.
Meeting the last stakeholder interest (the patients)
is done in the master problem, where every constraint will correspond to a
speciﬁc task, which must be completed that particular day. As previously
described most of these tasks should be “equal”, (see table 2.1 in section 2.1)
but in the modeling every task’s personal requirement is chosen to be greater
than or equal (≥) to the required number of nurses. The aim is to create a
more ﬂexible model, more suitable for CPLEX. More technically, this means
that the dual variable will be greater than or equal to zero instead of a free

4.1
Solution Method
The approach is broken into 7 steps:
1. Create initial columns/solution and add them to the master problem.
The initial columns are found randomly or by using the same start solu-
tion.
2. Solve the relaxed master problem.
3. Take the dual values from the solution and update the edges in the
directed graphs/subproblems.
4. Find the most negative, shortest paths (using the label-setting algorithm)
in the directed graph for every nurse. (Only negative columns are added.
Paths with costs greater than or equal to zero are omitted).
5. Add the negative feasible columns for each nurse to the master problem.
6. Go to 2 until no negative cost paths are found, the iteration limit is
reached or the time limit is reached.
7. Solve the MIP master problem (non-relaxed problem)
Step (1) creates the initial columns for the nurses. Section 4.7 explains this
step in further detail.
In step (2) the relaxed master problem is solved based on the columns in
the problem. The master problem is relaxed such that the variables xi
r ∈{0, 1}
are converted to xi
r ≥0.
Step (3) updates the graphs for all nurses with the new dual values. This
is explained in section 4.6.
In step (4) the shortest path problem is solved for all nurses using a label-
setting algorithm. This is explained in section 4.8. The negative paths found
are added to the master problem step (5). In the program the user deﬁnes
how many negative paths should be added per nurse.
Step (6) decides whether the iteration should be stopped. This is either
if no negative paths can be found, the iteration limit is reached or the time
limit is reached.
Step (7) The unrelaxed binary master problem is solved to optimality and
the best MIP solution is found and examined.

Graph Disadvantages
There is a major disadvantage by using the graph described in section 4.6.
One is that many possibilities are omitted, possibilities which are legal in the
sense of the collective agreement. An example is that it would be legal for
a nurse to accomplish task Day 1 N1 and then take task Day 5 D1, but due
to the way the graph is constructed, this is not possible. If a nurse has been
assigned a night tasks (N) the nurse can only complete night tasks in the
future. The reason for using this approach is that the graph would otherwise
be very complicated if all the combinations were included. As shown in the
test chapter, the solution time increases rapidly, so it is crucial to create a
graph which is not that complicated.
Graph Advantages
The advantage by creating the graph in this way is that nurses will not be able
to rapidly shift between day and night tasks. If a nurse has been allocated to
a night task, he/she will always accomplish night tasks in the future. This is
in the interest of the nurse stakeholders, since it is then easier for the nurses
to create a work/life balance. Another advantage is that the graph is kept
simple and not overly complicated.

4.7
Initial Problem
The ﬁrst solution has to be generated before the algorithm can start. This is
step 1 in section 4.1. A ﬁxed number of columns per nurse are added to the
master problem. This could for example, be 300 columns. The columns are
all created pseudo-randomly from the graph for the nurse (an example of such
a graph can be seen in ﬁgure 4.2 for a nurse able to take the shifts D1,A1,N1).
Taking the graph as an example, the algorithm starts in the “Source” vertex
and creates a random number between 1 and 6 (both inclusive), where 6 is
the number of edges going out of “Source”. If the pseudo-random number is
3, the path goes to vertex “N1”. Here a new pseudo-random number between
1 and 2 is generated, where 2 is the number of edges going out of “N1”. If
the number generated is 2 the next vertex in the route will be “ON”. So the
ﬁnal pseudo-random road in the graph will be “Source” ⇒“N1” ⇒“ON” ⇒
“Sink”. The amount of initial columns generated for each nurse is done e.g. 300
times. A parameter can specify how many initial columns are added. Having
72 nurses each with 300 columns gives a total of 21600 columns added to the
master problem initially. Another approach for creating the initial columns is
to generate the same initial solution. This approach is also tested in chapter
5. Both the random approach and generating the same initial solution are
implemented in the program and further studied in the test section (chapter
5).
The start columns of the initial solution are not feasible in the sense of the
collective agreement for the nurses, so it is crucial that they are not present in
a ﬁnal solution. The columns are not feasible because it is possible to generate
random initial rosters where a nurse will work e.g. 7 days in a row, and the
collective agreement states speciﬁcally that the nurses should have at least
2 oﬀdays each week. The cost Ci
r for each start column is therefore set to
99999. This is to ensure that CPLEX will not choose the columns in the ﬁnal
solution.

4.9
Choosing the Feasible Paths
After all the labels have been generated the optimal paths in the graph should
be chosen. This is also step 4 in section 4.1. The optimal paths from source
to sink are chosen based on the three costs on the edges of the graphs: Cost,
oﬀdays and working hours.
Cost
In the program, the user has the ability to specify how many
columns should be added to the master problem. If the user has speciﬁed that
1 column should be added each iteration, the path with the lowest negative
cost less than 0 for each nurse is added. If the user has speciﬁed that more
than 1 column should be added (e.g. 10), 10 columns with negative costs less
than 0 are added. The algorithm simply needs a negative column to ﬁnd a
better solution, so it is not required that the most negative one is added. The
total cost is the reduced cost rc(ac∗) described in section 4.6 and this has to
be less than 0 to be chosen as a feasible path.
OﬀDays
The feasible routes in the graph have a number of oﬀdays
greater than or equal to 2 per week. So if the roster is made for 2 weeks, the
feasible paths contain at least 4 oﬀdays. The reason for having “at least” is
because some nurses work part-time, which means they should have more than
2 oﬀdays per week. If a nurse works full time, he/she should have exactly 2
days oﬀeach week.
Working hours
The number of working hours in the feasible paths are
greater than or equal to their number of hours per week (H/W) * planned
weeks. So if the nurse should work 30 hours per week and the roster is made
for 2 weeks, the optimal path for the nurse contains at least 60 working hours.
Of course sometimes it is not possible to meet these demands. In that case
a new roster is not made for the nurse and no new column for that particular
nurse is added to the master problem.

Elimination and Pareto Optimality
The edges in the graph has 3 costs: Cost calculated from the dual values, oﬀ

(−100, 0, 8)) where x1 is the cost calculated from the dual values, x2 is the oﬀ

Branching
In order to ﬁnd the optimal MIP solution, it is necessary to implement a

6.2
Special Costs
The special costs are the following (and speciﬁed in section 2.2):
• A treatment nurse who is given the telephone task is paid an additional
100 DKK per shift.
• Coordination gives an additional 200 each shift (max 3 times a day).
• A night shift between Saturday and Sunday gives an additional 150 DKK
per shift.
• A treatment nurse who is assigned the Næstved task is paid an additional
74,15 each hour.
In the model a treatment nurse can always be assigned a telephone task. In
the model it is also possible for a treatment nurse to be assigned the Næstved
task. The special costs are not implemented in the model and the program.
However they can be estimated by using a worst case scenario in combin-
ation with the solution described in section 5.4:
1. Lets us assume that a treatment nurse is always assigned the telephone
task. During the week a total of 39 nurses are assigned the telephone
task in the solution. This gives 39 · 100 = 3900 DKK.
2. The special cost for the coordination task can at most be paid out 3
times a day for 7 days. This gives 3 · 7 · 200 = 4200 DKK.
3. In the solution, 7 nurses are assigned a night shift between Saturday and
Sunday. This gives 150 · 7 = 1050 DKK.
4. There are 14 nurses who can accomplish the Næstved task and they are
given 34 Næstved tasks in total during the week in the solution. The
Næstved task lasts 8 hours and assuming all nurses are treatment nurse
this gives 34 · 8 · 74.15 = 20169 DKK.
The sum of these are 29319 DKK and the best solution found for the Original
Model is 507814. Adding these numbers give a total cost of 537133 or an
increase of at most 5.77%. So the special costs only have a minor inﬂuence
on the total cost. The special costs are not implemented in the model and
program since they are insigniﬁcant. It is always possible to keep implementing
new rules and costs from the collective agreements and in this particular case,
the special tasks are left out in the modeling and the program. If the special

Recommendations
During this thesis and tests some basic issues which can be solved with simple
rules of thumb or structural changes become apparent. These are described
here. These are necessary in order to achieve lower costs and save up to 23%.
• Use one of the rosters generated by the model as a template in the roster
creation procedure. Of course the model is very general and does not
take holiday planning and leave into account. Despite this, the rosters
created can still serve as inspiration in the practical roster creation.
An example of a schedule that could be used is the one analyzed in
section 5.4. The complete roster is in appendix A. In section 6.6 it is
concluded that only 9 out of 72 nurses create problem if the roster should
be repeated. Solving this problem is done by allocating the problematic
nurses to other tasks which do not create problems if the roster should
be repeated.
• Make it possible for the head planning nurse to see the wages for each
nurse for better utilization of the cost diﬀerence between the nurses who
are employed at Slagelse Emergency Department. This will enable the
planning nurse to allocate the cheap nurses to tasks with a high wage
factor, making it possible to save money.
• According to the conducted tests there should not be a surplus of nurses
available during the weekend, as this is more expensive. If a nurse needs
to work to fulﬁll the work hours required in the normperiod, the nurse
should be allocated to a daily task during the week. This is cheaper
than allocating to tasks during the weekend or in the evening and night.
In chapter 6 it is shown that the realized schedule allocates 98 nurses to
evening shifts while the model allocates 91 nurses. So 7 nurses could be
allocated to daily tasks instead, hence saving money. The same counts
for the night tasks. Here 52 nurses are allocated in the realized schedule,
while the model allocates 50 nurses. These 2 nurses could be allocated
to daily tasks, making the roster cheaper.

Discussion
The models and graph described in this thesis solve the real-life case at Sla-
gelse Emergency Department while taking most rules from the contracts and
collective agreement into account. The graph takes the rules from the collect-
ive agreement for the nurses into account and makes it possible to generate
feasible solutions. The master problems described in section 4.2 minimizes the
total cost for all tasks while making sure all tasks are done during the day.
The model therefore accommodates all three stakeholders (the patients, the
nurses and Region Sjælland) to a satisfying level. The data which has been
used is real-life data from the Slagelse Emergency Department, which gives a
very realistic picture of the daily work at the Emergency Department. The
model creates much better solutions than those currently realized at Slagelse
Emergency Department and therefore improves the roster creation process.
The head planning nurse said it was hard to accomplish every task which
must be completed each day.
This is a constraint in the model (equation
4.3) which can be met when optimizing. If it was not possible to meet this
constraint, feasible solutions could not be found. Chapter 6 relates the model
solution to the real world and it is shown that Region Sjælland can save up
to 23% of their costs, even when the extra tasks (ﬂowcoordination and shift
tasks) are taken into account. This is a signiﬁcant amount of money, which
clearly shows that there is room for improvement of the rostering quality at
Slagelse Emergency Department. This is a conclusion shared by the report of
“The Danish National Audit Oﬃce” [24].
By using the cost in the objective function it was expected that all the
cheap nurses would be allocated to night shifts, but, as shown in ﬁgure 5.8,
this is not true. The cheap nurses get the majority of the tasks during the
night, but not all of them. This is due to the fact that some of the night
tasks require extra skills, which only more expensive nurses are capable of
completing.
The MIP solutions found have solution values of about 500000 for one
week. This is acceptable. The MIP solutions found give a signiﬁcant reduction

The way the graph is created does not make it possible for a nurse to be
scheduled a daily task after a night task. This is in order to make the graph as
simple as possible, to reduce computation time, and because, in order for the
graph to be successful, it is not necessary to include the extra combinations. A
schedule with a lot of shifts between night and day is undesirable. In the form
of the graphs implemented in the model, it is possible to solve the problem
with 2x35 oﬀhours (or combined into 55-64 oﬀhours). If implementations
are done such that the graph only operates with an oﬀday period of 55-64
hours it would most likely be solved faster, since an oﬀday period would
consist of 2 oﬀdays instead of 2 periods of one day, thereby creating fewer
edges in the graph. Overall, the graph is successful and proves to be a good
approach for solving the rostering problem at Slagelse Emergency department.
As H. Poulsen points out in his thesis [22] the local rules of each department
is diﬀerent, so it would probably be very hard to transfer the solution method
from this department to other departments in other hospitals.
In chapter 5 section 5.7 the number of nurses required to accomplish the
tasks is analyzed. This shows that it is possible to settle with 45 nurses with
the Original Model in order to complete all the tasks for one week. For the
Realistic Pay Model the minimum number of nurses required are 60. The
diﬀerence occurs because the Original Model can operate with overtime work,
while the Realistic Model cannot. Overtime work is not preﬀered, hence the
Realistic Pay Model was proposed as a solution to this problem. Furthermore,
the extra money which overtime work generates are not included in the calcu-
lation, so the Original Model is even less realistic. It is true in the sense of the
model that there could be employed 45 or 60 nurses depending on the model,
but in reality there is a need to have more people employed. This is because
people will be on sick leave or not able to work due to other reasons. In ad-
dition to this, the ﬂowcoordinator role and shift tasks are left out, so these
tasks should also be covered in the planning process. Other employees must
step in instead of their colleagues and an employee buﬀer is needed in order to
complete all tasks during the week. A test is made in section 5.8 which shows
that it only costs 13045 DKK extra each week to plan with 2 extra nurses on
the Hospital task each day. This extra nurse buﬀer can be used when a nurse
is sick, so there always will be enough nurses at work. This overstaﬃng roster
can also be used if a nurse has an unbalanced work schedule, and has worked
too much during a period. The nurse can then have compensatory time oﬀ
instead of working.

Chapter 9
Future work
If the time span for the thesis was longer, it would be interesting to include
some of the following:
• Combine several one week plans in order to create rosters for a longer
period than one week. One approach for solving this task would probably
include using a metaheuristic. The metaheuristic would have to take the
problem of change in shifts into account. e.g. if a nurse is assigned a
night task on Sunday in the ﬁrst week, he/she cannot attend a daily
task on Monday in the next week.
• Include others types of employees in the model (doctors, social and
healthcare assistants). This would include creating new type of graphs,
which comply with the collective agreements for the other professions.
Including these other professions will make the model more realistic, but
will deﬁnitely also create a more complex problem, which will take much
longer to solve by the method proposed in this thesis.
• Another interesting approach would be to create a model where the tasks
can be any length between 5 and 12 hours. In this thesis the tasks always
have length of 8 or 8.25 hours long. Such a model will most certainly
create better solutions but will not be in the interest of the nurses.
• Include the rest of the tasks: Flowcoordination nurse and the shift task.
This will create a more realistic model. If it should be implemented using
the graph, the consequence would be that there should be created 3 new
oﬀday and 3 new task nodes in the graph. This would mean that the
graph would be more complicated, hence the labeling algorithm would
take longer to complete. E.g. including all three tasks for a period of 7
days would increase the number of vertexes by 6 · 7 = 42 and increase
the number of edges by 12·6 = 72 new edges. This should e.g. be added
to the total number of edges and vertices of a graph of a nurse who
can accomplish task D1, A1 and N1. Without the extra tasks, there

• Include the special costs in the model/program as described in section

Chapter 10
Conclusion
The overall goal of the thesis is to see whether it is possible to create a roster
which covers all tasks with the number of nurses already available at Slagelse
Emergency Department. To solve this problem a Dantzig-Wolfe decomposition
model has been proposed and used to solve the problem. A set of assumptions
have been necessary in order to address the problem in this thesis. As seen
in the test section, good solutions have been found. The interests of every
stakeholder have been met. Region Sjælland wants to save money and this
is done by creating an objective function in the model which minimizes the
amount of money used on the rostering. The interests of the nurses have been
met in the sub-problem where the graph takes the rules from the collective
agreement for the nurses into account. The patients are accommodated in
the master problem where the required tasks each day are fulﬁlled by creat-
ing constraints for each task each day. So the models meets the interest of
every stakeholder and is therefore a success. The problem of unbalanced work
schedules where some nurses work overtime, while others work less than stated
in their contracts, are also adressed. This is done in The Realistic Pay Model,
where nurses always are paid out their respective wage, while always work-
ing maximum their weekly hours (e.g. 37 hours). In this way no nurse work
overtime. The result is that the Realistic Pay Model gives solution around
480000 DKK while the Original Model (which has the overtime possibility)
gives results around 510000 DKK. The Realistic Pay Model not alone is more
realistic, it is also cheaper than the Original Model.
By using the Realistic Pay Model, it is estimated that Region Sjælland
theoretically can save up to 157000 DKK each week by creating better rosters
for the nurses. It is important to note that this saving occurs without needing
to ﬁre nurses. This is a saving of approximately 23 %, which is a signiﬁcant
amount. It is concluded that the savings are realistic. Maybe it is not possible
to save 157000 DKK in practice, but there is most deﬁnitely a potential for
some savings by allocating the nurses diﬀerently. This is a statement shared
with “The Danish National Audit Oﬃce” in their report [24]. It is important to

The Original Model creates overtime work, which is not preferred. In order
to solve this problem a new model, the Realistic Pay Model was proposed as
a solution. This model only plans for less hours than what is stated in the

tasks. The diﬀerence occurs because the Original Model has the possibility to
operate with overtime work, while the Realistic Pay Model creates schedules
which are below what nurses should work (e.g. 37 hours). The Realistic Pay
Model seems more realistic as nurses will be on leave, holiday or be sick, so it

Metaheuristic. Despite not having created rosters for more than one week, the
