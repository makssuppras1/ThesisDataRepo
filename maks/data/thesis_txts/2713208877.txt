Abstract 
The global SARS-CoV-2 pandemic has prompted increased research into the airborne 
transmission of the virus between people in indoor environments. Researchers of various 
disciplines are focusing on the characteristics of the spread of exhalation droplets and droplet 
nuclei. The virus-laden particles, emitted when speaking, breathing, coughing or sneezing, travel 
through the indoor environment, causing a health risk to occupants. The transmission of viruses 
depends on the air distribution, which is determined by the supply air flow rate or the total volume 
distribution of the air. Special attention has been given to describing the different ventilation 
methods and their impact on reducing the risk of exposure. Although ventilation is commonly used 
to provide a healthy and comfortable environment, it may be insufficient in providing adequate 
clean air. 
 
An air purifier may be helpful in this regard which has been extensively researched. Studies 
are being conducted on whether an appropriate ventilation method in combination with an 
operational air purifier is effective in reducing SARS-CoV-2 concentrations in indoor air. Air 
purifiers equipped with a HEPA (High Efficiency Particulate Air) filter already show satisfactory 
results in filtering particles of a size corresponding to virus-laden particles. In addition to the 
filtration efficiency, the performance of an air purifier is judged by its effect on indoor air 
distribution. For this reason, it is advisable to obtain information on the performance of the unit at 
different locations in a room. As air distribution is a major aspect of assessing the performance of 
air purifiers, numerical analyses are of help. Many numerical studies have already been performed 
on the performance of air purifiers. Computational fluid dynamics (CFD) has been proven to 
reliably investigate indoor airflow patterns. Therefore, in the study presented in this paper, CFD 
was applied to determine the importance of the location of air purifiers in reducing airborne cross-
infection. 
 
The location of the air purifier was found to have a strong influence on the distribution of air 
in the room. This leads to different exposures of the occupants in the room to air exhaled by an 
infected person. The effectiveness of the air purifier in reducing cross-infection was found to vary 
between the locations tested, leading to an increased likelihood of infection for some subjects and 
a decreased likelihood for others. The effect of the angle of the airflow discharged from the purifier 
on room air distribution and occupant exposure was found to be inconclusive. A change in direction 
is met with a reduction in exposure for some people and an increase for others. An effective 
reduction in the likelihood of infection was observed with the use of an air purifier. The use of an 
air purifier as a supplement to mechanical ventilation at the beginning of the occupancy period, 
when the concentration of virus-laden particles begins to build up, may increase the likelihood of 
infection in some occupants, compared with the use of ventilation alone.

1. Introduction 
1.1 
Indoor environment 
Understanding the correlation of the indoor environment and human health is fundamental to 
improving indoor conditions to reduce negative impacts on occupants. During the 1980s, excellent 
research was carried out [1], resulting in the determination of human emission rates of air pollutants 
and the quantification of the concentration of air pollutants perceived by people. The work on 
indoor air quality focuses on pollutants that affect both health (aerosols, chemicals, particles) and 
comfort (humidity, temperature, air flow) simultaneously [2]. The evolution of ventilation design 
is significantly influenced by the complex relationship between the built environment and people. 
This means that occupants themselves negatively affect indoor air quality by generating pollutants. 
The concentration of human-derived factors in indoor air is a function of the strength of the indoor 
source, the rate of removal and the rate of deposition [3]. Humans, the primary source of indoor 
pollutants, emit bioeffluents that include volatile organic compounds, particles formed from hair 
and skin and particularly dangerous aerosolized infectious agents [4]. 
 
Jenkins et. al. [5] showed that, on average, humans spend 87% of their time indoors. This 
observation influences the concern for maintaining safe indoor conditions. The problem posed by 
indoor pollution is largely related to human health. Volatile organic compounds can cause chronic 
health problems, including asthma and allergies. Respiratory and cardiovascular diseases can result 
from exposure to biological pollutants [3], [6]. Carbon dioxide significantly affects susceptibility 
to stress and work performance [3]. Indoor pollution is counteracted by maintaining adequate levels 
of protection. The air exchange rate, the filtration efficiency of HVAC systems, the surface 
materials present indoors, and the occupancy pattern is all building characteristics that are regulated 
for this purpose. 
 
Given the challenges posed by the SARS-CoV-2 virus pandemic, particular attention is being 
paid to factors posing a risk of airborne transmission through the transport of virus-laden aerosols. 
The assessment of indoor air quality depends on the rate of formation of pollutants in the room and 
the rate of their removal. Carbon dioxide concentrations are commonly used as an indicator of air 
quality and ventilation system performance. However, this practice is subject to uncertainties due 
to ventilation rate effectiveness, initial conditions, build-up time of concentrations, parameter 
fluctuations or the decay of indoor CO2 concentrations related to occupant load variability [7]. A 
practical direction for research is therefore engineering simulations, which largely compensate for 
the problems and additionally allow the detailed investigation of the airborne transmission of 
hazardous aerosols.

1.2 
Airborne exposure 
A group of people in an indoor environment creates the risk of transmission of anthropological 
contaminants, including potentially dangerous microorganisms causing respiratory or 
cardiovascular diseases [6]. Lungs are also exposed to particulate matter, the smallest fractions of 
which can penetrate the deeper structure of the organ and cause serious health problems [4]. 
Understanding the origin and transmission characteristics of these pollutants has a real impact on 
the development of technologies that act against adverse complications. 
1.2.1 Pollution 
Particulate Matter 
Indoor sources of particulate matter can be found in people themselves, who generate 
pollutants from their hair or skin. Indoors, it is common to find pollutants from animals, plants, and 
building materials, as well as sources from activities such as cooking and cleaning [8]. The 
diversity of suspended particles is essential, especially in terms of size, observed concentrations, 
and the chemical compounds of which the particles are composed. PMs are subject to numerous 
transport phenomena and forces affecting their behaviour. Three main mechanisms affecting the 
deposition of inhaled particles can be distinguished. Gravitational settling describes the deposition 
of particles under the influence of gravity. Brownian motion deposition results from random 
movements of particles due to collisions with gas molecules. Inertial settling occurs when there is 
a sudden change in the direction of flow when, due to inertia, the particles retain their original 
trajectory resulting in collisions with the walls of the respiratory system [8]. 
 
The commonly used classification of particles by size Includes the distinction between coarse, 
fine, and ultra-fine particles. Coarse particles, labelled PM10, are characterised by an aerodynamic 
diameter of less than 10 𝜇𝑚. PM2.5 (fine) particles are less than 2.5 𝜇𝑚 in diameter. The smallest 
particles of this classification, ultra-fine, are marked PM1, which corresponds to a size of less than 
1 𝜇𝑚. Standard ISO 7708:1995 provides a refined classification of particle deposition in the human 
respiratory system which, in conjunction with a size grading, reveals details of the hazard of 
exposure to airborne dust. The fraction inhaled through the nose and mouth, which depends on the 
speed and direction of air movement in relation to the head, respiratory rate, and respiratory 
volume, is called the inhalable fraction. Particles that do not cross the larynx are the extrathoracic 
fraction. This group includes some allergens. The thoracic fraction exceeds the larynx and is 
matched to the size of PM10 particles. Respirable particles represent the fraction that penetrates the 
unciliated airways and carries a high risk to human health [9].

Anthropogenic pathogens 
Air pollutants that pose a particular risk to humans in indoor environments are pathogens. 
Regarding respiratory cross-infection, the emphasis is on infectious pollutants of human origin. 
Intensive research is being conducted to better understand the nature of microbiological agents and 
their effects on the human body, and the characterisation of transmission or its mitigation is of 
particular importance to the ventilation industry. 
 
SARS-CoV-2 virus measures between 80 and 160 𝑛𝑚 in diameter [10]. Particles exiting the 
respiratory system can settle on a surface or remain suspended in the air in each indoor 
environment. According to a study [11], the activity of the virus after contact with a surface is 
strongly related to the material of that surface and the virus-carrying medium. Known data are 
showing that the virus can survive on surfaces for hours or even days [12]. In the context of cross-
infection, HVAC specialists are developing measures to reduce the risk of airborne transmission. 
In the airborne state, the duration of the presence of the virus up to 3 hours has been observed in 
indoor environments [13]. In studies on the spread of virus-laden droplets, it has been observed 
that large particle droplets are resistant to air movement and tend to fall on surfaces, while their 
smaller equivalents can move longer distances in a closed space, subject to air streams [14].  
 
Emission sources include speech, breathing, coughing, or sneezing. The results of a study [15] 
on particles formed during speech showed that the distribution of diameters varies from 0.5 to 20 
μm, confirming the diversity of states of virus presence in the indoor environment, as this range 
effectively applies to the characterisation of spread. 
 
CO2  
Addressing carbon dioxide concentration has been a major aspect of the science on ventilation 
and indoor air quality. It is a factor that significantly influences the assessment of the air quality 
perceived by the occupants and the performance of the ventilation system operating in each indoor 
environment. As far as the occupants are concerned, CO2 has a significant effect on their mood, 
ability to concentrate and level of productivity. In such an arrangement, the vulnerability of human 
health is a natural link [7].  
 
In times of increased risk of airborne disease transmission, carbon dioxide concentration 
measurements are treated as an indicator of infection probability risk by assessing the ventilation 
efficiency based on measured CO2 concentration. A major conclusion of the debate on this topic is 
that higher CO2 concentrations relate directly to lower air exchange rates, which increases the risk 
of infection. OSHA (US Occupational Safety and Health Administration) and NIOSH (National 
Institute for Occupational Safety and Health) specify a safety CO2 concentration over an 8-hour 
working day to be a maximum of  5,000 ppm  [16], [17].

1.2.2 Cross-infection 
Airborne infection occurs when microorganisms are passed from an infected person to an 
exposed person by the transmission of droplets or droplet nuclei. Upon exhalation, droplets (>5 
𝜇𝑚) desiccate and droplet nuclei are formed [23]. The level of risk of infection at distances greater 
than 1.5 𝑚 from the source of pathogen emission can be effectively regulated by implementing an 
optimal ventilation system. Air distribution in the indoor environment through effective control of 
HVAC equipment can significantly affect the process of contaminant spread. This is a research 
area of particular interest as these systems are the predominant means of regulating air quality in 
buildings [24], [25], [26]. Despite active ventilation, small rooms occupied by even a limited 
number of people show an increased risk of cross-infection [19]. 
 
A distinction is made between two dominant scenarios affecting the range of probability of 
infection, based on distance from the infected individual [24]. The first scenario specifies the 
transmission of virus-laden particles at 1 to 2 𝑚 from the source emitted by speech, coughing, 
sneezing, and breathing. This is followed by long-range transmission by droplets capable of 
reaching a state of airborne suspension. A third method not dependent on air distribution is direct 
contact with the fomite i.e. the contaminated surface.

1.3 
Operation of ventilation systems 
Airborne transmission in the indoor environment is controlled by ventilation as commonly the 
most influential method. Adequate air distribution and ventilation airflow rate are crucial in 
reducing the risk of transmission of virus-laden particles and consequently reducing the risk of 
infection [27]. 
 
Following the instructions and recommendations of DS/EN 16798-1:2019 standard [28], there 
are three methods for designing a ventilation system based on different parameters affecting the air 
quality in the indoor environment, i.e., using perceived air quality, using predetermined and 
acceptable values for the concentration of a given substance in the air and proceeding based on 
fixed air flow rates. As stated in CEN CR 1752:1999 [29], a properly designed ventilation system 
is characterized by an adequate distribution of air in space. The distributions of temperature, 
velocity, and air quality in the occupied area are influential parameters in the design process, so 
the initial assumptions on the value of such quantities should be known.  
 
The air distribution provided by mixing ventilation aims at a uniform concentration of 
pollutants in the room volume, which is achieved by mixing the supply air with the air already 
present indoors. According to a study [30], desiccated droplets travel further providing a lower risk 
of exposure to infection. In addition, through a reduced probability of thermal stratification, a 
shorter residence time of virus-laden particles in the breathing area of occupants is observed. 
Although mixing and displacement ventilation is characterised by a low momentum of the airflow 
generated, so it has a minor effect on the spread of droplets that have a high initial momentum.

Displacement ventilation is an exemplary application of non-mixing air distribution. The 
technology ensures the removal of polluted air and the introduction of clean air in its place. The 
parameters determining the operation of the DV are calculated based on the expected temperature 
in the room. Cool outdoor air is supplied at low velocities from an inlet at the bottom of the room, 
closer to the floor where it spreads and starts to displace the warmer air present in the room to the 
ceiling. This arrangement causes vertical gradients in air velocity, air temperature and contaminant 
concentration [31]. Displacement air distribution provides a lower probability of infection 
compared to Mixing air distribution [32].  
 
Focusing on the individual using the indoor environment, personalized ventilation provides 
clean air directly to the area of exposure. PV is known to provide a better perceived air quality [33]. 
In cooperation with common ventilation systems, PV shows exceptional effectiveness in reducing 
the risk of cross-infection [27]. The effect on exposure risk is dictated by the type of system used, 
for example, personal ventilation set up on a desk, close to the occupant, shows a range of 41 to 
99% effectiveness compared to mixed ventilation [34]. The use of PV by an infected person is 
associated with increased dispersion of exhaled pollutants. This creates a risk of cross-
contamination, which depends on the direction of airflow supplied by the PV, the distribution of 
background air and the location of susceptible and infected occupants [27]. 
 
Interestingly, the use of mixing ventilation alone shows a slightly higher efficiency when 
personal ventilation is used only by the infected person. In addition, PV in such an arrangement 
still shows a better reduction in risk of exposure than displacement ventilation [27].  
 
Ventilation has a critical role in controlling exposure to airborne contaminants. Current 
systems show a need for modification and greater sophistication focused on identifying and 
controlling the likelihood of infection at the source. The indoor environment requires to be treated 
not holistically, but rather with details in terms of breathing regions [35], [36]. By keeping air 
distribution focused on each occupant, ventilation systems can provide comfortable and safe indoor 
environments. Increasing the supply air flow with mixing ventilation reduces the concentration of 
polluted air and thus reduces the risk of airborne cross-contamination, which is also dependent on 
the local distribution of pathogens in the air. However, it also increases energy consumption and 
requires extensive ventilation systems. Such measures result in high costs and complexity of 
operating in the absence of pandemic risk.

1.4 
Air filtration 
A better understanding of the problem drives innovation and therefore the creation and 
application of new technologies to overcome the issue. Until now, the concentration of carbon 
dioxide or micro-organisms and pollutants of external origin has been levelled only by ventilation. 
It has been shown that mechanical systems equipped with HEPA filters show highly satisfactory 
efficiency in removing particulate matter from the supply air before it reaches the room [37]. 
Widely used natural ventilation in living environments, however, is not very often adapted to the 
use of filters, which opens the way for the development of portable air purifiers. According to 
experimental research reports, these devices show satisfactory effectiveness in reducing particulate 
matter and allergen concentrations [38], [39].  
 
Filtration units can be distinguished into two categories, i.e. those operating in ducts and those 
commonly referred to as portable [39]. For the research described here, the current chapter 
considers the characteristics of air filtration in portable units.   
 
Portable air purifiers are widely available, inexpensive, and effective solutions to improve air 
quality in indoor environments. In 2020, of the 1,237 people surveyed 8% had purchased an air 
purifier or similar type of device citing the COVID-19 pandemic as a motivation [40]. The ability 
to autonomously capture pollutants and thus ensure air purity is the main feature of air cleaners 
that contributes to the assurance of healthy residential conditions. The device can be considered an 
additional element of the ventilation system, which supplies the room with disinfected air. It should 
be noted that it cannot be a replacement for ventilation, but rather helpful addition. The need to use 
an air cleaner results from the increased presence of airborne pollutants such as dust, 
microorganisms, chemical compounds, or allergens. Naturally, the effectiveness of an air purifier 
in reducing exposure to pathogens of anthropological origin such as the SARS-CoV-2 virus is then 
questioned [40]. In the perspective of improving air quality, it is important to mention that the 
impact of an air purifier is not directly correlated with carbon dioxide concentrations as is the case 
with particulate pollutants [41]. 
 
Common choices for applications in regular conditions such as living rooms and offices are 
purifiers using fibrous filters, such as HEPA filters, which prove to effectively reduce PM 
concentrations [42]. HEPA filters are an example of panel filters that are often fitted with fine 
active carbon granules to counteract unwanted odours. Their efficiency in removing particulate 
contaminants of varying sizes is 99.97% [37].In environments with an increased risk of micro-
organisms in the ambient air, UV-based units are generally used. Less common yet equally 
effective in deactivating pathogens are purifiers equipped with electrostatic precipitation, ion 
generators or catalysts [39].

Performance evaluation of air cleaners comprises two parameters, i.e. efficiency and 
effectiveness. The first is a measure of the ability to reduce the concentration of contaminants in 
the air drawn in by the purifier. Effectiveness, on the other hand, is a measure of the ability to 
remove pollutants in the operating environment of the unit. Efficiency is tested under laboratory 
conditions, which also allows comparison between different purifiers. Effectiveness is, however, 
influenced by factors within the control of the indoor environment, such as the location of the air 
purifier, the airflow rate, and the hours of operation. 
 
It should be mentioned that the devices discussed in the current chapter are not capable of 
reducing the risk of human-to-human contamination over a short distance, as they are usually 
placed further away from the designated workspace in the room, i.e., away from the breathing 
region. They do, however, have the potential to reduce the concentration of anthropological 
contaminants in the room, namely exhaled aerosols [43] with the exception that, as recirculating 
devices, they do not affect the concentration of carbon dioxide exhaled by humans. 
 
The key performance indicator, as defined by the ANSI/AHAM AC-1-2006 standard [44], is 
the CADR (Clean Air Delivery Rate). It is defined under laboratory conditions and its formula is 
described as the rate of pollutant reduction in the test chamber when the unit is on, minus the rate 
of natural decay when the unit is not operating, multiplied by the volume of the test chamber 
measured [44].  With the placement of the air purifier, CADR is significantly involved in the impact 
on effectiveness [45].  
 
The utility of air purifiers in the fight against cross-infection of airborne viruses is still under 
consideration and requires meticulous research both experimentally and virtually. From a technical 
point of view, HEPA filters demonstrate the ability to remove virus-laden particles present in the 
ambient air [46] and therefore have significant potential.  Currently, however, air purifiers are 
evaluated as an optional add-on in counteracting airborne transmission [47]. One should consider 
whether the effect of the purifier on the distribution of air in the indoor environment also affects 
the character of pathogen transport. As studies [48], [49], indicate, portable filtering devices are 
capable of counteracting aerosol transmission of the SARS-CoV-2 virus, and further research into 
efficiency components in this category is desirable to successively reduce the risk of exposure.

1.5 
Numerical modelling in IAQ applications 
Owing to the technology of computational fluid dynamics, it becomes possible to solve 
problems for which analytical way to obtain results is extremely difficult or even impossible. With 
mathematical models and adequate computing power, numerical interpretation of difficult concepts 
becomes feasible. Numerical analyses are often used as a complement to experimental studies, and 
after successful verification and validation of the model, it provides a valuable basis for new 
research that does not require physical input into the empirical experiment, thus saving time and 
money.  
 
CFD is already being successfully applied to the study of pathogen transport and the effect of 
ventilation on pathogen distribution. Research [25] points out that even the complex process of 
controlling microorganisms in a room with the help of ultraviolet germicidal irradiation and aided 
ventilation can be successfully set up numerically. Successful verification and validation of air 
distribution models under the influence of key factors is the foundation for a mechanism to predict 
phenomena affecting safety in indoor environments. 
 
The character of numerical modelling for HVAC applications brings benefits for 
understanding the spatial distribution of CO2, the characteristics of different distribution schemes, 
air change rates and occupancy. As indicated by a study [50], simulations directed towards 
determining the influence of CO2 sensor location and demand-controlled ventilation effectiveness 
demonstrate confidently that the two parameters are strongly correlated. 
 
A natural direction for research in ventilation using computational fluid dynamics is to reliably 
visualise the transport of contaminants in the shape of particles, aerosols, or virus-laden particles. 
Today, numerical simulation software is equipped with effective tools for characterizing complex 
and customized multiphase flow problems. Demonstration of particle distribution in indoor 
environments [51], dispersion and deposition under indoor conditions [52] are successfully 
arranged numerically. Simulations of more difficult mechanisms, i.e., realistic airborne 
transmission paths are also now being reported [53]. 
 
Relying on the known successful applications of numerical modelling in HVAC and cross-
infection issues, science is turning its attention towards new technologies that can provide 
predictability of infection mechanisms. Regarding exhaled virus-laden particles, an integral aspect 
is the human respiratory cycle, which is also being successively studied numerically in the present 
[54]. The alignment of experimental technologies with the full verification and validation of 
numerical models represents the future of understanding and predicting the safety components of 
indoor environments. As can be deduced from the global events of recent years, knowledge and 
experience of epidemiological protection measures require meticulous research, both empirical and 
digital.

1.6 
Justification 
The COVID-19 pandemic affected all nations by becoming a global problem. Measuring 
between 80 and 160 𝑛𝑚 in diameter, the SARS-CoV-2 virus can be transported in an aerosol 
composed of droplets and droplet nuclei [10]. Virus-laden particles are emitted during speech, 
breathing, coughing, or sneezing [24]. Realistic particle sizes during speech range from 0.5 𝜇𝑚 to 
20 𝜇𝑚 [15]. Under normal indoor conditions, virus particles can travel more than 1.5 m during 
emission, after which they remain suspended in the air, depending on the particle diameter [24].  
In the indoor environment, the risk airborne of cross-infection is particularly elevated [55]. 
Therefore, special attention should be paid to ventilation systems as the main preventive measure 
used in indoor environments [27]. As a supportive addition to ventilation, air purifiers can be used, 
the effectiveness of which is also being thoroughly investigated [47]. 
 
Airborne transmission is a major cause of infection, and it mainly depends on the air 
distribution in indoor environments. Room air distribution is defined by many factors including the 
supply airflow rate, the total volume air distribution method, whether it is mixing or displacement 
air distribution method, buoyancy flows generated by occupants and heated objects, occupants’ 
activities, and movement etc. [27]. Different ventilation methods provide different effectiveness in 
maintaining acceptable conditions for human health and comfort. Studies conducted on the effects 
of different ventilation methods on airborne transmission of infections provide different 
conclusions. Some indicate that displacement ventilation (DV) is more effective in reducing the 
risk of cross-infection than mixing ventilation (MV) by providing a stratification of the polluted 
air above the occupied zone, thus reducing the horizontal dispersion of the polluted exhaled air 
[56]. However, it has been shown that DV can also increase the probability of infection. It is 
possible to have in the occupied zone stratified polluted exhaled or coughed air due to large 
temperature gradient [57]. Mixing ventilation is more effective than displacement ventilation as it 
limits the distance over which droplet nuclei can travel [58]. Furthermore, MV exhibits a lower 
probability of retaining droplet nuclei in the breathing area [58].  
 
Controlling the source of virus-laden particles is difficult due to the inability to identify 
asymptomatic individuals. Exclusion of symptomatic persons, limiting the number of people in the 
room, physical distancing, and use of personal protective equipment (e.g., masks) will not always 
eliminate the risk of transmission [59]. It is widely recommended to increase the ventilation rate to 
the maximum. However, this is seldom possible with the existing ventilation systems designed to 
be energy efficient by providing clean air as recommended in the standards to maintain acceptable 
air quality for occupants. Some guidelines recommend energy inefficient strategy of continuous 
operation of ventilation systems regardless of the occupants’ number. It is also recommended that 
the future ventilation systems are designed to be large and able to provide substantially increased 
ventilation air during pandemic events.  
==PAGE:18==
Szymon Piotr Caban 
 
Master’s Thesis 
18 
 
However, such systems will operate inefficiently during the periods when pandemic is not 
present. When these methods are not applicable and the ventilation used is insufficient, it is 
reasonable to consider the use of an air purifier [46]. 
 
When the ventilation and filter media implemented in the air ducts are not sufficient in 
providing adequate clean air, a portable air purifier has the potential to improve the air quality. 
Together with adequate ventilation control, an air purifier can effectively reduce the concentration 
of SARS-CoV-2 in the air [47]. An air purifier is a stand-alone device used to reduce the 
concentration of pollutants in an indoor environment. The most common category of air purifiers 
implemented in residential, or office spaces are those equipped with fibrous air filters, capable of 
filtering suspended particles. HEPA (High Efficiency Particulate Air) filters commonly used in air 
purifiers may provide additional protection against airborne COVID-19 [46], [47].  
 
According to Novoselac and Siegel (2009) [45], the location of the purifier in the room has a 
major impact on airflow. The effectiveness in reducing exposure to particulate matter in the indoor 
environment depends on the location of the purifier and the CADR (clean air delivery rate) value. 
Park et. al. (2020) [60] report that a purifier in close proximity to the occupants is not sufficiently 
effective when the discharge angle of the air outlet is not adjusted accordingly, and the air velocity 
is not adequate. In such circumstances, the efficiency of particle concentration reduction in the 
breathing region is not improved. The authors report that adjusting the location of the air purifier 
and the velocity of the discharged air to the number of people in the room provides the potential to 
improve effectiveness of contaminants reduction [60]. However, the suggested above measures can 
not be effective implemented in practice because the number of occupants and their location which 
affects the room air distribution is not known and may change in time, the location of the infected 
asymptomatic occupant is not known, etc. and as a result the air distribution, including virus laden 
aerosols is not uniform. Thus, depending on the operation mode of the air purifier, its location and 
direction of the discharged disinfected air the airflow interaction and thus the spread of virus laden 
aerosols exhaled by an infected occupant will be different leading to different infection probability 
for the room occupants. It is important to know the differences for the ventilation design and 
selection of the air purifier type, operation and optimal location. 
 
The use of purifiers becomes popular and their design improves. The operation of the device 
is fast. Typically in practice the air purifiers are turned ON at the beginning of the room occupancy, 
for example start of a meeting, start of classes, etc. while the operation of the background 
mechanical ventilation starts 1-2 hours earlier, i.e. it operates at steady-state level. In room with 
mechanical ventilation only the build-up of exhaled viruses will start from the beginning of the 
occupancy time. The concentration of viruses will increase in time until it reaches steady state 
level. This time interval is known as build-up time. During the buil-up time the concentration of 
viruses will be lower than at steady state ventilation level and thus the risk of airborne cross 
infection will be lower.  
==PAGE:19==
Szymon Piotr Caban 
 
Master’s Thesis 
19 
 
However, use of air cleaner during the build-up (transient) period will change the airflow 
interaction in the room compared to the condition without air cleaner, i.e. only background 
ventilation. Thus, due to the change of the airflow pattern the spread of exhaled viruses will change 
and the infection probability of the room occupants will be different. Therefore, use of the air 
cleaner during the build-up period may increase the exposure to viruses for some occupants 
compare to the case without air cleaner in operation. This needs to be studied.       
 
Both experimental studies and numerical analyses are often employed to investigate indoor 
environmental conditions. Airborne transmission phenomena are studied empirically through 
physical measurements of concentrations in the breathing region. However, these are limited in 
time and space [27]. Computational fluid dynamics (CFD) provides a reliable analysis of the 
transient in time dispersion of exhaled pollutants, as well as their interaction with other sources of 
exhalation. Furthermore, the simulations effectively complement the empirical studies with 
additional insights into the mechanisms of cross-infection and air distribution [54], [61]. 
 
CFD is also used to analyze the factors affecting air purifier efficiency in relation to regions 
of interest. Yu, S, et. al. [62] successfully optimized purifier performance using numerical 
simulations. A robust numerical model exploring different locations of the air purifier in the 
bedroom indicated a direction for improving air purification efficiency. However, the use of the 
CFD to quickly control and optimize the air distribution in rooms in time, including airborne 
transmission of viruses under transient and changeable conditions is still not possible.

2. Objectives 
The overall objectives of this work are based on CFD simulations to study the performance of 
air cleaner in a mechanically ventilated room with regard to reduction of occupants’ exposure to 
virus-laden aerosols exhaled by one infected person. The specific objectives are: 
1. To develop numerical model (CFD model) for predicting quantities describing the state 
of the indoor environment and the exposure of occupants in a mechanically ventilation 
room with and without air purifier; 
2. To perform verification and validation of the numerical model against data obtained 
during physical experiments;  
3. To investigate performance of an air purifier under steady-state conditions focusing 
on:  
a. The importance of location 
b.  The influence of the angle of the flow of disinfected air discharged in the room 
by the air purifier; 
4. To examine performance of the air purifier under transient conditions starting from the 
time of initial room occupation when the concentration of virus-laden particles start to 
build-up.

3. Methodology 
The development of a numerical model of the climate chamber and the physical phenomena 
occurring within it was based on a similar study carried out empirically at The International Centre 
for Indoor Environment and Energy (ICIEE) at The Technical University of Denmark. The results 
of the physical climate chamber experiments served as the basis for full verification and validation 
of the simulation results.

The ICIEE climate chamber 
The real experiment was carried out in chamber 5 in ICIEE, which has dimensions of 4.2 
m x 4.1 m x 2.9 m. The room was furnished as a conference room by placing a table and 8 chairs

3.5 
Multiphase flow 
In the designed numerical model, dynamically complex multiphase flows occur, with the air 
being the gas phase and the virus-laden particles in the liquid phase (assumed to be water droplets). 
The particles exhaled by the subjects interact with the continuum (surrounding air) using a discrete-
phase model [66]. A two-equation, SST k-omega turbulence model based on the RANS (Reynolds 
Averaged Navier-Stokes Simulation) approach was used. This is the recommended model for 
simple cases, while providing a reasonable computational cost [71]. 
3.5.1 Discrete-phase model 
Transport of pathogens between test subjects in the indoor environment was performed using 
the discrete phase model (DPM). Dynamics of multiphase flow are modelled using the Euler-
Lagrange approach. The liquid phase is considered as a continuum by solving the Navier-Stokes 
equations, while the dissipated phase is addressed by tracking particles or droplets in a calculated 
flow domain. The dissipated phase is capable of exchanging momentum, mass, and energy with 
the liquid phase.

particle, which is stored in a Lagrangian reference frame. This balance of forces equalizes the

Updating regions 
 
Since the geometry intends to have the air surrounding the active surfaces enclosed by the 
walls of the room, two types were available for defining the regions. The region defined as “dead” 
is all the elements that are part of the equipment in the room, i.e., table, chairs, lamps, air purifier 
and even mannequins. This reduces the number of cells that construct the grid, as the element 
defined as dead becomes hollow in the centre while maintaining the shape of the surface. Another 
type is the fluid, which is used to determine the volume of air filling the room.

were used, which gives a satisfactory level of accuracy. A transition ratio of 0.272 was left as the 
default suggestion of the software.

influenced the choice of a polyhedron-hexahedron mesh.

Numerical investigation of air purifier placement on particles reduction

and the exposure of the occupants with the working air purifier placed in different locations. The 
purifier was operated under nominal conditions with the maximum output of the generated

some residents or decreased exposure for others. This was studied.

4.26). An air purifier can increase the mixing of exhaled particles with room air while reducing 
particle concentrations and occupant exposure. The flow of disinfected air generated by the purifier 
changes the flow interaction in the room and results in increased mixing. Therefore, the effect of 
the location of the air purifier on the distribution of air in the room was investigated (Figs. 4.27-

4.4 
Air purifier performance examination under transient conditions 
In practice, when people occupy a ventilated space, for example meeting rooms, classrooms, 
etc., the human body generated pollution, including exhaled particles by an infected occupant, 
starts to build up. After some time, which depends on the ventilation rate, the particle concentration 
reaches steady state level. Thus, during the build-up period the concentration of virus laden aerosols 
generated by an infected occupant is lower than at steady state conditions and thus the risk of 
airborne cross-infection is lower. Air distribution pattern in spaces is result of complex interaction 
of the ventilation airflow and the buoyancy flows generated by occupants and other heated object. 
The air distribution pattern in rooms defines the transport of virus-laden aerosols exhaled by an 
infected occupant, the exposure of the other occupants to the viruses and thus the risk of airborne 
cross-infection. Typically, in practice the operation of air cleaner starts when the occupants enter a 
room, i.e., at the beginning of the build-up period. Under these conditions, the flow of disinfected 
air discharged by an air cleaner affects the room airflow interaction and thus the airflow pattern 
and the transport of virus-laden aerosols generated by an infected occupant. This may lead to 
increase of the exposure of some occupants to the viruses. It may also increase the mixing of the 
exhaled viruses with the room air thus decreasing their concentration and the exposure of other 
occupants. Thus, the use of the air cleaner during the build-up period, referred in this report as 
“transient conditions”, may increase the probability of airborne cross-infection for some occupants 
compared to the conditions when air cleaner is not used.   
 
The transient numerical analysis allowed to compare the indoor environment in the meeting 
room and occupants’ exposure with and without the air purifier in operation from the beginning of 
the meeting room occupation time. The performance of the unit operating at nominal conditions, 
with the maximum output of the generated disinfected air stream directed vertically upwards, was 
investigated. The unit was located in the corner between the west and north walls (see Fig. 3.12).  
Figure 4.22 compares the increase of the average room air temperature for the cases without and 
with air purifier in operation. It can be seen that without air purifier steady state level of the 
temperature is not reached after 10 min from the beginning of the build-up process. When the 
purifier is used the generated flow of disinfected air change the flow interaction in the room and 
causes more mixing. As a result, a steady state level of the average room air temperature is chieved 
already after 5 min. Considering this result and the time restrains of the computing the transient 
numerical analysis during the first 10 min were performed.

5. Discussion 
5.1 
Impact of air purifier location on cross-infection exposure 
The portable aspect of air purifier allows for its placement at different location in the room. 
However, the effectiveness of the device depends on the location relative to the source of the 
pollutants, as demonstrated in Section 4.2. The simulation results of an air purifier operating at 
three locations differ in terms of the reduction in exposure of individual occupants and in terms of 
air filtration efficiency. Because in real life the positioning of an infected person (asymptomatic or 
pre-symptomatic) is not known, it is difficult to find the best location for the purifier. However, in 
the future, based on the results of CFD analyses, the location of the purifier may be suggested to 
users by manufacturers. 
 
Particle concentrations calculated in close proximity to the nostrils of the occupants showed 
susceptibility to a change in the location of the purifier. This observation therefore correlates with 
the effect of the air purifier location on the magnitude of the risk of cross-infection. For the 
conditions of the present study (fixed background ventilation rate and direction, and discharged 
flow rate from the purifier) location number 1 proved to be the most effective in this regard, 
providing on average the lowest particle parcel concentrations. 
 
 
 
==PAGE:80==
Szymon Piotr Caban 
 
Master’s Thesis 
80 
 
The average concentration of particle parcels caught by the air purifier appeared to be highest 
when it was standing in location number 3 (in the corner between the east and north walls). This 
was followed by location number 1 (in the corner between the west and north walls), having a 
slightly lower average concentration. The worst performance of the purifier presented itself at 
location number 2, which is in the middle of the north wall. 
 
The reduction in the risk of exposure to airborne pollutants is the highest when the purifier is 
placed in location number one (Fig. 4.25). Then, the uniformity of air distribution is least disturbed 
by the operation of the device (Figure 4.26), while satisfactory levels of captured particles are 
observed (Figure 4.29). The purifier placed in location number two has one of the worst impacts 
on the reduction of the exposure risk (Figure 4.25, subject 5 exposure).  
5.2 
Impact of discharged jet angle on air purifier effectiveness 
Conventional air purifiers blow the disinfected air straight upwards. We considered the 
effectiveness of a device with supplied jet directed towards the occupants at an angle of 45o. A 
comparison was made between the two airflow settings when the purifier is operating at location 
number 1. 
 
The implemented change in angle resulted in an increase in the risk of infection for four 
occupants. Occupant number 5 had the greatest increase in parcels concentration at the nasal area. 
 
Three occupants (1, 4 and 7) showed reduced exposure to cross-contamination compared to 
the case with a conventional air purifier setting. Based on the average concentration of filtered 
particle parcels, the effectiveness of the purifier with the implemented jet angle is higher than that 
of the purifier whose jet is directed upwards. However, changing the angle of the jet did not provide 
a better quality of inhaled air. Approximately 50% of the occupants had a higher exposure with an 
angled jet than with a vertical jet. 
 
Due to the direct interaction of the disinfected air stream with the occupants’ breathing area, 
the operation of the air purifier affects the air distribution in this region. More extensive numerical 
studies are desirable to investigate the phenomena occurring in such an environment in more detail. 
 
There are noticeable differences in the maximum concentrations of the parcels relative to the 
breathing region of a particular occupant (Figure 4.30). An increase in the risk of exposure of some 
occupants (2, 5, 6 and 8) was observed compared to the case with vertical jet (Figure 4.30). The 
change in angle results in a decrease in parcel concentrations in the breathing region of occupants 
1, 4 and 7 as well. 
 
==PAGE:81==
Szymon Piotr Caban 
 
Master’s Thesis 
81 
 
The most significant effect of the change in angle is observed in the air distribution in the 
breathing region of all occupants (Figure 4.31). The flow discharged from the purifier significantly 
disrupts the pattern of air movement in this area, which affects the degree of cross-infection 
reduction. 
5.3 
Infection probability prediction during transient conditions 
A comparison of the indoor environment with and without the purifier in operation provided 
valuable data confirming the effectiveness of the device in reducing the risk of cross-infection. 
Conducting transient simulations positively contributed to the reliability of the results obtained. It 
was observed how the operation of the air purifier accelerates the attainment of the set room 
temperature. The relatively short simulation time (10 minutes) provided insight into the generation 
of virus-laden particles and their propagation in the indoor environment. By tracking the generation 
of parcels, it was possible to observe the transport paths of virus-laden particles both without and 
with the air purifier running. This also made it possible to identify occupants particularly vulnerable 
to cross-infection in the purifier location tested. 
 
The discrete-phase model implemented provided reliable data on the distribution of parcels 
inhaled by each occupant. The number of parcels filtered in the air purifier and the number of those 
remaining in the room that were not inhaled or captured by the purifier were also examined. 
 
As much as 35.9 % of the parcels generated in general are drawn in by the air purifier's 
filtration system and consequently eliminated from the room. Compared to room conditions 
without an air purifier, a decrease in the number of parcels removed through the ceiling air exhausts 
is observed. 
 
The contribution of an air purifier during the build-up stage is certainly valuable in reducing 
the number of parcels once they have been generated by an infected person. However, the results 
also reveal that the disinfected flow discharged by the air purifier affects the airflow interaction in 
the room leading to increase of the exposure to exhaled infected air for some of the occupants. For 
the studied positioning of the purifier these are occupants number 6 and 7.

further opportunities to develop the model to represent airborne cross-infection mechanisms more 
faithfully in real time. Consideration should certainly be given to improving the discrete-phase 
model or creating a user-defined function that prescribes more realistic numbers of exhaled 
particles. Adequate reservation of time and computational resources is also desirable in order to 
simulate a longer period of time with the full achievement of steady-state conditions in the room.  
 
5.4 
Limitations 
In order to capture completely the importance of air purifier use and respective airborne 
transmission processes contributing to cross-infection, it would be necessary to run each of the 
simulations presented under transient conditions and meeting the build-up time.  With this comes 
requirements for large time resources and, in particular, computing power resources. Both of these 
factors contributed to the restriction of the performed simulation as well as discussion of the 
obtained results. 
  
Investigating the impact of operating an air purifier in different locations on the risk of 
exposure to airborne transmission showed the expected results. Still, the steady-state numerical 
analysis carries limitations in tracing the development of air distribution and particle propagation 
in the indoor environment. A similar relationship applies to the analysis of the discharge jet angle 
from the air purifier.  
 
==PAGE:83==
Szymon Piotr Caban

Furthermore, in order to have full confidence in the results obtained from the analysis of the 
performance of the air purifier, it would be necessary to possess experimental results, involving 
the generation of particles and the measurement of their concentration, against which the model 
could be fully verified. 
6. Conclusions 
The main point of interest in the numerical analysis presented here was to investigate the 
feasibility of an air purifier in reducing cross-infection. To this end, the focus was on developing a 
numerical model to predict the indoor environment and exposure risk in a ventilated room with and 
without an air purifier. A full verification and validation of the numerical model against physical 
testing was performed. A study of the importance of the location of the air purifier and the flow 
angle of the disinfected air discharged from the air purifier was conducted. An examination of the 
performance of the air purifier under transient conditions was carried out, starting from initial 
conditions when the concentration of virus-laden particles begins to build up. 
 
The main conclusions are the following: 
• The location of the air purifier in the room has a significant impact on the distribution 
of air in the room leading to different exposure of the occupants’ to air exhaled by an 
infected occupant. Therefore, the effectiveness of air purifier in  reducing the risk of 
airborne cross-infection will be different. At some location of the air purifier the 
infection probability for some occupants may increase; 
• The angle of the airflow discharged from the purifier has an ambiguous effect on room 
air distribution and occupants’ exposure to infected air. Present results reveal that it is 
difficult in practice to define best direction of the discharged air. Changing of the 
direction may lead to reduction in exposure for some occupants and at the same time 
increase in exposure for other occupants; 
• The infection probability can be effectively reduced using an air purifier. For 
mechanically ventilated rooms the use of air purifier, in addition to background 
mechanical ventilation, during the transient period, i.e. the start of the room occupancy 
when the concentration of viruses generated by an infected person start to build-up, 
may increase infection probability for some occupants compared to use of background 
ventilation alone.
