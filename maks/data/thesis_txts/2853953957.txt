Abstract
This thesis explores the application of Large Language Models (LLMs), specifically Ope-
nAI’s GPT-3.5-turbo, for generating daily activity sequences. The limited existing research
on using LLMs in activity-based modeling serves as a key motivator for this study. To
address this, the thesis employs and compares two distinct methods: the Few-Shot Learning
framework and the Fine-Tuning process. Each method utilizes two different temperature
settings for the LLMs—lower (0.3) and higher (0.7). The effectiveness of these methods
is thoroughly assessed on both a collective level and an individual basis, employing a
variety of visualization techniques and statistical analyses. The results reveal that the
Fine-Tuning method, combined with a higher temperature value, consistently outperforms
other strategies, yielding more accurate activity sequences. However, at lower temperature
settings, both methods demonstrate similar performance, exhibit similar performance,
with neither method producing particularly strong results. This research highlights the
potential of LLMs to significantly improve activity sequence modeling while also identifying
the limitations of current approaches under varying conditions.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Activity sequence modeling is a dynamic field that integrates insights from computational
techniques and behavioral sciences to predict and analyze the patterns of daily activities
undertaken by individuals. This interdisciplinary approach enhances understanding of
urban mobility and optimizes transportation systems. By simulating how people allocate
their time and choose locations for activities such as work, shopping, and leisure, these
models offer valuable insights into the daily rhythms of urban environments. As cities
evolve, accurately predicting and planning for these patterns becomes crucial, not just for
everyday convenience but also for addressing larger issues such as energy consumption,
traffic congestion, and public health [Panos and Margelou, 2019], [H¨orl and Balac, 2021].
Recent advancements have expanded the scope of activity sequence modeling to include
factors like bounded rationality, acknowledging the limitations in human decision-making
processes rather than assuming perfect rationality. This shift allows for more accurate
representations of real-world behaviors, making these models more relevant for applications
ranging from epidemic modeling to targeted urban planning [Chorus, 2012]. As the field
evolves, the integration of sophisticated data analysis tools, such as Large Language
Models, is beginning to capture the complex interdependencies of activities and travel
behaviors, providing a deeper understanding of the factors that drive urban mobility.
Large Language Models (LLMs) are a significant advancement in artificial intelligence,
enabling machines to process and generate human language with remarkable depth and
accuracy. Unlike traditional models designed for specific tasks, LLMs are trained on mas-
sive datasets, allowing them to capture complex patterns in language, including context,
tone, and meaning. Their ability to generalize across tasks has made them indispensable
tools for applications like content generation, language translation, summarization, and
even code creation.
What makes LLMs especially impactful is their growing application in areas beyond
language processing. They are increasingly being leveraged for complex problem-solving
tasks, such as simulating human decision-making, analyzing large-scale data patterns, and
assisting in fields like scientific research and engineering. Their flexibility enables them
to extend beyond language processing, contributing to advancements in complex data
analysis, decision-making systems, and innovation in technology-driven fields.

Chapter 1. Introduction
Despite the remarkable capabilities of LLMs, their application in fields such as activity
sequence modeling and urban mobility remains relatively unexplored. Few studies have
investigated how these models can be applied to capture the complexities of human travel
behavior, leaving significant room for research into their broader application in these fields
[Wang et al., 2024].
1.1
Aim & Objectives
The present master’s thesis, titled ”Activity sequence modelling for TU data”, constitutes
a project in DTU Management in the framework of the ”MSc Eng programme in Business
Analytics” and is equivalent to 35 ECTS out of a total of 120 ECTS required for the
programme’s completion. It examines how LLMs can be applied to enhance the effectiveness
of activity sequence modeling, with a focus on generating accurate activity sequences.
Specifically, OpenAI’s GPT-3.5-turbo will be utilized, employing two key approaches: Few-
Shot Learning, which enables the model to make accurate predictions based on a limited
number of examples, and Fine-Tuning, which involves adjusting the model’s parameters
to better align with the dataset and improve prediction accuracy.

Chapter 2
Related Work
2.1
Synthetic population and travel demand for Paris
and ˆIle-de-France based on open and publicly
available data
Synthetic travel demand is increasingly acknowledged for its applications in transport
simulation, energy analysis, and epidemiological studies. In their study, [H¨orl and Balac,
2021] established a thorough pipeline using publicly accessible data from the ˆIle-de-France
region, to construct a synthetic travel demand model, alongside the people, for the area
surrounding Paris. The data contained information about the population and the house-
hold in the area, alongside their sociodemographic features. In addition the trips and
activities data were utilized.
The authors initiated their process by crafting a representative demographic profile of
ˆIle-de-France, incorporating extensive sociodemographic characteristics. They applied a
technique similar to statistical matching to assign synthetic identities precise primary
locations for work and education, effectively linking these to real-world geographic locations
through residential and workplace data. Furthermore, secondary activities like shopping
and leisure were systematically determined using an algorithm that calculated travel times
and distances, accurately placing these activities based on a comprehensive service and
facility census.
The authors emphasized the importance of validating their model against actual behavioral
data. The results demonstrated the model’s ability to replicate common activity patterns by
following the distributions of the original dataset, such as daily commutes, while capturing
subtle gender differences in activity frequency that mirror real societal behaviors.
2.2
Latent representations in Travel Demand Models
In chapter 4 of [Arkoudi, 2024]’s PhD thesis, she uses the TU dataset to generate daily trip
sequences. For this purpose she introduces a Latent State Model (LSM) with Variational
Inference (VI) to generate trip sequences. This LSM is described as a multivariate dynamic
3
==PAGE:12==

Bayesian Networks model, that uses latent state vectors of fixed dimensionality to model
the behavior of individuals over time. In addition to this model, Arkoudi utilizes an
autoregressive Bayesian Network (BN) model, similar to the LSM but without using the
latent states (refered as AM). She also compares the previous two with an LSM with a
Maximum Likelihood Estimation model that maximizes the likelihood of observed activities
and modes across time steps.
The results of the models are compared in both an aggregated level, as well as at an
individual one. For the former assessment of the models’ performance, probabilities are
assigned to the unique sequence patterns between the true and the generated sequence
trip sets. As for the individual level, a string similarity based on Levenshtein distances is
utilized between the original and the generated activity sequence.
After conducting two levels of analysis -one on all the observed sequences and another
focusing on top 10 most frequent sequence patterns -, the results suggested that the LSM
with MLE consistently outperforms the alternative LSM with VI, showcasing superior
capability in capturing and replicating complex travel sequence behaviors. The model
excelled particularly in generating activity sequences and showed results that matched the
distribution of the originals in activity sequences and modes.
2.3
Large Language Models as Urban Residents: An
LLM Agent Framework for Personal Mobility
Generation
In the work of [Wang et al., 2024], the authors introduced a novel approach to generating
daily activity trajectories by integrating LLMs with personal mobility data. This study
aims to align these models with real-world data to generate reliable activity trajectories,
focusing on urban mobility. The model uses a two-phase process: initially identifying
activity patterns from historical data and subsequently generating activities based on
motivations.
To achieve their results, in their methodology they leveraged data from Tokyo, to simulate
activities under both typical and pandemic conditions. This dataset is analyzed under
normal conditions and during the COVID-19 pandemic to assess the model’s adaptability
to abnormal scenarios.
Results indicate that the model excels in capturing temporal aspects of mobility, such
as daily routines, though it is less effective in spatial detail simulation. Performance
evaluations through Jensen-Shannon divergence show that the model effectively adapts to
varying scenarios, suggesting its utility for scenario-based planning and real-time urban
mobility analysis.

3.1
Artificial Intelligence
Artificial Intelligence (AI) utilizes algorithms and models to perform tasks typically
requiring human cognitive ability. Currently, AI focuses mostly on Machine Learning
(ML), its most advanced form, Deep Learning (DL) and its branches [Theodosiou and
Read, 2023]. A representation of this relationship is visualized in the Figure 3.1.
Figure 3.1: The relationship between AI and its subcategories
Machine learning is a branch of AI where algorithms autonomously establish rules from
data through iterative training, without direct human instruction. This field includes
supervised learning, which uses labeled data for training algorithms, unsupervised learning,
which utilizes unlabeled data for training through data-driven methods and reinforcement
learning, which operates on a system of rewards or penalties based on performance against
set benchmarks [Theodosiou and Read, 2023].

Chapter 3. Theoretical Background
Deep learning, a complex branch of machine learning, uses artificial neural networks with
multiple layers. It is more computationally intensive than traditional machine learning
methods and presents challenges in understanding and interpreting the model due to the
complexity of its numerous layers [Theodosiou and Read, 2023].
Large language models (LLMs), a category of deep learning, have undergone rapid de-
velopment since 2018. These models, which include billions of parameters, are trained
unsupervised on extensive text data. The evolution of these models is exemplified by
systems like GPT-4, which contains more than 100 trillion parameters [Birhane et al.,
2023].
3.1.1
Data Requirements and Data Representations
LLMs are complex AI systems that are intended to interpret and produce human language
[T¨ornberg, 2023]. Through thorough training with vast datasets of natural language,
these models acquire a strong understanding of linguistic structures and the ability to
generate text. The training datasets consist of a diverse range of materials, including web
articles, dialogue excerpts, and literary texts. Specialized data from various fields, such as
programming or scientific research, is integrated to enhance the functionality of the models
and make them more applicable in different professional domains. Using a wide range of
textual sources expands, the model’s applicative scope and requires careful selection of
high-quality data to enhance both performance and security during preprocessing [Liu
et al., 2024], [Smith et al., 2022].
Before processing, data needs to be converted into a numerical format. Segmenting text into
discrete units, called tokens, involves identifying words or punctuation marks. These tokens
are then transformed into high-dimensional vectors that capture the semantic meaning
of each token and its contextual relationships within the dataset. In most cases, each to-
ken represents approximately two-thirds of a word [T¨ornberg, 2023], [Mikolov et al., 2013a].

highlighting the model-specific division of words compared to conventional word
separation

At first, these vector representations are generated randomly. During the training process,
these vectors are fine-tuned to better capture the meanings of tokens and their relationships,
similar to how weights are adjusted in a neural network. This adjustment process, referred
to as ”embedding,” transforms non-vectorized data such as tokens into a vector space that
enables the model to understand, making it easier for the model to independently learn
linguistic relationships [Mikolov et al., 2013b].
Once trained, LLMs have the ability to directly process human language. Therefore, there
is no need for the initial preprocessing steps like tokenization. The models are capable of
handling unstructured and diverse linguistic data, including text in multiple languages
[T¨ornberg, 2023].
3.1.2
Operational Parameters of LLMs
LLMs like GPT rely on various operational parameters. One of them is the Temperature
parameter, which is critical for influencing the model’s outputs. This parameter modifies
the probability distribution for predictions of the next token through a method called
temperature sampling. This process uses the softmax function, which applies a non-
linear transformation to the network’s output logits, essentially transforming them into a
probability distribution. The softmax function is defined mathematically as:
softmax(zi) =

where zi are the logits, and T is the temperature.
The temperature parameter adjusts this distribution by shifting the probability mass to
alter the distribution’s flatness based on the set temperature. For example, a temperature
above 1 decreases the likelihood of more probable outcomes while boosting less likely ones,
thereby increasing the variability of the generated text. In contrast, a temperature below
1 increases the probability of more likely outcomes, reducing randomness and enhancing
the predictability of the output, as can be seen in Figure 3.3. Setting the temperature
to 0 results in greedy sampling, where the model consistently chooses the token with the
highest probability, ensuring maximum predictability. The temperature typically varies
between 0 and 2, with higher temperatures raising both entropy and perplexity, thus
introducing more randomness and uncertainty into the generation process [Peeperkorn
et al., 2024].

Apart from the temperature parameter, some others worth mentioning, would be the
”frequency penalty”, ”logit bias” and ”max tokens”. The former one guides the model to

from producing particular, unwelcome tokens or words, while the later one sets an upper
limit in the tokens that the model can generate [OpenAI Create].
3.1.3
Architecture
Earlier LLMs used recurrent neural network (RNN) models that processed data sequen-
tially, limiting their efficiency [Irie et al., 2018]. In contrast, modern cutting-edge LLMs
are constructed on the Transformer architecture, which processes data in parallel, thereby
enhancing both performance and accuracy significantly [Schoenegger et al., 2024], [Liu
et al., 2024].
The foundational paper by [Vaswani et al., 2017]. introduced this architecture, which
includes mechanisms like attention, self-attention, and positional encoding to process data
more effectively. The attention mechanism is responsible for identifying and extracting es-
sential information from massive datasets. It prioritizes relevant details while disregarding

to assign weights to different words in a sentence based on their relevance to the word
currently being predicted, thus determining which words should influence the next word
the most. This process involves computing a weighted sum of all words’ values in a
sentence, where the weights are determined using the formula:

The Transformer’s encoder is structured into multiple identical layers, each equipped with a
multi-head attention mechanism and a feed-forward neural network. Multi-head attention

3.2
Prompt engineering
Before diving into the concepts of prompt engineering, the explanation of ’prompts’ is
provided to facilitate understanding.
Prompt engineering involves crafting instructions for LLMs to help them analyze text,
similar to providing guidance to an assistant, as shown in Figure 3.5. Attention to the
design and detail of these prompts is crucial as they have a significant impact on the
model’s behavior and the accuracy of its responses.

Regarded as a qualitative approach, it involves the careful development of instructions that
precisely capture elements of social reality. When designing a prompt, there are various
factors to keep in mind. These include providing a clear task description, specifying the
desired output, finding the right balance between length and specificity, incorporating
relevant context, using existing research, ensuring the results can be easily analyzed, and
conducting iterative testing [T¨ornberg, 2023].
Within the architecture of the GPT model, prompts are divided into three distinct roles:
system, user, and assistant. The optional system role defines the assistant’s behavior
parameters. User messages are either requests or comments that elicit responses from the
assistant, and assistant messages contain either the model’s past responses or examples
created by developers to demonstrate desired behaviors. This configuration is designed to
mimic realistic interactions the model will encounter in production, thereby enhancing its
practical effectiveness [Chat Completions API].
3.2.1
Fine-tuning
After their initial training on diverse and extensive token datasets from various resources,
LLMs often require task-specific adaptation in order to achieve optimal performance for
specific applications [Raj et al., 2024]. In recent times, fine-tuning (FT) has become the
most common technique for this purpose. This approach requires additional training on
specific datasets, allowing the model to fine-tune its pre-trained parameters and enhance
its accuracy when dealing with queries related to a particular domain [Raj et al., 2024].
As shown in Figure 3.6, during the fine-tuning process, a large number of new data points
are integrated into the prompt contexts. These prompts usually consist of an instructional
prompt and a corresponding response, which may also include extra contextual information
[Jeong, 2024].
11
==PAGE:20==
Chapter 3. Theoretical Background
Figure 3.6: Graphical representation of Fine-Tuning process
By integrating these targeted updates, LLMs can significantly outperform existing baselines
[Shin et al., 2023]. In addition, fine-tuning provides a more efficient approach to re-training
models, allowing for the conservation of computational resources and time [Raj et al., 2024].
Nevertheless, fine-tuning often requires an extensive dataset of examples, each customized
for particular tasks, resulting in significant limitations. Assembling these datasets can
be quite challenging and often requires a significant investment, which can restrict their
application in various linguistic tasks. Moreover, as models become more complex and train
on more targeted datasets, they may begin to depend on weak or unreliable correlations
within the data. This complicates the training process, where models are first broadly
pre-trained on large, diverse datasets and then fine-tuned on narrow, specific tasks. The
risk is that the fine-tuning process might cause the model to focus too much on patterns
that don’t generalize well outside the narrow dataset.
In addition, NLP technologies currently face challenges in adapting to new language tasks
with minimal examples or straightforward instructions, unlike humans who can easily ad-
just. This emphasizes a significant drawback and indicates the need for NLP systems that
can effectively manage various tasks and seamlessly shift between them [Brown et al., 2020].
Facing these fine-tuning challenges, the increase in transformer model parameters—from
millions to billions—has significantly improved their ability to learn from minimal examples.
This progress supports the use of techniques such as Zero-Shot Learning (ZSL) and Few-
Shot Learning (FSL), allowing models to handle new tasks without much specific training
[Brown et al., 2020].
3.2.2
Few-Shot Learning
Few-shot Learning approach (FSL) is a technique where the model learns to execute
tasks with only a minimal set of examples [Logan et al., 2021]. This specialized subfield
of machine learning is inspired by the human brain’s proficiency in quickly identifying
patterns from scarce data [Wang et al., 2020].
12
==PAGE:21==
Chapter 3. Theoretical Background
FSL operates by providing the model with a few examples, each a prompt consisting of a
’context-completion’ pair. After being trained on these prompts, the model is presented
with a new context and must use its acquired understanding to predict the appropriate
completion. Crucially, this learning happens without any further modifications to the
model’s weights [Brown et al., 2020].
This approach substantially reduces the reliance on large, task-specific datasets and
minimizes the likelihood of the model becoming overly specialized to a narrow set of
data patterns. However, the use of only a few examples to represent the entire data
distribution can introduce significant biases. This not only affects the model’s performance
on unfamiliar tasks but may also limit its ability to transfer learned knowledge to new,
varied scenarios [Song et al., 2023].
Figure 3.7: An example displaying the Difference between Few-shot learning technique
and Fine-Tuning process (Retrieved from: [Brown et al., 2020])
3.2.3
Zero-Shot Learning
Zero-shot learning is viewed as an even more challenging extension of Few-Shot Learning
(FSL), where the model is tasked with recognizing new concepts without any prior examples.
Instead, it utilizes natural language instructions that describe the task [Romera-Paredes
and Torr, 2015]. This method maximizes convenience and potential robustness while
helping to avoid reliance on potentially misleading correlations [Brown et al., 2020]. The
effectiveness of ZSL heavily depends on the choice of instruction prompts; well-crafted
prompts can significantly enhance accuracy [Ye et al., 2022].

After converting the numerical values to qualitative data, next step would be the prepro-
cessing of the dataset, before running the models. In order to create the training and
test data, and thus formulate the activity sequences of the TU database, a critical action
was the mapping, from the original activity categories to the new merged ones. This was
achieved through the use of Regular Expressions (RegEx).
The next action for the acquisition of the respondents’ activity sequence, was the identifi-
cation of all the unique interviews. The selected dataset had 93,894 unique interviews,
which later was shuffled and was split in a train and a test set. The train set was assigned
7,000 of these unique interviews, leaving the rest for the test set. The main reason for
this split, is the limitation of the maximum context length to 16,385 tokens. Out of these,
4,096 can be the maximum number of output tokens.
Following this, an issue that arose and required further action, was the filtering of the
incomplete interviews. By filtering the selection of the data, some trips might had been
remained unselected, because of the missing values. Thus, taking a preventive measure
would ensure that only complete sessions would be included. Therefore, a function was
designed which counted the total number of rows for each interview id and compared it
with the number of rows that had the same interview id of the trips of the day dataset. If
these two numbers mismatched, the whole interview was removed from the final dataset.

4.3.1.1
Few-Shot Learning
During the FSL approach, the first 100 examples of the train set were used, to provide
examples to the model. This was achieved through the user-assistant prompt conversation.
Essentially, these two roles of the GPT models, are used to train the model throughout
their communication. During the training, the user role would provide the socio-economic
data of each respondent to the GPT and then the assistant would respond with their
activity sequence. This correspondence remained for all the instances that were utilized
from the training set. Following the last example, the subsequent prompt will require the
user to enter the socio-economic data of the first respondent in the test set. Using the
completions method of the GPT, the model would try to predict the sequence of activities
that it assumed the respondent had.
The System prompt once again delivered instructions to the model regarding its intended
actions, behavior, and the basis for its predictions. It explained to the model what each
attribute represents, what the acceptable answers should be, as well as, what each category
of the acceptable answers represents. Through the system prompt, the acceptable format
of each prediction was also answered. As there were a few cases where the model would
provide an empty value, or would declare that it was not able to provide an answer based
on the examples, an additional prophylactic measure was deployed. The system prompt
would declare to the model that responses without a complete plausible activity sequence
are not acceptable. As a result, this improved the predictions, since after this enhancement,
the model would always try to provide a valid answer. a representation of the system

Possible age-related issues
After removing the irrational journeys, the artificial predictions were further evaluated

Denmark, the minimum legal working age is 13. Consequently, all sequences involving

that included ”work” as an activity for a person under 12. This sequence, created at a

to the higher temperature setting, which generally prompts the model to generate more
creative and less conventional outputs. Additionally, it suggests that the model might not
have adequately accounted for age-specific restrictions in its generation process.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
In this project, the primary objective was to investigate the application of Large Language
Models (LLMs), specifically OpenAI’s GPT, for automatically generating new activity
sequences using the TU dataset. This endeavor involved two types of data: the activities
recorded by each respondent on the interview day and their socioeconomic data. This
setup enabled the model to identify patterns across different interviewee profiles. The
procedure started with the transformation of numerical values into qualitative data, which
was then preprocessed and categorized by the session of recording. To streamline the
complexity, initial activities were classified into seven broad categories: home, school, work,
leisure, personal, shopping, and escort.
Subsequently, two techniques were employed to generate activity sequences. The first one,
FSL, provided the model with numerous prompt examples related to socioeconomic data
and previous activity sequences, challenging the model to predict the activities of an unseen
respondent. The second technique, Fine-Tuning, involved an extra tailored training based
on TU’s dataset to refine the model’s parameters, enhancing its precision for related queries.
Each method was tested with two different temperature settings to identify which one
provided the most accurate results. The newly created data were carefully evaluated for
their plausibility and analyzed both collectively, by comparing them against the original
data, and individually, using Levenshtein distance and Normalized Edit Similarity as
evaluation tools.”
All evaluation metrics indicated that the Fine-Tuned model, with the temperature set at
0.7, surpassed all other methods. This superiority was evidenced by the closer alignment
of its generated sequence distribution with the actual data. Moreover, it recorded the
lowest mean Levenshtein distance and the highest NES score, demonstrating a greater
similarity to the actual sequences than other methods. Additionally, the metrics revealed
that both methods at lower temperature settings, such as 0.3, yielded comparable results,
suggesting that choosing between these two approaches at this temperature level would
likely make no difference.

Chapter 7
Future Perspectives
The evaluation of the results has highlighted several areas for improvement and potential
weaknesses for future research.
To begin with, the model generated more accurate results during higher temperature
settings compared to the lower ones. However, lack of financial resources restrained
exploring additional temperature settings, which might have yielded even more precise
activity chains. Therefore, further experimentation with higher values, close to 0.8 or even
up to 0.9, may deliver better results as the model would operate more creatively. This
experimentation, alongside instructing the model to strategically generate several different
predictions for each scenario, may enable the model to produce a variety of creative activity
chains. This approach increases the likelihood of accurately predicting some of the more
complex current activity chains, thereby enhancing the results on an individual level.
Furthermore, while 400 activity guesses might appear substantial, they constitute a rela-
tively small fraction of the initial dataset. Consequently, a crucial recommendation for
future research is the generation of a larger dataset. Expanding the dataset would help
eliminate any coincidental distribution similarities or anomalies and could align the new
data more closely with the distribution of the original dataset. This expansion is likely to
enhance the accuracy of the FSL approach, which did not perform as effectively as the
Fine-Tuning approach in this study.
Lastly, incorporating additional data columns could significantly improve the precision
of activity sequence generation. In this project, only socioeconomic data were utilized
to analyze potential trends in the trips and thus to predict the activity sequences of the
respondents. However, following the approach of Ioanna Arkoudi in her research [Arkoudi,
2024], employing other variables such as the ”Weekday of the trip diary” or the ”Type of
day” could greatly benefit the model. This additional information could enable the model
to uncover deeper insights within the input data and consequently generate more accurate
activity chains.
