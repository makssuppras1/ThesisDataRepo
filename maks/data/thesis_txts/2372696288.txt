Abstract
Maritime shipping is important as 90% of world’s trade happens through sea. Con-
sidering the recent economic uncertainties in the world of shipping, it becomes highly
important that the eﬃciency of maritime transportation is increased. Large com-
mercial ships such as container vessels and bulk carriers are run by two stroke diesel
engines. Optimization of these large two stroke engines is highly desired considering
the economy and environment as these engines are of high fuel consumption and
emissions.
The aim of this master’s thesis is to conduct numerical simulation of the working
principles at full and low load conditions of a two stroke diesel engine (4T50MX en-
gine). A three dimensional CFD model of the engine is constructed to represent the
full scale engine model and the major in-cylinder parameters during the functioning
of the engine are studied. The motion of the piston is simulated using a morphing
operation. A remeshing strategy is used to avoid over compression of cells due to
the piston motion. The burnt gas from the engine combustion is ﬂushed through an
exhaust valve, where the valve motion is simulated by using an overset mesh. The
exhaust valve motion data from the real engine valve is used for this purpose.
A 3D full scale engine model developed for the thesis which consist of 30 ports
with a port angle of 20◦allowed to visualize the mass ﬂow into the engine cylinder
volume from the scavenge box and also visualization of the velocity pattern inside
the engine. The scavenge port angle of 20◦allowed to create a swirling ﬂow inside
the engine. Reynold’s Averaged Navier Stokes (RANS) is used to solve the ﬂow as
the engine ﬂow is in the turbulent regime. The working ﬂuid of air is simulated
as a compressible real gas. An Euler implicit unsteady method is used to ﬁnd the
solution at a new time step. A study about the combustion inside the engine is
also carried out in this thesis. An Eddy Break Up (EBU) model is set up to solve
the combustion. The fuel used is n-dodecane (C12H26). A Lagrangian multi phase
approach is used for the fuel injection and mixing inside the combustion chamber. A
comparison is made between using air and multi component formulation of air as the
working ﬂuid. This is necessary as the combustion process needed the components
of air to specify the combustion stoichiometric reactions.
The CFD model results are validated against the data from the test engine (4T50MX)
extracted from the report of [Sigurdsson, 2011].

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Marine diesel engines propel most of the modern ships which carry out 90% of the
world’s trade. Considering the stricter emission laws and the fact that shipping
at sea is responsible for 2-4% of the world’s annual fuel consumption, it is of high
importance to carry out research in the ﬁeld of large low speed two stroke diesel
engines used by majority of the ships at sea.
Due to the increase in oil prices and stricter emission regulations on air pollutants
in gaseous and particulate matter(soot) considering environmental protection, the
eﬃciency of the engines is a topic of high research and commercial interest.
The two major processes that take place inside the engine that can aﬀect the eﬃ-
ciency are scavenging and combustion. Scavenging process is one of the main factors
that decide the eﬃciency of an engine. Improvements in scavenging increases the
overall engine eﬃciency and ensures a smooth running of the engine. Scavenging
process is responsible for setting the conditions such as the ratio of fresh air and
burnt gas inside the chamber for combustion. An eﬃcient combustion is required for
the optimal use of fuel and also reduce the emission of pollutants from the engine.
Experimental investigation on marine diesel engines are expensive due to the large
size of engines and the maintenance cost that comes with it. The optical diagnostics
that can be done on an engine is also expensive considering high speed cameras and
the set up required.
The developments in the ﬁeld of Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) along with
the high performance computing available makes numerical modelling a viable and
eﬀective method to conduct research on engines. The numerical models developed
for the engine still needs to be validated against the experimental data for the
correctness of the used methods.

1.4
Working principle of a two stroke diesel engine
Figure 1.1: A depiction of the complete two-stroke engine cycle
[EKS, 2005]
In Figure 1.1a, the piston is at the bottom dead centre (BDC) and the inlet ports
(scavenging ports) and the exhaust valve are fully open. Pressurized air enters the
cylinder volume from the scavenge box through the inlet ports and displaces the
burnt gas through the exhaust valve. This is called the scavenging process.
In Figure 1.1b, the scavenge ports are closed as the piston have covered them and
the exhaust valve is also closed. The cylinder now consist of a mixture of fresh air
from the scavenge ports and the burnt gas from the previous cycle which was not
scavenged by the incoming fresh air.
In Figure 1.1c, the piston is at the top dead centre (TDC). When the piston starts
moving downwards, fuel is injected into the cylinder which is at a high pressure
due to being compressed by the piston. The fuel is evaporated when it enters the
cylinder and reaches a temperature where the fuel self ignites and combustion takes
place. The combustion leads to expansion of the fuel-air mixture and a high pressure
which pushes the piston downwards. This is called the power stroke.
==PAGE:21==
4
1.5. SCAVENGING PROCESS IN TWO STROKE ENGINES
In Figure 1.1d, the piston moves downwards and the cylinder volume expands. This
stroke is called the working stroke as the piston movement is extracted as mechanical
work in the form of a rotating the crank shaft. The exhaust valve opens before
the scavenge ports are uncovered.
This helps to ﬂush out combustion products
and reduce the pressure inside the cylinder so that when the scavenge ports are
uncovered, fresh air can enter the cylinder and scavenge majority of the remaining
combustion products.
1.5
Scavenging process in two stroke engines
Scavenging is the process by which the burnt gas from combustion process is dis-
placed by fresh air that enters the cylinder volume from the scavenge ports and then
the next cycle of compression-combustion-expansion takes place. The inlet pressure
can be increased to facilitate the incoming of the fresh air into the cylinder by using
either a blower or a turbocharger [Andersen, 2015]. When the piston uncovers the
scavenge ports,the pressure diﬀerence between the intake manifold and the cylinder
would push fresh air into the cylinder volume which displaces the burnt gas from
the cylinder volume.

1.5. SCAVENGING PROCESS IN TWO STROKE ENGINES
1.5.1
Types of scavenging in two stroke engines
Figure 1.2: Most common scavenging methods in engines (a) cross scavenging (b)
loop scavenging (c) uniﬂow scavenging [Andersen, 2015]
A number of scavenging methods are used in low speed two stroke engines.
In
all scavenging methods the fresh air enters the cylinder volume as the scavenge
port is uncovered during the downward movement of the piston and continues until
the scavenge ports are closed by the upward moving piston. The ﬂow path of the
scavenge air is decided by the engine port shape, piston design and the exhaust
arrangements. The most common methods as shown in Figure 1.2 are the cross
ﬂow, the loop ﬂow and the uniﬂow scavenging.
Figure 1.2a shows cross ﬂow scavenging. In this conﬁguration the scavenge ports
and the exhaust ports are milled with the cylinder liner but on opposite sides. When
the piston is uncovered fresh charge comes in through one side and the burnt gas is
displaced through the opposite side. To avoid the short circuiting (passage of fresh
air without being retained in the cylinder) of the fresh air, deﬂectors are mounted
in the piston crown.
Figure 1.2b shows loop ﬂow scavenging which is similar to cross ﬂow scavenging
except that the scavenge ports and the exhaust ports are milled to the same side of
the cylinder liner. This conﬁguration helps to avoid the short circuiting as the fresh
air is redirected inside the cylinder. Also this conﬁguration do not need a deﬂector
==PAGE:23==
6

on the piston crown to redirect the ﬂow and leads to a more compact combustion
chamber.
Figures 1.2c and 1.3 shows uniﬂow scavenging method.
The uniﬂow scavenging
consist of the scavenge ports milled to the cylinder liner and the exhaust valves
located on the cylinder cover. Exhaust valve increases the complexity of the engine as
they needed to be controlled for exhaust valve opening and closing timings. Usually
the exhaust valves are electronically controlled for their movements and the timings
can be optimized for optimal scavenging and optimal engine performance.
1.5.2
Uniﬂow scavenging in two stroke engines
Figure 1.3: Cross sectional view of a large two stroke uniﬂow scavenged engine
[Sigurdsson, 2011]
The uniqueness of the uniﬂow scavenging is the presence of exhaust valve which
can be controlled for optimizing the engine performance. The exhaust gas drives
the turbocharger which ensures that air is delivered to the scavenge receiver at a
high pressure. From scavenge receiver, the air enters the scavenge box and then to

the scavenge ports. Each cylinder has its own scavenge box and ports. Air enters
the cylinder through the scavenge ports which are at an angle with respect to the
cylinder to create a swirling ﬂow inside the cylinder. Once the air is compressed in
the stroke towards TDC from BDC and combustion takes place and pressure rises
inside the cylinder volume. The increased pressure pushes the piston downward
from the TDC. The exhaust valve opens during the downward motion of the piston
so that the combustion products are ﬂushed out of the cylinder through the exhaust
duct into the exhaust receiver. The pressure of the combustion products is still high
enough to drive the turbocharger which ensures a high mass ﬂow of fresh charge
into the cylinder.
The scavenging process is of high importance in an engine cycle as it dictates the
ratio of fresh air and burnt gas inside the engine which has a direct inﬂuence on the
engine performance, speciﬁc fuel consumption and the emission of pollutants.
1.6
Combustion in CI engines
In two stroke compression ignition engines, combustion takes place due to the high
temperature and pressure created inside the chamber during the compression stroke.
Fuel is injected into the high pressure chamber towards the end of the compression
stroke at high velocity through fuel injectors. The fuel atomizes at the nozzle tip
and penetrates into the combustion chamber where it vaporizes and mixes with the
combustion chamber air. When the temperature inside the combustion chamber is
above the fuel’s self ignition point, the spontaneous ignition of the fuel air mixture
starts marking the beginning of combustion. The diﬀerent phases of combustion are
as shown in Figure 1.4 and explained in section 1.6.1.
1.6.1
Combustion phases
The combustion happens in diﬀerent phases inside the chamber starting from the
ignition of the fuel air mixture by the high pressure and temperature environment
created inside the chamber during the compression stroke.
The diﬀerent phases
according to [Heywood, 1988] are:
• The ignition delay period(a-b), the time between the start of injection (SOI)
and beginning of combustion. The fuel atomizes, vaporizes and mixes partially
with air during this period.
• The premixed combustion phase (b-c), where the fuel that is vaporized and
mixed with air reaches the self ignition point and starts burning. The ﬂame
from the burning reaches the surrounding vaporized fuel air mixture which has
also reached the ﬂammability. This continuous spread of the ﬂame results in
a high heat release rate as can be see in Figure 1.4.
==PAGE:25==
8

• The mixing controlled combustion phase(c-d) takes place after the premixed
fuel air mixture is completely burnt. In this phase the rate of combustion is
determined by the rate at which the fuel vapour and air can mix.
• The late combustion phase(d-e), which happens into the expansion stroke due
to the presence of unburnt fuel in the form of fuel rich products and soot.
The temperature and pressure are decreasing during this phase and hence the
chemical kinetics of this combustion is slow.
In the T450MX engine the fuel injection starts after the top dead centre (TDC).

1.7
Two stroke uniﬂow scavenged engine cycle
The overall cycle of a two stroke uniﬂow scavenged engine(Figure 1.5) can be divided
into two phases.
1. Closed volume phase: compression, combustion and expansion
2. Open volume phase: blow down, scavenging and push out
Closed volume phase is between the exhaust valve closing(EVC) and exhaust valve
opening(EVO) of the next cycle. During this phase there is no mass transfer to or
==PAGE:26==
9
1.8. THE 4T50MX TEST ENGINE
from the cylinder. Compression, combustion and expansion takes place during this
phase.
The open volume phase is between the exhaust valve opening(EVO) and exhaust
valve closing(EVC) of the next cycle. During this phase, blow down takes place
which is the pushing of the burnt gas through the exhaust valves due to the high
pressure diﬀerence between the cylinder volume and the exhaust manifold. The com-
bustion products are further scavenged through the exhaust valves by the incoming
fresh charge from the scavenge ports after the inlet port opens(IPO) till inlet port
closes (IPC). The blow down process is necessary to adjust the cylinder pressure
with the scavenge box pressure to facilitate scavenging. The push out process is an
extension of the scavenging process after the scavenge ports are closed(IPC) until
when the exhaust valves are closed(EVC).

2.3
Turbulence Modelling
From the work of [Sigurdsson, 2011], it is seen that the ﬂow inside the engine is
highly turbulent(Re = 2.5 ∗106). The Navier Stokes equations given by equations
2.5 and 2.6, becomes computationally expensive as the grid size has to be smaller
than the smallest scale of eddies (Kolmogorov microscale η) where the kinetic energy
dissipation occurs and the computational domain is of the order of characteristic
length scale L of the object. Since the Kolmogorov microscale and the characteristic
length are of diﬀerent order of magnitude [Kundu et al., 2012] the computational
domain has to be very ﬁne and thus computationally expensive to solve the Navier
Stokes equations. Direct Numerical Simulation(DNS) method solves the whole range
of spatial and temporal scales of turbulence which is computational demanding.
Methods have been developed which are computationally less expensive and provide
reasonably accurate results for turbulent ﬂow. Reynolds Averaged Navier Stokes
(RANS) equations and Large Eddy Simulations (LES) are two of the used modelling
approaches for turbulence. In this thesis RANS is chosen to model the turbulent
ﬂow inside the engine.
In RANS modelling, the turbulent velocity is decomposed into a mean component
(ui) and a ﬂuctuation component (ui′) known as Reynolds decomposition as given
in equation 2.17.

2.4
Eulerian-Lagrangian multiphase modelling for
the dispersed phase
The fuel used for combustion in this thesis is n-dodecane C12H26, a good surrogate
for diesel. The fuel particles are modelled using a Lagrangian approach in this thesis.
Air is modelled as a multi-component gas consisting of O2 and N2 and an Eulerian
approach is used where the Navier Stokes equations of mass, momentum and en-
ergy are solved. Air is therefore the continuous phase in the Eulerian-Lagrangian
modelling used in this study. The fuel, C12H26 is then released as individual liquid
particles into the continuous phase of air according to the injection timings.
The governing equations for the dispersed phase are based on Lagrangian formula-
tion which is applied to each individual elements in the dispersed phase. Both the
continuous and dispersed phases inﬂuences each other through inter-phase mass,
momentum and heat transfer eﬀects. Since the dispersed phase is volatile and reac-
tive (the fuel- C12H26), and is introduced into the combustion chamber containing
the continuous phase just after the top dead center(TDC), the pressure and temper-
ature in the combustion chamber aids the evaporation and subsequent combustion
of the fuel which results in inter-phase mass, momentum and energy transfer.
Since the dispersed phase is introduced into a turbulent continuous phase, a turbu-
lent dispersion is imposed on the dispersed particles using random walk technique
[CD-adapco, 2016]. This along with a ﬂuctuating turbulent velocity ﬁeld results in
a random path to the dispersed particles. The size of the particles can decrease
due to breaking up of the particle due to aerodynamic force or increase through
collision between particles resulting in coalescence. The Lagrangian formulation for
individual particles is explained in section 2.4.1.

2.5
Spray model
In 4T50MX engine, the diesel is sprayed into the combustion chamber just after
TDC. In the CFD model developed in this thesis, the surrogate fuel C12H26 is mod-
elled as droplets and needs to be injected into the combustion chamber. The droplets
then undergo breakup and mix with the air due to the swirl inside the engine and
undergo combustion. The spray model and breakup model used are blob model and
KHRT model respectively [CD-adapco, 2016].
2.5.1
Primary Breakup: Blob model
The primary breakup model determines the initial conditions for the subsequent mix-
ture formation inside the combustion chamber. Blob method is used as the primary
breakup model. A blob model is simple in the sense it assumes that the atomization
and primary break up are indistinguishable. Instead of a break up model, the blob
model injects droplets with the same diameter as that of the nozzle. The spherical
droplets that are injected are subject to instabilities created by aerodynamic forces
which stimulates a secondary breakup of the droplets.

3.1
Boundary Conditions
Boundary conditions are an important aspect of a CFD model. The conditions at
the boundary determine the direction of the ﬂow. In this thesis, Dirichlet boundary
conditions are used at both the inlet and outlet of the engine, i.e. the pressure value
is mentioned at both the inlet and outlet. The boundary conditions for the full load
and the 25% part load are diﬀerent from each other, as explained in section 3.1.2.
3.1.1
Wall boundaries
The wall boundaries represent the physical boundary of the computational domain.
The thermal speciﬁcation on all the walls are set as adiabatic, i.e there is no heat ﬂux
across wall boundaries. Also a no slip condition prescribed on the wall boundaries
means that the ﬂuid velocity is zero relative to the wall on the cell faces at the wall.
The wall boundaries are set as impermeable meaning there is no mass ﬂux across
the wall.
3.1.2
Pressure boundaries
The ﬂow inside the engine is driven by the pressure diﬀerence between the scavenge
box and the exhaust manifold.The inﬂow and outﬂow conditions to the engine are
therefore described using pressure boundaries.

replaced by a multi component gas formulation as explained in chapter 6, the mass
fraction of O2 was set as 0.233 and that of N2 was set to 0.767 at both the inlet and
outlet to the engine. This mass fraction replicates the composition of air.

Overset Boundaries
Since the exhaust valve motion takes place inside the cylinder of the engine, the
movement involves overlapping meshes. The exhaust valve mesh and the cylinder
mesh overlap each other. An overset mesh was implemented in-order to simulate
the exhaust valve motion. In overset meshing, there involves two types of meshes,
the overset mesh and the background mesh which overlaps. In this case the exhaust
valve mesh is the overset mesh and the cylinder mesh is the background mesh.
An overset mesh is created using an overset mesh boundary ,which is the common
boundary of the overset mesh and the background mesh.

Regions and Interfaces
A multi region strategy is used in the CFD model because of the movements (piston

domain was divided into four regions as shown in ﬁgure 3.2 with four diﬀerent colours

• Region 4: The exhaust valve region

the same physics continuum is used for all the regions,an internal interface is used
between the regions. For the overset mesh described in section 3.1.3, an overset zero
gap interface is created since the overset mesh and the background mesh make close
contact during the simulation.

Mesh Generation
The mesh generation for IC engines has to take into consideration the movements

cells. The exhaust valve motion happens inside the computational domain of the
cylinder and the transonic diﬀuser.

in section 3.2 is required to incorporate the piston motion and the exhaust valve
motion. The remeshing of cylinder volume is described in section 4.2.

4.2
Re-meshing of the cylinder volume
As the piston motion is simulated by compressing and expanding the cylinder volume

conﬁguration which overlaps with the cylinder volume mesh. In the CFD model,
the exhaust valve solid portion is subtracted from the cylinder volume and then the
movement of the exhaust valve is simulated by stitching the subtracted portion to
the cylinder volume mesh using an overset mesh boundary.

4.4. TIME STEP SIZE
4.4
Time step size
The time step size is determined based on the Courant number which is deﬁned by
Courant-Friedrichs-Lewy(CFL) condition as
Cr = (u + c)∆t
∆x
(4.6)
where u is the local cell velocity, c is the speed of sound, ∆t is the time step size and
∆x is the cell size in the computational domain. A physical interpretation of the
Courant number is the fraction of cell length a ﬂuid particle will propagate during
one time step.
Courant number also gives an interpretation of the stability of the solution for the
time step size that is used in an iteration. Since an implicit scheme is used for time
stepping in this study a larger time step size can be used, which would keep the
solution stable [Bingham et al., 2015].
Since a diﬀerent number of cell sizes are used for diﬀerent regions of the engine CFD
model and also combustion inside the engine requires a smaller time step size than
other engine processes like scavenging, the time step size is based on two criterion:
• The maximum value of Courant number in all the regions involved in the
model.
• The Crank Angle Degree(CAD) - the time step size is determined based on
the stage of the engine process.
The combustion and exhaust valve opening (EVO) requires smaller time step size
compared to the scavenging, port opening and closing, and the exhaust valve closing
processes. This is due to high pressure and velocities that occur during combustion
whereas a smaller time step size is required during the exhaust valve opening due
to the high pressure diﬀerence between the exhaust duct and the cylinder volume
(≈13 bar)

Reasons for deviation of motoring results between air
and multi component gas
The diﬀerence in the pressure between air and when components of air is used in
the simulation are because of following reasons:
• The temperature inside the engine for multi component gas is considerably
lower than the temperature when air is used as seen in ﬁgure 6.2. This is
due to the speciﬁc heat capacity of the multi component mixture being higher
than air. The multi component mixture considers the eﬀect of temperature
on the speciﬁc heat capacity. Speciﬁc heat capacity increases with an increase
in temperature. As the cylinder volume is compressed, the molecules inside
the cylinder are at a higher temperature and the speciﬁc heat capacity of the
molecules increases resulting in a lower rise of temperature. A study about
the eﬀects of speciﬁc heat capacity on the in-cylinder parameters in given in
chapter 7.
• The density of the ﬂuid inside the engine when using the multi component gas
is lower than that when air is used. Also the molecular weight of the multi
component gas is lower than that of air. This results in a lower amount of mass
trapped inside the engine after EVC and results in a lower pressure inside the
engine.
The two reasons mentioned above contribute to a lower pressure value inside the
engine compared to the results when air is used as the working ﬂuid. An inference

6.6.1
Reasons for the diﬀerence in combustion pressure be-
tween CFD model and experimental value
In the experimental set up, a pressure rise of 19.5 bar is achieved because of the com-
bustion inside the engine where as the pressure rises by 13.14 bar when combustion is
simulated in the CFD model. The diﬀerence in the pressure rise during combustion
between the CFD model and the experiment could be due to the following reasons.
• The initial conditions for combustion: The pressure and temperature at TDC
==PAGE:89==
72
6.6. PRESSURE INSIDE THE ENGINE FOR A FULL CYCLE
forms the initial conditions for the combustion process inside the engine. The
peak pressure achieved during combustion inside an Adiabatic Isochoric Com-
plete Combustion(AICC) setup is directly proportional to the initial pressure
as per the studies conducted by [Tieszen, 1993]. The combustion inside the
4T50MX engine is adiabatic and complete combustion. The combustion is
almost isochoric during the combustion as can be seen from the piston posi-
tion in ﬁgure 1.7. The lower pressure of 135 bar at TDC in the CFD model
compared to 152.3 bar in the experimental setup is therefore one of the most
signiﬁcant reasons for the deviation in the pressure rise during combustion in
the CFD model from the experimental value.
• The fuel used to simulate combustion in the CFD model is n-dodecane(C12H26),
which is considered a good surrogate for diesel. When using n-dodecane the
chemical reaction is a simple 5 species reaction explained in section 6.1. Al-
though n-dodecane resembles diesel in terms of ignition delay [Pitz, 2009], the
complex chemical reactions of diesel is not captured when n-dodecane is used.
The combustion reaction used is thus over simpliﬁed and has an eﬀect on the
heat release rate and the pressure rise during the combustion process.
• The EBU combustion model: The combustion rate inside the engine is deter-
mined by the ﬁnite rate kinetics during the premixed combustion phase and
by the turbulent mixing time scale during the mixing controlled combustion
phase. This is not taken into consideration by the EBU model and hence the
rate of reaction is not replicated during the premixed combustion phase. EBU
model assumes the reaction rate is solely determined by the turbulent mix-
ing time scale [CD-adapco, 2016], which is valid only in the mixing controlled
combustion phase. The method of determining the reaction rate by the EBU
model thus has an eﬀect on the combustion. As the reaction rates are not
correctly controlled, the combustion model used is a possible reason for the
diﬀerence in the pressure rise during combustion.

valve and then increases into the exhaust duct. In the region of the cylinder volume
close to the exhaust valve, the velocity is increasing as the area is decreasing since
the ﬂuid is in the subsonic range (M < 1). In the exhaust duct close to the exhaust
valve, the ﬂuid is in the supersonic range (M > 1) and an increase in area leads to
an increase in speed [Kundu et al., 2012], as seen in ﬁgure 6.21a and 6.21b . The
ﬂow has a re-circulation pattern near the exhaust valve walls. This is due to the
sharp edges of the exhaust valve designed in this thesis. In reality, the exhaust valve
has a smooth proﬁle and the re-circulation is thus not expected to happen in the
engine near the exhaust valve.

Chapter 8
Partial Load on an Engine
8.1
Importance of part loads on an engine
The brake thermal eﬃciency of an engine varies with the load under which the engine
runs. The eﬃciency ﬁrst increases with load, reaches a maximum eﬃciency and
then decreases with an increasing load [Heywood, 1988]. It is therefore important
to study the in-cylinder processes when the engine is run at part loads. A CFD
study can be a better option to visualize the processes inside the engine compared
to experimental set ups as the engines speeds are quite high even at low loads and
a relatively small optical view compared to the cylinder size of large marine diesel
engines, limiting a complete monitoring of the in-cylinder events during optical
diagnostics [Pang et al., 2016].
Due to time limitations a complete cycle of the engine under part load could not be
achieved. The results of this CFD study for the parts loads are thus limited to the
combustion and the ﬂame pattern inside the engine.

Chapter 9
Conclusion and Future work
9.1
Discussion of the results
A full scale model of 4T50MX two stroke marine diesel engine is investigated and
studied using Computational Fluid Dynamics. The purpose of the full scale model
is to understand the ﬂow pattern in the engine including the inlet ports, the cylinder
volume and ﬂow past the exhaust valve. The three dimensional model developed
also helps to understand the tangential velocity pattern and the swirl ratio inside
the engine at crank angles close to TDC, which is very important considering the
eﬀect of tangential velocity on the ﬂame propagation as explained in section 6.3.
A study of the cycle dependency of pressure inside the cylinder volume and mass
ﬂow rate into the cylinder volume from the inlet ports showed that the results for
cycles after the ﬁrst cycle are within a maximum of 0.5% deviation between each
other. This result is crucial considering that further studies about ﬂow inside the
engine is conducted on the second cycle. A similar study needs to be conducted
on combustion simulations to ﬁnd out whether the solution is independent of the
engine cycle. As the simulations are run in a transient state, this study about the
dependence on cycle is important to understand the convergence of the solution.
A mesh resolution study showed ﬂuctuations in the results within 0.2% for the pres-
sure inside the engine. A particular pattern could not be found for the dependency
of the in-cylinder parameters on the mesh size. An 8mm mesh is adopted for studies
in this thesis considering the computational time required for the full engine cycle.
The turbulence models of k −ω SST and standard k −ϵ gives similar values for
in cylinder parameters and the mass ﬂow rate into the engine from the inlet ports.
The diﬀerence in the results is due to the diﬀerence in the transport equations
solves for the two models as explained in section 5.5. The turbulent kinetic energy
proﬁle inside the engine prior to combustion looks similar to [Lendormy et al., 2008].
The values are lower than [Lendormy et al., 2008].This is due to the diﬀerence in

quantities are sensitive to the quality of the mesh which is a reason for the diﬀerence
in the values from [Lendormy et al., 2008]. The CFD model in this thesis uses 8mm

A switch is made from air to multi component gas formulation to simulate combus-

For the full load case, pressure rise of 13.14 bar is achieved by the CFD model com-

inside the engine at low loads. A comparison between the full load and partial load
in-cylinder parameters is conducted to understand engine behaviour at part loads.
9.2
Scope of future research
This thesis is a groundwork that can be used for future studies on two stroke marine
engines. Some of the suggestions for future study are:
• Modify the geometry of the engine to match the exact geometry of 4T50MX
engine and reﬁne the mesh for better resolution. Conduct mesh convergence
study for the in-cylinder parameters. Investigate about possible meshing tech-
niques to avoid the remeshing of the cylinder volume. Remeshing process was
not able to map the solution exactly to the new mesh in this study
• Conduct the numerical simulation of full engine cycle of the two stroke engine
at 50% and 75% part loads. Compare the result with experimental values and
study the ﬂow pattern and combustion inside the engine at part loads
• Model the combustion process with diesel. Include all the chemical reactions
that happens inside the engine during combustion. Investigate on the diﬀerent
combustion models that can replicate the combustion inside the engine. EBU
model used in this thesis has limitations as explained in section 6.6.1
• Modify the geometry to include the metal liner around the cylinder volume
and study the heat transfer to the walls from the cylinder of the engine
• Investigate the eﬀect of injector location, injector diameter, direction of in-
jection, injection timings, fuel ﬂow rate and air/fuel ratio on the combustion
process and performance of the engine.
