Abstract
In the context of rapid environmental change, it is imperative to develop a comprehen­
sive understanding of and ability to anticipate changes in marine biodiversity. This study
proposes an ANN­based joint species distribution modeling framework applied to fish as­
semblages in the Northeast Atlantic. The integration of species traits with environmental
data is a process which enables the model to capture both individual and community­level
ecological responses. A temporal cross­validation scheme with decadal splits is used to
assess the relative stability of performance over time. This approach demonstrated high
predictive power for species occurrences (mean AUC = 0.932) and more moderate per­
formance for biomass predictions (mean R² = 0.386). Sea temperature appears to be
the most significant driver of marine biodiversity, with detritus concentration and phyto­
plankton seasonality also playing a role. Utilising environmental forecasts, the model was
employed to generate predictions of species distributions and community structure from
2020 to 2050. The resulting outputs provide insight into the expected redistribution of
biodiversity in coming decades, demonstrating the value of ANN­based approaches in
ecological forecasting.

Introduction
Marine biodiversity supports fundamental ecosystem functions and services, including
the supply of food, climate regulation, and cultural values. This biodiversity is increas­
ingly threatened by a broad range of anthropogenic stressors, including most notably cli­
mate change, overfishing, and habitat destruction [1] [2]. Climate­induced environmental
changes are already shifting species distributions, particularly toward higher latitudes, as
species track their thermal niches [3] [4]. However, these range shifts vary among taxa,
with potentially profound consequences for community composition, as well as the struc­
ture and functioning of ecosystems [5]. Protecting the integrity of marine ecosystems
needs to be founded on a good understanding of the underlying environmental drivers
and ecological processes that determine the distribution, composition, and dynamics of
marine communities, especially in the context of global change. Furthermore, reliable
forecasts of species distributions and biodiversity patterns at large are essential, not only
to understand the mechanisms behind species distributions and diversity [6], but also to
inform conservation action and spatial planning, such as the placement and design of
Marine Protected Areas (MPA) [7].
MPAs are a keystone of marine conservation policy, aimed at safeguarding biodiver­
sity, preserving ecosystem functioning, and managing sustainable use. They range from
fully protected zones with stringent restrictions to minimally protected areas offering lim­
ited conservation benefit [8]. Effective planning and prioritization of MPAs require spatially
explicit, ecologically informed models that anticipate species and community responses to
environmental pressures. Many different indices have been developed to describe biodi­
versity various dimensions [9]. However, using a consistent framework makes it easier to
monitor biodiversity over time and across different regions and ecosystems. The Essential
Biodiversity Variables (EBVs) [10], offer a framework identifying key biodiversity indicators
that can be applied consistently and shared across studies. This approach helps improve
communication between scientists and policymakers, and supports more effective biodi­
versity monitoring. The EBVs are grouped into six main categories: genetic composition,
species populations, species traits, community composition, ecosystem structure, and
ecosystem function (Figure 1.1). Together, this framework provide a clear and comple­
mentary view of biodiversity, helping to track changes and understand the processes that
shape it.

Ecological models have long been used to understand and forecast species responses
to environmental gradients. Among them, Joint Species Distribution Models (JSDMs)
have emerged as important tools to model several species simultaneously with interspe­
cific dependence and trait­based data [11]. These models enable inferring functional asso­
ciations between environmental conditions and traits of species, with improved predictive
performance, particularly for rare or data­poor species [12]. However, while recent studies
have demonstrated the strengths of Bayesian JSDMs in reconstructing past community
dynamics and biodiversity indicators [6], they remain computationally intensive and may
become increasingly complex with the integration of temporal and functional dimensions.
The latest developments in machine learning, in the form of Artificial Neural Networks
(ANNs), offer a promising alternative.
ANNs are highly flexible, non­linear modelling
frameworks capable of representing complex interactions and high­dimensional relation­
ships without strict distributional assumptions. Their scalability with large data sets and
suitability for many forms of input data (e.g., traits, spatial, and temporal covariates) make
them particularly well suited for biodiversity modelling. Importantly, ANNs can now inte­
grate ecological theory through structured architectures, improving both predictive accu­
racy and interpretability. Including species characteristics in such models not only im­
proves interpretability but also enables greater generalization. Trait­based approaches
support the extrapolation of species­environment relationships into novel or undersam­
pled habitats, extending model applicability across broader spatial and temporal contexts.
This character­based transferability is especially valuable in conservation, where data lim­
itations often constrain planning in ecologically important but under­monitored regions.
This study tests the use of a novel ANN­based framework to model marine fish assem­
blages in the North­East Atlantic, using high­resolution monitoring and species trait data
to reveal consistent trait­environment relationships and improve predictions of species
presence and community structure under future conditions. The objective is to adapt the
method used in Bourhis et al. (2022) [13] to the North­East Atlantic region. The model is
an ANN­based JSDM trait­based approach that combines species­specific responses to
the environment and shared responses across the community mediated by species traits,
in order to determine the presence or absence of each species on a given location.

Building on this foundation, the thesis is structured around three central research ques­
tions, each designed to deepen our understanding of the ecological drivers of fish com­
munity structure in the Northeast Atlantic and to critically evaluate emerging modelling
approaches.
First, what are the main environmental and biotic factors shaping the distribution and
composition of fish species across the region? This includes identifying key covariates
and understanding the relative contributions of different ecological assembly processes.
Second, how do species traits modulate their responses to environmental gradients?
Trait­based approaches provide a mechanistic lens to understand species’ ecological
strategies and can offer insights into how functional characteristics influence community
patterns.
Third, how does the performance of ANNs compare to traditional Joint Species Dis­
tribution Models (JSDMs) in modelling biodiversity patterns? This includes evaluating
predictive accuracy, interpretability, computational demands, and scalability. As ecologi­
cal datasets become increasingly complex and high­dimensional, it is essential to assess
the trade­offs and advantages of adopting more flexible, data­driven approaches over
conventional statistical methods.

2.1
Data Description
The data used in this research is a comprehensive set of fish species distribution and
biomass from 13 scientific bottom­trawl surveys conducted across the North and North­
east Atlantic Ocean. The data set encompass 90,029 unique hauls spanning 33 years
(1989­2021), thus capturing the active period for most surveys and providing an extensive
spatio­temporal coverage of our study area (Figure 2.1). The species names were cross­
referenced and updated using the World Register of Marine Species (WoRMS Editorial
Board 2022). For each of the hauls, the presence­absence of species is recorded, as well
as their biomass standardized for sampling area (km²) [14] [15].

2.2
Traits
The selection of traits in this study is based on data available from established trait
databases [16]. These traits encompass key aspects of the ecology of species, including
diet, habitat, reproduction, and morphology. Specifically, habitat, age, and growth coef­
ficient characterize the species’ ecological niche; fecundity, offspring size, and spawn­
ing mode describe reproductive strategies; trophic level and feeding mode reflect dietary
habits; and length, aspect ratio, body shape, and caudal fin shape capture morphological
features.
As the analytical model requires numerical inputs, categorical traits were initially trans­
formed using one­hot encoding, where each category of a trait is converted into a separate
binary variable (e.g., habitat.pelagic) [17]. In this representation, each species is as­
signed a value of 0 or 1 depending on its association with the trait category. Although
this encoding increases the dimensionality of the dataset, comparative analyses revealed
minimal differences in model outcomes with or without the inclusion of categorical traits.
Consequently, to reduce model complexity, the decision was made to retain only contin­
uous traits for the final analysis. Detailed descriptions of the selected traits are presented
in Table 2.1.
Further dimensionality reduction was conducted by excluding traits exhibiting high mul­
ticollinearity. Pairs of traits with a Pearson correlation coefficient greater than 0.7 or less
than ­0.7 were evaluated, and within each correlated pair, the trait with the lower overall
correlation to other variables was retained (see Supplementary Figure A.3). To standard­
ize trait magnitudes and reduce skewness, a logarithmic transformation, ln(x + 1), was
applied to all continuous traits, except for aspect ratio and trophic level, which already
exhibited appropriate scaling [14].

Environmental Covariates
To characterize the environmental conditions at each unique haul location, physical
and biogeochemical variables are extracted from the reanalysis products of the NEMO­
MEDUSA ocean model [18]. These variables included both surface and bottom values for
temperature (°C), salinity (PSU), detritus concentration (mmol N m­3), phytoplankton con­
centration (mmol N m­3), and dissolved inorganic nitrogen (DIN; mmol N m­3), in addition
to bathymetric depth (m). As proxies for seasonal variability, the annual standard devia­
tion for temperature, salinity, and phytoplankton are also calculated at each sampling site
[19].
To mitigate multicollinearity and reduce variable redundancy, we computed pairwise
Pearson correlation coefficients among all covariates. For any pair with an absolute cor­
relation coefficient exceeding 0.7, the covariate with the weaker overall correlation to the
rest of the variables was excluded. The correlation structure is illustrated in Figure A.3,
and the final set of selected environmental predictors, along with their descriptions, is
summarized in Table 2.2.

2.4
Methodology
2.4.1
Model Architecture
Figure 2.3: Feedforward Neural Network
The model used in this study is a feedforward neural network (FNN) with a two­branch
architecture (Figure 2.3). A FNN is a type of ANN where information moves in one di­
rection — from the input layer, through intermediate hidden layers, to the output layer.
Each layer consists of neurons that perform simple mathematical operations on the in­
8
Modelling marine biodiversity patterns and changes in North­East Atlantic using ANN
==PAGE:16==
put they receive. The network learns to make predictions by adjusting the weights of the
connections between these neurons based on how accurate its predictions are during
training.
The specific modelling framework is designed to capture both community­level and
species­specific responses to environmental variation. It consists of two components that,
for each observation (defined as a site × year), generate predicted occurrence probabil­
ities for all target species, resulting in an output matrix of shape n samples × q species
(Figure 2.4). This multi­output architecture enables shared hidden layers across species,
allowing the model to leverage inter­species patterns and improve generalization through
shared representations [13]. The two components differ in their input structures:
Shared responses — This component integrates environmental covariates with species
traits through a Kronecker product, delivering an input tensor of dimensions n samples ×
(m covariates ∙(p traits +1 intercept) ×q species. The inclusion of an intercept term allows
the model to account for community­level responses not explained by traits alone.
Species­specific responses — This component uses a conventional input format, con­
sisting of environmental covariates for all samples (n samples × m covariates), to model
species­level effects directly and independently of trait information.
Two training strategies were evaluated on the basis of an ensemble approach and a
multi­branch approach. In the ensemble approach, both components are trained sepa­
rately and independently. In contrast, the multi­branch approach sequentially trains the
shared­response component until signs of overfitting emerge, at which point the species­
specific branch is unfrozen and trained using the learned representations of the first
branch. Despite these methodological differences, both strategies produced comparable
predictive performance [13]. Given its simplicity and comparable accuracy, the ensemble
approach was selected for this study.

2.4.2
Training
Model training was performed with 33% of the entire dataset, reserving 67% for testing
and validation. To monitor training and prevent overfitting, part of the training data was
reserved as a validation set. Training was terminated if the validation loss did not improve
during ten successive epochs. To address the issue of class imbalance in species occur­
rence data, we applied a weighting scheme within the loss function of the model (binary
cross­entropy loss). More specifically, for each species, weights were assigned based
on the relative frequency of presences and absences in the training data, so that rarer
classes would contribute more strongly to the loss, reducing the bias toward more com­
mon species. To moderate the effect of extreme imbalance, these weights were further
adjusted using a square­root transformation. This strategy ensures that both rare and
common species influence model training, promoting balanced predictive performance
across the full range of species.
Given the highly imbalanced nature of the data robust model evaluation metrics are
required. Matthews correlation coefficient (MCC) was used as the primary measure of
model selection. MCC quantifies the relationship between observed and predicted binary
classifications and is very robust to class imbalance [20]. It takes into account true posi­
tives and negatives as well as false positives and negatives and is thus informative and
balanced even when species presence is rare. In addition, area under the receiver op­
erating characteristic curve (AUC ROC) was computed for the sake of comparison, as it
is widely used in ecological modeling [21]. However, AUC tends to deliver too optimistic
results in this use since there is dominance of absence data, which generates inflated true
negative rates and undermines the performance of the metric in capturing weak predictive
differences. Hence, even though AUC scores are given for reference, MCC provides a
more reliable basis for model performance assessment in this study.
2.4.3
SHAP Analysis
To assess the effect and relative importance of model predictors we used SHAP anal­
ysis [22]. SHAP provides local explanations at the sample level, which, when averaged
over many samples, reveal global patterns of feature importance. Features in our case
include both environmental covariates and trait­covariate interactions. For each feature,
SHAP values show the amount by which the feature contributed to the model’s prediction
relative to a baseline in which the feature is replaced by random background values. Pos­
itive or negative values are assigned based on whether the feature adds to or takes away
from the predicted probability of the presence of species. To simplify interpretation, we
aggregate SHAP values on their corresponding feature values for the samples and taking
the sign of the resulting regression coefficient summarizes the feature’s effect across all.
We then add these coefficients together to form two numbers, one of which forms the
matrix of the trait­mediated inputs in matrix format (m environmental covariates × p traits
+ intercept), a structure similar to the fourth­corner matrix commonly used in ecology [23].
2.4.4
Temporal Cross­validation
To evaluate the predictive performance of our models, we use a temporal cross­validation
strategy (Figure 2.5) [14]. The data was divided into three decades (1989–1999, 2000–
2009, 2010–2021), with separate models trained on each. Model performance was as­
sessed using AUC and MCC for presence­absence, and R² for biomass. Each model
was tested on the two remaining decades to evaluate out­of­sample prediction. For ex­
ample, a model trained on 1989–1999 was evaluated on 2000–2009 and 2010–2021.
Each decadal model used the same process as the full­period models but fitting data was
restricted to data from its corresponding decade.

2.4.5
Forecasts
To estimate spatial and temporal dynamics of fish community structure, we drew three
complementary indicators of biodiversity from model predictions: species richness, Shan­
non diversity index, and species evenness. These were estimated with the help of the
Python package ecopy, which provides standardized implementations of ecological di­
versity functions. Species richness is the count of species predicted to occur in each grid
cell. It was calculated from binarized probability of occurrence (thresholded at 0.5) and
represents a simple but informative measure of composition of communities. Shannon
diversity, as calculated from estimated biomass values, captures both species richness
and relative abundance and quantifies the uncertainty of being able to predict the identity
of an arbitrarily sampled individual. It increases with the number of species and evenness
of biomass distribution. Evenness of species normalizes Shannon diversity to idealized
maximum diversity for a given richness, producing a value between 0 and 1 reflecting how
evenly biomass is apportioned among co­occurring species. All the predictors were es­
timated at the grid­cell scale from monthly projections of species presence and biomass
for 2020–2050 under simulated future climate scenarios by the NEMO­MEDUSA coupled
physical–biogeochemical ocean model [18]. Model results permitted uniform predictions
of diversity patterns under future change.

performance of the biomass model, the study will primarily focus on occurrences.

4
Discussion
The high AUC values observed for species occurrence are comparable—or even superior—
to performance reported in traditional JSDMs, which typically achieve AUC scores in the
range of 0.8–0.9 for well­sampled communities [24] [25]. This confirms that the current
model, despite its complexity and spatio­temporal scope, maintains robust discriminative
power. However, the more modest MCC and especially R² reveal important limitations.
Classical JSDMs often struggle with abundance predictions due to high variance, and
weak species­environment relationships [11] [24] . Similar challenges are reflected in
the present results where presence­absence patterns can be accurately learned from the
data, while predicting continuous biomass is far more difficult. The average R² of 0.386
falls within the expected range for community­level models, though it is slightly above
what is typically seen in non­hierarchical models fitted to sparse or noisy biomass data
[26].
The discrepancy between the high AUC and the lower MCC suggests that although
the model is effective at distinguishing presences from absences in general, it does not
always produce balanced predictions across all outcomes. This issue is commonly ob­
served in ecological modeling, where datasets are frequently imbalanced, with many more
absences than presences, particularly for rare species [27]. The elevated AUC values
(Figure 3.2) indicate strong overall model performance, but this can be misleading. In
presence­absence datasets dominated by absences, AUC may appear high primarily be­
cause the model successfully identifies the majority class, rather than making accurate
presence predictions. This limitation is particularly relevant when the objective is to de­
tect rare species. A more balanced evaluation is provided by the MCC metric (Figure 3.2),
which accounts for both false positives and false negatives. With a mean MCC of 0.27,
the model clearly performs better than random guessing, yet it also reveals limited predic­
tive power for species with low prevalence. This observation is critical when using such
models for biodiversity forecasting. Examination of the confusion matrices (Figure 3.3)
further supports this interpretation. While overall accuracy is high, it is largely driven by
correct predictions of absences. Precision and recall values indicate that the model takes
a conservative approach in predicting presences, thus reducing false positives but poten­
tially overlooking actual occurrences. The consistently low false positive rate and high
true negative rate reflect this cautious and potential bias in prediction.
The performance difference between branches, reflected in the segment lengths of
Figure 3.2, illustrates the strength of a dual­branch approach. While both shared and
species­specific components contribute meaningfully, their integration provides clear ad­
ditive value, capturing both generalized and individual species responses to the environ­
ment.
The spatial gradient in model performance revealed in Figure 3.4 underscores the
strong influence of sampling effort and data quality. The spatial patterns of model per­
formance are slightly correlated with data availability (Supplementary Figure A.5). Areas
with fewer trawl samples and few neighbouring data points tend to correspond to lower
AUC and MCC values, suggesting that data density is a key driver of spatial model accu­
racy. Regions such as the Norwegian Sea, where the model underperforms, are known
to suffer from inconsistencies in sampling protocols and data quality—factors that likely
contribute to the reduced predictive skill observed there. Similarly, peripheral areas like
20

==PAGE:28==
western Greenland and the southern reaches of the Iberian shelf are characterized by
sparse sampling coverage, limiting the model’s ability to generalize and leading to poor
AUC scores. Furthermore, these peripheral zones often represent the ecological fringes
of certain species’ distributions, especially more warm­water associated species with a
center of distribution further south. In such cases, the model is trained on an incom­
plete representation of the species’ environmental niche, which compromises its predic­
tive reliability. As a result, poor performance in these boundary areas is not solely a
reflection of data sparsity, but also of ecological undersampling. This issue aligns with
known limitations of species distribution models trained on incomplete range data [28]
[29]. Interestingly, some regions with moderate sample density but strong AUC and MCC
scores—such as the Barents Sea—demonstrate that model performance is not strictly
dependent on the number of hauls alone. Rather, spatial representativeness and ecolog­
ical coherence of the samples also play a critical role in shaping the model’s predictive
power.
The patterns identified in Figure 3.6 reflect coherent ecological strategies shaped by
environmental gradients. Warmer bottom temperatures are associated with species that
grow faster, are longer, and have higher fecundity and trophic levels—traits typically linked
with more opportunistic and productive life histories [30] [31]. In contrast, the negative
relationship between temperature and offspring size may indicate a trade­off favoring
quantity over quality in warmer environments, consistent with classical life­history theory
[32][33].Increased detritus availability, a proxy for benthic food supply, is positively linked
to larger and more fecund species, suggesting a shift toward species capable of exploiting
richer food resources. This agrees with findings that benthic productivity supports larger­
bodied, energy­demanding species in demersal food webs [34]. On the other hand, higher
phytoplankton seasonality—indicative of pulsed and irregular primary productivity—is as­
sociated with reduced length, growth, and fecundity. This may reflect adaptations to more
variable environments, where smaller, faster­reproducing species are favored due to more
unpredictable energy inputs [35]. Finally, increasing depth is associated with decreasing
body size, growth, and fecundity, likely reflecting the energetic constraints of deep­sea en­
vironments, such as lower food availability, reduced temperatures, and higher pressures
[36]. Overall, these patterns reveal how ecological traits mediate species’ environmental
responses, consistent with expectations from life­history and trait­based ecology, while
confirming that a few key environmental variables—especially SBT and detritus—drive
the strongest trait­linked gradients in the model. These patterns suggest ecologically
meaningful differences in how species respond to environmental gradients. The negative
response to phytoplankton seasonality aligns with the idea that many species prefer sta­
ble primary production regimes, potentially because erratic peaks in food availability can
disrupt trophic dynamics or recruitment timing [19].
The species­specific branch shows a generally positive association with detritus (Fig­
ure 3.5), which may reflect its role as a key source of benthic food, particularly for dem­
ersal species dependant on detrital pathways [37]. Meanwhile, the mixed responses to
temperature and depth are expected, given the wide range of thermal preferences and
bathymetric distributions among species in the dataset. This diversity of responses un­
derscores the need for flexible modeling frameworks capable of capturing heterogeneous
ecological responses across taxa.
Model performance across species was also evaluated with and without the inclusion
of spatial predictors such as spatial lag, distance to species­rich buffers, and general
spatial relationships (Supplementary Figure A.6). These features are commonly used in

ecological modeling to account for spatial autocorrelation—where nearby sites are more
likely to share similar species compositions—and to capture unmeasured environmen­
tal or dispersal processes. Specifically, the spatial lag represents the average predicted
presence in neighboring locations, while the distance buffer reflects the proximity to high­
density species clusters. Together, these predictors aim to supplement local covariates
by encoding broader spatial structure [13]. However, during preliminary analyses, it was
observed that spatial features dominated species­specific responses, often overpowering
the influence of environmental covariates. This suggested that the model might be learn­
ing spatial patterns rather than the underlying ecological processes, potentially reducing
interpretability. Removing spatial predictors addressed this issue without degrading over­
all performance. AUC values remained consistently high across species, indicating that
the model retained strong predictive accuracy and continued to effectively leverage en­
vironmental and trait information alone. This outcome highlights the robustness of the
model and the sufficiency of its core ecological predictors. While spatial features can be
beneficial under strong spatial autocorrelation or data sparsity, their exclusion here sim­
plifies the model and reduces computational cost, offering practical advantages without
compromising quality or insight.
The temporal cross­validation experiment highlights the robustness of the ANN­based
species distribution model in predicting species occurrences across multi­decadal time
scales. The overall stability of both AUC and MCC metrics, despite validation windows
extending up to 20 years beyond the training data, suggests that the learned ecological
relationships generalize well over time, at least within the environmental envelope cov­
ered by the dataset. The modest decline in MCC for earlier training periods, particularly
for models trained on 1990s data, might reflect the limitations of older training data in
capturing more recent environmental regimes or ecological dynamics. However, the con­
sistent AUC values indicate that the model retains strong discriminative power even when
predicting well beyond the training horizon. This pattern aligns with findings in ecological
modeling where AUC tends to be more robust to temporal shifts than threshold­dependent
metrics like MCC [29]. Notably, our results compare favorably with classic Joint Species
Distribution Models (JSDMs), which often exhibit performance drops over long tempo­
ral extrapolations due to their reliance on parametric assumptions and limited capacity
to model non­linear interactions [14] [24]. By contrast, ANNs are particularly well­suited
to learning complex, nonlinear, and high­dimensional relationships [38], which likely con­
tributes to the temporal stability observed here. The absence of marked temporal decay
in predictive performance is encouraging for applications in forecasting biodiversity re­
sponses under future climate scenarios. Still, the slight decline in MCC cautions that
while presence/absence discrimination remains high, the precise prediction of balanced
classification may degrade over time, highlighting the importance of periodic retraining as
ecological baselines shift.
Using the validated model as a basis for future simulations the observed northward
shift in projected species richness and Shannon diversity (Figure 3.8) aligns with expec­
tations under ocean warming scenarios, where marine species move toward cooler north­
ern waters to track their environmental niches [39] [40]. This redistribution may lead to
increased local (alpha) diversity, especially at higher latitudes. Furthermore, the increas­
ing evenness in northern regions could point to a trend of community homogenization,
where newly arriving species from the south integrate without any one species becoming
dominant. This may reflect shifts in environmental constraints, or competition dynamics
under climate change [41]. However, it is important to consider the Shannon index and
evenness projections with due caution. Because these metrics rely on outputs from the

relatively limited. Nevertheless, our projections suggest a potential future redistribution
and restructuring of biodiversity in response to climate change, rather than a uniform gain
or loss. Trait­based neural models offer valuable insights for projecting such shifts, espe­
cially in data­poor regions, but uncertainty, particularly in the biomass model remains a
key consideration for downstream applications such as conservation planning.

Conclusion
The present study test a novel ANN­based approach to modelling the distribution of
marine fish assemblages in the Northeast Atlantic. Our findings demonstrate that temper­
ature, detritus concentrations, and phytoplankton seasonality were the most significant
drivers influencing species distributions overall. This underscores the important roles of
thermal regimes and trophic dynamics in shaping marine communities. Forecasts based
on future environmental conditions from 2020 to 2050 suggest a biodiversity redistribution
in response to projected changes, particularly at higher latitudes.
The integration of ecological relevance with computational efficiency of the approach
provides a flexible and scalable tool for marine biodiversity modelling. The proposed
framework has the potential to facilitate ongoing initiatives aimed at anticipating and man­
aging ecological responses to climate change, particularly within data­rich systems where
the integration of species traits and environmental variability can produce valuable in­
sights.

A.2.5
Including the categorical traits
Originally, this study included discrete traits, that are described in Supplementary Ta­
bles ?? and A.3. As the model takes numbers as input, the categorical traits need to be
converted. The easiest way to perform the conversion is a one hot encoding, creating
for each trait category a trait of its own (e.g habitat.pelagic instead of habitat) [17]. Each
species then has a binary value depending of the appartenance or not to the trait. This
method add more dimensions to the model, which increases its complexity. The inclusion
of discrete input variables made interpretation challenging, as certain traits—particularly
