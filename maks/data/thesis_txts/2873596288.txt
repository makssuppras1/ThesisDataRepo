Abstract
This research presents a comprehensive characterization of a unique dual-pumped laser
scheme for amplified mid-infrared pulses, from a synchronously pumped mode-locked
Optical Parametric Oscillator combined with linear intracavity gain, with the goal of
creating an efficient amplification technique for mid-IR pulse generation. A generalized
concept of the scheme is investigated from a specific study with nonlinear Periodically
Poled Lithium Niobate (PPLN) and Nd:YAG as gain material, correlating influences on
the laser power, gain, spectra, and dispersion with known physical effects. Experimental
analysis of two PPLN lengths of 20 and 10 mm grounded the data in known theory. The
key tunable parameters, temperature and cavity length, provided a combination to control
the phase matching criteria efficiently and the oscillating signal field, ensuring optimal
choice of wavelengths. The 20 mm PPLN crystal was shown to produce broader spectra
with greater idler power, but lacking amplification from the dual-pumped scheme, as well
as suffering from dispersion-induced chirping. The lower length for the 10 mm PPLN
reduced gain and dispersion, but demonstrated the gain amplification through the linear
Nd: YAG crystal, up to the continuous wave lasing threshold. An intensity autocorrelator
setup determined pulse durations, yielding ∼1.7 ps for the 10 mm and ∼3.8 ps for the
20 mm crystal, with time-bandwidth products indicating non-transform-limited pulses in
line with previous observations. Results indicate that the theoretical models align well
with experimental data, though improvements are suggested to simulate pulse buildup
and dispersion management. Finally, the report recommends improvements to the cavity
setup in the form of dispersion compensation and beam shaping for beam expansion in
the linear gain to improve the potential gain extraction for signal pulses in the OPO.
Overall, the study advances understanding of hybrid laser systems and outlines pathways
to improve mid-IR pulse generation efficiency and quality.

1
Introduction & Motivation
In optics, the idea behind lasers originated in the 1950s and sparked a revolution within
optical sciences and applications after its application was demonstrated in the 1960s [1].
Since then, lasers have only grown in a wide variety of fields, including medical diagnostic
tools, powerful industrial cutters, spatial sensing LIDAR, and not to mention their partial
support of worldwide communication and the internet through overseas optical fibers [2].
Historically, the wavelength of lasers has been dictated by the gain material, of which
multiples have been discovered and invented, covering a large region of the electromag-
netic spectrum from UV to near infrared areas. Still, even with innumerable materials
and despite advances in laser technology, some areas of the spectrum remain difficult to
produce, especially around the mid-infrared region [3].
Each wavelength region has its unique uses, and this is no different for the mid-infrared
region (≈2µm - 5 µm), which is in high demand. Among some examples, the ”finger-
print” for molecular binding is located here, allowing for sensing and identification of
gases, useful for both pollution tracking and scientific sensing [4]. The atmospheric layer
contains important transparency windows, valid for communications in higher layers, and
a list of bio-medical devices requires this wavelength range [5, 6]. The uses are vast, but
sources are limited; among the current ones are Quantum Cascade Lasers (QCL), which
fulfill this purpose but come with challenges in price from complexity, low duty cycles to
manage thermal issues, and spectral tuning constraints. [7]. Nonlinear optimal processes
enable the generation of a broad spectral range through optical parametric oscillators
(OPO) [8], which solves the issues of wavelength tuning and reduces complexity, but in-
creasing the power to stand up to QCL through these can come with its own challenges
as well.
This project seeks to remedy these issues through the characterization of a synchronously
pumped OPO with internal linear gain. Combining the flexible tunability of the new non-
linear optics with the cheap and efficient gain of old linear optics. Specifically, this study
will investigate the tuning and output characteristics of the dual-pumped laser setup,
using MgO-doped Lithium Niobate and Nd:YAG as the nonlinear and linear gain mate-
rials, respectively, but the purpose of the laser extends to other materials, to investigate
a generalized setup that may create intense pulses in the mid-infrared region or deeper.
Being based on a new concept [9], the project will focus on gaining an understanding of
the effects of various underlying parameters on the laser through experimental practice
coupled with theory, to achieve the goal of fully characterizing the lasing method.

The Dual-Pumped Setup
This project aims to characterize the nuanced mechanics of a generalized laser setup that
can create empowered infrared pulses of a desired wavelength, based on the intermin-
gling of an external mode-locked laser and an intracavity linear gain for amplification.
This laser scheme seeks to combine the benefits of each gain mechanism: The wavelength
flexibility in nonlinear, parametric processes, and the efficient amplification of a linear
gain crystal. While the intercations that lead to creating this type of mode-locked lasing
have been demonstrated before [9], the finer mechanisms in these complex interactions
and tuning properties have not been fully appreciated, nor investigated. The following
section will therefore provide a detailed overview of the optical properties of a specific ex-
perimental laser cavity that facilitates the pulse generation, how this output is measured,
and which parameters may affect the outcome of the process.

The principles being that an OPO using PPLN sets the stage for generation of 1 µm and
3-4 µm photons from the TiS pump, while the mode-locked nature ensures short pulses
for idler generation. It is when the YAG crystal is included in the cavity and pumped ex-
ternally that the oscillating fields should gain access to more gain through the population
inversion of the crystal. All of this facilitates the creation of much-desired, high-energy,
short infrared pulses around 3-4 µm, but it does come at the cost of strict requirements
for the cavity. The laser’s operation is only possible if there is a proper temporal overlap
between the mode-locked TiS pulses and the intracavity pulse, which additionally needs
to overlap with the emission wavelength of the linear gain, in this case being 1064 nm.
Of course, the phase-matching (PM) conditions of the PPLN must be manipulated to fit
DFG for TiS and YAG pump wavelengths.
The latter criterion is accomplished with an oven-mounted PPLN containing five channels
of varied poling periods from 21 to 23 µm, in steps of 0.5 µm between each channel. The
flexible choice of poling period and temperature will enable smooth control of the PM
conditions, but that leaves the control of repetition rate and signal wavelength. The trick
is that the tunability of the cavity length via. M8 solves both problems at once: To have
the signal make a full round-trip for each TiS pulse, the dual-pumped and TiS cavity
must be of equal lengths to match repetition rates (rep. rates), but the dispersion of
the PPLN and YAG in the laser limits the signal wavelength due to group delay. The
PPLN introduces a relatively high refractive index (around 2.3), which delays each pulse
through group delay, making the rep. rate wavelength sensitive. Tuning the cavity length

parameters, length, and mirrors are part of a conscious decision to enable the mixed OPO
+ mode-locking with linear gain incorporated in the cavity.
This does not imply that the cavity requires both pump sources to be active simultane-
ously. It can operate either in continuous-wave (CW) mode using the YAG laser, or syn-
chronously pumped OPO using only the mode-locked TiS laser, or combined operation.
The individual operation is used to characterize the two gain mechanisms individually.

3.1
Introduction to nonlinear optics
The field of nonlinear optics springs from the polarization induced in materials when
interacting with light. Figure 3 cleanly illustrates the differences between the induced
polarizations in the linear and non-linear cases, showcasing low amplitude fields producing
polarizations with linear dependence, but the anharmonic potential in the other case
produces a polarization consisting of higher order harmonics.
Figure 3: Differences between linear and nonlinear induced polarization in an anharmonic
potential. Left: Linear case of low input field modulation Right: Larger field perturbations
result in higher order induced polarization. Figure reproduced from [14].
A general Taylor expansion of the induced polarization introduces the nonlinear polar-
ization, consisting of higher-order susceptibilities and (E)n dependence on the electrical
fields involved:

Where the χ(n) susceptibilities are tensor elements of n’th order. These susceptibilities
χ(n) describe how the medium responds to applied electric fields at increasingly nonlinear
regimes, with each term contributing specific physical effects depending on the material
properties and the field strength. The first-order term, χ(1), governs the linear optical
response, including refractive index and linear birefringence. The second-order suscep-

non-centrosymmetric materials. The third-order susceptibility χ(3) contributes to effects
such as third-harmonic generation, intensity-dependent refractive index, and self-focusing.
The specific structure and component values of each χ(n) tensor are dictated by the crystal
structure and composition, influencing their nonlinear interactions in different aspects.

3.2
Parametric processes
One of the key contributions of nonlinear optics is the ability to transfer energy between
different frequencies of light, enabling the generation of photons at new wavelengths from
a single light source. While the frequency of a given laser amplifier, diode, or any fluo-
rescence is defined by the energy difference between excited and ground states, nonlinear
materials can take these sources and convert them for other use cases. The application
range varies greatly, from green laser pointers [15], to infrared detection schemes, and
amplification schemes.
A common way to achieve conversion is through parametric processes, which are a type
of nonlinearity that leaves the material unchanged while converting energy from field
to field. For second-order materials, a parametric process represents energy exchange
between three frequencies, with the nonlinear tensor and energy conservation as follows
[16, 17]:
χ(2)(−ω3; ω1, ±ω2),
ω3 = ω1 ± ω2
(3.2)
The process can be either degenerate or non-degenerate depending on whether ω1 and ω2
are identical. Separating all the possibilities, there are three distinct parametric processes
for second-order materials: Second Harmonic Generation (SHG) for the degenerate case,
and Sum Frequency Generation (SFG) and Difference Frequency Generation (DFG) for
the additive and subtractive non-degenerate cases, respectively [18]. The illustration in
Figure 4 displays the differences between each process. In SHG, two photons can enter
from the same pump and generate an output at twice the frequency. In SFG and DFG,
three unique frequencies are required for the process.
The discerning factor between
SFG and DFG is the flow of energy: SFG uses two lower-energy photons to generate
one of higher frequency, while DFG splits one into two, essentially reversing the process.
SFG and DFG are not two different processes, but rather depend on where the energy is
currently migrating.

3.3
Phase Matching principle
As previously mentioned, phase-matching (PM) is an essential concept for nonlinear pro-
cesses. As the pump field travels through the NL material and generates an incrementally
rising signal, each contribution must add up coherently in phase, otherwise the gener-

3.3.1
Phase matching schemes
Nonlinear optics boasts the potential to generate an almost arbitrary range of colors from
a given light source. The limits lie in the energy and momentum conservation equations,
ensuring that it is difficult to pick a specific set of desired frequencies without the correct
knowledge and preparation. Additionally, the material must remain transparent for all
three different frequencies, increasing the problem complexity.
A variety of methods exist to force phase matching in a crystal that may be characterized
in two different categories, depending on how the nonlinear material is affected:
• Dispersion control using external parameters and forces, includes: Temperature,
propagation angle, modality, wavelength, etc.
• Engineered nonlinear materials that have permanently altered their composition to
fit phase matching criteria.
Belonging to the first category are Critical Phase Matching (CPM) and Non-Critical
Phase Matching (NCPM). The former technique applies to anisotropic materials, match-
ing the phase of the fields by adjusting the propagation angle with respect to the crystal
axis. This method is reliable, requiring no actual modification of the nonlinear material
or change in temperature, but it enforces a strict requirement on the polarization of the
field and the nonlinear coefficient. Additionally, the Poynting vector and wave vector
of the fields often do not match in direction, causing spatial walk-off that broadens the
product spatially, and reduces efficiency [20]. NCPM does not rely on the anisotropic
characteristics of a material, with polarizations often aligning with the principal axis of
the crystal to avoid birefringence. Instead, NCPM achieves phase matching by tuning
the temperature of the crystal until phase matching is achieved. The simplicity of this
technique makes it very approachable for different types of configurations, e.g. for an
intracavity non-linear process, where a change in crystal angle would misalign the laser.
NCPM is only possible for a limited number of conversion processes and wavelengths
however, and extreme required temperatures can be a problem as well. Therefore, both
the material and wavelengths should be considered before using the technique to avoid
thermal damage.
The second category of methods can be engineered to avoid total reliance on temperature
and/or angular tuning for the crystal. Instead of outside forces, the environment within
the crystal is designed to suit the nonlinear process. One such method used in this project
is Quasi-Phase Matching (QPM). Returning to phase mismatching, the coherence length
established that the turning point in conversion occurred at lcoh/2 in the crystal, which

therefore the center of the spectrum. Figure 14 shows that increased temperatures result
in a longer signal wavelength for a fixed cavity length, so this is also a plausible theory.

4.3.2
OPO tuning mechanisms
The temperature and cavity length are capable of altering the phase matching conditions
and oscillating wavelength, making for a very flexible pair of tunable knobs for choosing
the optimal cavity output. Whether the actual laser meets these expectations remains to
be demonstrated; accordingly, the following measurements will examine the dynamics of
cavity tuning.
The cavity length tuning results in an instant change in wavelength, due to the syn-
chronous pump overlap requirements, compared to the slower setting of temperature,
which additionally modifies the cavity length through the refractive index. The spectral
measurements in Figure 29 represent an example from the 10 mm PPLN cavity, of the
TiS spectra and generated signal of the OPO, for varied mirror positions. The position

4.4
The Dual-Pumped laser cavity
The YAG crystal’s purpose is to contribute extra power to the OPO process through
linear gain to amplify the signal for DFG. The supplied power must be balanced; adding
too much will make the cavity CW, while an optimal amount still must be extracted.
The effectiveness of pulse amplification can be gauged from the 3 µm idler generation,
as it is difficult to assess whether the signal is composed of pulses, CW + pulse, or full
CW from the power alone. As the circulating field rises in power with the addition of the
linear gain, the idler power in Figure 33 descends for the increased power. The 10 mm
PPLN cavity benefits slightly from the linear gain, decreasing shortly after the CW lasing
threshold, yet the idler power falls instantly in the 20 mm case with the supplementary
power. With the longer interaction length and greater intracavity power, the pump may
become sufficiently depleted for back-conversion to occur in the 20 mm case, explaining
the instant drop in idler power. As the power increases, the idler outputs for both crystals
converge towards one another, likely signifying that the laser is predominantly CW and
disregarding the low-powered TiS input.

5
Discussion and Outlooks
The experimental work and applied theory of this project have achieved innumerable
measurements, models, and insights, through progress towards a total characterization of
the dual-pumped laser cavity. While the tuning mechanisms and generalized concept for
IR-pulse generation are now more well understood, the work still leaves some open ends
to be pursued further in the future. The following sections, which present the results
obtained during the project, are subjected to further scrutiny to analyze possible sources
of errors, identify potential remedies, and outline future work for the project.
When executed properly, experimental work is meant to be replicable as long as the setup
and material remain consistent. Still, it is exactly consistency that was a significant factor
in the trouble in this project. Specifically focusing on the TiS laser, the observed output
was proven to vary both in wavelength and power during the project, and both are critical
factors that should remain relatively fixed to evaluate the OPO’s performance.
The
decreased TiS performance in the 10 mm PPLN case leaves some amount of ambiguity
regarding the validity of specific comparisons between the crystals, especially for the pure
OPO characterizations. Some of the variability comes from the mode-locking mechanism,
requiring mechanical interference in the cavity; the overall changes are likely a result of
minute misalignment in the cavity, either from vibrations or temperature changes. While
the variations are problematic, attaining control over the TiS spectra can prove helpful,
as shown using the 20 mm PPLN, where a spectral peak provided a stable point for
maximum conversion power.
Large sections of the results rely on comparing the 10 and 20 mm PPLN crystals to
”ground” the observations in the known theory.
The length difference influences the
nonlinear conversion and group delay, but the 10 mm crystal received greater loss upon
its insertion, as evident in CW measurements. One can argue that this is inconsequential,
as an increased current can compensate for a worse slope efficiency. Still, the increased
absorbed power can give a more substantial thermal effect within the Nd:YAG crystal
[34]. Even a slight increase in thermal lens can be significant for a cavity length of 1.7 m,
changing the beam profile and overlap with the TiS pump. Because of this, it is crucial
to identify whether the loss comes from the cavity adjustments between the 10 and 20
mm crystals, damage in the crystal, or coating issues.
During spectral measurements of the signal, an uncharacteristically broad pulse spectrum
is present for the 20 mm PPLN, and to a lesser degree in the 10 mm cavity. The wings
of the conversion efficiency did not cause the shoulders observed. Still, the more likely
reason was the total accumulated GVD in the cavity, combined with pump depletion in
the PPLN. Pulse chirping was further demonstrated in autocorrelations from the time-
bandwidth product, proving that the dispersion plays a part in how the signal pulse
interacts with the pump pulse. While a broad spectrum could theoretically be helpful
to reach shorter pulse lengths, the dispersion counteracts this, and the dual-pumped
principle also works mainly around the emission wavelength of 1064 nm. This could be
counteracted in the future with dispersion-compensating mirrors or a dual-prism setup.
Suppose the goal is to prevent chirping entirely through dispersion compensation; in
that case, the Self-Phase Modulation of the PPLN should also be modeled to achieve a

Of particular interest were the minor oscillations observed in the spectral measurements
of the TiS pump, and to some extent in the idler spectrum as well, despite the latter
being measured using entirely different equipment. These oscillations were not present in
all measurements, so it is difficult to rule this out as just instrumental errors. This effect
should be studied further to rule out any undesired oscillations in the cavity.
The theoretical models also require improvement and further development. All of the
numerically derived theories are based on analytical derivations, from phase matching
to wavelength tuning. Future works could incorporate simulations of the pulse buildup
within the cavity, incrementally adding the influence of each gain element, dispersion
effects, and delay to the TiS pump. This requires a lot of partial contributions using dif-
ferent theoretical estimations, but a full-scale model predicting the steady-state evolution
would make for a valuable tool to estimate the effects of the various parameters.
5.1
Future works
With the constraints of time and unforeseeable challenges imposed by working with a
complex, experimental laser setup, some of the objectives set for the project remained
incomplete by the end of the experimental process. If the questionable autocorrelation
traces are solved in the future, the next step is to attempt cross-correlation between the
signal and idler pulses. Developing a method for this will not only determine the idler
length, but also set the stage for the probing mechanisms utilizing the idler.
This endeavor would have had a larger impact if the amplification scheme with the linear
gain from Nd:YAG had yielded greater results. Currently, the amplification showed a
minor positive impact for the 10 mm crystal, likely stemming from the increased laser
threshold. Through the report, the data indicated that the CW takeover of the linear
gain is counter-productive to DFG, therefore, the next logical course of action would
be to increase the lasing threshold, still ensuring that potential gain is not negatively
impacted. The theory section covering the linear gain mechanisms [11] identifies that the
lasing threshold can be correlated to a specific internal photon flux value. As the photon
flux is photon count over area, the pump power threshold is lowered significantly by
decreasing the laser spot size, as fewer population states have to be inverted. Conversely,
a broadening of the beam profile at the laser crystal would result in a greater pump
threshold, but there are far more excited states to pull energy from. By modifying the
cavity with, for example, a beam expander setup before the YAG crystal using lenses,
the threshold and gain for parametric conversion can be increased. On the negative side,
this would require a significant overhaul of the cavity design; therefore, it would be a
time sink for further work.
A simpler suggestion for future work with the setup is to split the signal output into more
arms for measurement. During the project, the OSA, oscilloscope, and autocorrelator all
shared the same output arm, and changing between measurements might result in some
discrepancies between the pulse characteristics.

Conclusion
The overall aim of the project was to understand and characterize a synchronously mode-
locked Optical Parametric Oscillator with additional intracavity gain, primarily focusing
on the influence that the tunable parameters have on the output power, spectra, and
time. These goals were achieved through experimental work and theoretical models for
the cavity.
Through the use of identical PPLN crystals of 10 mm or 20 mm in length, the various con-
figurations of the laser were examined to identify important characteristics. The results
showed that the longer crystal was capable of producing spectra of greater bandwidth
than the shorter one, as the cavity dispersion might undermine the potential gain from
the parametric conversion in the OPO. The combined gain and peaks in the TiS spectra
favoured signal and idler generation in the 20 mm crystal. The signal bandwidth could
be improved with dispersion compensation.
The tunability of the cavity was explored, showing some correspondence between the
experimental measurements and theoretical standpoints. The tuning slope measured 375
nm
mm and 710 nm
mm for the 20 and 10 mm crystals, respectively, compared to the theoretical
values at 420 nm
mm and 840 nm
mm, suggesting that some of the models approximations have
an off-set. The additional manipulation of the phase matching conditions through tem-
perature proved helpful in extracting the maximum gain from the TiS pump, regardless
of the spectral distribution, which varied throughout the project.
The intended idler amplification scheme was not entirely successful for either crystal case:
In the 20 mm PPLN, the idler power was at a strong 6.5 mW from the start, but dropped
immediately following the linear laser threshold. The shorter PPLN crystal amplified the
idler, but the maximal power was only 4.7 mW. The results indicate that the amplification
may be improved by expanding the beam at the YAG crystal, thereby increasing the CW
lasing threshold.
An intensity autocorrelator was used to investigate the signal pulse length progression
as the linear gain is added to the mode-locked OPO. Using the transform-limited ap-
proximation, the pulse length is reduced from 1.9 to 1.67 ps for the 10 mm case, and
from 4.5 to 3.8 ps for the 20 mm. The TBP is at least 2x above the transform limited
for either the Gaussian or sech2 shaped approximation, indicating a chirped pulse from
cavity dispersion.
In conclusion, the cavity was thoroughly studied in multiple aspects. A deeper under-
standing of the cavity output has been attained, and potential modifications can improve
the desired amplification of IR pulse generation.
