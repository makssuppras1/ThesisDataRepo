Abstract
Reproducing Kernel Hilbert C*-modules (RKHMs) serve as a generalization of Reproduc-
ing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHSs), where kernel functions map to elements of a C*-algebra
instead of the complex numbers. Hashimoto et al. [5] suggests that this generalization pos-
sesses new applications in the context of more structured data, such as functional data.
This Thesis studies the underlying theory of C*-algebras and Hilbert C*-modules to re-
cover the results of Hashimoto et al. [5].
Applications of RKHS theory in practice rely on the Representer Theorem, which states
that solutions to regularized empirical risk minimization problems lie in the span of kernel
sections with the observed data. A proposed generalization of this result, namely Gener-
alized Representer Theorem in [5], argues that a similar property is attained in RKHMs.
The Theorem relies on the assumption that the C*-span of a finite collection of elements is
a closed Hilbert C*-submodule. This thesis argues that this is an overly crude assumption
in the context of RKHMs, which limits the applicability of RKHMs in practice.
For an application of RKHM in functional data analysis, this thesis considers PCA for
a C*-valued kernel which maps to L∞([0, 1]). This thesis proves a trivialization of the
principal components in the RKHM, namely that they are given as point-wise solutions
to the equivalent PCA formulation in an RKHS.
In total, the thesis argues that the current state of the suggested data analysis framework
in RKHMs does not prove applicable in Machine Learning.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In recent decades, kernel methods have become a central component of modern machine
learning, providing a framework for linear modeling under a non-linear feature transfor-
mation [6]. These methods are based on the theory of Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces
(RKHSs), which connects kernel functions with Hilbert spaces, enabling algorithms in e.g.,
classification, regression, and dimensionality reduction. However, classical RKHS theory
considers complex-valued functions and inner products, limiting its ability to model more
complex or structured data types, such as functional data [5].
A possible extension of this framework is provided by Reproducing Kernel Hilbert C*-
Modules (RKHMs), which generalize RKHSs by allowing inner products to take values in
a C*-algebra rather than in the complex numbers. Originating from the theory of Hilbert
C*-modules, Murphy [9] introduced the RKHM generalization in 1997. While theoretical
properties of Hilbert C*-modules and RKHMs have been studied, their connection to
machine learning has remained largely unexplored.
In their 2021 paper ”Reproducing kernel Hilbert C*-module and kernel mean embeddings”,
Hashimoto et al. [5] appear to be the first to propose concrete applications of the theory of
RKHMs to practical machine learning problems. Their work introduces a mathematical
generalization of kernel methods that could, in principle, model functional or operator-
valued data.
This thesis aims to provide a detailed and critical examination of the theoretical framework
introduced in Hashimoto et al. [5], with a focus on clarifying key definitions, results, and
assumptions. Our goal is twofold: First, we seek to make the theory of RKHMs more
accessible by grounding it in the more well-established theory of operator algebras and
Hilbert C*-modules. Second, we explore the potential for applications in machine learning,
specifically in functional data analysis. This detailed study of the foundation of RKHMs
turns out to be essential, as we discover several, potentially invalidating, shortcomings in
Hashimoto et al. [5] framework.
A key result in Hashimoto et al. [5] is the Generalized Representer Theorem, which enables
practical applications of the theory. This result relies on an assumption that may seem
reasonable to readers only familiar with Hilbert space theory, but proves to be critical, and
possibly over-restrictive, when extended to the generalized setting of Hilbert C*-modules,
hence limiting the practical value.
In the cases where the assumption holds, we present the generalization of kernel PCA
proposed in Hashimoto et al. [5], and apply it with the Lebesgue space L∞([0, 1]) as the

Chapter 2
C*-Algebras
Before delving further into the concepts presented by Hashimoto et al. [5], a foundation
in the theory of C*-algebras is needed to understand Hilbert C*-modules.
While the
algebraic structure and basic definition of a C*-algebra as seen in Hashimoto et al. [5] are
conceptually accessible, the underlying theory is significantly more intricate.
At a high level, a C*-algebra is a normed set equipped with operations of addition, multipli-
cation, scalar multiplication, and an involution (an operation similar to that of conjugate
transpose). These operations mimic the behavior of objects such as complex numbers or
bounded linear operators on Hilbert spaces, and are said to generalize complex numbers.
A well-known example of a C*-algebra is the space of n × n complex matrices, denoted
Mn(C). This example allows us to draw on intuition from linear algebra. As we will see
in later chapters, many of the key results about spectrum (Eigenvalues of matrices) and
positivity in C*-algebras have direct analogues in familiar matrix or function spaces.
The chapter begins with an introduction to C*-algebras and the spectrum of an element.
Afterwards, we introduce the continuous functional calculus for normal elements in a C*-
algebra, which plays a central role in the proofs for the properties of positive elements.
Most importantly, we will establish three key results concerning positive elements in a
C*-algebra: (i) an element is positive if and only if its spectrum is contained in the non-
negative real numbers, (ii) every positive element admits a unique positive square root,
and (iii) the concept of positive elements induces a partial ordering on a C*-algebra. The
existence of a partial ordering allows us to construct optimization problems where the
objective is C*-valued.
The theory of C*-algebras presented in this project is based on and follows the book ”An
Introduction to Operator Algebras” Zhu [19]. Algebras in this thesis are always considered
over the field C and often assumed to be unital (existence of a multiplicative unit), which is
different from the standard setting but similar to Zhu [19]. To build intuition and prepare
for later applications involving RKHMs, we introduce examples based on the Lebesgue
space L∞([0, 1]).

2.1.2
Spectrum
This subsection presents the spectrum of an element in a Banach algebra. One can consider
this a generalization of the concept of eigenvalues for operators. The spectrum is central
in describing positive elements and the continuous functional calculus in a C*-algebra.
Definition 2.1.6 (Spectrum and Resolvent). Suppose A is a Banach algebra and x ∈A.
The spectrum denoted σ(x), is defined as the set of complex numbers λ ∈C, such that
λ1A −x is not invertible.
The resolvent is defined as the complement of the spectrum, i.e. ρ(x) := C\σ(x).
The following results concern the spectrum of the inverse.
Proposition 2.1.7. Suppose x is an invertible element in a Banach algebra A. Then
σ(x−1) = {λ−1 | λ ∈σ(x)}
Proof. First we show that if λ ∈σ(x), then λ−1 ∈σ(x−1).
In order to arrive at a
contradiction, take λ ∈σ(x) and assume λ−11A −x−1 is invertible.
Since λ−11A −x−1 is invertible,
(λ−11A −x−1)(λ−11A −x−1)−1 = 1A.
Multiplying with −λx from left and −λ−1x−1 from right gives,
(λ1A −x)(λ−11A −x−1)−1(−λ−1x−1) = 1A.
The above shows that λ1A −x is invertible, which contradicts that λ ∈σ(x).
By symmetry of the proof σ(x−1) = {λ−1 | λ ∈σ(x)}.
The subsection concludes with an example that finds the spectrum of an element in
L∞([0, 1]).
Example 2.1.8. Consider the C* algebra L∞([0, 1]) presented in Example 2.1.4 and the
function f ∈L∞([0, 1]), f(t) = et. Since ||f||∞= e, Corollary A.1.9 states that |λ| ≤e
for all λ ∈σ(f).
According to Definition 2.1.6 of the spectrum, we aim to find λ ∈C, such that λ1 −f is
not invertible.
Following that f is continuous, so is the function λ1 −f. In Example A.1.7, we saw that
a continuous function was not invertible when 0 was contained in the range. For λ ∈C,
(λ1 −f)([0, 1]) = [λ −1, λ −e], thus σ(f) = [1, e].
Generally for a continuous function g ∈L∞([0, 1]), the inverse is well defined for λ /∈
g([0, 1]) see Example A.1.7. We conclude that the spectrum of g is equal to the range.

The Continuous Functional Calculus
The continuous functional calculus defines a concept of applying complex-valued functions
to elements in a C*-algebra. It will allow us to interpret normal elements in C*-algebras
through continuous functions defined on their spectrum. In this section, we develop the
foundations of continuous functional calculus to utilize its properties to characterize pos-
itive elements in the next section.
To keep the main chapter focused, the intermediate results leading to the continuous
functional calculus are presented separately in Appendix A. For readers unfamiliar with
the contents of the Appendix, we now provide a summary both to highlight the main results
that we will utilize in this section and, equally important, to introduce the notation.

Summary
For a C*-algebra A we introduced the set of all characters Φ(A) (Definition A.2.1) where
a character ϕ ∈Φ(A) was a linear functional with the additional property that
ϕ(xy) = ϕ(x)ϕ(y),
∀x, y ∈A.

Chapter 3
Hilbert C* Modules
Recalling Chapter 2, we mentioned that C*-algebras are a generalization of the com-
plex numbers. This perspective becomes especially clear when working with Hilbert C*-
modules. Hilbert C*-modules can be seen as a generalization of Hilbert spaces where the
complex numbers are replaced by elements of a C*-algebra A. In other words, vector
addition and scalar multiplication are preserved, but scalar multiplication now involves
elements of A. While this shift preserves some of the Hilbert space properties, it also
introduces differences. Understanding these distinctions is crucial when working with Re-
producing Kernel Hilbert C*-modules (RKHMs) and their applications, as some familiar
results from Hilbert space theory no longer apply in this broader setting.
We begin by introducing the basic definitions of Hilbert C*-modules and the essential
properties of the C*-valued inner product. A particularly important example used re-
peatedly throughout this thesis is the Hilbert C*-module An, given as the direct sum of
n ∈N copies of a C*-algebra A. This structure serves as a generalization of Cn and plays
a central role in representing ”finite-dimensional” submodules.
We then turn to operators between Hilbert C*-modules for which the adjoint exists. This
leads to results regarding orthogonal complements of submodules, which will be discussed
in relation to finitely generated Hilbert C*-submodules.
Here, it becomes essential to
distinguish between algebraic and topological generation, a subtle but important nuance
unique to the Hilbert C*-module framework. This distinction will be of particular im-
portance in the coming discussion of the Generalized Representer theorem in the next
chapter.
The theory in this chapter is based on the book ”Hilbert C-Modules: A Toolkit for Opera-
tor Algebraists” by Lance [8]. The focus is to provide a minimal but self-contained selection
of definitions and results necessary for the construction and application of RKHMs in later
chapters.

3.1
Basic Definitions
To familiarize the reader with the concept, we pay special attention to showing that the
definition of a Hilbert C*-module is indeed well defined when considering an arbitrary
C*-algebra. Similarly to Hilbert spaces, we will show a variation of the Cauchy-Schwarz
inequality for C*-valued inner products.
We start by introducing the underlying algebraic structure, known as a C*-module.
Definition 3.1.1 ((Rigth) C*-module). Consider an abelian group (M, +), a C*-algebra
A and an operation · : M × A →M. We say, M is a C*-module over A if:
1. (u + v) · a = u · a + v · a for all a ∈A and u, v ∈M.
2. v · (a + b) = v · a + v · b for all a, b ∈A and v ∈M.
3. v · ab = (v · a) · b for all a, b ∈A and v ∈M.

3.2
Linear Operators on Hilbert C*-modules
In Hilbert space theory, linear operators are central in understanding the structure of
subspaces. This extends to Hilbert C*-modules, where adjointable operators serve as ana-
logues of bounded linear operators in Hilbert spaces. However, subtle differences emerge
due to the generality of C*-algebras.
In this section, we explore linear maps between Hilbert C*-modules, focusing on adjointable
operators. These are maps that, like bounded operators on Hilbert spaces, admit a well-
defined adjoint satisfying an inner product identity. Adjointable operators are central to
the module-theoretic framework and are necessary for many structural results, such as
defining projections and studying decompositions.
A key concept is that of an orthogonally complemented submodule, a generalization of
closed subspaces in Hilbert spaces which is a direct summand. Unlike in Hilbert spaces,
not every closed submodule of a Hilbert C*-module is orthogonally complemented, and
understanding when such complements exist is important.
In this section, we introduce the basic theory of adjointable operators, define orthogonal
complements in the module context, and discuss conditions under which the image of an
operator is orthogonally complemented. These ideas will be foundational for later sections,
particularly in understanding how principal components behave in RKHMs.

Chapter 4
Reproducing Kernel Hilbert

Reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHSs) connect Hilbert space theory and kernel meth-
ods. A fundamental strength of RKHSs is that given a kernel function k : X ×X →C, there
exists a unique corresponding RKHS where the inner product attains the reproducing prop-
erty. In practice, this means that the inner product of a potentially infinite-dimensional
function space is determined by evaluations of the kernel function.
The Representer Theorem guarantees that for certain optimization problems in RKHSs,
solutions lie in the finite-dimensional span of kernel sections k(·, x) evaluated at observed
data points [15]. These two properties, (i) the ability to define function spaces through
kernels, and (ii) the computability of solutions in terms of observed data, are key to the
practical relevance of RKHSs.
In this chapter, we generalize the RKHS framework to the setting of Reproducing Kernel
Hilbert C*-Modules (RKHMs). Here, inner products and kernel functions map to elements
in a C*-algebra A. This abstract setting is suggested to arise when dealing with operator-
valued data, and has the potential to extend the applications of kernel methods in domains
such as functional data analysis [5].
First, we introduce the basic definitions of RKHMs and C*-valued positive-definite kernels.
Similarly to the RKHS construction in Appendix B, we define a feature map ϕ : X →
F(X, A) and show that the A-span of ϕ(X), together with a suitable A-valued inner
product, forms a pre-Hilbert C*-module which inherits the reproducing property. Similarly
to RKHSs, we can complete this pre-Hilbert C* module to obtain an RKHM
Further, we address one of the most important theoretical questions in this framework: to
what extent does the Representer Theorem generalize in the RKHM setting? We present
the version of the theorem proposed by Hashimoto et al. [5], which states that the solutions
to regularized learning problems in RKHMs can be written as finite A-linear combinations
of the kernel sections. However, the theorem relies on the assumptions that the kernel
sections of forms a finitely algebraically generated Hilbert C*-submodule. We critically
examine this assumption and demonstrate that this condition fails in general, even when
considering Von Neumann algebras. Through an example involving L∞([0, 1]), we show
how non-closed A-spans arise and how this undermines the general applicability of the
theorem.

4.2
Representer Theorem in RKHMs and Implications
Considering RKHSs, the Representer Theorem plays a central role in connecting the ab-
stract construction of the space with practical computation.
It allows one to express
the solution to a regularized risk minimization problem in terms of kernel evaluations
at observed data points {x1, . . . , xn} [15]. This result is crucial for making RKHS-based
methods applicable to real-world machine learning problems.
In the context of RKHMs, an analogous Representer Theorem is not just desirable - it is
essential. Without such a result, it becomes infeasible to compute solutions or to develop
practical learning algorithms in this more general setting.
Hashimoto et al. [5] provides a version of the Representer Theorem for RKHMs, stated as
Theorem 4.8 in their paper. While this may initially appear to be a satisfactory general-
ization, the proof relies on an assumption that, although seemingly harmless, turns out to
be highly nontrivial and even problematic in the full generality of C*-algebras and Hilbert
C*-modules.
In this section, we present Theorem 4.8 from Hashimoto et al. [5] and examine the underly-
ing assumption in detail. We will clarify why this assumption poses a serious obstruction
to the general applicability of the result and discuss its implications for RKHM-based
machine learning.

Chapter 5
Applications of RKHMs in
Functional Data Analysis
In the previous chapter, we investigated the theoretical foundations of Reproducing Ker-
nel Hilbert C*-Modules (RKHMs), culminating in a critical analysis of the generalized
Representer Theorem proposed by Hashimoto et al. [5]. We demonstrated that the the-
orem does not hold in full generality; therefore, this assumption requires a case-by-case
verification for the specific dataset and C*-valued kernel.
Despite these challenges, it remains valuable to explore whether the RKHM framework
can yield useful insights and computational tools in cases where the assumptions do hold.
Therefore, we proceed in this chapter under the assumption that it holds. Accordingly,
the results in this chapter should be interpreted as exploratory: they illustrate what is
computationally and conceptually possible within the RKHM framework. This chapter
presents an application of PCA in an RKHM in the context of functional data analysis,
where input functions are modeled in L2([0, 1]) and kernel values lie in the C*-algebra
L∞([0, 1]).
PCA is a method commonly used in statistics and machine learning. In multivariate data
analysis, PCA provides a lower-dimensional approximation of a dataset by projecting it
onto a subspace of reduced dimensionality [18]. Specifically, datapoints in Rn are projected
onto an r-dimensional subspace U ⊂Rn, where r ≤n see Figure 5.1.
PCA was later generalized to find principal components in a reproducing kernel Hilbert
space, known as Kernel PCA by Schölkopf et al. [14]. See Figure 5.2 for an example.

5.2
L∞([0, 1])-valued Kernels
In this section, we show how to explicitly compute the principal components in an RKHM
when the underlying C*-algebra is the Lebesgue space L∞([0, 1]). This setting is of par-
ticular interest in Hashimoto et al. [5], where it is motivated as a functionally defined
similarity measure for functional data analysis.
We will show that instead of working with abstract elements of an RKHM, we can reduce
the problem to a family of finite-dimensional optimization problems, one for each point
t ∈[0, 1]. Each of these problems takes place in the finite-dimensional Hilbert space Cn,
enabling the use of standard linear algebra techniques.
The key tool in this section is the structure of L∞([0, 1]) as a commutative C*-algebra.
We will exploit the point-wise behavior to show that the optimization problem in Theorem
5.1.4 admits a closed-form solution: the principal components correspond to the leading
eigenvectors of a matrix-valued function G2(t) for each t ∈[0, 1].
Hashimoto et al. [5] proposed an iterative variant of gradient descent in the context of
RKHMs to solve the optimization problem, which finds the principal components seen in
Definition 5.1.1. Our approach yields principal components that are not only exact but
also computationally eﬀicient to obtain. This section presents the theoretical results.

Chapter 6
Discussion
This project has presented a detailed study of reproducing kernel Hilbert C*-modules
(RKHMs), motivated by their proposed generalization of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces
(RKHSs).
Our goal was to investigate their potential applications in functional data
analysis. From the theory of C*-algebras and Hilbert C*-modules developed in Chapters
2 and 3, we demonstrated that a C*-valued kernel gives rise to a well-defined RKHM. These
closely mirror many classical properties of RKHSs: a C*-valued kernel defines a feature
map into a C*-valued function space, and the RKHM satisfies a reproducing property,
allowing point-wise evaluation of functions via inner products with kernel sections.
Building on this foundation, we considered the Representer Theorem, which plays a cen-
tral role in kernel-based methods within machine learning. In the classical RKHS setting,
this theorem ensures that solutions to a broad class of regularized empirical risk mini-
mization problems lie in the finite-dimensional span of kernel sections corresponding to
the observed data points. In other words, the theorem provides an explicit representa-
tion of the elements of interest in the RKHS, making it the essential bridge between the
abstract theory and practical applications. Hashimoto et al. [5] proposed a Generalized
Representer Theorem for the RKHM framework. However, we found that this result relies
on a critical assumption: namely, that the A-span of the kernel sections {k(·, xi)}n
i=1 form
a closed submodule of the RKHM. This, in turn, implies that the submodule is a finitely
algebraically generated Hilbert C*-module and is therefore orthogonally complemented.
This assumption is nontrivial and, as we have demonstrated, does not hold in general.
The challenge of verifying this assumption becomes especially apparent in concrete settings
involving more than a single element. The closedness of the submodule generated by kernel
sections depends on the choice of C*-algebra A, the structure of the C*-valued kernel, and
the specific observed data points. Even for a single data point, problems can arise when the
kernel evaluation k(x, x) yields an element of A whose spectrum is not bounded away from
zero. In such cases, the generated submodule may fail to be closed, and the Representer
Theorem is no longer applicable. Importantly, this case is neither rare nor special, but
rather a consequence of the generality of C*-algebras and Hilbert C*-modules.
In an attempt to mitigate the versatility of general C*-algebras, Hashimoto et al. [5]
suggests that working within the framework of von Neumann modules (Hilbert C*-modules
equipped with additional topological structure), provides additional properties required
for data analysis, and help ensure the key assumption of the Representer Theorem holds.
However, we note that no result is provided in Hashimoto et al. [5], which establishes that
a von Neumann algebra-valued kernel necessarily gives rise to an RKHM that is itself a
68
Reproducing Kernel Hilbert C*-Modules and Applications within Machine Learning
==PAGE:77==
von Neumann module. As such, the connection between von Neumann algebra-valued
kernels and what might be termed “reproducing kernel von Neumann modules” remains
an open and unaddressed problem.
Moreover, while the added structure of von Neumann modules is mathematically appealing
and shares certain desirable features with Hilbert spaces, we argued through a counterex-
ample that it is insuﬀicient in resolving the fundamental issue.
Even in the principal
example highlighted by the authors, the commutative von Neumann algebra L∞([0, 1]),
the closedness assumption fails. Taken together, these observations make it diﬀicult to
argue for the general applicability of the Representer Theorem in its current formulation.
Despite these limitations, we explored the application of RKHMs for functional data anal-
ysis. We considered functional data valued in L2([0, 1]), and constructed a kernel with
values in L∞([0, 1]). Under the assumption that the Representer Theorem holds, we de-
rived an RKHM-based formulation of kernel PCA. We provided a new explicit solution to
PCA RKHM, where interestingly, the resulting optimization problem reduces point-wise
to a family of classical kernel PCA problems. This trivialization arises as a consequence
of the algebraic structure of the Lebesgue space L∞([0, 1]), and in this case, RKHMs ef-
fectively behave as bundles of RKHSs, indexed by the underlying domain [0, 1]. In other
words, each point in t ∈[0, 1] gives rise to a standard RKHS problem.
More generally, by [19, Theorem 22.6], every commutative von Neumann algebra is C*-
isomorphic to an L∞space over some measure space; hence, this reduction is not unique
to our example. This suggests that in any commutative setting with the restriction to Von
Neumann algebras, RKHM-based methods offer no fundamentally new modeling capacity.
Rather, they reorganize existing methods into an abstract framework. This is particularly
evident when comparing RKHM solutions with standard techniques for hyperparameter
sweeps across multiple kernels, a task that data scientists already perform without the
added complexity of C*-algebraic structures.
This observation raises a more critical question: What, if anything, is gained by intro-
ducing RKHMs in machine learning applications? At present, the theoretical machinery
required to define and work with RKHMs is substantial, and the assumptions needed to
obtain computable results, such as a valid Representer Theorem, are diﬀicult to verify or
simply false in concrete cases. Without a deeper understanding of when a kernel generates
a finitely algebraically generated Hilbert C*-submodule, or a class of kernels where this
structure is guaranteed, the practical utility of RKHMs remains limited.
In our view, the current theory of RKHMs presents a mathematically elegant but overly
abstract framework, whose application to learning problems is, at best, unproven. While
the generalization is theoretically clean, it has yet to deliver insights or methods that
go beyond what is already available through classical RKHS theory. For this reason, we
believe that the future of RKHMs as a practical tool depends on the development of
either (i) concrete kernel constructions tailored to C*-algebras with verifiable structural
properties, or (ii) a reworking of the Representer Theorem under weaker or more general
assumptions. Until then, the gap between theory and application remains significant.
6.1
Conclusion
C*-valued kernels give rise to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert C*-modules (RKHMs), which
generalize Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHSs) by replacing complex-valued inner
products with C*-valued inner products.
However, exchanging complex numbers with
C*-algebras introduces different structural properties compared to RKHSs.
One such
Reproducing Kernel Hilbert C*-Modules and Applications within Machine Learning
69
==PAGE:78==
difference is that the A-span of a finite series is not necessarily closed. This difference is
critical, as the Generalized Representer Theorem proposed in Hashimoto et al. [5] depends
on exactly this assumption. As a consequence, this needs to be verified case-by-case, which
heavily limits the applicability of the result in Machine Learning. Applying RKHM PCA
using the commutative C*-algebra L∞([0, 1]), we proved that the problem reduced to a
point-wise reformulation of standard kernel methods, similarly to a hyperparameter sweep
over multiple kernels. Therefore, this thesis concludes that in its current form, the RKHM
framework is not proven applicable in Machine Learning.

Appendix B
Reproducing Kernel Hilbert

This Appendix introduces reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The main result
is the theorem ”Kernel generated RKHS,” which states that a positive definite kernel
corresponds to a unique RKHS. The article Hashimoto et al. [5] briefly defines a ”positive
definite kernel” and its associated feature space as an inner product space. The article
simply states that any positive definite kernel defines a specific RKHS given the completion
of the feature space, which is both an abstract result and quite technical to prove. Showing
that the map on the feature space defines an inner product and the fact that the completion
of the feature space is an RKHS is not trivial. Therefore, this section aims not only to
define RKHSs and kernels in the context of RKHSs, but also to give the reader through
understanding of how exactly one implicitly works with an RKHS when utilizing kernel
methods in machine learning.
In the literature, naming conventions used for kernels and RKHSs are quite widespread.
For instance, these kernels are both referred to as ”Positive definite kernels”, ”Positive
semi-definite kernels”, and sometimes simply ”Kernel functions” [11]. In operator theory,
a Positive semi-definite matrix is a positive definite operator, which might confuse readers
with different backgrounds, such as one working primarily with machine learning versus
mathematics.
To avoid confusion, this text will opt for the approach used in Paulsen and Raghupathi
[11]. Therefore, we refer to kernels in the context of RKHSs as ”Kernel functions”. What
exactly defines a ”Kernel function” will become clear in subsequent sections.
An inner product can serve as a similarity measure between data points, for instance, the
cosine similarity. From a machine learning point of view, one usually considers a specific
kernel function which is then utilized to construct methods that are linear on the RKHS [6].
Kernel functions serve as generalized similarity measures, that is, they represent an inner
product. Furhter, kernel functions enable the definition of feature maps and corresponding
feature spaces that naturally inherit an inner product structure.
This chapter begins by defining RKHSs as Hilbert spaces of complex-valued functions
with the condition that all evaluation functionals are bounded. We will delve into the
construction and properties of feature spaces, demonstrating how they form inner prod-
uct spaces when equipped with an appropriately defined inner product. This leads to
the establishment of the RKHS as the completion of the feature space, maintaining the

B.2
The Quotient Space of Cauchy Sequences
To construct the completion of the feature space, we introduce a quotient space generated
by the set of Cauchy sequences on the feature space. We begin by defining an equivalence
relation on the set of Cauchy sequences in the feature space, identifying sequences that
converge to the same limit. The quotient space Hk is then formed by considering the set
of equivalence classes under this relation.
This construction leads to a space where each element is represented by an equivalence
class of Cauchy sequences.
We proceed to show that this quotient space inherits the

This appendix formalizes groups, rings, fields, and Algebras. Furthermore, it provides

E.1
Groups, Rings and Fields
First, we define groups, rings, and fields.
Definition E.1.1 (Group). Let G be a set and + a binary operation of G then the ordered
pair (G, +) is a group if it satisfies the following axioms.
1. Let a, b, c ∈G, then (a + b) + c = a + (b + c) (associative).
2. There exists an identity element e such that a + e = e + a = a for all a ∈G.
3. For each a ∈G there an inverse element denoted a−1 ∈g s.t a + a−1 = a−1 + a = e.
Definition E.1.2 (Abelian group). A Group (G, +) is called an abelian group if, a + b =
b + a for all a, b ∈G.
Definition E.1.3 (Ring). A ring R is a set equipped with two binary operations: addition
+ : R × R →R and multiplication · : R × R →R. It satisfies the following properties:
1. The set forms an Abelian group under addition.
2. Multiplication is associative: (a · b) · c = a · (b · c) for all a, b, c ∈R.
3. Multiplication distributes over addition: a·(b+c) = a·b+a·c and (b+c)·a = b·a+c·a
for all a, b, c ∈R.
4. There exists an identity element 1 such that a · 1 = 1 · a = a for all a ∈R.
Definition E.1.4 (Field). Let K be a set together with two operation additions + :
K × K →K and multiplication · : K × K →K. Then K is a field if K is a commutative
ring, and if for each element α ∈K where α ̸= 0 there exists a multiplicative inverse
denoted α−1 = 1/α ∈K such that αα−1 = 1.
