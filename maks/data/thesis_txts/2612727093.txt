Abstract
Transportation and energy sectors are responsible for a large amount of emissions.
The growing need for carbon free technologies is fast changing both sectors. The
success of this change is interdependent on both of them. The energy sector might
solve partially the storage and imbalance problems with the integration of electric
vehicles. In addition, pollution generated by internal combustion vehicles may be
reduced dramatically with this integration. The electric vehicle integration is chal-
lenging and it might cause congestion to road and energy network. Strict policies
for the installation and the use of public charging sites should be applied. There is
clearly the need for a tool which is able to simulate realistically the needs of a private
electric fleet. This thesis modifies the existing electric vehicle simulation framework
of MATSim. The existed framework is set for long distance trips, while the focus
in this study is on Frederiksberg municipality which is part of Greater Copenhagen
Area, meaning that, short trips are the vast majority in this case of study. The thesis
starts by discussing the motivation and the objectives of the study, followed up by
the presentation of related macroscopic and microscopic studies. MATSim and its
electric vehicle extension is introduced. The creation of MATSim’s input for the local
Danish setting is described. The selection of 25 different scenarios and their input
parameters are explained. Finally, results on predetermined metrics for every sce-
nario are discussed, and a more detailed analysis for 3 selected scenarios is presented.
The results showed that the tool behaves according to the expected behavior which
is defined by the input parameters.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In this chapter the challenges of Electric Vehicle (EV) integration are presented. Also
the motivation for writing this master thesis. Finally, the objectives of the project
are stated and the structure of the thesis is introduced.
1.1
Challenges and Motivation
As the climate on earth changes mainly by human activities and particularly from the
usage of fossil fuels (Sims, 2004), the increasing demand of carbon free technologies
will not extinguish the next decades. The transportation sector is identified as one
of the main causes of Greenhouse Gasses (GHG) emissions causing global warming,
and local pollution. In Europe during 2015, 23% of the total emissions were produced
by the transportation sector. In addition transportation sector was the only sector
which demonstrated an increase in total emissions during the previous years. This
contributes significantly to climate change and if actions are not taken in the near
future to control the trend, a deterioration of the increased emissions phenomenon is
certain. (Ashkrof, Almeida Correia, and Arem, 2020).
A part of the solution could be the integration of Electric Vehicle (EV) as the main
mode of transport. EVs are wheeled vehicles which use an electric motor powered by
a battery to convert electrical energy into mechanical energy (Clairand et al., 2019).
These vehicles must be recharged to function. This charging activity commonly takes
part at home, at work, or in public chargers. Especially, EVs do not burden the
local environment directly, and they also have a remarkable well-to-wheel energy
efficiency compared to internal combustion vehicles. Nevertheless, the lithium and
cobalt metals, which are used in the battery, create negative environmental impact in
terms of toxicity. For this reason, it is critical to develop technologies that increase the
life expectancy of the battery, and also develop suitable tools to recycle it. Regardless
these issues, EVs can lower down GHG emissions in most generation mix scenarios
due to the high well-to-wheel energy efficiency (Clairand et al., 2019).
Despite the several advantages, EVs also create issues. The daytime charging will
mostly take place at public chargers located in streets and at least one parking spot
is needed to perform this action.
However, the parking supply in the city is not
infinite, and particularly, in the city center the number of simultaneously charging
vehicles is limited. In addition the seek for charging at public spaces will eventually
==PAGE:20==
2
1 Introduction
lead to waiting queues for plugging-in and it might alternate the observed congestion
(Marmaras, Xydas, and Cipcigan, 2017).
On top of that, high EV penetration rate could generate several problems to the
grid. A massive EV introduction could create voltage fluctuations and instability to
the distribution system. In addition, EVs integration could increase significantly the
distributed load in the grid and due to that, increase the power losses. Moreover,
most trips finish at the same time as the peak of electricity demand, which means
that the power system will struggle to generate high amounts of energy if the cars
are plugged immediately after the end of the trip (Bellekom et al., 2012). Further-
more, if the power required to charge the EVs is produced by fossil fuels then the
positive environmental impact decreases dramatically and even vanishes (Clairand
et al., 2019).
However, the energy production from Variable Renewable Energy (VRE) sources,
such as solar and wind is expected to continue increasing in the next decades. These
technologies will become the main energy sources towards a carbon free power system.
The Danish state aims to 73% VRE share by the year 2050 (Gea-Bermúdez et al.,
2019). Especially, in the North Sea a vast offshore wind energy penetration is expected
soon. The increased share of VRE technologies in the grid creates new conditions in
the system since these technologies are inherently intermittent.
This creates uncertainty in the power system, and imbalances are occurred and
challenge its reliability and security. These imbalances are expected to continue grow-
ing the following years (Kanellas et al., 2020). Thus, flexible power plants are vital
to produce electricity when the VRE production is low, and also storage technologies
are required when surplus electricity is produced. Batteries of EVs can be used to
store this excess of energy. (Bellekom et al., 2012).
Combining an increase in VRE with the penetration of electric vehicles can lead
to significant reduction of GHG emissions. Connecting EVs to the grid enables the
option of charging their batteries when the electricity demand is low and there is a
surplus of electricity production. For example, during the night the electricity demand
is off peak, but the wind turbines still generate energy since there would be a waste
of 0 cost energy if they did not. In this case an excess of energy is produced and
it can be transmitted to EVs. Consequently the EVs are charged by ”clean” energy
which results to lower GHG emissions. In addition charging EVs during off peak is
a technique of load management and leads to a ”smarter” electricity demand. Hence,
the cooperation of VREs with EVs generates advances to both transport and energy
sector (Lund and Clark, 2008).

2.1
Macroscopic
In this section macroscopic studies are presented which try to capture the EV inte-
gration mainly in Denmark, but not only.
Christensen, Nørrelund, and Olsen, 2010 used the Danish National Travel Survey
and a GPS-based data to model the driving behavior of EV owners and the need
for charging. They concluded that EVs will be accepted as a first option vehicle if a
strict defined strategy for the placement of EV charging sites is applied.
An analysis for 100% EV penetration rate based on the Danish island of Bornholm
is presented in (González-Garrido et al., 2019). The analysis addresses the challenges
of a transportation system which shifts completely to electric power. It is shown that
smart charging techniques can significantly reduce the charging costs and battery
degradation.
In (Nicholas, Tal, and Woodjack, 2013) EV commute and charging is simulated
based on a survey in the state of California in the U.S.A. Different charging behaviors
taken into account. It is assumed that a free flow speed is achieved during commuting.
Results showed that the location of charging sites are highly correlated with the travel
patterns in urban areas.
A macroscopic multi-agent based simulator is developed in (Marmaras, Xydas,
and Cipcigan, 2017). The study is based on the macroscopic fundamental diagram,
and a detailed representation of EVs.
The impact of EV owner behavior on the
road network and on the electricity grid was simulated. The results showed that the
behavior of the agents affected directly or indirectly both the road network and the
power grid.
Fotouhi et al., 2019 described a general model for EV drivers charging behavior
based on data gathered from Denmark within 5 months. The model is applied in
several scenarios in a part of the urban area of London and in cities in North America,
in order to capture the congestion on charging sites.

2.2.1
MATSim
MATSim development started in early 2000’s. The goal of the project was to create
an open-source software which is able to simulate in a realistic way, transport demand
and supply.
Initially, MATSim supported only car mode and it was able to capture the traffic
conditions. Along the way, more developers joined the team and MATSim got further
developed. At this moment there are two main groups developing MATSim, one in
TU Berlin and one in ETH Zurich. Nowadays, MATSim has numerous extensions
each dealing with a specific aspect of transportation.
The first EV application on MATSim took place in (R. A. Waraich et al., 2012).
The study is based on the city of Zurich, where a real-live scenario implemented
with high resolution spatial detail.
In this early stages a discharging model was
not implemented in detail. The proposed framework allows investigation of smart
charging schemes. Similar applications of the implemented framework published in
(R. Waraich and K. Axhausen, 2013), where the authors proposed methods for the
optimization of charging locations.
Further application of the early stages of the
framework are also published in (Galus et al., 2009), where in this case, hybrid electric
vehicles are modelled. However, this model is out of date and its development has
terminated.
The first complete EV extension of MATSim is developed in (R. Waraich, Georges,
et al., 2014), ETH Zurich. Bischoff and Maciejewski, 2014 used this extension to
simulate the operation of an electric taxicab fleet in the Polish city of Milec. The
results showed that there is not a negative impact on the level of service due to the
required battery charging, however conventional taxi fleets are more capable of serving
peak demands. The extension suddenly did not get any further updates, and in 2018
a new electric vehicle extension for MATSim emerged, which is also the extension
that is used on this thesis.
Published studies applying the new extension are (Bischoff, Marquez-Fernandez,
et al., 2019, Marquez-Fernandez et al., 2019) in which a fleet to simulate long distance
trips across Sweden is modelled. They created a detailed charging and discharging
model allowing to study many aspects of EV integration, such as, charging supply
and demand. The results showed that a sufficient charging infrastructure could be
developed in the current locations of gas stations.
In (Vosooghi et al., 2020) a MATSim model is implemented for a shared au-
tonomous EV service located in Rouen Normandie metropolitan area in France. This
study showed that the performance of the service is highly correlated with the charg-
ing infrastructure.

CHAPTER 3
Simulation Framework
This chapter describes and explains the simulation tool called Multi-Agent Transport
Simulation (MATSim), and its electric vehicle extension framework.
3.1
Introduction to MATSim
As it is stated in the book written for MATSim ”MATSim is an activity-based, ex-
tendable, multi-agent simulation framework implemented in Java” (Horni, Nagel, and
K. Axhausen, 2016). MATSim is an open-source software and it can be used and
downloaded by anyone freely on the internet.
3.1.1
The Loop
Figure 3.1: The MATSim Cycle (Horni, Nagel, and K. Axhausen, 2016)
A MATSim run consists of a predetermined number of iterations, shown by the
graph of Figure 3.1. It begins with an initial demand concluded from population’s
daily activities of the area of interest. The set of people that belongs to the population
is the set of agents. Activities are commonly based on real demand data and they
are created via sampling or discrete choice modeling techniques. In each iteration,
every agent executes a plan which is a sequence of usually predetermined activities,
==PAGE:28==
10
3 Simulation Framework
and trips that connect them. After the execution of a plan, a score is matched with
the specific plan, expressing how beneficial was that plan.
Each agent has the ability to remember a fixed number of plans and their respec-
tive scores. The scoring is a tool that helps the agents optimize their initial demand
in the course of a run. In each iteration and before mobility simulation (mobsim)
(which is where the actual simulation happens), every agent chooses a plan from the
plan set which is stored in its memory. The choice is based on the plan score. A
predetermined fraction of the population is authorized to copy the chosen plan and
modify this copy, so this is where replanning takes place (Horni and K. Axhausen,
2012). If an agent at this point has more plans than the maximum allowed number,
then during the following iteration agent’s memory is increased by one plan, how-
ever the agent will drop one after the end of the iteration, in order to reach again
the maximum allowed number. Usually, the plan with the lowest score is dropped.
Agents that have not been selected for replanning chose from their current plan set.
Commonly, the plan selection model generates a logit distribution which is used to
select the plan for the next iteration. An iteration is finalized after the calculation of
selected plan’s score.
The previously described iterative process continues until the total number of iter-
ations is reached. This number is typically selected equal to the number of iterations
where the average population score stabilizes. The regular score curve is shown in
Figure 3.2.

3.2.1

3.2.1.1
Transport Demand
MATSim’s travel demand is described by the agents’ daily plans. The set of agents
is the entire population that commutes through the network. The plan consists of
activities and trip legs, commonly alternating with each other, since most of the times
the agents must commute from one activity to the other. However, if activities take
place at the exact same location,for instance, if someone is working from home, there
is the option in which activities can be consequent without a leg trip among them,
but in this case the model should be treated cautiously.
The different activity types that this thesis’s population takes part in are: work,
home, other, school, home charging, work charging, public charging, and missing.
Each activity takes place in a specific unique facility. The charging activities are the
only activities which are planned in a probabilistic framework during the simulation,
meaning that they are not included in the initial plans of the agents but only when an
agent decides to perform them. All other activities simply exist or not in the initial
plans of an agent, and they must be performed in every plan that the agent selects.
The leg modes that are allowed in this case of study are only car and walk. The
model does not distinguish between Conventional Vehicles (CVs) and EVs, since both
commute via the same network and the size, and the average speed in the city are
very similar.
However, a distinguish between the owners of CVs and EVs is applied. MATSim
gives the option to treat subsets of population differently, registering agents to unique
subpopulations. In this project 2 subpopulations exists, the EV subpopulation and
the CV subpopulation.

3.2.3.1
Car Routing Algorithm
For the rest of this subsection it is assumed that the reader has basic knowledge on
graph theory algorithms.
The car routing algorithm which is formed based on ALT (A∗+ Landmarks +
Triangle inequality) developed in (Goldberg and Harrelson, 2005).
A∗algorithm
(Hart, Nilsson, and Raphael, 1968) greedily selects the next vertex to be explored
according to an heuristic function and the cost until the current node n: f(n) =
g(n) + h(n), where g is the cost for reaching n and h the heuristic function that
estimates the cost from n to destination node. It can be said that Dijkstra’s algorithm
(Dijkstra, 1959) is a special case of A∗where h = 0. A∗solves always the shortest
path problem if and only if h is admissible, in other words, if h never overestimates the
cost for reaching destination, otherwise A∗can overlook the shortest path (Russell,
2003). If h is admissible then A∗will conclude to the same solution as Dijkstra’s
algorithm but notably faster since it explores a smaller part of the solution space.
The ALT algorithm uses a set of landmarks D, which are spread across the network.
The cost between every landmark and every node is pre-calculated bidirectionally and
stored. This means that 2 · |N| · |D| shortest paths estimations must be made in the
pre-processing. ALT applies an heuristic which is based on the triangle inequality,
where cs(N, M) is the lowest cost between any node N ∈N and any landmark D ∈D.
The triangle is formed by 2 nodes and a landmark, and the inequality has the form
of

3.2.3.2
Walk Router
MATSim also routes walk. However, walking does not take place in the car network,
rather than in beelines. So the walking router is not complex at all, and there is no
need of a specific algorithm to calculate the shortest path, since a beeline is always
the shortest path. The travel time between origin and destination for walk mode
depends on the beeline distance, the walking speed, and a beeline distance factor.
The walking speed is chosen equal to 1 m/s and the beeline distance factor is set to
1.3, explaining the fact that the commuted distance is usually higher than the beeline
distance.

The Node Model
Nodes in MATSim take no spatial space as aforementioned. They act as connectors
of upstream and downstream queues. The consistency of the node model with the
KWM is established in (Tampère et al., 2011). There, a set of 7 requirements is
specified in order to achieve consistency.
1. Flows must not be negative.
2. Flows must be in accordance to supply and demand constrains.
3. Flows must be conserved within a node.
4. Flows must exploit all the available space.
5. The invariance principle of (Lebacque and Khoshyaran, 2005) is satisfied. The
most important derivation of the principle is that the advancement of a queuing
vehicle is not affected by the vehicles behind it.
6. Exogenous turning constrains must be obeyed.
7. The model must be general enough to handle all kind o different intersections.
MATSim uses the algorithm in (Flötteröd and Rohde, 2011) to implement the node
model. There, is proposed a node model which is consistent with the abovementioned
requirements, and provides an efficient algorithm to achieve it. In every simulation
time step δ the algorithm forwards flows from upstream links to downstream links.
During time step δ all the previously mentioned requirements are satisfied.
The
algorithm halts if and only if there is no more flow to be moved during δ.

3.5
Replanning
Replanning is the process in which new plans, which does not belong to the plan
memory set yet, are scheduled. A plan that belongs in the memory set is chosen for
replanning generating a new plan. If the memory set is full then a plan is selected to
be removed from the memory. The scheduling of new plans and the removal of old
ones is discussed in this section.
In this project the agents have initially scheduled plans in which the location of
activities, the mode of transport for commuting between activities, the duration of
activities, and departure times, are fixed and do not change during any iteration, run,
or scenario. The only part in the plans that is allowed to alternate is the route.
Hence, in this context, replanning is referred only to the replanning of routes. In
this case of study, the plan with the best score is selected for replanning, and then
every leg of the plan, is replaced by a leg that has the shortest path based on the
previous iteration. When searching for the shortest path, the graph in which the
search is performed uses average travel times on links based on 15 minutes intervals.
A replanning takes place if the agent is selected to follow a reroute strategy for the
next iteration. In this case of study the rerouting strategy has 10% probability to be
chosen and the rest of the time the plan with the best score is selected. If the number
of plans is higher than the allowed number of plans then a plan is removed. In this
thesis the plan with the worst score is removed.
3.6
Analyses
MATSim’s cycle finishes with the analyses. In the analyses various stats and plots
can be extracted. Analysis usually takes place after a desired equilibrium state is
reached.
From this point on plan innovation (replanning) is terminated and the
model is stabilized in order to extract the needed information.
3.6.1
Equilibrium
MATSim is a dynamic activity based model, thus, an equilibrium state with strict
definition is hard to be achieved. The selection of a day plan is heavily based on
the scoring process, rather than trip durations. A halting criteria for MATSim does
not exist. The most common approach within MATSim’s community, is an approach
based on the executed plan’s score. MATSim is set to run for a predetermined number
of iterations based on the average score of the executed plan of every agent during
an iteration. When the average executed plan’s score does not change dramatically
(according to desired tolerance) then a MATSim equilibrium is reached (Horni, Nagel,
and K. Axhausen, 2016).

Charging Model
The EVE is a simulation framework created for the investigation of long distance trips
between cities. This means that assumptions for the charging behavior of the agents
are based on the fact that agents have to commute long distances. It is assumed that
each vehicle at the beginning of the day has a fully charged battery, since it is a long
distance trip and the agent fully charged the vehicle the previous night. In addition,
vehicles charge only in charging stations which lie on the motor ways, hence it is
assumed that there is always space to park in the station but not always available
charger.
==PAGE:44==

3.7.2.1
Charging Behavior
MATSim as aforementioned is activity based. Every agent has a set of activities to
fulfil. This set is predetermined before mobsim and during mobsim it can not be
modified. This means, that every agent has to schedule a charging activity before the
actual simulation.
For this reason at the beginning of the day each agent estimates the energy con-
sumption for the whole trip (link by link) based on the travel times of the previous
iteration. During this estimation, if there is a link in which the aggregate battery
consumption from the beginning of the tip until the current link reaches the battery
capacity, then the agent selects randomly between the 2 closest charging stations and
schedules a charging activity to the selected one.
It is assumed that the agent waits for the whole duration of charging at the charg-
ing station and during this time she can not perform any other activity. Furthermore,
the vehicle remains plugged until it is fully charged.
3.7.2.2
Battery Charging Profile
The power transfer during the charging of battery depends on the voltage V and the
current I. The product of their multiplication results to the power transfer (Mies,
Helmus, and Hoed, 2018).
The charging process of a Li-ion battery consists of three phases. The first phase
is a ’pre-charge’ phase in which I remains low while V is steadily increasing until
a SoC of 10% is reached.
If the initial SoC of the vehicle is over 10% then this
phase is negligible. In the first phase only a small part of the battery’s capacity is
charged. Most of the energy is transferred during the second and third phase (Yong
et al., 2015), which are illustrated in Figure 3.7.
In the second phase I remains
constantly high, and V is increasing until a high voltage level is reached. This phase
is also called constant current phase. During the third phase V is constantly high
and I dramatically decaying, thus, the charging speed is slower (Haidar and Muttaqi,
2016).

5.2.1
Varying Parameters
EV penetration rate (rp) is the most significant parameter. Not only directly affects
the number of EVs in the simulation, but it also, highly affects the computation
time. Penetration rate is allowed to take values between 0.01 (which approximates
the current situation in Frederiksberg) and 0.35. It is expected that the higher the
penetration rate, the higher the charging demand and the higher the expected queuing
time.
Max charging time (tmc) is the parameter which is equal to maximum allowed
parking duration in seconds, as explained in chapter 4. This parameter affects sig-
nificantly the time a charger is occupied. The allowed range for max charging time
parameter is set to (1,12) hours.
Total number of plugs (np) is the only parameter which directly affects the charg-
ing infrastructure. This means that, position and supplied power per plug of charging
sites, remains fixed. The parameter refers to the sum of plugs of all charging sites,

5.2.2
Sampling Method
The considered scenarios are defined by the combination of the varying parameters. A
sampling method to draw scenarios from, is required to form scenarios which explore
input space to a sufficient extent.
The selected method is Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS). LHS described for the
first time in (McKay, Beckman, and Conover, 1979).LHS is a sampling method which
generates a pseudo-random stratified sample of parameter values from a multidimen-
sional distribution. This sampling method is suitable for drawing parameter values
to form different simulation scenarios. (Menčı́k, 2016).
The 1-dimensional LHS is achieved by stratifying the cumulative density function
of a variable into equal intervals, and then selecting at random one data point in each
strata. In the example illustrated in Figure 5.5, one random value of F is selected in
each layer, resulting to 5 corresponding x values, which represent the sample. The
2-dimensional LHS (also called Latin square sampling) is achieved by following the
1-dimensional method for two variables and then combining into pairs the individual

5.2.3
The Runs
Each of the 25 scenarios is run 10 different times, using for each of the 10 times a
different random seed value, since the process is stochastic and is affected by random
variables. Meaning that, the MATSim equilibrium could be different and the decisions
on charging also are affected by the random seeds. Each run consists of 70 iterations,
split in 60 non-analyzed iterations and 10 which are part of the analysis. In the end,
100 (10·10) iterations (days) for each scenario are considered for the analysis process.
These iterations could be analyzed separately for each different random seed. For
reasons of simplicity one pool of 100 days is assumed.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
In this chapter the conclusion of the study takes place.
In the first section the
summary of the work is written. In the final section, future improvements on the
limitations of the model are discussed together with future applications of the tool.
7.1
Synopsis
As the need for carbon free technologies increases, the transportation sector should
play a main role in the transition to zero emissions society, since it is one of the
most polluting sectors. In this transition, the energy sector also plays a critical role.
Renewable energy technologies are emerging and the co-operation of the two sectors
could be mutually beneficial. Various renewable energy technologies are inherently
intermittent and an EV fleet could be used to utilize the nature of these technologies,
alongside with the zero local emissions that an EV fleet offers. However, the integra-
tion of EVs is challenging rather than trivia. The charging peak demand is expected
simultaneously with the electricity peak demand and this will generate heavy load
to the already burdened power network. In addition, the selection and placement of
charging infrastructure should be sufficient and cover the needs of the electric fleet.
For these reasons, the need of a consistent tool, which is able to analyze the whole
system, is significant. This thesis discussed and modified an already existing tool for
the purposes of urban transport electrification.
In chapter 2 related work was presented to inform the reader about the studies
which has been made. At first, some macroscopic studies taken place in Denmark
were discussed and then macroscopic studies abroad. The history of development of
microscopic simulation tool MATSim was noted together with any EV study which
used an EVE tool based on MATSim. Furthermore, other microscopic tools with the
capability of simulating an electric fleet are briefly presented.
Chapter 3 introduced MATSim mechanics. Moreover, the required initial demand
for MATSim and its EVE was described. Then the routing algorithms which are
responsible for founding shortest paths for car and walk mode was presented. Fur-
thermore, mobsim, which is the part of MATSim where the actual simulation takes
place, alongside principal kinematic theory were explained. In addition, the scoring
process of agents’ which helps identify a MATSim equilibrium was noted. The replan-
ning process which gives agents the ability to explore more routes within the network
==PAGE:86==
68

was described. Also in chapter 3 the long distance model of EVE of MATSim and its
models which rule charging and discharging were introduced.
Chapter 4 presented the input for the multi agent simulation. At the beginning,
a brief introduction of Frederiksberg’s municipality was made alongside with the
presentation of Frederiksberg’s location in the Danish country. The selection of the
road network and the set of agents, by the application of ray-casting algorithm, was
described. Simulation’s road network, and population was discussed by the help of
graphs and maps. At the end of chapter 4, modifications and additions to EVE, in
order to transform its application from long distance trips to short distance trips,
were explained.
In chapter 5 the selection of the different scenarios and performance metrics was
discussed. The parameters and the reasons why these parameters remain fixed among
all scenarios were presented first.
Then, the varying parameters were introduced,
which the combination of them actually defines the different scenarios. Next, the
LHS method for sampling the varying parameters was described.
The results of the simulations were presented in chapter 6. A discussion on the
over all metrics results took place at the beginning of the chapter and then, a scenario
with 0% EV penetration rate and 3 selected scenarios were discussed in more depth.
A scenario which resulted no queuing times was firstly considered and analyzed. Sec-
ondly, a scenario with high EV penetration rate was presented and lastly a scenario
with low average battery gain during charging activity. The results showed that the
model behaves according to the the input parameters. An EV penetration rate up to
35% does not seem to generate significant congestion differences compare to the 0%
EV penetration rate scenario.
7.2
Future Work
As aforementioned this thesis should be considered as an initial effort for creating a
tool which can analyze, predict, and optimize the mechanics of massive EV introduc-
tion.
In this thesis the creation of the base tool is attempted. Further development
of the tool should be made to ensure a more realistic behavior of EV owners. The
biggest drawback of the tool in its current version, is the absence of a scoring process
which could score charging activities based in charging queue, charging duration, and
SoC gain. Such a scoring function should also include in the calculations, the travel
time before the start and after the end of the charging activity.
The plan-based nature of MATSim is a drawback it self. The decision making on
charging (or not) should be probabilistic. This means that this probabilistic nature
of charging decision making should be included in the plan selection algorithm. In
addition a replanning process for selecting chargers, rather than just selecting different
routes to the same charger, should be implemented.
The auxiliary system consumption model of the current framework consists of only
the climate control system. Although, this is the main cause of consumption, a more
==PAGE:87==
7.2 Future Work
69
detailed study could create consumption models for the rest of the auxiliary systems.
Applying the tool on a specific area, should be accompanied with the specific
input parameter calibration, which has not implemented in this thesis.
In addition, this simulation framework could be used as the base for optimization
processes to optimize the locations and specifications of charging sites. Furthermore
the tool could be applied to define smart (charging) demand policies to match electric-
ity demand with the supply and not the opposite. Moreover, parking price policies
could be optimized to meet public charging space demand.
If a reliable simulation framework is created, which can capture the human be-
havior on charging decision making and model precisely the charging and discharging
process, then this tool could create a sufficiently large database of simulation results,
which may be used to train machine learning algorithms to predict the results of a
simulation, using as predictors the input parameters, without executing the actual
simulation. This way optimization process could dramatically be shortened. In addi-
tion, computational resources which are scarce could be freed.
