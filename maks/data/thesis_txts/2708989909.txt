Abstract
Proton exchange membrane fuel cells are a promising alternative to conventional power
sources in automotive systems due to their pollutant free operation, low environmental
impacts and high volumetric power density. PEMFCs are used in many applications be-
cause of their quick start-up time and low operating temperature. However, certain technical
challenges – such as cell degradation – still need to be overcome for large-scale deployment.
Eﬀective water management is a particularly important aspect in for PEMFCs since accumu-
lation of water leads to cell ﬂooding and increased degradation. A critical aspect for eﬃcient
water management is the two phase ﬂow in PEMFC ﬂow channels. This work analyses the
eﬀect of varying ﬂow channel geometries, air ﬂow rates and water ﬂow rates on the dynamic
water transport characteristics using an Ex-Situ PEMFC testbench that enables direct opti-
cal access to the ﬂow phenomena. A ﬂuorescence setup was used to visualize water transport
on the gas diﬀusion layer and synchronized to diﬀerential pressure data. An automated
image processing methodology was developed, classifying water transport phenomena in
ﬁlm, slug and mist ﬂow. It was found, that the ﬂow channel geometry, air ﬂow rate and
water ﬂow rate inﬂuenced the ﬂow regimes. A increasing number of ﬂow channels promotes
ﬁlm ﬂow over slug and mist ﬂow. A transition from slug dominated to ﬁlm dominated ﬂow
regime was seen at the transition from 5 m s−1 to 15 m s−1. The ﬂow regime transition was
also seen by a decrease of high-amplitude frequencies in a frequency domain analysis of the
diﬀerential pressure signal. At higher velocities, the water hold up in the ﬂow channels –
expressed as water coverage ratio – decreased signiﬁcantly as the ﬂow regime approached
mist ﬂow characteristics. A sensitivity analysis assessing the impact of the liquid phase
on the pressure drop showed high sensitivity at low air velocities. A ﬂow regime map was
created based on the air- and water ﬂow rate. Further, ﬂow channel pressure drop-, water
coverage ratio- and GDL breakthrough pressure signals were synchronized allowing for an
elaborate analysis of droplet emergence and detachment processes.

Problem description
In PEMFC operation, water is formed at the cathode and shall be eﬃciently removed
from the electrochemically active sites to prevent what is commonly referred to as ﬂooding.
Accumulation of water in the ﬂow channels would limit the oxygen mass transport, leading to
a reduction in cell performance and cell degradation. Thus, a comprehensive understanding
of water transport processes within a PEMFCs is of great importance.

Aim of the thesis

inﬂuence of diﬀerent ﬂow channel geometries, air ﬂow rates and water ﬂow rates on the
dynamic droplet behavior at the interface of a gas diﬀusion layer and a free ﬂow.

2.1
Proton Exchange Membrane Fuel Cell
2.1.1
Overview
A fuel cell is a device that directly converts the chemical energy of a fuel into electrical
energy and heat. Fuel cells resemble batteries in many aspects, e.g. both systems provide
electricity by means of charge transport between electrodes through an electrolyte. However,
a battery only contains a limited amount of fuel and oxidant that is depleted over time,
whereas a fuel cell has an external fuel source. Many types of fuel cells exist, greatly varying
in used fuel, materials and operation conditions, for instance molten carbonate fuel cells
(MCFCs), solid oxide fuel cells (SOFCs) or direct methanol fuel cells (DMFCs). This work
investigates components of polymer exchange membrane fuel cells (PEMFCs), thus this
section introduces the principles of a PEMFC.
PEMFCs are comprised of multiple individual cells that are connected to each other in
order to achieve a higher power. A series of connected cells is referred to as stack. However,
for the understanding of the fundamental fuel cell principles, it is suﬃcient to only consider
a single cell. Figure 2.1 shows the cross sectional view of a single PEMFC cell. The main
parts of the cell are the bipolar plate (01), the gas diﬀusion layer (GDL) (02) that is coated
with a micro porous layer (MPL) (03), a catalyst layer (08, 09, 10) and the electrolyte (04).
During operation, hydrogen is circulated in the PEMFC stack and distributed in the cells
by the ﬂow channels (07) in the bipolar plate. Hydrogen then enters the cell via a porous
gas diﬀusion layer and ultimately reaches the anode by passing through the microporous
layer.(II) Due to the catalyst layer present at the anode, hydrogen is oxidized as shown by
equation 2.1.

According to Dick et al.[6], the catalyst layer has a thickness of ≈10 µm and comprises very
small platinum particles (08) that are ﬁnely divided on a porous carbon carrier (10). Thereby
resulting in a higher electrochemical active surface area (ECSA) is achieved, enabling more
reactions to occur simultaneously. (I) It is interesting to note, that platinum has a high
activity to both HOR and ORR. According to Horton et al[7] the high activity stems from
the fact that platinum facilitates the dissociation of loosely adsorbed oxygen and hydrogen
molecules into adsorbed atoms, which are able to react.
The second phase is the proton conducting electrolyte and was well described by Dick
et al.[6] Typically, perﬂuorinated sulfonic acid (PFSA) membranes are used in PEMFCs.
A speciﬁc type of PFSA membrane that is prominently used for PEMFC applications is
NaﬁonTM. Essentially, NaﬁonTM is created from a polytetraﬂuorethene (PTFE) or TeﬂonTM
backbone that is chemically modiﬁed in order to achieve its ion-conducting properties –
making it an ionomer. In the treatment, side chains are added to the PTFE backbone,
whereas each side chain is terminated with a sulfonic acid –SO3H group. The sulfonated
polymer is then converted to its acidic form, resulting in SO3 – ions that are bound to the
carbon chain via the sulfur atom and give the polymer its ion conducting properties. It is
noteworthy, that sulfonic acid is highly hydrophilic, whereas PTFE is highly hydrophobic.
The hydrophilic regions lead do a signiﬁcant uptake of water in the polymer, thus water
clusters are formed at the hydrophilic regions within a generally highly hydrophobic polymer
structure. The introduction of said water clusters essentially results in a dilute acid, in which
the H+ ions are only loosely bound to SO3 –. It was found, that the proton conductivity of
membranes is higher than a simple migration of ions would imply and consequently that
proton conductivity is based on the Grotthus mechanism. Here H+ ions can move from
move from one water cluster to the next one due to weak hydrogen bonds that need to be
overcome.[8]
The third phase is the gas diﬀusion layer. Since GDLs present a major topic in this work,
a whole section is devoted to their introduction, cf. sec. 2.2.3.
In order for the ORR in equation 2.2 to take place, all three phases need to intersect. At
the three-phase boundary, the electrolyte provides ionic conductivity for H+ transport. The
GDL enables mass transport of reactant gasses and provides electric conductivity. Finally,
the catalyst layer lowers the energy barrier that needs to be overcome in order for the reaction
to take place, whilst also providing electric and ionic conductivity. In order to provide the
catalyst layer with proton conductivity, the electrodes are impregnated with an ionomer
i.e. electrolyte material[9]. The ionomer coating is depicted around the platinum particles
in the detail view of ﬁgure 2.1. Thereby, HOR and ORR exclusively occur at the three
phase boundary, marking their paramount importance in the design of membrane-electrode-
assemblies (MEAs).

the carbon ﬁbres with PTFE[15]. As previously mentioned, a surface is wetted by a liquid,
if the surface tension of the liquid is lower than the surface tension of the solid. According
to the Zisman plot, PTFE has a very low critical surface tension of γcrit = 18.5 mN m−1 at
20 ◦C[11]. Thus, it is very diﬃcult to be wet by ordinary liquids.(I) With its highly hydrophobic
properties, PTFE is an ideal candidate for the promotion of water removal in porous GDL
structures. Another practice that was increasingly adopted in recent years is to coat the GDL
substrate with a microporous layer (MPL), cf. (03) in ﬁgure 2.1. The MPL consists of carbon
black with a 10-40 wt% PTFE loading, depending on the type of carbon[6]. The purpose
of the MPL is primarily to enhance water removal from the catalyst layer, minimizing the
electrical contact resistance to the catalyst and preventing the leakage of ionomer that was
used to coat the catalyst particles through the GDL. The MPL thus helps to prevent ﬂooding
of the catalyst layer and improving catalyst utilization[15].

3
Literature review
3.1
Overview
The published literature discussing two phase phenomena in PEMFC systems is extensive
and includes the domains of experimental methods[23;24], numerical methods in terms of
computational ﬂuid dynamics[20;25;26] and increasingly machine learning of image data analy-
sis[27–30]. As this work is mainly experimental, this section will discuss the currently available
experimental methods. Researchers further classiﬁed the experimental literature into In-Situ
and Ex-Situ setups.
In In-Situ experiments an operational PEMFC in laboratory scale is used. They are
generally single cell setups and include the required main components such as bipolar plates,
membrane electrode assembly and gas diﬀusion layers[27;31–37]. Consequently, the observed
ﬂow phenomena in In-Situ are subject to similar complex conditions as in real PEMFC
systems, as described in detail in section 2.1. The setups are further complicated by a
inhomogeneous current density distribution that might result in a non-uniform temperature
proﬁle causing local hotspots aﬀecting chemical reactions and evaporation phenomena. Also
the molar fraction of the reactant decreases with increasing channel length as being consumed
by the reaction[23]. In-Situ setups are therefore ideal to study coupled events, the analysis
of individual phenomena, however, might be challenging.
Ex-Situ setups do not involve electrochemical reactions. In contrast to In-Situ, a typical
Ex-Situ setup therefore does not have an operational PEMFC but rather mimics the two
phase ﬂow characteristics of real PEMFC ﬂow channels. This provides the advantage of a
considerable simpler setup. Furthermore, In-Situ setups are generally subject to lifetime
and degradation restrictions in terms of operational conditions. Ex-Situ setups provide more
ﬂexibility to study the interrelations of ﬂow dynamics at diﬀerent geometries or varying
process parameters, such as temperature, humidity and mass ﬂow rates. Further, complex
ﬂow phenomena are decoupled from chemical reactions as well as heat and mass transfer[23].
This allows for a more reﬁned discussion of the results if the investigation isolated ﬂow
phenomena – such as droplet dynamics at the interface of GDLs and a free ﬂow – are of
primary interest. However, careful attention has to be paid to what extent experimental
Ex-Situ results are transferable to real PEMFC setups. Given the fact that In-Situ and
Ex-Situ setups are fundamentally diﬀerent in multiple aspects, and in order to keep this
chapter concise, it was decided to limit this review to Ex-Situ literature.

3. Literature review
3.2. Ex-Situ setups
3.2
Ex-Situ setups
According to Anderson et al[23], Ex-Situ reports typically investigate the inﬂuence of ﬂow
conditions, channel wettability, channel geometry and ﬂow media on hydrodynamic parame-
ters. More speciﬁcally on droplet formation, two-phase pressure drop, ﬂow pattern and liquid
holdup[23]. However, no common basis for the experimental setup in terms of ﬂow chan-
nel design and instrumentation exists. With vastly diﬀerent parameters regarding channel
geometry, materials, surface properties, pressure measurement and operating conditions, a
throughout comparison of the published literature remains challenging. Another uncertainty
is the used imaging technology, which can be classiﬁed into two cases. In the ﬁrst one a
transparent endplate is used, enabling direct top-view access to the two-phase transport
within the ﬂow channels. Water movement is then typically tracked with a camera and a
corresponding lense setup, e.g. a microscope[29;38–57]. The second method involves advanced
visualization techniques such as magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), neutron radiography or
x-ray tomographic microscopy[35;58–60]. Since the testbench used in this work uses a trans-
parent top plate with a camera setup, this chapter is narrowed down to literature reporting
similar imaging technology.(I)
The relevant literature is listed in tables 3.1, 3.2 and 3.3. The experimental setups were
classiﬁed into ﬂow channel design, type of gas diﬀusion layer, imaging setup, type of water
feed, air- and water ﬂow rates as well as the aim and results of the carried out studies.
3.3
Conclusion
The literature discussing two phase phenomena in PEMFC ﬂow channels is extensive. How-
ever, the reported Ex-Situ setups vary largely in terms of number and geometry of ﬂow
channels, used materials and surface treatment, imaging setup, type of porous medium and
experimental conditions. The widely diﬀerent setups stem from the fact, that many studies
focussed on individual aspects such as channel wettability, droplet growth and detachment
or ﬂow channel geometry and designed relatively simple setups accordingly. Data regard-
ing complex Ex-Situ testbenches is scarce. Only one promising setup was identiﬁed by Lu
et al[38–40;44], which will be used as reference later in this work, cf. sec. 7.
The surface properties and ﬂow channel geometries greatly aﬀects ﬂow regimes. The
concept of two phase pressure multiplier is commonly used to assess the impact of the liquid
phase on the pressure drop[38;40;44;51;61–67] The concept of water coverage ratio is commonly
used to assess the water hold up in ﬂow channels[20;32–34;44;65]. The type of GDL (PTFE
content, MPL) alters water breakthrough and ﬂow characteristics. Droplets on the GDL
surface are pinned down when connected to water clusters within the GDL. A higher air ﬂow
rate leads to faster detachment of smaller droplets. Flow regimes are commonly classiﬁed
into slug, ﬁlm and mist ﬂow.

In this section the pathways of the air and water ﬂows shall be described based on the main
components shown in section 4.1.1. Air enters at the right pedestal bottom and ﬂows up
through a porous media before reaching the Support plate, cf. ﬁg. 4.4(a). The porous media
shall homogenize the air ﬂow and prevent the emergence of vortexes. The air then ﬂows
through the inlet bore and along the indicated line towards the test section that is depicted
in ﬁgure 4.4(b). The test section is represented by the Insert. The air then enters the ﬂow
channels that guide the air over the over the GDL.(I) At the center of the test section, water
is introduced from the reservoir, through the GDL and into the ﬂow channel, indicated by
magenta arrows in ﬁg. 4.4(a). The water is then transported along the ﬂow channel as a
consequence of viscous shear forces and momentum exchange, as described in section 2.3.2.
The two phase ﬂow ultimately leaves the testbench through the outlet pedestal.

4.2. Optical setup
4.2
Optical setup
A major advantage of Ex-Situ PEMFC setups is the direct optical access via transparent ﬂow
channels. This enables the real time analysis of water breakthrough at the gas diﬀusion layer
and its subsequent interaction with gas ﬂow within the ﬂow channels. The ﬂow visualization is
a complex task and, as shown in the state of the art review sec. 3, diﬀerent methodologies have
been proposed – chosen based on the aim of the respective study. Part of the objective in this
work was the quantiﬁcation of water transport phenomena. In order enhance visualization
capabilities, a ﬂuorescence setup was used. This enabled the detection of emitted light
by water that has entered the ﬂow channel through the gas diﬀusion layer. This section
introduces the ﬂuorescence technique and presents the image acquisition.
4.2.1
Camera
A pco.2000 camera with a monochrome CCD sensor was used in this work. The camera
creates images with 16384 (14bit) gray levels at a spatial resolution of 2048x2048 pixel.
The camera was controlled via the software module camware that enables the adjustment
of camera speciﬁc settings such as as exposure time and frame rate. Depending on the
exposure time, a maximum frame rate of 2.14 is supported by the camera. The images
recorded a GDL area of 24 x24 mm2. This corresponds to 85.33 pixel/mm or a spatial
resolution of 11.72 µm/ pixel. Similar resolutions were reported in the literature, ranging
from 5.35 µm/ pixel to 26 µm/ pixel[45;57;70]. The recorded image sequences were available
in .b16 RAW format and were exported as TIFF ﬁles in 16bit depth for lossless conversion.
The TIFF ﬁle format was required for image processing using the software ImageJ, which is
described in section 5.4.1.
4.2.2
Lense system
This section discusses the optical setup that is used on the testbench. The aim of the setup
is to project a beam from a LED light source on water located at the GDL surface, thereby
activating the Rhodamine B solution, and direct the emitted wavelengths towards the camera
sensor. The individual parts are schematically shown in ﬁgure 4.8 and numbered in the order
as the light beam passes them.
A Thorlabs Solids-525C LED provides the light source with a dominant wavelength of
λpeak = 525 nm, which is within the absorption spectrum of Rhodamine B, as will be shown
in the next section 4.2.3. The light beam is indicated by green arrows in ﬁgure 4.8. Since
the provided Thorlabs LED is based on a 2" lense system, and the in-house lenses are on a
1" basis, it was required to adjust the light beam accordingly. A built-in 2" Thorlabs lense
(02) converges the beam to a 1" plano convex lense (03), which focuses the beam out of the
lamphouse to a bi-convex lense (4). To reduce the ﬂare of the created light beam, a 6 mm
aperture (05) was used. The reﬁned light beam is then projected on a collimating lense (06),

4.3.2
Testbench procedure
The previous section discussed the detailed steps and signal sequences required for the sensor
and image data acquisition. This section, however, elaborates the testbench operation that
was carried out in order to obtain the full dataset that will serve as a basis for the results
presented in this work.
First, the GDL-Insert assembly was prepared, cf. ﬁg. 4.7(c). The Insert was cleaned
with isopropanol before a thin layer of 2-component epoxy resin adhesive was spread on the
200 µm deep lug described in section 4.1.4. Next, a GDL piece with 30 x 120 mm2 was cut
from a sheet received by the manufacturer and glued on the adhesive surface. The Insert
was then mounted on the Base plate, where it remained for all main experiments. Thus, the
same GDL was used for all experiments.(I)
Next, the Cover was mounted on the Support plate and the ﬂow channel height was
measured with a depth micrometer at the four reference bores shown in ﬁgure 4.4(b). The
mean ﬂow channel height ¯h was obtained by averaging the four measurement points. After
measurement the reference bores are sealed with ∆pref1 and ∆pref2 sensors. With the Insert
and Cover assembled, an air ﬂow of 50 l min−1 was introduced to the ﬂow channel to test
for potential leakages.
Once the testbench was assembled and veriﬁed to be leak proof, a breakthrough test
was carried out. During breakthrough tests, no air ﬂow is imposed but a water feed of
˙mw = 1.4 µl s−1 is introduced in the reservoir until water is transported through the GDL
and into the ﬂow channel – i.e. a breakthrough event. During the experiment, images are
recorded and sensor data is logged. After observing the water breakthrough, the reservoir
ﬁlling level is lowered until the reservoir pressure reaches about 600 Pa. This procedure is
repeated for a total of three times. The breakthrough tests are carried out to track potential
GDL aging.
After completed breakthrough tests, the testbench was operated according to the operation
points that will be discussed in more detail in section 5.1. In principle, each investigated
operation point OP is deﬁned by a constant air velocity va,OP and water ﬂow rate ˙mw,OP .
First, the operation point speciﬁc air velocity va,OP is introduced to the ﬂow channel. Once
steady state can be assumed, the experiment is started with ˙mw,OP , corresponding to t0
in ﬁgure 4.13. The experiment is then carried out as described in section 4.3.1. At t5 the
reservoir level is lowered until a reservoir pressure of pres ≈600 Pa is reached and the air
ﬂow is gradually reduced to va = 0 m s−1 and thereby ending the experiment. The procedure
is repeated for a total of three times per operation point.

5.3.2
Water ﬂow reproducibility and behavior
This section discusses the reproducibility of the water ﬂow – analogously to the air ﬂow in
section 5.3.1. The water mass ﬂow rate ˙mw is established by powering the spindle drive of
the syringe pump. With the relay valve setting the ﬂow direction, water is pressed through
the 1/16" tubing, through a ﬁtting and into the water reservoir. Aspects that could alter
the reproducibility were identiﬁed to be system elasticity, trapped air and leakage.
As the pump starts feeding water into the reservoir, the reservoir level slowly rises and
so does the hydrostatic pressure detected by pres. At some point the reservoir is fully ﬁlled
and the level is in contact with the Insert. This is shown in the range t = 0 s to t = 5 s
in ﬁgure 5.3. However, no water ﬂow through the insert is observed initially, as the Insert
exerts a ﬂow resistance that causes a linear pressure build up in the reservoir, cf. t = 5 s to
t = 60 s.(I) Said pressure translates to the tubing connecting pump and reservoir, which is
subject to elastic elongation. This volume change could potentially alter the mass ﬂow rate
of water. To decrease this eﬀect, the tubing size was reduced to a thick walled 1/16" tube
diameter (≈1.6 mm) in order to increase its stiﬀness.
Another aspect to consider is trapped air. The system can be considered open towards
the Insert but closed towards the syringe pump feed. During syringe pump reﬁlling, air
could get sucked into the system. For instance, if the syringe pump is reﬁlled with a high
mass ﬂow rate, air could get sucked in at tubing connection points as a consequence of the
low pressures. Feeding the trapped air during testbench operation would then inﬂuence the
water ﬂow rate accordingly. In order to remove trapped air eﬃciently, the syringe pump was
mounted vertically. A maximum reﬁll ﬂow rate of 200 µl s−1 was found to be good practice.
Towards the Insert side, it is assumed that all air leaves the system upon pressure build up.
Water leakage was not noticed during testbench operation. However, an attempt was
made to optically validate the mass ﬂow that is leaving the Insert to the one that is fed to
the reservoir by the syringe pump. The procedure is discussed in section 5.3.3.

Gaussian ﬁlters can be tuned with the σ parameter, which is equivalent to the standard
deviation of a Gaussian distribution. A small sigma (σ = 1) will mostly suppress noise. As σ

into their surroundings. Since noise was found to be the primary target of the ﬁlter, a small

channels. The second objective is to link classes of the dimensionless visualization to classes
of water transport phenomena. For this purpose, three parameters were introduced and are
described in the following:
The eﬀect of air- and water ﬂow rate ˙mw is considered with the mass ﬂow ratio χ, cf.
eq. 6.1. The air mass ﬂow rate ˙ma was obtained with the ﬂow rates listed in tab. 5.3.

6.4
Time series data
The eﬀect of the water ﬂow rate, air ﬂow rate and Cover conﬁgurations was already partly
considered in previous sections, however only based on averaged data in steady state opera-
tion. This section elaborates on the eﬀect on time dependent pressure signals as well as water
coverage ratio. All ﬁgures follow the same schematic, in which the diﬀerential pressure signal
per channel ∆pi is shown in the upper plot and the water coverage ratio WCR in the lower
plot. The lower plot further includes the water reservoir pressure pres to provide additional
information of breakthrough characteristics. All data is time synchronized, whereas t = 0 s
corresponds to the time, where the ﬁrst water was detected by the camera, i.e. t0 = tBT .
Heat maps are included to support the discussion by a visual representation of the water
transport phenomena. The variation of the water ﬂow rate will be discussed based on Dual
at operation points OP 1, OP 5, OP 9 and OP 13, cf. matrix in ﬁg. 6.7. The impact of the
air ﬂow rate is based on the same experimental set with OP 5, OP 6, OP 7 and OP 8.
Here, it stressed WCR data should be correlated to ∆pi data rather than the other way
around. This is because pressure data is retrieved from a 60 mm range, whereas the camera
view is only 24 mm, cf. ﬁg. 4.4(b) and consequently some phenomena are captured by the
pressure sensors that are not recorded by the camera. All events in the WCR signals are
included in ∆pi signals, but not all events of ∆pi signals are included in WCR signal.
For further analysis of the pressure signal data, a frequency domain analysis was carried
out via fast fourier transform. The FFT was applied to study the eﬀect of water ﬂow rate
(cf. sec. 6.4.1) and air ﬂow rate (cf. sec. 6.4.2) on the pressure signature of channel ∆p2 at the
Dual setup that was introduced in section 4.1.3. For the signal data acquisition, a sampling
frequency fs = 20 Hz is used, cf. sec. 4.3.1. Due to the Nyquist frequency with fN = 1
2fs the
frequency domain spectrum is considered only from 0 Hz to 10 Hz. The experimental runtime
was 120 s thus, at a sampling frequency of 20 Hz, a total number of samples Ns = 2400 is
collected. Then, the frequency resolution fr is obtained with fr = fs
Ns as fr = 8.3 × 10−3 Hz.

Discussion
7.1
Inﬂuence of ﬂow channel geometry
A major part of this work is the analysis of two phase ﬂow characteristics at diﬀerent ﬂow
channel geometries. For this purpose three Cover conﬁgurations – resembling diﬀerent ﬂow
channel geometries – were designed and manufactured for the use on the Ex-Situ PEMFC
testbench. A total of 16 testbench operation points were deﬁned based on an estimation
of conditions typically found in commercial PEMFC stacks as well as several testbench
pre-studies. Finally, the two phase ﬂow characteristics were studied at the deﬁned testbench
operation points for each Cover conﬁguration and the results were presented in section 6.
This section discusses the relation of these results to each other as well as to published
literature.
7.1.1
Flow regime
General trends
In section 6.2 a ﬂow regime "map" was presented for each testbench operation point. The
operation point speciﬁc ﬂow regime maps were then combined to a 4 x 4 ﬂow regime matrix
to visualize the change of ﬂow regimes slug, ﬁlm and mist at varying air- and water mass
ﬂow rate. The matrices for single, dual and hexa were shown in ﬁgures 6.8, 6.9 and 6.10
respectively.
It was found, that air velocity va and water ﬂow rate ˙mw fundamentally alter the ﬂow
characteristics of water for each ﬂow channel geometry – i.e. Cover conﬁguration. As dis-
cussed in section 6.2, two general trends were found across all conﬁgurations: First, as the
water ﬂow rate increases, the fraction of slug ﬂow increases. Secondly, ﬁlm ﬂow becomes
more pronounced at increasing air ﬂow rate, ultimately approaching mist ﬂow at a very high
va.
The interrelation of both trends to each other was visualized by the arrangement of
the ﬂow regime maps into a matrix. For example, it was seen at Single, that a high air
velocity at a low water ﬂow rate results in mist ﬂow (OP 4), whereas ﬁlm ﬂow is dominant
at high air velocity and high water ﬂow rate (OP 16). Thus, the extent of each ﬂow regime
fraction correlates with va and ˙mw. Further, both correlations appear to have a diﬀerent
signiﬁcance. Considering the ﬁrst correlation with va: At Dual with ﬁgure 6.9, it is seen,

Next, the variations of ﬂow regimes is discussed in respect to varying Cover conﬁgurations.
Comparing the ﬂow regime matrices, it is seen, that the ﬂow channel geometry has a strong
impact on the observed ﬂow phenomena. A drastic increase of ﬁlm ﬂow fractions is seen
across all operation points at the transition from Single to Dual – i.e. from a wide channel
to two smaller channels separated by a land. Operation points at Single were generally
characterized by relatively large slug and mist ﬂow fractions, whereas both fractions were
reduced and resulted in a more dominant ﬁlm ﬂow at Dual. Further reducing ﬂow channel
width and increasing land area at the transition from Dual to Hexa shows the same trend:
Mist and slug ﬂow fractions are less pronounced with ﬁlm ﬂow becoming the dominant ﬂow
regime at all operation points where va > 5 m s−1. This eﬀect can be described with the
characteristic water transport phenomena that were shown in section 6.5.1. A consequence of
reducing ﬂow channel width is an increasingly large fraction of land area. Three mechanisms
establishing ﬁlm ﬂow were identiﬁed in section 6.5.3, whereas two of them require a land for
formation. In the ﬁrst mechanism, a breakthrough location is below the land, feeds water
into the channel and creates a ﬁlm ﬂow at the channel wall. In the second mechanism water
from a breakthrough location in the channel center established contact with a channel wall
and created a ﬁlm ﬂow. Thus, the higher the land fraction, the higher the probability of
either mechanisms, which gradually increases the ﬁlm ﬂow fraction in the ﬂow regime matrix
at the transition from Single to Hexa.
The ﬂow regime matrices shown in this work are speciﬁc to the Ex-Situ testbench used in
this work. However, it was decided to compare the observed results to literature. As shown in
section 3, experimental data for similar Ex-Situ setups is scarce, however a similar testbench

the same notation. Thus, the ﬂow regime matrices were converted accordingly and are shown

7.2
Dynamic water-pressure interaction
The previous section analysed averaged data of operation points. In this section, relevant
phenomena regarding water-pressure interaction will be discussed based on time series data.
7.2.1
Flow channel cross communication
In order to assess potential ﬂow channel cross communication, an exemplary experiment
is studied in detail. Figure 7.3 shows the time series data of 101-H-15-01-x2 including the
respective heat map.
WCR2 shows a periodic signal with three droplet growth and detachment processes in
the time period t0 < t < t50. The corresponding breakthrough location is seen in the heat
map. A land breakthrough feeds water in channel 2. At t50, a second land breakthrough
emerges, which also feeds water in channel 2. Hence, the droplet is now fed from two sources,
which accelerates its growth and leads to faster detachment under the imposed air ﬂow, as
seen at t > t120. Each detachment is reﬂected by a corresponding peak in ∆p2. From the
heat map it is evident, that no water breakthrough was detected in channels 1, 3 and 4. This
is also seen by WCR1, WCR3 and WCR4. Therefore, no peaks are expected in the pressure
signals ∆p1, ∆p3 and ∆p4. However, it is seen that ∆p3 and ∆p4 show pressure peaks at the
same frequency as ∆p2 – suggesting cross communication between the ﬂow channels. The
same is seen for the breakthrough at channel 6. The pressure signal ∆p6 shows ﬂuctuations
according to droplet detachments – synchronized with WCR6. And the pressure signal of
∆p5 ﬂuctuates at the same frequency as ∆p6, despite no detachment processes are shown
by WCR5. It is noteworthy, that ∆p1 does not show similar peaks. Therefore, it seems
that center channels 3 and 4 are more sensitive to cross communication. This might be an
indication that the GDL is bending as a consequence of pressure peaks, creating an air gap
and thereby enabling cross communication. Another reason might be an air gap resulting
from the height diﬀerences shown in table 5.3.

7. Discussion
7.3. Testbench- & methodology improvements
7.3
Testbench- & methodology improvements
In this work an Ex-Situ testbench was modiﬁed for the phenomenological analysis of two
phase ﬂow in ﬂow channels for PEMFCs. This study is thus the ﬁrst of its kind and potential
improvements of testbench setup and methodology were identiﬁed during the course of this
work, which are brieﬂy described in this section.
7.3.1
Operation points
As mentioned in section 7.1.2, it is seen, that φ2 has a high sensitivity at air velocities
va < 5 m s−1. As stated by Owejan et al,[35] automotive fuel cell systems operate the majority
of their lifetime at less than 20 % of their nominated power. This regime corresponds to
low current densities and consequently low air velocities, making the va < 5 m s−1 regime
particularly interesting. Considering the shift from 25 m s−1 to 35 m s−1, no signiﬁcant change
of pressure signature was found by frequency domain analysis via FFT, which was also seen
by the relatively small eﬀect on the ﬂow regime, cf. sec. 6.2. Thus, the testbench operation
points could be shifted towards lower air velocities va. If the concept of superﬁcial water
velocity vw based on total cross sectional ﬂow channel area is continued to be used, the
water ﬂow rates ˙mw are dependent on potential future ﬂow channel geometry changes.
7.3.2
GDL aging
In section 6.1 it was shown, that the GDL breakthrough pressure pBT decreased during the
course of this work. The same GDL was used throughout the three experimental blocks that
were listed in table 5.2. Thus, the GDL might have experienced a change of properties which
could be a consequence of the relatively high water ﬂow rates of up to 25 µl s−1. Another
reason could be the use of a 10 µmol l−1 Rhodamine B solution as ﬂuorescence medium,
where residual particles might have altered the surface properties of the GDL. In future
studies, it might thus be advantageous use new GDLs in the experimental procedure.
7.3.3
Fluorescence medium
Rhodamine B was used as ﬂuorescence medium to visualize water transport on gas diﬀusion
layers, similarly to what has been reported in the literature[41;49]. In the course of this work
it was found that Rhodamine B altered the ﬂow characteristics on the GDL. It was shown
in ﬁgure 4.11 that an increasing Rhodamine B concentration lowers the surface tension of
water γw. Further, it was shown that the ﬂuorescence intensity decreases with decreasing
Rhodamine B. Thus, a compromise between high surface tension and suﬃcient ﬂuorescence
intensity was found at 10 µmol l−1 resulting in a surface tension of 67.5±0.7 mN m−1 com-
pared to ≈72 mN m−1 of pure water, cf. ﬁg. 4.11. It was shown that this concentration still
altered the ﬂow characteristics on the GDL surface.(I)

7.3.4
Methods for data acquisition
In the analysis of time series data in section 6.4 it was mentioned, that a considerable
amount of pressure peaks could not be related to a corresponding peak in the water coverage
detection. This is because the camera view captures an area of 24 x 24 mm2, whereas the
diﬀerential pressure signal is measured along a ﬂow channel length of 60 mm, as seen in the
technical drawing in section 9.2. However, a Cover design providing simultaneous diﬀerential
pressure measurement and undisturbed optical access is non-trivial. Also, a greater imaging
area would considerably reduce the spatial resolution of the camera and thereby aﬀecting
the quality of image processing.
In the discussion of characteristic water transport phenomena sec. 6.5, it was seen that
transport events of smaller droplets at elevated air velocities and water ﬂow rates were not
captured due to limitations of the camera frame rate. For this reason a higher frame rate
might be advantageous if high speed visualization is the primary objective. Since accurate
detection and classiﬁcation of water occurrences was the primary aim in this work, a lower
framerate was chosen in favor of a high spatial resolution.

8
Conclusion
In this work a Ex-Situ PEMFC testbench was modiﬁed for the phenomenological analysis
of two phase ﬂow phenomena in ﬂow channels. Three diﬀerent ﬂow channel conﬁgurations
were designed based on literature, manufactured and tested in respect to their inﬂuence
on the dynamic water transport characteristics. For the visualization of water transport on
the GDL, a ﬂuorescence setup was designed with Rhodamine B as ﬂuorescence medium and
set up accordingly. For an appropriate analysis, a data acquisition system was set up to
directly synchronize of pressure data to image data by means of a pulse delay generator.
The two-phase ﬂow characteristics were analysed at varying air- and water ﬂow rates at
a total of 16 testbench operation points (OP) that were identiﬁed based on commercial
PEMFC operation conditions. With three ﬂow channel geometries, 16 OPs and assessing
statistical uncertainty, a total of 144 experiments were carried out. An automated image
processing methodology was developed for the OP and ﬂow channel speciﬁc classiﬁcation of
water transport into slug ﬂow, ﬁlm ﬂow and mist ﬂow as well as for the creation of water
coverage ratio (WCR) time series data.
It was found, that the ﬂow channel geometry inﬂuences the ﬂow regimes. A higher number
of ﬂow channels increased the fraction of ﬁlm ﬂow events considerably, while lowering the
fraction of of mist ﬂow. The ﬂow regime maps were compared to literature and reasonable
agreement was found given the deviations of the experimental setups. A two phase pressure
multiplier sensitivity analysis showed a high sensitivity at superﬁcial air velocities va =
5 m s−1 to va = 15 m s−1 due to the transition from slug dominated to ﬁlm dominated ﬂow
regime. The change of ﬂow regime was also seen in a FFT analysis on the pressure signals at
varying air ﬂow rates. Variation of water ﬂow rates was found to have less eﬀect on the ﬂow
regime but resulted in a signiﬁcant increase of detachment events and water breakthrough
locations at the GDL. Two mechanisms were identiﬁed for the water breakthrough that
would result in a ﬁlm ﬂow event, where water would be either sourced from a breakthrough
location under a land or from the channel center. Time series analysis enabled the combined
evaluation of water transport events in terms of WCR, diﬀerential pressure in the ﬂow
channel and water reservoir pressure. It was found that the GDL breakthrough pressure
decreased over the experimental procedure, suggesting potential GDL aging that could be a
consequence of degradation due to high water ﬂow rates or due to Rhodamine B particulates.
Time series data showed a potential cross communication between the ﬂow channels that
might be the result of GDL deﬂection or an air gap between the ﬂow channels.

Outlook
This work reported the ﬁrst testbench design iteration and consequently signiﬁcant changes
to the ﬂow channel design, optical setup and data acquisition system of the Ex-Situ PEMFC
testbench. On the basis of the presented results, a second iteration of design changes ad-
dressing the ﬂuorescence medium, GDL aging and ﬂow channel cross communication are
expected. Additionally, the design for diﬀerential pressure measurement could be changed
to remove blind spots in the synchronization of pressure data to image data. Thus, the
testbench will be improved accordingly.
Many aspects of two phase ﬂow characterization in commercial PEMFC systems were
disregarded in the course of this work and will be considered upon the second design iteration.
For example, the eﬀect of diﬀerent gas diﬀusion layers with and without microporous layer on
the water breakthrough characteristics. Another important parameter in PEMFC operation
is humidity and – in connection to evaporation – temperature. Thus, further studies with pre-
conditioned airﬂow will be carried out as well. Further relevant aspects are a more elaborate
ﬂow channel geometry design with surface treatment to study the eﬀect of hydrophilic and
hydrophobic ﬂow channels on the water transport characteristics and the pressure drop.
