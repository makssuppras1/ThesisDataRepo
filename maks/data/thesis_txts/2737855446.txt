Abstract 
When crossing the South Pacific Ocean from Fiji to Peru a steady increase in vertically integrated 
phosphate, a key nutrient for phytoplankton growth, can be observed. The aim of this project is to 
investigate this gradient in order to explain it with regards to the general circulation patterns of the 
South Pacific mainly the upwelling along the Peruvian coast and the equatorial current systems that 
dominate the tropical Pacific. The project is based on the hypothesis that the uptake of dissolved 
nutrients by primary producers and their eventual sinking as detritus to be remineralized at depth will 
trap nutrients is the eastern Pacific in what could be called a “tumble dryer” effect. If this is actually 
what is happening, we would expect to see similar gradients for other biological compounds such as 
nitrate and silicate and particularly for oxygen. The gradient in oxygen could be cause for concern 
with the observed expansions and intensification of Oxygen Minimum Zones (OMZ) like the one off 
the coast of Peru.  
Based on data from the PACIFICA database and information about the systems and processes that 
take place in the area a quantitative description of the zonal phosphate pump was made and verified 
against other stoichiometric compounds via the Redfield ratio. Furthermore, comparison was made 
with a transect in the northern Pacific all in order to describe and confirm the existence of the tumble 
dryer effect.

Introduction 
 
 
Figure 1: The transect across the South Pacific between Peru and Australia which is used for analysis in this paper.

bottom is a map showing the transect.  
 
In the tropical Pacific Ocean between Peru and Australia a clear gradient in phosphate content can be 
observed with a high concentration of phosphate close to the coast of Peru and a much lower 
concentration at the opposite side of the ocean near Fiji and Australia. The buildup of phosphate is 
especially noticeable from the coast of Peru going west to about 140ºW with the highest concentration 
==PAGE:6==

between the coast and 100 ºW. In this same area of the ocean a large area of low oxygen concentration 
or even oxygen depletion can be observed overlapping almost exactly with the phosphate buildup.  
Because of the way phosphate enters the system and is distributed it is not normal to see a build up 
like this and since it lines up so perfectly with the oxygen depleted area it seems to indicate a 
connection between the two.  
 
Figure 2: The basis of the tumble dryer effect. Particulate phosphate can move somewhat independently while dissolved 
phosphate will move with the water. 
 
An explanation could be that the two are linked driving an underwater circulation of phosphate similar 
in character to a tumble dryer which essentially traps the phosphate in the eastern part of the ocean. 
The basis of this tumble dryer effect is that dissolved phosphate will move with the water while 
particulate phosphate will have the ability to move somewhat independently and thus up the 
concentration in one place.  
In very general terms the processes of the tumble dryer could be explained like this: Near the coast 
nutrient rich water is brough to the surface through upwelling. Here the nutrients including phosphate 
is used by phytoplankton for the synthesis of organic building blocks such as proteins and lipids. The 
phosphate now in its particulate form is then returned to the deep ocean when it sinks to the bottom 
==PAGE:7==
6 
 
as detritus and eventually it is returned to its dissolved form through remineralization and the circle 
can restart as shown in Figure 2.  
 
 
 
Figure 3: Nitrate (top) and total carbon (bottom) content for the transect shown in figure 1 
 
It is also interesting to note that the same transects also show a similar gradient for nitrate. In fact, the 
transect for nitrate is almost identical to that of phosphate only with concentrations roughly 15 times 
higher indicating that these two concentrations are also linked. Similarly, the total carbon 
concentration is also high in that area and especially close to the coast though the highest total carbon 
concentration is found slightly deeper in the water column than it is the case for phosphate and nitrate. 
This connection could be a sign that the tumble dryer effect is indeed happening.

Theory 
In order to explain the connections found in the introduction and perhaps confirm or deny the theory 
of the tumble dryer effect it is first necessary to understand the area and the processes that might 
influence the concentrations of different nutrients and what might link them to each other.  
First, we will take a look at what is going on around the Peruvian coast and then some of the processes 
will be explained in more general terms.  
 
The Peruvian Current system and the Pacific Ocean 
The current system off the coast of Peru is fairly unique. A similar system can only be found in a few 
places and of these the Peruvian system is the richest due to coastal upwelling making the waters near 
the coast very rich in nutrients (Brink et al. 1983). In fact, the coastal upwelling region off the coast 
of Peru is one of the most productive in the world (NC State University 2003). The primary production 
off the coast of Peru is estimated to be around 200-400 g C/m2/year where the global average is only 
around 75-150 g C/m2/year (Webb 2023). 
The Peruvian Current System is made up of four individual currents. The Peru Coastal Current also 
known as the Humboldt Current flows northward close to the coast. The Peru Oceanic Current flows 
similarly northward but further from the coast until it bends of and flows westward just south of the 
Galapagos Islands. Next to these two the Peru-Chile Countercurrent flows southward and underneath 
all three currents flows the Peru-Chile Undercurrent which also flows southward (NC State University 
2003). A poleward undercurrent flowing counter to the currents near the surface is a common feature 
of the major coastal upwelling regions. In fact the undercurrent is the source of much of the water 
that upwells along the coast (Brink et al. 1983). 
 
==PAGE:9==
8 
 
 
Figure 4: The Peruvian Current System. (source: Climate vulnerability assessment of key fishery resources in the 
Northern Humboldt Current System - Scientific Figure on ResearchGate) 
The upwelling itself happens because of wind. When the south and southeast trade winds blow 
towards the north steered by the Andes, the surface water near the coast is deflected to the west away 
from the shore due to the Coriolis effect. This causes a divergence in mass near the coast. Deep ocean 
water rich with nutrients such as phosphate and nitrate will then well up to replace the displaced 
surface water securing a constant renewal of phosphates and nitrates making phytoplankton growth 
possible on a large scale (NC State University 2003; Webb 2023).  
This overall flow pattern over the continental shelf with offshore and northward flow at the surface 
and onshore and southward flow in the deeper water allows for a recycling of nutrients and 
phytoplankton seed populations within a region of strong upwelling and it is an important factor in 
maintaining the phytoplankton bloom at the center of the upwelling around 15ºS which is right around 
where the transect for this project is taken. (Brink et al. 1983).  
 
El Niño 
Most of the time conditions off the coast of Peru is as described above. But periodically, every few 
years or so, the system is disturbed by El Niño – Southern Oscillation often just referred to as El Niño. 
During El Niño the cold nutrient-rich waters is replaced by unusually warm and nutrient-poor water. 
The phenomenon is caused by a weakening or in extreme cases reversal of the trade winds which 
==PAGE:10==
9 
 
happens because the high-pressure system over the eastern Pacific is diminished. This causes the 
warm surface water to flow towards South America instead of away from it which prevents upwelling 
and dramatically reduces productivity (Webb 2023).  
El Niño and its effects both on local and global climate is an interesting and complex subject in itself 
but it is beyond the scope of this paper and thus not something that will be discussed in further details.  
 
Productivity 
Phytoplankton make up most of the organic matter in the sea. Made up mostly of single-celled algae 
and bacteria it is very diverse in both morphology and biochemical behavior as well as evolutionary 
history. All photosynthetic forms of phytoplankton convert sunlight into chemical energy which is 
then used to make the organic components of life from carbon dioxide, water, and nutrients such as 
nitrate and phosphate. Photosynthesis by phytoplankton is known as primary production because it is 
the basis of production for nearly all food for other marine organisms.  
Some of the organic matter from primary production is leaked or excreted from the cells in its soluble 
form, some is lost when the phytoplankton is eaten by zooplankton, and some is lost during feeding, 
death and decay in the subsequent parts of the food web (Pilson 2012). Eventually however, nearly 
all organic carbon is converted back into dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) by some form of 
respiration. This can happen either by the phytoplankton that produced it, the metazoans that 
consumed it or the bacteria that break down dead organic material. Only a very tiny fraction of the 
organic matter is preserved on a geological timescale.  
Photosynthesis requires sunlight. Because of this primary production happens in the upper layers of 
the sea in what is referred to as the euphotic zone. The phytoplankton-based ecosystem found in this 
layer is characterized by rapid grazing and metabolism of the organic matter that is produced. The 
system is never truly in steady state but is instead constantly influenced by outside forces such as 
wind, turbulence and weather but a continuous rain of particles sinking from the surface can always 
be found. This rain carries down nutrients from the surface to the deeper waters. These nutrients are 
then later resupplied to the surface through vertical and lateral advection and mixing or through 
upwelling.  
 
When talking about primary production various terms are used because it can be difficult to accurately 
measure the rate at which it happens. Gross production is used for the total amount of carbon that is 
fixed by phytoplankton per unit time. In other words, it is a measure of how much CO2 is converted 
==PAGE:11==
10 
 
to organic matter. Net production is the same thing, total carbon fixed, but corrected for the carbon 
that is respired by the phytoplankton themselves. New production is the fraction of the net production 
that happens using nutrients from outside the region where the production takes place. The opposite 
of new production is recycled production which means that the nutrients that are used are recycled 
within that same region. Finally, we have export production which refers to the fraction of the net 
production that is not consumed and regenerated as inorganic carbon and nutrients in the surface layer 
but rather is transported out of the area mainly downward by sinking particles of organic debris or 
whole phytoplankton cells. It also includes what is removed from the area by fish export. If the area 
is in steady state, the export production should be equal to the new production. It is also worth noting 
that when surface water is subducted into deeper regions, the organic matter that is carried along also 
counts as export production. When measuring and reporting primary production it is usually intended 
to be net production (Pilson 2012). 
 
Upwelling  
Upwelling is a process that brings deeper water rich in nutrients up to the surface where the nutrients 
are used in primary production. In the open ocean upwelling occurs where surface currents diverge 
or move away from each other. Where the surface waters diverge, deeper water is brought to the 
surface to replace it. This is what creates so-called upwelling zones. These zones are usually highly 
productive because of the nutrients that are brought up with the water and many of the most 
productive regions on the planet are found in upwelling zones. An example of an open ocean 
upwelling zone can be found in the equatorial Pacific where the trade winds blow the North and South 
Equatorial Currents to the west while Ekman transport causes the upper layers to move away from 
the Equator (Webb 2023).  
==PAGE:12==
11 
 
 
Figure 5: The principle of upwelling. Drawn as it is in the Southern Hemisphere. 
 
Upwelling can also happen along the coast as is the case at the Peruvian coast. Here it is the surface 
water being moved away from the coast that leads to upwelling when deep water is brought to the 
surface to replace the missing surface water. Coastal upwelling zones are often also associated with 
some of the most productive commercial fishing grounds (Webb 2023). This is also the case off the 
coast of Peru whose anchovy fishery remains one of the largest commercial fisheries in the world 
(NC State University 2003). 
 
Ekman transport 
Surface currents are ultimately created by the wind. But the water does not just move in the direction 
of the wind, instead because of the Coriolis effect the surface water is moved roughly in a 45-degree 
angle from the wind direction either to the right in the Northern Hemisphere or to the left in the 
Southern Hemisphere. When the uppermost layer of the water moves the friction of the movement 
then sets the layer just below it in motion. This layer then sets the next layer in motion and so on. 
However, with each layer a little energy is lost so each new layer of water will not move as far as the 
one just above it. At the same time all the layers are affected by the Coriolis effect just like the top 
layer and are deflected relative to the layer above it with the deflection following the same rule of to 
the right in the Northern Hemisphere and to left in the Southern Hemisphere. This creates what is 
known as the Ekman spiral because the movement of each successive layer creates a spiral pattern 
down through the water column. The spiral usually reaches down to around a hundred meters depth 
==PAGE:13==
12 
 
before motion ceases and if the magnitude and direction of all layers are added together the resulting 
movement of the upper hundred meters of water is 90º relative to the original wind direction. This 
net movement is known as Ekman transport (Webb 2023).  
 
 
Figure 6: The principle of Ekman transport. Drawn as it is in the Southern Hemisphere. 
 
Particle rain 
When the respiratory needs of the phytoplankton itself has been satisfied most of the remaining net 
production will go to growing the phytoplankton and a smaller fraction of maybe 10 % on average 
will leak out or be excreted as organic carbon. The phytoplankton and the dissolved organic carbon 
then serve as the base for the rest of the food web in the sea. Some fraction of phytoplankton 
themselves as well as a fraction of the organisms that eat them and their fecal products and other 
detritus will sink below the euphotic zone to be consumed further down. This fraction varies but it 
tends to be higher with higher production. Sudden blooms in particular yield a lot of sinking particles 
once the bloom comes to an end. The sinking particles carry down nutrients, trace metals and other 
substances to the deeper parts of the ocean (Pilson 2012). 
 
==PAGE:14==
13 
 
 
Figure 7: Marine snow. (source: Microbial ecology of organic aggregates in aquatic ecosystems - Scientific Figure on 
ResearchGate) 
 
Marine snow is a term used to collectively describe the many kinds of particles both organic and 
inorganic that can be found suspended or usually sinking in the ocean at varying rate. The name 
marine snow originates from the fact that the particles look similar to snow when observed under 
water. The particles are varied in both nature and source and include clays and other minerals from 
dust-fall or rivers, shells of marine mollusks and protozoa, and coccoliths as well as phytoplankton 
cells, exoskeleton molts from crustacea, and fecal pellets from many different organisms. The marine 
snow also provides a surface for bacteria and microbes to attach to. When the particles sink the 
organisms are carried down through the water column where they become like little islands of high 
biological activity contributing to the oxygen consumption and total metabolism deep in the water 
column (Pilson 2012). 
 
Oxygen minimum zones 
Oxygen minimum zones are persistent layers of water that have low oxygen concentration due to 
biological, chemical and physical processes in the ocean. As the oceans become warmer the size and 
number of oxygen minimum zones in the world increase. 
Oxygen minimum zones are often found in between two layers of water with a higher concentration 
of oxygen somewhere between 100- and 1500-meters depth. The surface layer of the ocean tends to 
have a higher concentration of oxygen as it is in constant exchange with the atmosphere and the 
deeper water will usually also have a higher concentration of oxygen because the colder bottom water 
can store more oxygen (US Department of Commerce 2023). If the water column is heavily stratified 
with little to no mixing these layers can persist for a long time as oxygen minimum zones are only 
==PAGE:15==
14 
 
ventilated in a diffusive manner (Sarmiento and Gruber 2006). Oxygen minimum zones do occur 
naturally in the water column but deoxygenation in the ocean is exacerbated by climate change. Cold 
water can hold more oxygen than warm water and thus the colder deepwater have a higher oxygen 
content than the warmer water in the oxygen minimum zone. As surface waters are heated, they also 
lose their ability to hold high concentrations of oxygen and as stratification is increased from the 
surface warming the oxygen cannot be replaced from below either. The upper boundary of the oxygen 
minimum zone then becomes more shallow (US Department of Commerce 2023).  
 
Fisheries 
For a long time the world’s largest commercial fishery was happening off the coast of Peru (Webb 
2023). The primary species fished was Peruvian anchoveta, a smallish fish of the anchovy family. 
The peak annual catch of the species was in 1971 where 13 million metric tons were caught but 
because of the heavy fishing pressure catches have been decreasing over time and for the last several 
years catches have been between four and eight million metric tons per year. The anchovetas are 
mainly used to make fishmeal or oil which can be used as animal feed, fertilizer and other industrial 
products (Oceana 2024).  
As mentioned further up the removal of fish counts towards export production and since this fishery 
is so large it is responsible for a fairly large amount of carbon and nutrients being removed from the 
area. How much can be calculated assuming 70% water content and 50% carbon content out of wet 
weight.  
This means that 6 million metric tons of fish wet weight translates to 0.9 million metric tons of carbon. 
0.9 ∙ 1012 g carbon is equal to 0.75 ∙ 1011 mol carbon. According to the Redfield ratio there should be 
one mol of phosphorus per every 106 mol carbon. This means that the fish caught equals 7.08 ∙ 108 
mol phosphorus which again equals 2.19 ∙ 1010 g, or 2.19 ∙ 104 tons phosphorus removed every year.  
 
The Redfield Ratio 
The Redfield Ratio describes the average composition phytoplankton and is also reflected in the ocean 
water in which they live. The ratio is widely used as a stoichiometric reference for nutrient limitation 
in planktonic production. It is named after American oceanographer Alfred Redfield who in 1934 
described the almost constant ratio of nutrients found in marine biomass in samples collected across 
the globe (Ptacnik, Andersen, and Tamminen 2010). What Redfield found specifically was a linear 
relationship between phosphorus and nitrogen corresponding to 1 P per 16 N in mole units. He 
==PAGE:16==
15 
 
suggested that this relationship reflected the average composition of organic matter that is 
metabolized and mineralized in the deep sea and he eventually extended the ratios to also include 
carbon as well as the oxygen that would be used for respiration and released during photosynthesis 
(Pilson 2012). Since then, more elements such as silicon have been added and the Redfield Ratio and 
especially the 1:16 average for P:N have become a standard used almost universally even outside of 
the original domain (Ptacnik, Andersen, and Tamminen 2010). Despite this general and widely used 
ratio however, it is well understood that different organisms can display variations of the Redfield 
ratio as they are composed of varying quantities of different chemical compounds and new 
calculations and estimations for specific areas or organisms are constantly being made. Especially the 
P:O2 ratio tends to vary a lot between estimates and studies have shown that the overall P:N:C:O2 
ratio may vary as much as threefold to fourfold between oceanographic biomes with lower P:N ratios 
being found in subpolar regions and higher P:N ratios being found in the subtropic regions (Carter et 
al. 2020).

Redfield ratio in life and in the environment 
Photosynthesis and respiration 
The chemical composition of phytoplankton and other organisms may vary a lot between different 
species or even between different life stages of the specific species but when averaged over large 
areas of the ocean the average composition of marine organisms appears uniform in accordance with 
==PAGE:17==
16 
 
the Redfield ratio (Pilson 2012). Thus, whenever photosynthesis exceeds respiration and biomass 
increases essential nutrients and CO2 will be taken up and oxygen released in almost constant ratios 
of each other. The same applies in the opposite situation when living matter is metabolized and 
remineralized. In due time nutrients and carbon dioxide is released, and oxygen consumed in the same 
ratio. Redfield calculated this ratio to be:  
 
1 P : 16 N : 106 C : -138 O2 
 
where O2 is estimated from the oxidation of 106 CH2O + 16 NH3. Based on this ratio as well as the 
composition of the organisms and oxidation of the carbon in them, it is then possible to create a 
formula for the average organic matter: 
 
(CH2O)106(NH3)16(H3PO4). 
 
This formula sometimes known as the Redfield molecule does not represent an actual molecule, but 
it is useful in relating the Redfield ratio to photosynthesis and respiration since it can be used in 
equations representing the two processes. Based on this molecule the manufacturing during 
photosynthesis and oxidation by respiration would be as follows: 
 
106 CO2 + 122 H2O + 16 HNO3 + H3PO4 ⇌ (CH2O)106(NH3)16(H3PO4) + 138 O2 
 
Where the photosynthesis is the equation read left to right and respiration in the equation read from 
right to left (Pilson 2012).  
 
Nutrients 
Nutrients are elements such as nitrogen and phosphorus which are necessary for plant growth both in 
terrestrial and marine environments. In the oceans they are an essential factor in determining the 
productivity of an area as the amount of nutrients present controls how much phytoplankton there can 
be. One nutrient will usually run out before the others. This nutrient is known as the limiting nutrient. 
Nitrogen has traditionally been considered as the main limiting nutrient in marine systems whereas 
lakes have been considered limited by phosphorus. It might however not be as simple as this since 
the two nutrients have been shown to be closely related and sometimes a co-limitation can be observed 
==PAGE:18==
17 
 
(Ptacnik, Andersen, and Tamminen 2010). The limiting nutrient also does not have to be the nutrient 
with the lowest concentration as not all nutrients are required in equal amounts. Some such as iron 
are only required in trace amounts and even between the major nutrients there is a difference in how 
much is required as can be seen from the Redfield ratio. In the following the three major nutrients 
phosphorus, nitrogen and silica will be presented.   
 
Phosphorus 
All living thigs require phosphorus. It is both an essential constituent in genetic material (RNA and 
DNA) and an essential participant in several mechanisms such as metabolism and hydrolysis inside 
all organisms. Alfred Redfield believed phosphorus to be the master variable that controlled the 
availability of other nutrients such as nitrogen and oxygen and since it is usually only available in 
very low concentrations in the ocean, it will often set the upper limit to the living biomass in an area 
(Pilson 2012). 
The general pattern for dissolved inorganic phosphate (DIP) in the sea is that it will be relatively high 
in high-latitude regions, low in the subtropical gyres and somewhere in the middle in coastal and 
equatorial upwelling zones (Martiny et al. 2019). DIP is an important driver in the ocean influencing 
phytoplankton growth, abundance and diversity and whereas a deficit in nitrate can be offset by 
nitrogen fixation there is no way to add more phosphorus to the system. Thus, any changes in the 
vertical supply of DIP will have a major influence on ocean productivity and downward carbon export 
(Martiny et al. 2019; Moutin et al. 2008).

==PAGE:19==
18 
 
 
Phosphorus comes from rocks. It is released through rain and weathering and then transported into 
the sea by rivers and coastal runoff. Phosphorus is the only nutrient where the availability is solely 
dependent on geology. In the ocean phosphorus is present either as simple inorganic phosphate in its 
various ionized forms, this is true for nearly all phosphorus found in deep water, or in combination 
with dissolved organic matter, this is more prevalent in open-ocean surface water. The concentration 
of phosphorus in the ocean tends to be higher in the deep ocean than in surface waters with the highest 
concentration found around 1000 meters depth (Pilson 2012). This difference is caused by biological 
uptake at the surface and downwards transport by organisms and their detritus. When phosphorus is 
found in its dissolved form it will move with the water leading to an even distribution of phosphorus 
across the ocean. For the concentration to be higher in one part of the ocean it requires that the 
phosphorus is found as particulate phosphorus which can move somewhat more independently 
around. But still some sort of outside force will be required to up the concentration in one part of the 
ocean. 
 
Nitrogen 
Nitrogen like phosphorus is essential for all living things and in accordance with the Redfield ratio it 
is generally required in amounts about 16 times greater than phosphorus. Nitrogen as an element and 
as a nutrient however is a bit more complicated than phosphorus.  
In seawater nitrogen is mainly found as the extremely inert N2 which means that before it can be of 
any use to the organisms in the sea, it needs to be transformed into a different form. The process of 
turning N2 into a different form of nitrogen is known as nitrogen fixation and it can happen in several 
different ways. In certain high-energy situations such as lightning discharges or fire N2 can react with 
O2 to form HNO3 in the presence of water or simply NO3- (nitrate) since nitric acid is a strong acid 
and will be completely dissociated under normal conditions. But this is not the only way to make 
nitrogen available for organisms, a whole range of biological and inorganic processes can do the 
same. Biological nitrogen fixation is limited to a few types of organisms mainly different bacteria, 
archaea and cyanobacteria that can convert N2 to ammonia (NH3). The ammonia from nitrogen 
fixation is mostly found in amino acids and proteins in the microorganisms responsible for the fixation 
and once these die the nitrogen is passed on in the system. 
 
==PAGE:20==
19 
 
Another very important process related to nitrogen is denitrification. Normally respiration uses 
oxygen but in situations where oxygen is limited or completely absent some organisms can instead 
use nitrate. This process known as denitrification reverses the fixation process and reduces the nitrate 
back to N2. The overall reaction of denitrification is as follows: 
 
4HNO3 + 5CH2O → 5CO2 + 7H2O + 2N2, 
 
though the actual process happens in several steps involving different enzymes. Denitrification 
typically occurs in places such as anoxic basins and sediments where nitrate is present, and oxygen 
is low or absent, but it can also happen in open-ocean areas where the oxygen concentration is very 
low. The most extensive region like this is found in the Eastern Tropical Pacific.  
Phytoplankton generally prefers to take up nitrogen in the form of ammonia since this is the form that 
it can be used in and reducing nitrate to ammonia is energetically costly. Thus, ammonia will be taken 
up until it is reduced to very low concentration after which the phytoplankton will turn to nitrate and 
nitrite. In waters too deep for phytoplankton to grow the nitrogen that is released from respiration 
will end up as nitrate and is not reused by phytoplankton until the deep water is brought back to the 
surface by upwelling. In anoxic waters the nitrate may be consumed as a part of denitrification as 
described above (Pilson 2012). 
 
Silicate 
In the ocean silicon exist in the form of silicate, a compound or ion containing the tetrahedral SiO4 
which can be either solid (silica) or in solution. In seawater it primarily exist as silicic acid Si(OH)4 
(Sarmiento and Gruber 2006).  
When thinking of nutrients silicate is usually not the first thing that comes to mind but nevertheless 
there are many organisms that are absolutely dependent on it. It is also important as an oceanic tracer 
as it affects the physical properties of the sea water and has a considerable impact on which planktonic 
species can be found in a particular body of water. This is because some species of plankton primarily 
diatoms use silicate to form their outer shells. These shells or frustules are made up of amorphous 
silicate SiO2 · nH2O which is also referred to as biogenic opal or just opal (Sarmiento and Gruber 
2006). Diatoms are often found to have a competitive advantage over other species of phytoplankton 
when silicate is present and in many regions of high productivity, they are often the dominant species. 
(Pilson 2012). The upwelling system off the coast of Peru is one of the areas that are dominated by 
==PAGE:21==
20 
 
diatoms, making silicate an important nutrient to consider for this particular system (Ehlert, Grasse, 
and Frank 2013; Grasse et al. 2021).  
The sources of silicate in the ocean are primarily rivers and the only real sink is the long-term burial 
of opal in the sediments. In the ocean the behavior of opal has many similarities to that of organic 
matter in terms of formation and recycling at the surface as well as sinking and dissolution in the 
water column. This extends to diatoms having nearly constant proportions of elements in accordance 
with the Redfield ratio just with the addition of silicate (Sarmiento and Gruber 2006). This ratio is 
C:Si:N:P = 106:15:16:1 (Brzezinski 1985). But silicate still differs from other nutrients in several 
ways. First of all, silicate is only used by diatoms where nitrate and phosphate are used by all 
phytoplankton. Secondly, siliceous materials are not passed on in the food chain like the other 
nutrients are. Regeneration of opal to silicic acid also only happens through dissolution and not 
through biological degradation. And finally, Si exist only in its inorganic form in seawater where N 
and P can exist in various inorganic and organic forms (Sarmiento and Gruber 2006). 
 In the ocean silicate is primarily found in high concentrations at the surface in the Southern Ocean 
and to some extent in the North and Equatorial Pacific. Concentrations tends to be much higher in the 
deep water with the lowest deep-water concentrations found in North Atlantic Deep Water and then 
gradually higher concentrations along the path of circulation all the way to the North Pacific where 
the highest deep-water concentrations are found. It is also almost solely in the North Pacific that silica 
can be found in the main thermocline (Sarmiento and Gruber 2006). 
 
Versions, variations, and deviations 
When modelling global nutrient budgets, production and mixing of water in the World’s oceans the 
Redfield ratio has commonly been used when estimating the synthesis and remineralization of organic 
matter. This however is an oversimplification as phytoplankton synthesis does not always follow this 
ratio (Ríos et al. 1998). Sometimes there are deviations from this rule between different species or 
different oceans or parts of an ocean depending on nutrient availability and other factors. It is really 
only in very specific cases or when averaged across all oceans that the classical Redfield ratio applies.  
 
Lipids and carbohydrates 
As mentioned in the previous section Redfield found the average composition of organic matter to be 
106:16:1:-138 for C:N:P:O2 but he also pointed out that these ratios may vary when considering 
reduced molecules such as lipids which consume a lot more oxygen than carbohydrates during 
==PAGE:22==
21 
 
oxidation and also do not include phosphorus and nitrogen in their structures. When considering this 
as well as the average lipid and carbohydrate contents of particulate organic matter, which are about 
24% and 17 % respectively, a new ratio of 106:16:1:149 is found (Ríos et al. 1998; Anderson 1995). 
This new ratio is actually found in both coastal and open ocean waters but can on some occasions be 
even further altered when synthesis of lipids and carbohydrates is high which usually happens towards 
the end of spring blooms.  
 
 
Figure 10: Oleic acid. Lipid molecules such as oleic acid consists of carbohydrate chains with only a few oxygens and 
no nitrogen or phosphorus. (source: Public Domain) 
 
One way to include a lipid component and possibly improve on the original ratios would be to add a 
number of CH2 units to the Redfield molecule. Including this an improved Redfield equation might 
look something like this: 
 
(CH2O)104(CH2)20(NH3)16(H3PO4) + 166O2 ⇌ 124CO2 + 140H2O + 16HNO3 +H3PO4. 
 
This equation includes more carbon per unit phosphorus and requires a little more oxygen per unit 
carbon to oxidize the carbon part of the Redfield molecule (Pilson 2012). This equation does not give 
the same ratios as was found by Rios et al, but that just reflects the impossibility of finding a ratio 
that is universally true. In literature the oxygen to phosphate ratio varies between the original 138 and 
all the way up to around 175 oxygen per phosphorus (Pilson 2012).  
 
Variations between oceans 
There are also deviations to be found between different oceans and these can provide an insight into 
various oceanic processes when comparing the ratios to each other. For example, the N:P ratio of the 
Atlantic tends to be a lot higher than what is true for the Pacific and Indian Oceans. The exact reasons 
for this are still subject to speculations. Various theories have been proposed to explain the difference. 
==PAGE:23==
22 
 
It could be due to differences in the composition of the organic matter that is delivered from the 
surface to the bottom in each ocean. Another option is that the Pacific Ocean is a net source of 
phosphorus whereas the Atlantic Ocean is a net sink. While both of these theories could be at least 
part of the explanation a more likely answer is that denitrification is more prevalent in the Pacific 
than in the Atlantic because of the large oxygen depleted areas resulting in a lower N:P ratio on 
average (Pilson 2012). The deep water in the Pacific is also older and have had more time to be 
exposed to denitrification in sediments. The difference would then lie in the nitrogen concentration 
and not in the phosphorus concentration. Denitrification has previously been shown to cause 
deviations from the Redfield ratio (Ríos et al. 1998). 
 
Redfield also pointed out that in coastal waters the ratios were likely to deviate from the average due 
to the proximity to terrestrial nutrient sources and differences in planktonic species composition 
(Ptacnik, Andersen, and Tamminen 2010). In general coastal waters and estuaries tend to have more 
complex and difficult to predict or interpret relationships between nutrient concentrations and if 
Redfield had only looked at samples from coastal waters he would have likely found a very different 
ratio if any at all as the ratios vary a lot more in these areas (Pilson 2012). 
 
Redfield Ratio in the Anthropocene  
Both phosphorus and nitrogen are important nutrients not only for marine life, but also for fertilization 
of crops on land. Thus, humans have intervened in the natural processes of these elements in order to 
utilize them for this and other purposes.  
 
One of the first big commercial fertilizer schemes was the export of guano from Peru to Europe in 
the mid-19th century. The phosphorus-rich guano was mined from small islands off the coast of Peru 
and sailed across the world to be sold as fertilizer on the European market. These islands located in 
the Humbold current were nesting places for various species of seabirds and literally white from the 
accumulated droppings from the generations of birds that had inhabited the islands. In some places 
the layers of guano were reported to be several feet thick, and the islands soon underwent intense 
exploitation. However, the workers mining the guano would often kill the native seabirds for their 
feathers during the mining process thus removing the source of the guano. The transport from the 
islands to Europe also was not without difficulty partly because of the difficult waters the ships had 
to pass through and partly because the guano tended to harden inside the hull. Often times ships would 
==PAGE:24==
23 
 
have to beach themselves because they were unable to cope with the dead weight of the guano. The 
export however was still profitable and in just thirty years the sources were almost exhausted and 
fishmeal replaced guano as the fertilizer export of Peru (Doughty 2014).  
 
Another more recent and much more extensive way humas have interfered with the natural 
distribution of nutrients is through the Haber-Bosch process. The Haber-Bosch process is used to fix 
nitrogen on a massive scale. The fixed nitrogen is mainly used as fertilizer, but some is also used in 
explosives and plastics as well as some minor uses. The Haber-Bosch process in which N2 and H2 
reacts to form ammonia at high temperature and pressure in the presence of a catalyst has been used 
for industrial production since 1913 on a scale large enough to change the amount of fixed nitrogen 
in the global system significantly (Pilson 2012). In fact in 2010 the amount of nitrogen fixed through 
the Haber-Bosch process was double that which was fixed by natural terrestrial sources and overall 
the anthropogenically fixed nitrogen has doubled the global cycling of nitrogen in the last century 
(Fowler et al. 2013). But the increased flux of fixed nitrogen is necessary as agriculture would not be 
able to support the current human population without the nitrogen produced through the Haber-Bosch 
process (Pilson 2012).  
 
Preformed properties 
Preformed properties are the properties that the seawater had when it was last at the surface. These 
properties are useful for quantifying biogeochemical cycling and mixing properties in the ocean. 
Based on preformed properties and interior ocean property distributions it is possible to determine 
remineralization ratios such as the Redfield ratio. It is also very relevant when looking at carbon 
sequestration as preformed nutrients have been strongly linked with the strength of ocean carbon 
sequestration (Carter et al. 2020).  
To determine or define preformed properties it is first necessary to define what is meant by the 
“surface mixed layer” as the water needs to have left this layer before one can begin talking about its 
preformed properties. The problem is that there is no depth range over which the water is perfectly 
mixed neither is there a depth below which there is no exchange with near-surface waters. One 
definition of preformed properties could be that “preformed properties are the properties of the 
interior water masses at the moment they leave surface mixed layers for the final time before being 
advected from the formation region.” This is the definition used by Carter et al. (2020).  
 
==PAGE:25==
24 
 
The deepest waters of the world are comprised of a mix of two main water types: North Atlantic Deep 
Water (NADW) and Antarctic Bottom Water (AABW). NADW tends to be low in preformed 
nutrients but rich in preformed oxygen because of the biological production and air-sea gas exchange 
that takes places when the source waters are at the surface in the Atlantic. AABW on the other hand 
is often rich in preformed nutrients but low in preformed oxygen because it spends less time at the 
surface and is shielded from gas exchange by sea-ice (Carter et al. 2020).  
Intermediate waters such as Antarctic Intermediate Water (AAIW) have a visual influence on 
preformed properties around 1000 meters depth in the subtropics and especially in the Southern 
Hemisphere. AAIW is created when well-ventilated and nutrient rich surface waters are mixed with 
the oxygen poor and nutrient rich Circumpolar Deep Water that also acts as the source for AABW. 
Intermediate waters in general often have lower preformed silicate than nitrate and phosphate. This 
is because silicate is striped more rapidly from the surface closer to the Poles while also being 
remineralized deeper in the water column than phosphate (Carter et al. 2020). 
In general, high preformed nutrient content is associated with regions of upwelling such as subpolar 
gyres, in the Southern Ocean and Equatorial regions and along the eastern boundaries of ocean basins 
where intermediate and deep water returns to the surface. The last of these being the most relevant 
for this study. Preformed oxygen on the other hand tends to be low in the Eastern Tropical Pacific 
and the Arabian Sea both of which are known for having large oxygen deficient zones (Carter et al. 
2020). 
 
Figure 11: 14C content for the transect between Peru and 180ºW shows that the water is very old. 
 
The age of the water is also important when discussing preformed properties. The youngest deep 
water is found in the North Atlantic and the oldest in the North Pacific. As the water gets older it 
==PAGE:26==
25 
 
accumulates nitrate, phosphate silicate and potential alkalinity but loses oxygen (Carter et al. 2020). 
The deep water in the Pacific Ocean known as Pacific Bottom Water (PBW) is a mixture of AABW, 
recirculated NADW and AAIW. PBW enters the Pacific from the south flowing northward from 
Antarctica. Because of the bathymetry the northwest Pacific basins are much more accessible for the 
northward flowing bottom waters than the northeast Pacific basins. Thus, the northeast basin is the 
final stop for PBW circulation. This is confirmed by the northeast basin having a maximum in 
dissolved silicate and a minimum in dissolved oxygen and 14C (Kwiek and Ravelo 1999).  
 
14C is a radioactive carbon isotope used for determining the age of organic material in a process called 
radiocarbon dating or carbon-14 dating. Since 14C is radioactive it will decay over time and so the 
fraction of 14C in the organic material will decrease. It is this decrease that is used to determine the 
age by comparing the decreased fraction with the fraction that naturally occurs in the atmosphere. 
Radiocarbon dating can also be used to determine the age of a body of water or rather how long it is 
since the water was last at the surface. Surface water or water that has recently been at the surface 
will have a fraction of 14C close to the atmospheric fraction whereas old water that has not been at 
the surface for hundreds of years will be depleted in 14C relative to the atmosphere. 
 
The Biological Pump 
The ocean is the second largest carbon reservoir in the Earth system second only to the solid Earth. 
It holds about 60 times as much carbon as the atmosphere and it is the largest reservoir that readily 
exchanges with the atmosphere. Because of this the ocean is the dominant control on atmospheric 
CO2 concentrations on a decade to millennia timescale (DeVries 2022a). 
The biological pump is responsible for the transport of carbon from the surface ocean down to the 
deep ocean through several different pathways. The carbon that is exported through these pathways 
can be sequestered in the deep ocean for years or even centuries as respired CO2. On average, the 
global carbon export is 10.2 Gt C yr-1 and the total amount of carbon that is sequestered by the 
biological pump is 1300 Gt C (Nowicki, DeVries, and Siegel 2022; Carter et al. 2020). This means 
that the average residence time for sequestered carbon is 127.45 years calculated as total amount of 
carbon sequestered divided by the average yearly carbon export.  
==PAGE:27==
26 
 
These numbers are a global average and the actual amount of carbon that is sequestered varies 
between oceans and parts of the oceans. The largest sequestration inventories and longest 
sequestration times are found in the northern high latitudes in both the Atlantic and the Pacific, in the 
subantarctic and in coastal upwelling regions such as that off the coast of Peru whereas the lowest are 
found in the subtropical gyres (Nowicki, DeVries, and Siegel 2022).  
 
Roughly 2/3 of the gradient in dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) between the surface and deep ocean 
is caused by the biological pump (DeVries 2022a). The biological pump begins in the surface ocean 
in the euphotic zone where DIC is fixed into organic carbon through photosynthesis by 
phytoplankton. Most of this fixed organic carbon is converted into CO2 by zooplankton grazing and 
microbial activities in the upper layers of the ocean but some is exported down and respired in the 
deep water where the resulting CO2 then can be sequestered for years to centuries depending on the 
circumstances. 
Figure 12: Total carbon content with depth for select stations across the transect from Peru to 180 ºW 
==PAGE:28==
27 
 
 
Figure 13: The basics of the biological pump with the individual pumps and processes that are involved. 
 
The biological pump is a combination of two different pumps: the soft tissue pump which concerns 
carbon from respired organic matter and the carbonate pump which concerns dissolved calcium 
carbonate. The summed effects of these two remove DIC from the surface and release it deeper into 
the water column (DeVries 2022a). There are three main pathways in the biological pump: the 
gravitational pump, the migration pump and the mixing pump. The gravitational pump is the passive 
sinking of particulate organic carbon (POC) in the form of aggregates, carcasses and fecal pellets due 
to gravity, the migration pump is the active transport of carbon that happens when zooplankton and 
fish migrate vertically in the water column and the mixing pump is the physical transport of both 
particulate and dissolved organic carbon by various types of mixing such as vertical mixing, eddy 
subduction and large-scale ocean circulation (Nowicki, DeVries, and Siegel 2022).   
The majority of the organic matter that is exported down from the surface ocean by the biological 
pump is converted back into its inorganic components within the water column or sediments by 
heterotrophic organisms in the process of remineralization. Once these minerals is brought back to 
the surface by an upward flux or through upwelling it closes the nutrient cycling loop which is driven 
by the biological pump (Sarmiento and Gruber 2006).

Phosphate and nitrate 
Perhaps the most obvious relationship and also the easiest ratio to explain is that of P:N which 
according to the Redfield ratio should be 1:16. From the plots it is very clear that most of the data fits 
a linear relationship very closely though with a slightly flatter slope than what is expected. This is a 
sign that remineralization of phosphate and nitrate is simply the reverse of photosynthesis. The data 
that does not fit this tight coupling can all be found in the eastern section closest to the coast as seen 
in Figure 20. This is also where the oxygen minimum zone is located as well as the most productive 
part of the transect. Normally oxygen is used for respiration and remineralization but when oxygen 
is not available, nitrate can be used instead through the process of denitrification as described in the 
theory section. This means that nitrate is removed which pushes the scatterplot away from the typical 
straight line and away from the Redfield ratio.

Results 
Overall, there is evidence to be found in all plots to support the hypothesis of the tumble dryer effect. 
All plots have shown that as we move west towards Fiji the amount of nutrients and carbon decrease 
not only in the surface but also deeper in the water column while the amount of oxygen increases. 
There are also clear signs that remineralization is happening all the way across at a ratio determined 
to be 1:12.67:130.67:-140.44 for P:N:C:O2. The higher numbers for carbon and oxygen are in 
accordance with what was described in the theory section when considering lipids and carbohydrates 
which would require higher fractions of both carbon and oxygen. Oxygen is not that much higher 
than for the traditional Redfield ratio but the ratios for the N:O and P:O plots were also skewed 
slightly by the inclusion of data from above 1000 meters. At the same time the N:P ratio is lower than 
expected due to denitrification in the eastern section. 
The plots have shown that carbon and silicate is remineralized deeper in the water column that nitrate 
and phosphate and that most of the remineralization happens in the east and middle sections of the 
transect which also fits with the theory of the tumble dryer effect.  
For further confirmation of the hypothesis comparisons can be made to a different transect further 
north which should be unaffected by the tumble dryer.

Conclusion 
One of the most productive areas in the world is found off the coast of Peru. The productivity is so 
high in this area because of the nutrients such as nitrate and phosphate that is brought up from the 
deep ocean by upwelling. In fact, the area has supplied phosphate to be used as fertilizer to Europe 
and the world since the mid-19th century first in the form of guano and later as fishmeal from Peruvian 
Anchovetas which constitutes one of the largest fisheries in the world. But going across the Pacific 
Ocean from Peru towards Fiji a clear gradient in the ocean phosphate content can be observed. The 
purpose of this project was to investigate this gradient in order to explain it. The hypothesis was that 
a sort of “tumble dryer” effect is happening below the surface in the eastern end of the transect near 
the Peruvian coast. Through analyzing data from a specific transect between Peru and Australia and 
considering the different processes going on in the area both biological and physical this hypothesis 
Figure 28: Total carbon content with depth for select stations across the comparison transect.

seems to have been confirmed. The gradient in phosphate across the transect as well as gradients in 
nitrate and oxygen was evident in all plots and it was also shown that the content of both silicate and 
dissolved inorganic carbon was higher in the eastern part of the Pacific than in the western. All of this 
supports the theory of the tumble dryer effect as it is driven by the bloom, death and remineralization 
of the phytoplankton in the eastern part of the South Pacific. The theory was further confirmed by 
comparison with a transect in the North Pacific which showed no signs of a tumble dryer effect though 
the Californian coast is also known as a rich upwelling zone.
