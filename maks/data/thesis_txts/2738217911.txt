Abstract
Due to its convenience and flexibility, wireless power transfer (WPT) technology is increasingly in
demand in various applications, such as electric vehicles and consumer electronics.
However, WPT
systems still face many challenges today, such as achieving high misalignment tolerance while operating
in constant current(CC) and constant voltage(CV) modes for charging purposes. Many WPT designs
have been proposed to obtain the desired capabilities through complex solutions, which will increase
cost and make it hard to replace physical connector chargers as they are cheap and reliable. A WPT
design will be proposed to have a high misalignment tolerance while operating at CC and CV mode to be
more cost-effective. The WPT design will have no transmitter-receiver communication and no additional
circuitry on the receiver side with only transmitter-side control. PT symmetry will be utilized to achieve
high misalignment and CC mode through a resonance frequency tracking circuit. A proposed control
strategy will be introduced to increase the accuracy of the resonance tracking circuit by using dynamic
delay compensation, thereby extending the CC control to a large frequency range. A transmitter-side-only
coupling coefficient estimation method will be proposed to achieve CV mode. The WPT design will be
simulated, built, and tested to validate the proposed WPT design for several misalignment cases, which
yielded a horizontal misalignment of 9.5 cm and a vertical misalignment of 2.8 cm with a transmitter
and receiver coil of 20 cm in diameter while maintaining CC and CV control. The proposed control
strategy successfully compensated for time delays, maintaining accurate resonance frequency tracking in
the range of 300 kHz-425 kHz and achieving a CC of 5 A. The proposed coupling estimation method was
able to estimate the coupling coefficient with a small margin of error. By using the estimated coupling
coefficient, CV control was obtained with a CV of 46 V. The total system efficiency from this WPT
design was 80% to 96%.

Introduction

Introduction
Wireless power transfer (WPT) systems have proven useful in various sectors, notably consumer electronics
and the electric vehicle (EV) industries.
These systems offer a more convenient and flexible alternative
to conventional charging by eliminating the need for physical connectors. However, significant challenges
remain, particularly for autonomous systems.
For instance, Amazon’s Prime Air drone delivery service
and Vay’s self-driving EVs still require manual charging, disrupting their operational cycles and autonomy.
Incorporating WPT technology could significantly enhance their autonomy and make self-driving EVs and
drone products more cost-efficient. Yet, this integration demands a high tolerance for misalignment between
the transmitter and receiver coils, a frequent issue due to inherent inaccuracies in EV parking and drone
landings. Traditional inductive WPT systems are highly sensitive to coupling variations, which can severely
impact their performance[1].
Moreover, the widespread use of lithium-ion batteries in drones, consumer electronics, and EVs adds further
complexity. These batteries need a carefully managed charging process involving constant current (CC) and
constant voltage (CV) modes to ensure safe and efficient operation. In CC mode, used when batteries have
a low state of charge (SOC), the system must provide a constant current until the battery voltage reaches
its rated voltage, then switch to CV mode to maintain steady voltage. This dynamic adjustment is crucial
for the longevity and reliability of lithium-ion batteries[2].
Many solutions exist in research to achieve high misalignment tolerance or CC and CV control using a WPT
system, but not many have both capabilities. Designs such as [3] utilize a homogenous transmitter coil
design, which can generate a constant magnetic field in a specific area within the transmitter coil. Such coil
design can yield a constant coupling between the transmitter and receiver coil within an area that will have
natural misalignment tolerance. This type of design can achieve CC and CV control without communication,
as the coupling is always known within the homogenous area.
Other WPT designs use clever ways to position the transmitter coil in the correct proximity to the receiver
coil, such as [4]. They use stepper motors to automatically position the transmitter coil close to the receiver
coil, which has the benefit of always having a constant coupling as the position between the transmitter
and receiver coil will always be the same. Apple’s MagSafe has ferromagnets connected to the transmitter
coil and receiver coil, which snap in place once the receiver coil is close enough. Other designs, such as
PowerMat’s WPT product seen in Appendix B, do have a high misalignment tolerance and are capable of
CC and CV control by utilizing frequency control and communication between transmitter and receiver. But
have increased costs due to the complex design.
This thesis focuses on designing a WPT system that offers high misalignment tolerance while seamlessly
supporting both CC and CV modes on the receiver side without needing transmitter-receiver communication.
This approach reduces costs and complexity by eliminating additional circuitry on the receiver side and
avoiding potential connectivity issues. In this project, misalignment will be defined as the working distance
between the center point of the transmitter and receiver coil. This means that the WPT system has to

function properly within the maximum misalignment distance.
To achieve this, the thesis explores the use of a parity-time (PT) symmetric WPT system, a concept in-
troduced by Assawaworrarit in 2017[5]. PT-symmetric WPT systems have unique properties that make the
voltage, current, power, and efficiency independent of the coupling coefficient under certain conditions. This
is achieved by using a negative resistance as a power source, which ensures that the voltage and current
remain in phase by operating at the system’s resonance frequency. However, current PT-symmetric WPT
systems rely on circuits highly sensitive to propagation delays, requiring compensation through phase shifts
via low-pass[6] or high-pass filters[7]. These methods often fail to perfectly compensate for propagation
delays, leading to frequency tracking errors, especially under high-frequency variations.
This thesis proposes a novel control strategy to address these challenges. By compensating for the propaga-
tion delay in the frequency tracking circuit, the proposed system aims to maintain accurate resonance
frequency tracking, ensuring stable and efficient operation even with high misalignment.
Transitioning from CC to CV mode presents additional challenges, as it disrupts the PT-symmetry of the
system. To maintain efficient and independent operation without transmitter-receiver communication, this
thesis introduces a new method for estimating the coupling coefficient between the transmitter and receiver
coils using only transmitter-side information. This approach eliminates the need for additional receiver-side
circuitry or complex communication protocols, streamlining the system and reducing costs. The specific CC
and CV characteristics are not based on any existing battery. This project will only show the CC and CV
control capability of a WPT design for a hypothetical battery.
In summary, this research aims to advance wireless power transfer by developing a system that tolerates high
misalignment and efficiently manages the charging process of lithium-ion batteries in autonomous systems.
It addresses the limitations of current PT-symmetric WPT systems and proposes a novel control strategy
and estimation methods.
The main contribution is the following:
1. A proposed control strategy using dynamic delay compensation to improve resonance frequency track-
ing for a large range of frequencies.
2. A method of estimating the coupling coefficient between transmitter and receiver coil without transmitter-
receiver communication and additional receiver-side circuitry.

The series-to-series compensation method is popular in WPT systems for its simplicity and ability to maintain

2.3
Constant Current Mode
Constant current (CC) mode is commonly used for charging batteries, especially when they are in a low
state of charge (SOC). In CC mode, the system provides a constant current to the battery until it reaches
the battery voltage threshold, regardless of its voltage. Achieving constant current on the secondary side of
a WPT system is crucial for efficient and safe battery charging, regardless of the battery’s load resistance.
The challenge in WPT systems, particularly those needing high misalignment tolerance, is maintaining con-
stant current despite variations in the coupling between the transmitter and receiver coils. This section
examines various strategies for achieving CC mode in different coupling scenarios.
In applications needing high misalignment tolerance, such as electric vehicles (EVs) or autonomous drones,
the coupling coefficient can vary significantly during operation. Traditional methods to achieve constant
current (CC) mode involve communication between the primary and secondary sides. The secondary side
measures the current and sends this information to the primary side, which then adjusts the primary current
page 8 of 49
==PAGE:14==

to achieve the desired secondary current[13]. However, this approach adds complexity and cost due to the
need for additional communication circuitry. This thesis aims to achieve CC mode without such communic-
ation, making the system simpler and more cost-effective.
To achieve CC and CV modes in a varying coupled WPT system without transmitter-receiver communic-
ation, the system must be invariant to changes in coupling and load resistance. This can be accomplished
by introducing parity-time (PT) symmetry to the WPT system. PT-symmetric WPT systems have unique
properties that make them independent of coupling variations under certain conditions.
A PT-symmetric WPT system requires a negative resistance (–Rn), as first proposed by Assawaworrarit in
2017[5]. Negative resistance ensures that the system only produces active power, with no phase shift between
the voltage and current. Consequently, the system adjusts its operational frequency to match the resonance
frequency, ensuring efficient power transfer.

Negative Resistance and Delay Control
This section will explore the theory behind the negative resistance, as well as the underlying problems with

requires the WPT system to operate at its resonance frequency. However, as the coupling coefficient changes,
the resonance frequency must adapt accordingly, as described in Equation 12.

There are several methods for tracking the resonance frequency, but the most common one in PT-symmetry
research is described here. Initially, the current is measured using a current transformer and then amplified
through a differential amplifier. A low-pass filter (LPF) removes higher harmonics before the signal passes
through a zero-crossing detector.
The zero-crossing detector compares the signal to zero, outputting a
boolean one if the signal is above zero and a boolean zero if it is below, resulting in a PWM signal with a
50% duty cycle.
In constant current (CC) mode, the FPGA allows this PWM signal to pass through, enabling the gate driver
to operate the switches using the PWM signal generated by the zero-crossing detector. This self-tracking
resonance circuit is inspired by the work of Kaiqin Yan [16], who noted the circuit’s sensitivity to time delays,
though the specifics were not elaborated.
This thesis provides a detailed explanation of the resonance tracker and analyzes its sensitivity to time delays.
A control strategy will be proposed to compensate for the time delay created by the different components
in Figure 9 such as the amplifier, LPF, zero-crossing detector, FPGA, and gate driver.

which is done by applying an initial frequency to the resonators. The choice of the initial frequency is crucial

4.1
Coil design
The transmitter and receiver coils were designed to have identical sizes and values. The coil diameter is 20
cm, and the shape is a planar spiral coil. The specific size and shape of the coils are chosen to compare with
industry WPT designs such as Powermat’s design seen in Appendix B. The coils designed in this project
are mainly done through The finite element method FEM simulation using Ansys Maxwell 2D and 3D. In
this project, there are two crucial parameters to optimize. To minimize winding resistance and maximize
the self-inductance. Reducing the winding resistance can achieve higher efficiency, as seen in Equation 7
and 20. By maximizing the self-inductance, a higher misalignment between the transmitter and receiver coil
can be achieved by maximizing the self-inductance, as seen in Equation 14. But, optimizing for these two
parameters can be challenging, as they naturally want to oppose each other. A higher inductance generally
means more winding turns, which means each winding turn is closer to one another.
For an operating
frequency range between 300 kHz to 425 kHz, the proximity effect and skin effect will increase the winding
resistance [20]. All these optimization parameters make it hard to find an optimal coil design analytically.
To simplify the optimization, a test setup in Ansys Maxwell 2d is made to optimize for the quality factor,
as seen in Equation 42. A good ratio between high inductance L and low resistance R can be achieved by
optimizing the quality factor.
Q = ωL
R
(42)
Ansys Maxwell 2d provides an optimizing tool to optimize for a given parameter by automatically changing
different other parameters. Utilizing the optimizing tool can simulate many coil designs automatically, and
a good coil design can be found. Figure 17(a) shows the Ansys Maxwell 2d simulation setup. The outer
coil radius router is set to be 10 cm. The dpitch is the distance between each winding, and rwinding is the
wire radius. The windings are rotated around ϕ, creating individual rings. The simulation will calculate
each inductance and winding resistance of each ring and sum them up, resulting in a total inductance and
winding resistance, which is a good approximation for a spiral planar coil. The Maxwell 2d optimization tool
will optimize the number of turns, the wire radius rwinding, and the distance between each winding dpitch
to find a suitable design based on the quality factor Q. Once a good coil design has been found, a 3d model
will be built in Maxwell 3d to verify the coil values which can be seen in Figure 17(b).

Control Circuit
The control circuit seen in Figure 9 will be realized in this section together with the components used. In
Figure 22, two control loop circuit is depicted. The first loop measures the current ip through a current
transformer (CU8965). The current signal on the secondary side of the current transformer is then amplified
using a differential amplifier (AD8056), which then gets filtered using a low-pass filter(LPF). The zero
crossing detector (TL3016) will then compare the incoming signal to zero, generating a PWM signal. The
PWM signal is then fed into the FPGA for compensation, which would output a delayed PWM signal to
operate the switches using the gate driver. Regarding the delay, the current on the secondary side of the
current transformer will be in phase with the current on the primary side. The differential amplifier will
not add a noticeable delay, whereas the significant phase shift will occur at the LPF. The LPF is designed
to have a resistance of 50Ωand a capacitance of 10n, which will result in a phase shift of 43◦to 53◦in a
page 31 of 49

FPGA will have a varying propagation delay depending on how the signal is processed, but through testing,
the FPGA will add around 10-12 ns delay. Lastly, the gate driver will add a 45 ns delay. It can be observed
that the propagation delay of the components is constant and, therefore, frequency independent, whereas
the LPF is frequency dependent. The sum of the propagation delays also has different phase shift effects of

4.3
Implementation of Control in the FPGA
An FPGA was chosen instead of a Digital Signal Processor (DPS) as it was required to run multiple codes
in parallel. An FPGA can run thousands of operations in parallel without dividing the clock cycle, whereas
a DSP can only run as many as it has cores. The clock cycle is crucial as it determines the number of
operations that could be done within one inverter period(300 kHz-425 kHz). The chosen FPGA has a clock
frequency of 100 MHz, which means that 114 and 166 operations could be done within half a period. It
also meant that using the proposed delay control, the FPGA had an incremented controllable delay of 10
ns, which is good enough for an operational frequency between 300 and 425 kHz. The written code for
the FPGA control can be seen in Appendix D. The code was written in the HDL called Verilog using the
software Vivado. Vivado will synthesize and analyze the Verilog code and generate a route design for the
FPGA. As the code is too long to explain, a simplified block diagram will be shown instead. Figure 24 shows
the block diagram of the proposed control strategy. The control happens entirely within the FPGA with two
main loops. The two loops were discussed in Figure 22, but in this section, the two loops will be explained
in more detail. The first loop is responsible for resonance frequency tracking. It starts with an input PWM
from the zero-crossing detector with added delay to represent the propagation delay ϕpd and phase shift ϕps
of the LPF.

To compensate for this delay, an additional control delay Tcontrol is added to shift the total angle ϕ for
a total of a half period π. The controllable delay Tcontrol is calculated using the measured frequency and
the equation derived in 32. As a result of the controllable delay, the total phase angle ϕ will be shifted
by an angle of π. Before sending the compensated PWM signal into the gate driver, the signal will have
to be inverted using a not gate to further shift it by an angle of π, which would result in a total angle of
ϕ = θ = 2π = 0 as seen in Figure 13. Depending on how the hardware is designed in Figure 22, the not-gate
might be connected to switches S3,4, but in this case, the not-gate has to be connected to S1,2. The second
loop is responsible for phase shift control of their inverter. It starts with an input DC current measured
by the 16-bit ADC. It then gets converted to the RMS current using the linear function found in Figure
23. An error is then calculated using the measured RMS current and the Ref, set to 5 amps. The block
fclk/2f calculates the number of clock cycles for a half period of any given frequency f, where fclk is the
FPGA’s clock frequency. By multiplying the output of the PI controller with fclk/2f, the amount of delay is
calculated in terms of clock cycles, which is essentially an angle between 0 and π in Figure 2(b).

6
Conclusion
This thesis details the design, implementation, and experimental validation of a wireless power transfer
system designed for high misalignment tolerance while maintaining CC and CV outputs without transmitter-
receiver communication. The proposed system significantly improves misalignment tolerance, efficiency, and
simplicity compared to existing solutions. The WPT system uses a parity-time (PT) symmetric configuration,
which maintains high efficiency and stable output despite variations in the coupling coefficient. The proposed
coupling estimation will eliminate the need for additional communication circuitry, reducing complexity and
cost.
The WPT design showcased robust performance with up to 9.5 cm horizontal and 2.8 cm vertical mis-
alignment, maintaining high efficiency and stable output in both CC and CV modes.
The experiments
showed a total system efficiency ranging from 80% to 96% across different misalignments, closely aligning
with theoretical predictions. This efficiency is competitive with some existing WPT systems. The system
simplifies the design and enhances reliability by avoiding the need for transmitter-receiver communication.
The PT-symmetric configuration ensures operation under varying coupling conditions without complex con-
trol algorithms, reducing costs compared to other WPT designs. The proposed control strategy showed a
perfectly compensated closed-loop system by utilizing delay compensation, resulting in reliable and precise
frequency tracking for a large range of frequencies.
A comparison between similar WPT designs revealed that all have either additional circuitry on the secondary
side or transmitter-receiver communication, which further increases cost and complexity. Despite having no
communication and additional circuitry on the secondary side, the proposed WPT design displays competitive
efficiency with other similar designs while having high misalignment tolerance.
A comparison between
the proposed design and an industry product from PowerMat also reveals similar capabilities. Although
PowerMat’s design can achieve 7 cm horizontal misalignment and 20 cm vertical misalignment, the proposed
design could achieve 9.5 cm horizontal misalignment and 2.8 cm vertical misalignment. A more significant
vertical misalignment could be done using different transmitter coil designs by focusing the magnetic field
more.
The proposed system offers a balanced trade-off between misalignment tolerance, efficiency, and simplicity.
Unlike other systems requiring additional secondary-side circuitry or communication, this system achieves
high performance with minimal additional components. The experimental setup validated the theoretical
models and simulations, confirming the system can effectively switch between CC and CV modes, providing
stable output even under significant misalignment.
In summary, this work advances WPT technology by addressing critical challenges related to misalignment
tolerance and system simplicity. The findings suggest that the proposed PT-symmetric WPT system is a
viable solution for applications requiring reliable and efficient wireless charging, such as electric vehicles and
autonomous drones.

Conclusion
6.1
Future Work
The main focus of this thesis was to design a WPT system that could operate in CC and CV mode while
having no transmitter-receiver communications or additional receiver-side circuitry. It also focused on the
proposed control strategy used to compensate for the intrinsic propagation delay in the closed-loop system
by utilizing delay compensation. It meant that other aspects of a WPT design were not optimized, such as
the transmitter and receiver coil design and the inverter design, which led to lower efficiency and capability.
Many transmitter and receiver coil designs could potentially improve the proposed WPT design, such as
efficiency and vertical and horizontal misalignment. Phase shift control on the inverter also seems to lead to
more losses due to hard switching. More elegant DC-AC typologies could be considered to increase system
efficiency. The rectifier is shown to be the most significant source of power loss due to conduction loss, which
could be massively improved by using active rectification.
