Abstract
Many of the products that are consumed in daily life contain chemicals that end up
in wastewater treatment plants (WWTPs) due to household cleaning and personal
care. Cyclic and linear siloxanes are very popular ingredients in the production
of most personal care formulations. The main removal pathways of siloxanes in
the WWTP are sorption to sludge and volatilisation. Since sewage sludge is used
in many countries as fertiliser for crop production, siloxanes may be discharged
into the environment and contaminate the soil and water.
In previous studies,
it was observed that the chemical activity of various (semi)-volatile hydrophobic
organic contaminants (HOCs) increased from primary to secondary and to digested
sludge. The aim of this study is to conﬁrm the increase in chemical potential of
three linear (L3, L4 and L5) and three cyclic (D4, D5 and D6) siloxanes due to
anaerobic digestion and measure the absolute chemical activities of these siloxanes
in sludge. Two types of sewage sludge were collected from a Danish WWTP on
three diﬀerent days: a mixture of primary/secondary sludge and digested sludge.
The analytical method was equilibrium headspace solid phase microextraction (HS-
SPME) coupled to gas chromatography (GC) and mass spectrometry (MS). Several
sludge samples were amended with sodium azide to investigate its eﬀect on the
chemical activity of the siloxanes. The purpose of adding sodium azide was to inhibit
the biological processes and stabilise the sludge samples. After obtaining results on
all target siloxanes, it was decided to focus the data treatment on D5 because
the best quality of data was achieved for this compound. The obtained activity
ratios of D5 in the sludge without azide showed that the chemical activity increased
from primary/secondary to digested sludge on two of the three days. However, the
chemical activity in the azide-treated sludge decreased due to anaerobic digestion
and the variance between test days was considerably reduced. Possibly in the case
of the untreated sludge, biodegradation in primary/secondary sludge caused a lower
chemical activity of D5, resulting in higher activity ratios and a wider spread of the
data. Furthermore, absolute chemical activity values were successfully determined
for D5 and ranged from 5.7 ·10−4 to 2.6 ·10−3 in primary/secondary sludge and
from 6.3 ·10−4 to 2.1 ·10−3 in digested sludge. These chemical activities of D5 were
compared with modelled values obtained by Gobas et al. (2015) and it was observed
that measured and estimated activities were consistent.

Introduction
1.1
Background
The majority of our daily activities include the use of chemicals.
Especially in
the household, many chemicals can be found in cleaning products or personal care
products. Whether they are ingredients from detergents, cosmetics or shampoos,
eventually they all ﬁnd their way into the wastewater system as so called down-
the-drain chemicals. In order to ensure a certain water quality in the environment,
wastewater treatment plants (WWTP) are supposed to purify the wastewater from
most chemicals before leading the eﬄuent back into the environment. However, it
is important to broaden the spectrum and take a look at the entire WWTP cycle.
In fact, these chemicals do not simply disappear, but they are either transformed
by a certain process or transported to another phase. Some compounds that are
very hydrophobic have a tendency to bind to the sludge, rather than dissolving in
the water-phase. Hence, it is important to realise that WWTP sludge may contain
pollutants and that possibly it could be a pathway for these chemicals to end up
in the environment. In Denmark, about 65% of sludge is recycled by application
as fertiliser on agricultural land. In 2015, that was equivalent to 85,000 tonnes of
sludge that were applied on soil (Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet, 2017).
This project focuses on a speciﬁc group of hydrophobic, volatile compounds called
siloxanes. These chemicals are often found in personal care products like shampoos,
conditioners and other cosmetics. Thus, they can be classiﬁed as down-the-drain
chemicals that ﬁnd their way into the environment through the wastewater system.
The cyclic siloxanes analysed in this study are classiﬁed as substances of very high
concern by the European Chemical Agency (ECHA). Two of them: Octamethyl-
cyclotetrasiloxane (D4) and Decamethylcyclopentasiloxane (D5) were banned from
use in wash-oﬀproducts over the course of writing this project. This ban points out
that the topic of siloxanes and their environmental fate is indeed a current issue.
Due to their physical and chemical properties, siloxanes are most likely to bind to
sludge after entering the wastewater system. In order to perform analysis on these
compounds in such a complex matrix, partitioning based sampling methods like
the solid-phase microextraction (SPME) present a great advantage. The analysis

on closed equilibrated systems allows to base all measurements on chemical activity
rather than concentrations. The chemical activity is a powerful measure, that gives
information on the energetic state of the analytes and enables comparison through-

1.2
Siloxanes
1.2.1
Chemical Structure of Linear and Cyclic Siloxanes
Siloxanes, also familiar as silicones, are chemical compounds with an oxygen-silicon
chain (Si-O-Si) where each silicon atom bears one or several organic groups, mainly
methyl, ethyl or phenyl groups (Nriagu, 2019). Silicon and oxygen atoms can form
a linear or a cyclic structure and therefore siloxanes are sub-categorised into linear
and cyclic siloxanes (Lassen et al., 2005). The three linear (L3, L4 and L5) and
three cyclic (D4, D5 and D6) siloxanes are classiﬁed as volatile methylsiloxanes
(VMS) due to their low molecular weight (Nriagu, 2019). Their chemical structure
consists of three to six units of silicon and oxygen atoms, with two or three methyl
groups linked to each silicon atom (Table 1.1).

1.2.2
Physical and Chemical Properties
The physical and chemical properties of compounds are used at an introductory
level in risk assessments as they provide an initial indication of the compounds’
fate and distribution in the environment.
These properties are easily accessible
data that can be found in handbooks and various databases, which contain a col-
lection of experimental and calculated values for each property. The availability of
experimental data is sometimes limited, especially for new compounds, and their
estimation is possible by using the Quantitative Structure Activity Relationships
(QSAR)(Baun et al., 2017). In this case, the already known data of compounds
with a similar structure than the compound in question, are used to predict the
needed properties (Baun et al., 2017).
Information from both literature and databases were used to assemble a rough envi-
ronmental proﬁle of the six target siloxanes. Table 1.2 includes the relevant physic-
==PAGE:22==

ochemical properties that were found in the Danish QSAR database, Helmholtz
Centre for Environmental Research (UFZ-LSER) database and US Environmen-
tal Protection Agency (EPA) database. An initial literature review showed that
the six siloxanes are highly hydrophobic and volatile compounds (Surita & Tansel,
2014). The characterisation of the chemicals as hydrophobic is explained by their
low solubility in water (less than 10 mg/L), whereas their Henry’s law constant
KH > 10−3 atm m3 mol−1 indicates that the compounds are volatile in aqueous
solutions (Ney, 1995; Baun et al., 2017).
Furthermore, the Koc parameter (organic carbon-water partition coeﬃcient) of all
six siloxanes is much greater than 5000, indicating that the compounds bind to the
soil with high aﬃnity when the content of organic carbon is more than 2 % (McCall
et al., 1980). In addition, it is observed that the logarithmic values of the partition
coeﬃcients between octanol and water (log Kow) are higher than 6, showing the
tendency of the siloxanes to accumulate to the lipid-phase of a system (Macek et
al., 1979).
In conclusion, under the hypothesis that there is a system with three diﬀerent media:
air, water and sludge with a high content of lipids and organic matter, it is expected
that the six siloxanes will have the tendency to escape from the aqueous solution
either by volatility or sorption to the lipids and organic matter of the sludge.

1.2.3
Use and Application of Siloxanes
Siloxanes have been produced for many decades, but because of the lack of analytical
data they were not seen as pollutants in the environment for a long time (Rücker
& Kümmerer, 2015). This changed over the recent years leading up to the decision
of the European Chemical Agency to ban the siloxanes D4 and D5 from wash-oﬀ
cosmetic products starting on the 31st of January 2020 (ECHA, 2020a).
The most common application of the six target siloxanes is in cosmetic and personal
care products like shampoos, creams and conditioners because of their soft texture,
while some of the compounds might also be used in washing products or waxes
(ECHA, 2020b). The concentrations and frequency of detection of cyclic siloxanes
in personal-care and household products are higher than linear (Horii & Kannan,
2008; D. Capela & Santos, 2016). Siloxanes are not limited to household products,
they can also ﬁnd application in various industries ranging from textile production
over construction to pharmaceuticals (Homem et al., 2016). Considering the type
of products that these compounds are used for, it is very likely that siloxanes can be
considered as down-the-drain chemicals. Once they enter the wastewater system,
their physical-chemical properties make them quite resistant against most of the
treatment processes of a WWTP and cause them to bind strongly to the sludge-
phase (Homem et al., 2016).
In fact, considering the enormous amounts in which certain siloxanes are produced,
the information about their fate in the environment, toxicity and persistence is
very limited. Although it is diﬃcult to get the exact tonnage of production for each
compound, several sources can show the order of magnitude of global and regional
siloxane production (Rücker & Kümmerer, 2015):
“Several billion kilograms of silicones are currently produced per year” (Brook,
2000). This includes, but is not limited to the 6 siloxanes analysed in this study.
Furthermore, China produced about 800,000 tons of cyclic siloxanes per year around
2008 (Xu, Shi, & Cai, 2013). Around 5 to 10 million pounds of D6 are produced
in the United States per year (PubChem, 2020). The amount of D4 produced or
imported in the European Economic Area is between 100,000 and 1,000,000 tonnes
per year (ECHA, 2020b). The quantity of D5 and D6 produced or imported in the
European Economic Area is 10,000 to 100,000 tonnes each per year (ECHA, 2020b).

1.3
Chemical Activity
The main concept used to perform analysis on the siloxanes in this study is the
chemical activity. Thus, it is essential to understand how it is linked to the equilib-
rium sampling methods used during this project. The chemical activity describes
the energetic state of a compound, which is often referred to as the chemical poten-
tial. Diﬀerences in chemical potential cause spontaneous processes in a substance
that can aﬀect the diﬀusion, sorption or partitioning (Reichenberg & Mayer, 2006).
In fact, the chemical activity can be used as an indicator to describe the chemical
potential and to predict the direction of these processes. In case of a diﬀerence in
chemical activity between compartments of a closed system, the direction of diﬀu-
sion, sorption and partitioning is always from high to low chemical activity until
a thermodynamic equilibrium is reached (Reichenberg & Mayer, 2006). In other
words, thermodynamic equilibrium can be deﬁned when the chemical activities of
a compound in two diﬀerent compartments are equal.
According to Gobas et al., 2018, chemical activity is closely linked to fugacity, since
both describe a chemical’s potential to distribute and react in the environment. In
environmental engineering, it is quite common to use chemical activity or fugacity
to study the distribution of compounds between various phases and media.
In
toxicology, the approach has also been used to determine the uptake and toxicity
of chemicals in organisms (Gobas et al., 2018). A big advantage of the chemical
activity is that it allows comparison of a speciﬁc compound across several media,
which is not possible by using only the concentration. The concentration does not
take into account a compound’s solubility in the medium, instead it is purely a ratio
between masses and/or volumes of the compound and the medium. Nonetheless,
chemical activity is not as widely used as the concentration and it is a rather
unknown concept to many outside of the ﬁeld of chemistry (Gobas et al., 2018).
Since the chemical activity relates to the energetic state of a compound, there is a
==PAGE:26==
8
Introduction
need for a reference state in order to express it. Schwarzenbach et al., 1993 suggest
that the chemical in its pure liquid form at a temperature of 298K (1 atm) is chosen
as the reference state. This causes the chemical activity to be a unitless measure
ranging from zero to one. A chemical activity of zero describes the very minimum
of the energetic state, meaning that there is no chemical activity at all, while a
chemical activity of one corresponds to the energetic state of the compound in its
pure liquid form at reference conditions (Reichenberg & Mayer, 2006).
Another measure that is closely linked to the chemical activity is the freely dissolved
concentration (Cfree). The freely dissolved concentration indicates the molecules
that are in aqueous solution and not attached to any particulate matter. Cfree can
also describe the potential of a substance to react with its environment or undergo
physiochemical processes (Reichenberg & Mayer, 2006). Hence, for hydrophobic
organic compounds like the siloxanes, the chemical activity can be estimated from
the freely dissolved concentration, if the solubility of the compound in the medium
(i.e. water) is known:

Chemical activity can also be determined experimentally by using passive sampling
methods. Many of the media that can be found in the environment have a complex
structure and are often not homogeneous. As stated by Gobas et al., 2018, passive
samplers allow the use of a known material, often polymers, with a homogeneous
composition and in most cases it is easier to determine the solubility of a compound
in the material of the passive sampler than the environmental medium directly. By
introducing a passive sampler to the system, the process of partitioning is initiated
and eventually leads to chemical equilibrium (Gobas et al., 2018).
Hence, the
chemical activity in the passive sampler can be used as a proxy to determine the
chemical activity of a substance in an environmental medium.

One example for these equilibrium sampling methods is the headspace solid-phase
microextraction (HS-SPME) which will be further explained in the Methods chap-

In general, this method utilises the partitioning between the sample, the
headspace and the polymer coating of the SPME ﬁbre in order to measure the
chemical activity. Volatile compounds like the siloxanes are able to enter the air-
phase in order to equilibrate the system. When it comes to mixtures between gas
and liquid phases, it is important to understand how the compounds are behaving
in the gas phase and which physical laws are applied.
Figure 1.1 shows the diﬀerence in behaviour of a gas between the Henry’s regime
and the Raoult’s regime.
In fact, since the vapour pressure corresponds to the
fugacity, which again is closely linked to the chemical activity, this graph applies
to the current study when it comes to determining absolute chemical activities of
siloxanes. It is noted that at low concentrations, the real behaviour of the gas is
equivalent to the behaviour by Henry’s law. This means that when the compound
is a minor component of the solution, the concentration is proportional to the
chemical activity. Nevertheless, at high concentrations and up to its pure state,
the compound behaves like an ideal gas and follows Raoult’s law (Ball, 2005). In
between both regimes lies an area where the real behaviour of the gas depends on
molecular interactions with other compounds in the mixture, thus the non-ideal

1.4
WWTP Sludge
The current study deals with hydrophobic down-the-drain chemicals, which end up
in the wastewater system and are likely to bind to sludge due to their physical
chemical properties. For many years, the application of sewage sludge on soil has
been one of the disposal alternatives along with incineration, dumping at sea and
landﬁlling (Singh & Agrawal, 2008). The high content of nutrients in sewage sludge
makes it an ideal fertiliser for arable soil, which is also in agreement with sustainable
development due to the recycling of nutrients and the reduction of using mineral
fertilisers (i.e. Phosphate and Nitrogen) (Jensen & Jepsen, 2005).
Even though land application of sludge is popular among many countries, the un-
certainty about the content of possible contaminants in sludge and their fate in soil
has led to a disagreement between the EU Member States (Milieu Ltd, WRc, &
Risk and Policy Analysts Ltd (RPA), 2009). A category of chemicals that raises
concern are the hydrophobic organic chemicals (HOCs), as they have the tendency
to sorb to the organic content of the sludge and be conﬁned between the organic
solids, where their biodegradation is limited (Hatzinger & Alexander, 1997; Wang
et al., 1993).
Considering that in many European countries sludge is applied to large areas of soil,
it is crucial to understand the behaviour of compounds that are likely to persist in
the sludge matrix. The use of sludge as fertiliser could possibly be a pathway for
siloxanes to spread in the environment.

1.5
Aims and Objectives of the Study
The primary aim of the current study is to test the hypothesis that the chemical
activity of linear and cyclic siloxanes in WWTP sludge is increased by the process
of anaerobic digestion. This hypothesis is based on the paper by Sjøholm, Flyckt-
Nielsen, et al., 2018 who observed that the chemical activity of a whole mixture of
(semi-)volatile HOCs increased from primary, to secondary and to digested sludge.
The secondary aim is to measure absolute values for the chemical activity of silox-
anes in sludge in order to set them in context of the modelled values from other
studies.
Hence, the following objectives are set in perspective of this study:
• Investigate the SPME sampling kinetics of the siloxanes L3, L4, L5, D4, D5
and D6.
• Determine chemical activity ratios between primary/secondary sludge and
digested sludge using SPME analysis in order to identify a diﬀerence in chem-
ical potential. Conﬁrm a change in chemical activity during the process of
anaerobic digestion by repeating measurements at multiple time points.
• Calibrate the results from the SPME analysis to obtain absolute values for
chemical activity in both sludge types and compare them with modelled values
from other studies.

Materials and Methods
Two diﬀerent sludge types, one from primary and secondary settling and another
from anaerobic digestion, were collected at a Danish WWTP on three diﬀerent
days. Automated equilibrium headspace solid-phase microextraction (HS-SPME)
coupled to gas chromatography (GC) and mass spectrometry (MS) was applied on
these samples to analyse the six target siloxanes. Firstly, a kinetic experiment was
conducted to ﬁnd the optimal SPME sampling time. Then, a series of three experi-
ments (one for each day) took place to determine the peak areas of the siloxanes in
sludge and obtain the chemical activity ratios of each siloxane between the two types
of sludge. The results from the analysis of the sludge samples that were collected
on Day 2, could not be obtained on the same day due to technical problems. Thus,
the experiment was repeated a week later, using the same samples that were stored
in the refrigerator at 4◦C. In the study by Trac et al. (2019), an experiment was
performed to determine the chemical activity of the siloxane D4 in toxicity tests.
The measured chemical activity of D4 from that experiment was plotted together
with a series of mass concentrations (% w/w) of D4 in miglyol to determine the
absolute chemical activity of the siloxanes in sludge. A detailed description of the
project plan is included in Appendix A. However, due to the outbreak of COVID-19,
the access to DTU and its lab facilities was shut down in March 2020 and several
of the planned tasks had to be adjusted.

2.2
Method Development
2.2.1
Sampling Kinetics
The main objective of the kinetic experiment was to determine the sampling kinetics
of linear and cyclic siloxanes, including their appropriate SPME sampling time.
The kinetic experiment was performed on spike oil containing a mixture of the six
analysed compounds: D4, D5, D6, L3, L4 and L5. Based on a literature review, it
was expected that equilibrium could be reached with an approximate temperature of
35◦C and an equilibration time of 60 minutes. The sampling times that were tested
in this experiment were 7, 15, 30, 60 and 120 minutes. A temperature of 35◦C was
used for this kind of experiment, since it is the closest temperature to environmental
conditions that could be controlled and stabilised on the autosampler. An agitator
was used while performing the experiment. For each sampling time, three replicates
were analysed, including one blank for every three samples. The volume of spike oil
contained in each vial was 1ml, with a mass concentration of 0.05% for each of the
analysed compounds. The available surface area for mass transfer was increased by
rolling the sample vial with an inclination, hence partially covering the sides of the
vial.
The purpose of this experiment was to ﬁnd an appropriate sampling time to get
equilibrium between the sample, headspace and PDMS ﬁbre coating of the SPME
method.
Subsequently, GC/MS was used to determine the peak area for each

to determine the optimal sampling conditions.

2.2.2
Blanks and Carry-Over
To ensure the quality of the acquired data, it was decided to test for possible
carry-over between samples in the kinetic experiment and adjust the thermal des-
orption time if necessary. Hence, one blank sample consisting of a vial containing
1ml of pure miglyol was added for every 3 samples. The samples were analysed
grouped in triplicates with increasing concentration, each group separated by a
blank. That way carry-over could be identiﬁed if the siloxane levels increased in
the blanks throughout the sequence. A further reason these blanks were added,
was to determine the background concentration of siloxanes. Equivalently, blanks
were included in the sludge experiments as well. On Day 1 and Day 3 the blanks
consisted of empty vials, whereas 1ml of pure miglyol was used on Day 2 just like in
the kinetic experiment. In this case, blanks were mainly placed in the sequence as
a separator between reference solutions, sludge samples and calibration solutions.
Potential carry-over was determined by calculating peak area ratios between a blank
and its preceding reference sample for each target siloxane during the kinetic exper-
iment. Under the assumption that there is a background concentration of siloxanes,
this method slightly overestimates the carry-over rate. This step was essential to
determine the absence of carry-over not only for the kinetic experiment, but also
for the three following analytical runs on sludge samples, since the experimental
setup did not change.
After receiving the results of Day 1 and Day 3, it was noticed that some blanks
from the sludge experiments had increased peak areas, about an order of magnitude
higher than the blanks in the kinetic experiment. Thus, it was decided to perform a
blank experiment, to investigate diﬀerent types of blanks and preparation methods.
The objective was to identify the source of the higher background and to ﬁnd the
type of blank causing the lowest signal to be used on the analytical run for Day 2.

2.2.3
Negligible Depletion and Mass Balance
Mass balance calculations were performed before conducting the experiments, to
demonstrate that during sampling the sorption of the analytes to the SPME ﬁbre
does not signiﬁcantly change their mass in the sludge. Since the SPME ﬁbre is
coated with polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) and the only content in the test vial was
sludge, a simpliﬁed assumption was that the system consisted of ﬁve compartments;
air, water, lipid, total organic carbon (TOC) and polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS). It
was planned to collect two types of sludge, one after primary and secondary set-
tling and another one after anaerobic digestion. Therefore the mass calculations
were done separately for each type using partition coeﬃcients and the volumes
of the ﬁve compartments (Appendix C). The air-water (Ka/w), lipid-water (Kl/w),
dissolved organic carbon-water (Kdoc/w), dissolved organic carbon-air (Kdoc/a) and
PDMS-water (KPDMS/w) partition coeﬃcients were obtained from the UFZ-LSER
database and can be seen in Table 6, Appendix C (Helmholz Centre for Environ-
mental Research – UFZ, 2020).
The rest of the partition coeﬃcients that were
needed for the calculations (lipid-air (Kl/a), lipid-dissolved organic carbon (Kl/doc),
PDMS-air (KPDMS/air), PDMS-lipid (KPDMS/l) and PDMS-dissolved organic car-
bon (KPDMS/doc)) were calculated from the above coeﬃcients and are included in
Appendix C as well.

are depleting with each sampling cycle. If the results for each sampling cycle are

the PDMS coating are negligible when removed from the system. In other words,
the amount removed by the PDMS ﬁbre is so small, that it does not change the
concentration of siloxanes in the sludge itself.

2.6
Chemical Analysis
Equilibrium headspace solid-phase microextraction (HS-SPME) coupled to gas chro-
matography (GC) and mass spectrometry (MS) was the method applied for the
chemical analysis of the six siloxanes. The instrument used to perform HS-SPME-
GC/MS analysis consists of a CTC PAL RSI 85 autosampler (CTC Analytics AG,
Zwingen,Switzerland), an Agilent 5977A MSD gas chromatographer and a 7890B
mass spectrometer (Agilent Technologies, CA, USA). Figure 2.3 shows a picture of
the instrument during analysis.
Figure 2.3: HS-SPME-GC/MS instrument analysing linear and cyclic siloxanes in
a sequence of sludge samples.
2.6.1
Equilibrium Headspace Solid-Phase Microextraction
The sampling method that was chosen for the extraction of the target siloxanes was
equilibrium headspace solid-phase microextraction (HS-SPME). SPME was intro-
duced in the 1990s as a free-solvent sampling technique and involves the absorption
of analytes into a coating and their desorption in analytical instruments (Pawliszyn,
1997). In the current experiment, the ﬁbre was set up with a polydimethylsiloxane
(PDMS) coating with a thickness of 7µm and needle size 23 (Supelco, Bellafonte,
PA, USA). The SPME ﬁbre was exposed only to the headspace of the test sys-
tem since the direct immersion in sludge would make it diﬃcult to clean the ﬁbre.
==PAGE:43==
2.6 Chemical Analysis
25
Therefore, the application of the equilibrium partitioning theory is important, as
after reaching equilibrium the chemical activity is the same in each compartment
of a system (Reichenberg & Mayer, 2006).
The ﬁrst step of the analysis was the conditioning of the ﬁbre which was heated for 1
hour at 315◦C, according to the manufacturers instructions. One vial at a time was
selected and transported to the agitator port by the autosampler, where the vial was
heated (35◦C) and agitated (250rpm) for 10 minutes to guarantee proper mixing
of the sample and facilitate the distribution of siloxanes within the vial. After 10
minutes the needle pierced the septum of the vial and the ﬁbre was extracting the
analytes for 60 min at 35◦C and with a continuous agitation. Then the ﬁbre was
introduced into the GC injection port for the desorption of the analytes.
2.6.2
Gas Chromatography (GC)
The combination of gas chromatography with mass spectrometry (GC/MS) was
chosen for the analysis of the six target siloxanes. Gas chromatography involves the
separation of analytes that is achieved by distributing them between two phases,
the stationary phase (inlet surface of a column) and the mobile phase (carrier gas
that transports the compounds through the column) (Kitson et al., 1996). In this
experiment, the column was polysiloxane (Agilent 122-5562UI DB5) of 60 m length,
0.25 mm inner diameter and 0.25 µm ﬁlm thickness and the carrier gas was helium.
After the extraction of the analytes, the ﬁbre was transferred to the injection inlet
of the GC for the desorption of the ﬁbre at 315 ◦C for 10 min. The application
of high temperature for several minutes was chosen to ensure that all six siloxanes
were desorbed and the ﬁbre was clean for the next sampling.
The column was
heated to guarantee the vaporisation of the compounds which were introduced into
the helium stream and were transported together through the column at a ﬂow rate
of 1.2 ml/min.
During the transport, the compounds are retained in the system for diﬀerent periods
of time (retention time), due to diﬀerences in their volatility and interaction with
the stationary phase. The compounds are eluted from the system at diﬀerent times,
with the most volatile compounds reaching the end of the column ﬁrst and the least
volatile reaching it last. In a mixture of compounds with a signiﬁcant variation
==PAGE:44==
26
Materials and Methods
in boiling points, temperature is an important factor.
In order to achieve the
separation of all compounds the analysis begins at a low temperature to enable
the distinction of the components with low boiling points and increases during the
separation to resolve the less volatile, with high boiling points.
Since the boiling points of the six siloxanes varied from 153◦C to 245◦C (Table 1.2),
the temperature of the column was programmed to increase gradually throughout
the separation process and the siloxanes were eluted from the system in the order
of their increasing boiling points. The initial temperature of the column was set
to 50◦C (held for 10 min), then it was gradually increased to 150◦C at a rate of
10◦C/min (held for 20 min) followed by increase to 250◦C at 1.5◦C/min and ﬁnally
to 310◦C at 30◦C/min (held for 1 min). After exiting the column, the separated
compounds were introduced into the mass spectrometer for their identiﬁcation and
quantiﬁcation. The ﬁbre was post-desorbed for 1 min to ensure that it was clean
for the analysis of the next sample.
2.6.3
Mass Spectrometry (MS)
Mass spectrometry was applied for the identiﬁcation and quantiﬁcation of the six
siloxanes.
The process of this technique involves the conversion of the samples
components into ions, their separation according to their mass to charge ratios
(m/z) and the record of their relative abundance. The individual spectra with the
selected m/z values for each compound can be seen in Appendix D. The ionisation
of the target compounds was achieved by electron ionisation (EI) at 70 eV. At this
step, a beam of electrons was directed towards the gaseous molecules of the injected
sample to form positive ions. These cations passed through the mass analyser and
reached the detector where they were transformed into an electrical signal. The
quantitative determination of the six siloxanes was done using the Selected Ion
Monitoring (SIM) method. This mode of monitoring allows to record only the ion
current of the selected m/z ratios that correspond to the compound of interest. A
mass spectrum was produced to show the correlation between the masses of the ions
and their abundances. The outcome was a total ion current (TIC) chromatogram
which illustrates the total abundance of each compound along with elution time
(Appendix E). (Hoﬀmannm & Stroobant, 2007) Figure 2.4 shows an illustration of
the entire HS-SPME-GC/MS process that was performed.

Blanks and Carry-Over
The possibility of carry-over of the six siloxanes between samples due to insuﬃcient
cleaning and desorption of the ﬁbre, was evaluated within the kinetic experiment.

3.2 Blanks and Carry-Over

Every sample in the kinetic experiment consisted of a vial with 1ml of miglyol spiked
with 0.05% of the target siloxanes. Blanks were used to separate the triplicates
for each sampling time. Thus, the carry-over ratios were calculated from the last
replicate of a certain sampling time with its immediate subsequent blank.
The
carry-over ratios for each siloxane are included in Appendix F and the detailed
results from the kinetic experiment are provided in Appendix G. It is observed that
the average ratios of all siloxanes are lower or equal to 0.05, indicating that the
level of siloxanes in blank samples is 95% lower than in the rest of the samples.
Thus, the carry-over for this method is below 5%, considering that there could be
an overestimation of the ratio because of the background concentration of siloxanes.
Additionally, blank vials were included in the sequence of all three analytical runs
of the sludge experiments and it was noticed that the levels of siloxanes in blanks
were about a factor of 10 higher than the blanks in the kinetic experiment. This
observation raised concern about possible background concentration either in the
analytical instrument or during the preparation of the samples.
Thus, a blank
experiment was performed in order to investigate diﬀerent types of blank samples
and their eﬀect on background levels.

Overall, the peak areas for linear siloxanes are much lower than for cyclic siloxanes.
The y-axis of Figures 3.4 and 3.5 have diﬀerent formats, indicating that background
levels for linear siloxanes are about three orders of magnitude lower than the back-
ground of cyclic siloxanes. Both ﬁgures show signiﬁcant diﬀerences of peak areas
between various types of blanks. More speciﬁcally, it is noticed that the levels of all
siloxanes are higher in the empty vials than in the vials containing miglyol. This
observation indicates the presence of siloxanes in the air-phase contained in the
vials. Since siloxanes are lipophilic compounds, they are expected to be retained
in the miglyol vials by the lipid-phase. Due to this observation, the analysis of
the sludge samples on Day 2 was improved by using vials with miglyol as blanks.
This choice is justiﬁed because just like the pure miglyol, the sludge also contains
a lipid-phase that keeps the background of siloxanes in the headspace lower.
In addition, the highest levels of all siloxanes were observed in the vials that were
washed with hexane, methanol and Milli Q ultrapure water. The non-polarity of
hexane and the methyl (CH3) group in methanol enables the attraction of non-polar
compounds such as siloxanes. The containers of hexane and methanol were stored
in the same fume hood, where the entire sample preparation took place. Hence,
since siloxanes are highly volatile compounds, there is a possibility that the high
peak areas were obtained due to the presence of siloxanes in the fume hood and
thus, in the two solvents used for the cleaning of the vials. In fact, this is consistent
==PAGE:53==
3.2 Blanks and Carry-Over
35
with the ﬁndings of another study, where methanol was used as a solvent to extract
siloxanes from soil samples and the recovery ended up being higher than 180% due
to solvent contamination (Sánchez-Brunete et al., 2010).
The last type of blank was a full immersion of the SPME ﬁbre into ethanol. For the
cyclic siloxanes, the ethanol vials reached the second highest peak areas (after the
washed vials). Even though the ethanol was not stored in the same fume hood as
the rest of the sample preparation materials, there is a possibility that volatilised
siloxanes distributed between the air-phase and the solvent.
In conclusion, the
use of non-polar solvents like hexane, methanol or ethanol resulted in the highest
levels for most siloxanes, which indicates that washing the vial can actually achieve
the opposite eﬀect and increase peak areas of blanks. Vials with 1ml of miglyol
delivered the lowest peak areas and were determined as the best type of blank
sample for further experiments. Nonetheless, certain challenges were faced during
this project that indeed conﬁrmed the diﬃculty of performing analytical work on
siloxanes.
Even though all necessary precautions were taken to prevent sample
contamination, the high volatility of siloxanes and their omnipresence caused certain
blank levels to be too high to consider the obtained data as valid for several of the
target siloxanes.
In fact, literature shows that keeping a low background concentration and sample
contamination when dealing with siloxanes can be quite challenging due to their
high volatility. The inappropriate handling of samples before the analysis of silox-
anes could lead to overestimation of their level (Boehmer et al., 2020). It has been
demonstrated that there is a possibility of contamination during sample preparation,
due to transport of siloxanes from the surrounding air, quality changes of laboratory
products or use of solvents (Boehmer et al., 2020). The use of personal care prod-
ucts that contain siloxanes, could contaminate the samples and equipment, during
sample collection and preparation but also during the production of the equipment
(Durham et al., 2020). Furthermore, the components of many analytical instru-
ments often contain silicones and there is a possibility to aﬀect the results of the
analysis due to background contamination (McNett et al., 2020). Consequently, in
recent studies special attention has been paid during the analytical process, from
sampling to measurement to ensure a high quality of the results (Abrahamsson et
al., 2020; Bletsou et al., 2013).

actual sludge. However, since the chemical activity is equal in all phases of an
equilibrated system, it is assumed that both data sets can be directly compared to
each other.

4.2
Comparison of Results with Experimental Data
The ﬁnal results on absolute chemical activity in the present study were only avail-
able for D5. This leads to the question if the obtained results for D5 can actually
be representative for the other ﬁve target siloxanes. It has been established that D5
has the highest production volume of all siloxanes analysed in this study (ECHA,
2020b). Hence, it could be argued that D5 is also the siloxane with the highest
environmental exposure, meaning that the absolute chemical activities of the other
siloxanes in sludge are likely to be lower. In fact, previous studies have shown that
from the measured concentrations in sludge of various volatile methylsiloxanes, the
D5 concentrations were the highest (Soreanu et al., 2011). These results are also
consistent with another study that was presented at SETAC Europe 30th Annual
Meeting about the occurrence of siloxanes in biogas from landﬁlls and WWTPs: this
literature review showed that cyclic siloxanes were present at much higher concen-
trations than linear siloxanes in WWTP biogas and D5 was the compound with the
highest levels (Bragança et al., to be published). The results of the present study
conﬁrm these ﬁndings under the assumption that there is equilibrium between the
digested sludge and the biogas in the digester tank at WWTP Lynetten.
4.3
Anaerobic Digestion and Chemical Activity
Anaerobic digestion is a process of the wastewater sludge treatment that combines
the decomposition of organic matter by microorganisms in the absence of oxygen
and the production of biogas (Rogoﬀ& Screve, 2011). It is an attractive tech-
nique since it oﬀers a renewable way of energy production via the exploitation of
the generated biogas (Gould, 2015).
The digestive sludge is used in many Eu-
ropean countries as fertiliser on arable soil due to the high content in nutrients.
However, in recent studies it was observed that the chemical activity of polycyclic
aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) and many other (semi-)volatile hydrophobic organic
contaminants (HOCs) increased during the anaerobic digestion treatment (Sjøholm,
Flyckt-Nielsen, et al., 2018; Sjøholm, Schmidt, et al., 2018). This observation raises
concern about the possibility of soil and water contamination, due to the reuse of
sludge in agriculture.
==PAGE:70==
52
Discussion
In the paper by Sjøholm et al. (2018), chemical activity ratios of various semi-
volatile HOCs were determined between diﬀerent types of sewage sludge. The sludge
samples were treated with sodium azide and the analytical method used was the
same as in the current study, enabling the direct comparison of the activity ratios
from both studies. Generally, the results obtained by Sjøholm et al. (2018) showed
that chemical activity for most analytes increased from primary to secondary and
to digested sludge. However, in the case of D5, chemical activity was the same in
the particular three stages of sludge treatment. In the current study, the activity
ratios of D5 in azide-treated sludge (Figure 3.10) were lower than 1 on all three
days, indicating that the activity of D5 decreased from the mixed to the digested
sludge. This observation is in agreement with the results obtained by Sjøholm et al.
(2018), since in both cases the samples were poisoned with azide and the chemical
activity was not increasing due to anaerobic digestion.
However, the activity ratios of the non-azide sludge on Days 1 and 2, were above
one, designating that chemical activity increased from mixed sludge to digested.
The diﬀerences between the ratios of the azide-treated and the untreated sludge
question whether the results for the azide-treated sludge are representative of the
real-world case. Due to the high variance between the activity ratios of D5 in the
untreated sludge, it is not possible to draw the conclusion that the activity of D5 is
increasing from the mixed to digested sludge. Further research is needed, in order
to include the analysis of a large number of sludge samples obtained over diﬀerent
days, weeks and months to account for temporal change in the sludge composition.
4.4
Eﬀect of Sodium Azide on Chemical Activity
The results of this study show that the chemical activity ratios of D5 between
digested and primary/secondary sludge were aﬀected by the addition of sodium
azide to the sludge samples. In fact, the addition of azide considerably increased
the chemical activity in primary/secondary sludge, but it had a lesser impact on
the digested sludge on 2 out of the 3 test days. Despite the variance between the
days, this shows that siloxanes are not equally biodegraded in primary/secondary
and in digested sludge. However, in all cases with the exception of one (digested
sludge on Day 2), the addition of azide caused an increase in chemical activity.
==PAGE:71==
4.4 Eﬀect of Sodium Azide on Chemical Activity
53
Since the main purpose of azide is the inhibition of microbial activity, the diﬀerence
in chemical activity ratios could be due to the absence of microbial processes in
the azide-treated sludge. A potential reasoning behind this phenomenon is that the
biological processes are constantly degrading the bioavailable part of the siloxanes
in sludge, which is keeping their free concentration low. If the free concentration
of siloxanes is depleted by microorganisms faster than the desorption from the
matrix can compensate, the system becomes dynamic and the free concentration at
equilibrium decreases. By inhibiting these biological processes, the siloxanes could
reach an equilibrated state within the system, that is only governed by sorption,
which would increase the free concentration and hence, the chemical activity.
Furthermore, the chemical activity ratios of D5 in the untreated sludge had a con-
siderable spread, while in the case of the azide-treated sludge they were more con-
sistent. The amount of substances in sludge and the environmental conditions in
which microorganisms grow and reproduce, vary a lot with time and the diﬀerence
in biodegradation causes high variance between the results obtained on diﬀerent
days (Henze & Comeau, 2008). The variance due to biodegradation is much bigger
than the variance due to the sorption of siloxanes in sludge. Therefore, in order to
obtain consistent data to conﬁrm the increase in chemical activity of siloxanes due
to anaerobic digestion, a large number of samples would need to be collected from
the WWTP at diﬀerent times to account for the changes in microbial activity.
Eventually, by obtaining information on both the sludge with and without azide, it
is possible to compare the chemical activity in sludge for two scenarios: One where
the only process is driven by sorption (with azide) and one where both sorption
and biodegradation drive the test system (without azide). In that case, the change
in chemical activity works as a parameter for the microbial activity. Potentially,
this might even lead to a method to investigate biodegradation processes as an
alternative to the conventional biodegradation experiments, that are based on the
biological oxygen demand (BOD) or substrate depletion.

4.5
Future Recommendations
Within the present study, it was possible to identify the absolute chemical activity
of D5 in WWTP sludge. In order to expand the spectrum, it is recommended to
continue with the analysis on other siloxanes. Even though the presence of linear
siloxanes was very low in the current study, there is a chance that the industry
could shift towards the use of linear siloxanes in the future, due to the recent ban
of D4 and D5 from wash-oﬀproducts, possibly followed by a ban of cyclic siloxanes
including D6 from all types of consumer products (European Chemicals Agency,
2019). Hence, it is recommended to observe the development of linear siloxane levels
in WWTP sludge in the future.
Furthermore, the underlying study focused on investigating the chemical activity of
D5 in WWTP sludge, which is a major exposure pathway for siloxanes to enter the
environment. In addition, it is recommended to extend the analysis to other envi-
ronmental media, for instance soil from agriculture sites that use sludge as fertiliser.
This could give more information about the environmental fate of siloxanes, for in-
stance if there is sorption of the compounds to the soil matrix or if the majority
evaporates to the air-phase over time. In a study by Sánchez-Brunete et al. (2010),
D5 and D6 have been detected in several agricultural soil samples with an average
concentration of 19.8 ng g−1 for D5 and 13.3 ng g−1 for D6. In the same study,
sludge treated soil samples were also analysed and presented higher means for both
compounds. The average concentrations in sludge amended soil were 34.2 ng g−1
for D5 and 27.2 ng g−1 for D6 (Sánchez-Brunete et al., 2010). This indicates that
using sludge as fertiliser could indeed increase the contamination in agricultural
soil with siloxanes. It is noted, that the presented mean values were not directly
retrieved from that study, but they were calculated from the available results of 8
agricultural soils and 2 sludge amended soils given in that paper.
In the previous section, it was discussed how the addition of azide aﬀects the chem-
ical activity. This could be an interesting topic to further investigate, in order to
better understand how chemical activity is linked to biodegradation. In fact, this
phenomenon is not only relevant for siloxanes and could be extended to many other
pollutants. Since azide is commonly used in experiments as an inhibitor for mi-
crobial activity in environmental samples, it is crucial to understand its eﬀect on
the target analytes. Otherwise, there is a risk that the obtained data is not fully
==PAGE:73==
4.5 Future Recommendations
55
representative for the real-world case.
Finally, it is recommended to study the removal of siloxanes, in order to minimise
the environmental exposure of these compounds. Next to the environmental con-
cern, there is also a mechanical concern, especially with siloxanes in biogas, since
they can form solids and considerably weaken the eﬃciency of processes that are
powered by biogas (Bragança et al., to be published). Previous studies reported
that co-composting of sludge with other types of biowaste oﬀers the potential for
reduction of several contaminants (Kupper et al., 2008; Brändli et al., 2007). Re-
cently, Sjøholm et al. (2018) observed that the amendment of sewage sludge with
activated carbon and biochar reduced the chemical potential of D5 but did not com-
pletely remove the compound. Since the WWTP marks the entry point of siloxanes
into the environment (either through sludge, biogas or eﬄuent), it is crucial to stop
the spread of these pollutants as early as possible in their pathway.

Conclusion
In the present study, HS-SPME GC/MS analysis was performed on WWTP sludge
samples to determine the chemical activity of siloxanes. The experiments were per-
formed on mixed primary/secondary and digested sludge both with and without
azide. It can be concluded from the results that the composition of siloxanes in
sludge reﬂects their production volumes. D5 has the highest levels in sludge, fol-
lowed by D4 and D6. Overall, the levels of these cyclic siloxanes is approximately
2 to 3 orders of magnitude higher than the levels of linear siloxanes.
Furthermore, absolute chemical activity values were successfully determined for
Decamethylcyclopentasiloxane (D5) and were consistent with the modelled data
from a previous study. Absolute chemical activity values of D5 ranged from 5.7 ·
10−4 to 2.6 · 10−3 in primary/secondary sludge and from 6.3 · 10−4 to 2.1 · 10−3 in
digested sludge.
Besides the absolute values, chemical activity ratios were determined to investigate
the change of the chemical potential of D5 in sludge during the process of anaerobic
digestion. Results from Day 1 and 2 showed an increase in chemical activity, while
Day 3 resulted in a decrease of chemical activity of D5 from mixed sludge to digested
sludge without azide. The activity ratios ranged from 0.38 to 1.95 between the three
days. Thus, the hypothesis that anaerobic digestion increases the chemical activity
of siloxanes can neither be conﬁrmed nor refuted based on the results from this
paper. However, it was established that by inhibiting the biodegradation of D5
with sodium azide, the activity ratios were stabilised and fell below 1 for each test
day, with values ranging from 0.57 to 0.89, which indicates a decrease of chemical
activity from mixed to digested sludge. In fact, this shows that performing siloxane
analysis on azide treated sludge is not fully representative for the real-life case, since
the addition of azide increases the chemical activity, especially in mixed sludge.
Hence, results obtained from azide treated sludge are likely to overestimate the
chemical activity of D5.
The HS-SPME GC/MS method used in this study proved to be eﬀective in order
to determine chemical activity ratios and absolute values for one of the six target
siloxanes. In future studies, it is recommended to develop the method in order to
==PAGE:76==

obtain absolute chemical activity values on other siloxanes as well, by increasing
the extraction time of the analytes and improving the quality of blanks. It would
also be of interest to perform analysis on sludge samples from numerous diﬀerent
days and weeks, to observe potential patterns in the variation of the results and
improve the quality of the data. Moreover, investigating the activity of siloxanes in
various environmental media, like agricultural soil could help to better understand
the environmental fate of these compounds.
