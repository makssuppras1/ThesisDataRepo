Abstract
I dette kandidatspeciale har vi forsøgt at ﬁnde analytiske udtryk og numeriske metoder til
at udregne overlevelsesfunktionen for multivariate fase-type fordelinger.
Vi har taget udgangspunkt i det Kulkarniligningerne, som er et system af partielle
diﬀerentialligninger overholdt af overlevelsesfunktionen. Det meste af arbejdet er dog
forgået ved at foretage sandsynlighedsteoretiske overvejelser.
Vi er i særtilfælde kommet frem til løsinger, der dog er meget beregningstunge. For
særtilfælde har vi vist hvordan overlevelsesfunktionen er langt nemmere at berenge end i
det konkrete tilfælde. Det har vist sig at to vigtige betingelser for at simpliﬁcere problemet
er at intensitetsmatricen er øvretriangular og/eller at alle elementer i rewardmatricen er
positve.
Derudover har vi generaliseret hvordan man kan reparametrisere fase-type fordelinger i
det univariate tilfælde til det multivariate tilfælde.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
When modelling multivariate stochastic phenomena one always hopes to be able to use
the normal distribution, since if this is possible a lot of things will be easily obtained, due
to the numerous theorems and deep understanding of the multivariate normal distribution.
However, when the phenomena can not be described by the normal distribution one is
usually left only with non-parametric ways of modelling the phenomena. When this is
the case the model’s usefulness decreases dramatically. If instead the phenomena could
have been modelled by another distribution for which we a priori had an understanding of,
then the model would be much more useful.
This is what multivariate phase-type (MPH∗) distributions might be able to do. I.e there
might be a lot of situations for which the normal distribution is not suﬃcient, but where a
suitable MPH∗distribution is.
Of course the normal distribution would still be preferred since our understanding of
MPH∗distributions is far from that of the normal distribution, but recent studies ([BN16])
of MPH∗distributions have revealed general results about the MPH∗distributions. These
include a closed form solution of the moment generating function and from this formulas
for the cross-moments. Moreover it was shown that the marginal distributions and linear
combinations of the marginal distributions are univariate phase-type distributed. The most
important result about MPH∗distributions in the context of this project is the Kulkarni
equations (See [Kul89] or [BN16]) which is a system of partial diﬀerential equations shown
to govern the survival functions of MPH∗distributions. This paper will deal with one of
the key-challenges of the MPH∗distributions, namely obtaining either the distribution
function, density function or survival function for given MPH∗-distributions.

2.1
Markov chains and Markov processes
Deﬁnition 1. A stochastic process, X, is a collection of random variables {Xi}i∈E, where
E = Z+ or E = R+.
In general E could be other sets, but for our purposes these two will suﬃce. We say
that X is discrete if E = Z+ and continuous if E = R+.
Usually we think of Xt as the state of the stochastic process at time t, which in general is
random. The set containing all states that Xt can possibly obtain for any t is called the
state-space.
The class of stochastic processes that we are interested in is a subset of the following
stochastic processes:
Deﬁnition
2. If a stochastic process, X, satisﬁes that P(Xt ∈A|{Xu : ∀u ≤s}) =
P(Xt ∈A|Xs) = P(Xt−s ∈A|X0), for all s < t and A in the state-space of Xt, then X is
said to be a time-homogeneous Markov chain in the discrete case and a time-homogeneous
Markov process in the continuous case.
We will assume time-homogeneity for all the stochastic processes that we work with.
Usually we are in the case where t represents some time in the future, while s is the time
of the present. Then the deﬁnition corresponds to saying that Xt only depends on the
past through the present.
Now we will go ahead and deﬁne the particular kind of Markov processes that will be used
to construct the Phase-type distributions:
Deﬁnition 3. A Markov process, X, is said to be a Markov Jump process if its state-space
is countable.
Even though Markov processes/chains are allowed to have countable state-spaces we
are only interested in the case where the state-space is ﬁnite, and so whenever we refer to
a Markov jump process we will assume that it has a ﬁnite state space.
The name of this kind of process is appropriate since the only way that a Markov jump
process can change state is by a jump between two elements from the state-space.
To characterize Markov jump processes with p states we need a row-vector of length p and
a matrix of dimension p × p, where p is the amount of possible values that Xt can take,
i.e the amount of states in the state-space. The vector is used to characterize the initial
distribution as follows:

Deﬁnition 4. If X is a Markov jump process or Markov chain with initial distribution
α then P(X0 = i) = αi.
Of course this deﬁnition requires that the elements of α sum to 1 and that each element
is non-negative.
The matrix will be used to describe how the Markov jump process/chain evolves over time.
The matrix is formulated in slightly diﬀerent ways depending on whether we consider a
Markov chain or a Markov jump process. The case of Markov chains is simplest, so we
start with this. One advantage of working with Markov Jump processes is that for each
state there is only a ﬁnite number of other states that the process can go to, likewise since
the probability of jumping from one particular state, to another particular state does not
change over time we can specify the probability of each of these events. This means that
if there is a total of p states we could make a p × p matrix where the i, j’th element would
specify the probability of a process currently being in state i, to go to state j during the
next time step:
Deﬁnition 5. For a Markov chain, X, we associate the transition matrix, P, given by
Pi,j = P(Xt+1 = j|Xt = i).

have to understand how long such processes spends each time they visit a state.

We are going to need the following concept of an absorbing state:
Deﬁnition 7. A state of a Markov process is said to be absorbing if once the process has
entered this state, it never leaves. That is state i is said to be an absorbing state for the
Markov process X if P(Xt ̸= i|Xs = i) = 0 for all t > s.
Absorbing states are easily characterized by the intensity matrix using the following
theorem:
Theorem 4. If X is a Markov jump process with intensity matrix S, then the i′th state
of X is absorbing if and only if the i′th row of S consists only of zeros.
Proof. Notice that the i’th state is absorbing if and only if the probability of moving from
state i to any other state is 0. This means that P(t)i,j = 0 for j ̸= i. Now the theorem
follows by noting that this is the case if and only if the i’th row of S is zero.

Phase-type distributions
Phase-type distributions oﬀers a way to model positive random variables. Phase-type
distributions are in fact so general that they are dense in the set of all positive-valued
distributions. This means that for any random variable that only takes positive values we
can make a sequence of phase-type distributions with densities that converges point-wise
to the density of this random variable.
The deﬁnition of phase-type distributions relies on Markov processes:

We denote the length of α by p, and usually its elements sum to 1. If they do not

starting in the absorbing state, meaning that P(τ = 0) > 0. S has to be a p × p matrix

at least one positive entry. The interpretation of −Se is clearly that it gives the intensities
with which the Markov process get absorbed from each of the transient states. We usually
denote it by s = −Se. Furthermore we require that all elements of eSt goes to zero when t
goes to inﬁnity. As will be shown this ensures that the probability of being in the ﬁrst p
states goes to zero as time goes to inﬁnity, meaning that eventually the underlying Markov
process will be absorbed in the p + 1’th state. For this reason the ﬁrst p states are called
transient.
So a phase-type distribution is simply the time to absorption in a Markov jump process
with exactly one absorbing state, and all other states being transient.
For a particular phase-type distributed random variable, we refer to the Markov jump
process generating the phase-type distributed random variable as the underlying Markov
process.

2.3
Multivariate phase-type distributions
Several ways of generalizing phase-type distributions to the multivariate case have been
proposed. We will only consider one case namely the class usually denoted by MPH∗
(multivariate phase-type, with the star to diﬀerentiate from the class denoted by MPH)
proposed by Kulkarni [Kul89]. Other more general classes have been proposed by Bladt
and Nielsen [BN16], namely MME∗, MV PH, MV ME, MBME∗and MV BME. Even
though we have these slightly more general classes of distributions the MPH∗is still
very general and like in the univariate case all positive multivariate distributions can be
approximated in distribution arbitrarily well by MPH∗distributions. Also it might be
that results regarding this class of distributions can be generalized to the more general
classes.
The idea behind MPH∗is to build upon the univariate phase-type distributions using a
reward structure.

Results
The aim of this thesis was to ﬁnd a way to calculate the survival function for MPH∗-
distributions. Unfortunately we have not been able able to come up with any analytical or
practically feasible numerical method for this. We have, however, come up with analytical
expressions in some sub-cases. Some of these are not suited for real applications since they
scale very poorly with the dimensions of the intensity matrix. Apart from these solutions
we have found results regarding the continuity and diﬀerentiability of the survival function
which will be presented. The last thing that we will go into is some of the attempts at

Before digging into the speciﬁc results a few general notes on the approaches and the
notation will be made here.

but we have not been able to formulate this in any useful manners.
For this reason we simply have to disregard the notion of time, and instead ﬁnd other
conditions that could be used to solve the PDE-system. These conditions should be tied
to something that we know about the distribution of Y.
One thing that we do know about the distribution Y is that the marginal distributions
are univariate phase-type distributions.
In the case where all elements of Y gains reward from all states, i.e where all elements of
the reward matrix, R, are strictly positive we can use this.
To see this consider for a particular choice of i, j ∈{1, 2, .., p} the maximum amount of
times that Yi can be larger than Yj. This has to be ki,j = maxx{ Rx,i
Rx,j }, since Rx,i
Rx,j is the
amount of reward that Yi gathers in state x for each point of reward gathered by Yj in
state x.
Thus if the underlying Markov process only visits the state for which Rx,i
Rx,j obtains its
maximum we get that Yi = ki,jYj.
This means that if Yi > ki,jyi then Yj > yi. Thus if we have a vector y where there exists
an i so that yi > ki,jyj for all j then Yi > yi ⇒Yj > yj for all j. This also means that
Gk(y) = P(Y > y|X0 = k) = P(Yi > yi|X0 = k).
The last probability can be computed based on the marginal distribution of Yi, which was
what we wanted.
This way we obtain a condition for each element of Y for a total of n conditions.
The problem with this approach is that as described in Section 4.8 on page 45 on diﬀer-
entiability of G, it is often not diﬀerentiable on the boundary of the range of Y, thus
rendering these boundary conditions useless.
Having all elements of R positive does, however, give us another possibility. Namely that
if this is the case we know that all elements of Y are positive, and thus G(δiyi) equals the
marginal survival function of Yi, just as we saw for the examples.
Thus in the case where R is positive and it is possible to ﬁnd solutions to the Kulkarni
equations, we will always get n boundary conditions as the marginal distributions, which
can be used to pick the right solution to the Kulkarni equations.
It should be noted that in the previous examples the marginal distributions were expo-
nential distributions, and therefore very simple to use. In the general case the marginal
distributions are going to be phase-type distributions, which will make things more
complicated.

reward matrix is the identity matrix. Here we will change our focus by looking at the case

can never go to state j if j < i.

Combining it all
Now that we have a formula for the survival function conditioned on the amount of
visits to each state, we just have to combine it with the probabilities of having these
amount of visits to each state. In section 4.5 on page 36 it was shown how to obtain
these probabilities. It was discussed how to obtain the probability of having more than N
changes of state before absorption and what this means to the approximation. Using all
this to approximate the survival function of
Y ∼MPH∗(α, S, R) :
1. Construct the matrices P and ˜P as described by (4.13) and (4.14).
2. Choose N so that αP Nδp is suﬃciently close to 1.
3. Compute α ˜P Nδp.
4. Compute the conditional survival functions given by either (4.15), (4.18) or as
described for (4.19), depending on whether S is upper-triangular and whether all
paths to absorption goes through a state where reward is gathered for all elements
of Y.
5. Sum the conditional survival functions multiplied by their respective probabilities
found from the coeﬃcients of the polynomial in step 3.
This works mathematically, but in practice it is very computationally demanding. In
practice one should always try to simplify the survival function as shown in the examples.
Even for small dimensions this can be crucial to make the survival function practically
computable. This general method can still serve as a valuable start for computing the
survival function.

Conditions for diﬀerentiability of the survival
function
Here we will investigate what conditions that are necessary for G to be diﬀerentiable, in
the sense that all partial derivatives exist.
We can split the task into three cases:
1. When a small change to yk does not change G(y), i.e locally G(y) does not depend
on yk.
2. When a small change to yk does change G(y) and changes to yl also aﬀects G(y) for
at least one l ̸= k, i.e locally G(y) depends on yk and other elements of y.
3. When small changes to yl does not aﬀect G(y) for l ̸= k, but only small changes to
yk aﬀects G(y), i.e locally G(y) only depends on yk.
It is the reward matrix, R, which determines what values of y that fall in each case.
In words the ﬁrst case is when G does not depend on the k’th argument in which case
∂G
∂yk

This can happen if yk is relatively small compared to the rest of y. The second situation
is where G depends on all or at least several of its arguments, which will tend to be the
case if all elements of y are similarly sized. The third case is when G only depends on one
of the variables, which can happen if yk is relatively large compared to the rest of y. In
this case we know that G is equal to the survival function of the marginal distribution of
yk which is known to be univariate phase-type, and so we can ﬁnd the derivative based on
this.
It is worth noting that if R is diagonal we will always be in the second case.

1 and 3. Thus we have to look into the second case, and of course the boundaries between
the 3 cases are candidates for non-diﬀerentiability.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
The aim of this thesis was to ﬁnd ways to obtain the survival function of multivariate
phase-type distributions. In this we have partly succeeded, by showing a method for
obtaining approximations of the survival function. The method is not practically feasible
due to heavy computational demands. However, it could prove useful to use the method
in combination with simpliﬁcations of the concrete applications.
It is clear that having an upper-triangular intensity matrix greatly simpliﬁes the matter
and allows for exact solutions due to the ﬁnite nature of this case.
Another condition that was shown to simplify the survival function is to have all elements
of the reward matrix be positive.
We have worked with the system of partial diﬀerential equations, shown to govern the
survival function by Kulkarni, and gotten minor results about the structure of the survival
function. For particular examples it was shown how the Kulkarni equations can be solved
with the Marginal survival functions as boundary conditions.
Furthermore it was shown that even under the assumption that the intensity matrix is
upper-triangular and the reward matrix only has positive elements the survival function
might not be diﬀerentiable everywhere. On the other hand we showed suﬃcient conditions
for making the survival function diﬀerentiable.
As a side product we showed how theorems about alternative parametrisations of phase-type
distributions can be generalised to the multivariate case.

Further research
It was shown that the survival function of multivariate phase-type distributions is diﬀeren-
tiable under some restrictions on the intensity matrix and the reward matrix. However,
this has not been utilised for anything. If this project were to continue we would put more
eﬀort into investigating whether classical methods for solving systems of partial diﬀerential
equations could be used to solve the Kulkarni equations under the restrictions found to
make the survival function diﬀerentiable.
Along the same lines it could be fruitful to further investigate the case where P(Y > 0) = 1,
since it was shown that the Marginal distributions could be used as boundary conditions
for the Kulkarni equations in this case.
