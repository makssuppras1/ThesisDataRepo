Abstract
In the evolving field of Natural Language Processing (NLP), Relation Extraction (RE)
is the task of identifying and categorizing relationships between entities in unstructured
text. This thesis explores the applications of various Large Language Models (LLMs)
for RE, focusing on adapting state-of-the-art models to handle a broader set of relations
beyond the original benchmarks while also providing deeper insights by including in-
dividual relation-type metrics. The thesis combines document-level and sentence-level
datasets to offer a diverse set of examples for evaluating two key models: Document-
level Relation Extraction with Evidence-guided Attention Mechanism (DREEAM) and
Relation Extraction By End-to-end Language generation (REBEL).
Based on transformer architectures, both models are adapted to suit different RE
tasks. In addition, prompt optimization techniques using the DSPY framework were
employed to enhance responses from proprietary language models such as OpenAI GPT,
along with a brief exploration of local language models within Demonstrate Search Pre-
dict, which resembles Pytorch (DSPY).
The results demonstrate that while models perform well on specific relation types,
they often struggle with the same classes, particularly those ambiguous or underrepre-
sented. Furthermore, the effectiveness of prompt-based techniques highlights the lim-
itations of both the models themselves and the evaluation methods, emphasizing the
need for greater flexibility in output. The thesis concludes with recommendations for
future work aimed at addressing these challenges and improving the models’ overall
performance.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In the advancing field of NLP, RE serves as a critical mechanism for identifying and
categorising entities and their relationship within unstructured text. With the continu-
ous expansion of knowledge across diverse domains, such as scientific and medical fields,
there is a growing need to organise vast amounts of generic data eﬀiciently[33]. One
approach to this organisation is the construction of Knowledge Bases (KBs), which facil-
itates more straightforward navigation and retrieval of relevant information[71]. These
KBs are often used to build Knowledge Graphs (KGs), which power various applications,
including search engines. However, despite the advancements in NLP, automated knowl-
edge extraction remains challenging, and many KGs have been manually constructed.
Nonetheless, the emergence of modern NLP architectures, larger datasets, and greater
computational power have significantly renewed interest in automating these processes.
RE is a subset of the broader field of Information Extraction (IE), which aims to
extract meaningful information from raw data in any format, whether human-readable
or computationally structured for databases. Although IE originated from extracting
information from raw text, it has evolved into a multi-modal problem with the growth of
web-based data sources, encompassing various formats[29]. IE includes several subtasks,
such as segmentation, part-of-speech-tagging and semantic role labelling. However, for
this thesis, Named Entity Recognition (NER) and RE are particularly important. State-
of-the-art research has shown that when these tasks are learned together, it can enhance
the performance of each task. These tasks are closely related and will be treated as a
single task for the rest of the thesis, with a focus on identifying the types of relations[23].
NER identifies and categorises important nouns, such as persons, organisations, or
locations within a text. For example, in the sentence “Before joining UCB, Lisa North
Worked for Pegasus Books in North Berkely”[53], the entities “UCB”, “Lisa North”,
“Pegasus Books” and “North Berkeley” represent distinct categories. NER is widely used
in everyday applications. For instance, during machine translation, proper nouns and
corporate names are often excluded from translation, as they do not typically translate
well into other languages. The complexity of NER arises from the inherent ambiguity of
words, making it impossible to rely on a predefined dictionary of terms to exclude from
translation[53].
In RE, the goal is to identify relationships between entities, such as persons, or-
ganisations or countries. RE can be framed as a classification task, where the type of
relationships are drawn from a closed set. For example, in the sentence “Ginni Rometty
works for IBM, and Paul Horn belongs to the same company as Ginni Rometty”, the rela-
tion between ”Ginni Rometty” and “IBM” is explicit. In contrast, the relation between
“Paul Horn” and “IBM” requires contextual understanding[53].

Various types of relations can be extracted, including ontology-style relations (e.g.
hyponyms and meronyms) and temporal relations, which describe the ordering of events.
The mentions of entities in a text can take the form of proper nouns, such as “Will
Smith”, or pronouns, where the reader must rely on context to determine the reference.
Mention detection, automatically identifying entity mentions within the text, is a crucial
component of RE. Whilst the problem can be furtherly described as n-ary RE, this thesis
focuses on the extraction of binary relations evolving two entities[53]
Whilst modern models can be applied to data for RE, two concerns arise. (1) Labeling
data is very costly; thus, obtaining models that can adapt easily is of great value (2)
many approaches using existing models learn to apply a set number of relation types
and therefore are not generally supported[35]. In this thesis, a combination of multiple
benchmarks is created to explore how state-of-the-art models adapt to more classes than
the original benchmarks supported. Existing model approaches are either applied to a
fixed document-based benchmark dataset or a fixed sentence-based dataset. The goal is
to provide a general approach with a combined dataset for a diverse set of examples as
the baseline.
Furthermore, initial impressions of studies related from Papers with code1, is the
reporting of general scores. Models do not provide insights into which classes obtain
better or worse scoring and what the model typically predicted for some more diﬀicult
relations. This makes it hard to identify the types of relations that cause problems.
I wish to explore a more general approach using readily available Language Model
(LM)s such as Generative Pre-Trained Transformer (GPT) to define a general approach
requiring no training or finetuning of models based on the models’ ability to understand
context and concepts rather than a classification problem. This is an approach to tack-
ling the problems of adding additional relation types to models once they have been
trained.

CHAPTER 2
Related Works
2.1
History of Named Entity Recognition and
Relation Extraction
NER and RE originated within the field of IE IE during the Message Understanding
Conferences (MUC) challenges back in the 1990s. The primary objective was extracting
information from unstructured text, such as news articles, typically focusing on domains
like corporate activities and defence-related subjects. The early systems for both were
heavily based on hand-crafted rule sets and dictionaries[48], [51]. Both early NER and
RE systems faced challenges in scaling beyond a narrow set of entities and relations.
NER systems struggled with classifying entities, as objects such as “The Supreme Court”
can refer to both a location and organization. This classification issue remains an issue
today for NER[48]. In RE, systems struggled with resolving noun phrases referring to
the same entity[51].
In the early 2000s, a shift occurred towards more statistical NER and RE models.
Feature-based classifiers such as Conditional Random Fields (CRFs) gained popular-
ity, applying probabilistic models to output sequences based on input sequences while
still utilizing hand-crafted features such as lexicons. Some models used the internet and
gathered web data to improve the lexicon complexity and achieve better performance[32].
In parallel, RE applied Kernel methods such as support vector machines, which lever-
aged similarity measures to separate relations from non-relations in a high dimensional
space[70].
The 2010s brought large unstructured text corpora. This shifted focus to data-driven
approaches. Utilizing unsupervised learning approach systems for both RE and NER
aimed to eliminate the requirement for human-annotated lexicons and data and instead
relied on statistical models to validate extracted facts from the web. However, especially
for NER, the systems still relied on some domain-specific rules, which were hand-created
and thus reduced the ease of adaptation[18]. For RE, the introduction of distant super-
vision provided a new way to utilize large amounts of unannotated corpora by aligning
it with KBs such as Wikipedia and Wikidata. This allowed for the automatic generation
of training data assuming that known relations between two entities were likely present
in sentences representing both entities. However, this introduced significant noise due
to incorrect alignments[40].
The development of neural networks in the 2010s benefited both NER and RE. Adapt-
ing deep learning techniques like Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and Recurrent

2.1.1
Transition to State-of-The-Art models
Various approaches to RE and NER exist, often categorized into either document-based
or sentence-based systems. Sites like “Papers with Code” make divisions into model per-
formance per dataset. For instance, DocRED is a frequently used benchmark for evalu-
ating and testing document-level RE systems. The top performing model on DocRED is
DREEAM[37], which has been selected for this project. Similarly, for sentence level RE,
the ConLL04 dataset is a common benchmark where REBEL achieves top results. Since
REBEL has also been evaluated on DocRED, it offers an opportunity for comparative
analysis between the models. Recent research has also explored advanced methods for
RE, notably utilizing Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG). This technique aims to
enhance the identification of relationships between entity pairs by integrating external
knowledge into the generative process, with the goal of reducing the occurrence of hal-
lucinations often present when using LLMss[16] The RAG process involves three stages.
First, relevant examples are fetched from a database using an embedding model. Sec-
ondly, the retrieved examples are incorporated into a prompt of the LLMs, enriching the
input. Finally, the augmented prompt is used to predict relations between pairs of enti-
ties in sentences. Efeoglu et al.[16] found that the integration of RAG revealed promising
results, improving accuracy, utilizing several large local models, such as LLAMA2.
2.2
Prompt based Relation Extraction
Prompt-based approaches using zero-shot and few-shot learningRE by prompt-tuning
have emerged to solve the problem of reliance on large-scale labelled data constraining
models to predefined relations. Furtherly restricting model usage when dealing with
newly emerging relations that lack data. Zhao et al.[72] explored the problem by mod-
elling the problem as a semantic matching task between pairs of entities in sentences
and textual descriptions of relations. Notably, they reformulated the tasks as cloze-style
tasks, something Bidirectional encoder representations from transformers (BERT) per-
forms relatively well at, essentially making the model predict the missing tokens based
on a prompt. This approach was developed on the BERT model but with prompting.
Yuan et al.[69] tried to solve the problem of RE with the popular and available ChatGPT
model by finding the temporal relations between events and deciding whether an event

3.1
Transformer Architecture
The transformer architecture was introduced in 2017 through the paper; Attention is All
you Need[57]. Since then, it has become the foundation of many NLP models, including
BERT, GPT, and Bidirectional and Auto-Regressive Transformer (BART), which will
be explored in this chapter.
Unlike previous architectures such as RNNs and Long
Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, the Transformer overcomes challenges like slow
computation and vanishing or exploding gradients when processing long sequences.
3.1.1
Input embeddings
Machine Learning models require human language input to be transformed into mathe-
matical representations, or vectors, for processing. This is achieved through tokenisation,
which varies across models but typically converts a sentence of n words into a tokenised
vector of size 1, dmodel, where dmodel is the model’s size. Each token is mapped to a unique
vector in the embedding layer, essentially a lookup table where each token corresponds
to a row in the embedding matrix of size V ×dmodel, with V representing the vocabulary
size[57].
3.1.2
Positional Encoding
The Transformer architecture lacks recurrence or convolutional layers. Therefore, po-
sitional information must be included to capture token order, which is important for
sentence structure and meaning. Positional encodings, with the same dimension dmodel
as the input embeddings, enable this. The original implementation uses trigonometric
functions to capture the encodings, which help the model generalise to longer sequences.
However, alternative encoding methods such as relative encoding can be applied with
different results[57].

3.1.3
Attention
The attention mechanism maps a query Q to a set of key-value pairs (K, V ) to an output.
In the transformer architecture, Q,K,V , and the output are vectors. The queries and
keys have dimension dk, and the value has dimension dv. The output is computed by
taking the dot product of the query with all keys, scaling it by √dk, and taking the
softmax function.
In the Transformer’s decoder, the attention mechanism is masked to prevent access
to future tokens. This is achieved by setting values above the diagonal in the attention
matrix to ∞, which becomes zero after the softmax operation.
As described in the original paper, the two primary attention mechanisms are addi-

attention for its computational eﬀiciency and lower memory usage, leveraging matrix
multiplication [57]. However, the softmax function’s range [0,1] can result in vanishing
gradients due to small values, which is mitigated by scaling the dot product by

Multi-Head Self-Attention Mechanism
The multi-head attention mechanism lets the model focus on different parts of the input
sequence simultaneously. For each word, attention is computed based on its relationship
with all other words, which previous RNNs struggled with.
Vaswani et al.[57] found it useful to project queries, keys and values h times, using
different learned linear projections. This performs the attention function in function par-
allel across multiple “heads.” The individual heads are then concatenated and projected
again to produce the final results.
This approach enables the model to attend to information from different subspaces
of the input sequence simultaneously, allowing it to focus on various parts.
In con-
trast, single-headed attention tends to average details, which prevents the model from
capturing this variety of perspectives[57].
According to Vaswani et al.[57], the default number of attention heads is set to
h = 8, reducing the dimensionality of each head to 64, as dmodel/h →512/8 = 64. This
reduction improves computational eﬀiciency, aligning the cost with that of single-headed
attention while gaining the full model dimensionality (512).
Attention mechanisms are used in various ways within the Transformer architecture.
In the encoder-decoder attention layers, queries come from the previous decoder layer,
while keys and values are sourced from the encoder’s output. This enables each position
in the decoder to attend all positions in the input sequence, similar to the attention
mechanism in sequence-to-sequence models.
In the decoder’s self-attention layers, each position attends to all previous positions,
including the current one. To preserve the auto-regressive property and prevent infor-
mation flow to future tokens, masking is applied by setting future token values to ∞,
forcing the softmax to output zero for those positions[57].

BERT
BERT, short for Bidirectional encoder representations from transformers, is, as the name
suggests, built on the Transformer architecture illustrated in Figure 3.1. The architecture
consists of 12 encoder layers with 12 attention heads. Developed originally by Google,
BERT allows researchers to fine-tune it for various NLP tasks without the need for
redesigning new architectures[41], [3].
==PAGE:20==
3.2 Model selection
11
Modifications differentiate BERT from the original Transformer architecture. The
hidden layer size, and consequently the model size, is increased from 512 to 768, while the
positional embeddings remain limited to a sequence length 512. BERT does not rely on
prompts but excels in tasks like RE through fine-tuning and domain-specific adjustments
to the final layer. BERT is trained on two tasks: (1) Masked Language Model (MLM)
and (2) Next Sentence Prediction (NSP). In MLM, random words are masked in the
input, and the model predicts the missing words based on context. NSP requires the
model to predict whether one sentence follows another. BERT’s bidirectional training
considers both left and right context, enabling it to capture the full context of words [13].
This bi-directionality is achieved through masking, inspired by the Gloze procedure, as
referenced by Devlin et al. [13]. Masking works as follows: In each sequence, 15% of
tokens are randomly masked. Unlike denoising autoencoders, BERT predicts only the
masked words, not the entire input. A challenge arises from the absence of the [MASK]
token during fine-tuning. To address this, during pretraining: (1) 80% of the time, the
token is replaced with [MASK], (2) 10% of the time with a random token, and (3) 10%
of the time remains unchanged.
Cross-entropy loss is then used to optimize BERT,
predicting the correct masked tokens [13]
In the Transformer architecture, −∞is used in attention layers to mask future
values via the softmax function. However, this masking is not applied in BERT, as it
only performs masking within the self-attention layers.
The NSP task is crucial for downstream applications like natural language inference
and question answering, which require an understanding of sentence relationships. For
NSP, 50% of the time, sentence B follows A, and in the other 50%, B is a randomly
selected sentence. Essentially creating a noisy training environment.
To store information between words, BERT uses the unique tokens: [CLS] and [SEP].
The [SEP] token separates multiple sentences, allowing BERT to process them as a single
input. The [CLS] token, placed at the beginning, captures the combined information
from the embeddings, positional embeddings, and attention mechanism [13].

BART
The most successful approaches in NLP have been variants of MLM, which have demon-
strated wide applicability across many tasks.
However, these models typically focus
on specific tasks. BART was introduced to combine the strengths of bidirectional and
autoregressive transformers.
BART is a denoising autoencoder designed as sequence-to-sequence model. Its per-
taining involves two stages: (1) the input text is corrupted using a noising function, and
(2) the model is trained to reconstruct the original text. A key advantage of BART is
its flexibility in applying various noising techniques, including altering text length. One
particularly effective technique involves randomly shuffling sentence order and using an
in-filling scheme where arbitrary spans of text are replaced with a single mask token.
This generalises BART words masking and NSP by encouraging the model to reason
about sentence length and make long-range transformations to the input[34]
BART is highly effective when fine-tuned for text generation tasks and performs
well in comprehension tasks. It matches RoBERTa’s performance on benchmarks like
GLUE and SQuAD, commonly used to evaluate Large Neural Networks (LNNs). BART
follows the Transformer architecture [57], but replaces the ReLU activation function with
Gaussian Error Linear Units (GeLUs)[34]. GeLU is a variant of ReLU that performs
better on classification tasks [56]. Unlike ReLU, which simply gates inputs by their sign,
GeLU weights inputs based on their values using the Cumulative Distribution Function
(CDF) of a Gaussian distribution, making it better suited for handling negative values
[25].
GeLUs are a variant of the ReLU activation function and perform better in classifi-
cation tasks [56]. Unlike ReLU, which gates inputs based on their sign, GeLU weights

BART utilises 6 layers in both the encoder and decoder, while BART-large uses 12

layer of the decoder performs cross-attention over the final hidden layer of the encoder,
as in traditional sequence-to-sequence models, and (2) BART includes an additional
feed-forward network before word prediction.

similarly to a LM.

Few-Shot Learning using DSPY
LM are highly sensitive to how they are prompted for specific tasks, and this sensitivity
particularly becomes evident in pipelines where multiple LM calls must interact. Even
small variations in wording or spelling errors, without changes to the underlying con-
text, can significantly alter the results. As a result, prompt engineering has become
a widespread practice to optimise LMs performance[5]. Other popular frameworks for
optimising prompts often rely on hard-coded templates, which can be brittle and lack

scalability across different models, data domains or inputs. Well-known frameworks like
LangChain frequently use lengthy prompt templates and face scalability this not being

user expertise, including chaining multiple LLM calls[65], [14].
DSPY addresses this challenge by automatically generating optimised LM strategies
and prompts through a compiler. Layers can be combined into different architectures
and optimised using various built-in techniques. Known as prompting methods, such as
CoT, are integrated into declarative modules similar to how PyTorch simplifies building
neural networks using building blocks[31]. CoT, proposed by Wei et al.[58], mimicking
human reasoning by breaking down complex problems into smaller steps before arriving
at a final answer.
Embedding such reasoning into LMs has been shown to improve
performance across a wide range of reasoning tasks[58].
DSPY Compiles these methods into programs, enabling flexible integration of models
and tasks. Unlike traditional prompt engineering, which relies on hard-coded templates,
similar to LangChain usage, DSPY makes use of signatures, which are instructions to
the LM written in natural language. The signatures serve as declarative specifications,
instructing DSPY on the task at hand and the expected input/output using either direct
LM calls or built-in datasets. This avoids prescribing how the LM should be prompted
but only defines an initial prompt which can be optimised.
Signatures offer several advantages over traditional prompts, such as the ability to
self-improve by bootstrapping examples of data into each signature. They are also ca-
pable of handling structured formatting, something that is relevant for IE. The simplest
form of a signature in DSPY is the predict module, which performs standard model in-
ference. However, more advanced modules as the previously mentioned CoT enable the
model to reason step-by-step[30]
Optimisers were formerly referred to in the oﬀicial documentation as teleprompters[31],
however they are now renamed to optimizers1, require a metric to validate responses and
optimise against, with users able to define custom metrics tailored to the specific needs
of their data. For instance, metrics like F1 scores can be used for RE tasks, as seen on
both DREEAM and REBEL[37], [8]. The compiler is capable of both fine-tuning and
optmizing prompts using the build abstractions to determine the most effective strategy.
Where fine-tuning is reserved for local models[30]. DSPY Conceptualises LM pipelines
as text transformation graphs where each node represents a distinctive task, such as
answering a question. By leveraging datasets along with guided examples, prompts can
be directed towards the desired behaviour. Through the aforementioned Bootstrapping
DSPY can also generate additional training examples for Few-Shot learning through
self-generated demonstrations. In-context learning is then employed to adopt the model
without requiring retraining. This enhances the model’s contextual understanding of
the specified task[30]
As a backbone model similar to DREEAM and REBEL had different backend models,
GPT is used in various formats, including GPT-4.0-mini and GPT-4. The reason for
selecting DSPY for the generative model approach is that it offers a simpler introduction

When comparing and evaluating systems for RE, various metrics are commonly used in

IE tasks can be viewed as classification problems, where tokens are classified based
on their type and relationship to one another. In this context, evaluation metrics can
provide meaningful insights into model performance, and the F1 score is derived from
such metrics. To understand the F1 score, it is necessary to first comprehend preci-
sion, recall, and the concepts of true positives, false positives, true negatives, and false
negatives.
1. True Positive (TP): Instances where the model predicts a positive case and the
ground truth (GT) data also label it as positive.
2. False Positive (FP): Instances where the model predicts a positive case, but the
ground truth labels it as negative.
3. True Negative (TN): Instances where the model correctly predicts a negative
case, and the ground truth also labels it as negative.
4. False Negative (FN): Instances where the ground truth labels a case as positive,
but the model predicts it as negative.

Accuracy
Accuracy is considered the simplest form of evaluation scoring. It is defined as the ratio
of correctly identified labels to the total number of classifications. However, accuracy is
highly dependent on the dataset being balanced, meaning that the classification classes
must be somewhat equally represented for this metric to provide meaningful results.

Precision and Recall
In many machine learning applications, datasets are not balanced across classes. This
imbalance can lead to inaccurate results when relying solely on accuracy as an evaluation
metric. To address this issue, precision and recall scores are often utilised.
Precision
Precision measures the proportion of correctly predicted positive cases out of all the
positive cases predicted by the model.
Precision is critical in tasks where the cost
of false positives is high, such as emergency response systems where rescue teams are
dispatched. A high precision ensures that rescue teams are sent to the correct locations,
reducing wasted resources, time, and effort.

high recall alone would be suﬀicient for such a system. Excessive false positives (high
recall) would waste resources, while excessive false negatives (high precision) would
result in missed individuals. Therefore, the F1 score provides a balanced evaluation of
the system’s performance.

In RE tasks, precision measures the proportion of correctly identified relations, while
recall assesses the proportion of all correctly recognised entities. For distant-supervised
RE tasks, where labels are generated automatically from external KBs, the generated
labels may not always be entirely accurate. Thus, the F1 score may not always reflect
real-world performance but serves as a strong indicative metric.
In such cases, the Precision-Recall Curve (PRC) and Area Under the Curve (AUC)
are often adopted as evaluation metrics. The PRC measures the proportion of correctly
identified relations among the top-k predictions made by the system. Precision requires
manual effort as researchers must annotate the top-k output results of the system. The
PRC and AUC help in understanding the precision-recall tradeoff across various thresh-
olds, providing a comprehensive assessment of distant-supervised RE models[73].

4.1.1
Intra-sentence Relation Extraction datasets
Intra-sentence datasets are among the smallest, as the number of possible relations
within a single sentence is naturally limited. However, they are also the oldest and most
extensively studied, providing a strong foundation.
CoNLL04
The CoNLL04 dataset is commonly used for benchmarking RE. It consists of 1,437
sentences. The dataset was developed as part of a challenge aimed at identifying entities
and their relations within sentences. CoNLL04 is formatted in a tabular structure, where
each table represents a sentence vertically. The columns in each table include the word,
the entity class label and additional features. Relations are documented as a sub-table
following each sentence table, with position indices provided. The dataset includes the
relations: Located in, work for, organization based in, live in and kill[17].
4.1.2
CrossRE
This dataset encompasses six different domains of relation and entity types and has
been manually annotated to cover 17 unique relation types. The included domains are
news, literature, natural sciences, music, politics and artificial intelligence. In total, the
dataset comprises over 5,000 sentences. According to Bassignana et al.[2], only 5,000
sentences are required for training a RE model, as these models are expected to adapt
relatively quickly to new domains. The annotations are performed at the sentence level
and are directional, meaning that the order in which entities are presented is significant.
Labels are applied consistently across all six domains, with the aim of generaliz-
ing and providing non-domain-specific relations. Multiple rounds of annotations were
conducted, with the final two rounds performed by experts in NLP and linguistics. A
notable feature of the dataset is the inclusion of annotation justifications, which provide
insight into the decision-making process. This is particularly valuable, as the professional
annotators encountered more diﬀiculty and uncertainty when handling domain-specific
content. The dataset sheds light on these challenges, offering a unique perspective on
the annotation process for highly specialized domains[2].
4.1.3
NYT
The NYT-H dataset, referred to as the NYT in this thesis, was created to address the
issue of noisy or incorrect labels commonly associated with distant supervision, which can
lead to high rates of False Positive (FP)s and False Negative (FN)s, ultimately affecting
the scoring of RE models. The dataset builds upon the existing NYT10 dataset, which
was itself constructed using distant supervision techniques. However, to mitigate the
noise problem, human annotators were hired to label the sentences in the test set using
a binary classification strategy. This approach simply determines whether the sentences

DocRED
Document-Level Relation Extraction Dataset (DocRED) was introduced as a benchmark-
ing dataset in 2019, incorporating both a supervised dataset and a human-annotated
dataset. It is constructed using data from Wikipedia and Wikidata, with annotations
for both named entities and relations. Unlike previous benchmarking datasets, DocRED
is document-based, meaning that relations can span across multiple sentences, providing
a more complex and realistic environment for RE tasks, depending on the use[68].
Yao et al.[68] highlights that based on the population of DocRED, 40.7% of all
relational facts can only be extracted from multiple sentences. As a result, DocRED was
created as a more realistic benchmark to advance RE from sentence level to document
level.
The dataset was compiled in multiple stages:
1. Stage 1: Select documents for human annotation - Aligning Wikipedia documents
with Wikidata. Wikidata relations are aligned with Wikidata. Discarding docu-
ments < 128 words and < 4 entities and < 4 relations.
2. Named Entity Recognition and Coreference Annotation: The entity mentions and
coreference information from Stage 1 is reviewed by human annotators. Any infor-
mation is supplemented if necessary.
3. Next, each named entity mentioned is linked to multiple Wikidata items to provide
relational recommendations. This is to help with Distant Supervision.
4. Stage 4: The annotation of relations and supporting evidence (sentences where
the entities are located – follow up) faces two challenges. (I) A large number of

CHAPTER 6
Experimental Setup
This chapter presents the experimental setup used to generate the results. It discusses
the preliminary fine-tuning steps conducted prior to obtaining the final results and the
outcomes from this stage, including the best hyperparameters.
6.1
Hyperparameters
6.1.1
Max epochs
The maximum number of epochs defines the total training duration of the model. An
epoch represents one complete pass of the dataset through the machine learning algo-
rithm, during which multiple weight updates are performed. These updates are opti-
mized iteratively using the gradient decent algorithm, refining the model parameters
incrementally rather than in a single step[63]. Since computational resources limit the
ability to process all data in a single epoch, the dataset is divided into batches. Thus,
a balance must be maintained: training for too few epochs can result in underfitting,
where the model fails to capture complex patterns in the data. Conversely, training
for too many epochs may lead to overfitting, where the model memorizes the training
data, causing poor performance generalization to unseen data, such as the validation
or test set[9]. To mitigate overfitting, models are evaluated on the validation set after
each epoch. This ensures the model generalizes well to new, unseen data, acting as a
regularization technique.
6.1.2
Learning rate
The learning rate is a hyperparameter that controls the magnitude of adjustments made
to the model’s weights during training using the gradient descent algorithm. It directly
influences how quickly or slowly the model converges toward the optimal set of weights
for the data. A high learning rate allows for rapid initial progress, but in later stages,
the large updates may cause the model to overshoot the optimal solution, leading to
suboptimal performance. Conversely, a very low learning rate may result in slow con-
vergence.
Hyperparameter tuning often starts by exploring learning rates on a logarithmic scale,
such as powers of 10, to identify a suitable range. Once a coarse range is determined,
==PAGE:45==
6.1 Hyperparameters
36
finer adjustments can be made to achieve optimal results.
An ideal approach would involve an adaptive learning rate, where higher rates are
used in initial phases to speed up convergence, followed by lower rates for fine-tuning,
allowing the model to approach the optimal solution more precisely[13]. However, start-
ing with too high a learning rate can destabilize the model by causing large, early weight
updates that skew the optimization process[19] requiring corrections at later stages[19].
Multiple strategies are available for using the learning rate and solving the problems
by overshooting or having a too-low learning rate.
6.1.3
Warm up
Another method for adjusting the learning rate is known as learning rate warm-up. This
technique involves starting the training process with a small learning rate and gradu-
ally increasing it over a few initial epochs or iterations. The purpose is to stabilize the
training process by allowing the model to adjust slowly before ramping up to higher
learning rates. For LLMs, particularly Transformer-based models such as BERT, learn-
ing rate warm-up has improved performance. For instance, using too short a warm-up
period resulted in lower Bilingual Evaluation Understudy (BLEU) scores, a metric for
evaluating the similarity between machine-translated text and high-quality reference
translations[57]. To address this, Popel et al.[46] proposed using a small learning rate
that gradually increases over time, enhancing convergence and improving performance.
This technique has also been beneficial for other types of neural networks.
For
example, Generative Adversarial Networkss (GANs) have shown improved stability when
applying learning rate warm-up to one of the models, helping to balance performance
between generator and discriminator. Various strategies exist for implementing warm-
up. One approach is constant warm-up, which corresponds to no warm-up being used.
Another approach is decay, where the learning rate gradually reduces after the warm-up
phase. Lastly, a linear warm-up can be employed, where the learning rate is consistently
increased over time[61].
6.1.4
Weight decay
Weight decay, also known as L2 regularization, is a fundamental technique to improve
model performance by penalizing large weights in the neural network. One of the pri-
mary benefits of weight decay is reducing overfitting. Large weights can cause the model
to memorize training data, leading to poor generalization. By penalizing large weights,
weight decay encourages the model to learn smaller weights that capture the underlying
patterns in the data rather than overfitting to specific details. Like learning rate adjust-
ment and warm-up strategies, large weight shifts can lead to instability during training
and increased sensitivity to noise in the data, a common issue when dealing with large
amounts of annotated text data. For instance, when annotators lack domain-specific
knowledge, biases can introduce noise, as mentioned in the dataset section.
==PAGE:46==

Weight decay also promotes more uniform weight distribution across neurons, encour-
aging the model to utilize fewer parameters and making it more eﬀicient and applicable
to different tasks. This is achieved by adding a penalty term to the loss function that
is proportional to the sum of squared weights in the model. Optimizing weight decay
is model-specific and depends on factors such as the size of the model, the amount of
available data, and the learning rate. Therefore it is important to adjust weight decay
alongside other hyperparameters[60].

6.1.5
Batch size
Batch size is a hyperparameter extensively studied in other machine learning fields,
such as image recognition, though its impact in NLP is less explored[46]. For models
like BERT, typical batch sizes involve 2048 tokens for single-GPU setups and 4096
tokens for multi-GPU configurations.
In models such as DREEAM, the parameter
max_sentence_length can also be set. Limiting sentence length can optimize memory
usage across various hardware setups. However, excluding long sentences may impact
the performance in environments where evaluation data is included in soch complex sen-
tences. The default value for DREEAM is 1024, tokens which is utilized in this project,
and is kept consitent with the token size for OpenAI GhatGPT Few-Shot Learning.
The combination of max_sentence_length and batch size is a useful indicator of
the total memory required, preventing the model from running out of memory. If no
max_sentence _length is set, the default batch size variable determines the memory
usage. Larger batch sizes enable the model to process more data per iteration or epoch,
accelerating the weight update process in gradient descent[46]. Demonstrated that using
smaller batch sizes when a base model results in lower BLEU scores than using a larger
batch size, which approaches the performance of larger models.
6.1.6
Max Gradient norm
Also referred to as gradient clipping, this technique is commonly applied in LNNs, partic-
ularly DNNs with many layers. These networks often contain steep regions resembling
cliffs during their training phase, which may cause instability during training. Cliffs
form when multiple large weights are multiplied together across layers, creating sharp
gradients. When the gradient descent algorithm encounters such steep gradients, it can
result in excessively large weight updates, destabilizing the network and causing it to
“jump off”. This poses significant risks to consistent training and the steady reduction
of the model’s loss. Gradient clipping mitigates the risk by constraining the size of the
gradient updates. The gradient indicates the optimal direction for weight adjustments
==PAGE:47==
6.2 Model selection
38
but not the step size. If the gradient decent algorithm attempts an overly large step, gra-
dient clipping steps in to limit the magnitude of the update, ensuring that the network
remains within a stable region. This helps stabilize the training process towards steady
convergence. There are several methods to perform gradient clipping. One common
approach is element-wise clipping, where the gradient of each parameter in a mini-batch
is clipped individually. Another method involves clipping the norm || g || of the gradient
g. The latter method can preserve the gradient direction while limiting its magnitude.
However, studies suggest that both methods are functionally similar in terms of their
effect on training stability[21].
Cliping the gradient norm introduces some bias into the training process. In tradi-
tional mini-batch gradient descent, the gradient direction is the average of all mini-batch
gradients. By stabilizing the gradient, a heuristic bias is introduced, which, while bene-
ficial for stabilizing training, alters the intended gradient direction slightly[21].

6.4
Deciding on the number of samples utilized
for DSPY
During pilot testing of DSPY for RE, it was observed that DSPY relies on several API
calls to OpenAI when utilizing teleprompters such as
BootStrapFewShotWithRandomSearch or BootStrapFewShot. Additionally, testing on
the full test set was deemed impractical due to both token costs and expected runtime.
Specifically, completion times were estimated to be around 240 hours using GPT-4o-Mini
and 350 hours for GPT-4.
As a result, a more cost-effective subset of the dataset was selected for evaluation.
However, as shown in Figure 6.10(a), the sample distribution was heavily skewed to-
wards the NYT dataset, which is not document-based. To address this, an equal sam-
pling of distributions across all datasets was performed the results are depicted in Fig-
ure 6.10(b) This approach aimed to create a balanced evaluation across both domain
and dataset types, ensuring coverage of mixed classes, including inter and intra-sentence-
based datasets. The intention was to capture a more representative performance image
across diverse data origins.
Guidelines are available on which teleprompter or optimizer to use depending on the
number of samples in the dataset[44]. Various teleprompters or optimizers are available,
such as the aforementioned BootstrapFewShotWithRandomSearch. The method focuses
on optimizing the output of the model using FewShot samples.
While the documentation recommends MIPROv2 for handling 200+, the reduced test-
ing size was 50 samples included in the dataset, and as such,
BootstrapFewShotWithRandomSearch was used as specified in the recommendations
when using 50 samples[44]. Through pilot testing, it was determined that including a
maximum of 10 few-shot labelled samples was most feasible, as further increasing the
number of samples did not improve performance. This is also reflected in the results.
The selection of labels depends on the number of tokens the model is expected to
process. Input tokens, which include sentences, relation types, and entity types along
with the initial pre-prompt token limits, are also critical considerations for model input.
While testing with increased token size and more samples could potentially yield
better results, the experiments and results were performed on 1024 tokens utilizing the
default temperature. Further experiments with temperature and the number of tokens
were not considered to be part of the scope of this thesis.

8.1
Comparing REBEL and DREEAM
Comparing the results outlined in subsection 7.0.1 and section 7.2 a clear pattern emerges.
DREEAM generally has a higher recall over the precision, indicative of a less confident
model trying to predict many relevant relations. This however, results in overall better
F1 metrics in the lowest end. BART, on the other hand, based on the data, looks to
be more conservative in making informed decisions when predicting relation types. The
results outlined in the Results chapter were to be expected based on the original papers
by Ma et al., Cabot et al., with DREEAM performing generally better. Furthermore, the
analysis highlighted the fundamental difference in the two models, with REBEL strongly
favouring predicting the sentence-based classes and DREEAM having a more rounded
approach for either or. These results seem to correspond to the results outlined in the
paper for REBEL and DREEAM, respectively, showcasing a better model performance
for sentence-based datasets in REBEL. This was also highlighted in the Table 7.1.
Whilst DREEAM has an overall significant performance increase, it comes at the
cost of a far more complicated training loop. Requiring a lot more hardware. Thus,
implementation of the model is strongly resource-dependent and constrained to time
due to comparably larger training times over REBEL. During the project, it was also
discovered that DREEAM scales badly when applied to larger datasets.
Whilst the
original authorsMa et al. outlined in a GitHub issue1 that the system utilizes approx-
imately 360GB ram for the DocRED dataset, development found that increased using
the Combined dataset; consuming up towards 600GB.
Similarly, REBEL offers a model that, out of the box, is less complicated to train and
maintain updated with new relation types and additional comparable datasets, however,
at the cost of performance, especially considering document-based corpora.
8.2
Prompt engineering using DSPY
Using DPSY gave mixed results, which seemed to be either a good performing system
for specific classes or not performing at all. Whilst the overall results seem to reflect
that GPT is able to capture details in both document-based and sentence-based systems,
an important detail is missing. The current implementation of the F1 metric related
to classifying the relations as correct or incorrect requires the output to be identical to

8.3
Practical discussion
From a practical perspective, several important considerations arise. The decision of
whether to create a combined dataset for RE depends on the intended use case. Most
public benchmarks are, as previously stated, either inter-sentence or intra-sentence-
based. Still, a combined dataset may serve as a more realistic benchmark, reflecting
the variety of data a model might encounter in real-world applications. However, this
approach is highly domain-specific and influenced by the scenario in which the system
is deployed.
For instance, in chatbot-based systems, such as those in the ChatGPT environment,
the model would likely need to perform well on both inter- and intra-sentence relations,
and possibly, when performing well at long context sentences, it would also perform great
on shorter spans. In contrast, for developing KGs using large corpora, a document-based
approach appears more suitable, as it aligns with the nature of the data and the model’s
expected performance. GPT-4 may perform adequately for simpler tasks, especially if
the prompt is well-targeted towards specific relation types. However, for more general
applications where complex relationships need to be extracted, GPT-4 may struggle to
capture all the necessary information. This finding is on par with that of the analysis
made by Zhu et al.[76].
8.3.1
Cost of training
Implementing a system like DSPY combined with OpenAI ChatGPT for RE proved to be
less complicated than implementing state-of-the-art models like REBEL and DREEAM.
However, it comes at a cost of tokens. If the system is expected to be highly utilized,
the token cost will quickly rise. On the other hand, purchasing hardware related to the
hardware section also comes at a significant cost. There is, therefore, a balance between
expected utilization.
Also taken into consideration that ChatGPT models are more
flexible due to their prompting nature. Suppose the system was expected to be utilized
a lot or have high requirements for good metrics related to the F1 score, models like
DREEAM still perform well. However, as mentioned from Zhu et al.[76], GPT shows
potential for further improvement. This is also present in research performed in this
thesis, where the approach could be further refined by optimizing the initial prompt
or perhaps even devising a section of the two tasks pipelined ER and RE. Thereby
creating individual scores for either instead of training a combined pipeline. The cost of
testing DSPY in this project has been approximately 2000DKK, showcasing that even
==PAGE:75==

small datasets (50 samples utilized for optimizing and 50 additional samples for testing)
multiplied by 8 tests result in quite a large token cost.
However, note that 70% of

8.3.2
Updates to models
Both REBEL and DREEAM have the advantage of being consistent. This means that
the implemented backbone model does not change and adapt during usage, while GPT
from OpenAI receives common updates.
These updates also included changing the
default models from GPT3 to GPT-4o-mini throughout this project, which resulted in
having to redo some of the results for that part. This can come as a future benefit, with
new and better models released for a cheaper and faster response time, whilst REBEL
and DREEAM are consistent in their implementation. However, a new implementation
of the GPT4 model, perhaps even GPT5, could also, with speculation, improve the
performance additionally.
In such a case, whether that might be a more easy and
predictable approach must be discussed.
Issues for both models come in the cost of both REBEL, DREEAM and GBT-4
having tradeoffs. DREEAM and REBEL require vast amounts of annotated data, which,
for very domain-specific tasks, can be an expensive feed to gather. Meanwhile, the GPT
models come at the cost of privacy. The models are not locally run, and data may be
collected for future models’ training, leading to privacy issues for sensitive tasks.
This could be fixed with a local LM utilizing the DSPY framework, such as LLAMA4,
with an estimated 70B parameter model. However, I have been unsuccessful in imple-
menting such a model on the DTU HPC due to incompatibility between software and
hardware possibilities. The software restrictions were placed in the issue of DSPY requir-
ing strict backend possibilities for LMs. Without implementing my own interface, this
resulted in either the HugginFace implementation or the VLLM Implementation, which
converted the HuggingFace API to an OpenAI API. This allowed me to run models
such as LLAMA4-7Bilion parameters locally on the HPC. Initial prompts and responses
would work; however, joining the pipeline with NER with subtasks and specially struc-
tured output set the model off balance, resulting in errors and no output. Therefore, no
F1 scores were even discovered in this phase. Changing the prompt descriptions once
again for that specific model may improve performance, however that would require re-
runining the DSPY Few-Shot learning again. Finally, the initial prompts included for
DSPY may introduce bias, as they fundamentally the introduction to the problem. Re-
finements to those can be made, however as discovered in pilot testing the initial prompt
has a large impact on performance. This leaves a door open for further development.

CHAPTER 10
Conclusion
This thesis explored the applications of large language models in relation extraction (RE),
focusing on the performance of models such as RoBERTa using DREEAM and REBEL.
Additionally, a general approach that requires no pre-training was investigated, enabling
the model to adapt to various relation classes without being restricted to predefined sets.
The results demonstrated that models like OpenAI GPT show promising performance
in few-shot settings, although their effectiveness varied across different relation types.
Attempts to apply a similar framework to local large language models did not yield
meaningful results due to output complexities, likely attributable to the framework’s
developmental stage or hardware restrictions limiting running larger models. This pre-
vented a fair comparison, leading to the exclusion of further exploration in this area.
In contrast, state-of-the-art models like REBEL and DREEAM (using RoBERTa)
showed significant performance improvements over Few-Shot learning approaches us-
ing prompts, often doubling or tripling performance. These models exhibited distinct
behaviours, some more conservative in their predictions while others prone to “wild
guesses.” The adaptation to include class-based metrics provides a deeper insight into
model evaluation and could help define which relation classes are diﬀicult for models to
conceptualize.
This thesis also underscored the trade-offs between hardware, training, and token
costs. While state-of-the-art models offered state-of-the-art performance, they required
complex training procedures and high hardware resources, whereas prompt-based sys-
tems incurred significant token utilization costs.
