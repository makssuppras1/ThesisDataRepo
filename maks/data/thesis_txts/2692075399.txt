Abstract
The process optimization of a potential biorefinery located in Denmark for the co­production
of biofuel and bioplastic precursors is studied in this project. This is achieved by minimiz­
ing waste generation (E­Factor) and maximizing operational profit (OP) using numerical
optimization. A superstructure representing several process alternatives and their logical
interconnections is built based on data available in the literature. A General Disjunc­
tive Problem (GDP) is formulated and converted into a Mixed­Integer Nonlinear Program
(MINLP). A multi­objective optimization was performed, having the following yearly pro­
duction as targets: 50 ktons of lactic acid, 50 ktons of succinic acid, 120 ktons of ethanol,
and 8 GWh of biogas. A Pareto Front is built, and 7 Pareto optimal solutions are deter­
mined. Each Pareto optimal solution provided an optimal flowsheet for all products. The
model identified the optimal operating profit to be equal to approximately 29.8 MM$ per
year and the optimal E­Factor to be equal to 2.36 kg of waste per kg of product. The model
determined the optimal operating cost to be 0.312 $/kg of ethanol, 1.26 $/kg of lactic acid,
and 1.58 $ /kg of succinic acid. Sensitivity analysis highlighted that the optimal operat­
ing profit and optimal E­Factor were significantly dependent on the selected pretreatment
method. It has also been demonstrated that the products’ selling price highly affects the
optimal solution and the operating profit.

Introduction
Modern industrial societies are highly reliant on cheap nonrenewable energy sources to satisfy
high living standards. In 2020, only 19.7 % of the energy consumed by European countries was
from renewable sources (36). This increased consumption of nonrenewable energy sources such
as petroleum, coal and gas, forces European countries to depend on foreign and often unreliable
countries for their energy imports. The consumption of fossil energy sources has a high environ-
mental impact; for example, it leads to greenhouse gas emissions (GHG). In fact, the use of coal as
an energy carrier is the ￿rst worldwide source of CO2 emissions closely followed by oil and gas con-
sumption (35). It is widely accepted that GHG contribute to global warming, which brings on severe
global warming related consequences. These consequences include the rising of sea levels, coastal
erosion, decline in fauna and ￿ora, deserti￿cation and more frequent and intense extreme weather
events (109). Therefore, one of the main challenges that our industrial societies currently face is the
healthy transition from fossil to renewable and sustainable sources. It is crucial to operate this shift
as fast and smoothly as possible since, for instance, the growth rate of CO2 is increasing rapidly -
from 0.6 ppm per year in the 1960s to 2.3 ppm per year between 2009 and 2018 (69). the measured
concentration of carbon dioxide atmospheric at the NOAA’s Mauna Loa Atmospheric Baseline Ob-
servatory peaked at 419 ppm in May 2021, the highest level ever measured (80).
To be able to decrease GHG emissions, it is crucial to develop credible alternatives to petrore￿neries.
These alternatives need to be both socially, economically, and environmentally sustainable. They
should also be able to ful￿ll the growing energy and materials needs of our society. As part of on-
going research and development is the development of integrated biore￿neries. A biore￿nery is a
facility that converts biomass into value-added products such as biofuels or chemicals by means of
physical, chemical, and biological conversion (47). Biore￿neries are already a reality. First genera-
tion biore￿neries have already been developed on an industrial scale and proven to be economically
viable (16). In the USA, roughly 40 % of corn crops are converted into bioethanol to be used as a
biofuel (105). Research in this ￿eld is now focusing on shifting from ￿rst generation to second and
third generation biore￿neries. This is necessary in order to develop biore￿neries that do not com-
pete with food production as well as allowing for a greater ￿exibility to adapt to market needs.
Denmark is one of the leading countries in the European Union, with the highest share of energy
coming from renewable sources. Over 30% of the danish gross energy needs are ful￿lled by renew-
able sources (36). To increase this even further, the development of biore￿neries in Denmark is a
promising prospect. Moreover, Denmark is already a world leader in biogas production from various
sources such as manure, residues, and household waste (102). Several lignocellulosic feedstocks such
as wheat straw and barley straw are readily available and could be used as raw materials. By using
appropriate chemical and physical processes, biomass can be converted into an array of valuable
products such as fertilizers, glycerine, biomaterials, or chemicals (21). These products have shown
to be a promising alternative to disturb the current market and thus displace petroleum-based prod-
ucts. Bioethanol can, for example, be used as a biofuel, upgraded to advanced biofuels, or converted
into other chemical precursors. Other highly encouraging biore￿nery products include chemicals
such as lactic acid, which can be used as a monomer to produce PLA, a biodegradable bioplastic (68).
Biore￿neries are based on novel technologies and it is therefore crucial to identify optimal pro-
cess con￿gurations based on techno-economic and environmental sustainability criteria. This step
is a prerequisite before considering industrial scale development. Given the multiple process al-
ternatives, the evaluation of each alternative on an individual basis is tedious and ine￿cient. An
alternative approach is the use of superstructure synthesis to model all possible process alternatives
1
==PAGE:13==
and their logical connections. By using superstructure synthesis, it is possible to explore in a sys-
tematic manner all alternative pathways and identify optimal process ￿owsheets by using numerical
optimization (76).
This is the approach followed in this work, which targets at modelling and optimizing a sustainable
integrated biore￿nery for the production of biofuel and bioplastics from organic waste. The output
products of the biore￿nery are considered to be the precursors of these bioplastics and biofuels. It
is considered that the optimal process pathways transforming these precursors into biofuels and
bioplastics are well known and documented.
The remaining sections of the thesis are organized as follows: in section 2, a literature review is
provided, introducing several relevant concepts related to biore￿neries and their conceptual design.
In section 3 the methodology applied in this thesis is formulated. The results are are presented and
discussed in section 4. Lastly, conclusions are drawn and future perspectives are discussed in section
5.

2.1
Biore￿nery
Several de￿nitions can be provided to explain the concept of a biore￿nery. According to the In-
ternational Energy Agency, a biore￿nery is a sustainable processing of biomass into a spectrum
of marketable products and energy (21). According to the National Renewable Energy Laboratory
(NREL), a biore￿nery is a facility that integrates biomass conversion and equipments to produce
fuels, power, and value-added chemicals from biomass. The biore￿nery concept is analogous to
today’s petroleum re￿nery, which produce multiple fuels and products from petroleum. Figure 1
illustrates the di￿erence between a biore￿nery and a conventional petroleum-based re￿nery.
Figure 1

Advantages and disadvantages of biore￿neries compared to petrore￿neries
The goal of a petrore￿nery is to convert crude oil into an array of products. These products can either
be fuel which is burnt to produce heat, electricity or mechanical energy or they can be high value
and commodity chemicals. Petrore￿neries are based on mature, highly optimized and predictable
technologies. They are also not dependent on seasonal variations of their supply. Nevertheless,
petrore￿neries also have disadvantages. They are a source of atmospheric pollution and green-
house gases when experts warn of the irreversible consequences of global warming. The price of
petroleum has also shown a high volatility in recent years and petroleum reserves are concentrated
in the hands of a few countries, meaning countries in Europe depend on foreign countries to ensure
their energy supply.(21)
The goals of biore￿neries are the same as petrore￿neries: produce energy and chemicals. However,
unlike petrore￿neries, biore￿neries use biomass as feedstock. Biomass has the advantage to be
widely available, cheap and renewable. Biomass however has a lower energy density than petroleum,
but thanks to its lower cost, has a price similar to petroleum on a energy basis. Biore￿neries also have
the potential to address the environmental, social and economic needs of our society. Indeed, multi-
product biore￿neries designs allow for the sustainable conversion of biomass to energy carriers
and a range of useful products while helping rural development and employment. Nevertheless,

Integrated biore￿nery
An integrated biore￿nery is a biore￿nery which converts biomass into value-added products while
focusing on reducing waste generation and energy consumption (13).
Types of biore￿neries
Biore￿neries can be categorized into three main types(16):
• Phase I biore￿nery (single feedstock, single process and single major product);
• Phase II biore￿nery (single feedstock, multiple processes and multiple major products);
• Phase III biore￿nery (multiple feedstocks, multiple processes and multiple major products).
Phase I biore￿neries are the ￿rst generation of biore￿neries. They already have been developed on
an industrial scale and have demonstrated their economic viability. As phase I are based on a sole
feedstock and major products, they have no ￿exibility to adjust based on the market price and de-
mand and therefore recover investment and operating costs. Some examples of phase I biore￿neries
are the vegetable oil-to-biodiesel plants, pulp and paper mills and corn grain-to-ethanol plants.(16)
Phase II biore￿neries transform a sole feedstock into multiple major products. They therefore gain
in ￿exibility compared to phase I biore￿neries as they can adjust their production based on market
price and demand. They also allow for better pro￿t margins as they allow for the coproduction
of fuels and chemicals. Several phase II biore￿neries have been developed in Europe as the Nova-
mont plant in Italy, which transforms corn starch into products such as biodegradable polyesters
and starch-derived thermoplastics.(16)
4
==PAGE:16==
Phase III biore￿neries are the most technologically advanced type of biore￿neries. They include
multiple processes and o￿er the possibility to transform multiple types of feedstocks into an ar-
ray of products. As phase II biore￿neries, phase III biore￿neries o￿er a high ￿exibility as they can
adapt to the market prices and needs. However, they also allow to cope with the variable availabil-
ity which is inherent to biomass feedstocks and therefore minimize the e￿ect of seasonality. These
biore￿neries also o￿er the highest potential pro￿tability as these highly integrated biore￿neries can
combine feedstocks in the most advantageous manner. As an example of a phase III biore￿nery, SP

Cellulose is the main structural constituent of the plant cell’s walls. Its three-dimensional struc-
ture is illustrated on ￿gure 4, along with the one of hemicellulose and lignin. Cellulose is a linear
homopolymer formed of D-glucose subunits linked to each other by β-(1,4)-glycosidic bonds. The
monomer which forms the cellulose chains is called cellobiose, a disaccharide composed of two glu-
cose units also linked by β-(1,4)-glycosidic bonds (81). Fermentable glucose can be produced from
the breaking down of the β-(1,4)-glycosidic linkages. However, this breaking down, called hydrol-
ysis or sacchari￿cation, is made di￿cult as cellulose is present in majority in a crystalline phase. (61)
Unlike cellulose, hemicellulose is a branched heteropolymer. These branches are made of monosac-
charides. They include pentoses (xylose, rhamnose, arabonise), hexoses (glucose, mannose, galac-
tose), and uronic acids (glucuronic, galacturonic acid). As hemicellulose is amorphous, it is easily
hydrolyzed compared to cellulose. (86; 61; 11)
Like hemicellulose, lignin is an amorphous heteropolymer. Its structure is complex and contains
cross-linked polymers of phenolic monomers. Lignin is located in the primary cell wall. It has
multiple goals: structural support, impermeability, and resistance against microbial attacks.(86)

Value-added products from lignocellulosic biomass
In Denmark, the most common use of straw is burning it to produce heat and electricity, even if
e￿orts have been made by danish industrials to convert straw into liquid fuels (79). This burning
does not exploit the high potential of LCF to produce biofuels and biochemicals. To produce biofuels
and biochemicals from LCF, it is necessary to convert them to a sugar platform (63). Adequate
treatment of lignocellulosic biomass is necessary to hydrolyse cellulose and hemicellulose into C5
(mainly xylose and arabinose) and C6 sugars (mainly glucose, mannose, galactose and rhamnose).
These steps, called pretreatment will be discussed later. As shown on ￿gure 3, these sugars can

Based on these criteria, the most promising biobased products have been identi￿ed (9). From this
list, it has been decided to incorporate these three products in the superstructure: lactic acid, suc-
cinic acid and ethanol.
Ethanol is a supercommodity. Its production from the fermentation of glucose is a well-known pro-
cess which has been studied extensively and developed on an industrial scale. Bioethanol can be
used as a liquid biofuel or it can be upgraded into advanced biofuels that could replace gasoline, jet
fuel or diesel (94). Bioethanol can also be further converted into valuable chemicals such as ethylene
which can be used to produce plastics. Bioethanol can therefore contribute to the society’s energy
and material needs with the advantage of being produced from local and renewable feedstocks.
Succinic acid (SA) production from biomass is also a hot research topic. SA is traditionally manufac-
tured from petrochemicals through expensive processes (67). One of the main interest of bio-based
SA is that it could therefore displace petroleum-derived chemicals and polymer precursors in many
applications (96). Bio-based SA is suited for applications in sports and footwear, automotive, packag-
ing, agriculture, non-wovens and ￿bres applications (PlasticsEurope). Several companies are active
in commercial scale development of bio-based succinic acid such as Bioamber, Myriant technologies,
BASF CSM and DSM Roquette. Bio-based SA was therefore deemed a promising candidate to in-
clude in the superstructure as even if SA production from biomass-derived hydrolysates is not fully
explored or developed, it has shown industrial interest and is a precursor used to produce bioplastics
(96).
As SA production, lactic acid (LA) production from biomass is a hot research topic. LA is mainly
produced (around 90 %) from the fermentation of ￿rst generation feedstocks like starch. Shifting to
second generations feedstocks such as LCF would help reduce the life cycle environmental impacts
and would not compete with food supply. Thanks to its biodegradable and biocompatible proper-
ties, LA has applications in various sectors such as the packing, medical, pharmaceutical, and food
industries (68). Bioplastics, such as Polylactic acid (PLA), can also be derived from bio-based LA.
Bio-based LA has therefore also the potential to displace petroleum-derived plastics by proposing
an alternative which can be degraded into innocuous LA in composting conditions (64).
The decision of grouping LA, SA and ethanol was motivated by developing a biore￿nery which is
capable of ensuring both energy and material needs.
2.4
Value-added products from manure
Several biore￿nery technologies can be applied to convert manure into more valuable products.
These technologies can be classi￿ed into two categories. The ￿rst one concerns the technologies
which produce energy products such as biogas technology, gasi￿cation combustion and hydrother-
mal liquefaction. The second category concerns technologies which produce non-energy products
such as composting, pyrolytic carbonization or hydrothermal carbonization (58).
Denmark is a world leader in biogas production. Biogas production has quadrupled in Denmark
from 2012 to 2020, reaching an annual production of 20 PJ (102). Two main feedstocks are used
to produce this biogas: manure and organic waste. Given the situation in Denmark relative to
manure valorization in biogas, it was decided to include into the superstructure technologies which
converted manure into biogas. This biogas, in the scope of developing an integrated biore￿nery,
can be used to provide energy to the other parts of the biore￿nery and therefore o￿ers the potential
10
==PAGE:22==
to develop a biore￿nery which is a net energy contributor. Biogas can also be further converted to
biofuel by using a Gas-to-liquids (GTL) process.

The goals of these steps are going to be brie￿y described in the following lines
Preprocessing
A preprocessing step is considered before pretreatment. This step consists of a size reduction of
biomass to improve handling and processing by increasing the surface area (61).
Pretreatment
As shown on ￿gure 4, the structure of LCF is closed. The goal of pretreatment is therefore to in-
crease the porosity of the material, dissolve lignin and hemicellulose and decrease the crystallinity
of cellulose. This ensures an increased e￿ciency of the cellulose and hemicellulose hydrolysis. The
schematic role of pretreatment is illustrated on ￿gure 8. There are several types of pretreatments
: physical, chemical, physicochemical and biological. Pretreatment methods should complete the
following requirements: improve the formation of sugars, avoid the degradation of carbohydrate
and avoid the formation of inhibitory products (61; 87).

Detoxi￿cation
Based on the selected pretreatment method, variable quantities of chemicals acting as fermentation
inhibitors are produced. These include aliphatic acids, furan derivatives and phenolic compounds
(88; 107). As these compounds hinder fermentation it is necessary to remove them. Various detox-
i￿cation methods can be used such as ion exchange resin, activated charcoal or solvent extraction.
Each method has variable e￿ciency in removing each type inhibitor. To include detoxi￿cation in
the model, it would be necessary to have quantitative data on the e￿ects of each inhibitor on the
selected LA, SA and ethanol fermentation strains’ yields. Data would also have to be added to the
model if it was to be extended to other main products in the future. As data was lacking for LA
and SA fermentation, it was decided not to consider the detoxi￿cation stage in the model. It was
therefore considered that all inhibitors produced during the pretreatment stage are removed and
therefore do not in￿uence the fermentation.
Hydrolysis and fermentation
After leaving pretreatment, the ￿rst step is to hydrolyze the cellulose and hemicellulose remain-
ing in the pretreated solid into fermentable sugars. This is done using enzymes. There are two
main families of enzymes used to degrade cellulose and hemicellulose: cellulases and hemicellu-
lases. These enzymes are sourced from microorganisms such as Trichoderma reesei. Cellulases and
hemicellulases can be furthered into di￿erent types. Commercially available enzymes used for sac-
chari￿cation/hydrolysis are a mix of di￿erent types of enzymes.(48)
Several organisms such as bacteria or yeasts are capable of fermenting sugars into ethanol, lactid acid
or ethanol. These organisms can be natural or bioengineered. For example, some organisms capable
of converting sugars into lactic acid are Bacillus coagulans LA204, Rhizopus oryzae NBRC 5384 or
Enterococcus faecium QU 50 (26). Papers studying an array of di￿erent strains can be found in the
literature. Most strains are characterized by an achievable titer (g/L), yield (g/g) and productivity
(g/L/h).

Conceptual design, modeling and optimization of a biore￿nery
There are two main approaches for conceptual design: hierarchical decomposition and superstruc-
ture synthesis (76). The latter is preferred as it allows to explore in a systematic manner the al-
ternative pathways (76). Superstructure synthesis has already been used in various papers for the
conceptual design of biore￿neries (107; 94; 30; 47; 90; 111; 41). Three sequential steps are involved
13
==PAGE:25==
when the superstructure synthesis approach is used: (i) the postulation of a superstructure, which
incorporates the set of all feasible alternative process structures; (ii) the translation of the super-
structure into a mathematical programming model; and (iii) the computation of an optimal structure
by solving the mathematical optimization model (76). The main drawback of this approach comes
from step(i): the superstructure is limited to the alternatives it incorporates and a poorly designed
superstructure may omit promising pathways (76).
Most of the time, papers related to the conceptual design of biore￿neries relate to the production of
biofuel and ethanol (45; 47; 94). For example, Vikash and Shastri (107) identi￿ed the best conversion
pathways of LCF into ethanol; Restrepo-Flórez and Maravelias (94) identi￿ed the best conversion
pathways for ethanol upgrading into advanced biofuels, Ponce-Ortega et al. (90) identi￿ed the best
conversion pathways of LCF into bioalcohols and Gabriel and El-Halwagi (41) identi￿ed the best
conversion pathways of various biomasses into alcohol. In a lesser extent, conceptual design of
biore￿neries producing high-value chemicals has also already been studied in the literature. For ex-
ample, Elyasi et al. (30) identi￿ed the best conversion pathways of household wastes into bioenergy,
microbial proteins, and biochemicals, (111) identi￿ed the best conversion pathways of lignocellu-
losic biomass and crude-oil into ethanol, butanol, succinic acid and blends of these chemicals with
fossil fuel based gasoline and (47) identi￿ed the best conversion pathways of lignocellulosic biomass
into levulinic acid, succinic acid, and ethanol.
When translating their superstructure into a mathematical programming model, or more gener-
ally when dealing with models involving both discrete and continuous domain spaces (which are
prevalent in process design), modelers typically formulate their mathematical models as MILPs or
MINLPs (14; 15). In the studies involving conceptual design of biore￿neries using superstructure
synthesis presented above, this is the case in Vikash and Shastri (107); Restrepo-Flórez and Mar-
avelias (94); Elyasi et al. (30); Giuliano et al. (47); Zondervan et al. (111) and Gabriel and El-Halwagi
(41).
It was claimed by Chen et al. (15) that most of these MILP/MINLP formulations arise from attempts
to model intuitively disjunctive (logical this-OR-that) problems. An alternative approach, intro-
duced by Raman and Grossmann (93), called Generalized Disjunctive Programming (GDP), proposes
a high-level modeling construct to model this logic in the from of disjunctions (15). The general form
for GDP optimization models is given in Chen and Grossmann (14) as well as the one of Mixed-
Integer Nonlinear Programming (MINLP) problems. What is the advantage of GDP compared
to MILP or MINLP ? This advantage relies on the possible solving strategies. Indeed, once a GDP
model is formulated, it can either be solved by directly using logic-based algorithms or reformu-
lations can be applied to transform the model structure to a MILP or MINLP to allow the use of
MILP/MINLP solvers. This o￿ers a high ￿exibility as several solving strategies can be attempted to
solve an unique GDP model formulation (15).
Several studies applied GDP optimization models to the conceptual design of biore￿neries: Ponce-
Ortega et al. (90) used it to identify the best conversion pathways of LCF into bioalcohols, Corbetta
et al. (19) worked on optimizing downstream processing of biofuels and bio-based chemicals and
Murillo-Alvarado et al. (78) formulated a GDP model to select potential feedstocks, products, and
processing steps of a biore￿nery based on technical, economical, and environmental objectives. GDP
optimization models are also applied in other areas such as heat exchanger network optimization
(57).
Two algebraic modeling languages (AML) o￿er the possibility of using GDP modeling and optimiza-
tion (15): Pyomo (12) and GAMS. The former is open-sourced and the latter is commercial. The two
standard conversion methods of GDP to MILP/MINLP, the Big-M (BM) reformulation introduced
14
==PAGE:26==
by Raman and Grossmann (93) and the hull reformulation (HR) described in Furman et al. (40),
are supported by both Pyomo and GAMS (15). Unlike Pyomo, GAMS allows the direct solution of
GDP problems by using logic-based outer approximation (LOA) via the LOGMIP solver (106; 15).
Nevertheless, recent capabilities were added to Pyomo modeling environment. These include two
advanced GDP reformulations to MILP/MINLP: (i) An hybrid BM/HR cutting plane algorithm (ii)
GDP Basic Steps. It also includes a new solver called GDPopt which is a ￿exible logic-based non-
linear GDP solver for Pyomo. More details about these new capabilities can be found in Chen et al.
(15).
In the papers which were reviewed above, the majority of modelers which formulated their mathe-
matical models as MILPs or MINLPs, used GAMS as their modeling language (107; 94; 111). Other
authors used python (30), AMPL (47) or Lingo (41). Among the authors which applied GDP opti-
mization models to the conceptual design of biore￿neries all used GAMS (90; 19; 78). No papers
were found that used Pyomo as their modeling language to develop a GDP optimization model for
the conceptual design of biore￿neries.
Some authors used Pyomo for superstructure synthesis. del Rio-Chanona et al. (23) built a MINLP
model into Pyomo to identify sustainable biopolymer synthesis routes via superstructure and mul-
tiobjective optimization. Savage and Zhang (97) also built a MINLP model into Pyomo to identify
sustainable synthesis routes of biobased nitrogen-containing polymers. Kazi et al. (57) also built a
MINLP model into Pyomo to optimize the synthesis of heat exchanger networks. Several papers pre-
senting frameworks for GDP in Pyomo using superstructure synthesis can be found in the literature
(99; 55; 38).
Several techno-economic criteria can be selected to evaluate and rank biore￿nery designs. Vikash
and Shastri (107) used the minimization of the total costx as their optimization criterium. Restrepo-
Flórez and Maravelias (94) optimized biore￿nery designs in order to maximize pro￿t. Elyasi et al. (30)
determined an optimal biore￿ning route by de￿ning three objective functions: the maximization of
energy savings and pro￿tability, and the minimization of greenhouse gases emissions. Ponce-Ortega
et al. (90) optimized biore￿nery designs in order to minimize total cost, minimize GHG emissions
or minimize environmental impact. Zondervan et al. (111) de￿ned four di￿erent optimization ob-
jectives functions: maximize yields, minimize costs, minimize wastes and minimum ￿xed costs and
￿nally Restrepo-Flórez and Maravelias (94) used the minimization of the production prices as their
optimization criterium.
In this project, superstructure synthesis is applied to the modeling and optimization of a biore￿nery
using GDP and Pyomo as a modeling language. No similar studies were found to have been reported
in the literature.

Methodology
The methodology used in this thesis is represented on ￿gure 9 and consists of 5 steps. This method-
ology is adapted from the one proposed by del Castillo-Romo et al. (22). Each step is going to be
described brie￿y hereafter.
Figure 9
–
Methodology for multiobjective optimization of a biore￿nery.
3.1
Step 1: Literature review and data collection
The ￿rst step of this methodology consists of making a literature review of the alternative processes
described in the literature which allow to transform LCF into ethanol, LA and SA. Subsequently
data has to be collected on these alternative processes to estimate their yield, the amount of raw
materials and energy needed as well as potential by-products. Furthermore, economic data also has
to be gathered in order to estimate the cost of the utilities, by-products and LCF. Lastly, data on the
LCF price and its composition is collected.
16
==PAGE:28==
3.2
Step 2: Superstructure generation
Based on the data collected in Step 1, the superstructure is constructed. The superstructure includes
all the alternative processes in the design space as well as the feedstocks and main outputs. All the
links between these elements also have to be de￿ned.
3.3
Step 3: GDP model construction
Once the superstructure is generated, a GDP model is constructed. This model is a mathematical
representation of the superstructure. In order to build this mathematical representation, it is neces-
sary to de￿ne variables. The GDP model uses binary (integer) as well as continuous variables. The
binary variables are used to model the selection (if the variable equals 1) or non-selection (if the
variable equals 0) of a feedstock or process alternative. Continuous variables are used to represent
elements such as mass ￿ows or utility consumption. Parameters are also de￿ned. They can be used
to represent economic or technical parameters. Parameters can also be used to model the possibility
or non-possibility of linkage between some process alternatives. Sets are also de￿ned. They are
used to index variables or parameters. The di￿erent hypothesis on which the model is based upon
are also cited. Equality and inequality constraints are de￿ned, they are used to represent elements
such as mass balances, target production goals, feedstocks’ maximum availability, etc. Lastly, the
objective function of the model is de￿ned. It can consist of the individual or simultaneous mini-
mization/maximization of techno-economic parameters such as operating pro￿t and environmental
impact.
3.4
Step 4: Problem formulation and solution
This step consists of formulating the mathematical model built in Step 3 in a modeling language
which enables the model to be solved using open-source or commercial solvers. To solve the mathe-
matical model developed in this thesis, the software used is Pyomo (Python Optimization Modeling
Objects). This software is embedded within Python, a high-level programming language that con-
tains a range of supporting libraries (12). During this phase, the model is tested. Several problems
can arise, for example the optimization problem may be infeasible or unbounded. This leads to going
back and modifying the GDP model until satisfactory results are obtained. The solver used to solve
the model was Gurobi (51) as it proved to be the most e￿ective (other solvers such as GDPopt were
tested). Gurobi is a commercial solver which is free for academic use. The computer used to solve
the model has the following characteristics: 2.9 GHz Quad-Core Intel Core i7 with 16 GB of RAM.
3.5
Step 5: Multi-objective optimization
After the problem is solved and proved to be feasible for each of the studied objective functions, it
is possible to perform a multi-objective optimization. When performing a multi-objective optimiza-
tion, single best solution exists. It is therefore necessary to identify the set of best solutions. This set
can be represented using a Pareto front. No solution that lies on the Pareto front can be considered
as better than the other solutions. However, preferences can be established which allows one to
choose a solution over the others. (22)
The method used to build the Pareto front is the ✏constraint technique. More details about the ✏
constraint method can be found in del Castillo-Romo et al. (22).
Several other cases which only require single objective optimization are also studied.
17

4
Results and discussion
As previously mentioned, the biore￿nery’s conceptual design is geographically framed to be located
in Denmark. The LCF are considered to be wheat and barley straw as they are the main agricultural
residues in Denmark. The conversion of manure into biogas is also considered since Denmark is a
leading biogas producer. Moreover, manure is readily available due to the fact that Denmark has the
highest pig-to-population ratio in Europe.. The biore￿nery is designed to be able to produce ethanol,
LA and SA given the promising potential of these products to be used as biofuel and production of
bioplastics, respectively.
4.1
Step 1: Literature review and data collection
The di￿erent process alternatives and data for each stage of the biore￿nery were collected. Several
factors may lead to the non-selection of process alternatives. Due to the fact that the superstructure
is built using a block-based approach, it is necessary to ￿nd: (i) estimates of the required inputs
(energy and raw materials) of a given process alternative; and (ii) data that allows to link the inputs
and outputs of the corresponding alternativea. Often during the literature review promising pro-
cess options were found that could not be included in the superstructure due to the insu￿cient data

Stage 1: Preprocessing
The preprocessing stage is used to reduce the particle size of biomass before it is sent to pretreatment.
The size reduction that has to be achieved depends on which pretreatment method is selected in the
pretreatment stage. Indeed, pretreatment methods are characterized by a maximal input particle
size. This size is de￿ned as the size at which maximal e￿ciency is reached and below which no
signi￿cant increase in e￿ciency can be achieved (107).
The preprocessing is therefore modeled by a hammer mill with ￿nite output particle sizes that cor-
respond to the maximum input size of the previously chosen pretreatment methods. The speci￿c
energy consumption of the hammer mill was determined for each of these output sizes by following
the correlation presented in (61). The corresponding estimates are presented in Appendix G. The
energy carrier was assumed to be electricity.
Stage 2: Pretreatment
The model considers six pretreatment methods: dilute acid, lime, ammonia ￿ber explosion (AFEX),
liquid hot water (LHW), and steam explosion (STEX). The pretreatment methods are modeled as
black boxes which are based on inputs and outputs. The pretreatment conditions are therefore not
optimized but considered at ￿xed operating conditions. It is considered that the output of the pre-
treatment stage is sent to a solid-liquid separator. The liquid and solid output composition depend on
the recovery factors of the pretreatment options. The recovery fractions for the compounds tracked
during pretreatment (polysaccharides and lignin) can be found in Appendix H. These recoveries are
assumed to be feedstock independent. This hypothesis is made due to lacking of available data but it

is not accurate as pretreatment e￿ciency varies (e.g., variation related to the presence of silica(107)).
The amount of by-products generated per kilo of feedstock processed for each pretreatment method
can be found in Appendix I. The raw material consumption for each type of pretreatment option is
reported in Appendix J.
Stage 3: Hydrolysis and fermentation stage
The enzymatic hydrolysis of cellulose and hemicellulose, and the fermentation of the released sug-
ars by bacterial strains, are modeled in a single stage. Four di￿erent fermentation techniques are
considered in the superstructure: separate hydrolysis and fermentation (SHF), simultaneous saccha-
ri￿cation and fermentation (SSF), separate hydrolysis and co-fermentation (SHCF) and simultaneous
sacchari￿cation and co-fermentation (SSCF). Simpli￿ed diagrams depicting the di￿erence between
these fermentation techniques are presented in Appendix A. The main features which di￿erentiate
these techniques are:
• If SHF is employed, the pretreated solid ￿rst enters a reactor in which cellulose and hemi-
cellulose are hydrolysed. Subsequently, the stream containing the released sugars enters the
fermentation reactor to produce one of the three main products considered in the model. The
xylose present in the liquid stream leaving pretreatment is fermented in another reactor.
• If SSF is employed, the pretreated solid enters an unique reactor in which cellulose and hemi-
cellulose are hydrolyzed; the released sugars are simultaneously fermented to produce one
of the three main products. The xylose present in the liquid stream leaving pretreatment is
fermented in another reactor.
• The SHCF technique is identical to SHF with the exception that the liquid stream leaving
pretreatment is mixed with the pretreated solid; all sugars are fermented together.
• The SSCF technique is identical to SSF with the exception that the liquid stream leaving pre-
treatment is mixed with the pretreated solid and all sugars are fermented together.
The hydrolysis conversion value for SHF and SHCF can be found in Appendix K as well as the
conversion values of xylose and glucose to ethanol is SSCF and SSF conditions.
Furthermore, the in￿uence of the pretreatment method is taken into account. The pretreatment
methods are assumed to be at ￿xed operating conditions. As mentioned in Vikash and Shastri (107),
data related to pretreatment methods highly depends on experimental factors such as the type of
enzyme or the operating conditions.
The fermentation is modeled in a simpli￿ed manner by considering ￿xed conversion factors of sug-
ars into fermentation products. The in￿uence of parameters such as temperature and residence
time are not taken into account. Fermentation reactors are therefore also considered to operate at
￿xed operating conditions. As for hydrolysis, fermentation data available in the literature has also
high variability since the fermentation is a￿ected by multiple factors such as solid loading, type of
fermentation (continuous or batch), residence/fermentation time and the organisms used in the fer-
mentation. The conversion of glucose and xylose into LA, SA and ethanol can be found in Appendix
L. The enzyme requirements according to the pretreatment method used prior to fermentation are
reported in Appendix M.

Stage 4: Product puri￿cation and concentration + lignin recovery and valorization
The puri￿cation and concentration of the main fermentation products is considered in the last step
of the superstructure, as presented in ￿gure 10. the puri￿cation and concentration technologies are
dependent on the main product. There are separate product concentration technologies for each of
the main products.
The alternative processes considered for ethanol recovery and concentration are conventional dis-
tillation followed by:
• Extractive distillation with ethyl alcohol,
• Azeatropic distillation with cyclohexane, or
• Adsorption with molecular sieves
The alternative processes considered for lactic acid recovery and concentration are:
• Conventional method (precipitation followed by esteri￿cation, distillation and hydrolysis), or
• Electrodialysis followed by esteri￿cation, distillation and hydrolysis
The alternative processes considered for succinic acid recovery and concentration are:
• Electrodialysis,
• Direct crystallization, or
• Reactive extraction
Two alternatives are considered for lignin: lignin is either converted into electricity using the BIGCC
technology (49), or disposed as waste. As for the other stages of the biore￿nery, the product puri￿-
cation and concentration processes, as well as the BIGCC process, are assumed to operate at ￿xed
conditions. Their operating conditions are therefore not optimized by the model. Each puri￿cation
process requires utilities such as steam, electricity or solvents to operate. In this model, the utility
consumption of each puri￿cation process is expressed per kilo of product recovered. Each puri￿ca-
tion process is also associated with a recovery factor. This recovery factor is de￿ned as the amount
of product which is recovered at the output of the process divided by the amount which is fed to the
process. For example, a recovery factor equal to 0.9 means that 90% of the product is recovered while
10% is lost. The recovery factor and utility consumption of each puri￿cation process is assumed to
be ￿xed and not dependent on factors such as the fermentation broth concentration. The utility
consumption and recovery factor of the lactic acid puri￿cation and concentration processes can be
found in Appendix O. The utility consumption and recovery of the succinic acid puri￿cation and
concentration processes can be found in Appendix N. The utility consumption and recovery of the
ethanol puri￿cation and concentration processes can be found in Appendix P.
The inputs and outputs of the BIGCC process converting lignin into electricity can be found in
Appendix Q. The details of this process is presented in González-García et al. (49). González-García
et al. (49) considers willow chips as input for the BIGCC process; in this work the hypothesis is that
the inputs and outputs are identical when lignin is used.

Step 3: GDP Model construction
Model assumptions
• Only glucose and xylose are considered to be generated. Other sugars obtained from cellulose
and hemicellulose are ignored. This hypothesis is made because, as previously mentioned,
cellulose is a homopolymer of glucose and because xylose is the major sugar in hemicellulosic
fractions - it represents approximately 70-80% (103).
• The e￿ect of inhibitors on the di￿erent fermentation yields is not considered i.e. it is consid-
ered that after pretreatment all the byproducts are removed.
• All ￿ows are de￿ned as greater or equal to zero. The big-M formulation is also applied (each
￿ow’s upper bound is given by a large constant M). This large constant M has no physical
meaning, the value of this constant therefore needs to be high enough not to cause a limitation
on the objective function. In other words, the constant M is chosen large enough for the
constraints not to be active.
• All fermentation by-products are considered to be removed during the puri￿cation and con-
centration stages. Thus, they are not included in the model.
• All ￿ows are calculated on a dry basis.
• Capital costs are ignored, only operating costs are taken into account.
• Process alternatives are considered as black boxes. Therefore, the model does not optimize
operating conditions.
• Process data is based on available literature. Hence, it is important to note that some data is
based on lab-scale experiments and simulations (e.g., Aspen + simulations). Thus, data may
not represent accurately the processes at industrial scale. This situation may lead to an unfair
comparison between processes as some processes are mature while others are only based on
simulations and lab-scale experiments.
• The feedstocks transportation and storage costs are disregarded as well as seasonal supply
￿uctuation.
• Ammonia used in AFEX is assumed to be 90% recycled (10% make-up), and 1-Ethyl-3-methylimidazolium
acetate used in the ionic liquid pretreatment is assumed to be 100% recycled.

After the pretreatment section, the liquid and solid streams are distributed between di￿erent fermen-
tation sections. Each of these fermentation sections produces one main product h 2 H. Di￿erent
fermentation techniques i 2 I can be used to convert fermentable sugars into more valuable prod-

in the same (SSF and SSCF). The second is that the xylose-rich liquid stream leaving pretreatment is
co-fermented with the sugars formed from the solid fraction leaving pretreatment (SHCF and SSCF)
or not (SSF and SHF).

In the two equations above, ↵L(h) and ↵S(h) represent the fraction of liquid and solid exiting the
pretreatment section sent to the fermentation section producing the main product h, respectively.
These fractions were also needed to track the amounts of solid and liquid stream components which
are sent to each fermentation section. The initial model, once programmed using Pyomo, failed to
converge to an optimum solution in a reasonable solving time.
It was therefore decided to reformulate the model to suppress these non-linear constraints. This was
achieved by assuming that all liquid and solids from the same feedstock b1 2 B1 has to be sent to an
unique fermentation section. This formulation eliminates the nonlinear constraints as ↵L(h) = 1
and ↵S(h) = 1. However, this formulation comes with a drawback: one feedstock b1 2 B1 can only
lead to one main output product h 2 H.
This is not a problem when trying to identify the best conversion pathway for a set of feedstocks but
makes the model infeasible if constraints on minimum production quantities of each main product
are de￿ned. Indeed if production targets are de￿ned for LA, SA and ethanol, as only two feedstocks
are considered in the model and one feedstock b1 2 B1 can only lead to one main output product h 2
H, the model becomes infeasible. To overcome this challenge, each feedstock can be duplicated by
the number of main products allowing for each feedstock-main product combination to be selected
when solving the model. This situation is illustrated in a simpli￿ed manner in ￿gure 11. Figure 11
illustrates a simple case with one feedstock and two main fermentation products. As shown on the
top-part of the ￿gure, the linear model does not enable the production of ’Product 1’ and ’Product 2’
using the same feedstock, in this case named ’Feedstock1’. However, as shown on the bottom-part
of ￿gure 11, one can duplicate ’Feedstock1’ to enable the production of ’Product 1’ and ’Product
2’ by the same feedstock. This ’trick’ however comes with the drawback of increasing the model
complexity. Indeed, with three main products h considered in this model, each feedstock has to
be arti￿cially tripled, increasing by the same factor the number of possible solutions of the model.

4.4
Step 4: Problem formulation and solution
After building the GDP model, it was implemented and coded using Pyomo, a software embedded
in python. The GDP model was converted into a MINLP using a standard reformulation 1. This
strategy was chosen as it was the more e￿cient in terms of solving time. Another approach was
tested; the GDP model was not reformulated into a MINLP but solved directly using GDPopt, a
￿exible logic-based nonlinear GDP solver for Pyomo (15). However the ￿rst approach (GDP refor-
mulation into MINLP using a standard reformulation method and subsequent model solving using
a MINLP solver) was more e￿cient in terms of solving time and therefore kept. After investigat-
ing several other solvers, Gurobi was selected as solver. Other solvers were incompatible with the
model. For example, IPOPT can solve nonlinear problems but cannot handle integer terms while
GLPK and CBC cannot solve nonlinear problems. The only nonlinear equation of the model is equa-
tion (77), which is a bilinear equality constraint. As Gurobi integrates a bilinear solver (which was
introduced in Gurobi 9.0) that can handle integer variables, it was chosen as solver. It is presumed
that the adopted strategy was more e￿cient due to the fact that Gurobi is a commercial solver while
GDPopt is an open-source solver.
As mentioned previously, the two most popular approaches to reformulate a GDP model into a
MINLP model are: the Big-M (BM) and the convex hull relaxation (HR) (106; 14). The choice be-
tween BM and HR is a trade-o￿: BM requires a lower number of variables2 and constraints than HR
but HR allows to have a better quality of the continuous relaxation and therefore stronger lower
bounds (14; 50). There is no way to know a priori which one will result in a smaller solving time as
the stronger lower bounds obtained using HR can potentially lead to smaller solving times but it not
always true because of the increased size of the reformulation (50). For the model developed in this
study, BM led to the smallest solving times (wallclock times around 100 to 200s) and was therefore
selected.
After several trials and adjustments, the problem ￿nally converged to the optimal solutions for
each objective functions: to minimize E-factor and maximize operating pro￿t. The next step was to
further assess the problem by performing a multi-objective optimization, as presented in the next
step.
4.5
Step 5: Multi-Objective optimization
Description of the studied scenario
The studied scenario consists of the multi-objective optimization of a biore￿nery producing RNG,
LA, SA and ethanol. To estimate a meaningful production of RNG from manure, data about the size
of biogas plants in Denmark was searched. It was found that 1,218 GWh of biogas was produced by

2For the studied model, there are 98895 constraint and 57239 variables when using the BM and 124967 constraint

154 plants in Denmark in 2013 (56). This represents around 8 GWh per plant or the equivalent of
1000 kWh produced each hour (assuming 8000 annual operating hours) per plant. A target produc-
tion of 1000 kWh of biogas per operating hour was therefore assumed. The target production for LA
and SA was assumed based on the scale of the SA plant of DSM & Roquette Frères (50,000 tons per
year of SA or 6.25 tons of SA per operating hour assuming 8000 annual operating hours (104)). A
target production of 6.25 tons of LA and 6.25 tons of SA per operating hour was therefore assumed.
The target production of ethanol was set to 15 tons per operating hour. This value comes from
Raftery and Karim (92) which determined in their study that the optimal bioethanol plant would
produce 42 million gallons of ethanol per year. This value is equivalent to about 15.68 tons per hour
(assuming 8000 annual operating hours) and was rounded to 15 tons per operating hour.
Other scenarios could have been studied instead of the one described above:
• Alternatively, a maximum and minimum yearly amount of input feedstock could have been
￿xed instead. If the objective is the maximization of the OP, the model would have selected
the process pathway which leads to the production of the most pro￿table single output. As OP
is a linear function of the biore￿nery’s capacity, the model would have chosen to use all the
available feedstock if the estimated optimal OP was positive and chosen to use the minimum
required if the estimated optimal OP was negative. This scenario would have led to only one
product being selected (the one allowing for the highest OP).
• A ￿xed amount of feedstock to be used each year could have been de￿ned. This scenario
would have been exactly the same as the previous one except that in the case of an optimal
OP which is negative, the model would still have to process the ￿xed amount of feedstock
Considering a biore￿nery which only produces one product goes against the philosophy of phase
II and phase III biore￿neries which have, as mentioned in the literature review section, the char-
acteristic of producing multiple products for better market ￿exibility. It is possible that with the
actual retail price of ethanol, LA and SA, ethanol production is the most pro￿table. However mar-
ket prices are not ￿xed and SA or LA could become the most pro￿table products if their market
prices go up. Is is relevant to consider producing several products as it allows a better process in-
tegration (energy can for example be recovered from the ethanol puri￿cation section to fuel the LA
puri￿cation section). Even if this recovery is purely hypothetical, only selecting one product rules
it out de￿nitely. Moreover the goal of developing biore￿neries is to develop credible alternatives to
petrore￿neries and be able to displace their production. Only producing the most pro￿table product
does not achieve this goal: if the other products are not produced by a biore￿nery they will have to
be produced by petrore￿neries.
If Capex is included in the model, the ￿rst alternative scenario could have been used to determine
the target production and process ￿owsheet which optimize the pro￿t for each product (this could
have be done by simply running the model and imposing each time a di￿erent output). All the
results could have then been combined to develop a multi-product biore￿nery. However in the
model developed in this study, CAPEX costs are not included and it is therefore not possible to
optimize the biore￿nery’s size.
Therefore the studied scenario , which has imposed production targets, was chosen for the following
reasons:
• It allows to identify the optimal ￿owsheet of a multi-product biore￿nery which has a better
￿exibility that a biore￿nery producing only a single product.
• By ￿xing the production target to ￿t the size of known biore￿neries, the biore￿nery has a size
46
==PAGE:58==
with is realistic.
• The production targets are based on existing biore￿neries, it can be reasonably assumed that
their size is close to an optimum; optimum which cannot be estimated by the model as it only
considers OP.
• To replace petrore￿neries, biore￿neries have to produce all the products produced by petrore-
￿neries or their substitute. Producing several of them in the same biore￿nery allows to inte-
grate the process together.
• Even if product allocation is not optimized, an analysis of the results allows to determine the
operating pro￿t and operating cost for the production of each product. It is therefore possible
to determine a posteriori which product has the highest pro￿t per kilo and which has the
lowest operating cost per kilo.

A but solution G leads to the production of less waste generation, ⇠38% less when compared to
solution A. This di￿erence is mainly linked to the puri￿cation stage. As shown in ￿gure 20, 94.7%
of the puri￿cation stage’s operating cost for solution G are due to energy requirements ( 107MM$
per year) while only 44.2% of the puri￿cation stage’s operating cost for solution A is energy-related
( 37MM$ per year).
The OP of solution G o￿ers a higher potential of being increased than solution A if energy recovery
techniques are implemented (e.g., HEN, etc.). The operating cost of the puri￿cation stage linked to
energy requirements are higher in absolute value in solution G compared to solution A. Therefore if
is it assumed that the same percentage of energy can be saved in both con￿gurations, the decrease
in the operating cost (and therefore increase in OP) will be in absolute value higher for solution G
compared to solution A. On the other hand, as solution A has a higher OP, it has a higher economic
margin to potentially invest in strategies to improve their environmental impact, here represented
by the E-Factor. Some of these strategies might include wastewater treatment facilities, solvent
recovery systems, etc. These considerations illustrate the di￿culty of selecting a solution from the
Pareto front over others only based on the simulation results. It is therefore recommended to model
both solutions with, for example, Aspen + in order to obtain more accurate results. It could also be
interesting to perform a water and heat pinch analysis in order to identify possible water and energy
recovery opportunities for both solutions.

Impact of the feedstock
As previously mentioned, wheat straw has been identi￿ed as the best feedstock in all solutions form-
ing the Pareto front. It was decided to perform a sensitivity analysis on the selected feedstock. This
analysis allows to evaluate the impact on both objective functions if barley straw is used instead
of wheat straw). The impact of shifting from wheat straw to barley straw on the optimal OP and
E-factor is illustrated in ￿gures 21 and 22, respectively. Choosing barley straw worsens both objec-
tive functions. This explains why barley straw was not selected in any Pareto optimal solution (all
other parameters remaining unchanged, a solution using barley straw is dominated by its counter-
part using wheat straw. The impact of shifting from wheat straw to barley straw does not seem to
be signi￿cant. This can be explained by the similar composition of both feedstocks (see appendix B)
and their price which was assumed to be the same. Given the uncertainty on the exact composition
of both feedstocks (95) and the small impact on both objective functions caused by the shift, it seems
relevant not to discard barley straw as a potential feedstock. In the model developed in this project,
feedstock’s seasonal availability has not been considered. However, this factor has an impact on
the operating and capital costs; if a feedstock is mainly produced in a given season, it has to be
stored in quantities large enough to be used during the whole year. This implies storage costs and
investment in storage facilities. For example, the variation in the production of straw in Denmark
is signi￿cantly a￿ected by the season; the production of wheat straw in the summer of 2019 was
approximately 35.1 million kg whereas in the winter of the same year the production was as high
as 2544.6 million kg (25).The same holds for barley straw; its production in the winter of 2019 was
approximately 385.4 million kg whereas in the in summer of the same year the production was as
high as 1631.6 million kg (25). These numbers show that only using wheat straw implies higher stor-
age costs than combining both wheat and barley straw since their main harvesting period is di￿erent.
As the impact of using barley straw on both objective functions is relatively small and since it is
readily available in summer whereas wheat straw in readily available in summer, there is a good
possibility that combining both feedstocks to account for seasonality can lead to a better operating
pro￿t than only using wheat straw. To determine if this is the case, it is necessary to expand the
model in order to include storage costs and their dependence on feedstock availability.

Impact of the pretreatment method
As wheat straw, dilute acid pretreatment was always selected as the pretreatment method for all so-
lutions forming the Pareto front. A sensitivity analysis was performed to estimate the impact of the
pretreatment option on both objective functions. Figure 23 and 24 show the optimal OP and optimal
E-Factor based on the selected pretreatment option. As expected from the Pareto front, dilute acid is
the optimal pretreatment method for both objective functions. The second best pretreatment method
based on the OP is Ionic liquid and based on the E-Factor, the second best is AFEX. With the selling
prices of ethanol, SA and LA considered in this model (see Appendix D), the estimated optimal an-
nual OP is negative for every pretreatment method except for dilute acid. However, it is important
to note that there are several factors omitted; thus, the ranking of pretreatment methods based on
optimal OP does not accurately re￿ect the reality. As the CAPEX costs are not included in the model
as well as the detoxi￿cation stage, the model developed in this project favors in some aspects meth-
ods such as ionic liquid and dilute acid. Dilute acid is one of the methods with the highest amounts
of by-products generated (see Appendix I). This means that detoxi￿cation is mandatory when using
58
==PAGE:70==
dilute acid in order to remove the by-products which act as fermentation inhibitors. Therefore, com-
pared to methods such as ionic liquid which do not produce by-products, dilute acid is favored by
not including the detoxi￿cation stage in the model. On the other hand, based on the OP, the model
predicts that ionic liquid is the second best option. However, Vikash and Shastri (107) estimated that
ionic liquid represented the worst method based on the minimum selling cost of ethanol produced
from LCF. This di￿erence can potentially be explained by the fact that the purchase cost of the ionic
liquid was not considered. Indeed, the operating cost of ionic liquid pretreatment calculated by the
model only takes into account water consumption as the ionic liquid was considered to be 100%
recycled (see Appendix J), i.e. as ionic liquid is not consumed, it is not considered as an operating
cost. However, ionic liquid still has to be purchased to ￿ll the reactor. Given the price of ionic liquid
this can represent an important investment (the price for 1-Ethyl-3-methylimidazolium acetate, the
ionic liquid considered in this model, is advertised by Proinic (91) at 170€ per kilo).
These economic considerations have no impact on the E-Factor of the biore￿nery. It can therefore
be considered, subject to the accuracy of the data, that among the pretreatment options included in
the superstructure, dilute acid results in the lowest amount of waste generated, with an estimated
2.36 kg of waste per kilo of product. The second best method based on waste generation is AFEX,
with an estimated waste generation of 3.07 kg of waste per kg of product. This represents a waste
generation 30.6% higher than when dilute acid is selected.
Based on the previous arguments, not taking into account detoxi￿cation gives an advantage to pre-
treatment methods such as dilute acid. A further improvement of the model could include the detox-
i￿cation stage so as to yield more accurate results. Furthermore, only accounting for the operating
costs of the pretreatment stage can lead to overestimating the potential of pretreatment techniques
such as ionic liquid as they require heavy investments for the purchase of their reaction medium.
Hence, another aspect to consider in order to improve the model would be to include the cost related
to the reaction media.

Impact of the price of straw
Increase in heat and dry weather negatively a￿ects straw harvest. In 2018, unfavorable weather
meant that wheat straw harvest was about half the harvest of normal years (31; 25). This decrease
in o￿er caused a price increase, with straw prices reported to have doubled, reaching around 140-
180€ per ton in some regions in Denmark (31). Given the price uncertainty of straw in Denmark,
a sensitivity analysis was performed in order to assess the impact of varying straw prices on the
optimal annual OP. The results of the sensitivity analysis are presented in ￿gure 25 for solution A.
As expected, the OP varies linearly with the straw price. All other things remaining equal, the shift
between a negative and positive OP occurs when the straw price reaches approximately 160 $ per
ton. As mentioned above, this straw price is exceeded in Denmark when unfavorable weather con-
ditions decrease straw o￿er. It is important to highlight that a positive OP does not necessarily mean
that the biore￿nery is pro￿table since all investment and miscellaneous costs will a￿ect the OP. This
analysis shows how dependent the optimal OP is on straw prices. Indeed, given the product outputs
de￿ned in the studied scenario, a 10$/ton increase in straw prices decreases the annual OP by 4.85
MM$. The variable price of straw in Denmark and its demonstrated impact on the OP, emphasizes
the importance to develop biore￿neries that have the ability to convert several feedstocks in order to
cope with price uncertainty. Therefore, future model developments could extend the list of potential
feedstocks to, for example, agricultural residues and biowastes.

5
Conclusion, recommendations & future perspectives
The goal of this project was the modeling and optimization of an integrated biore￿nery for the
production of bioplastics and biofuels from organic wastes. This hypothetical biore￿nery is geo-
graphically located in Denmark. Barley straw, wheat straw (lignocellulosic feedstocks) and manure
are considered as feedstocks; they are organic wastes readily available in Denmark. The main out-
puts of the biore￿nery are on one side lactic acid and succinic acid which can be used to produce
bioplastics, and on the other side ethanol which can be used as biofuel, upgraded to advanced biofu-
els or converted into polymer precursors. Manure is converted into biogas which can be used as an
energy source for other parts of the biore￿nery or converted into biofuel through the gas-to-liquids
(GTL) process.
The modeling and optimization of biore￿neries is essential to identify process designs that are eco-
nomically viable and have the lowest environmental impact. In this study, the modeling of the biore-
￿nery is performed by superstructure synthesis in order to include the di￿erent process alternatives.
The superstructure is divided into several stages: manure conversion into biogas, lignocellulosic
feedstock conversion (preprocessing, pretreatment, fermentation and product puri￿cation and con-
centration) and lignin conversion into electricity. The superstructure is built based on a literature
review, in order to identify several process alternatives for each stage. The superstructure is mod-
eled by formulating a model using general disjunctive programming (GDP). GDP was chosen over
MILP/MINLP since it gives more ￿exibility. The GDP model is implemented using Pyomo, an open-
source software embedded into python that is compatible with open-source and commercial solvers.
The GDP model was solved using Gurobi, a free solver for academic use. The biore￿nery base case
is built on an annual production of 50000 tons of lactic acid, 50000 tons of succinic acid and 120000
tons of ethanol. The ✏-constraint method was used to build a Pareto front, where two di￿erent ob-
jectives are assessed. The ￿rst objective is the maximization of the operating pro￿t (OP) which is
used to identify the ￿owsheet with the highest potential of being economically feasible. The second
objective is the minimization of the E-Factor, since one of the main goals of an integrated biore￿nery
is to decrease waste generation as much as possible. E-factor is a metric used in green chemistry to
assess and compare the amount of waste generated by chemical processes, here used to represent
the biore￿nery’s environmental impact.
The Pareto analysis allowed the identi￿cation of 7 Pareto optimal ￿owsheet con￿gurations. The
di￿erences among these 7 con￿gurations are related to the processes used for the puri￿cation and
concentration of lactic acid and succinic acid. They also di￿er on the processes used for the conver-
sion of manure into biogas. For all the solutions forming the Pareto front, wheat straw is selected as
the main feedstock, dilute acid is selected as the pretreatment method and lignin is recovered and
converted into electricity by using BICGG technology. The optimal E-Factor is equal to 2.36 kg of
waste per kg of product and optimal operating pro￿t 29.8 MM$.
Several sensitivity analysis identi￿ed aspects of the model that have to be improved. Uncertainties
on parameters, such as product selling prices or fermentation yields need to be taken into account
as values found in the literature are inconsistent. For example, it was shown that, depending on
the reference source of products’ selling prices, the ranking of each product based on operating
pro￿t changes considerably. Indeed with the selling prices ￿rst considered in the model, the optimal
product based on operating pro￿t was ethanol, followed by succinic acid and lactic acid. By taking
revised values from (42), the optimal product based on operating pro￿t was lactic acid, followed by
succinic acid and ethanol. This illustrates the fact that there are no absolute all-time best product
65
==PAGE:77==
can be identi￿ed due to market price uncertainty. However by forcing the production of several
products, the model identi￿es the optimal process ￿owsheet for the production of each product.
All in all, given a set of assumptions, it was determined that the development of an integrated biore-
￿nery located in Denmark has the potential of being economically viable. The model developed
in this study demonstrated the ability to identify at early stage of design process pathways which
optimize operating pro￿t and optimize waste generation. The model however fails to account for
seasonal feedstock availability, and cannot determine an optimal biore￿nery scale. This can be fur-
ther improved by including storage costs and their dependence on feedstock availability as well as
CAPEX costs.
Recommendations & future perspectives
To further improve model accuracy, some recommendations and future model modi￿cations are
given as follows.
• Include Capital Expenditure (CAPEX) estimation in the model. This would allow to account
for the biore￿nery scale and to estimate minimum product selling prices (MSP).
• Perform a uncertainty analysis on parameters that carry high uncertainty such as feedstock
prices, composition and fermentation yield. This analysis would increase the con￿dence in
the model results.
• Review and model novel puri￿cation process for lactic and succinic acid to be able to include
them in the superstructure. For example by using Aspen+.
• Identify the opportunities of using other feedstocks available in Denmark to cope with the
￿uctuation of feedstock prices.
• Include feedstock seasonality in the model and their associated storage costs. This modi￿-
cation could be used to identify opportunities of advantageously combining feedstocks with
di￿erent seasonal availability.
• Model the detoxi￿cation stage for a fairer comparison among the di￿erent pretreatment meth-
ods. An approach as the one used in Scott et al. (98) could be used as a starting point.
• Adapt the target production of biogas. An idea could be to ￿x a target biogas production to
ensure that all energy requirements of the biore￿nery are met by the biogas production.
• Once the model is improved based on the suggestions provided, identify the best ￿owsheet
con￿gurations and perform a heat and water pinch analysis to identify heat and water re-
covery opportunities. The best ￿owsheet con￿gurations can then be modeled with Aspen+
in order to yield more accurate results, and thus lead to a more informed decision about the
optimal con￿guration.
