Abstract
The purpose of this project is to design and fabricate a device based on Dielectrophoretic Field-
Flow-Fractionation able to separate Candida Albicans from blood platelets based on density diﬀer-
ences. A channel design using a triangular cross-section was used, where the electric ﬁeld between
the contact on the V-groove and at the bottom were the source of the dielectrophoretic force.
The exact geometry was found by doing numerical simulations using numerical methods. By cross
referencing the eﬃciency of diﬀerent designs with available fabrication tools, resulted in a good
compromise between the diﬃculty to fabricate and eﬃciency. The device was built by micro milling
the channels and in/outlets in PEEK. Then a shadow mask in PMMA was cut out with a laser
and using it to deposit Titanium and gold on the channels. Lastly the device was sealed by taping
a piece of Indium Tin Oxide covered plastic with double sided tape to the front side of the device.
Simultaneously a series of measurements were performed, where polystyrene beads, platelets and
Candida Albicans were dropped through water and their terminal speed and size was recorded.
This was used to calculate ﬁrst the viscosity of the water, and then the density of the cells. The
measurements showed that the density of Candida Albicans is 1080 ± 20 kg/m3 and platelet is
1050±30 kg/m3. This is much closer than the values the of 1200±75 kg/m3 and 1060±10 kg/m3
the project and the initial theoretical investigations was based on. Based on these results it seems
unlikely that the device will be able to separate the two cell types.
The two chips that ended out being fabricated, was tested using beads. During the measurements
on the ﬁrst chip it proved diﬃcult to ﬁnd the beads, but something suspected be the beads were
found on the bottom of the channel. In the measurements done on the second chip it was much
easier to ﬁnd the beads in the channel. However, they did not seem to react to the applied electric
ﬁeld in any way. The metal contacts on this chip was damaged during fabrication, which might
have led to a severed connection.
No functioning chips, based on the developed fabrication process, were constructed during the
project. All apparent ﬂaws were localized and addressed however.

Introduction
Candidemia is a broad term for sepsis involving species of yeast called Candida where the
most prominent (up to 70%) is Candida Albicans [1] (Candida A.). Candidemia is often
contracted during general surgery or organ transplant, but also illnesses like HIV and
Diabetes increase the risk of infection [2]. Candidemia is relatively rare compared to other
sepsis but the mortality rates are very high if treatment is not initiated early [3, 4]. For
this reason patients are often screened for Candidemia if they show any symptoms. The
current methods used for detection of Candidemia are slow and the reliabilities are low
[5]. A faster and more reliable detection method would not only increase the survivability
of patients, but also decrease the medical expenses involved in preemptive or prophylactic
treatment. At the moment the average cost to treat a patient with Candidemia exceeds
30,000 USD [6]. A faster method for diagnosing Candidemia is thus required.
Such a method was developed by S. Preus in her master thesis [7]. Preus developed a
protocol to purify a whole blood sample and an optical method for detecting Candida in
said sample. However, blood platelets (PLT) in the sample interfered with the detection
and obscured the results. The aim of this project will therefore be to develop a device
capable of separating PLT from Candida A. in such a treated sample. The method used
for this project is Dielectrophoresis (DEP). DEP is a well known technique used to control
small volumes and objects, and works on small timescales, making it a candidate for this
problem. This will be combined with the method Field-Flow-Fractionation (FFF) where
objects are placed at diﬀerent heights of a ﬂow, making them move at diﬀerent velocities,
thereby separating them in space.
1.1
Diagnosis of Candidemia
The method currently used by hospitals to diagnose Candidemia is by growing the fungi
in a blood sample. This does not only take a long time, two to four days, but the reliance
is also low at only 50-70% [5]. As mentioned this is far from ideal, since Candidemia is
connected with very high mortality rates of more than 30% [3]. The mortality rate rises
steadily every hour until treatment is initiated. A study showed an increase from 15% to
37% if treatment were initiated at day two instead of day zero. Having up to 10 infections
per 100,000 persons per year in some parts of Europe and USA, and costing around

Theory
In this chapter the theory involved in the project will be introduced and explained. The
chapter will describe the physics of dielectrophoresis and the meaning of the Clausius-
Mossotti factor. Followed by the basics of micro ﬂuidics along with concepts like viscosity,
Reynolds number and hydraulic resistance.
Note that all permittivities mentioned throughout this chapter is the product of the relative
permittivity and the permittivity of free space unless otherwise stated.
2.1
Dielectrophoresis
Many methods exist for manipulating biological samples in a volume of liquid. The most
widely used are a group of methods called electrokinetics all revolving around the use of
electric ﬁelds. Alternating Current (AC) is often preferred to Direct Current (DC) since
typically high voltages (5-20V) are needed; in a DC setup this often causes bubbles, heat
and might also result in unwanted electrochemical reactions [12].
In this project an AC method called dielectrophoresis is used.
A dielectric particle is
polarized in an electric ﬁeld. Any gradient in the electric ﬁeld will then cause a force to
act on the particle. By designing the electric ﬁeld the particle can be manipulated. Since
its discovery in 1958 by Pohl it has become the most widely used method [12].
2.1.1
Polarization
When an electric ﬁeld acts on a dielectric in a vacuum, the dielectric becomes polarized.
The reason originates from the electron structure of dielectrics. Dielectrics have all their
electrons tightly bound to the atoms, in other words, there are no electrons in the conduc-
tion band. Unlike conductors, where introducing an electric ﬁeld will drive a current, in
a dielectric the electrons and nuclei will only be shifted slightly, the positive nuclei in the
direction of the electric ﬁeld and the electron against it. As long as the electric ﬁeld is not
strong enough to ionize the dielectric this induces a dipole. The dipole is approximately
proportional with the electric ﬁeld [13].
p = αE

Eﬀectively this means that charge is accumulated on the surface, with negative charge on
one side and positive on the other. The magnitude of this charge accumulation depends
on the dielectric’s permittivity and conductance [12].
The behavior changes if the particle is suspended in a liquid instead of a vacuum. Since
both the particle and the liquid are dielectrics both will be polarized and charges will
gather at the liquid particle interface on both sides but with opposite signs. The two
materials counteract each other, meaning that only if the polarizability is not the same a
dipole will be formed. If the liquid is more easily polarized more charge will form in the
liquid resulting in the dipole pointing in the opposite direction of the electric ﬁeld. If the
particle is more easily polarized the dipole will follow the electric ﬁeld. A sketch of this
phenomena is shown in Figure 2.1.

2.4
Dielectrophoretic Field-Flow-Fractionation
The principle of Field-Flow-Fractionation (FFF) is to position particles at diﬀerent heights
or positions in a channel, which makes the particles move at the velocity of the ﬂuid at
that point. As it was seen earlier in the previous section the velocity proﬁle for a channel is
non-constant, and is often to either the second order of the distance from a wall. Therefore
if one particle is placed at a wall while another is placed in the middle of the channel, the
one in the middle will move several times faster than the other.
In this project only three major forces act on any particles in the channel; The DEP force,
the buoyancy force and Stokes drag. Stokes drag only acts as a dampener orthogonal to
the channel length, and is the force that causes the particle to move as a result of a ﬂow.
For this reason it can generally be ignored, if the particle is assumed to have the exact
speed, in the channel direction, as the ﬂow of liquid. In the orthogonal direction the two
forces buoyancy;
Fbuoyancy = (ρm −ρp)gVp = ∆ρgVp
(2.39)
and DEP governs. Here Vp is the volume of the particle, ρm and ρp is the density of the
medium and particle respectively and ∆ρ = ρm −ρp is the eﬀective density.
The buoyancy force is constant throughout the channel, the DEP force however, is not.
This means that stationary points where the two forces are equal can exist within the
channel. Since these stationary points are particle dependent, if optimized they can be
used to place two types of particles at two diﬀerent heights in the channel. Due to the
diﬀerences in the velocity ﬁeld, the two types of particles will be subject to FFF. In this
way an eﬀective density can be coupled to a velocity, and a diﬀerence in eﬀective densities
will result in a diﬀerence in velocity. A sketch of the principle is shown in Figure 2.5.

A sample volume was scanned by taking 24 images spaced 2.45 µm from each other.
This was done 20 times with 15 seconds between each scan. 10 cells/beads of each type
was manually tracked as they fell through the medium. By correlating the distance the
cells/beads had fallen with the time it had taken a velocity could be calculated for each
cell.
The program UniExplorer was used to automatically measure the cross-sectional area of
around 100 cells/beads of each kind. This area was then recalculated into a radius under
the assumption that the cells/beads were spherical.

incorrectly identiﬁed. For each type of cell/bead the settings for segmentation used by
UniExplorer were optimized to ensure the most correct choice of cell/bead boundary.

5.1
Cleanroom Process
The initial plan was to fabricate the device using common silicon based cleanroom pro-
cesses. This would decrease uncertainties in the fabrication and already gained experience
with cleanroom fabrication would be applicable. There is however a list of problems that
needs to be addressed if such a process ﬂow is to be used. This section is split into subsec-
tions dedicated to each of the problems and lastly a subsection for the proposed process
ﬂow. The problem of adhering the top and bottom together is not addressed in this sec-
tion, since it is a persistent problem that will also present itself when micro milling.
In this section the reader is assumed to have a basic knowledge of cleanroom fabrication.
5.1.1
Meandering Channels
For most etch types in silicon, making meandering channels is not a problem, but it does
present a challenge if KOH is used. KOH is an anisotropic etch, that has a high selectivity
for the (100) and (110) crystal planes compared to the (111) crystal plane. In practice this
means that it automatically etches reversed pyramids in most silicon wafers. It also means
that making round or even convex square corners is exceedingly diﬃcult. The problem
arises when the corner is formed. At the edge of the corner a crystal plane other than (111)
will appear; this plane will then immediately begin to be etched. In order to counteract
this corner protection is added. Typically a rectangle extruding from the corner. Due to
the crystal planes this rectangle will be etched away under the mask while the channel is
being etched. By using the correct size of rectangle the etch of the rectangle and channel
can be matched in such a way that only a perfect corner is left.
In this case however the channel needs to be 350 µm deep and 500 µm wide. This would
result in a corner protection going 560 µm out from the corners in a 45◦angle and being
350 µm wide. This can ﬁt inside the corner of the channel, in practice however a several
hundred microns long and at most a micron thick piece of silicon nitride only clamped on
one side will probably bent during the etch and give rise to series of new problems. This
method is therefore not necessarily a good idea.
A diﬀerent way to create slanted walls is to use an Advanced Silicon Etch (ASE). An
ASE is a Deep Reactive Ion Etcher (DRIE) and is produced to primarily etch silicon
using a Bosch process. In a Bosch process the surface is bombarded in intervals with
ions that etches it in a mix of physical collision and chemical etching. In between these
bombardments a thin passivation layer is added. When the bombardment begins again it
will only be able to break through the coating leaving sidewalls unaﬀected. By optimizing
the duration of the two steps the angle of the groove can be chosen. The primary problem
with this method is that it makes so called scallops in the surface. How these are going
to aﬀect the electric ﬁeld in the channel later on is unknown.

5.1.2
Contacts
The contacts are not as much a problem when treated correctly. The problem is to add a
layer of metal on the V-groove part of the channel. Normal photo lithography is not an
option since the depth of the grooves makes it impossible to spin the resist on the wafer
uniformly. This can be circumvented by either reusing the mask from the etch or using a
shadow mask. Each of these solutions have diﬀerent problems.
Starting with reusing the etch mask. The etch mask’s shape depends on whether a KOH
etch has been used or an ASE. In the case of the KOH etch, the mask will cover the
corners in such a way that these will not be coated with metal. This is probably not a
huge problem since such a small part of the channel consists of these. Additionally it would
take several days to etch the nitride from under the metal once it has been deposited.
A shadow mask requires no actual layer to be deposited on the wafer before metalization
in order to work. Alignment is however much worse than photo lithography. For many
systems when using shadow masks alignments is done by eye. This is however not really a
problem in this case since as long the metal covers the V-groove, a small amount of metal
outside the channel shouldn’t have signiﬁcant eﬀect.
5.1.3
Inlets and Outlets
Some of the thickest wafers readily available in the Danchip Cleanroom has a thickness
of 775 µm. Since the channel needs to be 350 µm deep it only leaves around 400 µm of
silicon in depth to make inlets and outlets (I/O) in. This requires a very diﬀerent design
to what was used in the ﬁnal micro-milled device. The easiest solution to this is probably
to make a hole and then glue a valve over it.

Fabrication - Micro Milling
This section contains the methods used for fabricating the device. The section will start
out by introducing the materials and tools used in the process. Afterwards each step of
the process will be examined one by one. A naming convention will be used where the
channel side of the chip is the front side and the other is the back side. In this report a
chip will refer to a single channel system. A device refers to the entire piece of processed
PEEK, and consist of two chips.
6.1
Materials and Tools
Two things needs to be fabricated in this project, the ﬁrst being the channel with in- and
outlets, and second being the mask used in metal deposition. The materials that have
been chosen is PEEK and PMMA respectively. In practice the channel could also have
been milled in PMMA, but PEEK has some distinct advantages. When milling, a lot of
heat is generated near the milling tool. It is a huge problem for PMMA that has glass
transition temperature of just 105◦C and a melting point of 130◦C [21], this means that
the heat from the tool is either enough to melt or severely soften the material. Combining
this with the rotation of the tool, the result is an uneven surface or in the worst case
scenario the PMMA will stick to the tool and harden. In that case it is necessary to clean
the tool, a slow process that may end in the tool getting ruined. This can be circumvented
by cooling the tool during the milling process with either an air stream or water. It proved
diﬃcult however even with water cooling to mill the in- and outlets in PMMA. Compared
to PMMA, PEEK is a thermally stable material, with a glass transition temperature of
about 143◦C and a melting temperature of around 320◦[22]. This means that little to no
cooling is necessary when milling. Additionally PEEK is far more chemical resistant than
PMMA making it easier to clean both during fabrication and after use. The device (two
chips) was fabricated on a 10 cm × 5 cm piece of PEEK with a thickness of 0.5 cm. Tests
were performed on pieces of PMMA often 10 cm × 10 cm with varying thicknesses.
The shadow mask used in the metalization step, was made with a CO2 laser. This means
that the more available and much cheaper PMMA could be used without problems, and

Tools
Three machines where used in the fabrication of the device. A micro milling machine, a

designed for milling tools in the 100 µm to a couple of millimeters range. The axis can

to consistently make, using the CO2 laser, was approximately 300 µm wide. The settings
for this line was found by testing a range of combinations of powers and scan speeds. The
settings used almost universally was 80% power and 20% write speed.
The Alcatel is a machine in the cleanroom that is able to do either E-beam evaporation
or sputtering. In this project it has only been used for E-beam evaporation.

The metalization was done with the Alcatel in the Danchip cleanroom at DTU, using E-
beam evaporation. In order to bring the mask and chip into the cleanroom, they had to be
cleaned. The standard procedure is to clean the object, ﬁrst in a ultra sonic bath of Triton
and deionized water for ten minutes, then in either 7-up or Piranha. 7-up is a chemical
solution consisting of Sulfuric Acid (98%) and Ammonium persulfate. Piranha consists of
Sulfuric acid (98%) and Hydrogen Peroxide (30%) in a 4:1 ratio [23]. Since PMMA can’t
survive such treatments, a special permission was obtained to skip the 7-up/Piranha step.
When the chip and mask were loaded into the Alcatel and the chamber was pumping to a
vacuum, it became clear that PMMA is a less than ideal mask material, since it degases
under a vacuum making it take more than 45 min to achieve a suﬃciently low pressure.
200 nm of Titanium followed by 200 nm of gold was deposited. Gold were chosen since it
is non-toxic, and isn’t chemically active, making it ideal for biological systems. Titanium
was chosen to form an undercoat since gold is notoriously known for not binding to certain
surfaces.

lines are interrupted at the middle. By adding the center line, it was found that the tape
was much easier to control and suﬀered less from deformations. Additionally the tape has
an advantage compared to PMMA, in that it is ﬂexible so deformations can to a certain
degree be counteracted. The lines needed to be about 700 µm wide, and as mentioned
before the laser cuts are 300 µm. Several lines were needed to give the required thickness.

600 µm could be achieved. If the cuts were placed further apart, there were a tendency to
form thin lines of residual tape between the cuts. If more cuts were added the remaining
tape between the openings, became so thin that they were either impossible to control or
simply broke when the tape was handled. To minimize warping of the tape, the ﬁrst cut
of each opening was done before moving on to the next cut. Using this method allowed
the tape to cool slightly between each cut, thereby improving control of the process.

7.1
Clausius-Mossotti Measurement
The purpose of this measurement is to determine the sign of the DEP force acting on a
polystyrene bead at sub MHz frequencies of AC.
A DEP chip, already constructed by a third party, was used in this experiment. The chip
was designed with a pattern that causes a repulsive force for negative DEP, and beads are
attracted to the edges of the electrodes for positive DEP. Flanking the electrodes is a layer
of PDMS, making a channel. The pattern can be seen on Figure 7.1 (Left), the dark yellow
line in the top right and the line in the bottom left crossing some of the electrodes is the
start of the PDMS. From the results in Subsection 2.2.3 it is expected that the polystyrene
beads will always experience negative DEP. The chip was placed under a microscope and
the two contact pads where connected to a voltage source of the type TG2000 20MHz
DDS Function Generator. The source was set to generate a potential of Vpp = 16V at a
frequency of 500 kHz. A sample of beads suspended in a solution of one part PBS and
nine parts Milli-Q was added on top of the chip using a syringe and the potential was
activated. The reaction was observed in the microscope, and the system was observed
multiple times as the potential was turned oﬀand on. It took a couple of attempts to get
the correct amount of liquid on the chip. There should be enough to allow the beads to
freely move within the volume, there shouldn’t however be so much that the meniscus of
the liquid exceeded the top of the PDMS superstructure, as this will give a convex surface
causing refractions.
Even before proper view of the DEP phenomena could be detected it became visible, that
when the liquid dried out the electrodes began to be scorched at the ends if the potential
was still on. Besides that current is clearly running through the system, this also means
that turning on the potential should be avoided when the electrodes are only partially
submerged. When ﬁnally the correct amount and spread of liquid was obtained pictures

Conclusion
The aim of the project was to design, fabricate and test a device using DEP-FFF to
separate Candida A. from PLT based on diﬀerences in density.
To fulﬁll these goals
numerical methods were used to optimize the design by Mandsberg et al. and to calculate
the eﬃciency of the devices. Based on this design a fabrication process were developed
and two chips were fabricated. The chips were tested with beads. In parallel a series of
experiments were performed to determine the density of both Candida A. and PLT.
The experiments performed showed that the densities of Candida A. and PLT to be almost
the same, with to much overlap for a density based separation method to be eﬀective.
The densities were measured as 1080 ± 20 kg/m3 for Candida A. and 1050 ± 30 kg/m3
for PLT. The spread of the data were in a high degree dominated by uncertainties in
the measurements. The experiments performed showed a distinctively diﬀerent picture
compared to the literature, which leaves the question of why. The reasons may be the
assumption that Candida A. is spherical. If it is the case that Candida A. is too non-
spherical to be accurately modeled using the Stokes drag of a sphere, it opens another
problem; what about the Clausius-Mossotti factor. CM used in this project is based on
the assumption of spherical particles. This may lead to unforeseen behaviors. However
based solely on these experiments there are no reason to believe that they should not be
correct.
The plan was to test the chips using two types of beads with diﬀerent densities, and
thereby demonstrate a proof of concept. Problems during the fabrication, resulted in one
chip having its inlet blocked and another having a severed connection. For this reason no
real data could be obtained about the functionality.
To continue this project, the primary task would be to measure the inﬂuence of Candida
A. not being spherical on both the density but also CM. Additionally it would be a good
idea to ﬁnd a better way of sealing the chip, as the double sided taped proved diﬃcult to
work with in a consistent manner. A possibility here would be to look into using polymers
such as Benzocyclobutene (BCB) as bonding material.
