Abstract
The increasing share of renewable energy sources in the modern power grid has height-
ened the need for accurate and reliable models to simulate their dynamic behavior.
Simulations tools are essential for the analysis of electrical system operations, under
steady-state or transient conditions. Between different softwares, PSCAD has emerged
as a leading tool for creating reliable offline models, enabling power system analysis
and performance optimization.
This study presents a comprehensive PSCAD model of a PEM electrolyzer-based power
plant with active front end, that allows to understand and manage the integration of hy-
drogen production into modern power systems. The model involves the design of each
single electrical component, from the power generation to the load.
The thesis focuses mainly on the converter dynamics and control strategies. Two dif-
ferent control techniques are implemented and taken into account, with the converter
specifically operating in grid-following or grid-forming mode. The grid-following control
strategy enables the converter to lock with the grid’s frequency and voltage and to ad-
just its output accordingly. On the other hand, grid-forming converters can establish
their own voltage and frequency, making them very suitable for applications where the
grid is less stable. The model allows to understand their dynamics, assuming therefore
a fundamental importance to provide safe and stable power system operations.
The PEM Electrolyzer stack model is first individually developed and then integrated
into the power system: a mathematical model is introduced to describe the internal
dynamics, developed on Python and implemented in PSCAD. Furthermore, the math-
ematical equations are investigated to test the Electrolyzer stack performance.
To conclude, a steady-state analysis of the electrical system is conducted, to observe
how the converter can provide stable operation when the Electrolyzer stack is inte-
grated in the model. Different simulation cases are performed to test the operations
under different conditions.

Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
The transition to renewable energy sources has become essential for a sustainable
future. As global energy demand continues to rise, due to a daily increase of world
population and growing goods and services needs, innovative energy solution are re-
quired in terms of power supply: among these solutions, hydrogen usage as a green
energy source has attracted many people to investigate in its production. According to
IEA datas[1], the hydrogen production demand reached 95 Mt in 2022, however only
a very small share of it (around 1%) was produced with low-emissions, with most of
this production based on fossil fuels plants with carbon capture utilisation and storage
systems. For this reason the impact on climate mitigation was still very low. A positive
signal has been given in the last years, with an increasing trend in low-emissions hydro-
gen production: still according to IEA, its total production could reach 150 Mt by 2030,
with more than half of it coming from low-emissions process, specifically water electrol-
ysis (50 Mt) and CCUS (30 Mt). To align with NZE scenario, by 2030 the electrolyzer
installed capacity must reach 550 GW, requiring therefore a rapid scale-up.
As fig.1.1 shows, there are many ways to produce hydrogen, with only a part of them us-
ing renewable energy sources. Despite the share of it is still low, the current trend offers
promising scenarios which need to be well investigated for a correct implementation.
Figure 1.1: Hydrogen production methods
This thesis covers the topic of hydrogen production through water electrolysis: this pro-
cess involves the splitting of water molecules into hydrogen and oxygen ions, through
electricity. It is realized within electrolyzers cells, devices that can facilitate the con-
version of electrical energy into chemical energy. Between different technologies, the
alkaline electrolyzers, using a liquid alkaline solution of sodium and potassium as elec-
trolyte, have dominated the market in the last years, thanks to their simplicity and cost-

Fig.1.2b[2] illustrates the operation ranges for the three different electrolyzers tech-
nologies, in terms of operating voltage and current density: a higher current density is
correlated to a higher hydrogen production, while a lower operating voltage improves
the efficiency. SOECs operate through a ceramic electrolyte, allowing them to with-
stand high temperatures and current density: this enables them to reach a very high
efficiency. However, they face significant challenges in terms of degradation and their
market application is still limited, with ongoing research to improve their scalability. On
the other hand PEM electrolyzer, working through a polymer conducting membrane,
have gained a lot of space inside the hydrogen market spectrum[3], for their ability to
work at higher temperature and current densities (compared to alkaline electrolyzers),
better efficiency and process purity: the current global trend, shown in fig. 1.2a, together
with their better performance, has addressed the interest of this study towards this tech-
nology, with the aim to provide a functioning model simulating the involved dynamics.
Within this context, the integration of PEM electrolyzer plants with renewable power
sources, such as wind farms, not only allows a renewable and clean hydrogen pro-
duction, but also provides different services which can help the power system[4]: in
particular, the hydrogen production can be adjusted depending on grid conditions, thus
helping balancing demand and generation. However, as renewable energy sources
become more integrated into the power grid, new challenges arise for the converter’s
operation, in terms of grid stability and security. Traditional grid-following converters
work well in stable conditions: they can provide reliable operations, synchronizing with
existing grid’s voltage and frequency. However, this control technique becomes less
effective when system parameters are less solid, for example in weak or isolated grids.
New control techniques must be therefore developed to make the entire system oper-

ation more reliable. This fact has led to a rising interest in grid-forming control tech-
niques. Unlike grid-following converters, the grid-forming control technique allows the
power electronic device to independently establish its own voltage and frequency. As a
result, the variability of wind and solar energy sources can find support in the services
provided by grid-forming converters.
Under this current scenario, this thesis project wants to give its contribution to the un-
derstanding and implementation of the converter control system techniques, aimed at
a clean hydrogen production. The project will provide a detailed and holistic model of
a PEM electrolyzer power plant, encompassing converter design, system integration,
and control strategies.
1.2
Thesis objectives
The main objective of this thesis is to provide two functioning PSCAD models of an
Electrolyzer power plant, where the converters can ensure safe and stable operation
of the power system. Each model corresponds to a specific converter’s operation. Un-
der this challenge, different secondary objectives must be accomplished and can be
summarized as follows:
• modelling and testing of the main components of the control systems, with a deep
understanding of the dynamics
• Optimization of the dynamics of the control loops
• Identification of the differences between the two control system’s approaches
• Understanding of the mathematical equations to describe the internal dynamics
of a PEM Electrolyzer cell
• Integration of the PEM elctrolyzer stack into the power system
1.3
Thesis Outline
This section summarizes the thesis structure, providing insights of the contents. Chap-
ter 2 introduces the modelling of each single components of the power system. This
chapter follows a ”step-by-step” approach: each component is modeled individually and
tested to check the correct operation. Once this is confirmed, the model can proceed
with the development and integration of the following component. Each section of this
chapter therefore follows the evolution of the model and provides description, analysis
and test validations. Chapter 3 is dedicated to the modelling of the Electrolyzer: a sin-
gle cell model is first developed and then extended to the whole stack. Chapter 4 finally
integrates the Electrolyzer model into the power system, providing simulations analysis
under different scenarios.

2.1
Power source model
The model starts with the grid modelling: it consists in a conventional 3 phase voltage
source, where voltage can be set at the desired level at the terminal. The source is then
connected to a 10 MVA transformer to step down the voltage level. The power source
has been then tested with a simple resistive load in order to be sure that the generated
power corresponds to the desired level.
Figure 2.1: Grid model schematic: 3 phase voltage source, transformer and resistive
load
Fig.2.1 shows the PSCAD schematics of the simple grid: the 3 phase source is grounded
and modeled with an RRL impedance, thus transmitting both active and reactive power
(impedance value can be found in tab.2.1); the load for the testing has been chosen to
be 0.1 Ohm resistive load.

2.2
AC/DC converter model
The following chapter aims to introduce the converter model dynamics. The ability of
converters to interface between alternating and direct current power sources or loads
defines their crucial importance in power systems. For this reason, an accurate mod-
elling and analysis of their operations is vital for guaranteeing the correct behavior of
the entire system, under varying application conditions. In this study case the converter
must send power to a load operating in DC, consisting in the electrolyzer plant, acting
therefore as a rectifier. This chapter will also include the description of the control sys-
tem, whose task is the regulation of the converter dynamics. Two different control tech-
niques will be developed, with the converter operating in grid-following or grid-forming
modes. The first control technique allows the regulation of active and reactive power
output independently, depending on a voltage and frequency reference coming from the
grid; with the second technique, the converter will act as a virtual generator, establishing
its own voltage and frequency, which are synchronized with the grid references. Effec-
tive control strategies are crucial for the stable and efficient operation of the rectifier
converter: a detailed analysis will be therefore reserved to the control system compo-
nents and dynamics.
For this study case, a voltage source converter (VSC) has been selected: it consists
in a power electronic device that thanks to semiconductor switching devices (in this
case IGBTs) can work with really high frequencies to convert voltage from one form to
another. For this reason, it presents many advantages in terms of high efficiency and
flexibility. To regulate the action of the switching devices, a pulse modulated signal is
needed: for this application, a 2-level VSC converter has been modeled for simplicity.
However, it allows to understand the functionality and the importance of the control
system, which plays an essential role. In addition, the simplicity of this topology facili-
tates easier control implementation and reduces complexity in the converter circuitry,
maintaining reliability for this kind of study cases.
Figure 2.8: PSCAD schematics of the 2-level VSC converter

pulse signals from the PWM and the converter directly switching between two voltage

flow towards the emitter when a positive voltage, above a certain voltage threshold, is

2.2.1
Control system: grid-following mode
The model is composed by the power source model, previously explained, the converter
block and finally a resistive load on the DC side to check the operation. The following
fig.2.9 represents the general overview of the model.
Figure 2.9: PSCAD schematics of generation and AC-DC converter
The control system has been modeled to operate in a grid-following mode[5]. This en-
ables the converter to continuously monitors the grid voltage and current through its
sensors. The overall goal of a grid-following rectifier control system is to provide effi-
cient, stable, and high-quality power conversion from AC to DC, with the ability to handle
variations in grid conditions and load demands. The whole control system is based on
the PLL (Phase Locked Loop), which locks onto the grid frequency and phase, providing
a reference for the control system; furthermore the control algorithms are fundamental
for voltage and current regulation: they consist in a voltage control loop, which ensures
that the output DC voltage is maintained at the desired level while synchronizing with
the grid, and a current control loop, providing precise regulation of the input current to
protect the equipment and ensure safe operations. Between the two loops, the current
loop has been modeled to be the inner loop, which means the faster and with higher
bandwidth: this has be done because of its primary function in responding to fast load or
grid condition fluctuations and to protect the switching devices, very sensitive to thermal
stress. This cascade control system structure allows the decoupling of the fast current
dynamics from the slower voltage dynamics, simplifying the tuning of the loops. The
last step consists in the modulation of the signal: a Pulse Width Modulation (PWM)
technique has been adopted, which allows the detection of the control signal from the
current loop, determining the duty cycle and directly controlling the on/off states of the
IGBTs, thus stabilizing the voltage at the desired rated value. Each single component
will be analyze in detail in the following paragraphs.

Park transformation
The first step to design a grid following control system implies
a change of reference frame: the involved transformation is noted as the Park trans-
formation, a mathematical technique used to simplify the analysis and control of three-
phase balanced electrical systems. The signals, voltages and currents, have been con-
verted from an ABC spacial distribution reference frame into a rotating reference frame
(DQ frame): the two axis, d and q (respectively direct and quadrature axis), rotate in
synchronous with the 3-phase system frequency. Therefore, if the signals in the ABC
reference frame were alternating signals, the same signals are now transformed into
DC quantities with the DQ representation. A middle step, called Clarke transformation,
allows the representation of the three phase quantities, in a two vectors stationary ref-
erence frame: this happens due to the fact that, in a 3-phase balanced system, one
of the three vector is redundant and not necessary for the representation. The Park
transformation, from abc to dq, represents the first step to provide independent control
of active and reactive power.
Figure 2.10: Park transformation: from abc to dq reference frame
The change of reference frame assumes an essential importance when related to PI
controller operations, like in this model. A PI controller that works with alternating sig-
nals, in a ABC reference frame, would still generate a steady-stete error, thus reducing
its operations accuracy. This does not happens with constant DC quantities: in this case

Phase Locked Loop
In a grid-following rectifier, the PLL is essential for synchro-
nizing the converter’s output voltage with the grid voltage or frequency. The PLL is a
closed loop control system that synchronizes its output with its input by means of a
feedback loop: it compares the phase and frequency of the grid reference voltage, with
the output voltage of the converter. After that, it generates an error signal proportional
to any phase difference. This error signal is then used to adjust the control inputs of the
converter, ensuring that its output remains in phase with the grid. Keeping the input and
output phases in lockstep also implies keeping input and output frequency the same:
consequently it can also track the input frequency.
Figure 2.13: PLL: main components
The PLL is composed by three main components, as shown in fig.2.13:
• A phase detector or comparator: it detects the phase difference comparing input
and output phases and generates an error proportional to it.

• A controller: usually based on a PI controller which determines the loop dynamics
and acts like a filter for high frequency AC components, leaving only DC o low
frequency components.
• An oscillator: it generates a periodic output signal which has the same frequency
of the input.
Fig.2.14 represents the internal schematic of the PSCAD component, which acts as a

proportional and integral action: by tuning the PI gains it is possible to take the steady
state error to zero in a certain time and with a certain accuracy. The output of the con-
troller is the angular speed, proportional to the frequency, which gives the phase angle,
after being integrated in the oscillator. The phase angle goes back to the comparator
through a feedback loop.

Pulse Width Modulation
The PWM is a technique where a continuous control signal
is converted into a digital signal that drives the switching devices. The generated signal
has a fixed frequency and variable duty cycle. The continuous control signal comes from
the current control loop after the modulation and is needed to modulate a high frequency
carrier wave, when compared to it. The pulse width modulated signal drives then the
gates of the switching devices, according to the duty cycle: when the signal is high,
the IGBT will be ”on-state”, when is low it will be ”off-state”. PSCAD library is already
provided with components that are used for the PWM. Six different modulators are
needed in total because of the six IGBTs, and 2 per phase. Each modulator generates
2 complementary PWM signals, determining on and off state. These signals are shown

2.2.3
Control system: grid forming mode
In this section the AC-DC converter has been modeled with the grid-forming control
technique[6]. The main characteristic of the GFM converter is to maintain the system
stability, establishing its own voltage and frequency: therefore the converter can be
seen as a controllable voltage source, that can adjust autonomously. Between different
techniques, this model will follow the Virtual Synchronous Generator strategy[7][8], with
the converter emulating the dynamics of the rotor of a synchronous generator: by doing
this, the converter can synchronize with the grid, without requiring a PLL, but only by
means of its internal dynamics and control loops. This fact can help the grid providing
more support in terms of voltage and frequency response, making it very suitable in
contexts like weak-grids or renewable power sources, more affected by system variable
conditions. The following fig.2.29 illustrates the external model taken into analysis.
Figure 2.29: Grid-following PSCAD model
The model follows the same grid structure of the GFL: however a breaker between
the source and the converter is needed to establish operation conditions, such as grid-
connected mode or islanded mode. The breaker furthermore defines the point of con-
nection, where the involved quantities will be measured. In addition to this, an RLC filter
is used, where the filter capacitor is used to detect the voltage at the point of connection
for grid alignment in the loops. On the load side, (DC link) , a constant voltage source
is used to energize the system, with the voltage set at 1.5 kV. The converter structure
is the same of the 2 level VSC previously explained and therefore not reported.

DC/DC Buck converter
A DC-DC Buck converter is needed to control the voltage using a combination of pas-
sive element together with a switching device[9][10]: the passive elements operate as
energy storage components to convert higher input voltage to a lower output voltage
whereas the switching element is needed to regulate the current flow by alternating ”on”
and ”off” status. These kinds of converter present usually very high efficiency (around
90%) and allow to control the output voltage by managing the duty cycle of the switch,
i.e. the ratio of the on-state time over the total switching period.

Conclusion
5.1
Final conclusions
The understanding of the dynamics of the converters is of fundamental importance, in
order to provide a safe and stable power supply for a variable load demand. Ensuring
a stable and correct operation in the converters, is directly connected to losses mini-
mization. The GFL converter can perfectly synchronize to the grid frequency, thanks to
the PLL, and align with the grid voltage. The correct modelling and tuning of the inter-
nal loops assures to provide the right reference to the switching devices: thanks to this
fact, the duty cycle can be correctly set and the IGBT’s alternating status can produce
the exact DC link voltage. The buck converter ensures that the exact load demand is
matched, in terms of output voltage and current: the tuning of the control loops, specif-
ically of the PIs, is of crucial importance to adapt the response to the demand.
Although the GFM control system can assure a stable operation, thus achieving this
thesis objectives, the safe operation of the system is not totally reached. A general
lack of accuracy of the GFM converter’s performance affects the simulation results and
could be risky, especially for high disturbances or power levels. Understanding there-
fore the causes of this inaccuracy is of crucial important for future work development.
The dynamics inside an electrolyzer stack play a main role in the correct sizing and
performance improvement: once those are understood, the control systems can be set
to adjust to desired demand, for different application purposes; depending on the appli-
cation it may be needed to have an higher production, at the cost of more losses, or a
better performance. The V-I characteristic incorporates all the stack’s internal dynamics
and therefore representing all the possible applications, with their advantages or disad-
vantages. The stack performance can be adjusted based on that. The model provides
a good insight of the behavior of PEM cells and the stack model is well integrated into
the power system.
5.2
Future Work
The developed model provides insights of the dynamics of an electrolyzer power plant,
for hydrogen production. The model aims to provide a general and simple structure of
the control systems, whose task is the regulation of the system operation. The model
therefore leaves space for future work implementation, concerning all the main model’s
components. It can be summarized as follows:
• Power source: substitute the conventional power source with a offshore wind
farm model, to test the control systems operations when a renewable power
source is integrated in the system. This can provides more insights about the
differences between GFL and GFM operations, specifically better clarifying the
need of GFM strategies in modern power systems.
