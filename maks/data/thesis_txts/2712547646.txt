PREFACE
This thesis describes the design, implementation, and testing of a telemetry downlink for
sounding rockets. The work was performed between the 15th of December 2021 and the
5th of June 2022, under the supervision of Sten Schmidl Søbjærg. The system was built
for DanSTAR (Danish Student Association for Rocketry), and is to be integrated in their
next rocket named Fornax which will launch in October 2022.

ABSTRACT
In this report, the design and implementation of a telemetry downlink for 9km sounding
rockets is detailed. From a specification of 36.8kBit/s bit rate and a maximum 46byte
package error rate of 5%, a system design was created, consisting of a transmitting board
with a power amplifier, and a receiving board with a low noise amplifier and two cascaded
gain block amplifiers. After choosing very small omni-directional patch antennas with a
low gain, the theoretical signal to noise ratio was deemed too low for ASK, FSK, and PSK
modulation types. To enable the telemetry downlink to function at low signal to noise
ratios, LoRa modulation was chosen. A link budget was created, assuming a maximum
of 10dB loss in the receiver and adding a 20dB link margin to compensate for unforeseen
losses.
Testing the system revealed losses on the transmitting board which could be compensated
for with the adjustable output power from the transceiver. Likewise, a large amount of
loss was discovered on the receiving board, which was covered by the initial loss estimate
in the link budget.
Range testing was performed over long range close to ground, which adds a large amount
of additional path loss. These tests revealed that communication at 4.5km at a data rate of
7.14kBit/s was very robust, although higher bit rates were not tested. However, moving up
to an 11km range resulted in little to no communication. After these tests were performed,
multiple issues were discovered, which when fixed should produce a much better result.
More range testing is therefore required, and will be performed.

INTRODUCTION
DanSTAR (Danish Student Association for Rocketry) is student team based at Denmark’s
Technical University, which builds and launches bi-liquid rockets.
These rockets are
completely student researched and developed, and are launched from a military area in
Ponte de Sor, Portugal. This is done at the annual competition EuRoC (European Rocketry
Challenge) in October.
The rocket is designed to hit a target apogee of 9km, after which it will first deploy a drogue
parachute, and then a main parachute at 500m. After landing, the rocket must be recovered.
The rocket can take a number of different flight paths depending on perturbations in wind
and slight changes in trajectory. Since the rocket is 200mm in diameter and 4.5m tall,
visible sight of it is lost a few seconds after it is launched, and it’s whereabouts become
entirely unknown through visual confirmation.
In order to get data from the rocket after it launches, it is necessary to have a wireless
telemetry downlink from the rocket, and a station with a receiver antenna on ground. The
data which comes back from the rocket contains GPS coordinates, altitude measurements,
acceleration measurements, gyroscopic measurements, and parachute statuses, and other
valuable data. The data which comes back from the rocket is crucial for the recovery of the
rocket, which may take place in tall grass or forests multiple kilometres from the original
launch.
In case of a failure in the parachute system, which leads to the destruction of the rocket, it
is possible that the flight data which is stored on the rocket is also destroyed. In such an
event, the only recorded data from the flight is that which is sent via a telemetry downlink.
Therefore, it is vital that the telemetry downlink not only has a strong connection, but
also has a high data rate. Due to weight and space limitation, transmitting antennas on
the rocket are very limited in their size. Additionally, their maximum transmission power
is limited by EU laws.
The downlink in this project will consist of an RF transmitter board which sits in the
rocket’s flight computer, a transmitting antenna which is mounted in the rocket, a receiving
antenna which is mounted on a rig on ground, and an RF receiver board which processes
the data and passes it onto Mission Control. A diagram of the operation can be seen in
figure 1.1.
This requires the design of a link budget, and the systems design of the entire downlink.
While many of the subsystems can be optimized in many ways, the main focus of the
project is the systems design and implementation, as well as verification of the prototyped
system.

THEORY
2.1
Link budget
A link budget is a diagram or table which summarizes and adds up all of the designer’s gains
and attenuations, granting an overview over the whole transmission path from transmitter
to receiver. Details and assumption can be added, to give transparency about assumptions,
dynamic ranges, noise figure requirements, etc. A link budget should include a sufficient
link margin, meaning an added margin of gain which compensates for additional unforeseen
losses.
Gain-wise, typical contents of a link budget includes:
• Transmitted power
• Received power
• Antenna gains
• Free space path losses
• Atmospheric losses
• Transmission line losses
• Insertion losses of components
• Amplifier gains
Similarly, link budgets may also include:
• Dynamic power ranges
• Noise figures of components
• Bandwidths of filters or receivers
2.2
Transmission lines on PCBs
When designing RF circuitry on PCBs, it is of great importance that the properties and
geometry of traces (wires) are taken into account. If trace properties are ignored and left up
to circumstance, a multitude of unwanted effects may occur, including signal attenuation,
unwanted reflections, little or no signal on outputs, or permanent damage to components.
2.2.1
PCB stackup and properties
A printed circuit board (PCB) consists of one or multiple layers of conductive material
and dielectric. Most commonly, the conductors tend to be copper, while the dielectric
typically consists of woven fiberglass-epoxy laminate. The dielectric is usually split into
two types: cores, and prepregs. Cores consist of fully cured fiberglass-epoxy laminate, and
is put in between copper layers in order to separate two conductive layers, and to provide

uncured fiberglass-epoxy laminate, and is used for binding copper layers together, as well

The dimensions are taken from PCBWay.com [3], and represent the physical dimensions

2.2.2
Implementation of transmission lines on PCBs
When considering the stackup shown in figure 2.1, it is noticed that the transmission lines
will consist of the signals on the outer layers, and the power/ground layers on the inner
layers, which have a very short distance compared to the total thickness of the PCB. While
there are available equations in books and papers, the PCB designing software KiCAD has
a built in transmission line calculator, which can be utilized to compare different types of
PCB trace transmission lines (microstrip, stripline, coplanar waveguide, etc.).
Imperfections in transmission lines on PCBs
It is to be expected, that a transmission line on a PCB will have imperfections. These
imperfections can, amongst others, come from:
• The manufacturer’s tolerances in the layer heights of the PCB’s stackup
• The manufacturer’s tolerances in the dielectric constants of the prepreg
• The dimensions and placements of component by the designer
• Cross-talk between different transmission lines
• Inaccuracies in theoretical equations or transmission line calculators
While cross-talk can largely be mitigated by sufficiently spacing transmission lines, the use
of passive components on a PCB for matching, power delivery, etc., will inherently lead to
imperfections in a transmission line. Consider the PCB layout in figure 2.2, consisting of a
microstrip line with some arbitrary matching circuitry. Since the pads of the components
are wider than the trace itself, the characteristic impedance changes in sections where the
transmission line is now wider. Likewise, if multiple components are shunts to ground, the
trace may behave more like a coplanar waveguide than a microstrip line in these areas,
due to the grounded copper being in close proximity.

2.3
Propagation losses
The majority of the signal loss over long distances occurs in the transmission path between
the rocket and ground antennas. This is mainly due to the transmitted power being spread
over a big area at longer distances. A minor contribution is the absorption by atmospheric
gases (oxygen and water vapour). At long range transmissions close to ground, ground
reflections can have a large effect, depending on the height which antennas are placed at.

2.3.3
Attenuation by atmospheric gases
Atmospheric gasses such as oxygen and water attenuate electromagnetic waves, depending
on their frequency. Generally, high frequencies are attenuated more with some additional
peaks where frequencies are being heavily attenuated due to resonance with water and
oxygen (at 22GHz and 60GHz respectively). The ITU (International Telecommunication
Union) recommendation ITU-R P.676-9 [7] can be used to plot and estimate the atmospheric
losses for frequencies between DC and 54GHz , as shown in figure 2.3 (although only
plotted up to 50GHz here). The average weather conditions in Ponte de Sor during October
have been used for plotting.

2.4
RF amplifiers
There are many different types of RF amplifiers, and their uses can vary greatly. Depending
on the manufacturer and their datasheet, there may be an abundance of data on a specific
amplifier, or as little as a simple recommended implementation accompanied by a table of
specifications.
2.4.1
Noise and noise figure
Noise appears in all electronics, in different forms (thermal, flicker, shot, burst, etc.). This
noise is a mixture of man-made and natural background noise and appears in different
intensities depending on the position, orientation, and shielding of the electronics. While
all components in the electrical system will introduce additional noise in the link budget,
the most critical noise is introduced on the receiving antenna. This is elaborated on in
section 2.6.4. When an estimate of the noise has been made, an RF designer is usually
mostly concerned about noise figure, and thereby the signal-to-noise-ratio (SNR).
In a perfect circuit SNR = ∞; the noise power is zero. In reality, noise is unavoidable,
and thus always present in an electrical system. In a perfect component (such as a filter,
amplifier, mixer, etc.), the SNRin = SNRout; the signal and noise are attenuated and
amplified equally. In reality, all components are noisy by nature, and thus add more noise
to a signal, meaning SNRin < SNRout.

means mismatching it gain-wise, thus resulting in a lower gain than what is possible.
2.4.2
Compression
The output power of an RF amplifier is usually limited by the input DC current, which is
used to bias the amplifier. In most amplifiers, the output will eventually go into saturation
when the output voltage swing gets too close to the positive or negative supply voltage.
Before going completely into saturation, a small region is defined where the otherwise
linear output power curve rolls off, before going into saturation - this is referred to as
"compression".
By convention, datasheets will list the "1dB compression point" of an RF amplifier, which
corresponds to the point where the output power of an amplifier is 1dB below the ideal
linear output power curve.
2.4.3
Relevant RF amplifiers
In this theory section, three different and relevant RF amplifiers will be described.
Power amplifiers (PA)
Power amplifiers are usually implemented right before the transmitting antenna in a
wireless system. Characteristically, they have a high 1dB compression point. The noise
8
==PAGE:17==
2. Theory
figure tends to be quite high, but this is rarely an issue since the input often comes directly
from the transmitter, rendering the signal to noise ratio insignificant compared to the
noise in the rest of the link budget. Power amplifiers are usually matched to deliver the
maximum output power possible. Disconnecting an antenna can damage a power amplifier
if it is turned on, as the potentially large reflections can harm the output of the internal
transistors. Thus, a PA should always be terminated with a load to avoid damage to it’s
output stage.
Low noise amplifiers (LNA)
The purpose of the LNA is to amplify as much of the desired signal as possible, whilst
worsening the SNR as little as possible. Thus, a good LNA has a large gain and a low
noise figure. Equation 2.7 shows, that the LNA serves it’s purpose best when placed at the
very beginning of a cascaded amplifier section, since it’s high gain will diminish the noise
contribution from all other amplifiers, while introducing the smallest possible amount of
noise for all other gain stages to amplify.
Gain block amplifier
Gain block amplifiers are usually cheap, with mediocre performance in all aspects. They
are often used for a straight forward gain, where noise and output power is not of great
importance. Many gain block amplifiers are internally matched on the input and output,
such that they are easy to cascade for larger gains.
2.5
Signal modulation and bit error rate
When choosing a modulation type, the three major trade offs are between bit rate, bit
error rate, and complexity. Unless the system is built at integrated chip-level, the issue of
complexity is largely mitigated by using integrated circuits which perform modulation and
demodulation for the designer. Thus, choosing a modulation type is largely based upon
bit rate and bit error rate, or rather a trade off between communication speed and range.
Modulation types such as FSK (frequency shift keying), ASK (amplitude shift keying),
and PSK (phase shift keying) are well known and thoroughly researched.
2.5.1
LoRa modulation
At the time of writing, commercially available LoRa modulation is less than a decade old,
with the first integrated circuits from Semtech coming to the market in June 2013. This
makes LoRa a very new and not as thoroughly researched type of modulation.
LoRa is based on chirp spread spectrum modulation. More precisely, bits are encoded
by performing a frequency chirp, and shifting the frequency at a precise moment during
the chirp, the timing of the shift corresponding to some value which can be demodulated
into a certain number of bits. This frequency shift in a chirp is essentially compared to
every possible timing of frequency shift using the correlation between the input signal
and a reference, before choosing the outcome with the highest correlation. Due to the
demodulation being based on a correlation function, LoRa is capable of demodulate signals

a number of bits, a LoRa symbol is a chirp with a frequency shift.

amount of bits that are encoded onto a chirp. For example, a spread factor of 8 means

symbol. The bandwidth determines the bandwidth at which the chirp sweeps across, but

2.6.2
Polarization and polarization loss factor
In general, electromagnetic waves can take on three kinds of polarizations; linear, circular,
and elliptical. The type of polarization, when transmitting over a distance, depends on the
type of antenna which radiates the electromagnetic waves. Linearly polarized antennas can
be subdivided into vertical or horizontal polarization, as well as a combination of the two
where and angle is attributed to the polarization. Circularly polarized antennas, which are
a special case of the elliptical polarization, are divided into right-hand circular polarized
(RHCP) and left-hand circular polarized (LHCP) antennas.
Linearly polarized antennas can encounter problems when it comes to angling; if a horizontal
linearly polarized antenna is transmitting to a vertical linearly polarized antenna, the
receiving antenna will theoretically receive 0% of the power. This is referred to as a
polarization mismatch, which is characterized using a polarization loss factor (PLF), which
spans between 1 and 0.
This mismatch can also occur if the two antennas are of different polarizations types. For
instance, a circular-linear mismatch results in a PLF=0.5[10].
Similarly, a RCHP-LCHP mismatch results in a PLF<1. To this extent, it is worth
considering that an RHCP wave will become a LHCP wave upon reflection, and vice versa.
This makes circularly polarized antennas more robust to ground reflections, since they
naturally have a mismatch with waves that are reflected an uneven number of times.

2.6.3
EU laws on transmitting power
The Danish government has published a document labelled "Bekendtgørelse om
radiogrænseflader" [11], which contains the deeds regarding radio interfaces.
In this
document, radio transmissions are grouped into 34 unique radio categories, with
specifications about frequency bands, maximum power, duty cycles, and other more
specific notes and rules.
These laws are based on the laws passed by the European
Parliament, to harmonize the laws and regulations across borders, and should therefore be
applicable for Portugal as well.
Of these different categories, the scope of this project falls within appendix 17 on radio
interface 00 032, "Low power radio system with integrated or dedicated antenna intended
for telemetry, remote control purposes, alarming, voice and data transmission, etc.".
This appendix provides the necessary information for choosing a frequency, which should
have no restrictions on duty cycle, and a high maximum power. Since the majority of
bands require duty cycles of 10%, 1%, or even 0.1%, these bands will not be able to support
big data transfers. Many bands however, have an attached note which states "Note 6)
or <xx% duty cycle". This note refers to the directive 2014/53/EU from the European
Parliament, which states that [12]:
"[...] in the case of a transmitter, when the transmitter is properly installed,
maintained and used for its intended purpose it generates radio waves emissions
that do not create harmful interference, while unwanted radio waves emissions
generated by the transmitter (e.g. in adjacent channels) with a potential negative
impact on the goals of radio spectrum policy should be limited to such a level
that, according to the state of the art, harmful interference is avoided [...]"
The quote above can essentially be interpreted to mean that duty cycles up to 100% may
be used, as long as it is guaranteed that the signal does not cause any harmful interference
with other devices.

2.7
Rocket overview
Fornax is the third bi-liquid rocket being built by DanSTAR, and subsequently the third
bi-liquid rocket built by a European student team. It measures 4.53m in height, 200mm
in diameter, uses isopropyl alcohol as fuel and nitrous-oxide as oxidizer, thus making it a
bi-liquid rocket. The electrical system of the rocket consists of a student researched and
developed (SRAD) flight computer, which controls a number of peripheral boards. The
transmitting telemetry board is a part of this flight computer.
While on the launch pad, the rocket communicates with Mission Control, which is a
station containing information screens for monitoring and controlling the rocket. This
communication is done through a 750m long CAT5e cable, which is attached to the rocket.
Mission Control also interfaces with the rocket through the telemetry link which will
be designed in this thesis. This communication link is kept from the time the rocket is
switched on, and until it lands.
The rocket deploys a two-stage parachute system, in order to come down safely and intact.
The shell of the rocket consists of carbon fibre panels which are conductive, and thus do
not allowing radio transmissions through them. However, a 70cm section in the upper part
of the rocket surrounding the flight computer is made of glass fibre, which acts as an "RF
window", allowing radio transmissions to pass in this section only.

IMPLEMENTATION
In this chapter, the design choices and their implementation will be described. It is worth
noting, that choosing frequencies, antennas, and modulation types, was not done in a
linear fashion, since they all influence each other. In order to fulfil the specification for
bit-rate, the frequency is an important factor. The frequency largely determines which
antennas one should choose, which in turn gives an SNR. This SNR should be considered
when choosing a modulation type, since it affects the bit-error rate. After the modulation
type has been determined, it may turn out that the frequency must be tweaked, and thus
a new design cycle commences.
Once these parameters have been decided, the design can begin. A transmitting and
receiving PCB will have to be designed, and tested. For PCB design, the CAD software
KiCAD 6.0 is used. This is also where the schematics for the transmitting and receiving
antenna will be designed.
3.1
Specifications
Before choosing between modulation types, frequency bands, antennas, and amplifiers, it
is necessary to understand the initial requirements and constraints of the downlink.
3.1.1
Bit rate
The receiving telemetry board will have to interface with Mission Control, a system
used by DanSTAR to control the rocket before launch, and receive data during launch.
Mission Control of DanSTAR requests the following of the packages which it receives from
telemetry:
• A package should contain 32 bytes of data
• A 12 byte preamble should be implemented
• A 2 byte CRC should be implemented
• 100 packages should be sent every second
• A maximum package error rate of 5% is accepted
From this, we can calculate a bit rate, based on a 46 byte package size:
Bitrate = package size × 8bits × rate = 46bytes × 8bits × 100/s = 36.8kBit/s
(3.1)
3.1.2
Mechanical and electrical constraints
As the transmitting PCB sits as a module in the flight computer, the dimensions of it are
fully defined. The telemetry board sits at the very top of the flight computer, with it’s

3. Implementation
neighbouring board spaced roughly 10mm from it, and with only 26mm of open edge facing
outwards. Since the standard RF signal connector in the rocket is the relatively large SMA
type, the antenna connectors have to be straight through-hole components, connected
somewhere in the middle of the board. Overall, the board has a lot of space constraints.
Due to these space constraints, the schematic of the transmitting board cannot contain too
many stages and components, and circuits involving electrical lengths should be avoided.
The receiving board however, does not have any space constraints, as it is located on
ground and does not have to adhere to any templates, although there is merit in keeping
the same electrical schematic template as in the flight computer. Thus, multiple stages
and components can be added to the receiving board.
On both the transmitting and receiving board, power consumption is not an issue. Multiple
large and powerful LiPo batteries are placed in the rocket, since it needs to run servo
motors for valve actuations and parachute deployments - the power consumed by the
transmitting board will hardly be comparable to those powers requirements. On ground,
230V power is available at Mission Control, meaning the receiver board can run on as
much power as necessary.
3.2
Choosing frequency
When choosing a frequency, the following considerations should be kept in mind:
• The electrical length of the frequency in copper should be relatively long, to reduce
the impact of stubs and imperfect transmission lines
• The frequency band should allow for legal high-power radiation
• The frequency should be supported by a large amount of available and well-
documented components (transceivers, amplifiers, etc.)
• The frequency should allow for a high bit rate with a decent margin
• The frequency band used should not be legally limited by a duty cycle, which would
lower the bit-rate substantially
• The frequency band should be broad enough to allow the use of multiple channels

3.3
Choosing antennas
Two sets of antennas must be chosen: one for transmitting and one for receiving.
3.3.1
Antenna specifications
The transmitting antenna must fit a number of criteria:
• The size of the antenna should be small and practical
• The antenna should not take up space which can be used for other flight hardware
• The antenna should radiate in all directions outwards of the rocket
• Polarization mismatch should be minimized
3.3.2
Antenna topologies
The first natural choice, is a simple dipole, sitting in the centre of the rocket. The dipole
radiates in an omni-directional toroidal shape around itself, and would thus radiate strongly
outwards of the rocket, and very limited into the rocket itself where the carbon fibre panels
would simply reflect and scatter the signal. Various antennas with similar radiation patterns
can also be considered, particularly ones with main lobes which can point downwards.
However, these types of antenna configurations require the antenna to be in the centre of
the rocket, and would thus take up a large section of space in the rocket, where mechanical
and electrical flight hardware and wiring should be.
To avoid colliding with the flight hardware, the antenna should preferably be placed as
close as possible to the surface of the panels, radiating outwards. This however, brings
another problem, in that the antenna would either have to be custom made and have the
structure of a hollow cylinder (possibly acting as the new panels), or be split into multiple
smaller antennas.
Making such a custom made antenna is beyond the scope of this project, but would be an
interesting future bachelor/master thesis. As such, the concept of having three separate

==PAGE:28==

antennas placed 120◦apart in the circumference of the rocket was chosen. Additionally,
this will theoretically increase the maximum allowed radiated power three-fold, since the
antennas are now separate entities. Technically, this scaling can continue by adding more
transmitters, but this will not be investigate in this thesis.
This three-antenna topology brings up an important interference consideration, since the
antennas will be transmitting the same data. There are two simple solutions to this
problem:
1. Using a Wilkinson power divider to transmit the same signal on all three antennas
2. Using three separate transmitting circuits on different frequency channels, one for
each antenna
While the Wilkinson power divider is an elegant solution, it requires a lot of space for
electrical lengths, whilst simultaneously introducing the worry of phase delays on the three
antennas. Additionally, since the power is literally divided, the transmitter would have to
supply three times the power (or 10 log(3) = 4.8dB in order to transmit at the maximum
allowed power. On the other hand, Wilkinson power divider is very cheap, since it mainly
consists of copper traces and standard resistors.
In contrast, using three separate transmitters is on the more expensive side. Like the
Wilkinson power divider, three separate transmitters also take up a lot of space. However,
the worry of phase delays can be mitigated by choosing three different frequency channels,
which in turn requires three antennas and receiver modules on the ground. The power
requirements are lowered for a hypothetical transmitter design, since each transmitter only
has to deliver power to a single antenna.
The solution of three separate transmitters was chosen, based on the considerations stated
above. Unlike the case of a dipole centred in the middle of the rocket, the antennas mounted
close to the panels should only radiate outwards. This, along with the requirement of
omni-directionality, makes patch antennas a natural choice. In order to avoid polarization
mismatches, a circularly polarized antenna is desired.

TESTS AND RESULTS
Each of the subsystems must be tested to verify that the system works as expected.
Performing a full scale test is not possible, since it would require the launch of a rocket. It
is also difficult to perform tests which mimic the launch of a rocket, since tests close to
ground require very long lines of sight, and has much more noise and reflections.
The most difficult element to mimic during a test is the signal to noise ratio, since it is
very hard to control the noise. Placing the transmitter on tall buildings makes the receiver
see the noise generated and reflected by the building, instead of the pure sky noise behind
it. Using drones and weather balloons in 100m height makes angling very difficult and
unpredictable.
Introducing attenuators on the receiving side to mimic additional path loss introduces large
amounts of noise, due to the loss temperature contribution in equation 2.21. Meanwhile,
introducing attenuators between the transmitting board and the transmitting antenna
will cause the wire traces on the transmitting board to act as more powerful antennas
than the antenna itself, letting the signal "bypass" the antenna with an unknown radiation
pattern and power. Likewise, simulating a path loss without noise by using a 10m long
cable with attenuators does not work, since the transmitting board on it’s lowest power
setting radiates powerfully enough for the very sensitive receiver board to still receive a
perfect signal, even if the cable is completely removed.
For long range testing, only a single transmitting and receiving channel is tested, for
simplicity. This is both because only three antennas were available at the time, and
because it is assumed that if one channel works, all three channels should be able to work.
The distance between each of the three circuits on the transmitting and receiving antennas
should be sufficient enough to avoid significant cross-talk, and the frequency channels can
be spaced far apart on the frequency spectrum.

Antenna testing
It is vital to test the antennas in an environment which is as close to the real case scenario,
with regards to radiation pattern. If the structure of the rocket, or the mounting and

in the link budget may not suffice.
4.2.1
Antenna radiation pattern test
Test goal
The goal of the antenna test is to find a radiation pattern, and determine whether or not
there are significant dead zones in the radiation pattern. The peak of the magnitude of the
signal bears no importance, as it can be assumed to be roughly equal to the specification
given in their datasheet. What is important, is to find the difference in directivity between
the peaks and the valleys in the total radiation pattern.
This is accomplished by measuring the radiation pattern from all antenna positions, and
finding the differences in amplitude between the peak intensities and the sections where
two lobes cross, thus giving a dynamic range of the total directivity of all three antennas.

5
DISCUSSION
In this chapter, the implementation and results of the telemetry downlink are discussed.
Lastly, future work will also be introduced.
5.1
Implementation and results
A link budget was first designed, and then implemented step by step. Each step of the
implementation should be compared to the yielded results.
5.1.1
Antenna design and performance
The transmitting and receiving antennas were chosen based on their form factor, which is
important when implementing them in the rocket. It was decided to use three antennas,
spaced 120◦degrees apart, angled 22.5◦degrees downwards. This angle was chosen on a
compromise with the mechanical team of DanSTAR, since it requires more space inside of
the rocket. The antenna configuration was tested using a representative mock-up of the
rocket in an anechoic chamber, and the radiation pattern was deemed acceptable. The
radial difference in gain was roughly 6.5dB, which while close to the expected value of 5dB
is not very good for the link budget.
The return loss was also determined for the antennas, and the frequency area 2460 MHz to
2470 MHz was deemed the most efficient for the antennas. A large difference in behaviour
between the antennas was observed, and has yet to be explained. There is a chance that
some of the antennas may have been damaged, and this should be investigated before
performing additional range tests.
5.1.2
Transceiver design and performance
The SX1281 was chosen as a transceiver, and used throughout the project. The PI filter
section which was recommended in the datasheet turned out to carry over 10dBs of loss
on the transmitting side, and was thus replaced by a 0R resistor and a 100pF DC-blocking
capacitor. Besides this, the transceivers worked well, and were easy to reconfigure and test
with.
5.1.3
Transmitting board design and performance
The SKY65162-70LF was chosen as the power amplifier, and was designed for a 13dB
gain. The resulting power gain was roughly 9.5dB, showing a 3.5dB loss. This loss is most
likely due to losses in transmission lines and slight mismatching on the input or output.
54
==PAGE:63==
5. Discussion
However, the loss of gain could be compensated for by increasing the output power from
the SX1281. Thus, the goal of outputting 13dBm of output power was achieved.
The power spectrum of the transmitting antennas was graphed, and an issue with
overheating at the highest output powers was discovered. The overheating caused the
power amplifier to output harmonics or intermodulated signals, which would occur multiple
minutes after switching the board on.
It was also observed, that the output power dropped by 6dB when using frequencies below
2415 MHz. This could be related to the overheating issues, but was not investigated
further.
A solution to the overheating issue is to use better heat-sinking on the power amplifiers.
This can be done by using a sufficient amount of vias beneath the thermal pads of the
power amplifiers, and placing a large copper zone on the other side of the board where the
heat can be dissipated.
5.1.4
Receiving board design and performance
The TQP3M9037 was chosen as the low noise amplifier, and the TRF37A73 gain block
amplifier was cascaded in two sections. Testing the receiver’s gain proved difficult, since it
required splicing into the circuit with a coaxial cable, introducing additional loss whilst
desoldering certain components which left microstrip line stubs. The gain was found to
decline drastically as a function of frequency, showing around 43dBs of gain at 2400 MHz,
and 34.5dBs of gain at 2500MHz. This means that the receiver gain is 6.5dB below the
desired 41dB gain at 2500 MHz. The loss in the transmission lines, microstrip line stubs,
and the spliced cable should be factored into this measurement, but the modelling of it
quickly becomes complex.
5.1.5
Range testing
Range testing revealed that it was indeed possible to get decent communication speeds over
a long distance. However, the background noise and additional path loss from long distance
propagation over ground significantly decreased the signal, and barely any communication
was possible at an 11km range. However, there were a number of issues which could be
improved on. If investigated properly, these improvements could potentially lead to a much
stronger connection, and thus a much higher bit rate. At present, a bit rate of 7.14kb/s
was achieved over ground at a 4.5km range, albeit without testing other bit rates.
Observing equation 2.5 for path loss for long range propagation over planar ground, it is
clear that adding extra height to the tripods would help immensely with testing.
5.2
Future work
A number of issues and ideas for future work should be discussed, regarding the telemetry
downlink.

5. Discussion
5.2.1
Sub-circuit evaluation boards
In the future, it would be beneficial to make a sub-circuit test PCB, where power amplifiers,
low noise amplifiers, and gain block amplifiers could be tested individually with proper
SMA connectors. Measuring the receiver board turned out to be very difficult due to the
lack of easy test points. It would make it much easier to identify issues in the sub-circuit,
when testing with RF equipment.
5.2.2
Smaller frequency band
While the SX1281 is capable of using a 100 MHz frequency range, it is not necessary to do
so in practice. As seen in the test of the receiver’s gain, there is a big difference in gains
and optimal performances in different parts of the system. Thus, it may be beneficial to
design the circuits for a smaller frequency band, in order to make optimization easier.
5.2.3
Wilkinson Power Divider
It may be worth revisiting the idea of using a Wilkinson Power Divider. It would cut the
cost of components down, reduce the amount of soldering required, and allow the system
to run on a single frequency channel. An investigation of microstrip line compared to
coplanar waveguides should be performed, since the trace topology changes the length
of a quarter-wave, and thus saves space on the board. If the Wilkinson power divider is
implemented, a new and more powerful power amplifier should be chosen, possibly along
with a boost converter for higher supply voltages.
The complexity and size of the design of traditional power splitters increases as the number
of power outputs increases due to the need of wiring in another dimension, which is
undesirable when working with microstrip lines. Here, inspiration can be taken from the
paper published by Andreiv Grebennikov [19], where two three-way power dividers using
only microstrip lines and resistors are described. Alternatively, the three-way power divider
can be made using a traditional unequal two-way power divider in combination with an
equal two-way power divider.
The idea of a power divider could also be implemented on the receiver side. Since the
entire receiver gain stage is already designed for a wide frequency band (100MHz), a
single antenna with a single gain stage (LNA and gain block amplifiers) could amplify the
signal, and feed it to a three-way power divider. This power divider would feed the three
transceivers equally, which would choose the frequency they are programmed to observe.
With a power divider, the receiving station could consist of a single antenna, and a single
gain stage (LNA and gain block amplifiers), followed by a power divider, at the cost of each
transceiver seeing three times as little signal corresponding to a minimum 1/3 = −4.77dB
loss at the end of the gain stage. Additionally, for a power divider on the receiving side,
the phase difference of the outputs of the power divider are not important. This would
significantly lower the cost of components.

==PAGE:65==
5. Discussion
5.2.4
Changing receiving antennas
Due to the transmitting antennas being decided first, the receiving antennas were mostly
chosen due to their availability. Since the biggest problems theoretically occur in the
receiving antenna, more options should be explored. Increasing the gain and lowering the
noise would make a big difference in the link budget, and should thus be investigated.
Polarization
Although it makes a lot of sense to use RHCP antennas on the receiving side, there may be
antennas with more practical directivities and gain. Switching from RHCP to linear on the
receiving side will inevitably result in a 3dB loss and more issues with ground reflections.
Directivity and antenna topology
Choosing antennas with a higher directivity introduces additional risk of misaligning the
antennas. Besides the benefit of additional gain, narrowing the beamwidth of the antenna
would also improve the antenna noise temperature, since the contribution from side-lobes
and the ground surface noise would decrease, thus increasing the SNR drastically. A horn
antenna, helical antenna, or a Yagi-antenna with a wide beamwidth may work for this. It
is also possible to use a larger antenna, although this would only improve the overall signal
gain, and not the signal to noise ratio. If the back and sides of the antennas could somehow
be shielded from the noise of the ground, this would also increase the SNR significantly.
Adding circuitry to antenna
It is possible to implement low noise amplifiers on the backside of the PCBs which the
antennas are mounted to. This would greatly reduce the additional noise which is picked
up in the SMA cable from the antenna to the receiver board. The only cost of this solution,
is that power would need to be brought to the receiving antennas, in order to bias the low
noise amplifier.
Tracking antenna platform
A tracking platform should be able to track the rocket’s position and align itself to point
directly at the rocket. This can be done in a few different ways - one can either build
a radar, use image processing, or use the data of the rocket to get an estimate of the
position. Using data from the rocket could potentially be very risky since it requires
constant alignment - however it would by far be the simplest. If the rocket could be tracked
perfectly, it would allow the receiving antenna to have a much higher directivity antenna,
which would increase both the gain and the SNR.
57
==PAGE:66==
6
CONCLUSION
The systems design of a telemetry downlink for 9km sounding rockets has been designed
and manufactured. The frequency of the system was chosen to span between 2400 MHz
and 2500 MHz, as this makes a wide variety of components available due to the popularity
of the frequency band. In order to better radiate in all directions, and increasing the
maximum legal power radiation three-fold, three antennas were implemented around the
circumference of the rocket’s outer panels. Small right hand circularly polarized omni-
directional patch antennas were chosen for the transmitting side due to their small size. In
a compromise with the mechanical team, a 22.5◦degree downward tilt on the antennas
was allowed, improving the alignment of the antennas slightly. The same patch antennas
were chosen for the receiving side, although this should be reconsidered in the future.
Due to the small size, omni-directionality, and low efficiency of the receiving antenna, the
signal to noise ratio proved to be very low, theoretically. This proved problematic, due to
the requirement of a maximally tolerated package error rate of 5%, corresponding to a
1.39×10−4 bit error rate. To add a significant margin in the signal to noise ratio, LoRa
modulation was chosen due to it’s robustness against very low signal to noise ratios. To
this extent, the SX1281 transceiver from Semtech was also chosen for both the transmitting
and receiving side. In order to meet the specification of a bit rate of 36.8kBits/s, spread
factors between 5 and 8 had to be utilized. Due to limitations in the telemetry driver, the
bit rate was limited to under 100kBits/s, leaving 6 possible combinations of spread factors
and bandwidths available.
A link budget was created, which assumed a maximum legal output power of 100mW
E.I.R.P. from the transmitter, a 120dB path loss, and a total receiver gain of 40dB, which
incorporates a 20dB link margin and up to 10dB of loss on the receiver side.
The antennas were mounted in an old section of the rocket and rested in an anechoic
chamber, to verify it’s radiation pattern, which proved to be unimpeded by the aluminium
frame of the rocket’s skeleton. The radiation pattern was graphed, and looked as predicted.
PCB designs were made for the transmitting and receiving boards, which could be measured
with RF equipment and used in range testing.
Due to the SX1281s feature of software-adjustable output power in steps of 1dBm, the
transmitting board was designed to operate with an output power around the legal
maximum, with the ability to compensate for any losses by simply increasing the output
power of the SX1281. This was achieved using the SKY65162-70LF power amplifier,
resulting in an output power just below the legal maximum as expected. At output
powers far above the legal maximum, the power amplifiers suffered from overheating issues,
resulting in harmonics on the output - however, this is not an issue at the legal maximum
output power.
58
==PAGE:67==
6. Conclusion
On the receiver side, the TQP3M9037 was chosen as a low noise amplifier to keep the
signal to noise ratio as low as possible, followed by two TRF37A73 gain block amplifiers for
further gain. When tested with a VNA, the gain varied from 43dB at 2400 MHz to 34.5dB
at 2500 MHz, whereas theoretical gain should be 41dB. This was likely due to component
tolerance on the input matching circuit of the LNA, as well as losses in components and
traces.
Finally, range testing at 0.5km, 4.5km, and 11km was performed. The 4.5km and 11km
range tests were performed under the conditions of long range propagation over planar
ground, making the path loss proportional to d4 instead of d2. Additionally, the tests
incorporated maximum noise due to the antennas aiming at the horizon instead of up
towards the sky.
At 0.5km, communication was perfect at all spread factors and bandwidths. At 4.5km, only
spread factor 9 and a bandwidth of 406 MHz was tested, resulting in perfect communication
at 7.14kBit/s. However, little to no communication was achieved at an 11km distance, even
when using spread factor 12 and a bandwidth of 203 MHZ. Since these tests, a number of
issues were discovered and documented in the results section of this report, motivating
another 4.5km and 11km range test where these issues are fixed.
Overall, a functional and promising telemetry downlink has been designed, which requires
additional range testing after a number of easily fixed issues are tended to.

 TEST DESCRIPTION 
This test is performed as a range test for the telemetry system of the rocket antennas across 
a large body of water. This is a worst-case scenario, since water introduces a lot of 
reflections, and angling antennas towards the horizon introduces a lot more noise than when 
they are angled towards the sky. Furthermore, the distance is 11km, whereas the distance 
we’ll most likely see in Portugal is at around 7-8km (when taking angle and distance from the 
launchrail into account). Still, it provides valuable information, and is much easier to set up 
than a weather balloon.
