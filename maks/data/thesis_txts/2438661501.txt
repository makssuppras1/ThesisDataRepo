Abstract
The harsh arctic climatic conditions of Greenland, the solitude of most of the cities and
settlements and the upcoming changes in the Greenlandic building regulations require
exterior wall constructions that are physically and economically suitable for these condi-
tions. In this thesis, the hygrothermal properties and the amortisation time of six external
wall constructions are assessed. The constructions are four different timber-frame walls,
a wall with a load-bearing structure made from cross-laminated timber disks and a wall
which is assembled from insulated ﬁbre-glass boxes. One of the timber-frame walls rep-
resents the current Greenlandic building practice, whilst the other ﬁve are designed to
fulﬁl the upcoming U-value requirement of 0,100
W
m2·K. After the hydrothermal assess-
ment, which includes the calculation of the U-values and the analysis of the water-vapour
transfer through the walls, it is found that only three of the ﬁve new walls are suitable for
the climatic conditions of Sisimiut. In the economical assessment, the ﬁve new wall con-
structions (and U-value alterations with different costs) are assessed regarding whether
or not their additional investment cost, compared to the current building practice, can
be compensated for by the ﬁnancial savings in heating energy which these walls achieve
compared to the current building practice. This assessment is conducted for two different
heating-energy cost-development assumptions. It is found that all ﬁve new walls and the
majority of their U-value alterations can be amortised within 50 years for both scenarios.
The overall conclusion is that two of the three new timber-frame constructions and the
cross-laminated timber wall are economically sustainable and physically suitable for the
location of Sisimiut in Greenland.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction and Hypothesis
There are still a lot of challenges in arctic regions for architects and engineers when it
comes to designing and constructing buildings, and Greenland is no exception to this.
One of the main challenges is the harsh climate. The temperatures in many of the Green-
landic regions drop far below zero degrees Celsius for a long period in the year which
causes even the seawater to freeze and form sea ice. Some regions get little to no daylight
during the winter, and the sun never sets in the summer [1]. Additionally to the climatic
challenges, logistical challenges must be faced.
There is a total of 73 cities and towns in Greenland. All of these places resemble is-
land economies [2]. Not a single city or settlement in Greenland is currently connected
to another one by a road. Even though planes exist to connect some cities, it is more
than unlikely that a full-grown road network can ever be implemented in Greenland. All
transportation to and within Greenland is therefore carried out on sea or air routes. The
city of Sisimiut, for example, which is the second largest in the country, is located within
the Arctic circle, but has a year-round ice-free harbour. This leads to the fact that the
city is connected to Aalborg in Denmark with a supply ship twice a month. The city of
Qaanaaq, on the other hand, which lies far north in Greenland is serviced twice a year
[3]. In combination with the fact that most common building materials are imported to
the country (492 million DKK in 2017 [4]), it is obvious that the logistical challenges and
shipment costs play a major role in the economical sustainability of a wall construction.
The exterior walls of a building, especially permanently inhabited ones, must therefore
be suitable to face both the physical as well as the economical challenges.
To be physically suitable for the Arctic environment, the walls constructions must be both
air tight and energy efﬁcient [5]. Apart from being sustainable in terms of energy effe-
ciency, the walls also have to live up to the requirements imposed by the future updated
Greenlandic building regulations. These changes will require new or adapted envelope
constructions with a better thermal resistance than the current regulations (cp. appx. B.1).

These requirements are based on a recommendation of the Danish Building Research
Institute [6].
The airtightness of these new constructions is important to make sure that mechanical
ventilation systems work properly and efﬁcient and thereby ensure a good indoor cli-
mate. Equally important for the indoor climate, is that the walls must to a certain degree
be vapour tight. Vapour that ingresses into the construction might cause high relative hu-
midity or even moisture accumulation within the building materials due to over-saturation
of the air. This can have two different consequences, one of which is the reduction of the
thermal conductivity of the insulation material due to accumulation of moisture within
the material [7][8]. This could lead to increased energy use for space heating and favour
the growth of mould. According to the WHO, exposure to those microbial pollutants is
highly associated with respiratory symptoms, allergies, asthma and immunological reac-
tions [9]. Contrary to common belief, a recent study shows that mould actually leads to
the deterioration of building material by increasing porosity and reducing strength and
durability [10]
The new and updated envelope constructions that will be affected by the upcoming changes
in the Greenlandic building regulations will not only have to cope with the climatic con-
ditions. The wall constructions must also be economically sustainable. The cost for
material, labour and shipment of the future wall constructions will be higher than the
cost for the common-practice wall constructions under the current building regulations.
This increased cost must be at least equalled by the savings in heating costs which can be
achieved by new constructions in order for the walls to be as economically sustainable as
current ones.
The aim of this thesis is to investigate the hygrothermal properties of six types of external
wall construction and the economical properties of the construction methods. This thesis
is therefore split into two separate parts. In the ﬁrst part, the hygrothermal properties
will be assessed for use under the arctic climatic conditions of the city of Sisimiut in
Greenland. In the second part, the economical suitability of the wall sections and their
alterations will be assessed with regard to their total cost per square meter.

CHAPTER 2
Wall Types
In this chapter, the six wall types that are subject to this investigation are graphically pre-
sented and brieﬂy described. One of the wall types represents the current Greenlandic
building practice and fulﬁls the requirements of the current Greenlandic building reg-
ulations. The other ﬁve wall types are constructions that have never been used under
Greenlandic climatic conditions before or are not widely used in the country. The con-
structions have either been speciﬁcally designed or adapted to fulﬁl the requirements of
the upcoming Greenlandic building regulation of 2020. Please note that the wall types
have not been designed by the author of this thesis. Furthermore, the materials of the dif-
ferent wall constructions are listed, and their hygrothermal properties presented. These
properties are, if not speciﬁcally mentioned, taken from the Danish/European standard
for building materials and products DS/EN ISO 10456 [11]. The properties of the vapour
barrier [12] and the air barrier [13] are product-speciﬁc values. The materials usde for
vapour and air barriers are identical in all wall constructions that contain an air or vapour
barrier. The same applies to the mineral wool which was used as an insulation material
[14].
For a better overview of the wall assemblies, the architectural design drawings can be
found in appx. C.1-C.6.

2.2
Wall 2, Greenlandic Building Regulations 2020
The Greenlandic Building Regulations 2020 wall (henceforth referred to as the GBR 2020
wall) is an advancement of the GBR 2006 wall. The assembly principle remains a timber-
frame construction. The two main improvements are a thicker insulation layer and a
reduction of the thermal-bridge effect of the wooden studs by using two layers of wooden
studs with layers of undisturbed insulation between them. Figure 2.2 shows a principle
sketch of the wall construction. The used materials, including their thickness, thermal
conductivity and vapour-resistance factor can be seen in Table 2.2. The biggest advantage
of this construction type is that it is merely an evolution from the previous one which
means that neither additional equipment nor knowledge will be required to implement the
system into the practice of the local craftsmen.

Wall 4, Masanti Wall
The Masanti wall is designed by and named after a engineering company with a branch
in Sisimiut. As can be seen in Figure 2.4, the core of the construction is a timber frame,

2.5
Wall 5, Cross-Laminated Timber
Using cross-laminated timber (henceforth referred to as CLT) walls is a comparably new
construction method. CLT walls are composed from load-bearing disc elements with
external insulation. The disc itself is consists of several layers of wooden boards which
are glued together. Each layer is orientated perpendicular to the previous one. I-joists are
ﬁxed to the outside of the disc elements. The spacing with between those is ﬁlled with
mineral-wool insulation. These insulation chambers are closed on the outside with an
air barrier. The rain screen of the construction is created by a ventilated facade. Figure

2.5. WALL 5, CROSS-LAMINATED TIMBER

2.5 shows the principle sketch of the construction. The used materials and the thickness,
thermal conductivity and vapour-resistance factor of these can be seen in 2.5.
The speciﬁc characteristic of this wall is that no vapour barrier is mounted on the inside.
Due to its thickness, the wooden-disc elements act as vapour breaks. However, this means
that the joints between the disc elements have to be sealed with extreme care. Further,
there is no installation space or internal ﬁnish on the inside of the wall, because the
electrical wiring can be done in pre-drilled channels inside of the disc and the wooden
surface can either be painted or left in its natural optics. An additional beneﬁt of this
system, compared to the other assemblies, is also its drawback. Due to their weight, the
disc elements can only be erected by using a crane, but for the same reason they add a
considerable amount of thermal mass to the building.

2.6
Wall 6, FIBRA
FIBRA is the name of an Icelandic company which builds houses from insulated glass-
ﬁbre boxes (wall 6, which is built using this method, will henceforth be refered to as
a Fibra wall). As seen in ﬁgure 2.6, this system uses prefabricated ﬁbre-glass laminate
boxes with a high density rock-wool insulation [17] as a core and a gel coating on the
outside-facing surface [18]2. These boxes are load bearing and mounted on site by gluing
them together. The ﬁbre-glass laminate is supposed to act as a vapour (and air) barrier as
well as a rain screen. The gel coating functions as ﬁre retardant. On the inside of the wall
is an insulated installation space with an internal ﬁnish made from gypsum wall boards.
It is worth mentioning that the Fibra wall is the only wall in this thesis that has no wooden
load-bearing structure. In fact, the system resembles more the hull of a boat than the con-
ventional outer wall of a house. This fact might bear a lot of potential for maritime-based
cultures like the ones in Greenland and other arctic coastal regions. Many inhabitants of
these regions sail for the purpose of hunting and transportation, and therefore, knowledge
about maintenance of boats is wide-spread. This means that there is a knowledge base in
these cultures on how to maintain and service ﬁbre-glass structures. A tabular description
of the wall construction can be seen in table 2.6.
Figure 2.6: Principle sketch of the Fibra wall

the values from DS/ES ISO 10456 for polyester resin were used.

CHAPTER 3
Method of the Hygrothermal
Assessment
In this chapter, the method for the calculation of the hygrothermal properties of the wall
types will be explained. Both the heat and vapour transfer will ﬁrst be assessed by means
of hand calculations. The heat transfer will then be veriﬁed by simulating the walls using
ﬁnite-elements simulation software. The following two sections will give a brief overview
of basic calculation principles and used input parameters. It is important to mention that

this assessment is simply that it is a realistic, conservative approach.
Measurements that have been conducted in a student dormitory in Sisimiut, which is con-
sidered a low-energy building under Arctic conditions found that the relative humidity
in both buildings never exceeded 35% on average, as long as the mechanical ventila-
tion was active. Without mechanical ventilation, the relative humidity was on average
between 30,7% and 47,4% [26]. Even though mechanical ventilation is considered stan-
dard equipment in new buildings, the walls should be designed to be able to withstand
an indoor climate that results from a total failing of the mechanical ventilation without
suffering severe damage.

13788.

and the other one through the undisturbed insulation. The former were on purpose taken
next to the wooden or epoxy load-bearing structures and not inside them. This is due to
the fact that the main part of the moisture transfer will take place inside the insulation,
because the vapour-transfer resistance of the other material is signiﬁcantly higher than the
one of the insulation material. By using those two gradients, it is possible to evaluate the
effect of the thermal bridges on the vapour transfer trough the walls.

4.1
U-Value Assessment
Table 4.1 shows the resulting heat-transfer coefﬁcients from the hand calculations and the
ﬁnite element simulation. It is apparent that there is a slight deviation between the hand
calculations and the ﬁnite element simulations. It is also apparent that all the resulting
U-values from the ﬁnite element simulation are lower than the ones from the hand cal-
culations. This circumstance is explained by the fact that the thermal resistance network
approach does not consider the actual position of the different materials in the three-
dimensional structure. The GBR 2016 wall, for example, has two insulation layers, one
with wooden studs and the other one with wooden battens. Due to the fact that the battens
are placed perpendicular to the studs, the area of contact between these is fairly small.
Different to the ﬁnite element simulation, the thermal resistance network does not take
this into account, which means that the thermal bridge effect is not considered satisfac-
tory. Depending on the type of structure, this circumstance can lead to deviating results.
However, in the case of the ﬁve new wall types which are subject to this investigation, the
deviations are negligibly small.

4.7
Discussion - Hygrothermal Assessment
The analysis of the U-values showed that the GBR 2020, CLT and the Fibra walls will
meet the upcoming requirements of the new Greenlandic building regulations regardless
of the amount of storeys. These walls will also not require the roof and/or ﬂoor construc-
tions to compensate in order to meet the required overall transmission heat loss of the
building. The Scanwo wall construction would require the roof and/or ﬂoor constructions
to compensate for its higher U-value in case of a one storey building. The Masanti wall
would only meet the requirements for a three or more storey building. For a building with
less storeys it would also require roof and/or ﬂoor constructions with U-values signiﬁ-
cantly lower than 0,100
W
m2∗K to meet the overall requirements for the transmission heat
loss. The GBR 2006 wall does, as expected, not fulﬁl the upcoming requirements.
The results of the vapour-transfer analysis show that, in terms of usability, the Masanti,
CLT and Scanwo walls can be considered suitable for the local climatic conditions. The
GBR 2020 wall was found to in principle be usable but to bear the risk of mould growth
during some months of the year. However, this issue could be ﬁxed by small adjustments
in the construction design. For the Fibra wall construction it was found that the results for
the vapour-transfer analysis depend highly on the material properties of the resin-coated
glass-ﬁbre construction. For the used material properties it was found that the polyester
resin makes the construction unusable under the local climatic conditions. However, the
same calculation with a phenolic resin on the inside and a polyester resin on the outside
improved the vapour transfer. For the Fibra wall, it can therefore be concluded that thor-
ough laboratory tests would be beneﬁcial to establish the actual material properties and
the vapour transfer through the ﬁbre-glass reinforced resin boxes.
Additionally, it was found that the rule of thumb to place the vapour barrier within the
ﬁrst third of the wall construction does not necessarily apply to every construction type.
The results from GBR 2006, GBR 2020 and the Fibra wall show that the vapour barrier
should be located further on the inside to avoid dangerous relative humidities in adjacency
to the vapour barrier.

walls have been considered amortised after the number of years it will take to make up

Built of the alterations:
As mentioned before, the economical analysis was performed
for the wall constructions and their alterations. The following tables give an overview
of the wall constructions and their alterations with respect to the actually-used insulation
thickness. This is important because the number and thickness of the various layers of
insulation used in an assembly highly inﬂuence the amount of man hours that have to
be spent on mounting the insulation. An effort was therefore made to keep the number
of used insulation layers as low as possible. It shall furthermore be mentioned that the
available insulation thicknesses did not in all cases allow to ﬁll the voids that are to be
insulated precisely. A tolerance of -5 mm and +10 mm was therefore accepted. E.g. the
void that is created by 50 x 50 mm battens was ﬁlled with 45 mm of insulation, because
the next thicker available version of 70 mm would be compressed to much when plywood
sheets are screwed to the battens. Those tolerances have have been accepted because the
product itself has a production tolerance of -5 mm to + 15 mm [14]. The inﬂuence of
these tolerances on the U-value of the wall has therefore been neglected. Furthermore, it
shall be mentioned that the used facade construction and internal ﬁnish was the same for
all the wall types and their alterations in order to ensure their comparability. The column
"THK" represents the constructive space demand of a layer, whereas the column "built"
represents the thicknesses of the materials used to assemble the layer. For the compress-
ible insulation, the THK value of a layer does not necessarily ﬁt the corresponding built
value. This means that the insulation material either gets compressed a bit or is built in
slightly looser.

CLT:
The tables 5.21 to 5.25 show the built that has been used for the bill of quanti-
ties for the CLT wall and its various alterations. For all of the alterations, the thickness of
the CLT element itself was kept constant at 100 mm and no additional internal ﬁnish was
applied. This is due to the fact that the price for the CLT element contains a surcharge for
a "visible surface".
The man hours for the erection of the CLT elements, the mounting of the I-joist and the
seam cover were estimated by BSc in Engineering, Christian Wenisch, who is a planner
for CLT single-family houses in Germany. Also the price estimation for the CLT ele-
ments and the seam cover have been made by Wenisch2. It shall be mentioned that the
man hours for the mounting of a running meter I-joist were estimated only for a thickness
of 360 mm. This value would theoretically change slightly for different thicknesses. Due
to a lack of further information, this change was neglected. The prices for the I-joist have
been obtained from the website of a danish hardwear store [29]. Wherever the thickness
of the I-joists deviated from the ones on the website, a linear interpolation was used to
get the required prices. Given the fact that these are online prices for end customers, the
prices are a conservative estimate. It shall furthermore be mentioned that the insulation
in each of the walls consists of at least three layers. This approach was chosen in order
to account for the geometrical characteristics of the I-joist. This measure ensures that
the insulation ﬁts the space more neatly and to thereby avoid the creation of cavities and
zones with compressed insulation. However, this measure also increased the required
work hours.

In this chapter, the results of the economical assessment will be presented. The input-
parameters and the calculations for the material, labour and shipping cost can be found in
appx. A.6. The input parameter and results of the amortisation calculation can be found
in appx. A.7. First, the initial investment costs for all the walls and their alterations were
calculated. Table 6.1 shows the material, labour and shipping costs as well as the total
cost for each wall and U-value alterations. The resulting values have been colour-coded
to allow a quick qualitative assessment of the results. Each crossing of a column and a
U-value category line is a group. The values in each group have been coloured in the
order red -> orange -> yellow -> green. Red represents the highest price and green the
lowest. From the table, the following statements can be deduced.
• Total costs: The GRB 2020 walls have the lowest cost followed by the CLT walls.
The Scanwo walls have the highest total costs in all cases except for the 0.20 cate-
gory. Here, the position of the Scanwo and Masanti walls are switched.
• Material costs: The GBR 2020 walls also have the lowest material costs, followed
by the Masanti and Scanwo walls. The costs of the Masanti and Scanwo walls are
very close, and the CLT walls have by far the highest material costs.
• Labour costs: In this category, the CLT walls have the lowest cost, followed by the
GBR 2020 and the Masanti walls. The Scanwo walls have by far the highest labour
costs.
• Shipping Cost: Here, the GBR walls again have the lowest costs, followed by the
Masanti and Scanwo walls. The CLT walls again have the highest costs.

6.1
Discussion - Economical Assessment
From the results of the economical analysis of the wall types and their U-value alter-
ations, several conclusions can be drawn. The ﬁrst conclusion is that all walls and their
alterations from 0.08 to 0.17 can be considered economically sustainable in comparison
to the reference wall because they all amortise within 50 years. The second conclusion
that can be drawn seams very obvious but is extremely important. The cost of a wall that
shall be erected in Sisimiut has transportation costs up to 25%. For locations further in the
north or in east Greenland this share will be even higher. This means that the transporta-
tion cost can, depending on the nature of the project, be determining which construction
principle should be used. However, this also means that the amortisation calculation,
which have been made are regarded valid for the location of Sisimiut (or locations with
similar shipping conditions).
==PAGE:78==
==PAGE:79==
CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
The aim of this thesis was to conduct a holistic assessment of ﬁve exterior wall con-
structions or rather construction methods to give a ﬁrst insight in whether or not these
walls would be suitable for the climatic conditions of Sisimiut. It was found that the The
Scanwo and CLT wall are suitable for the climatic conditions and fulﬁl the requirements
of the upcoming building regulations. The Masanti wall was also found suitable but, but
does not meet the requirements. The Fibra wall was ﬁrst found to experience critical
internal condensation. However, uncertainties regarding the actual used building and ap-
plicability of the calculation method lead to the conclusion that no statement about the
Fibra wall can be given without further testing. The GBR 2020 wall was found to bear
the risk of mould growth. However, it can be considered suitable because a small adjust-
ment in the structure could eliminate this risk. The economical analysis showed that the
ﬁve walls and almost all their U-Value alterations can be amortised within the assumed
building-lifetime of 50 years. The only exception from this are the Masanti and Scanwo
wall with a U-value of approximately 0,170. It was also found that each construction
method would have an ideal U-value for which the cost of the wall could be amortised the
fasted. However, this is strongly depending on the development of the heating cost and
cost and thermal properties of the reference wall.
Apart from concluding which wall construction types are the most suitable for the physi-
cal and logistical condition of the location of Sisimiut, another important main conclusion
can be drawn. This is that it is not possible to give a clear answer to whether or not a
wall-construction type is suitable for the Arctic in general. The differences in climatic
conditions, quality of the infrastructural connection to the rest of the world and the ability
of the home owners to carry out the construction work themselves are the determining
factors for whether or not the construction principle is suitable. Especially architects and
engineers therefore have to discard the notion of the Arctic as one single region with
homogeneous demands to the designs.

CHAPTER 7. CONCLUSION
7.1
Further work
As far as the physical assessment goes, the further work that will be carried out is a ﬁeld
test with the six wall types as they have been described in chapter 2. Wall sections of
1,5 m in width and 2,3 meters in height will be built into a mobile test facility. This
facility resempbes a shipping container with six bays which allow the mounting of the
wall segments. Inside the facility, the temperatures and relative humidities which are
representative for normal dwellings will be simulated, while the external facade of the
segments will be exposed to the local climatic conditions of Sisimiut in Greenland. Each
wall segment will be equipped with temperature and moisture sensors which are placed in
different depths and locations throughout the constructions. The monitored data can then
be compared to the data from this analysis. The tear-down of the test walls after their
lifetime will also allow a qualitative analysis of possible moisture damages and mould
growth in the structures. This ﬁeld test is part of the Bjarne Saxhof Research Project.
In order to gain further insight into the economical aspects of this research, it would
be very interesting to do two different things. The ﬁrst thing would be to create a holis-
tic building design, including roof, ﬂoor, windows and HVAC systems for the wall types
that have been considered usable for the local climate of Sisimiut. This complete design
could then be used to perform a Cost of Conserved Energy analysis. This analysis should
preferably be performed by utilising building energy simulation software to also account
for the effects of solar gains, thermal mass and ventilation systems. The CLT and Fibra
walls will most likely be inﬂuenced differently by these circumstances than the timber-
frame constructions. If this analysis is performed with an energy framework as the goal,
it should be possible to give a more precise statement about the insulation thicknesses that
are actually required to make the construction economically sustainable.
