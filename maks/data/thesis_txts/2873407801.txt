Summary (English)
This thesis investigates Fair Identity Normalization (FIN) [47] as a fairness-
preserving mechanism for deep learning in medical imaging. FIN is adapted
and evaluated on binary, multi-class, and multi-label classification tasks across
diverse clinical datasets. The method ensures equitable feature representations
without altering input data or decision thresholds. Experiments span glaucoma
diagnosis, skin condition classification, and chest X-ray interpretation, assess-
ing both performance and demographic fairness. Results demonstrate that FIN
improves fairness across demographic subgroups, while maintaining clinical util-
ity. The study offers insights into fairness-performance trade-offs and advances
fairness-aware modeling in real-world healthcare settings.

1.1
Motivation
Deep learning (DL) models have made impressive strides in medical imaging,
powering everything from disease classification to image segmentation and triage
support. However, despite their strong overall performance, these models often
don’t perform equally well across different demographic groups, for example
across gender, race, or skin tone. These gaps can stem from imbalanced training
data, biased labels, or differences in how features are learned and represented.
When left unaddressed, such disparities can lead to unequal clinical outcomes
and may even worsen existing health inequalities [20, 22, 31]. At their core, these
issues highlight deeper concerns about fairness in algorithmic decision-making.
The urgency of addressing these unfair issues in medical Artificial Intelligence
(AI) DL models has become increasingly evident. If diagnostic systems sys-
tematically perform worse for certain patient populations, they may contribute
to misdiagnoses, delayed treatments, or reduced trust in automated healthcare
tools[9, 30]. Hence, fairness is not merely a secondary objective but a central
requirement for the ethical deployment of AI in clinical contexts.
Among the recent approaches developed to address fairness in medical AI, some
methods have begun to explore direct interventions within the model architec-
ture itself. One such direction involves modifying the internal feature represen-
tations of neural networks using group-specific transformations. These architec-
tural strategies aim to reduce disparities not by altering the data or adjusting
outputs after training, but by embedding fairness into the learning process itself.
==PAGE:12==
2
Project Scope and Research Objectives
Preliminary studies have demonstrated the potential of such techniques in con-
trolled settings. However, their applicability to more complex, large-scale med-
ical imaging tasks remains an open challenge, particularly in the presence of
label imbalance, multiple disease classes, and real-world clinical variability.
This thesis tackles that challenge by exploring how Fair Identity Normalisation
(FIN) performs across two very different medical imaging domains, each varying
in terms of imaging modality, label complexity, and demographic distributions.
Rather than focusing only on simplified, binary classification tasks, the study
pushes further to test whether fairness-aware normalisation techniques like FIN
can generalise and remain effective in more complex, real-world settings within
medical AI.
1.2
Research Question
Building on the motivation to address demographic disparities in medical AI,
this thesis examines the applicability of FIN [47], a feature-level fairness method
that incorporates group-specific normalisation parameters within deep learning
models. Originally designed for binary glaucoma classification under balanced
group settings, FIN demonstrated promising reductions in gender and racial
performance gaps.
This work investigates whether such benefits extend to more complex diagnostic
tasks. Specifically, FIN is evaluated in two distinct clinical imaging contexts:
• NIH ChestXray14: a large-scale dataset for thoracic disease classifi-
cation, where each image may contain multiple co-occurring pathologies.
The dataset exhibits gender imbalance and considerable label noise, mak-
ing it an ideal multi-label stress test for fairness-aware methods.1
• PASSION: a dermatology dataset composed of clinical photographs taken
in teledermatology settings, mostly using smartphones or similar consumer
devices rather than specialised dermatoscopic equipment. Each image is
labeled with a single skin condition.
The dataset presents high visual
variability and an uneven distribution of skin tones across different dis-
ease classes, making it a realistic and challenging testbed for evaluating
fairness in multi-class classification.2

These two applications serve to test FIN’s generalisation in both multi-label
and multi-class classification settings under demographically imbalanced and
visually heterogeneous conditions. The original FIN implementation is extended
to accommodate these complexities, adapting both training pipelines and eval-
uation metrics for each dataset’s specific task and unique sensitive attribute.
Accordingly, this thesis is guided by the following central research question::
Can Fair Identity Normalisation (FIN) - originally developed for
binary, balanced classification tasks - be successfully adapted to mit-
igate demographic disparities in complex, multi-label and multi-class
medical image classification problems, without degrading overall pre-
dictive performance?
To explore this question, the first step was to replicate the original FIN results in
both imaging domains, using the authors’ code and fairness-focused evaluation
metrics.
This provided a reliable baseline to build on.
Unlike the original
study, though, this thesis takes a closer look at how performance varies across
disease classes. The evaluation metrics were adapted to better highlight group-
level disparities per disease-class. In doing so, this work offers a detailed and
practical assessment of FIN’s usefulness as a fairness tool in real-world medical
AI systems.
1.3
Research Objectives and Approach
To evaluate the effectiveness of FIN in broader clinical settings, this thesis begins
by establishing a strong empirical foundation. Using the original codebase and
evaluation protocol, the baseline results from the Harvard Glaucoma Fairness
dataset [40] were replicated and validated. Building on this, the approach was
extended to more challenging scenarios involving multi-label and multi-class
classification.
In contrast to the original work, which jointly considered two types of ophthalmic
imaging modalities, this thesis focuses exclusively on Retinal Nerve Fiber Layer
Thickness (RNFLT) scans. This choice allows for better control over the exper-
imental setup and enables clearer comparisons with follow-up datasets, which
are limited to a single imaging modality. Furthermore, RNFLT images are di-
rectly linked to structural changes in the optic nerve and offer a clean spatial
representation that simplifies the interpretation of fairness-related behavior at
the feature level.
==PAGE:14==
4
Project Scope and Research Objectives
Our primary contribution lies in extending the original FIN framework [47] to
handle more complex and demographically imbalanced classification settings.
Beyond applying FIN to new datasets, this thesis introduces several method-
ological adaptations, including task-specific training pipelines, enhanced fair-
ness metrics for multi-label and multi-class evaluation, and a detailed per-class
performance breakdown across demographic groups. These extensions aim to
rigorously test FIN’s robustness and generalisability in realistic clinical contexts.
To facilitate future comparisons with targeted datasets like the one used in [47],
a modular approach is adopted where each modality can be tested independently
while applying a consistent evaluation framework.
The methodology involves a full re-implementation of FIN within convolutional
neural network (CNN) architectures, each tailored to the unique characteristics
of the datasets in question. For the ChestXray14 dataset, a model designed
for thoracic disease prediction was used, while for PASSION, a CNN backbone
better suited for clinical skin images was adopted. In both cases, FIN is com-
pared to a baseline model trained under the same conditions but without any
fairness-specific components. To better understand the trade-offs introduced by
fairness interventions, the key FIN hyperparameter m - which determines the
degree of identity-aware normalisation - is systematically varied. Performance
and fairness metrics are then tracked across these configurations to evaluate the
overall impact.
A key part of the approach is the use of fairness-aware metrics. Alongside tradi-
tional performance indicators such as the Area Under the ROC Curve (AUC),
the Equity-Scaled AUC (ES-AUC) is employed - a metric originally proposed
in [47] - to jointly capture accuracy and fairness by penalising large disparities
in AUC between demographic groups. In addition, the mean and maximum
AUC gaps between groups (e.g., gender in ChestXray14 and Fitzpatric skin
type in PASSION) are computed, in order to quantify both average and worst-
case disparities. These metrics allow us to perform a detailed empirical analysis
of model fairness, going beyond overall predictive accuracy to assess equitable
behavior across identity-sensitive subpopulations.
To ensure that our conclusions are statistically reliable, all experiments are
repeated across multiple random seeds, and results are aggregated to reduce
sensitivity to stochastic variations in training. Also, the original experimental
settings from [47] are replicated where relevant, to provide a point of comparison
and validate the generalisability of FIN to new data regimes.
Through this process, not only is a robust assessment of FIN across two realis-
tic medical imaging scenarios intended, but also the development of a general
framework for fairness-aware evaluation that can be extended to other multi-

Chapter 2
Background and Motivation
DL methods, particularly convolutional neural networks (CNNs), have surpassed
traditional handcrafted pipelines in a wide range of medical imaging tasks [13]
- including disease classification, lesion detection, and organ segmentation -
by automatically learning rich hierarchical features from data.
While hand-
crafted features remain relevant in specific low-data or interpretable settings,
DL has become the a common standard for many large-scale diagnostic bench-
marks and clinical research pipelines. However, concerns about algorithmic bias
have become increasingly important [20]. Neural networks trained on demo-
graphically imbalanced datasets tend to exhibit reduced diagnostic accuracy on
under-represented subgroups [38]. In high-risk domains like healthcare, these
disparities are especially problematic, as they risk amplifying existing inequities
in access to care and diagnostic accuracy.
For example, in the context of thoracic imaging, Seyyed-Kalantari et al. [34]
demonstrated that commonly used chest X-ray classifiers underperform on un-
derrepresented groups, such as female patients. Such disparities arise not only
from sampling imbalance but also from anatomical and physiological heterogene-
ity that standard models fail to account for [47]. When models are trained using
global normalisation techniques like BatchNorm, such Group-specific anatomi-
cal or phenotypic differences (sex-based anatomy, skin tone variations) can lead
to systematic misclassification errors, including both underdiagnosis and over-
diagnosis, depending on which features dominate model predictions.
To overcome the limitations of global normalisation in capturing subgroup-
specific patterns, Luo et al. [47] introduced the FIN framework, originally de-
signed for Glaucoma detection using RNFLT maps. Their study found that al-
though Glaucoma is more prevalent among Black patients, deep learning models
using standard normalisation techniques - like BatchNorm - tended to perform
worse on this group compared to White and Asian patients. FIN addresses this

issue by learning demographic-specific normalisation statistics, allowing feature
distributions to be standardised within each subgroup. When paired with the
ES-AUC metric, FIN showed improved AUC parity across racial groups, with-
out compromising overall classification performance according to both standard
and fairness-aware evaluation metrics.
While promising, FIN has not yet been validated beyond the original Glaucoma
diagnostic setting. Its behavior across diverse imaging modalities, tasks, and
sensitive attributes (e.g., race, sex) remains underexplored. Motivated by this
gap, the present thesis aims to replicate and rigorously benchmark the FIN
framework in broader contexts, specifically on large-scale chest X-ray datasets
such as NIH ChestXray14 and dermatological datasets such as PASSION. This
work primarily relies on ES-AUC and subgroup-specific AUC to assess fairness.
While criteria such as Demographic Parity Difference (DPD), Equal Opportu-
nity (EO), and Equalized Odds (EqOdds) are widely used in fairness literature,
they are not directly applied in this study due to the complexity of evaluating
them in multi-label, clinical scenarios.
Beyond the technical goals, this work is grounded in the ethical imperative to
build AI systems that serve all patients equitably. Fairness in medical AI is not
merely a desirable property but a clinical necessity [35]. As diagnostic algorithms
are increasingly integrated into patient care, ensuring their robust performance
across demographic lines is critical to fostering trust, preventing harm, and
advancing healthcare equity.
This thesis contributes toward operationalising
fairness as a first-class objective in medical AI design and evaluation.
2.1
Medical Imaging and AI
Medical imaging is a fundamental tool in clinical practice, enabling non-invasive
visualisation of anatomical structures and pathological changes.
Techniques
such as X-rays, magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), and Optical Coherence To-
mography (OCT) have become indispensable for disease detection, diagnosis,
monitoring, and treatment planning.
In recent years, the increasing availability of large-scale, annotated medical
imaging datasets has facilitated the development of AI systems that assist clin-
icians in a wide range of diagnostic tasks.
DL models, in particular, have
demonstrated strong performance in image classification, segmentation, and
anomaly detection, offering the potential to enhance diagnostic accuracy and
efficiency [13, 16].
==PAGE:18==
8
Background and Motivation
By leveraging patterns in imaging data that may not be easily perceptible to
the human eye, these systems can support decision-making in high-throughput
clinical environments and extend diagnostic capabilities to settings with lim-
ited expert availability. As such, AI-enabled medical imaging holds significant
promise in improving access to care and supporting precision medicine.
Despite these promising advances, the integration of AI into clinical imaging
also raises important ethical and societal challenges. A central concern is that
of fairness: as deep learning models are trained on historical medical data, they
may inadvertently inherit and exacerbate biases already present in the data. In
the next section, how such disparities can arise is examined, their significance is
discussed, and the efforts made to measure and mitigate unfairness in medical
AI systems are reviewed.
2.2
Fairness in Medical AI
While the issue of bias in medical imaging models was introduced in the preced-
ing sections, the concept of fairness is now examined, with a particular focus on
how performance disparities arise, how they are quantified, and how they can
be systematically mitigated. Unlike conventional performance metrics - such as
accuracy or AUC - fairness metrics are designed to reveal subgroup-level harms,
particularly when predictive performance degrades for marginalised populations.
In critical diagnostic contexts, these disparities are not merely technical short-
comings - they can result in unequal clinical outcomes and further deepen ex-
isting healthcare inequalities.
These inequities are often rooted in structural imbalances within the training
data. Algorithms trained on real-world clinical datasets may inadvertently en-
code societal biases, especially when demographic groups are underrepresented
or exhibit distinct data distributions [20]. The result is unequal model perfor-
mance across racial, gender, or age subgroups, violating the ethical imperative of
equitable care. Empirical studies have demonstrated that DL classifiers trained
on large-scale chest X-ray datasets, such as ChestXray14, often exhibit marked
performance disparities across demographic sub-groups [34]. These gaps could
be not merely statistical anomalies but symptomatic of broader systemic in-
equities, raising questions about the safe and ethical deployment of such models
in clinical practice.

2.3
Fairness Metrics for Clinical Evaluation
As DL models become increasingly embedded into clinical decision-making pipelines,
the evaluation of fairness emerges as a critical component alongside predictive
performance. Unlike global accuracy or AUC, fairness metrics expose subgroup-
level disparities that may otherwise remain hidden, particularly when demo-
graphic groups differ in disease prevalence, data representation, or anatomical
features. In high-risk medical contexts, such disparities translate directly into
the unequal quality of care, making fairness not merely a technical issue but an
ethical imperative.
2.3.1
Challenges and Definitions of Fairness Metrics in
Healthcare
To quantify and mitigate these disparities, researchers have proposed several
group fairness definitions drawn from the broader ML literature. However, their
direct application to medical imaging is often problematic due to the domain’s
multi-label nature, prevalence imbalance, and clinical cost asymmetry.
This
section defines classical metrics and highlights their limitations in healthcare.
Demographic Parity
A classifier satisfies Demographic Parity (DP) if the
probability of a positive prediction is the same for all groups, regardless of the
actual label:
P( ˆY = 1 | A = a) = P( ˆY = 1 | A = b),
∀a, b ∈A
(2.1)
While conceptually simple, DP is often inappropriate in healthcare[38]. Disease
prevalence naturally varies across populations due to biological and environ-
mental factors. Enforcing DP would require assigning equal rates of positive
diagnoses across groups - even when the disease is more prevalent in one - lead-
ing to potential over- or under-diagnosis. This not only violates clinical intuition
but also raises ethical concerns about introducing artificial harm to achieve sta-
tistical symmetry.
Equal Opportunity
A classifier satisfies Equal Opportunity (EO) if the true
positive rate (TPR) is equal across groups:
==PAGE:20==
10

P( ˆY = 1 | Y = 1, A = a) = P( ˆY = 1 | Y = 1, A = b)
(2.2)
Equal opportunity aligns more closely with clinical goals by focusing on sen-
sitivity: ensuring that no group is disproportionately likely to have a missed
diagnosis. However, EO does not consider false positives (FP), which can still
generate harm (e.g., anxiety, unnecessary procedures), and assumes sufficient
positive cases in each group - a condition often violated in under-represented
subgroups[49].
Equalized Odds
Equalized Odds (EqOdds) extends EO by also requiring
equal false positive rates (FPR) across groups:
P( ˆY = 1 | Y = 1, A = a) = P( ˆY = 1 | Y = 1, A = b)
P( ˆY = 1 | Y = 0, A = a) = P( ˆY = 1 | Y = 0, A = b)
(2.3)
While EqOdds offers a more comprehensive fairness guarantee, it is fragile
in medical imaging, especially with multi-label, imbalanced datasets such as
ChestXray14 [39]. When disease prevalence is low or labels are noisy, small pre-
diction shifts can create large disparities in FPR or TPR. This may exaggerate
apparent unfairness or obscure genuine clinical risks.
Broader Limitations in Medical Imaging
Beyond statistical fragility, these
metrics do not explicitly account for anatomical and phenotypic variation. For
instance, differences in lung size, breast tissue density, or skin pigmentation may
alter how diseases manifest in images. A model may perform differently across
groups not due to algorithmic bias, but because disease appearance genuinely
differs - an effect that generic metrics often misinterpret as unfairness[38]. More-
over, the binary setup assumed by these metrics fails to capture the complexity
of multi-label clinical classification.
In sum, standard metrics can be misleading or insufficient in healthcare. They
often lack robustness to skewed base rates, are sensitive to noise, and disre-
gard the clinical consequences of false positives (FP) and false negatives (FN).
These conceptual and practical shortcomings have prompted the development
of fairness metrics tailored to the clinical domain.

To address the limitations of classical definitions, Luo et al. [47] introduced the

a unified score. ES-AUC penalises disparities in AUC across identity subgroups,

This metric forms the basis of the Harvard Glaucoma Fairness (Harvard-GF)

subgroup equity.
2.3.3
Bias Mitigation Strategies
To address performance disparities across demographic groups, a wide array
of techniques has been developed in the algorithmic fairness literature. These
methods are commonly categorised into pre-processing, in-processing, and post-
processing interventions [15, 44].
Pre-processing approaches operate on the training data itself, aiming to re-
duce bias before model training.
For example, datasets can be rebalanced
through resampling or reweighting to ensure more equal representation of de-
mographic groups, or augmented with synthetic samples for underrepresented
populations [52]. By altering the input distribution (e.g., removing sensitive
attributes or generating additional minority-class images), pre-processing meth-
ods seek to prevent the model from learning spurious correlations present in the
original data.
In-processing techniques modify the learning algorithm or model architecture
to enforce fairness during training. This category includes introducing fairness-
aware loss functions or regularisation terms (to penalise disparities in error rates)
and employing adversarial training setups that encourage latent representations
to be invariant to sensitive attributes [11]. For instance, an adversarial network
can be added to the classifier, using a gradient reversal layer to remove encodable
bias from intermediate features.
Post-processing methods act after model training by adjusting model outputs or
decision boundaries to improve fairness. Examples include threshold optimisa-
tions that apply different classification cutoffs for each subgroup or probability
calibration procedures that adjust predicted scores to satisfy fairness criteria [10,
15]. Such post-hoc adjustments (e.g., the Equalized Odds post-processing of

12

Hardt et al.) do not require retraining the model and can be applied to any
classifier’s predictions, making them relatively easy to deploy in principle.
These various strategies have also been explored in the context of healthcare
and medical imaging, as researchers seek practical ways to alleviate performance
gaps between groups in clinical AI systems.
This thesis focuses on an in-processing approach, FIN, implemented at the fea-
ture level, amidst these broader classes of mitigation techniques. FIN offers a
lightweight, architecture-level fairness intervention that does not require exten-
sive data augmentation or adversarial training. Instead, it standardises feature
representations separately for each identity group via group-specific normalisa-
tion parameters, thereby directly compensating for distributional shifts between
subgroups. FIN will be presented in detail in Chapter 3, alongside a discussion
of its design motivations and implementation within this thesis.
2.4
Ethical Implications of Fairness in Medical
AI
The deployment of AI in medical contexts raises not only technical and clinical
challenges but also pressing ethical and philosophical questions.
Fairness in
medical AI is not merely an operational criterion or a statistical constraint - it
is fundamentally a normative concept, grounded in ethical theories of justice,
responsibility, and human dignity.
From a bioethical perspective, the principle of justice demands that health-
care interventions, including algorithmic decision-making, be equitable and non-
discriminatory [3]. If an AI model systematically under performs for patients
from marginalised groups, it risks violating this principle by distributing harms
and benefits unevenly. Such disparities, even when unintended, can undermine
trust in both the technology and the healthcare system more broadly [18].
Philosophically, fairness intersects with theories of distributive justice. Accord-
ing to Rawls’ difference principle, "social and institutional arrangements - in-
cluding technological ones - are justified only if they benefit the least advantaged
members of society" [1].
An AI system that amplifies existing inequities in
health outcomes would therefore be unjust, not because it makes errors, but
because it systematically fails those who are already vulnerable. In this view,
fairness is not simply about equal treatment, but about equitable treatment that
takes historical and structural disadvantages into account.

2.4 Ethical Implications of Fairness in Medical AI

Furthermore, from the perspective of the philosophy of technology, AI systems
do not merely reflect society, they also shape it. Technologies are not neutral
tools; they embody values, assumptions, and worldviews [2]. The design choices
embedded in medical AI, such as which features are used, which fairness metric
is optimised, or whether identity attributes are included, carry moral weight.
For instance, the inclusion of race or gender in algorithmic models may help
correct biases, but it also risks concentrating complex social constructs unless
handled with care[25].
This thesis embraces the dual imperative of technical robustness and ethical
responsibility. It investigates a fairness-aware method, FIN, precisely because
it offers a structured way to reduce performance disparities without erasing de-

At the same time, it remains critically aware of the
philosophical tensions such methods entail: between personalisation and stereo-

ing solutions and moral commitments.
Ultimately, the development of fair medical AI is not just a technical undertaking
but a fundamentally normative process that necessitates sustained collaboration
among data scientists, ethicists, philosophers, clinicians, and the communities
impacted by these systems.

Fair Identity Normalisation
FIN is a technique proposed by Luo et al. [47] to reduce identity-related dis-
parities in DL models for medical imaging. It addresses group-specific biases,
particularly those associated with sensitive attributes such as gender or race, by
intervening at the feature level.
Instead of applying fairness constraints on the output logits or loss, FIN intro-
duces group-specific transformations that normalise identity-sensitive variance
within the feature representations, thus shaping how the model internally pro-
cesses demographic signals before classification.
This approach was originally validated on the Harvard Glaucoma Fairness dataset,
a benchmark designed to evaluate algorithmic fairness in Glaucoma detection.
The dataset includes 2D and 3D OCT images, along with demographic labels
such as race, gender, and ethnicity.
Importantly, it ensures balanced repre-

3.3
Extension of FIN
While originally developed for binary Glaucoma classification on balanced RN-

its effectiveness across three diverse medical imaging contexts: (1) the origi-
nal RNFLT setting, (2) the multi-label NIH ChestXray14 dataset, and (3) the

3.3.1
Motivation and Challenges
Extending FIN to real-world datasets like NIH ChestXray14 and PASSION in-
troduces methodological challenges not present in its original binary setting.
These include multi-label annotations, substantial class and demographic im-
balance, and potential interactions between sensitive attributes (e.g., gender)
and specific disease classes.
Such complexities require robust loss functions,
refined normalisation strategies, and granular fairness evaluations.
In multi-label and multi-class classification tasks, each image may exhibit mul-
tiple co-occurring conditions. Fairness must therefore be assessed both per class
and in aggregate, using disaggregated AUC and recall metrics across identity
groups. To support this, FIN is integrated into convolutional backbones such
as DenseNet121 or ResNet, applying group-specific normalisation at the fea-
ture level. The resulting embeddings feed into classification heads trained with
Binary Cross-Entropy (BCE).
This extension enables FIN to operate under high-dimensional, imbalanced, and
multi-pathology conditions, maintaining both predictive performance and fair-
ness across demographic subgroups.
3.3.2

tion
In the context of multi-label classification (e.g., chest X-rays with 14 pathologies)
and multi-class classification (e.g., skin condition classification with mutually ex-

each label as a binary prediction task. This class-wise adaptation retains the
theoretical properties of ES-AUC, making it compatible with high-dimensional,
real-world datasets. In our implementation, AUC values for each class and de-
mographic subgroup are computed using predicted probabilities and binarised

the presence and severity of Glaucoma [8].

4.1.3
Motivation for FIN
In the Harvard-GF dataset, Black patients exhibit a notably higher Glaucoma
prevalence (61.4%) but suffer from lower classification performance (AUC 0.81)
compared to White (AUC 0.87) and Asian (AUC 0.88) patients (Table II in [47]).
This gap persists even under fairness-aware training regimes.
For example,
Demographic Parity Difference (DPD) measures disparity in positive-prediction
rates across groups, while Difference in Equalized Odds (DEOdds) measures
disparity in both true-positive and false-positive rates. As shown in Figure 1
of [47], optimising solely for DPD or DEOdds often leads to a drop in overall
accuracy, revealing a nuanced trade-off between fairness and performance that
off-the-shelf metrics alone cannot resolve.

Standard group-fairness measures can also be misleading. A model that predicts
“no disease” for all patients would achieve perfect parity across groups but would
be clinically useless. To address this limitation, the paper introduces an equity-
scaled performance metric, which multiplies the aggregate AUC by an equity
factor derived from subgroup performance gaps. This penalises uniformly poor
solutions and rewards models that maintain strong accuracy while minimising
disparities.
4.2
Experimental Design
All experiments were conducted using an EfficientNet-based backbone architec-
ture, trained on the RNFLT modality from the EyeFair dataset, with pre-trained
weights (EfficientNetB1Weights.IMAGENET1KV2). The task was binary clas-
sification, and models were evaluated across racial subgroups to assess both
accuracy and fairness.
Training was performed using the AdamW optimiser with parameters lr=5e-5,
betas=(0.0, 0.1), and weight decay=0. Each model was trained for 10 epochs
with a batch size of 6, using BCEWithLogitsLoss as the classification loss.
To compare the influence of the FIN module, the experimental setup from
the original Harvard-GF paper [47] was faithfully replicated, implementing two
pipelines: one with FIN and one without (referred to as No-FIN). Checkpoints
and logs were correctly separated using a timestamp-based naming convention.
In the FIN experiments, the identity-specific normalisation layer used group-wise
statistics with learnable parameters µ and σ. These parameters were initialised
per identity group and updated during training. The initial values were set as
µ = 0.001 and σ = 1 for all groups.
In contrast to the original paper, which reported results averaged across 5 ran-
dom seeds, the number of repetitions was increased to 10 different seeds. This
choice provides a more robust estimate of average performance and variability.

Dataset Description
The ChestX-ray14 dataset, introduced by the NIH Clinical Center [17], is one of
the most comprehensive publicly available chest radiography datasets, compris-

is annotated with up to 14 disease labels: Atelectasis, Cardiomegaly, Consolida-

extracted using NLP methods applied to the corresponding radiology reports,
introducing label noise due to limitations in the extraction pipeline, as previ-
ously documented in [23, 51]. The dataset is inherently imbalanced, with certain
conditions occurring far more frequently than others [17, 51]. This motivates
careful modelling and evaluation choices, particularly when aiming for fairness
across demographic subgroups.
Patient metadata, such as gender and age, is available and used in fairness-aware
evaluation. In this work, gender is treated as the sensitive attribute of interest,
consistent with recent fairness literature [22, 51].
The test set used in our
evaluation maintains a balanced gender distribution, with approximately 52%
male and 48% female samples, and preserves disease prevalence representative
of the full dataset.

5.1.1
Challenges with the Dataset
Multi-label classification of chest X-ray images, as required by the NIH ChestXray14
dataset, presents a unique set of challenges that must be addressed to ensure
both predictive reliability and fairness across demographic groups.
Label Noise and Overprediction:
One of the most prominent issues is the
presence of label noise, introduced primarily through the automated labelling
pipeline that uses NLP to extract diagnoses from radiology reports [28]. This
process often introduces cases where a pathology is visually present but not
captured in the associated report, leading to FN in the label set.
A particularly problematic example is the label No Finding, which is often used
as a fallback when no positive condition is extracted, rather than represent-
ing a confirmed absence of abnormalities. In practice, it is not uncommon for
No Finding to co-occur with other pathologies, leading to contradictions that
confuse both training and evaluation. This partially explains the tendency of
models to over predict certain labels, such as No Finding, Effusion, and Infil-
tration, even when not present in the ground truth.

Class Imbalance:
Another complicating factor is the highly imbalanced class
distribution (see Fig. 5.2). Some conditions, such as Hernia or Fibrosis, repre-
sent less than 3% of the dataset (with prevalences of 0.45% for males and 0.69%
for females in the case of Hernia), whereas others, like Infiltration, may appear
in more than 35%. This imbalance encourages the model to focus on frequent
pathologies, reducing sensitivity to rare but clinically significant conditions.
Demographic Fairness and Hidden Stratification:
Label frequency often
correlates with patient demographics, compounding fairness challenges. Several
studies [23] have highlighted how hidden stratification, when a model performs
well on average but poorly on specific subgroups, can undermine clinical safety.
For example, studies have noted that conditions like Cardiomegaly may present
differently between genders due to anatomical and physiological factors. Men
generally have a larger thoracic cavity and greater heart mass, affecting heart-
to-lung ratios on X-rays. In contrast, women may exhibit variations in breast
tissue and thoracic fat distribution that obscure cardiac features. [50]
The evaluation metric AUC can also be affected. In our experiments, we ob-
served that even when a model always predicts a class as positive, the AUC
score can still appear artificially high. This happens because AUC does not
penalise false positives as strongly as other metrics like precision. Therefore,
models that tend to over-predict frequent classes might still achieve high AUC,
even if their real performance is poor for minority or less frequent classes.

To address these issues, our evaluation pipeline adopts a two-fold mitigation
strategy centered on enhancing fairness and reliability. First, we employ FIN
to explicitly account for identity-dependent variability in medical imaging mod-
els. As a complementary step, we apply per-class threshold optimisation based
on F1-score. While FIN primarily mitigates disparities in representation and
group-level performance, threshold calibration helps align the model’s proba-
bilistic outputs with discrete classification tasks and mitigate over-prediction
of dominant classes such as No Finding, Effusion, and Infiltration. Together,
FIN and threshold optimisation provide a more robust and fairness-aware eval-
uation framework for multi-label medical image classification. These strategies
are aligned with broader calls in the literature for modelling approaches that
explicitly incorporate structure and fairness considerations in medical imaging.
5.1.2
Group-specific Disease Prevalence Bias
Beyond general class imbalance, demographic disparities in disease prevalence
pose specific challenges for fairness in clinical modelling.
In particular, the
distribution of conditions across gender groups is uneven, and this can lead to
biased predictions if not properly addressed.

this imbalance by summarising the distribution of positive cases per disease and

The observed disparities are substantial: for instance, Emphysema occurs in
males nearly twice as often as in females (ratio 1.78), while Mass, Pleural Thick-
ening, and Atelectasis also exhibit strong male-skewed distributions (ratios 1.57,
1.52, and 1.48, respectively). Conversely, some conditions such as Cardiomegaly
and Hernia are slightly more prevalent in females.
These prevalence imbalances present a dual challenge for the development of
fair clinical models. First, they can lead to performance biases, as models may
over-fit to the patterns more common in the majority group, resulting in re-
duced sensitivity or specificity for under-represented subgroups. Second, they
complicate fairness evaluation: metrics such as subgroup recall, precision, or
AUC can be misleading when sample sizes differ across groups.
Importantly, these disparities may reflect genuine clinical differences, arising
from anatomical factors, making naive rebalancing strategies (e.g., oversam-
pling or equal weighting) potentially inappropriate. As a result, fairness-aware
training methods must mitigate representational biases without distorting the
underlying clinical signal. In this context, FIN, as described in Chapter 3, of-
fers a principled approach to adjust feature distributions across subgroups while
preserving medically relevant variation.

5.2
Experimental Design
All experiments are conducted using the NIH ChestXray14 dataset, which con-
tains 112,120 frontal-view chest X-ray images annotated with up to 14 thoracic
pathologies. Labels are binarised in a multi-label format, and patient gender is
retained to support subgroup fairness analysis.
Images are resized to 320 × 320 pixels, converted to RGB, and normalised using
ImageNet mean and standard deviation. No data augmentation is applied to iso-
late the effects of fairness-aware training. The official train/validation/test splits
are used, ensuring consistency and preventing data leakage through patient-level
sampling: no individual appears in more than one split. Duplicate patient en-
tries are removed, and multi-label stratified sampling is employed during train-
ing to preserve label co-occurrence.
This chapter focuses on evaluating gender-based fairness, analysing whether
model performance differs between male and female patients, and whether these
disparities can be mitigated through group-aware normalisation. Gender is cho-
sen as the sensitive attribute due to its reliable annotation and well-documented
influence on disease manifestation [6].
5.2.1

To evaluate the impact of group-aware normalisation, we compare two model
variants. The baseline is a standard DenseNet-121 architecture pre-trained on
ImageNet The baseline is a standard DenseNet-121 architecture pre-trained on

independent probability scores for each pathology.
Two loss functions were considered during training: binary cross-entropy (BCE)
and Focal Loss. The latter addresses class imbalance by down-weighting well-
classified examples and emphasising harder cases, controlled by tunable param-

The PASSION repository[46] is a dermatological dataset curated to address the
long-standing imbalance in skin medical image datasets, which have tradition-
ally under-represented individuals with darker skin tones. Data was collected
between 2020 and 2023 from four sub-Saharan African countries. The dataset
comprises 1,653 unique patients and a total of 4,901 clinical images with
multiple images per patient. These images were collected under real-world tele-
dermatology conditions using standard smartphone devices, ensuring both prac-
tical realism and accessibility in regions with critically limited access to derma-
tological care[46]. Notably, in many African countries, there are fewer than one
dermatologist per million individuals, as reported by Mosam et al. [32].
The dataset includes metadata such as patient age, sex, body site of the con-
dition, Fitzpatrick skin type (predominantly types IV to VI), and clinical diag-
noses aligned with ICD-10 and ICD-11 standards[46]. The dataset highlights
prevalent pediatric skin diseases in the region, including eczema, scabies, fungal
infections, and impetigo, which together account for approximately 80% of the
cases represented. The datasets added strength lies in its focus on paediatric
patients, a demographic frequently under-represented in dermatological research
despite bearing a significant burden of disease. Moreover, each record includes
an indicator for the presence of impetigo as a co-morbidity, which is important
for differential diagnosis and treatment planning.
This chapter introduces the structure and content of the PASSION dataset, with
a focus on its clinical relevance and utility for training fair and generalisable ML
models in dermatology.

6.1
Skin Tone, Dataset Diversity, and the Impact
on AI Performance in Dermatology
The rapid development of AI models for automated skin disease classification has
brought renewed attention to the importance of dataset diversity, particularly
for skin pigmentation. In dermatology, the Fitzpatrick scale is commonly used to
classify skin types from I (very fair) to VI (very dark) based on their response to
ultraviolet radiation and baseline melanin content. However, the overwhelming
majority of publicly available dermatological image datasets are heavily skewed
toward lighter skin types (Fitzpatrick I–III), with relatively few samples from
individuals with darker skin (Fitzpatrick IV–VI) [27, 36].

6.1.1
Lack of Diversity in Dermatology Datasets and Its

The consequences of this imbalance have been explored in several recent studies.
Daneshjou et al. [36] demonstrated that less than 5% of images in the largest and
most widely used dermatology datasets represent Fitzpatrick IV–VI skin types.

This lack of representation leads to ML models that generalise poorly to under-
represented groups [36]. Specifically, AI algorithms trained predominantly on
lighter skin types exhibit lower accuracy, higher misclassification rates, and a
tendency to miss atypical or subtle presentations in darker skin [27, 36]. As
a result, AI-based diagnostic systems risk perpetuating existing healthcare dis-
parities and may be less reliable for those most likely to benefit from improved
access to dermatological expertise.
This phenomenon mirrors the broader challenge of algorithmic bias in medical
imaging, as discussed in other specialities such as ophthalmology and radiol-
ogy [24, 37]. In particular, just as anatomical differences (e.g., thinner retinal
nerve fiber layers in Black patients) can affect the performance of AI models
for Glaucoma diagnosis [37], phenotypic differences in the appearance of skin
diseases across Fitzpatrick types can have profound effects on the diagnostic
accuracy and fairness of dermatology AI.
6.1.2
Physical and Clinical Differences Across Fitzpatrick
Skin Types
The physical manifestation of many common dermatological diseases can differ
markedly depending on the patient’s skin pigmentation, further compounding
the risk of bias in both clinical and automated diagnosis.
Eczema (Atopic Dermatitis)
Atopic dermatitis, also known as eczema, typically presents with erythema (red-
ness) and scaling. In lighter skin types, erythema is a sensitive marker for disease
activity, and AI models trained on such images often rely on colour signs for clas-
sification. However, in darker skin types (Fitzpatrick IV–VI), erythema may be
absent or much less visible, and lesions often appear as violaceous, dark brown,
or gray patches [48]. Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation is also more pro-
nounced in these populations, potentially leading to underestimation of disease
severity by both clinicians and algorithms.
Fungal Infections
Superficial fungal infections such as tinea corporis are another area where phys-
ical presentation differs across skin tones. Typical signs—such as ring-shaped

rashes with red, raised edges and clearer skin in the center—are often harder to
notice on darker skin types [12]. In Fitzpatrick types IV–VI, redness can appear
as dark spots or a purple tint, and the edges of the rash may look less clearly
defined. As a result, both clinical and automated diagnoses may be delayed or
incorrect if trained predominantly on lighter skin presentations.
Scabies
Scabies is a skin condition leading to severe itching and visible skin changes. In
lighter skin tones, it often shows as red bumps and thin, curved lines. In con-
trast, people with darker skin types may develop darker-colored bumps, thicker
or rougher patches, and may not show the typical redness seen in lighter skin
[5, 7]. Chronic or untreated scabies may also result in pronounced pigmentary
changes that persist long after the primary infection. These differences compli-
cate both human and AI recognition of disease, as features used for diagnosis
are often less conspicuous in skin of colour.
Other Dermatological Conditions
Many other inflammatory, infectious, and autoimmune conditions have different
clinical morphologies depending on skin pigmentation. Even ’non-pigmentary’
findings, such as atrophy, and induration, may be more subtle or require a
different diagnostic emphasis in patients with darker skin.
6.1.3
Implications for AI Fairness and Future Directions
These phenotypic differences have important implications for AI fairness. With-
out explicit efforts to ensure diversity in training data and to evaluate model
performance across all relevant subgroups, DL algorithms risk reinforcing in-
equities and failing those populations most in need of improved dermatological
care.
As recently noted in the ophthalmology literature [47], fairness-aware model
development must consider group-specific anatomical and phenotypic variation.
In dermatology, this translates to collecting balanced and annotated datasets
across Fitzpatrick types, reporting performance metrics stratified by group, and
developing model architectures that do not rely solely on colour-based cues.
Recent AI studies have begun to address these concerns by introducing datasets

such as the Fitzpatrick 17k and by advocating for fairness-aware evaluation
protocols [27, 36].
In summary, the intersection of physical disease expression, dataset diversity,
and AI model design is critical for achieving equitable outcomes in dermatology.
Ensuring balanced representation of Fitzpatrick skin types is not only a matter
of scientific rigour but an ethical imperative in the era of AI-driven healthcare.

6.2.1
Baseline Model
All experiments were performed on the PASSION dataset, which consists of
dermoscopy images annotated by clinical experts. Before training, each image
was resized to 256 × 256 pixels and randomly cropped to 224 × 224 pixels. Data
augmentation strategies included random horizontal and vertical flips as well as
random rotations up to 90◦. All images were normalised to the ImageNet mean
and standard deviation (µ = [0.485, 0.456, 0.406], σ = [0.229, 0.224, 0.225]) to
ensure compatibility with the pre-trained model weights.
A four-class classification task was defined to predict different skin conditions
(condition). To ensure subject-level separation between training and validation,
the dataset was split based on subject identifiers—so that no individual appeared
in both sets. These splits were guided by external files listing the subject IDs
assigned to each partition.
The baseline model used a ResNet18 convolutional neural network pre-trained
on ImageNet as a feature extractor.
The original fully connected layer was
removed and replaced with a randomly initialized linear classifier, mapping the
extracted features to either four outputs for the multi-class task or a single
output for the binary classification task.
No additional normalization layers
(e.g., BatchNorm1d) or Dropout were applied after the backbone, and no FIN
was used in this baseline configuration.
Model training was performed using the Adam optimiser with a learning rate
of 10−4 and a batch size of 32, for up to 50 epochs.
The loss function was
CrossEntropyLoss, early stopping was employed, whereby training was halted
if the validation AUC did not improve for 20 consecutive epochs. After each
epoch, the model achieving the highest validation AUC was saved for later
evaluation.
The primary evaluation metric was the AUC on the validation set, which was
computed at the end of each epoch. Validation loss was also monitored through-
out training, although it was not used for model selection. All experiments were
implemented in PyTorch with data augmentation managed by the torchvision

6.2.2
FIN Model
For experiments with the FIN model all images were preprocessed following the
same pipeline used with the baseline model.
The FIN model architecture is based on a ResNet18 convolutional neural net-
work pre-trained on ImageNet, used as a feature extractor. The original fully-
connected layer was removed and replaced by a randomly initialised linear clas-
sifier, producing four outputs (multi-class). A FIN layer was inserted between
the feature extractor and the classifier. This layer performs normalisation of
the extracted feature vectors according to the group membership of each in-
put, parameterised by the chosen identity variable. The FIN layer maintains
learnable group-wise statistics (mean and variance for each group) and applies
a convex combination of group-normalised and original features, controlled by
a momentum parameter.
During training, both the identity variable and the image were passed to the
model. The number of identity groups and the column used for identity assign-
ment were provided as arguments, allowing experiments across different defini-
tions of sensitive groups. The training protocol was identical to the baseline:
models were trained for up to 50 epochs using the Adam optimiser with a learn-
ing rate of 10−4 and a batch size of 32. The loss function was CrossEntropyLoss

In the RNFLT dataset for Glaucoma classification, FIN effectively reduced racial

The experimental findings indicate that FIN functions as a broadly effective

7.2
Future Work
Evaluation on New Datasets and Clinical Domains
To fully assess the generalisability of FIN, future evaluations should consider its
application to other imaging modalities, such as Magnetic Resonance Imaging
(MRI, as well as to different task types, including segmentation and prognosis.
Moreover, testing FIN on real-world clinical datasets, particularly those drawn
from diverse or historically under-represented populations, would provide valu-
able insights into its robustness and effectiveness in addressing various forms of
bias beyond the original domain.
Methodological Enhancements to FIN
Several directions could be explored to improve FIN. One approach is to extend
the normalisation to incorporate not only demographic attributes (like gender or
race), but also the disease class itself. The intuition is that different pathologies
can introduce specific patterns or shifts in the learned features, and this may
affect the way the model learns in multi-label settings, especially when some
disease classes are more frequent or dominant than others.
In this extension, instead of computing the normalisation statistics (mean and
standard deviation) only by identity group, they are computed conditionally on
the disease label. This could help the model focus more on separating the disease
features and reduce confusion between similar conditions. The normalisation
can be written as:

Conclusion
In conclusion, this thesis replicated and extended the FIN approach in new
contexts within the medical imaging domain. Originally proposed for binary
classification, FIN was here evaluated across increasingly complex tasks, in-
cluding multi-label thoracic disease classification (ChestXray14) and multi-class

skin disease diagnosis (PASSION), while addressing fairness concerns related to
race, gender, and skin tone. The experimental results demonstrate that FIN
can reduce inter-group disparities, as measured by fairness-aware metrics such
as ES-AUC, without compromising standard metrics like AUC and accuracy.
The analysis confirmed FIN’s potential to generalise across various domains and
demographic attributes, with consistent improvements for under-represented
subgroups. Nonetheless, certain limitations emerged, such as residual dispari-
ties in specific classes (e.g., Hernia in thoracic disease classification), motivating
the need for future improvements to the work. Furthermore, the thesis explored
and proposed a class-aware extension of FIN, in which normalisation statistics
are computed conditionally on the disease label to account for label-dependent
feature shifts. Additionally, the importance of parameter tuning (e.g., blending
coefficient m) and the potential for multi-attribute extensions were identified as
promising.
Overall, this work strengthens the empirical foundation for FIN as a fairness
intervention and highlights its applicability to real-world, imbalanced, and het-
erogeneous clinical datasets, paving the way for more equitable DL models in
healthcare.
