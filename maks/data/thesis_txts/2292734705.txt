Abstract
The use of carbon ﬁbre reinforced plastics in diﬀerent types of products is increasing world wide.
A new market where the use of carbon ﬁbre composites is growing is the wind turbine industry,
where the requirements for structural strength and quality of applied materials is very impor-
tant. High quality pultruded carbon spar caps with unidirectional ﬁbre alignment is amongst
the carbon ﬁbre reinforced composites being applied today.
In unidirectional carbon ﬁbre composites the main loading limit comes from the compressive
strength of the carbon ﬁbre reinforced composite, which generally is signiﬁcantly lower than the
tensile strength of the carbon ﬁbre composite. Using new µCT tomography scanners and ad-
vanced micro-mechanical ﬁnite element modelling, the micro structure of pultruded and ﬁlament
winded carbon composites was quantiﬁed by software code implemented in the programming
language python, and subsequently modelled in Abaqus using a UMAT model.
All scanned
specimens were subsequently subjected to compression testing in an attempt to validate the
micro-mechanical model.

Chapter 1
Introduction & motivation
1.1
Motivation and scope of project
Carbon ﬁbre reinforced polymer composites are being used to an ever greater extent as the mate-
rial is not only being applied in the classic industries of sporting goods, automotive manufacturing
and aerospace industry, but are also seeing interest from new industries as viable replacement for
heavier or less stiﬀmaterials. One new industry that is starting to view carbon ﬁbre composites
as a viable material is the the wind turbine industry where glass ﬁbre composites has tradition-
ally been the material of choice due to its relatively high weight to stiﬀness ratio and low cost. A
new important market is the wind turbine industry in which the demand for MegaWatt output
wind turbines, that requires extraordinarily long blades, that are pushing blade manufacturers
to start employing carbon composite as well as glass/carbon hybrid composites in their designs.
These kind of application, where structural and material strength is often the limiting factor,
requires a better understanding of how the strength of carbon composites are aﬀected by diﬀerent
methods of manufacturing.
One of the main limiting strength factors when using unidirectional carbon composites is the
compressive strength, which is widely quoted to be as low as 60 % of the tensile strength of the
material [2] [3] [4] [5]. Furthermore it is also generally accepted that ﬁbre kinking as a result of
plastic microbuckling is a predominant failure mode in carbon composites [6]. The development
of kink bands in composites are generally preceded by a localization of deformation that may
be formed in areas of ﬁbre imperfections - such as patches of high ﬁbre misalignment - which
subsequently leads to shear yielding of the matrix, and subsequent ﬁbre fracture due to bending,
and eventually to the kink band it self [6] [4]. In other words patches or areas of ﬁbre misalign-
ment are very likely to be the initiator of failure when loading carbon composites in compression,
which means that a better understanding to what extent such areas of imperfections are present
in actual composites, and linking it to the compressive strength, can give important clues to
what measures should be taken in composite manufacturing in order to produce stronger and
better materials.
The project described in this report was originally projected to mainly focus on quantiﬁcation
of ﬁbre misalignment in carbon ﬁbre composites manufactured by pultrusion or ﬁlament winding,
as well as subsequent modelling of the compressive strength of the carbon composites using
Abaqus FE in combination with the user subroutine developed by Sørensen, Mikkelsen and Jensen
[7]. In short the goal is to model the actual ﬁbre misalignments found in the carbon composites

Literature review
The ﬁrst paper to touch on the subject of ﬁber microbuckling by Rosen (1965) considered elastic
microbuckling [8]. The analysis considered kink bands from a perspective of elastic shear buck-
ling. The conclusion was that initially straight ﬁbres can be subjected to rotation at compressive
stresses beyond critically compressive stresses equal to that of the materials elastic shear modulus
[9]. This equation was later generalized by Budiansky to eq. (1.1) - where β is the kink band
angle and ET the eﬀective transverse elastic modulus - under the assumption of inextensible
ﬁbres. Experiments using glass ﬁbres and a silicone rubber matrix showed that for long lengths
of ﬁbres the model is valid [2] [6] for elastic microbuckling, but Budiansky and Fleck points to
microbuckling in conventional polymer matrix materials is a plastic rather than an elastic event.

The literature on compressive properties and strength of ﬁbrous material is vast and includes
several contributions of diﬀerent micro mechanical models. These micro mechanical models are
almost exclusively applied to simple analytic or random distributions of ﬁbre misalignment. With
the use of µCT-scanners the ability to obtain information about the actual ﬁbre misalignment
in a composite sample and subsequently subject the same sample to mechanical testing. So far
there have been some attempts to quantify ﬁbre misalignments in composites, but very few seems
to have actually used the found data in mechanical models. Please take note that the symbol σ
will be used as a mathematical quantity describing the variance of digital ﬁlters in the following
section and is not associated with mechanical stress in this section of the report.
Anisotropic Gaussian Filtering Method
Information about the ﬁbre orientation of the 3-dimensional images obtained in the micro-
computed tomography (µCT) can be achieved in a few ways described in the literature.
A
recent article [14] describes three veriﬁed working methods: The Structure Tensor method, the

the rating gives insight into the accuracy when the step size of the elevation angle is reduced to
1◦. Since the Anisotropic Gaussian Filtering method is described as the algorithm with lowest
memory usage, this method is best suited for the 3D images produced in this project which in
general are sized approximately 2000 · 2000 · 2000 = 8 · 109 voxels per image, requiring relatively

The principle idea of applying Anisotropic ﬁltering to obtain ﬁber orientation is rather simple.
First consider a simple 3D volumetric image containing a single ﬁbre, that is misaligned with
an arbitrary elevation angle with respect to the main ﬁbre axis X1 and an arbitrary azimuthal
angle. The ﬁbre is represented by a cylindrical shape of high scalar valued voxels1 similiar to
the cylinders drawn in Figure 2.1, with all surrounding voxels corresponding to matrix material
having the value zero. At all voxels in the volumetric image a 3 dimensional ﬁlter is applied.
The 3D ﬁlter is best visualized as 3 dimensional prolate sphere - or a 3D ellipse - that can be
rotated into any direction, and are similar to the ellipsoids drawn in Figure 2.1. When the ﬁlter
is applied in the image at any given voxel it performs a convolution from the center of the ﬁlter
and outwards, and of course longer in the long direction (σ1 direction, see Figure 2.1) of the
ﬁlter. In simpler words, this means that the ﬁlter produces a response at the center voxel of the
ﬁlter, which has a magnitude based on how well the ﬁlter values match the volume voxel values
that coincide with the ﬁlter. Thus when the ﬁlter is applied such that is has no interaction with
a ﬁbre, the response at the center voxel will be very low or zero. When the ﬁlter is fully or
partially coinciding with a ﬁbre - where the voxels has a high value - the direction of the ﬁlter
becomes important for the response. In Figure 2.1b the ﬁlter center position coincides with the
center of the cylinder that represents a ﬁbre, but the direction of the ﬁlter is badly aligned with
the direction of the ﬁbre. In Figure 2.1a the ﬁlter center has the same position as in Figure 2.1b,
but is better aligned with the ﬁbre, thus producing a higher response and a higher probability
of the ﬁbre actually pointing in that direction. The basic idea then comes down to applying the
ﬁlter at all voxel positions, and trying out all possible directions of the ﬁlter while saving the

programming language Python is described in more details in the following sections.

Finite Element modelling of Kink-bands
The ﬁnite element kink band model applied in Abaqus with the previously mentioned UMAT
subroutine is created as a rectangular slab of material controlled by the ORIENT subroutine
which is a part of the UMAT subroutine and by user inputs in Abaqus (or the corresponding
script that creates the model). Figure 3.29 shows how the model is loaded and how boundary
conditions are applied. When reporting results from the kink band model the stresses given are
the remote stresses which are equivalent for the sum of the forcing shown on the right side in
Figure 3.29 (marked by the blue dashed line) divided by the area at which the force is applied.
The strains reported are based on the average displacement at the remote edge where the force is
applied (blue dashed line) over the original length of the block of material. This of course means
that stresses and strains inside the block of material may be much larger than what is reported
due to localization of deformation. The incrementation in the ﬁnite element model is controlled
by increments of the displacement at the right edge of the material block. Importantly it should

Chapter 5
Discussion and conclusion
5.1
Discussion
The anisotropic Gaussian convolution ﬁltering, also described as the ﬁbre misalignment proce-
dure, constitutes the basis for using the micromechanical model of the ﬁbre composites used in
the project. This of course means that any result eventually produced in the Abaqus model
is heavily dependent on the accuracy of the ﬁbre misalignment results, which, with the results
presented so far, is not considered adequate. The θ ≈5◦bias of the procedure, that was clearly
present in the results for the scanned ﬁbre composites, have no obvious origin. Several features
could be contributing to the inaccuracy of the procedure, including the splitting of the data for
the multiprocessing scheme as well. The equations that the ﬁlter is based on could theoretically
also be at fault, but this would most likely have been discovered in the validation test sets with
10◦ﬁbres. Some tests set using ﬁbres of 0◦misalignment could possible have revealed the issue
sooner, but the other test sets was chosen because a previous implementation that included errors
showed a deﬁnite bias towards ﬁnding angles of 0◦. Albeit it should be noted that the procedure
is not only usable for perfect cylindrical shapes, another obvious source of error could be the
’bulkiness’ of the carbon ﬁbres in the scanned images, showing that the ﬁbres are far from the
ideal cylindrical shape that would suite the procedure best. One more source of error could also
be that the samples when scanned were already misaligned at approximately 5◦, but not only
is it quite improbable by itself, the similarities seen in the standard deviations of the ﬁbre mis-
alignment also points to the results being controlled more by the way the procedure works than
by the actual ﬁbre misalignment in the ﬁbre composites. The orientation tensor was shown to
have a very positive impact on the accuracy of the validation test results, but was unfortunately
never applied to the scanned and processed data due to implementation issues that were not
solved within the time frame of the project. The orientation tensor is, however, applied to the
misalignment data before passing it to the Abaqus FE routine, but the impact on the accuracy,
positive or negative, is not known. As for the input to the FE routine, the variation of the
ﬁbre misalignment indicated in the visual presentation of the misalignment seems to capture the
variation of the misalignment quite well (if of course one can look beyond the pattern introduced
by splitting the data into small pieces). This could mean, if one is to be very optimistic, that
the FE simulations do capture some of the actual behaviour of the ﬁbre misalignment, but only
with scaled up values of the ﬁbre misalignment. A ﬁnal thought on the misalignment procedure
would be a general concern for the need to discretize the angles into integer values (1◦,2◦,3◦and
so on) for the purpose of keeping memory usage to a reasonable limit. It might very well be
that a much ﬁner discretization is needed for misalignment angles in the vicinity of 0◦. This is
65
==PAGE:76==
however not a possibility for data sets of the size used in this project, as the needed amount of
memory would be astronomical for any use of ﬂoating or double precision values1.
The preliminary testing of the compression specimens, and the subsequent FE-modelling of
the standard specimen geometry, gave strong indication that resulting failure stresses measured
in testing are not the same as the actual failure stresses of the composite, since the failure
occurs in regions where the stress state is complex and inﬂuenced by stress concentrations. Fur-
thermore, it was found that the stress state in the area where the specimens actually fail was
likely to be inﬂuenced by the glue ﬁlm thickness of the specimens, which is never recorded or in
anyway accounted for when testing is performed. The alternative geometry of the compression
specimens developed had one major goal: making sure that at least some of the specimens that
were scanned prior to compression testing would in fact break in the scanned area. Despite
the promising results shown in the initial tests of the alternative specimen geometry, the ﬁnal
test were, sadly, a failure with respect to this main goal. The results of the ﬁnal test reported
in section 4.2 is generally quite inconclusive. The specimens that scanned and adhered with
individually cut tabs material seemed to perform poorly, especially for the EPB01-S specimens
that routinely suﬀered from delamination of the tabs. It could easily be argued that, in the
light of these results, it would have been better to use the standard geometry specimens for all
testing. This would however have been doing something that was doomed to fail even before
it was started. As a ﬁnal thought on the geometry of the compression specimens, it should be
noted that the normally advised width of geometry is 10 mm, and that the specimens used in
this project was only 5 mm wide such that it was possible to obtain 3D µCT scans of them.
The results presented from the ﬁnite element simulations, that uses the experimentally ob-
tained ﬁbre misalignment, presents a bit of a paradox. As discussed previously, there is a strong
indication of the ﬁbre misalignment procedure producing inaccurate results, and a complimen-
tary concern that the ﬁbre misalignments found with the procedure is numerical nuisance rather
than actual physical quantities. The results of the ﬁnite element simulations - greatly helped
by the ﬁbre smoothing procedure - is basically very close to what the results could be expected
to look like. This is especially due the lower variation of misalignment in the pultruded EPB01
plate compared to the ﬁlament winded EPA01b plate which can be observed in all the ﬁugres in
section 4.3. These observation are a very strong indication that the ﬁbre misalignment procedure
actually works, and is not just generating random values, albeit something in the procedure still
seems to skew the result towards an incorrect mean value. Putting the quality of the misalignment
input data aside for a moment, the simulations presents a relation between ﬁbre misalignment
and failure stresses that have the same tendencies as widely quoted analytical expressions, but
with a much lower limit load. Comparing the ﬁnite element simulations to the compression tests
performed on the same specimens, the failure stresses found by experiments and those found
by simulations are in the same range, but a direct comparison of the values obtained would not
make sense given the failure mode in the compression tests and the uncertainties of the ﬁbre
misalignments in the specimens.

5.2
Conclusion
A procedure for obtaining ﬁbre misalignment in carbon ﬁbre composite was developed, but the
results of the procedure renders it inadequate for application to uni-directional carbon ﬁbre com-
posites, at least with the current implementation. Other methods should be tested, but with the
requirement of being able to give ﬁner intervals to the misalignment angles than integer values.
The data sets of ﬁbre misalignment should be processed using another method, to see if the scan
quality is actually good enough to be used at all. The pultruded plate (EPB01) is perceived to
have a lesser degree of ﬁbre misalignments than the ﬁlament winded plate (EPA01b). This is not
supported by the ﬁbre misalignment obtained directly from the ﬁbre misalignment procedure,
but is indirectly supported by the ﬁnite element simulation results. No conclusions are made
regarding the actual ﬁbre misalignment of the composites. A better procedure for using multiple
processors with the ﬁbre misalignment procedure must be found if the ﬁbre misalignment pro-
cedure is to applied in the future.
Given the massive complications of the compression ﬁxture, and corresponding compression
test specimen geometry, it should be considered whether there is an alternative way of testing
the compressive properties of (carbon) ﬁbre material. The current method is unlikely to produce
reasonable results, as has been shown in this report and is also indicated in at least two previous
works [23] [25]. For the EPB01 plate compression specimens the alternative specimen geometry
yielded worse results than the standard geometry, while the opposite is true for the EPA01b
plate specimens. A ﬁnite element model, with an elastic-plastic material law for the glue, should
applied in the nearest future to investigate if the delamination of the tab material is caused by to
thin a glue ﬁlm. In general the results are inconclusive at best, and diﬃcult to use as validation
experiments for the ﬁnite element kink band model.
A set of procedures was developed that takes compressive testing of carbon ﬁbre reinforced
composites all the way from macro-scale testing to micro-mechanical modelling.
Great care
was taken in the attempt to achieve the ultimate goal of being able to link micro-mechanical
imperfections, in the form of ﬁbre misalignments, to the results of actual compressive testing
of the very same piece of material. Unfortunately the combined results of poor compression
testing and less than accurate ﬁbre misalignment quantiﬁcation meant, that the link between
the micro structure of the material and the compressive properties were not completely fulﬁlled.
The original scope of the project was far more concerned with the diﬀerence between pultruded
carbon ﬁbre composites and ﬁlament winded carbon ﬁbre composites, but as a result of several
complications along the way, the project ended up focusing on the processes of quantifying
and modelling the micro structures of the materials. The ﬁnite element results gives the best
indications of how the material compares, and the the indications is clearly in favour of smaller
misalignments, and better compressive strengths, for the pultruded (EPB01) material.
