Abstract
Fires following earthquake occurred several times in past (San Francisco, Tokyo,
Kobe or Fukushima) but engineering community did not pay enough attention to
it.
Nowadays, assessment of post-earthquake ﬁre response of building is applied
in performance-based design approach following prior hazard analysis but it is not
incorporated in current standards.
This thesis investigates post-earthquake ﬁre response of a steel building and attempts
to provide insight on behaviour of the structure and its elements during collapse. The
investigation is carried out by means of case study. For that purpose an unprotected
moment-resisting steel frame designed according to the standards on earthquake
resistance (EN 1998-1) is engaged. The analysis is performed with commercial FEM
software Abaqus which allows to simulate both earthquake and ﬁre load.
The seismic response of the building is evaluated by using nonlinear time history
analysis allowing to observe and assess plastic deformations and inter-storey drifts
of the frame which are essential for investigation of the post-earthquake ﬁre. Then
few critical ﬁre scenarios are identiﬁed and subjected to standard ﬁre. The thermal
map of the building is then assessed by using a method for the steel heating provided
by the EN 1993-1-2 for elements directly heated by the ﬁre. The eﬀect of heating
the damaged structure is then observed and special attention is paid to the collapse
mechanisms caused by diﬀerent ﬁre scenarios.
Suggested methodology is tested on simple benchmark models representing main
aspects of the investigation such as dynamic response, geometrical nonlinearities,
plastic behaviour, thermal response etc. Those are validated analytically and against
literature.
Results of the investigation show that the ﬁre resistance of case study building is
not signiﬁcantly reduced as the steel structures is well-designed against earthquake
and does not experience severe damage. Besides, some interesting observations in
collapse mechanisms are described.

Preface
This thesis was prepared at the Department of Civil Engineering at the Technical
University of Denmark in partial fulﬁllment of the requirements for the degree of
Master of Science in Civil Engineering. The work was done from January to July 2016
under supervision of Luisa Giuliani and Varvara Zania, both Associate Professors at
the Technical University of Denmark.
Chapter 1 begins with introduction to the problem of the post-earthquake ﬁres and
provides brief information on ongoing research from which the objectives of this
study are outlined.
Theoretical background necessary to conduct the investigation is described in Chap-
ter 2 along with detailed description of current state of the art. Chapter 3 then
reports methodology of this thesis.
In Chapter 4 the case study building is introduced and subsequently some initial
analyses are undertaken in order to obtain better understanding on behaviour of the
structure.
Chapter 5 is devoted to the structural response to an earthquake. In total seven seis-
mic motions are considered and two of them are analyzed into detail with emphasize
on plastic deformations.
Chapter 6 provides the investigation of structural response to the post-earthquake
ﬁre. Three diﬀerent ﬁre scenarios are analyzed and compared to the response to the
ﬁre acting without prior earthquake. Not only is assessed the reduction of ﬁre resis-
tance after the earthquake, it is also highlighted diﬀerence in behaviour of individual
structural elements during collapse.
Last Chapter 7 contains summary of the investigation, discusses ﬁndings and provides
conclusions and suggestions for further research. Appendices consist of supplemen-
tary information on benchmark testing and additional results of analyses.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Earthquake represents one of the most devastating natural disasters in the world.
Shaking of the ground surface is usually accompanied by some other eﬀects such
as fault rupture, liquefaction or landslide. Earthquake may also trigger a volcanic
activity in aﬀected region, or cause a tsunami, if the epicentre is located below sea
bed. In case the seismic event strikes urban area, it can cause conﬂagration. Many
of earthquake-related phenomena are rare but, however, their consequences have
potential to grow into catastrophic dimensions [1].
Figure 1.1: Post-earthquake ﬁre in San Francisco (USA) in 1906 [2]
Fire following earthquake is probably least studied of them and history shows that it
had been disregarded in a past [3]. Earthquakes in 1906 in San Francisco and in 1923
in Tokyo were followed by ﬁres which are considered to belong to the most destructive
in a peace time history. While San Francisco events resulted into approximately 3000
deaths, number of casualties in Tokyo was around 140000. Nevertheless, the attention
of engineering community was little. Scawthorn and Eidinger [1] name several factors
causing that (in the United States) and these are as follow. Structural engineers
and seismologists were interested only in earthquakes and had limited knowledge
of ﬁre. Fire engineers, on the other hand, focused on ﬁre mitigation and ignored
earthquakes. Finally, conﬂagrations in urban areas happened from time to time in
the United States and the San Francisco post-earthquake ﬁre in 1906 was thereby

regarded as one of many urban conﬂagrations. As time went on, codes on seismic
and ﬁre design were gradually improved, however, connection between two hazards
remained unnoticed.
The United States and Japan were struck again in mid-nineties: Northridge in 1994
and Kobe in 1995. Both earthquakes had similar parameters but regarding the ﬁre
following earthquake, events were diﬀerent. In both cases, approximately 100 igni-
tions was reported. A source of ignition was also similar, ﬁres broke out mainly due
to gas leaks and electric arcs [4]. However, no conﬂagration occurred in Northridge
and ﬁres remained mostly conﬁned in buildings and did not spread whereas in Kobe
large conﬂagrations developed within 2 hours. Extensive spread of the ﬁre was caused
by inaccessibility of many areas due to many collapsed buildings and structures [1].
Nevertheless, wind which usually spreads the ﬁre was calm in both events [4].
In 2010 and 2011 Christchurch in New Zealand was struck by two strong earthquakes.
As reports Baker [4] no major ﬁres occurred. It was likely due to time of a day (no
cooking) and season (no heating in summer). Baker mentions also eﬀect of active
ﬁre protection which is required by New Zealand codes.
But one can ﬁnd another cases when ﬁres occurred. In 1999 ﬁre broke out in reﬁnery
in Izmit in Turkey (Marmara region) right after an earthquake [1], and in 2011
devastating earthquakes struck east cost of Japan and caused dangerous ﬁres among
other consequent eﬀects (such as tsunami, ﬂooding, nuclear accidents in Fukushima
etc.) [5].
Figure 1.2: Fire following earthquake and tsunami in Natori (Japan) in 2011 [6]
So ﬁre do not follow each earthquake as showed some presented events and there
could be ﬁnd many others. One could then contradict that likelihood of a ﬁre oc-
currence after an earthquake is small and therefore the problem of ﬁres following
earthquake does not deserve any special attention in sense of worldwide standard-
ization. But there are two levels or scales of the problem [7]. Large-scale dimension
with ﬁre aﬀecting whole urban areas and low-scale with ﬁre acting in single build-
ings. Since large-scale problem really depends on many factors and there are many
uncertainties therefore probabilistic modelling tools and hazard analyses on urban-
planning level are necessary. But in case of single buildings another fact needs to be
considered (hazard analysis approach may also be used in general). Not only might
ﬁre break out right after an earthquake, it could also occur any time later (before the
building is repaired). Since current seismic design philosophy allows plastic damage

of load-bearing structures [8], buildings become more vulnerable after experienced

1.2
Research Background
Presumably ﬁrst investigation of the problem was done by Della Corte et al. [10]. The
study examined structural response of unprotected moment-resisting steel frames to
both ﬁre following earthquake and ﬁre acting on unaﬀected frames. Response to
earthquake was evaluated by using nonlinear dynamic analysis and ﬁre was modeled
as standard.
By means of parametric study of simple frames and more detailed
research of multi-storey frames Della Corte et al. showed that with increasing damage
of the frames caused by earthquake the ﬁre resistance is reduced.
Similar conclusion brought study of Zaharia and Pintea [7]. Unprotected moment-
resisting steel frames were design according to Eurocode 8 and the seismic resistance
was determined using pushover analysis.
Then the frames were exposed to both
standard and natural ﬁre whereas an inﬂuence of active protection was also con-
sidered. Frames which experienced permanent deformations due to earthquake had
lower resistance to ﬁre and collapsed earlier.
Faggiano and Mazzolani [11] also investigated structural performance of steel frames
exposed to post-earthquake ﬁres and developed method called robustness assessment.
It is based on performance-based approach from US standard FEMA 356 using per-
formance levels newly considering seismic damage and consequences of potential
post-earthquake ﬁre.
Study on 3-dimensional steel frame performance subjected to post-earthquake ﬁre
was conducted by Pantousa and Miskatidis [12]. Their parametric study wass ex-
ploring inﬂuence of strains and rotations in plastic hinges induced by earthquake on
ﬁre resistance and concluded what studies before: initial deformations decrease ﬁre
resistance of a structure.
Important fact was highlighted in study by Yassin et al. [13]. Steel structures are
very vulnerable in case of ﬁre in general therefore they are usually protected by some
sort of ﬁre-proof material. However, earthquake is likely to damage (or partially
damage) this protection and consequently the ﬁre resistance is signiﬁcantly reduced
in comparison to protected structures.
This topic was abundantly investigated in recent past.
Tomecek and Milke [14]
reported by using 2-dimensional example that even relatively small loss of ﬁre pro-
tection of steel column leads to signiﬁcant reduction of the ﬁre resistance. Similarly
Ryder et al. [15] conducted investigation on 3-dimensional steel column protected
by sprayed material and conclusion was the same. Braxtan and Pessiki [16] focused
on beam-column joint in steel moment frame protected by sprayed ﬁre resistive ma-
terial (SFRM) subjected to earthquake and following ﬁre. The study described that
earthquake caused damage to protective layer on beam where plastic hinges formed
and that lead to increase of temperature in both joint and column resulting in faster
reduction of strength and stiﬀness.

Recently the post-earthquake ﬁre issue was investigated by Behnam and Ronagh
[17] who performed analyses of unprotected moment-resisting steel frames. They
used pushover analysis to evaluate seismic response and applied both standard and
parametric ﬁres and results conﬁrmed conclusions of previous studies. Both authors
also investigated vertically travelling ﬁres in tall steel structures [18] and Behnam
[19] then compared post-earthquake ﬁre performance of regular and irregular tall
steel structures. Irregular buildings suﬀered more damage during earthquakes and
hence the ﬁre resistance was reduced more.
Memari at al. [9] focused on steel frames with reduced beam section connections.
The analysis was conducted using nonlinear dynamic analysis to determine seismic
response and uncoupled thermal-mechanical analysis to assess eﬀects of following
ﬁre. Authors investigated behaviour of structural elements with respect to reduced
beam section connections. Besides, conclusion said that global performance of tested
frames was not aﬀected by earthquake (due to selected material model and ﬁre
scenarios).
1.3
Objectives of Research
Message of presented investigations is very clear.
Post-earthquake ﬁres threaten
structural integrity of steel frames and need to be considered in design process as
one of possible scenarios. Reason supporting this stance has already been mentioned.
Current seismic design philosophy allows certain level of damage to the structure and
hereby left it more vulnerable to potential subsequent ﬁre.
However, is it really so? Should the earthquake and the ﬁre be considered as multi-
hazard event and incorporated into current prescriptive codes? Or it is suﬃcient to
include post-earthquake ﬁre only if hazard analysis recommends to do so?
To ﬁnd answers to these questions or at least to contribute with some information
that could help to answer them in the future, that is the main scope of this the-
sis. Another and not less important goal is to get better understandings what is
happening in the structure during collapse caused by this exceptional condition.
For that purpose the research is conducted by means of case study building designed
according to seismic standards and subjected to both post-earthquake ﬁre and di-
rectly acting ﬁre (without any ﬁre protection). Then comparison of collapse times
and mechanisms and behaviours of individual structural elements is undertaken.

Chapter 2
Theoretical Background
Before getting to any calculations or analyses, it is necessary to establish a theoretical
framework which will then serve as a foundation for intended investigation. As this
study aspire to examine post-earthquake ﬁre response of steel building, there are two
main subjects that need to be introduced. This chapter thus explains how does a
steel structure respond to earthquake and how does it behave when the steel elements
are heated. Furthermore, current state of the art is described in the end.
Theory of analytical solutions for seismic response presented in ﬁrst part is limited
to linear systems assuming elastic behaviour of material. The analytical solutions
are subsequently compared to numerical ones in benchmark problem (see Appendix
A). However, theory of numerical methods is not explained here (except evaluation
of Duhamel’s integral) as it is rather large topic and vast mathematical explanations
of these methods can be found in literature.
Second part of this chapter contains theory needed to study behaviour of steel ex-
posed to ﬁre. Beside standard approach to describe heating of steel, material proper-
ties varying with temperature are presented and then some remarks on behaviour of
steel elements are mentioned. All this is later used in investigation of post-earthquake
ﬁre response.
2.1
Structural Response to Earthquake
This section introduces dynamics of structures and especially its practical use in
evaluation of structural response to earthquake. As a ﬁrst step, single-degree-of-
freedom (SDF) systems are brieﬂy introduced along with basic methods applied in
earthquake engineering. These methods - response spectrum and response history -
are subsequently elaborated in context of multi-degree-of-freedom (MDF) systems.

2.3
State of the Art
As it was said in the beginning of this thesis, for a long time structural and ﬁre
engineers did not pay enough attention to the problem of post-earthquake ﬁres.
Research started only two decades ago and the most of interesting scientiﬁc works
was introduced in Chapter 1. This section explores those investigations into detail
and thus reviews current state of the art.
A study by Della Corte et al. [10] is most likely ﬁrst of its kind and serves as source
for later investigations therefore it deserves special attention.
Authors introduce
the problem of post-earthquake ﬁre and then focus on basis of the analysis.
As
also already mentioned an earthquake induces certain level of damage to ordinary
buildings (that is a philosophy of seismic design) and the authors recognize two kinds
of such damage: geometrical and mechanical. Former denotes residual deformations
of a structure and latter degradation of mechanical properties of plasticized regions
in structural elements whereas mechanical damage is disregarded in the study.
In order to determine structural response to ﬁre, following assumptions are ap-
pointed. The ﬁre is modeled using ISO-834 [27] standard ﬁre curve despite it does
not represent realistic ﬁre that could occur in any building. Material model of steel
is adopted from EN 1993-1-2 [28] and eﬀects of hardening or creep are not taken into
account. Temperature ﬁeld is calculated on the cross section whereas heat transfer
along the element is not considered but thermal elongation of elements is. Regarding
the steel elements imperfections and residual stresses are neglected.
Subsequently the authors mention possible eﬀects of active and passive ﬁre protec-
tion and admit that both may be damaged by an earthquake, however, as their
interest is to examine how earthquake-induced structural damage aﬀects consequent
ﬁre resistance, all analyses are carried out on unprotected steel frames.
Once all the assumptions are established two investigations are introduced. A para-
metric study exploring especially eﬀects of geometrical damage on single-story frames
==PAGE:32==
Theoretical Background
24
and a study of multi-storey frames focused on ﬁre resistance reduction.
Results of the parametric study conﬁrm that ﬁre resistance reduces with lateral
deformation and show that vertical load in columns does not have signiﬁcant inﬂuence
on ﬁre resistance if the load reaches at most 30 % of column capacity.
Investigation of multi-storey moment-resisting frames is particularly related to this
thesis and hence even higher attention shall be paid to it. Della Corte et al. introduce
two frames designed according to Eurocode 8 (ENV 1998:1994) and having rigid
beam-column joints, whereas the ﬁrst frame fulﬁlls requirements only for ultimate
limit state (ULS) but the second also for serviceabilty limit state (SLS). Both frames
can be seen in Figure 2.18.

using method of incremental dynamic analysis which consists of a set of nonlinear
dynamic analyses with several ground motions (8 accelerograms) scaled to increasing
intensity (the method is thus some sort of dynamic pushover) [31]. The damage
induced by earthquake is then evaluated in terms of residual inter-storey drifts.

3.1
Concept of Investigation
The core of this study is to determine response of a structure to both ﬁre following
earthquake and directly acting ﬁre and compare them whereas the comparison has
more levels. The basic one may be done by means of ﬁre resistance expressed in
time, i.e. how long is the structure able to withstand the ﬁre before failure [25].
More advanced level lays in determination of the failure mode which can consist
of diﬀerent failure mechanisms [32]. As the failure may be either local (failure of
structural element) or global which is nothing but global collapse of whole structure,
it is essential to observe what is happening in the elements during the ﬁres.

to the building which is essential for subsequent PEF analysis.

analysis is identical for both PEF and DF, the only diﬀerence is potential damage of

temperature in steel elements is evaluated by means of standard method from EN
1993-1-2 [28]. The steel heating curves are then applied in structural analysis.
It is important to note here that all analyses in this thesis are undertaken with a
planar models. This approach has suﬃcient accuracy and requires less computational
eﬀort in comparison to 3-dimensional models [9] [33].
3.3
Analysis Procedures
This section brieﬂy reviews analysis procedures oﬀered by commercial software Abaqus.
Aforementioned steps Static, general and Dynamic, implicit are to be applied in main
investigation. However, some other procedures are employed in diﬀerent stages of
this study either in benchmark tests or preliminary analyses of case study building.
The step Static, general analyzes structural response to static load as its name
cues.
Response can be linear and nonlinear depending on behaviour of material
(elastic or inelastic) and whether large deformations or friction are present. In case
==PAGE:41==
Methodology
33
of nonlinear analysis an option of including nonlinear geometry (nlgeom) need to
be applied. In general, this step is used when inertia eﬀects and time-dependent
material eﬀects may be neglected, therefore it is employed only for gravity load step.
Modal analysis can be run with step Frequency which provides modal character-
istics such as natural frequencies, mode shapes etc. The basic principle is identical
to one described in Section 2.1.2.
Eigenvalue problem may be solved by several
methods, however, default Lanczos method is suﬃcient unless large number of eigen-
modes is required (other methods are computationally more eﬀective). This step is
restricted to linear problems and can be used only for symmetric mass and stiﬀness
matrices [34]. It is requested as a prior step in case of steps based on modal analysis
(response spectrum or modal dynamics).
Step Response spectrum estimates peak response of linear system subjected to
base motion or dynamic force [34]. One needs to deﬁne spectrum (displacement,
velocity or acceleration) and damping of a system which can vary with natural fre-
quencies or modes. Then it is necessary to chose combination method for directions
(as earthquake is not predictable) and combination method for modal responses (lost
time information). The former is not important in this study as all analyses are con-
ducted on planar models and the latter can be found in Section 2.1.2 explained in
detail.
The step Modal dynamic is also based on using a subset of the modes of the system
like previous Response spectrum step and must be also preceded by Frequency step
which provides modal characteristics, hence it too is restricted to linear systems.
The step provides response history that is integrated for each mode and these are
subsequently superposed [34].
It may be thus directly comparable to solution of
Duhamel’s integral.
The step Dynamic, implicit represents a direct-integration method which is rel-
evant when nonlinear eﬀects are expected. For time integration a Hilber-Hughes-
Taylor method is used and it is actually an extension of the Newmark β-method.
Since the method is implicit, the operator matrix is inverted and set of nonlinear
dynamic equilibrium equations are then solved at each time increment [34].
The advantage of this method is unconditional stability of calculations which however
results in high computational cost. The computational power needed to perform the
analysis is thus dependent on a time increment. If time increments are small, whole
set of equations has to be solved many times while if increments are larger, less
computational power is needed [34]. This however depends on what kind of problem
is investigated and therefore the size of time increments must be carefully handled
by the user of FEM software.

it can be seen, this approach of behaviour factor estimation is highly dependent on
assumptions and interpretation of results. It can be, however, concluded that the
behaviour factor q = 3.5 −4 and one can thus assume that the structure belongs to
medium ductility class (DCM) which means that it has increased ability to dissipate
energy in plastic mechanisms.

fact that the motion is relatively small, approximately another additional 20 seconds
would be needed (and it would increase the computational time even more). But one
can aﬀord to move to next step - ﬁre analysis, and let the vibrations vanish. During
ﬁrst 20 seconds of exposure to standard ﬁre temperature in steel element increases
less than 1 °C and one can thus be sure that it does not aﬀect ﬁre analysis.

Chapter 6
Fire Response Analysis
This chapter provides analyses of structural responses to both direct and post-
earthquake ﬁres and compares them. Beside determination of ﬁre resistance for both
events collapse mechanisms are described and special attention is paid to observation
of behaviour of individual structural elements.
Post-earthquake ﬁre scenarios are applied to steel frames that experienced certain
level of damage in prior seismic events. In the last chapter two such events were
selected and analyzed in detail. Consequent ﬁres are then located with respect to
the damage so that one can observe collapse mechanism of whole or, at least, part
of the structure.
Fire scenarios with exactly the same location are afterwards applied to undamaged
steel frame and evoked collapse mechanism is then compared to the one induced by
post-earthquake ﬁre. Of interest is also to compare a time of global collapse in order
to assess reduction of ﬁre resistance in case of scenario with prior earthquake.
6.1
Time-Temperature Curves
The ﬁre is model as standard ﬁre from ISO-834 [27] and it was deﬁned in Chapter
2. Principle of heating of a steel can be also found there and Section 2.2.1 provides
explanation of how to calculate temperature of the steel exposed to ﬁre.
Time-temperature curves of standard ﬁre and heated steel elements can be found
in Figure 6.1. Diﬀerent heating curves are applied for beams (IPE 400, 360 and
330) despite they do not diﬀer much as one can notice. All beams are assumed to
be heated from 3 sides whereas top of the proﬁle is presumed to be protected by
concrete slab.

Collapse Mechanism Summary
Since the columns and beam aﬀect each other it may be helpful to summarize all the
steps of the collapse mechanism so that another scenarios can be easily confronted
to it. A list of collapse sequence is as follows:
• All elements expand in longitudinal direction with increasing temperature.
• Left end of the beam is relatively free to expand, that lead to lateral deforma-
tion of the left column. Right end of the beam and the right column are rather
’supported’ by the rest of the frame structure.
• As those deformations continue, deﬂection of the beam begins being more dis-
tinct and causes rotation of the ends (and whole joints).
• When reached 400°C, steel starts to degrade (eﬀective yield strength and stiﬀ-
ness) that lead to larger deﬂection of the beam and overall reduction in bearing
capacity of all heated elements.
• Then ends of the beam become perfectly plastic and hinges form.
• Consequently, third hinge forms at mid-span of the beam thereby beam be-
comes a mechanism and loses its ability to carry the load.
• From that moment, left column returns to its initial (undeformed) position.
• Beam deﬂects further and then begins being a catenary and hence starts to
pull the columns into a bay.
• Constantly increasing deﬂection causes excessive rotations at the ends of the
beam until ultimate strain is reached and therefore consequent steps shall be
interpreted as likely to happen but without considered possible eﬀects of failed
beam.
• Later on with continually increasing temperature, the load-bearing capacity of
both columns is exhausted and their failure follows.
• Collapse of two columns results into global collapse of whole frame structure.

Chapter 7
Conclusions
7.1
Summary and Discussion
This thesis investigated structural response to post-earthquake ﬁre and attempted
to provide insight what is happening in structural elements during the action and
consequent collapse. The investigation was conducted by means of case study and
for that purpose moment-resisting steel frame was engaged. Important feature of
selected structure was its design against earthquake according to European standard
EN 1998-1 (Eurocode 8) and as it turned out this fact plays central role in this study.
However, before the main investigation itself, necessary theoretical background was
introduced.
Then current state of the art was described and based on that the
methodology for the case study was developed. Consequently, it was important to
make sure that selected analysis procedures in commercial software Abaqus provide
correct results and therefore couple of benchmark tests was performed. Then the
aforementioned steel frame (selected from [7]) was introduced and its ﬁnite element
model established.
Structural response to earthquake was determined by using nonlinear time history
analysis and in order to obtain representative results seven earthquakes was analyzed.
Two of them were selected and examined in detail and subsequently subjected to
post-earthquake ﬁre scenarios. In total three diﬀerent scenarios were analyzed and
always compared to directly acting ﬁre without prior earthquake. Special attention
was paid to observation of collapse mechanism and behaviour of individual structural
elements.
Results showed that ﬁre resistance was not signiﬁcantly reduced by prior earthquake
in the case study. This ﬁnding seemingly contradicts conclusions of the state-of-the-
art scientiﬁc works. The fact, however, is that it does not. Taking into account stud-
ies following European standards [7] [10] it turns out that well-designed steel frames
(fulﬁlling conditions of serviceability limit state) do not experience large deforma-
tions during earthquake and hence the ﬁre resistance reduction is not substantial.
One can ﬁnd this in graph from paper by Della Corte et al. [10] in Figure 7.1.
The results are then in agreement with ﬁndings from aforesaid study. One should
notice, however, that Della Corte et al. were following provisional version of Eurcode
8 from 1994 and that the frame seems to have relatively large dimensions as the design
PGA was 0.2 g (see Figure 2.18).
==PAGE:113==
Conclusions
105
Figure 7.1: Graph showing low reduction of ﬁre resistance for design intensity of
the earthquake [10]
Nonetheless, this does not mean that post-earthquake ﬁre is not threat at all. Studies
following US standard FEMA 365 which is based on performance levels legitimately
call for PEF consideration in codes. The reason is that FEMA allows larger deforma-
tions in buildings (especially in life safety (LS) and collapse prevention (CP) levels)
and that leads to earlier collapse in case of post-earthquake ﬁre.
This thesis had two main goals. First, to answer the question whether it is really
necessary to implement post-earthquake ﬁre problem into current standards. Limited
to European standards and to structural point of view the answer is rather no if the
seismic design fulﬁll damage limitations.
However, there are other aspects that need to taken into account, especially ﬁre pro-
tection. Both active and passive ﬁre protection play considerable role in ﬁre ﬁghting.
Being limited to passive protection (matter of structural engineering) and admit
conservative assumption that undamaged structure would be protected and dam-
aged (after an earthquake) would completely lost its protection, then the diﬀerence
in times of collapse would really be remarkable. One can suggest that this scenario
is rather the worst case and more realistic is to assume only partial lost of protection
(either on some parts of structure or reduce the insulation for whole elements) and
would be probably right. Therefore this issue deserves to be investigated in detail.
The second main goal was to contributed to better understanding of what is hap-
pening in the structure during the ﬁre and consequent collapse and address potential
diﬀerences when acting post-earthquake ﬁre and direct ﬁre.
As it was presented in Chapter 6 the main diﬀerence between scenarios with post-
earthquake ﬁre and direct ﬁre was initial distribution of bending moments. It was
shown that in spite of this initial diﬀerence, ﬁre resistance remains almost unreduced.
One may ask question how is it possible that two frames with diﬀerent moment
distributions respond almost identically to ﬁre? In order to answer that it is necessary
to learn about origin of the diﬀerent distributions ﬁrst.
It was mentioned many times in this thesis that the seismic design philosophy allows
formation of plastic zones in structural elements in order to ensure dissipation of
energy which entered the system during earthquake. Well-established design concept
of strong column and weak beam wants such plastic zones (plastic hinges) to form
in beams since it is good to provide structural stability.
==PAGE:114==
Conclusions
106
As the sections at the ends of the beams plasticize during earthquake, the bending
moment decreases. One can compare presented moment-curvature diagrams (Fig-
ures 5.14 and 5.15 for Artiﬁcial earthquake and Figures 5.27 and 5.28 for Montenegro
earthquake) and see that the decrease depends on rotation (curvature) and whether
the section experienced both positive and negative maximum moments during seismic
motion. If both extremes are registered in diagram and the curvature is rather large,
the moment drops close to zero at the hinges. Due to this the bending moments re-
distribute so that their distribution (after an earthquake) may then be approximated
like one for simply supported beam (Figure 7.2).
Figure 7.2: Parts of the frame with typical distribution of bending moments in
both cases of PEF and DF with analogy to beams
Nevertheless, with increasing temperature the hinges that formed at the ends of
beams lose ability to deform (increase strain) without increase of stress. It would
be better to use Figure 7.3 for explanation. In fact the earthquake-induced strain
is not very high (less than 2 %) and therefore it lays in plastic region (between
proportional limit strain and 2 % strain). The stress in this region is reduced from
very beginning of heating the element (see Figure 4.6 and Appendix B). Thus, as one
can see in Figure 7.3, section ﬁbers with the earthquake-induced strain leave plateau
when temperature rises.
Figure 7.3: Stress-strain diagram for a steel at elevated temperatures
Later on as the temperature still grows and the deformations increase, the section
ﬁbers at the ends of the beams regain plateau and the hinges are thus perfectly plastic
again. Remarkable is the fact that this happens within a range of few seconds in
==PAGE:115==
Conclusions
107
PEF and DF scenario (for speciﬁc ﬁre location). And the reason for it is that hinges
form due to large rotations which are caused by deﬂection of the beam and the
defection is governed by stiﬀness degradation. As the material degrades equally for
both scenarios the perfectly plastic hinges occur almost at the same time. Small
diﬀerence (10 to 20 seconds) in formation of third fully plastic hinge in midspan of
the beam is likely given by initial deformation (slight deﬂection of the beam) induced
by an earthquake.
As it has already been mentioned earlier the earthquake-induced strain is too small
to aﬀect structural response to ﬁre somehow signiﬁcantly. Maybe if it was larger
(more than 2 %) the initial hinges would remain perfectly plastic and that would
lead to earlier collapse (beam with hinges acting as simply-supported would deﬂect
faster than ﬁxed beam) or it could result into diﬀerent failure mode.
Another remark concerns with Scenario B where post-earthquake ﬁre resistance was
paradoxically higher than in case of directly acting ﬁre. The reason was already
explained: in case of PEF scenario bending moments at the ends of the beam is
lower and therefore lower moment is transferred into the columns and that causes
later failure of them.
Nevertheless, this failure mode may be product of assumptions taken in the be-
ginning. One may ask whether the beam would not experience out-of-plain failure
much earlier if the investigation was not treated as a planar problem? And these
doubts could be expanded to whole investigation in this study. The earthquake is
highly unpredictable phenomenon and can struck the building in any arbitrary direc-
tion. If the case study was conducted as 3-dimensional one might observe diﬀerent
distribution of damage (plastic hinges) and perhaps diﬀerent collapse mechanisms.
Also regarding the structural elements the 3D model might provide better insight
into their behaviour. Current 1-dimensional elements do not allow to capture local
failures (such as local buckling) and therefore the investigation is rather limited to
in-plane collapse.
7.2
Conclusions
These conclusions can be outlined based on investigation performed in this thesis:
• Using versatile ﬁnite element modelling software which can simulate both earth-
quake and ﬁre allows to keep all information in one model and maintains, for
instance, dynamic eﬀects to the structure.
• Reducing the investigation to planar problem is computationally eﬀective but
1-dimensional elements cannot capture all failure modes (especially local).
• Dropping decay phase of stress-strain diagram of steel at elevated tempera-
tures allows to observe collapse mechanism but implementation of steel dam-
age model (i.e. the decay phase) and considering detaching of elements in the
model could provide diﬀerent and perhaps more realistic failure modes.
• Reduction of proportional limit suggested in EN 1993-1-2 is conservative and
leads to underestimation of structural response to ﬁre.
Using deterioration
factor, ξ, provides results closer to experimental data.
==PAGE:116==
Conclusions
108
• Damage induced by earthquake to case study building is relatively small. As
for selected seven earthquakes, maximal strains in plastic hinges are in range of
0.6 % to 1.6 % and maximal displacements of the roof between 10 and 19 cm.
Inter-storey drifts fulﬁll conditions of damage limitation in EN 1998-1 in case
that applied earthquake at least roughly ﬁts elastic response spectrum from
the same standard.
• Fire resistance of case study frames that experienced prior earthquake was
reduced only poorly as it was not more than 3.5 %.
• Moment-resisting steel frames designed according to Eurocode 8 and fulﬁlling
conditions of damage limitation (SLS) do not experience large deformations
if subjected to earthquake of design magnitude (or close to it) and their ﬁre
resistance is then not reduced signiﬁcantly. This applies only to unprotected
steel frames.
• Collapse mechanism of beams followed always identical sequence of steps: (a)
thermal expansion, (b) increasing deformations as material degrades, (c) for-
mation of plastic hinges at ends of a beam, (d) further deformations leading
to creation of third plastic hinge at midspan, (e) beam becomes mechanism,
(f) progressing deformations lead to catenary eﬀect, (g) large deformations
(rotations) at the ends cause crossing ultimate strain.
• Initially beam experiences compressive axial force due to thermal expansion.
Consequently, due to large deﬂection axial force changes to tensile and cate-
nary eﬀect occurs. Then the beam behaves like a cable and pulls its supports
inwards. This determines collapse mechanism if the ﬁre is located in outer bay.
• In case that the ﬁre is located in central bay the collapse is governed by columns
(although the time of collapse is inﬂuenced by amount of bending moments
transferred from the beam to columns).
7.3
Further Investigation
Following list summarizes suggestions for further investigation related to this topic.
Some of them have already been outlined in this chapter:
• To include eﬀects of passive ﬁre protection. As mentioned considering conser-
vative assumption that undamaged building is protected and damaged building
lost its protection will unsurprisingly lead to signiﬁcant diﬀerence of ﬁre resis-
tance. More interesting would be to perform a parametric study with certain
levels of insulation reduction (e.g. 90 %, 75 % of insulation etc.) which would
simulate use of ﬁre-proof panels, for instance. Another option is to simulate
use of intumescent painting or spray which is expected to be damaged at lo-
cations with plastic deformations (plastic hinges). Those locations would be
then modeled as unprotected while the rest of the structure would keep its
insulation. In such case a thermal analysis would need to be performed and
consequently the model would be subjected to structural analysis.
• To use 3-dimensional model. As an earthquake can struck the building in any
direction one could simulate the earthquake acting in some nonorthogonal angle
==PAGE:117==
Conclusions
109
and that would perhaps provide unusual distribution of damage. Consequent
ﬁre could then result into diﬀerent collapse mechanism than in case of directly
acting ﬁre.
• To use more advanced representation of material. One could include decay
phase in stress-strain diagram and establish damage model in FEM software
and one could also consider detaching of ’damaged’ ﬁnite elements (exceed-
ing ultimate strain) which could lead to observation of more realistic collapse
mechanisms. Material description could also contain eﬀects of hardening or
creep.
• To apply parametric ﬁre. As standard ﬁre is used for ﬁre resistance evaluation
it was reasonable to apply it in this thesis. However, the standard ﬁre does
not represent any realistic ﬁre and therefore better understanding of structural
behaviour could be obtained by using natural/parametric ﬁre.
One might
observe what is happening especially during colling phase or investigate both
horizontally and vertically traveling ﬁres.

Displacement time history at any node of the frame can be calculated using step
Modal dynamics whereas such displacement response may be directly compared to
Duhamel’s integral (as described in section 2.1.2). Another option represents step
Dynamic, implicit which is direct-integration method primary intended for nonlinear
dynamic analysis [34] but there is no restriction on using it used also for linear
analysis (although it is not usual due to high computational cost).
In this case, two Abaqus methods diﬀer (beside diﬀerent computational approach)
in deﬁnition of damping and and how the seismic motion is applied. Step Modal
dynamic may use direct modal damping (structural damping can vary with modes).

nodes are restricted. In Dynamic, implicit step, Rayleigh damping is used (deﬁned

unrestricted degree of freedom. For both methods, the time step increment ∆t =

vertical direction in order to ensure same boundary conditions like in analytical

A.2
Fire Response Benchmark
A.2.1
Introduction
This section reports ﬁre response test performed with Abaqus. Main scope of this
benchmark test is to ensure correct behaviour of Abaqus in case of steel structure
exposed to ﬁre and to evaluate several material models and select one which seems
to provide most realistic performance.
For this purpose the frame described and experimentally tested by Rubert and Schau-
mann [36] was chosen. It was also used for benchmarking of ﬁnite element models in
several papers [9] [37] so that the comparison can also be done against another ﬁnite
element models.
==PAGE:128==
Benchmarks
120
The frame called ZRS [36] is used for the benchmark test. It is one story and two
bay steel moment-resisting frame. Dimensions and all the test setting is shown in
following Figure.
Figure A.14: Conﬁguration of the test on steel frame [9]
All cross sections are made of IPE 80 proﬁles and steel S355 with yield strength
fy = 355MPa. In top nodes of the frame, vertical forces P1 are acting and in one of
the nodes is acting also a lateral force P2. This forces arrangement regulates future
collapse of the frame. Fire load is located in the left bay.
