Abstract—Urban flooding is a growing problem due to climate
change, posing significant risks to infrastructure and urban
mobility. Although detailed flood simulations provide accurate
results, they are too slow and expensive to integrated urban adap-
tation planning, as envisioned in the MA’AT framework. In this
study, we develop and evaluate a surrogate modeling framework
designed to accelerate flood scenario assessment which combines
advanced graph embedding techniques (Node2Vec, GIN) with
Gaussian Process regression to approximate the impacts of heavy
rainfall events on the Copenhagen transport network. Using high-
resolution SCALGO flood data for multiple rainfall scenarios,
we extracted key metrics, such as mean flood depth and travel
disruption, and used them to train the surrogate models. This
graph-based approach significantly reduces computational time
while retaining high predictive accuracy, enabling cities to assess
flood risks more effectively and improved climate adaptation
planning with broader decision-support tools.

I. INTRODUCTION
Flooding remains one of the most disruptive natural dis-
asters worldwide, causing significant infrastructure damage,
economic loss, and disruption to daily life [1]. Researchers
often look at flooding caused by rivers (fluvial) or heavy
rain (pluvial), but flooding induced by rising groundwater has
gotten less attention even though it has long-lasting and serious
effects [2] [3].In cities like Copenhagen, which are low-lying
and have a lot of roads, pluvial flooding may quickly fill
up stormwater systems and make it hard to go around, even
when it’s only light rain [4]. The traditional way of simulating
floods with SCALGO [5], for example, is not appropriate for
large area scenario simulations and for simulations that need
to happen in real-time. An acceptable solution to the issue is
Machine Learning based flood surrogate systems, which learn
from simulation data to estimate the level of flooding with
a certain degree of accuracy. Recent research has explored
the use of graph-based representations and neural methods for
flooding and spatial analysis [6]. Graph structures capture the
spatial relationships between regions, and graph embeddings
can summarize complex interactions for machine learning.
Surrogate models like Gaussian Process Regression (GPR) are
well-suited to hydrological modeling tasks because they can
quantify predictive uncertainty, enabling risk-aware decision-
making [7]. There have been many studies that show Gaussian
Process (GP) models are good at predicting different things
about water resources [8] [9] but they are still not widely
used for predicting floods. One of the first surrogate models
based on the GP (Kriging) approach for predicting floods in
mountainous areas was created by Contreras [10].They showed
that it can quickly and accurately estimate flood response
for extreme precipitation events with very little computational
resources.
Several studies have used Gaussian Process Regression
(GPR) and other surrogate modeling techniques to predict
flood impacts using tabular input data such as rainfall, ter-
rain slope, and soil type. For instance, [11] modeled coastal
flood risks using GPR with functional inputs, but without
considering spatial structure. Similarly, [12] compared GPR
and Polynomial Chaos for predicting flow in open channels,
focusing on input parameter uncertainty. Other recent studies
apply GPR to simulate flood depth in urban areas [13], or
use Kriging-based models to estimate flooded regions [14],
but these also do not model spatial relationships between
zones. The integration of graph embeddings as input features
to GP models provides a flexible and powerful framework for
rapid flood impact prediction, facilitating scenario analysis and
adaptation planning at urban scale [15].
In this work, we develop and evaluate a surrogate modeling
workflow that combines advanced graph embedding methods
(Node2Vec, GIN) with GP regression, trained using flood
depth and transport network metrics extracted from high-
resolution SCALGO flood simulations for Copenhagen. Our
goal is to assess the trade-off between computational efficiency
and predictive accuracy, providing actionable insight for inte-
grated assessment models like MA’AT [16] and supporting
scalable, data-driven urban resilience planning [17].
This paper is organized as follows.Section 2 describes the
overall methodology, including data collection and preprocess-
ing steps. SCALGO flood modeling process, and our surrogate
modeling approach using graph embeddings (Node2Vec and
GINE) combined with Gaussian Process Regression (GPR).
Section 3 presents the experimental results and evaluates the
performance of the surrogate models. Section 4 contribution
of our work. Finally, Section 5 provides a discussion of the
findings and concludes the study.

B. Flood Depth Extraction
An essential part of our process was making sense of
the raw flood raster data by translating it into zone-specific
insights. For each rainfall scenario, we assessed how much
flooding occurred within each Traffic Analysis Zone (TAZ)
in Copenhagen. We did this by overlaying the high-resolution
SCALGO flood maps with the TAZ boundaries, which helped
us move from pixel-level detail to summary statistics that
could actually inform planning and analysis at the neighbor-
hood level.We carried out this process using Python, primarily
with the rasterio and geopandas libraries, which made it easier
to handle the spatial data and automate the extraction.
It was very important for this study to divide the zones
into two groups: ”flooded” and ”not flooded.” We used a
practical thresholding method here that was based on research
in hydrology and urban planning that had already been done.
If the average or maximum flood depth in a zone was more
than 0.3 meters, that zone was considered flooded. This level
is the usual depth at which water starts to seriously affect
transportation and daily life. It is commonly used in the
literature to show when conditions become dangerous or im-
passable for vehicles and pedestrians [20]. Setting a threshold

C. Network Construction
To understand how flooding might affect travel in Copen-
hagen, it was important to have a clear picture of the city’s
transportation system. The idea behind this method was to
represent the city as a graph, with each node representing a
Traffic Analysis Zone (TAZ) and each edge showing a possible
route between neighboring zones. This method of graph-
based network abstraction is widely used in transport and
infrastructure research, as it provides both analytical flexibility
and interpretability for scenario analysis.
Edges were primarily defined by spatial adjacency, meaning
that two TAZs were connected if their polygons shared a
boundary. This adjacency-based approach ensures that the
network topology closely reflects the real connectivity of urban
spaces, as supported by studies in urban graph analytics. In
addition to adjacency, we use non-touching zones manually
because of the significant connections for real-world linkages
such as bridges or tunnels that might not be captured by pure
geometry alone. The geometric centroids of each TAZ served
as the reference points for calculating edge attributes.
We gave edge weights in two steps to show both the
network’s geographic layout and how it works. The first step
was to find the Haversine distance between the centers of
connected zones. This gave us a simple way to measure how
far apart they were. The second step was to use the baseline
travel times from the OD matrix to describe the ”costs” of
edges when there was no flooding. The model changed these
weights in real time when simulating flood scenarios. If either
node of an edge was classified as flooded (i.e., went above the
critical flood depth threshold), the edge weight was set to a
value so high that it made the route impossible to pass.

III. RESULT
This section presents the outcomes of our proposed graph-
based surrogate modeling approach for predicting flood im-
pacts across varying rainfall scenarios in Copenhagen. We
analyze the progression of flood depths, spatial network ef-
fects, learned graph embeddings, and the predictive accuracy
of Gaussian Process Regression (GPR) using both Node2Vec
and GINE-derived embeddings.
A. Scenario-Level Metrics and Network Impact
To quantify the system-wide effects of flooding, we compute
a set of spatial and network-level metrics for each rainfall
scenario: mean flood depth, maximum flood depth, percentage
of flooded nodes (defined as nodes with depth > 0.3 m), per-
centage of disconnected nodes, and percentage of unreachable
origin-destination (OD) pairs.
Figure 3 presents the evolution of these metrics with in-
creasing rainfall intensity. The percentage of unreachable OD
pairs increases rapidly, surpassing 70% beyond 40 mm and
approaching 99% beyond 120 mm of rainfall. Likewise, the
percentage of flooded nodes rises steadily and reaches nearly
80% in the most extreme scenario. These results underscore
how even moderate rainfall events can induce widespread
disruption in the urban road network, especially in dense and
low-lying areas.

B. Embedding Sensitivity to Rainfall and Flooding
To explore how learned graph representations capture struc-
tural changes under flooding, we plot selected embedding
dimensions from both Node2Vec and GINE models as a
function of rainfall intensity Figure 4 and the percentage of
flooded nodes Figure 5. For Node2Vec, the dimensions exhibit
smooth nonlinear trends aligned with rainfall progression.
For GINE, the embeddings vary more sharply due to direct

These plots confirm that both embedding strategies encode
meaningful structural differences between scenarios. Notably,
Node2Vec embeddings change more smoothly, suggesting
greater robustness for interpolation tasks, while GINE reflects
sharper transitions tied to localized disruptions.

V. SUMMARY AND CONCLUSIONS
In this work, we developed a surrogate modeling framework
to predict urban flood impacts more efficiently, with a focus
on integrating into climate adaptation tools like the MA’AT
framework. By combining high-resolution flood simulation
data from SCALGO with detailed transport network modeling,
we created a spatial graph where each node represents a
Traffic Analysis Zone (TAZ), and edges reflect travel-time-
based connectivity.
We generated graph embeddings using Node2Vec and GINE
to capture the structural behavior of the network under var-
ious rainfall conditions. These embeddings were fed into a
Gaussian Process Regression model along with the intensity
of the rainfall. The surrogate model included uncertainty
quantification, which is essential for risk-informed decision-
making, in addition to providing precise estimates of flood
metrics like zone-level flood depth and travel disruption.
Our studies demonstrate that Node2Vec works equally as
well as the more sophisticated GINE model, which makes it
a good choice. The most essential thing is that the surrogate
model makes computation time a lot faster than full SCALGO
simulations,enabling faster evaluation of multiple flood scenar-
ios. This makes it perfect for use in real-time decision-support
systems and makes it easy to scale up for citywide resilience
planning.
a) Future Work.: Although the model performs well for
most types of rain, it is less accurate for more intense events.
This is probably because the training data doesn’t contain
many examples like this. More varied and incredibly wet
scenarios should be included in the training.Examining the
model’s response to other factors that are known to contribute
to urban flooding, such as the size of the drainage system,
the tides, or the soil’s moisture content, would be beneficial.
Finally time-based data or graph models that are subject
to change over time may aid in our understanding of how
floods occur, particularly in areas with intricate landscapes or
infrastructure.
