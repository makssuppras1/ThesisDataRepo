Abstract
Bacillus cereus is a human pathogen that can be present in food, especially dairy products.
It is important to control the presence and growth of this bacteria to ensure consumer
safety. For this purpose, predictive models for mesophilic and psychrotolerant B. cereus
has been developed by Maktabdar (2024) and these are evaluated in the following study.
Multiple methods for ensuring the safety of dairy products exist. The addition of antimi­
crobial substances to the products is one such method. Phosphate melting salts are an
important ingredient of processed cheese, that also has antimicrobial effects. The organic
acids benzoic and sorbic acid also has an antimicrobial effect in dairy products.
However, these antimicrobial effects that can be added to processed cheese need to be
taken into account by the models, for the models to be able to predict correctly and to be
validated for the use on processed cheese.
Here, the inhibitive effect of phosphate melting salts and specific organic acids in pro­
cessed cheese on the growth of mesophilic and psychrotolerant B. cereus is shown from
14 challenge tests and minimum inhibitory concentrations (MICs) were investigated for
these. Further model evaluation of the models by Maktabdar (2024) using the obtained
data and data from relevant literature is carried out.
The MICs of di­ and triphosphate melting salts in processed cheese for mesophilic B.
cereus are found to be 1.64% and 3.73% in the water phase of the product respectively.
For psychrotolerant B. cereus the MICs of di­ and triphosphate melting salts and sorbic
acid was found to be 1.33% and 1.42% phosphate melting salt in the water phase respec­
tively, and 0.24 mM undissociated sorbic acid the water phase.
From the model evaluation, the bias factors for the mesophilic model are found to be
acceptable when using data from challenge tests and good when using data from the
literature. In contrast, the psychrotolerant model shows unacceptable bias factors with
challenge test data but good bias factors with literature data. No accuracy factors were
found to be acceptable.
The MICs found in the current study are important for the future evaluation of the models
by Maktabdar (2024), since these can be considered when evaluating processed cheese.
Further experiments are however needed to obtain more secure MICs.

Introduction
1.1
Aim and purpose
This Masters thesis aims to investigate the effect of specific antimicrobials on the
growth of mesophilic and psychrotolerant Bacillus cereus in processed cheese. These
antimicrobials include phosphate melting salts (di­ and triphosphate salts), benzoic acid,
and sorbic acid. The limited information available in current literature regarding their im­
pact on B. cereus growth motivates this selective investigation. Furthermore, the project
aims to use the obtained knowledge, to evaluate the performance of predictive models
for growth of mesophilic and psychrotolerant B. cereus by Maktabdar (2024).
Over the years several studies have been conducted on the growth of B. cereus in
certain dairy products (Bursová et al., 2018; Necidová et al., 2014; Tirloni et al., 2017a;
Tirloni et al., 2017b; Valík et al., 2003), however the inhibitory effect of phosphate melt­
ing salts has not been extensively reported (Fusieger et al., 2024). The inhibitory ef­
fect of phosphate melting salt has however been reported for other organisms such as
L. monocytogenes, Clostridium sporogenes and Bacillus thuringiensis (Fusieger et al.,
2022; Koukou et al., 2022; Martinez­Rios et al., 2019b).
The inhibitory effect of benzoic and sorbic acids on B. cereus has been investigated by
Bannerjee and Sarkar (2004) , Roy et al. (2007) and Thomas et al. (1993) in laboratory
media where MIC values of 0.06­1.02 mM undissociated benzoic acid and 0.44­5.65 mM
undissociated sorbic acid in the water phase were determined. This knowledge is how­
ever not specific to dairy products. The inhibitory effect has also been analysed in other
food products, such as tomato juice (Biswas et al., 2015).
Based on the available data from previous research a hypothesis is formed that the
minimum inhibitory concentrations (MIC) of both phosphate melting salts and benzoic and
sorbic acid for psychrotolerant and mesophilic B. cereus in processed cheese are equal
to the ones found in the model by Maktabdar (2024). It is further hypothesised that the
similarity of MIC values will lead to acceptable model evaluation for this specific product.
1.2
Introduction to Bacillus cereus
Bacillus cereus sensu lato (s. l.) is a group of closely related microorganisms. They
are found in soil and are therefore able to contaminate many types of food products, such
as vegetables, fruits, grains, milk and others (Lindbäck and Granum, 2019; Matthews
et al., 2017). All bacteria within the group are Gram­positive, spore­forming, facultative
aerobic and rod­shaped.
Until 2014 there were eight officially recognised species included in the group. These
were B. anthracis, B. cereus sensu stricto (s.s.), B. cytotoxicus, B. mycoides, B. pseu­
domycoides, B. thuringiensis, B. toyonensis and B. weihenstephanensis (Carroll et al.,
2021; Lindbäck and Granum, 2019; Liu et al., 2015; Matthews et al., 2017). The de­
velopment within the field of genetic techniques keeps expanding, and therefore more
bacteria are continuously added to the B. cereus (s. l.) group. In 2016 B. wiedmannii
was added and in 2017 the list was expanded drastically because of whole genome se­
quencing (WGS). In 2017 B. albus, B. luti, B. mobilis, B. nitratireducens, B. pacificus, B.
paramycoides, B. paranthracis, B. proteolyticus and B. tropicus were added. In 2020 the
addition of B. fungorum was made (Carroll et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2015).

Not all B. cereus are equally pathogenic. B. anthracis is able to cause anthrax which
is not a foodborne illness and other strains are able to cause either food poisoning or
food intoxication. However, there are also organisms within B. cereus (s.l) that are used
as probiotics and therefore are seen as having a positive effect on the microbiota in the
human gastrointestinal system. Because of this, and the fact that the different isolates
are so closely related, it is important to be able to classify the different strains of B. cereus
(s.l.) into different taxonomic groups (Carroll et al., 2021; Guinebretiére et al., 2008).
To better categorise the bacteria within the B. cereus (s.l.) group Guinebretiére et al.
(2008) were able to establish a genetic structure based on Fluorescent Amplified Frag­
ment Length Polymorphism (fAFLP) banding patterns and pantoate­β­alanine ligase gene
(panC) phylogeny, that includes 14 subgroups and seven major genetic groups. Each
panC group have specific ranges of temperatures at which growth is possible and there­
fore also different cardinal parameter values for the minimum temperatures for growth.
These are shown in table 1.1 as well as values for minimum pH and minimum water ac­
tivity Aw at which growth is possible (Carlin et al., 2013; Guinebretiére et al., 2008).

The groups with the lowest growth temperature range are group VI and group II. It is
therefore suggested that these two groups include most of the psychrotolerant B. cereus
strains. It is interesting to note that the two phylogenetic groups II and VI are distant from
each other. Group VII can be considered moderately thermotolerant, since the Tmax of
this group is 55°C and this also reflects on the high Tmin. The groups that lie in the middle
of the temperature scale are considered to be mesophilic and these groups are I, III and
IV. Group V is considered to be an intermediate group. All phylogenetic groups have
a similar breadth of temperature range at which growth is possible (Carlin et al., 2013;
Guinebretiére et al., 2008).
All phylogenetic groups can colonize food products, but it is important to consider the
specific environment of each food product to determine which groups the relevant strains
belong to(Carlin et al., 2013; Guinebretiére et al., 2008).
Of the eight taxogenomic species within the B. cereus (s. l.) group in 2014 the

highly polyphyletic, meaning different strains obtained from different sources are found
in different phylogenetic groups, not only the psychrotolerant groups but also group III,
which is known to include B. anthracis and emetic­toxin producing strains (Carroll et al.,

1.2.1
Endospores
There are three spore­forming organisms of significance when considering food safety
and these are Clostridium botulinum, Clostridium perfringens and B. cereus. The for­
mation of endospores is called sporulation and occurs mainly in the stationary phase
of growth because of nutrient depletion (Lindbäck and Granum, 2019; Matthews et al.,
2017). Once conditions become more favourable the endospores are able to transform
into vegetative cells. These processes, the structure of the endospore and the resistance
will be described in this section (Matthews et al., 2017).
1.2.1.1
Sporulation
The sporulation process has seven stages. In the first stage, cell division occurs,
producing a larger mother cell and a smaller forespore, which initially has only 30% of its
chromosomal material. The remaining material is transferred to the forespore, resulting
in both cells having complete, nearly identical chromosomes, with the forespore’s being
more condensed (Setlow and Johnson, 2019).
In the second stage, the forespore is engulfed by the mother cell, ending up within
two membranes: the inner spore membrane (IM) and the outer spore membrane (OM).
Transitioning to stage three involves septum hydrolysis and the movement of membranes
(Setlow and Johnson, 2019).
During stage four, the cortex forms between the two spore membranes, and the
forespore synthesizes glucose dehydrogenase and small acid­soluble proteins (SASPs),
creating resistance to UV and chemical stress. The forespore’s pH drops by 1 to 1.5 units,
and dehydration begins (Setlow and Johnson, 2019).
In stage five, proteinaceous coat layers are added outside the OM by the mother
cell, using about 80 different proteins, providing gamma radiation resistance. Dehydration
continues (Setlow and Johnson, 2019).
Stage six involves the mother cell producing dipicolinic acid that chelates with cal­
cium (CaDPA), which is transported into the spore core, completing its dehydration and
leading to metabolic dormancy (Setlow and Johnson, 2019).
In the seventh stage, autolysin is produced by the mother cell, causing lysis and
releasing the newly formed spore (Setlow and Johnson, 2019).
In figure 1.1 some of the stages of sporulation are shown.
1.2.1.2
Activation, germination and outgrowth
When conditions become favourable, endospores can return to their metabolic state.
Activation is not required for germination but speeds up the process. Activation agents
include low pH, specific chemicals, and sublethal heat. The specific changes during acti­
vation are largely unknown (Setlow and Johnson, 2019).
Germination does not require metabolic energy, distinguishing it from outgrowth. It can
be triggered by compounds like amino acids, sugars, salts, calcium dipicolinate (CaDPA),
long­chain alkylamines, or high pressure. Nutrient germinants activate nutrient germinant
receptors (nGRs) in the inner spore membrane (IM) (Setlow and Johnson, 2019).

Figure 1.1: Some stages of the sporulation process including the cell division (stage II),
engulfment of the forespore into the mother cell (stage III), cortex formation and syntheti­
sation of SASPs (stage IV), complete dehydration and spore dormancy (stage VI), lysis of

During germination, several processes occur. Initially, endospores reach a point of
no return, releasing protons and monovalent cations, and changing IM permeability. About
10% of CaDPA leaks out, which can take as little as 30 seconds, though there may be a
significant lag time. Endopores with long lag times are called superdormant endospores.
The release of CaDPA and ions increases the water concentration in the spore core by
about 30%, reducing heat resistance (Setlow and Johnson, 2019).
The second process is cortex degradation, allowing the spore core to rapidly increase in
volume due to water intake once the restricting core is removed (Setlow and Johnson,
2019).
Cell outgrowth begins when core hydration matches that of a growing cell, enabling
protein mobility and reactions. The cell’s volume increases, necessitating the synthesis of
cell wall and membrane components. ATP and NADH are generated from phosphoglyc­
eric acid (PGA) stores, and small acid­soluble protein (SASP) degradation begins (Setlow
and Johnson, 2019).
Early in outgrowth, glycolysis is the primary carbohydrate metabolism, though some
endospores may lack TCA cycle enzymes, stopping metabolism at acetate. RNA synthe­
sis occurs rapidly, utilizing stored nucleotides or those from degraded endospore RNA.
mRNA synthesis begins, leading to protein synthesis, supported by amino acids from
SASP breakdown. DNA replication starts late in outgrowth (Setlow and Johnson, 2019).
1.2.1.3
Structure of endospore
The structure of endospores is very different from that of vegetative cells. Endospores
consist of six layers, some of which are also found in growing cells, while other are not.
These layers and their function are described in the following and the endospore structure
is seen in figure 1.2.
4
==PAGE:11==
Figure 1.2: Structure of the endospore including exosporium, spore coat, outer mem­
brane, cortex, germ cell wall including nutrient germinant receptors, inner membrane and
core. Within the core calcium dipicolinate and DNA bound to small acid­solubke proteins.
The figure is not to exact scale. The figure was created using Biorender.com
The outmost layer of the B. cereus endospore is the exosporium, but not all types
of endospores have this layer. The exosporium is built to exclude large molecules and
also plays a role in the endospore’s adhesion to surfaces. The constituents of the ex­
osporium are lipids (18%), polysaccharides (20%), and proteins (52%) that are specific to
endospores including some glycoproteins and ash (4%) (Matz et al., 1970).
Beneath the exosporium, the spore coats are found. The coats can consist of up
to 80 different proteins that are all unique to endospores. The different roles of all the
proteins are not known but some are needed to facilitate the access to nGRs for nutri­
ent germinants, and others can inactivate possible germinants when germination is not
wanted. Within the endospore coats protein­protein cross­links are found which protect
the inner layers against some chemicals and play a minor role in UV and wet­heat resis­
tance (Choi and Kim, 2020; Setlow and Johnson, 2019).
Beneath the spore coats the outer membrane is found. This membrane is of importance
to the developing forespores, but it is not known what function it serves in the dormant
endospore (Setlow and Johnson, 2019).
Beneath the outer membrane, the cortex is found, which is made up of peptidoglycan
(PG). The PG of the cortex is similar to the PG of vegetative cell walls, but with several
differences such as the content of diaminopimelic acid and the amount of cross­linkage
within the PG. During sporulation, the cortex is hydrolysed, which is a crucial process for
successful sporulation. The main function of the cortex is to protect the inner membrane of
the endospore against organic solvents, while at the same time letting germinants through
(Cho and Chung, 2020; Setlow and Johnson, 2019).
5
==PAGE:12==
Under the cortex the germ cell wall is found and beneath this the inner membrane
of the endospore lies. The IM is a functional membrane with a strong permeability, but a
frozen structure, meaning the membrane is mostly immobile. The phospholipid content
of the IM is similar to that of vegetative cells. The purpose of the IM is to protect the spore
core from chemicals that can otherwise damage the endospores DNA (Cho and Chung,
2020; Setlow and Johnson, 2019).
The innermost layer of the endospore is the core and this contains the DNA, CaDPA
and other divalent cations that are released during germination. It also includes the large
pool of SASP, that is mostly bound to the endospore DNA. The difference between the
spore core and the core of vegetative cells is the concentration of water. Vegetative cells
have 4 g of water per g of dry matter, while endospores only have between 0.4 and 1 g of
water per g dry matter. This low water content plays a large role in the dormancy of the
endospore and the resistance (Setlow and Johnson, 2019; Cho and Chung, 2020).
1.2.1.4
Resistance of endospores
Endospores are known to be very resilient and are therefore resistant against many
types of environmental factors. These and the mechanism of resistance will be described
in the following.
Resistance against both wet and dry heat of endospores is of crucial importance
in the food industry as this is the main methods used for the elimination of vegetative
cells. The heat resistance of endospores can be quantified with a DT value meaning a
time in minutes at temperature T°C at which it takes to eliminate 90% of an endospore
population. Generally endospores are able to withstand 40°C more wet heat than their
vegetative counterparts and 30°C for dry heat (Setlow and Johnson, 2019; Setlow, 2014).
The mechanisms for the resistance to both wet and dry heat are very similar, mainly by
the saturation of endospore DNA with SASPs. However dry heat is able to create some
DNA damage, such as depurination where the N­glycosidic bonds are cleaved to release
the corresponding adenine or guanine from DNA. This damage is repaired during the
outgrowth of the endospores, meaning that proteins aiding in this process are important for
the dry heat resistance of endospores. The large amount of DPA stored in the endospores
also plays a role in the dry heat resistance, but exactly how is not known (Setlow, 2014).
Wet heat resistance is obtained from the same factors as dry heat resistance, but for this
the DPA concentration in the core also plays a role. The low water content of the core
is also a part of the heat resistance. Wet heat does not cause DNA damage, the same
way that dry heat does. This is specifically because of the DNA saturation with SASPs,
meaning that wet heat inactivates endospores in other ways than DNA damage. The
role of DPA is indirect in the wet heat resistance. The high content of DPA in the core
leads to the low water content, which in turn creates resistance, because of the reduced
molecular mobility of the core proteins, leading to higher resistance of proteins to thermal
inactivation (Setlow, 2014).
Desiccation is extreme drying and can occur from freezing, where water is forced out
of the cells creating DNA damage. Again SASPs and DPA play an important role in the
resistance of endospores against dessication. The mechanism of the resistance is not
entirely described, but it is known that endospores that do not contain SASPs and DPA
are not very resistant to desiccation and they are killed from DNA damage (Setlow, 2014;
Setlow and Johnson, 2019).
UV and γ­radiation are factors that can kill vegetative cells, but resistance is seen in
endospores. The two main parts of the endospores resistance to UV is due to the binding
of the SASPs to DNA which changes the UV photochemistry of the DNA. The broken DNA
6
==PAGE:13==
is repaired during endospore outgrowth with the help of endospore enzymes. The same
type of resistance is seen against γ­radiation (Setlow, 2014; Setlow and Johnson, 2019).
Endospores are more resistant than vegetative cells against different toxic chemi­
cals. This is for example genotoxic agents, oxidizing agents, aldehydes, acid, and alkali.
The reason for this resistance is based on different factors, such as the enzymes of the
spore coats ability to detoxify chemicals, general detoxification in the spore coat, the low
permeability of the IM, protection against chemical attack to DNA by SASP binding, and
lastly the repair of the DNA damaged induced by chemicals during the endospore out­
growth (Setlow, 2014).
High pressure is able to kill vegetative cells, but endospores are much more resis­
tant. Instead, pressure can act as a germinant to endospores and start the germination
process. The specific reason for resistance against high pressure is not known, but it is
known that neither SASP bound to DNA nor an intact cell spore coat is needed.
High pressure especially coupled with heat treatment is also known to kill endospores,
since the pressure can begin the germination process and once germinated the endospores
are more susceptible to heat (Setlow, 2014).
1.2.2
Pathogenicity of B. cereus
Pathogenicity is the ability of microorganisms to cause illness in humans. B. cereus
are able to cause illness in humans in two different ways. These illnesses are called the
emetic and the diarrheal syndromes and the difference between them is that the emetic
syndrome is a food intoxication and the diarrheal syndrome is a food poisoning ((Lindbäck
and Granum, 2019; Ehling­Schulz et al., 2005)).
Some strains are capable of causing both types of illness as emetic strains have been
identified, which also have the gene for a specific enterotoxin (nhe) ((Carroll et al., 2019;
Carroll et al., 2021)). Symptoms of the emetic and diarrheal syndromes are often reported
to occur simultaneously ((Glasset et al., 2016)). It is common that illness is caused by in­
gestion of cooked food products, since the endospores of B. cereus are able to germinate
during processing and the resulting vegetative cells can grow well after heat treatment and
ultimately cause illness. This ability to grow well after heat treatment is further strength­
ened, by the limited inhibiting microbiota in the products after heat treatment. Because of
the relatively mild symptoms, the illnesses are underreported and more cases than those
which are investigated are likely. This is made even more likely by the fact that the ill­
nesses are not reportable diseases in any country. Some countries do however collect
data such as Norway, Iceland and Denmark (Lindbäck and Granum, 2019; Granum and
Lund, 1997).
1.2.2.1
Emetic syndrome
The emetic syndrome causes nausea and emesis (vomiting). The incubation time
from ingestion to illness is between 0.5 and 5 hours, and the duration of the illness is
6­24 hours. It is normally self­limiting and only a few cases have reportedly caused death
(Lindbäck and Granum, 2019; Granum and Lund, 1997).
Generally, the emetic toxin is not produced at temperatures below 8°C. Since most emetic
strains belong to mesophilic panC group III, which do not grow below 7.4 °C. Some
strains of the psychrotolerant B. weihenstephanensis has however been found to pro­
duce cereulide (Thorsen et al., 2006; Lindbäck and Granum, 2019).
The emetic toxin (cereulide) is produced in food products when high concentrations
of B. cereus is reached (>5 log (cfu/g). Cereulide is a dodecadepsipeptide, which is re­
sistant to heat, pH and proteolysis. It has a ring structure with repeats of four different
amino acids and oxyacids, which are acids that contain oxygen. The structure is as fol­
7
==PAGE:14==
lows: D­O­Leu­D­Ala­L­O­Val­L­Val and this is repeated three times. The toxin works as
an K+ ionophore, meaning that it inhibits mitochondrial activity through the inhibition of
fatty acid oxidation. This leads to binding to the 5­HT3 receptor, which stimulates the
afferent vagus nerve, resulting in the symptoms (Granum and Lund, 1997; Lindbäck and
Granum, 2019).
The production of cereulide is carried out by the multienzyme complex Ces­NRPS,
which is found on a megaplasmid, similar to pXO1 plasmids of B. anthrasis. The ces
synthetase regulators are found chromosomally. There is a 24­kb cereulide synthetase
gene cluster and this is transcribed as an operon exclusively during the middle to late
exponential phase of growth and the cereulide production is therefore tightly regulated.
The main promoter within the gene cluster is called cesP1, but there are several others
(Lindbäck and Granum, 2019).
Strains producing cereulide have been investigated and it has been found that they
have low genetic variability in 8 chromosomal loci and in genotypic characteristics. This
means that every cereulide producing B. cereus is part of a single lineage, that shares
similar characteristics. These characteristics could be the inability to hydrolyse starch and
that the Hbl gene is absent, so no or little haemolytic activity is seen (Ehling­Schulz et al.,
2005).
1.2.2.2
Diarrheal syndrome
The diarrheal syndrome causes abdominal pain and diarrhea and the incubation time
from ingestion to illness is between 8­16 hours but can also be longer in some cases. The
duration of the illness is typically 12­24 hours but can also be longer. The illness is self­
limiting, meaning that no medicine is needed to cure it (Lindbäck and Granum, 2019;
Granum and Lund, 1997).
Most strains from dairy products are cytotoxic, meaning that they produce enterotoxins
but only around 1.5% to 2.9% are able to produce cereulide (Maktabdar, 2024; Tirloni
et al., 2022).
There are three main enterotoxins, namely haemolysin BL (Hbl), Nonhaemolytic en­
terotoxin (Nhe) and Cytotoxin K (CytK). Hbl and Nhe are three­component enterotoxin
complexes and the CytK is a single protein. Because they are proteins they are suscep­
tible to heat, pH and proteolysis and can therefore be broken down after production. The
toxins are created by the ingested B. cereus in the small intestine. A high concentration
of cells is needed in the food to cause illness since a lot of cells will be killed in the gastric
juices by low pH. Illness is usually associated with concentrations above 5 Log(cfu/g), but
cases of both diarrheal and emetic illness have also been seen where the concentration
was between 3 and 5 Log(cfu/g) (EFSA Panel on Biological Hazards (BIOHAZ), 2016;
Lindbäck and Granum, 2019).
The first enterotoxin to be described was the three­component enterotoxin haemolysin
BL. The components of the enterotoxin are B, L1 and L2 in a 1:1:1 ratio and all of them are
necessary for the highest biological activity of the toxin. Around 60% of the different B.
cereus strains carry the gene for Hbl, while for B. thuringiensis this number is somewhat
higher (Lindbäck and Granum, 2019).
It can be difficult to distinguish between Hbl and the other three­component enterotoxin
Nhe, since all known entheropathogenic B. cereus carrying the Hbl gene also carries the
Nhe gene. Strains that cause foodborne illness, that do not contain the Hbl gene have
also been found (Tausch et al., 2017).
Nonhaemolytic enterotoxin (Nhe) toxigenicity was described by Granum and Lund
8
==PAGE:15==
(1997). This enterotoxin consists of the components NheA, NheB and NheC and individ­
ually these are soluble proteins. To be most biologically active the components need to
be in a ratio of 10:10:1. The Nhe enterotoxin is pore­forming, meaning that these insert
transmembrane pores through the lipid bilayers of cells which leads to disruption of the
epithelial plasma membranes. Afterwards, colloid osmotic lysis is induced in the cells and
this causes an increase in the size of the cells from an influx in water causing diarrhea
(Lindbäck et al., 2004; Lindbäck and Granum, 2019).
Cytotoxin K (CytK) is a one­component protein enterotoxin. This toxin is similar to
the β­barrel toxins of Clostridium perfringens and similarly to Nhe creates pores in the
lipid bilayer, leading to diarrhea.
Even though the illness caused by this enterotoxin is usually mild and self­limiting, cases
leading to death have been seen, that has included the toxin CytK­1, which has about
5 times higher toxicity than other CytK variants such as CytK­2 and is produced by B.
cytotoxicus (Lindbäck and Granum, 2019).
1.2.3
Epidemiology
Outbreaks of B. cereus are greatly underreported mainly because of the short dura­
tion of illness and complete recovery after a short time. Another contributing factor to the
underreporting is that the syndromes caused by B. cereus are not reportable diseases
(Lindbäck and Granum, 2019; Granum and Lund, 1997).
It differs between countries which type of B. cereus illness is the most common. This
can be explained by differences in diets between different countries. In Europe and North
America, the diarrheal syndrome is most frequently described, while in Japan the emetic
syndrome is more prominent. However, it is important to note that countries surveillance
of this illness varies greatly and therefore the numbers cannot be compared directly (Lind­
bäck and Granum, 2019).
In The European Union One Health 2022 Zoonoses Report, 13,902 cases of food­
borne illness caused by bacterial toxins are described of which B. cereus toxins are ranked
as the largest known cause. Cases were reported by 8 separate member states (Belgium,
Finland, France, Germany, Hungary, Italy, Portugal and Spain). An increase in cases was
seen from the previous year. The increase in cases was mainly due to five large food­
borne outbreaks caused by B. cereus in Spain and France each involving more than 100
cases. In total 3192 individual cases were seen and 306 outbreaks were seen for the year
2022, which made B. cereus the highest contributor to the total cases of foodborne illness
caused by bacterial toxins seen in the European Union (European Food Safety Authority,
2023).
Multiple outbreaks have been investigated in recent years. A few examples of these
will follow.
In 2021 an outbreak of acute gastroenteritis occurred at two middle Schools in the Chongqing
region of China. Both adults and children were effected by the illness. Samples from pa­
tients and food were taken and tested and both types of samples were positive for B.
cereus. The main symptom of the illness was vomiting, as this was found in 100% of the
198 cases. The food that tested positive for B. cereus was rice noodles, produced at a
facility within the same region. The food producer did not have a valid food production
license and the production environment and food safety was found to be substandard (T.
Li et al., 2023).
In 2016 an outbreak of gastrointestinal illness occurred in upstate New York and
either emetic or diarrheal symptoms were reported in the 179 cases. Samples were ob­
9
==PAGE:16==
tained from stool and B. cereus was isolated from 7 of these samples. 88% of the cases
reported vomiting and 38% reported diarrhea, meaning that presence of different types
of B. cereus is speculated. The incubation period was found to be between 0.25 and 24
hours with a 2 hour median and a median duration of illness of 6 hours (Carroll et al.,
2019).
Of the 179 cases 169 reported to have eaten re­fried beans from a Mexican fast food
chain. A trace­back of the beans was conducted and it was found that they were repacked
at a production plant in Pennsylvania before reheating and being kept hot at the fast food
restaurant where the outbreak occurred. The contamination was determined to have oc­
curred before handling at the fast food restaurant (Carroll et al., 2019).
Whole genome sequencing (WGS) was used to determine the type of bacteria for 33 iso­
lates, causing the illness. All isolates were determined to be B. cereus from PanC groups
III and IV and it was therefore determined that B. cereus was the cause of the outbreak
(Carroll et al., 2019).
1.2.4
Occurrence of B. cereus in dairy products
The main reservoirs of B. cereus is decaying matter, water, plants and soil. The soil
contamination occurs because certain animals are carriers of the bacteria in their intestine.
Because of their presence in natural environments, they can easily contaminate raw in­
gredients and food products, especially plants used as primary food, such as vegetables,
spices, rice are some of the most common, but so are dairy products, since the species
most frequently identified in dairy production is B. cereus (Lücking et al., 2013). The raw
ingredients containing the microorganisms can further lead to contamination of ingredi­
ents, that did not originally contain B. cereus. This means that the range of potentially
contaminated products is very wide and includes dairy, pasta, rice­based dishes, meat
and derivatives thereof, soups, bakery products and sauces. They are also frequently
isolated from production environments which can lead to contamination throughout all
stages of production (Lindbäck and Granum, 2019; Matthews et al., 2017; Tirloni et al.,
2022).
The contamination of dairy products occurs through contact between grass, soil,
bedding, fodder and the cows’ udders. The bacterial endospores can then be passed
onto the raw milk through milking and then the endospores can germinate during different
processing of milk such as pasteurisation (Lindbäck et al., 2004; Tirloni et al., 2022).
Once the bacterial endospores have been introduced to the raw milk, it is expected that
they can be found throughout the entire production process, so decontamination and re­
contamination can occur multiple times. Dairy processing plants are known to be difficult
to clean since they are very complex and can therefore harbour spoilage bacteria. B.
cereus are able to produce biofilms, which are complexes of microorganisms, extracel­
lular polymeric substances (EPS), proteins, DNA and exopolysaccharides. This matrix
makes the biofilm able to adhere to almost any type of surface, but hard­to­clean areas
are more at risk. The biofilms can release bacteria and this leads to contamination of dairy
products (Tirloni et al., 2022).
B. cereus do not only pose a risk to consumers because of their ability to cause
illness, but they also have a spoilage potential, meaning that their presence in certain
concentrations can lead to the spoilage of food products. The main spoilage activities of B.
cereus in dairy products is their ability to produce proteases, lipases and phospholipases
that are resistant to heat. These enzymes are involved in the spoilage of dairy products
causing off­flavours, bitterness and rancidity of lipids (Tirloni et al., 2022).
Depending on geographical location, the prevalence and concentration of B. cereus
10
==PAGE:17==
in dairy products varies extremely and it also varies between the specific dairy products.
For raw milk from Egypt and Turkey, the prevalence varied from 3.8% to 100% of sam­
ples, while the counts were between 4 · 101 to 3.8 · 105 (cfu/g) (Tirloni et al., 2022).
For pasteurised milk samples from the Netherlands, Denmark, Taiwan, India, Egypt, Turkey,
China, Canada, Poland and Japan the prevalence varied between 2% and 65.3% with
variance in the cell counts between 101 to 105 (cfu/g). The similarity in prevalence and
cell count data for raw and pasteurised milk shows that the milk pasteurisation is not ad­
equate to reduce the amount of B. cereus. Because of the bacteria still being present,
strict temperature control is needed, so that the bacteria do not grow to dangerous levels
during the shelf­life ((Tirloni et al., 2022)).
For dried milk powders and infant formula from Taiwan, Chile, Italy, India, Egypt, and
China the prevalence is between 6.8% and 52% with cell counts between 5 · 100 and 104
(cfu/g). It is especially important to consider the safety of infant formula since the dehy­
dration process for milk does not give adequate decontamination. The endospores are
able to survive in the powdered milk and can easily germinate once moisture is introduced
to the powder and are then able to grow rapidly at high temperatures and during storage
of reconstituted powders (Tirloni et al., 2022).
For different types of cheeses, both soft, fresh, and ripened from Argentina, India, Italy,
Ghana, Turkey, Poland, Mexico, and Egypt the prevalence is between 0% to 95% with
cell counts varying between 2 · 101 and 4.2 · 106 log(cfu/g). This very large difference in
the prevalence might be explained by the difference in the cheese itself. There is a dif­
ference in the matrix of soft and harder cheeses, as bacteria will be immobilised within or
on the surface of solid food products. Therefore microorganisms might not have sufficient
access to the nutrients necessary for survival and growth (Jeanson and Thierry, 2015).
Other factors that can cause a difference in the prevalence of B. cereus in different cheese
are that some products have added preservatives such as acids, and salt, while others
have a low water activity from the ripening process (Tirloni et al., 2022).
The ability of B. cereus to grow in different dairy products is dependable on the consti­
tution of the product and the storage temperature. For raw milk stored at 15°C the growth
is out­competed by other present microorganisms that occur naturally in the microbiota
and the acidification occurring from the presence of lactic acid bacteria. This naturally
occurring microbiota is however eliminated during pasteurisation of milk leading to near­
optimal growth conditions for B. cereus. At 15°C B. cereus is able to have a 3 log(cfu/g)
increase over five days, while at 30°C the increase was more than 6 log(cfu/g). In recon­
stituted milk, an increase of more than 4 log cfu/g is seen at temperatures between 12
and 42°C in between 1.6 and 12.5 days (the time for the 4 log increase depended on the
temperature) (Tirloni et al., 2022).
The ability of B. cereus to occur in pasteurised milk is important to keep in mind for direct
sale, but also for further processing, since if the milk is stored incorrectly before or during
processing high cell counts might occur (Tirloni et al., 2022).
In mascarpone, an increase of around 4 log (cfu/g) is seen in 5 days. Similar findings for
fresh ricotta, which is also a fresh cheese suggest that the almost neutral pH and high
water activity of these cheeses provide almost optimal conditions for growth (Tirloni et al.,
2022).
1.2.4.1
Occurence in processed cheese
Processed cheese is typically known as a product that is microbiologically stable
since it is thoroughly heat­treated. However, the heat treatment is not enough to inacti­
vate endospores of B. cereus, since these are very heat stable. This might lead to food
safety and stability problems because of the long shelf­life of the product. If the prod­
uct is sterilized, then storage at room temperature for multiple months is possible, but if
11
==PAGE:18==
pasteurisation is the main heat treatment, the products need to be stored at refrigeration
temperatures (Oliveira et al., 2016). The initial concentration of B. cereus can depend on
the time of year the processed cheese is manufactured, but generally, the highest initial
concentration is found to be 2.4 Log(cfu/g) (Catania et al., 2021)
The non­spore microbiota consisting of vegetative cells are eliminated during the process­
ing, meaning that the B. cereus have better growth conditions, since there is no compe­
tition. The final spore­load of processed cheese is determined by the total contribution of
endospores from each ingredient (Catania et al., 2021; Oliveira et al., 2016).
The microbial load of processed cheeses can change depending on the season of
production, where a lower total concentration is found during autumn than during the
summer months. The microorganisms found in processed cheese at a specific production
plant in Italy by Catania et al. (2021) are identified as B. cereus and B. subtilis using
Matrix Assisted Laser Desorption Ionization­Time of Flight Mass Spectrometry (MALDI­
TOF MS) and 16S rRNA Sequencing. For the B. cereus found in processed cheese, none
had genes coding for the production of Hbl or cereulide, but genes were present for Nhe,
CytK and another enterotoxin called FM (Catania et al., 2021).
The specific strains of B. cereus reported by Catania et al. (2021) have not been connected
with dairy products before, except for one, ST­32, which has previously been connected
with milk as well as other sources.
Other organisms within the B. cereus group can survive and grow in processed
cheese because they share similar growth requirements and are spore­forming, which
allows them to endure the various processing steps.
This poses a problem for the industry because these bacteria can produce compounds
that lead to spoilage and some strains can produce toxins, potentially causing adverse
effects for consumers. (Catania et al., 2021; Oliveira et al., 2016).
Testing of the strains found in processed cheese revealed the presence of genes
responsible for biofilm production, with varying levels of biofilm formation observed. Most
strains were strong biofilm producers and fewer were moderate or weak producers. This
corresponds to the theory of recontamination with B. cereus endospores in products that
are otherwise free of vegetative cells, since the processed cheese might come into contact
with biofilms on the surface of production equipment (Catania et al., 2021).
1.2.5
Factors influencing growth of B. cereus in food products
Multiple factors play a role when considering the growth of microorganisms, espe­
cially temperature, pH and water activity (aw). However other factors should also be
considered such as the content of salt and dry matter in the food matrix, as well as antimi­
crobial compounds added such as organic acids and melting salts in processed cheese
(Fusieger et al., 2024).
The minimum, maximum and optimum values of the most important environmental fac­
tors are crucial knowledge, to be able to characterise microbial strains. The same is true
for B. cereus and these values are known as the cardinal parameter values. They can
be used for cardinal parameter models (CPM). Within the B. cereus (s.l.) there is a large
variability in the cardinal parameter values since the group has a broad diversity (Le Marc
et al., 2021).
The pH also affects the growth of B. cereus (s. l.), but there are currently no robust
estimates of cardinal pH values for the phylogenetic groups. Despite this Carlin et al.
(2013) found that group VI grow worse at low pH than the other groups (Carlin et al.,
2013; Le Marc et al., 2021). It is also known that the effect of pH on the maximum growth
rate of B. cereus is almost linear at low pH, while a near­plateu is seen between 5.8 and
12
==PAGE:19==
7.0 where pH has little effect on growth (Le Marc et al., 2021).
The cardinal parameters can affect each other meaning that at near optimum tem­
peratures the pHmin is lower than at suboptimal temperatures. Topt is however not the
temperature that supports the lowest pHmin value. This knowledge can be used for the
selection of cardinal pH values leading to the lowest possible amount of fail­dangerous
estimations (Martinez­Rios et al., 2019b; Le Marc et al., 2021).
To account for the interaction between the cardinal parameters in models, interaction fac­
tors are typically included, since these can improve the accuracy of the model when work­
ing near the growth limits of B. cereus (Le Marc et al., 2021).
The models by Maktabdar (2024), considered in the current study, are cardinal pa­
rameter models, that include 11 factors and the interaction factor (ξ) between these. The
11 parameters include temperature, pH, water activity, organic acids (acetic, citric, lactic,
benzoic and sorbic acid) and phosphate melting salts (Orthophosphate, diphosphate and
triphosphate). Phosphate melting salts and benzoic and sorbic acid and their inhibitory
effects are described more thoroughly as these are the parameters considered in the
current study.
1.3
Effects studied in current experiments
The following section has information on the different types of antimicrobial com­
pounds used to change the characteristics of the processed cheese used in the challenge
tests in the current study. These are phosphate melting salts and the two organic acids,
sorbic and benzoic acid.
1.3.1
Processed cheese and the effect of melting/emulsifying salts
Melting salts or emulsifying salts are important ingredients in processed cheese, be­
cause of the effect on texture and other quality attributes (Buňka et al., 2022). It is allowed
by Codex Alimentarius to use phosphoric acid, monophosphates, diphosphates, triphos­
phates, and polyphosphates in the production of processed cheese, with a maximum level
of 9,000 mg/kg calculated pure phosphorous (Buňka et al., 2022; FAO and WHO, 1995).
Processed cheese is produced by using multiple different dairy ingredients that are
made up of proteins, fat, water as well as other components in smaller concentrations.
Especially proteins and fat can be a problem when it is wished to make a homogeneous
processed cheese since these can split into a product that is not consumer­acceptable.
This heterogeneous mixture occurs because of the processing which includes heating and
stirring. During processing, the dairy fat is melted because the fat droplet membranes are
destroyed and then the uncoated fat starts to aggregate into larger units. Caseins are also
expected to aggregate and contract during the heating and this leads to the separation of
the hydrophilic (water and proteins) and hydrophobic (fats) phases (Buňka et al., 2022).
To prevent the separation of the hydrophilic and hydrophobic phases and achieve
a stable oil­in­water emulsion, emulsifiers are needed. Not only do these need to be
effective, but they also need to be able to break down calcium bridges present in the raw
materials, to release the casein and hereby achieve the natural emulsifying effect (Buňka
et al., 2022).
There are two phases in the production of processed cheese using melting salts.
The first is called the ion­exchange phase, where the casein is released from the cal­
cium bridges. This release occurs when the divalent calcium ions interact with polyvalent
anions (phosphate and polyphosphate) and the monovalent cations (sodium) during the
production of processed cheese (heating and stirring). For this reaction to occur, enough
13
==PAGE:20==
water also needs to be present in the processed cheese mixture.
In the cheese protein network, calcium paracaseins are found and these are very insolu­
ble, but during the ion­exchange phase, they are changed into sodium paracaseins which
are a lot more soluble, making them disperse in the processed cheese where they act as
emulsifiers (Buňka et al., 2022).
During the processing with melting salts added, the pH rises about 0.1­0.5 pH from
the original pH of the mix of ingredients. This affects the negative charge of casein and
this helps the proteins disperse better (Buňka et al., 2022).
The melting salts are not emulsifiers themselves, since they do not have amphiphilic
properties. However the polyvalent ions bind to the caseins via calcium ions in the pres­
ence of water, and through this binding the hydrophilic property of the protein is increased,
making them even more efficient in binding water. Meanwhile fat is emulsified by dis­
persed caseins and the lipoprotein membranes left from the disruption of the fat­covered
droplets. This protein hydration and fat emulsification leads to a higher viscosity of the
melted cheese mix (Buňka et al., 2022).
The second phase of the production of processed cheese is called creaming. This process
includes heating and agitating for several minutes as well as the cooling of the mixture,
during which the new structure of hydrated protein network with fat emulsified is estab­
lished. A lot of different interactions occur during this phase, including the formation of
new calcium bridges, phosphate­calcium complexes, hydrophobic interactions and others
(Buňka et al., 2022).
1.3.1.1
Phosphate melting salts
Phosphates are derived from phosphoric acid and are a large group of different
chemicals. The most simple phosphate is PO4, but phosphates can be much more com­
plex as other atoms can bind to one or more of the oxygen atoms. Polyphosphates have
long series of P­O­P linkages (Buňka et al., 2022).
Depending on the number of linearly bonded phosphate units the ion exchangeability
of the salt changes. This is further influenced by the production temperature of the pro­
cessed cheese. The ion exchangeability and thereby casein dispersion increases with
the length of phosphate salt and temperature. pH also plays a crucial role in these activi­
ties, since when pH is slightly lower (values within pH 5.0 and 6.5) the ion exchangeability
increases. The aggregation of caseins is more intensive, the closer the cheese is to the
isoelectric point of 4.6. However excessive aggregation is also not a wanted effect as this
can give rise to poor fat emulsification and protein hydration (Buňka et al., 2022).
Some sodium phosphate salts have buffer capacities, meaning that they can stabilize
the pH of processed cheese. The buffer capacity is higher, the shorter the chain of the
sodium phosphate salt is (Buňka et al., 2022).
1.3.1.2
Antimicrobial effects of phosphate melting salts
It is known that phosphate has an inhibiting effect on growth of some microorganisms
including Bacillus cereus (s. l.) and other Gram­positive bacteria. The inhibitory effect
depends on the length of the chain of phosphates in the melting salt. The longer the chain,
the better inhibitory effect. However, the antimicrobial effect caused by phosphate melting
salt chain length is influenced by the temperature and the pH of the environment, here,
processed cheese (Buňka et al., 2022).
The mechanism behind the antimicrobial effect of phosphate melting salts include
their affinity for divalent metal ions, as these are important for Gram­positive microorgan­
isms’ ability to maintain a stable cell wall, by creating bridges between teichoic acid within
it (Buňka et al., 2022). Further, the divalent ions bound by the phosphate melting salts
14
==PAGE:21==
become unavailable for the microorganisms to use in their physiological processes, af­
fecting the growth of the microorganisms.
In the case of spore­forming bacteria, the spore germination is suppressed by the phos­
phate melting salts, meaning that the endospores cannot undergo the transformation into
vegetative cells. When the divalent ions are cleaved, the resulting compounds have bac­
teriocidal or bacteriolytic effects (Buňka et al., 2022).
1.3.2
Organic acids
Naturally occurring organic acids act as antimicrobial agents in different food prod­
ucts and can also be added to inhibit the growth of microorganisms in food (Coban, 2020;
Davidson et al., 2021). Organic acids are considered to be chemical antimicrobials even
though they occur naturally in some food products. Most of the organic acid used for food
preservation is however manufactured chemically and then added to the food product
(Matthews et al., 2017).
There are many different types of organic acids and these all have different proper­
ties and they affect different microorganisms differently. The pKa value for organic acids
differs and that also means that the amount of undissociated organic acids also differs in
a certain product depending on the pH of the product. It is especially important to con­
sider the dissociation constant pKa, since it is the undissociated acid that contributes to
the antimicrobial effect (Coban, 2020; Davidson et al., 2021).
Organic acids are used as preservatives in foods with pH at different levels, since ef­
fect of even low amounts of undissociated acid has an antimicrobial effect. Organic acids
are however most effective as antimicrobials at pH­values below their pKa, since the fur­
ther you are below the pKa, the more of the acid is in the undissociated form (Davidson et
al., 2021). That an organic acid is undissociated means that it is uncharged or protonated,
and this means that they are able to cross the cell membrane of the microorganisms and
enter the cytoplasm (Matthews et al., 2017).
The cytoplasm of bacteria has a neutral pH and because of this, once the undis­
sociated organic acids cross the cell membrane, the acid dissociates, meaning that the
proton bound to the acid is released. This acidifies the cytoplasm of the bacterium. The
bacteria now needs to use energy (ATP) to transport the proton out of the cell, to main­
tain the neutral pH state. The energy used for this process cannot be allocated to other
functions, such as growth (Matthews et al., 2017; Hirshfield et al., 2003). Another part
of the antimicrobial effect of organic acids is the ability to penetrate the membrane lipid
bilayers of microorganisms and within the membrane, they have an effect on the proteins
and other functions of the membrane (Hirshfield et al., 2003).
1.3.2.1
Sorbic acid
Sorbic acid is an organic acid that was first isolated from rowanberries in 1859. The
pKa value of sorbic acid is 4.7 and is therefore 50% undissociated at pH 4.7. Depending
on the organism certain amounts of undissociated acid is needed to cause inhibition of
growth. For Listeria monocytogenes and Clostridium botulinum the MIC is 1.90 and 1.16
mM undissociated sorbic acid respectively. The antimicrobial effects are normally used
to inhibit yeast and moulds in food products and has been used specifically in processed
cheese (Matthews et al., 2017; Zeece, 2020). Sorbic acid is legal to use as a preservative
in Europe and therefore has an E­number: E200 (European Commision, 2008).
Sorbic acid is also covered by the term sorbates, but this term also includes the
sorbic acid salts; sodium sorbate, potassium sorbate and calcium sorbate. These salts
are also often used as preservatives in food products such as dairy products, bakery
items, products made from fruit and vegetables as well as fish and meat products. These
15
==PAGE:22==
salts are also legal preservatives in Europe and therefore also have E­numbers E201,
E202 and E203 for sodium sorbate, potassium sorbate and calcium sorbate respectively
(Sofos et al., 1986; European Commision, 2008).
Sorbic acid is an unsaturated fatty acid with two C=C double bonds and is relatively
fat soluble. Therefore the fat content of the products, where sorbic acid is used as an
antimicrobial, plays a role in the acids’ ability to inhibit microorganisms. This factor is
called lipophilicity and varies between the different organic acids.
Because of the high lipophilicity of sorbic acid, it is able to penetrate membrane lipid
bilayers of the microorganisms. From here the acid is able to interfere with the activity of
the membrane proteins (Hirshfield et al., 2003; Sofos et al., 1986).
Sorbic acid is generally used as a preservative against yeasts and moulds (Zeece,
2020; Berk, 2013), but it has also been found that it can be an effective preservative
against various bacteria, including spore­formers such as B. cereus (Sofos et al., 1986).
For the spore­formers, it has been found that sorbic acid can inhibit both spore germina­
tion, outgrowth and vegetative cells. It achieves this by inhibiting metabolic activity, dis­
rupting various transport systems, and interacting with membranes. (Sofos et al., 1986).
1.3.2.2
Benzoic acid
Benzoic acid is an organic acid often used as a preservative in food, because of its
antimicrobial properties. Benzoic acid has low solubility in water since the molecule is
made up of a non­polar benzene ring as the bulk, with only one polar carboxylic group.
This means that for benzoic acid the fat content of the product makes a difference in
the ability of the acid to inhibit the growth of microorganisms. So benzoic acid has high
lipophilicity, much like sorbic acid and therefore uses the same antimicrobial mechanisms
in the form of inhibiting the transport of different compounds into the microorganisms
(Freese et al., 1973).
Benzoic acid has a pKa value of 4.2 because of the carboxylic group. This means that
optimum antimicrobial properties occur at pH lower than 4.2 so that it is primarily undis­
sociated (Berk, 2013). Benzoic acid is accepted as an additive to food in Europe and
therefore has the E­number E210 (European Commision, 2008).
Because of the low solubility in water salts of benzoic acid are often used instead
of pure organic acids. These salts are collectively known as benzoates and individually
as sodium benzoate, potassium benzoate and calcium benzoate. These have the E­
numbers E211, E212 and E213 respectively (European Commision, 2008).
The benzoates are used in a lot of different food products for their antimicrobial effect.
These are food products such as different beverages, fruit juice, pickled vegetables, jams,
prepared salads, soy sauce and others (Berk, 2013; Shibamoto and Bjeldanes, 1993).
Benzoic acid can also occur naturally in low concentrations in some products such as
fruits, vegetables, spices, nuts and most importantly dairy products (Sieber et al., 1995).
Milk contains up to a few mg/kg of benzoic acid, while cultured or fermented milk products
such as yoghurt, creme fraiche and others contain up to 50 mg/kg and an average amount
of 20 mg/kg. Benzoic acid has also been found in various fresh and fermented cheeses
as well as lactic casein whey and whey protein powder. For fresh cheeses, the amount
of benzoic acid varied between 8.0 and 39.1 mg/kg, for solid cheeses between 5.7 and
96.4 mg/kg and for processed cheese between 6.0 and 13.1 mg/kg. It is expected that
this higher amount of benzoic acid in fermented and cultured dairy products is due to the
conversion of hippuric acid to benzoic acid by lactic acid bacteria (Sieber et al., 1995).

2.1
Challenge tests
Challenge testing is a useful tool used to determine the ability of a food matrix to
support microbial growth and survival, establish the shelf­life of products and to determine
product formulation in terms of intrinsic control factors such as pH, water activity and an­
timicrobial concentration (Komitopoulou, 2011; Pérez­Rodríguez and Valero, 2013).
There are four main parts of challenge testing and these are experimental design, inoc­
ulation, analysis and reporting (Komitopoulou, 2011). A description of these steps in the
current study is seen in the following sections.
14 challenge tests were carried out to evaluate the effect of diphosphate melting salt
(P2), triphosphate melting salt (P3), sorbic acid (SAC) and benzoic acid (BAC) on the
growth of both psychrotolerant and mesophilic B. cereus in processed cheese. A control
treatment without the addition of any of the studied antimicrobials was also included in the
series of challenge tests (Table 2.1). Different concentrations of the antimicrobials were
studied as specified in Table 2.1.
The models by Maktabdar (2024) to be evaluated in the current thesis include di­ and
triphosphate melting salts as factors of inhibition. It is therefore important to evaluate their
ability to correctly predict growth of psychrotolerant and mesophilic B. cereus in products
containing these. Processed cheese is a dairy product that includes these melting salts
and this product is chosen for challenge testing. Multiple concentrations of phosphate
melting salts are investigated and the purpose of this is to assess the models abilities to
correctly predict growth with different concentration of antimicrobials. Minimum inhibitory
concentrations (MIC) of these phosphate melting salts are found based on the growth
response in the challenge tests.
The models by Maktabdar (2024) also include terms for the inhibition of the organic acids
benzoic and sorbic acid. The ability of the models to correctly predict growth when these
acids are included is also assessed by challenge testing.
To investigate one inhibiting effect at once and to obtain easily comparable results,
all challenge tests are stored at the same temperature of 15°C and the pH is adjusted to
5.50. No further inhibiting ingredients such as salt, other organic acids etc. are added.

Preparation of inoculation cocktails
The B. cereus strain cocktails used for inoculation are mixtures of the mesophilic

added depending on the increase in absorbance to have a similar concentration of each

absorbance. The cocktail is mixed and the cell density is calculated using phase­contrast
microscopy.

loop and a cover glass is added on top. A drop of microscope oil is placed on top of the
cover glass and the microscope is adjusted, so that cells can be seen in the objective.
Now 10 randomly picked fields of view are picked and the cells are counted within each.

cells per field of view is used to adjust the cocktail concentration to ∼106 using equation
2.3.

2.4.1
Extraction of data from literature
Multiple papers considering the topic of growth of B. cereus in different dairy prod­
ucts, including milk, whey, different cheeses, reconstituted milk powder and infant formula
and yogurt were found. From these, different data was collected such as B. cereus strain,
temperature of experiment, product characteristics such as pH, salt content, dry matter,
fat and so on. The data needed is the same as what is included in the models by Maktab­
dar (2024) and the models are then used to predict the maximum specific growth rates
from the product characteristics. The data has been collected from 8 different papers by
Bartoszewicz et al., 2013; Bursová et al., 2018; Buss da Silva et al., 2017; Ellouze et al.,
2021; Necidová et al., 2014; Tirloni et al., 2017a; Tirloni et al., 2017b and Valík et al.,
2003.
In most of the articles, the actual µmax was not given, but growth curves were shown.
In these cases the maximum specific growth rate was estimated using the WebPlotDigi­
tizer tool by Automeris.io. Here the growth curve is uploaded and then two known points
on each axis is marked. Then each point on the growth curve is marked and the data is
extracted. The data points obtained are now fitted using the logistic model with delay as
described in 2.2.
If some of the data needed for the model evaluation such as different product charac­
teristics values were not described in the literature, these were estimated using different
sources; Adhikari et al., 2001; Brezovečki et al., 2015; Ciressa srl, 2024; DTU Fødevare­
instituttet, 2024a; DTU Fødevareinstituttet, 2024b; DTU Fødevareinstituttet, 2024c; X. Li
et al., 2016; U.S. Department of Agriculture, 2019 and Zeppa, 2004.
It is generally not reported whether strains are mesophilic or psychrotolerant in the
literature and it was therefore investigated whether this information could be found out for
the different strains mentioned in the articles. It was however only found that one strain
used by Tirloni et al., 2017a and Tirloni et al., 2017b could be identified as belonging to
PanC group IV and is therefore mesophilic. This specific strain was used for code A1 and
A4 (Table 3.6) and during the model evaluation the values from this strain was not used
for evaluation of the psychrotolerant model.

3.2
Microbial changes of non-inoculated samples
Non­inoculated samples of the exact same cheeses are used as controls for the in­
oculated samples. The control experiments were also sampled during the storage period.
From these, it is found that the powder­based processed cheeses generally had some
growth on BHI agar, while the samples from mascarpone­based processed cheeses had
no growth on both BHI and MYP. The growth curves for non­inoculated samples are seen
in appendix A.4 and the raw data for these curves are also described in appendix A.2.
The growth seen on BHI is expected to be from the naturally occurring microbiota in the
powdered ingredients used, since these are not sterile when acquired from the producers.
Further the powders are not stored in air­tight containers and therefore microorganisms
from the surrounding environment can contaminate them during storage.
The powdered dairy ingredients used for processed cheese naturally contain mi­
croorganisms common in dairy production, including lactic acid bacteria and spores of B.
cereus. During cheese production, the mixture is heated to 80°C for 10 minutes and this
time is not sufficient to eliminate spores, as these have a D­value of 360 minutes at 85°C
(Setlow and Johnson, 2019).
In appendix A.4 figure A.5 growth on MYP and BHI for the non­inoculated sample
of powder­based processed cheese with no addition of phosphate melting salt or organic
acid is seen and this shows that B. cereus is detected in the sample with a cell concen­
tration of 6.92 Log(cfu/g) after 4 days at 15°C. For powder­based processed cheese with
0.3% P3 4.63 Log(cfu/g) is found on MYP after 9 days of storage.
This growth of B. cereus in two non­inoculated samples can be explained from B.
cereus being present in the natural microbiota of the powdered ingredients or by contam­
ination occurring during the packaging of the non­inoculated samples, where B. cereus
might have been introduced accidentally since the packaging is a manual process. An­
other explanation of why B. cereus is found in the non­inoculated samples is that contam­
ination during sampling has taken place.
For the processed cheese containing 0.3% P3, since the B. cereus is only seen for one
sampling point on MYP plates. This is more likely due to contamination during sampling
but it could also be due to the survival of B. cereus spores with low concentrations in the
cheese ingredients (dairy powders). However for the non­inoculated powder­based pro­
cessed cheese containing no phosphate melting salt or organic acid, the presence of B.
cereus is expected to be from the ingredients since germination is possible and the bac­
teria is less inhibited than in cheeses containing either phosphate melting salts or organic
acids.
The concentration of B. cereus in the non­inoculated samples do not reach the same con­
centration as the inoculated samples, in either of the cases where B. cereus was found.
Growth to 9.30 and 8.55 Log(cfu/g) on BHI is seen in non­inoculated processed
cheese samples containing 142 and 265 ppm sorbic acid respectively. This corresponds
well with the high cell concentration seen on BHI for the inoculated samples containing
the same amount of sorbic acid. Growth on BHI for non­inoculated processed cheese
samples containing 366 ppm benzoic acid shows growth to 3.84 Log(cfu/g), which is not
significantly different from the growth on BHI for inoculated samples containing the same
amount of benzoic acid. When diluted samples of powder­based processed cheese con­
taining sorbic and benzoic acid were investigated under the microscope, coccus­shaped
bacteria were seen, which could be lactococcus.

Diphosphate and triphosphate melting salts are able to gradually inhibit growth of

values for phosphate melting salts and sorbic acid found for processed cheese compared
to the MIC used in the model.
The distributions between the observed square root transformed µmax from scientific
literature and the predicted values from the psychrotolerant model or mesophilic model
are very similar (Figure 3.11 B and D respectively). Outliers are seen on both distributions
and these are similar in their placement on the distribution, but not exactly alike. This could
be explained by the use of almost the same literature dataset for both psychrotolerant and
mesophilic models and that both models predicted similar growth rates for these specific
conditions.
The extreme outlier seen in both Figure 3.11B and D at the coordinates 0.0 ; 1.8 for
both models, has a considerable effect on the model evaluation. When this datapoint is
removed the bias and accuracy factor for the psychrotolerant model improves to 1.10 and
1.98 respectively. For the mesophilic model the accuracy factor improves to 1.66, while
the bias factor decreases to 0.92, leading to the value becoming acceptable instead of
good.

Discussion
4.1
Relevance of current study
The processed cheese in the current study has a pH of 5.50 which is somewhat
low when comparing to commercial products investigated by Koukou et al. (2022) and
Martinez­Rios et al. (2019b) who found pH values between 6.10 and 6.30. The processed
cheeses investigated by Fusieger et al. (2024) had pH values between 5.49 and 5.71.
These products are however not commercial products but produced specifically for the
study. Glass et al. (2017) investigated processed cheese with pH values of 5.40, 5.80
and 6.20. Here 5.40 pH is considered to be low, while 5.80 is intermediate. Therefore a
pH value of 5.50 in the current study is on the low end of the scale, but not unlikely for
processed cheese products.
Therefore the pH value of 5.50 of the current study is considered low, meaning that the
results are not directly relevant for commercial products.
The pH value of 5.50 was chosen for the current study because the predictive models
evaluated include the effect of pH as a parameter. Because of this, it is important to
evaluate if the model is able to correctly handle low pH values for processed cheese and
other products.
Koukou et al. (2022) investigated the fat content of commercial processed cheese
and here concentrations between 23 and 30% were found. This corresponds well with
the fat content of the two types of processed cheese used in the current experiment with
20 and 34% respectively for powder­based and mascarpone­based processed cheese.
In this regard, the results generated in the current experiment are relevant for commercial
products.
A higher content of the organic acids acetic, citric and lactic acid was found by
Koukou et al. (2022) and Martinez­Rios et al. (2019b) in commercial processed cheese,
than in the current study. It is a possibility that the acids found in the commercial products
are added for their antimicrobial effects, while the concentrations in the processed cheese
in the current study are naturally present in the ingredients. Therefore the results from
processed cheese in the current study are not directly relevant for commercial processed
cheese.

in processed cheese for mesophilic and psychrotolerant B. cereus in the present study.

Even when considering the 95% confidence intervals for the current experiment the MIC

drastic.
From the challenge testing, it was found that concentrations of 0.6% and 1.2% triphos­
phate melting salt in processed cheese resulted in very similar growth rates of mesophilic
B. cereus. This was not expected based on the prediction by the mesophilic B. cereus
model and results in a MIC value higher than that of the model.
The MIC of P3 for
mesophilic B. cereus is very uncertain and this is also seen from the very large span of the
confidence interval. To be able to lower the uncertainty of the MIC of P3 for mesophilic,
more experiments are needed, so that the MIC can be based on more data. This is ex­
pected to lead to a more stable MIC.
The MIC values used in the model by Maktabdar (2024) have been found in the
medium BHI at 15°C for psychrotolerant and 37 °C for mesophilic B. cereus strains. The
higher MIC values for mesophilic models by Maktabdar (2024) could be explained by the
higher experimental temperature.
It has been found by Le Marc et al. (2021) and Martinez­Rios et al. (2019a) that tem­
perature has an effect on the minimum pH at which growth is seen (pHmin). It could be
expected that the pHmin would be highest at the optimum temperature but for L. mono­
cytogenes and mesophilic B. cereus, this was not found to be true. The pHmin for these
organisms were found at suboptimal pH of around 20­30°C for mesophilic B. cereus and
15­25°C for L. monocytogenes (Le Marc et al., 2021; Martinez­Rios et al., 2019a).
This effect of temperature on the pHmin might be transferable to other inhibitory effects
such as MICs of phosphate melting salts and organic acids. Therefore the higher experi­
mental temperatures at which MICs are determined for the models by Maktabdar (2024)
might be because of the difference in temperature at which they are obtained, since the
challenge tests of the current study were carried out at 15°C.
BHI is considered as a close to optimal growth media for many microorganisms in­
cluding B. cereus. This means that the B. cereus has more effective growth in BHI than in
processed cheese. This is because processed cheese naturally contains different factors
that have an inhibitory effect on the growth of the bacteria, such as organic acids (citric,
acetic and lactic acid) and salt. Another factor that contributes to the difference between
49
==PAGE:56==
BHI and processed cheese is the presence of background microorganisms, that compete
for the nutrients needed for growth. Further, the matrix of the processed cheese itself can
play a role as some nutrients can be inaccessible to the bacteria.
Contrary to the above Buňková et al. (2008) investigated the effect of the long­chain
polyphosphate HBS at different concentrations in laboratory media and found that the
MIC value of this is 0.3% for B. cereus. Based on their findings they expect the MIC to
be higher in processed cheeses, than in laboratory broth (Buňková et al., 2008). They do
however not consider the difference between psychrotolerant and mesophilic B. cereus
and no MIC in processed cheese is given. This corresponds to what is seen for triphos­
phate melting salt on mesophilic B. cereus in the current study, since the MIC found in
the current study in processed cheese is higher than the MICs from the models in BHI.
The present results show that other than the added phosphate melting salts and
organic acids, many other factors including naturally present organic acids, salt and the
matrix of the processed cheese, can influence the inhibition of growth of B. cereus. The
lower MIC values for phosphate melting salts and sorbic acid found in processed cheese
means that the models by Maktabdar (2024) overpredict the growth of psychrotolerant
and mesophilic B. cereus in these products.
The predicted and the observed maximum specific growth rates of B. cereus for processed
cheese with no addition of phosphate melting salt or organic acid, as shown in Table 3.1
is considered to be close and therefore it is found that the model is usable for the matrix of
processed cheese. The growth rates when phosphate melting salts or organic acids were
added to the processed cheese were not predicted correctly by the models. Further, the
interaction between these growth inhibitory factors and the naturally present factors, such
as other naturally present organic acids, also affects the growth. An interaction factor
is integrated into the models, but the interactions and their inhibiting effect might not be
described correctly for processed cheese.
4.3
Model evaluation
When considering the model evaluation of the psychrotolerant and mesophilic mod­
els by Maktabdar (2024) using the data generated in the current study from challenge
tests, it should be noted that the model evaluation is not valid. This is because of the low
amount of maximum specific growth rate data. A minimum of 20 separate µmax values is
needed when evaluating a predictive model (Ross, 1996; Pérez­Rodríguez and Valero,
2013).
Therefore the model evaluation based solely on these values is only a general estimate
and not a true evaluation. When the models are evaluated using data 57­59 specific max­
imum specific growth rates obtained from literature on the subject of B. cereus in dairy
products, the evaluation is valid. This is based on the amount of data included.
4.4
Inhibitory effect of other phosphate salts on B. cereus
The inhibiting effect of other phosphate salts in processed cheese such as short­
chain polyposphates, long­chain polyphosphate and sodium phosphates with different
condensation levels (meaning the length of the phosphate chain) has been investigated
for B. cereus. Fusieger et al. (2023) found that there is a significant inhibiting effect of
1.5% short chain poly­phosphate on B. cereus in processed cheese and that polyphos­
phate salts with a longer phosphate chain had a greater inhibitory effect than those with
shorter phosphate chains. This corresponds with the fact that Fusieger et al. (2024) found
that higher concentrations of P2O5 had a larger growth inhibitory effect than phosphate
salts with a lower concentration of P2O5. This is confirmed by the findings of Buňková
50
==PAGE:57==
et al. (2008) and Lorencová et al. (2012) performed in laboratory broth, that the longer the
chain of the polyphosphate is, the greater the inhibitory effect is.
Compared to the sodium diphosphate and the sodium triphosphate used in the current
experiment this effect is not seen since the MIC values for the two different melting salts
are very similar (as seen in Table 4.1 for the models by Maktabdar (2024) and cur­
rent challenge test data) even though the triphosphate contains one more phosphate ion
than diphosphate. It should however be considered that di­ and triphosphates are not
polyphosphates and therefore the effect of more phosphate meaning greater inhibition is
not directly transferable.
The study by Fusieger et al. (2024) also found that both polyphosphates and sodium
phosphates with concentrations of P2O5 between 59.7 and 69.0% inhibited the growth of
B. cereus at 6°C, but it is not known whether the investigated strains are psychrotolerant
or mesophilic and neither are the specific phosphate melting salts used. At 30°C the
inhibitory effect was not as great as at the lower temperature, meaning that the effect
of temperature is also important to consider when predicting the growth of B. cereus in
processed cheese containing phosphate melting salts.
4.5
Inhibitory effect of phosphate melting salts for other
microorganisms
The inhibitory effect of ortho­, di­ and triphosphate salts on Clostridium sporogenes
in processed cheese has been investigated by Koukou et al. (2022). C. sporogenes is
also a spore­forming, Gram­positive bacteria like B. cereus and therefore some similar­
ities of inhibition can be expected. However, this is not the case when considering or­
thophosphate, since the MIC for C. sporogenes is found to be about 3% higher than for
both psychrotolerant and mesophilic B. cereus found by Maktabdar (2024) (MIC for C.
sporogenes is 8.05% and for psychrotolerant and mesophilic B. cereus MIC is 4.93% and
4.98% respectively).
MIC of diphosphate is 2.87% for C. sporogenes and similar values of 2.26% and 2.38%
are seen for psychrotolerant and mesophilic B. cereus respectively, however, these val­
ues are slightly lower.
Triphosphate has a MIC of 2.08% for C. sporogenes and 2.29% and 2.32% for psychro­
tolerant and mesophilic B. cereus, which is again somewhat close.
This means that the MIC of diphosphate and triphosphate for C. sporogenes are compa­
rable to those of B. cereus found by Maktabdar (2024), while the MIC of orthophosphate
is not.
This similarity is not found when looking at the MIC of ortho­, di­ and triphosphates for
Listeria monocytogenes in processed cheese investigated by Martinez­Rios et al. (2019b)
since these values are generally a lot higher than those found for B. cereus. The MIC
of orthophosphate for L. monocytogenes is 14.9%, for diphosphate it is 9.4% and for
triphosphate it is 7.6%.
4.6
Inhibitory effect of background microorganisms
During the challenge test using processed cheese made from powdered dairy ingre­
dients containing sorbic acid in two concentrations, the growth of background microorgan­
isms was observed on BHI agar. There are multiple possible reasons for the occurrence
of these microorganisms, one being the natural contamination of the raw milk from soil,
bedding, milking equipment, udders of the cow and feed (Quigley et al., 2013). The mi­
croorganisms able to contaminate raw milk are plentiful, but some bacteria commonly
51
==PAGE:58==
found are lactic acid bacteria (LAB) from the genera Lactococcus, Lactobacillus, Leu­
conostoc, Streptococcus and Enterococcus (Quigley et al., 2013). These bacteria can
then be introduced into the processing environment and can from here contaminate prod­
ucts after finished processing, e.g. from biofilms (Sadiq et al., 2018; Tirloni et al., 2022).
Microorganisms present in dried dairy powders do not normally cause spoilage, since
vegetative cells will have no metabolic activity in the dry environment and the spores
present are not able to cause spoilage, since these are not metabolically active either.
However, once the dried dairy powders are reconstituted the metabolic activity can restart
and the growth of present bacteria can start (Sadiq et al., 2018), resulting in the back­
ground microbiota found in processed cheese in the current study.
Inhibition of growth of B. cereus can occur because of high concentrations of other
microorganisms in processed cheese. This type of inhibition is called the Jameson effect
and has been described for multiple microorganisms such as Salmonella, Staphylococ­
cus aureus and L. monocytogenes. The Jameson effect occurs when the growth of all
microorganisms present in food products is suppressed by the maximum population den­
sity (MPD) being reached (Ross et al., 2000).
The challenge tests using processed cheese with added sorbic acid with psychro­
tolerant B. cereus has rapid growth of background microorganisms to a level close to
the expected MPD (9­10 Log(cfu/g)). Once this concentration is reached the growth of
B. cereus stops and the curve flattens. This is expected to be because of the Jameson
effect as described above.
During the challenge test with sorbic acid and psychrotolerant B. cereus a decrease
in pH from 5.5 to 4.75 is seen. This decrease pH can be explained by the production of
lactic acid by LAB. LAB are able to transform different carbohydrate sources in to lactic
acid, and this can in turn decrease the pH of a product (Y. Wang et al., 2021).
The pKa of sorbic acid is 4.7, meaning that the closer to pH 4.7 the matrix is, the
more sorbic acid will be undissociated. It is the undissociated sorbic acid that has an
inhibitory effect on the growth of B. cereus and therefore the decrease in pH leads to
further inhibition of growth of B. cereus (Sofos et al., 1986).
In this way inhibition by microorganisms occurs by two different mechanisms: the
Jameson effect and the production of lactic acid, lowering the pH of processed cheese.
4.7
Concentrations of benzoic and sorbic acid
When benzoic and sorbic acid are added to processed cheese this is done using
0.25% (w/v) solutions. The theoretically added concentration of the acids did not corre­
spond with the concentrations measured using HPLC. The HPLC method used for analy­
sis of organic acid concentrations (Agilent/HP1100) is developed for use in fish and shell­
fish and is therefore not standardised for the use in dairy products.
Marine and dairy lipids are not the same but do have some similarities. Dairy lipids
have around 98% triacylglycerol, 0.5­1% phospholipids and 0.2­0.5% sterols. The rest
are made up of minor lipid components. The lipids in milk are found in milk fat globules
and their membranes and these are present in the milk in an emulsified state (fat­in­water
emulsion) (Jensen et al., 1991)
Marine lipids have around 95% triacylglycerol followed by the second and third largest
constituents of phospholipids and sterol, similarly to dairy lipids. However, in marine lipids
two specific long­chain polyunsaturated omega­3 fatty acids are found: eicosapentaenoic
52
==PAGE:59==
acid (EPA, 20:5 n­3) and docosahexaenoic acid (DHA, 22:6, n­3). These specific omega­3
fatty acids are only found in marine products (Falch, 2023).
The difference in the lipid composition and the availability of these lipids in the food
matrix is a possible explanation for the difference in measured concentration of organic
acids to the theoretical concentration of benzoic and sorbic acid added to processed
cheese.
4.8
Inhibitory effect of benzoic and sorbic acid on B. cereus
For psychrotolerant B. cereus inactivation occurred in processed cheese with an
addition of 366 ppm benzoic acid. The same is true for mesophilic B. cereus with 366
ppm benzoic acid and 265 ppm sorbic acid. At these concentrations of organic acids,
growth was expected by the models produced by Maktabdar (2024).
Multiple reasons for the inactivation should be considered, such as the concentration
of other organic acids present. Hsiao and Siebert (1999) investigated the magnitude of
the inhibitory effect of different organic acids on different microorganisms. B. cereus was
one of the organisms investigated and they found MICs for acetic, benzoic, citric and
lactic acids of 2.02, 0.296, 3.68, and 3.48 g/l respectively. None of the MIC’s have been
achieved in the processed cheese used in the current experiments. The synergistic effect
of organic acids on the growth of microorganisms should however be considered. J.
Wang et al. (2019) found that a mixture of lactic acid and acetic acid had an additive
inhibitive effect, but if a third organic acid such as citric acid was added, a synergistic
inhibitive effect was achieved for Escherichia coli O157:H7 and Listeria monocytogenes.
L. monocytogenes is a Gram­positive microorganism meaning that the effect might be
similar for the Gram­positive B. cereus (J. Wang et al., 2019). Both acetic acid, citric and
lactic acids are naturally present in processed cheese made from powdered ingredients
and this might explain why inhibition is observed when growth is expected.
The inhibitory effect of benzoic and sorbic acid on B. cereus was investigated by
Thomas et al. (1993) on Brain Heart Infusion Yeast Extract Glucose Agar (BHIYEGA)
with different concentrations of the acids. Here the effect of 1.0% benzoic acid and 2.0%
sorbic acid was investigated and it was found that at pH values below 6.4 and at 20°C the
growth of B. cereus was totally inhibited in these conditions. To be able to compare the
study by Thomas et al. (1993) and the current study, the amount of undissociated acid has
been calculated in the conditions described. The concentrations are found to be 0.06 mM
undissociated benzoic acid and 0.44 mM undissociated sorbic acid. The concentrations
of undissociated benzoic acid are quite similar to the concentration used in the current
study (0.05 mM undissociated benzoic acid), while that of sorbic acid is more than twice
as high (0.19 mM undissociated sorbic acid) but total inhibition was likewise seen for these
concentrations of benzoic acid and sorbic acid. When comparing these values it should
however, also be considered that the temperature used in the current study was 15°C,
whereas the temperature of the study by Thomas et al. (1993) was 20°C and this lower
temperature will also add to the inhibition of B. cereus. Another factor is the difference in
matrix since laboratory broth is generally better at supporting growth than food products
and multiple factors of inhibition are naturally present in processed cheeses.
MIC of benzoic acid and sorbic acid on Nutrient agar (HiMedia M561) incubated at
37°C was determined by Roy et al. (2007) for B. cereus isolated from legumes. The
minimum inhibitory concentration is found to be between 0.04 and 0.07% (0.85­1.02 mM
undissociated) of benzoic acid at pH 5.0 to 4.2 and between 0.05 and 0.06% (2.17­2.36
mM undissociated) for sorbic acid at pH 5.0 to 4.8. The current study shows no growth at
53
==PAGE:60==
a concentration of 0.05 mM undissociated benzoic acid which is a lot lower than for the
study by Roy et al. (2007). For sorbic acid, the MIC in the current study is found to be 0.19
undissociated sorbic acid which is also a lot lower than the MIC found in the study by Roy
et al. (2007). Again the considerations of this difference in MIC value are the temperature
at which the experiment was carried out and the matrix. Here the temperature is a lot
higher than the temperature of 15°C used for challenge tests in the current study and this
will have a large effect on the growth of B. cereus.
B. cereus isolated from spices has been investigated by Bannerjee and Sarkar (2004)
to find the MIC of benzoic and sorbic acid in Tryptone Soya Broth (TSB) at 37°C. The MIC
of benzoic acid was found to be 0.04­0.05% (0.25 mM undissociated) and for sorbic acid
0.06­0.10% (1.75­2.50 mM undissociated). Similarly to the comparison with MICs from
Roy et al. (2007) the MICs found by Bannerjee and Sarkar (2004) are a lot higher than the
MICs in processed cheese from the current study. Again temperature and matrix of growth
media and the differences between these can be used to explain the difference in MIC.
When considering the MICs used by the mesophilic B. cereus model by Maktabdar (2024)
these are more similar to these of Bannerjee and Sarkar (2004). This might be because of
the use of laboratory media (BHI for Maktabdar (2024) and TSB for Bannerjee and Sarkar
(2004)) at the same temperature (37°C) when investigating MICs.
Inhibitory effect of benzoic and sorbic acid on B. cereus in tomato juice was investi­
gated by Biswas et al. (2015) and MIC values were determined to be 0.08% for benzoic
acid and 0.20% sorbic acid. The temperature and pH at which these MIC values are de­
termined are not described. Therefore when finding the concentration of undissociated
organic acids, 20°C is estimated as room temperature and a pH of 4.59 of tomato juice
(Igile et al., 2016) is used. The concentration of undissociated benzoic acid is 0.67 mM
and sorbic acid is 5.65 mM which is a lot higher than the MIC found in the current study.
These high concentrations of dissociated acid occurs because of the low pH of tomato
juice.
Tomato juice is a liquid food product and therefore the matrix and availability of nutrients
to microorganisms is different than for processed cheese, which is more solid. Therefore
the matrix of the products as well as temperature should be mentioned, when considering
the difference in MIC for these two products.
Overall the MICs of benzoic and sorbic acid found in other studies investigating the
growth of B. cereus strains in laboratory broth and food products, do not correspond well
with the results from the current study. However the MIC of benzoic acid found by Thomas
et al. (1993) is close to that found in the current study and this study also had the MIC of
sorbic acid, closest to the MIC found in the current study.
4.9
Inhibitory effect of benzoic and sorbic acid on other
organisms in processed cheese
No literature data on the inhibition of B. cereus by benzoic and sorbic acid in pro­
cessed cheese was found and neither was data for other dairy products. However, the
inhibition of the two organic acids was investigated for C. sporogenes in processed cheese
by Koukou et al. (2022) where MIC values of 0.26 and 1.10 mM undissociated organic
acid for benzoic and sorbic acid respectively were found. C. sporogenes is also a Gram­
positive spore­forming bacteria and therefore somewhat similar growth conditions can be
expected as for B. cereus. The MIC of undissociated benzoic acid for the psychrotoler­
ant model by Maktabdar (2024) is 0.122 mM and for the mesophilic, it is 0.359 mM. The
MIC of benzoic acid for C. sporogenes is between these two values, and that they are
54
==PAGE:61==
not entirely similar is because of the difference in tolerance of different microorganisms,
since even though both B. cereus and C. sporogenes are Gram­positive spore­formers,
they are still different microorganisms.
The MIC of sorbic acid in the B. cereus models are 0.686 mM and 1.500 mM undissoci­
ated sorbic acid in the water phase for psychrotolerant and mesophilic. The MIC values
of sorbic acid is somewhat similar for C. sporogenes and mesophilic B. cereus, while the
psychrotolerant B. cereus are more sensitive to sorbic acid.
Mejlholm and Dalgaard (2013) investigated the MIC of both benzoic and sorbic acid
for Lactobacillus spp. and found values of 1.51 and 12.6 mM respectively for the two
acids. These values are a lot higher the MICs used by Maktabdar (2024) in the models
for psychrotolerant and mesophilic B. cereus. Lactobacillus is a species of lactic acid
bacteria and therefore have a higher acid tolerance than B. cereus (Menconi et al., 2014).
The high acid tolerance influences the MIC of organic acids to become higher, than for
microorganisms, that are less tolerant to low pH environments.
The growth and growth boundary model for Listeria monocytogenes by Mejlholm and
Dalgaard (2009) has been validated for use in non­fermented dairy products by Mejlholm
et al. (2010) and later in processed cheese by Martinez­Rios et al. (2019b). In this model
the MIC of benzoic and sorbic acid are found to be 0.35 and 1.9 mM of undissociated
acid respectively. Compared to the MIC used in the models by Maktabdar (2024) the
sorbic and benzoic MICs are similar to those of the mesophilic model, while the MICs
for the psychrotolerant model are lower. This means that the inhibitory effect of benzoic
and sorbic acid is somewhat similar for mesophilic B. cereus and L. monocytogenes in
processed cheese, while they are not similar for the psychrotolerant B. cereus. These
results are similar to those of the comparison with C. sporogenes.

4.10
Application of models
Predictive models can be used in the formulation of new products or optimisation of
already existing products. Preservatives should be added to food products in an amount
that ensures high quality of the product and the safety of the consumer. Predictive models
can be used to figure out the balance of several inhibiting factors to obtain these prod­
ucts. Preservatives are expensive and therefore the smallest amount that still ensures
safe food products is wanted (Membré and Lambert, 2008).
Further, it is important to consider consumer trends when formulating new products. Through
the last decades focus has been on food products that contain less preservatives (Guzik
55
==PAGE:62==
et al. (2022) and Zink (1997)). This is another reason to consider the smallest amount of
preservatives needed to obtain safe food products.
Since 11 factors are taken into account in the models by Maktabdar (2024) as well
as the interaction factor between these factors, it is possible to use the models for the
formulation of dairy products since this is the area within which the model is calibrated.
However, from the results of the model evaluation found in the current study, the models
are not yet successfully validated, since the bias and accuracy factors are not satisfactory.
This is especially true for the case of processed cheese with the additions of phosphate
melting salts and organic acids since the predicted values for these products did not cor­
respond to the observed values. For product formulation of processed cheese products,
the MIC values found in the current study for di­ and triphosphate melting salt and sor­
bic acid could be used instead of the MIC values found in the models to obtain a better
prediction.

5
Conclusion
The psychrotolerant B. cereus model by Maktabdar (2024) is not able to acceptably
predict the growth of these microorganisms in processed cheese according to the bias
and accuracy factors. The model is, however, able to predict growth with a good bias
factor, based on data from literature which does not include any phosphate melting salts
or sorbic or benzoic acid.
The mesophilic B. cereus model has an acceptable bias factor for processed cheese and
a good one based on data from literature. The accuracy factor is however not acceptable
when evaluating using data from processed cheese or from dairy products found in the
literature.
The inability of models to correctly predict the growth of B. cereus in processed
cheese is caused by a lower MIC for processed cheese than the MICs used in the models.
The effect of the antimicrobials di­ and triphosphate melting salt and benzoic and sor­
bic acid on psychrotolerant and mesophilic B. cereus has been described in processed
cheese and from this it is found that the hypothesis that the MIC of di­ and triphosphate,
benzoic and sorbic acid are equal in the processed cheese to the MICs used in the mod­
els by Maktabdar (2024), is not correct. It is found that the model evaluation for the
mesophilic model, is acceptable for this type of product. but the psychrotolerant model is
not, because of the difference in MIC and the interaction between these factors and the
naturally occurring inhibiting factors.

6
Perspective
Within the thesis period, a limited amount of challenge tests have been carried out.
This means that the MIC values found in the current study are based on very few data
points. To ensure that these MIC values are correct, further experiments should be carried
out using different concentrations of all the preservatives tested in the current study. More
data points are especially needed for the concentration of benzoic acid as the only chal­
lenge test carried out in the current study showed no growth and since the concentration
of benzoic acid used in the processed cheese was lower than the MIC value found in the
models for both psychrotolerant and mesophilic B. cereus this needs to be reconsidered.
For the model evaluation using bias and accuracy factors more maximum growth
rates are needed, to make the result relevant. At least 20 µmax are needed (Ross, 1996;
Pérez­Rodríguez and Valero, 2013) and in the current case, only 7 were used. Therefore
at least 13 more µmax are needed for both the psychrotolerant and mesophilic B. cereus,
to be able to perform a proper model evaluation.
In the future, the MICs for phosphate melting salts, benzoic and sorbic acid could be
reevaluated since these factors were the main reason behind the unacceptability of the
models in processed cheese.
