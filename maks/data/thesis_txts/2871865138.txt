Abstract
This thesis explores the design, development, and practical implementation of a highôÙ
speed LiDAR ranging system utilizing a SingleôÙPhoton Avalanche Diode (SPAD) array
integrated with Field Programmable Gate Array (FPGA)ôÙbased processing. The primary
aim of this project was to develop a robust and flexible platform suitable for advanced 3D
imaging applications, particularly in autonomous driving, precision mapping, and industrial
automation scenarios. The work involved designing a custom Printed Circuit Board (PCB)
connection board specifically tailored to interface seamlessly the SPAD sensor, laser modôÙ
ule, and two FPGA controllers, AXU3EG and CrossLink, thereby ensuring effective and
precise timing synchronization critical for accurate TimeôÙofôÙFlight (ToF) measurements.
Significant emphasis was placed on achieving reliable highôÙspeed MIPI (Mobile Industry
Processor Interface) data handling, differential pair impedance matching, and sophistiôÙ
cated FPGA timing control strategies implemented through finite state machines (FSM).
The project also extensively explored SPIôÙbased register configuration methodologies,
critical to managing the SPAD array and laser operations effectively.
Throughout the development process, the project encountered various technical chalôÙ
lenges, notably hardware interfacing issues arising from misaligned FFC (Flexible Flat
Cable) connector orientation and limited I/O availability on the FPGA development board.
Additionally, constraints imposed by the selected laser moduleãs slow control response
times significantly restricted the systemãs intended operational speed and overall perforôÙ
mance capabilities. These challenges limited comprehensive validation of the full system
at the intended highôÙspeed parameters.
Despite these setbacks, substantial progress was achieved in several critical areas. Key
accomplishments included successful PCB fabrication and verification, validation of highôÙ
speed differential pair signal routing integrity, robust SPI register operations, and the sucôÙ
cessful implementation and testing of FPGAôÙbased FSM synchronization logic. PrelimiôÙ
nary data conversion from MIPI to AXI data streams was also successfully demonstrated,
establishing a reliable foundation for future system enhancements and optimizations.
The practical experiences and insights gained through this project provided valuable learnôÙ
ing opportunities regarding PCB design intricacies, hardware debugging techniques, FPGA
programming intricacies, and critical management of highôÙspeed signal integrity. Future
development efforts will focus on addressing the identified hardware limitations through
PCB redesign, optimizing timing synchronization processes, and refining data handling
strategies to fully harness the potential and enhance the performance of the developed
LiDAR system.

1.2
Flash LiDAR vs. Scanning LiDAR
A wide laser pulse simultaneously illuminates the entire scene, and the SPAD array deôÙ
tects the returning photons in parallel. Although Flash LiDAR can instantly capture an enôÙ
tire image frame, it inherently suffers from limited detection range and low signal strength
for distant targets because the laser power is spread over a large area. This scattering
poses challenges for applications requiring longôÙrange detection and high measurement
accuracy.
By contrast, scanning LiDAR adopts a more focused approach, sequentially directing a
narrow laser beam to specific points in the scene. Concentrating laser energy into a tightly
controlled beam significantly enhances the strength of the return signal. As a result, scanôÙ
ning LiDAR solutions can achieve longer detection distances and higher measurement acôÙ
curacy, advantages that are particularly beneficial and increasingly favored in Advanced
DriverôÙAssistance Systems (ADAS) and autonomous driving platforms.

MEMS MicroôÙMirror Scanning
MEMS (MicroôÙElectroôÙMechanical Systems) microôÙmirror scanning uses a tiny tilting reôÙ
flective mirror to deflect the laser beam, achieving scanning in a twoôÙdimensional FOV.
MEMS mirrors are typically on the millimeter scale, made with siliconôÙbased microfabricaôÙ

In a typical design, one MEMS mirror can oscillate/tilt around both horizontal and vertical
axes, directing the laser beam to scan the space point by point. Since only a small mirror
moves while the laser transmitter, detector, and other components remain stationary, the
scanning structure is greatly simplified. Therefore, MEMS scanning is often referred to
as a ãquasiôÙsolidôÙstateã LiDAR solution, representing a compromise between mechanical
rotating LiDAR and fully solidôÙstate solutions.[14]
Although the MEMS mirror can oscillate at a high frequency to achieve rapid scanning,
its deflection angle is limited, resulting in a relatively small FOV per mirror . To achieve a
wide FOV, multiple mirrors or multiple laser channels often have to be combined, which
can lead to overlapping or distorted point clouds and calibration challenges . [15]Overall,
MEMS scanning retains the efficient scanning capability offered by moving parts while
significantly reducing the scale and mass of the scanning assembly. It is an important
technology pathway for achieving compact LiDAR systems.
1.2.3
Optical Phased Array (OPA) Scanning
An Optical Phased Array (OPA) uses integrated optical devices to control the phase of
emitted light, creating interference patterns in space and thus achieving inertialess, elecôÙ

numerous miniature antenna elements (e.g., grating couplers) are integrated on a chip,
and by adjusting the phase differences of the light emitted by each antenna, a beam with
specific directivity is formed . Changing the relative phases of these elements causes the
main lobe to tilt, realizing twoôÙdimensional FOV scanning. OPA controls beam direction
entirely via electrical signals, with no mechanical moving parts, and thus represents a
truly solidôÙstate scanning method[16].
Typically, OPA scanning architectures couple the laser source into onôÙchip waveguide
networks, where the light is split and its phase controlled by multiple phase shifters beôÙ

of emitting elements on a chip, an OPA can redirect the main beam in microseconds,
achieving extremely fast, inertialess scanning . OPA solutions also feature high inteôÙ
gration and miniaturization: key components can be manufactured on a single chip usôÙ
ing mature CMOS silicon photonics processes .[17] With no mechanical parts, OPAs are
highly resistant to vibration and shock and offer excellent reliability, theoretically reaching
mean time between failures of hundreds of thousands of hours. However, implementing
highôÙperformance OPAs requires overcoming challenges such as synchronizing numerôÙ

1.3
Comparison of SingleôÙPoint Detection Systems
From the above brief introduction to the three scanning methods, the performance advanôÙ
tage of OPA is obvious. Therefore, this project is designed to be used in conjunction with
OPA in the future.
Essentially, ranging modules all operate in a ãsingleôÙpointã mode. In contrast, scanning
lidar adds beam pointing control to singleôÙpoint ranging, enabling the same transmit/reôÙ
ceive unit to cover multiple directions in a timeôÙdivision manner. The singleôÙpoint lidar
detection system uses only one laser beam for ranging, has no scanning mechanism
(or manual/fixed aiming), and can only obtain distance information in one direction. Its
structure is similar to that of a typical laser rangefinder: it consists of a laser transmitter,
a receiving detector, and ranging electronics, but lacks any beam control components.
The singleôÙpoint system determines the distance by repeatedly emitting laser pulses (or
continuously modulated light) in a fixed direction, receiving the reflected signal from the
target, and measuring the time of flight (ToF).
However, because development of the OPA intended for use in my system could not
be completed as planned during the thesis period, I decided to begin with a singleôÙpoint
detection approach to observe the overall system performance.
1.3.1
Signal Processing Chain Testing
LiDAR ranging involves tasks such as echo detection, filtering, peak extraction, multipleôÙ
echo recognition, and distance calculation. In singleôÙpoint mode, the entire signal proôÙ
cessing chain can also be debugged and validated. A singleôÙpoint system can also place
reflectors at different distances to verify the algorithmãs capability to distinguish multiple
echoes. Because singleôÙpoint data are simple (usually just distance values or a small
amount of waveform data), they can be readily compared with theoretical calculations, alôÙ
lowing incremental optimization of signal processing parameters. By testing on a singleôÙ
point system, one can discover and resolve issues (e.g., calibrating absolute distance
errors) to ensure that each pointãs distance calculation is reliable once OPAôÙbased point
cloud imaging is implemented.
1.3.2

In scanning LiDAR, synchronization between beam steering and distance acquisition is
critical. Mechanical or MEMS scanners rely on mechanical position encoding synchroôÙ
nized with the LiDAR triggerëƒ OPAôÙbased electronic scanning requires synchronizing the

singleôÙpoint system does not change beam direction, it still requires precise synchroôÙ

a timestamp or sync signal is generated at each emission to start the reception timer.
Through singleôÙpoint testing, one can perfect the timing and synchronization mechanism,

Linking of logic modules
In this design, a self define AXI bridge module is first used to handle parallel data and clock
signals from CrossLink. The bridge module operates in the byte clock domain output by
CrossLink, receiving the capture_en signal and 8ôÙbit data. On each valid data cycle, it
generates the standard AXIôÙStream control and data signals such as TVALID and TDATA.
Since there is no pause mechanism on the sensor side, any blockage in the subsequent
channel may prevent data from being transmitted in time. Therefore, the bridge module
needs to detect frame end and set TLAST=1 on the final valid data cycle. Additionally, given
that CrossLinkãs byte_clk is independent from the FPGAãs main clock, the subsequent
crossôÙclockôÙdomain operation is crucial for ensuring data integrity.
To address data synchronization across different clock domains, this design inserts XilôÙ
inxãs AXI4ôÙStream FIFO between the bridge module and AXI DMA, configuring it for inôÙ
dependent clock mode. The FIFOãs write port receives the AXIôÙStream output (includôÙ
ing TVALID, TDATA, TLAST) from the bridge module, with the write clock source being the
CrossLink output clock, while its read port is connected to the FPGAãs internal main clock.
Through dualôÙport RAM and synchronization logic, the FIFO provides buffering and safe
data transfer between clock domains. When the FIFO is nearly full, it pulls TREADY low to
notify the upstream bridge module to pause sending, so as to avoid overflow. However,
if the bridge module cannot respond to backpressure, additional design considerationsã
such as increased FIFO depth or further bufferingãmay be needed. Depending on the
bandwidth and frame size requirements, a FIFO capacity of 4 KB, 8 KB, or larger can be
chosen to accommodate varying throughput over time.
After crossing the clock domain, the FIFOãs read port then outputs standard AXIôÙStream
data to the S2MM interface of the AXI DMA. Typically, the AXI DMA runs at the same
frequency as the FPGAãs main clock, writing data into DDR on the PS side. In this process,
each frameãs transmission end is flagged by the TLAST signalëƒ upon detecting TLAST, the
DMA updates the corresponding buffer descriptor and can trigger an interrupt to notify
the processing system. Since the DMA clock (operating around 200 MHz) can fully utilize
the Zynq MPSoCãs HP interface bandwidth, it achieves fast data writes to external DDR.
With this design, even if the sensor side does not support backpressure, frame data can
be reliably transferred from CrossLink to PS DDR, fulfilling realôÙtime capture and storage
requirements.

FPGA platforms (CrossLink and AXU3EG) were chosen for their processing power and
