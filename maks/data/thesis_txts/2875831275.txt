Introduction
1.1
Background of study
In recent decades, the Arctic has emerged as a region of growing strategic and commercial interest,
driven by climate change, resource exploration, and the opening of new shipping routes.
As a
result, the demand for safe and robust polar class vessels capable of operating in ice-prone waters
has significantly increased. To support this development, classification societies such as DNV have
continued to evolve their technical standards, particularly the Polar Class (PC) rules, which provide a
framework for designing and assessing ships intended for polar operation.
In recent years, the DNV Polar Class rules have adopted the International Association of Classification
Societies (IACS) Unified Requirements (UR) rule, first introduced around 2000 based on Daley (1999),
which was established based on the best available knowledge of ice loads and ship-ice interaction at the
time. However, with increasing operational experience, improved simulation capabilities, and evolving
environmental conditions, it is essential to reassess whether these rules continue to accurately represent
the ice loads encountered by vessels in service today.
In particular, DNV emphasized two main categories of ice loading scenarios:
• Design ice loads on the hull, including glancing impacts on the bow and side loads on non-bow
areas, together with ice compression loads amidships.
• Ramming impacts on the bow, with special attention to vertical ice forces.
This thesis, based on a realistic ship bow model provided by DNV, concentrates primarily on the
assessment of glancing impacts on the bow. In addition, it will examine and discuss the ice compression
loads amidships to provide a more comprehensive understanding of ice-induced loading on polar class
vessels.
1.2
Previous study
Back in 2012, Daley was involved in a comparison between analytical and numerical models of
glancing ship-ice collisions by J. Liu et al. (2012). In their study, they
• Use a simplified rigid plate model to represent the ship’s bow
• Includes dynamic friction between bow and ice
Page 1 of 74
==PAGE:4==

• Use simplified isotropic elastic-plastic ice model
The intention behind this modeling approach is to reflect the actual physical scenario of a glancing
impact.
However, it cannot be directly compared with the rule-based model, as the additional
assumptions introduced may lead to further discrepancies. For instance, the rule-based formulation
assumes frictional interaction between the bow and the ice; introducing friction in the numerical
model would alter the distribution of kinetic energy, reducing the portion available for ice crushing.
Moreover, in the rules, the ice load is dependent on the hull shape, whereas using a simple flat plate in
the simulation cannot capture the variation in load magnitude across different positions on a realistic
bow geometry.
1.3
Overview of the Present Study
This study was designed to evaluate and compare rule-based and numerical predictions of ice loads on
a Polar Class vessel, focusing on both glancing impacts at the bow and compression loads amidship.
The main work carried out includes:
• Using the rule requirements and a realistic ship geometry model to calculate the rule-based
design load.
• Developing a numerical simulation model following the rule requirements to replicate the
scenarios described in the background note of the design ice load formulation.
• Performing a systematic comparison between the rule-based and numerical results, and identi-
fying key similarities, differences, and their implications for design.
Building on insights gained from previous studies and addressing their identified limitations, while
aligning with the DNV Polar Class requirements, the technical innovations, advantages, and unique
aspects of this study are as follows:
• The numerical setup was carefully designed to closely follow the original assumptions described
in the background note of the design ice load, thereby reducing potential errors from introducing
additional assumptions not present in the rule formulation.
• A real-life ship bow geometry was used in the numerical model, enabling a more accurate
representation of how ice loads vary across different impact positions on the bow.
• A strain-rate-dependent ice material model provided by Mokhtari, E. Kim, et al. (2023) was
adopted, providing a more realistic representation of ice mechanical behavior.

• A glacial ice model was specifically chosen to meet the DNV requirement for extreme loading
conditions, providing an upper-bound estimate of ice-structure interaction forces.
• In addition to the fixed glancing impact scenario described in the rule, a sliding glancing

dynamics.

Analytical (DNV) Design Ice Load
2.1
Introduction
When designing ships intended to operate in ice-covered waters, specific rules and standards must
be followed to determine the magnitude and distribution of forces and pressures applied to the ship’s
hull. These rules ensure that the vessel’s structure can safely withstand the unique and severe loads
imposed by interactions with sea ice, including both local pressures and global hull forces.
In the DNV Polar Class (PC) rule, the requirements for achieving an additional class notation PC -
indicating a vessel’s capability to operate in varying levels of ice severity - are, in general, aligned with
the IACS UR for Polar Ships. This aims to provide consistent safety standards across classification
societies, ensuring that ships designed to a given Polar Class meet internationally recognized structural
integrity and performance criteria for polar operations.

Glancing impact load on the bow
In the DNV Polar Class rule, the glancing impact definitions related to this study are defined and
explained as follows:
• A glancing impact on the bow is considered the design scenario for determining the required
scantling to withstand ice loads.
• The design ice load is modeled as an average pressure 𝑃𝑎𝑣𝑔uniformly distributed over a rectan-
gular contact area of height ℎand width 𝑤.
• For Polar Class ships and specifically within the bow and bow intermediate ice belt areas of
𝑃𝐶6 and 𝑃𝐶7, the ice load parameters depend on the actual bow geometry.

• Different equations are used for different hull forms. For a general bow related to this study, the
geometry angles need to meet the following criteria: 0° < 𝛾< 80°, 𝛽′ > 10°.
To obtain the design ice load, DNV has begun to adopt the method long accepted by IACS UR.
The method for calculating the design ice load included in the IACS Unified Requirements was
initially proposed by Claude Daley in 2000. Subsequently, Daley formalized and summarized this
approach within the IACS framework (Daley 2000). In recent years, DNV has also begun to adopt
this method for designing ice loads on ships. The method defines a design scenario based on several
key assumptions:
Ice:
• The ice wedge has an edge open angle of 150° as shown in Figure 3
• Ice thickness and flexure strength are Polar Class dependent
• The pressure-area relationship is Polar Class dependent (the power relation takes a constant
𝑒𝑥= −0.1 in Equation 9)
• Very massive, stays still after glancing
Ship:
• Glancing occurs at the shoulder of the bow
• Ship hull considered rigid
• Ship has an initial velocity (Polar Class dependent) when glancing occurs, the velocity is Polar
Class dependent
Interaction:
• Ship kinetic energy is equated to the ice-crushing energy
• No friction between the ship hull and ice wedge
• ’Popov’ type of 1-D collision, no ’ride-up’ on the ice (Daley 2000)

Ice Model
3.1
Development of ice model
The accurate modeling of ice behavior under collision loading is critical for predicting ice-induced
damage to marine structures. Due to the inherent heterogeneity and complexity of ice, including its
viscoelastic, brittle, and ductile characteristics under different strain rates and pressure conditions,
developing a universal ice material model remains a significant challenge. (Brodeau et al. 2024; Pronk
et al. 2024)
In current practice, plasticity-based models are commonly adopted to simulate inelastic deformation
in ice-structure collisions due to their computational efficiency and relative simplicity in calibration.
Although ice exhibits viscoelastic properties, especially at low strain rates, plastic rheology is favored
for high-rate impact scenarios where brittle failure dominates (Mokhtari, E. Kim, et al. 2022). Fur-
thermore, most ship-ice interactions occur at strain rates exceeding the ductile-to-brittle transition
threshold (10−4-10−3 s−1), where viscous effects become negligible and plastic deformation governs
the material response (Mokhtari, E. Kim, et al. 2022). Another important factor is that plasticity mod-
els such as Crushable Foam by Deshpande et al. (2000), Drucker et al. (1952), and Mohr-Coulomb
(Bai et al. 2010) are widely available in commercial finite element software like Abaqus, making them
accessible and practical for engineering applications. However, these models were initially developed
for materials like metallic foams, soils, or concrete, and are not specifically tailored to capture the
pressure-sensitive and rate-dependent behavior unique to ice (Bigoni et al. 2004).
Furthermore, while the Crushable Foam (CF) model has been effective in specific applications due to
its simplicity and availability in commercial software, it lacks inherent mechanisms to differentiate the
mechanical response of ice in high-pressure zones (HPZ) and low-pressure zones (LPZ) unless these
are manually prescribed (Gagnon 2011; H. Kim et al. 2015). This manual strength zoning introduces
artificial partitioning and fails to reflect the continuous distribution of strength and pressure observed
in real ice crushing processes.
A notable advancement is the Tsai-Wu-based model proposed by Z. Liu et al. (2011), which was
developed explicitly for ice. This model has shown improved accuracy in simulating the confining
(hydrostatic) pressure effects observed in physical ice crushing tests. However, Liu’s model remains
rate-independent, making it less suitable for scenarios where the strain rate sensitivity of ice is
prominent, particularly at lower loading rates.
To overcome these drawbacks, the present study adopts the advanced VUMAT model proposed by
Mokhtari, E. Kim, et al. (2022). This model extends Liu’s formulation by incorporating a rate- and
pressure-sensitive Tsai-Wu yield surface that evolves dynamically in response to changing loading
Page 20 of 74
==PAGE:23==

Spring 25
conditions. Unlike CF, this model naturally captures confinement effects, produces realistic HPZ/LPZ
distributions, and eliminates the need for manual zoning or artificial layering.
It also accurately
simulates the strain-rate-dependent failure behavior of ice, as validated against experimental ice
crushing tests conducted at multiple indentation speeds.

Ice wedge shape
The ice wedge used in this study has an opening angle of 150°, consistent with the definition provided
in the rules. The mesh is generated such that all elements are of identical size, ensuring numerical
consistency and stability. This approach is adopted to:
• Minimize numerical errors caused by irregular element shapes or sizes.
• Ensure that, during the crushing process, each element exhibits the same mechanical response
in terms of strain evolution, energy absorption, and stress distribution.
• Prevent artificial localization of deformation due to mesh bias, thereby improving the accuracy
of contact force and energy dissipation predictions.
• Maintain stable element distortion behavior, as recommended by Mokhtari, E. Kim, et al. (2023),
which is particularly important for simulating progressive crushing and element erosion.

discussed above. Two alternative geometries were developed through a process of trial and error:

Conclusion and Discussion
This study employed the numerical tool ABAQUS to investigate the glancing impact load at the bow
area and the compression ice load amidship on a Polar Class vessel. A novel pressure- and strain-rate-
dependent glacial ice material model was implemented to capture the mechanical behavior of strong
glacial ice under dynamic loading. This choice reflects the intention of the DNV Polar Class rules to
evaluate vessels under the most severe and demanding ice conditions, providing a conservative basis
for structural design. A real-life Polar Class vessel geometry model is used to study and reflect the
ice load distribution along the bow region both numerically and analytically. Following the mission
framework defined by DNV, the numerical results were compared with the analytical design ice loads
in terms of both pressure and line load specified by the rule-based method. The comparison provided
new insights into the differences between numerical predictions and rule-based estimates, particularly
under severe ice conditions.
This study made a dedicated effort to reproduce the conditions described in the background note of
the IACS design ice load formulation, which is now adopted by the DNV design ice load approach,
considering the problem from the integrated perspective of the ice, the ship, and their interaction
dynamics. By closely matching these original assumptions, the study minimized potential errors that
could arise from introducing additional, non-essential assumptions. For example, if friction between
the ice and the hull were included in the model, a portion of the initial kinetic energy of the ice would
be dissipated through friction, rather than being fully converted into indentation energy. This energy
loss would reduce the predicted loading magnitude, potentially producing results lower than those
intended in the original rule formulation. By adhering to the same underlying assumptions as the
rule, this work ensures a more direct and fair comparison between the numerical simulations and the
rule-based predictions, strengthening the validity of the findings.
Findings of the study
This study compares the numerical results with the rule-based predictions for glancing ice loads.
The result shows that the force and area from numerical simulation, regardless of fixed or sliding
glancing impact, are generally in line with the rule-based results, with lower force and smaller contact
area at the left and right ends of the examined ship bow area. However, the analysis shows that the
rule-based glancing ice load formulation not only overestimates the force acting on the bow region
but also the final contact area between the ice and the bow. This discrepancy ultimately results in an
underestimation of the pressure-area relationship compared to the results obtained using the glacial
ice material model.
However, due to the intentional reduction of contact area into the ’design contact area’ in the rule-based
Page 63 of 74
==PAGE:66==
Marine Technology, Master’s Thesis
Spring 25
method, the final design ice load for the bow region, particularly along the upper ice waterline, tends
to overestimate the local pressure. From a structural safety standpoint, this conservative solution is
beneficial, as it provides an additional safety margin for the bow structure design in polar navigation,
even for strong glacial ice.
This finding highlights that while rule-based methods may lack precision in capturing the detailed
mechanics of ice-structure interaction, their built-in conservatism in particular aspects can still serve
as good engineering practice for ensuring structural integrity.
For the glancing impact at the ship bow, this study advances beyond the rule-based fixed glancing
impact scenario by introducing a sliding glancing impact model. This refinement was motivated by
the need to more accurately represent the realistic post-contact “sliding” behavior of an ice wedge
after it strikes the bow. The numerical simulations reveal that, particularly for impacts occurring in
the near-bow intermediate region, commonly referred to as the shoulder area, the sliding behavior
persists longer than in the fixed scenario. As a result, the ice wedge can migrate into regions of the
bow that are not structurally optimized to withstand high glancing loads. This occurs even when the
sliding and fixed scenarios involve the same initial kinetic energy and result in a similar total volume
of ice crushed.
From a design and safety perspective, this finding is significant: it suggests that the fixed glancing
impact assumption used in current DNV Polar Class rules may be sufficient for general glancing
positions but could underestimate risk in specific regions such as the bow shoulder. The research,
therefore, provides evidence that additional design attention should be given to this area under more
realistic sliding impact conditions. The approach presented in the thesis not only enhances the accuracy
of numerical ice impact modeling but also provides a framework for refining classification society
guidelines to address operational risks in polar navigation better.
Taken together, these findings indicate that while the fixed glancing impact assumption in the current
DNV Polar Class rules is adequate for most glancing positions, it may not fully capture the dynamic
movement of ice wedges in realistic conditions, particularly in the shoulder area. The sliding glancing
model introduced here not only improves the physical realism of numerical ice impact simulations but
also provides actionable insights for refining classification society guidelines to address operational
risks in polar navigation better.
For ice load amidship, the analysis also shows an ultimate linear loading result compared with the
rule-based calculation, as it uses a strong glacial model. Providing an upper limit for the future study.
It confirms that when realistic high-strength ice properties are applied in numerical simulations, the
resulting loads can significantly exceed those predicted by existing rule-based methods. This reinforces
the importance of incorporating up-to-date material models and empirical validation into classification
society formulations, ensuring that ship structural designs remain robust under extreme ice conditions.

Limitations of the study
Legacy assumptions in bow and amidship ice load assessment
The IACS design ice load formulation for both the bow region and the amidship compression load
was developed more than two decades ago, and some of the requirements and assumptions in its
background note Daley (2000) may no longer fully reflect present-day operational and environmental
conditions. Nevertheless, this study is intentionally based on that background note to ensure a direct
and fair comparison with the rule-based methodology.
For the bow region, specific physical parameters defined in Table 2-such as the design ice velocity and
ice thickness for different Polar Classes-are adopted as essential input parameters for the numerical
simulations. Although these values are no longer explicitly stated in the current IACS and DNV
rules (which now only retain class factors such as 𝐶𝐹𝐶and 𝐶𝐹𝐹), they remain critical for faithfully
reproducing the original design approach.
A similar situation applies to the ice compression load amidship. In earlier formulations, the ice
compression class factor was treated as the equivalent ice thickness. Still, in today’s IACS and DNV
rules, it is expressed directly as a class factor rather than a physical dimension. In this study, these
older physical interpretations are still used as key setup parameters, ensuring consistency with the
original methodology and enabling a more meaningful numerical-rule comparison.
Given the central role of these parameters in determining both glancing impact and compression loads,
further laboratory experiments and full-scale measurements are recommended to verify whether these
original physical values remain valid for modern ice conditions and ship operations. Such validation
would help bridge the gap between historical design assumptions and current classification society
practices.
Limitations of the elastoplastic ice material model at low velocities
As noted by Mokhtari and Leira (2024), the elastoplastic ice material model performs well in represent-
ing ice behavior in the brittle range, i.e., at strain rates above the ductile–brittle transition. However,
E. Kim, Amdahl, and Wang (2021) reports that it is often the ductile range—at strain rates below
this transition—that can cause more damage to the vertical sides of a ship’s hull. In this lower strain
rate regime, viscoelastic ice models provide a more accurate representation of the ductile deformation
characteristics of ice.
In many realistic scenarios, ice floes pressing against a ship’s hull move at relatively low speeds, often
below 2𝑚/𝑠. Below this velocity range, the elastoplastic ice material model may not capture the ice
Page 65 of 74
==PAGE:68==
Marine Technology, Master’s Thesis
Spring 25
behavior with sufficient accuracy. Consequently, the load-velocity relationship for speeds below 2𝑚/𝑠
is not addressed in this study.
