Abstract
The rotor downwash created by vertical take-off and landing aircrafts has long since been of
interest to academics and industrialists alike. With increasing requirements in wind comfort
studies on pedestrians, it is crucial to accurately predict the downwash from helicopters oper-
ating in the airspace immediately above urban areas. Currently, modelling techniques based on
linear 1D Momentum theory are being utilized to model the effect of rotor downwash in urban
settings. It is proposed that employing more detailed modelling techniques such as the Blade
Element Momentum or an Actuator-Line theory might be more accurate in predicting mean
and peak velocity profiles at ground level. This study also seeks to relate traditional wind-
comfort relations for effective gust values to mean and peak values obtained from numerical
rotorwash simulations.
By the use of Computational Fluid Dynamics, the 1D Momentum method is compared to the
Blade Element Method with regards to mean and peak velocity profiles. The results of the study
are verified against the experimental results already obtained on a Sikorsky CH-53E helicopter.
By verifying that an effective gust can be calculated from velocity fluctuations, the paper’s
results indicate that the Blade Element Method is superior in modelling the rotor-downwash.
The thesis is meant to provide the reader with a solid base of knowledge on how to understand
and implement the three modelling techniques described in this study.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Helicopter downwash or rotorwash describes the high-velocity flow field that is generated
beneath an operating vertical takeoff and landing (VTOL) aircraft as a result of the thrust
generated by the rotor. It is of special concern with larger helicopters as the downwash
created at ground level can be a disturbance and in some cases dangerous. Therefore,
numerical simulations are carried out to analyze the impact of the downwash in certain urban
areas when deciding at which height the helipad must be installed at to ensure safe passage
for people on the ground below. Utilizing Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD), several
different methods are available to simulate the forces imposed on the surroundings by the
rotor blades in a computational domain. The methods increase in both accuracy and
computational cost as the complexity of the blade modelling increases. It then becomes of
interest to analyze and quantify the difference between these methods to better predict
downwash analysis in industrial applications. This study sets forth to analyze and compare
the three rotor modelling methods: 1D Momentum method (1DM), Blade Element Method
(BEM) and Actuator-Line method (ALM).
1.1
Wind evaluation
As the capabilities of modern engineering increase and high-rise buildings are sprouting up in
cities, the challenge will be to design comfortable and safe spaces in urban areas. Pedestrian
comfort is increasingly taken into consideration when designing buildings, and this of course
extends to the design of helipads. In the design of e.g. hospitals, the likely flight paths of
incoming helicopters are mapped based on Windrose diagrams. Thus, simulations can be
carried out with initial building designs to identify areas of high wind speeds due to the
downwash from an approaching helicopter. Over the years, wind comfort criteria have been
formulated to form a basis for evaluating these results, often relying on wind velocity ranges
for certain activities and frequencies of occurrence ([3]). As the wind is a highly fluctuating
entity, much focus has gone into defining a characteristic velocity that can be used when
discussing wind criteria such as mean velocities and gust velocities ([4]). Recent studies have
moved towards using a Gust Equivalent Mean (GEM) process for wind-tunnel studies which
essentially relates the size of the gust with its probability, making it comparable to the
recorded mean values ([5]). This study seeks to examine whether the opposite is true and if
the peak velocities can be determined from the measured mean velocities.

1.2
Case introduction
In 1994, the Federal Aviation Agency (FAA) released their Rotorwash Analysis Handbook [1],
which was a culmination of work started in 1985 regarding different types of rotorwash. The
handbook is accompanied by their "ROTWASH"-software; a program that is built on modified
analytical solutions to predict rotorwash in different, preset cases. However, the usefulness of
predictive methodologies and mathematical models relates directly to their ability to predict
experimental test cases accurately. This statement becomes even more true as the complexity
of the problem at hand increases, as is the case with rotorwash. As the FAA’s ROTWASH
software comprised a number of different cases, the handbook includes experimental data
from several different sources of various helicopters. The experimental data was subjectively
ranked with respect to the quality of the experiment and data. The winner, a Sikorsky
CH-53E based experiment, gathered mean and peak velocity flight data in various positions
from the rotor in a series of different rotor heights. This same dataset is chosen as the
foundation for this study several reasons. Firstly, it is good practice in engineering
applications to design for the worst-case scenario. While many civil rotorcrafts are small, it is
not unlikely that a large rotorcraft may have to land on a hospital helipad, in which case the
heavy downwash created from these larger rotorcrafts must be taken into account during the
design phase. Secondly, the amount of flight test data available on the CH-53E outclasses all
other rotorcrafts, having carried out experiments varying in both Gross Weight (GW) and
Rotor Height Above Ground Level (RHAGL), which allows for more rigorous testing of the
methods with more dataset to compare. Lastly, the quality of the data is preferable to any of
the other single-rotor helicopters on which data was available.
1.2.1
FAA experiment description
1.2.1.1
The CH-53E helicopter
The Sikorsky CH-53 is a heavy lift military helicopter of which several configurations exists,
with the CH-53E Sea Stallion being the rotorcraft used in this study. A three-sided view
courtesy of the FAA ([1] p. 67) can be seen in figure 1.1.
With a rotor diameter of 24m and a max GW of 31 tonnes, the experiment was conducted by
loading the helicopter to its max GW, having it hover in a fixed RHAGL by utilizing a tether
line from the cargo hook to the ground and then measure the mean and peak profile velocity
data at different distances from the rotor center (DRFCs). The thrust output from the main
rotor was kept constant through the use of a load cell. A schematic of the experimental setup
can be seen in figure 1.2.
The measurements were taken with an ion-beam deflection anemometer of which a schematic
can be seen in figure 1.3.
The ion-beam deflection anemometer works by relating the point force F acting on the sphere
to the resulting deflection s of the rod. Note that Dsphere ≫Drod, as the accuracy of this
anemometer directly relates to the assumption that the only contributing factor to the
deflection is the force on the sphere and not the rod itself. Experiments conducted by Hölling
et al. (2007) suggest the superiority of the ion-beam deflection anemometer compared to the
regular cup anemometer in regards to both mean wind measurements and the response time
of the anemometers [6].

2.1.2
Turbulence modelling
The discovery can best be attributed O. Reynolds’ now-famous experiment in 1883, where he
studied the transition from laminar to turbulent flow in a pipe. As a result of this, once A.
Sommerfeld and G.G. Stokes began working with the non-dimensional number to analyze this
phenomenon, it was named the Reynolds number:
Re = uL
ν = ρuL
µ
(2.1.3)
The Reynolds number essentially balances the inertial forces against the viscous forces in the
flow. While the exact value of the Reynolds number at the transition to turbulence has been
hard to determine and is also very case-dependent, it can clearly be seen that once the
characteristic length L or relative velocity u increases, the Reynolds number increases rapidly.
With regards to rotor modelling the flow is inherently turbulent, and special care must be
taken in order to resolve this quantity of the flow. There are three main methodologies to
solve the turbulent flow field: Direct Numerical Simulation (DNS), Large-Eddy Simulation
(LES) and Reynolds Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS). As listed, they decrease in accuracy
but also heavily increase in computational cost. Carrying out a DNS of a project of this scale
is not feasible, and so one is left with two different approaches; the averaging method of the
RANS model or the scale resolving method of LES. While LES is more accurate than RANS,
in order to resolve enough of the eddies in the flow, the mesh requirements would be too large
to accomplish the goals set forth. That leaves the RANS approach, which is the preferred
approach for many engineering applications where the turbulence field itself is not the subject.

2.1.2.3
Realizable K-Epsilon All Y+
The Realizable K-ϵ All Y+ model is an improvement over the standard K-ϵ model by
incorporating a new transport equation for the turbulent dissipation rate ϵ as well as
including a damping function in the eddy viscosity approximation. The damping function is
what separates the Realizable K-ϵ-model from the regular K-ϵ-model. In the original
formulation, the damping function would be equal to one, essentially not offering any
dampening at all. A two-layer approach is also available in which the computation is divided
into two layers; in the layer that is against the wall, turbulent viscosity µt and dissipation rate
ϵ are specified in relation to the wall distance. The values are then smoothly blended with the
values obtained from solving the transport equations far away from the wall. The transport
equation for TKE is solved in the entire flow domain. This essentially allows the model to be
efficient in simulations where one region may be of low Reynolds number mesh (y+ ≈1) and
in the wall-function meshes (y+ > 30).

2.1.3
Segregated and coupled flow solvers
Star-CCM+ offers two main ways of solving the governing equations: the segregated flow
solver and the coupled flow solver. The segregated flow model solves each of the momentum
equations in sequence in varying dimensions. To subsequently link the momentum and
continuity equations, a predictor-corrector approach is invoked. It is well-suited for
constant-density flows and can be considered a robust approach in absence of high-Mach
number flows. The coupled model instead solves the equations for continuity and momentum
simultaneously with a pseudo-time approach. This approach is well-suited for simulating the
capture of shock-waves and other high-Mach number flows ([15]).

Ground Effects
When flying a VTOL, depending on at what height the vehicle is being operated, there is
either the Out of Ground Effect (OGE) or In Ground Effect (IGE). The effects are describing
how the lift on the rotor blades changes with operating height, specifically for the IGE. When
the helicopter is operating near or within 1/2 rotor diameter of the ground, it is subjected to
the interference of the surface on the would-be airflow pattern as a result of rotor operation.
This phenomenon increases the closer the helicopter is to the ground. As the ground does not

turn, results in less induced drag and a higher vertical lift. A VTOL operating in IGE thus
requires a smaller AoA to produce the same lift had it been in OGE.
2.3
Implementation of rotor models in Star-CCM+
The modelling of a rotor in a computational domain can be carried out in different ways.
Fundamentally, it requires that the source term of the momentum equations be updated with
the effects of the rotor blades in the computational cells that are associated with the rotor
blades. Star-CCM+ has a built-in physics model called "Virtual Disk" which contains three
different modelling methods:
1. The Body Force Propeller method
2. The 1D Momentum method
3. The Blade Element method
All three are specifically tailored towards different modelling purposes. The body force
propeller method is primarily designed to model the propulsion of ship propellers without
actually having to model the geometry of the propeller blades. The 1DM is specifically
designed to model the effects of wind turbines geared towards analyzing the effects wind
turbines have on the surrounding flow given the extraction of mechanical energy from the
wind. Lastly, the BEM is designed to model the effects on the flow field as a result of the
presence of helicopter rotors, again without having to directly model the helicopter rotors.
Naturally, the latter method is directly suitable for this study, and as such will be employed
in the modelling of the BEM. This model includes the option to switch between two
time-modelling approaches: time-averaged and time-accurate. The following sections describe
how the source term is updated in the case of each different modelling method; 1DM, BEM
time-averaged and BEM time-accurate.

2.3. IMPLEMENTATION OF ROTOR MODELS IN STAR-CCM+
2.3.1
1D momentum
As the built-in method of Star-CCM+ that uses the 1DM is directed towards the modelling of
wind turbines and the power output of these, it was not found suitable to try and fit this
model into a study of helicopter rotors. Instead, an actuator-disc method has been manually
implemented by simply removing a circular ’slice’ of the computational grid and applying the
thrust equivalent induced velocity as boundary conditions on the disc. This is a time-efficient
method of modelling the appearance of a rotor disc, and several improvements can be made
to improve the accuracy of this modelling approach. The presence of the rotor blades is thus
being modelled by the induced velocity which quickly can be calculated from equation 2.2.10
as the required thrust to hover T and the area of the rotor disc AP2 is readily available. This
induced velocity is then prescribed as boundary conditions on the faces in accordance with
the depiction in figure 2.3. This method thus prescribes a fixed induced velocity throughout
the entirety of the actuator-disc, which is of course not physical.
2.3.2

As mentioned in section 2.3, Star-CCM+’s built-in virtual disc model can use BEM. By
prescribing airfoil properties and the general geometry of the rotor-blades, Star-CCM+ is

stated earlier, the force can be applied in two different ways: time-averaged and time-accurate.

The time-averaged approach is a virtual disc model. The disc is radially distributed into N
prescribed bins, which constitutes the resolution of the blade. The elements are subdivided
into radial and tangential resolutions, as can be seen in figure 2.7. It is this interpolation grid

only from the single finite volume cell which is closest to the center of the interpolation cell.
It is in these cells that the aerodynamic properties such as the local Mach number and AoA
are calculated and then interpolated onto the underlying computational grid.

Initially, all cells that are contained in the virtual disk are selected to receive the source term
of BEM. These cells are then assigned a bucket number that corresponds to their location
relative to the interpolation grid.

momentum conservation equations. It is a time-averaged source term that averages the

Ideal gas
The equation of state model is responsible for computing the density of the medium with
respect to the pressure and temperature. While special models are available, the three
standard models for the equation of state are the constant density, ideal gas and real gas
models listed in increasing accuracy. While there are no critical points in the flow where the
conditions are extreme enough to require the Real Gas model, nor is it of interest, it would
not be representative to use the constant density model in this study. While an argument
could be made it would be sufficient for 1DM, for the BEM and especially the ALM, there are
regions where the flow is accelerating where a constant density model would not suffice. It is
therefore decided to keep this model constant throughout the study so as not to introduce
unnecessarily different variables.

3.2.3
SST K-Omega
The SST K-ω model is employed to handle the turbulence quantities of the flow. It is run
alongside its All Y+ Wall Treatment model which allows it to work in the boundary layer and
the free stream. A few settings are changed in the model controls which will be listed and
covered here:
1. Physics model: Realizability coefficient 0.6 →0.7
2. Turbulence solver: Under relaxation factor 0.8 →0.4
3. Turbulent viscosity solver: Maximum ratio 1e + 06 →1e + 08
First, the realizability coefficient is a built-in parameter to handle unexpectedly large growths
of K ([18]). This usually happens at stagnation points, such as at the tip of airfoils or
impinging jets. It is increased slightly over the original value as this simulation has stagnation
points in both the surface of the fuselage and the ground below. Second, problems with
divergence in turbulence quantities can happen if there are large increases in turbulence,
especially at the start of the simulation. This is partly handled by ramping the rotation rate
on the rotor, but guidelines suggest reducing the value to somewhere fitting between 0.3 and
0.5 ([19]). Third, Simulations operating with high Reynolds numbers can reach the standard
limit on the turbulent viscosity solver ([19]). It was discovered relatively early that this limit
was reached by the rotor-blades. Raising the limit had immediate effects on the quality of the
results.

Mesh, convergence study & validation
Once the models are built, they are ready to be run in the CFD software. Before extracting
data for post-processing, it is crucial to examine the models for convergence to ensure that
the results you get are the results you want. Software such as Star-CCM+ is built to be
robust and can solve problems even though the solution might not be correct. No matter how
accurate one might think a model has been built, it is due diligence to examine model
parameters that affect the solution, such as the mesh and model input parameters. The
purpose is to examine the effect of e.g. the mesh on the desired results and subsequently
refine/coarsen the mesh to find a mesh-independent solution. The purpose of the following
chapter is twofold: present and analyze relevant convergence studies conducted and cover the
control of the thrust for the BEM time-averaged and the ALM.

4.1.2
Ground refinement
As previously mentioned, a refinement box is placed on the ground to refine the mesh in the
region where measurements are taken. A block part is created with the following parameters:
• Corner 1: [-55.0, 5.0, -55.0] m
• Corner 2: [55.0, -1.0, 55.0] m
The box is specifically designed to refine the prism layers on the ground in the region of [0,
5]m to make sure that all measurement points are included in the refined area (3.1).
These cells are created with the same purpose in mind as when the surface refinement was
made on the fuselage; to achieve favorable wall y+ values to properly resolve the flow
characteristics in the regions of interest. As opposed to the fuselage mesh, the ground
refinement has to be controlled differently for each RHAGL configuration. Moving the rotors
up and down will change the velocity profile at the ground significantly, and without
reconfiguring the prism layers, the wall y+ values will change. It is therefore necessary to
re-evaluate wall y+ values when changing RHAGL.
4.2
Convergence
Convergence is of major concern when using CFD software. Fluid mechanics is in its essence
complex with non-linear processes, containing inherently unstable concepts such as
Page 43 of 80
==PAGE:62==
CHAPTER 4. MESH, CONVERGENCE STUDY & VALIDATION
Figure 4.5: The prism layer cells on the ground.
turbulence. Numerical methods aim to iteratively improve the solution until convergence is
reached. In mathematics, convergence describes a limiting behaviour on series that tend
toward a limit. To assert convergence is claiming the existence of such a limit, which in most
cases is not known. Therefore, convergence must always be associated with a particular level
of accuracy which can vary from problem to problem. The determination of convergence relies
on several different factors: local convergence parameters, global convergence parameters,
visualization of flow field values and the residuals ([26]).
4.2.1
Local convergence
In this study, local convergence is determined by the convergence of velocity values in points
that are associated with the planes on which the results are being monitored. That is,
monitoring the change in velocity at eight individual points placed at ground level at the
same DFRCs from 3.1. The velocity is traced with each iteration, as can be seen in figure 4.6.
While the velocity converges towards constant values, small fluctuations can still be observed.
As one might imagine, the highest velocity fluctuations occur at or around the disc diameter,
meaning DFRCs four through six. Deviations are in the ranges of 0.25 m/s which is around
1.7% of the mean velocity value (see appendix A.1). As the velocity may never converge
within a very small parameter (e.g. 0.1%), the simulation is allowed to run until the
magnitude of the velocity fluctuations no longer change. That is, when the fluctuations
observed remain the same.
The 1DM is significantly faster at reaching convergence in the velocity measurements. While
the BEM has to run for thousands of iterations in order to get good, stable measurements,
the 1DM converges within the first couple of thousand iterations.

value, at which point one could argue that the flow field has fully propagated from the rotor
down to the ground.

4.3
Mesh convergence study
Finding a mesh-independent solution relies upon refining the number of gridpoints until
certain monitors of interest no longer change with a finer mesh. In theory, a refinement will
almost always result in some sort of change in the results - in reality however, one might have
to compromise between accuracy and computational costs. The study is done to quantify the
error that is made and to identify the coarsest mesh to be utilized while being as close to the
real solution as possible. While carrying out the mesh convergence study for the 1DM would
be the fastest, it might not be representative of the other models. The simpler 1D model
might be content with a coarser mesh than what the BEM model would accept, and thus the
convergence campaign is carried out on the BEM model. Preliminary results indicated that
velocity fluctuations grew in magnitude with increasing RHAGL, which is why the
convergence study is carried out at the highest RHAGL. The reason is that if the mesh suits
the most critical model, it will also suit the rest. The convergence study is carried out on the
base size of the wake refinement mesh which subsequently will affect the number of prismatic
layers and the total prismatic layer thickness near the ground. A total of six mesh
configurations in addition to the reference model will be examined:

4.4
Thrust control
Thrust is arguably the most important parameter to monitor and control between the three
models. It is the basis for the comparison that is to be made between the velocity profiles.
Should the thrust not match, it would directly influence the magnitude of the velocity
measurements. Controlling the thrust is done differently between each model. The work
required to achieve the desired thrust is significantly different between the models as well. For
the 1DM, the thrust is simply controlled by equation 3.3.1. For the BEM models, the thrust
is dependent on the pitch angle of the rotor-blades, for which some calibration must be done
to achieve the desired results. Lastly, adjustments are made to account for the drag on the
fuselage. As the thrust is given in [1] as the GW of the helicopter, it follows that the rotor in
the experiment compensates for the drag on the actual Sikorsky CH-53E helicopter. While
the fuselage model included in this study is not nearly as detailed as the actual Sikorsky
CH-53E, an attempt is still made to ensure an accurate estimate of the drag on the
helicopter’s surface mesh and associated prism layer cells. These should be adequately
resolved in order to model the near-wall flow and give reasonable estimates of the total drag.
The drag is computed for each method and adjusted individually since the drag on the
helicopter changes significantly with the method of choice.
4.4.1
1D thrust control
Adjusting the thrust to match the experiment parameters for the 1D case consists of
estimating the drag force on the fuselage, as this force effectively adds to the thrust that is
needed for the helicopter to hover. The drag essentially steals some of the thrust that is being
generated by the rotor.
As the rotor is placed at the same height from the fuselage in all simulation models, the drag
is expected to be similar between all RHAGLs. The drag force is estimated for the 1D model
to be 3.18e + 04 N, which is 10% of the thrust. Replacing the thrust T in equation 3.3.1 with
a new Tnew that accounts for the fuselage drag, the new velocity boundary condition is
changed to 18.29 ms .
4.4.2
BEM thrust control
As the BEM already knows both the diameters of the rotors and the speed at which it turns,
the relative velocity is readily acquired. This, along with the AoA, is what effectively
determines the thrust of the rotor. The effective AoA is a combination of several angles: the
inflow angle, the blade flap angle and the geometrical pitch and twist angles, which are
combined into the collective pitch. Blade flapping is an up-and-down motion of the
rotor-blades which are designed to eliminate the asymmetric lift in forward flight, and is thus
of no concern in this hovering case. The thrust is then adjusted by changing the collective
pitch. For the BEM time-averaged method, a thrust trimmer is invoked to identify the pitch
angle at which the rotor produces the correct thrust, see figure 4.18.
The trimming algorithm begins once the main flow solver is ready to accept source terms into
the momentum equation. In the first step, the algorithm takes the main flow parameters such
as pressure, velocities, etc., and computes the BEM source term. A subsequent check is then
performed on both the BEM source term convergence and the ramping of the rotation rate.
The BEM source term convergence is evaluated with respect to the source tolerance which is
set to 1% since there is no need to start trimming the rotor-blades if the source term is not
Page 54 of 80
==PAGE:73==
4.4. THRUST CONTROL
Figure 4.18: The trim convergence algorithm to control the rotor thrust.
converged yet. There is also no need for trimming before the ramp is finished, which is why
this check incorporates both. Once fulfilled, the algorithm checks the trim state. It is checked
with respect to the trim convergence control which is essentially the same as the source
convergence control in the previous step. Should the trim state not converge, the rotor trim
angles are updated and the algorithm is repeated.
To account for the drag on the fuselage as with the 1DM, a test-case is set up where the
trimmer is set to match the thrust given in 3.3.2 to estimate the drag on the fuselage. The
drag on the fuselage using the BEM fluctuated around 6e+03 N, which is approximately a 2%
increase in thrust value. This is considerably lower than the 1DM, the reason being the
velocity distribution on the virtual disc. In the 1DM, the velocity is evenly distributed across
the virtual disc in the radial direction, whereas for the BEM the thrust increases with the
radial distance. The target thrust for the trim convergence algorithm is then adjusted to
3.20e+05N.
The ground effects (section 2.2.2 must also be considered for cases one and two (table 1.1)),
as these are operating within a vertical height of one rotor diameter. As the helicopter is
operating within IGE, it must change its blade pitch to keep the same thrust output.
Therefore, each case must be run initially with the thrust trimming algorithm enabled to
identify at which pitch setting the rotors are producing the right amount of thrust. Then the
pitch setting is set manually and the trimmer turned off to avoid any unnecessary fluctuations
caused by the trimmer pitching the blades.
Figure 4.19 illustrates how the flow stabilizes at the measurement points after adjusting the
pitch setting to match the correct thrust. For the first 2000 iterations, the simulations have

4.4.3
ALM thrust control
The control of the thrust on the ALM is not as straightforward as for the BEM. The pitch
trim algorithm (figure 4.18) is mutually exclusive with the ALM method due to the
time-discretization; the trimmer is only valid for steady solutions and the ALM is only valid
for unsteady solutions. There are two other obvious choices to control the thrust for the ALM
method: manually or by implementing a Newton-Rhapsons method. Siemens Simcenter,
which is the support site for Star-CCM+ users, offers articles geared towards guiding users in
implementing different methods such as the Newton-Rhapsons method ([29]). The
Newton-Rhapson method can only be implemented through the adjoint solver which
regrettably is not available for the implicit unsteady solver. Instead, the Secant method can
be implemented through an approximation loop built in Star-CCM+’s simulation operations.
Meanwhile, the initial guesses on the pitch angle derived from the BEM only required two to
three iterations of manual adjustment before matching the correct thrust output.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
6.1
Summarizing discussion
In chapter 5, the comparisons between the 1DM, BEM and ALM models are presented. In
general, the BEM and ALM were able to better capture and reproduce the trend of both the
mean and peak velocity profiles. The 1DM consistently underpredicts the velocity profile,
especially inside the rotor diameter. This might be of interest for VTOL simulation problems
such as brownouts where dirt from the ground can be whirled up due to the turbulent nature
of the downwash and impair the pilots’ vision. The modified 1DM helped rectify this problem
since the velocity profile in this case better matched the results from the BEM inside the
rotor radius. The FAA also mentions the inner-radius DFRCs as problematic in their report
([1], p. 68). The quality of the experimental data must also be taken into account. While it
was deemed the highest quality reference data at the time, having only six reference points in
the range [0, 3.5]m it cannot be expected to properly resolve the near ground and boundary
layer flows. This has the most influence on the inner-rotor measurements as the adverse
pressure gradients and turbulent quantities are more prevalent at these positions. The
underprediction decreases as DFRC increases since the best predictions for the 1DM were
observed at DFRC seven and eight (1.5D and 2.26D). The difference is unfortunately quite
pronounced with increasing RHAGL as was shown by the simulations at 50m. The cause for
the underprediction may be found in the application of the thrust. As the 1DM imposes the
velocity uniformly on the disc, more of the flow is going to be slowed down by the presence of
the fuselage. In the case of the BEM, the only place where the flow from the rotors directly
impinges on the fuselage is on the tail. Otherwise, the main core high-speed flow passes by
the fuselage, effectively resulting in accurate velocity readings on the ground (figures 4.9 and
4.10). The author recognizes the fact that the BEM is not solved to a satisfactory level of
convergence regarding the residuals (figure 4.13). However, the main purpose of this study
has been to identify whether the BEM method could be a viable substitute for the 1DM in
industrial applications. Running unsteady BEM calculations at heights of [50, 100]m might
not be feasible if several configurations must be run. As such, it has been of interest to
examine whether or not steady BEM simulations could produce equal or better results with
the proper settings and post-processing. By averaging the velocity field and approximating
the σu as u′ in equation 2.3.2, peak velocity profiles were very much comparable to the
experimental results. It proved more useful for the BEM since the turbulence generated from
the non-uniform and tangential velocities resulted in larger TKE values, which directly
translated into the peak velocity profiles. The computational cost is also of relevance as it is
an important parameter when deciding on a model in an industrial setting. While the BEM is
superior to the 1DM, it is also more expensive. With the same base size of 0.25m, the 1DM
Page 75 of 80
==PAGE:94==
CHAPTER 6. CONCLUSION
could reach convergence in around two hours on one to two XEON24 nodes depending on the
simulation height. The BEM required much more computational power at this base size with
at least three XEON24 nodes for five to six hours of run-time. The ALM is not suitable for
industrial applications of this nature as even with 60 hours on three XEON40 nodes
convergence has still not been completely satisfied. According to the mesh study conducted in
section 4.3, the mesh could be coarsened to a base size of 1m without much loss of precision
which significantly reduces the computational cost. If one would be content with the error
observed with a base size of in the wake, the 1DM can be run on as little as six cores in a
matter of hours and the BEM can converge on one or two XEON24 nodes in the same span of
time. As previously mentioned, the computational cost of the BEM primarily comes from the
virtual disc resolution. However, considering the resources of large companies, it is indeed
feasible to use BEM in future studies to ensure proper measurements of the mean and peak
velocity profiles at ground level for pedestrian comfort studies.
6.2
Conclusions
To quantify the differences between the numerical modelling techniques – 1DM, BEM and
ALM – their ability to predict mean and peak velocity profiles have been analyzed against
reference data on a Sikorsky CH-53E VTOL aircraft from the FAA ([1]). Through extensive
testing of the models to achieve the best results for each case, it was concluded that the BEM,
though suffering from some overpredicting tendencies, is superior in its ability to accurately
predict the velocity profiles to the 1DM which consistently underpredicted the results. This
trend of underpredicting is exacerbated by increasing RHAGL. Modifications were proposed
to the 1DM to improve its results but the end results were not good enough to favor the 1DM
in the overall comparison. The ALM proved very computationally demanding, requiring
between 60 and 80 hours on three to four XEON40 nodes before having comparable results. It
can also be concluded that, while not suited for the 1DM due to the lack of turbulence, for
the BEM and ALM the approximation of σu = u′ produces decent peak velocity profiles with
C = 3.5, and this formulation can be used for future studies on BEM simulations.
6.3
Future studies
As this study is concluded, this section proposes areas of interest for future work. The
discussion will be restricted to cover one topic; further investigation into the accuracy versus
the computational cost of the BEM, and suggestions for future modifications of the 1DM.
6.3.1
Unsteady BEM and virtual resolution
The computational cost of the BEM is both reliant on the mesh size and the virtual
resolution of the disc. In this study, Only the sensitivity to the mesh has been presented
which makes an analysis of the virtual disc resolution effect on the results relevant. While
some modifications were made in this study to examine the general behavior of the resolution
on the results, it has not been a main focus of interest and as such is open for further
interpretation. It could also be of interest to carry out the same measurements using an
unsteady solver for the BEM to investigate what kind of effect it has on the results. While
unsteady simulations were tested on the BEM to analyze the behavior of the residuals, it has
Page 76 of 80
==PAGE:95==
6.3. FUTURE STUDIES
not been the goal to obtain fully unsteady solutions for the BEM. It could likewise be
interesting to see what effect it would have on the final velocity profiles.
6.3.2
Modified 1DM
As has been discovered in this study, the uniform velocity boundary of the 1DM does not
produce enough force turbulent fluctuations for the σu = u′ approximations to work. It was
investigated whether different turbulent inlet parameters could improve this and while it did,
it still did not solve the underpredicting problem of the 1DM. Therefore, it is posed that
future work could go into modifying the 1DM by applying a non-uniform outlet boundary
condition similar to the force profile of the BEM to address the problem of general
undereprediction in the flow, as this would reduce the amount of high-speed impinging flow
on the fuselage. There could also be prescribed tangential velocities to better mimic the
turbulent quantities of the BEM. In addition, it could be interesting to see how well the 1DM
could be modified to improve its predictive abilities.
