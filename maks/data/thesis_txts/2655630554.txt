Abstract
Topology optimization is a tool for maximizing the performance of a structure given
certain boundary conditions, constraints, and loads. In this report 3D topology opti-
mization is used to optimize for minimum weight for a rotor in a direct drive wind
turbine generator with constraints on deformations.
The report concludes that the optimized topologies show great dependence on the ini-
tial guess as well as the imposed length scale. It is established that sheet-like topologies
show the greatest performance but that imposing a minimum length scale can lead to
frame-like topologies.

Introduction
1.1
Background and motivation
More energy is needed around the world and wind energy is one of the renewable
power sources on the rise that can help deliver this while also helping in the ﬁght
against global warming. This abundant energy source needs to be harnessed as ef-
ﬁciently and effectively as possible to reduce the cost of power thus increasing the
incentive towards renewable energy sources.
The area swept by the blades of a wind turbine, and therefore also the extractable
energy, increases with the square of the wing length, making this factor especially in-
teresting to improve. Longer blades obviously lead to the need for taller towers. The
increase of tower height also increases the extractable energy from the wind as wind
speeds generally are faster at higher altitudes [Lantz et al., 2019]. Longer blades and
taller towers increase the loading of the whole structure, both the loads from the wind
and from gravitational loads from the structure itself.
One of the critical components in many wind turbines is the gearbox. [Dao et al.,
2019] did a study of failure rate and downtime for over 18 000 wind turbines corre-
sponding to over 90 000 turbine years. The study looked at the subassemblies of the
wind turbines causing these failures and showed the distribution of failures for each
subassembly. The study shows that even though the gearbox is not one of the sub-
assemblies with the highest failure rate, it is the one with the highest median downtime
per failure, see Figure 1.1.

The downside of direct drive generators for large wind turbines is that they need to be
very large, with weights of 200 to 500 tons [Hayes et al., 2018] using traditional design
for wind turbines producing around 10 MW. This massive size is caused by the much
higher transferred torque compared to generators in geared wind turbines, because
the direct drive generators operate at much lower rotational speeds. This leads to ev-
erything in the structure (e.g. the tower and foundation) having to be strengthened,
further increasing cost. However, the total cost can potentially be reduced by mini-
mizing the weight of the generator, which would in turn also reduce emissions from
production and material extraction. A possible way to do this is to utilize topology op-
timization, described in [Bendsøe and Sigmund, 2003], the purpose of which in general
terms is to optimize the distribution of material in a structure to improve performance.
For a ﬁnite element discretized domain this can be achieved by determining which
elements should be solid, and which should be void (no material). This is done based
on boundary conditions, loads, constraints, and an objective to optimize for, e.g. weight
minimization. One hindrance however, is that topology optimization often leads to
complex geometries which can be difﬁcult and expensive to manufacture using tra-
ditional methods such as milling. In recent years this obstacle has been reduced with
the increased availability and competitiveness of additive manufacturing which makes
complex geometries easier and cheaper to produce.

1.3
Problem statement
The goal of this project is to utilize topology optimization to reduce the weight of the
rotor seen in Figure 1.3, which is discretized into a ﬁnite number of elements. The col-
laborators from the University of Colorado Boulder have speciﬁed certain constraints
on allowable deformations, such that failure and excessive wear is avoided.
Two scenarios are considered for the rotor: operation and transportation. During op-
eration the rotor is supported by a shaft and subjected to magnetic forces along with
gravitational and centrifugal loads. When being transported the rotor is laying down
while being supported on one of the ﬂat surfaces of the outer rim and is only subjected
to gravitational loading.
The magnetic forces consist of a shear stress of 40 kPa and a normal stress of 0.2 MPa.

1.5
Topology optimization using parallel computing in C++
The work in this project is conducted on the basis of the 3D parallel computing code
for topology optimization using unstructured meshes started by [Tr¨aff, 2019]. The code
is written in C++ using PETSc [Balay et al., 2019, Balay et al., 2020, Balay et al., 1997]
(Portable, Extensible Toolkit for Scientiﬁc Computation).
The authors’ main contributions to the code are:
• Method to apply cylindrical traction load
• Method to apply gravity load and to derive sensitivity contribution from said
load.
• Method to apply centrifugal load and to derive sensitivity contribution from said
load.
• Method to constrain axial, radial and torsional deﬂection for selected nodes in a
cylinder.
• Method to optimize for multiple load cases with various boundary conditions.
• Method to solve adjoint problems required to calculate sensitivities.
• Method to indirectly impose axial symmetry inspired by center of mass.

Initial work conducted at CU Boulder
Researchers from the University of Colorado Boulder (CU Boulder) have looked into
the use of direct drive generators for wind turbines producing several megawatts of
power. One challenge discovered is that the mass of the rotor will be large when using
conventional rotor designs. New development within metal additive manufacturing
allows the manufacturing of more complex structures meaning structural optimization
can be utilized for mass reduction. This has been looked into by researchers from
CU Boulder from several angles. In [Hayes et al., 2018] topology optimization using
ANSYS is used on a spoked arm design as well as for a disk in the active domain, see
Figure 1.3. Parameter optimization is used on a truss-like support structure for hollow
and U-beam trusses.
In later research [Hayes, 2021] three mathematically deﬁned triply periodic mini-
mal surface (TPMS) lattice structures are chosen as candidates to minimize the mass
of the rotor: Diamond, Schwartz Primitive, and Gyroid. Parameter optimization using
a genetic algorithm is ﬁrst used to determine lattice parameters based on an objective
function containing contributions from mass and deformation constraints, see (2.1).
After this step, optimization of the lattice thickness is performed by looking at the
strain ﬁeld.
Other studies have also tried to reduce the mass of direct drive generators for large
wind turbines. [Zavvos et al., 2013] looked at radial and transversal ﬂux generators
and used a genetic algorithm in MATLAB to optimize deﬁning dimensions for the
rotor and stator. The shape optimization tool in ANSYS was then used for the opti-
mized dimensions. [Mueller and McDonald, 2009] reduced the mass by making a new
topology where some of the electromagnetic forces cancel out reducing the load on the
structure.
The structure proposed by researchers from CU Boulder [Hayes, 2021] uses a Schwartz
Primitive lattice structure and is shown in Figure 2.1. Since the speciﬁc loads, bound-
ary conditions, and dimensions are known for this structure and the mass along with
deformations of it are given, it will be used as the benchmark structure in this report.
Three loads are considered during optimization by CU Boulder: torsional shear stress,
normal Maxwell stress, and gravitational loading in the axial direction (see sections
5.1.2, 5.1.1, and 5.1.4). The boundary condition used is the one that applies for oper-
ation, i.e. fully supported by a shaft in the center (see section 5.2.1). The maximum
deformations along with the total mass of the rotor is given in Table 2.1.

Finite element discretization of rotor
In this report, all FEM meshes have been generated using 8-node hexahedral elements
with isoparametric formulation. Full numerical integration is used for all elements.
The material is considered isotropic and linear elastic.
The ﬁnite element meshes used for computations have been generated with Cubit1
using the automatically calculated meshing scheme in the program. For the rotor the
sweep scheme is used ensuring the same cross section for all layers in the axial direction.
Both free and mapped meshes can be used to model the rotor. A coarse version of a free
mesh of a quarter of the rotor is seen in Figure 3.1a. The mesh consists of several layers
looking like the one depicted. A mapped mesh of the same size is seen in Figure 3.1b
for comparison.
It is desired to have a mesh where all elements have similar dimensions in all di-
rections to ensure better element integration. It is also desired to have a mesh where
all elements are similarly sized to avoid unnecessarily small and thus unnecessarily
many elements. One important criteria is that the largest elements in the mesh have
to be able to represent the ﬁnest allowable details. For a free mesh this is not a prob-
lem in the case analyzed in this report. A mapped mesh, as seen in Figure 3.1b, will
result in big size differences between elements closest to the center axis compared to
those furthest away from the center axis. This problem can be solved by splitting ele-
ments into smaller chunks when they become larger than a set threshold as shown in
Figure 3.1c. This will give rise to hanging nodes not shared by all elements touching
them, which will require a routine interpolating displacements of these using multi-
point constraints [Cook et al., 2001]. The complexity of the implementation of this is
unknown to the authors.
For the reasons discussed above mapped meshes, with or without hanging nodes,
will not be explored further, and the free mesh will be used in all of the following work.

Topology optimization
4.1
Background
The distribution and layout of material can have great importance on the performance
of a structure depending on its purpose. Structural design optimization is therefore
highly relevant and comes in various forms, one of them being topology optimization
[Bendsøe and Sigmund, 2003].
The general topology optimization problem for continuum structures can be stated as
follows: Determine where in the design domain there should be material and where
there should not for a given set of loads, boundary conditions and constraints, with
the intention of improving performance as much as possible.
The performance of the structure is measured by a mathematical function called the
objective function, here denoted by Φ. Constraints for the optimization must also be
expressed as mathematical functions called constraint functions. A constraint function
is in this report denoted by g.
In this report a density-based approach to topology optimization [Bendsøe, 1989] is
followed. In this approach the design domain is discretized into a ﬁnite number of con-
tinuum elements. Each element must then be determined to be either solid or ”void”.
For this purpose each element, e, is assigned a relative density, ρe, which can vary con-
tinuously between one and zero, where a density of one denotes that the element is
solid, while a density of zero denotes that the element is ”void”. The relative densi-
ties are the design variables of the discretized optimization problem. The optimization
problem is seen as an optimization problem in the design variables only, and can be
written as:

It is important to note that in the density-based approach described above (and used in
this work), the design domain as well as the FEM mesh is ﬁxed, such that the amount
of nodes and node locations are not considered design variables and do not change.
An example of a classic continuum mechanics topology optimization problem is op-
timizing for minimum compliance (global maximum stiffness) for a structure, with a

constraint on the maximum amount of available material. In this example the relative

In many industries topology optimization is useful, for instance in the aerospace in-
dustry where saving even small amounts of weight could result in a signiﬁcant reduc-
tion in fuel consumption. [Aage et al., 2017] estimated that 100-200 kg of fuel may be
saved annually for every 1 kg of saved weight for a commercial aircraft. On a much
smaller scale topology optimization is also applicable, as it can also be used for making
compliant mechanism, e.g. when creating Micro Electro-Mechanical Systems (MEMS)
[Philippine et al., 2013, Gerrard et al., 2017, Giannini et al., 2020].
4.2
Filtering and projection
The density based approach to topology optimization in the general continuum setting
gives rise to several well known complications [Sigmund and Petersson, 1998]. One of
these complications is the lack of existence of solutions. For a ﬁnite element discretized
problem, this becomes evident when the mesh is reﬁned. As the number of elements
in the mesh is increased, more holes are generally introduced without changing the
structural volume, since this increases the efﬁciency of the structure. Thus, smaller
and smaller patterns emerge, ultimately leading to patterns similar to microstructures.
In a case like this, where a reﬁnement of the mesh leads to a signiﬁcantly different
topology instead of simply a better ﬁnite element model of an optimized topology on
a coarser mesh, the solution is said to be mesh dependent. Another complication of the
the density based approach is known as the checkerboard problem, where elements in
areas of the discretized domain alternate between solid and void. Such a topology re-
sults in an artiﬁcially large stiffness due to bad numerical modelling.
There are several ways to negate these two complications, one of them being ﬁltering
[Bourdin, 2001]. In this report the isotropic density ﬁlter based on the Helmholtz-type
partial differential equation is used [Lazarov and Sigmund, 2011]. With ρ being the
unﬁltered relative density ﬁeld, the ﬁltered relative density ﬁeld, ˜ρ, is given by the
solution to the following partial differential equation with homogeneous Neumann

4.2.1
Robust formulation
Although ﬁltering ensures global mesh independence, meaning that the general topol-
ogy converges under mesh reﬁnement, it does not necessarily ensure local mesh inde-
pendence in the sense that it does not prevent small local features such as ”one-node-
hinges” in compliance mechanisms where two parts of an optimized structure could
essentially be connected by one node, see for instance [Sigmund, 1997]. Furthermore,
ﬁlters inherently produce elements with intermediate densities between solid and void
regions of a structure, such that the boundaries are not well deﬁned. This can lead to
issues in terms of manufacturing, where it can be difﬁcult to decide which elements
should be part of the structure to be manufactured.
For the reasons listed above, the robust formulation suggested in [Wang et al., 2011]
is applied. In this robust formulation, the ﬁltered design is projected into 0/1-space
using the smooth threshold projection function given by

0, while ﬁltered relative densities above η are projected towards 1. The ﬁltered and pro-

4.4
Optimization algorithm, Method of Moving Asymptotes (MMA)
In this report the Method of Moving Asymptotes (acronym: ”MMA”) [Svanberg, 1987]
is used as optimization algorithm. The MMA algorithm is a ﬂexible tool able to handle
general non-linear programming problems. It should be able handle many different
kinds of design variables, as well as any constraint, as long as the sensitivities with re-
spect to the design variables can be calculated either analytically or numerically. MMA
has also been proven to be suitable for large scale topology optimization problems
[Aage and Lazarov, 2013].
4.5
Optimizing for multiple load cases
Some structures are loaded differently in different scenarios. The rotor considered in
this report will for example be loaded differently when being transported compared to
when it is in operation. This must be modelled as separate loading cases. When several
loads act at the same time it is also sometimes necessary to model the loads separately
as well as acting together. This is to safeguard against failure of the structure if e.g.
slight changes in the loads occur. If separate loading scenarios are instead modelled
as one load case, with all the forces acting at once, the optimization algorithm can
potentially take advantage of e.g. forces in opposite directions cancelling each other
out.
The way optimization for multiple load cases is handled in this report is explained
further in section 4.7, where the optimization problem used in this report is explained.

4.10.1
Solving the adjoint problem
The code for solving adjoint problems when evaluating sensitivities was written to take
into account load cases with different boundary conditions. First the stiffness matrix is
assembled, and boundary conditions are applied for one set of boundary conditions.
The structure stiffness equations is then solved for all load cases which share this one
set of boundary conditions. Then the all the adjoint problems for these load cases are
solved, and sensitivities are computed. The stiffness matrix is then reassembled and
boundary conditions are applied for the next set of boundary conditions, and so on.
This way the stiffness matrix is only assembled once for each boundary condition,
which saves computational time.

Self weight gives rise to certain problems in topology optimization, as documented
in [Bruyneel and Duysinx, 2005], one of them being that it tends to generate elements
with intermediate relative densities. The reason for this phenomenon is that the ratio
between the weight and the effective Young’s modulus of the element is very large
when the physical density is low, meaning that displacements become large as well.
The optimization algorithm therefore tries to reduce the issue of large nodal displace-
ments by assigning some material to the element.
To negate this problem, a modiﬁed version of the SIMP scheme was proposed,
where the stiffness is linearly interpolated under a certain threshold of the physi-
cal relative density. While using the sensitivity ﬁlter proposed in [Sigmund, 1997],

In [Lapinskas, 2020] multiple stiffness interpolation schemes were tested for minimum
compliance problems with structures subjected to self weight, including the modiﬁed
SIMP scheme proposed in [Bruyneel and Duysinx, 2005], a RAMP scheme, and the
SIMP scheme used in this work described by (4.7). A density ﬁlter as well as threshold

SIMP scheme proposed in [Bruyneel and Duysinx, 2005] was not prioritized.

Study of Young’s modulus of void elements
In this section the effect of the Young’s modulus ratio, α, from the SIMP interpolation
scheme is studied, see section 4.6. As stated earlier a minimum stiffness is assigned to
void elements to ensure the system is solvable. Assigning a minimum stiffness to void
elements does however affect the total stiffness of the structure and thereby also the
deformations. Furthermore, since a multigrid preconditioned iterative solver is used
for solving the structure stiffness equations, α also has an effect on solve times [Amir
et al., 2014].
To assess the inﬂuence of the minimum stiffness the structural stiffness equations
are solved for various values of Young’s modulus ratios. For this study the rotor is
discretized into 2 million elements, supported using BC1, and is loaded using L1, L2,
and L4, which is the load case the collaborators from CU Boulder used for their op-
timizations. In this section the material density of the outer ring is the same as for
the rest of the material as described in Table 1.2. The physical relative densities in the
active domain are set according to a simple scheme described in section 7.1, such that
the structure mimics the design proposed by CU Boulder. When setting the physical
relative densities as such, the mesh consists mainly of void elements.
7.1
Meshing proposed structure from CU Boulder
The Schwartz Primitive triply periodic minimal surface structure suggested by re-
searchers from CU Boulder is provided in the form of an STL ﬁle. The conversion to a
mesh consisting of hexahedral elements is done by ﬁrst creating a mesh as done for all
other analyses. The STL is then read by the program and it is found which elements lie
within this proposed structure. A simple scheme determining which elements are set
as void and which are set as solid is created. This scheme works by setting elements
as solid if one or more of its nodes lie within the STL structure. The reasoning behind
this choice is that the structure contains very ﬁne details such as thin walls, that will
not be captured if all element nodes have to be within the structure to result in a solid
element. This choice, however, is likely to overestimate the amount of material in the
CU Boulder design, which can be seen in Figure 7.1, where the method is visualized in
2D.

Conclusion
In this report topology optimization for minimum weight with a constraint on defor-
mations has been conducted for a rotor in a direct drive wind turbine generator. The
effect of various loads which the rotor is expected to be subjected to is discussed. It
is determined that the dominating load is torsional shear stress during operation and
that the gravitational load during transportation is not important for optimization.
The optimization algorithm is veriﬁed using a cross check method. Results show that a
topology optimized for a certain loading scenario also performs the best in this loading
scenario, compared to structures optimized for other loading scenarios.
It is seen that there is a correlation between the iteration time and the volume frac-
tion of the active domain. As the volume fraction is decreased, the amount of iterations
required by the multigrid preconditioned FGMRES solver to reach convergence is in-
creased.
To decrease solving times and compensate for underestimated deformations by the
p-mean aggregation function, a safety factor is introduced forcing the use of more ma-
terial.
The importance of the initial guess is established as the volume fraction and topology
of the converged structure greatly depends on this. It is seen that the best performing
structures have sheet-like topologies and it is established that frame-like structures are
a result of the imposed minimum length scale. It is seen that when the dominating
load is torsion more material near the rotational axis is preferred to alleviate stresses.
