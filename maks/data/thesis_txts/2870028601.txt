Abstract
Background: Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) is widely used, therapeutically to influence
neural activity, and for studying brain function, particularly in the motor cortex. Motor evoked
potentials (MEPs) are electrical signals elicited by TMS stimulation to the motor cortex, providing
an indirect measure of motor cortex activity. Transcranial evoked potentials (TEPs), are brain
potentials appearing as positive and negative deflections in the averaged electroencephalogram
(EEG). TEPs are influenced by TMS artifacts and have long latencies that are unlikely to represent
an immediate response to TMS. Recently, immediate transcranial evoked potentials (iTEPs) have
been discovered, a series of peaks superimposed on a slower wave, occurring 2-6 ms after stimulation.
However, a problem still exists that we are not able to separate the two components, which limits
the use in research.
Methods: An EEG preprocessing pipeline was designed to visualize the iTEPs, recorded in a single
subject, and effectively remove the TMS pulse artifact. Quantification of the iTEPs was conducted
with peak detection and time-frequency analysis to inspect the power of the signal. EEG features
from the two individual components were extracted and used to do a Spearman’s rank correlation
with the MEP peak-to-peak amplitudes.
Results: Preprocessing pipeline, where TMS pulse was removed, was successful to visualize iTEPs
and quantify the peaks. Time-frequency analysis showed an increase in power in two frequency
band, a low and a high range, which were used to separate the two iTEP components. Trial by
trial correlation showed significant weak positive correlation between peak amplitudes of the high
frequency peaks and the MEPs within subject. The correlation increased to a strong positive cor-
relation with increasing window size of the sliding window. This significant correlation was found
for some conditions, while not in others.
Discussion: These findings give further evidence that iTEPs are a compound signal of a slow
wave and high frequency peaks. The results suggest a positive relationship between the iTEPs and
MEPs within subject in such that large amplitudes in the high frequency iTEP peaks are linked
to large MEP responses. However, further research is required to confirm this relationship.

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
1.1
Background and motivation
Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) is non-invasive brain stimulation method and a powerful
tool widely used in both clinical and research settings to modulate and investigate the brain’s
activity. When combined with electroencephalography (EEG), the cortical responses to TMS can
be investigated by looking at the averaged brain response, commonly known as transcranial evoked
potentials (TEPs). TEPs are brain potentials elicited by the TMS pulse that consist of a series
of positive and negative deflections in the EEG, time-locked to the pulse [20]. Additionally TMS
can be combined with electromyography (EMG) to quantify motor evoked potentials (MEPs).
MEPs provide an indirect way of measuring motor cortex activity as the signal is confounded by
non-cortical sources that it has been affected by, for example in the spinal cord [25, 33]. There-
fore, by combining TMS with EEG and EMG, a more more comprehensive image of the effect
of TMS can be obtained with the information that can be derived from the TEPs and the MEPs [37].
A recent breakthrough occurred in the field of TMS-EEG when Beck, Christiansen et al. (2024)
performed a TMS-EEG study, where they recorded EEG with ultra-high sampling frequency to
reduce the artifact from the TMS pulse. By doing so they revealed immediate transcranial evoked
potentials (iTEPs) which are a series of 2-3 peaks superimposed on a slower wave that occur 2-6 ms
post stimulation [2]. The high-frequency component of the iTEPs has a frequency of approximately
600-1000Hz. This resembles the descending volleys in the corticospinal tract, that can be measured
invasively at the spinal cord level after stimulating the motor cortex. The underlying mechanism
of these descending volleys, or I-waves as they are named, has not yet been confirmed although
evidence suggest they originate in the motor cortex. The goal of the study by Beck, Christiansen et
al. was to investigate whether stimulation at M1_HAND induced a similar synchronized response
in the cortex that would be detected by the EEG. Their findings gave rise to the hypothesis that
the iTEPs are the cortical source of the I-waves [2, 42].
Due to the recent discovery of the iTEPs, the knowledge about them is minimal. Additionally,
research has been limited as it has yet not been manageable to separate the two frequency com-
ponents in a way that allows to research features in each component. This has been attempted by
Stango et al. in their study, where they showed positive correlation between the power of the slow
wave and the high frequency components of the iTEPs and the MEPs [38]. However, this has not
yet been researched for the separated components of the iTEPs, such as individual quantification
of each of the high frequency peaks. This is important, so it can be investigated if and how each
component of the iTEPs contribute to the MEP, because even though MEPs are commonly used
as markers to evaluate corticospinal excitability, they do not represent direct cortical activity [25].

An additional challenge, will be the design of a preprocessing pipeline suited for the iTEPs, in-
cluding the effective removal of the TMS artifact. Current preprocessing methods are designed
to preprocess TEP components of longer latencies than the iTEPs, and studies are showing that
the methodological choices made during preprocessing TEPs matter. However, even though iTEPs
are currently preprocessed more conservatively than conventional TEPs, the effects of each step
in the preprocessing of the iTEPs is completely unexplored. Therefore it is unknown how their
morphology and quantification changes and what is the ground truth of the signal. Most pre-
vious TEP research have removed the TMS pulse artifact from -5 to 10 ms around the pulse,
which will need to be re-evaluated in this thesis due to the short latency of the iTEPs. Additon-
ally, a concern has arisen regarding the use of methods to remove the TMS artifact as they might
unintentionally remove part of the underlying neural signal during the pulse artifact removal [3, 20].
Therefore, in this thesis, the aim is to provide an analysis pipeline to be able to quantify the iTEPs
for further use in research. This requires that an EEG preprocessing pipeline will be established
where the TMS pulse artifact can be effectively removed, while preserving the integrity of the EEG
signal. Different methods will be explored to quantify the iTEPs and various iTEP features will be
extracted. These features will be used to characterize the relationship between iTEPs and MEPs
within individual subjects. It has been previously shown that TEP features and MEP peak-to-peak
amplitude correlate [7]. Therefore the ability of the iTEP components to explain MEP variability
will be investigated. This will allow for a more robust method to link cortical and motor responses.
1.2
Objective/hypothesis
The objective of this thesis is, as mentioned, to develop an EEG preprocessing pipeline and explore
different approaches to quantify the iTEPs and use them to characterize the relationship between
iTEPs and MEPs within individual subjects. It is hypothesized that the implemented preprocessing
pipeline will be successful at removing the TMS pulse artifact, while preserving the iTEP compo-
nents, which will enable the analysis of iTEPs, both visually an quantitatively. Additionally it is
hypothesized that certain iTEP features will correlate with the peak-to-peak amplitude of the MEP
and that those features will have a stronger correlation when data is recorded under contraction
than at rest. Consequently, the research questions are:
−Is there a relationship between features of the iTEPs and MEP peak-to-peak amplitude within
individual subjects?
−Is the relationship between iTEPs and MEPs affected by muscle state (rest vs. contraction)?

Background
2.1
Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation
TMS is a widely used non-invasive brain stimulation method. TMS is applicable both in the clinical
setting, where it can be used to modulate neuronal activity in the brain, and in research settings,
where it can be used, for example, to map the function of the brain where the motor cortex has
been the primary focus [18]. TMS is delivered using magnetic pulses generated in a magnetic coil.
An electric current sent to the coil is conducted through the copper wire windings in the coil and
the flow of electrical current induces a fluctuating magnetic field perpendicular to the plane of the
current that reaches the brain, when the coil is placed on top of the head. A fluctuating magnetic
field such as these brief, powerful magnetic pulses, induces an electrical current in the brain tissue,
in the opposite direction of the current in the coil as can be seen on figure 1. This electrical current
is able to depolarize neurons, and if strong enough, generate an action potential [18, 36].
Figure 1: Illustration demonstrating the principles of TMS. The figure shows the coil current and
the fluctuating magnetic field, as well as the induced electrical field generated in the brain tissue
[21].
The shape of the TMS coil determines the induced electrical field as well as the size of the brain
are stimulated in terms of focality and depth. Therefore the shape of the coil used in TMS has a
great influence on how stimulation affects the brain [20]. Round coils are powerful, but stimulate
a relatively large area as the current is stronger furthest away from the center of the coil. Figure
of eight coils are more focal as the current is strongest at the intersection of the two coils which
allows stimulation on a smaller area [18]. The coil shapes and their induced electrical field can be
seen on figure 2.

As well as the coil shape, a few other parameters are important when it comes to the design of a
TMS protocol. The pulse shapes of the TMS are either monophasic or biphasic, where biphasic
pulses are generally more effective, which allows for stimulation at lower intensities. This can be
preferred for multiple reasons, one of which is to reduce the effect of possible TMS-evoked muscle
artifacts [20]. The orientation of the coil is important as well as it affects the direction of the electric
field generated in the brain, which consequently has an effect on the ability to evoke a motor evoked
potential. The directions are either posterior-to-anterior (P-A) or anterior-to-posterior (A-P), the
previous generally considered to be more effective [36].
2.2
Primary Motor Cortex (M1) and Corticospinal Tract
The primary motor cortex (M1) is located in the frontal lobe of the brain, more precicely in the
pre-central gyrus and plays a key role for voluntary movement. Motor control of different parts of
the body, such as the tounge, face, hand, arm, leg and foot, are represented in specific regions of the
M1, so called somatotopic organization of M1 [5, 29]. A illustration of the somatotopic organization
of M1 can be seen in figure 3. The connection between the cortex and spinal cord is called the
corticospinal tract (CST), which is part of the pyramidal tract. The axons of the corticospinal tract
descend from the primary motor cortex and converge into a tract at the internal capsule. This tract
then passes through the brainstem and down to the spinal cord. The majority of the fibers cross at
the medulla pyramid and move into the lateral corticospinal tract, which primarily controls distal
limbs. Therefore the CST plays a crucial role in voluntary movement as a major pathway where
descending signals travel down from the brain to the spinal cord [29].

2.2.1
I-waves
Indirect waves (I-waves) are a series of high frequency descending volleys in the corticospinal tract,
elicited by a single TMS pulse. Since its discovery in animals in 1954, they have been reported
in humans when recorded invasively on a spinal cord level. Evidence from the multiple studies
regarding the I-waves, point towards the motor cortex as the origin of the I-waves. Additionally,
when the motor cortex is ablated the I-waves are not generated, which further supports this evidence.
However still, the exact generation of the I-waves and their underlying neural mechanism is not
completely understood [27, 43]. Many hypothesis have been made regarding the complex physiology
of I-waves where recently researchers wodner whether the I-waves are generated by a synchronized
activity of multiple neurons, with a 1.5 ms interval which would correspond to a peak frequency at
667 Hz, rather than being generated by a very high frequency burst from a single neuron [36].

2.3
Electroencephalography (EEG)
Electroencephalography is a non-invasive method that allows the recording and measurement of the
electrical activity of the brain, and is commonly used in combination with TMS [20]. In general it
is considered that the electrical signal that EEG records is not action potentials, which are brief in
duration caused by neuronal depolarization. In stead the electrodes capture the synchronization of
postsynaptic potentials, which precede action potentials. These postsynaptic potentials have longer
duration and are generated by the flow of ions in response to neurotransmitter activity at synapses
[6]. Figure 4 shows an illustration of the chain of events that occur and what is measured with the
EEG. EEG reflects the synchronized activity of many neurons, meaning that the signal is detected
at the scalp level when the postsynaptic potentials of several thousands of neurons summate. EEG is
widely used in neuroscience and clinical settings due to its high temporal resolution of milliseconds,
but limited spatial resolution of centimeters [20].
Figure 4: Schematic generated by Hernandez-Pavon that describes the flow of events that occurs
from stimulation to the cascade of events that occur in the brain until the TEPs can be recorded
on the EEG [20].
Using EEG, the electrical activity is measured using a cap with multiple electrodes which are placed
on the scalp of the subject. The recorded EEG signal is the difference in electric potential between
the activity in one electrode in reference with another [20]. EEG captures only a fraction of the
brain’s electrical activity. To help understanding the extent of the signals it can detect, consider

2.4
Transcranial evoked potentials
TEPs are brain responses elicited by a TMS pulse, consisting of a series of positive and negative
deflections in the EEG that are time-locked to the stimulation. As mentioned in 2.3, EEG can be
combined with TMS to record the electrical activity in the brain post stimulation. By averaging
Page 7 of 79
==PAGE:18==
2
BACKGROUND
multiple trials of EEG, the TEP signal can be visualized and studied up to 300 ms post stimulation
[3, 20]. By stimulating the brain directly, the TEPs have shown to be an important tool when
studying cortical excitability and connectivity, as well as they might proof to be valuable as a
clinical tool for assessing diseases involving the brain [16].
When TMS is applied over the primary motor cortex, a series of characteristic TEP peaks can be
seen on the averaged EEG, defined by their latency and polarity. Therefore peaks with negative
deflection are named with N, and peaks with positive deflection are named with P. These peaks
range from N15 to N280 and can be seen on figure 6. As previously mentioned, TEPs offer valuable
insight into cortical excitability and are considered a reliable measure of cortical reactivity within
individuals[16, 20]. Even though the precise neurophysiological origin of the TEPs is not fully
understood, research of the TEPs suggests that each peak corresponds to the activation of specific
cortical regions [14, 20].
Figure 6: Butterflyplot of TEPs after stimulation over the left primary motor cortex. On the plot
all channels can be seen with the gray color and the averaged TEP resonse can be seen with the
black color. On the plot some characteristic peaks can be seen such as the N15, P60 and P180.
Additionally, topoplots for each TEP component can be seen as well as the source localization of
the TEP component [1].
However, the TEPs are unlikely to represent the brain’s immediate response to TMS. This is due to
several factors, including their relatively long latencies [2]. Evidence suggests that TEP components
may be influenced by multisensory-evoked potentials, such as auditory and somatosensory responses,
as well as by network-level interactions across multiple cortical regions [10]. As a result it can be
assumed that TEPs represent a composite signal, originating from both neural and non-neural
sources [14].

2.4.1
Immediate transcranial evoked potentials (iTEPs)
In a recent study, immediate transcranial evoked potentials (iTEPs) were discovered during an
ultra-high sampling frequency TMS-EEG study. The immediate TEP peaks have been obscured by
the TMS pulse artifact that usually lasts until 10-15 ms post stimulation. By sampling the EEG
signal with a frequency of 50 kHz, the pulse artifact post stimulation was reduced to approximately
2 ms which revealed the immediate TEPs occuring approximately 2-6 ms. iTEPs are a series
of 2-3 high frequency peaks, with a peak-to-peak interval of ≈1.1-1.4 ms, superimposed on a
slower positive deflecting wave. Figure 7 shows the some of the findings of their study and shows a
visualization of the iTEPs.

raw signal (top) and filtered signal (bottom). (C) Individual iTEP amplitudes and latencies [2].

minimized scalp muscle artifacts. When the current direction was reversed, it was observed that the

2.5
EEG signal preprocessing
When working with EEG signals, preprocessing is a critical step. The choice of preprocessing
methods, the order of steps taken, choices of filters etc. can greatly affect the signal and must
therefore be chosen carefully. Results of studies researching the impact of different preprocessing
pipelines commonly used, showed that the resulting TEPs varied depending on the method chosen.
TEP features that have been mentioned to vary, depending on preprocessing method, are the peak
amplitudes and global mean field power (GMFP) [8]. When the ground truth of the signal is not
known the choice of preprocessing pipeline is especially difficult [20].
When working with TMS-EEG there are various artifacts that the TMS pulse can introduce in
the EEG signal. These artifacts can be either physiological or non-physiological. Examples of such
artifacts are:
• Pulse artifact

==PAGE:22==
2

• Recharge artifact
• Decay artifact
• Ocular artifacts
• TMS-evoked muscle artifacts
• Auditory artifacts
• Somatosensory artifacts
Other artifacts not related to the TMS can include physiological artifacts such as eye blinks, swal-
lowing, heart rate and respiratory signals, but also electrical interference from devices around that
can disturb the EEG signal. Artifacts can be handled in various ways even though some of them
cannot be avoided all together. The solutions to avoid or limit the effect of these artifacts include
using adequate instrumentation that can sample data at a high rate, changing stimulator settings,
choice of electrodes, minimizing impedance between skin and electrode, training of subject, noise
masking and coil positioning. In general the best way to deal with artifacts is to take actions before
and during the data collection to avoid the artifacts [20].

Epoching around TMS pulse is common to do early on, while the time segment varies depending
on the analysis. As can be seen in figure 10 here above, epoching is performed in all pipelines prior
to filtering. As this is a common procedure, it can be good to ensure that the length of the epoch
is long enough to avoid that artifacts, that can occur post filtering, don’t affect the data of interest.
A common length is to use epochs of 1 second. Removal of bad channels and trials is done to
reduce artifacts and noise in the data which is good, although it can come at a price of reduced
data [20]. Removal and interpolation of TMS pulse artifact is commonly performed where
the data contaminated by the pulse artifact is replaced with zeros or surrounding data and then
interpolated with a cubic function around the time of the pulse. The interpolation creates a more
smooth transition after the pulse has been removed which is important to avoid artifacts from
filtering [19, 20]. It is worth mentioning that in figure 9 they remove and interpolate from -2 - 5 ms,
but a more common range is up to 15 ms [8]. The choice of method to remove the pulse artifact
also matter, especially when the ground truth of the signal is unknown. That is because methods
such as ICA, have been shown to not only remove the pulse but also removes true brain signal
[19]. Filtering is commonly used to remove noise in certain frequency bands but must be applied
with caution and after the TMS pulse artifact has been removed. Otherwise a high frequency
ringing artifact can be introduced in the signal, as has been mentioned, due to the sharp changes
in frequency content in the signal [20]. Baseline correction is performed in near all pipelines
to center the signal from all electrodes of the signal. When baseline correction is performed, it is
important that the window used does not contain large artifacts, such as the TMS pulse. Therefore
a common range is to use at least 100 ms before the pulse and up to a few milliseconds before the
pulse [8, 20].
As mentioned above the choice of preprocessing pipeline has an effect on the TEPs and influences
their reproducibility. This has been found especially true for early latencies of the TEPs, as they
are most impacted by the strong TMS pulse artifact as well as other artifacts. Therefore caution
should be exercised particularly when it comes to the study of early latency TEPs [4].
2.6
Electromyography (EMG)
An EMG signal indicates the amount of electrical activity present in a muscle. A motor unit (MU)
is the smallest functional unit in the motor system that can be voluntarily activated. It consists of
a motor neuron and the muscle fibers it innervates. When a neural signal stimulates the motor unit,
an action potential travels down the motor neuron, triggering motor unit action potential (MUAP)
Page 13 of 79
==PAGE:24==
2
BACKGROUND
in the single motor unit. The signal that the EMG measures is essentially the summation of multiple
MUAPs from single motor units, as multiple motor units are recruited during muscle contraction
[11, 30]. The electrical activity measured by the EMG is in millivolts (mV) and measured for a
period of time (ms) [11]. One of the great advantages of combining TMS with EMG is the ability
to assess the corticospinal excitability by observing the evoked muscular response, otherwise known
as MEP[7, 37].
2.7
Motor evoked potentials
The MEP is an electrical response that is elicited after a TMS stimulation over the primary motor
cortex (M1), which activates the motor pathways. As the physiology of an MEP is not the topic
of this thesis, a short introduction of the MEP will be provided here. When M1 is stimulated
with TMS, using a high enough intensity, this leads to a cascade of events on the cortical and
corticospinal level which leads to the activation of motor neurons and the muscle contracts. This
leads to a motor evoked potential that can be quantified by EMG. MEPs are frequently used in
relation to TMS to estimate and quantify corticospinal excitability as it is a non-invasive tool [37].
Although MEPs are commonly used to measure motor cortex activity, it does so in an indirect way
as the signal has been filtered with activity in the spinal cord [25].

2.8
Summary of current methods and their limitations
An introduction to TEPs and various methods of how they are preprocessed has been given in this
section. As mentioned and showed in figure 9, the three pipelines presented are all designed for
later, more conventional peaks of the TEP and not suited for investigating immediate TEPs. Due
to the variety of methods used, the resulting TEPs vary in terms of amplitude and other features
which can make it difficult to compare. This becomes even more challenging when working with
TEPs where the ground truth is unknown and therefore increased caution must be taken [4, 8, 20].
The methods presented by Beck, Christiansen et al. (2024) proposed a very minimal preprocessing
approach consisting only of epoching, filtering (0.1-2000 Hz) and baseline correction. This approach
enables the visualization of the iTEPs and quantification in terms of peak-to-trough amplitudes
and inter-peak latencies. The TMS pulse artifact and its presence in the data, limits the use of
this method and the two iTEP components cannot be separated by filtering [2]. In the method
presented by Stango et al. (2024), the TMS pulse artifact is removed, allowing for a time-frequency
analysis of the signal. By doing so, it allowed for further feature extraction in terms of power in the
two frequency ranges and testing the relationship with the MEP amplitude. However, the limits
of this method still lie in the fact that individual features within the two iTEP components, slow
wave and high frequency peaks, have not been investigated [38].
This leads to the aim of the work done in this thesis, which is to build upon current knowledge
about TEPs and iTEPs and investigate within-subject relationship between iTEPs and MEPs,
when using an iTEP specific preprocessing pipeline and extracting features from the separated
frequency components of the iTEPs.

3.2.2
EMG preprocessing
The EMG data was filtered using an infinite impulse response (IIR) notch filter with a center
frequency of 50 Hz to remove line noise present in the data. This unwanted noise often occurs
due to electrical interference by nearby electronic devices, insufficient electrode placement or skin
preparation. Additionally if the EMG is not grounded properly, the system can pick up 50 Hz noise
more easily. The notch filter is commonly used to remove the 50/60 Hz noise in EMG data as it
removes the unwanted frequency from the data. The name notch filter refers to the shape of the
filter applied, as it is a sharp filter where the center frequency is at the peak of the notch and a
bandwidth (BW) is set to determine the width of the notch. When working with EMG data, a
BW of 2-3 Hz is commonly used. This is to ensure that the noise in the power line is effectively
removed because it can differ ±1 Hz, but without disturbing the frequency content of the EMG
too much as the frequency content of the EMG signal lies between 5-500 Hz [30]. An illustration
of a notch filter can be seen in figure 17 here below.

Time-frequency analysis was performed on the EEG signal in order to investigate the power of

et al. (2024). In that study they showed that there is increased power in two frequency bands,

considered to indicate the use of short-time Fourier transform (STFT). They reported that they
performed a time-frequency analysis on data from 30 ms prior to the TMS pulse to 100 ms after
the pulse between 30-1000 Hz, using a frequency dependent Hanning taper window of five cycles
per frequency with a sliding step of 1 ms. Additionally they reported that they computed power

After some testing, the time-frequency analysis was carried out using a wavelet analysis, utilizing
Matlab and EEGlab. For the wavelet analysis, the Matlab-EEGlab function pop_newtimef was
used. According to the function script from EEGlab, the wavelet analyis in EEGlab uses a Hanning-
tapered Morlet wavelet [12]. A Morlet wavelet is a sinusoidal wave multiplied by a Gaussian function
and is often used in time-frequency analysis of EEG signals. A key parameter that must be set, is
the number of cycles, which represents essentially the width of the Gaussian that the sinusoidal wave
is tapered with. This parameter controls the time-frequency analysis trade-off between spectral
and temporal resolution, where a lower number of cycles is more temporally precise while a higher
number of cycles is more spectrally precise [9]. Additionally to the number of cycles, EEGlab uses
a scaling factor, q, which determines the number of cycles at highest frequency and influences the
time-frequency trade-off. A visual explanation of the scaling factor can be seen in figure 16.

3.5
Separating the two iTEP components
One of the main objectives of this thesis was to separate the two components of the iTEPs to be
able to further investigate the components individually of each other. After the time-frequency
analysis had been performed, it gave rise to the idea of using bandpass filtering to separate the two
components using the frequency ranges where an increase in power was noticeable. A signal can be
bandpass filtered when it is desired to include certain frequencies in the signal and exclude ones
that are not of interest [20]. This can be done by defining a lower and upper cutoff frequency, f1
and f2, which are the frequencies at the -3dB cutoff. A common filter in EEG signal preprocessing
is the Butterworth filter.

seen. The cutoff frequencies determine the range of frequencies that the filter includes in the
signal [28].

on several factors. Firstly the signal showed relatively higher power in both the ERSP plot and
the power spectral density (PSD) plot than for other nearby frequencies. The PSD can be seen in
figure 18. Second, this range aligns with the frequency of the I-waves [43] and finally, a previous
study successfully examined activity within 600–800 Hz [38]. The cut-off at 600 Hz (instead of 550
Hz or lower) and 1000 Hz (instead of 800 Hz) was additionally based on the latency of iTEP peaks,

3.6.1
Challenges during preprocessing due to low signal to noise ratio
The second dataset was preprocessed in the same manner as previously described in section 3.2.1.
However, the quality of the second dataset was not as good as the first one. The EEG recordings
contained a lot more noisy trials and artifacts, as well as there was more 50 Hz noise in the EMG

resulted in high frequency ringing artifact in the first peak after preprocessing.

3.7
Correlation between iTEPs and MEPs
3.7.1
EEG features
Once the two iTEP components, the slow wave and the high frequency peaks, had been obtained,
features from the components could be extracted. The features that were decided to use for the
correlation with the MEPs were the absolute peak amplitude of the high frequency peaks, after
bandpass filtering between 600-1000 Hz. This gave in total four features, peak 1 amplitude (P1), peak
2 amplitude (P2), peak 3 amplitude (P3) and the sum of these three peaks together (P1+P2+P3).
The absolute peak amplitude was used, following Tomasevic et al. (2022), where they only used
the positive peak of high-frequency oscillations. They explained action potentials typically have
dominant polarity and that if both the positive peak and negative trough would be included this
could induce ringing effect. Therefore the negative peaks might not be reliable, which is why it
was decided here to use the absolute peak amplitude rather than using peak-to-trough amplitude
[39]. For the slow wave the peak amplitude of the wave was used as the feature for the slow wave.
Additionally the sum of the three high frequency peak amplitude and the amplitude of the slow
wave was used as the last feature. Therefore, a total of six features of the iTEPs were tested for
correlation with the amplitude of the MEPs.
3.7.2
MEP amplitude
The MEP peak-to-peak amplitude was obtained by using the filtered EMG signal and using peak
detection in Matlab with findpeaks. A visual inspection was used to find the ranges for the upper
and lower peak of the MEP and each trial plotted to ensure that the correct peak had been found.
Once the peaks had been found the peak-to-peak value was calculated as the difference of the
maximum peak and the minimum peak. The FDI muscle had been the target during the stimulation,
and was therefore used for the correlation analysis. However, as the EMG signal was measured
from four muscles, it was decided to investigate if a compound score would show different results
than using just the FDI. To ensure that the compound score reflected the effects of TMS on the
excitability of hand muscles, only the FDI and ADM were included and z score normalization was
applied between muscles. While ECR and FCR also showed responses, FCR had extremely low
mean amplitude and variance, and was therefore excluded. ECR exhibited a more stable response,
with lower variability than the FDI and ADM, but the MEPs were more flat and polyphasic. This
is common for MEPs in other muscles than in the hand, and in those instances where there is a
polyphasic response the peak-to-peak amplitude is not a valid metric [37]. The decision to exclude

3.7.3
Sliding window average
A sliding window average, using movmean in Matlab, was used to obtain the features of the iTEPs
and the MEP peak-to-peak amplitude. The moving average filter works as such that it computes
the moving average of the data using a sliding window of a size that can be chosen [22]. The sliding
window was applied over a certain number of trials, not data points in the time-series. A moving
average can be used to reveal a more clear pattern in data by reducing noise. Additionally it can
indicate the presence of a slow drift or coupling in the data over a longer timescale, when averaging
over several trials. It was decided to test, in addition to a single trial analysis, a couple of window
sizes for the sliding window analysis to observe how that would impact the correlation between the
iTEP features and the MEPs. The window sizes used were 5, 10 and 20 trials. It was decided to
use a maximum of 20 trials due to the size of the datasets.
3.7.4
Spearman’s rank correlation
Once the EEG features had been computed, it was decided to investigate how the different features
related to the MEP peak-to-peak amplitude. The method chosen for this was Spearman’s rank
correlation. Spearman’s rank correlation is a method that can be used to measure the relationship
between two variables that are not linearly dependent, as is the case when Pearson correlation is
computed. In stead it measures the monotonic relationship between the two variables and returns
the strength and the direction of the correlation. Additionally, it is considered that Spearman’s
correlation is a more suitable method in case of outliers and handles them in a more robust way
[41]. The correlation was computed to assess the relationship between each feature and the MEP
amplitude. It should be mentioned here that the natural logarithm of the MEP amplitudes was
used as the amplitude distribution was very right skewed, so this was done in attempt to obtain a
more Gaussian distribution.

3.8
Statistical analysis
To test the statistical significance of the correlation analysis, it was decided to use permutation
testing. Permutation testing is a non-parametric test, meaning it does not assume a certain dis-
tribution in the data. Permutation testing is a structured way to measure how likely it is that
the correlation result is due to chance. In permutation testing, one of the variables is permuted or
rearranged randomly, and the correlation is performed again. This is done multiple times, which
estimates the probability of the correlation under a null distribution. It has been shown that this
is effective with using as little as 1000 permutations, though a higher number of permutations is
preferred. By doing this, it allows for the calculation of p-value for the correlation and testing its
statistical significance [32].
For the analysis in this thesis, a blocked permutation testing was performed using a block size of 20
and 10,000 permutations per test. A block size of 20 was used to maintain some temporal structure
in the MEPs, as it has been shown that 20-30 trials are needed to obtain a stable measure of the
MEP peak-to-peak amplitude [17]. The assumption in using blocks of 20 for permutation testing is
that the variability of the MEP amplitude within each block is independent of the variability in the
adjacent blocks. This is an assumption made based on the previous statement of using 20-30 trials,
and is considered a more robust test than assuming complete independence of each trial. For one
dataset, 105% RMT contraction from subject 1, there were only 88 trials available. In that case the
permutation test was performed using blocks of 10. The limitations of this will be discussed later on.
The blocked permutation testing works as such that the Spearman correlation is computed for an
iTEP feature and the MEP amplitude. Then the MEP amplitude data is blocked, into blocks of 20,
and the correlation is computed again 10,000 times. For each correlation computation the order of
the blocks is randomly rearranged, while keeping the data structure within the blocks intact. This
creates a null distribution for the correlation and the p-value is calculated, based on the number
of correlations that are at the tail of the distribution. A two sided t-test was used, which means
that the absolute value of the correlation was used to compute the p-value, where both the positive
and negative tail of the distribution was used. The statistical significance of each correlation was
tested using blocked permutation testing, which resulted in a correlation coefficient and a p-value
for each correlation.

4.4.3
Conflicts between time-frequency analysis methods
As shown here above, the two methods yield quite different results and don’t show the same changes
in signal power. As the ground truth of the iTEPs is not known it is difficult to determine which
method represents the true power changes in the signal. The two methods showed different rela-
tionship between the changes of power between the 30 lowest trials and the 30 highest trials within
the two frequency ranges. In addition to that there were some difficulties when trying to obtain the
time-frequency analysis on a trial by trial basis. Due to this it was decided not to move forward
using either method to obtain the power to correlate to the MEPs as it might depend a lot on the
time-frequency method that is chosen for the analysis.
Rather, the results of the time-frequency analysis in terms of the ERSP plots and the power spectral
density plots shown previously in 3.5, were used to determine the optimal frequency ranges to
bandpass filter the signal and obtain the two iTEP components. The wavelet method was chosen
in that case based on the ERSP plots, which were considered to have a smoother time-frequency
representation. Additionally, the frequency ranges that could be seen on the wavelet-ERSP plots
were considered to be more consistent with the literature in terms of I-wave frequencies etc.

5
Discussion
5.1
Summary of main findings
Using the preprocessing pipeline designed for the iTEPs, it was possible to visualize and quantify
the iTEPs on a single trial level and as an average of trials. Splitting the data, based on MEP
amplitude, showed strong indication of a relationship between the iTEPs and the MEPs for the
105% RMT dataset, while the relationship seemed to diminish with increasing stimulation intensity.
The time-frequency analysis of the iTEPs confirmed an increase in power in two frequency ranges, a
low and a high range, which was used to separate the two iTEP components with bandpass filtering.
By doing so two components were obtained, a slow positive deflecting wave and high-frequency
peaks. Findings of the correlation analysis showed a significant relationship between the absolute
amplitude of the high frequency iTEP peaks and the MEP for both datasets using 105% RMT
stimulation intensity during rest. However, for the second dataset, the correlation was only found
to be significant on a trial by trial basis. Additionally, a significant correlation was found for the
first dataset under contraction, using 105% RMT intensity, but due to the small size of the dataset,
it is not possible to conclude if the correlation is stronger under contraction than during rest.
5.2
Reproducing iTEPs
In the tested subjects, iTEPs were found and were consistent with previous reportings by Beck,
Christiansen et al. (2024) and Stango et. al (2024) [2, 38]. As mentioned before, conventional EEG
preprocessing pipelines are designed to analyze later peaks of the TEPs, and up until recently the
iTEPs have been concealed by the the TMS pulse artifact. The preprocessing pipeline presented
in this thesis, aimed to successfully preprocess the iTEP data and remove the TMS pulse artifact
without removing the underlying neural signal. As the ground truth of the iTEPs is unknown there
is no way to measure the success of this. However as demonstrated in figures 20 and 21 the TMS
artifact was successfully removed, by optimizing the removal range, as such that the iTEPs could
be visualized and quantified after preprocessing, without visible impact on the peaks of the iTEPs.
5.2.1
iTEPs can be visualized and quantified on a trial by trial basis
The butterflyplots shown in figure 19 show the acquired iTEPs on a trial by trial basis, as well as
the average across all trials for two stimulation intensities. The iTEPs here have been preprocessed
minimally, using the method by Beck, Christiansen et al. (2024) where the data is epoched, filtered
and baseline corrected. On both subfigures, the peaks of the iTEPs can be seen, with a clear first
and second peak as well as a slightly noticeable third peak. When working with TEPs, an average
of multiple trials is used to quantify the TEPs with a median value of 100 trials [3]. Therefore
it must be highlighted here how remarkable it is to be able to see the iTEP peaks on a trial by
trial basis, as this is not common when working with EEG signals. This indicates that there is a
Page 58 of 79
==PAGE:69==
5
DISCUSSION
difference between iTEPs and the traditional TEPs in regards to the signal to noise ratio.
When looking at the iTEPs from the two datasets, 105% RMT and 115% RMT, it seems that there
is an increase in iTEP amplitude with increasing stimulation intensity.
5.2.2
Effect of stimulation intensity
As mentioned before, by looking at figure 19, it seemed that higher stimulation intensity resulted in
higher peak amplitude of the iTEPs. The peak amplitudes were quantified after the iTEPs had been
preprocessed and the results can be seen in figures 20 and 21. The results of the peak quantification
support this observation, as it can be seen that the peak amplitudes as well as the peak-to-trough
and trough-to-peak amplitudes are larger for increased stimulation intensity. The relative differences
in P1 to T1 and T1 to P2 between the two datasets showed that the 115% RMT dataset had an ≈
20% higher amplitude for P1 to T1, and ≈22% higher amplitude for T1 to P2 amplitude, relative
to the 105% RMT dataset. These results are in accordance with the results from Beck, Christiansen
et al. that reported that both absolute peak amplitude and peak-to-trough amplitude are highly
affected by the stimulation intensity. In their study, they compared subthreshold (95% RMT) and
suprathreshold (110% RMT), and reported an effect in amplitude by stimulation intensity in all
subjects [2]. This has been reported in other studies, where it has been demonstrated that the
amplitudes of the conventional TEP peaks are affected by stimulation intensity [3].
On figures 19-21, the peaks of the iTEPs can be seen, with a clear first and second peak as well
as a slightly noticeable third peak. This peak is not detected with the peak detection in the 105%
RMT dataset, while it is detected for the 115% RMT dataset. This is somewhat consistent with
the reported iTEPs by Beck, Christiansen et al. that reported that the first two peaks were found
in all 25 of their participants, for stimulation intensities at both sub-and suprathreshold. Regarding
the third peak, that is not as visible, it was absent in the majority of subjects, or 16/25, during
subthreshold stimulation and observed in 15/25 subjects for suprathreshold stimulation [2]. If the
iTEPs are the cortical source of I-waves, the increase in iTEP peak amplitude with increasing
intensity is in accordance with studies that show the I-waves are intensity dependent. Additionally
the presence of the third peak might be in accordance to studies that have shown that the later
volleys accompany the first I-wave (I1) with increasing stimulation intensity [26].
5.3
Splitting data based on MEP amplitude reveal differences in iTEP
amplitudes for some but not all conditions
The results of splitting the data into 30 highest and 30 lowest trials based on the MEP amplitude,
can be seen in figures 22 and 23. These results gave strong indication for a relationship between
the iTEPs and MEPs for the 105% RMT data. A ≈55% and ≈25% increase between the P1 to

T1 and T1 to P2 amplitude for the 30 highest trials, relative to the 30 lowest trials, suggests that
the amplitude of the first two peaks is an important feature in relation to the MEPs. The average
MEP amplitude for 30 lowest trials of the 105% RMT data was 0 mV. Despite this, the iTEPs
were visible and the peaks could be detected and the peak to trough amplitude could be measured.
As mentioned previously, it has been suggested that there are swings in corticospinal excitability,
which results in the highly variable MEP output, especially during rest. Kiers et al. (1993) reported
that it is not only a cortical effect, but also an effect from motor neurons on the spinal level [24].
The presence of the iTEPs despite a zero MEP response could be an indicator that a neural signal
is generated, but is not strong enough to fire the motor neurons over the resting threshold due to
subthreshold excitations of the spinal motor neurons.
The indication of the relationship seen here, when investigating the extremes is a good first feasibility
check to see if there is an underlying relationship between the iTEPs and the MEPs. However, the
same pattern could not be seen for the 115% RMT dataset where the P1 to T1 amplitude was
only ≈11% higher for the 30 highest trials, and T1 to P2 was ≈5% lower, relative to the 30 lowest.
This suggest that there is a stronger relationship between the iTEPs and the MEPs at an intensity
just above threshold, which weakens when stimulated at a higher intensity. There can be various
reasons for this. One reason can be that it is known that MEP amplitude does not increase linearly
with increasing stimulation intensity, but rather exponentially. This leads to a plateau at a certain
intensity that is high enough, and the MEP amplitude does not increase further [37]. As 115%
RMT is a high intensity, it is possible that at this intensity the corticospinal output has reached
saturation.
5.4
Time-frequency analysis shows increase in two frequency ranges
Figures 24 and 25 show the results of the time-frequency analysis as the ERSP plots. Additionally,
tables 2 and 3 show the results of the average power. When the results of the time-frequency
analysis are analyzed, there are few things that can be noted. When comparing the ERSPs between
methods, it can be seen that there is an increase in two frequency bands for both methods, a low
and a high frequency band. This is true for both 105% RMT dataset and 115% RMT dataset and
is in accordance to what was reported in the study conducted by Stango et al. (2024) [38].
It can be seen that there is more increase in power in the 30 highest trials compared to the 30
lowest trials. This was confirmed for the 105% RMT dataset when the average power is computed.
There the power increase in the high frequency band was greater between the 30 low and the 30
high trials than the increase in the low frequency band which indicates that the higher frequency
has more effect. This applied for both methods. However for the 115% RMT dataset there was
more increase in the low frequency range between the 30 low and 30 high for the wavelet method
and even a decrease in the high frequency range between the 30 low and the 30 high for the STFT

method. As mentioned previously, this resembles in some ways how the peak-to-trough amplitude
relationship changed for the 115% RMT dataset. When the two methods are compared, there is a
difference in both the ERSPs and in the average power for the two frequency bands, especially for
the higher intensity dataset.
These results are highly variable, depending on the method used, which creates a problem when
the ground truth of the iTEPs is unknown as it is hard to evaluate which method reflects the true

as the iTEP feature does not enable the investigation of the individual contribution of each high
frequency peaks, which is desired. Due to these reasons, as well as the the difficulty of retrieving
trial by trial results of the signal power, it was decided to use other features of the iTEPs to explore
the relationship to the MEPs. However the results of the time-frequency analysis, the ERSP plots
and the power spectral density, was of assistance in separating the two components of the iTEPs.
To do this the wavelet method was chosen based on the more smooth time-frequency representation
on the ERSP plots as well as the frequency ranges observed for the wavelet method were considered
to be more in accordance with the literature in terms of the frequency of the I-waves and the
iTEPs. The I-waves have a frequency of around 600 Hz and the iTEPs have inter-peak intervals of
approximately 1.1-1.4 ms which gives a frequency range of approximately 715-910 Hz [2, 43].
5.5
Separating the two iTEP components
By bandpass filtering the EEG signal, it was successful to separate the two iTEP components. The
results of this was two different looking components, one resembling a slow positively deflecting
wave as seen in figures 26 and 28 and the other one showing the high frequency peaks as seen in
figures 27 and 29. This was one of the objectives of this thesis and allowed for feature extraction
from each component separately.
The slow wave was obtained by filtering the signal from 1-150 Hz. This range was determined by
looking at the power spectral density plot in figure 18 and the ERSP plots from figures 24 and 25.
In the study by Stango et al. they reported that they used frequencies in the range of 100-200 Hz,
which was based on their time-frequency analysis and inspection of the resulting spectrogram [38].
However, from the observations made in this thesis in the previously mentioned figures this was
not considered to be the optimal range. The PSD plot showed much activity in lower ranges from
30 Hz, and at 200 Hz the power/frequency had reached zero. Additionally by observing the ERSP
plots there was not much power increase in the low band above approximately 150 Hz. Therefore
it was decided to make the cutoff at 150 Hz for the upper limit and decided to include frequencies
below 30 Hz as well as those frequencies are commonly observed in brain activity, making the lower
cutoff limit 1 Hz [6].

The high frequency peaks were obtained by filtering the signal from 600-1000 Hz. Similarly to the
slow wave, this was determined using the PSD plot and the ERSP plots. Stango et al. reported
that they used a frequency range of 600-800 Hz which is based on their time-frequency analysis
[38]. However, when the ERSP plots in figures 24 and 25 are inspected it can be seen that there
seems to be activity at higher frequencies as well. This was confirmed with the PSD plot in figure
18. Additionally, the inter-peak interval of the iTEPs reported by Beck, Christiansen et al. is ap-
proximately 1.1-1.4 ms, which corresponds to a frequency range from approximately 715-910 Hz
[2]. Therefore it was decided to include frequencies up to 1000 Hz, to avoid excluding important
information at a higher frequency.
For both the 105% RMT dataset and the 115% dataset, the three characteristic iTEP peaks could
be seen when the signal was filtered from 600-1000 Hz. Additionally another peak could be seen
before P1, that was named P0 here and the following trough T0. As previously mentioned, the
TMS pulse artifact was removed and interpolated from -1.2 to 2.1 ms, and the latency of P0 is at
1 ms. Therefore P0 is considered to be related to the ringing artifact that can occur when filtering
over data that has been removed and interpolated. The ringing artifact is generally seen more
in cases where linear interpolation has been applied but the range of data interpolation can also
influence the ringing effect [20]. Due to the short latencies of the iTEPs and the fact that they occur
immediately after the TMS pulse, the interpolation range was only used up to 2.1 ms to try to
preserve the true signal, but that might come at the cost of ringing artifacts, as mentioned. However,
it is important to recognize this and mention that although P0 is believed to be of artifactual origin,
the possibility of physiological origin cannot be excluded entirely.
By comparing the peak-to-trough amplitudes between the high frequency peaks on and the ones
from the conservatively filtered data it can be seen for both datasets that the relationship changes
when the components are separated. As mentioned in the results and can be seen in figures 26 and
27 the amplitude difference between P1 to T1 and T1 to P2 is not present for the high frequency
peaks, as it was before. Additionally the third peak was seen very clearly for the high frequency
peaks that had not been detected before. Similarly, for the 115% RMT dataset the relationship
between P1 to T1 and T1 to P2 changed when the components were separated. As is seen on figure
21 there is a difference of 1.5 µV, with P1 to T1 amplitude being larger than T1 to P2. However this
relationship is reversed for the high frequency peaks as per figure 29 where T1 to P2 amplitude is
slightly larger, 0.3 µV. This shows the influence of the slow wave and how it impacts the iTEP peak
amplitudes. When the two components are compared, it can be visualized that the first two peaks
are riding on top of the positive slow wave, which might increase their amplitude and therefore
the peak-to-trough and trough-to-peak amplitude are greater before the components are separated.
On the contrary, the third peak might be riding on the deflection of the wave, making it appear

5.6
Challenges encountered and dealt with for a low SNR dataset
The lower quality of the second dataset lead to the discovery of the way TESA toolbox performs
their filtering. Despite having already epoched the data, the filtering is performed over concatenated
trials. This was noticed due to the high frequency ringing in P1 that can be seen in figure 30. The
high frequency ringing most likely occurred due to the stretching of the TMS pulse artifact into
the first peak, which could not be effectively removed by filtering. As has been mention previously,
the ringing effect commonly occurs when there is a sharp change in frequency content, such as if
the TMS pulse artifact is present [20]. As the pulse artifact stretched into P1, the TMS pulse could
not be removed completely, which has left residuals of the artifact still in the signal and resulted in
the ringing artifact. A possible explanation for why the TMS pulse artifact was stretched further
into time in this dataset can be the result of suboptimal EEG electrode preparation, as is known
to lead to prolonged artifacts, or it could be due to the position of coil not being at the optimal
TEP hotspot, which could lead to increased artifacts from both the coil and TMS-evoked muscle
artifacts [2, 20].
When it was examined to use a trial by trial filtering, this removed the high ringing in the first
peak as can be seen in figure 30. The reason for this is not completely understood because even
though the trials are concatenated it would be expected to notice ringing artifacts, due to filtering,
around the edges of the epochs as is usually the case, which is far away from the iTEPs [20]. Figure
31 shows the comparison of the two methods when filtering from 600-1000 Hz. As can be seen,
the high frequency ringing is not present here, indicating that it occurs at a frequency above 1000 Hz.
However, even though this improved the filtering in this dataset, the TESA butterworth filter had
been used for the other dataset, without experiencing any trouble. Therefore it is not believed that
this has influenced or invalidates the results that have been presented in this thesis. It is merely an
observation that is believed to need further investigation in future research. Additionally, as the
high frequency ringing could not be seen after filtering with the two bandpass filters to obtain the
"slow wave" and the high frequency peaks, the TESA butterworth filtering method was used for
the second dataset, to keep consistency across datasets.

blocked permutation test with only 10 samples per block in this case due to the size of the dataset.
Therefore, due to the difference in block size, the comparability of the correlations between rest
and contraction is affected. Therefore, even though the results indicate stronger correlation under
contraction, this can not be confirmed with statistical significance. However, it indicates a reason
for further research regarding this.

were found to significantly correlate but not P1. No significant correlations were found on a trial
by trial basis for the other condition in dataset 1 with the 115% RMT intensity.

on a trial by trial basis for the two contraction states, using 105% RMT and 95% RMT in dataset 2,
except for a couple of significant correlations for a single feature for a single window size, but that
is not considered to reflect a true relationship. It is important to state that data quality is of most
importance, and the lack of significant correlations for the second dataset can possibly be traced
back to low signal quality. When the second dataset was acquired, the coil placement was moved
around to avoid artifacts. This can affect the response both in terms of the iTEPs and MEPs and
can influence the relationship between the two. To determine this, further investigation is needed.
5.8
Answering the research questions
When these results have been presented, the research questions asked in the beginning of the project
can be answered.
−Is there a relationship between features of the iTEPs and MEP peak-to-peak amplitude within
individual subjects?
−Is the relationship between iTEPs and MEPs affected by muscle state (rest vs. contraction)?
Regarding the first one, the correlation results indicate that there is a relationship between the
absolute peak amplitudes of the high frequency peaks of the iTEPs and the MEP peak-to-peak
amplitude. This is seen in both subjects, especially with the amplitude of P2 as well as P1+P2+P3,
although only for trial by trial basis for the second subject. Regarding the second question, the results
of the correlation for 105% resting condition in dataset 1 showed significant positive correlation
with the absolute peak amplitude for P2 and P1+P2+P3 with higher correlation coefficients for
these features under contraction than during rest. This indicates that the relationship is affected by
muscle state. However, as mentioned, the comparability of the correlations for rest and contraction
are might be affected by the difference in block size and therefore this can not be confirmed with
statistical significance.
5.9
Future work
For this thesis, the methods and the results that have been presented here are primarily based
on the observations from a single subject. This was examined in a second dataset from another
subject, with a less optimal signal quality, as has been discussed. However, both subjects showed
statistically significant correlations between certain features of the iTEPs and the MEPs, indicating

a relationship between the iTEPs and MEPs. Future research should focus on validating these
findings by testing the preprocessing pipeline and perform correlation analysis with statistical
testing to confirm these findings on a group level. Additionally, it is worth noting that high-quality
data with sufficient number of trials is crucial to improve reliability of the results.
Related to the preprocessing pipeline, it would have been interesting to investigate the effects of
using the TESA filter compared to filtering on a trial by trial basis. However, due to time limita-
tions, this was not possible during the work of this thesis. As mentioned earlier the ground truth
of the iTEPs is unknown, and this must be kept in mind for preprocessing steps such as signal
filtering. Therefore it is believed that this investigation is an important part of future research,
where comparison between the obtained results is made between methods. Additionally for future
research it would be relevant to investigate further the contribution of multiple muscles to the
correlation analysis. This applies for individual muscle contribution and in a compound score, where
a comparison is made to using only the FDI target muscle as has been presented here.
The results of this study indicate a relationship between the iTEPs and MEPs which is a motivation
for further research. By addressing the aspects mentioned here above, it is believed to improve the
research and consequently increase the understanding of the effects of TMS on the central nervous
system.

6
CONCLUSION
6
Conclusion
The objective of this thesis was to develop an EEG preprocessing pipeline, designed to visualize and
quantify iTEPs, and use them to characterize the relationship between iTEPs and MEPs within
individual subjects. The results of the correlation analysis showed that the absolute peak amplitude
of the high frequency iTEP peaks was significantly positively correlated with the MEP peak-to-peak
amplitude, suggesting a within-subject relationship between the iTEPs and the MEPs. This was
found for both subjects, although only on a trial by trial basis for the second one. Additionally, the
correlation results for iTEPs and MEPs in the contraction state for the first dataset, indicated that
there might be a stronger relationship between the iTEPs and MEPs during contraction. However
due to the small size of the dataset, it is not possible to conclude if the correlation is stronger under
contraction compared to rest.
Overall the findings of this thesis indicate a within-subject relationship between the iTEPs and
MEPs. These findings contribute to increase understanding on the effects of TMS on the central
nervous system, but require further studies to confirm these results across multiple subjects.
