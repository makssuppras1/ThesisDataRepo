Abstract
For any onshore wind farm, the access road network is a crucial component, with-
out it, the wind turbine components would never make it to the site locations to be
installed. For a wind farm location in complex terrain, the design and construction
of this access road network is of even greater importance. Building the access road
network for these wind farm comes with added complexities due to increased con-
struction costs and the longer road lengths required in order to feasibly navigate the
topography.
Traditionally wind farm developers have been reluctant to pursue wind farms in com-
plex terrain due to these added complexities and the added uncertainties involving
the wind resource in complex terrain. However, these areas are becoming more at-
tractive in recent years due to many factors, including advances in wind resource
modelling in complex terrain and the fact that fewer ”Good” locations remain that
haven’t already been exploited for renewable energy production.
Thus far, the access road network has mostly been treated as an after thought in the
wind farm layout optimization process. The wind farm layout is optimized account-
ing for items such as the wind resource, wake effects, estimated AEP and the net
present value of the estimated lifetime costs. In this mindset, the access road net-
work is assigned an estimated cost and lumped in with the overall estimate. Leaving
the detailed design of the road network for experts at a later point in time, once the
layout had been optimized and decided upon. This process also leaves the developer
dependant on the skills of the individual(s) who prepared the lifetime cost estimate.
This thesis intends to examine the idea that, for a wind farm in complex terrain, there
are advantages to conducting a preliminary road network design during the layout
optimization process, and calculating a cost estimate based on this design. Conduct-
ing this process using computerized algorithms allows this process to be conducted
quickly and efficiently. It would complement the layout optimization process in the
sense that, each layout generated in the optimization process would also have a road
network designed for that specific layout, along with a calculated cost, which in turn
would be fed into the overall layout optimization so a more optimal wind farm layout
can be selected.
This thesis presents the automated route projection algorithm and the cost model
which have been developed. As well, Prim’s Algorithm has been implemented as a

part of the presented optimization tool in order to find the minimum spanning tree of
the associated graph problem. This results in a detailed access road network design,
along with the cost of constructing such a network. The final road network must also
conform to constraints associated with transporting the heavy wind turbine compo-
nents along gravel roads in complex terrain. Finally the developed program is tested
using a real wind farm located in China as a case study.
The results of this thesis show that, while shorter, feasible routes between nodes may
exist, it is preferable, and cheaper, to design the road link to take a longer route
and avoid any major terrain features like valleys or hills. This Optimal road network
designed in this report had a length of 13.45 km, a 2.36 km road to connect the wind
farm to a main road, and and overall cost for the road network and main road access
of 291,696.12 kr.
It was also noted that depending on the location of the wind farm, existing rods
may be available and it is always cheapest to take advantage of existing roads as
much as possible, designing the road network around them is worth the reduced costs.
This is because the additional road length is inconsequential in the long term as the
major transportation events occur once when erecting the turbine and again when
decommissioning it, at the end of it’s feasible lifetime. Thus, there is typically a 20
year period in between these events and the regular maintenance activities required
to operate the turbines for those 20 years, are typically not very frequent. Again
making the length of the wind farm network less important than the construction
costs of the access road network.

1 Introduction
Wind energy has seen a prolific rise in acceptance and utilization in the last decade
and is fast reaching maturity. By the end of 2018, nearly 600 Gigawatts (GW) of
installed wind turbine capacity had been installed worldwide. [1] While the large off-
shore wind farms steal the headlines, onshore wind power still has a large role to play
in order to meet the levels envisioned in the Sustainable Development Scenario (SDS).
Currently, onshore wind capacity is not on track to meet the levels desired for 2030
and in order to meet these levels capacity growth will have to increase much quicker
than has been observed over the past few years. [2] Of the many challenges facing
onshore wind farms, finding suitable locations that are environmentally and socially
feasible is proving to be more and more difficult as the best locations are typically
already occupied with wind turbines. This is causing wind farm developers to search
harder to find a suitable location, which also dissuades developers from pursuing on-
shore wind farms in the first place. One onshore location that still has not seen many
development efforts is areas with complex terrain. [3]
Complex terrain is defined as areas that include features which influence the winds
found in the atmospheric boundary layer (ABL) through surface roughness effects
and terrain topology. These locations have been avoided thus far due to the fact
that turbulence intensity and wind shear are higher and more unpredictable at these
locations. Thus it is more difficult to forecast wind conditions and model wake effects.
All of these issues create conditions where estimates of the Annual Energy Production
(AEP) of a wind farm located in complex terrain are more uncertain and thus the
investment required is considered riskier, even under ideal conditions.[3]
For countries where a large proportion of the topography is considered complex, such
as China, advancements in this area are very appealing as it opens up large areas for
onshore wind power generation opportunities. But now, complex terrain is becoming
a more appealing location even from the overall wind industry’s point of view, due
to the lack of better alternatives. There are ongoing efforts to improve modelling of
turbine wake effects and the winds in the ABL when located in complex terrain. As
well as adapting LIDAR and other wind measurement techniques for reliable use in
complex terrain. These efforts should give developers the information they require
to start considering wind farms in complex terrain more seriously. [3][4] Figure 1.1
shows an overview of a sample wind farm located in complex terrain.

Another challenge with developing a wind farm in complex terrain is that the access
road network is no longer trivial or simple. Due to the size and weight of the turbine
components which need to be transported to the Wind turbine generator (WTG) site
locations there are constraints placed the road design such as; the slope (grade) of the
access roads and how tight the curve radii can be.[6] This necessitates a meandering
route between WTG site locations in order to circumvent the frequent changes in
elevation due to terrain features such as hills and valleys. As such the costs associated
with designing and constructing the access road network for a wind farm located in
complex terrain are higher and the layout of the wind farm will have an effect on
these costs.
As such it is important to consider these costs when optimizing the
layout of the wind farm. It is in this area that this thesis will focus and a real wind
farm with 25 wind turbines, located in Shaanxi province near the city of Jingbian in
northwestern China, will be used as a test location.
1.1
Motivation and Justification
Access roads are a critical component of a wind farm and are used primarily in the con-
struction phase of the wind farm to facilitate the movement of construction vehicles
and transport of the wind turbine components to each WTG site location. However,
these access roads are also necessary to facilitate operation and maintenance activi-
ties during the lifetime of the wind farm. The designed road network should provide
direct connections between the WTG site locations and avoid large detours and the
associated costs with building and maintaining the access road network should be
minimized.[7] For wind farms located in flat areas, these access roads are relatively
easy to build and the design of the road network is less important to the overall cost.
However, when the wind farm is located in more complex terrain, there are additional
costs that accompany designing and building the road network.
These additional costs are borne mainly due to the longer road length required to con-
nect two WTG sites since the road must avoid major terrain features and maintain a
==PAGE:17==

road profile which adheres to the constraints required in order to transport such large
and heavy turbine components to each site. This results in a twisting road which
can be difficult to navigate with multiple changes in elevation along the route and
requires additional volumes of cut and fill material when building the road, which is
one of the main drivers of costs in road construction.[8]
It is also more difficult to clear ground cover along the route as often complex terrain
has more dense tree cover. In order to maintain road integrity during its lifetime,
proper drainage is crucial to divert water to prevent erosion and depending on the
location, a route may encounter streams or rivers which will need to be avoided or
crossed. All of these factors contribute to make building roads in complex terrain
more expensive and more difficult. As such, the design of the access road network
for wind farms in complex terrain is not a trivial task and becomes a more important
piece of the wind farm design puzzle.
It is common during the development stage of a wind farm to utilize an automated
optimization approach to determine the layout of the wind turbines. This process
has thus far primarily focused on minimizing the levelized cost of energy (LCoE) and
maximizing the AEP of the wind farm layout. However, when located in complex
terrain, it may be beneficial to include additional, auxiliary design tasks such as ac-
cess road network design or cable routing design in the optimization process due to
the additional costs associated with installing these civil works in complex terrain. In
this report, only the design optimization of the access road network is investigated
with the design of the cable routing for wind farms in complex terrain a subject left
for future investigation. The addition of these civil works in the optimization process
will allow more optimal solution to be found. Thus, placing the turbines at locations
that balance optimizing the wind resource utilization, with minimizing the costs as-
sociated with building the wind farm and building the access road network.
It appears that several methods to optimize access road network design for wind farms
have been developed and published already. However, the algorithm proposed in this
report takes into account additional design factors and constraints that are involved
with road design and the demands placed by the transportation requirements for wind
turbine components.
1.2
Objective
The objective of this thesis was to develop a novel contour-based Route Projection
Algorithm (RPA) to find the minimal feasible route projection between two nodes
located in complex terrain. This algorithm was applied to the problem of designing
the access road network between wind turbines for a wind farm located in complex
terrain. When used in conjunction with the Optimization Tool, a minimal design
was obtained for the road network between wind turbines for an entire wind farm,

This algorithm was programmed in Python and was designed to find the optimal route

a nature reserves. The optimal road network design presented minimizes the costs

2.3.1
Road Construction
Today, most access roads for wind farms measure between 5m and 6m with some
as narrow as only 4.8m in areas where there is a limited right of way or in environ-
mentally sensitive areas. This trend toward narrower roads, has largely been driven
by reducing costs and ever stricter environmental requirements. Legislation varies
between countries but road designs must take the local flora and fauna into consider-
ation. This can include anything from ensuring certain tree species are not cut down,
to providing corridors for local animals to cross the road safely. Often there are also
aesthetic requirements to ensure that the roads do not disrupt the local aesthetic and
blend into the landscape by using using specific coloured gravel so the roads are not
overly visible.[6] A well designed access road network can reduce costs and limit the
environmental impact of the wind farm[10]

specific constraints.[9] A cross section view of the earth works required for a typical

2.3.1.2
Drainage
Another aspect of road construction which is often overlooked is drainage. Insufficient
drainage is one of the most common causes of civil works failures in wind farms. Such
issues like flash flooding can occur even in fairly dry areas and areas with impermeable
soil are also susceptible to failures if drainage has not been given sufficient attention.[6]
Since the effects of water erosion is compounded in areas of complex terrain, the added
drainage requirements also increase the associated costs, especially when drainage
has been given insufficient attention as any road failures will require additional costly
remediation work in the future.
2.3.2
Transport Considerations
For the large trucks needed to transport the WTG components, there are numerous
challenges involved with navigating the route to each WTG site. However, the trucks
and trailers used are becoming more and more advanced, and as such can deal with
a broader range of challenging scenarios.
Corners present a sizeable challenge for the longer WTG components. Any time a
the road changes direction by more than 135◦from straight ahead this requires a
”hairpin bend” also known as a ”switchback corner”. Construction of these types of
corners involves a considerable amount of additional earthwork in order to build a
berm so the corner can progress on a flat surface which allows the trucks to navigate
them safely. This results in significantly higher costs associated with these corners
and thus, it is preferable to try and avoid them in the route projection.[9] Figure 2.6
below shows a truck transporting a section of the WTG tower through a tight corner,

2.4
Access Road Network Design
With all the previous discussed considerations and how they influence the cost of road
construction it is easy to see how these costs can balloon when designing access roads
for a wind farm located in complex terrain.
The configuration of the access road network for a wind farm can be thought of as a
weighted undirected graph with nodal locations at each WTG site location with the
potential roads between each WTG site location forming the edges of the graph with
the length of the route between nodes or the cost of the road between two notes as the
edge weight (the choice between minimizing length and cost will be discussed later).
The optimal road network is then represented by the minimal path which connects
all the WTG site locations.[10] The theory behind graphs and how this branch of
mathematics is utilized in this report is explained further in the next section.

Prim’s Algorithm is a ”greedy” algorithm, which, by definition means that the algo-
rithm builds up the solution one step at a time, always choosing the next option which
is the locally optimal choice; hoping that these locally optimal choices will lead to a
globally optimal solution. Greedy algorithms are utilized in many different optimiza-
tion problems. Prim’s Algorithm is closely related to Kruskal’s Algorithm and varies
only slightly in how it builds up the MST. Both are elaborations of the generic-MST
method and operate similarly to Dijkstra’s algorithm for finding the shortest path in
a graph.[19]
Prim’s Algorithm operates by selecting the first node in the graph and adding it to
the list holding the nodes in the MST. It then grows the MST by evaluating all the
edges which branch out from the nodes in the MST list and following the minimal
edge length to the next node and then adding this node to the MST list.[19] The
steps implemented in the algorithm are listed below:
1. Create a list set ”MST-list” that keeps track of the nodes already included in
MST.
2. Assign a key value to all nodes in the graph and set this value to infinity initially
for all nodes
3. Assign the key value of the first node in the graph to zero so it is selected first
4. Add the first node to the set ”MST-list
5. While the set ”MST-list” doesn’t contain all the nodes in the graph; do the
following:
a) pick a node ”c” which has the lowest key value (first node added will be
the one with key value 0 as set in set 3)
b) add this node selected in step 5a to the set ”MST-list”
c) update the key values of all the adjacent nodes to node ”c” (To do this,
iterate through all of the adjacent nodes. For every adjacent node ”n”,
evaluate if the weight of edge ”c”-”n” is less than the previous key value
of node ”n”, if this is True, update the key value to the edge weight of
”c”-”n”
d) start over at step 5a until all nodes are in the set ”MST-list”
The steps listed above are taken from [20]. The idea behind using the key values
for each node is to select the minimum weight edge which branches out from the
nodes already in the MST and these key values are only used for nodes not yet in the
MST. They represent the minimum weight edge connecting them to the set of vertices
included in MST.[20] This can also be visually described in figure 2.12. Here, the key
value of each node is shown above each node in red with the green key value indicating
the node which was added to the MST list at each step. Prim’s Algorithm can result
==PAGE:35==
2.5 Graph Theory
21
in slightly different Minimum spanning trees depending on which node is taken as
the start node, so it is always prudent to iterate through the different nodes in the
graph trying using each node as the start node and recording the resulting MST. This
results in ngraph solutions to the MST problem where ngraph is the number of nodes
in the graph. The optimal MST will be the MST with the minimal total weight.

3.3.1
Maximum Road Grade
The maximum gradient (slope) of the road is a critical constraint in the Route Projec-
tion Algorithm. road gradient is measured in percentage and represents the percent-
age of elevation change over a set horizontal distance. for example, a −10% grade
would represent 10m of elevation loss over a 100m horizontal distance.
This constraint is primarily dependant on the type of vehicle that is utilized for
transporting the WTG components. Steep, long downhill road sections are typically
the most hazardous encountered since it is difficult for the truck to brake sufficiently in
order to maintain a safe speed on the descent. for a gravel road, a maximum gradient
of 8-9% gradient is typical for trucks loaded with WTG components. However for
short sections, gradients up to values of 14-15% may be acceptable provided such
mitigating efforts are undertaken such as oiling the road, putting down concrete
slabs or applying another type of special pavement which provides more traction for
the truck and trailer. Another strategy for navigating gradients up to 14-15% is to
have another vehicle such as a bulldozer tow the truck and trailer carrying the WTG
==PAGE:45==

associated with allowing the road gradient to go above 8-9% and these costs are not
fully understood. As such the maximum gradient used for this report will be limited
to γmin = 8% to be on the conservative side.
3.3.2
Minimum Turn Angle
The factors that influence minimum turning angle between segments or the minimum
turn radius for access roads in a wind farm are related to the very large dimensions
of a wind turbine blade. Blade lengths less than 40m are no longer common in the
industry and the trend is for ever increasing blade lengths.
Thus for wind farms
in complex topography, every horizontal curve is a challenge as the area needed to
accommodate the sweep of the blade increases with tighter and tighter turns. The
usage of load path simulation software such as AutoTURN© or AutoPath© is common
in industry in order to plan out the route travelled by the WTG components on their
travels to the site location.[6]
As was previously discussed in section 2.3.2, when the road direction changes by
more than 135◦between segments, a ”hairpin” bend is required. These corners are
very difficult to navigate for the long trucks and trailers required to transport WTG
components, especially when any sizeable elevation change is also present, thus they
should be avoided. There are also substantial additional earth work costs associated
with building a ”hairpin” bend due to the road widening and extra material required
to build the berm which facilitates a relatively flat turn surface. Both of these con-
siderations dictate that the minimum turn angle should be 135◦(βmin = 45◦when
measured with respect to the previous route segment).

4 Road Network
Optimization Tool
The objective of the overall program developed for this thesis is to create what will be
referred to as the ”Road Network Optimization Tool” This tool is envisioned to be uti-
lized as a part of the overall WTG micro-siting process when determining the layout
of the wind farm during the development stage of a wind farm. When determining
the WTG site locations the layout is typically optimized with respect to minimizing
the LCoE. This is a measure of the marginal cost to produce a unit of energy by
taking into account lifetime costs, the time value of money and the estimated AEP of
the wind farm. The specific site location of each WTG can have a substantial impact
on how much energy can extracted from the wind.
Generally it is ideal to place WTGs in areas with the highest wind resource available
taking wake effects into account. In flat terrain the site location of each WTG doesn’t
have much impact on the costs associated with the road network, however this does
not hold true for a wind farm in complex terrain. As such, when optimizing the WTG
layout for a wind farm including the road network design in the overall optimization
process should lead to a more optimal result.
There are many different wind farm layout optimization techniques available and ir-
respective of whichever technique is utilized, they all progress in a similar fashion
where they iterate through different layout arrangements and recalculate parameters
between each iteration. It is envisioned that the Road Network Optimization Tool
described here would be implemented so that it would determine the optimal road
network for each iterative layout option and the corresponding network cost would be
factored into the overall project lifetime costs. This would then be used as the basis
of calculating LCoE along with the estimated AEP of each iterative layout option.

4.4
Route Projection Algorithm
In road engineering, the route between two nodes is created by progressing from the
start node to the finish node by creating a series of control points along the way.
The route the road will follow is thus made up of the line segments that connect the
control points. Each control point is selected subject to the constraints explained in
section 3.3.[10] The route projection between the two nodes of each route leg in the
MST is independent of direction, thus the route projection will be conducted twice,
once from start node to finish node, and again from finish node to start node. This
results in 2 route projection options from which the optimal route will be selected as
is described in more detail in section 4.6.
The Route Projection Algorithm (referred to as the RPA henceforth) is utilized twice
in the Optimization Tool, once to find the detailed route projection for all the legs in
the MST and again to find the route projection from the closest MRAP to the wind
farm access road network. There are slight differences in how the RPA is implemented
to find the road from the wind farm to the MRAP and these differences are described
in section 4.7. Within the RPA, several embedded functions are referenced which will
be explained in further detail in section 4.4.1. When initializing the RPA, several

The initial step is to determine the current leg, which direction the route will progress
and to get the elevation at the start and finish nodes using the ”get z” function that
was discussed in section 4.2.
Next, the RPA checks if it should run the Way-Point Algorithm (WPA). If the de-
veloper using this Optimization Tool wishes to use the WPA to develop the optimal
access road network, it is run prior to finding the directional route projection between
the start and finish node. The idea behind this algorithm is to evaluate the elevation
profile between the start and finish nodes to determine if there is a substantial terrain
feature (such as a steep hill or valley) between the two nodes.
If the WPA does not detect a major feature between the start and finish nodes, the
algorithm bypasses the creation of any way-points and moves onto the detailed route
projection step. If, on the other hand, the elevation profile indicates the presence of
a major terrain feature, the algorithm creates nq number of ”way-points” that are
intended to direct the Route Projection Algorithm to avoid this feature.
Thus, instead of trying to determine the route directly to the finish node, these way
points act as intermediate ”gates” for the route projection to pass through on the way
to the finish. This is done to avoid areas of steep terrain as the costs associated with
road construction are higher when the terrain slope is steeper. In addition, the final
route projection between the start and finish node can also be shorter by using way
points and avoiding any major terrain features. This is because often, to navigate
major terrain features, roads take a meandering route to ensure road constraints are
met. They follow the curvature of the topography and occasionally have to use a
switchback to double back and gain further elevation in a feasible manner. Both of
these consequences result in an increased overall route projection length for the leg.
The WPA is described in more detail in section 4.4.1.1.
Finally, the detailed route projection between the start and finish nodes needs to
be created. This is done using the ”Next Control Point Algorithm” (NCPA) This
algorithm builds up the route projection from the start node to the finish node by
creating control points along the way. The detailed route projection starts by setting
the current control point (CCP) to be the start node, then the current end point
(CEP) is set to be either the finish node (if no way points have been created) or to
the first way point (if there are any).
Then the straight line distance from the current control point to the current end
point is calculated (called D2f). Each subsequent control point in the detailed route
projection is found by first drawing a search circle around the current control point
with a given radius of r (r = 40m used initially in this report). The ideal next control
point (NCP) is the target point (TP) which is the point on the search circle which
is in line with the path from the current point to the finish node. The algorithm
starts by evaluating the target point to see if it is valid and adheres to the route
constraints shown in table 4.1. If the target point is a valid control point, then this
==PAGE:56==
4.4 Route Projection Algorithm
42
point is selected and added to the route projection and then this point becomes the
new current control point and the process repeats.
If the target point is not valid, then the algorithm alternates between searching to
the left of the target point and then to the right. With the deviation angle (α) from
the target point getting larger for each consecutive point on both the left and right
sides. i.e. searching 1◦to the left, then 1◦to the right, then 2◦to the left, then 2◦to
the right and so forth. Thus the first valid point found will minimize the deviation
away from the target point, which is ideal for minimizing the length of the detailed
route projection. This valid point is selected and added to the route projection and
then this point becomes the new current control point and the process repeats.
This continues until the the calculated distance to the current end point is less than
2 times the radius (D2f ≤2·r). If the end point is a Way Point, this is the only eval-
uation criteria and once this returns a value of true, the current end point is updated
to be the next Way point (if there is one) otherwise the current end point becomes
the finish node. When the current end point is the finish Node, the evaluation crite-
ria mentioned earlier becomes (D2f ≤2 · r AND gradient from the current control
point to the finish node (γccp,f) can’t be greater than 1.25 times the max gradient
(γccp,f ≤1.25 · γmax).
The extra 25% allowance is rationalized as the area surrounding the finish node will
be levelled and cleared in reality to make way a pad area surrounding the WTG to
allow for the erection crane and other materials. The detailed route projection is
completed once these two statements evaluate as True with the final route segment
spanning from the last node added to the finish node.
This process repeats going in the opposite direction between nodes this time, and
then both detailed route projections are compared and evaluated as is described in
section 4.5.
Once a detailed route projection has been found for all legs in the MST, the algorithm
is finished. It is run one more time without any Way Points to find the detailed route
projection between the closest main road access point(MRAP) and the closest WTG
in the wind farm as is described in section 4.7.

The Way-Point Algorithm is designed to help guide the detailed route projection
around major terrain features that lie between the start and finish nodes for a Leg.
This is conducted by first finding the elevation profile along the straight line between
the start node and the finish node (shown by the black dotted line in figure 4.6). This

in different scenarios using the Case Study wind farm and seems to work relatively
well for most of the scenarios tested.

4.4.2.1
Edge Profile
The edge profile function takes an input array with all the control points in a detailed
route projection and looks at each route segment between two control points.
It
further divides that segment up into 4 sub-segments (each sub-segment length would
be 10m for a 40 segment length) and finds the elevation at each of the intermediate
points between sub-segments. The function then calculates the gradient by dividing
the change in elevation (∆z) by the change in length (∆x) between two adjacent
points. It also calculates the real road length which is the 3 dimensional (3D) length
the road would travel taking elevation into account. This can be thought of as the
hypotenuse of the triangle comprised of ∆x and ∆z. This process is shown visually
in figure 4.10 with the actual route profile shown by the light blue line. This function
was also previously used in the WPA and is shown in figure 4.5.

point to select for the next control point (if it is valid). The rest of the search circle
is constructed by finding the points on the circle at radius rsc, separated by the angle

4.4.2.3
Point in No-GO area
This function takes an input list of points and determines if any of the input points
are contained within the boundary of any of the No-Go areas present. This function
returns a list of the same length as the input points list with entry’s in the list either
reading ”True” or ”False”. Any point that returns a value of ”True” will be considered
a non valid point
In some cases, a point would be physically outside of a No-Go area, however the line
segment between this point and the previous point would cross through the No-Go
area. To combat this, for every input point, a this line segment would be created
and additional intermediary points would be created and each line segment with
intermediate points was evaluated separately. Here the requirement is that all of the
points along the line segment would have to be outside the boundary of the No-Go
area for the input point to return a value of ”True” from this function.
4.5
Route Costs
Once the route projection has determined a feasible route from the current start to
finish nodes, the cost of that current route leg is determined in a segment-wise manner
as is described in further detail in chapter 5. The total calculated leg cost is then
used as a decision variable when selecting between route legs as is described in section
4.6.
4.6
Selection of Leg Route
At this point two route projections have been created between the two nodes under
consideration for the current leg (legi), one going from start to finish (called legi−A),
and the other going from finish to start (called legi−B). Each route projection has
an associated length and cost. Depending on the desire of the individual running the
Optimization Tool either the length or the cost can be utilized as the minimization
decision variable.
It should be noted that during the construction of the road network, if the topography
between two nodes allows it, the Route Projection Algorithm is able to plot a route

5 Cost Model
Typically, road networks are optimized to minimize the length of the road links be-
tween nodes. However, as has been previously discussed, building roads in complex
topography induces additional costs and the shortest straight line path between nodes
is often unfeasible for road construction. Since the steeper the terrain angle, (η), the
higher the cost of the associated earth works, sometimes it is cheaper to to build a
longer road link between nodes if this avoids building through more complex terrain
features. The following section details the cost model developed for this report that
will assign costs to the different aspects of road construction in complex terrain. This
cost model will be used to determine cost to build the road network, which will be
minimized to find the optimal road network design for the test wind farm.

6 Discussion & Results
The Case study presented for analysis in this report was a real wind farm with 25
wind turbines, located in in Shaanxi province near the city of Jingbian in northwest-
ern China. The final python program developed to implement the Road Network
Optimization Tool contained approximately 3200 lines of code including 45 separate
functions.
Conducting the case study analysis was an invaluable process as it allowed the devel-
oped program to be tested in different terrain conditions. The observed behaviour of
the program to these different conditions informed decisions on how to improve the
different algorithms in order to create a robust program, able to deal with almost all
scenarios put before it.
In each tested scenario there are 4 main ways the overall Optimization Tool can be
configured:
• Configuration 1: (C1) - Program uses the Way Point Algorithm and tries to
minimize costs
• Configuration 2: (C2) - Program uses the Way Point Algorithm and tries to
minimize length
• Configuration 3: (C3) - Program does not use the Way Point Algorithm and
tries to minimize costs
• Configuration 4: (C4) - Program does not use Point Algorithm and tries to
minimize length
Results from both of these test cases using the different program configurations will
be presented and analyzed, as well, some of the input and constraint parameters will
undergo a sensitivity analysis in order to observe the effect of changing them on the
program results. This has also been conducted to give some idea of where the optimal
values may lie for these parameters. Finally, Some individual legs withing the MST
route will also be presented to see how the program overcomes the challenges involved
with the respective terrain features which were encountered and how the program can
be best implemented to find the minimal cost route projection between the two nodes.

7 Conclusion
The final Road Network Optimization Tool which was developed for this thesis report
was very complex, utilizing over 3200 lines of code to implement the Tool in Python.
This Optimization tool could be operated in 4 different configurations, toggling be-
tween using or not using the Way-Point Algorithm, and combining that selection with
the choice of optimizing the resulting road network with respect to cost or length.
The presented Algorithm borrowed from existing literature with the aim of making
improvements to provide a more accurate and better design for the access road net-
work of a wind farm. This model addresses some perceived issues with the existing
literature such as not taking road construction costs into account adequately or ac-
curately.
As well, it appeared that existing literature was not accounting for the
turn angle between adjacent route segments, allowing the detailed route projection
to freely alternate direction in a zig-zag pattern in order to navigate terrain. This is
not a good solution as it ignores some of the constraints encountered when designing
a road network which is able to allow the safe transportation of WTG components to
the site locations. Additionally any time a sharp corner is encountered, the associated
costs of building the road go up due to increased earth work requirements.
As with most problems, the quality of the solution is directly dependant upon the
quality of any data inputs. For the road network optimization problem, the qual-
ity of the presented road network design is dependant on the quality of the input
contour map. The higher the resolution for this map, the better. This allows more
information regarding the terrain to be transmitted to the program, allowing for less
uncertainty in the final road network design.
The use of an automated tool such as this has some drawbacks. Since the multitude
of different topographies that exist in the world, it is difficult to develop a tool that
will be able to solve them all automatically. There is a reason detailed road design
typically involves a human touch. As such it is likely that the detailed design process
will never escape having a road engineer fine tune any automated design. However,
there is a great potential to use a tool such as the one presented in this report. It can
be used to provide a good basis to start with for the detailed design phase, saving
precious hours for the engineering team. What is more likely however, is this Opti-
mization tool will be utilized as part of the micro-siting process for the wind turbines.
This will allow the optimized wind farm layout to include the costs and considerations
associated with the challenges of building a road network in complex terrain. This
should lead to more optimal wind farm layouts.

7.1 Future Work
87
The Road Network Optimization Tool was tested by analyzing a case study wind farm
located in northern China. This analysis exhibited the strengths and weaknesses of
the different components of the optimization tool. It was evident from this process,
that using the Way-Point Algorithm generally lead to a lower cost solution, however
it was noted that in some instances, the Way-Point Algorithm would actually create
erroneous way points that ended up causing the Route Projection Algorithm to con-
struct a longer, more expensive route projection between the nodes for that particular
leg. So there is still room for improvement in this algorithm.
The Best Access Road Network which could be generated using the Optimization Tool
was found using configuration C1. This involved optimizing the road network with
respect to cost and utilizing the Way-Point Algorithm to steer the resulting route
projections around any major terrain features between the nodes for the MST legs.
These results show that it is preferable, and cheaper, to design the road network to
take a longer route and avoid any major terrain features like valleys or hills. The
Optimal road network found in the report had length of 13.45 km, a 2.36 km road to
connect the wind farm to a main road, and and overall cost for the road network and
road to reach the main road access point of 291,696.12 kr.
7.1
Future Work
Throughout the process of writing this thesis, several areas were identified as promis-
ing avenues for future work. In addition, areas where improvements could be made
to the methodology were also identified. In this section an attempt will be made to
convey some thoughts on these identified areas and lay the groundwork for improving
upon the subject matter presented in this report.
One area that would see an extensive change in how the Optimization Tool and Route
Projection Algorithm operate would be by incorporating using existing roads in the
road network. This would substantially reduce the costs, but would require the devel-
oper to have access to a good map, something that is not always available, or requires
manual effort to map roads out in google earth for example. As such, automating this
process might be difficult. If feasible however, the algorithms would become more like
the route finding algorithms employed by google maps for example. and the program
would attempt to select the route, regardless of length in order to prioritize using
existing roads.
It would also be advantageous to have more data on the geological nature of the
ground in the area of the wind farm. This would allow a more accurate cost model
to be developed based on local costs and the soil composition. Allowing the model to
better select the appropriate embankment model for example, based on terrain slope
and the soil composition and stability. During the literature review process a docu-
==PAGE:102==
7.1 Future Work
88
ment called a soil map was encountered which would contain this type of information.
However, these soil maps appear to typically be special documents, generated and
paid for as part of a specific project. It is not outside the realm of possibility that
the wind farm developer would see value in having such a document created by con-
tracting to a Geo-technical specialist for an area survey. However, that would likely
be something that would occur later on in the development process, and this report
is focused on developing an Optimization tool for use closer to the beginning of the
development process to help determine an optimal wind farm layout, likely occurring
in the feasibility review for the wind farm.
Another avenue to pursue for improvements would be to implement a code to solve
for the Steiner minimum spanning tree as opposed to a regular MST. The Steiner
minimum tree (SMT) creates intermediate node points that the MST is allowed to
use. This results in a SMT solution that looks more similar to the actual road network
as noted in figure 6.20 The developed code is also fairly slow for implementation in
an automated optimization application. There are likely many areas within the code
that could be coded more efficiently and better, however the efficiency and quickness
of the code was a target, but not a measure upon which to measure the success of
the developed Algorithms and Optimization Tool.
The developed Route Projection Algorithm accounts for certain No-Go areas, however
this process was conducted manually using google earth and only focused on building
obstructions. Additional areas where it would be desirable to classify as a No-Go area
would be:
• environmentally sensitive areas such as reserves of parks,
• areas already in use for other purposes, such as farms.
With the ever advancing transportation solutions for WTG components, the con-
straints put in place for this report may be obsolete. As such a more in depth study
of the capabilities of new WTG transport solutions would potentially for the relax-
ation of a number of the constraints used in this report.
Finally, This report focused on the optimization of the access road network for a wind
farm in complex terrain. However, the optimization of the electrical cable routing for
wind farms in complex terrain is another area that would benefit from an automated
optimization process.
The argument could be made to bury the cables alongside
the access road, avoiding duplication of earth work activities. However, the costs
associated with electrical cables are substantial and are dependant on the length of
cable require, thus minimizing this problem with respect to length is much more
critical. Cabling also has the ability to be run overground using poles to suspend
them at heights. Overall, an interesting area for further study.

7.2
Known Issues
While developing the code for the implementation of the Road Network Optimization
Tool, some issues were noted towards the end which went uncorrected due to a lack
of time, but also due to the fact that these issues rarely occur. Since the focus of this
report is more regarding the developed Algorithms and how the Optimization tool
goes about finding an optimal solution for the road network, these coding issues are
viewed as important, but do not detract from the purpose of this thesis.
Currently the code raises an error whenever the Route Projection Algorithm is unable
to find a feasible route for both directions of the same route leg. Ideally, this should
trigger the Algorithm to exit back into the main Optimization tool and mark the two
nodes where no feasible route projection was found. The the program would redo
finding the MST for the wind farm, taking this into account. This would result in a
different MST and the process to find detailed route projections would start over using
this new MST. This capability was not implemented however due to time constraints.
Finally it was noted in the cost model that the calculated costs were different for
a route leg where the length is the same in both directions (route projection was
a straight line between nodes). This stems from an issue with how the cost model
calculates the embankment costs. Since these costs are dependant on the average
terrain slope along the length of the segment, to have the same average terrain slope,
one would require the same control points to be evaluated when progressing in either
direction.
This is not the case however as the control points are offset depending on which
node the route progresses from. for the first direction the route goes from start to
finish with control points at 40m intervals, however the last segment before reaching
the finish node is of a different length due to the fact that the distance between
nodes is never equally divisible by the search circle radius. So when finding the route
progression from finish to start, the inverse is true, which leads to the mismatch in
control point locations. This issue results in slightly different average terrain slopes
for each segment, which in turn leads to slightly different costs.
