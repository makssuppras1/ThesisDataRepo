III 
Abstract 
This study is a sustainability assessment of vertical hydroponics. Vertical hydroponics is a novel 
method for cultivating crops. Instead of using land and soil is vertical hydroponics based on a 
system where nutrients are recirculated around the plant roots and LED light. The sustainability 
assessment in this study has focus in environmental and social sustainability. For the assessment 
of environmental sustainability life cycle assessment has been applied. The life cycle assessment 
was done as a comparative study, where vertical hydroponics was compared with conventional 
and ecological agriculture. For the comparison four different baseline systems was modelled. The 
study was done in collaboration with Nordic Harvest. Nordic Harvest is a startup company that 
soon will produce lettuce with the vertical hydroponics method. Nordic Harvest provided the data 
used for modeling the vertical hydroponics system, for the life cycle inventory. The data for the 
conventional and ecological system was based on literature. The crop assessed in this study was 
green leaf lettuce. And the functional unit is this study is: “100g of lettuce ready to eat in the 
kitchen 3 times in a week in 1 year” And the corresponding reference flow “15,6 kg of eatable 
lettuce at home.” The environmental impact from each system was calculated using the life cycle 
impact method called ReCiPe, where both midpoint and endpoint results were presented. From 
the midpoint result the global warming potential for the three systems was 25,44 𝑘𝑔 𝐶𝑂2 −𝑒𝑞 
for the conventional lettuce, 45,73 𝑘𝑔 𝐶𝑂2 −𝑒𝑞  for the ecological and 108,97 𝑘𝑔 𝐶𝑂2 −𝑒𝑞 for 
the lettuce from vertical hydroponics. With primary contributions from respectively: Aircraft 
freight and electricity production for the vertical hydroponics system. Other point from the mid-
point result was that the positive effect from hydroponics higher fertilizer utilization was insignif-
icant because of the negative impact from electricity use. From the endpoint result vertical hydro-
ponics has a lower impact on Ecosystems Quality but in single point impact it is placed between 
conventional and ecological lettuce. The impact from the vertical hydroponics systems was also 
calculated where wind was used as electricity source. This resulted in a global warming potential at 
10,16 𝑘𝑔 𝐶𝑂2 −𝑒𝑞 There were identified some trade-offs to more toxicity oriented impacts for 
the vertical hydroponics system. Both for wind based electricity and for average electricity use. At 
endpoint did the wind based vertical hydroponics have lower impacts in all categories. The vertical 
hydroponics system was also assessed on future scenarios. There seems to be a benefit with the 
use of solar panels, and a robustness towards future changes in electricity grid. There was also 
found a potential in using vertical hydroponics for growing meat replacement plants. But this needs 
to be investigated further. The assessment of social sustainability was conducted doing a survey 
and an interview. There seems to be a lack of knowledge on hydroponics. The attitude towards

this might be a challenge for vertical hydroponics. Not to mention that there is also some current

Chapter 1  Introduction 
This thesis is a sustainability assessment of the agricultural method called vertical hydroponics. The focus 
of the thesis is to identify the potential of vertical hydroponics in comparison with conventional and 
ecological agriculture. The environmental and social aspect of sustainability is in focus in this thesis 
(Brundtland, 1987). 
The thesis is written in corporation with the company Nordic Harvest, which future vertical hydroponics 
lettuce production also is the baseline for the assessment. The economic aspect of sustainability in vertical 
hydroponic is assessed by Nordic Harvest and is not included in this study. The environmental aspect of 
sustainability is in this thesis assessed through a life cycle assessment and the social aspect of sustainability 
is as assessed through a qualitative analysis. 
 
Figure 1.1 Mood picture of lettuce growing in a vertical hydroponics system. (by Nordic Harvest) 
Lifecycle assessment is a method used to assess potential environmental impacts from a product or a 
product system. (ILCD, 2010) It normally considers the whole lifecycle of the product or product system. 
E.g. from raw materials production to the use and disposal phase of the product. Often referred to as a 
cradle-to-grave approach.  
The purpose of the LCA in this thesis is to identify potential environmental impacts from the vertical 
hydroponics method. The environmental impact from vertical hydroponics is then compared to the en-
vironmental impacts from conventional and ecological produced crops. As the baseline for the assess-
ment of vertical hydroponics is Nordic Harvest, this study focuses on the assessment of lettuce produc-
tion, as this is what Nordic Harvest will be growing when they start production in June 2019. The assess-
ment considers the life cycle stages from raw materials to prepared lettuce ready to eat by the consumer.  
The social sustainability assessment of vertical hydroponics discusses the consumer’s relationship to pro-
duction of vegetables. A survey is the baseline for the discussion where both the vertical hydroponics,

1.1 Problem formulation 
In 2018 under the United Nations Climate Change Conference COP24, participating countries settled 
on a “rulebook” for putting the 2015 Paris Agreement into practice. This is an agreement on how to 
measure and report the emissions-cutting efforts. (The Guardian, 2018). The aim of the Paris Agreement 
is to keep the global temperature rise in this century below 2 degrees - even 1,5 degrees.  
    But just two months before COP24, did the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) warn 
that 2 degrees was not enough to avoid devastating consequences for life on earth, and that the global 
warming temperature rise is likely to hit 1,5 degrees already between 2030 and  2052 if the current rate is 
kept up (Masson-Delmotte et al, 2018). 
     At the same time is the world population growing, and soon reaching 9 billion people with a need for 
food security. This is in a world where the agricultural sector already contributes between 17% and 32% 
of the anthropogenic greenhouse gasses globally (Smith et al, 2012), 33% of the impacts on ecotoxicity 
and 60% on eutrophication (Guinee et al, 2006). Not much can be done about a growing population, in 
effort to reduce emissions, hence there might be a need to radically change the food production methods.  
    Ín 2017 was the author of this thesis first introduced to vertical hydroponics. This was during a course 
called Sustainability Challenges at DTU. The course is cross-disciplinary with students from KU, CBS 
and DTU, concerning global sustainability issues. Vertical hydroponics was introduced as a promising 
alternative to conventional agriculture seen from an environmental perspective. Vertical hydroponics 
already exists and some of the benefits are suggested to be: 
- 
Compact and space saving 
- 
Does not require soil 
- 
Efficient and productive 
- 
Minimal waste and maintenance 
(Schubert et al, 2013) 
At that time, the author of this thesis could not find any studies on how the environmental impact from 
hydroponics was compared to conventional open field agriculture. This lead up to conducting this study.

    In projecting this thesis, comprehensive research was conducted to find a current functioning vertical 
hydroponics system to analyze. This was though not possible to find in Denmark. It was possible to find 
small-scale roof-top farms, but not industrial scale vertical hydroponic systems. At last was contact es-
tablished to Anders Riemann from Nordic Harvest who was projecting to build an industrial scale vertical 
hydroponics farm in Taastrup. However, the projected farm would not be producing lettuce before June 
2019. This would give the study a more theoretical direction, but Riemann already had many data available 
about the production site. And this project was considered possible. 
The final problem formulation: 
Is vertical farming a possible sustainable alternative to other conventional agricultural methods? 
- 
Is vertical farming currently a sustainable alternative? 
- 
What are the possibilities for vertical hydroponics now and in the future?  
Henceforth, the purpose of this thesis is to identify whether vertical hydroponics could be a possible 
alternative method to conventional and open field agriculture. If this is true, vertical farming might be 
able to help limit the global warming temperature rise to 1,5 degrees, in a world where the rising popula-
tion still gets fed.

Chapter 2  Methods 
In the following section, the methods used in this thesis are presented. This gives a general understanding 
of the methods used and why the methods are relevant in the context of sustainability. It is important to 
understand that this thesis does not have its focus in testing and discussing the methods in detail. The 
focus of this thesis is to apply the methods and the included tools, to analyze a new technology and from 
the following result give an assessment of the technology from a sustainability perspective. 
2.1 Life Cycle Assessment 
The methodology used in this LCA study is based on the International Organization for Standardization

Stage 4: Interpretation: Is the interpretation of the life cycle result. It involves a critical review, determi-
nation of data sensitivity and a presentation of the result.

3.1.1 The production process of hydroponics system at Nordic Harvest 
The crops cultivation and growing process will be introduced shortly in this section. Compared to on 
field agriculture it is more technical, but the principle is still the same. Plants needs water, nutrients and 
light. 
1. At first is the lettuce seeds sown in sponges with water and nutrients. After that is the sponge 
with the seeds put aside in a dark room to sprout. (Figure 3.1) 
2. The grow-sponges where the seed did not sprout are removed. 
3. When the sprouts are big enough the sponges are moved to bigger trays, where the roots will 
stick out of the bottom to absorb nutrients from the water underneath. (Figure 3.1) 
 
Figure 3.1 1left: Seeds sown in sponges in until the seed sprouts. 2 right: The sponges are moves to bigger trays, where the roots are sticking out. 
4. The trays are placed in a shelf system, where water and nutrients are recirculated around the roots. 
This ensures that the plants always has enough water and enough nutrients to grow. At Nordic 
Harvest the nutrients comes from fermented plant waste. In the whole growth period, LED light 
provides artificial light for the plants to grow. The LED light is shut off half of the time, to 
simulate night. This helps the plants to grow.

The overall purpose of this thesis is to make a sustainability assessment of vertical farming.  The LCA

make an assessment on the social or the monetary aspect of sustainability. Later in the thesis, the social

4.2.7 Lifecycle Impact assessment  
The LCIA result will be presented in both midpoint and endpoint level. The midpoint level provides a 
scientific view on the environmental impacts, such as global warming potential and water depletion. The 
endpoint categories are weighted to the three areas of protection: Human Health, Resources and Eco-
system Quality, which are easier to interpret for a non-technical audience. The endpoint level result also 
makes it possible to give highly subjective weighted single-point impact score. 
    The impact calculation method used is ReCiPe. This method is widely used I Europe (Prado, 2017) 
hence has a better geographical representation. In this study is the hierarchical worldview used, where 
weighting has been applied. 
     The impact categories first presented in the LCIA at midpoint are the impact categories considered 
representative for agriculture: Agricultural land occupation, global warming potential, fossil depletion, 
freshwater ecotoxicity, freshwater eutrophication, marine eutrophication and water depletion (Goldstein, 
2016). As this study is moving outside the conventional understanding of agriculture, it is though neces-
sary to also include other categories to identify trade-offs between impact categories. In the LCIA all 
midpoint impact categories will be presented. But the impact categories concerned with agriculture will 
be discussed, and then other important impact categories will be included and discussed afterwards.

Chapter 5  Life cycle inventory 
In this section, the data collection process required for the development of the LCI is presented, then 
followed by the general flow diagram of the four different baseline systems: Conventional farming sys-
tem, ecological farming system, the vertical hydroponics system based electricity supply from the average 
Danish electricity grid and last the vertical farming system with electricity supply from Danish windmills. 
Respectively: CF system, EF system, VF system and VF Wind system. 
5.1 LCI data collection 
In the development of the LCI, it is important to get as high quality data as possible, to get as representa-
tive result as possible in the LCIA. The planning of data collection for the LCI is an iterative process as 
well as the collection of data. (Hauschild et al, 2018). This means that it in practice can be a very time-
consuming process. It is usually possible to find better and more data on a studied system if the timeframe 
is long enough, but at some point a decision has to be made about the satisfactory of the data. Further-
more is it often possible that the data planning and collection phase can be limited due to budget re-
striction, time and availability. (ILCD, 2010) 
5.1.1  Coping with uncertainty on data collection 
Uncertainty in data is inevitable; hence, the LCI also has to be able to cope with uncertainty. One way to 
do this is to be able to analyze, the impact of uncertainty on the result. In this thesis are the quality 
indicators and the pedigree-matrix from (Ciroth et al, 2013) and (Weidema et al, 1996) used as the data 
quality management system. This makes it possible to quantify the quality of the data in respect to relia-
bility, completeness, temporal correlation, geographical correlation and technical correlation. Within each 
of the uncertainty categories can the evaluation score from 1 to 5. Respectively from “very poor” to “very 
good” each with a qualitative statement associated. The assessment system and pedigree matrix can be 
seen in annex 4.2. 
Data used in this thesis comes both with and without statistical uncertainty. Data with statistical uncer-
tainty comes primarily from other scientific studies, and data without statistical uncertainty, comes pri-
marily from expert statements. 
When calculating the final LCI result for each system it is possible to visualize the uncertainty from both 
the data quality indicators, and the statistical uncertainty. This can be done with a Monte Carlo Simulation 
(Lewandowska, 2004). The Monte Carlo Simulation makes it possible to identify if the accumulated un-
certainty in the LCI and a possible implication on the conclusion in the result interpretation. Under the

5.2.1 Conventional vs. ecological grown lettuce 
The convention and ecological methods to grow lettuce resembles each other. Ecological farmed lettuce 
though needs to live up to certain requirement regarding chemicals, fertilizer, and the origin of the agri-
cultural inputs has to be certified as ecological. (Landbrugsstyrelsen, 2018). The main difference is with 
pesticides and artificial fertilizer use. In ecologically grown lettuce can artificial fertilizer not be used, 
hence manure and nitrogen binding plants are used instead (Gartnerirådgivningen, 2007). In ecological 
grown lettuce, pesticide use is also very limited, and before the lettuce is planted, the plant bed needs to 
be cleared from weeds. Therefore is the weed burned instead of using pesticides like glyphosate, which 
is done with conventional grown lettuce. This also makes it necessary to remove weed mechanically more 
often than for conventional lettuce. Anti-fungicide use is also limited in ecologically grown lettuce. This 
contributes to the negative effect that the general yield pr. area is lower for ecological lettuce (annex, 1.4). 
5.2.2 Open field grown lettuce vs. vertical farming 
In the VF system under study at Nordic Harvest, is plant waste from other agricultural processes used as 
fertilizer. First, it undergoes a fermentation process, so it can be added to the recirculating irrigation 
water. In the two open field agriculture systems, the plants need water from rain and irrigation. The water 
on the field is exposed evaporation. This is not as much the case with the VF system, where the water is 
recirculated and low temperature minimizes evaporation. 
In the VF system, the use of land is limited to the factory ground area where open field agriculture needs 
more land, as it cannot use occupied space in the vertical direction. However, as VF plants are stacked in 
multiple floors, this limits the amount of light the plants gets from the sun. This makes it necessary to 
put artificial lighting above each plant bed.  
The low space requirement and the lack of soil in the VF system though removes the need for use of 
agricultural machinery and automatic shelfs are used in collaboration with a few people instead. The more 
sterile environment inside makes it possible to completely remove the use of pesticides and other chem-
icals in the growth process. As the plants are grown locally (in this case Copenhagen), the need for trans-
portation is also lower. Another benefit is the inside environment, that allows for production all year 
around. The open field systems can only grow lettuce 50% of the year. (Grønttorvet, 2018) hence lettuce 
need to be imported with air freight (Jyllandsposten, 2016).

5.7 Main assumptions in the models 
Because of the lack of time and budget in the study, assumptions has been necessary to include in the 
development of the lifecycle inventory. In this section the main assumptions done in the modeling will 
be explained. The full detailed assumptions regarding the model and the processes used in the database 
can be found in annex 2. 
The production of lettuce is in this study to be representative for the environmental sustainability assess-
ment. If other plants were to be assessed the result would probably be different. The growth process of 
lettuce is also assumed to follow the best-practice in the growth guides, hence there might be a discrep-
ancy between the theoretical growth and the one actually being extradited.  
In this study the field has been assumed to be a part of the technosphere. This means that, only emissions 
to the ecosphere outside the field is considered an emission. The field is considered a part of the techno 
sphere until 1,5 m deep in the ground. Furthermore is the lettuce modelled as an annual crop, where 
emissions and resources from previous crops does not influence inputs and outputs to the current crops.  
For all the systems, it is assumed that 𝐶𝑂2 fixing in the in the whole lifespan of the plant is 0. As well as 
direct 𝐶𝑂2 emissions from soil and the breaking down of sediments in soil is not included in the model 
as well. 
Fertilizer used by the ecological system is not considered burden free even though manure normally is 
considered waste. This is because that ecological manure is scarce. (Landbrugsstyrelsen, 2018) The same 
applies for the VF system where the fertilizer is based on plant waste as the plant waste in theory can be 
replaced as animal feed. 
In the period where the lettuce is imported from Spain, the same production method is assumed. This 
means that the impact from the lettuce produced in Spain, has the same impact as if it was produced in 
Denmark. This gives an incorrect geographical representativeness from the production method in Spain 
which also is technologically unknown.  
All 3 systems consist of processes, where some waste and small emissions of e.g. dust and, chemicals are 
inevitable due to e.g. human errors. In this study it has not been possible to identify such emissions, due 
to its theoretical nature. But in this study they are considered neglible in difference across the compared 
system and this should not be disagreement with the Goal & Scope of the study.

Chapter 6  Baseline LCIA Result 
In this section will the LCIA result from the four baseline systems be presented. At first the midpoint 
result, then the endpoint result. After both the midpoint result and the endpoint, the overall result will 
be assessed with a sensitivity analysis, an overall data assessment and Monte Carlo Simulation. 
6.1 LCIA result midpoint 
To comply with the Goal & Scope of the LCA, first the environmental impact results from the four 
different baseline systems; CF, EF, VF and VF Wind are presented by using ReCiPe Midpoint (H). They 
will be presented and discussed in detail, where there at first is focus on the impact categories in the scope 
of agriculture. According to (Goldstein et al, 2016) are the impacts categories; agricultural land use, cli-
mate change, fossil depletion, freshwater toxicity, freshwater eutrophication, marine eutrophication and 
water depletion representative for the impact of agriculture. As this study move outside the traditional 
understanding of agriculture other important impact categories from the midpoint result will be included 
and discussed later.

6.1.2 Pure wind energy as electricity source for vertical farming 
As mentioned earlier, Nordic Harvest will make sure they use only wind electricity for their production 
They do this by making a contract with an electricity company that guarantee that they only sell electricity 
produced by windmills. Like vindstød.dk (Vindstød, 2018). It is though not exactly sure how this works 
in practice. As the price elasticity of electricity is considered low (Lijesen, 2006), the demand for electricity 
can momentarily be seen as constant. This would mean, that if someone buys the electricity only pro-
duced by wind, another one is forced to buy electricity from a production method with a higher impact. 
Hence the average electricity mix stays the same. 
    It might though have other benefits and as the exact dynamic of such scheme is difficult to see through 
it is still relevant to model the system as such. Maybe also to identify a hidden potential. The vertical 
farming system based on wind is here assessed independently. It is done with an electricity load divided 
50% to offshore wind and 50% to onshore wind based on (Birkved, 2016). 
When taking an overview look of Table 6.1 at page 58 it is possible to identify the impacts from the VF 
Wind system. Looking left to right is it easy to see that the VF Wind system has a significant lower impact 
in all the categories except for freshwater ecotoxicity and freshwater eutrophication, compared to the 
open field systems and the vertical hydroponics system supplied with electricity from the average Danish 
grid. 
    To giver an overview of the difference in the impact categories factor difference calculations are made 
where the VF Wind system has been compared with the best-case impact from the 3 other systems.

Chapter 7  Social sustainability 
One of the important areas in the holistic view on sustainability is the social and people perspective. 
Hence, to assess whether a new possible technology could be a more sustainable alternative to a current 
technology, a deeper understanding of the social structures around the current technology is necessary. 
From the result in the LCIA, it was assessed that vertical farming, as an alternative to open field farming 
might have some environmental benefits. Therefore, it is also interesting to analyze vertical farming in a 
social perspective to find and discuss possible obstacles and benefits here. 
    This section first presents the result of the conducted survey, and then discusses the result in a con-
sumer perspective where the interview and literature is involved. Hereafter, is the framing of agriculture 
as technology widened and the general structures in the technology is postulated and discussed. 
7.1 Analysis of survey  
To get a grasp of the general knowledge and attitude towards, current open field farming technology and 
vertical farming, a survey was conducted. The survey was developed and structured with inspiration from 
the Harvard Survey guide (Harvard, 2007). The developed questions for the questionnaire is in (annex 
5.1). The questionnaire was developed in both Danish and English language as it was handed out to both 
English and Danish speaking students at DTU. 
    The questions were formulated in such way, to get a general grouping of the respondents and their 
knowledge about the different production methods. This included a question about the respondents 
knowledge on the different methods. And a question about which agricultrual method the respondent 
assessed to have the highest impact on the environment. To force the respondents to give a more quali-
tative evaluation of the production methods, the respondents was also asked to describe their thoughts 
with 5 words on the different production methods.  
    The questionnaire was handed out (as a link) via the online-survey provider Surveymonkey.com in the 
two courses, where the composition of students is considered satisfyingly random, though it consists 
primarily of people in their twenties. 44 respondents answered the questionnaire. This is considered sat-
isfying number as the survey is used only as a baseline for discussing the social aspect of sustainability 
regarding vertical farming. The questionnaire is not used to draw any direct conclusion, about the social 
sustainability of vertical farming.

7.2 Sustainability in a consumer perspective 
The consumer perspective and acceptance is crucial to consider, when assessing how a technology is to 
be developed and implemented. 
    To get a better understanding of the consumer’s behavior and the cultural understanding of food, a 
qualitative approach is required. In the authors search for information about the area, Professor at KU 
foods: Wender Bredie came up. He is head of Section for Food Design and consumer behavior and had 
the expertise in need for a better understanding of the consumer behavior in relation to vertical farming. 
An interview was planned and conducted by the author (appendix 5.4). The results from the survey in 
the previous section was used as a baseline for the interview. 
    The section below is based on the interview with Bredie and the article “Factors influencing public 
acceptance of innovative food technologies and products” by Michael Siegrist. (Siegrist, 2008). In the 
article, his definition of the term perceived benefits distinguishes the consumer’s actual benefits from buying 
a certain food, from the benefits as perceived by the consumer. In his article, Siegrist points out, the 
necessity of putting the consumer in focus, with focus on notions like perceived benefit, perceived risk 
==PAGE:104==
96 
7 Social sustainability 
________________________________________________________________________________ 
and perceived naturalness being the key aspects of consumer acceptance of new food production meth-
ods. He also points out that consumers base their choice on presumptions about benefits and risks, and 
that consumers lack understanding of the underlying technologies. This is in line with the interview where 
Bredie mentions, “We (consumers) have a strong conception that everything, that comes from nature is 
good for us” and that “we (consumers) nowadays only have perception about where the food comes 
from.” Bradie couples this tendency to the historical urbanization, where there has been a “decoupling” 
from the origin of food. In the conducted survey, there also seems to be a coupling of health and natu-
ralness, in the phrases about ecology as agricultural method. 
In 2017, Landbrug & Fødevarer published a survey about the Danish people’s consumption of ecological 
food. The survey shows the status in 2017 and the development since 2013. There is a clear picture that 
more and more Danish people cares about ecology and buys more ecologically. The survey actually indi-
cates that 70 % of the Danish population buys ecologically sometimes or more often. (Landbrug og 
Fødevarer, 2017). The reason why is different, but the increased attention on pesticides has a significant 
influence for > 50 % of the tested ecology oriented consumers. Other reasons is health, animal welfare 
and the environment. The environmental benefit has an increased focus in 2017, compared to 2013, 
where more people in 2017 buys ecological products of environmental reasons. In addition, the price of 
ecological goods is less central for the consumers as well and it has to be of Danish produce. The article 
concludes that the number of people buying ecological products has increased, the number of people 
never buying ecological products has decreased and the number of people always buying ecological prod-
ucts is unchanged. This movement could be explained by Siegrist and Bredie; that consumers perceive 
ecology as more natural and “…everything that comes from nature is good for us.” 
Looking at conventional agriculture, consumers perceive the technology as efficient but not as natural 
(See Table 7.3). Bearing in mind the arguments from Siegrist and Bredie, consumers will then seek to-
wards the more natural perceived food production processes. Which is an important point, when con-
sidering a new food production technology such as vertical hydroponics.

8.3 Conclusion on scenarios 
The baseline systems previously used in this study has been used to assess the potential environmental 
impact in the future. The result from the scenarios indicate that solar panels used as electricity source 
together with electricity from wind in the VF system has lower impact in in GWP100, FDP, though not 
compared to the VF Wins system. At the endpoint there is a lower impact on the Ecosystem Quality and 
the single point score. As in the VF system based on wind, there are some impact trade-offs. 
The impact from the VF system with electricity supply from the future grid, was also assessed, which 
indicated that the VF system will have a lower environmental impact that the open field systems in the 
future. This also applies even though the open field systems uses biofuel for transportation and machin-
ery. A further study into how the VF systems impacts change along with the open field systems would 
be interesting to do.  
Also an even lower impact could be achieved if other foods such as meat could be replaced by plants 
from vertical hydroponics. There seems to be benefits, and more opportunities to be investigated regard-
ing vertical hydroponics as an alternative technology to open field farming. Open field farming is as stated 
earlier a stable technology, where future possibilities seems to be limited and vertical farming might be 
the methods agriculture where future possibilities should be found.

Chapter 9  Conclusion 
9.1 General 
This study has assessed the sustainability of vertical hydroponics. This was done with focus on the envi-
ronmental and social pillars of sustainability (Brundtland, 1987). The assessment of the environmental 
aspect of sustainability was done by applying a comparative LCA, where it was necessary to develop three 
different system models representative for the methods under study: The conventional agricultural 
method on a field, ecological method on a field and then vertical hydroponics represented by the system 
to be used at Nordic Harvest. The life cycle inventory of each model was calculated and a life cycle impact 
assessment was done using ReCiPe at mid and endpoint. The assessment of the social aspect of sustain-
ability was done by conducting and analyzing a survey on DTU students. The result from the survey was 
then discussed in relation to an interview and included literature. 
The results will be used to give Nordic Harvest aid in the decision making regarding the ongoing devel-
opment of their vertical hydroponics system. From the result in this thesis, Nordic Harvest will be able 
to make decision, with a both environmental and social sustainable perspective. 
In the life cycle impact assessment on midpoint level the vertical hydroponics method had the highest 
impact on the global warming potential, which was of 108,97 𝑘𝑔 𝐶𝑂2𝑒𝑞 This is a factor 4 higher impact 
than for the conventional and ecological lettuce, which had of impact of respectively 25,44 𝑘𝑔 𝐶𝑂2𝑒𝑞 
and 25,73 𝑘𝑔 𝐶𝑂2𝑒𝑞. The high electricity use in the vertical hydroponics system is the most contributing 
factor, because of the impact from the background system of electricity production. The production of 
electricity in Denmark is partly based on non-renewable energy sources, which contributes to the higher 
impact result. The main impact from the two open field methods on global warming potential was though 
because of the airfreight in the import season of lettuce.  
The possibility of using 100% wind electricity for the vertical hydroponics growth systems was also ex-
plored. When this is a possibility then the global warming potential decreases to 10,16  𝑘𝑔 𝐶𝑂2𝑒𝑞 This 
is a factor 2,5 improvement compared to both of the open field systems. It is though a discussion whether 
the possibility of using 100% wind electricity for the hydroponics system currently exists. 
When the environmental impact of the two vertical hydroponics systems were interpreted and compared 
with the two open field systems, it was evident that there is some trade-off to more toxicity oriented 
impact categories. For both systems was the most contributing process the electricity production,

When considering the weighted endpoint result the vertical hydroponics method has a lower impact on

which is higher. If the vertical hydroponics system has the possibility get electricity from wind power, the 
impact at endpoint is lower in all categories. 
Whether the vertical hydroponics method is a sustainable alternative to the open field systems seen from 
an environmental perspective is difficult to assess, even after this study. From the LCIA interpretation 
there is clear indication that the vertical hydroponics method has s higher global warming potential, but 
there also seems to be other benefits e.g. on water depletion. But as it is now, it seems if the environmental 
sustainability is determined by the electricity grid in the background system. Hence a self-sufficient re-
newable electricity source for the vertical hydroponics system would be the better choice.   
This is though not all bad as even though it is not possible to use wind or solar electricity, the environ-
mental benefit seems to emerge in the future. The electricity mix of the Danish grid changes over time, 
and more renewable energy sources with a lower environmental impact will be the norm. This is visible 
in the GWP100 of the vertical hydroponics scenario in the year 2050 as well as the single point impact. 
Considering environmental sustainability in the assessed vertical hydroponics system it is also important 
to mention that this study is based on the production of lettuce. Why this result cannot conclude whether 
other vegetables would be more environmental sustainable to grow. Hence, to get a broader assessment 
of vertical hydroponics as alternative environmental sustainable method for agriculture in general, further 
studies are necessary. But from the perspective of this study it seems as if there is potential that vertical 
hydroponics can be an environmental sustainable alternative. See the recommendations in the next sec-
tion. 
In the social aspect of sustainability, a survey and interview has delivered the main results. Again it is 
difficult to give an exact conclusion on sustainability, but in the analysis it was found that, the open field 
agriculture as method currently is difficult to replace for something knew and more or less unknown.

9.2 Recommendations 
9.2.1 General 
This study is based on more or less theoretical data for all the systems assessed in the LCA. A more 
practical study, would give at better indication of the actual environmental impacts from the systems. 
Data collection for such s study could be done after Nordic Harvest starts producing lettuce in June. For 
the open field system a collaboration with a lettuce farmer has to establish. As agricultural LCA’s are 
complex, assumptions would still have to be made on theoretical data. 
Better data quality in a new study would give a more precise result for all systems. E.g. a database with 
better temporal representativeness for the background system. 
A study with another type of crops, would give a broader view of the environmental impacts from vertical 
hydroponics and the possibilities for growing plants with a higher nutrition value. 
Make a dynamic LCA, where it is possible to see the environmental impact from vertical hydroponics 
changing over time, as the electricity grid changes over time. This was unfortunately not possible in this 
study due to the time limit. 
9.2.2 For the company 
As discovered in the study is the production of electricity the most contributing process. Hence to de-
crease the environmental impact from vertical hydroponics the focus should be put here. Possible ways 
to decrease impact from electricity: 
- 
As solar panels at the factory site are already planned, this would be the natural choice to source 
the electricity from here. Maybe together with the electricity produced from wind. 
- 
Make sure the electricity comes from an energy source with a lower environmental impact such 
as electricity from wind. The electricity could e.g. be produced on the site. 
- 
Decrease the use of electricity in the growth process. This could be done by use of e.g. more 
efficient light, pumps etc. 
One of the process discovered also to contribute to the impact, was there process of making fertilizer 
used in the vertical hydroponics. The use of waste plants might have a higher environmental impact 
compared to e.g. manure. This though this has to be investigated further.
