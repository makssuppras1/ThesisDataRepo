Abstract 
The purpose of the project described in this paper was to demonstrate that typical 
multifamily buildings built in Poland and in Denmark could be compared and retrofitted to a 
"nearly zero" energy building according to regulations existing in both countries. The same 
improvement steps were implemented in both cases – the change of the windows, addition 
of insulation, installation of a central mechanical ventilation (VAV), reduction of domestic 
hot water consumption and installation of solar panels. Additionally, the temperature, 
humidity and     concentration were evaluated according to EN 15251 and building 
regulations. All theoretical calculations were made in a simulation software - IDA ICE 4.6.1 
The results show that following the steps it is possible to reduce theoretical energy use from 
120.5 
   
   to 34.6
   
  , which corresponds to 63.1 
   
   of primary energy consumption in the 
Polish building and from 139.5
   
   to 18.7
   
   in the Danish building. The theoretical energy 
use after the renovation fulfills the requirements of Polish Building Regulations, NF40 and 
Danish Building Regulations. In addition, the indoor climate doesn’t decrease drastically 
during the modernization steps, except for     concentration which must be controlled by a 
mechanical ventilation. At the end it is calculated that retrofitting of the multi storey 
buildings to nZEB level is not cost-efficient according to NPV.

Introduction 
Both Poland and Denmark are members of European Union which obligates them to help in 
the fulfillment of goals that lead to the improvement of the environment. Together with the 
industry and transport, buildings have an influence on the energy consumption and 
greenhouse gas emissions. According to Energy Performance of Buildings Directive 
(2010/31/EU) all new buildings must be nearly zero energy buildings by 31 December 2020. 
Public authorities that are owners or occupants must ensure that the new buildings are 
classified as the nearly zero energy. Nearly zero energy building is defined as : "…a building , 
that has a very high energy performance as determined in accordance with Annex I. The 
nearly zero or very low amount of energy required should be covered to a very significant 
extent by energy from renewable sources , including energy from renewable sources 
produced on-site or nearby". Annex 1 presents general frameworks which should be 
considered in the evaluation of the energy performance of buildings. Member States must 
determine a minimum energy performance for new and retrofitting buildings, present cost-
optimal way to renovation, eliminate the market barriers and guarantee economic support. 
Energy efficiency has a big influence on ambitious European Union's targets declared for 
2020. The main aim is to cut the emission of CO2 by 20% according to 1990 level, decrease 
energy consumption by 20% and gain 20% of energy from renewable sources. Building 
sector has a very big influence on meeting these goals because buildings use about 40% of 
energy and emit 36% of CO2. [1,2] 
More and more of new buildings are constructed with a high energy performance, while the 
existing building stock in general consumes a large amount of energy. Residential buildings 
represent around 75% of overall buildings’ floor area in EU where single family houses cover 
64% and multi storey family buildings 36% [4]. What is more, less than 1% of old buildings in 
Europe is replaced with new low-energy buildings [17] There is a great potential for receiving 
a high energy and CO2 savings by renovation of these buildings. According to EU policies and 
strategies, taking care of existing buildings is a key to reach a long time energy and climate 
goals. [3] What is more, by retrofitting buildings, not only the energy performance can be 
improved but also indoor environment which increases the quality of people's life. Although 
thermal modernization requires a large amount of money  spent in the initial phase, it also 
means much smaller energy bills later on, increase in the value of the building and the 
possibility of architectural lift. [10]

Objectives 
Existing building stock has an enormous potential in overall energy savings of EU, especially 
in countries where the average temperature in winters decreases below 0 °C. While the old 
buildings consume 100 - 300 
   
  , billions of tones of non-renewable energy materials are 
wasted. Using a higher potential of the buildings’ renovation it is possible to spare the 
energy sources that has been already greatly exploited. In the article [30] it is proven that it 
is possible to reduce the energy consumption of an old building from 162.5 
   
   to 51.5 
   
   
which corresponds to Danish Building Regulation 2015. In addition, several studies [13,31-
32] proved, that it is even possible to decrease the energy consumption of multifamily 
building under 20 
   
  . 
The main aim of this project is to evaluate, whether it is possible to achieve the nZEB level 
for multi storey buildings of similar areas in two countries - Poland and Denmark according 
to local building regulations. Moreover, the aim was to analyze the behavior of the buildings 
which are exposed to different weather conditions in the similar steps of modernization 
according to the initial state of the building. To achieve the best result, it is important to 
consider not only the energy consumption but also a good indoor climate. Thus, in the 
project it will be evaluated, whether the thermal modernization may proceed parallel with 
the thermal comfort in the building according to EN 15251 [33] .

3.1.2 Buildings 
The global demand for the energy will increase by 33% by 2035 according to International 
Energy Agency [6]. Building sector has a significant influence on the energy consumption 
mainly because of not-retrofitted, old buildings. As it was mentioned in [7] it is proven that 
energy renovation can decrease the energy demand in buildings by up to 75%. What is more, 
Danish Building Research Institute has estimated that the potential for energy savings in 
existing buildings is around 70%-75% by the year 2050[8]. It means that the existing 
buildings sector cannot be neglected in Denmark's and EU's targets.

Methodology 
A first step in the project is to choose two typical multi-storey buildings in Poland and in 
Denmark. A typical building is the one which represents the number of similar buildings built 
in the same way, with the usage of the same materials, similar shape, conditions and similar 
orientation in each of the countries. Buildings must have been built before 1980 and have a 
poor energy performance. The area, number of storeys, apartments and occupants should 
be the same. Both buildings must be connected with a district heating which is the most 
popular source of heating in Poland and Denmark.  
In both cases, only the last floor of the construction will be considered to show the worst 
case in the whole building. Apartments on the top level of the building are the ones most 
exposed to weather conditions because they are connected with external walls and a roof.  
Models of the buildings and theoretical calculations will be conducted in a numerical 
building energy simulation software IDA ICE 4.6.1. This software allows user to perform a 
whole year full scale simulation and show different behavior of the building accordingly to 
external weather conditions and input data. 
In this report there will be presented 5 steps of the improvement leading to the 
achievement of nearly zero energy building level for building regulations in both countries: 
1. Actual case 
2. Changing the windows

3. Adding the insulation 
4. Installing mechanical ventilation system with the heat recovery

5.1 
Description 
The analyzed building depicted in the Figure 4 is located in Łódź, in the center of Poland. It 
was built in 1979 in a system called "Dąbrowa 78". The main feature of this system is that 
the whole building was built of concrete, prefabricated plates of the thickness between 
14cm and 16cm located inside the building and aerated concrete blocks with a better 
thermal resistance, used to build external walls. Load bearing concrete walls are spaced 
every 2.4m, 3.6m and 4.8m. Ceiling was made of 16cm concrete slab and the whole building 
is covered by a flat roof made of corrugated boards located on the openwork walls and 
cross-reinforced plate.

5.2.4 Ventilation 
Regulations enforce installation of a mechanical ventilation in buildings. Natural ventilation 
does not work properly when the external and internal temperature difference is small, 
what is often experienced during the summer. What is more, very tight building prevents 
proper working of natural ventilation which leads to environmental discomfort of 
inhabitants and the possibility of the mould growth. 
Mechanical ventilation with recuperation provides fresh, warm air during the winter which 
reduces the need to warm up the air by the central heating. 
There are considered three types of ventilation: central (CAV), central (VAV with 
temperature and CO2 control) and decentral. Due to the energy consumption, investment 
costs and overall thermal comfort, the VAV ventilation was chosen. Supply air chart is shown 
below.

Buildings which are used in the project are located in Łódź ( 51°46′N 19°27′E ) and in 
Copenhagen (55°40′N 12°34′E) . In the straight line both cities are 640 km away from each

because Copenhagen is located on a small peninsula which is surrounded by Baltic Sea 
==PAGE:80==

whereas Łódź is located in the center of Poland. Because of the location, the climate is 
different - in the center of Poland there occurs mostly hot summer continental climate and 
in Copenhagen there is experienced maritime temperate climate according to Köppen 
classification. The first type of climate is characterized by the variation of weather which 
changes according to the inflow of the masses. There are high temperature amplitudes and a 
rain occurs throughout a whole year, mainly in the summer. The Copenhagen's climate is 
characterized by delicate winters and chilly summers with a small amplitude of 
temperatures. The humidity is high, wind is strong and it is blowing from the seaside in the 
summer and to the seaside in the winter.[34]

The last step is to decrease the use of domestic hot water. This is the issue which is hard to

beginning it was assumed that one occupant uses 50 liters of hot water daily. In the last step 
it was showed that the domestic hot water consumes 63% of the energy in the Polish 
building and 79% in Danish building. There are some methods which can decrease the 
domestic hot water use. First of all, there can be installed aerators in the taps. Those devices 
give a feeling of a whole water jet but in fact, the water leaves the tap with a higher pressure 
and the resulting amount of the water is decreased by 50%. It can be applied to reduce 
water consumption during both showers and cleaning the hands etc. Secondly, the 
thermostatic mixers can be applied. They prevent water outflow before it reaches the 
required temperature. What is more, there are others types of mixer especially those 
equipped with sensors to reduce the water flow. Additionally, the energy efficient 
equipment like a washing machine can reduce 50% of the water which is needed to wash the

9 
Conclusions 
A case study presented the energy retrofit of two multistorey building with similar areas 
located in Denmark and in Poland. The results of the study show that the regulations in both 
countries are similar but it is easier to lower the energy demand in the Danish building than 
in the Polish building. To achieve the required energy consumption, in both cases it was 
needed to install a renewable energy source which in case of this project was a solar panel. 
However, without renewable energy it is possible to achieve in Poland 53.96 
   
   and in 
Denmark 34.01
   
   of the energy consumption which is respectively 55% and 76% less than 
an actual state. The indoor climate is evaluated by the temperature, humidity and     
concentration in the living room and bedroom. It was noticed that the high temperature 
during the summer might create a problem and it exceeds 100 hours above 26° C. 
Nonetheless, the problem was solved by the overhang and a mechanical ventilation. In the 
final design, temperatures are assigned to the I and II class of thermal comfort. In addition, 
by tightening and warming the building, the     concentration dangerously exceeded 
acceptable levels. Such a problem was solved by the controller installed in the ventilation. 
Humidity level was constantly improved during the modernization, but due to the 
installation of mechanical ventilation, during the winter it causes drying of the air which 
results in the decrease of the indoor climate. The mentioned examples show, that the 
mechanical ventilation has a significant influence on the indoor climate and must be 
designed very accurately to provide a high standard. Even if it is possible to achieve the nZEB 
standard, it is not cost-efficient. The lifetime of retrofitted elements of the building is shorter 
than a predicted payback of the invested money. Nevertheless, the modernization of the 
buildings is an important topic and finding solutions which are cost-optimal but not fulfill 
strict requirements will have a positive impact on the environment and future money 
savings.
