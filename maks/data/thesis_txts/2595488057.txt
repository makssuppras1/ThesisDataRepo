Summary
Train passage of high-speed trains across long-span bridges are subject to substan-
tial dynamic interaction, aﬀecting both passenger comfort and runability in terms
of possible derailment and overturning. This thesis is a continuation of an ongoing
collaboration with Rambøll, with the main goal of further developing a numeri-
cal vehicle-bridge interaction (VBI)-program that can be used to assess passenger
comfort and safe runability of high-speed train passage of long-span bridges.
The VBI-program is a further development of a similar program created by Jacob
Brandt Groes for his Master’s Thesis [1], and is designed to rely on input from
Finite Element (FE) bridge models created in commercial software. The vehicle
model is designed as a 13 degree of freedom (DOF) dynamic multi-rigid body system
consisting of four masses suspended by linear spring-damper suspension systems.
The vehicle model is based on speciﬁcations given in the Storestrøm Bridge (SSB)
Design Basis [2], with an aim of developing a realistic train model.
Kinematic relationships between the displacements of the vehicle and the global
bridge structure is established with a time- and location-dependent interaction ma-
trix considering both vertical and horizontal interaction. System matrices describing
the vehicle are constructed based on the principle of rate of work, and are coupled
with the bridge structure matrices by the use of the interaction matrix. The result
is a number of coupled system matrices representing the full vehicle-bridge dynamic
system as one. Through Newmark time-integration where the time-dependent sys-
tem matrices are updated, the response of the vehicle and the bridge is obtained.
Geometrical irregularities in the rail tracks are implemented in the kinematic rela-
tionship between the vehicle and the bridge. Derailment and overturning criteria
are adapted from the SSB Design Basis [2], and are computed from the response
time series produced by the VBI-program.
The VBI-program is validated through isolated cases that are comparable to an-
alytical solutions or results from similar analyses.
A case study is done on the
Storestrøm Bridge, where the main capabilities and use cases of the VBI-program
are demonstrated in terms of passenger comfort, derailment and overturning.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Modern long span bridges are designed with increasing lengths and slender free
spans, often carrying both high-speed railways and highways. Train passage of long
span bridges is associated with substantial dynamic interaction, where safety of the
bridge structure and safe runability of the train must be evaluated. Long slender
bridge structures sustain global deformations at low natural frequencies, and can
be excited by nature loads like wind, or external impacts from ship collisions.
The project is primarily concerned with the dynamic interaction between high-speed
trains on long-span bridges, where passenger comfort and runability in terms of
possible train derailment and overturning is analyzed. The project is a continuation
of an ongoing collaboration with Rambøll, with the aim of developing a numerical
vehicle-bridge interaction (VBI) tool that can be used to analyze high-speed train
passage across long span bridges like the new Storestrøm Bridge in Figure 1.1.

Introduction
The project is done in collaboration with Rambøll Group A/S, with the main ob-
jective of further development and delivery of a numerical tool for vehicle-bridge
interaction analysis originally made by Jacob Groes in the MSc Thesis "Numerical
Analysis of Vehicle-Bridge Interaction during passage of High-Speed Trains across
Long-Span Bridges" [1]. The VBI-program is expanded to include a more realistic
vehicle model with additional rigid components and degrees of freedom allowing for
horizontal interaction. This allows for analysis of possible derailment and overturn-
ing of the train, which are key aspects in safe train passage of long-span bridges.
For a more realistic dynamic analysis and assessment of passenger comfort, vertical
and horizontal rail irregularities are implemented in the current VBI-program.
Analysis of high-speed train passage on long-span bridges regard both the train
and the bridge as dynamic systems. Coupling between the two systems is therefore
necessary in order to analyze their interaction and response. Numerical software for
vehicle-bridge interaction analysis exists, but with limited access to the underlying
theory and implementation. Development of a well documented numerical VBI-tool
with easily accessed implementation is therefore of interest by Rambøll.
The VBI-program is designed to function as tool in addition to commercial Finite
Element (FE) software capable of modelling and analyzing the dynamic charac-
teristics of spatial linear-elastic beam structures. The FE-software produces the
input for the VBI-tool in a speciﬁc 3d-tensor format established by Rambøll, which
consists of mode shapes, natural frequencies and bridge geometry.
The vehicle model is designed as a multi-rigid body spring-mass-damper dynamic
system, and is based on the description of realistic train model in the Storestrøm
Bridge (SSB) Design Basis [2]. The vehicle model is coupled to the bridge model
through coupled location-dependent system matrices.
The key to this location-
dependent coupling is the interaction matrix which describes the kinematic rela-
tionship between the displacements of the vehicle and the global bridge structure.
Through comparisons with analytical solutions and similar analyses of isolated
cases, the implementation and reliability of the VBI-program is validated.
Ca-
pabilities and use cases of the developed VBI-program are demonstrated in a case
analysis of the Storestrøm Bridge where a FE-model is provided by Rambøll. It
is demonstrated how passenger comfort and runability in terms of derailment and
overturning can be analyzed with the developed VBI-program.

Chapter 2
Bridge Structure
The VBI-program is designed to rely on input from an existing FE-software. The
requirements of the software is that spatial linear-elastic beam elements are used
to model the vehicle path of the bridge, and that the program can capture the
diﬀerent vibration modes of the structure. Interaction between the vehicle and the
bridge takes place in the vehicle path, so only the node coordinates of this path are
needed as input for the VBI-program. The full FE-model of the bridge can consist
of additional substructures like pylons, piers and columns, but these are not directly
interacting with the vehicle.
To get a complete and realistic analysis of the vehicle-bridge interaction, the ex-
tracted vehicle-interacting part of the bridge must still retain the same dynamic
behaviour as the full FE-model. This is done by using a method called truncated
structure development by modal expansion, described by Groes in [1].
The method develops the bridge structure based on three input parameters; node
coordinate array X푖푛of the path of the vehicle motion, mass normalized mode-
shape array S푖푛describing the same nodes as the coordinate array, and a vector
f containing the natural frequencies of each mode-shape. The method is brieﬂy
introduced in this chapter, and the reader is referred to [1] for the full derivation of
the concept.

errors, degradation of rail and supporting materials, settlements and combinations
of these [4].

gauge irregularities, which from here on will be referred to as vertical and horizontal

5.3
Generation of rail irregularities
Rail irregularities can be numerically generated in a number of ways depending on
the desired complexity and characteristics.
The irregularity records can also be
taken from real world measurements if these are available. Rail irregularities are
generally considered a stochastic stationary process with a mean of zero. Irregular-
ities are commonly generated from a power spectral density (PSD) function, since
the wheel-track interaction depends on both the geometrical amplitudes and the
wavelengths of the irregularities [11]. Spectra properties are commonly found in
standards for various grades of irregularities depending on the assumed or known
track quality [4].
Since the aim of this report is primarily to implement rail irregularities in the theory
and VBI program, it is chosen to focus on three relatively simple methods of rail
irregularity generation.

Irregularity records can be generated as a set of random normal distributed numbers
with a mean of zero. Maximum vertical and horizontal deviations are determined in
accordance with [12] and are set to 4.63 mm and 4.25 mm, respectively. Examples
of random normal distributed irregularity records are illustrated in Figure 5.3.

Chapter 6
Derailment and Overturning
Derailment is described as when the wheels of a train loses contact with the rails
it is running on. Train derailment can be caused by errors and failure of the rail,
sleeper and ballast components, or by the eﬀects of external excitation of the train
[14] [15]. External causes include earthquakes and wind loading in general, and ship
impact on the bridge and wind gusts on the train from pylon passing for long-span
bridges speciﬁcally.
Since the ﬁrst high-speed railway opened in Japan in 1964 with train speeds between
210-230 km/h, new railways have been developed around the world with train speeds
up to 350 km/h with increasing passenger volume [14] [15]. The running safety of
trains is mainly concerned with avoiding derailment, and have become progressively
important since the consequences can lead to both human and materialistic losses.
The assessment of derailment can be based on a number of theoretical models,
describing diﬀerent mechanisms of loss of wheel-rail contact like ﬂange climbing,
overturning and wheel jumping. In this report the main concern is ﬂange climbing
derailment, further referred to as just derailment, and overturning. An illustration
of the mechanics behind derailment and overturning is shown in Figure 6.1.

The most commonly used assessment method of derailment is the ratio between
the horizontal and vertical wheel-rail contact forces, referred to as the L/V limit

forces for a single wheel, but has later been adapted and developed to be used on

it is chosen to adapt the criteria given in the SSB Design Basis in [2], which is a

conscious decision should be made when performing the derailment analysis.

Vehicle-bridge interaction validation
With the static response and implementation of the 13 degree of freedom vehi-

Because of the complexity of the vehicle model and its dynamic characteristics
there are not a large variety of analytical solutions or similar cases to compare
the results of the VBI-program with. The chosen validation procedure is therefore
largely to compare the results from the current VBI-program to the VBI-program

VBI-program Evaluation and
Conclusion
Through the validations in Chapter 8, a high level of trustworthiness in the VBI-
program is generally established.
The analyses performed in the case study in
Chapter 9 demonstrate the main abilities of the program in terms of passenger
comfort, derailment and overturning, and also includes some comparisons to other
similar analyses. Limitations of the VBI-program from the choices and assumptions
made are discussed in this section, along with potential weaknesses and discrepancies
in the program. A general conclusion is made, along with thoughts on future work.
10.1
VBI-program limitations
10.1.1
Underlying limitations from previous VBI-program
Since the fundamental theory and general structure of the VBI-program is based
on the work done by Groes [1], some underlying limitations because of this are
present. This includes the fact that vehicle models with diﬀerent degrees of freedom
cannot be run in the same analysis, since the driver ﬁle and additional functions are
customized for each vehicle model. The 13 DOF model developed in this program
can therefore not be run in the same analysis as the 3 DOF model by Groes [1].
The parameters for vehicle velocity 푣is assumed constant for all vehicles in the
analysis, and eccentricity between rails and bridge center of gravity are chosen as
constant through time for simplicity. No coupling between individual vehicle wagons
is present, because of the assumption of constant vehicle velocity. Realistically all
independent train wagons should be coupled together, since each wagon is essentially
pulled by the wagon in front of it. The modelling of the bridge is based on the
method of truncated structure development by modal expansion from [1], which
excludes the possibility of placing loads directly in pylons, piers and cables. The
manual deﬁnition of the third node coordinates can also be regarded as a limitation
in the current VBI-program.

VBI-program Evaluation and Conclusion
10.1.2
Constant rail-wheel contact assumption
The construction of the interaction matrix, and thus the general vehicle-bridge
coupling procedure is based on the assumption that the wheel is always in contact
with the rail. Realistically separation between the wheel and the rail easily occur
during high-speed train passage [14], but since the contact forces are non-existent
at this moment there is no way of analyzing the possible derailment mechanism.
10.1.3
Rigid track structure
The track structure is regarded as completely rigid in the VBI-program, and is
essentially seen as a part of the bridge deck. Realistically the track structure is a
ﬂexible structure consisting of the rails, the sleepers separating the rails, and the
ballast foundation. The assumption of rigid tracks lead to a sharp transition from
the completely rigid oﬀ-bridge positions to the ﬂexible bridge structure. A result
of this is often seen as initial spikes in train response when it enters and leaves the
bridge. The response in these transition stages should therefore be viewed with
this in mind, and not be considered as a realistic response. Investigations done by
[14] show that neglecting ﬂexible track modelling can lead to overestimation of the
dynamic performance of the vehicle.
10.1.4
Discrepancies with DynFrame.m bridge models
In the validation of the VBI-program in Chapter 8, discrepancies are found when
comparing train passage on the bridge models from the DynFrame.m program to
similar analyses with the VBI-program by Groes [1]. It is seen that analysis with
the current VBI-program lead to approximately twice the mid-span deﬂections as
in the VBI-program by Groes, when running multiple trains. This error is only seen
in the bridge models SimpleBridge.mat and ContBridge.mat which are made with
the DynFrame.m-program presented in [1], and not in any of the models provided
by Rambøll. A solution to this is not successfully found, so the user of the VBI-
program should be aware of this ﬂaw. It is assumed that there might be an error
somewhere in the loading and bridge modelling, or in the implementation of the
multiple train expansion.

10.2 Conclusion
101
10.2
Conclusion
The aim of this thesis is to further develop and deliver a VBI-program capable
of capturing both vertical and horizontal dynamic interaction between vehicle and
bridge, in order to make it possible to analyze derailment and overturning. Addi-
tions to the former VBI-program by Groes [1] include implementation of a realistic
vehicle model with 13 degrees of freedom with additional rigid components, where
horizontal loading and interaction is possible. The additions also include implemen-
tation of rail irregularities in the VBI-program.
The 13 degree of freedom vehicle is modelled according to the realistic model in the
SSB Design Basis [2], consisting of four rigid coach- and bogie masses. Horizontal
degrees of freedom are included in all masses, and horizontal interaction points are
modelled in the wheelsets to allow for horizontal interaction. The vehicle model
is successfully assembled using the principle of rate or work, and is coupled to the
bridge model through the use of a newly developed position-dependent interaction
matrix covering both vertical and horizontal interaction. Through the use of the
interaction matrix the coupled system matrices are time-dependent and are there-
fore included in a modiﬁed Newmark time-integration algorithm where both system
matrices and the state vector is updated for each time step. The response of the
coupled system is successfully determined through the time integration.
The VBI-program is validated step by step starting with the vehicle model, and
ending with veriﬁcation of full vertical and horizontal vehicle-bridge interaction.
The dynamic characteristics of the vehicle model are investigated by determining
and validating natural frequencies and mode shapes.
The rail irregularities are
validated through investigations on various irregularity functions. The results are
seen to be reliable for all bridge models provided by Rambøll, but discrepancies are
found in validations with bridges produced by the DynFrame.m-program.
The case analysis of the Storestrøm Bridge demonstrate the key capabilities and
use cases of the VBI-program, which includes analysis of passenger comfort with
and without rail irregularities, and derailment and overturning analyses performed
for diﬀerent scenarios. Through comparisons with a speciﬁc ship-impact case by [5]
the response of the bridge is seen to be comparable, but a scenario of overturning
due to coinciding lateral bridge modes and vehicle roll modes is not successfully
replicated due to diﬀerences in the used FE-bridge models and vehicle models.
All the planned additions to the former version of the VBI-program by Groes [1]
are implemented and veriﬁed, along with additional improvements in the assembly
of the interaction matrix and in the implementation of multiple train extensions.
The result and conclusion of this thesis is a well-documented VBI-program ready
to be delivered to Rambøll, which can be used to assess derailment and overturning
for high-speed trains on long-span bridges.

10.3
Future work
Suggestions for future work to be done to improve the VBI-program in its current
state are discussed in this last section.
10.3.1
Rail irregularities
Since the aim of this report is to implement rail irregularities in the VBI-program,
the generation of the irregularity records is not investigated in great detail. A sug-
gestion for future work is therefore to investigate how realistic rail irregularities can
be generated. The SSB Design Basis speciﬁes that irregularities should be gener-
ated based on spectra given in the standard DS/EN ISO 3095. Other suggestions is
various methods of ﬁltering the random generated irregularities so that resonance
can be investigated in the train for certain ranges of train velocity.
The addition of rail irregularities are generated in the program as a data point for
each time step of the analysis. An improvement of this could be to generate a record
for the actual length of the bridge in the x-axis.
10.3.2
Investigation of resonance phenomenons
In the ship impact analysis it is seen that the resonance phenomenon where the
train roll mode coincides with the lateral bridge mode is not replicated successfully.
Investigations show that this might be due to diﬀerent modelling of the vehicle,
which again leads to diﬀerent natural frequencies. A more in-depth investigation of
the vehicle model dynamics and especially the cases with resonance in both bridge
and train could be done in future work.
