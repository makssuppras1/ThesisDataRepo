Aeroacoustics: Computational Study of Noise Emission of Leading Edge Damaged Aerofoils
Abstract
This computational study has looked into the trailing edge emission of damaged airfoil, of four configurations
previously measured in an aeroacoustics wind tunnel PLCT. The four configurations include a clean case, used
as a baseline, and three LER, that would represent defects commonly occurring on wind turbine blades. The
CFD simulations were first validated by comparing the lift, drag, and noise level to those measured. The CFD
simulations have shown a great reliability. This was followed by a grid convergence study to ensure that the
results are from physical processes and not from the grid resolution. The simulations results were then processed
and presented by configuration, from its resultant noise level, to its BL characteristics. Some of the noise results
and characteristics were then further compared by the Reynold’s number, the angle of attack, the flow case
and/or the location, whenever possible and relevant. It was found that the trailing edge noise does not increase
by LER size or by location on the airfoil. It was shown however, that the noise does change by LER type,
more precisely continuous roughness elements such as the rough mesh, have shown a ∼5 dB increase compared
to the clean case, and compared to the step and stallstrip who have only shown a difference of ∼2 dB in the
lower frequencies. This was then explained by the behaviour of the BL over the roughness. The outcome of
the BL mechanisms were also shown to depend on other variables such as Re or the AoA varies when on a
smooth surface, to a surface with multiple roughness elements, to a surface with a single roughness element.
Greater noise level occurred from higher energy dissipation when the flow continuously interacted with roughness
elements. On the other hand, when the flow reattaches after a single roughness element, the noise level did not
show any difference. The frequency range of the noise, was shown to depend on the BL thickness which in turn,
was shown to be more sensitive to the roughness elements than to the Reynold’s number, increasing further
with the height of the roughness element. Limitations to the assumptions and conclusions made were provided,
along with other aspects to this projects that could have improved the interpretation of the results.

Introduction
Airfoils, whether they are part of a propeller, aircraft wing or a wind turbine blade, are exposed to an aggressive
environment quite early on in their operation. This entails that slits, icing, erosion, dents, and dust accumu-
lation on the blades’ surface are inevitable, in particular on the leading edge. Due to the sensitive nature of
aerodynamics, any change of mass and/or geometry of the airfoil insinuates a change in performance; and while
intuitively thinking that would be for the worse, the actual outcome and prediction are far more complex as
there are other decisive parameters related to the surrounding environment that come into play such as the local
angle of attack, the Reynold’s number, and the rotational speed of the rotor among others. Investigating how
the aerodynamic performance of an airfoil changes when damaged is particularly of interest when this damage
translates to a safety hazard or a production loss. This can be observed in wind turbines, for which it was
reported (4) that accumulated insect debris around leading edge of the blades likely has caused an approximate
power loss of 25% on wind turbines in California. A study (5) conducted on a 300 kW wind turbine in a dusty
region in Egypt found that a power loss of about 50% in nine months can occur. Aside from energy production
losses, another challenge the wind energy development sector as whole faces is public perception, precisely the
visual and acoustics factors of wind turbine, deemed annoying and disagreeable. A survey conducted in the
U.S.A in 2018 estimates that 62% of the individuals consider wind turbine noise bothersome, and that for each
1 dB increase, the odds of moving to a higher degree of annoyance increases by 9% (6). The noise emitted by
wind turbines can be divided into mechanical noise and aerodynamic noise. Mechanical noise, from machinery
components, was only an issue for older and smaller wind turbines and has been successfully managed since.
On the other hand, aerodynamic noise still is a challenge in the industry. Also because, unlike mechanical
noise, aerodynamic noise is proportional to the dimensions of the wind turbine (3), and in the last 10 years,
the average wind turbine rotor diameter has increased by 50% (7). Hence the necessity of understanding the
generation of aerodynamic noise. Building on recent pilot studies and measurement campaigns [(8), (9)] about
the increase in noise level and performance loss due to leading edge roughness, this project is a computational
study focused on the identification of the physical mechanisms in the boundary layer of the airfoil responsible for
the noise emission. It is first aimed to correlate the theory behind the trailing edge noise, to the BL mechanisms
over rough surfaces. Following the measurements’ configurations, the CFD simulations will be validated, by the
noise level and the lift and drag coefficients. A grid convergence study is also conducted to ensure the reliability
of the simulation results. Lastly, the simulation results will be looked into by noise, type of roughness and BL
characteristics.

is larger than the airfoil chord, the airfoil is under a global loading and the wavelength of the radiated sound is
larger than the airfoil, which means the sound is in the lower frequency range and varies with M 6. Following
the Kolmogorov energy cascade, these eddies will eventually dissipate into smaller and smaller eddies, up until
the the Kolmogorov scale where viscous forces dominate and dissipate the kinetic energy kt into thermal heat.
In the case of these smaller eddies, their size is significantly smaller than the dimensions of the airfoil, so they
induce only local pressure fluctuations which results in a higher frequency noise varying with M 5 [(28), (3)].

wavelength to be smaller than the surface for an acoustically non-compact source. Howe’s model determines the
far-field acoustic spectrum in terms of the surface pressure spectrum based on a single surface pressure wave.

2.2
Turbulent Flow over Rough Surfaces
A turbulent BL consists of an inner layer and an outer layer, the former is linked to the region of turbulent
flow that is affected by viscosity shear force and local flow parameters, whereas the outer layer is linked to the
turbulent region affected by the wall geometry and is expected to be up to 80% of the total BL thickness δ.
The inner later of the BL can be further divided into the viscous sublayer, the buffer layer, and the logarithmic
layer (2). As shown in the sketch in Figure 2, over a smooth surface, a laminar flow forms along the full length
of the surface, representing the viscous sublayer. In contrast, over a rough surface, the roughness increases flow
instability close to the surface, leading to localised turbulence disrupting the viscous sublayer, generating the
roughness sublayer - the roughness counteract of the viscous sublayer. According to Townsend (30), the inner
layer is responsible of the momentum transfer while the outer layer is not, with no interaction between the two
layers. This observation has later on led to the Townsend wall similarity hypothesis, stating that outside the
viscous sublayer, the turbulent motion in a BL at high Re number is independent of surface conditions and
viscosity, and thus the outer layer of BL is unaffected by the roughness (2).
Figure 2: Sketch of turbulent BL layers interaction with roughness

• Smooth regime, where the roughness elements are small and entirely submerged in the viscous sublayer, so
the friction and drag coefficient are unaffected.
• Transitional rough regime, where both the Reynolds number and roughness elements influence the friction
and drag coefficients.
• Fully rough regime, where roughness elements intrude the viscous sublayer, destroying it by the turbulence

of the viscous effects (Re) (32).
In turn, the influence of roughness, and thus disruption of the viscous sublayer, can be observed through the
following effects (2):
• Induced pressure drop
• Altered laminar to turbulent transition
• Secondary flow motions
• Improved flow mixing and heat transfer
This project will mainly touch on the induced pressure drop, and laminar-turbulent transition.
Secondary
motions from roughness are deemed relevant for moving surfaces (WT blades) in addition to the computations
being two-dimensional. As for the improved heat transfer, it is beyond the scope of this project. Pressure drop
occurs in turbulent flows over rough surfaces due to the drag and blockage effects created by the roughness
elements. Aside from smooth regimes, in which roughness elements are too small compared to the viscous
sublayer, it has been observed that the rougher the surface, the greater the friction and in turn, the greater the
pressure loss (31). The roughness of a surface also affects the flow transition from laminar to turbulent, as shown
in Figure 3 (1). When no disturbance is introduced to the flow, the attached BL transitions naturally from
laminar to turbulent due to Tollmien-Schlichting (TS) 2D waves, which are the primary instability, then followed
by the secondary 3D waves, forming vortices. The latter will eventually break down to smaller structures in the
BL, generating turbulence. On the other hand, when localised perturbation is introduced, such as in the case
of roughness elements, turbulent spots form, propagate laterally and grow longitudinally overtaking the main
turbulent region (33). An earlier transition means the boundary layer starts growing thicker earlier and has less
momentum to overcome the adverse pressure gradient. As a result, the separation point moves upstream too
giving rise to increased drag (2).

As stated previously, roughness is not an exclusively accidental and negative feature of a surface. Phenomena
such as heat transfer and momentum transfer are highly dependent on the formation of the BL over the
surface and so it can also be intentional to reduce drag or improve heat transfer, and it rather depends on the
application of the flow and the desired outcome. This is due to the BL behaving and growing differently between
the roughness elements. This behaviour is, in turn, dependent on the geometry of the roughness elements, their
dimensions, turbulence intensity, and the flow viscosity. Based on the latter, three types of two-dimensional
roughness were established: the d-type roughness, the intermediate roughness type and the k-type roughness.
The d-type roughness describe tightly packed ribs or grooves, while widely spaced ribs are described by the
k-type roughness (34). Their designation of "d" or "k" refer to the length scale order. A full distinction between
the roughness types has not yet been agreed on, some researchers suggested that the difference relies on the
state of the vortex shedding (35), others on the roughness density (36) and others on the relative magnitude of
the frictional and pressure drag caused by the roughness type (37). Onto their influence on the flow, the d-type
roughness can reduce the drag by trapping pockets of fluid between the roughness elements if narrow enough.
Stable eddies occupy the cavity isolating the outer flow from roughness [(38), (36)]. However, this would still
depend on other properties, such as the airfoil, flow characteristics..etc. In contrast, the k-type roughness allows
the flow to reattach before it encounters the next roughness element continuously, imposing additional drag.
However, these distinguished roughness types and corresponding behaviour still can not fully and describe a
rough surface and enough room should be given to uncertainty and unexpected behaviour in the results (2).

2.2.1
Equivalent Sand-grain Roughness
A damaged surface could either mean a texture change, a shape change, or both. Modelling wise, a chip or icing
would be included by changing the airfoil geometry. Irregular and non-uniform damage such as dust or debris
has no specific physical shape and is rather a surface texture. That is modelled following the equivalent sand-
grain roughness approach. Considering the roughness element height is not the only parameter to describe a
rough texture, Schlichting (39) introduced the concept of a equivalent sand-grain roughness height ks to include
the roughness element shape and density and that yields the same drag increase in the fully rough regime (40).
It is a theoretical height, not physical, that relates any rough surface to a spherical sand grain size as shown
in Figure 5 (2). Therefore, in the CFD simulations, when roughness is "applied", that is not on the wall but
rather on the boundary conditions at the still smooth wall.
Figure 5: Sketch of the sand-grain roughness, with spherical elements of height k equal to the equivalent sand-
grain roughness height ks and a more realistic roughness with randomly distributed elements of different shapes,
described by a different theoretical height of ks (2).
2.2.2
SST k −ω Turbulence Model
The source term in equation 7 represents turbulence, therefore its modeling is critical to describe acoustics.
The CFD RANS turbulence simulations are based on the Shear Stress Transport model, with an extension to
include wall roughness. The SST k −ω is a two equation linear eddy-viscosity model meaning that it includes
two transport equations to model turbulent flow, accounting for the transport and the dissipation of turbulent
energy (41).
As its name indicates, the transported variables are the turbulent kinetic energy kt, and the
turbulence dissipation rate ω, which also determines the length scale of turbulence L. This model is valid
through the viscous sublayer to the wall, for adverse pressure gradients and separating flow. The extension
aims to improve turbulence in the wall region caused by roughness elements by modifying kt and ω right at the

wall (40). Further corrections of the model allow the simulation of flows over rough surfaces at the same grid
resolution as for smooth walls without modifying the SST k −ω model, and are based on Wilcox roughness
modification, see (42) for more details.
2.3
Previous Work
2.3.1
PLCT Wind Tunnel Measurements
Recent 2D noise measurements (8) were conducted in the Poul La Cour Wind Tunnel on a NACA 633-418 with
stall strips of three heights (1, 2 and 3 mm) at the LE to simulate roughness. Stall strips of all heights have led
to a significant increase in the noise emission. However, a further increase in the roughness height did not show
a further increase in the noise level. For the same Reynold’s number of 5·106, it was found that at AoA = - 0.5°,
the clean airfoil generates less noise in the low frequency range, from 400 Hz to 2000 Hz, than airfoils with stall
strips. In contrast, when the AoA was increased to 5.2°, the clean airfoil (smooth surface) has shown higher
noise levels from 1000 Hz and higher. When the AoA was further increased to 8.1°, the clean airfoil was also
noisier in the low frequency range of 400 to 1000 Hz. Dr.Fischer has explained this to be due to NACA633-418
having a design AoA of ∼8°, which means separation is delayed and it is operating at a higher lift, assuming a
positive correlation between the lift and noise emissions at positive AoA. These measurements have also shown
that vortex generators on airfoil with LER led to a noise reduction in the low frequency range, and a noise
increase in the high frequency range. This is an advantageous feature since high frequency noise is more easily
attenuated.
2.3.2
CFD simulations
Through DTU Wind Energy in-house solver EllipSys2D, it was possible to simulate several types of roughness
at different locations on the LE on the same airfoil NACA633-418, for both transitional and turbulent BL case
scenarios (9). These CFD noise results were also compared to measurements in the Poul La Cour Wind Tunnel
for validation, when the LER were equivalent to each other. In a general manner, LER configurations have led
to a low frequency noise increase of up to 5 dB. While at first impression a turbulent BL would be expected to
have a higher noise level than a transitional BL, all types of LER at all the Reynold’s numbers simulated, have
shown that it actually was the transitional BL flow case that is noisier, for up to a 3 dB difference. Noise has
also increased with the roughness height for most defects, but not all. Another important conclusion from this
report is the non-linearity of the results with the Reynolds numbers simulated (from 3·106 to 10·106). Based on
these results, one can not assume that the higher the Reynold’s number, the greater the noise, or performance
loss for any defect. For instance, when a flat patch on the LE was simulated, the greatest performance drop
and noise increase was observed to occur at a Reynold’s number of 3 · 106 for a transitional BL flow case. Also
worth noting that for the same defect on a turbulent flow case did not submit the same pattern. Regarding the
correlation between the lift and the noise emission, it was seen that the greater the performance loss, insinuating
a decrease in lift,increase of drag, or both, the greater the TEN emitted. This result is in disparity with the
result of the clean airfoil measurements at AoA = 8.1°, which has shown the latter to be noisier (8), indicating
that there might be specific conditions to achieve a positive correlation between the lift and the noise.

3.1
EllipSys2D
The results were computed using the DTU in-house incompressible Navier-Stokes flow solver Ellipsys2D. The
2D polar generator is defined within a python class "E2dpolar". The latter relies on PyEllipsys for setting
up and running the CFD simulations. E2dpolar is the basis for all the methods, including that of the volume
mesher HypGrid2D and that of the flow solver itself EllipSys2D, which is based on the Reynolds Averaged
Navier-Stokes (RANS) equations. More details can be found on (43). Figure 6 shows a simplified sketch of the
main script defining the E2dpolar class "building blocks", with the blue blocks representing the user inputs.

As shown in the sketch, the solver gives enough flexibility to the user to compute CFD simulations for different
airfoils in all kind of scenarios; clean, damaged, with or without a rough surface in flow conditions set by the
user. The grid resolution and method of data extraction are also adjustable. These will be discussed in more
details in Section 3.2. For the flow cases, three options are available; transitional case, turbulent case and fully
turbulent for roughness. The transitional BL flow case is solved with the eN stability model (9). The turbulent
BL flow case is based on the SST k −ω model described in Section 2.2.2. As its name indicates, the EllipSys2D
solver computes in two dimensions. Two-dimensional simulations have been partly validated by Dr.Kruse (44),
from wind tunnel measurement results of an airfoil with an extruded strip on its leading edge. The solver also
assumes an incompressible flow, this might seem counter-intuitive for an acoustics study, however, not only is
the trailing edge noise obtained in a post-processing stage from the BL properties, but the flow is limited to M
< 0.3, which validates the assumption (45).
3.1.1
Trailing Edge Noise
Two different noise models are included in the solver: Howe model and Amiet model (Section 2.1.2). Amiet
is also used in the simulations. The noise sources are the surface pressure fields on either sides, SS and PS,
obtained from the BL profiles. Note that the trailing edge from the SS and PS are treated as uncorrelated
noise sources. This is merely an assumption to simplify the model, and it might not be the case in practice.
In this project, noise data was extracted at x/C ≃0.97 from each side, which is the furthest point on the TE
with stable data from the BL for the solver. The results are taken from 100 Hz, even lower frequencies would
make the airfoil an acoustically compact source (Helmholtz number ka < 1), due to the size of the wavelength
compared to the chord length, as mentioned previously in Section 2.1.2. Following the measurement setup (8),
the observer location was specified to be at 2.8 m above the TE, same as the microphone array.
3.2

One of the building blocks of the E2dpolar class, is the grid generation as shown in Figure 6, which is based
on the volume mesher method HypGrid2D. The latter generates a volume grid from a surface mesh based on
profile coordinates. It includes inputs such as: the mesh type, the number of points along the surface ni and
normal to the profile nj, both being multiples of 32 and of the grid block size, the starting grid level varying
from the coarsest 4 to the finest 1, the grid level at which polars are extracted, the domain height from profile
to the outer boundaries, the first cell height on profile surface, the grid normal distribution defining the mesh
growth, the stretching function, dissipation factor and wake contraction (43).

it was necessary to first assure they stem from an appropriate grid resolution. By appropriate, it is meant that
the results are due to the flow conditions and physics used and not due to the resolution and within a reasonable
computational time. While an infinite number of points and an infinitely small first cell height would be ideal,
it is not computationally realistic. On the other hand, very few points and large cell height would either not
compute the results at all, or lead to wrong results (46). The convergence study was conducted by gradually

4
Results and Discussion
This section includes both results, those of the simulations validation, and of the LER simulations, following the method-
ology and inputs given in the previous section. All data obtained from the solver is processed and plotted separately on
Python. For the same quantity, the plots might have different scales. All the noise results given are of the unintegrated
and unweighted SPL. Unless specified otherwise, the noise is that of both sides SS and PS. Note that, "noise", and
"trailing edge noise (TEN)" are used interchangeably here, and noise does not refer to other noise sources than that of
the TE. Similarly for "combined noise", "total noise", "SS/PS contribution". Lastly, non-dimensionalized quantities are
represented by + (y+ here refers to the height normalized by the BL thickness δ, and not that of the law of the wall).
4.1
CFD Simulations Validation by Wind Tunnel Measurements
Prior to looking into the simulation outputs, the simulations first needed to be validated. This was done by comparing
both the noise level and the aerodynamic coefficients to those of the measurements done in the wind tunnel on the same
airfoil. The PLCT Kevlar walls wind tunnel measurements results (8) for the noise level dB from 500 Hz to 5000 Hz, and
the aerodynamic coefficients from measurements done with hard walls. Those two quantities were taken from different
measurement campaigns only because it was reported that some of the pressure taps were covered during the acoustic
measurements making the aerodynamic coefficients unreliable, particularly the drag coefficient CD. The measurement
results were also used to adjust the LER and the defects to match the results of the measurements as much as possible.
For example, while the plain paper measurement is simulated by a step, it is unsure at this stage which height would
give most similar results, nor whether the step should be smooth or with roughness. That is also the case for the mesh
in Figure ??, while technically there is a small step at the mesh’s end, it is unsure at this stage whether it should be
simulated solely as roughness or a step with a rough layer. Lastly, it is important to note limiting factors from either
sides, such as the uncertainty of the AoA, the inflow velocity varying throughout the measurements, the theoretical split
of the contribution of the SS and PS noise to the overall TEN noise..etc; and so it is not aimed to perfectly match them,
nor is that feasible at this stage. Some measurement results are also not available for the full frequency range, hence
there will be some gaps or early cut offs in some of the plots.

Mesh configuration
The effect of two different roughness densities was measured using two different meshes (Figure 12), it was reported they
were of similar height, and it is aimed to find the most suitable for each, by comparing four different roughness heights
hr, and two cases where a step, covered by a roughness layer, was added to the LE, each of a different height hs. Here,
height hr refers to the equivalent sand-grain roughness height that covers the roughness shape and density too, denoted
by ks in Section 2.2.1. The results are given in Figure 18. They show that a relatively large sand grain roughness height
is required to obtain results close to those of the measurements. The finer mesh resulted in higher drag than the rough
mesh in the measurements above AoA 7.5°, see Figure 18 (b), in contrast with the simulations where the higher the
roughness, the higher the drag. This can be assumed to be due to the greater elements density, and thus more blockage
effect between elements causing drag. This assumption however insinuates that at high AoA, more of the flow is pulled
towards the mesh of high density, than that of low density. Initially, it was aimed to assign a low roughness height hr
to simulate the fine denser mesh, and a greater height to simulate the rough and less dense mesh. However, based on
these results, relying on the roughness height hr to mirror those meshes’ different densities, might not have been the
best approach. Nonetheless, the simulated results without the step, are still deemed reliable as they follow the trend of
the measurements very closely which is the aim of this validation, see Figure 33 (c). Note that the drag is lower and lift
is higher in the presence of a step of 2 mm with a roughness layer, than of roughness alone of the same height 2 mm (see
light and dark green curves). One hypothesis for that is the flow being less affected by the vertical rough surface over
the step and reattaching to the surface right after the step, compared to the roughness layer where the friction by the
rough elements is continuous. However, results showed otherwise for the rough step of 5 mm and hr = 0.8 mm which
has observed most loss, so that is likely not a correct hypothesis, or that a separation bubble occured from the higher
step. The roughness elements over the step (hs = 2mm) might also have acted like vortex generators as well, slightly
improving the drag and friction first caused by the step. It might have been smarter to simulate smaller steps, with
higher roughness instead.

4.3
General Comments on the Influence of LER
From the results at all four configurations, there are few common observations. Firstly, while intuitively "greater" damage
is thought to increase the noise, the results have consistently shown that it does not. Meaning the noise level is not linear
to the extent of the damage, for instance a chip from delamination of height 6 mm would show similar results to that of
2 mm. This was also noticed in the acoustics measurements (9). However, greater defects did show to shift the noise to
the lower frequencies. Though the shift was not as significant as when the LER is introduced to the clean case, there is
no real "clean" case in practice. Even if lower frequencies are the hardest to attenuate, "greater" damage might still be
unproblematic as the A-weighting emphasizes the higher frequencies. Furthermore, even if the damage extends further
downstream, closer to the TE, the simulations have shown that the TE noise still does not increase, nor does it experience
a frequency shift. This would rather depend on the AoA or perhaps the TE noise is insensitive to the location of the LER.
Regarding the BL mechanisms responsible for the noise, the interaction of the BL characteristics, and their dependency
on other variables such as Re or the AoA varies when on a smooth surface, to a surface with multiple roughness elements,
to a surface with a single roughness element. Over a smooth surface, the turbulent BL ends up with a lower noise level
due to its higher kinetic energy, allowing momentum transfer towards the wall and delaying separation (which causes an
increase in the pressure drag, and so is the worst case scenario for noise.) further than when transitional. In contrast,
over a rough surface, this same mechanism leads to an increase of friction, followed by a pressure drop due to more
energy dissipation and thus more noise. Furthermore, greater Re insinuates a thinner BL from the inertia and viscous
forces processes, which was observed in the smooth case. Over a rough surface however, Re’s influence is "overturned"
by how the BL grows due to the additional viscous forces from the roughness elements. Meaning that the influence of
the LER on the BL thickness is greater than that of the Re. This also insinuates that the frequency range of the noise
emitted is more sensitive to the type of surface. Another difference in the BL mechanism observed between continuous
roughness element and an individual one, is that the flow can reattach after a step or stallstrip, which was observed to
increase kt, and so even in the presence of a defect, granted smooth defect, the noise level does not increase. Lastly, it
was difficult to draw a consistent conclusion on how the BL mechanisms change when turbulent and when transitional
over a rough surface, as that is further dependent on the AoA.
4.4
Limitations
The approach taken and the results of this study are based on a number of assumptions that might challenge their
interpretation if found to be wrong or missing conditions to be valid. There are also aspects to this project that were
aimed to be touched on, but were not due to time constraints. Including simulating higher Re, and other airfoils of
different thickness. There are obviously more and other comparisons and validations to make to gain full confidence in
the conclusions and assumptions made, however the comparison pairs are endless.
There are also aspects that were neglected, and it is unknown how telling their results would be. For instance, the
momentum thickness and shape factor were briefly seen for the clean configuration. Negative AoA’s were neglected and
the results on PS were crudely assumed to be the same as those of SS on the same AoA. It was also aimed to plot CP
and CF of different configurations against each other.
There were other more general limitations, such as the assumption of the simulated 2D LER having the same result as that
of 3D. While Dr.Kruse (44) has indeed found that 2D simulations suffice, this conclusion was based on the measurements
of one type of defect. It is unknown whether that would apply for other airfoils and/or defects of different geometries.
Following this same train of thought, these results are also limited in terms of how they were based on an individual
defect with a rough surface.
It is unknown how much would differ, and to which extent, if there were consecutive
different defects along the surface for instance. Realistically, it is expected to have different three-dimensional defects in
all directions and with different roughness height along a WT blade. Nonetheless, this does not invalidate this study’s
approach or assumption, but rather requires a further study or extension to see how and if the behavior changes when
multiple defects are present. Lastly, the inputs and properties chosen in this project are widely based on current wind

are rapidly growing, the flow can no longer be assumed to be incompressible, it is unknown to what extent these results
would change.

5
Conclusion
This computational study has looked into the trailing edge emission of damaged airfoil, of four configurations previously
measured in an aeroacoustics wind tunnel PLCT: - Clean case, with the airfoil surface assumed to be smooth, -mesh,
with two meshes of different densities over the LE, -plain paper, simulated with a step and -stallstrip, which includes
two sub-configurations, one of a stallstrip changing height and the other of the stallstrip changing location. The CFD
simulations were first validated by comparing the lift, drag, and noise level to those measured. The CFD simulations have
shown a great reliability. In most cases, the simulated results overestimated the noise at high frequencies and high AoA.
Which is not necessarily a negative feature for noise prediction in practice. This was followed by a grid convergence study
to ensure that the results are from physical processes and not from the grid resolution. The simulations results were
then processed and presented by configuration, starting from its resultant noise level, to its BL characteristics, including
the velocity profiles, the pressure distribution, the friction coefficient, the displacement thickness, the length scale, the
kinetic energy and finally the dissipation rate. Some of the noise results and characteristics were then further compared
by the Reynold’s number, the angle of attack, the flow case and/or the location, whenever possible and relevant. It was
found that the trailing edge noise does not increase by LER size or by location on the airfoil. It was shown however,
that the noise does change by LER type, more precisely continuous roughness elements such as the rough mesh, have
shown a ∼5 dB increase compared to the clean case, and compared to the step and stallstrip who have only shown a
difference of ∼2 dB in the lower frequencies.
The limitations of the results and their interpretation, whether based on the research available, the computations, or the
understanding, was also provided.
