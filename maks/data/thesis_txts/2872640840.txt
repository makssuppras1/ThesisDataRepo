Abstract
Traditional methods of bathymetry surveying are both expensive and time-consuming. As a consequence, the
use of publicly available remote sensing data for bathymetry detection has become of increasing interest. Geo-
Danmark provides yearly orthophotos covering all of Denmark, including the coastal areas, which offers a
unique and potentially valuable data source for Remote-Derived Bathymetry (RDB). In this master’s thesis, an
algorithm was developed that utilizes the spectral bands from the orthophotos for RDB and Sandbar Crest De-
tection (SCD) based on the Ratio Band Method (RBM). The algorithm is developed using orthophotos from
2023 from a coastal area north of the town of Ballen on the east coast of the island of Samsø. The RDB opera-
tion was performed with and without bottom distinction and resulted in a Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) of
0.28 m and 1.13 m, respectively. The SCD operation was able to detect the sandbar crest location with a mean
absolute error of 3.67 m and a correlation with measured data of R2 = 0.95. When analyzing the sandbar crest
migration at Samsø from 2015-2024, minimal movement was observed and with no correlation to the yearly
wave conditions. In addition to the algorithm, this thesis delivers a proof of concept for using the NASA Ice,
Cloud, and land Elevation Satellite 2 (ICESat-2) LIDAR data as an alternative method for bathymetry detection.
This method shows promising results; however, the sparse data availability presents a challenge for practical
application.
An artificial reef has been built at Samsø partly with the purpose of acting as a submerged breakwater to
mitigate coastal erosion. Prior to construction, MIKE 21 Spectral Wave (SW) model simulations were included
in a MIKE 21/3 coupled model to predict the shoreline response of the reef. In this master’s thesis, an analysis of
the wave transmission across the reef was conducted to evaluate and compare the SW model and the MIKE 21/3
Wave Model for Boussinesq Wave simulations (BW model). For the BW model, the simulated wave transmission
aligned with results from literature, while the SW model allowed for too much wave transmission across the
reef. The SW model was calibrated by reducing the wave breaking parameter, γ, from 0.8 to 0.5 across the
reef. Implementing the calibrated SW model resulted in an increase in accretion of 34-39% behind the reef
when simulating constant wave conditions for 2 months. Additionally, an increase in erosion of 21-29% was
observed. Based on the results, it is concluded that the artificial reef will produce a larger shoreline response
than previously predicted.

Introduction
The coastal zone is highly dynamic due to natural processes, anthropogenic activity, and climate change (New-
ton and Weichselgartner, 2014). Accurate and reliable information on the coastal bathymetry and morpholog-
ical changes is important for a wide array of applications, including coastal management and coastal erosion
monitoring. Current methods of bathymetry measurements conducted in the field are both time-consuming
and expensive (Kutser et al., 2020). Using remote sensing data for Remote-Derived Bathymetry (RDB) has the
potential to provide both cheaper and quicker results with the most common data type being satellite images
(Stumpf et al., 2003; Lyzenga et al., 2006; Ma et al., 2014; Figliomeni and Parente, 2023; Amini and Kakroodi,
2024). Satellite images are widely available but have a limitation in the spatial resolution which is often > 10
m. More recently, using aircraft and Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV) remote sensing orthophotos have shown
promising results (Sun et al., 2019; Rossi et al., 2020; Alevizos et al., 2022; Apicella et al., 2023). These images
have a higher resolution, which provides the potential for deriving the bathymetry more accurately. The issue
with these types of images are the availability as they generally need to be acquired for the specific project.
conveniently, yearly orthophotos from aircraft imagery covering all of Denmark are published by GeoDanmark
and an obvious source to use for RDB.
In this thesis, the Area of Interest (AOI) is a beach on the eastern side of Samsø, north of the town of Ballen
where an artificial reef was constructed in the spring of 2025, as part of the BARREEF-project (Christensen and
Larsen, 2023). In the preliminary study of the site, two reports have been published with extensive analysis
of the site conditions and predicted shoreline response of the reef (Christensen and Larsen, 2023; Larsen and
Christensen, 2023). In the shoreline response model, the wave conditions were simulated using a MIKE 21
Spectral Wave Model (SW model), which is a computationally effective model for long term simulations. Over
the coming years, coastal monitoring will be necessary to ensure that the artificial reef has the expected effect.
Performing RDB based on orthophotos from GeoDanmark would be a cheap and effective alternative to a field
measurements based monitoring program.
Project aim and structure
The aim of this thesis is to explore the current methods for bathymetry detection using publicly available data
such as orthophotos from GeoDanmark. During the initial research phase, it was found that RDB and Sandbar
Crest Detection (SCD) could be integrated using similar techniques. As a result, the goal became to develop
a first-version algorithm for RDB and SCD using orthophotos from GeoDanmark. Additionally, while working
with the data from the AOI it became of interest to perform a calibration of the SW model to improve the
simulation of wave transmission across the reef. The report is divided into two distinct parts. The first part
provides a description of the site conditions and the calibration of the SW model. The calibrated SW model is
then implemented and the resulting shoreline response is compared to the uncalibrated model. The second part
of the report describes the development of the algorithm. This is done based on orthophotos from GeoDanmark
of the AOI, and validated using the field measurements. An analysis of the sandbar crest migration from 2015-
2024 is made to investigate the morphological changes prior to the construction of the reef. Additionally, a proof
of concept for using the NASA Ice, Cloud, and land Elevation Satellite 2 (ICESat-2) LIDAR data for bathymetry
detection is presented in this section.

Site description and wave conditions
The AOI is a beach on the east coast of Samsø north of the town of Ballen. Figure 2.1 shows a Google Earth
image of Samsø with the beach marked along with a close-up of the AOI. The artificial reef was build in April
2025 with the purpose of increasing biodiversity and to function as a submerged breakwater to mitigate erosion
of the shoreline. This thesis focuses only on the latter of the two objectives. Prior to building the reef analysis
of the site conditions, wave climate, and effects of the reef on the adjacent shoreline were conducted. An
extensive description of the site conditions for the AOI is given by Christensen and Larsen (2023). In the report,
the evaluation of historic coastal development and protection measures is described. Additionally, a suggestion
for the placement and design of the submerged reef is made based on MIKE 21/3 coupled model simulations
as described by Larsen and Christensen (2023). The most relevant information on the site conditions from
Christensen and Larsen (2023) and Larsen and Christensen (2023) will briefly be presented below. Furthermore,
the wave conditions and the corresponding sediment transport of the AOI prior to building the reef will be
assessed.
2.1
Site description
The site is mainly sandy with areas of rocks or vegetation in patches close to shore which begins to dominate
100-200 m offshore. There is a natural headland on the beach just south of the suggested position of the reef.
This has been subjected to erosion which is estimated to occur at a rate of 5-10 cm per year (Christensen and
Larsen, 2023). The shoreline north and south of the headland is stable. The goal of implementing the reef is to
stabilize the headland and stop further erosion. There are some pre-established groynes along the shore which
are noted to have a stabilizing effect on the shoreline. A nearshore tombolo-like feature stabilized by rocks is
present just north of the suggested location of the reef. This feature can potentially affect the shoreline response
of the reef by hindering sand transport from the north and by trapping sand from the south

2.3
Littoral drift simulations
Littoral drift simulations were made in the MIKE littoral drift (LITPACK) model to gain an initial understanding
of the expected longshore sediment transport. The LITPACK model simulates the longshore sediment transport
of a cross-shore profile given wave climate data. The model considers wave refraction, shoaling and wave break-
ing. Figure 2.4 shows the profile used in the simulation which was extracted from bathymetry measurements
of the AOI conducted on 28th and 29th of March 2023 as part of the BARREEF project.
Figure 2.4: Cross-shore profile of the AOI used for LITPACK model simulations. The profile is extracted from field measure-
ments from 28th and 29th of March 2023.

as Hrms = Hs/1.414, a spreading factor of 0.6, and a reference depth of 20 m was used. The spreading factor

Janssen waves breaking description was used with wave breaking parameters αb = 1 and γ = 0.8 (Battjes and
Janssen, 1978). The roughness height was constant at 0.004 m, the water level was constant at 0 m, and no

2.4
Sandbar migration
The wave conditions at the AOI will affect the sandbar migration as sandbar formation and movement are
primarily driven by wave-induced sediment transport (Fredsøe and Deigaard, 1992). Generally, a shoreward
sandbar migration is expected in calm conditions, whereas an outward migration is expected during storm
conditions. Sandbars are formed by opposing cross-shore directed sediment transport. There is an offshore
directed transport in the surf zone due to undertow and a weakly onshore directed transport in outside the surf
zone due to processes like streaming and wave skewness. As a result, there is a converging sediment transport
where the waves break, leading to a sandbar (Fredsøe and Deigaard, 1992). During constant wave conditions,
sandbars move outwards due to deposition of sediment on the back side causing the position of the breaker
point to move outwards (García-Maribona et al., 2022). When the wave conditions change between calm and
storm, the sandbars move towards where the waves break. In storm conditions, the waves break further out
and the sandbars follow. In calm conditions, the waves break close to shore making the sandbars move inwards
(Fredsøe and Deigaard, 1992).
A way of estimating in which direction the sandbars are expected to migrate is to correlate the sediment
transport to the surf similarity parameter, ξ. ξ is a measure of the wave steepness compared to the bed slope
which gives a measure of the breaker type of the wave. Additionally, ξ indicates the wave conditions where
lower values are observed during storms and higher values during calm periods. Equation 2 gives an expression
for calculating ξ for breaking conditions, ξb.

3
Hydrodynamics of breakwaters
A submerged breakwater is a less visually intrusive alternative to a traditional emerged breakwater. While it
is often a more desirable solution, the associated hydrodynamic processes are more complex, and the resulting
shoreline response is more difficult to predict (Villani et al., 2012). In the following section, the hydrodynamics
and expected shoreline response of shore-parallel emerged and submerged breakwaters are presented.
3.1
Hydrodynamics of an emerged breakwater
An emerged breakwater almost completely blocks the incoming waves to shelter the shoreline as a coastal
protection measure (Mangor et al., 2017). The incoming waves break at the breakwater whereas the waves in
the adjacent exposed area are undisturbed. This produces a larger set-up of the exposed waves at the shoreline
compared to the sheltered area. This gradient in the longshore set-up causes a water flow from the large
set-up in the exposed area towards the lower set-up in the sheltered area. This occurs on both sides of the
breakwater to form converging circulation currents towards the sheltered area. The circulation currents block
the longshore current which causes deposition of sediment behind the breakwater and erosion on either side.
Due to wave diffraction, the area behind the breakwater is not completely sheltered from waves, and diffraction
of the incident waves will cause additional deposition of sediment (USACE, 2008).
3.2
Hydrodynamics of a submerged breakwater
The shoreline response of a submerged breakwater depends on the distance to the shoreline. Ranasinghe et al.
(2006) found that erosion occurs when the submerged breakwater is too close to the shoreline due to a diverging
2-cell circulation pattern in the current behind the structure. Alternatively, accretion occurs when the submerged
breakwater is further away due to a nearshore converging 4-cell circulation current behind the structure. Both
situations are shown in Figure 3.1 and the hydrodynamics of the process is explained by Ranasinghe et al.
(2006) as follows. In the case of shore normal waves, no wave-induced longshore current is present and the
current pattern behind the submerged breakwater is solely due to the structure. When the waves break over
the submerged breakwater, it creates wave set-up and an onshore directed current balancing the gradient in
radiation stress. In the exposed area, the waves do not break and no set-up is created. This leads to a gradient
in set-up which is balanced by a longshore flow away from the sheltered area. As a result, the onshore directed
current over submerged breakwater will diverge behind the structure and form a 2-cell circulation current, as
seen in Figure 3.1a. This current system will cause erosion behind the submerged breakwater as sediment will
be moved by the current away from the lee side.
If the submerged breakwater is further away from the shoreline, there will still be a similar diverging flow
right behind the structure from the set-up due to the breaking of waves over the structure. Furthermore, the
larger distance to the shoreline allows for an additional set-up due to breaking of the transmitted waves. Here,
the waves next to the structure are larger, producing a larger set-up, and the resulting current due the gradient
in set-up will be towards the sheltered area. This forms a converging circulation pattern at the shoreline which
together with the diverging right behind the structure forms a 4-cell circulation pattern. In this situation, the

submerged breakwater should be 2 times the reef length from the shoreline to result in accretion.

3.2.1
Oblique waves
For oblique wave conditions, Ranasinghe et al. (2006) found that the same cell patterns were formed but that
they were asymmetric due to interaction with the wave-induced longshore current. For the 2-cell circulation
pattern, the structure-induced current on the up-drift side of the structure opposes the wave-induced longshore
and the longshore current is weakened.
On the down-drift side of the structure, the longshore current is
enhanced as the structure-induced current is complementary to the wave-induced current. This results is a
diverging by asymmetric longshore current in the lee of the submerged breakwater with an enhanced current
on the down-drift side. When the structure is further from the shoreline, the interaction is opposite. The
converging current on the up-drift side of the structure enhanced the wave-induced longshore current while the
down-drift converging current opposes it. This results in a converging but asymmetric longshore current in the
lee of the structure with an enhanced current on the up-drift side.

Calibration of the spectral wave model
Larsen and Christensen (2023) made simulations of the expected shoreline response when constructing the
artificial reef. The simulations were made with a coupled MIKE 21/3 coupled model where the waves were
solved using the SW model. The SW model is computationally efficient for long-term simulations of large areas.
However, it is a relatively simple model for simulating wave interactions with structures such as breakwaters.
In contrast, the BW model incorporates processes such as diffraction and reflection but due to its high compu-
tational cost, the BW model is less practical for long-term, large-scale simulations, such as those conducted by
Larsen and Christensen (2023). In this section, a wave transmission analysis is conducted for both models to
evaluate how well the simulated Ct compares to values from literature. Based on the results, the SW model is
calibrated to improve the simulated wave transmission.
4.1
Spectral wave model
The spectral wave model is a phase-averaged model that simulates the waves using the wave action density,
N. The model simulates the wave spectrum instead of the individual waves. The wave action density spectrum
varies in time and space which can be described by the relative angular frequency, σ = 2πf, and the wave
direction, α. The governing equation is the wave action conservation equation, given by Equation 8.

wave action conservation equation states that changes in N is caused by source/sink terms. These terms include
different components depending on the model situation e.g, wave breaking, and bottom friction. In the model,
the wave direction changes with depth as caused by refraction. Following Holthuijsen et al. (1989), the wave
action conservation equation can be solved using by a directionally decoupled parametric formulation. The
parametrization is of the wave action spectrum which is described in terms of the zeroth and first-order spectral
moments instead of the full frequency spectrum (DHI, 2025a). This reduces the complexity of the model while
maintaining the spectral representation of the directionality of the waves (Holthuijsen et al., 1989).

SW model setup
The mesh for the SW model simulation is 80 m wide with triangular cells of a maximum of 30 m2. Each

Inputs
The waves are solved for using a quasi stationary formulation and the directionally decoupled parametric for-

Inputs

From the wave transmission analysis, it was found that the SW model allows for too much wave transmission
across the reef compared to the BW model and the expected Ct values. Alternatively, the BW model with the
reef as part of the bathymetry produces accurate Ct values but due to the high computational requirements, it
would be more favorable to use the SW model to simulate shoreline response. A calibration of the SW model
is performed to reduce the wave transmission and produce Ct values similar to the BW model. The calibration

is performed by adjusting γ in the wave breaking module. As denoted by Equation 9, γ determines the water
depth for wave breaking and reducing γ will lead to more wave breaking and thereby a lower wave transmission.
According to CIRIA (2007), the lower limit for a realistic γ-value is 0.6 but lower values are included as the goal
is not to use a realistic parameter but to produce realistic values of Ct.

found that γ = 0.5 resulted in Ct values most similar to the BW model for most wave conditions. Changing
the other variables in the breaking model, α and γ (wave steepness), was also tested but did not improve the
results. From the calibration it was determined that the most accurate wave transmission was simulated with γ

model directly to the HD model, to capture the combined wave statistics and current. These are then passed to
pre-computed sediment tables in the sediment transport (ST) model to give the sediment transport rates and
shoreline response.

A brief overview of the set-up and parameters are given but the model set-up for the uncalibrated model is
identical to that used by Larsen and Christensen (2023) and the only change made in the calibrated model is
the γ-value across the reef. The domain bathymetry has a constant profile which is based on the shape of a
profile extracted from the field measurements at the AOI. The domain is approximately 9.5 km long and 3 km
wide with a constant shoreline normal of 102◦. The mesh cells are largest furthest from the shore and gradually
becomes smaller approaching the shoreline. Figure 5.1a shows the bathymetry profile, and Figure 5.1b shows
the full mesh along with a close-up of the area in the vicinity of the reef. The bathymetry has a fairly even slope
with a sandbar approximately 100 m off the shoreline. The reef is positioned 200 m from the shoreline and is
incorporated as a part of the bathymetry with a length of Lr = 100 m, B = 4 m, Rc = -1 m and Sr = 1:3. In
the mesh, the reef is discretized by 7 cells over the width and 12 cells over the length.

HD model

is modeled in the middle third of the domain. The initial shoreline is a straight line following the shore-normal
and divided into segments in 25 m intervals. The morphology is driven by the gradient in longshore transport
rates between each segment which are integrated until dl = 4 m.

6
Remote Sensing for bathymetry detection
The second part of the thesis will explore the current options for using orthophotos from GeoDanmark for
bathymetry detection. Additionally, a proof of concept of using remote LIDAR measurements from the ICESat-
2 satellite is presented. In the following, background information on bathymetry detection methods and the
investigated data sources is presented.
6.1
Remote sensing
Raw remote sensing data are stacks of geo-referenced images where each image corresponds to a band of the
electromagnetic spectrum (Khorram et al., 2012a). These bands are not limited to the visible spectrum; Near-
Infrared (NIR) bands are also commonly included. A sensor measures the observed reflectance, R, of the surface
in a pixel for each band. A true color image is represented by displaying R for the red, green, and blue band of
the raw image data. The red band image will range over the wavelength corresponding to the red color on the
electromagnetic spectrum, and similar for blue and green bands. Different algebraic combinations and ratios of
the bands can lead to enhanced information about the observed areas which is not visible when displaying the
true color image (Khorram et al., 2012b).
6.1.1
Orthophotos
Orthophotos are remote sensing images that have been geometrically corrected to make the images uniform
from edge to edge and removed of distortions from the camera (Apicella et al., 2023). Since 2004, GeoDan-
mark have been responsible for providing yearly orthophotos for all of Denmark which are publicly available at
"Dataforsyningen" (Dataforsyningen, 2024). The purpose is to provide properly scaled geographical information
for public administration tasks. The orthophotos are produced in ESPG: 25832 projection with a spatial resolu-
tion of 0.125 m (Dataforsyningen, 2024). They are captured every year in the period from the start of March to
mid-May before leafing to ensure high visibility. The images are multi spectral, containing a red, a green and a
blue band with an additional NIR band (750-1400 nm) included from 2015 and onward. The intensity of each
spectral band in each pixel is provided as digital numbers (DN), which in this thesis are used as R. In this thesis,
only orthophotos from 2015 and onward will be included as the NIR band is necessary for NDWI calculations
to distinguish between land and water pixels. When referring to orthophotos from GeoDanmark used in this
thesis, they will simply be called orthophotos. If not clarified, orthophotos used in cited papers are not from
GeoDanmark but from other sources acquired by the authors.
6.2
Remote-derived bathymetry
Studies developing methods for RDB using remote sensing have mainly been focused on satellite images, also
known as Satellite-Derived Bathymetry (SBD). These images are well-suited as they cover large spatial areas and
have become widely available. Various studies have explored different approaches for developing RDB methods
which can generally be split into two categories: empirical and analytical methods. The main difference being
the need for field measurements to calibrate the prediction in the empirical methods. Analytical methods do
Page 31 of 60
REMOTE SENSING FOR BATHYMETRY DETECTION
==PAGE:40==
not require field measurements but instead utilize a larger amount of data input e.g. hyperspectral imagery (<
4 bands) or data from additional sensors on the satellite (Kutser et al., 2020; Almar et al., 2024; Alevizos et al.,
2022; Wei et al., 2020; McIntyre et al., 2006). For this thesis, only empirical methods are applicable as the
orthophotos only provide four bands and no additional data is available.
The empirical methods are based on band ratios with the most commonly used being the ratio band method
(RBM) method by Stumpf et al. (2003) and a log-linear inversion method by Lyzenga (1978). Both have been
tested extensively on satellite images (Lyzenga et al., 2006; Ma et al., 2014; Figliomeni and Parente, 2023; Zhu
et al., 2020; Amini and Kakroodi, 2024; Narciso et al., 2023) and recently, Rossi et al. (2020) and Apicella et al.
(2023) tested them using orthophotos. The algorithm developed in this thesis is based on RBM.
6.2.1
Ratio band method
Light penetrating the water column decreases exponentially with depth with the rate of the decrease determined
by the color of the light (Thomas and Bowers, 2018). Blue has the lowest water absorption and will penetrate
the water column furthest whereas red has the highest and will penetrate the column least. In optically shallow
waters, the R is both a function of how much light is absorbed through the water column but also the bottom
albedo, as described by Lyzenga (1978):
R = (Ad −R∞) exp(−kih) +R∞
(17)
where R∞is the observed reflectance at optically deep water, Ad is the bottom albedo and ki is a function of
the attenuation of both the downwelling and upwelling light. The dependency on both depth and bottom albedo
is an issue when predicting the bathymetry in areas with heterogeneous bottom features. A change in bottom
type becomes indistinguishable from a change in depth. RBM tries to solve this dependency by proposing a
logarithmic band ratio between Rw of two visible bands with two tunable parameters m1 and m0 (Equation 18-
Equation 19). The idea is that a change in albedo will affect both bands similarly, whereas a change in depth
affects the high absorption band more (Stumpf et al., 2003). It should thereby be possible to estimate the depth
independently of bottom albedo.
Zrel = ln (nR (λi))
ln (nR (λj))
(18)
h = m1 · Zrel −m0
(19)
where R(λ) is the observed reflectance for two visible bands λi, and λj and n is a fixed constant generally
taken as 500-1500 (here 500) (Stumpf et al., 2003; Ma et al., 2014). All combination of the visible bands can
be used where λi is the lower absorption band, e.g. blue and λj is the higher absorption band e.g. red. The
logarithmic ratio, Zrel is a relative depth, which is scaled to the actual depth by m1 and m0 is the offset for a
depth of 0 m (h = 0). n is taken as a large number both to ensure that the logarithm will be positive under any
condition and that the logarithmic ratio changes linearly with depth. When n is large, it dominates the constants
in Equation 17, resulting in a linear relationship between the logarithmic ratio and depth. Consequently, m1
and m0 can be estimated using linear regression with field measurements.

6.3
ICEsat-2 satellite
An alternative to remote sensing images for bathymetry detection is the ICESat-2 satellite photon-counting
LIDAR. The LIDAR is a green laser (523 nm) that produces geolocated photon detections given as ellipsoid
height measurements i.e. relative to a reference ellipsoid. This provides an active measurement of bathymetry
which makes the data less susceptible to false measurements due to environmental variables e.g. bottom type
or sun glint (Jung et al., 2025). A green laser at 532 nm is optimal for penetrating the water column as longer
wavelengths are easily absorbed by the water and at shorter wavelengths will encounter stronger scattering and
absorption by in-water constituents e.g. gasses or minerals (Parrish et al., 2019).
The satellite repeats orbit every 91 days and travels in a near-polar orbit, passing close to Earth’s poles. An
issue with the ICESat-2 LIDAR measurements is the limited coverage. The laser is split into six beams which are
arranged in three pairs of tracks, illustrated in Figure 8.1. Each track is 3 km apart in the across track direction
and each pair is 90 m apart in the across track direction. The measurements are limited to these tracks and
does not give a full coverage measurement of the bathymetry, like the remote sensing images. Additionally,
the measurements are sensitive to cloud coverage and turbid water and as a result it is not guarantied that the
bathymetry is detected in all tracks. In optimal conditions, measurements of bathymetry have been detected at
a depth of 40 m, and this data therefore has good potential for bathymetry detection in nearshore areas (Parrish
et al., 2019).
The data is provided by time, latitude, longitude, and ellipsoidal height (WGS84) for each photon event
for each beam. The height has been corrected for phenomena such as effects of atmosphere and solid earth
deformation. The data can be inspected and downloaded from the OpenAltimetry database.

Algorithm development
The following section describes the developed algorithm as well as considerations made during the development
process. The algorithm was developed based on the AOI using the 2023 orthophotos and field measurements.
In 2023, the orthophotos were taken 1st of May 2023 and the measurements were conducted on the 28th and
29th of March 2023 which is close enough in time to assume that the bathymetry is unchanged. The field
measurements (2,285,516 data points) are split into 60 % for learning data and 40% for validation data. The
image pixels and water depth measurements are matched by coordinates to use in the RBM and validation. It
was not possible to develop a fully automated algorithm as the orthophotos could not be accessed in python
due to access restrictions imposed by ’Dataforsyningen’.

step between operations and lines indicate an automatic step. Orthophotos are manually extracted using a GIS

7.2
Land masking operation
A land mask needs to be applied if the orthophoto contains land pixels, as only water pixels should be included
in the following operations. The land mask operation is inspired by the method described by Vos et al. (2019)
and Almeida et al. (2021), and Figure 7.2 shows each step of the operation.
Figure 7.2a shows the input NirRG .tif file which is loaded into the python script. The Normalized Difference
Water Index (NDWI) for each pixel is calculated using Equation 20 (McFeeters, 1996).
NDWI = R(λG) −R(λNIR)
R(λG) + R(λNIR)
(20)
where R(λG) and R(λNIR) is the observed reflectance for the green and NIR bands, respectively. NDWI is a
band ratio index used to highlight open water surfaces to distinguish water and land pixels. Figure 7.2b shows
the resulting NDWI image where larger values indicate water and lower values indicate land. The threshold
for land/water pixels is found using otsu’s threshold method which finds the maximum inter-class variance.
The threshold was found using the ’threshold_otsu’-function from the scikit-image library, which takes an image,
in this case the NDWI raster, and calculates the NDWI value with the largest inter-class variance. Figure 7.2c
shows the histogram and threshold produced by the threshold function. The threshold is used to create a binary
raster where land pixels (below threshold) are equal zero and water pixels (above threshold) are equal one
(Figure 7.2d).
The intersect between land and water pixels is found using the ’find_contours’-function which computes iso-
value contours for a given level, here 0.5. The longest intersect between land and water masses is assumed
to be the shoreline which is shown in red in Figure 7.2d. All pixels on the landward side of the shoreline are
masked and the output of the operation is a new NirRG .tif file where land pixels are masked and water pixels
are identical to the original .tif file band values, shown in Figure 7.2e.
The aim of the operation is to perform land-masking but a potential additional output of this operation is a
shoreline position measurement. This works especially well in calm conditions and for beaches with no added
features e.g, groynes. However, the shoreline position detected by the algorithm cannot be validated as no field
measurements are available. As a result, the shoreline position will not be further utilized.

7.3.2
Outlier and sun glint analysis
As part of the development process, Zrel was analyzed as a function of distance from the baseline. Figure 7.5
shows a general increase in Zrel between 100 m and 180 m, followed by a decrease at approximately 200 m.
This pattern reflects the bathymetry, characterized by increasing depth with a possible sandbar formation at 200
m. Beyond 200 m, the variability in Zrel increases substantially and upon closer inspection of the orthophoto,
surface reflections known as sun glint are observed. Sun glint occurs when sunlight reflects off the water surface
at angles that direct it into the sensor, resulting in bright pixels that do not accurately represent depth (Tiškus
et al., 2023). This phenomenon introduces noise into the data and can explain some of the variability observed
in Figure 7.5.

7.5
SCD operation
The SCD operation takes inspiration from the workflow described by T˘atui and Constantin (2020) while utilizing
the Zrel from RBM. Like, the RDB operation, the inputs are the RGB and NirRG .tif file images. The best spatial
resolution for this operation was found to be approximately 0.5 m which through iterative testing showed good
results with less noise than lower resolutions.
The relative depth is calculated using Equation 18 and smoothed using a Gaussian filter. The Gaussian filter
computes each pixel value as a weighted average of the pixel itself and the neighboring pixels. The weight of the
pixels is calculated by a Gaussian kernel (Davies, 1990). The filter is applied using the ’gaussian_filter’-function
from the SciPy Library, which takes a 2D array, here the Zrel image, and a standard deviation,σ. σ controls
how many neighboring pixels are considered and thereby the level of smoothing. Through iterative testing, a
value of σ = 6 was found to be appropriate as it creates a blurred image where only the overall bathymetry is
preserved.
To detect the sandbars, Zrel values are extracted for a transect normal to the baseline, as illustrated in
Figure 7.10a. Linear regression is performed to fit a straight line through the transect. Figure 7.10b shows an
example of Zrel values and the fitted line. There is a negative relationship between Zrel and the water depth
Page 42 of 60
ALGORITHM DEVELOPMENT
==PAGE:51==
and potential sandbar crests are therefore found at minimum values of Zrel. The residuals between Zrel and
the fitted line is calculated and potential sandbar crests are found as local minima using a moving window
approach. Using a window size of 40 pixels (20 m), a potential sandbar crest is detected if it is < 0 and the
minima in the window. Figure 7.10c shows an example of the residuals with the potential sandbar crests marked
with stars.

The SCD operation is validated using the same data as the RDB validation. Shore-normal transects of the
validation data with a width of 0.5 m are extracted for all appropriate areas of the field measurements. Outliers

validation transects are found using a moving window of 15 elements (7.5 m). Figure 7.12 shows an example

ICESat-2 LIDAR data for bathymetry detection
While the primary focus is the developed algorithm, the use of ICESat-2 satellite data for bathymetry detection
is also included as a demonstration of another type of remote sensing bathymetry detection. The developed
method is inspired by the approach of Parrish et al. (2019) and Kliving (2021). No tracks with detectable
bathymetry were found at the AOI; instead, the method is developed using data from the coastal area on the
west coast of Jutland between Hirtshals and Tversted. Depth measurements from ’vestkystlinjer’ are provided
by ’kystdirektoratet’ for validation. The ICESat-2 LIDAR data is manually inspected to find tracks with de-
tectable bathymetry and extracted from the OpenAltimetry database. Only two ICESat-2 tracks with detectable
bathymetry were found for the area between Hirtshals and Tversted. Figure 8.1 shows the two tracks which
were attained on the 21st of April 2022 and the 24th of June 2022. The latter is used to visualize the method
while the results from the other track is found in Appendix 6. The data is provided in LAT/LON coordinates but
will be shown in Easting/Northing coordinates in EPSG 25832 projection and as distance along the track from
the shoreline. Figure 8.2a shows the raw data of the photon height measurement in ellipsoid height. The data
should be seen as a cross section of the water column where the water surface is the straight line just below 40
m ellipsoid height. The water surface is very clear as most of the photons are reflected off it while some photons
penetrate the water column to detect the bottom (Jung et al., 2025). The bathymetry is visible as a sloping line,
most clearly seen near the shore where more photons have detected the bottom.
Figure 8.1: ICEsat-2 tracks with detectable bathymetry for the area between Hirtshals and Tversted.
8.1
Bathymetry detection
The still water level, SWL, is found by removing the surrounding geolocated photons using a local 3 MAD filter
in windows of 10 geolocated photons. SWL is assumed to be horizontally smooth and is taken as the average
of the filtered geolocated photons, shown by the red line in Figure 8.2b. All geolocated photons > (SWL - 0.3
m) are removed and the rest is kept as potential bathymetry measurements. The remaining geolocated photons
are transformed to a depth measurement by subtracting SWL.

To find the geolocated photons reflected by water bottom in the remaining geolocated photons, five iteration
of local 3 MAD filtering is performed with a window size of 30 elements. Each iteration removes outliers and
concentrates the density of geolocated photons at the water bottom. Additionally, a correction of the bathymetry
data is performed to account for refraction. When the LIDAR laser penetrates water it is affected by refraction
as there is a difference in the speed of light in air versus water. The photons are geolocated based on the
assumption that they only travel in air and therefore follow a straight line. When the laser penetrates water
it is slowed down and bends, resulting in both a vertical and a horizontal displacement. Parrish et al. (2019)
proposes a method for correcting this error by calculating the vertical and horizontal offsets that can be applied
to the uncorrected coordinates. The method is based on a geometrical derivation of the offset values which is
thoroughly explain in Parrish et al. (2019). The offset values of the depth and coordinate displacement, ∆Z,
∆E and ∆N, are calculated using the ’photon_refraction’-function which is available in the ICESat2-Bathymetry
github repository and based on Parrish et al. (2019). ∆Z, ∆E and ∆N are added to the uncorrected depth and
coordinates. Figure 8.3a shows the uncorrected bathymetry data and the refraction corrected bathymetry. To
predict the bathymetry a moving average is taken of the refraction corrected bathymetry data in windows of 30
elements. Figure 8.3b shows the refraction corrected bathymetry data and the resulting detected bathymetry
line.

10
Discussion
In this section, the algorithm results and how it compares to other published RDB and SCD algorithms are
discussed. Suggestions are made for potential improvements that could be implemented in the algorithm as
future work. Additionally, general considerations of the use of publicly available data for bathymetry detection
are included. First, the results of the SW model calibration will be discussed.
10.1
Shoreline response
The calibration of the SW model resulted in a 34-39% increase in shoreline accretion behind and just north of
the reef when running a constant wave climate for 2 months with α = 180◦. Additionally, erosion north of
the reef increased by 21-29%. The results of the calibration indicate that the shoreline response will be more
pronounced than previously estimated by Larsen and Christensen (2023).
The intention was to run the shoreline response model with the wider range of α, but due to time con-
straints, only a wave climate with α = 180◦was modeled. This was chosen as it is one of the wave directions
used by Larsen and Christensen (2023). As seen from the LITPACK model analysis, the sediment transport is
mainly event-driven and the large storms tends to approach the shore at α = 60◦-80◦. The expected result of
simulations with α in that range would be a similar shoreline response but on the opposite side of the reef.
In reality, the tombolo-like feature north of the reef will likely interfere with the southward-directed longshore
current produced by waves from the north-eastern direction. This will block the sediment transport and the
shoreline response due to the reef will be reduced. To further understand the effects of calibrating the SW
model results from modeling more realistic long-term wave conditions would be needed.
10.2
Benchmarking with other RDB algorithms
The RDB operation developed in this thesis provides fairly good results when used for uniform bottom condi-
tions, presented as the bottom distinction RDB. Using the operation for varying bottom types as in the full RDB
did not provide as successful results. There is a general issue with noisy data which is likely partially due to the
lack of sun glint correction but potentially also due to other unknown sources of noise in the data.
The RDB operation was developed based on the approach of Apicella et al. (2023) which was the only
study found that uses orthophotos from aircrafts for RDB and which implements bottom distinction. The results
presented by Apicella et al. (2023) reached maximum R2 values of 0.85 and 0.91, and minimum RMSE values
of 0.52 m and 0.44 m for sandy and natural rock seabeds, respectively. Rossi et al. (2020) used a UAV for RDB
over a uniform sandy area, resulting in a RMSE of 0.36 m for depths of 0-5 m and 1.06 m for depths of 0-11 m.
The results from both studies are similar to those of the bottom distinction RDB developed in this thesis.
RBM was originally developed to be used on satellite images and to be independent of bottom conditions.
For studies using satellite images, the RMSE is in the range of 1-4 m (Lyzenga et al., 2006; Ma et al., 2014;
Wei et al., 2020; Figliomeni and Parente, 2023; Narciso et al., 2023). No bottom type distinction was made
in any studies using satellite images and the results are more similar to the full RDB. From this, it seems that
using aircraft orthophotos with higher spatial resolution improves the results but bottom distinction becomes
necessary.

10.3
Benchmarking with other SCD algorithms
The SCD operation was successful at locating sandbars at the AOI based on 10 years of orthophotos. Most of
the orthophotos were captured during calm water conditions; however, the operation was also successful using
the orthophoto from 2022, where the orthophoto was captured with breaking wave conditions. This shows that
the operation works for various wave conditions.
The SCD operation was developed with inspiration from the approach of T˘atui and Constantin (2020) which
demonstrated a strong correlation between detected and measured sandbar locations with R2 = 0.98 and a
MAE = 6.22 m. A study by Janušait˙e et al. (2021) found a correlation between detected and measured sandbar
locations of R2 = 0.99 with a RMSE = 5.8-7 m. Both studies used satellite imagery, and based on the results of
this thesis, it appears that using orthophotos provides more accurate locations of sandbar crests. Compared to
the RDB operation, SCD is more readily implemented as it does not require field measurements for calibration
and is not sensitive to bottom conditions.
10.4
Future work to improve the algorithm
In the following, considerations regarding possible improvements of the algorithm which could be implemented
as future work are presented. First, the algorithm was developed and tested using data from a single location.
To better assess its broader applicability, it would be necessary to validate the algorithm with data from multiple
locations. Additionally, this would provide the option of utilizing machine learning techniques. Studies such
as Amini and Kakroodi (2024) and Narciso et al. (2023) have had success in implementing machine learning
regression methods in combination with empirical RDB methods e.g, RBM.
The orthophotos from GeoDanmark are provided in DN which would normally be transformed to reflectance
using calibration based on information of the sensor. With this transformation, the pixel values become inde-
pendent of atmospheric and illumination conditions at the time of acquisition (Teixeira Crusiol et al., 2020).
GeoDanmark does not deliver information about the method used for calibrating of their orthophotos and at-
tempts to contact GeoDanmark for information about this was unsuccessful. As a result, the algorithm was
developed without calibration and instead the raw DN values were used. The orthophotos from GeoDanmark
are mosaics of many individual images which are overlapped to provide an image of all of Denmark. For a
specific area, the images are taken at approximately the same time and the conditions are therefore essentially
the same. This is why using DN as observed reflectance instead of calibrated reflectance values is acceptable.
However, reflectance calibration of the DN would be a crucial step if the RDB operation should be applicable in
a larger area or if comparison of results between years is to be made.
Many studies perform sun glint correction on the raw remote sensing data (Lyzenga et al., 2006; Ma et al.,
2014; Figliomeni and Parente, 2023; Rossi et al., 2020). As described in section 7, sun glint correction is not
performed on the orthophotos in the algorithm due to the lack of sufficient deep water areas on the images.
As a consequence, unnecessary noise is included in the RDB operation, especially when performing full image
RDB. Finding a way to perform sun glint correction would provide a better input for the RDB operation.

DISCUSSION
==PAGE:67==
10.5
Publicly available data for bathymetry detection
Throughout this thesis, different types of publicly available data suitable for bathymetry detection have been
considered. It was quickly determined that testing orthophotos from GeoDanmark as input to the RBM would
be of interest. As described throughout the report, some issues were found with using the orthophotos from
GeoDanmark e.g. lack of information in calibration and difficulties with the sun glint correction method. Here,
it is important to recognize that the orthophotos from GeoDanmark are not provided with the intention of being
used for scientific purposes. The intended purpose is to provide properly scaled geographical information for
public administration, such as city and nature planning. Very little information is given on how the orthophotos
are captured and generated. While the orthophotos seem suitable for a simple operation like SCD but using
the orthophotos for a more complex operation like RDB could potentially be improved if additional information
regarding the generation of the orthophotos were available.
Using the ICEsat-2 satellite data for bathymetry detection showed promising results. The data is an active
measurement of bathymetry which makes it less sensitive to issues such as sun glint and varying bottom types.
The main issue with the method is the sparse availability of the data. The satellite only produces three tracks
of data for each orbit which are 3 km apart. when data from a specific area is needed, it is not guaranteed that
a track hits the area every time. Additionally, the measurements are sensitive to cloud cover and water clarity.
For days with cloudy or rough water conditions the bathymetry cannot be detected. This meant that only two
tracks in the area between Hirtshals and Tversted had detectable bathymetry in the period of available data
from 2018-2024.
In summary, several observations are made based on the exploration of the use of publicly available data
for bathymetry detection. Orthophotos require extensive data processing, and further development of the RDB
operation is necessary to produce reliable results. However, the key advantage is that they provide a reliable
source of high resolution data with large spatial extent. In contrast, ICESat-2 satellite LIDAR measurements only
need simple data treatment to provide promising results but the data availability is too unreliable for consistent
application.

DISCUSSION
==PAGE:68==
11
Conclusion
In this thesis, an algorithm was developed for RDB and SCD based on the RBM method using orthophotos from
GeoDanmark. Additionally, a calibration of the SW model was performed. The calibrated SW model showed that
the shoreline response of the artificial reef constructed at the AOI is likely larger than that originally predicted.
A wave transmission analysis of the SW model and BW model was conducted using a simple bathymetry
including a reef with similar dimensions as the reef at the AOI. Additionally, using a porosity boundary in the
BW model instead of having the reef as part of the bathymetry was tested. The analysis showed that the SW
model allowed for too much wave transmission when comparing Ct to values from literature. The BW model
with the reef as part of the bathymetry produced Ct values very close to values from literature while simulating
the reef as a porosity boundary allowed for almost no wave transmission. Through a calibration of the SW
model it was found that reducing γ to 0.5 across the reef produced results which aligned with the BW model.
The calibrated SW model was implemented in a shoreline response model where two months of constant wave
conditions with α = 180◦, Hs = 1.5 m and 0.75 m, and Tp = 5 s were conducted for the uncalibrated and
calibrated SW model. The result of using the calibrated model was an increase in accretion of 34-39% behind
the reef and an increase in erosion of 21-29% north of the reef. The calibration had a lower effect on the
shoreline response at lower wave heights. Based on the results, it can be concluded that the shoreline response
of the artificial reef will likely be larger than what was originally predicted.
The developed algorithm performs RDB and SCD for orthophotos extracted from ’Dataforsyningen’. A land
mask is applied and the relative depth is calculated using RBM. Based on this, RDB or SCD can be conducted,
where RDB requires an input of field measurements. The RDB operation finalizes the RBM by performing linear
regression between the relative depth and the field measurements. The operation was tested both with and
without bottom distinction with noticeably better results with bottom distinction. Performing full image RDB
resulted in RMSE = 1.13 m and R2= 0.52, while performing bottom distinction RDB improved the results with
RMSE = 0.28 m and R2 = 0.81. The SCD operation was successful at locating sandbar crests to an accuracy of
MAE = 3.67 m and R2 = 0.95 when comparing to measured data. SCD was successful at detecting sandbars in
both calm and breaking wave conditions.
From the LITPACK simulations, it was found that the sediment transport at the AOI was largely event-
driven. Two major storms occurring in the period between 2023 and 2024 were responsible for significant
sediment transport. Investigating the sandbar development for the AOI using orthophotos from 2015-2024
showed minimal sandbar migration. Overall, no relation was found between the wave conditions and sandbar
migration. A detailed analysis was made of the 2023 and 2024 orthophotos. Although clear visual changes were
seen in the seabed of orthophotos, no corresponding changes were observed in the sandbar migration pattern.
Lastly, a proof of concept of using the ICEsat-2 satellite LIDAR measurements for bathymetry detection was
provided. The method was used to detect bathymetry from two LIDAR measurement tracks, and the results were
compared to ’Vestkystlinje’ measurement conducted by ’Kystdirektoratet’. Bathymetry detection using ICESat-
2 satellite LiDAR measurements showed promising results. However, the sparse data availability presents a
challenge for practical applications.
