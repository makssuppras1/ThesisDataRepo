Abstract
The rapid development of Renewable Energy Sources (RES) is driving the European power sys-
tems towards a green transition. Growing shares of wind power create the need for development
of accurate models to describe the variability in wind generation.
For accurate modelling using the reanalysis approach, meteorological data along with technical
parameters are needed. The present study has focused on the technical parameters of Wind Power
Plants (WPP). Speciﬁcally, hub height and turbine type which are essential inputs for an accurate
modelling are often missing and thus need to be estimated. The analysis is done on a technical
dataset of approximately 16000 recordings which represent onshore wind power plants in Europe.
Data analysis techniques are implemented for the accurate estimation of the missing hub heights
and turbine types of the dataset. Random forest algorithm is implemented to recover the missing
information for both problems. As a result, a fully developed technical WPP dataset without
missing values is developed for reanalysis modelling.
For the validation of the random forest model (RF model), time series analysis across a list of
European countries is then presented. CorRES, a software developed by DTU Wind Energy, is
used for generating the wind power time series. The RF model is then compared against measured
(historical) data. In the analysis, another model referred as baseline, is also presented where the
missing values are imputed using simple data imputation techniques. The results suggest that
RF model performs considerably better compared to the baseline for almost every country in the
analysis.
Finally, the results of the comparison between the two models indicate the importance of having
accurately technical parameters. Considerable diﬀerences are found especially in countries where
the available information is limited leading to some deductions for future work and extensions.

Chapter 1
Introduction
An introduction of the project is presented in the following chapter. Required background infor-
mation for the development in wind industry is included. The scope of the project along with a
list of objectives are also presented.
1.1
Background information
The increasing amount of installed wind power around the globe, creates the need for accurate
modelling of wind generation. The generation levels coming from renewable energy sources (RES),
like wind power and photovoltaics have increased dramatically over the last few years and are
expected to increase even further. Only in power systems across Europe, renewable energy will be
around 60% by 2030, with wind and solar accounting for more than 40%. Land based wind will
be the most important source of production standing for 17% by 2030 [Dalla Riva et al., 2017].
As the degree of interconnections around Europe has grown, the problem should be modelled on a
European, rather than country level. Time series simulation tools are therefore needed to model
accurately both current and future with varying VRE installations.
1.2
Motivation of the project
The adequate modelling of renewable sources, relies on the correct representation of the spatial
and temporal features of power resource. In the case of wind power, two are the necessary parts;
a detailed knowledge of the technical parameters of wind power plants and wind speed data used
for conversion of speed to power output values. The highest possible accuracy of both is required
for the highest possible level of accuracy in modelling the wind time series.
==PAGE:14==
2
Introduction
Wind speed data are taken by reanalysis products, after being down-scaled from a mesoscale level
to wind farm level. Nevertheless, to apply reanalysis wind speed data for such simulations, technical
information about wind power plants (WPP) is needed. Speciﬁcally, hub height and power curve
of WPPs are two essential inputs, which are often missing.
Therefore, certain techniques are
developed in the study for the accurate estimation of both missing types.
The technical dataset used in the report covers the whole European region, including only the
onshore WPPs. It is consisted of approximately 16000 recordings, accounting for almost 120 GW
installed wind capacity. Nevertheless, WPPs with missing values in hub height and turbine type
account for a big share of total installed capacity. In terms of hub height, 60% of the recordings in
the dataset have missing values while the respective percentage for missing turbines types stands
for 35%. Therefore, the original objective of this study is to develop a strategy for the accurate
estimation of these missing values and to examine their impacts on wind time series modelling.
Brieﬂy, the scope of the project is to:
• Understand the diﬀerent aspects of large-scale wind time series simulation and their impacts
on the results.
• Focus on the estimation of missing technical parameters.
• Validate the results using time series analysis.
The objectives of the project are:
• Investigation of technical parameters inﬂuence on wind power simulation series.
• A detailed strategy developed for estimation of the missing technical parameters.
• Development of a dataset, where the missing technical parameters are estimated for WPPs
across Europe.
1.3
Thesis structure

Chapter 2
Literature Review
In this chapter, a review of previous studies and research about wind time series simulation and
data imputation techniques is presented. The aim of the literature review is not only to inform
the reader about the previous knowledge but also to establish the present study in this academic
dialogue.
2.1
Wind series analysis using reanalysis data
For modelling purposes of wind power generation, diﬀerent approaches have been implemented the
last few years. Historical data has been widely used as it is easily available from meteorological
monitoring stations which collect data for many decades. It can therefore be used in studies focus-
ing on variability of wind generation [Kiviluoma et al., 2016][Louie, 2014][Malvaldi et al., 2017].
Nevertheless, the problem which arises from using historical data from meteorological stations is
that these are not placed in areas where a wind farm is located or they are in considerably lower
height than a modern wind turbine. Extrapolating the wind speed in the correct height can be
done but such extrapolations are uncertain because in most cases atmospheric stability and surface
roughness, which is needed for a vertical extrapolation are not measured at all. An alternative to
these models is to use reanalysis data which seem to gain rapidly popularity in the ﬁeld due to its
convenience and global coverage.
Several reanalysis products are available, with MERRA, MERRA 2, CSFR and more recently
ERA5 being the most used ones. Diﬀerent studies have been published using reanalysis data for
modelling wind generation time series for future scenarios. Firstly, Gonzalez et al. [González-
Aparicio et al., 2017] examined the diﬀerences in wind speed between diﬀerent reanalysis products,
highlighting also the importance of using high resolution data for the analysis. Thus, a method-
ology is explained for increasing the spatial resolution while statistical downscale is applied to
convert the wind speeds from mesoscale to wind farm level.
Nuño et al. [Nuño et al., 2018] simulate aggregated renewable energy sources time series, using
reanalysis data that were produced by dynamical downscaling. Marinelli et al. [Marinelli et al.,

2015] also used reanalysis data from dynamical downscaling to produce large scale regional models
for evaluation of the normalized outputs coming both from wind turbines and photovoltaic plants
on a European scale. A diﬀerent technique has been proposed from Staﬀell and Pfenninger [Staﬀell

Regarding the technical parameters of time series analysis, limited work can be found as most of

given. For the missing turbine types, the power curve of the most common type has been assigned.
Koivisto et al. [Koivisto et al., 2018] also focuses on the impact of missing hub heights in wind
simulations. A linear regression model is developed for the calculation of the missing recordings,
while the importance of estimating accurately the hub height is highlighted using representative
examples from European countries.
2.2
Missing data imputation techniques
The other important part of this project, focuses on the estimation of technical parameters of
WPP. Many of the research and industrial databases suﬀer from data incompleteness, thus certain
techniques have been developed the last few years. Two are the general approaches used for the
missing values; they could be ignored completely or imputed with new values. As it is stated in the
novel framework of Farhangfar et al. [Farhangfar et al., 2007], the former approach is applicable
only when a small amount of data is missing but still there is the risk of losing important informa-
tion to support the decision making process. This is why several data imputation techniques have
been proposed in the literature. Traditional techniques use statistics and rely on simple algorithms
like mean or hot-deck imputation but in the recent years a new family of imputation methods,
which uses machine learning algorithms has been proposed. Neural networks [Ghahramani and
Jordan, 1997], Naive Bayes [Farhangfar et al., 2004], k-nearest neighbors [Batista and Monard,
2003] or Random Forest [Pantanowitz and Marwala, 2008] are some typical examples of machine
learning methods used for data imputation.
In the wind energy ﬁeld, ML algorithms have been used the last few years due to their wide appli-
cability and versatile skills. Applications of machine learning algorithms can be found in condition
monitoring of the wind turbines [Zekveld and Hancke, 2018][Zhao et al., 2009], fault detection
[Bakri et al., 2016] or power forecasting [Dolara et al., 2017][Kariniotakis et al., 1996][Li and Shi,
2010].
Regarding imputation in wind speed data, in He et al. [He et al., 2013], a neural network is devel-
oped for estimating missing value recovery for wind data. In Shukur et al. [Shukur and Lee, 2015]
a comparison between classical methods with a complex ANN-AR model is presented, for impu-
tation of wind speed data. Finally, a clustering method developed by Mohandes et.al [Mohandes
et al., 2011] is used to ﬁnd the wind speed at height of 100m based on wind speed knowledge of
lower heights.

3.1
The role of reanalysis data
From meteorological side, wind speeds are obtained from an WRF model.
WRF model is a
mesoscale modelling system [Skamarock et al., 2008], giving hourly meteorological time series
on 10x10 km grid. The mesoscale dynamical downscale method has been used to generate time
series of wind speed and other meteorological terms of interest [Hahmann et al., 2010].
Nevertheless, using reanalysis data directly in the analysis, error in the capacity factors can occur
as shown in many studies [González-Aparicio et al., 2017, Staﬀell and Pfenninger, 2016]. Therefore
a common approach used widely by research groups is to apply a scaling factor for correction of
the results. The scaling factor is calibrated using historical capacity factors and is applied to the
wind speeds. For cases where the capacity factor is not directly available, it will be calculated
using data referring to installed capacities and generation levels according to formula 3.1:

3.3
The role of turbine type
Estimating accurately the turbine type is another crucial parameter for the analysis. Power curve
is an essential input in time series simulation and having the correct one, aﬀects greatly the output.
The wind speed at which a turbine reaches its rated power, is one of the most important features of
a power curve and is directly aﬀected by the speciﬁc power of the turbine [Agency, 2016]. Speciﬁc
power can be used to numerically express the diﬀerent turbine types.
It is deﬁned as the ratio between the rated power of the wind turbine, measured in Watt, and
the swept area, measured in m2. In contrary to the hub height, the shape of the power curve
is directly inﬂuenced by the speciﬁc power and thus it is of great interest for the wind turbine
manufacturers. In the last few years, the speciﬁc power, has been decreasing, based mainly on the
idea of higher swept areas and hub heights for the same rated power[Paul Gipe, 2017]. This shift
towards lower speciﬁc power ensures higher capacity factors for the same wind speeds and as a
result the rated power of a turbine is achieved at lower wind speeds[Ryan Wiser, 2018]. Again the
following example is considered:

diﬀerent turbine types. The two wind turbines are Vestas V100/1.8MW and Vestas V80/1.8MW.

accounts for 229 W/m2 while for the second is 358 W/m2. The comparison for the two cases, as

4.1
Linear Regression
In statistics, linear regression is a linear approach to modelling the relationship between a scalar
response (or dependent variable) and one or more explanatory variables (or independent variables).
Linear regression is the most basic and commonly used type of predictive analysis. Applications
of regression analysis can be found in many scientiﬁc ﬁelds like engineering, biology, mathematics
etc [Bollinger, 1981].
The mathematical formulation of a multiple linear regression problem is the following :
y = β0 + β1x1 + β2x2 + .. + βnxn + ϵ
(4.1)
where y is the dependent variable, β0, β1, β2, ..., βn are the regression coeﬃcients and x1, x2, ..., xn
are the independent variables of the model. In the classical regression approach setting the error
term ϵ follows the normal distribution with E(ϵ) = 0 and a constant variance V ar(ϵ) = σ2.[Xin Yan,
2012]

4.2
Random forest
Random forests are an ensemble learning method that can be used to solve both regression and
classiﬁcation problems. The algorithm was initially created by Tin Kam Ho [Ho, 1995], based
on the idea of random subspace method. An extension to the algorithm was developed by Leo
Breiman, who also registered the algorithm as trademark[Breiman, 2001].
The algorithm is based primarily on two methods, namely bagging and random subspace method.
Bagging is an ensembling technique for improving the accuracy and stability of the models. Suppose
we have the following training set of size N :
Z = {(x1, y1), (x2, y2), ..., (xn, yn)}
By repeatedly sampling N’ observations (where N’=N), the B following training sets are created:
Z1, Z2,, .., ZB. Each of this is called bootstrap dataset. The important detail is that bootstrapped
datasets can have duplicate data of the original records. Two thirds of the initial recordings are
included in the bootstrapped datasets while around one third is being left out. The above is called
out of bag data (oob) and can be used to get a running unbiased estimate of the algorithm’s error.
The algorithm adds additional randomness to the model while growing the trees.
Instead of
searching for the most important feature while splitting a node (classic procedure followed in a
decision tree), it searches for the best feature among a random subset of features. This results in
a wide diversity that generally results in a better model. This is called random subspace method.
The aforementioned procedure is then repeated creating a ﬁnite number of decision trees, each one
of them with diﬀerent datasets and diﬀerent selected features. Brieﬂy, the steps of the algorithm
are presented:
1. Draw a bootstrap sample from the original set.
2. Grow a tree, using created data from step 1. At each node, determine the optimal split from
randomly selected variables. Specify the depth of each tree.
3. Repeat the steps 1 and 2, B times (B: number of trees).
4. Combine the B created trees to form the ensemble predictor.

The algorithm has clear advantages compared to more classic approaches of machine learning in
terms of bias and variance reduction. To be more speciﬁc:
• The trees are more independent due to the diﬀerent combination of bootstap samples and
random selection of the predictors. Averaging over trees can reduce instability that might
otherwise result. In addition to this, working with diﬀerent sample of predictors at each
split the ﬁtted values across trees are more independent and consequently the gains from
averaging over a large number of trees (variance reduction) can be dramatic.
• More complex (non linear) dependencies between the data can be discovered. This is because
the data is clustered by the algorithm using multiple nested ’if-else’ statements.
These
statements draw vertical/horizontal lines between the data and cluster them in rectangles.
Consecutively, rectangles of the same class can be far away from each other (with other class
rectangles in between) but still they belong to the same class. This is how nonlinear relations
are modeled.

Random forest include 3 main tuning parameters which need to be deﬁned in advance with respect
to diﬀerent cases. The parameters are the following:
1. Number of Trees .
2. Number of minimum observations per leaf.
3. Number of predictors sampled.
As far as the number of trees are concerned, generally it holds true that higher number of trees
ensure better predictive performance of the model. Nevertheless, the computational speed of the
model is also increased, which is non desirable and makes diﬃcult to easily run diﬀerent simula-
tions. As a general approach, it is desirable to select as many trees as the processor can handle.
The error term will stop decreasing signiﬁcantly after a certain number of trees is grown, therefore
a highly complex model with many trees is not justiﬁed.
Determining the minimum number of observations in the leaf is also important as it is the end
node of a decision tree. A small leaf makes the model more prone to capture noise in training
data while bigger leaf size has a poor predictive performance as it will stop growing the tree early.
Therefore, multiple leaf sizes in general have to be tested to show which one is the best option for
the data.
Finally, the number of predictors sampled refers to the maximum number of features that our
model is allowed to try in individual trees. Increasing the number, generally improves the per-
formance of the model as at each node, more options can be considered as the optimal ones.
However, this is not necessarily true as the diversity of each individual tree can sometimes be af-
fected. Therefore, again multiple numbers have to tested in order for the right balance to be found.
4.3
Cross validation
For a fair comparison between the two methods, cross validation is used. Cross validation, in
general, is considered a statistical method used to estimate the accuracy of a machine learning
model. The goal of the method is to test the model’s ability to predict new data that was not used
in training procedure in order for problems like overﬁtting to be avoided. The most famous types
of cross validation are (a) hold out method (b) k- fold (c) one out of K [Refaeilzadeh et al., 2009].
In our problem, the second method, namely k-fold was used, due to easy implementation and lower
computational time compared to one out of K. The steps for the implementation of k-fold cross
validation are being described below:
• The original data set is being split into k equal subsets. Each subset is called a fold. Let the
folds be assigned the names f1, f2, ..., fk.
• For i=1 to k
1. The fold fi is being kept aside as a validation set while the remaining k-1 folds will be
the training set.

Metrics selected
For a regression problem (hub height estimation), residual is the ﬁrst important term which needs
to be introduced for a valid comparison between the methods.
Residuals (e) are actually the
diﬀerence between the true (observed) value of the dependent variable (y) and the predicted value
(ˆy):
e = y −ˆy
(4.2)
A residual plot is a graph very useful for interpreting the results of a regression model.
The
residuals are shown on the vertical axis and the ﬁtted values (from the model) on the horizontal.
It is quite often to include standardized residual on the vertical axis (ratio of raw residual with
standard deviation of residual) as it is scale-independent. As a general rule, a residual plot with
points randomly dispersed around the horizontal axis suggest that a linear model is generally a
good approach. On the opposite case when speciﬁc patterns are created, the model has room
for improvement or even a non linear model would be more appropriate. Therefore, inspecting a
residual plot of a regression model could give valuable information for the predictive performance
of the model.

the average magnitude of the error. It is always non negative and a value of 0 would indicate a
perfect ﬁt to the data. In general, lower RMSE values are better than higher ones. Note also that
RMSE is scale dependent so comparisons across diﬀerent types of data would not be valid due to

and easily understood. Nevertheless, it is a representative metric when there is an equal (or almost
equal) sample in each class (balanced datasets). Classes with bigger number of samples will have
a higher representation on the accuracy, resulting in low attention on the non- frequent classes.
This is the main reason why, in imbalanced datasets other metrics are used as well in order for the
above described issue to be tackled.
Confusion matrix, also known as error matrix, is a table layout that allows visualization of the
performance of an algorithm (ﬁg. 4.3). Each row of the matrix represents the predicted class,
while each column represents the actual class.
==PAGE:30==
18
Theory behind linear regression and random forest
Figure 4.3: A typical example of a confusion matrix
In a confusion matrix the following terms are met:
• True Positives (TP): True positives are the instances where the actual class was True, and
the predicted is also True.
• True Negatives (TN): True negatives are the instances where the actual class was False, and
the predicted is also False.
• False Positives (FP): False positives are the instances where the actual class was False, and
the predicted is True.
False is because the model has predicted incorrectly and positive
because the class predicted was a positive one.
• False Negatives (FN): False negatives are the instances where the actual class was True, and
the predicted is False. False is because the model has predicted incorrectly and negative
because the class predicted was a negative one.
After having deﬁned the terms that are necessary in understanding a confusion matrix, the sup-
plementary metrics used for the classiﬁcation problem are also presented:
• Recall: Number of items correctly identiﬁed as positive out of total true positives in the
dataset (REC =
TP
TP+FN ).
• Precision: Number of items correctly identiﬁed as positive out of total items identiﬁed as
positive (PRE =
TP
TP+FP ).
Recall gives information about a classiﬁer performance with focus on false negatives (how many
missed in total), while precision gives information with focus on false positives (how many caught
in total). For a multi class classiﬁcation, like the one having in our case, for each separate class
recall and precision are computed.
==PAGE:31==
4.4 Metrics selected
19
Nevertheless, it is always better to establish a single evaluation metric to be optimized. Both terms
(recall and precision) are included in the calculation of F1-score which is shown below:
F1 = 2 · PRE · REC
PRE + REC
(4.8)
F1- score is an harmonic mean between precision and recall. Harmonic mean is a kind of an average
when the two aforementioned values are equal otherwise it is closer to the smaller number of the
two, Therefore, if one number is really small between precision and recall, it somehow raises a ﬂag
and is closer to the smaller number than the bigger one.

6.3
Linear Regression
After having deﬁned the theoretical formulas for linear regression model in Chapter 4, a speciﬁc
model for the estimation of hub height will be developed.
Diﬀerent variables were tested at this stage to be the potential predictors in the regression problem.
After trials, rotor diameter, wind speed and country of where the wind farm is located are ﬁnally
selected in the model. Note that wind speed and rotor diameter are numerical attributes while
country is a categorical one. Country as a categorical attribute can not be inserted directly as one
variable so dummy coding was implemented [Long et al., 2006]. A dummy variable is a numeric
variable that represents categorical data and help us to include them in a regression problem.
Generally, dummy variable are limited to two speciﬁc values namely 1 or 0, where 1 represents the
presence of the attribute while 0 the absence. The total number of variables that will be selected
to be included in the model depends on how many diﬀerent values can this categorical attribute
take. In our case there are 33 diﬀerent counties in the dataset therefore 32 dummy variables are
included in the model, each one of them representing a diﬀerent country.
The mathematical formulation of the regression model explained above is shown at equation 6.1:
yhh = β0 + βrotxrot + βwsxws + βrotwsxrotxws + β1x1 + β2x2 + ... + βkxk + ϵ
(6.1)
The following terms are explained :
• yhh : dependent variable of hub height
• xrot : predictor variable of rotor diameter (based on the turbine type used)
• xws : predictor variable of wind speed at 100m
• x1 + x2 + .. + xk : predictor variable of countries selection (dummy variables)
==PAGE:43==
6.3 Linear Regression
31
• ϵ: error term
The β parameters shown in equation 6.1 are the coeﬃcients that have to be estimated. Since both
xrot and xws are both continuous predictors, the coeﬃcients βrot and βws represent the diﬀerence
in the predicted value of y for each one-unit diﬀerence in the one predictor, if the other remains
constant. Nevertheless, for the coeﬃcient of the interaction term βrotws, the eﬀect on the dependent
value is examined as the eﬀect of the other predictor is already accounted for. The inﬂuence of the
interaction term should be always taken into account in problems where at least more than two
predictors are used. Finally, an error term ϵ is also included.

model, making it simpler and faster to predict.
Two diﬀerent ways of estimating the importance of a predictor can be implemented in a random
forest classiﬁer.
The ﬁrst and the most straightforward approach is known as accuracy based
importance, and it measures how much the accuracy is decreased when a speciﬁc predictor is being
excluded from the model. The second way is based on the decrease of Gini impurity when a speciﬁc
predictor is being chosen for the splitting procedure of a node. In our case, the latter method was
implemented and it is brieﬂy described in the following steps:
• For every tree of the forest, an out of bag sample is created. The error of the out of bag
sample is calculated and denoted as ϵt.
• The observations of each predictor are then permuted and a new error is computed, denoted
as ϵtj.
• The diﬀerence dtj = ϵtj −ϵt is calculated.
• For each predictor the mean value (dj) and standard deviation (σj) of the diﬀerences are
computed.
• The out of bag importance by permutation is ﬁnally dj/σj.

The ﬁrst issue that has to be tackled before processing to the implementation of algorithm is the
signiﬁcantly diﬀerent number of appearances each turbine type (class) has in the dataset. For that
reason, the classes are considered imbalanced and certain techniques can help us, overcome the
problem.
One of the techniques used for that issue is oversampling [Chen et al., 2004] which refers to the
addition of copies of recordings of the underrepresented class. On the one hand, it is considered
to be more reliable compared to the undersampling approach, since in the latter there is the
risk of erasing valuable information for the training of the algorithm. On the other hand, the
clear disadvantage of this method is that the dataset gets signiﬁcantly bigger by replicating the
recordings of non frequent classes and therefore the computational time increases as well.
In addition to this, special attention should be given when oversampling is done on a cross validated
dataset. If oversampling is done before cross validation, recordings of the training set will appear
exactly the same in the validation set, resulting in overﬁtting. As a result, the dataset ﬁrstly needs
to be cross validated, ensuring that there is a part of the data that will be used only as validation
set, and then oversampling is applied to the remaining set. In ﬁgure 7.6, the procedure of correctly
oversampling the data is presented:

Chapter 8
Large scale time series simulation
After the missing parameters have been estimated, a large scale time series simulation is carried out
on a European level. CorRES, a software developed by DTU Wind Energy is used for generating
the wind power time series [Koivisto et al., 2019]. Two diﬀerent models are presented; a baseline in
which the missing parameters are calculated by simple imputation methods and a complex one in
which the developed model of random forest is applied. An extensive comparison between the two
models is presented focusing mainly on the calculated capacity factors compared to the measured
ones for a list of European countries.
8.1
Overview of CorRES
Correlations in renewable energy sources (CorRES) is a simulation tool, capable of simulating
accurately both wind and solar generation. It is based both on meteorological data, obtained from
a WRF model, but also on stochastic simulations to provide generation levels in the minute scale.
The combination of both, provides a better representation in the temporal dependencies in wind
generation.
As far as the meteorological data is concerned, they are obtained from the WRF model, a mesoscale
modeling system [Hahmann et al., 2010]. A mesoscale downscaling method is needed to correctly
represent wind speeds from a mesoscale level to a wind farm level. Initial, boundary and grids for
nudging the WRF simulations are supplied by ERA Interim Reanalysis [Dee et al., 2011] .
As shown in [Larsén et al., 2012], mesoscale models and in our case WRF underestimates the short
term variability in wind speeds. This is the reason why CorRES also adds ﬂuctuations to WRF
wind speed to generate more realistic simulations. The ﬂuctuations are simulated as stochastic
time series that have expected value of zero in order not to change the long term mean wind speeds
obtained form WRF. Most importantly, ﬂuctuation modelling allows simulation of intra hour time
series by adding realistic short term variability to the hourly WRF data.

8.2 Inputs of the diﬀerent models

8.2
Inputs of the diﬀerent models
Two diﬀerent models are presented and compared against the measured (historical) data. The
comparison is mostly done on annual capacity factors, but for some countries which measured time
series data are also available, the actual time series are compared.
The inputs needed for both models in CorRES are divided into two categories; wind speed values
from meteorological side and information about WPPs from the technical one.
From meteorological side reanalysis wind speeds should not be taken directly in the simulation,
as it has been argued in Chapter 3. Mesoscale reanalysis products can not accurately capture
local eﬀects, thus it is common either to overestimate or underestimate wind speeds. A scaling
factor is therefore applied separately to each WPP of the dataset for correction. For the numerical
calculation of the scaling factor, the wind speed at a WPP is taken both from Global Wind Atlas
and a mesoscale model. Dividing these two yields the scaling factor (diﬀerent for each location)
which is assumed to keep the same value in future scenarios as well. It is important to clarify that
the aforementioned procedure for the calculation of scaling factor in the present study is diﬀerent
than the one followed in earlier studies in the ﬁeld where the scaling factor was calibrated using
measured capacity factors [Staﬀell and Pfenninger, 2016].
From the technical side, two are the essential inputs; hub height and the power curve of the
turbine. Hub height is important due to the logarithmic proﬁle of wind speed while power curve
describes the relationship between wind speed value and power output. For the turbines with
missing power curves, the described procedure in section 7.2 is implemented. Nevertheless, the
power curves describe the output in a turbine level which is not really the case in a wind farm.
Turbine availability consists the most impactful technical loss type in an onshore WPP thus need
also to be taken into account in the model. Wake losses should also be considered but in onshore
power plants they are not expected to signiﬁcantly mitigate the production. Thus, a 5% decrease
in the power production has been assumed separately for every WPP in order for these technical
losses to be also considered [Mortensen, 2016].
Bases on the previous, two models are developed. The ﬁrst one includes simple imputation methods
of the missing values both for hub height and turbine type. To be more speciﬁc, a mean value of
the known heights is calculated for the hub height in that country and assigned to the missing rows
(numerical attribute). For turbine type, the most frequent type used in each country is assigned
to the missing recordings (categorical attribute). In table 8.2 the imputed values on a country
level for the baseline model are presented. For the second model, the developed random forest
algorithm explained in chapter 7 is implemented and applied to missing values of hub height and
turbine type. In table 8.1, the inputs for the two diﬀerent models are presented:

installations in the country and the really good wind resource. In ﬁgure 8.10 a screenshot from
Global Wind Atlas is included, showing the capacity factors of wind turbines IEC Class I. It is seen
that the capacity factor for onshore farms in UK ranges approximately from 0.31 to 0.34, which
diﬀers signiﬁcantly to the one found in ENTSO-E data. UK is the only country in the study where
the capacity factors present such a diﬀerence between the diﬀerent sources, therefore the quality
of measured data should be further investigated.

Chapter 9
Conclusion
The ﬁnal chapter includes a summary of the report along with the key ﬁndings and a general
discussion. In the last part, suggestions for future research are also given.
9.1
Findings of the project
The main ﬁndings of this report are summarized. The conclusions are divided into the two main
sections of the project; missing data imputation part and time series analysis part. In the for-
mer, the methods used for the imputation of missing values are recapped while in the latter the
conclusions from time series analysis are summed up.
9.1.1
Missing data imputation
Hub height and turbine type constitute the crucial technical parameters in wind time series mod-
elling, thus diﬀerent methods were evaluated and interesting ﬁndings emerged from the analysis.
More speciﬁcally:
• In Chapter 6, the regression problem of hub height estimation is under examination. Linear
regression and random forest were the two methods tested. The same list of predictors used
in both methods for a fair comparison between them. Rotor diameter was found to be the
most important predictor in both, while wind speed value and country of the wind farm do
also contribute signiﬁcantly. As it was expected, random forest outperforms linear regression
presenting lower RMSE errors for every fold of the dataset. The main reason is that linear
regression uses only linear dependencies between the variables in contrast with the random
forest where also complex dependencies can be identiﬁed. The hub height estimation problem
is not truly linear, therefore random forest algorithm is found to be more appropriate.
==PAGE:77==
9.1 Findings of the project
65
• In Chapter 7, the focus is drawn on the implementation of random forest algorithm not only
for hub height estimation but also for turbine type. A more complex model is implemented
on diﬀerent stages depending on the availability of target variables at each stage. Rotor
diameter -if available- is found again to be the most important predictor for estimating the
hub height. It inﬂuences greatly the accuracy of the algorithm due to the high correlation
with the target variable (hub height). Nevertheless, when rotor diameter was unavailable,
rated power of the turbine had great inﬂuence on the accuracy and ensured the satisfactory
results of the model.
For the classiﬁcation problem since rotor diameter is always unavailable, rated power is found
to be the most important predictor independently of the stage. Given the limited information
and the complexity of the problem, random forest is evaluated to perform satisfactorily
especially in the stage where also hub height is available and used this time as predictor.
9.1.2
Time series simulation
For the time series simulation part, two models were compared against measured data from tech-
nical reports published by Nordpool, Fingrid and ENTSO-E. In the ﬁrst one, referred as baseline
model, simple imputation techniques are established for data imputation while in the second one
the missing values are calculated using the established model based on random forest algorithm.
The comparison was done on the diﬀerent capacity factors deducted across diﬀerent European
countries. The way that the missing technical values are imputed is the only diﬀerence between
the models, thus diﬀerent values for capacity factors are computed. The importance of having
accurate information in technical parameters is then highlighted:
• For countries where time series data are also available, a more detailed analysis of the results
is presented.
Time series data are generally of great importance because extra valuable
information can be extracted for a country.
In our case, CorRES time series results of
RF model simulate better the measured data compared to the results of baseline model.
Especially in Finland, the diﬀerence between the two models is high, as the increase of hub
height (due to the new installations in country which have the most missing values) can
not be explained accurately by the baseline model. In Sweden RF model is also signiﬁcantly
better while in Estonia the two models are really close, presenting similar summary statistics.
• In rest of the countries where just the annual capacity factor is known since time series data
are not available, the results verify again that RF model is able to simulate more accurately
compared to the baseline model. In countries like Germany Italy and Denmark where the
diﬀerences between the technical inputs of the models are quite signiﬁcant, the simulated
capacity factors vary. RF model always performs better, achieving capacity factors closer to
the measured data. In Spain and France the results from both models are almost identical
since the added value of RF model is hardly shown in these two countries. Finally, UK is the
only country for which the baseline model performs marginally better for both years 2014
and 2015. Nevertheless, questions can be raised for the reliability of the measured data in
the country since Global Wind Atlas reveals a completely diﬀerent image of the country in
terms of capacity factor.

Conclusion
9.2
Discussion
9.2.1
Missing data imputation
As far as the machine learning part is concerned, for the hub height estimation problem random
forest algorithm performs great even for the recordings where rotor diameter is not available.
Rated power is luckily always available leading the algorithm to great results. On the other hand,
in turbine type problem, the limited available information leads to moderate but still acceptable
results, especially in stage 3 dataset. It is worth mentioning that in the initial trials instead of
approaching directly the problem of turbine type, it was tried to estimate it in two steps with
estimation of missing rotor diameter to be the intermediate step. Nevertheless, the results of the
ﬁnal model proved to be worse (accuracy=35%), since the RMSE error of the intermediate step
(rotor diameter estimation) had great inﬂuence. The importance of rotor diameter in the turbine
type problem can be also veriﬁed from the fact that if rotor diameter was available, the accuracy
of the classiﬁcation algorithm could rise up to 80% in some cases. Sadly, this is not the case in this
study since the rotor diameter is also missing for the recordings where turbine type is unknown.
9.2.2
Time series simulation
The original objective of the study is to accurately estimate the missing technical parameters of
European onshore WPPs and examine their inﬂuence on wind time series modelling. Neverthe-
less, it is unquestionable that the eﬀect of technical parameters on time series analysis is limited
compared to the one deducted from meteorological data.
Wind speed eﬀect on time series modelling has been extensively investigated by research groups
the last few years and it has proved to be the most crucial parameter in such simulations. In
our project, the quality of wind speeds (identical for both models in CorRES) is considered quite
accurate as diﬀerent scaling factors have been applied separately to each WPP. Nevertheless, the
scaling factor has been calculated for a representative height of 100m, for reasons of simplicity.
The scaling factor changes slightly depending on the height, e.g. for a height of 50m it has slighter
lower value. Therefore, for WPPs where the total height is signiﬁcantly diﬀerent (especially in
farms erected before 1990), CorRES time series results may be marginally overestimated.
Also, the technical losses of a turbine assumed in the project is deﬁnitely a factor which can in-
ﬂuence the outcome of time series analysis. In our study, the losses are assumed to account for
5% which is generally a representative value but in some occasions the losses can account for even
further. Control losses (SCADA), environmental losses (blade degradation due to icing, high and
low temperatures) or even curtailments due to noise can be also considered, increasing further the
technical losses of a turbine up to 10% in some cases [Mortensen et al., 2015].
Regarding the measured capacity factors for the European countries presented in tables 8.6 and
8.7 certain assumptions have to be made in the procedure. The technical ENTSO-E reports pro-
vide information only about wind generation and installed capacity on a country level. For the
calculation of capacity factor, an average value of installed capacity had to be assumed as the
representative capacity of the year. Note also that in countries like Germany, generation coming
from oﬀshore farms is a signiﬁcant part of the production, leading to diﬀerent capacity factors.
Grid curtailment is also an issue that should be considered separately as it can have an impact
on the measured data. Especially in countries such as Germany and UK where the roll out of
==PAGE:79==
9.3 Future research
67
infrastructure is lagging behind the development of wind plants, curtailment is expected to have
an inﬂuence on wind net generation leading to reduced capacity factors. As stated in a technical
report published by WindEurope, curtailment rates in 2014 for Germany and UK stand for 2.1%
and 3.1% respectively [WindEurope, 2016]. Nevertheless the rates in both countries have further
increased since then, reaching up to 5% in some cases [Joos and Staﬀell, 2018].
Grid curtail-
ment could also explain partially the relatively low measured capacity factors both in Germany
and UK. For example, in Germany the measured capacity factor in 2014 accounts for only 0.182,
while in reality it should be a bit higher, decreasing the diﬀerence with the simulated CorRES data.
9.3
Future research
Firstly, diﬀerent machine algorithms can be tested for the imputation of missing values. Regarding
the hub height problem, neural networks would be a great start since they can tackle quite eﬀec-
tively a regression problem in same cases. For the classiﬁcation problem either a neural network
or k-nearest neighbors algorithm could be tested.
The problem of imbalanced data set in the classiﬁcation problem is also possible to be approached
in a diﬀerent way. More sophisticated techniques like SMOTE (synthetic minority over-sampling
technique) where synthetic data are created could outperform the simple oversampling approach
presented in the project.
Regarding the creation of missing power curves in the dataset, the procedure described in section
7.2 could be formulated diﬀerently especially in the range of the wind speeds after the cut out
value. For example, a mean value of the cut out speeds from the turbines consisting that group
could be taken and assigned to the created power curve. The results will still be hardly aﬀected
but diﬀerent approaches could be implemented.
Measured data of wind generation and installed capacity across the diﬀerent European countries
could also be cross checked from diﬀerent sources to ensure the reliability. Normally, the data
coming from the transmission system operator of the country are more accurate compared to the
ones found in technical reports of ENTSO-E. Unfortunately, they are not easily available but im-
provements can be done towards that direction, especially in countries with high wind generation
like Germany.
Finally, the present work focuses only on scenarios regarding the current wind power situation
in Europe.
The analysis could be extended to future scenarios, considering also the expected
technological development of installations. Interesting results could be deducted from further in-
vestigation helping to steer the development in a desired direction.
