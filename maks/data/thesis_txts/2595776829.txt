Summary
Typically, engineering wake models assume the inflow of the most upstream wind
turbines in a wind farm to equal the ambient free-stream wind speed.
In reality,
however, this assumption does not hold as it leaves out blockage effects such as single
wind turbine blockage and wind farm blockage.
This thesis aims to quantify the single wind turbine blockage and wind farm
blockage in terms of energy production based on the engineering blockage-wake-model
framework PyWake and CFD RANS simulations. Furthermore, this project aims to
verify the results obtained with PyWake against CFD RANS simulations.
In PyWake, the wind speed deficit in the region upstream of a wind turbine is
obtained by using the Self-similarity model. Meanwhile, the wake deficit of the wind
turbine is computed using Fuga. Both models can be coupled so they can be used to
determine the power production of wind farms by taking into account blockage and
wake deficits. For the CFD RANS simulations, the Python interface to EllypSys3D,
PyWakeRANS, is used.
And the k −ε −fP model is chosen to simulate the flow
around the V80 wind turbine, which is modeled based on a Joukowski rotor.
The use of Fuga as blockage model is assessed and found to be unsuitable. Hence,
the Fuga+SS model is proposed and used to investigate the wind turbine blockage
and wind farm blockage. However, the wake region computed by Fuga+SS cannot
predict with a good accuracy the results of the RANS simulation. Thus, Fuga should
be improved.
The blockage wind turbine is found to be the deficit in the upstream region of a
wind turbine which can affect neighbouring wind turbines. The wind farm blockage
produces a slight gain in power when wind turbine are placed along the crosswind
direction of another wind turbine.
Moreover, downstream wind turbines cause a
power reduction on the upstream ones. These effects have a bigger impact in the
power production for wind farms with small wind turbine spacing.
In general, the blockage effects slightly reduces the AEP energy of wind farms.
Even though this effect is small, it can affect the prediction of energy production
of wind farms. For this reason, it is necessary consider the blockage effects in AEP
evaluations and optimization.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Within the wind energy field, the power production of a wind farm is a key charac-
teristic to predict and estimate its profit during the project assessment. The power
production is obtained by computing the annual energy production of the entire wind
farm. For that, it is necessary to know the power curve of the wind turbines and the
wind resource of the site measured by a nearby meteorology mast. The power curve
is generally based on measurements of the “free-stream” wind speed at a distance of
2.5D, where D stands for rotor diameter, from the wind turbine according to the IEC
standards [1]. Moreover, the measured free-stream wind speed is used to calculate
the inflow wind speed for each of the turbines in a wind farm. Hence, the interaction
between wind turbines and the flow has been studied to quantify the reduction in
wind speed along the wind farm. As the wind passes through the wind turbine, the
latter extracts some of the kinetic energy of the wind causing a momentum deficit
which leads to a lower inflow wind speed for downstream wind turbines.
To quantify the wind speed reduction in wind farms, wake models have been devel-
oped and are widely used in the wind industry since they provide a fast and acceptable
prediction of how much power is being produced in the wind farms. But recently, the
offshore wind energy company Ørsted announced that the company has changed its
long-term expectation of earnings in the offshore business [2]. In this announcement
the company describes how the effect of two wind shade phenomena around the wind
farms, namely the blockage and wake effects, has been underestimated. This means
overestimating the electricity generation of the wind farms. Hence, Ørsted has written
down the load factor over the lifetime from 48%-50% to 48% for a defined European
portfolio of offshore wind farms and construction and development projects [2].
The wake effect is well described and quantified by the wake models since several
years ago. And it is known that this effect reduces the annual energy production
in a wind farm by 10%-15%, compared to if the wind turbines stood separately [2].
However, even though the blockage effects are known since many years, the reduction
on the annual energy production caused by them has been ignored by the industry for
a long time. The blockage effects are a consequence of the wind turbine extracting
energy from the wind, causing the wind to slowdown in front of the turbine. For
a single wind turbine, Borraccino et al. [3] observed that at 2.5D upstream of
the turbine, the wind experiences a speed deficit up to 0.7% due to the turbine’s
induction. Although the blocking effect of the individual wind turbine itself is small,
the sum of all contributions is not insignificant [2].

1 Introduction
Wind farm modelling focuses on characterize the wake flow behind wind turbines.
Several wake models have been proposed to describe this phenomenon. In general,
the models can be divided as shown in Figure 1.1. The starting points are models
based on Large Eddy Simulation (LES) where the wake interaction is implicit, and
then the models based on the Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes equations (RANS),
which is a simplification of LES. From the RANS equations, steady state models have
been developed and are divided into two categories: linearized RANS equations and
semi-empiric engineering wake models. In the first category, Fuga is an engineering
tool based on the linearized RANS equations which constructs the velocity field be-
hind a turbine by using a system of look-up tables [4]. Regarding the semi-empiric
engineering wake models, Jensen [5] proposed a linearly expanding top-hat inflow
profile as function of the downstream distance x. Larsen [6] introduced a wake cal-
culation procedure using the axi-symmetric form of the RANS equations with the
thin shear layer approximation [4]. Finally, Bastankhah and Porté-Agel [7] de-
rived an analytical model based on mass and momentum conservation together with
the assumption of a self-similar Gaussian shape of the velocity deficit. From LES,
dynamic models have been developed such as the dynamic wake meandering model
proposed by Larsen et al. [8], [9] and Madsen et al. [10] which describes the wake
as a passive tracer driven by the large-scale turbulence structures in the atmospheric
boundary layer [4].
In general, the Jensen [5], Larsen [6], Gaussian [7] and Fuga [11] models fall into
the single category of engineering wake interaction models, as shown in Figure 1.1.
For wind farms, all the wake effects of upstream turbines account to the total velocity
deficit, which is obtained by using the wake superposition concept where the deficit
is computed, e.g., by geometric sum, linear sum, energy balance, or quadratic sum
[4]. In general, these models are still useful tools to predict wind turbine wake flows
and their effect on power production. Moreover, they are widely used due to their
simplicity and lower computational cost [7].
So far, the aforementioned wake models refer to the wind turbines on the upstream
edges of a wind farm as “free”, “free-stream” and/or “undisturbed” [12]. In other
words, the models make the assumption that a wind turbine only affects turbines
located downstream. This assumption is called the “wakes-only” approach to wind
turbine interaction. The experiments performed by Dahlberg and Hägglund [13],
and the model developed by Ebenhoch et al. [14] and Segalini [15], indicated,
however, that the assumption that the first row is unaffected by the rest of the
farm is incorrect. Bleeg et al. [12] showed that measurements from three wind
farms revealed that the upstream wind speed of each wind farm decrease relative
to locations farther away after the turbines are turned on.
At a distance of two
rotor diameters upstream, the average reduction in wind speed was found to be 3.6%
[12]. Furthermore, Branlard [16] showed that the wind speed at a distance of 2.5D
in front of a wind farm may easily be reduced by 3% with respect to the actual
incoming flow speed. Moreover, Segalini and Dahlberg [17] made an experimental
and numerical study of upstream effects of wind farms. The results indicated that
the effect of the wind farm is perceived farther upstream than the 2-4 turbine rotor

Currently, three different blockage effects have been identified in prior studies:
wind turbine blockage, wind farm blockage, and change of the geostrophic wind.
Regarding the wind turbine blockage, Medici et al. [18] and Simley et al. [19]
have shown that the main impact of a wind turbine on the induction region, which is
the region upstream of the turbine, is the reduction in wind speed. This reduction can
be estimated based on the vortex sheet theory [18]. Also, Bleeg et al. [12] referred
to a local turbine-scale blockage effect that contributes to the upstream slowdown.
In recent years, some models have been developed to assess the induction zone
in front of a wind turbine. For example, Branlard and Forsting [20] applied an
analytical formulae for the velocity field induced by a cylindrical vortex wake model
to assess the induction zone in front of aligned and yawed rotors. The results from the
model are compared to CFD actuator disk simulations for different operating controls.
The mean relative error is estimated in the induction zone and found to be below 0.4%
for the aligned flows tests and below 1.3% for the yawed ones [20]. Moreover, the
simulation time required by the model is in the order of thousands less than the
time required by the actuator disk simulations. In another study, Troldborg and
Forsting [21] proposed a simple model of the wind turbine induction zone derived

from numerical simulations called Self-similarity (SS) model. The model describes the
relation between the streamwise velocities in the induction zone to the free-stream
velocity. Several simulations were performed using the incrompressible RANS solver
EllypSys3D [22], [23] for different wind turbines and operating conditions to obtain
the induction profile of each scenario. Troldborg and Forsting [21] showed that
for distances beyond 1R upstream of the rotor, the induced velocity is self-similar
and independent of the rotor geometry.
Self-similar means that the shape of the
profiles stays unchanged with upstream position. Moreover, the induction profile can
be expressed as a function of the thrust coefficient CT and the free-stream velocity
U∞. Since the model has only 2 unknowns, CT and U∞(or only CT if the normalized
wind speed is used), if they are provided, then, the model can predict the velocity
everywhere more than 1R upstream of any wind turbine. Furthermore, Troldborg
and Forsting [21] showed that the maximum deviation from the simple model to
the RANS results is less than 0.7% of the free-stream velocity in the entire region 1R
upstream of the wind turbine.
Regarding the wind farm blockage, Segalini and Dahlberg [17] performed an
experimental and numerical study of wind farm blockage to quantify the velocity
reduction that the first row of a wind farm experiences due to downstream wind
turbines. Porté-Agel et al. [24] mentioned that there is an accumulated blockage
effect induced by the wind farm as a whole, producing a deceleration of the incoming
boundary-layer flow which deflects upwards and sideways due to mass conservation.
Branlard [16] showed that the extent and strength of the wind farm blockage depends
on the wind farm size, layout, wind direction, turbine spacing, and thrust coefficient.
Furthermore, Branlard [20] showed that the reduction of the wind speed at 2.5D
upstream of a wind farm, increases as the wind turbine spacing decreases. Moreover,
the reduction in wind speed increases when CT does. Another result is the influence
of the wind farm layout. At a distance of 2.5D upstream of a squared, single row,
and single column wind farms, the reduction in wind speed is approximately 0.2%,
0.4% and 1% respectively, compared to the actual free-stream velocity when the wind
turbine spacing is 2D.
As for the blockage due to change of the geostrophic wind, some studies have inves-
tigated the interaction of wind farms with the atmospheric boundary layer. Volker
et al. [25] showed, for neutral atmospheric conditions, that large offshore wind farms
become less efficient due to smaller momentum supply from aloft over smooth water
surfaces. In the case where thermal effects are considered, Wu and Porté-Agel [26]
investigated the flow inside and around large finite-size wind farms in neutral atmo-
spheric boundary layers using two free-atmosphere stratification levels Γ. For Γ = 5
K/km, Wu and Porté-Agel [26] showed that the vertical deflection of the subcriti-
cal flow induced by the wind farms triggers gravity waves, which propagates upstream
of the wind farms generating a high pressure region at the leading edge, which ex-
tends far upstream of the farms. Moreover, the gravity-wave-induced blockage effect,
triggered by the wind farm, decelerates the flow upwind of the farm significantly.
Two engineering tools have been developed to simulate the interaction of wind
turbines with the free-stream: PyWake and PyWakeRANS. PyWake is an AEP cal-

Based on the previous discussion, the present thesis aims to answer the following
questions:
1. Can Fuga be used to predict the induction zone of a wind turbine?
2. Can PyWake, using the Self-similarity model and Fuga, predict the power pro-
duction of a wind farm with a fair agreement with RANS simulations?
3. What are the effects of the single wind turbine blockage?
4. What are the effects of the wind farm blockage?
5. How is blockage affecting the AEP and can blockage explain Ørsted reduced
expectations?
In order to answer this questions, the present thesis is divided in several chapters.
An overview of PyWake and PyWakeRANS is given in Chapter 2 describing their
structure and parameters used throughout the thesis. The single wind turbine block-
age effects are studied in Chapter 3, addressing the induction models and comparing
them with a RANS simulation. Furthermore, an AEP sensitivity analysis is presented.
Subsequently, the blockage effects in wind farms are investigated and the consequence
of omit them, in terms of AEP, is discussed in Chapter 4. The research question #1
(RQ1) is answered in Section 2.3.5. RQ2 and RQ3 are answered in Chapter 3 and
Chapter 4, respectively. The findings of RQ3 are used to answer RQ4. Lastly, the
findings presented through Chapter 3 and Chapter 4 enable the answering of RQ5.
Furthermore, it is important to mention that the blockage due to change of the
geostrophic wind is out of the scope of this study.

2.1
Overview
PyWake is an open source wind farm simulation tool implemented in Python capable
of calculating flow fields, power production and annual energy production (AEP) of
wind farms [27]. An overview of PyWake is shown in Figure 2.1. In general, PyWake
can be divided in three objects:
• Site: For a given position, reference wind speed WSref, and wind direction
WDref, provides the local wind condition in terms of wind speed WS, wind di-
rection WD, turbulence intensity TI and the probability of each combination of
wind direction and wind speed. Furthermore, Site is responsible for calculating
the downwind, crosswind and vertical distance between wind turbines [28].
• WindTurbines: For a given wind turbine type and effective wind speed WSeﬀ,
provides the power and thrust coefficient CT , as well as the wind turbine hub
height H and diameter D [28].
• WindFarmModel: For a given set of wind turbines, defined in terms of their
position and type, returns a SimulationResult object containing the calculated
effective wind speed, power production, thrust coefficient as well as methods to
calculate the AEP and generate flow maps [28].

2.3
Engineering models
In Section 2.3.1, an overview of the elements that compose the engineering wind farm
models in PyWake is given. Then, the deficit models (Section 2.3.2), the superposition
model (Section 2.3.3), and the rotor averaged model (Section 2.3.4) used in the thesis
are described. Lastly, two cases are studied in Section 2.3.5 to show the use of Fuga
as blockage deficit model and to demonstrate its unsuitability to predict blockage
effects.
2.3.1
Overview
The engineering wind farm models in PyWake are composed of one of two wind
farms models in combination with a wake model, a blockage model, a superposition
model and, optionally, with a deflection and a turbulence model [28], as illustrated
in Figure 2.4.
Figure 2.4: Overview diagram of the engineering models in PyWake. Figure obtained
from [28].
A brief description of each element shown in Figure 2.4 is presented next as de-
scribed in [28]:
• WindFarmModel: Defines the procedure that determines how wake and block-
age deficits propagates in the wind farm.
==PAGE:19==
2.3 Engineering models
11
• WakeDeficitModel: Calculates wake deficit from one wind turbine to down-
stream turbines or sites in the wind farm.
• SuperpositionModel: Defines how deficits from multiple sources sum up.
• BlockageDeficitModel: Calculates blockage deficit from one wind turbine to
upstream turbines or sites in the wind farm.
• RotorAvgModel: Calculates the rotor-averaged wind speed by doing a (weighted)
mean of the wind speed at a number of points covering the rotor.
• DeflectionModel: Defines the wake deflection due to yaw misalignment, sheared
inflow, etc., but modifying the down- and crosswind distances.
• TurbulenceModel: Calculates added turbulence in the wake from one wind tur-
bine to downstream turbines or sites in the wind farm.
2.3.2
Deficit models
It is important to mention that DeficitModel models the wake and/or blockage deficit
caused by a single wind turbine [30]. For that, 3 different models can be selected as
WakeDeficitModel (NOJ, Fuga and Gaussian) and 2 models as BlockageDeficitModel
(Fuga and Self-similarity). The deficit models are listed below and, in order to exem-
plify, the deficit map computed using each model is shown in Figure 2.5.
• NOJDeficit: Implemented according to Jensen [5], i.e., a top-hat wake. Valid
only in the far wake. Figure 2.5a.
• FugaDeficit: Based on a set up of look up tables as described by Ott et al.
[11]. The model models both near/far wake and blockage deficit. Figure 2.5b.
• GaussianDeficit: The model is implemented according to Bastankhah and

Fuga as blockage deficit model
As mention before, PyWake can use several wake models to estimate the power pro-
duction, thus, the annual energy production of wind farms. Recently, the framework
of PyWake has been updated by adding a new engineering wind farm model called
All2AllIterative [27] so it can calculate the wake and blockage deficits from all wind
turbines to all points of interest. The calculations follow an iterative approach until
convergence, e.g., the change of effective wind speed is less than a certain convergence
tolerance. In each iteration, the deficit from all the wind turbines in a wind farm is
summed up and, then, the deficit caused by this “global deficit” on all wind turbines
is computed [30].
In general, Fuga is a fast and reasonably accurate model for the prediction of
energy production in offshore wind farms [11]. Fuga can model both near/far wake and
blockage deficits, however, the velocity profiles calculated by Fuga present “spurious

used by Fuga. Ott et al. [11] described that the solution method consists of using
the fast Fourier integral transform (FFIT) of results derived from generic look-up
tables, and that some inaccuracies occur when making interpolations in the tables
and when the FFIT is applied. Moreover, it is suspected that these interpolation

2.4
PywakeRANS
In this section, an overview of CFD-RANS simulations is given (Section 2.4.1). Then,
the simulation set-up in PyWakeRANS is described by defining the grid, domain
and turbulence model in Section 2.4.2; and the AD model and the force method in
Section 2.4.3. Lastly, the difference between inflow and local wind speed found in the
simulation of a “dummy” wind turbine is presented (Section 2.4.4).
2.4.1
Overview
A CFD-RANS simulation has the advantage that the flow in the up- and downstream
region of a wind turbine is solved, thus, the blockage effects of a wind turbine are taken
into account intrinsically during the solution of the RANS equations. However, the
computational resources and time required by the simulations are much more than the
ones used by engineering tools such as PyWake. As mentioned before, the WindFarm-
Model object in PyWake can use CFD-RANS to calculate the power production of
wind turbines. PyWakeRANS is a Python interface to EllipSys3D [22], [23] that can
be used in the same way as the other engineering wake models in PyWake [31].
2.4.2
Grid and Domain definition
As described in [31], the domain of the RANS model consists of a box-shaped flow
domain.
The domain uses a Cartesian coordinate system where x and y are the
horizontal coordinates, which denote the streamwise and crosswind directions respec-
tively; and z is the vertical direction, also called height. Inside the flow domain, a
smaller uniform spaced (along the x and y directions) domain is defined where the
wind farm is placed, and the wakes and induction zones of the wind turbines are
resolved.
The RANS model requires a numerical grid that can be used by EllipSys3D; thus,
a grid is defined in the flow domain. A scheme of the grid and its parameters, together
with the boundary conditions of the domain, are shown in Figure 2.11. In general,
most of the grid parameters are normalized by the rotor diameter D and this is
indicated by “_D”, as seen in the figure. The grid spacing in the refined wind farm
domain is determined by only one parameter: D/n, which determines the size of the
grid cells in the area where the wakes are located; n is the number of cells covering

2.4.3
AD model and Force method
PyWakeRANS represents the wind turbines as actuator disks (AD). An AD consists
of a polar grid at which the wind turbine forces are prescribed or calculated from the
local flow [31]. The AD polar grid is connected with the main flow domain grid [31],
and is determined by an equidistant number of radial, nr, and azimuthal, nθ, points.
Baungaard [33] showed that an AD polar grid with nr = 64 and nθ = 64 has a
relative difference in power and wake recovery of less than 0.1%, compared to finer
AD grids; hence, these values are used in the present project.
Moreover, the AD force method used in the thesis is the 2111 [31]. The first index
2xxx indicates that the analytical force distributions are based on a Joukowsky rotor.
The second index, x1xx, indicates that tangential forces, which induce wake rotation,
are included. The third index, xx1x, indicates that the force distributions are non-
axi-symmetryc; hence, inflow effects, such as sheer and veer, are included. Lastly, the
forth index, xxx1, indicates that the control of the rotor is obtained by a calibration
based on the CT and rpm curves of the wind turbine.

CHAPTER 4
Wind farm blockage
So far, the single wind turbine blockage effect has been study in Chapter 3. This
chapter investigates the blockage effect when more than one wind turbine is con-
sidered, i.e., a wind farm. Throughout the farm, both wake and blockage deficits,
generated by each single wind turbine, interact from all wind turbines to all wind
turbines. Moreover, this interaction changes the effective wind speed of each wind
turbine, thus, the power production is affected by the presence of nearby turbines.
First, in Section 4.1, the power production of each wind turbine in a 2x4 wind farm
is compared between deficit models and a RANS simulation. Then, the downstream
blockage (Section 4.2) and crosswind blockage (Section 4.3) are investigated. Subse-
quently, the feature in PyWake that enables to “turn on/off” the blockage deficit is
used in Section 4.4. Furthermore, the additional blockage effects caused by adding
extra rows of wind turbines in a wind farm is presented in Section 4.5. The afore-
mentioned studies enables to answer RQ4. Lastly, the consequence of considering
blockage effects in the AEP of a wind farm is investigated in Section 4.7. Based on
the results of this study, RQ5 is answered.
4.1
Power production / Engineering models and RANS
As mentioned in Section 2.3.2, PyWake has other predefined deficit models besides
Fuga, such as the Jensen [5] and Gaussian [7] models. Hence, in this section the power
production of each wind turbine in a 2x4 wind farm is investigated using different
deficits models. Regarding the Jensen and Gaussian models, the wake expansion coef-
ficient and the wake growth rate are set to k = 0.04 and k∗= 0.0324555 respectively,
as discussed in Appendix A.1. Furthermore, a RANS simulation is used to verify the
results obtained by the engineering models. The layout of the wind farm is shown in

interest both wake and blockage deficits, as discussed in Appendix B.1.

Conclusions and

Conclusions
The single wind turbine blockage effect was defined as the upstream wind speed deficit
caused by the interaction of the wind turbine with the free-stream. Two deficit models
were tested using the new All2AllIterative engineering wind farm model in PyWake
to predict the blockage deficit: Fuga and Fuga+SS.
When investigating the use of Fuga as both blockage and wake model, it is found
that the wriggles, from its solution method, cause nonphysical effects that result in
the over/under-estimation of the power production of neighbouring wind turbines.
Therefore, Fuga is unsuitable as blockage model.
In contrast, Fuga+SS is a better alternative to predict the blockage effects of a
single wind turbine. When compared against RANS, the induction zone computed
by Fuga+SS is in good agreement, with relative differences around 0.1%.
In order to determine if it is plausible that Ørsted’s load factor is decreased
from 48%-50% to 48%, an AEP sensitivity analysis is proposed and, as a result,
two wind speed change intervals are found: ∆u0.5 = [−0.35%, 0.38%] and ∆u0.1 =
[−0.07%, 0.08%], which corresponds to a resolution in AEP of 0.5% and 0.1%, respec-
tively.
These intervals are used to determine the accuracy of Fuga+SS against the RANS
simulation of a single wind turbine. When comparing the wind speed profiles com-
puted by Fuga+SS and RANS, it is found that, although the SS model yields relative
differences to RANS within the interval ∆u0.5, the current state of Fuga is not pos-
sible to achieve the desired accuracy. The relative difference between Fuga+SS and
RANS in the wake region x > 3D is between −2.97% and 11.55%. Therefore, this
difference is outside both wind speed change intervals.
Moreover, the blockage deficit is up to 2% less than the free-stream speed in the
upstream region above 2D. Hence, the blockage deficit is less than the accuracy of the
wake deficit, which is between −4.33% and 11.48% in terms of AEP variation. This
indicates that the wake model must be improved in order to obtain more accurate
results when blockage and wake deficits from different wind turbines interact, i.e.,
when simulating wind farms.

5 Conclusions and Future research
Several blockage effects in wind farms were studied: crosswind blockage, down-
stream blockage, the effect of “turn on/off” the blockage deficit, the additional block-
age caused by adding extra rows of wind turbines in a wind farm, the effect of wind
farm spacing on the blockage, and the effect of omitting blockage effects on the AEP
of wind farms.
When investigating the crosswind blockage, it is found that a wind turbine can
slightly increase its power production when placing crosswind wind turbines on each
side of it. Moreover, this enhancement is observed to increase as more wind turbines
are added to the row. However, in comparison with RANS, this effect is underes-
timated by Fuga+SS because of the smaller crosswind deficit predicted by the SS
model.
The downstream blockage effect results in the decreased power production of a
reference wind turbine when wind turbines are placed downstream of it. Hence, the
blockage deficit of downstream wind turbines affects the inflow of the upstream ones
in such a way that it is no longer the free-stream. In comparison with the RANS
simulations, Fuga+SS can predict the same behaviour, however, it underestimates
the blockage. This underestimation can be attributed to the different wake deficit
predicted by each method. In comparison with the NOJ+SS model, the wake deficit
computed by Fuga+SS has a worse agreement with RANS. Nevertheless, Fuga+SS
does not have an empirical wake expansion parameter dependency.
When computing the power production of a wind farm with and without blockage
effects, it is found that Fuga+SS predicts a power decrease for all the wind turbines
in the wind farm, except for the most downstream ones, which presented a gain in
power, when the blockage effects are considered. The most downstream wind turbines
have this gain in power because of the absence of a downstream wind turbine and the
reduced thrust of the neighbour upstream wind turbine.
By placing extra rows of wind turbines on the top and bottom sides of a reference
wind farm, the RANS simulations predicted that an additional blockage is induced,
which yields a power redistribution along the middle row of the wind farm. The power
production of the first two upstream wind turbines is decreased and this is reflected
in the downstream turbines as a power increase. Moreover the total power of the
row is slightly increased, meaning that, besides the power redistribution, the induced
blockage forces more wind between the rows of the reference wind farm. The same
study is carried out using Fuga+SS and results in an overestimation of the blockage
effects, which exposed the influence of the nonphysical wriggles, predicted by Fuga,
on the power production of wind turbines.
This highlights again the necessity of
improving Fuga’s solution method.
The AEP of a wind farm is decreased when blockage effects are considered. In
the case of a 2x4 wind farm, the AEP is decreased around 0.23% meanwhile for the
Horns Rev 1 offshore wind farm it is around 0.33%.
Even though this reduction
seems to be small, by doing a quick income assessment, it is showed that neglecting
blockage effects can overestimate the predicted income of a wind farm, which agrees
with Ørsted’s decision of decreasing the load factor of its wind farms. However, as
explained before, Fuga+SS cannot predict this effect with the required accuracy.

Future research
Based on the results obtained in the thesis, some suggestions are provided as future
research.
Improvement of Fuga wake model
The wriggles predicted by Fuga could be minimized by using look up tables based
on RANS simulations, or maybe by replacing the look up table method by fitting
Gaussian functions.
Implementation of more induction models
More induction models are now available in literature and the code to compute the
induction field using these models is open source. Based on this, the open source code
can be adapted and implemented in PyWake to expand its catalogue of induction
models.
Increase the RANS simulations accuracy
During the wind farm blockage study, a grid size of D/8 was used to minimize com-
putational costs, as 80 wind turbines were simulated. The wind farm blockage results
to be in the order of magnitude of 0.1%, hence, based on the grid study, the minimum
grid size that should be used is D/16. Therefore, a more accurate study would be
done by using at least grid sizes of D/16.
Investigate blockage effects under different conditions
Several studies can be done around the blockage effects. Throughout the thesis, only
one value of thrust coefficient was considered, CT = 0.806. Therefore, the blockage
effects can be investigated at lower (or higher) thrust coefficient. Moreover, the effect
of the wind farm layout on the blockage effects could be investigated to see optimum
conditions to minimize (or increment if its the case) the power production of the wind
farm.
Furthermore, the turbulence intensity that represents an offshore site was used.
Therefore, it would be interesting to investigate blockage effects under different tur-
bulence intensity conditions, such as for an onshore wind farm.

m/s), hence, the wake behind this wind turbine recovers faster so wt46 experiences
a higher inflow wind speed, thus, a higher power production than wt45. Then, the
wake of wt46 cannot recover so fast (because of the increased thrust of wt46) and
wt47 experiences a lower wind speed, then, the same behaviour of wt45 and wt46 is
repeated for wt48 and wt49.
In terms of the total power production of the middle row, Table A.2 shows how
RANS and Fuga+SS predict an enhanced total production when the extra rows of
wind turbines are placed at the bottom and top sides of the 3x10 wind farm. However,
Fuga+SS overestimates this effect due to the speed ups discussed in Section 4.5. Fur-
thermore, NOJ+SS and Guassian+SS predicts a decreased total power production.

APPENDIX B
Grid study
It is well known that the grid size in a RANS simulation controls the solver and
its degree of accuracy. Thus, a good grid size has to be chosen in order to obtain
meaningful and representative results of the flow phenomena being simulated. How-
ever, it is always necessary to keep in mind to have a balance between accuracy and
computational cost.
The influence of the grid size on the wind speed field, yield by the interaction
of a single wind turbine with the free-stream, is investigated in Appendix B.1. Fur-
thermore, the influence of the grid size on the wind farm blockage is presented in
Appendix B.2.
B.1
Single wind turbine
The interaction between a single V80 wind turbine, at CT = 0.806, and the free-
stream is simulated using the following grid sizes: D/8, D/16 and D/32. The latter
is considered as the reference case because D/32 corresponds to a finer mesh, as
discussed in Section 2.4.2.
The change of the wind speed field, when the grid size is reduced from D/32 to
D/16 and D/8, is shown in Figure B.1. Note that, to compare the results from dif-
ferent grid sizes, the wind speed field of each simulation has to be interpolated to
the same xy-points; hence, it is important to acknowledge that there are some inter-
polation errors added when computing the relative difference between the simulation
results.
In the upstream region of the wind turbine, both grid size reductions yield a wind
speed change between −0.1% and 0.1%, as observed in Figure B.1. Moreover, the
same change is found at crosswind distances above 2D.
Hence, these regions are
slightly affected by the grid size.
On the other hand, the wake of the wind turbine is highly affected by the grid
size. The maximum change yield by reducing the grid size to D/8 and D/16 is 24.6%
and 9.4%, respectively. However, in the region of interest x > 3D, this change is up
to 6% and 2%, in absolute value, for D/8 and D/16, respectively. Furthermore, in
the speed ups around the wind turbine rotor, the change is up to 5% and 1% for D/8
and D/16, respectively.

B.2
Wind farm blockage
The influence of the grid size on the blockage is investigated. For that, the blockage
generated by adding crosswind turbines at the sides of a 1x2 wind farm is computed
using D/4, D/8, D/16 and D/32. The wind farm layouts used in the grid study are
shown in Figure B.3. The reference case is a 1x2 wind farm with a spacing of 5D.
The second farm is the same 1x2 wind farm (blue colored in Figure B.3), but adding a
wind turbine to the left- and right-hand side of the row; thus, the wind farm becomes
of size 1x4.
The free-stream velocity in both simulations is U0 = 8 ms−1 and perpendicular to
the wind farm. Furthermore, a turbulence intensity of 10% is considered. The power
production of the reference wind turbines in each wind farm layout is obtained from
the simulations and, then, the blockage is computed using Equation 2.4. In this case,
Pref corresponds to the sum of the power of both wind turbines in the reference case,
wt0 and wt1 in Figure B.3; meanwhile, P is the sum of the power of the same wind
turbines but in the 1x4 wind farm, blue colored wt1 and wt2 in Figure B.3.
The blockage for each grid size, and the relative difference to the reference case
of D/32, is shown in Table B.2. As observed, the relative difference between D/16
and D/32 is only 3.6%.
So, for blockage in the order of magnitude of 0.1%, the
grid size of D/16 can fairly predict the results of D/32, which enables to reduce
computational costs. The simulation with a grid size of D/32 required 200 CPU’s
and ran for 158, 400 seconds; meanwhile, the one with a grid size of D/16 used 40
CPU’s and ran for 115, 200 seconds.
