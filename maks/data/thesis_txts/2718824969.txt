ABSTRACT 
Great effort has been put in recent years in the development of new technologies for the 
sustainable generation of chemical compounds. Hydrogen peroxide is considered as one of the 
greenest oxidizing agents, since its only by-product being water. Because of its oxidizing 
properties, it is used in a wide range of applications. Currently, the main process being used for 
hydrogen peroxide generation is the anthraquinone auto-oxidation (AO) process, which 
presents a high yield but has also some disadvantages. The main disadvantages of the AO 
process are the use of complex and toxic solvent system, the generation of wastewater 
contaminated with hydrogen peroxide and organic solvents and being limited only for-large 
scale production. Several alternatives have emerged as an alternative to the AO process, among 
them, the electrochemical generation of hydrogen peroxide. Electrochemical generation of 
hydrogen peroxide is based on the oxidation of water in the anode through the oxygen evolution 
reaction (OER) and the reduction of oxygen in the cathode through the 2-electron oxygen 
reduction reaction (ORR) in acidic conditions. 
HPNow company is focused on the development of hydrogen peroxide reactors based on 
polymer exchange membrane (PEM) electrolysis cells for the generation of hydrogen peroxide. 
The system is based on a membrane-electrode assembly (MEA), which contains both catalysts 
for the anode and cathode half-cell reactions, and a proton conductive Nafion ® membrane 
which allows the movement of protons from the anode to the cathode side. Until now, 
concentrated oxygen (95%) at about atmospheric pressure has been used as the feeding gas for 
the cathode half-cell reaction. However, the oxygen concentrators used to provide the cathode 
with concentrated oxygen have a high energy consumption and high maintenance costs. For 
that reason, the use of compressed air at a pressure of 6 bar has been investigated as a lower 
energy consumption and more economically feasible alternative. This project is focused on the 
investigation and optimization of electrochemical cells using air at 6 bar at the cathode side 
instead of concentrated oxygen. The long-term electrochemical performance (faradaic 
efficiency and voltage) of cells running with air at 6 bar is studied. A comparison between the 
performance of cells running with air 1.2 bar and 6 bar is also offered in this project. Finally, 
metal contamination in the cathode side is investigated through EDX and ICP-MS.

1. Introduction 
Hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) is a clear and colourless liquid, miscible with water in all 
proportions. Because of its high active oxygen content (47.1%), it is one of the most efficient 
oxidizing agents, with a high oxidation potential and effectiveness in a wide range of pH [1]. It is 
considered as one of the greenest oxidizing agents since its only by-product is water [2].  
1.1. Hydrogen peroxide applications 
Because of its properties, hydrogen peroxide is used in a wide range of applications. 
Figure 1 shows the most important applications of hydrogen peroxide. 
 
Figure 1. Main applications of hydrogen peroxide [1]. 
One of the most important applications of hydrogen peroxide is its use in pulp and paper 
bleaching, where its being used as an environmentally friendly alternative to chlorine and 
chorine containing bleaching agents [3,4]. Hydrogen peroxide is also used as a bleaching agent 
in the textile industry. Another important use of hydrogen peroxide is in the treatment of a 
variety of industrial wastes and wastewaters, since hydrogen peroxide is capable of 
decomposing nitrate, chlorine, hypochlorite, thiocyanate, sulphide, and other toxic chemicals 
present in wastewater [5–8]. Hydrogen peroxide is also involved in several applications in the 
mining and processing of metals. It is used for the extraction and purification of uranium, 
recovery of gold, extraction and separation of cobalt, copper, tungsten, and molybdenum, 
among other metals [9,10]. High purity water with hydrogen peroxide is used in the 
microelectronics industry for cleaning germanium and silicon semiconductors wafers and 
etching printed circuit boards (PCB) [1,11]. The cosmetic and pharmaceutical industries use 
disinfecting properties of hydrogen peroxide. In the production of detergents, it is used as a 
==PAGE:8==
-2- 
substitute for hypochlorite [12]. Finally, hydrogen peroxide finds some applications in the 
chemical synthesis of organic compounds. It is involved in processes such as oxidation [13–15], 
hydroxylation [16–18], and oxohalogenation reactions[19], among others. Since 2008, the 
demand of hydrogen peroxide for the chemical industry has increased significantly because of 
the development of the hydrogen peroxide-propylene oxide (HPPO) process, in which the 
hydrogen peroxide is required for the epoxidation of propylene [20].

As shown in Figure 2, the main use of hydrogen peroxide in 2021 was in the pulp and 
paper industry, followed by the chemical synthesis, and the textile industry. 
 
1.2. Hydrogen peroxide production 
Hydrogen peroxide was first made by J.L. Thénard in 1818 [22], who acidified barium 
peroxide according to reaction shown in equation 1, and removed the excess water by 
evaporation under reduced pressure.

Based on this reaction, commercial production of hydrogen peroxide begun around 
1880. Industrial plants using the barium peroxide process were still operating until the mid-
1900s [23]. Around beginning of the 1900s, approximately 2·103 t/a of hydrogen peroxide were 
produced by this process. Produced hydrogen peroxide from the barium oxide yielded a solution 
with a concentration of around 3%. However, this solution found only limited applications 
because of high production cost, low hydrogen peroxide content and unsatisfactory stability due 
to impurities [24,25].

After the introduction of the electrochemical processes, production rate increased 
steadily and in 1950, reached approximately 35·103 t/a (calculated as 100% hydrogen peroxide)

On an industrial scale, hydrogen peroxide now is almost exclusively prepared by the 
auto-oxidation (AO) process. Around 95% of hydrogen peroxide is produced by the AO process 
[29]. The first commercial AO process, also known as Rield-Pfleiderer process, was developed 
between 1939 and 1945 at I.G. Farbenindustrie AG [30].  
 
1.2.1. Anthraquinone auto-oxidation (AO) process 
In the AO process, an alkylated anthraquinone (normally 2-ethylantrhaquinone) is 
dissolved in a mixture of organic solvents (usually ester/hydrocarbon or alcohol/hydrocarbon 
mixtures) and reduced by a Raney nickel or supported palladium catalyst to the corresponding 
quinol. The catalyst is then separated (to avoid hydrogen peroxide decomposition) and the 
quinol non-catalytically reoxidized with oxygen (usually a stream of air) with simultaneous 
quantitative formation of hydrogen peroxide. Figure 3 shows the catalytic cycle used in the 
production of hydrogen peroxide through the AO process.  
 
 
==PAGE:10==
-4- 
 
Figure 3. Catalytic cycle used in the manufacture of hydrogen peroxide through the AO process [27]. 
After the catalytic cycle, hydrogen peroxide is extracted by water and concentrated by 
distillation under reduced pressure. Therefore, the raw materials for the anthraquinone AO 
process are hydrogen, anthraquinone, and air [24–26,31,32]. Figure 4 shows a block diagram of 
the main steps that are involved in the industrial production of hydrogen peroxide through the 
AO process. 
 
Figure 4. Block diagram of the AO process for the production of hydrogen peroxide [33]. 
As can be seen, the main steps involved in the industrial production of hydrogen peroxide are:  
1. Hydrogenation: 
From a storage tank the working solution (organic solution dissolving anthraquinone) enters 
the hydrogenator, where it is hydrogenated in the presence of a slurry or fixed-bed catalyst, at 
a temperature between 40 and 70 ˚C, and a H2 pressure of 4 atm. The catalyst used in plants 
nowadays is palladium, normally supported in Al2O3. Historically, Raney nickel has been used as 
catalyst, but it is no longer in industrial use due to the need to operate under alkaline conditions, 
its pyrophoric properties, and its toxicity even in very low concentrations in the hydrogen 
peroxide product [25].  
==PAGE:11==
-5- 
It is particularly important that the product mixture does not contain leached catalyst 
(palladium or Raney nickel) because even traces of it will catalyse the decomposition of H2O2 in 
the oxidation and extraction steps. If a slurry stirring tank reactor is used, the hydrogenation 
step includes a main filtration stage that retains the catalyst and allows to be return to the 
hydrogenator. Rather than old slurry-type hydrogenation reactors equipped with filters, modern 
technology employs a fixed-bed reactor that avoids the need for filtration [33]. By using fixed-
bed reactors, product mixture is easily separated from the hydrogenation catalyst to be aerated 
with compressed air in the AO step. Before the oxidation step, the heat of reaction resulting 
from the hydrogenation needs to be removed, normally by cooling the working solution after 
the hydrogenation.  
2. Oxidation:  
During the oxidation step, the hydrogenated working solution is gassed by bubbling 
compressed air at a pressure of 5 bar at slightly higher temperature than room temperature. 
Dissolved hydroquinones are non-catalytically oxidized to quinones, and hydrogen peroxide is 
formed. After a residence time of around 40 min in the oxidation reactor, a diluted solution of 
hydrogen peroxide at 0.6–1.8 wt% is obtained [31], along with regenerated anthraquinone. The 
loss of quinone in the reaction cycle is compensated for by the periodic addition of fresh 
anthraquinone to the working solution.  
3. Extraction and recovery of the working solution: 
The hydrogen peroxide present in the oxidized working solution, is extracted from the 
organic phase with demineralized water in an extraction column. The working solution is added 
to the top of a tall (>30 m) liquid–liquid extraction column. To ensure maximum contact of water 
with the working solution, water flows down the column over perforated trays, while the 
working solution is pumped up the column. The aqueous crude reaching the bottom of the 
column contains 25–35 wt% (w/w) of hydrogen peroxide. The hydrogen peroxide free working 
solution that leaves the top of the column is adjusted to a specific water content and then 
pumped back to the hydrogenator. Therefore, the working solution containing the organic 
solvent and anthraquinone is recycled, after having removed the excess water taken during the 
extraction [25]. 
4. Hydrogen peroxide concentration: 
Crude aqueous hydrogen peroxide from the extraction stage with a concentration of 25-35 
wt% is purified and concentrated through distillation. With steam, hydrogen peroxide does not 
form azeotropic mixtures with water and, therefore, can be completely separated by distillation. 
Typical commercial grades are in the range between 50 and 70 wt%. During distillation, 
hydrogen peroxide is also freed from most of its impurities. After distillation, the concentrated 
hydrogen peroxide is condensed, stabilized against decomposition by adding stabilizers and 
pumped to product storage tanks [25,31]. 
==PAGE:12==
-6- 
The AO process is currently the main method used for production of hydrogen peroxide, 
mainly because its advantages against other methods. The main advantages of the AO process 
are: 
• 
The process avoids explosive H2/O2 mixture, which makes the AO process safe for large-
scale and continuous operation.  
• 
The process has a very high yield of hydrogen peroxide per cycle [33]. 
However, the AO process also has some disadvantages towards emerging alternatives. The main 
disadvantages of the AO process are: 
• 
The process can only be operated at large scale, thus some security measures during 
transportation and storage must be undertaken [29]. 
• 
The process generates wastewater contaminated with ppm levels of peroxide, organic 
solvent and quinone residues [31]. 
• 
The process uses a complex and toxic solvent system. 
• 
The working solution and hydrogenation catalyst need to be regenerated because of 
side reactions in the hydrogenation step [33]. 
• 
The produced hydrogen peroxide contains trace of organic impurities, which require to 
be further purified [34]. 
The AO process is economically viable only for a large-scale production (>40·103 t/a). 
Therefore, hydrogen peroxide is produced in one location and then transported, creating 
additional safety concerns since high concentration hydrogen peroxide can be explosive if it 
violently decomposes. The high capital investment and operating costs for the AO process 
economically prohibit producing hydrogen peroxide on-site at the end users facility [32].  
1.2.2. Direct synthesis 
Direct synthesis of hydrogen peroxide is an alternative to the AO process. It implies 
direct reaction between oxygen and hydrogen to produce hydrogen peroxide. Great attention 
has been devoted to the development of a direct synthesis method for hydrogen peroxide 
generation with noble-metal catalysis [29,32]. The main difficulty in the direct synthesis method 
is the flammability of H2-O2 mixture over the range of 4-94% H2 by volume. Figure 5 shows a 
schematic representation of the direct synthesis method. 
==PAGE:13==
-7- 
 
Figure 5. Schematic representation of the direct synthesis method [31]. 
 
The ideal process for direct combination should be [25]: 
1. High hydrogen peroxide production rate and low decomposition 
2. A catalyst with long lifetime and minimal precious-metal losses. 
3. Use of a nonexplosive gas mixture. 
4. Synthesis of hydrogen peroxide at low pressure and ambient temperature. 
5. Noncorrosive reaction medium 
6. High hydrogen selectivity and hydrogen peroxide concentrations of at least 5%. 
In 2006 a demonstration plant was built in Germany to evaluate data necessary to design a 
20·104 t/a plant to begin commercial production of hydrogen peroxide by direct synthesis. The 
process used a carbon-supported PdPt/C catalyst comprised of 4 nm metal alloy nanoparticles 
[31]. Several alternative catalysts have been investigated for the direct synthesis of hydrogen 
peroxide [32]. However, the AO process is still the process used for large scale production of 
hydrogen peroxide, since it is still more economically viable than direct synthesis [35]. 
The main disadvantages of the direct synthesis which make it difficult to implement are [31]: 
1. Direct synthesis is only achievable with costly pure O2 in excess, but not with freely 
available air. Furthermore, the reaction pressure is generally much higher than 
pressures typically employed in the AO process. 
2. In the commercial grade of H2O2 produced by direct synthesis, hydrogen peroxide is 
present in methanol at low (<10%) concentration. The product is typically demanded in 
an aqueous phase; thus, this process requires an extraction step from hydrophilic, 
expensive methanol. That makes this step much costlier than the simple extraction from 
the water-immiscible organic phase of the AO process.

As it can be deduced from the Tafel and Butler-Volmer equations, as higher is i0, lower 
is the kinetic overpotential. The values of i0 depend on several factors and can be 
increased by raising the cell temperature and pressure, using a more effective catalysts, 
increasing the reactant concentration and increasing the real surface of the electrodes, 
among other factors. 
2. Internal currents: Internal currents are generated by electron conducting through the 
electrolyte in the opposite direction rather than as an external current, and therefore 
causing an electron waste. Typically, the internal current has a low impact compared to 
other type of overpotential and can be avoided by choosing a highly electronic insulating 
electrolyte.  
 
3. Ohmic resistance: the ohmic overpotential is due to the resistance to the flow of 
electrons through the electrodes and the resistance to the flow of ions through the 
electrolyte. Ohmic losses become particularly important at moderate current densities. 
The voltage drop is linearly proportional to the current density according to ohms law 
(V=IR). The resistance of the electrolyte has typically a higher influence than the 
electrodes. To reduce the internal resistance of the electrolyte, it is crucial to decrease 
the thickness of the separator or/and to increase the ionic conductivity.  
 
4. Concentration or mass-transport overpotential: these losses arise from concentration 
gradients along the cells and become important at higher current densities. As the

1.4. HPNow system 
HPNow uses a polymer exchange membrane (PEM) electrolysis cell for the 
electrochemical generation of hydrogen peroxide. With that system hydrogen peroxide can be 
generated with the use of electricity, water, and oxygen, offering a safer and more 
environmentally friendly alternative to the main conventional method for hydrogen peroxide 
==PAGE:18==
-12- 
production (anthraquinone auto-oxidation process). Moreover, this system enables the 
generation of hydrogen peroxide on-site, in contrast to other methods for hydrogen peroxide 
production.  
1.4.1. Materials design 
 
HPNow electrochemical system materials design is based on a membrane electrode 
assembly (MEA). In addition, there are some gas-diffusion layers (GDLs) in the cathode side, 
which main function is to facilitate gas diffusion to the catalyst layer. 
1. Membrane Electrode Assembly (MEA) 
The electrolyte in a PEM electrolysis cell is a proton-exchange polymeric membrane. 
Thus, its main function is to transport protons from the anode to the cathode. However, in 
addition to a high ionic conductivity (S/cm) an electrolyte membrane also needs to present the 
following characteristics [44]: 
1. Low gas permeability to prevent crossover from one compartment of the cell to another. 
2. Chemical and thermal stability. 
3. Mechanical strength and flexibility. 
4. Low water drag. 
 
The most widely used membrane is Nafion®, produced by DuPont company. Figure 6 
shows the chemical structure of Nafion®.  
 
Figure 6. Chemical structure of Nafion membrane [45]. 
 
As can be seen in figure 6, Nafion® is formed by an aliphatic perfluorinated backbone 
with ether-linked side chains ending in sulfonate groups [46]. Therefore, it is a copolymer of 
tetrafluoroethylene (Teflon) and sulfonyl fluoride vinyl ether [47]. This membrane segregates 
into hydrophobic and hydrophilic regions. The sulphonic acid groups create hydrophilic regions 
that are strongly acid, while the tetrafluoroethylene backbone is hydrophobic. The two domains 
are randomly connected. The sulfonic acid groups present fixed anions in the polymer 
membrane. When the cell is under operation, the protons are transported via two mechanisms: 
==PAGE:19==
-13- 
1. Grotthuss mechanism (proton jumping): during transport, protons hop from acid group 
to acid group. 
2. Vehicular mechanism: the protons are dissociated from the sulfonic groups and 
hydrated and move by molecular diffusion. 
 
Another important characteristic of Nafion ® membranes is the ability to take up large 
amounts of water, since ionic conductivity in PEM cells increases with the water content [42]. 
With respect to the catalysts, for the oxygen evolution reaction in the anode side, iridium oxide 
(IrO2) is used as a catalyst. Up to date iridium oxide offers the best compromise of a corrosion-
stable and active catalyst material under the harsh oxidising and acidic environment of a PEM 
anode [48,49]. With regards to the cathode catalysts for the oxygen reduction reaction, a 
carbon-based catalyst is used. Electrocatalysts with high activity and selectivity for 2-electron 
ORR for hydrogen peroxide generation have been much less investigated than the 4-electron 
ORR for water generation in fuel cells [50]. 
 
Figure 7 shows a schematic representation of the parts contained in a the MEAs used 
for HPNow system. 
 
Figure 7. Schematic representation of the MEA used in HPNow system. 
 
As shown in figure 7, the cathode side of the MEA is formed by a carbon gas-diffusion 
layer (GDL). One side of the GDL (the one closet to the membrane) is coated with the carbon-
based ORR catalyst. The external side of the GDL is coated with polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE) 
to enhance the hydrophobicity and promote the hydrogen peroxide removal. The membrane is 
made of Nafion® and the anode side of the membrane is coated with the OER catalyst (based 
on IrO2). Finally, a titanium felt is attached to the anode catalysts, which acts as current collector. 
==PAGE:20==
-14- 
3. Gas Diffusion Layers (GDLs) 
 
The main function of the GDL is to diffuse the gas. The porous nature of the GDL 
facilitates the effective diffusion of the gas reactants to the catalyst layer on the MEA. The GDL 
also acts as electrical connection between the cathode catalyst and the current collector. Finally, 
the GDL helps in managing the water in the cell as it carries the water away from the electrode 
surface [51,52]. 
 
The GDL is usually made of carbon-based porous substrate, such as carbon cloth or 
carbon paper. Generally, there is a first layer of the GDL, which is in contact with the gas and 
water inlets. It serves as a current collector, a physical support for the catalyst layer and an 
elastic component of the MEA, to handle the compression needed to establish intimate contact. 
The second layer of the GDL, in contact with the catalyst layer is typically hydrophobic, which 
provides hydrophobicity to avoid flooding (see section 1.4.2) and enhance intimate electronic 
contact at its interface with the catalyst layer [53]. 
In summary, an ideal GDL must present the following characteristics: 
1. Effective transport of gas reactants to the catalyst layer. 
2. Low electronic resistance. 
3. Have a surface that enhances good electronic contact. 
4. Have some degree of hydrophobicity close to the catalyst layer. 
In the cells used for this work, different proportions of GDLs were used. 
1.4.2. Water management 
 
Water management during the operation of PEM electrolysis cells is extremely 
important to ensure proper performance of the cell. As mentioned in section 1.4.1, water 
content will increase the ionic conductivity of the Nafion ® membrane. Through the electrolyte 
membrane, two modes of transport water take place. First, there is electro-osmotic drag from 
the anode to the cathode along with the protons generated in the anode side. The electro-
osmotic drag will be proportional on the proton concentration, and thus, it will increase with 
the current density. Secondly, there is diffusion transport caused by the concentration gradient 
of water across the membrane [54,55]. However, in HPNow system, in addition to the water 
introduced to the anode side needed for the anode half-cell reaction, water is also introduced 
in the cathode side to remove generated hydrogen peroxide from the catalyst. This addition of 
water in the cathode side will account for the majority of water in the cathode, in comparison 
to that from the electro-osmotic drag and diffusion transport. Moreover, some additional water 
can be generated by the competing four-electron pathway ORR.  
 
A sufficient amount of water must be removed from the cathode catalyst layer by 
diffusion and capillary transport of liquid water through the GDL and then leave the cell through 
the outlet. If excessive liquid water is accumulated at the cathode side, water flooding of the cell 
==PAGE:21==
-15- 
will occur. Flooding of the cell will hinder the transport of oxygen by blocking the pores in the 
GDLs, covering up active sites in the catalyst layer and blocking the gas transport channels in the 
cell. Moreover, flooding can cause non-uniform distribution of reactants over the catalyst active 
area [54].  
 
Water management in the cell is mainly affected by inlet water and gas flow rates, the 
design of the cell, and GDL hydrophobic and hydrophilic proportions. Low gas flow rates and 
high-water flow rates will generally enhance the chance of flooding. As explained in section 
1.4.1, adding a hydrophobic proportion of the GDLs will help to expel water in the cathode side.  
 
Overall, a balance between water input (input cathode water, generated water and 
water transport from anode side) and water removal is of great importance for stable cell 
operation [56]. If working at constant voltage, flooding of the cell will decrease the current 
density because of an increase in the overpotential. 
1.4.3. Changing from oxygen to air 
As shown in section 1.2.3, oxygen is required as a reactant for the cathode half-cell reaction. 
Ideally, the source of oxygen should be atmospheric air since is freely available. HPNow system 
currently uses air as a source of oxygen, however, the system includes oxygen concentrators 
that provide the cathode of the cell with an oxygen purity of around 95%. The main 
inconvenience of these oxygen concentrators is its energy consumption. To avoid using these 
concentrators, a more energy viable alternative is the use of compressed air. 
Since the oxygen molar fraction in air (0.21) is lower than that in concentrated oxygen (0.95), 
the cell can be pressurized in order to achieve a higher oxygen partial pressure with air at a 
higher pressure than that with air at atmospheric pressure. The relationship between the partial 
pressure and molar fraction of a component in a gas mixture can be described by Dalton’s law, 
shown in equation 19.

A further discussion of the partial pressure of oxygen in concentrated oxygen and air at 
different pressures is provided in section 2.6. 
The oxygen concentration used for HPNow commercial reactors use a power of 650 W 
and they work continuously while providing oxygen to the cell, that means that they will have 
an energy consumption of 650 Wh. These oxygen concentrators provide with a maximum flow 
of 10 l/min. Contrary, an air compressor to be used in a reactor (STANLEY FATMAX compressor) 
uses a power of 746 Wh. However, the compressor can provide a higher flow, and moreover it 
==PAGE:22==

has a storage tank of 6 L at pressure of 8 bar. That means that the compressor will not be using 
power continuously, only when filling the tank.  
Considering a flow rate of 10 l/min of compressed air at 6 bar, and the volume of the 
tank, it can be estimated that to provide 10 l/min at 6 bar it requires around 460 kWh in contrast 
to the oxygen concentrator that would require 650 kWh for providing a flow of 10 l/min at 
atmospheric pressure. In addition to the energy savings of using an air compressor instead of an 
oxygen concentrator, the air compressor is cheaper in cost, less fragile to breakdowns (less 
maintenance and longer life) and, can handle humidity and temperature better than the oxygen 
concentrator. Therefore, overall using an air compressor will reduce the energy and economic 
costs. 
1.5. Thesis outline 
The main scope of this thesis was to investigate and optimize the performance of 
polymer exchange membrane (PEM) electrolysis cells to produce hydrogen peroxide by using air 
at high pressure (6 bar) at the cathode side of the cell. The motivation behind this research was 
to reduce the energy consumption of the state-of-of the art HPNow reactors by avoiding the use 
of oxygen concentrator, which feed the cathode chamber of the cells with high purity oxygen 
(~95%) but have a high energy consumption and maintenance costs.

2.6. Oxygen concentration 
As explained in section 1.4.3, one of the most important points of this thesis was the 
replacement of concentrated oxygen by compressed air at a higher pressure so that the oxygen 
partial pressure will be similar in both cases. However, there were some uncertainties in the 
partial and total pressure measurements that must be considered to do a proper analysis of the 
results.

2.7. Characterization techniques 
2.7.1. Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) 
Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) is one of the most versatile techniques available for 
the examination and analysis of microstructure morphology and chemical composition. The 
working principle of SEM is based on the interaction between an electron beam and the 
specimen, and thus a nearly monochromatic electron beam with homogeneous energy 
distribution is required. The electron beam is generated by the electron gun, which can be a 
thermionic or a field-emission. In a thermionic electron gun, electrons are emitted from a heated 
filament, typically tungsten or lanthanum hexaboride (LaB6). In field-emission electron guns, a 
strong electric field is used to extract electrons from a very sharp single-crystal tungsten tip [58]. 
After emission, the electrons are accelerated by an electric field towards an anode. A field 
emission gun required two anodes, the first regulated the field strength and emission current at 
the tip, and the second accelerates the electrons. After being accelerated, electrons are 
deflected by magnetic lenses and consequently focused by magnetic condenser lens to a small 
spot on the specimen surface. Controlled by the scan coils, the electron beam moves along the 
x- or y-axis over the specimen surface and generates point-to-point image of the scanning area 
with help of the detectors [59]. Figure 14 shows a schematic representation of a SEM 
microscope. 
==PAGE:33==
-27- 
 
Figure 14. Schematic representation of a SEM microscope  [60]. 
When operating SEM, a high-vacuum environment is needed to avoid contamination of 
the electron source and interference of gas molecules in the electron beam. The vacuum range 
is 10-3 to 10-5 Pa for a thermionic electron source and 10-6 to 10-8 Pa for a field-emission gun. 
When the electron beam hits the sample, the interaction between the beam electrons 
from the electron gun and the sample atoms generates a variety of signals. When hitting the 
sample surface, the electron beam penetrates the sample into the specimen while scattering 
with its atoms. There are two types of scattering, elastic scattering and inelastic scattering. 
Elastic scattering has negligible energy loss during the collision and the scattered electrons have 
a wide-angle directional change. When the directional changes are more than 90 ˚, the electrons 
become backscattered electrons (BSEs). Because of the elastic scattering of the primary 
electrons, BSEs scape from the surface with an energy in the same range of the primary 
electrons. Its energy is strongly dependent on the sample atom mass. Therefore, the signal 
obtained from the BSE contains information about the material composition and the 
morphology of the sample. 
In an inelastic scattering, a significant amount of energy is transferred from the primary 
electrons to electrons in the sample. Some of these electrons can leave the sample and be 
detected. These electrons are known as secondary electrons (SE) and have a lower energy than 
BSEs.  
2.7.2. Energy Dispersive X-ray (EDX) 
Energy dispersive X-ray (EDX), also known as energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDS), is a 
nonquantitative analytical method used to analyse the surface material composition. EDX is 
typically used in combination with SEM, since the working principle is also based on the 
interaction of the electron beam with the matter in the sample and thus the electron gun can 
be shared. However, in EDX detector processes photons instead of electrons as in SEM. When 
an inner-shell electron is displaced by collision with a primary electron, an outer-shell electron 
will fall into the inner shell in order to fill the vacancy. During this process, an X-ray photon with 
==PAGE:34==
-28- 
an energy equivalent to the difference between these two atomic orbitals will be emitted, in 
order to bring the atom back to the ground state. The various shells of an atom have discrete 
amounts of energy and, thus the energy difference between shells is characteristic of the 
element. Therefore, the emitted photons will carry information about the element from which 
they originate from. Figure 15 shows a representation of the processes induced by electron 
collision. 
 
Figure 15. Atomic model and principle of the types of emission induced by electron collision. Emitted 
secondary electrons are detected with SEM and emitted photons by EDS [60]. 
Generally, only elements in which the energy gap between the atomic shells is in the 
range of the energy of the primary electrons can be detected. Hydrogen and helium have only 
one atomic shell and, therefore, cannot be detected. The microscope can produce images in X-
ray, where characteristic X-ray from a given element are detected and their position recorded, 
with a resulting map showing the distribution of specific elements. If EDX is coupled with SEM, 
these maps can be recorded alongside with the corresponding SE or BSE images [44,60]. 
To study the composition of a cross-sectioned MEA and find possible Ir cross-over from 
the anode to the cathode side of the cell, a FEI Quanta 200 ESEM FEG scanning election 
microscope with energy dispersive X-rays (Oxford Instruments 80 mm2 X-Max silicon drift 
detector, MnKα resolution at 124 eV) was used for the recordings of EDX spectra. 
2.7.3. Inductively coupled plasma mass spectroscopy (ICP-MS) 
Inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS) is a highly sensitive technique 
with an extremely low detection limit, allowing the measurement of concentrations in the parts 
per trillion (ppt) range. ICP-MS is typically used to analyse samples that are liquids or that can 
be dissolved, or acid digested to give a liquid. Most of the elements in the periodic table can be 
analysed in solution as major, minor or trace components in a wide range of sample types. The 
==PAGE:35==
-29- 
only elements that ICP-MS cannot measure are H and He (which are below the mass range of 
the mass spectrometer), Ar, N and O (which are present at high level from the plasma and air), 
and F and Ne (which cannot be ionized in an argon plasma) [61,62]. Figure 16 shows the 
elements in the periodic table which concentration can be measured by ICP-MS. 
 
Figure 16. Periodic table indicating the elements (in colour) that can be measured by ICP-MS [63]. 
ICP-MS measures the actual number of atoms in a sample as singly charged ions in a 
plasma. Therefore, this requires that the element or compound of interest is in the gaseous state 
and ionized. ICP are very efficient at atomizing and ionizing almost all elements in the periodic 
table. The ICP conditions are optimized in order to maximize the formation of singly charged 
ions and trying to minimize the formation of multiply charged ions and molecular species [64]. 
These positive ions are sampled from the plasma and directed into the mass spectrometer. The 
mass spectrometer separates ions according to their mass/charge (m/z) ration, before reaching 
the instrument detector. 
An ICP-MS instrument consists of the ion source (the ICP), a mass spectrometer (MS) – 
usually a scanning quadrupole mass filter, and a detector. The ICP is at atmospheric pressure, 
while the MS and detector operate in a vacuum chamber, so an ICP-MS also requires a vacuum 
pump, a vacuum interface, and some electrostatic ion “lenses” to focus the ions through the 
system. Modern ICP-MS systems also typically contain some device or mechanism to resolve 
spectral interferences. Figure 17 shows a schematic an ICP-MS instrument. 
 
Figure 17. Schematic representation of a ICP-MS instrument [63]. 
==PAGE:36==
-30- 
The main components of a single quadrupole ICP-MS instrument are [63,65]: 
1. Sample introduction system: the liquid sample is introduced to the system and a 
fine aerosol mist is formed. 
2. Plasma (ICP): the elements in the sample aerosol are converted into ions at 
atmospheric pressure. 
3. Vacuum interface: the plasma and the quadropole mass spectrometer are 
separated by a vacuum interface, which transfers ions from the plasma (at 
atmospheric pressure) to the mass spectrometer (in a vacuum chamber). 
4. Ion lens: the ions are focused into a narrow beam and separated from the ions 
from the neutral particles and ions extracted from the plasma.  
5. Mass spectrometer (MS): after the ion beam is created, the ions are filtered by 
mass to charge ratio (m/z). Ions are separated in response to an electric field, 
created by a quadrupole.  
6. The electron multiplier detector: The ICP-MS uses an electron multiplier to 
measure the number of ions present for each mass that is individually filtered 
from the ion beam by the quadrupole. Most ICP-MS instruments use a discrete 
dynode detector. An ion striking the first dynode of the detector releases 
electrons which accelerate towards and into the next dynode, which in turn 
releases more electrons to strike the third dynode and so on down the detector. 
By the final dynode, the cascade of electrons has built up to a high enough level 
that it can be recorded as a pulse by the electronics of the electron multiplier.

2.8. Electrochemical techniques 
2.8.1. Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) 
Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) is a non-destructive technique that 
provides information about a wide range of electrochemical phenomena involved in 
electrochemical cells. EIS involves applying a small variable-frequency alternating current (AC) 
waveform through the electrochemical cell and measuring the response of the cell to this 
stimulus (AC voltage across the cell and AC current through the cell), from which the impedance 
of the cell can be determined by taking the ratio of AC voltage/AC current. Typically, a swept 
frequency sinewave is used as the stimulus so that the impedance can be evaluated across the 
frequency range of interest. Since the AC frequency can be quite low, it is important that the 
electrochemical cell is operating under steady conditions [60,66]. 
The AC waveform is usually applied to the cell via a potentiostat. Potentiostat is connected 
to the cell so that potential across the cell and the current through the cell are monitored. The 
potentiostat provides buffering of the voltage input signals so that voltage measurements may 
be performed with minimal disturbance to the cell. The pontentistat also provides current to 
voltage conversion to convert the AC current passing through the cell into an AC voltage 
waveform that can be measured by a Frequency Response Analyser (FRA) to provide impedance 
analysis. The FRA generates a reference voltage sinewave of given amplitude and frequency, and 
the magnitude and phase of the resulting AC current is measured and recorded [60].  The

hypothesis explaining these differences in cupper concentration depending on the potential 
could be that Cu (with a standard reduction potential (E˚Cu2+/Cu) of +0.340 V) is found as

in the cathode side of the cell. When lowering potential, or even going to negative potential,

possibility could be that copper from the pressure gauge is accumulated in the cathode catalyst 
under reducing conditions and when oxidized at lower potential it is more easily removed from 
the cell. Another possibility could be additional sources of Cu in the cell, such as the aluminium 
cathode housing (although the manufacturer of aluminium housing claims that there is no Cu in 
the material) or the Ti porous plate. 
3.4. Electrochemical investigations 
 
Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS) is a non-invasive, in-situ method for 
diagnosing behaviours in a system by sending an electrical stimulus through the cell and 
analysing the response signal, as explained in section 2.8.1. It provides a more in-depth analysis 
of various electrochemical processes taking place within the cell than a polarization curve.
 
To investigate the different impedances in the cell, electrochemical impedance 
spectroscopy (EIS) was recorded under different conditions in the cathode chamber 
(concentrated oxygen at 1.2 bar, and air at 1.2 and 6 bar). EIS measurements are typically carried 
out over a frequency range of 10 mHz to 100 kHz, with an acquisition of 10 points per decade, 
and with a signal amplitude of 10 mV around 2V. Data analysis was performed by fitting the 
experimental results to equivalent circuits using a nonlinear least-square fitting procedure with 
the chi-squared value minimized to 10−4. 
 
Figure 32 shows the Nyquist plot obtained for the EIS measurements. The Nyquist plot 
is employed to represent the data collected during EIS analysis. For an electrochemical cell 
reactor, Nyquist diagram is typically consisting of two semi-circular regions which represent the 
activities at the anode and cathode, respectively. In a Nyquist diagram, the frequency increases 
form left to right, with the semicircle on the left side corresponding to the high frequency 
spectra of a few hundred kHz thus correlating with activity in the anodic side of the cell and the 
right side corresponds to low frequency spectra of a few hundred mHz and correlates with 
activity in the cell’s cathodic region.

4. Conclusions 
The main goal of this thesis has been to study and optimize the performance of 
electrochemical cells for hydrogen peroxide generation by using air at high pressure (6 bar) as a 
feeding gas in the cathode chamber instead of concentrated oxygen at a lower pressure. The 
main motivation was to avoid the use of oxygen concentrators in HPNow reactors which have a 
high energy consumption and other disadvantages. Therefore, research has been focused on 
studying the performance of electrochemical cells working with air at high pressure. The 
conclusions of this research are summarized: 
1. 
Cells working with air at a pressure of 6 bar in the cathode side show a similar 
electrochemical performance for more than 500 hours in terms of faradaic efficiency 
and voltage than when working with concentrated oxygen at 1.2 bar. 
2. 
Cells working with air (21% of oxygen) at 1.2 bar show an average lower initial faradaic 
efficiency (~10%) and higher voltage than when working with concentrated oxygen 
(95%) at the same pressure.  
3. 
With respect to the effect of stopping the production of hydrogen peroxide temporary 
and restarting it in the reactors (start-stops), no effect in the electrochemical 
performance was observed after the application of different procedures. Both faradaic 
efficiency and potential were shown to be similar before and after applying a start-
stop. 
4. 
In regard to the hydrophobic/hydrophilic proportion of gas-diffusion layers (GDLs), a 
proportion of 0.7 mm of hydrophobic GDLs, and 0.8 mm of hydrophilic GDLs (46.7% of 
hydrophobic GDLs) showed the highest faradaic efficiency with air at 6 bar, even 
though it exhibited the highest potential. 
5. 
Electrochemical investigations showed that the charge transfer resistance was higher 
in the case where the cathode of the cell was feed with air at 1.2 bar, followed by air 
at 6 bar and, finally, concentrated oxygen at 1.2 bar.

5. Outlook  
During this research, some questions arose that could not be fully investigated within 
the period and sources of this project. Therefore, some future lines of investigation could be 
done to continue with the research done in this project: 
1. 
The presence of some metal contamination such as Cu in the cathode side was 
confirmed by ICP-MS, although the levels of metal detected by ICP-MS were only in 
the range of a few ppb. Moreover, by ICP-MS only metal present in the effluent of the 
cell can be detected. In addition, the presence of metal contamination could not be 
related to any important decrease in the faradaic efficiency. Therefore, further 
research could be done first of all, by better quantifying the amount of these elements, 
and secondly, by applying similar experiments in cells where a sudden decline in 
efficiency is observed (generally cells with long operation time). Furthermore, the 
source of contamination was not fully identified (it could be from pressure gauge or 
cell housing for instance). Consequently, future experiments could be performed 
isolating the effect of different components and trying to find the source of 
contamination if a relation between a drop in performance and metal contamination 
is found in previous experiments. 
2. 
The air flow rate used for the experiments in this project was highly in excess. To avoid 
flooding of the cell and carry on with the research, the air flow rate in the cathode 
chamber was set to a high value. However, further investigation could be done to 
optimize the gas flow rate and study the effects of decreasing the flow rate in the 
performance of the cell. 
3. 
Although the differences in electrochemical performance between using air at 6 and 
1.2 bar at the cathode chamber were studied in this project and some conclusions 
could be obtained, it was not possible to find a clear relation between the air pressure 
and the faradaic efficiency of the cell within the range from 1.2 to 6 bar. Therefore, 
future research could be done to investigate the relationship between the faradaic 
efficiency and air pressure in that range.
