ABSTRACT
The purpose of this report is to document the process and results of the 
master thesis project: Development of a dehumidifying flowering pot.
The initial idea was to develop a dehumidifying flowering pot that waters 
the plant. The motivation for this idea came from both team members 
having experienced signs of excessive humidity in their apartments. 
Research showed that 1 in 5 buildings in Denmark has problems with 
their indoor climate. Excessive indoor humidity causes growth of mould 
and dust mites which pose a health risk to the inhabitants such as 
headache and allergy. Besides humidity, the indoor climate problem 
consists of particle pollution which is the largest contributor to 2 million 
DALYs lost yearly in Europe. The majority of indoor particle pollution 
comes from indoor sources such as cooking and burning stoves. Upon 
the realization that solving humidity problems alone was not enough, 
but that the solution should focus on the entire indoor climate led to 
revising the design goal. The revised goal of the project was to develop 
a desirable indoor-air-quality assistant that synergizes with houseplants 
and blends into the interior decor of Scandinavian households.
The approach to this project has been an Agile/Stage-Gate hybrid 
model allowing iterative development, continuously integrating new 
insights into the final solution. The agile approach has been crucial as 
the team had no previous experience or knowledge within the field of 
indoor climate. This meant new insights were gained throughout the 
project leading to repeating iterations and re-evaluations of previous 
decisions. Brainwalking and drawstorming led to countless ideas which 
were initially cut down to nine. These nine were further developed and 
re-evaluated in relation to design objectives and a Product Specification. 
This evaluation ended with the selection of just two concepts. These two 
concepts were developed to a high detail level using CAD-modelling 
and digital renders. They were then evaluated by involving users in 
interactive feedback sessions using boundary objects such as design 
posters and cardboard mockups resulting in one final concept: NJORD.
NJORD is the first IAQ-assistant for Scandinavian households. With 
its unique design, it blends perfectly into a modern Scandinavian 
interior decor. NJORD secures a healthy indoor climate for the users 
by monitoring the indoor air quality and automatically dehumidify and 
purify the air.
The problems of poor indoor air quality were through research established 
as substantial and widespread, but we believe that NJORD could be 
part of the solution to this problem in many Scandinavian households.

1.1	
Introduction
The starting point for this project was that both team members 
experienced signs of excessive humidity in their apartments such as 
the beginning growth of mould and condensation on the windows 
(see Figure 1-1). When searching the market for a dehumidifier no truly 
desirable options were to be found. After a while, this led to the idea 
of developing a new type of dehumidifier that was integrated into a 
flowering pot that could water the plant with the extracted water. 
Through research, new insights led to a revised design goal targeting 
not just dehumidification but the indoor climate as a whole. The initial 
idea of a dehumidifying flowering pot remains a clear inspiration for the 
final solution although its physical representation has changed.
This report chronologically describes the project from research-phase, 
through conceptualization into the detailed design. The research phase 
investigates the problems of excessive indoor humidity and analyzes 
the current market in terms of products and technologies. The research 
leads to insights from which design specifications are defined. Based on 
these, several concepts are generated and evaluated utilizing physical 
prototypes and feedback sessions ending with the choice of one concept 
to finalize. The result is a detailed design of the solution presented in 
the form of digital renders, a CAD-model and a digital user interface 
prototype.
1.2	 Motivation
The idea for this project originated from the humidity problems 
experienced at home by both team members. The motivation for 
carrying out this project, however, lies more with the proposed idea of 
a dehumidifying flowering pot which is found to be an intriguing idea 
as it tabs into multiple trends such as Smart Home and the Houseplant 
Craze. As soon-to-be Design engineers, the idea also presents several 
interesting challenges to solve. The possibility of doing a start-up around 
the final concept does sound exciting, however, being pragmatic this 
will depend on the outcome of the project.

1.3	 Project Goal
The main goal of the project changed from initially being:
The goal is to develop a concept along with a functioning prototype 
for a novel and innovative dehumidifier disguised as a flowering pot for 
the smart home.
to being:
The goal is to develop a desirable indoor-air-quality assistant that 
synergizes with houseplants and blends into the interior decor of 
Scandinavian households.
This change of goal was a result of the insights gathered in the research 
phase and the realization that focusing on indoor humidity alone wasn’t 
enough. With the updated goal in mind, the project should result in a 
novel solution that improves the indoor air quality of people’s homes. 
The concept should act as an assistant helping the user to take control of 
their indoor climate in a user-friendly way. The concept should integrate 
into the users living space and blend into a home with a Scandinavian 
interior. It integrates itself by synergizing with a houseplant, watering it 
while taking the shape of a flower pedestal.

Before the start of the project, an application for a permanent work 
desk in the new Developer Hall at DTU Skylab was sent and accepted.

concept.

design process (see Figure 2-2). The addition of a simple green paper 
clip changed this static overview by making it possible to dynamically 
visualize where in the process the project was. Having an ever-present

2.3	 Prototyping Planner
The Prototyping Planner Tool (PPT) is a prototyping support tool that 
structures prototyping activities and helps designers create purposeful 
prototypes and evaluate results from prototyping (Hansen, 2020). The 
PPT was introduced to the team by Camilla Arndt Hansen the 30th 
September 2020 and was from there on incorporated into the project. 
Camilla Arndt Hansen is currently writing a PhD at the Department of 
Mechanical Engineering at DTU about the use of prototyping in design 
projects and she has taken part in developing the PPT.
Being new to the PPT it was decided to use its simplified version to ease 
the implementation of it into the project. Figure 2-4 shows a Prototyping 
Planner template which is used to document the work with a single 
prototype. When using PPT the work with a prototype is split into three 
stages; Frame, Build & Test and Act. The Frame stage ensures that the 
purpose, desired outcome and focus of the prototype has been thought 
upon. The Build & Test stage is for planning how to build the prototype 
and document the testing of it. The Act stage makes sure that the 
designer reflects upon the gained insights, which actions to take based 
on this and evaluate the success of the outcome of the prototyping. 
During a design project such as this, there is rarely just one ongoing 
activity and this also means juggling between the work with many different 
prototypes. To help structure and gain an overview of the statuses of the 
prototypes in a project, PPT suggests the use of a Prototype KANBAN 
which is shown in Figure 2-5. The Prototype KANBAN is an overview 
of the prototypes done within a certain timeframe. Each prototype is 
given an ID like PP1, PP2 and PP3 in the template. As the work with 
each prototype proceeds their ID moves towards the right from Frame 
→ Build & Test → Act. 
Figure 2-4: Prototyping Planner template for documenting the work with a prototype
Figure 2-5: Prototyping KANBAN

On the horizontal axis, the prototypes can be divided into themes either 
by which part of a product is prototyped like ‘electronics’, ‘mechanics’ 
etc. Or, as the team chose to do, by which of the aspects in the innovation 
trifecta the prototype targeted; Desirability, Feasibility or Viability. As 
long as the Prototype KANBAN is up to date it provides a well-structured 
overview of how far the work with each prototype has come. It can also 
show in which domain the project might be well-founded and if there is 
a domain the designers might want to focus more on. A project doesn’t 
necessarily have to touch upon all three domains or have the same 
amount of prototypes in each. The important thing is to reflect upon 
these aspects so that the prototyping will be more purposeful. Although 
the team used PPT digitally a physical copy of the PPT template was 
placed on the backboard of the work desk to continually remind the 
team to use and update it. 
Figure 2-6 shows the Prototype KANBAN from this project at two 
different stages; on the left at an ongoing stage and on the right at 
the end of the project. The experience with using PPT in this project

the prototyping process. Especially answering the framing questions

meaningful. There were some elements of the PPT that weren’t used, e.g. 
the backlog function as backlog were already in use. An element that was 
found to be limiting was the amount of space on the templates. Many 
of our prototypes were complex and resulted in multi-page appendices

2.5	 Our Design Process
The team has previous experience with several different design processes. 
The process deemed appropriate for this project was a hybrid of multiple 
design approaches. The process has especially been approached using 
elements from the Double Diamond, Agile Development Methods and 
as already mentioned the more traditional Stage-Gate approach.
2.5.1	 Agile Meets Stage-Gate
Cooper and Sommer state in their article The Agile-Stage-Gate Hybrid 
Model: A Promising New Approach and a New Research Opportunity 
that “agile methods can be integrated with traditional gating approaches 
to yield an Agile-Stage-Gate Hybrid model” (Cooper and Sommer, 
2016). This hybrid model has been the foundation for the design process 
of this project. One of the greatest advantages of this model is that 
the development is much more responsive to changing user insights. 
As the team gradually gained insights and knowledge about e.g. the 
problem to solve, the design context and existing technologies, the 
requirements and criteria changed. With an agile approach, the team 
was able to handle these changes in an appropriate manner and could 
move forward without a complete understanding of an aspect knowing 
that a later return to the matter was possible.
“That (The Agile-Stage-Gate Hybrid) is, for more radical new-product 
projects, Agile-Stage-Gate offers the additional benefits of managing 
high uncertainty through incremental product versions (protocepts), 
quick learning cycles and frequent customer involvement” 
(Cooper and Sommer, 2016). 
Agile-Stage-Gate was found suiting for this project and development of 
a novel IAQ-assistant.

Project heartbeat
The ongoing development process has had a certain rhythm to it; a project 
heartbeat (see Figure 2-7). As new insights were gained e.g. feedback 
from users (surveys, interviews etc.) they fed into the set of design 
objectives and product specifications. Based on this manageable tasks 
were formulated to drive the development and eventually meet these

3.1	 Introduction
The initial goal for this project was to create a solution that could help 
people lower their indoor humidity by developing a solution that re-
thinks the conventional dehumidifier by synergizing it with a floor plant. 
The goal at the initiation of the project was:
The goal is to develop a concept along with a functioning prototype for 
a novel and innovative dehumidifier disguised as a flowering pot for the 
smart home.
The first activity to do to get from goal to solution is to properly research 
the problem to solve. This is very important as the proposed solution 
could otherwise end up useless due to unforeseen reasons. 
3.2	 Research of Indoor Climate
3.2.1	 Indoor Humidity
The research started by investigating the problems of excessive indoor 
humidity. 1 in 5 danish households suffers from excessive indoor humidity. 
There are many reasons for excessive humidity in a home. Today most 
people own a washing machine and in climates like Denmarks’, they 
are often forced to dry their clothes inside due to poor weather. This is 
however a bad idea as a standard wash (4-6 kg of clothes) will release 
2.5 litres of water to the air when drying. Other sources of humidity are 
cooking, showering and humans; on average a human releases approx. 
2 litres of fluid per day (Patursson & Blomsterberg, 2018). 
Excessive humidity in itself is not the problem, but it causes growth 
of mould and dust mites which can cause health issues and structural 
damage. Over time an untreated mould infestation will cause structural 
damage to the material on which it grows such as windows or internally 
in void walls. These damages are expensive to repair as the damage is 
often irreversible and drying out the area will only kill the living mould 
but leave behind its spores which grow if the conditions become suitable 
again.
The general level of indoor humidity varies throughout the year and is 
lower in the winter and higher in the summer. (Patursson & Blomsterberg, 
2018) The optimal humidity level is often considered to be between 40-
60% relative humidity (RH). RH is the amount of moisture in the air at 
a certain temperature relative to the amount of water that the air can 
maximally ‘hold’ at that temperature (Vanvuren, 2018). RH is given as 
a percentage and is the most common unit used to indicate indoor 
humidity. Another unit that is however also used is absolute humidity 
which is the actual amount of water vapour in the air measured as weight 
per volume, often g/m3. RH will be the unit used throughout this report 
as it’s the most common. 
An interesting, and perhaps overlooked, aspect of humidity is that 
humans do not have a sensory organ that can sense humidity on 
inhalation (Wolkoff, 2018). Humans are very capable of sensing e.g. 
temperature on their skin, however, with humidity people have to rely 
on signs such as condensation on the windows or a musky odour to tell 
them that the room they are in is humid. 
Whilst researching it became clear that many factors other than humidity 
have a great influence on the overall indoor climate and how this indoor 
climate is perceived.

3.3	 The Indoor Climate
An indoor climate is affected by several factors including temperature, 
noise, light, particle level and humidity (CISBO, 2016) (see Figure 3-1). 
The following section aims at briefly describing these aspects and the 
most important insights to use moving forward. The aspect of humidity 
will not be further elaborated.
Temperature 
Room temperature plays an important role in the perceived indoor 
climate (Fang et al., 1998). The human body accurately senses and reacts 
to temperature fluctuations making us e.g. sweat or feel cold. Which 
room temperature is perfect is subjective as some like it warm and 
others don’t. Also, the perfect temperature is dependent on the context 
e.g. a bedroom is often preferred colder than a living room. Although 
the IAQ-solution developed during this project is not to actively control 
temperature levels it’s important to consider this.
Noise
According to WHO’s guidelines for environmental noise in the European 
region: “Environmental noise is an important public health issue, featuring 
among the top environmental risks to health. It has negative impacts on 
human health and well-being and is a growing concern among both the 
general public and policy-makers in Europe.” (WHO, 2018) Especially 
noise sources such as road traffic, airplanes and construction have been 
in focus during recent years and new regulations have been put to law. 
In terms of noise emissions from household appliances, the regulations 
are few and non-restricting. 
Figure 3-1: Factors influencing the indoor climate

According to WHO, few people become moderately annoyed by noise 
with a sound pressure level below 50dB during the daytime. In the 
evening and night, the sound pressure level should be 5-10dB lower. 
Effect on sleep from background noise is measurable at sound pressure 
levels of about 30dB (WHO, 1999). These values will be useful when 
setting specifications for the solution.
In the field of Product Sound Design, the sounds generated by a product 
is distinguished into two categories; consequential and intentional 
sounds. The consequential sounds are those produced by the operation 
of the product whereas intentional sounds are sounds added to a product 
(Langeveld et al., 2013). Consequential sounds are the ones that users 
typically express as being ‘noisy’. The sound of a product can play a big 
role in the product experience, and as the solution developed in this 
project will most likely use a fan to move air it will inevitably produce 
consequential sound, noise. 
Light
Indoor lighting, be it natural or artificial, is important for us to see and 
function in our everyday life and plays a huge role in how an indoor 
climate is perceived. As the solution developed won’t control the 
lighting of a room this aspect won’t be a focus point. Light is, however, 
often used in electronic appliances for giving feedback or indicating a 
status. This type of lighting is not considered as affecting the indoor 
climate and will be discussed elsewhere in the report.        
Particles
The sources for particle pollution at home are many and even though 
some come from outdoor sources like traffic most of them are created 
by the occupants themselves. Candlelights, smoking, cooking, burning 
stoves, chemicals from e.g. cleaning products, painting and building 
materials are all examples of sources for particle pollution (CISBO, 
2016). There are many sorts of particles which are unwanted due to their 
negative health effects. In this report, the word ‘particle’ will often be 
used to cover all these types which include: chemicals, volatile organic 
compounds (VOCs), bacteria and allergens.
Just as it can help in reducing indoor humidity, airing out can help reduce 
the particle pollution. An experiment, conducted by CISBO, has shown 
that airing out with more than one window open is three times more 
effective than airing out by opening just one window. The experiment 
showed that the particle concentration was reduced by 8% after airing 
out properly for ten minutes. CISBO, therefore, recommends that 
people should air out multiple times a day (CISBO, 2016). According to 
a study by Kantar Gallup only 30% of the Danes air out more than once 
per day (Kantar Gallup, 2019). This insight partly led to the idea that the 
solution should be acting as an assistant which could interact with the 
users. One of these interactions could then be to nudge them to air out 
when appropriate.
3.3.1	 Consequences of a Bad Indoor 
Climate
The average Danish citizen spends 80-90% of their life indoors 
(Miljøstyrelsen, 2020).  This means that the indoor climate has an 
enormous impact on the well-being of Danes. A poor indoor climate 
can cause fatigue, headache, allergy and in worst cases severe diseases 
like lung and cardiovascular diseases and cancer (CISBO, 2016). As 
mentioned, high humidity will lead to the growth of mould and thriving 
dust mites. Moulds release millions of spores into the air which can 
irritate the airways and cause allergy. Moulds also release mycotoxins

absorb moisture through their skin which is why they thrive in humid

Having mentioned all these negatives of humid air it should be noted

Compressor type dehumidification
Compressor type dehumidifiers are the most common type for residential 
use and have been for the past decades. It works by drawing in the 
damp air and running it over a refrigerated coil/radiator. The water in 
the air condensates upon contact with the cold surface. This is how a 
compressor-based dehumidifier separates the water from the air. The 
amount of water a compressor-based dehumidifier can extract depends 
largely on the surface area of the cold coils. The larger the coil surface 
area the more water can be extracted however this also increases the 
requirements for the compressor and the power consumption. The dry 
air is reheated over a warm coil before it is released back into the room. 
See Figure 3-4. Compressor dehumidifiers work best in environments 
with a high ambient temperature (+15 °C) as the coils have to be 
significantly cooler than the intake air. Compressor dehumidifiers are 
generally heavier than other types of dehumidifiers often weighing over 
10 kg (Gan, 2019). Furthermore, compressors are quite noisy in operation 
(see Table 3-1).

Peltier-based dehumidification
Peltier dehumidifiers rely on thermoelectric cooling using the Peltier 
effect achieved with a so-called Peltier element. When electricity is 
run across a Peltier element one side turns cold and the other hot. A 
fan pushes humid air across a metal heatsink on the cold side causing 
condensation, just as a compressor-type dehumidifier. When enough 
condensation has gathered droplets gather and runs off the condensator 
(See Figure 3-5). Typically the airflow is then led across the heat sink on 
the hot side, ensuring that it doesn’t overheat and at the same time 
warming up the exhaust air (Dehumidifier Buyer’s guide, 2020). For pros

Desiccant dehumidification
A desiccant dehumidifier works by using a hygroscopic chemical that 
absorbs moisture from the air (see Figure 3-6). Desiccant dehumidifiers 
typically utilize a so-called ‘desiccant wheel’ which is a circular disc with a 
monolithic structure covered in the hygroscopic material. The desiccant 
wheel slowly rotates while damp air flows through it, absorbing the 
moisture. A section of the wheel is dried by a warm airflow evaporating 
the absorbed water carrying it into a condenser unit in which it liquifies. 
The desiccant wheels’ cycle of absorbing water and being dried repeats 
itself as the wheel slowly rotates. The air exiting a desiccant dehumidifier 
is often a couple of degrees °C warmer than room temperature which 
makes it suitable for keeping rooms frost-free during winter. Desiccant 
dehumidifiers are capable of being very quiet and are often slightly more 
compact than compressor dehumidifiers. (Wright, 2016) (Gan, 2019). See 
Table 3-3 for pros and cons.

Passive chemical absorption
A fourth option is passive chemical absorbers which do not move air 
through them but rely solely on the natural drafts in a room to move

from the air. Some are one-time use only while others are designed 
to be refilled when the desiccant can’t extract more water or has run 
empty. An advantage of passive chemical absorption is that no energy

3.4.2	 Reverse Engineering a Dehumidifier
It was decided to buy a dehumidifier to disassemble and learn from. 
The parts from this could then later be used for the construction of a 
functional prototype. The choice fell on buying a desiccant dehumidifier 
as this technology seemed the most suitable for this project. It’s quieter 
than a compressor dehumidifier with similar efficiency and it’s more 
compact which might be necessary later on to avoid a bulky solution. 
The dehumidifier bought was a Toyotomi TD-ZB80 which has a 
dehumidifying capacity of 8 l/day at 27°C 80% RH (Toyotomi, 2016). This 
means it is capable of dehumidifying large rooms of up to approx. 35 m2 
which is more than the typical living room in an apartment. 
The operation and functionalities of the dehumidifier were tested as the 
first thing. One of the impressions was that when put in “low”-mode 
it was really quiet. The next step was to disassemble to see how it was 
constructed and how the different parts related to each other. This was 
a great exercise and gave us invaluable insights on how a desiccant 
dehumidifier works and how the components are assembled. Some 
pictures from the process of disassembling the dehumidifier can be 
seen in Figure 3-8.
Having disassembled the whole dehumidifier it was decided to leave the 
internal components out of the cabinet. With a few workarounds, such as 
short-circuiting the sensor for detecting the presence of the water tank, 
it was still able to operate allowing us to see it in action. Without the 
cabinet, it couldn’t create a pressure drop across the desiccant wheel 
meaning that it didn’t create a proper airflow. The insides of the cabinet 
were further investigated to understand how it divided the component 
assembly into two, allowing pressure to be created by the fan. These 
insights will be great when constructing the functioning prototype.

3.4.3	 Bio-Inspired Design
To truly broaden the solution space it was decided to look to nature 
for inspiration. Through the means of evolution plants and animals 
have developed incredible solutions to very specific problems. Nature 
is, therefore, a great resource for novel and innovative solutions. An 
interesting finding was the Namibian Darkling beetle which lives in one 
of the driest deserts in the world. To gather the water they need, they 
have a unique micro-grooved surface on their forewings that combine 
hydrophilic and hydrophobic areas to condensate and collect water 
from the air (AskNature, 2017) (See Figure 3-11). Directly inspired by the 
beetle a graduate student named Shreerang Chhatre of MIT designed 
a fog harvesting mesh (AskNature, 2016). The efficiency of such static 
fog collectors is, however, very low despite being used in conditions 
with almost 100% RH. The hygroscopic material used for coatings in 
desiccant dehumidifiers seems more effective.

3.5.1	 Design Development at a Standstill
When researching the dehumidifier market it was found that the design 
of dehumidifiers nearly hasn’t changed in a decade, see Appendix III. A 
reason for this standstill might be that dehumidifiers are often bought to 
be used in moist cellars or similar spaces. The dehumidifiers are therefore

3.9	 Clarifying Objectives
3.9.1	 Objectives Tree
The starting point for this design project was an ill-defined problem 
and some vague requirements. Every design project needs a set of 
clear objectives that the design team can work towards. To finish the 
research phase the insights must be translated into clear statements of 
objectives. It was chosen to create an Objectives Tree that visually shows 
the objectives and how they relate (see Figure 3-30). The Objectives Tree 
Method is a tool to help clarify vague objectives and hierarchically order 
them which help expand them (Cross, 2000). The process of creating the 
Objectives Tree helped the members of the design team to clarify and 
agree on the objectives. 
To go from vague statements to more precise objectives it’s helpful to 
question them. Questions beginning with “How?” and “What?” typically 
help expand an objective into lower-level objectives. To help create 
higher-level objectives one can typically ask questions beginning with 
why. Using an example from the Objectives Tree is asking why should 
the solution “Control the relative humidity”. This leads to the higher-
level objective “Control the indoor air quality”. Asking “how do we do 
that?” sends us back down the Objectives Tree, and as the air-quality 
is made up of more factors than just humidity two sub-objectives are 
defined such as particle pollution. This shows how “... reconsidering the 
level of problem definition is often a stimulus to the designer to propose 
more radical or innovative types of solution.” (Cross, 2000). The level at 
which the problem is defined has a huge impact on the result. If it had 
been chosen to keep the design objective at the level of “control the 
relative humidity” the final design had been a dehumidifier. The research 
and the work with the Objectives Tree have moved the scope to a higher 
level to “control the indoor air quality” and includes other air quality 
parameters than just humidity.

can contribute towards three of the UN’s development goals, see Figure

not use clean drinking water where it is not necessary (Naturstyrelsen, 
2014). By using the extracted water from the air for watering the plants 
the solution contributes towards SDG 12 - “Responsible consumption 
and production”.

4.1	 Introduction
The problem & needs analysis led to many valuable insights that had 
not been considered had the project gone straight to idea generation 
and conceptualization. Based on all the insights a Design Brief was 
formulated setting the design goal and outlining the context for the 
new concept.
4.2	 Design Brief
4.2.1	 The Identified Problem
The overall problem is that the IAQ is poor in too many homes which 
potentially lead to health problems for the residents and in the end a loss 
of DALY’s. Relative humidity and particle level are the main components 
of indoor-air-quality. Excessive humidity results in mould growth and 
dust mites thriving which leads to irritation, allergy and asthma with the 
residents. Harmful combustion particles from cooking and candle lights, 
as well as chemicals from cleaning agents, building material, paints etc., 
can irritate the airways and over time lead to serious heart and lung 
diseases.
The research showed that dehumidifiers on the market aren’t aesthetically 
desirable to have and use in the living area of your home. The design 
language of dehumidifiers hasn’t changed in the last decade whereas 
the design of air-purifiers have developed and are aesthetically more 
pleasing. Having only an air-purifier is however not solving the overall 
problem, and having both an air-purifier and a dehumidifier in a standard 
size apartment will crowd it with appliances.   
Having many houseplants is a rising trend and people are bringing 
nature into their homes because it’s beautiful and makes them feel 
good. When keeping multiple plants at home it can become tedious 
to water them all, and many aren’t aware of the individual plant’s need 
for water resulting in suboptimal conditions for the plants. Most people 
water their houseplants with clean tap water which has undergone a lot 
of work to become drinkable which is a waste of energy and valuable 
resources. 
4.2.2	 The Concept
The idea with the concept is that it must improve the IAQ by dehumidifying 
and filtering out harmful particles. The concept should integrate into the 
home e.g. by creating a synergy with a houseplant watering it with water 
extracted from the air. 
The concept taps into the growing focus on the negative impacts of a 
bad IAQ. To be desirable it must be aesthetically different from existing 
products with similar functionality and fit into a home with Scandinavian 
interior decor.
4.2.3	 Targeted Customer Segment
The target segment is primarily people living in apartments in Scandinavia 
as the rainy and seasonal climate makes it challenging to control the 
indoor climate. People in the cities spend almost all of their time indoors 
and would therefore benefit greatly from having a great indoor climate. 
The target segment consists primarily of young to mid-age adults as 
these have a good purchasing power, are more likely to be tech-savvy 
and follow trends such as the indoor plant craze that is spurred on by 
social media and advertising. People in the target segment care about

their well-being and can see the potential benefits from a healthier 
indoor climate at home. 
The corona-virus has, without doubt, increased the number of hours we 
all spend indoors at home, and even though the vaccines are speeding 
through the big pharmaceutical companies pipelines the trend of 
working from home is predicted to be an increasing trend moving into 
the future.
4.2.4	Targeted Market
The market for air purifiers is increasing and more and more people are 
becoming aware of the negative consequences of a poor IAQ. In recent 
years more high-end, aesthetically pleasing and desirable air purifiers 
have become available on the market. Such air purifiers are expensive 
compared to high-performance residential dehumidifiers, suggesting 
that people are willing to spend a lot of money if it benefits their health 
and they can feel an instant effect.
In terms of competing products, there are some examples of IAQ-
improving standalone products. These, however, are often designed for 
heavy-duty performance which means they are bulky in size and weight. 
Furthermore, their design language stems from the typical industrial 
white dehumidifier. There is one startup on Kickstarter that is trying to 
launch a product named Urbie which is somewhat similar to the initial idea 
of a dehumidifying flowering pot. Urbie combines Peltier dehumidifier 
and air purification technology with the possibility to automatically 
water a plant. With Urbie the plant is however planted into Urbie itself 
and the soil is, according to their website, acting as a particle filter when 
drawing air through it. A few concepts for aesthetically interesting air 
purifiers and dehumidifiers were found during research, however, these 
were as mentioned solely concepts and not available for purchase. See 
Appendix III for pictures and information on the mentioned products.
4.2.5	 Criteria
Following is a list of criterias that the IAQ-assistant should meet.
	-
The IAQ-assistant should not look like the average existing 
dehumidifiers and air purifiers on the market. 
	-
It should be able to maintain an RH between 40-60% in a 40 m2 room.
	-
It should provide feedback on temperature, RH and particle level.
	-
In order for the IAQ-assistant to function automatically/optimally, it 
should always be ready to turn on and therefore always be plugged 
to power.
	-
It should be easy to empty the water reservoir.
	-
It should be able to easily be turned on/off via a physical interface.

4.3.2	 Drawstorming
A clear problem statement formulated as a question as a starting point 
is vital for a productive brainstorm (Cross, 2000) The overall starting 
point for the brainstorming sessions was: “How can the IAQ-assistant 
be integrated into the home?”. 
This question was too broad for framing the generation of ideas. 
Therefore, the topics formulated before the brainwalk in IKEA along 
with the topics that arose during the brainwalk were used as topics for 
brainstorming sessions. This was done to thoroughly explore the solution 
space. When generating ideas it’s important to have some frame, or 
restrictions, to be in the sweet spot between creativity and relevance of 
the ideas. 
Instead of a “classic” brainstorming session where the ideas are 
generated as words or sentences describing the ideas, it was decided 
to do a drawstorm and draw all generated ideas. To keep the creativity 
flowing it was not allowed to criticize any of the ideas during the 
brainstorming sessions. 
The drawstorming sessions were structured into short time slots for

the creativity and restricts the time spent on each drawing so that no 
time was wasted on unnecessary detailing. To ensure a wide exploration 
of the solution space the topics, and therefore the ideas, did not have

this point that synergy with a plant was the best secondary function for 
integrating the solution into people’s homes. A small selection of the 
generated ideas is shown on the next pages along with the topic from

All sketches have been made using pen and paper. To digitize the

Humidity propagation experiment
It was decided to try and answer how the placement of the IAQ-assistant 
would affect its performance. It wasn’t however possible to find good 
data on airflows and the propagation of humidity in an apartment like 
setting so it was decided to try and collect some ourselves. This is also 
an example of the iterative nature of the project process, having to 
research further during the concept development phase. 
A long story short, three battery-powered sensor units capable of 
measuring humidity and temperature were built and programmed 
(see Figure 4-7). The sensors measured data once every thirty second 
and sent this data to an online database. The sensor units were placed 
in three adjacent rooms in Jakob’s apartment to collect data on how 
the humidity levels increased/decreased relating to activities such as 
showering, clothes drying etc. The goal was to analyse this data and see 
if/how humidity would propagate from e.g. the bathroom to adjacent 
rooms after a shower, and hopefully use the results from this to determine 
how the placement of the IAQ-assistant would affect its performance. 
Unfortunately, a lot of interruptions in the test data occurred due to 
technical issues, connectivity problems and batteries running dry. This 
led to corrupted and noncontinuous data from which it was impossible 
to truly conclude anything. The data indicated that humidity did indeed 
rise in adjacent rooms e.g. after a shower in the bathroom the humidity 
in the living room would slowly increase the following hours. With the 
time scope and focus of the project in mind, it was decided not to redo 
the experiment and move on. See Appendix X for a more thorough and 
technical description of the experiment, sensor units and data analysis.

‘sweet spot for innovation’ which happens at the intersection of the 
three aspects. The desirability of a concept depends on aspects such as 
aesthetics and usability. Feasibility is about technical uncertainties and 
performance of the solution. Using well-known technology will mean 
high feasibility whereas developing novel technology will be riskier and 
have less feasibility of success. The final aspect in the trifecta is viability 
which relates to business sides of things. This isn’t only about profit but 
also about having a sustainable business model ensuring growth and 
viability for the future (Orton, 2017).

4.5.3	 Cardboard Mockups
Creating three-dimensional objects using two-dimensional cardboard 
works surprisingly well and the limit isn’t just rectangular shapes or plane 
surfaces. Using laser cutting and utilizing what’s known as a living hinge 
it’s possible to make rounded objects and very intricate shapes. A living 
hinge works by cutting slits in the cardboard on the area that should 
bend (see Figure 4-14). Dependent on the cardboard thickness and the 
pattern of the slits cut, the flexibility can be varied to fit the purpose.
The cardboard mockups gave us a much better understanding of how 
the different shapes actually look on a wall and how a change of a few 
centimeters can change a shape from looking good to be ill-proportioned 
(see Figure 4-15). One thing is to assess the size in a very large room 
like the Developer Hall in Skylab in which there are 10 meters to the 
ceiling (see Figure 4-16). Another thing is to assess the size of an object 
in the right context, which in this case is in the living room. In order 
to ensure that the proportions weren’t too big the cardboard mockups 
were brought home and hung up in Mads’ living room (see Figure 4-17).

4.5.5	 Design Renderings
When deciding which of the two concepts was to be the final, the inputs 
and opinions from other people were needed. It can however be difficult 
for people to look at a white cardboard model and imagine it as a finished 
product with a whole other appearance. You must have a relatively 
high level of abstraction in order to “see-through” the cardboard and 
image the final design of the product. Pointing and explaining at the 
cardboard-mockup e.g. this surface will be wood and this will be a white 
plastic surface might help some, but there will still be some who won’t 
be able to imagine it. It was, therefore, necessary to get the design to 
a higher detailed level for people to give input on. With the timeframe 
in mind, it was not a possibility to start building a lot of highly detailed 
mockups with different designs. 
It was decided to do CAD-models of the two concepts and use digital 
rendering to allow for faster completion of multiple design proposals. 
CAD-modelling was done in Autodesk Fusion 360 which is a software 
none of the authors has used before. It was nevertheless chosen to use 
this software as it presumably should have a great rendering studio and 
a cloud rendering function free to use for students. Rendering a picture 
of a CAD-model takes up a lot of computing power and it can take 
hours for even a well-equipped PC to finish just one render. The cloud 
rendering function allows the user to borrow computing power from 
‘the cloud’ thereby reducing the time it takes to render significantly. 
Although choosing to model with a new CAD-software led to some 
struggles it saved significant time when rendering.

which one of the designs they would be most likely to bring into their

4.7	 Selecting the Final Concept
With the insights from both the Instagram poll and the wall-feedback 
sessions, it was clear that people generally preferred the Flowerpot 
Base. Additionally, the Flowerpot Base is deemed as the most feasible 
of the two concepts. The opinion of the team is that an IAQ-assistant 
should be felt and not seen. The Flowerpot Base subtly integrates into 
the household and does the job without being noticed, whereas the 
Vertical Garden is more of a statement product hanging on the wall like 
a unique piece of art.  
The arguments against the Vertical Garden were, among others, that 
installation would be troublesome, and that when installed it was a 
very permanent thing. People often liked the aesthetics of the Vertical 
Garden and how it looked hanging on the wall in the renders. They did, 
however, also express concern about it being too big to have at home 
and that hanging plants seemed impractical and restricting in terms of 
plant types and sizes. 
Therefore, the Flowerpot Base was chosen as the final concept.
The two design proposals that got the most votes on the wall were both 
Flowerpot Bases and are shown in Figure 4-26. Both of these design 
proposals were with ‘high’ legs and combined wood and black elements.

design. It should be noted that the designs proposed in this phase were 
simply the result of the teams attempt to come up with designs of many 
different styles and not a methodical design approach. The final design 
might, therefore, despite insights from the above, end up looking quite 
different.

Key Takeaways
Conceptual development process
A design brief was formulated and used as a starting point for ideation. 
A large number of ideas were generated, evaluated and the nine 
most promising were selected. These nine concept ideas were further 
elaborated and re-evaluated ending in a selection of just two concepts. 
Multiple design proposals were created for each of the two concepts 
and given feedback. This feedback led to choosing the final concept.
Idea generation
The boundaries of the solution space were expanded via an inspiration 
trip to IKEA. Initial ideation was done by sketching all ideas using pen 
and paper. As the need for details grew higher, such as materials and 
colours, the media changed to CAD-modelling and digital renders. 
Concept evaluation
For evaluating the concepts it was important for the team to gain other 
people’s feedback and opinions. The team, therefore, did interactive 
feedback sessions with posters and physical cardboard mockups to have 
people think-out-loud and ask questions. To reach even more people an 
Instagram poll was conducted. 
Flowerpot base
The final concept chosen was the floor-standing Flowerpot Base as it was 
preferred not only by the team but more importantly by the people. The 
Flowerpot Base acts as a flower pedestal and thereby subtly integrates 
into the home without taking up additional floor space.

5.3	 Exploring Means to the

Having established the criteria and requirements of the IAQ-assistant 
the means to achieve these could be explored. This section will present 
alternative means to achieve each core functionality along with a 
specification of the chosen one.
5.3.1	 Dehumidify Air
Dehumidification of the air can be done in several ways and a selection of 
the existing technologies was described in section 3.4.1 Dehumidifying 
Technologies on page 26. Desiccant dehumidification is evaluated as 
the most suitable technology for the IAQ-assistant due to the following 
reasons. 
Desiccant dehumidification technology is just as efficient as compressor 
dehumidification at room temperature. However, the desiccant 
dehumidifying technology doesn’t utilize a compressor and can 
therefore be quieter during operation which is important. Secondly, the 
components of a desiccant wheel dehumidifier can be arranged to be 
very compact. This is needed to keep the cabinet of the IAQ-assistant 
small and with desirable dimensions. The reasons for not choosing Peltier 
dehumidification, although this is even more quiet and very compact is 
that the dehumidification capacity of a Peltier dehumidifier is not good 
enough. The thermodynamics of the system with a desiccant wheel can 
be seen from Figure 5-3. Humid air at room temperature is drawn into 
the system by a centrifugal fan (1). The air passes by the desiccant rotor 
in which the water molecules are caught (2). Finally, the air passes the 
condensator which is warm, and the air at room temperature is heated a 
bit. Dry and warm air is now exhausted from the system.

5.3.2	 Purify Air
As described in section 3.5 Air-Purifying Technologies on page 33 
there exist a lot of different technologies for purifying the air. The choice 
of technology depends on what kind of particulate matter should 
be removed from the air. The IAQ-assistant should work indoors and 
therefore remove particles such as dust, dust mites, mould spores, 
allergens, VOC’s and odours. The IAQ-assistant will utilize an air-quality 
sensor to determine whether or not it’s necessary to start operating and 
purifying the air. The purification of the air is done in three steps (see 
Figure 5-4):
1.	 Silver coated pre-filter: Filters large objects such as dust balls, hair 
etc. and has an antibacterial silver coating that releases nanoparticles 
which help kill bacteria.
2.	 HEPA PM2.5 filter: Removes 99,97% of the particles larger than 0,25 
μm including dust, dust mites, and mould spores.
3.	 Activated carbon filter: Removes VOC (volatile organic compounds), 
strong odours and harmful gasses.
The frequency of filter changes highly depends on the amount of air 
processed and the amount of particulate matter in that air. The pre-filter 
will have to be cleaned more often than the other filters are exchanged. 
This is simply done by washing it in water. If the filters get too dirty it 
will affect the efficiency of the IAQ-assistant. The more dirt caught in 
the filters the more resistance in the airflow. In general applications, a 
carbon filter needs to be changed twice a year and a HEPA filter needs 
to be changed once a year.

5.3.3	 Provide Feedback on IAQ
An insight from the survey and the interviews is the importance of giving 
feedback on the IAQ status to the user. Feedback can be provided by a 
physical user interface (UI) as sound, light or on a display. The feedback 
could also be given via the digital UI either in the app or as a widget 
on the home screen of the smartphone. The typical dehumidifier or air-
purifier has a physical UI with a range of buttons, LEDs and/or a display. 
This UI then controls all functions of the product as well as giving 
feedback on the current status of the different parameters.
It was discussed a lot how the physical UI of the IAQ-assistant should 
be. Should it be allowing control of desired humidity level, fan speed, 
operation timer etc? If so it should at least be aesthetically different from 
the typical dehumidifier UI i.e. the one on the Toyotomi dehumidifier and 
perhaps draw inspiration from more stylish physical UI’s like on modern 
Bluetooth speakers.
From the beginning of this project, it was thought that the solution 
should be for the Smart Home and therefore be internet-connected and 
controlled via an app. Having an app reduces the need for a physical 
UI. It was decided that the physical UI on the IAQ-assistant should be 
just one single button, a Standby button. With the Standby button, 
the user can always turn on/off the IAQ-assistant e.g. if it’s running at

5.3.4	 Allow User Control
The Standby button is the only control on the IAQ-assistant itself and 
will when pressed put the IAS-assistant into a standby mode. When the 
IAQ-assistant is put in standby mode the fan is turned off. It is still able 
to monitor the IAQ but it will not do anything about it. The IAQ-assistant 
is activated by tapping the button again, and the fan is now “allowed” to 
work again. The backlight of the standby button dims out when going in 
standby and dims up when activated again. The single most important 
function of the Standby button is to allow the user to stop the assistant 
from operating at inappropriate times. 
The decision of having just a single button means the user is to control 
all other functionality in the app. As the idea with the IAQ-assistant is 
that it works automatically the need for control is minimal. The app will 
therefore primarily be used to get status feedback, look at historical 
data etc. The app should, however, allow the user to configure the IAQ-
assistant to the user’s individual preferences, e.g. the user should be 
able to specify their wanted humidity level. Another essential feature of 
the app is the scheduling of operating hours for the IAQ-assistant. This 
will allow the user to configure when the IAQ-assistant should prioritize 
to operate e.g. while the user is at work. The user will also be able to 
configure a duration of the standby mode that is entered when pressing 
the Standby button. This duration could either be defined as a timeframe 
or as a postponement until next scheduled operation time. 
The app will have an onboarding session to guide the user when setting 
up the IAQ-assistant at home. This onboarding will e.g. help the user 
connect the IAQ-assistant to the internet via WiFi. By following the 
onboarding the user will automatically try most of the functionalities 
of the app enabling them to further explore the app later on. In this 
project, the scope is to build a wireframe for the app that can interactively 
visualize all core functionalities.
5.3.5	 Emptying the Water Tank
A requirement in the product specification is “The solution must ease 
the emptying of excess water”. It should perhaps be clarified that the 
reason it’s even necessary to be able to empty the water tank of the IAQ-
assistant is that it will dehumidify more water than a single plant needs. 
This requirement was based on the teams own experience and insights 
from the qualitative interviews where two interviewees mentioned how 
annoying it was to empty the water reservoir of their dehumidifiers. 
All the existing dehumidifiers analysed in this project comes with a 
removable water reservoir that the user has to empty. 
To enable the IAQ-assistant to water the plant on top of it, it was clear 
that a pump was needed to raise the water upwards. Having a removable 
water tank attach/detach to a pump every time it was removed/inserted 
would be unnecessarily complicated. It was therefore chosen not to 
have a removable water tank and instead empty the water tank using a 
pump. At this point, the most natural choice was to empty the water tank 
using the same pump and hose used for watering the plant. Another 
option was to place a tap on the bottom of the side of the IAQ-assistant,

an interaction and addition to the product. It was decided to stick with 
the emptying of the water tank through the same water hose used for 
watering the plant.
Having decided this, it had to be decided how the pump should be 
activated by the user. Three different ideas were on the table:
1.	 The user could press a button on the IAQ-assistant itself while 
holding the hose in the other hand emptying the water into a vessel

2.	 The user could place the nozzle into a vessel and activate the pump

3.	 The user activates the pump with a press on a button placed at the

vessel creating spillage. Using a button on the IAQ-assistant requires 
the user to bend over while emptying the tank is being emptied. The

is placed in the bottom of the cabinet at a height of approx. 0,3 m above

the water tank into another vessel in a height of up to 1,3 meters.
A float-switch inside the water tank ensures that the dehumidifying 
components turn off when the water tank is full, and the user is notified 
and asked to empty it. A full water tank does not mean that the IAQ-
assistant turns fully off. Dehumidification is disengaged by not rotating 
the desiccant wheel and turning the heater off. The fan can then still 
drag air through the IAQ-assistant and purify it.
How often the tank of the IAQ-assistant needs to be emptied depends 
on factors such as relative humidity and how much water the plant uses. 
In the summer the relative humidity is higher and the dehumidifier will 
extract more water. Likewise, the plant drinks the most water in the 
summer, and vice versa in the winter season.

5.4	 Design of Mechanics
After exploring and choosing means to the functions the mechanical 
structure of the IAQ-assistant could be designed. The internal structure 
of the IAQ-assistant must first and foremost be designed to ensure good 
performance of it’s two main functions: dehumidification and purification 
of the air.
5.4.1	 Airflow
To ensure the core functionality it’s key that the airflow is well designed. 
The airflow must first of all be designed to avoid short-circuiting, and 
secondly, it must be as silent as possible. 
Short-circuit of an airflow
A short circuit of the airflow would mean that the air exhausted from 
the IAQ-assistant would be drawn back into the inlet and pass through 
it once more (see Figure 5-17). This would mean that the IAQ-assistant 
would repeatedly process the same air over and over again and thereby 
not have an actual effect on the IAQ of the room. If the exhaust of the 
IAQ-assistant is too close to the intake or if the air is exhausted towards 
the intake short-circuiting could quite possibly occur. To avoid this it 
was decided to exhaust air upwards and outwards in an omnidirectional 
fashion.
The inlet is then placed underneath the IAQ-assistant which hopefully 
means that short-circuiting won’t be a problem. The hope is that this 
design of the airflow would mean that the slightly warmer air from the 
exhaust of the IAQ-assistant will rise towards the ceiling pushing air 
downwards eventually meaning that all air in the room will have been 
exchanged.

Design of a silent airflow
An important aspect to remember when designing the IAQ-assistant is 
the noise level as it should not be an annoyance if running while people 
are at home. This means that the sound pressure level should be less 
than 40dB. The most prominent noise from the IAQ-assistant will be the 
consequential noise of the airflow. The noise of an airflow is created by 
turbulence and friction of the molecules in the air. To minimise this noise 
it should be tried to design a smooth airflow and avoid having sharp 
corners and edges which the airflow has to pass.
Another factor attributing to the noise created by an airflow is its 
power i.e. the volume and velocity of the air flowing. To get air to flow 
through a filter a certain air pressure is needed, and the more air and 
the smaller the filter’s surface area, the more pressure needed. Having 
an omnidirectional exhaust allows having circular filters increasing the 
surface area and thereby decreasing the air pressure needed. 
None of the team members has previous experience with airflow design 
and neither has taken courses in fluid mechanics. The design of the 
airflow in this project is based on rules-of-thumb and basic principles 
and has not been analysed mathematically in any way. To try and get 
guidance and expert insights contact was initiated with Brian Elmegaard 
and Lei Fang. Brian Elmegaard is a professor at DTU who among other 
things teaches thermodynamics. Lei Fang is also a professor at DTU and 
an expert in building ventilation and heating systems. Lei Fang was, 
unfortunately, not available although he probably would be the right 
guy to talk to. It was however possible to meet with Brian Elmegaard 
who gave us his inputs on the thermodynamics of our system but could 
not give us much insight on the design of the airflow.
Airflow experiment
As mentioned it was decided to place the inlet underneath the IAQ-
assistant. To test if this would cause any issues a test setup for the 
component assembly from the Toyotomi dehumidifier was built. One of 
the aspects the team wanted to test was how close to the ground the 
inlet could be without restricting the airflow. 
The temporary cabinet was made from laser-cut acrylic boards with slits 
cut in the bottom and on the sides, see Figure 5-18. With this test setup 
it was possible to test the capabilities of the fan when drawing air into 
the cabinet either from the bottom, the sides or both.
The reason that the whole component assembly is tilted and not fully 
horizontal, is to assure that the water runs out of the condensator and 
not backs up in the machine. An insight from the process of building this 
test setup was that a tilt switch secured that the dehumidifier couldn’t 
operate if the dehumidifier was not standing upright. The team had 
to short circuit the tilt switch to make the dehumidifier operate in this 
position. A tilt switch should also be included in the design of the IAQ-
assistant as it is important to ensure that the water ends up in the water 
tank.
The test showed that the assembly was still able to dehumidify when 
lying horizontally. Due to the closing of Skylab this experiment was not 
finished and the team was not able to conclude anything about the 
height needed to ensure a proper airflow.

5.4.2	 Dehumidification Rate
The cabinet of the IAQ-assistant is dimensioned so that the necessary 
components (desiccant wheel, heater, condensator, fan, filters etc.) can 
realistically fit inside of it. The sizes of these components are based 
on the sizes as the ones in the disassembled Toyotomi dehumidifier 
(see section 3.4.2 Reverse Engineering a Dehumidifier on page 
31). It can therefore be argued that the IAQ-assistant will achieve a 
dehumidification capacity similar to the Toyotomi dehumidifier. The 
maximum dehumidification capacity of the IAQ-assistant will be up to 8 
litres per day in optimal conditions (27°C 80% RH). This is a lot, however, 
the optimal conditions will likely never occur in a Scandinavian living 
room meaning that the actual dehumidification rate is a lot less. It is 
a good thing to over-dimension the dehumidification capacity as this 
means it will need a shorter amount of time to lower the RH than a 
smaller dehumidifier would.

In Figure 5-19, is shown an exploded view of the internal components of 
the IAQ-assistant. At the bottom of the assembly is the water tank which 
is doughnut-shaped to maximize its volume while allowing the airflow 
up through the middle. A section of the doughnut-shape is cut away to 
make space for the bottom half of the heater unit. Then come the rotor 
and the opposite part of the heater which collects the hot damp air and 
leads it into the condensator. 
The condensator is placed at a slight angle to ensure that the condensed 
water runs towards the outlet in the lower end. This outlet leads down 
into a little water trap compartment in the top of the water tank. When 
filled, the excess water in the trap overflows into the water tank itself. 
The reason for the water trap is to deny any hot air in the condensator 
escaping through the water outlet. The hot air is instead led out of the 
condensator in the opposite side and fed back into the heater closing 
the loop. In the middle of the condensator is a cutout to make space for 
the fan motor. The fan motor drives a centrifugal fan that sucks the air up 
through the assembly and pushes it out through the two circular filters in 
the top. To ensure proper airflow an airflow divider is used to ensure that 
all the air is led into the centrifugal fan.

Key Takeaways
Product specification
A product specification has been used throughout the project to organize 
requirements and criteria for the IAQ-assistant. It has worked as decision 
base for evaluating the different means to each function.
Dessicant dehumidification
Dessicant dehumidification technology was chosen as the most suitable 
to use in the IAQ-assistant. Reasons for this, is a combination of a low 
noise level and a suitable dehumidification capacity for a typical living 
room.
HEPA and Activated Carbon Filter
For purifying the air HEPA and Activated Carbon Filter have been 
chosen. HEPA is able to remoce very small particles from the air and the 
Activated Carbon Filter is effective in removing VOC’s and odours.
The Standby Button
It was decided to have just one physical button as user interface on the 
IAQ-assistant itself. The primary reason for this was the vision: IAQ-as-
furniture.
App controlled
The user will primarily use the app for interacting with the IAQ-assistant. 
This includes gainnig feedback on IAQ and configuring parameters.
Air flow design
The IAQ-assistant is designed to have a smooth airflow ensuring a low 
noise level. The risk of short circuiting the air flow has been minimized 
by exhausting upwards and intake air from beneath.

5.7	 Specifications of NJORD
5.7.1	 Foiled Medium-Density Fibreboard
The cabinet of NJORD should be made of medium-density fibreboard 
(MDF) covered with a melamine foil imitating the look of ash. First 
of all, using MDF covered with foil instead of solid wood is cheaper. 
MDF can be manufactured to any shape using industrial manufacturing 
processes and is available in boards of larger size than real wood. So 
instead of glueing several pieces of ash together to make the top and 
bottom plates, an MDF board with melamine foil look like a single piece 
of wood. If the top board should have been made from real ash some 
huge trees would have been required to cut a board of ø400 mm. Wood 
expands when wet, but is treated with the right lacquer or oil the wood 
could become somewhat resistant. MDF covered with a melamine foil is 
water-resistant and is widely used e.g. kitchen cabinets (Sebring, 2016).
5.7.2	 Power Consumption
The power consumption of NJORD will be similar to that of the Toyotomi 
dehumidifier which is 350 watts on average and 620 watts at maximum. 
Let’s say that NJORD has to run at low effect for two hours and at high 
effect for one hour per day to control the indoor air quality. This would 
result in 350 W x 2 h + 620 W x 1 h = 1320 Wh consumed.  1 KWh costs 
approximately 2,25 kr so running NJORD in one day would cost  2,97 kr 
per day and approximately 90 kr per month.

5.8.5	 Onboarding
Onboarding is, in the field of user-interaction design, a term describing 
the user’s first experience with an app. The onboarding provides the 
necessary instructions and highlights the key features and functions of 
the app. If done correctly the onboarding will greatly improve the user 
experience and leave the user feeling confident in using the app and 
explore it further.
In the case of NJORD, the onboarding will not just introduce the app 
itself but also guide the user in setting up the physical product. NJORD 
is by default set to initiate in auto mode in all settings, and the user can 
afterwards configure NJORD with personal preferences.
The following list describes each step in the onboarding that happens 
the first time the user opens the NJORD app.
1.	 The user is told to place NJORD and plug it to power. When NJORD 
is turned on it creates a local network that the user connects to by 
NFC or scanning the barcode as previously described.
2.	 When the local network connection is established NJORD guides 
the user in connecting NJORD to the home WiFi network.
3.	 NJORD helps the user specify the plant that is placed on top of it 
and what size the pot is.
4.	 The user is introduced to the home screen and the elements on this.

5.8.7	 Discussion on App Prototype
Particle level mapping 
The standard unit for measuring particle pollution ug/m3 would not 
make much sense for regular people. It was therefore decided to map 
the particle level on a scale from 0-100. This was inspired by a project 
from the Indian government that created an air quality index (Airveda, 
2017). A low value is good, indicating few particles in the air and vice 
versa. In the centre of the gauge is shown the status as either being Bad, 
Okay or Good accompanying the mapped number. The gauge uses a 
colour range from green to red to indicate the status of the particle level.
Cockpit analogy
The three gauges for temperature, humidity and particle level are all a bit 
different. The relative humidity is good when the indicator is at the top 
whereas the particle level is good when the indicator point to the lower 
left of the gauge. This inconsistency could be confusing if the gauges 
were to be shown beside each other e.g. as in a cockpit. However, as the 
gauges, each have a separate screen the user will never look at more 
than one of them at a time.
Multiple NJORD’s
Having multiple NJORDs should definitely be possible just as one of the 
respondents in the feedback asks about. This was also discussed during 
the project where it was for a moment discussed targeting office spaces 
with the IAQ-solution which would most certainly require more than one 
unit. However, in the current app wireframe, there isn’t incorporated the 
option of changing between controlling multiple NJORDs. This would 
potentially be part of future work.
Global indicator
In the feedback given one asked for a graphical global indicator for 
the IAQ instead of just text. However, the team believes it’s more 
educational to have the user know that the indoor climate quality is a 
result of multiple parameters. Using text instead of only graphics helps 
personalize NJORD making it feel more interactive and communicative.

6.1	 Discussion on NJORD
It can be discussed whether the idea of an IAQ-improving product is 
even a good idea. A dehumidifier doesn’t fix the causes of excessive 
humidity, it treats the symptoms. The same goes for an air-purifier. The 
causes for bad indoor climate are however almost impossible to fix as 
they are such a big part of our way of living. People can’t stop doing 
activities such as showering or cooking food. Neither can everyone live 
in recently constructed buildings that are well isolated and ventilated. 
The solution must therefore be to treat the symptoms instead, with 
e.g. an IAQ-assistant such as NJORD. Early on in the project, we asked 
ourselves “Why don’t more people own a dehumidifier or air-purifier?”. 
With insights from research and survey, the answer is a combination 
of people not recognizing the problem and not prioritizing buying a 
solution. The recent years have shown a growing trend in the market 
for air-purifiers (Markets and Markets, 2020) and people are becoming 
increasingly aware of the health effects of living in a bad indoor climate. 
Corona-virus forced us all to be even more at home doing 2020 which 
we believe will increase the momentum of this trend. 
Despite having put some thought into the internal structure of 
components and the airflow of NJORD it’s far from finished and ready 
for production. One of the big question marks is the airflow design 
and the noise created by this airflow. However, not having any previous 
experience with design of airflows meant that we relied on basic 
principles to help us guide the design. An unknown factor is whether 
or not having to draw the air through both the desiccant wheel and 
the filters will create a need for higher air pressure resulting in more 
noise. We tried to get some expert knowledge on our structural design 
by reaching out to professors Brian Elmegaard and Lei Fang, without 
much luck. Detailing of the internal components assembly will be part of 
future work and require expertise in multiple fields of engineering.
It’s important to understand that NJORD is thought of as more than a 
combined dehumidifier and air-purifier controlled by an app to be smart. 
NJORD is an assistant and will besides taking care of the IAQ provide 
users with educational information and preventive actions. Through this 
project, it has become clear that there are many aspects of which we had 
never thought of in relation to the indoor climate. The same will apply to 
most other people. Providing users with essential information on their 
indoor climate will help them to a better understanding of not just the 
effects of poor IAQ, but also what actions they can take to improve or 
prevent it. 
NJORD is designed to be aesthetically pleasing. It looks more like a 
piece of furniture than an appliance. Furniture design is a balance 
between practicality and aesthetics. Aesthetics are subjective but it’s still 
possible to design something that many people like. Look at the iconic 
pieces by the many famous Danish designers. Almost everyone agrees 
that these are fantastic both in terms of aesthetics but also in use. We 
have designed a product that a lot of people would benefit from, and 
we believe that the design of NJORD is something that many people 
would like to have in their home. NJORD appeals widely with its subtle 
design.
In many parts of the world, the problem is not excessive indoor humidity 
but instead very dry air. It’s very unpleasant to be in dry-air for elongated 
periods of time, but it was decided not to include the ability to humidify 
in the concept of NJORD. This would have been necessary if NJORD 
was designed for e.g. California

6.2	 Discussion on Our Design 
Process
Throughout a design project such as this a lot of decisions are made, 
some on a very thorough basis and others not so much. It will always 
be possible to gather more knowledge on a matter before making a 
decision, but in order to move forward you will sometimes have to use 
‘gut-feeling’ or instinct. During this project, a lot of the aspects have 
been very subjective and difficult to quantify, such as the aesthetics 
of NJORD. We have, however, tried to methodically approach these 
decisions and gather feedback as often as possible to help guide the 
development using other than our own opinions. Nonetheless, there will 
always be someone who dislikes the design, the colour or the material. 
Looking back we were probably a little blind to this fact and kept trying 
to find arguments for why some subjective matter would be preferable 
to another. We could have decided to purely base the aesthetics on our 
own preferences and spend time on other aspects, however, it has also 
been educational to go about it as we did.  
Prior experience with design projects meant that we had a large variety 
of tools, methods and process approaches to choose from when starting 
this project. This experience made it easier to imagine and plan out this 
project and helped to initially select an approach. We have to some extent 
followed the ‘Design Bible’ for methodical development of physical 
products, “Engineering Design Methods” by Nigel Cross. Both team 
members have previously used this approach and the accompanying 
methods. Having prior experience with this methodology meant that we 
could more precisely select which methods to use when and which to 
not use at all. As an example, Nigel Cross is very insisting on quantifying 
the performance of concepts in order to evaluate them against each 
other. Having used this approach before we knew this is most suitable 
for mechanical concepts with more easily quantifiable performance 
parameters, but not suitable for evaluating our concepts as these were 
at a less tangible state where the performance of the parameters was 
subjective. 
It could be discussed whether or not our early broadening of the solution 
space beyond the initial scope of a combination between dehumidifier 
and plant was necessary. After all, we ended up choosing a concept 
quite similar to the initial idea. We were however not sure whether this 
combination was the best and if there were better alternatives out there. 
Therefore the time spent on exploring and evaluating this seems well 
spent and our belief in the idea was strengthened.  
Both team members usually tend to focus on technical problem-solving, 
mechanics and electronics, rather than socio-technical aspects. It was 
intended to have this focus during the final phase of the project when 
building a functional prototype of NJORD. Corona restrictions meant 
we couldn’t access workshops meaning that building the prototype 
was not an option. We had however foreseen the restrictions and had 
a plan B ready with a change of focus to the digital user interface. It 
was a great challenge to develop the app-wireframe and was very 
educational both in terms of UI-theory and hands-on experiences 
with the Adobe XD software. Looking back we have however used our 
technical problem-solving skills and used rapid prototyping methods 
such as 3D-printing and laser cutting to build and test prototypes. The 
Humidity Propagation experiment also had us apply our knowledge on 
electronics and programming, even though the experiment ended up 
proving difficult to complete.

A student who has completed this project can:
1.	 Collect and assess data related to user needs.
Through the initial research phase of the project, we have proven that 
we are able to methodically collect and assess data. Especially the work 
with the survey proves how we thoroughly prepared the questions to 
allow for the gathering of better data in a format suitable for analysis.
2.	 Specify product specifications based on insights from research.
The iterative work with the product specification proves how we 
continuously and methodically translated insights to design objectives

Our market research of dehumidifiers, air purifiers, combined solutions

the initial idea of a synergy between a dehumidifier and a plant. The ideas

and brainwalking sessions but evaluated and selected in a systematic 
fashion.

empirical insights.
Throughout the project have we sought feedback from users to help our 
decision making. This has been shown extensively with the cardboard

6.	 Evaluate concepts based on a created set of specifications resulting 
in a final concept.
Through two iterations of concept development and evaluation have 
we shown that we can methodically approach concept evaluation. As 
described in the report have we used both design objectives and later 
on more specific requirements and criteria to evaluate concepts. For

6.4	 The Next Step
As mentioned the design of NJORD is far from ready to market, and the 
design still contains uncertainties. In terms of product development the 
components used in NJORD have to be designed in detail. Experts in 
the field of fluid mechanics should design the airflow and thermodynamic 
experts should help dimension the components to the specific use-case 
of NJORD. The app wireframe is almost at a state where it can be used 
by app developers to start develop an actual app.
Specific tasks are:
	-
Detailed design of internal components, ensuring a silent but 
functional airflow.
	-
Add an onboarding session to the wireframe and develop a real app.
	-
Add the ability to connect more than one NJORD to the app. 
	-
Include the option to define room size, use of the room (e.g. bedroom, 
living room etc.) in the app, to integrate NJORD even better by 
turning LED off at night if NJORD is placed in the bedroom.
NJORD to market
To get NJORD to market the first step would be to apply for design 
protection in the EU. A way to get to the market could be to contact 
a furniture retailer like IKEA and ask for a chance to pitch the idea for 
them. If they like the idea a license agreement could be established. The 
license agreement would allow e.g. IKEA to produce and sell NJORD 
against a share of the turnover paid to the creators of NJORD.
IKEA would be a great sales channel as they are the world’s largest 
furniture retailer with roots in Scandinavia. The fact that they have started 
to integrate technology into furniture, as in the case of SYMFONISK (the 
collaboration between SONOS and IKEA), is indicating that they could 
as well be ready for a product such as NJORD.
A partnership with a retailer like IKEA would mean that NJORD could 
get ready for production and reach the market much faster.

6.5	 Conclusion
People spend up to 90% of their time indoors and the indoor climate, 
therefore, has a huge effect on people’s lives and well-being. In 
Denmark, every fifth building has problems with excessive humidity and 
22.000 DALY’s are lost every year. The main contributor to this is particle 
pollution with the majority coming from indoor sources. To resolve this, 
the novel concept of NJORD was developed. NJORD dehumidifies 
and purifies the air and utilizes the extracted water for watering a plant 
instead of discarding it. 
The design goal was to develop a desirable indoor-air-quality assistant 
that synergizes with houseplants and blends into the interior decor of 
Scandinavian households. NJORD ensures a healthy indoor climate 
for the user without them having to compromise on aesthetics as with 
the typical appliances on the market. NJORD fits into the Scandinavian 
interior decor with its minimalistic cylindrical design made in ash with 
french wicker panels. NJORD is controlled via an app which has been 
developed to have matching visual identity and high user-friendliness. 
The wireframe is presented in the report as an interactive prototype. 
A well structured design process ensured that the project team was 
capable of developing an innovative solution to the defined problem. The 
Agile-Stage-Gate hybrid approach allowed for an iterative development 
where new insights could be implemented. 
The problems with indoor air quality are substantial and we believe that 
NJORD could be a great part of the solution to this problem in many 
Scandinavian households.
