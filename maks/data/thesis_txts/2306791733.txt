Abstract
Sound equalization in enclosure at low frequency is a decisive topic for many applications
where a PA system is used, such as music studios or home cinema. Traditional acoustic
solutions involving sound absorber being very expensive and inconvenient at these frequen-
cies, other solutions are needed. In particular, extensive research has been done concerning
the possible use of additional sound sources, microphones and signal processing to solve
these problems through active control. By considering the sound ﬁeld in a room as the
sum of modes of resonance it is possible to stimulate those modes so as to obtain a given
target sound ﬁeld in the room.
An analysis of one these solutions based on the simulation of a plane wave in a rectangular
room is performed. From this analysis, a new approach using multiple-point equalization
is proposed. This method aims at optimizing both the loudspeaker positions and ﬁlters
necessary to achieve equalization in a given listening area.
Simulations are performed
to investigate the eﬃciency of this technique. Finally, measurements are performed to
examine problems linked to practical implementation.

Chapter 1
Introduction and Motivation
1.1
Introduction
Because of resonances, the low frequency sound ﬁeld in a room is highly dependent on its
shape, as well as the position of the sound sources and the listeners. This becomes a major
issue when a PA system is used in small or medium sized rooms, at the frequencies with
a wavelength comparable to the size of the room. In music studios for example, the audio
engineer should ideally listen to the sound as it is recorded but the room will inevitably
change its spectral content. Typical room acoustic treatment is usually ineﬀective at these
frequencies or comes with a high cost. Recent technological advancements have brought
the opportunity to use sound sources combined with microphones and a signal processing
unit to deal with this kind of issue [Hansen and Snyder, 1997]. This method, called active
sound control, uses additional sound waves to destructively interfere with the unwanted
ones and is particularly suited for low frequencies.
One of the ﬁrst idea of an application of active control was described by Paul Lueg in [Paul,
1936], where the author suggested to use an additional sound source to control the sound
ﬁeld in a waveguide. Later in the 1950s, [Olson and May, 1953] proposed an ”electronic
sound absorber”, device that aims at reducing the sound pressure using a loudspeaker cou-
pled to a microphone. The implementation of both systems were delayed by the available
technology and the lack of understanding of the physical principles involved. It was not
until the beginning of the 1980s that active control regained popularity, partly because
the advances in electronics allowed for commercial production. The further advances in
microelectronics and digital signal processing in the 1990s contributed even more to the
general interest in this domain.
While some applications of global control aim at cancelling the eﬀect of an undesired
source, other focus on limiting the eﬀect of the room on the output of a given source. Most
of nowadays commercial applications focus on the ﬁrst approach, for example reducing the
noise in ventilation ducts or transportation [Nelson and Elliott, 1992]. The second one,
which is the main focus of this thesis, is more appropriate for applications such a music
studios, small music venues or home cinema. Even though extensive research has been
made on the possible ways to achieve equalization, few are the solutions that resulted in
a commercial applications until now. As a result, most of the problems linked to room
resonances at low frequencies are still dealt with using empirical methods.
This aim of this thesis was to investigate if a general procedure to achieve global equal-
ization in a room using active control could be provided. It seemed important that this
solution should allow enough ﬂexibility to be applied to diﬀerent room shapes, but also
to to take other practical considerations into account, such as the cost. This was done by
ﬁrst studying a speciﬁc system, based on the simulation of a plane wave in a rectangular
room [Celestinos and Nielsen, 2008b]. Because of the intuitive nature of this approach and
its eﬃciency, it was of interest to get a better understanding of its working principles in

Chapter 2
Theory
According to [Jacobsen and Juhl, 2013], in lightly damped enclosures, standing waves
appear at certain frequencies due to sound reﬂections against the walls. At these so called
resonance frequencies or natural frequencies, the sound pressure adopts speciﬁc spatial
distributions called mode shapes. This eﬀect is particularly important at low frequencies,
where the modal density (the average number of modes per Hertz) is low. By assuming
loudspeakers as being omnidirectional at low frequencies, they can be assimilated to point
sources which become a convenient model [Leach, 2003]. In the following report, harmonic
signals are assumed and the time dependency of the quantities involved is omitted.

Chapter 3
Choice of an equalization method
In this chapter, an overview of active equalization methods is presented. Then, one of
these solutions is studied more in details. Finally, a new method is formulated based on
this investigation.
3.1
Existing approaches
Equalization ﬁlters can be used to modify the input of loudspeakers in order to compensate
for the undesired eﬀect of the room. Several approaches exist concerning the criteria that
should be used to optimize these ﬁlters in order to achieve equalization. Using only one
sensor, the use of FIR ﬁlters can provide satisfying results around the sensor position, but
generally deteriorates the sound ﬁeld elsewhere in the room. Minimizing the least-mean
squares diﬀerence between the sound pressure recorded at several sensor positions and a
target can provide good results in an extended area [Elliott and Nelson, 1989]. This has
also been investigated in the frequency domain in [Asano and Swanson, 1995], with the
goal to approximate a given target sound ﬁeld through modal reconstruction. The time
domain solutions have the advantage of being able to equalize random signals, but are not
able to achieve the same level of equalization as frequency domain solutions. The ﬁlters
produced by the second method might however have non-causal impulse responses, which
has to be compensated for with a modelling delay when implementing them.
The quantity and positions of the sources and the sensors will inﬂuence the quality and
area of equalization, because it will aﬀect how the overall system is able to control the
resonance modes. The optimal source placement to achieve low frequency equalization
was for example investigated by [GROH, 1974], while [Welti, 2002] studied the level of
equalization that is achievable in a given area depending on the number of loudspeakers
that are used. Similarly, several loudspeaker positions, combined with optimal ﬁlters, were
compared by [Santillan, 1999]. It resulted that the size of the equalization area was limited
by the maximum frequency to equalize.
In order to achieve global equalization in a rectangular room, the simulation of a plane
wave was suggested by [Santillan, 2001]. In this method, a large number of loudspeakers
placed on two opposite walls is used along with a multiple-point equalization method to
excite mainly the modes that are in the direction of propagation of the desired plane wave.
While all the sources at one wall a playing the same signal in phase, the reﬂections at the
other end of the room are cancelled by another set of sources, playing the same signal in
anti-phase, delayed by the time it takes for the plane wave to cross the room.
Later research has shown that the number of needed loudspeakers can be signiﬁcantly
reduced by placing them on appropriate locations [Celestinos and Nielsen, 2005], without
the need for mutiple-point equalization. This has resulted in the creation of the Controlled
Acoustic Bass System (CABS) [Celestinos and Nielsen, 2008b] shown on ﬁgure 3.1. In this

It has later been shown by [Stefanakis et al., 2008] that using the multiple-point method
with a plane wave as the target gave the best results when the sources were placed on
the nodal planes of the ”undesired” modes, in the case of a rectangular room but also
for other room shapes. These modes have mode shapes with a pressure gradient that is
globally perpendicular to the desired direction of propagation, so that mainly the modes
for which the pressure gradient is globally parallel to this direction are stimulated.
A plane wave is the perfect match for the geometry of a rectangular room, because it allows
to avoid reﬂections from all the walls that are parallel to the direction of propagation.
However, there does not seem to be an equivalently eﬃcient solution for other room shapes.
In fact, even in a rectangular room, any surface that is tilted or shifted away from a wall will
create undesired reﬂections and change the mode shapes of the room, hence deteriorating
the quality of equalization. A sound wave that would go through the whole area of the
room and avoid most reﬂections at the same time would have a highly complex shape and
would thus require a excessive number of sources to be created. Since it cannot be done
in most practical cases, because of the high cost and the amount of space necessary, it is
seems that an approximated method based on modal control could be applied in a more
general case. What is more, these approaches rely heavily on the possibility to place the
sources at the ideal positions, which is not necessarily the case in practice.
For this reason, it was investigated how the behaviour of the sources in [Santillan, 2001]
contributes to the creation of a plane wave, as well as the possible role of modal control.
The goal of this investigation was to understand the inner mechanisms behind this method,

3.3
Optimization problem formulation
In order to provide a practical, systematic solution to equalization issues, a general problem
was stated as follows:
If a main source is placed at a ﬁxed position in a room, how can other loudspeakers be
used, in order to reduce the eﬀect of the room on the listeners experience?
This could be applied to practical cases in which a source is placed at a ﬁxed location and
cannot be moved. This could be for example because the sound needs to be perceived as
coming from that location, or simply because it is physically impossible to place the source
somewhere else. By studying the eﬃciency of the equalization that can be achieved for
diﬀerent numbers of additional loudspeakers, it would then be possible to choose a solution
depending on its cost and feasibility. Following a multiple-point optimization method, this
would be done by optimizing the secondary sources positions, as well as the ﬁlters applied
to all the sources. The number and positions of the points over which to optimize could
be adapted depending on the application. For music studios for example, the area covered
by the sensors would be rather small, around the head of the audio engineer.
For a given set of L sound source positions, equation 2.28 provides the best set of ﬁlters
to apply to these sources, in order to approximate a given target sound ﬁeld in the least-
squares sense over a set of sensors. Hence, for a given room shape, number of sources and

Simulation study
This chapter describes the simulations that were made in order to test the optimization

4.1
Simulation problem parameters
For the simulations, the main source was placed in the corner of a rectangular room of
dimensions Lx = 3.29m, Ly = 4.38m and Lz = 2.97m. These dimensions correspond to
the ones of DTU’s ﬂanking transmission room. A rectangular room was chosen at ﬁrst,
because of the simplicity of implementation of Green’s function in this case. 9 sensors
were placed in a square centred at x = Lx/2 and y = Ly/2 in the horizontal plane at
z = 1.8 m, which would correspond the height of a standing listener’s head. A distance of
λmin/10 between the sensors was used both in x and y directions, where λmin = c/fmax is
the smallest wavelength in the frequency range of interest, corresponding to the maximum
frequency. This distance, was chosen in order to avoid unexpected behaviour between the
sensors. The dimensions of the listening area is here 68.6 cm by 68.6 cm.
Because this method is aimed at being later applied to non rectangular rooms, it was
decided to place the primary source at rp = (0.3, 0, 0.3) m in order to avoid being located
on any nodal plane in the frequency range of interest. Because the target sound ﬁeld
contains phase information, it was ﬁrst expected that the secondary sources would behave
similarly to in [Santillan, 2001] and [Celestinos and Nielsen, 2008b], that is absorbing
the incoming wave. Following this strategy, a large number of secondary sources would

4.1.1
Algorithm convergence and computing time restrictions
When using this method, it soon appeared that the cost function had several local min-
ima, number that increased with the amount of secondary sources. In order to overcome
this problem, the initial positions of the secondary sources were randomized and the opti-
mization routine was run a large number of times. A comparison of the diﬀerent possible
solutions based on the value of Jo was then made in order to choose the most relevant one.
This approach being very time consuming, some choices were made in order to reduce the
computing time at each iteration:
• The number of sensors was limited to 9 at ﬁrst.
• The frequency range of equalization was limited to 1 < f < 100Hz, with frequency
step of df = 1Hz.
• The secondary sources were forced to be on the walls, so that each source position
was only deﬁned by two variables.

4.3.3
Sensitivity change in source position
In this section, the stability of the equalization with respect to the sources’ position is
investigated. First, the position of each secondary source was moved along the wall in a
random direction. This was done 50 times for each source. The mean value of the MSFD
and the corresponding standard deviation are shown in ﬁgure 4.19. Then, the position
of the secondary sources was moved oﬀthe wall, towards the inside of the room. Figure
4.20 shows the corresponding changes in the MSFD. In these two cases, the optimal ﬁlters
obtained for the initial positions of the sources were kept.
It can be seen that both the SD and the MD increase when the source positions are
changed. The MD in particular increases signiﬁcantly. Already with a shift of 10 cm, a
1 dB increase is observed. This can be explained by the fact that the sources’ ﬁlters are
meant to stimulate the room modes in a speciﬁc way. Since the way a point source is
exciting the modes is very dependent on its position, it makes sense that the ﬁlters are not
adapted when the position is changed. The value of the standard deviation increases with
the distance, because since the further away a source is moved in a random direction, the
more chances it has to stimulate the modes in a completely diﬀerent way.
When the sources are moved towards the inside of the room, no signiﬁcant change is seen
before a distance of 10 cm, after which both the SD and the MD start to increase. Once
again, the MD seems more sensitive to source displacement.
The same process was used again, this time by recalculating the optimal ﬁlters for the new
source positions. For the sensitivity along the wall, the position was randomly changed
20 times. Because of the size of the error bars, the Magnitude Deviation and the Spatial
Deviation in ﬁgure 4.21 are plotted separately. Figure 4.22 shows the changes in the MSFD
when the sources are moved away from the wall.
In this case, the changes in MSFD are much smaller, as both the SD and the MD stay in
the same order of magnitude until a distance of 1 m.

It can be seen that neither the values the SD or the MD are monotonically increasing
with the mean value of Jo. It means that this criteria for choosing the best solutions does
not accurately represents the quality of equalization, if the MSFD is used as a reference
criteria. However, calculating the MSFD requires to know the pressure at all the sensor
points. This could have been done during the optimization process, at the cost of a longer
computation time.

Chapter 5
Measurements
In order to verify the eﬃciency of the system in a real situation, measurements were per-
formed in DTU’s ﬂanking transmission room. Unfortunately, problems were encountered
when processing the results of these measurements and it was not possible to perform
them again.
==PAGE:54==
Chapter 6
Conclusion
An analysis of a global equalization method based on plane wave simulation was done.
From there, a new approach base on multiple-point optimization was formulated. Simula-
tions showed good theoretical results in the frequency domain, but faced some limitations
when moving to the time domain. Measurements were performed to quantify the practical
limitation of the method, but time constraints and technical issues did not allow to use
the corresponding results.
