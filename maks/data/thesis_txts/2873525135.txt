Abstract
With the rapid development of data centers and increasing demands for computational
capability, power converters featuring high power density and high eﬃciency are increasingly
required. In this thesis, an LLC resonant converter combined with a current-doubler rectiﬁer
topology is selected to address high-current and low-voltage conversion scenarios, speciﬁcally
for a DC-DC converter rated from 48 V down to 1.8 V. An integrated magnetic structure,
combining the transformer with the current-doubler inductors, is designed and implemented.
The converter is estimated to achieve an eﬃciency of approximately 96.7 %. Additionally,
detailed PCB layout design and experimental veriﬁcation are presented to validate the pro-
posed approach.
Keywords: LLC resonant circuit, current doubler rectiﬁer, magnetic integration, core losses

1.1
Background
Now days, as AI computing demand continues to rise, there is huge demand for high
eﬃciency and high power density power converters. However, In order to make voltage step
down from utility to low-voltage domain for processors, there are amounts of losses in power
conversion. In traditional data center power architecture , 12V bus voltage was included in
server rack, leading to an approximate 80 % eﬃciency from medium voltage to chip voltage.
According to recent surveys of voltage regulation modules (VRMs) for 48V data-center, it
subscribes the 12 V voltage stage with single 48V-low voltage (1.8 V) enable >700 w/m3
power density and and peak eﬃciency over 93%. Hence directly single stage power conver-
sion topologies such as LLC and hybrid switched capacitors VRMs are now widely applied
for high power eﬃciency and high current density demand situation.
Conventional hard-switching converters such as synchronous multiphase buck work un-
der hundred kilohertz.
As switching frequency increases to mega hertz, the product of
MOSFET parasitic energy, diode or body-diode reverse-recovery charge and gate-driver loss
grow much higher with switching frequency, which consequently result in higher turn-on and
turn- oﬀconduction loss. Hence, soft-switching are employed to high switching frequency
circuit, such as introducing a resonant tank so that voltage or current is forced to zero before
switching transition.
The LLC topology oﬀers simultaneous ZVS on the primary and ZCS (or near-ZVS) on
the synchronous rectiﬁers, a ﬂat gain curve that tolerates 2:1 bus variation, and naturally
sinusoidal tank currents that minimize EMI. GaN-based demonstrations > 93 % at 48 V to 1
V VRMs with power densities above 700 W/m3. These attributes make soft-switching LLC
converters the potential model for next-generation single-stage 48 V to sub-1 V regulators
in data-center racks.

Topology Selection and Analysis
In this chapter, primary side and secondary side topology are decided based on com-
parison and analysis. Complete theoretical topology is shown and analyzed for steady state.
Moreover, parameters of topology are determined combined with selection of semiconductors.
2.1
Soft Switching Circuit
In this project we focus on zero-voltage switching (ZVS) and zero-current switching
(ZCS) techniques in circuit operation[2][3].
In ZVS, the power devices, typically FETs,
are turned on when the voltage across their drain and source terminals is naturally driven
down to zero. This process is achieved by resonant tank, with discharging and charging
of devices’ parasitic capacitor Coss. Thus, switching losses due to discharging Coss at high
voltage and current level are signiﬁcantly reduced. During turn-oﬀtransitions, the reso-
nant circuit ensures a gradual reduction of current, facilitating the switching node voltage
to transition softly, thereby minimizing switching stress. Similarly, in ZCS operation, the
switching devices transition states when the current ﬂowing through them naturally falls
to zero. This eliminates reverse recovery losses and associated tail currents, signiﬁcantly
reducing switching losses.
2.1.1
Comparison of Diﬀerent Type of Soft-Switching Circuit
According to soft-switching topology selection criteria, mainly 3 soft-switching topolo-
gies are considered as candidates:
phase-shifted full-bridge (PSFB)[4] converter, quasi-
resonant single stage converter (QR)[5] and LLC resonant converter[6]. All three topologies
share the following characteristic in common for power conversion:
• Integration of parasitic components into resonant components: All three
topologies utilize leakage inductance from transformer and parasitic capacitance Coss
from MOSFETS as integral parts of resonant tank, in this way they convert otherwise
undesirable parasitic eﬀects into beneﬁcial resonant components.
• Primary zero-voltage turn on: Resonant tank is applied to charge and discharge
parasitic capacitance paralleled with MOSFETS, consequently MOSFETS are forced
to turn on in zero voltage condition. Hence, conduction loss is reduced signiﬁcantly,
especially those related to capacitive energy dissipation.
However, there are notable diﬀerences among them in achieving ZVS:
• PSFB Converter: PSFB circuit keeps the input voltage square-wave excitation and
resonant behaviors only occurs during dead time interval. Consequently, a minimum
circulating current, necessitating at least a minimum load condition, is required to
achieve ZVS.
• QR Converter: QR circuit operates with a ﬁxed turn on time. The dedicated Lr
and Cr tank guarantees that the primary circulating current remains nearly constant,

allowing ZVS or ZCS conditions to extend to relatively lighter load ranges compared
to the PSFB topology.
• LLC Converter: LLC circuit includes the magnetizing inductance Lm to deliver a
suﬃcient magnetizing current even at no load. Hence, primary ZVS and secondary
ZCS are achievable across the whole load range. In this way enhancing eﬃciency of
converter.
Moreover, LLC resonant circuit includes both the resonant gain response of LLC tank
and turn ratio of transformer to realize designed voltage step down. By selecting proper
tank parameters, it can signiﬁcantly reduce turn ratio. Hence, LLC resonant circuit emerges
as preferred primary-side topology based on above.
Half-bridge and full-bridge switching topology are most widely used within LLC resonant
circuit. Full-bridge requires more semiconductor switches along with drivers, which leads to
higher cost and slightly increased conduction losses due to series of semiconductors Rds(on).
Conversely, full-bridge will reduce turn ratio by half, with which minimize copper loss in
transformer design.
Moreover, full-bridge has potential to be applied into higher power
more than 1 kW. Hence, the primary side topology is decided as a full-bridge LLC resonant
topology.

2.3.1
From Conventional Current Doubler to Synchronous Current Doubler
For low output voltage, high current situation in this project, current doubler rectiﬁer
(CDR) has advantages over center-tapped rectiﬁer on secondary side[2]. In conventional
CDR, each of the diode arm will carry half of output current load at half of switching
frequency, consequently reduce both copper losses and core losses. Moreover, 180 °phase
shift between inductor currents permits it to cancel most of current output ripple seen by
output capacitance, reducing the required capacitance and equivalent series resistance (ESR)
losses by approximately 30-75%[11]. Figure 1 shows conventional current doubler rectiﬁer

3.2
Phase Number Comparison
In the proposed coupled-inductor structure, introducing an air gap in the center leg
increases the magnetic reluctance seen by the alternating component. As a result, the ac
ﬂux is redistributed more uniformly across the core, mitigating local hot spots and lowering
core-loss density. In a negatively coupled arrangement, the ac ﬂux components in the center
limb further cancel each other.
The same center-leg gap partially blocks the dc magnetizing component. Consequently,
even when a larger self-inductance is selected, the net dc ﬂux bias remains essentially un-
changed, provided that the instantaneous eﬀective inductance matches that of a non-coupled
design.

Planar Transformer Comparison
Planar transformers oﬀer signiﬁcant advantages compared to conventional wire-wound
core transformers, especially in high-frequency power conversion applications. In particular,
Page 19 of 56

planar designs achieve a much lower proﬁle and provide a greater surface area for heat
dissipation, leading to improved thermal performance. Studies have shown that, compared
to a traditional core, a planar transformer exhibits a lower hot-spot temperature and a more
uniform temperature distribution, which is a direct result of its larger surface-to-volume
ratio that enhances cooling.
These beneﬁts make planar transformers more suitable for
high-frequency operation and enable higher power density in now days converters.
This
combination of low proﬁle, improved thermal management, and high-frequency capability
is why planar magnetic technology is increasingly replacing classic wire-wound designs in
many advanced power electronics systems.

In this project, aiming at reduce the size of magnetic component, resonant inductor Lr
is expected to be integrated into transformer. Thus, it is essential to ﬁnd a way to both
increase leakage inductance but at the same time do not raise the core loss signiﬁcantly.
Conventionally, interleaving structure is introduced to cancel leakage inductance because of
negative coupling will cancel magnetic ﬂux which in turn reduce leakage inductance, and this
will be explained further in the following part based on MMF analysis. Finally, a insertions
of external magnetic shunt will be used for this high leakage inductance situation.

Primary side gate driver LMG1205 operating circuit set up is shown in ﬁgure 38 from
[23]. According to HOH and HOL, based on the rest area on PCB, both turn-on and turn
oﬀresistor R1 and R2 are added as gate resistance. At high frequency,the gate loop stray
inductance Lg and the device input capacitance Ciss form an LC resonator that produces gate
voltage ringing; this oscillation can push VGS above or below safe limits, triggering dv/dt,
induced false turn-on, overshoot or undershoot and extra EMI. Using split gate resistors lets
each edge be damped separately: a larger Rg on slows the charge current and reduces the
amplitude of the Lg-Ciss ringing, while a smaller Rg off gives a low impedance discharge
path that quickly dissipates the oscillation energy during turn oﬀ, eliminating the ringing
driven false turn on and shoot through.
According to my board structure and space limitation near the FETs, high side FETs
will have turn-on and turn oﬀresistor separately, while for low side FET resistor is shared

In the primary side prototype, 4 layers are employed. For left part, the winding copper
foils are allocated without other inner plane in order to reduce eddy-current losses. In the

inter-connector is inserted for PWM input for ICs.
• L2(GND Plane): Continuous GND reference plane, which provides low impedance
return for switching node as mentioned above.
• L3(VIN Plane): VIN plane make sure high-current path to the vertical via group from
layer 1 to layer 3, which in [31] shows that GND-VIN structure will act as broadband
and low ESI capacitance. This capacitance sources the sub-nanosecond di
dt demanded
by GaN FETs and relieves the discrete ceramic capacitors of the highestfrequency
content, keeping the supply impedance below 50 mW up to several MHz.
• L4(Bottom Layer): GND plane with 5 V power supply and PWM path for gate drivers,
which are shielded from high di
dt impact from switching nodes on L1.

Conclusion and Future Work
7.1
Conclusion
In this project, full-bridge LLC resonant circuit and current doubler topology is derived
and all the parameters are set as desired. We learn widely about soft-switching topologies
and their drawbacks and advantages. Moreover, current doubler acting as widely used topol-
ogy for high current low voltage situation made this project challenges a large amount of
current 100 A.
Integration of magnetic components are designed and implemented. In this part ﬂux
direction and ﬂux density, material magnetic characteristics and geometric set up are dis-
cussed and compared. Winding structure and magnetic shunt are precisely designed after
comparison of diﬀerent implementation way and method.
Semiconductor selection and PCB design bring the theoretical part into practical part,
in which case more practical phenomenon should be taken into consideration such as turn
on and oﬀringing eﬀect, also EMI on PCB winding layout and geometric symmetrical
conﬁrmation of PCB layout design.
Finally experimental practice evolved me into the whole integration of theory and equipment.
Even though there are mistakes happening in PCB board, such as a short circuit between
solder paste under EPC and there still have the secondary board characteristic to be tested,
this whole process of theory learning, simulation and practical made it a complete process
to design a DC-DC converter, which is widely used in now days electronics ﬁeld.
7.2
Future Work
Firstly the experiment will be continued as to gain output of secondary side the circuit.
Resonant capacitor Cr will be changed to reach 600 kHz and in this case we can continue
test parameters that have been derived. Moreover, structure of cores set and PCB can be
improved, since those 2 separate and confronted board is hard to assembled stable and when
testing it is hard to measure signals from both side of those boards. Previously, this separate
structure is proposed based on the insertion of magnetic shunt and as to conﬁrm the shunt
will locate in the middle of cores and windings at the same time windings are on the maxi-
mum distance from shunt. Since what we have from experiment illustrate there is more than
desire leakage inductance with this set up, distance from primary and secondary side wind-
ing to shunt can be adjusted to unequal. Consequently in this way, 2 boards can be merged
into single 4-layer PCB board with primary and secondary side winding locate not as far
as the set up right now. That will be a more scientiﬁc way both for assembling and for testing.
Moreover, this project focus on the integration of magnetics and utilizes leakage induc-
tance of transformer as part of resonant tank. To push this further, integration of winding
capacitance from transformer into resonant capacitor is also a possible way to reduce the
magnetic component volume. By utilizing both leakage inductance and winding capacitance
