Abstract
This thesis investigates the feasibility and economic viability of integrating nuclear energy
with Solid Oxide Electrolyzer Cells (SOEC) for hydrogen production. With the increasing
urgency to address climate change and meet the ambitious goals of the Paris Agreement,
innovative solutions for sustainable energy are crucial. Hydrogen, especially green hyВӯ
drogen, has emerged as a key player in this transition due to its cleanВӯburning properties
and high energy density.
The study begins by examining why we are witnessing a hydrogen momentum, highlightВӯ
ing several key factors that suggest a promising future. These include global commitments
to decarbonization, robust regulatory and policy support, and significant technological adВӯ
vancements in hydrogen production and utilization.
Focusing on the coupling of Small Modular Reactors (SMRs) with SOECs, the research
explores how this emerging nuclear technology, though not the most prominent for genВӯ
erating clean electricity, can be pivotal for hydrogen production. The highВӯtemperature
capabilities of SOECs, combined with the reliable energy output of SMRs, offer a subВӯ
stantial opportunity to enhance efficiency and reduce costs.
A detailed technoВӯeconomic analysis is conducted using Aspen Plus simulations to model
the integration process, assess energy demands, and identify optimal heat extraction
points from the SMR steam cycle. The Levelized Cost of Hydrogen (LCOH) is calculated
to evaluate the economic competitiveness of this approach. According to this assessment,
nuclear hydrogen could become competitive in terms of LCOH. However, the primary
disadvantage compared to other technologies is its current level of maturity, indicating
that it might be more viable for future deployment between 2040Вӯ2050.
This study provides valuable insights for policymakers, industry stakeholders, and reВӯ
searchers in the pursuit of sustainable hydrogen production methods, underscoring the
longВӯterm potential of nuclear energy to support the hydrogen economy.

Introduction
2.1
Background
In 2015, the Paris Agreement was adopted as a collective effort by the international comВӯ
munity to address the urgent threat of climate change. The Agreement aims to limit the
temperature increase to 1.5В°C above preВӯindustrial levels by 2050. To achieve this, particВӯ
ipating countries have been taking ongoing actions to reduce greenhouse gas emissions,
as outlined in their national climate action plans, known as nationally determined contriВӯ
butions (NDCs) [1]. Every five years, countries must publish updated NDCs, reflecting
a higher degree of ambition compared to the previous version [1]. Despite these efforts,
climate change action needs to be significantly accelerated to meet the goals of the Paris
Agreement.
Key achievements, such as the implementation of lowВӯcarbon solutions, are already unВӯ
derway, leading to new business opportunities for early adopters, particularly in the Green
Hydrogen and its Derivatives Market. This sector represents a significant opportunity for
the hydrogen industry to capitalize on the growing demand. Hydrogen is poised to become
a cornerstone of the clean energy transition, providing scalable and efficient alternatives
to fossil fuels.
Simultaneously, the energy industry is experiencing a profound transformation, marked
by global trends like digitalization, decarbonization, and diversification. This era is charВӯ
acterized by the anticipated phaseВӯout of fossil fuels within the next 30 years and a rapid
increase in renewable energy generation, driven by the urgent need to mitigate climate
change.
The potential to produce hydrogen from renewable energy sources through waterВӯsplitting,
along with its cleanВӯburning and versatile properties, positions hydrogen as a key player
within this broader decarbonization pathway. Moreover, hydrogen has an energy density
of 120 MJ/kg, which is about three times that of diesel or petrol [2].
Given this context, the coupling of hydrogen with nuclear power plants emerges as a
pivotal strategy. Nuclear power, with its capability to provide largeВӯscale, reliable, and lowВӯ
carbon electricity, can be effectively integrated with hydrogen production. This integration
can address the intermittent nature of renewable energy sources and ensure a steady
supply of clean hydrogen. The synergy between nuclear power and hydrogen production
not only supports the decarbonization goals but also leverages the existing infrastructure
and technological advancements in both sectors.
The following chapters will delve into the technical and economic aspects of coupling
hydrogen production with nuclear power plants, exploring the potential benefits and chalВӯ
lenges of this innovative approach.
2.2
Problem
The world has set an ambitious goal to mitigate climate change within the next 30 years,
sparking a race to find and implement effective solutions quickly. Hydrogen has emerged
as one of the key options, with its rising popularity suggesting smooth adoption. HowВӯ
ever, redirecting market and investment dynamics is not straightforward, especially when

Several pressing questions arise that can currently only be addressed through pathway

2.3
Purpose
The purpose of this thesis is to investigate the feasibility of producing hydrogen through
nuclear energy. This study will begin by assessing the viability of the relevant technoloВӯ
gies, examining how effectively they can be integrated. The coupling of nuclear power
and hydrogen production is significant not only for its potential hydrogen output but also
for enhancing the market competitiveness of both technologies. Ultimately, the research
aims to present a comprehensive analysis of the overall economic value, providing a
solid foundation to support further investment in research and development, and offering
strategic insights for policymakers and industry stakeholders.
2.4
Goals
The goal of this thesis is to determine the potential of hydrogen production through the
nuclear energy route. This has been divided into the following subВӯgoals:
вҖў Understand the Hydrogen Economy Momentum: Analyze the current state and
growth trends of the hydrogen economy to identify areas that require innovation
efforts.
вҖў Assess the Current Nuclear Energy Industry: Examine the existing landscape
of the nuclear energy industry to identify new opportunities for integration and adВӯ
vancement.
вҖў Explore Synergy Opportunities: Investigate the potential for synergy between hyВӯ
drogen production facilities and nuclear power plants.
вҖў Demonstrate Technical Feasibility: Prove the technical feasibility of coupling hyВӯ
drogen production with nuclear energy.
вҖў Evaluate Cost Competitiveness: Assess the cost competitiveness of hydrogen
produced through nuclear energy compared to other methods.

Phase 1: Qualitative Assessment In this phase, a comprehensive assessment will be
conducted using secondary data from various sources, including industry reports, acaВӯ
demic literature, market analyses, regulatory documents, and case studies of similar inВӯ
tegrated energy systems. The assessment will aim to cover three primary goals:

вҖ“ Benchmarking: Compare the LCOH of hydrogen produced from nuclear sources

Through these assessments, the study aims to provide a detailed understanding of the
technical and economic feasibility of integrating hydrogen production with nuclear energy,
ultimately identifying the most promising paths forward for these technologies.

Environmental Necessity
The global commitment to limit the average surface temperature rise to 1.5В°C above preВӯ
industrial levels, in line with the highest objective of the 2015 Paris Agreement, will require
all sectors of the economy to decarbonize by 2050. This is a great challenge that will

operate.
In their 2021 NEO report, BloombergNEF estimated that more than 30% of global emisВӯ
sions were due to electricity and heat, while around 18% and 17% came from industrial
sectors and transport, respectively [3]. Since then, governments and companies have
been taking actions to reduce emissions in these sectors. The decarbonization path for
electricity supply is clear: renewable technologies have matured, become commercially
available, and more affordable over the past couple of decades. As a result, an unpreceВӯ
dented deployment rate was registered, with cumulative global capacity rising from 53%
in 2013 to 87% in 2023. Moreover, the adoption of battery electric vehicles is rising exВӯ
ponentially, decreasing the emissions from passenger road transport [4].

3.1.2
Economic Potential
The latest available report from BloombergNEF, вҖқNew Energy Outlook, 2024,вҖқ shows an
upcoming relevant market growth for hydrogen in various applications [5]. Under the
Net Zero Scenario (NZS)1, demand for hydrogen grows from 94 Mt today to 390 Mt by
2050. Most of the hydrogen appetite will appear after 2030, when a cost reduction of
technologies is expected to become a reality along with the beginning of commercializing
hydrogenВӯcompatible gas turbines, boilers, and direct reduction furnaces.
Iron and steel manufacturing uses a combined 99 Mt (25%) in 2050. Hydrogen in the form
of eВӯkerosene in aviation accounts for 87 Mt (22%) and shipping in the form of ammonia or
methanol for 39 Mt (10%) of demand, see Figure 3.4. The driving sectors of this demand
are predicted to be those that are hardest to electrify with renewables, either because
of the lack of commercially available electric processes or because batteries present a
limited energy density for the scale of use (i.e., shipping or aviation). Hydrogen in other
proposed use cases is either insignificant, uneconomic, or both. Wherever electrificaВӯ
tion is possible, it will be easier and cheaper. We therefore expect little or no demand
from heating for commercial and residential buildings, rail transport, and passenger road
transport.

3.1.3
Regulatory and Policy Support
Despite its significant potential, the hydrogen value chain faces substantial challenges,
particularly in creating a compelling business case amidst uncertainties about secure deВӯ
mand and committed offВӯtakers. This uncertainty pertains to the lack of assurance reВӯ
garding the stability and reliability of hydrogen demand or the commitment from potential
buyers to purchase the produced hydrogen [6]. Therefore, transitioning green hydrogen
from a niche player to a widespread energy carrier requires integrated policy support,
strategic frameworks, and subsidy actions to overcome initial resistance and achieve a
minimum threshold for market penetration.
Hydrogen Strategies in Major Markets
Since 2020, many major markets have announced national hydrogen strategies crucial
for advancing the hydrogen economy. These strategies provide the necessary support,
funding, and infrastructure development to overcome current barriers, stimulate market
growth, and drive technological innovation. Key points from the analysis of official docuВӯ
ments published by governments, including updates of the strategies and roadmaps, are
highlighted in Figure 3.5:
вҖў By May 2024, 60 markets, including the European Commission, have issued their
hydrogen strategies.
вҖў 60% of the published strategies are in the EMEA region, with the EU being the most
advanced, accounting for 45%. The Americas and APAC regions each account for
20%.
вҖў Additionally, some governments, such as Japan, Germany, Argentina, and France,
have updated their strategies.
вҖў If these ambitions are realized, by 2030 at least 16 markets are expected to produce
24.4 Mt of hydrogen per year.

What is the Current State of the Nuclear Energy Industry?
Among its many advantages, nuclear powerвҖҷs key business advantages include low greenВӯ

As of July 2024, nuclear energy contributed 9.2% to global electricity production. ThirtyВӯ

of 74GW, while the cumulative nuclear power plants shut down represent 60GW, or 80
reactors [15]. As a result, there is a perception that the number of new reactors added
to the grid is routinely offset by reactors that are permanently shut down. Consequently,
given this deployment landscape and the unprecedented boom in renewable energy, the
future of nuclear power by 2050 has become an important debate to address.
WhatвҖҷs next for nuclear energy? Mixed messages emerge
In 2018, the IPCC published results of 90 possible energy transition pathways consistent
with the 1.5В°C scenario. In almost all of them, an increase in nuclear power was seen as
indispensable to reduce emissions sufficiently to limit average global warming by 2050.

levels of 370GW to 1,100GW by 2050 [16] [17].

5.2.2
How does a nuclear reactor work
Nuclear reactors are the heart of a nuclear power plant, containing and controlling nuclear
chain reactions that produce heat through a physical process called fission. Uranium
serves as the nuclear fuel in reactors, and it is processed into small ceramic pellets, which
are stacked together into sealed metal tubes known as fuel rods [27].
The vast majority of commercial nuclear reactors are lightВӯwater reactors, which come in
two designs: pressurized water reactors (PWRs) and boiling water reactors (BWRs).
In a boiling water reactor, such as the BWRXВӯ300, the fuel rods are immersed in water
within the reactor vessel. This water plays a dual role, acting both as a coolant and a
moderator. The primary function of the coolant is to absorb the heat generated by the
fission process, preventing the reactor from overheating. As a moderator, the water also
plays a critical role in sustaining the chain reaction by slowing down the neutrons produced
during fission [27].
The heat produced by fission heats the water in the reactor vessel, turning it into steam.
This steam is then directed towards turbines, which are connected to generators. When
the turbines spin, mechanical energy is converted into electricity. Consequently, the proВӯ
cess results in the production of carbonВӯfree electricity [28].

6.2
Determining nuclear Hydrogen Market Position:
Levelized Cost of Hydrogen
In order to make a well infomred decisiosn regarding resource allocation and stratgies for
pormoting costВӯeffective, sustianable hydrogen production, for policymakers, investors
and inustry players implment the Levelized Cost of Hydrogen (LCOH) metric that will proВӯ

6.2.1
LCOH concept
The Levelized Cost of Hydrogen (LCOH) is a comprehensive metric representing the avВӯ
erage cost per unit of hydrogen produced, considering all expenses incurred throughout
the lifetime of the production facility, taking into account the time value of the money. This
metric is essential for evaluating the viability of hydrogen compared to alternative energy
sources and assessing its competitiveness in the market [33].
The LCOH is influenced by the specific hydrogen production technology and the energy
source utilized. Different production methods, such as steam methane reforming (SMR),
which can be combined with carbon capture and storage (SMR+CCUS), or various forms
of electrolysis (whether connected to the grid or directly to renewable energy sources),
each have distinct cost structures. Therefore, estimating the LCOH for the case study
presented in this report is crucial to determine its market position and attractiveness [33].
In addition, the LCOH value serves as an indicative price for Hydrogen Purchase AgreeВӯ
ments (HPAs), analogous to Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs). These agreements
stipulate the delivery of hydrogen at a predetermined price, frequency, and quantity. For
sellers to achieve profitability, a margin is added to the LCOH [34]. Consequently, a lower
LCOH compared to competitors allows for higher profit margins and a more competitive
price offering for potential offВӯtakers. Hence, achieving a low LCOH is vital not only to
attract investors but also to propose competitive prices to offВӯtakers.
It is worth noting that while offВӯtaker agreements for hydrogen from nuclear sources are
not yet available, they represent a potential future market scenario.

feasibility of hydrogen production projects. It represents the constant value that, when

Conclusions
This thesis set out to explore five primary goals: understanding the hydrogen economy
momentum, assessing the current nuclear energy industry, exploring synergy opportuniВӯ
ties between hydrogen production and nuclear energy, determining technical feasibility,
and evaluating cost competitiveness. The conclusions drawn from these goals are as
follows:
The hydrogen economy is gaining significant momentum driven by several key factors.
Environmental necessity, as emphasized by global commitments like the Paris AgreeВӯ
ment, underscores the need for decarbonization across all sectors by 2050. Hydrogen,
especially green hydrogen, is pivotal due to its cleanВӯburning properties and potential
to reduce CO2 emissions in hardВӯtoВӯabate sectors such as industry and transport. The
economic potential of hydrogen is substantial, with reports predicting a surge in market
growth postВӯ2030 as technology costs decrease and hydrogenВӯcompatible infrastructure
becomes more widespread. Furthermore, robust regulatory and policy support, alongside
significant technological advancements in hydrogen production and utilization, are crucial
drivers of this momentum. Finally, hydrogen offers advantages over fossil fuels, such
as reducing dependency on volatile markets and benefiting from potential carbon pricing
mechanisms
On the other hand, while nuclear power provides substantial benefits like low greenhouse
gas emissions, high energy density, and reliable base load electricity, it faces signifiВӯ
cant challenges, including high capital costs, social and political opposition, and technical
and environmental concerns. Future expansion of nuclear energy is deemed crucial for
achieving climate goals, with projections requiring a tripling of nuclear capacity by 2050.
Small Modular Reactors (SMRs) emerge as a promising technology, offering enhanced
safety, lower costs, and greater flexibility. However, despite growing interest and pubВӯ
lic funding, the deployment of SMRs and the broader expansion of nuclear energy face
uncertainties, boosting interest to seek for new market opportunities.
Integrating hydrogen production facilities with nuclear power plants, particularly through
SMRs, presents significant synergy opportunities. This integration would reduce overall
electricity demand. which will lead into cost reduction. SMRs offer new market opporВӯ
tunities with their flexibility, scalability, and lower costs, making them ideal for industrial
applications, including hydrogen production. Furthermore, integrating nuclear power with
hydrogen production can complement renewable energy sources by providing a stable
and reliable energy supply, addressing the intermittency issues of renewables and ensurВӯ
ing continuous clean hydrogen production.
The technical feasibility of integrating nuclear power with hydrogen production through
SOECs has been examined using Aspen Plus simulations. The simulations provided
detailed insights into energy and mass balances, optimal heat extraction points, and sysВӯ
tem efficiencies. Results show that the integration is technically viable, with significant
potential for efficiency improvements. Heat extraction before the LowВӯPressure Turbine
1 (LPT1) proved more efficient than other points, maximizing the hydrogen production
capacity. Despite some technical challenges, the study confirms the feasibility of this inВӯ
tegration, paving the way for future research and development to optimize and scale these
systems.

The Levelized Cost of Hydrogen (LCOH) is a critical metric for assessing the economic
feasibility of hydrogen production projects. The study determined that under a discount
rate of 6%, an LCOH of 3.47 вӮ¬/kg H2 is achievable. This value is influenced by various
factors, including capital expenditures (CAPEX), operational expenditures (OPEX), and
maintenance costs. Sensitivity analysis revealed that financial parameters (discount rate)
and operational period (full load hours) are the most critical factors influencing the LCOH.
While hydrogen produced from nuclear energy could become costВӯcompetitive, the priВӯ
mary disadvantage is the technologyвҖҷs limited maturity. Consequently, nuclear hydrogen
is projected to be more viable for future deployment between 2040 and 2050.
These conclusions underscore the strategic importance of hydrogen in the energy transiВӯ
tion, the critical role of nuclear energy in achieving decarbonization goals, the promising
synergy between nuclear power and hydrogen production, the technical feasibility of such
integration, and the potential cost competitiveness of nuclear hydrogen for a sustainable
energy future.
