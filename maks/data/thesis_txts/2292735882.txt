Introduction · i 
 
Abstract 
 
The global plastic consumption is growing and plastic is almost exclusively made from fossil re-
sources. As a consequence, focus on plastic recycling as a measure to mitigate climate change and 
resource depletion has increased. In order to quantitatively evaluate the environmental perfor-
mance of a plastic recycling system a life cycle assessment (LCA) can be performed. The environmen-
tal impact does, among other, depend on the quality of the recycled plastic produced, compared to 
the substituted virgin plastic. In previous literature, quality of the recycled plastic has been estimated 
based on monetary values of recycled and virgin plastic, included as a substitution ratio (SR). Mone-
tary values do, however, not include the impacts of important factors, such as chemical properties, 
on the quality. As a result, a more detailed, consistent, and transparent way to estimate quality is 
necessary. The aim of this study is to develop a framework for quantification of SRs for recycled 
plastic, representing the quality of the recycled plastic, compared to the quality of the virgin alter-
native and apply it on collected plastic samples. The framework focuses on Europe, includes the 
polymer types PET, HDPE, LDPE/LLDPE, PP, and PS and is based on chemical characteristics. Devel-
oping the framework, three main areas were found to be important for the quality of recycled plastic: 
1) the possible uses based on legislative requirements, 2) the market shares of these uses, and 3) 
industry behaviour and market responses related to use and substitution of recycled plastic. Within 
these areas several findings were identified, whereof the most important were: 1) the magnitude of 
the market share of food packaging (FP) and non-food packaging (NFP) are large within the market 
of each polymer type, 2) less than 100% recycled plastic is used within FP and NFP, even though legal 
requirements for use are fulfilled, 3) the origin of recycled plastic is crucial for the industry’s desire 
to use it, and 4) recycled plastic used within products for building and construction, agriculture, etc., 
often substitute other materials than plastic. In order to apply the framework on the 28 collected 
samples, including virgin plastic, recycled plastic from household waste (HHW), and recycled plastic 
from industrial waste (IW), relevant experimental analyses were performed. From the results of the 
analyses, one of the recycled PET samples from HHW was found to be polymer cross-contaminated 
with PE. In regards to metal concentrations, they were in general found to be higher within samples 
from HHW, compared to samples from IW. Additionally, CMR classified phthalates were identified in 
15 out of 20 recycled samples. Several other results were obtained, however the importance of some 
of these to the quality of the recycled plastic, are still uncertain. Applying the results of the experi-
mental analysis to the framework, SR intervals for all recycled plastic samples were estimated. It was 
found that potential use within FP is crucial for the plastic SRs. These were found to vary between 
0.55-0.95 for recycled plastic suitable for FP (highest substitution ratio can be reached for PET) and 
0.10-0.40 for recycled plastic not suitable for FP. No recycled samples from HHW were found to 
potentially fulfil the requirements for FP and as consequence the latter interval is recommended for 
recycled plastic from HHW. In addition to the plastic SRs, a wood and steel SR, varying within 0.00-
20 and 0.00-0.12, respectively, needs to be included in the LCA, to compensate for the part of the 
recycled plastic not expecting to substitute virgin plastic, but instead wood or steel. A sensitivity 
analysis showed that the substitution ratios were most sensitive towards change in assumptions re-
lated to commodity groups with large market shares. As a result, the SRs and thereby the quality of 
the recycled plastic, could in the future be increased by focussing on suitability for use within FP and 
focussing on increasing the percentage of recycled plastic used within FP and NFP.

Introduction · 1 
 
1 Introduction 
In the modern consumption based society, plastic has become an important material for many ap-
plications, due to its durability, low weight and low costs, compared to many other materials 
(Hopewell et al., 2009). In the last half century the global production of plastics have grown con-
stantly (Serranti et al., 2015) reaching 299 million tonne in 2013, of which 57 million tonne were 
produced in Europe (PlasticsEurope and EPRO, 2015). As a consequence, the amount of plastic waste 
has been increasing as well (Serranti et al., 2015). Plastic are almost exclusively produced from fossil 
oil or gas and according to Hopewell et al. (2009) 4% of the annual petrol production is converted 
directly into plastics. Moreover, additional petroleum is used to produce energy for the plastic pro-
duction. Thus, recycling of plastic waste will both contribute to a reduction in oil and gas depletion 
as well as a reduction in greenhouse gasses emitted from waste management systems (such as in-
cineration). Hence, recycling of plastic can be used as a measure to mitigate the challenges of climate 
change and resource depletion. Consequently, the political interest in recycling of plastic has in-
creased, and mechanical recycling of plastic has in the last couple of years grown by 10% annually 
within Europe (Ignatyev et al., 2014).  
However, many factors, besides the recycling rate, have an influence on the environmental perfor-
mance of a recycling system, which is why life cycle assessments (LCA), specially designed to quantify 
environmental impacts from waste management systems, are important. Figure 1.1 illustrates the 
typical processes included in an LCA of a recycling system. Below the processes the potential losses, 
both physical and value-based, are indicated. Physical losses of material can happen within several 
processes along the waste chain and as a result efficiencies representing these losses need to be 
included in the LCA. These include collection, selection and reprocessing efficiencies where e.g. the 
collection efficiency expresses the amount of collected material as a percentage of the total amount 
of material generated. Additionally, a potential loss of value needs to be included in the LCA, when 
the material is taken into use within a new application, where it substitutes virgin material (defined 
as new raw plastic material, entering its first life cycle). Including value loss is important since the 
quality of the recycled material might not directly correspond to the quality of virgin material (Riga-
monti et al., 2014). Hence, a substitution ratio needs to be defined.  
In regards to losses within plastic recycling, several studies have included collection efficiencies (e.g. 
Rigamonti et al., 2010; Song et al., 2013, Buttol et al., 2007), selection and reprocessing efficiencies 
(Rigamonti et al., 2014; Rigamonti et al., 2009). For the substitution ratio, however, some studies 
include substitution with virgin plastic, without specifying a value for the ratio of substitution (De 
Feo and Malvano, 2009; Buttol et al., 2007). Other studies include a substitution ratio less than 1, 
however, without an explanation of why that specific value was chosen (Song et al., 2009). Finally, 
several studies have used substitution ratios based on the price of recycled plastic relative to the 
price of virgin plastic (Rigamonti et al., 2014; Rigamonti et al., 2009). Thus, no consistency exist, when 
it comes to choice of substitution ratio. Rigamonti et al. (2009) found, among others, that the sub-
stitution ratio is important for the outcome of an LCA, making the inconsistency in defining substitu-

it represents a problem in relation to comparison to other recycling system, if the substitution ratio 
does not represent the actual quality of the recycled plastic, and thus either over or underestimates

Determining the substitution ratio from monetary values are in accordance with the guidelines in 
the ILCD Handbook (Reference Life Cycle Data System Handbook), when uses of the secondary good 
are not known (EC, 2010). Monetary values, however, do not entirely represent the quality of the 
recycled material, since they do not include impacts of important factors, such as physical and chem-
ical characteristics, on the plastic quality. Consequently, substitution ratios based on monetary val-
ues should only be used, when other information is not possible to obtain. A consistent and trans-
parent framework for determination of substitution ratios is therefore necessary in order to achieve 
comparable results. This study aims towards elucidating important factors and circumstances influ-
encing the uses of secondary plastic goods, and on that ground give a more substantiated estimate 
of plastic substitution ratios, based on physical plastic samples collected for the purpose.  
In the following sections the scope of the study will be described into more details and the precise 
objectives will be presented. 
1.1 Scope 
The ultimate aim of this study is to develop a framework to quantify substitution ratios for recycled 
plastic, i.e. assess the quality of recycled plastic compared to the quality of the virgin plastic substi-
tuted. While the mechanical properties of recycled plastic may allow remanufacturing, chemical sub-
stances may potentially be present in, or released from the plastic during a secondary use phase. 
The content of toxic and regulated substances may therefore limit the range of applications for the 
plastic. Consequently, this may affect the substitution of virgin plastic. The focus of the project is 
therefore directed towards quantification of basic chemical characterisation and potential problem-
atic substances in recycled plastic. 
Based on basic characterisation tests and analyses of collected plastic samples, used to give a pre-
liminary idea of the chemical characteristics, the study aims at quantifying the quality of the recycled 
plastic samples with respect to selected application scenarios. This will in most cases be done by 
comparing the results of the characterisation tests and analyses of recycled plastic samples, with the 
results of the characterisation tests and analyses of virgin plastic samples.  
The intended outcome of the project is a classification framework for recycled plastic relating the 
plastic quality to potential downstream applications, including estimations of relevant substitution 
ratios to the extent possible. On this basis, LCA practitioners evaluating plastic recycling may apply 
substitution ratios representing the actual application possibilities of the recycled plastic compared 
to the virgin plastic substituted, based on characterisation and extraction profiles of the recycled 
plastic. 
The study will be based on European conditions and legislation and has a focus on mechanical (not 
feedstock) recycling of polypropylene (PP), polyethylene (PE), polystyrene (PS) and poly(ethylene 
terephthalate) (PET). Detailed arguments for this focus is given in Chapter 2.

2 Theoretical background 
In this chapter theoretical background in relation to virgin plastic, including information of morphol-
ogy, properties, additives, use and processing, will be presented. Moreover, plastic recycling in rela-
tion to types of recycling and potentially problematic substances in recycled plastic will be described. 
Finally, the European legislation affecting the use of plastic will be review and presented.  
2.1 Virgin plastics 
Plastics can be defined as organic long chain polymers with a carbon backbone produced from 
polymerization of smaller molecules or monomers. Feedstock for plastic production, from where the 
monomers are produced, can be cellulose, coal, natural gas, crude oil and salts (PasticsEurope, 
2015c). The majority, however, is produced from crude oil and natural gas (Pedersen, 2001; 
Hopewell et al., 2009), both fossil, non-renewable resources.  
Plastics can be divided into two main categories (Christensen and Fruergaard, 2011a): Thermoplas-
tics and thermosets, depending on the thermal processing behaviour. Thermoplastics represent the 
majority of produced plastic (around 80%) and are very chemically stable. When increasing the tem-
perature the thermoplastics become soft and can be shaped, without losing their chemical proper-
ties. Thus, thermoplastics can be heated, shaped and cooled down many times keeping their original 
long chain polymers, which make thermoplastics very popular. The polymers in thermosets, on the 
other hand, form a grid structure resulting in high resistance towards e.g. mechanical force, heat, 
chemicals, etc., which make thermosets difficult to recycle. As a result, thermosets are not included 
in the scope of this study, since the focus is on recycled plastic. 
On a chemical level plastics can be divided into groups according to the main polymer within the 
plastic (Figure 2.3 presents the chemical structures of PET, PS, PE and PP). Labelling of PET, HDPE, 
LDPE/LLDPE, PP and PS on packaging is recommended by both the European Commission and the 
European Committee for Standardization (CEN) and PlasticsEurope (Christensen and Fruergaard, 
2011a). Even though labelling is not mandatory in the EU, most plastic packaging are labelled any-
way, making these plastic types easy identifiable. Therefore, and since these four plastic types rep-
resented 62.5% of the entire European plastic demand in 2013 (PlasticsEurope and EPRO, 2015), 
they are the focus of this study.  
When a producer has to decide what polymer type is best suited for a specific application, a lot of 
different aspects come into question, including price, processing properties, resistance towards the 
surrounding environment, barrier properties, mechanical properties, fire resistance, electrical prop-
erties (Hansen et al., 2013), opacity, etc. Thus finding the right plastic type for a specific application 
can be a complex procedure, including many different considerations. E.g. the higher the viscosity 
(as a result of high molar weight of the polymer), the more difficult it is to process the plastic (Hansen 
et al., 2013). However, high molar weight of the polymers increases the mechanical properties of 
the plastic (Hansen et al., 2013), which for some applications are essential. The chemical composition 
of the polymers within the plastic is therefore crucial when it comes to plastic properties. Despite 
this fact, the chemical composition of the main polymer is not the only factor determining the prop-
erties. Almost all properties can be modified using specific additives during plastic production, why 
huge variations, even within the same polymer type, exist. 
Brief theory, related to morphology (referring to the chemical composition of the polymer) and ad-
ditives, will therefore be presented in the first two sections of this chapter, in order to create a basis 
for understanding the complexity of plastics and the variations of properties within the individual

2.1.1 Morphology and tacticity of polymers 
Morphology refers to the structure, shape and arrangement of polymers in space (Byun et al., 2013), 
which much often refers to the degree of crystallinity of polymers. The morphology of polymers 
determines a wide range of important polymer properties. Among these are clarity, impact strength, 
density, melting point, stiffness and flexibility. E.g. when the crystallinity of a polymeric material in-
creases, the stiffness, melting point and density increases, whereas the flexibility, clarity and impact 
strength decreases (Harper, 1996; Equistar, 2015). Furthermore, amorphous polymers do not have 
a melting point (Harper, 1996), meaning that the material can become soft and elastic, but not liquid. 
Thus, the morphology of a polymeric material is important when the right material has to be chosen 
for a specific application. 
Amorphous and crystalline structures are the two main polymer patterns. When polymers have 
amorphous structures, the polymers are arranged randomly in space, with no recognisable pattern. 
In crystalline polymer structures, on the other hand, the polymers are arranged in repeating pat-
terns. Amorphous and crystalline polymer structures are shown in Figure 2.1(iii) and Figure 2.1(i), 
respectively. All polymers tend to find a structure or arrangement with the lowest level of energy. 
The crystalline structure is the structure representing the lowest energy state, however, even though 
crystalline structures occur in nature, most plastic polymers are arranged in semi-crystalline struc-
tures (Harper, 1996), where parts of the polymer is crystalline and parts are amorphous, as illustrated 
in Figure 2.1(ii) (Harper, 1996).  
The morphology is determined by the chemical composition, degree of polymerisation and chain 
configuration (Harper, 1996). The longer the polymer chain, and thereby the higher the degree of 
polymerisation, the more amorphous the polymer gets, since shorter chains tend to form structured 
patterns more easily compared to longer chains. Furthermore, the symmetry of a polymer is im-
portant. The more symmetrical a polymer is, the higher the tendency to crystallise. Thus symmetrical 
polymers like PE (see Figure 2.3(iii)) crystallise easily where asymmetric polymers like PP (Figure 
2.3(iv)) need regular stereo configurations or tacicity  in order to crystallise (Harper, 1996).   
The tacticity of a polymer describes the stereochemistry of chiral centres within the polymer. Three 
tacticity states exist; Isotactic, syndiotactic and atactic. In an isotactic polymer the radical group is 
placed on the same side of the polymer backbone on every chiral centre, as illustrated in Figure 
2.2(i). In syndiotactic polymers the radical groups are placed on opposite sides of the polymer back-
bone in shifting order, creating a regular pattern as illustrated in Figure 2.2(ii). Finally, in atactic pol-
ymers the radical groups are placed randomly along the polymer backbone, creating no recognisable

2.1.2 Additives 
Additives are chemical substances added in the production of plastic to ensure maintenance, modify 
the properties or performance of the polymer (Pfaendner, 2013) and/or reduce economic costs 
(Hansen et al., 2013). A characteristic of most additives is that they do not chemically bind to the 
polymers (with an expectation of certain reactive organic additives, such as flame retardants) (Han-

the additives. Thus, as was concluded by Jakobsen (2015), potential identification of additives within 
recycled plastic, compared to virgin plastic, might be important in regards to the potential uses of 
the recycled plastic and thereby the quality.

2.1.4 Polyolefins  
Polyolefins (PO) is a very important group of plastics with a wide range of application possibilities 
(E.g. films, toys, bottles, etc.), representing 48.5% of the entire European plastic demand in 2013 
(PlasticsEurope and EPRO, 2015). PO is a common name for all polymers that are produced by 
polymerisation of olefins, also called alkene monomers. Alkene monomers are among the cheapest 
raw materials within polymer production and it is expected that new catalysts will make it possible 
to precisely control the properties of PO materials, substituting other more expensive plastic types,  
and thereby increasing the demand of PO’s in the future (Pedersen, 2001).  
The most important plastic types within POs are polyethylene (PE) and polypropylene (PP) and they 
will be described in more details in the following sections.  
2.1.4.1 Polyethylene  
Within the group of POs PE is an important polymer, representing 29.6% of the European plastic 
demand in 2013 (PlasticsEurope and EPRO, 2015). PE is produced from polymerisation of ethylene 
monomers, a symmetrical molecule, and in Figure 2.3(iii) the chemical structure of PE is presented. 
PE is a very versatile polymeric material with an application range from food packaging to electronics 
and industrial storage (PlasticsEurope, 2015a). The large versatility within PE plastics is due to the 
fact that many different types of PE with different properties exist, depending on branching, density 
and copolymerisation (Harper, 1996). Of these low density PE (LDPE), high density PE (HDPE), linear 
low density PE (LLDPE) and copolymeric PE are essential.  
PE can be both linear and branched, depending on how the polymers are produced. However, linear 
molecules are in most cases not completely linear, since branching or coplymerisation is used to 
control the precise density of the material, why even linear polymers can have a low degree of 
branching.  
LDPE is branched homopolymeric PE with a relatively low density, melting temperature range and 
crystallinity. The branching of PE result in properties like clarity, flexibility, ease of processing and 
==PAGE:20==
10 · Theoretical background 
 
sealability (Harper, 1996). The low melting temperature range makes LDPE suitable for flexible pack-
aging, such as stretch-wrap films, which is often produced from LDPE (Harper, 1996). LDPE provides 
a good water barrier, however, it has a very high oxygen, CO2 and organic vapour permeability, lim-
iting the potential applications. Since mechanical properties improves with increasing density or lin-
earity (Pedersen, 2001), LDPE is the homopolymeric PE material with lowest mechanical properties.  
LLDPE is a linear polymer with very short branches. Due to the linearity LLDPE is stiffer and have a 
higher degree of crystallinity than LDPE, which result in a higher melting temperature range, better 
mechanical properties and reduced clarity (Harper, 1996). Approximately 50% of LDPE/LLDPE is used 
for packaging material (Hansen et al., 2013) and within packaging LLDPE is used to produce e.g. shop-
ping bags and heavy-duty shipping sacks (Harper, 1996). 
HDPE is a linear polymer with only low degree of branching, resulting in high crystallinity, which 
makes it a better barrier against water and oxygen and increases the chemical resistance and opacity 
compared to LDPE (Harper, 1996). Application of HDPE range from thin-walled dairy containers to 
deodorant, shampoo and cosmetics containers (Harper, 1996). 
Copolymeric PE is produced by polymerisation of ethylene monomers and either alkenes with four 
to eight carbons, which as described earlier is often done to control density, or polar functional 
groups. Adding polar functional groups to the polymerisation process increases flexibility, improves 
barrier properties and reduces crystallinity below LDPE (Harper, 1996). A lot of different polar func-
tional groups can be added, thus the range of potential applications of copolymeric PE is huge.  
2.1.4.2 Polypropylene  
PP is produced by polymerization of propylene monomers and has a chemical structure as presented 
in Figure 2.3(iv). In general, most polypropylenes are used within a wide range of film applications, 
due to the strength, toughness and high melting point and if the right additives are added good 
processability can be reached as well (Calhoun, 2010). Within PP plastics, different types exist, de-
pending on the more detailed chemical structure; PP homopolymers and PP random copolymers 
(Harper, 1996).  
The group of homopolymers consists of both isotatic, syndiotactic and atactic stereo configurations. 
The isotatic PP is the most common form of PP used in industry (Harper, 1996; Daugaard, 2015). Due 
to its chemical structure it is a highly crystalline material and therefore also opaque, unless additives 
are added. Isotatic PP has a high level of stiffness, high heat resistance and high chemical resistance 
(Equistar, 2015) and is very suitable for wrapping and stretching applications, as well as high tem-
perature use (Harper, 1996; Equistar, 2015). Isotatic PP is however not well suited for low tempera-
ture applications (Equistar, 2015). Syndiotactic PP is a lower value products than isotactic PP, due to 
the lower level of crystallinity, lower melting point and lower density (Calhoun, 2010). Atactic PP has 
a melting temeprature below room temperature (Daugaard, 2015), why industrial processes are of-
ten design to minimize the amount of atactic material (Harper, 1996).  
In PP random copolymers typically 1.5%-7% ethylene by weight is added during the polymerization 
process (Harper, 1996). The ethylene is randomly placed on the PP polymers, making stereoregular-
ity impossible, reducing crystallisation, which results in clarity, marginal impact improvement at low 
temperatures and a reduction in stiffness, due to increased flexibility (Harper, 1996; Equistar, 2015). 
The slightly different properties of PP random copolymers, compared to PP isostatic homopolymers, 
makes it suitable for e.g. transparent freezer storage containers (Equistar, 2015).

GPPS is typically an amorphous material with no melting point and excellent optical properties (Har-

2.1.7 Other plastic types 
Another important polymer type, representing 10.4% of the European polymer production (Plas-
ticsEurope and EPRO, 2015), is polyvinyl chloride (PVC). PVC polymers are never used alone, but 
always as an elaborated blend of polymers and additives. Due to the large amount of different addi-
tives that can be added to PVC, the application of PVC plastics range from very rigid to very soft 
materials (Vandevyver, 2015). Examples of application of PVC plastics are wallpapers, toys, furniture, 
window frames, boots, etc. (Björkner et al., 2011; Vandevyver, 2015). Hydrogen chloride is, however, 
released from PVC compounds when exposed to high temperatures (Björkner et al., 2011), making 
recycling of PVC difficult, which is the reason why it is excluded from the scope of this study. 
Other important polymer types worth mentioning are polycarbonate (PC), polyurethane (PUR) and 
acrylonitrile butadiene styrene (ABS). However, none of these polymer types are included in the rec-
ommended plastic labelling system and none of them represent more than 8% of the European plas-
tic market (PlasticsEurope and EPRO, 2015), which is why these are not included in the scope. 
2.1.8 Processing of plastics 
In order to produce plastic products or plastic parts for products, the raw polymer material, often in 
the form of granules, powder or flakes, needs to be transformed. This transformation process is 
called processing and includes transformation of the raw material into a fluid state by heating (Valle, 
2013).  For thermoplastics, the desired product can be shaped or moulded from the fluid state and 
then cooled down, making the raw material keep the desired shape. The processing highly depends 
on the viscosity of the plastic (Valle, 2013). The higher the viscosity, the more difficult the plastic is 
to process. The viscosity depends on both inherent properties within the plastic, such as the molec-
ular weight of the polymers, and external factors, such as temperature. The higher the molecular 
weight of polymers within the plastic, the higher the viscosity (Hansen et al., 2013). However, the 
viscosity decreases with increasing temperature. Consequently, heating is required during pro-
cessing, in order to get the plastic into a fluid state. 
Raw polymer material can be processed in several different ways, depending of the intended appli-
cation of the plastic output. This includes methods such as injection moulding, blow moulding, and 
extrusion. As an example, blow moulding is a method used when the plastic product needs to be 
hollow. However, other factors beside the intended application, such as the price are furthermore 
determining for the choice of processing method. The cost related to processing is very different for 
the different methods, and depends strongly on the number of articles produced per unit of time 
(Valle, 2013). The more articles produced per unit of time, the better the economy.

Common for the processing methods is that the raw polymer in most cases has to be compounded, 
that is mixed with additives, before the actual forming phase. In order to ensure adequate dispersion 
a mixing phase is added before forming (Valle, 2013), ensuring an ideal mix of plastic in the desires 
product or recycled raw material. 
2.2 Plastic recycling 
In this study recycling of plastic is defined as processes where the original plastic polymers (or the 
monomers from which the polymers are produced), are recovered and used again. Thus, thermal 
treatment of plastic with energy recovery, where the polymers undergo complete combustion and 
therefore can not be used as polymers or monomers again, is in this study not included in the term 
recycling.  
In the following sections different types of recycling will be described and potential problematic sub-
stances in recycled plastic will be presented. 
2.2.1 Types of recycling 
Plastic recycling can roughly be divided into three main categories; primary mechanical recycling, 
secondary mechanical recycling, and feedstock recycling (Ignatyev et al., 2014).  
Primary mechanical recycling is where the waste is mechanically treated and then used in the same 
application as before it was turned into waste, keeping the original properties. This is also referred 
to as closed-loop recycling and is often seen in industrial processes for post-industrial waste (Ignat-
yev et al., 2014). This kind of recycling is very rarely performed on post-consumer waste, since post-
consumer waste is a highly heterogeneous waste stream. An exception is washing and refilling of PET 
bottles, which represent closed-loop or primary mechanical recycling (Hopewell et al., 2009).  
Secondary mechanical recycling is where the waste is mechanically treated and then used in appli-
cations different from the original use, or turned into an intermediate such as flakes or pellets that 
can be sold to industry. This kind of recycling is the most widely used method for post-consumer 
waste and often involves some kind of reduction in mechanical properties (due to e.g. contamination 
with other polymer types or shortening of the polymer chains (Ignatyev et al., 2014)), which reduces 
the quality of the recycled plastic compound (Brems et al., 2012), making it useful only for lower 
quality applications. This kind of recycling is also known as downcycling.  
The third kind of recycling is called feedstock or chemical recycling. This includes recycling of the 
monomers, so to speak, from which the polymers originally were produced. Through chemical pro-
cessing the long chain polymers are broken down into monomers, which is then used as feedstock 
for new polymer production. The depolymerisation process is however very energy intensive, and 
feedstock recycling is therefore less energetically favourable than mechanical recycling (Hopewell et 
al, 2009). Furthermore, feedstock recycling is much less economically favourable than mechanical 
recycling, due to low feedstock prices and high operation costs, making industrial implementation 
require economically subsidies (Hopewell et al., 2009; Ignatyev et al., 2014). All of which contribute 
to the reason why feedstock recycling is not a focus of this study.   
2.2.2 Potential problematic substances in recycled plastics 
During reprocessing of plastic waste into recycled raw plastic material, extra additives are expected 
to be added, in order to ensure sufficient physical, mechanical and aging properties, compared to 
virgin plastic. Consequently, it is expected that recycled plastic contains higher concentrations of 
additives than virgin plastic. Furthermore, it is generally believed that recycled plastic contains higher 
==PAGE:24==
14 · Theoretical background 
 
concentration of impurities, such as metals, than virgin plastic material (Roh et al., 2013) and several 
problematic substance might therefore potentially be present in recycled plastics. 
Hansen et al. (2013) found that substances with a high potential of migrating from the plastic, e.g. 
certain additives not chemically bound to the polymer, represents an especially high risk. This in-
cludes additive groups such as plasticisers (Hansen et al., 2013). As presented in Section 2.1.2, plas-
ticisers includes phthalates and Hansen et al. (2013) found the phthalate DEHP to be of special con-
cern, due to a migration rate of maximum 0.1%-1% per year. Several phthalates, including DEHP, are 
classified as toxic for reproduction (EU, 2006), why migration of these and thereby removal from the 
plastic by evaporation or contact are unwanted (Hansen et al., 2013). The presence of phthalates 
within plastics have been supported by several studies, including studies on recycled plastic 
(Stangenberg et al., 2004; Shen, 2005). 
Furthermore, several metals were identified as potential problematic in plastics (Hansen et al., 
2013). As presented in Section 2.1.2 metals can be part of several additive groups, such as colorants, 
biocides, heat stabilisers, etc. Most metal compounds were found by Hansen et al. (2013) to be solid 
bound and as a result release are expected only through wear and tear. These include metals such 
as Cr, Cd, etc. However, according to EU (2006) certain compounds including these metals are clas-
sified as carcinogenic, mutagenic or toxic for reproduction (CMR classified). Consequently, they still 
represent a risk. Other metals, such as Hg, are expected to migrate from the plastic and do therefore 
represent special concerns (Hansen et al., 2013). 
Moreover, other problematic substances, such as polyaromatic hydrocarbons, originating from im-
purities, brominated flame retardants, etc. were identified by Hansen et al. (2013) to be of concern.

3.2 Experimental setup 
Stangenberg et al. (2004) evaluated that for the purpose of quality assessment of recycled plastic 
differential scanning calorimetry (DSC), thermogravimetric analysis (TGA), Fourier transform infrared 
spectrometry (FTIR), and chromatography are relevant. Furthermore, Luijsterburg and Goossens 
(2014) and Borovanska et al. (2012) used DSC and FTIR in the assessment of quality of plastic pack-
aging waste and recycled LDPE/PP blends, respectively. On this basis both DSC, TGA and FTIR will be 
a part of the quality assessment in this study. Moreover, size exclusion chromatography (SEC) will 
also be performed for polymers soluble in tetrahydrofuran (THF). In regards to the chemical charac-
teristics, relevant from a legal perspective, two analyses were evaluated to be relevant and possible 
to perform within the given time frame: Total metal content, and phthalate screening.  
All analyses were performed with the purpose of creating a preliminary idea of the characteristics of 
recycled plastics compared to virgin plastics. Thus, creating very precise results, statistically repre-
sentative for an entire sample, is not the purpose of the analyses.   
A description of the method used when performing the mentioned analyses as well as a more de-
tailed argumentation for why these analyses were found relevant, are given in the following sections. 
3.2.1 Fourier Transform Infrared spectrometry  
As part of this study FTIR is used to confirm the dominating polymer in both virgin and recycled 
plastic samples, as well as to identify variations in the FTIR spectra of recycled samples compared to 
virgin samples of the same polymer type. Furthermore, if large amounts of other polymers or addi-
tives are present, these might be detected as well, however, a high limit of detection will limit this 
process, since certain additives is expected to be added in amounts too small to be detected. Trace 
amounts of other polymers will be most likely to detect in PE, due to the simple FTIR spectrum of PE.  
FTIR was performed on a PerkinElmer Spectrum One model 2000 FT-IR with a universal attenuated 
total reflection sampling accessory on a ZnSe/diamond composite. No sample preparation were nec-
essary. 
In the following sections it is explained how the samples were analysed, depending on the form of 
the sample. 
3.2.1.1 Analysis of virgin samples 
All virgin samples consist of uniform granulates or pellets. As described in Section 2.1.8 the plastic 
material is mixed before formation into granules or pellets, resulting in a close to ideal mix. Based on 
this, as well as practical experience with high degree of uniformity within virgin plastic pellets (Dau-
gaard, 2015), one pellet or granulate was analysed from each sample. 
3.2.1.2 Analysis of recycled samples on flake form 
A selected amount of flakes were chosen to be analysed from each sample. The flakes were selected 
according to colour and shapes. The main type of flake in the mixture were analysed three to five 
times and if no significant differences in the spectra were observed, it was assumed that all flakes of 
this specific appearance would have the same polymeric composition. In order to analyse potential 
impurities flakes with a different appearance (colour, shape, thickness, etc.) than the dominating 
were selected and analysed as well.  
3.2.1.3 Analysis of recycled samples on granule or pellet form 
As mentioned earlier, during the processing the plastic material, in this case recycled plastic waste, 
is mixed before formation into granules or pellets. Thus, recycled plastic granulates or pellets are 
==PAGE:31==
Method · 21 
 
expected to be relatively homogeneous. However, since much less knowledge exist in regards to 
recycled plastics compared to virgin plastics, five pellets or granulates from each sample were chosen 
and analysed. The variation within are expected to be representative for the entire sample.  
3.2.2 Differential scanning calorimetry  
DSC is used to determine glass transition temperature (Tg) of amorphous and semi-crystalline plastics 
and melting temperature (Tm) of semi-crystalline plastics. DSC can be performed on all substances, 
and thereby also on all polymer types within the scope of this study.  
The samples for DSC were prepared by encapsulating approximately five mg (maximum 20 mg and 
minimum one mg) of sample in an aluminium pan. Since all granulates or flakes were either too big 
for the pan or too heavy, a piece of suitable size was cut from a randomly chosen granulate or flake. 
In sample rPET(HHW)-3, where both PET and PE flakes were identified by FTIR, the sample was pre-
pared by choosing a random PET flake (identified on its appearance), since PET is the main polymer 
in this sample. Optimally a disc of flakes, including both PET and PE flakes should have been prepared 
and analysed, however, it was not possible to prepare such a disc due to too low temperatures of 
the available instrument as well as different melting temperatures of PET and PE (see Section 2.1.6).  
DSC was performed on a DSCQ1000 from TA Instruments. The temperature intervals for the different 
polymer types were: 
- 
PS: Room temperature  200ᵒC 
- 
PET: Room temperature  300ᵒC 
- 
PP: -50ᵒC  200ᵒC 
- 
PE: -90ᵒC  200ᵒC 
The heating and cooling rate was 10ᵒC/min and all samples were heated once, cooled down to erase 
thermal history and then heated a second time. Tm and Tg were determined from the last run, in 
order to avoid thermal history. Tm was reported as the peak temperatures of the endothermic melt-
ing peaks and Tg was measured at the inflection point. 
3.2.3 Thermogravimetric analysis  
TGA is used to measure weight loss during a controlled heating ramp as function of temperature. 
The weight loss directly reflects the thermal stability of the polymer and illustrates its degradation 
mechanism. The degradation mechanism is expected to be influenced by trace elements both of 
other polymers and additives within the plastic. Especially inorganic fillers are expected to be de-
tected, since these will be included in the part of the sample that does not degrade, and since inor-
ganic fillers in many cases is added to the plastic, contributing to a significant percentage of the final 
weight.  
On basis of the results of the FTIR investigations (see Section 4.1.1) TGA was performed on sub-
samples from the following samples: vHDPE-1, vLDPE-1, vLLDPE-1, vPP-1, vPP-2, rHDPE(HHW)-3, 
rLDPE(HHW)-1, rPP(HHW)-4, and rPP(IW)-5. Furthermore, TGA was performed on sub-samples from 
all PET samples, since this is a requirement in order to run DSC on these.  
The sub-samples for TGA were prepared by weighing approximately 10 mg of sample in Pt pans. In 
sample rPET(HHW)-3, where both PET and PE flakes were identified by FTIR, the sub-sample was 
prepared by choosing PET flakes from the sample. 
The TGAs were performed using a TA Q500 instrument with an inert atmosphere of N2 gas, running 
temperatures up to 700ᵒC with a heating rate of 10ᵒC/min.

3.2.4 Size exclusion chromatography  
Size exclusion chromatography (SEC) is used to analyse the molecular weight and size distribution of 
polymers within plastics.  
SEC was performed in THF on a Viscotek model 200 operating on two Polymer Laboratories PLgel 5 
μm MIXED-D columns at a flowrate of one mL/min. A refractive index/viscometry detector and a PS 
calibration were used to determine Mn and PDI. 
The samples were prepared by weighing approximately five mg sample, dissolving it in one mL THF. 
Due to the volatile nature of THF measures were taken to account for the exact amount of THF 
added.  
3.2.5 Analysis of total metals 
When information of potentially problematic metals in plastics, presented in Section 2.2.2, and limits 
of total metal content included in regulations or directives, presented in Section 2.3, are taken into 
consideration, it was found relevant to analyse all samples for total content of the following metals: 
Al, K, Cr, Mn, Co, Ni, Cu, Zn, As, Se, Sr, Cd, Sn, Sb, Ba and Pb.  
The analyses were conducted following the US EPA method 3052 using Inductively Coupled Plasma 
Mass Spectrometer (ICP-MS). Triplicates of all samples were investigated. Following this method ap-
proximately 0.25 g sample was analysed. Thus, to evaluate whether the samples needed shredding 
in order to represent variation within the samples sufficiently, 0.25 g sample on pellet/granu-
late/flake, from selected samples (samples evaluated to represent the variation in pellet/flake size 
and density within all samples), were weighted before sub-samples were taken. Illustrations of 0.25 g 
sample from selected samples are presented in Appendix III. On this basis, it was evaluated that sub-
samples from non-shredded samples represent the variations within the samples sufficiently, for the 
purpose of performing preliminary characterisations. As certified reference materials were not avail-
able, relative recoveries were calculated from comparison of the digestion methods, as described by 
Götze and Astrup (2015). 
3.2.6 Phthalate screening 
As described in Section 2.1.2, phthalates are often used in plastic as plasticisers, thus phthalates are 
expected to be found in plastic. An assumption supported by Shen (2005) who found that phthalates 
were detected in 24 out of 25 plastics for food use, and by Stangenberg et al. (2004) who found DEP 
and DPB phthalate in recycled LDPE granulates and bags. Furthermore, as explained in Section 2.3, 
several phthalates are classified as toxic for reproduction, making the plastic, to which the phthalates 
are added, unsuited for use in several commodity groups. Consequently, it was chosen to screen the 
virgin and the recycled plastic samples for phthalates. The following phthalates were included in the 
screening: DMP, DPP, DPB, DEP, DiBP, DCHP, DnOP, BBzP and DEHP. 
Since no standardised method for screening or analysis of phthalates in plastics exist, the method 
used for phthalate screening was developed for the purpose. Since the different polymer types react 
differently in solvents, different solvents, best suited to dissolve the specific polymer type, should 
optimally have been identified and used, if accurate quantification of phthalate concentrations was 
the purpose. However, due to the nature of the intended screening analysis, the strongest overall 
solvent was chosen for the dissolution purpose for all polymer types, which result in incomplete 
dissolution of certain of the polymer types. It is therefore important to notice that the method can-
not be used for quantification of neither the total phthalate content nor migration of phthalates. 
Instead the method were used as a screening for comparison of phthalate levels in virgin and recy-
cled plastics. The sample preparation method included the following steps.

3.3 Framework for quantification of substitution ratios  
The aim of the framework is to investigate and analyse important factors and circumstances crucial 
for the quality and uses of recycled plastic and on that basis estimate substitution ratios for recycled 
plastic streams. Thus, the outcome of the framework is substitution ratios representing the quality 
of the recycled plastic, relative to the quality of the virgin plastic substituted, rather than the actual 
physical amount substituted. Additionally, the framework can be used to identify how a potential 
increase in quality and thereby substitution ratio can be achieved.  
The framework is based on chemical characteristics and migration behaviour of the recycled plastic 
sample in question. The framework does not take mechanical or strength related properties into 
account, since these, in most cases, can be manipulated to reach the same standards as virgin plastic, 
by addition of additives during the upgrading process (Daugaard, 2015). Furthermore, the frame-
work does not focus on the physical losses happening during the recycling process, only the loss of 
quality compared to the virgin plastic alternative. 
The framework includes four steps: 
 
Step 1 – Potential uses from a legal perspective 
 
Step 2 – Mass-based market shares, based on commodity groups 
 
Step 3 – Industry behaviour and market responses 
 
Step 4 – Estimation of substitution ratio 
In the following sections the purpose of the four steps will be presented. 
3.3.1 Step 1 – Potential uses from a legal perspective 
Hopewell et al. (2009) stated that a way to reduce value loss when recycling is to recycle plastic to 
the point where it is usable as FP. Following this line of thinking, the more advanced commodities 
that a plastic can be used within, the higher the value of the plastic. Consequently, the first step 
==PAGE:34==
24 · Method 
 
towards determining the quality of a recycled plastic sample is to establish what commodity groups 
the plastic sample can legally be used within, which is the aim of Step 1. 
The aim is achived by presenting limit values and guidelines, established by European regulations 
and directives, relevant for use of plastic within different commodity groups. Based on these limit 
values and guidelines it will be possible, performing the relevant experimental analyses, to determine 
which commodity groups the sample in question legally can be used within. 
3.3.2 Step 2 – Mass-based market shares, based on commodity groups 
In Step 1 of the framework the potential uses of the plastic, from a legal perspective, are determined. 
The aim of Step 2 is to identify the market shares of these potential uses on the European plastic 
market. The market shares are used to illustrate the importance of the commodity groups.  
The first part of Step 2 has a focus on the general European market of plastics, whereas the second 
part has a focus on the market shares within the markets of the five individual polymer types.  
3.3.3 Step 3 – Industry behaviour and market response 
In Step 3 market responses and industry behaviour in relation to use of recycled plastic under differ-
ent circumstances are analysed and the influence on substitution discussed. The analysis and discus-
sion are based upon the following areas. 
- 
Polymer cross-contamination 
- 
Limitations to processing of single polymer streams 
- 
Limitations to processing when mixing streams of the same polymer 
- 
Mistrust due to lack of knowledge and variation in recycled plastic composition 
- 
Substitution of other materials than plastic 
3.3.4 Step 5 – Estimation of substitution ratio 
The substitution ratio estimated from this step will be an expression of quality loss of the recycled 
plastic sample, compared to the virgin plastic, rather than an expression of the physical substitution.  
The formula will be a function of information gathered in the previous steps. This will include the 
market shares of the commodity groups within which use of the specific recycled plastic sample has 
been found suitable. In addition it will include assumptions regarding industry behaviour and market 
responses. All this information and assumptions are translated into parameters based upon which 
substitution ratio formulas are configured and substitution ratios are calculated.  
3.4 Life Cycle Assessment  
Life cycle assessment (LCA) is a holistic assessment method used to evaluate the environmental im-
pacts from products or systems. The environmental impacts are given within several impact catego-
ries, depending on the life cycle impact assessment (LCIA) method in use. LCA is well suited to com-
pare two or more products or systems and has been widely used within the last couple of decades 
(E.g. Buttol, 2007; Rigamonti et al, 2009, etc.). Due to the ability to estimate environmental impacts 
within several different impact categories, LCA is furthermore well suited for identification of burden 
shifts from one impact category to another.  
Performing an LCA includes four steps; 1) Goal and scope, 2) Inventory Analysis, 3) Life cycle impact 
assessments and 4) Interpretation (EC, 2010). Performing the assessment is an iterative process since 
especially goal and scope and inventory most often have to be adjusted at several stages along the 
process. Originally LCA was developed to assess environmental impacts from products or product 
==PAGE:35==
Method · 25 
 
systems (Clavreul, 2013). However, due to simplification of the waste management step, LCA spe-
cially designed for assessment of waste management systems, was developed. 
Several different waste management systems exists (e.g. landfill, incineration, composting, recy-
cling), which need to be modelled in different ways when setting up the LCA. However, common for 
all systems are that if they produce a usable output, such as energy or recycled material, the output 
needs to be credited when performing the assessment, since it is assumed that the output from the 
system will substitute something else when being used. As an example, for a recycling system pro-
ducing recycled PET used in the production of new PET bottles, it is assumed that the recycled PET 
will substitute virgin PET, which would else have been used. Consequently, all emissions related to 
production of the virgin PET is saved. However, recycled material might not always have the same 
value as the virgin material (Rigamonti et al., 2014), why the substitution ratio (SR) might not be 
equal to 1.  
Often, many assumptions have to be made when performing an LCA, which is why it is essential as 
part of the assessment to investigate the sensitivity of these assumptions to the results of the LCA. 
One way to do this is to calculate a sensitivity ratio of the different parameters within the assessment 
in order to identify which parameters are the most sensitive. According to Clavreul et al. (2012), for 
linear systems, the sensitivity ratio is calculated as follows.

∆𝑝𝑎𝑟𝑎𝑚𝑒𝑡𝑒𝑟
𝑖𝑛𝑖𝑡𝑖𝑎𝑙_𝑝𝑎𝑟𝑎𝑚𝑒𝑡𝑒𝑟

where Δresult and Δparameter are the change in results, and parameter, respectively, and initial_re-
sult and initial_parameter are the value of the results and the parameter, respectively, used in the

As can be seen from Eq. 1, the sensitivity ratio expresses the relative change in results, relative to 
the relative change in parameter. Consequently, the higher the change in results relative to the 
change in the parameter, the higher the sensitivity ratio and the higher the sensitivity.

4 Results and discussion  
In this chapter, the experimental results and the content of the framework will be presented and 
discussed. Following this, the framework will be applied on the results with the intention of estimat-
ing a substitution ratio interval for all the collected recycled plastic samples. Finally, suggestions of 
how to improve plastic substitution ratios in the future are presented.  
4.1 Experimental results 
In the following sections the experimental results of FTIR, DSC, TGA, SEC, metal analysis and 
phthalate screening of all samples will be presented and discussed. 
4.1.1 Fourier Transform Infrared spectrometry  
All samples were characterised as explained in Section 3.2.1, depending on whether the sample was 
in flake or pellet form. In none of the samples significant variations within the individual pellets or 
flakes were found (see Appendix IV). Hence, when illustration the FTIR spectrum of a sample in the 
following sections this is done by illustrating the spectrum of a single flake or pellet within that sam-
ple. The only exception is rPET(HHW)-3, where both PET and PE flakes were identified. This will be 
explained in details in the following where FTIR spectra of all samples will be presented based on 
polymer type.  
4.1.1.1 PET 
The PET polymer have four main characteristic peaks at around 1700cm-1, 1250cm-1 1100cm-1, and 
720cm-1 (Stuart, 2007; Oromiehie and Meldrum, 1999), which are all present in the FTIR spectra of 
all four PET samples (vPET-1, rPET(HHW)-1, rPET(IW)-2 and rPET(HHW)-3), as seen in Figure 4.1(i). 
The peak around 1714cm-1 is due to the ester carbonyl stretch (Oromiehie and Meldrum, 1999), the 
peaks at 1250cm-1 and 1100cm-1 illustrates stretches from C-O bonds and the last peak around 
720cm-1 illustrates a C-H long chain band (Stuart, 2007). All functional groups naturally part of a PET 
polymer. Thus the spectra confirm that the main polymer within the PET samples are PET. This was 
expected since all recycled PET samples were on flake form, not having been through an extrusion 
process mixing and transforming all the recycled material into pellets. Thus, the individual flakes 
represent virgin plastic. 
In sample rPET(HHW)-3, however, flakes of PE were identified as well, as illustrated in Figure 4.1(ii), 
which means that polymer cross-contamination was identified within this sample. Thus, the individ-
ual flakes within the sample are pure flakes of either PET or PE, which represents an issue when pro-

4.1.1.2 PE (HDPE, LDPE and LLDPE)

are used as plasticisers and contain by nature ester carbonyl stretches. The peak might therefore be 
caused by addition of plasticisers.  
Observing the FTIR spectra of LDPE/LLDPE in Figure 4.2(ii), however, more distinct differences can 
be identified at the spectrum for rLDPE(HHW)-1, compared to the spectra of the other LDPE/LLDPE 
samples. This variation is clear between 1500cm-1 and 700cm-1, and small peaks at around        
1100cm-1 and 900cm-1 can be identified, that are not present at the other spectra. What exactly 
causes these small peaks to occur is not possible to conclude from the spectra. 
Furthermore, small peaks at 1726cm-1 and 1645cm-1 are observed for vLDPE-1, vLLDPE-1, 
rLDPE(HHW)-1, and rLLDPE(IW)-1. As for rHDPE(HHW)-4, this might indicate addition of either plas-
ticisers or stabilisers, containing ester bonds or carboxyl groups.

types, it is difficult to determine the specific reason for the variation and whether they are determin-
ing for physical, mechanical, processing or chemical properties of the plastics. Consequently, these 
results needs to be complimented with DSC, TGA and SEC.

4.1.1.3 PP 
A characteristic FTIR spectrum of isotactic PP has multiple peaks around 2800cm-1  to 3000cm-1, and 
two smaller peaks around 1300cm-1 to 1500cm-1 (Stuart, 2007; Oromiehie and Meldrum, 1999). Ob-
serving the spectra of all PP samples, presented in Figure 4.3(i), it can be seen that all the analysed 
samples have these characteristic peaks. Thus, it can be confirmed from the FTIR spectra that the 
main polymer within all PP samples is PP, as expected.  
Comparing the individual spectra for each PP sample it is clear that small differences exist. The spec-
tra of vPP-1 and rPP(HHW)-1 show a small peak around 1730cm-1, a peak that cannot be seen clearly 
in the other spectra. A peak around this wavenumber may indicate ester carbonyl stretches, which 
is present in many different stabilizers. Furthermore, the spectra of vPP-1 and rPP(HHW)-1 shows a 
small broad peak around 3750 cm-1, indicating presence of O-H (Stuart, 2007). This peak might there-
fore indicate that water has been added to the samples. Thus, it seems that the same additives have 
been added to vPP-1 and rPP(HHW)-1, which has not been added to the other samples to the same 
extend.  
Furthermore, noticeable variations in the rPP(IW)-2 and rPP(IW)-3 spectra can be observed in the 
interval 500cm-1 to 1350cm-1. The cause and importance of these variations are, however, not 
known.   
Finally, the spectrum from rPP(HHW)-4 shows peaks at around both 1730cm-1 and 1645cm-1, which 
is somewhat similar to vPP-2, even though these peaks are much smaller in vPP-2 than rPP(HHW)-4. 
Again, these peaks might indicate ester stretches, thus, vPP-2 and rPP(HHW)-4 might be stabilised in 
the same way, even though the degree might differ.   
4.1.1.4 PS 
FTIR spectra of all PS samples are presented in Figure 4.3(ii). From the figure it is clear that the spec-
tra from all PS samples show three significant peaks between 750cm-1 and 520cm-1, which are peaks 
corresponding to those in a characteristic IR spectra of PS (Stuart, 2007). Furthermore, multiple 
peaks are seen between 1300cm-1 to 1600cm-1 and 2800cm-1 to 3100cm-1, which again correspond 
to that of a characteristic PS spectrum (Stuart, 2007). As a result, it can be confirmed that the main 
polymer within all PS samples is PS. 
When comparing the FTIR spectra of the PS samples no significant variations were found.

4.1.2 Differential scanning calorimetry  
DSC were performed on all the collected samples of single polymer types. Since no virgin PO sample 
were collected it was chosen not to perform DSC on the rPO samples. In the following sections, the 
results of the DSC of the second heating run will be presented, based on polymer types. See Appendix 
VI for presentation of both the first and the second heating run as well as the cooling run for all 
analysed samples.    
4.1.2.1 PET 
The DSC thermograms of all PET samples are illustrated in Figure 4.5(i). When observing the thermo-
grams it is clear that rPET(IW)-2 is different from the others, since no peak, referring to a melting 
temperature, is present. As described in Section 2.1.1, amorphous polymers do not have a melting 
temperature. Thus, from the thermograms it can be seen that rPET(IW)-2 is amorphous as opposed 
to the other PET samples (vPET-1, rPET(HHW)-1 and rPET(HHW)-3), which are semi-crystalline. From 
Tabel 3.2 it can be seen that rPET(IW)-2 originated from industrial waste and was labelled PETG. As 
explained in Section 2.1.3 PETG might refer to glycerol modified PET which has a lower degree of 
crystallisation than normal PET, corresponding well to the fact that rPET(IW)-2 is amorphous. How-
ever, PETG could also refer to glassy PET, again corresponding well to the fact that rPET(IW)-2 is 
amorphous. Furthermore, the thermogram of rPET(IW)-2 differs from the others by having a much 
steeper glass transition point. As a result, it can be concluded that rPET(IW)-2 has different thermal 
properties compared to the other PET samples.  
From the thermograms of vPET-1, rPET(HHW)-1, and rPET(HHW)-3 it can be seen that all three sam-
ples have a Tm around 247ᵒC. Tm is indicated by one peak for vPET-1 and rPET(HHW)-3, whereas the 
Tm peak for rPET(HHW)-1 shows a small ‘bump’ before the actual peak, resulting in a small double 
peak. Multiple peaks can be due to several factors; 1) more than one polymer type is present in the 
sample, 2) the length of the polymers are different, 3) the sample consists of two or more types of 
crystals or 4) a degraded sample (Albertsson et al., 1994; Stangenberg et al., 2004; Borovanska et al., 
2012).

4.1.2.2 HDPE 
Thermograms for all collected HDPE samples are presented in Figure 4.5(ii). As expected the ther-

et al., 2004). For rHDPE(HHW)-3 and rHDPE(HHW)-4, however, two peaks can be seen. One large 
peak around 131ᵒC, as is expected, and one small, wide peak between 160ᵒC and 175ᵒC (marked 
with a square in the figure). This last peak might be caused by some degree of polymer cross-con-
tamination with PP, since the melting point of isotactic PP lies in the interval of the last peak (see 
Section 2.1.6). This corresponds with the fact that polymer cross-contamination of PE and PP is ra-
ther common since separation of these polymers gravimetrically is difficult (Borovanska et al., 2012).  
4.1.2.3 LDPE/LLDPE

et al. (2012) found Tm of 109.4ᵒC and 107.2ᵒC for technical and postconsumer LDPE waste, respec-
tively, which is within the range of the Tm found in this study. However, the variation is still signifi-
cantly large.

4.1.2.4 PP 
The thermograms for all PP samples are presented in Figure 4.6 (ii). From the figures it can be seen 
that all thermograms show a main peak indicating the melting point. Consequently, all PP samples 
are semi-crystalline. Tm for all the samples, except rPP(IW)-5, lies within an interval of 162.1ᵒC to 
168.5ᵒC, which correspond to Tm for technical PP waste and postconsumer PP waste of 160.7ᵒC and 
163.5ᵒC, respectively (Borovanska et al., 2012). rPP(IW)-5, however, is different from the others with 
a Tm at only 125.1ᵒC. As presented in Section 2.1.6 syndiotactic PP has a considerable lower melting 
point than isotactic PP of 138ᵒC. Thus rPP(IW)-5 might be syndiotactic and not isotactic polypropyl-
ene, even though the Tm is still low compared to the known Tm for syndiotactic PP.   
From the thermograms it is clear that multiple peaks appear for all recycled PP samples, with an 
exception of rPP(IW)-2, which by the producer was labelled to be a copolymeric plastic. However, a 
small double peak also occur for vPP-2, which then is not expected to be due to degradation or 
polymeric cross-contamination. This conclusion, however, cannot be transferred to the multi peaks 
for the recycled samples, since these are more distinct and occur at lower temperatures (around 
125ᵒC). It seems that the additional peak at the thermograms of rPP(HHW)-1, rPP(IW)-3, and 
rPP(HHW)-4 appear at the same temperature as the main peak for rPP(IW)-5. As a result, the addi-
tional peaks might be due to a mixture of iso-, and syndiotactic PP within the samples. The theoretical 
Tm for PE lies within 110ᵒC-130ᵒC, hence, the additional peaks might also be due to trace amounts 
of PE in the recycled PP samples. As described earlier polymer cross-contamination between PE and 
PP is rather common, due to a difficulties in regards to separation (Borovanska et al., 2012). As a 
consequence, the additional peaks raise a concern of polymer cross-contamination.

4.2.3.1 Polymer cross-contamination 
As was concluded by Jakobsen (2015) recycling of plastics into polymer blends, where compatibilisa-
tion is necessary in order for the polymers to stick together, cannot be classified as high quality re-
cycling. Compatibilisers can be either reactive or non-reactive, nevertheless, in both cases it is as-
sumed that the polymers within the mix will be impossible to separate and recycle again, making 
recycling with compatibilisation classified as low quality recycling.  
Jakobsen (2015) produced Table 4.6, where limits for polymer cross-contaminations for certain sin-
gle polymer streams are given. If polymer cross-contamination is detected in a sample, exceeding 
the limit value in Table 4.6, the sample can only be used for low quality recycling. 
In some industries, however, producing high purity plastic products such as FP, a recycled plastic 
sample will not be used in any case, if suspicion of polymer cross-contamination exists (Daugaard, 
2015).  
4.2.3.2 Limitations to processing of single streams 
As described in Section 2.1.8 an important characteristic influencing the processing rate is the mo-
lecular weight of the polymers within the plastic samples in question.  
If the polymers within a recycled sample is shown by e.g. SEC to have a significantly broader range 
of molecular weights than the virgin, it could be indicative of a somewhat degraded sample or indi-
cate that the sample might contain trace amounts of other polymers with different molecular 
weights. In either case, the sample might not be processed as easily, since the different polymers 
within the sample have different weights and thereby also different processing rates. Consequently, 
when reaching the desired processing temperature, some parts of the plastic will run faster than 
others, making the processing difficult for the industry. Hence, these kind of samples are not ex-
pected to be used within high quality products, such as FP. Nevertheless, due to companies’ potential 
desire to have a green profile (Bitsch, 2015), this kind of recycled sample might still be used within 
lower quality products.  
If a recycled plastic sample is shown to have polymers with a significantly higher distribution of mo-
lecular weights than the virgin alternative (but not significantly broader), the industries willingness 
to use the sample is dependent on the price difference of the recycled and virgin plastic. Higher 
polymer weights result in a lower processing rate, which result in less articles produced per unit of 
time. As stated in Section 2.1.8, the number of articles produced per unit of time is crucial for the 
economy of the system (Valle, 2013), which is why a reduced processing rate is expected to lead to 
reduced economical gains. However, if this reduction is compensated by the reduction of price of 
the input material, the industry may want to use the recycled material anyway. Consequently, 
whether or not the industry will use a recycled sample having a higher range of molecular weights 
of the polymers, compared to the virgin alternative, depends on the price of the recycled and virgin 
plastic.

For some products the industry will mix either recycled and virgin plastics (Bitsch, 2015) or several 
recycled plastic streams, of the same polymer type. However, if the melting points of the recycled 
and virgin plastics varies significantly or if the different recycled plastic streams have different melt-
ing points, this may represent a problem for processing.

4.2.3.4 Mistrust due to lack of knowledge and variations in composition 
As previously mentioned, HHW is a more heterogeneous waste stream than IW (Hopewell et al., 
2009) and large variations regarding HHW composition from different geographical areas are com-
mon (Christensen and Fruergaard, 2011b). Additionally, HHW has been through a use phase whereas 
IW comes directly from production. As a consequence, knowledge of the origin and ‘lifetime’ in most 
cases exist for IW but not HHW. All of these factors contribute to a disincentive for the industry to 
include especially recycled plastics from HHW in their production. E.g. several companies have ex-
pressed concern regarding potential contamination of HHW by the consumers (Daugaard, 2015), if 
the consumer uses the plastic product in a different way than intended (e.g. having gasoline in a 
bottle for mineral water). Simply the possibility of having a contamination from an unknown source 
will be a concern for productions where the end product will be used in biomedical or food applica-
tions (Daugaard, 2015). As a consequence this may create general mistrust related to use of recycled 
plastic from HHW within FP, PH and assumed also in T+T36. Moreover, lack of knowledge about 
availability of recycled plastics as well as missing information about quality and suitability for differ-
ent applications works as another disincentive for the industry to use recycled plastic especially orig-
inating from HHW (Hopewell et al., 2009).  
As a consequence, it has been found that companies producing high quality products, such as FP, do 
not use 100% recycled plastic in their production, even though it fulfils the legal requirements for 
use. One example is the company Faerch. In their production they use recycled PET, fulfilling the 
requirements for plastics in FP. However, in order to be completely sure that no products are sent 
back as a result of too high concentrations of contaminants, the outer layer of the product is coated 
with virgin PET, corresponding to 10% of the final weight (Daugaard, 2015). Another example is the 
organisation Recycling of Used Plastics Limited (RECOUP). On their website they advertise for plastic 
products, already launched on the market, made from recycled plastics. However, the only packaging 
products suitable for food contact contains ‘more than 50% post consumer recyclate’ (RECOUP, 
2015), and not 100%. Furthermore, according to Hopewell et al. (2009) most PET sheets for ther-
moforming are produced with a virgin layer, then a recycled layer, and then one more virgin layer, 
where the recycled PET represents 50%-70% by weight. Additionally, Hopewell et al. (2009) presents 
that for some PET bottles, 100% ‘super clean’ grade of rPET approved for food contact is used. 
Even within low quality recycling, recycled plastic is mixed with virgin plastic in the production. Su-
perfos, a company producing FP and NFP, uses 25%-50% recycled PP, expected to be partly from 
HHW, in the production of NFP (Bitsch, 2015). Superfos has experienced problems with strong odour 
from the recycled plastics (Bitsch, 2015) and the low percentage of recycled material in the products 
is therefore used in order to maintain the same properties as if 100% virgin plastic is used. Hence, 
properties, such as odour, seem to play an important role for the percentage of recycled plastic used 
in the production of NFP. Based on personal experience from handling of the collected plastic sam-
ples, the problem with odour seems only to exist from samples originating from HHW and on that 
ground, the percentage of recycled plastic from IW in low quality products cannot be assumed to be 
the same as for recycled plastic from HHW.  
Acording to Bitsch (2015) the decision of using recycled plastic in the first place is encouraged by a 
desire to use less CO2 and thereby giving them the possibility of using ‘green’ marketing and is in 
general not related to the price of recycled and virgin plastics. Thus, it is expected that the desire for 
an environmentally friendly image for certain industries are more important than the plastic prices.   
Moreover, within the pharmaceutical industry, one company has expressed that they do not even 
consider use of recycled plastics in their production (Daugaard, 2015), due to lack of precise infor-
mation about chemical composition and purity. The same could be suspected to be true for certain

PS Extrusion and G9 are two Danish companies using very low quality plastic to produce plastic lum-

the cases the recycled plastic do not substitute virgin plastic, but instead wood (Plastindustrien,

posed to substitute hard wood.  
Moreover, RECOUP advertise for plastic products containing recycled plastic from postconsumer 
waste. Observing the list of products, most of them substitute other materials than plastic, such as 
wood or steel. E.g. the category in which most products exist is garden furniture, all expected to 
substitute wood (RECOUP, 2015). The same situation is observed for the Dutch company Lankhorst 
and the company British Recycled Plastic (BRP), who produces products from recycled plastic. Almost 
all of their products are within the field of building and construction, recreational industry or agri-
culture and include poles, planks, pontoons, sheet piling, decking, facade panels, bridges, ground re 
inforcement grids, raised beds, and pile planking (LRP, 2015; BRP, 2015). Many of which are substi-
tuting either wood or metal.

4.2.4 Step 4 – Estimation of substitution ratio 
All of the knowledge and assumptions presented in the previous steps are in this step translated into 
parameters. All parameters are presented in Appendix XI and explained in details. The parameters 
are used to set up an equation for the calculation of plastic, wood, and steel substitution ratios, as 
presented below. 
SR_P = (([FP · SR_FP + T_T36 + PH] · PCC · PCC_EX · PRO · HHHW · BPW 
+ [EE + BC · SR_BC_P]) · PCC_EX  
+ NFP · SR_NFP_HHW · HHHW + NFP · IW + OTAU · SR_OTAU_P) · HPW 
Eq. 2
SR_W = (BC · SR_BC_W · PCC_EX + OTAU · SR_OTAU_W) · HPW 
Eq. 3
SR_S = (BC · SR_BC_S · PCC_EX + OTAU · SR_OTAU_S) · HPW 
Eq. 4
 
where SR_P is the plastic substitution ratio, SR_W is the wood substitution ratio, and SR_S is the steel 
substitution ratio.  
The three different substitution ratios express the quality of recycled plastic related to the quality of 
virgin plastic, wood, and steel. The point of the three substitution ratios is that they are all calculated 
for every recycled plastic sample investigated, and all substitution ratios have to be included in the 
environmental evaluation of the recycling system. This has been done since the loss of quality within 
the plastic substitution ratio (caused by the fact that some of the BC, AU, and OT products produced 
from recycled plastic, substitute wood and steel) needs to be compensated by a certain degree of 
substitution of wood, and steel. Thus, by summing up the three substitution ratios, a generic total 
substation ratio can be calculated. However, in order to include these into an LCA, they have to be 
split up so that they substitute different virgin materials.  
A fundamental principle of the formulas is that the market share of the commodity groups, within 
which the recycled plastic is suitable, is an expression of the quality of the recycled plastic, assuming 
that the virgin alternative can be used within all commodity groups. Furthermore, the percentage of 
recycled plastic used in products within a certain commodity group is assumed to further express 
the quality of the plastic when used in this specific commodity. A detailed explanation and argumen-
tation for the configuration of Eq. 2 to Eq. 4 is given in Appendix XII. Several simplifications have been 
made in regards to the parameters and the formulas in order to make the calculations possible. 
These are presented in Appendix XIII and potential improvements are discussed. 
It is important to notice that when the wood and steel substitution ratios are calculated the differ-
ences in densities of the materials are not taken into account. Consequently, this difference needs 
to be accounted for in the specific case.

4.3 Determination of substitution ratios from experimental re-
sults 
In the following sections four scenarios will be set up and the influence of the experimental results 
on the substitution ratio of the 20 recycled plastic samples, using the method developed in Sec-
tion 4.2, will be presented for all four scenarios. In addition, both sample and scenario dependent 
parameter values will be defined.  
4.3.1 Scenarios 
From the results presented in Section 4.2, it is clear that uncertainty is associated with several of the 
findings and assumptions. Thus, in order to take these uncertainties into account, both in relation to 
use within commodity groups where the necessary chemical analysis have not been conducted, and 
in relation to assumptions regarding industry behaviour, four different scenarios have been set up. 
These include two best case scenarios (B), two worst case scenarios (W), two mistrust scenarios (M), 
and two trust scenarios (T). As a result the following four scenarios have been set up: Best-Trust (B-
T), Best-Mistrust (B-M), Worst-Trust (W-T), and Worst-Mistrust (W-M). 
The point of the B and W scenarios is that the parameters are assigned with either the best possible 
or the worst possible value (in regards to the environmental performance), in cases where the values 
cannot be precisely determined due to lack of knowledge  
The point of the T and M scenarios is that all the parameters values, depending on the level of trust 
shown by the industry, are assigned with the best and worst value, respectively. 
The parameter values included within the different scenarios will be presented in the following sec-
tions.

4.3.4.1 Polymer cross-contamination 
From the DSC thermograms presented in Section 4.1.2 potential polymer cross-contamination was 
found in rHDPE(HHW)-3, rHDPE(HHW)-4, rLDPE(IW)-2, rPP(HHW)-1, rPP(IW)-3, rPP(HHW)-4 and 
rPP(IW)-5. Thus, the parameter PCC is equal to 0 for these samples. Furthermore, even though the 
DSC thermogram of rPET(HHW)-3 did not show multiple peaks, the FTIR showed that both PET and 
PE flakes were present in the sample. Thus, the parameter PCC is equal to 0 for rPET(HHW)-3 as well. 
The influence on this potential polymer cross-contamination is, however, very uncertain. Conse-
quently, the parameter PCC is only included in Eq. 2 in the W scenarios. In the B scenarios, on the 
other hand, the parameter is taken out of the equation, following the best case assumption, that the 
suspicion of polymer cross-contamination does not influence production. 
It was not possible to quantify the level of potential polymer cross-contamination within the previous 
mentioned samples and whether the limits presented in Table 4.6 are exceeded can therefore not 
be certain. However, since the FTIR spectra of rHDPE(HHW)-3, rHDPE(HHW)-4, rLDPE(IW)-2, 
rPP(HHW)-1, rPP(IW)-3, rPP(HHW)-4 and rPP(IW)-5 were all relatively close to the spectra of the vir-
gin polymer, it is not assumed that the limits for polymer cross-contamination was exceeded for 
these samples, thus PCC_EX is equal to 1. 
For rPET(HHW)-3 on the other hand, it was not possible to melt a disc of several flakes of the sample, 
since the PE flakes got black and started to burn before the PET flakes melted. This illustrates exactly 
the kind of problem polymer cross-contamination poses. Thus, it is expected that rPET(HHW)-3 will

need addition of compatibilisers in order to be processed in a satisfactory way. Hence, PCC_EX is 
assumed equal to 0 for rPET(HHW)-3.  
PCC, and thereby also PCC_EX, are equal to 1 for the rest of the samples, since no reason for concern 
of polymer cross-contamination was discovered within these samples.  
4.3.4.2 Limitations to processing of single streams 
SEC, analysing the molecular weight of polymers within a plastic samples, was performed on all the 
collected PS samples. From the results, presented in Section 4.1.4, no significant difference of the 
molecular weights of the polymers within the samples was found. Thus, HPW and BPW are both 
equal to 1 for rPS(IW)-1 and rPS(IW)-2.  
It was not possible to perform SEC on the rest of the samples. As a consequence, it cannot be ascer-
tained whether a difference in polymer molecular weights within the recycled and virgin samples 
exists. However, based on the fact that the SEC results for all PS samples were very similar despite 
differences in thermal properties, it is assumed that the SEC results for the rest of the samples are 
similar as well, even though difference in the thermal properties are observed. As a result HPW and 
BPW are assumed equal to 1 for all recycled samples. 
4.3.4.3 Limitations to processing when mixing streams of the same polymer type 
In Section 4.1.2 large varieties of the Tms within samples of LDPE/LLDPE and PP were found. Thus, 
PRO is equal to 0 for all LDPE/LLDPE and PP samples. As described earlier variation in Tms might 
influence the processing of the samples if industry wants to mix virgin and recycled plastic or several 
recycled plastic streams. However, the degree of influence on processing is uncertain. Thus, in order 
to take this uncertainty into account the parameter PRO is only included in Eq. 2 in the W scenarios. 
This means, that it in the W scenarios is assumed that a variation in Tms will influence production and 
as a result recycled LDPE/LLDPE and PP streams will not be used within FP, T+T36 and PH. In the B 
scenarios the parameter PRO will be taken out of Eq. 2 assuming that the variation in Tms will not 
have a significant influence on production. 
4.3.4.4 Mistrust due to lack of knowledge and variation in composition 
In Section 4.2.3 several cases were described regarding companies mixing or coating recycled plastic 
with virgin plastic, when producing FP, in order to make sure that no contaminants will migrate from 
the plastic. Consequently, recycled plastic fulfilling the requirements for use in FP still does not rep-
resent the same high quality as virgin plastic. The market share of plastic potentially used for FP 
therefore needs to be corrected. In Section 4.2.3, two cases were presented. One company used 
90% recycled plastic and another company used above 50% (assumed to be 50%) recycled plastic in 
the FP production. A certain degree of uncertainty is related to this percentage and the degree of 
trust exhibited by the industry. Thus, in order to take this difference into account the value of SR_FP 
are in the M scenarios set to 0.556 (average of 0.5 and 0.9), whereas it in the T scenarios is set to 
0.9.  
In addition, it was in Section 4.2.3 presented that 25%-50% recycled plastic partly from HHW was 
used in the production of NFP products. However, this information originates from one case only. As 
a consequence, in order to take uncertainty into account, several values of SR_NFP_HHW are deter-
mined. In the W scenarios SR_NFP_HHW is assumed equal to 0.375 (average between 0.25 and 0.50). 
Since no legal requirements, in regards to the chemical composition, exists for NFP it is assumed that 
use of 100% recycled plastic from HHW could be possible, if the industry exhibit the highest possible 
level of trust towards to recycled plastic streams. Hence, in the T scenarios, SR_NFP_HHW is assumed 
equal to 1. No information has been found in regards to use of recycled plastic from IW within NFP

4.3.4.5 Substitution of other materials than plastic 
It was in Section 4.2.3 found that a lot of products made from recycled plastics, within BC, AU and 
OT substitutes wood or steel instead of virgin plastic. Based on these findings SR_BC_P is assumed 
equal to 0.2 and SR_BC_W and SR_BC_S are assumed equal to 0.4 (summing up to a total of 1). For 
OT and AU the assumption is that SR_OTAU_P is equal to 0.45, SR_OTAU_W is equal to 0.5 and 
SR_OTAU_S is assumed equal to the remaining 0.05 (again summing up to a total of 1). Most of the 
products containing recycled plastics that are not within BC were found to substitute wood, such as 
benches, litter bins, etc., whereas no knowledge was obtained about recycled plastic products within 
AU. Hence, these are assumed to substitute plastic.

4.3.6.1 Sensitivity analysis  
A sensitivity analysis of all the parameters included in the calculation of the substitution ratios was 
performed. The sensitivity analysis is used to investigate to which parameters a potential change 
have the largest potential to increase the substitution ratio, resulting in more environmentally sound 
recycling. The sensitivity analysis has been performed on scenario B-T for both rPP(IW)-2, and 
rHDPE(HHW)-4. This scenario was chosen since most parameters are included in the calculation of 
substitution ratios for scenario B-T. All parameter values were decreased by 10% and sensitivity ra-
tios were calculated using the method presented in Section 3.4. The parameters PRO and BPW can 
only have the value 1 or 0. For these parameter the value was changed from 1 to 0, and a sensitivity 
ratio was calculated based on a change of 1, instead of 0.1. Additionally, the parameter HPW can 
also only be changed from 1 to 0. However, when changing the value of HPW to 0, all substitution 
ratios become equal to 0, and as a result no recycling can take place. Consequently, since HPW af-
fects all the other parameters within the substitution ratio formulas, this is by far the most sensitive 
parameter. In addition, large uncertainties are related to the HPW value as well as the way it is in-
cluded in the sensitivity ratio formulas (for more details see Appendix XIII). On that ground it is obvi-
ous that more investigation needs to be put into the use of the parameter, which is why it is not 
included in the further sensitivity analysis. 
The results of the sensitivity analysis are presented in Figure 4.19 and is estimated from the environ-
mental impact to ‘climate change’. From the figure, it is clear that a lot more parameters are included 
in the calculation of the substitution ratios for rPP(IW)-2 than rHDPE(HHW)-4. This is a result of 
rPP(IW)-2 having the potential of being used in more commodity groups than rHDPE(HHW)-4. E.g. 
the parameters PRO and SR_FP are parameters related to FP, T+T36, and PH, all commodity groups 
in where rHDPE(HHW)-4 legally cannot be used. Consequently, the sensitivity of 16 parameters were 
analysed for rPP(IW)-2, and the sensitivity of 10 parameters were analysed for rHDPE(HHW)-4.

order to get a more precise and detailed picture of the sensitivity of the parameters, an analysis 
including changes to a reduction in parameter needs to be conducted. This, however, requires a lot 
a knowledge of the market responses, knowledge that have not been obtained in enough details in 
this study.

4.5 Potential future improvement of substitution ratios  
From Section 4.3.6 and Section 4.3.6.1, it was made clear that the potential uses of the recycled 
plastic, the origin and the percentage of recycled plastic used within important commodity groups 
are crucial for the magnitude of the substitution ratios. On this ground two general suggestions to 
how to improve the substitution ratios in the future are given in the following. 
Firstly, as was observed, the recycled plastic sample’s suitability for use within FP is very important 
for the substitution ratios, and if substitution ratios as high as the ones used in literature for single 
polymer streams (0.755-0.9) have to be reached, use within FP is a necessity. Several of the recycled 
samples originating from IW were potentially found suitable for use within FP and concentrations of 
metals were similar to them of the virgin plastic samples. As a result, it is expected that with more 
control of IW streams and more awareness of the environmental benefits, it will be possible to pro-
duce recycled plastic from IW suitable for FP. These changes will not be easily implemented, but they 
are assumed possible.  
The situation is quite different for recycled plastic originating from HHW. These samples were in 
general found to be more contaminated than recycled samples from IW and virgin plastic samples. 
Furthermore, lots of mistrust is related to use of recycled plastic from HHW, since no or very little 
information about its history or ‘lifetime’ exists. It is therefore expected to be a much bigger chal-
lenge to recycle HHW into a point where it is suitable for FP. However, the study shows, that it has 
been done for recycling of PET bottles (represented by rPET(HHW)-1), which are a part of the HHW 
stream. Consequently, by focussing on easily recognisable waste products in the HHW and creating 
special collection schemes or deposit systems, avoiding the majority of impurities within the HHW, 
recycling of HHW plastics into FP might in the future be possible. 
A measure that might be easier and more short-sighted, used to obtain higher substitution ratios for 
recycled plastic originating from HHW, is to focus on increasing the amount of recycled plastic used 
within NFP. NFP represents relativity high market shares within the market of all investigated poly-
mer types, and no legal requirements in regards to chemical composition exists, which is why an 
increase of recycled plastic used within NFP products has the potential to increase the substitution 
ratios considerably.

Conclusion · 65 
 
5 Conclusion 
With the purpose of developing the framework, estimating quality of recycled plastics, scientific lit-
erature and European legislation were reviewed and characteristics of the polymer types in focus 
were identified.  
Developing the framework three main areas were found to influence the quality of recycled plastic 
and thereby the substitution ratio. These were 1) the possible uses of the recycled plastic based on 
legislative requirements, 2) the market shares of these uses, and 3) industry behaviour and market 
responses relevant for use of recycled plastic. Within these areas it was found that 
- Legislation related to use within food packaging and toys are especially comprehensive, 
whereas no legislation regarding chemical characteristics exists for non-food packaging, auto-
motive and others (including furniture, clothes, etc.).  
- Food packaging represents an important commodity group within all polymer types, with mar-
ket shares of 67%-32% and non-food packaging were found to be important within the market 
of all polymer types as well.  
- Within food packaging less than 100% recycled material is used, since most companies coat 
the outer layer with virgin plastic.  
- Within non-food packaging significantly less than 100% recycled plastic is used, when it origi-
nates from household waste.  
- Within commodity groups producing high quality plastic products, the origin of the plastic is 
generally crucial for the potential use, since a higher level of mistrust is related to recycled 
plastic originating from heterogeneous waste flows where no knowledge of the prehistory 
exists.  
- Recycled plastic within the commodity groups building and construction, automotive, and oth-
ers often substitute wood or steel rather than virgin plastic.  
Performing relevant experimental tests identifying contaminants and characterising the chemical 
composition of the plastic samples, several results were found to be important for the potential sub-
stitution. E.g. severe polymer cross-contamination were found within one recycled PET sample from 
household waste eliminating the potential for use within food packaging, toys, and pharmaceuticals. 
Furthermore, it was found that the metal concentrations within recycled samples from household 
waste in general were higher than the concentrations within recycled samples from industrial waste 
as well as virgin samples. Both indicating that recycled plastic from heterogeneous waste sources 
might be more contaminated and thereby have a lower quality than recycled plastic from controlled 
homogeneous waste stream. Additionally, it was found that 15 out of the 20 collected samples con-
tained at least one CMR classified phthalate, excluding potential use within food packaging and toys.  
Several additional results were obtained performing IR, DSC, TGA, and SEC. It was found that the 
melting temperature of the recycled LDPE/LLDPE and PP samples varied a lot, potentially influencing 
processing of the plastic if two or more recycled plastic streams are mixed. However, the exact im-
portance of these results to the quality of the recycled plastics were rather uncertain, and as a con-
sequence further investigation into the importance of the results to the substitution is necessary.

intervals, presented in Table 5.1, were obtained. The table presents plastic substitution ratios for 
both recycled plastics suitable for food contact and recycled plastics not suitable for food contact as 
well as wood and steel substitution ratios. It is important to notice that the substation ratios for plas-

Further work · 67 
 
6 Further work 
In order to further improve the framework and thereby increase the amount of knowledge related 
to quality and secondary uses of recycled plastic, the following areas should be further investigated. 
Firstly, the framework is built on the assumption that there is no limitation in plastic recycling, in 
regards to mechanical, physical, age or strength related properties, only in regards to chemical com-
position and migration behaviour. However, for some applications these properties might influence 
the substitution. E.g. Bitsch (2015) expressed problems related to odour from recycled plastic, and 
physical properties may therefore still be a problem related to recycling of plastics. Furthermore, 
according to Vandevyver (2015) low-end recyclates, including high amounts of solid contaminants, 
might only be recycled into objects with a certain thickness, in order to obtain reasonable resistance 
towards external impacts. Thus, it would be relevant in future studies to investigate the potential 
limitations from reduction or change in mechanical, physical, age or strength related properties, and 
include the potential limitations into the framework.  
Secondly, in order to reduce uncertainty of the framework the behaviour within the pharmaceutical 
and especially the building and construction industry should be further investigated. For these areas 
of use only directives exist, including no specific limit values. It is required to list the hazardous sub-
stances within the plastic on the produced product. Step 1 of the framework is built upon the as-
sumption that the industry follows the guidelines within these directives and list the hazardous sub-
stances in the plastic. However, it has been found that many different building and construction 
related products are produced from very low quality recycled material (RECOUP, 2015; LRP, 2015; 
BRP, 2015). Thus, it can be questioned whether the directive guidelines are observed. The behaviour 
of the pharmaceutical industry and especially the building and construction, since the market share 
of this is in general much larger, should therefore be further investigated and the framework edited 
according to the findings.   
In order to further improve the knowledge in regards to marked responses when using recycled plas-
tic, it would be relevant to investigate the price of virgin and recycled plastics more into details, as 
well as investigating how the market will respond to a given difference of these prices. In continua-
tion hereof impact of the difference in price needs to be investigated for all assumptions related to 
industry behaviour, and potential findings should be implemented into the framework. 
Moreover, according to Bitsch (2015) the main reason for Superfos to use recycled plastic within 
their production of NFP is the desire to reduce CO2 emissions and thereby be able to use green 
branding in their marketing. Thus, in order to improve the framework the importance of the desire 
of green marketing and the impact of this desire on the other assumptions within the framework, 
needs to be investigated more into details. 
Finally, the chemical composition of the virgin plastics, that the quality of the recycled plastic is based 
upon, were found to vary. Consequently, no general virgin quality within the different polymer types 
exists. As a result more information about the virgin plastic, that the recycled plastic is compared to, 
needs to be obtained, in order to get an idea of the range of compositions within virgin plastics.
