Abstract
The global shift toward high shares of wind, solar and battery systems is transforming power sys-
tems into fully inverter dominated grids. As synchronous machines disappear, maintaining voltage
stability depends increasingly on the reactive power capability of inverter based plants. Modern
grid codes now require hybrid power plants to deliver prescribed reactive power (P–Q) perfor-
mance across wide voltage ranges, making the sizing of inverters and external compensation
devices a central design challenge.
This thesis develops a unified framework to evaluate and size the reactive power capability of large
wind solar battery plants. A steady state electrical model is constructed to represent converter
limits, voltage dependent network behaviour and offshore transmission constraints. This model is
embedded in a plant level assessment that verifies compliance with grid code P–Q requirements
under realistic operating conditions, and is integrated within the DTU HyDesign tool to couple
reactive capability with energy dispatch and economic metrics.
The results reveal clear structural trends. When the plant operates in a current limited regime,
additional inverter headroom provides reactive capability at significantly lower cost than external
compensation. As export cables become longer or the external grid weaker, the plant transitions to
a voltage limited regime where additional headroom cannot recover inductive margin and external
compensation becomes necessary to satisfy the required reactive envelope.
The framework identifies when inverter only solutions are sufficient, when external devices such
as STATCOMs become technically mandatory, and how these choices influence feasibility, robust-
ness and project value. It provides a structured basis for designing hybrid power plants that remain
compliant and cost effective as power systems become fully inverter dominated.
Resume
Den globale overgang til en høj andel af vind-, sol- og batterisystemer er ved at omdanne el-
systemerne til net, der er fuldstændig domineret af invertere. I takt med at synkrone maskiner
forsvinder, afhænger opretholdelsen af spændingsstabiliteten i stigende grad af inverterbaserede
anlægs reaktive effektkapacitet. Moderne netkoder kræver nu, at hybridkraftværker leverer en
foreskrevet reaktiv effekt (P–Q) over brede spændingsområder, hvilket gør dimensioneringen af
invertere og eksterne kompensationsanordninger til en central designudfordring.
Denne afhandling udvikler en samlet ramme til evaluering og dimensionering af reaktiv effektka-
paciteten i store vind-, sol- og batterianlæg. Der konstrueres en elektrisk model i steady state
til at repræsentere konvertergrænser, spændingsafhængig netværksadfærd og begrænsninger i
offshore-transmission. Denne model er indlejret i en vurdering på anlægsniveau, der verificerer
overholdelse af netkodens P–Q-krav under realistiske driftsforhold, og er integreret i DTU HyDe-
sign-værktøjet for at koble reaktiv kapacitet med energiforsyning og økonomiske målinger.
Resultaterne afslører klare strukturelle tendenser. Når anlægget kører i et strømbegrænset regime,
giver ekstra inverter-headroom reaktiv kapacitet til betydeligt lavere omkostninger end ekstern
kompensation. Når eksportkablerne bliver længere eller det eksterne net svagere, overgår an-
lægget til et spændingsbegrænset regime, hvor ekstra headroom ikke kan genoprette den induk-
tive margin, og ekstern kompensation bliver nødvendig for at opfylde den krævede reaktive ramme.
Rammeværket identificerer, hvornår inverterløsninger alene er tilstrækkelige, hvornår eksterne
enheder såsom STATCOM’er bliver teknisk obligatoriske, og hvordan disse valg påvirker gennem-
førligheden, robustheden og projektets værdi. Det giver et struktureret grundlag for at designe
hybridkraftværker, der forbliver kompatible og omkostningseffektive, når elsystemerne bliver fuldt
ud inverterdominerede.

Introduction
1.1
Growth of Variable Renewable Energy (VRE) and Impact on Grid Stability
The global power landscape has significantly transformed over the past three decades, with vari-
able renewable energy sources shifting from a marginal contribution to a predominant role. Ac-
cording to data from the International Energy Agency and IRENA, renewables constituted over
90% of new global capacity additions in 2024, largely propelled by approximately 452 GW of
solar photovoltaic and 113 GW of wind power installations 2. Across various regions, including
Denmark, Spain, Germany, and parts of India, instantaneous renewable energy penetration has
already exceeded 80%. This development necessitates grid operators to prepare for integrating
entirely non synchronous systems 1.
Figure 1: Current status of renewable penetra-
tion in selected countries and states.
Figure 2: Global installed capacity addition per
fuels group (2001-2024)
While these transitions offer clear climate benefits, they also present new challenges for power
system stability. Traditional synchronous generators naturally provide rotational inertia, shortcircuit
strength, and voltage support through electromagnetic coupling. In contrast, inverter based re-
sources supply power via power electronic interfaces, demanding careful design and coordinated
strategies to replicate these essential grid functionalities. As synchronous generation is gradu-
ally removed, the resulting decrease in system inertia and weakening of voltage stability increase
the grid’s vulnerability to disturbances. Consequently, maintaining system stability evolves from an
inherent feature of synchronous plant operation into a critical engineering requirement, necessitat-
ing advanced inverter control algorithms, optimized converter sizing, and proactive reactive power
deployment.
Observations from power systems with high variable renewable energy integration confirm
these operational challenges. For example, the 2019 blackout in Great Britain and subsequent
analyses revealed that reduced system inertia and increased non synchronous generation pen-
etration lead to new operational constraints [1]. reland has progressively raised its system non
synchronous penetration (SNSP) limit from 50% to 75%, with a stated ambition of reaching 95%
by 2030 as grid forming and stability enhancing controls continue to mature [2]. Denmark and
Germany exhibit similar trends, increasingly utilizing fast frequency response, synthetic inertia,
and advanced voltage control mechanisms to maintain secure system operation.
10
==PAGE:13==
These dynamics are equally visible in emerging markets experiencing rapid renewable de-
ployment. In the Dominican Republic (DR), accelerated growth in PV and wind generation has
begun to stress the transmission system, especially during periods of high solar output and low
synchronous generation. Recent national regulations now require large renewable projects to in-
tegrate battery energy storage systems (BESS) to provide frequency regulation, synthetic inertia,
and voltage control effectively transitioning from policies that incentivise clean energy production
to frameworks that mandate robust grid support functionality[3]
Within this evolving landscape, reactive power capability has become a critical design and
operational constraint. Modern grid codes specify explicit reactive power envelopes at the Point of
Interconnection (POI) and require compliance across a defined voltage range. The question of how
and where to deliver this capability whether through wind and PV inverters, BESS converters, or
external compensation devices such as STATCOMs directly affects technical feasibility, regulatory
compliance, and project economics. Insufficient reactive capability may lead to voltage violations
or curtailment, whereas excessive oversizing inflates capital expenditure and erodes project value.
Balancing these considerations is therefore central to the design of next generation renewable and
hybrid power plants.
1.2
Problem Statement
Transmission system operators (TSOs) are tightening grid code requirements for voltage control,
forcing renewable plants to deliver substantial reactive power capability across wide operating and
voltage ranges. For large inverter based renewable (IBR) plants, this requirement is no longer
marginal: it directly influences technology selection, sizing decisions, and project economics. The
central design question is therefore how reactive power should be supplied through inverter head-
room, external FACTS devices, or a combination of both.
However, reactive capability cannot be inferred from device ratings alone. Internal voltage
drops, cable charging, transformer reactances, and operating point dependent converter limits all
distort the aggregate reactive envelope as seen at the Point of Interconnection (POI). Existing
commercial tools (for example: NREL SAM) do not include these electrical constraint , therefore
cannot provide reliable planning stage estimates for hybrid plants.
This thesis addresses this gap by developing a unified methodology to compute the reactive
power capability of large scale hybrid power plants (HPPs) composed of wind, PV, and BESS units.
The methodology is implemented within HyDesign and integrated with an economic optimisation
layer, enabling simultaneous evaluation of active power scheduling, reactive power sizing, and
long term financial performance (NPV, IRR).
By extending beyond single technology models and embedding an explicit economic trade off
between inverter oversizing and external reactive compensation, the work provides a practical
planning stage tool for developers facing increasingly demanding grid code requirements.

Q1: What specific grid code requirements particularly regarding reactive power capability, voltage
stability are critical for renewable and hybrid power plants, and how are these expected to
evolve towards 2030-2050?

1.4
Methodology and Objectives
The methodology couples electrical aggregation, optimisation, and time series reduction to quan-
tify and optimise reactive power capability at the POI. It proceeds through five steps:
1. Electrical abstraction: Each subsystem (WT, PV, BESS) is represented by its converter
capability curve (PQ capabilibty). Internal networks are reduced using Muljadi equivalencing,
yielding a Thevenin equivalent that relates converter currents to POI voltage and reactive
output [4].
2. Aggregated reactive capability model: Building on [5] and [6], device limits are propagated
through the equivalent network to derive the plant level U-Q/Pmax envelope under voltage
constraints (Vc,min-Vc,max).
3. Integration into HyDesign: The model is embedded in HyDesign’s optimisation frame-
work to ensure that dispatch solutions remain within feasible P-Q limits while maximising net
present value (NPV) and internal rate of return (IRR).
4. Representative day clustering: A clustering algorithm identifies critical operating days
those exhibiting high reactive stress to reduce computational time while retaining accuracy
over long term operation.
5. Sensitivity analysis: Variation of grid voltage, converter parameters, and cable length is
done to quantifies their impact on reactive power.
Objectives
• Develop a unified, voltage aware reactive capability model for hybrid power plants combining
wind, PV, and BESS.
• Integrate the model into HyDesign to jointly optimise energy dispatch, reactive sizing, and
long term economics.
• Apply clustering and sensitivity analysis to ensure computational efficiency without compro-
mising model fidelity.
• Provide design guidance on the trade offs between inverter oversizing and STATCOM de-
ployment.

1.5
Limitations
This thesis adopts a steady–state modelling approach with hourly temporal resolution. Sub hourly
dynamics including fast frequency response, converter control interactions, and electromagnetic
transients are not simulated explicitly. Their influence is only approximated through conservative
constraint margins. Dynamic stability, although increasingly important in future power systems
with high IBR penetration, falls outside the scope of the present work due to time and resource
constraints.
All simulations are carried out under Danish grid code conditions; therefore, the results reflect
the requirements and operational philosophy of a single jurisdiction. International code variations
particularly those in weak grids or regions with different UQ/PQ obligations are not examined.
At plant level, the wind, PV, and BESS subsystems are modelled as aggregated units. This
requires several simplifying assumptions on converter coordination, collector topology, and internal
control behaviour. Detailed EMT level component models are not used. Furthermore, a single wind
turbine generator type (Type 4 full converter) is considered throughout the analysis.
Additional limitations are discussed where relevant in subsequent chapters, particularly in re-
lation to numerical resolution, cable modelling fidelity, and the feasibility assumptions embedded
in the EMS-PF cosimulation framework.

This chapter provides a structured literature review of (i) the grid codes and connection require-

capability,and the evolution of these requirements in the transition toward high or fully renewable

2.1
Grid Codes and Connection Requirements for IBRs (Focus: Voltage & Reac-
tive Power)
Grid codes constitute mandatory technical and performance standards that generators, energy
storage systems, and substantial electrical loads are required to satisfy for connection to pub-
lic electricity networks. Their primary objective is to safeguard system security and maintain the
quality of supply, particularly as non synchronous, inverter-based resources become increasingly
prevalent. These connection requirements specifically address steady-state operational capabil-
ities, dynamic behavior, frequency response, power quality, testing procedures, and operational
coordination. A recent, extensive review highlights these codes as a pivotal instrument for ac-
quiring reactive power support from IBRs and ensuring effective voltage control at the point of
interconnection [7].
2.1.1

ENTSO-E’s Network Codes are grouped into Market, Operation, Connection, and Cybersecurity.
For IBR connection, the relevant documents are the Demand Connection Code (DCC), Require-
ments for Generators (RfG), and HVDC code. This review focuses on RfG provisions affecting
steady state voltage regulation and reactive power capability at the POI, and the dynamic obli-
gations (FRT/reactive current injection) that constrain steady state capability envelopes [7] [8][9].

Generator Classification (Types A-D) and Applicability
The European Network of Transmission System Operators for Electricity Network Code on Re-
quirements for Generators (RfG) categorizes generating units into four types based on their impact

power capabilities, Type A generators are exempt. For Type B, reactive power requirements typ-
ically apply to synchronous generating units but generally not to inverter based resources. How-
ever, Types C and D explicitly mandate reactive power capability for IBRs. It is important to note
that the specific thresholds for these classifications can differ across synchronous areas and are
defined by individual Transmission System Operators..

2.1.3
Reactive power Capability at the POI: PQ and UQ Envelopes
Figure 5b illustrates representative PQ envelopes by country: Continental Europe (black), Den-
mark (red), and the United Kingdom (blue–green).
At the point of interconnection, inverter based resources must demonstrate compliance with
two regulatory capability domains: the active reactive power region (PQ) and the voltage reactive
power region (UQ). The outer boundary is defined by ENTSO-E, while each TSO imposes an inner
envelope tailored to local system conditions. Typical inner envelope magnitudes vary significantly
across synchronous areas, for example, approximately 0.80 in the Baltic, 0.75 in Continental Eu-
rope, 0.66 in Great Britain, 0.66 in Ireland, and 0.95 in the Nordic region with additional tightening
in national grid codes.
2.1.4
Reactive power Controllability (Setpoint Modes & Dynamics)
Most grid codes require three automatic reactive control modes: Voltage Control (closed-loop with
droop), reactive power Control (Q-set), and Power-Factor Control (PF-set). Energinet (Denmark)
specifies these ranges and dynamics explicitly. The voltage setpoint must be adjustable within
95–105% of nominal (resolution ≤1%), and the voltage droop must be selectable within 2–7%
(step size ≤0.5%). Dynamic requirements are also quantified: the facility must reach 90% of the
commanded reactive step within ≤1 s and fully settle within ≤5 s. Steady-state tracking accuracy
must remain within ±5% of the device’s rated reactive capability. Equivalent accuracy requirements
apply when operating in Q-set or PF-set mode.

2.1.5
Dynamic Obligations that Constrain Steady-State Capability
Low-/High-Voltage Ride-Through (LVRT/HVRT) and Reactive Current Priority. During voltage
dips, inverter based resources must prioritise reactive current injection over active power delivery
to support grid voltage [10–12]. This “Q-priority” requirement forces the converter to allocate part
of its current limit to reactive support, reducing the available steady-state active-power region [13,
14]. In Denmark, reactive current injection must begin once the POI voltage drops below 0.90 pu
and continue until the voltage fully recovers [15]. Danish specifications explicitly state that reactive
current takes precedence during disturbances [16]. Although thresholds differ slightly between
DK1 and DK2, the principle of reactive priority remains uniform across the Danish transmission
system [16]. Furthermore, many grid codes mandate a minimum reactive current contribution often
up to 100% of rated reactive current when the voltage dip exceeds a defined severity threshold such
as 0.50 pu [17].
Zero Active-Power Compensation.
When the plant is not generating active power (e.g.,
during low-wind or outage conditions), it must still compensate the reactive demand of its own
infrastructure including submarine cable charging, HVAC/HVDC reactive consumption, and trans-
former magnetizing current. This ensures that POI-level reactive requirements remain satisfied
regardless of generation output.
2.1.6
Country Example: Denmark (Energinet) Capability Requirements
Energinet defines clear percent-based reactive power and control obligations for inverter based
Power Park Modules (PPMs). The main quantitative requirements are as follows:
• U-Q capability at rated active power. At Pmax, the plant must provide a reactive capability
of ±33% of rated active power at the POI 5b 5a.
• Capability across the operating range. A complete P-Q envelope applies across all active
power levels. Reactive capability may scale when the plant is derated, but the unit must still
reach any admissible P-Q setpoint within the response times required by the TSO.

• Control-mode support. The plant must operate in voltage-control, Q-set, and PF-set modes.
Each mode must satisfy the specified performance: voltage reference within 95–105%,
droop between 2–7%, 90% response in under 1 s, settling in under 5 s, and steady-state
accuracy within ±5%.
• Fault-ride-through and reactive current injection. Reactive current injection must begin

2.2
Anticipated Evolution of Grid-Code Obligations (2030–2050)
Across Europe and other advanced power systems, the direction of travel for inverter based
resources is clear. Recent grid-code reviews and roadmaps (ENTSO-E, IRENA, NREL, ESIG,
NERC) point toward: tighter power-factor or Q/Pn bands at rated active power; explicit guaran-
tees of reactive capability at depressed voltages for weak-grid and system-restoration conditions;
formalisation of grid-supporting and grid-forming behaviour for a subset of converters (often linked
to VSC–HVDC and large PPMs); stronger and more structured fault-time reactive current injection
with controlled active-power recovery; and increasingly fast frequency and voltage support from
power-electronic interfaced devices.[18–21]
Several technical documents now propose that grid-forming capability be embedded in revised
connection network codes (RfG 2.0, NC HVDC 2.0) with dedicated performance and testing re-
quirements for PPMs. In parallel, market-oriented studies and TSO reports emphasise the need
to explicitly value reactive power in planning and ancillary-service markets, including the provision
of reactive power at or near zero active power and differentiated capability margins beyond the
minimum grid-code envelope.[22]Taken together, these developments imply that by 2030–2050,
reduced tolerance for “zero Q at low P” is likely, especially for large hybrid plants expected to
contribute to system strength, restoration, and black-start capabilities.[18, 23]
17
==PAGE:20==
For the purposes of long-term planning studies, it is therefore reasonable to treat the binding
case as one in which reactive capability must remain available down to low active-power levels,
and part of the converter current budget is expected to be reserved for fast grid-support services
(fault ride-through, inertia-like response, very fast volt/VAR control), even if current national codes
do not yet enforce the full spectrum of these behaviours.
2.2.1
Modelling Assumptions and Implications for Sizing and Scheduling
Within this thesis, the above trajectory is reflected in a simplified but explicit modelling framework:
1. POI-level envelopes as hard constraints. The plant is constrained by POI-level U–Q and
P–Q envelopes representative of the jurisdiction, e.g. Energinet requirements for PPMs with
Q/Pn = 0.33 at Pmax and associated inner envelopes at partial active power.[18, 24] Con-
verter current limits are propagated through transformers and collector/export impedances
so that compliance is checked at the POI rather than at device terminals.
2. Converter headroom: full utilisation in steady state (limitation). Conceptually, future
grid codes and GFM specifications assume that part of the converter current margin is re-
served for dynamic services (fault current, fast voltage support, frequency response).[19,
21, 25] In this thesis, however, the steady-state PQ capability at the POI is computed using
the full nameplate current of the converters, without an explicit headroom reservation for dy-
namic services. This choice simplifies the optimisation but overestimates the steady-state
PQ region and should be interpreted as a best-case steady-state capability. The missing
headroom reservation is highlighted as a modelling limitation rather than as a design recom-
mendation.
3. Zero-active-power compensation and parasitic Q. Cable charging and transformer mag-
netising reactive power are included explicitly, so that the requirement to meet a specified
reactive setpoint at P ≈0 (e.g. to hold POI voltage during low-generation or outage condi-
tions) is satisfied with realistic network-side Q flows.[18, 26]
These modelling choices have direct implications for sizing and scheduling. Sizing of inverter
MVA and any additional FACTS or STATCOM capacity is formulated as a constrained optimisation
over the POI envelopes: minimise CAPEX and losses while satisfying RfG/national P–Q and U–
Q constraints, voltage-droop targets, and energy-delivery objectives. Active-power scheduling,
including strategic curtailment, is treated as an explicit degree of freedom that trades energy yield
against compliance margins under binding current limits. In other words, curtailment is not an
afterthought but part of the design space: for a given network strength and PQ envelope, the
optimal combination of converter MVA rating, STATCOM size, and curtailment pattern determines
whether the plant can maintain its reactive obligations under both present and anticipated future
grid-code conditions.[22, 27, 28]

Literature Review: Reactive Power Sources and Shunt FACTS for Renewable-
Dominated Grids
Historically, the critical function of reactive power regulation within interconnected electrical grids
has been almost exclusively fulfilled by conventional sources.
These devices, primarily syn-
chronous generators and static equipment, operate as variable reactances at the point of com-
mon coupling, serving as the foundational means for voltage control. This traditional paradigm,
as illustrated in Fig. 34, is now undergoing a fundamental transformation. The escalating integra-
tion of inverter based resources is compelling a decisive shift from synchronous generator-backed
reactive power to static, power-electronic-based solutions [7].

2.3.1
Rotating Machines
Synchronous generators (SG)
provide continuous leading and lagging reactive power through
excitation-current control via an automatic voltage regulator (AVR). Their reactive capability does
not collapse with local voltage, enabling stable Q delivery even under depressed-voltage condi-
tions. Limitations arise primarily from stator current, rotor heating, and stability constraints. Al-
though their location is fixed at the generating site, their inherent voltage control and fault-current
contribution make them a reference technology for grid strength.
Synchronous condensers (SC)
are unloaded synchronous machines operated solely for reac-
tive power support, making their full MVA rating available for Q exchange. They offer continuous
and controllable reactive capability, inherent harmonic robustness, and short-circuit strength, and
they can be enhanced with flywheels to provide additional inertia attributes that are increasingly
valued in IBR-dominated systems. While many FACTS installations require external harmonic

filters that enlarge their spatial footprint [7], synchronous condensers typically avoid extensive
filtering, resulting in a more compact overall design. Repurposing decommissioned synchronous
generators as condensers is common practice and remains a cost-effective source of grid strength
during the energy transition.

Static equipment (non-rotating)
Mechanically Switched Capacitors/Reactors (MSC/MSR).
Low cost, simple shunt compen-

FACTS overview and generations.
Power electronic FACTS devices enhance the stability and
efficiency of electrical power systems through continuous and rapid control. They are categorized
into two generations:
• First Generation: Utilizes thyristor-based controls, such as Thyristor Controlled Series Capac-
itor (TCSC) and Static Var Compensator (SVC). These devices, while effective, offer slower
response times for managing power flow and reactive power compensation.
• Second Generation: Employs Voltage Source Converters (VSCs), including Static Synchronous
Series Compensator (SSSC), Static Synchronous Compensator (STATCOM), and Unified Power
Flow Controller (UPFC). VSCs provide faster and more flexible control over voltage and power
flow.
The connection type plays a vital role in determining functionality:
Shunt connected devices, such as STATCOM, regulate voltage and provide reactive power ( Q),
while series-connected devices like TCSC and SSSC manage power flow and reactance. Hy-
brid devices combine both shunt and series functionalities, allowing for comprehensive control.
Together, these advancements in FACTS technology significantly improve the performance and
reliability of modern power systems [29].

2.3.5
Implications for Renewable Hubs and Future Status of Conventional VAR Sources
At the point of interconnection for wind, PV, and BESS plants, shunt Flexible AC Transmission
System (FACTS) devices remain critical sources of reactive power. Static Synchronous Compen-
sators (STATCOMs) provide a near constant current characteristic, which substantially enlarges
the voltage reactive power (U–Q) capability particularly at low voltages where grid codes increas-
ingly require dependable reactive support. In contrast, Static VAR Compensators (SVCs) exhibit
a strong voltage dependence, with reactive current output collapsing rapidly below roughly 0.8 pu.
Their performance often requires additional filtering and oversizing, reducing their effectiveness in
weak grid and low voltage conditions. These differences directly affect compliance margins and
curtailment strategy as conventional synchronous generators retire and system strength declines.
Emerging converter based technologies, including storage converters (SCs) and hybrid inertial
storage converters (HISCs) equipped with flywheels, aim to replace the stability attributes histor-
ically supplied by synchronous generators. By combining fast reactive power capability with syn-
thetic or physical inertia, these devices contribute to voltage stability, fault support, and frequency
resilience in IBR dominated grids.
While legacy devices such as mechanically switched capacitors (MSCs) and reactors (MSRs)
remain cost effective for bulk reactive compensation, the system wide shift toward rapid dynamic
support is accelerating the adoption of STATCOMs at renewable nodes. Hybrid architectures that
integrate STATCOMs, flywheel based SCs, and BESS converters are increasingly viewed as es-
sential for maintaining grid reliability [7]. In this thesis, the comparative roles of SVCs, STATCOMs,
and internal inverter based resources (IBRs) are evaluated with respect to voltage regulation and
reactive power compliance at the POI.

Methodology
3.1
System Modeling and Reactive Power Capability Formulation
This section describes the modelling approach used to represent the reactive power capability
of the hybrid power plant. The formulation builds on the methodologies of Saktar [5] and Ullah
[6], who emulate synchronous generator voltage and current limits to derive aggregate reactive
capability at the point of interconnection. In this thesis, the same principles are extended to a multi
technology plant comprising wind, PV, BESS, and FACTS devices, allowing device level current
and voltage constraints to be translated into a unified POI level reactive power envelope.
The theoretical foundation for modeling the reactive power capability of aggregated inverter based
resources (wind, PV, and BESS) stems from the methodology of Ullah [6] and the refinement by
Saktar [5] for large wind power plant (WPP) collector systems. [6] first introduced the converter
voltage limited capability model by analogy with the field current limitation of a synchronous gener-
ator, representing the converter as a controllable voltage source behind an impedance. However,
the original formulation neglected resistive effects and only considered the upper converter voltage
constraint.
[5] extended this approach by including both resistance (RWT ) and reactance (XWT ) in the equiv-
alent impedance between the converter and the collector bus and by accounting for both maximum
and minimum converter voltage limits. This enhancement allows a more realistic description of re-
active power behavior under variable voltage levels.
Figure 8: On left Initial network and on right aggregated network
The same physical principles extend to PV and BESS converters, as developed in [32, 33]; how-
ever, these methods are generally applied to a single technology at a time and do not capture

interactions among multiple converter based sources. The present work generalizes these formu-
lations to a unified hybrid power plant (HPP) framework that jointly represents wind, PV, BESS,
and STATCOM components within a single reactive power capability model.
As illustrated in Figure 8, the methodology aggregates all generating units into an equivalent
source and represents the collector system by an equivalent line, with filtering elements neglected.
The following sections detail the formulation and modeling steps used to derive the POI-level
reactive power capability of the hybrid plant.

3.1.6
Critical Assessment and Relevance
Sarkar’s formulation provides a robust analytical expression for voltage limited reactive capability,
capturing resistive reactive coupling ignored in the Ullah model. However, it remains specific to
wind turbine converters. Because PV and BESS interfaces share identical circuit topologies, the
same derivation holds. The innovation in this thesis is the extension of the Ullah and Sarkar frame-
work to an integrated hybrid power plant (HPP) encompassing wind, PV, BESS, and STATCOM.

3.2.1
Tool Overview
HyDesign is an open source Python framework developed at DTU for the optimal sizing of hybrid
power plants through a nested optimisation architecture [35]. An outer loop explores the design

of charge constraints. To reduce the number of expensive model evaluations, HyDesign employs
a Gaussian process surrogate with parallel Efficient Global Optimisation (EGO) [35].

3.2.4
Limitations of the Baseline Implementation
• Wake modelling. The wake module employs a simplified circular-layout surrogate; it does
not capture site-specific aerodynamics, complex terrain effects, or offshore blockage phe-
nomena.
• Absence of electrical modelling. The network is represented only through economic pa-
rameters (e.g., per-MW connection and export costs), with no explicit electrical description of
the collector system or export infrastructure. This means distance to shore, cable impedance
are not captured. Likewise, the tool has no internal modelling of voltage, or reactive power
behaviour, treating all devices purely as energy converters rather than voltage supporting as-
sets. As a result, it cannot assess grid code compliance (e.g. P–Q envelopes, U -Q curves),
nor evaluate how inverter or STATCOM sizing affects reactive power capability at the POI
an increasingly critical limitation as hybrid plants transition from passive generators to active
grid-supporting resources.
Such simplifications are common in pre-feasibility tools (e.g., SAM [36], PVsyst [37]), which focus
primarily on energy yield and financial indicators. However, with the rapid expansion of inverter
based resources (IBRs), project viability is now constrained as much by electrical performance re-
active power provision, voltage control, fault recovery, and black-start capability as by economics.
For example, by 2025 the Dominican Republic requires all hybrid projects above 20MW AC to in-
clude at least 50% BESS capacity with a four hour energy duration and to demonstrate grid support
functions to qualify for a power purchase agreement (PPA). This regulatory evolution highlights the
growing need for feasibility stage tools to embed electrical performance evaluation from the outset.
3.2.5
Extension of the HyDesign Framework
This thesis extends the HyDesign methodology by adding an electrical module that models the
steady state network and its reactive power and voltage capabilities at both device and plant levels.
The new layer evaluates P–Q performance across all nodes and at the point of interconnection
(POI), enabling assessment of grid code compliance. Only steady state conditions are considered,
focusing on P–Q capability rather than dynamic or transient behaviour.
A representative-day simulation is introduced to accelerate computation while preserving the
temporal variability of dispatch. Electrical elements (cables, transformers, STATCOMs, etc.) are
included in CAPEX through parametric cost functions dependent on distance to shore and sys-
tem rating, allowing tradeoffs between reactive power capability and investment cost.
To balance accuracy and speed, an aggregated network model based on the Mulaju equivalenc-
ing method replaces the fullscale layout with an equivalent impedance representation, maintaining
the key electrical behaviour at reduced computational cost (Fig. 7).
In summary: The extended framework integrates an electrical capability layer that com-
putes and enforces device and system level P–Q envelopes (WT, PV, BESS, STATCOM/SVC)
through the network to the POI within the sizing optimisation.

3.4
EMS Optimization (Inner Loop, for each sizing x)
This section explains how the electrical calculations are integrated with the EMS within a sequen-
tial optimisation framework. First, the EMS determines the optimal active power schedule by jointly
solving for battery dispatch and curtailment. These EMS outputs are then passed to the electrical
model, which evaluates the corresponding reactive power capability at the POI. In other words, ac-
tive power scheduling is performed first, and the resulting operating conditions are subsequently
used to compute the feasible reactive power envelope of the hybrid plant.
Network Definition and Power Flow Framework
The aggregated hybrid power-plant network is implemented in pandapower, and all AC power flow
(PF) calculations in this work (base PF, device capability checks, and the +Q/Q certification runs)
use the Newton Raphson solver in pandapower. The topology follows the schematic in Figure 15: a
400 kV transmission connection, a 220 kV PCC, an offshore 220/66 kV OSS transformer and 66 kV
wind collector, and an onshore 220/33 kV transformer feeding 33 kV PV and BESS collectors.
Wind, PV and BESS are modeled as PQ generators (sgen); FACTS are represented either as a
STATCOM with controllable QS ∈[−SFACTS, +SFACTS] or as an SVC modeled as a fixed shunt
QS(U) = U2Bsh. Collector systems are captured with single equivalent lines sized by thermal
feeder count rules (Appendix A). This aggregation balances fidelity (correct Thevenin response
and U dependent reactive behaviour) with speed to support large parametric studies.

EMS–PF Interaction Framework (linked to Figures 16–17 and Algorithm 2)
Figures 16 and 17 illustrate how the Energy Management System (EMS) and the electrical power-
flow (PF) model operate together in a sequential loop. The EMS determines the active power
operating point of the hybrid plant (wind, PV, BESS), and the PF model certifies whether this

At each timestep t, the EMS first proposes active-power setpoints for wind, PV, and BESS after

be met. The plant-wide reactive power is the sum of all device contributions,
QTOT(t) = QWT (t) + QPV (t) + QB(t) + QS(t),
where QS is provided by the STATCOM/SVC if present.

The first task is to load the EMS-proposed
active powers and clamp them within device ratings. A base AC power flow with Q = 0 is run to
reveal the natural network Q (mainly cable charging) and to initialise voltage previews at all buses.
Figure 17 shows this step as the “PF init” block.

Given the initial POI active power and voltage, the grid
PQ/UQ envelope (from the selected grid code) is evaluated. This defines the maximum reactive
injection and absorption the grid will accept at that operating point. These limits are denoted in
Figure 17 as targets T+ (max injection) and T−(max absorption). The EMS -PF loop must ensure
the plant can reach both limits.
Step 2.3: Shared-curtailment logic.
At this stage, the PF engine checks whether the current
active power levels allow devices to collectively produce the Q needed at the voltage corners.
Each converter has a Q(P) capability derived from two constraints:
• the current/VA ellipse, representing internal inverter limits;
• the receiving-end circle, derived from the Thevenin impedance between the device and the
POI.
The admissible Q for each device is the intersection of these two shapes. If the sum of device
capabilities does not meet the grid targets T+ or T−, one shared active power update is computed.
This update reduces P in a fixed order (PV→BESS→WT) so that both the positive-Q and negative-
Q requirements move closer to feasibility. The logic is shown in the central block of Figure 17.
Step 2.4: Device reactive limits at the curtailed P.
After shared curtailment, the PF model
recalculates each device’s reactive power bounds at the new operating point. This includes new
ellipse values, updated voltage-corner limits, and the STATCOM/SVC contribution (constant-Q for
STATCOM, U2Bsh for an SVC). This block appears on the right side of Figure 17.
36
==PAGE:39==
Step 2.5: Two certification PFs (positive-Q and negative-Q).
To certify the timestep, two AC
PFs are performed:
(+Q) : devices at Qmax,
(−Q) : devices at Qmin.
The STATCOM is tested at ±SFACTS, while the SVC uses the fixed shunt value.
These PFs
determine whether the POI can reach both boundaries of the grid envelope with the current P and
voltage previews.
Step 2.6: Acceptance and state update.
If both PF results satisfy the grid PQ/UQ envelope
(within tolerance), the timestep is accepted. The voltage previews (Vmin, Vmax) are updated to the
PF voltages for (−Q) and (+Q) respectively, ensuring that the next timestep begins with realistic
voltage corners. If either PF fails, one corrective shared-curtailment pass is performed, and steps
2.4 -2.5 are repeated.

in pseudocode. Together, these components describe how active power scheduling from the EMS

all PQ/UQ requirements for the given grid code more details in Appendix A.3.

3.6
Representative Days: Method and Rationale
To further reduce runtime while preserving EMS–PF fidelity, we adopt a representative days ap-
proach inspired by Carpe Diem [39]. The idea is to replace the full set of Ndays with k ≪Ndays
medoid days and associated integer weights. Because medoids are real calendar days (not syn-
thetic centroids), they preserve feasibility, extreme behaviour, and realistic intraday shapes all
essential for PQ-capability verification.
Literature basis.
Carpe Diem [39] introduced reproducible feature based clustering for long-
term power system modelling. Follow up work on clustering for energy systems emphasises two
consistent insights: (i) extreme days (peaks, lulls, ramps, high curtailment) must be preserved
explicitly; (ii) synthetic clusters (e.g. k-means centroids) distort operational feasibility unless linking
constraints or chronological reconstruction are added.
Why k-medoids instead of k-means.
Figure 18 highlights the key difference. k-means creates
synthetic average days. These smooth out NET extremes, distort WT/PV/BESS interactions, and
produce non physical curtailment levels, making them unsuitable for PQ-capability assessment. In
contrast, k-medoids selects actual observed days, which is crucial because:
• PQ capability depends on extreme NET trajectories, not averages.
• Curtailment has direct economic consequences; synthetic centroids mis estimate it.
• Device P–Q limits are nonlinear; averaged inputs destroy the true feasible geometry.
Therefore, only k-medoids preserves the operational realism required for EMS–PF validation and
sizing.
Figure 18: k-means (left) produces synthetic centroids; k-medoids (right) selects real calendar
days, preserving extremes [40].
Method: seeded extremes + k-medoids.
The procedure is designed to keep the days that
matter most for voltages, PQ limits, and curtailment:

Fd = [WTd, PVd, CURTd, NETd, harmonics],

3. Apply k-medoids. If the number of seeded extremes is larger than k, we down select them;
if smaller, we add additional medoids by clustering all days.

⇒smaller feasible Q band, especially at higher P where the current ellipse already bites.

If the converter is supplied with a narrow voltage window (e.g. [0.90, 1.10] pu), exter-

capacity. Both approaches should be validated in the EMS PF loop, since the binding mechanism
(voltage vs. current limitation) determines which euro delivers the most effective MVAr at the POI.
Rule of thumb. A narrow Vc window makes STATCOMs the cost effective source of MVAr at the
POI. A wide Vc window often enables a cheaper solution through modest inverter oversizing.
Always verify against the site Thevenin equivalent and the applicable grid PQ envelope.

Level 2 : Transmission Level
Reactive power capability at the POI is not solely determined by inverter limits. Once power is
exported through long HVAC submarine cables, the transmission system itself begins to dominate
the voltage and reactive power behaviour. Even when the device level capabilities are generous
(wide converter voltage window and modest oversizing), the POI capability may shrink or shift if
the export path imposes significant reactive flows.
Setup.
To isolate the effect of distance, the plant side characteristics are kept constant across all
cases: a wide converter window Vc ∈[0.80, 1.20] pu and an apparent power ratio Sinv/Prated ≈1.06
(312 MW wind, 330 MVA inverter). The transmission system is a single HVAC cable connecting
the offshore platform to an onshore substation and finally to the POI. Only the cable length L is
varied, with four representative values: 0.1km, 20km, 50km, and 100km.
For each distance, we compute:
1. the P-Q capability at the POI compared against the Danish envelope,
2. the mapping between device capability and POI capability (device →POI transformation),
3. the min/max voltage evolution at key buses (offshore and onshore 220 kV, PCC, and POI).

1. Translation at low P. Increasing L increases V 2BcL/2; the system arrives “pre charged.”

3. Shift of the limiting mode. For short L the limit is device current (QI). As L increases, the

Design implications (without external compensation).
• For L≲1km, POI ≈device; sizing at device level is representative.
• Around L∼20km, expect noticeable ∆Q between device and POI: check Qmin at low P (charg-
ing) and Qmax near Pmax (series drop).
• Beyond L ≳50km, compliance becomes conditional on dispatch: the admissible POI band is
both shifted (up) and narrowed. Reinforcement (electrical design choices) becomes a system
question, not a device question.

even when device limits are unchanged and generous. Here appear the importance of sizing
inverters and facts devices.

A preliminary comparison of device characteristics clarifies this transition. STATCOMs deliver
(almost) constant current over the voltage range, whereas SVCs have a Q output proportional to
V 2, becoming ineffective at low voltages and excessively strong at high voltages. Therefore:
At short distances (below roughly 30 km), voltage constraints remain secondary. SVCs operate
poorly because of their voltage squared characteristic, STATCOMs are typically too expensive to
justify, and converter oversizing still provides meaningful reactive flexibility.
At intermediate distances (around 30-40 km), cable charging becomes the dominant effect. Volt-
age, not current, sets the export limit. In this regime, additional converter oversizing offers almost
no usable Q range, and dedicated FACTS devices become the only robust means of satisfying the
POI P-Q and voltage requirements.
At long distances (beyond 40-50 km), the export system shifts into a naturally high voltage regime.
SVCs become technically effective because their Q ∝V 2 characteristic can be fully utilised, while
STATCOMs retain superior control during high power, low voltage operating points.
Overall, increasing export distance progressively shifts the limiting mechanism from converter
current to cable voltage: beyond a certain length, almost the entire POI P–Q envelope is defined
by voltage constraints rather than by the device’s current limit,the use of dedicated FACTS devices
is no longer optional.

Comparison frameworks for reactive capability
Two complementary frameworks are used to compare inverter oversizing and STATCOM capacity.
They answer different system design questions: the constant Q framework addresses feasibility
for a fixed reactive requirement, while the constant S framework addresses economic optimality
for a fixed apparent power budget.
(i) Constant Q framework (feasibility question). A target reactive capability Qtot at Prated is
fixed and split between inverter and STATCOM via a share finv ∈[0, 1],
Qinv = finvQtot,
QSTATCOM = (1 −finv)Qtot.
The corresponding apparent powers follow from PQ geometry. Because Sinv + SSTATCOM varies
with finv, the oversizing factor k changes. This framework answers the question: “What total
MVA is required to achieve a given Qtot at rated power?” It identifies feasible inverter/STATCOM
combinations but does not yet provide a fair CAPEX comparison.
(ii) Constant S framework (economic question). The total apparent power budget is fixed at
Sopt = 330MV A so that k = ktarget is constant. A sharing parameter f ∈[0, 1] redistributes this
capacity between inverter and STATCOM:
Sinv(f) = Prated + f (Sopt −Prated),
SSTATCOM(f) = Sopt −Sinv(f).

Conclusion
6.1
Discussion and Conclusions
This thesis sought to address four distinct research questions:
• Q1: How grid code requirements for reactive power and voltage support are evolving towards
2030-2050.
• Q2: Which electrical subsystems dominate grid compliance and project economics at the pre-
feasibility stage.
• Q3: How an optimisation framework can jointly handle active power scheduling and reactive
power compliance, with the implementation carried out within the DTU HyDesign tool.
• Q4: What techno economic consequences arise when electrical subsystem sizing is incorpo-
rated explicitly in early stage project development.
Inverter oversizing vs. STATCOM: core findings.
Across all experiments, inverte oversizing
proves systematically more efficient than STATCOM capacity whenever the plant operates in a
current limited regime. Inverter heavy designs reduce LCOVAR by 30-60%, cut CAPEX per MW by
nearly an order of magnitude, and reduce CAPEX per MVAr from ∼80 kC/MVAr to ∼8 kC/MVAr.
These results agree with external literature: PV STATCOM studies report effective VAR costs
around 20 $/kVAr, while standalone STATCOMs lie in the 77-290 $/kVAr range. The message
is clear when current is the binding constraint, VARs delivered through inverter headroom are
dramatically cheaper.
Distance and grid strength qualify the conclusion.
The cable distance analysis shows that
the POI P-Q envelope departs from the device boundary as export length increases. For short
routes (L ≲1 km), POI device and inverter vs. STATCOM economics can be evaluated directly at
converter terminals. Around ∼20 km, shunt charging and series reactance distort Qmin and Qmax
respectively; at ∼50-100 km, the POI envelope becomes voltage limited rather than current limited.
In this regime, additional inverter MVA cannot recover missing inductive margin the export cable
voltage profile becomes the bottleneck, making STATCOMs (or equivalent shunt compensation)
structurally necessary.
Weak grid conditions strengthen this effect: low SCR increases voltage sensitivity to reactive
flows and compresses the admissible POI envelope. Therefore, the final conclusion is nuanced:
• Short/moderate cables & strong grids: inverter oversizing dominates (current limited regime).
• Long cables & weak grids: STATCOMs become unavoidable (voltage limited regime).
Link to the research questions.
• Q1 : Grid code evolution. The results confirm that future 2030-2050 envelopes will tighten
power factor and Q/P requirements, especially at low P and low voltage. Export systems
and converter voltage windows must therefore be designed explicitly for VAR duties, not simply
energy delivery.
70
==PAGE:73==
• Q2 : Influential electrical subsystems. Converter MVA and STATCOM capacity are the dom-
inant levers for steady state compliance and cost. Export cable length and grid strength de-
termine whether these levers actually translate to POI capability; transformers and collector
topology matter mainly through their effect on the Thevenin equivalent seen by the plant.
• Q3 : Extended optimisation framework / HyDesign. Building on the optimisation methodol-
ogy laid down by Ullah and Saktar, this thesis extends their framework from single technology
renewable plants to full hybrid power plants (HPPs). The EMS PF loop developed here provides
DTU HyDesign with a unified mechanism to cooptimise active power scheduling, inverter head-
room, STATCOM rating, and POI level reactive power compliance within a single algorithmic
structure.
• Q4 : Techno economic impacts. LCOVAR and CAPEX results show that ignoring VAR sizing
at pre feasibility can lead to misallocations of tens of kC/MW and order of magnitude deviations
in VAR cost, especially if STATCOMs are overspecified in current limited settings or underspec-
ified in voltage limited, long cable scenarios.
6.2
Limitations
Several methodological limitations must be kept in mind when interpreting these results:
• Single lifetime ratings and no explicit ageing. Converter and STATCOM ratings are treated
as fixed over the entire project lifetime. Studies by Gandhi et al. and Brito et al. [41, 42] show
that operating inverters close to reactive limits accelerates ageing and can shorten lifetime. The
LCOVAR of inverter heavy designs is therefore slightly optimistic.
• Purely steady state analysis. The work considers static P Q envelopes and voltage magni-
tudes only. Dynamic behaviour (fault ride through, control bandwidth, oscillatory modes, sub-
synchronous interactions) is not represented, even though these are precisely the conditions
where STATCOMs often outperform inverter only solutions.
• Simplified economics and flexibility value. The economic model does not include the sys-
tem level value of flexibility such as converter/STATCOM reconfiguration for future grid code
updates, repowering, or ancillary service markets. Maintenance, failure rates, and reinvestment
cycles for FACTS and converters are also not internalised.
• Pre feasibility scope. The framework intentionally operates at a screening level. Substation
layout, harmonic filters, protection systems, and OEM specific control implementations are ag-
gregated into simplified parameters.
• Simulation cost. Even with representative days and a fixed admittance matrix, each EMS-PF
evaluation takes roughly three minutes. A typical sizing run with 60-150 evaluations therefore
requires 3-4 hours, depending on the dimensionality of the design vector.
• Network modelling. The study considers only HVAC AC export systems. For distances be-
yond ∼80 km, HVDC becomes the dominant option because of its predictable reactive power
behaviour and lower voltage sensitivity.

6.3
Future Work
These limitations point directly to several avenues for future development. The most promising
directions are:
• Lifetime aware LCOVAR. Integrate thermal and lifetime models for converters and STATCOMs
so that the cost of operating near reactive limits, including temperature cycling and degradation,
is explicitly monetised.
• Dynamic and grid forming studies. Extend from static envelopes to time domain simulations
including FRT, voltage and frequency control, grid forming behaviour, and weak grid oscillations.
Reactive power weighting or dynamic constraints could also be embedded directly into the
optimiser.
• Upgrade HyDesign. Extend the DTU HyDesign optimiser so that inverter headroom, STAT-
COM rating, export cable design, and curtailment strategies are cooptimised within a single
EMS-PF loop rather than treated sequentially. This includes embedding POI reactive power
envelopes directly as constraints.
• Alternative power flow solvers. Since this work uses the Newton Raphson method in pan-
dapower, future pre-feasibility versions could adopt linearised or hybrid PF solvers to accelerate
the optimisation.
• System level interactions and markets. Extend the analysis from a single plant to multi-
ple hybrid plants, transmission upgrades, and reactive power markets to determine when VAR
investment should occur at plant level versus grid level.
