Abstract
During this thesis, literature concerning different elements of a SLM system was reviewed. The
laser, powder properties and the melting process proved important factors when enabling a reliable
SLM process. The laser used was a ﬁbre laser capable of delivering 250 W in pulsed or CW mode.
The scanner system was controlled by a RUMBA micro controller. Two experimental scanner
systems for powder bed fusion was developed and tested. Using stepper motors to manoeuvre
the beam around proved to be inadequate, as the resolution of the stepper motors were to low,
and micro stepping was inaccurate. Furthermore the stepper motors induced vibrations to the
hatching disturbing the scan path. Second option was developed using galvanometers as rotary
elements. This showed promising treats, and displayed homogeneous and accurate scan paths.
Alongside the development of the scanner systems, a software platform was developed. A slicer
program was created in matlab, enabling easy job planning through a graphical user interface. The
graphical user interface allows loading custom CAD geometries saved in binary STL format, and
controlling the slicing, laser mode and scan parameters. The job ﬁle created by the slicer should be
transmitted to the micro controller through a custom gcode sender written in python. The gcode
sender was developed with a graphical user interface, allowing communicating both entire job ﬁles
and single line commands. Melting of EOS MS1 powder was done at different scan parameters,
showing that complex geometries can be constructed through the experimental SLM scanning
platform.

1 Introduction
Additive manufacturing has become a preferred manufacturing technique for prototypes and complex
geometries, as the additive approach has potential of producing near ﬁnal parts. The additive process
is prominent within hobby manufacturing to produce low strength plastic parts, but is also applicable
to high strength metal parts. Selective Laser Melting (SLM) allows the production of high strength parts
and inclusion of complex features, not possible through standard tooling processes. To experiment
and test current theory within metal laser processing of metal powder, an open and customizable SLM
scanner platform is developed and constructed as the prime focus of this dissertation. The platform
seeks to fully support and enable the laser driven process of selectively fusing metal powder together
layer by layer, covering the process from computer generated model to ﬁnal product.
SLM is a multi physical discipline as several scientiﬁc ﬁelds has to intertwine in order to produce a
stable and reliable production process. A principal drawing of the SLM machine is seen in Figure 1.1.
The powder delivery system consists of a recoater that moves the powder into the build chamber
and onto the powder bed. The powder bed is adjustable in height, and is lowered between each
layer to allow a layer-wise recoating. The entire powder depositioning system is kept in a controlled
atmosphere to avoid oxidation during melting. A scan window is added to the powder depositioning
cabinet to allow the laser beam to enter and fuse the metal powder. The laser is aimed into a scanner
system. The scanner system takes care of moving the laser beam, and thereby selectively melt powder
particles. The manufactured part increases in size, layer by layer, until it has reached its full height. The
untreated powder in the build chamber serves as support for the melted particles, and therefore allows
structures not necessarily self-supporting. The focus of this project has been the scanner system.

1. INTRODUCTION
At the core of the rig, a laser emitting a high power energy ﬁeld, has to be carefully controlled to
selectively fuse ﬁne grained metal powder into solid parts. The energy source is focused through optical
elements allowing very localised heat treatment. The density of the end product is highly dependent on
the powder distribution, scanning speed of the laser source, laser power and metallurgical properties
of the build material.
SLM is applied in a great variety of ﬁelds, taking advantage of the increased complexity offered by
the process. The features added could be in molds for injection moulding [1, 2], where conformal
cooling introduced through powder bed fusion techniques. Introducing conformal cooling offers
signiﬁcant advantages as lower cycling times, yielding a higher production rate and end products
suffering from far less shrinkage defects. Furthermore the amount of necessary cooling lubricant is
decreased. Likewise cutting tools beniﬁts from internal cooling channels that would not be possible to
produce with standard subtractive tooling [3].
Selective Laser Sintering (SLS) was introduced in the late 1980s [4]. The use of a computer controlled
platform to selectively sintre the powder particles has since become a common industrial process.
The success of this production method as a rapid prototyping (RP) and rapid manufacturing (RM)
technology is attributed to the possibility of fusing almost any kind of metal powder. To produce
functional prototypes with adequate mechanical properties the sintered parts had to undergo density
and strength increasing post heat treatments. The change into SLM where the particles are instead fully
molten, allows the production of near full density parts without the need for additional post-treatment
[5].
The use of SLM in production is increasing, and the supply of machinery is equally on the rise. State
of the art metal powder bed fusion solutions is provided by companies as RealiZer, EOS and SLM
solutions[6]. Companies that sells SLM machines capable of using several different materials; pure
titanium, titanium alloys, tool steels, stainless steels, aluminium alloys, cobalt-chrome and super-
alloys. To be able to work with metal powders in molten state the systems provide an inert gas
atmosphere. Producers promise a density of 99 - 100 % of the ﬁnished parts, and a ﬁnal composition
of the parts similar to the powder used. The SLM process is capable of producing parts with a detail
level of < 0.1 mm, and a accuracy of ±0.05 mm over 100 mm, surface roughness being < 10 µm. SLM
Soluthons GmbH produces different models of metal powder bed fusion systems. These systems work
with several different metal powders, and range from having two to four lasers installed on independent
scan systems, providing ﬂexibility and high production rate. High power lasers ranging from 400-watt
to 1000-watt is utilized. As the lasers is controlled through independent scanning systems the build
speed is able reach productions rates of 105 mm3 per hour.
The immediate challenge met by the ready made machinery is that their knowledge base is accessible
only to the manufacturers, hiding the underlying assumptions and considerations from the operator.
The operator has access to very basic settings, and the rest has been predetermined by the companies
producing the SLM machines. As the companies often provide both the material and the hardware the
process can be controlled in this way. The operator can change parameters as layer height, scan speed
and choose between preplanned scan strategies. Optimization of the parameters is already done, and
correlates with the brand build material. This gives a rather limited operator freedom, but also a stable
and reliable process that can be introduced into production, [7].
As the core goal of this thesis is to understand and challenge the SLM scanner system. An industry SLM
machine will not do the job, as the experimental rig needs customizability and even more important it
has to be fully controllable. How should an open platform be constructed, and what considerations has
to be done?

1.1.1
Energy Source
To selectively fuse metal particles together, a focused heat source is required. As the name of the
technology gives away, the energy source is a laser. The acronym laser carries the meaning; "light
ampliﬁcation by stimulated emission of radiation". In simple terms the process works by sending
light into a cavity, where the light is bounced back and forth through a doped medium between two
mirrors, one of them being partial reﬂective, see Figure 1.3, this is called a resonant cavity. The incident
radiation from the light source then stimulates the emission of photons from atoms in exited state.
The emitted photon will always be in-phase, share polarization and propagate in the same direction
as the stimulating radiation. To ensure that most of the radiation does not exit trough the sides of
the medium, the cavity contains a cladding with another refractive index than the core medium. The
mirrors bouncing the photons back and forth will lead to ampliﬁcation as every pass through the
medium will increase the photon density. The emission used in the SLM machine passes through the
partial mirror, as this has been carefully designed to only allow a predetermined fraction of light to
pass. The follwing theoretical discripitions is primarly based on educational text books concering
optics [8] and [9].

1.1.2
Fusing Process
This section will examine the process of melting and solidiﬁcation of metal. The state of the fusing
process is treated as comparable to fusion track welding, and will therefore also share some of the
same challenges.
During the fusing process a localized heat source is moved along a path, and the heat propagation into
the material causes the metal to melt and solidify relatively fast, Vs, as the temperature gradient, G, is
steep. In welding the term Heat Affected Zones (HAZ) is widely used as the local area inﬂuenced by the
heat source, in SLM this will be the case for the entire part. The microstructure is controlled by the
metal composition and the amount of heat (and subsequent cooling) that goes into each weld line.
Defects typically seen are being de-lamination, cracking, embrittlement and others.[13]
In the HAZ the metal is heated to its melting temperature, during this temperature the microstructure
is changed into austenite, a FCC structure, and the grains grow in size. During cooling the austenite
transforms to martensite, bainite, ferrite + pearlite or mixtures of these, what is formed depends on the
cooling rate. As the temperature gradient is steep, the cooling happens fast, and therefore transforms
7
==PAGE:28==
1. INTRODUCTION
the austenite into martensite. Martensite is observed as a needle like structure with retained austenite,
and is a metastable BCT structure. The tranformation is a diffusionless process, and happens due to
quench-like cooling happening almost instantanious and therfore prevents carbon fusion [14].
As SLM is several consequent lines, the HAZ from one line inﬂuences the already melted powder.
The reheating of the material causes the neighbouring micro structure to change, some is reﬁned
other grow in size. To achieve a fusing process that is capable of producing the required properties
the possible defects has to be controlled. Oxidation of the high temperature metal is controlled by
introducing an inert gas into the build chamber. [13]
Hydrogen cracking is the most likely cause of metallurical problems when welding ferritic steels. This
fault occur due to the hydrogen that embrittles ferritic steel. As martensite is typically brittle it is very
prone to suffer from hydrogen cracks, the presence of hydrogen in the metal powder has to be carefully
controlled.[13]
Solidiﬁcation cracking occurs as the material contracts when changing phase from liquid to solid state.
If the heat treated part is fully ﬁxed onto the build plate and the build plate is rigid the solidiﬁcation
might cause cracking. In SLM this will show as residual stress, and possibly distort the ﬁnal geometry of
the part. The challenge of residual stresses introduced by solidiﬁcation is treated by several authors in
literature. As this has shown to be highly dependent on the scan strategy subsection 1.1.4 is dedicated
to the predictability through simulation and effects of the the residual stresses.
Describing the process analytically and numerically provides a great tool for simulating the process.
To ﬁnd the energy necessary for fusing a speciﬁc powder particle can be found by an analytical
ray-tracing model, suggested in [15], this allows the calculation of the dimensions of the sintered
zones.
Em = (cp ·∆Tm +Lm)·ρ ·Vp
(1.8)
Where cp is the speciﬁc heat capacity of the powder , ∆Tm is the temperature change needed to bring
the particle into molten state, and Lm describes the latent energy contribution from the phase change,
ρ is density and Vp is the spherical volume of the particle heated. Em is energy required to melt the
particle, and should be compared to the energy absorbed by the powder.
[16] concerns the multi physical simulation of selective laser melting, and predicts a long melt pool
is present when treating steel powders, and therefore concludes that input power is the dominant
process parameter.
1.1.3
Powder Proprieties
Metal powder particles in manufacturing has been known for a long time, and have given name to an
entire ﬁeld called Powder Metallurgy (PM). This term covers all cases where parts or products is made
from metal powder. From Powder Forging and hot isostatic pressing through metal injection moulding
and AM. Different processes requires different powders, the geometrical traits and composition of the
powder particles should enable the desired process.
One of the challenges faced when producing parts through powder bed fusion is to achieve a high
density, most manufacturers strive to deliver systems providing ﬁnished parts with near fully dense
8
==PAGE:29==
1.1. Literature Review
parts, and mechanical properies close to the bulk material. This can be achieved by carefully controlling
both the build parameters; scan speed, laser power and hatch distance, but equally important is the
powder used. [17].
The preferred particle shape for SLM is spherical, these are produced with a gas atomizer, a process

The main objective of this research was to ﬁgure out what consequence changing the scan speed has.
To track the state of the the micro structure both LOM, SEM and XRD is used, furthermore hardness
measurements was done on cross sections.
The preparation procedure and tests of the cross sectional samples is done in the following steps:
• Polished to 1 µm
• Microhardness tests was done with a load of 100 g

1.1.4
Scan Strategies
The primary beneﬁt of SLM is that the heat source can be selectively moved round the build plate. But
to do so, the consequences of different scan strategies must be considered.
One of the challenges faced when choosing scan strategy is to avoid that the treated part warps
during solidiﬁcation. The warpage of the part is caused by a build up of residual stresses. The steep
temperature gradient causes the upper layer to expand, expressed as thermal strain ϵth. As the
substrate and solidiﬁed material constrains the expansion, the elastic and plastic strain will result in a
compressive state. Causing the part to bend towards the heat source, see Figure 1.11. During cooling
the previously plastically compressed upper layer becomes shorter and therefore caused a warpage
away from the heat source [5].
Figure 1.11: Temperature gradient mechanism during heating and cooling[5]
Figure 1.12: Different scan strategies tested in [5]
The inﬂuence of the scan strategy is described in several studies. Kruth et al.[5] experimentally test
the inﬂuence of six different scan strategies, see Figure 1.12, and found that the curvature of the ﬁnal
product is highly affected by the scan strategy applied. The average curvature along X and Y was
measured, see Figure 1.13. From here it becomes obvious that using parallel lines throughout the
scan (scan strategy 1 and 2), causes the lowest curvature in the scan direction, but the highest in the
perpendicular direction. Whereas using a scan strategy where the entire scan is divided into smaller
areas, and then altering the scan direction in each neighbouring area, causes an overall smaller and
more uniform deﬂection in both x and y.

2.1.1
Mechanical System
Basically the scanner system has to facilitate accurate relocation of the focused beam, relative to the
powder bed. This can be solved in different ways, two options is listed below:
• Fixed Beam and Movable Powder Bed
• Manoeuvrable Beam and Fixed Powder Bed
The ﬁrst option is displayed in Figure 2.2(a). In this setup the laser is ﬁrmly mounted and the beam
path enters the focusing lens at a ﬁxed angle, the powder bed is then moved along the x and y direction
to ensure a build envelope of the desired dimensions. Having a ﬁxed laser path is great for controlling
the homogeneity and incident angle of the beam as it targets the powder bed. Moving the bed on the
other hand provides limitations to the manoeuvrability of scan pattern. The scan velocity is limited, as
moving the entire mass of the powder bed at high speed will vibrate and shift the position of the raw
metal powder.
Second option, Figure 2.2(b), eliminates the need for moving the large mass of the powder bed. The
only movable parts in this setup is the scan mirrors, allowing a larger range of applicable scan velocities.
The challenge in this setup is that the incident angle is changed continuously and therefore distortion
of the focused spot has to be considered.
Industrial SLM machines utilizes option number two with a scanner system that changes the path of
the laser with two adjustable mirror. To account for the distortion of the spot geometry, a fθ lens is
introduced, as it is designed speciﬁcally to minimize the distortion at different incident angles.
To ensure some form of laser beam characterisation both options are used. Option number one is
solely being used for beam characterisation, in section 4.1, and option two is the actual focus of the
scanner platform as this allows greater parameter ﬂexibility.
From now the terminology is getting a bit more strict, when mentioning scanner system the entire part
from laser to lens is included, scanner refers to the two active mirrors determining the angle of the
beam and talking about the scanning platform refers to the entire package including scanner and the
developed software.

Software platform
To enable selective melting of metal powder, the work ﬂow must be understood and controlled. The
ﬂow from CAD model to ﬁnished part is showed in Figure 2.4.
Initially the 3D model is constructed in a CAD software. The different CAD programs are many and
diverse, but most have the possibility of saving exporting the geomtery to .STL, as this is the de-facto
cross platform ﬁle format of additive manufacturing. During this project the CAD program SolidWorks
2016-2017 was used.
The movement paths is planned in a so-called slicer. As the name states the CAD created geometry is
sliced into predeﬁned slices. Each slice is then mapped out and the movement pattern is correlated to
the wanted process parameters and everything is saved in a job ﬁle. The format of the job ﬁle is gcode,
being the standard format for Computer Numerical Control (CNC).
Executing the job ﬁle is done in collaboration between a sender and a micro controller responsible for
actually sending out move signals to the hardware. To keep track of the process, the sender dispatches
one line of move code and then it waits for the micro controller to send a conﬁrmation of execution
before sending the next line of code. When all the lines of code contained in the job ﬁle is send and
executed the part is ﬁnished and should resemble the initial geometry construed in CAD.

Safety
When dealing with a high power laser, exposure to the beam can have fatal consequences. Therefore
the entire set up has to meet certain safety regulations. The emission of laser has to be done under
controlled circumstances. In this setup this has been solved by moving the laser delivery system into a
cabinet. To ensure that emission is only possible when the cabinet is securely closed, a safety system is
introduced.
This safety circuit is from "Jokab Safety", and has a PLC "PLUTO" at the core. This PLC then monitors
the safety sensors, and only when all safety sensors is activated an "okay to arm" signal is send to the
Laser. The system consist of two emergency stops, and four interlock non-contact safety sensors. The
emergency stops work by breaking the circuit if pushed and ending the laser emission. The reason for
using interlocking systems, is that safety sensors is designed to only work in speciﬁc pairs. Sensors
are designed to only activate when the designated opposite part is within proximity. The proximity
sensors is activated when the doors of the cabinet is closed.
If the laser has to be run with open cabinet, the safety system has to be deactivated. For this purpose

Laser
The Laser chosen is a CW / modulated high power ﬁbre laser, "SP-250C-0003-000", from SPI Lasers. A

Operating the laser can be done in both Continuous Wave (CW) and modulated mode. Running in CW
will be the simplest application, whereas if a pulsating laser is needed the modulation mode comes
in handy. Triggering the laser can be done remotely, allowing for a centralised control of the scanner

Controlling the laser in modulated mode is done by sending an ’on’ signal with a given frequency, see
Figure 2.5. The duty cycle ranges from 0% - 100 %, meaning that the ’on’ signal makes up a fraction of
the total cycle time. At 0% the laser is never on, and at 100 % the laser is always on, also recognized as
CW mode. The length of these pulses is determined by the signal frequency.

laser will result in it actually fading. Both the rise and fall time are low, < 20µs, and therefore becomes
dominating factors only when the frequency becomes high, around 50 kHz. What should more be
noted from the Figure 2.6 (a), is that the laser initiates emission it has a brief overshoot of several times
the nominal value, before settling at the desired power level.

Revisiting the data from SPI Table 2.1, the laser lies well within the capabilities of the mirror. As the
scanner system is able to rotate, the angle of incident is an important factor.

second mirror. The smallest diameter is a 12.7 mm, hence a maximal beam diameter of 12.7 mm·0.85 =

Beam Alignment
Even though the f-θ scan lens negates a lot of the distortion taking place in the image-plane, careful

the beam onto the scan mirrors provides the greatest scan range.
Aligning the beam is done via highly reﬂective mirrors, mounted in brackets adjustable by a thumb

ﬂush from the beginning, as this is not the case. The mirror bracket is adjusted, and the laser path is
adjusted, to ensure an appropriate incident angle.
The adjustable mirror bracket is designed in a way that offsets the center of the mirror from the center
of the bracket itself, to account for this and enable a uniform solution for all three alignment mirrors, a
base mechanism taking the offset into account is constructed.

2.4.4
Stepper Motor
The motor type used in the ﬁrst setup is stepper motors. Stepper motors is electro motors and is
activated in pulses to turn the rotor electromagnetically. The nature of the stepper motor is that is
moves in steps, hence every time a pulse is send the motor moves one step. The motors used in the
setup is: NEMA11, NEMA17 and NEMA23. The larger the number carried by the motor the larger is the
torque provided by the motor itself.
35

The smaller stepper motors NEMA 11 is used to manoeuvre the lightweight scanner mirrors, NEMA
17 motors is used for all implementations regarding powder deposition, whereas the large NEMA 23
motors controls the more heavy duty task of moving the entire modular Scanner System vertically.
All the motors used has a resolution of 1.8°/step giving a total of 200 steps pr revolution.
2.4.5
Step Stick
Controlling the motors is done through step stick controllers. Steps sticks changes the pulses send
by the micro controller into appropriate signals, and send it through the stator in the stepper motor,
turning the rotor exactly one step when a pule is send.
The stepper stick therefore controls how the command send from the controller is executed. This
means that certain step sticks is optimized with certain uses in mind. In this set up three different step
sticks is used.
• RAPS 128
• A4988
• DRV8825
Most step sticks are able to deliver micro stepping, this means that each actual each step is divided
into a number of additional steps. Traditionally step sticks can deliver up to 16 micro steps per step.
The RAPS128 used to control the stepper motors in the scan system, is capable of delivering an extra
128 steps per regular step.
Galvanometers (Galvo)
Industrial SLM machines does not rely on stepper motors, but on galvanometers. Galvanometers is
constructed to produce a rotary displacement as a response to an electric current ﬂowing through a
coil. The galvanometers is abbreviated to galvo.
The need for a constant electric current, requires delivery of an analogues output. And therefore an
Digital to Analogue converter is used.
2.4.6
Digital to Analogue Converter (DAC)
The DAC is used to convert the digital signal from the micro controller to and analogue signal. Con-
trolling the DAC is done though a I2C (Inter-Integrated Circuit) bus, the protocol is used to control
an integrated circuit (IC) connected to the micro controller. In this way every IC connected through
the I2C has an individual address, and the micro controller sends the command to the individual
addresses, a so called master-slave control.
As two individual galvoes are used, the Ardunio has to control two DAC outputs. They are connected
through the SDA (data) and SCL (clock) pins, see Figure 2.16. Figure 2.17 shows the layout of the IC,
and in addition to SDA and SCL, VCC is connected to a 5 V source, GND to ground and the OUT port
delivers the analogue signal.

Firmware
The software interpreting the signals send from the computer, and thereby executes the order sent is
called the ﬁrmware. The ﬁrmware is uploaded to the micro controller.
In the pilot setup stepper motors was used to rotate the mirrors. Within FDM machines stepper
motors is used for both movement and extrusion. The ﬁrmware used in entry level machines is widely
available as open-source, and comes in different ﬂavours. The version Marlin was downloaded and
ﬁtted. The ﬁrmware was altered to interpret angular move commands instead of standard linear
Cartesian commands. In marlin the command controlling how far the stepper moves per unit, is set to
71 steps per degree.
This value comes from a combination of the stepper motor used, see subsection 2.4.4, having 200 steps
per revolution, and the RAPS128 stepstick, see subsection 2.4.5, allowing 128 micro steps per regular
step. Giving a total of 200·128 = 25,600 steps per revolution, dividing with 360°, hence a value of 71
steps per degree.

Ensuring a correctly aligned laser beam, eg. the incident angle is reﬂected though the center of the scan
lens when the scanner is in mid range. To carry out this test, the scan mirrors must be positioning in
the middle of their range, i.e. what corresponds to an expected center position on the build platform.
A circular alignment tool is constructed to test if the the beam goes through the the middle of the scan
lens. The tool takes the shape of a circular plate with a center hole. The center hole is just below the
beam diameter of the laser, and the outer diameter makes a tight ﬁt with the f-θ mounting bracket.
When installed the pilot laser is turned on, and the alignment mirrors is adjusted so the beam passes
straight through the hole, see Figure 2.27.
During calibration it is important to note that the beam should pass through the clear aperture of the
alignment mirrors, i.e. as close to the center as possible, similarly a centred beam is required on the
scan mirrors.

2.8
Slicer
To enable a fully automated movement of the heat source, the path of the heat source has to be mapped.
The mapping of the CAD model is done layer by layer. The layer height is determined by the rapid
prototyping process, and the vertical resolution achievable with the rig used. This division of the CAD
ﬁle is popularly called slicing, as the part is sliced into a number of layers. The slicer outputs gcode,
that is used to control the behaviour of the additive manufacturing rig.
2.8.1
From CAD to STL
Initially the part that has to be constructed in a CAD software and exported as an STL (STereo Lithog-
raphy). The STL ﬁle format was originally invented by 3D Systems[35] and has become the de facto
industry standard in the ﬁeld of rapid prototyping. Two different formats of STL ﬁles exists, one called
ACSII and the one considered in this thesis is the other binary format.
The STL ﬁle is a surface representation of the model. The CAD model is approximated by tesselating
the surface into triangles. Due to the nature of triangles the deviation of the approximation is very
dependent on the shape of the CAD model. Figure 2.28(a)(c) shows a CAD model of a cube, and the
tesselated representation of the model. This is the geometry demanding the least number of triangles
as is can be completely represented by 12 triangles. Whereas a cylinder Figure 2.28(b)(d) theoretically
need an inﬁnite number of triangles to accurately represent the base circle, a degree of deviation has
to be accepted in the approximating STL ﬁle, the accepted deviation is set in the CAD software used to
export the STL ﬁle.

convert them into executable gcode. The ﬁrmware implemented in the SLM reads angular coordinates
for setting the mirrors. Gcode has comments in semicolons.
To ensure the right angular coordinates is send, the position of the scanner mirrors has to be known.
In the pilot scanner system, the location of the zero point could be determined in the ﬁrmware, as the
location of the end stop was deﬁnable. The stepper motors has possible range of 360°. Whereas the
Galvo system requires zero to be when the beam is reﬂected through the center of the scan lens. At this
position the mirror is turned to 45°relative to the incident beam. This position will be used as half the
total range, in this setup the full range is 10°, leaving the center position at 5°.

Layer Change
The powder depositioning system [27], is designed without any positioning feedback, leaving the
homing unreliable. Therefore a pseudo code has been developed, in practice this should not be
implemented before homing is possible.
1. Lower the build plate one layer height
2. Rotate the powder wiper into position
3. Move the wiper across the chamber, adding powder onto the build plate
4. Rotate the wiper into position so the surplus powder can be used for re-coating next layer
This layer change routine is added in between each layer, to efﬁciently automate the building process.

The signal generated has a period of 1 s and an on-signal of 5 ms. The scan speed is 100 deg/min,
meaning that the entire scan area, 10 deg is covered in 6 s. But the important factor is the time between
each laser mark, 1 s. The scan speed can then be calculated by the distance between each point divided
by 1 s, giving the speed along that move.
Figure 4.14 shows the speed calculated along the x-axis, y-axis and diagonal, see Figure 4.13. It is see
that the speed stays very similar across the different locations on the build plate. The speed when
moving with a speed of 100 deg/min is approximately 750 mm/min. As the factor between the angular
speed of the mirror and the speed of the spot is constant, the angular scan speed can be multiplied
with 7.5 and the speed in mm/min is found. From the Figure 4.14 (c) it can be seen that the speed
when moving along the diagonal is slightly lower. This issue is addressed in the discussion.

5 Discussion
Through the development of the SLM scanner system it has become clear that obtaining an open
an fully customizable SLM scanner platform, requires development and control of all intertwining
elements. This leads to a discussion starting with the software platform, then the hardware and laser
that was used. The focus is on the characterisation of the scanner system, and ﬁnally a brief discussion
about what was reached of actual laser melting.
5.1
Programming
The scanner system was enabled through an extensive software platform, optimized to take care of the
individual tasks presented in Figure 2.4. During the development phase is became evident, that the
open source software solutions present for both slicing, sending gcode, and ﬁrmware did not ﬁt the
purpose of SLM. The existing open source solutions, the optimization for FDM brought obstacles to
the SLM process.
Overcoming these obstacles, was solved by taking full control over every step in the process chain.
The development of a slicer taking care of the job planning process showed promising traits. After
optimizing the slicing algorithm the processing time became comparable to the open source solutions
available
Planning and altering the different SLM parameters the is done easily through the GUI. Further
implementations should be including a feature allowing the user to change the scan strategy, and even
better alter the scan strategy intelligently according to the geometry as discussed in subsection 1.1.4.
The choice of developing a custom gcode sender originated from the fact that the open source solutions
available monitored several FDM parameters, aside from sending gcode. Writing a gcode sender in
python was done to ensure an open source cross platform solution. The implementation of a GUI
enables a graphical feedback from the micro controller, and also allows for sending single lines of code.
Development ﬁrmware for the Galvo Stepper System, showed that full control was beneﬁcial to the
understanding of the execution of the commands received. The current micro controller, was limited
to only accurately execute scan speeds of up to 1000 deg/min. This limitation could be solved by
lowering the resolution, but a better solution would be to implement a micro controller capable of
faster execution.
5.2
Hardware
The hardware discussed here will be the two different scanner systems. The differences between the
galvoes and the stepper motors became evident during experiments, and it showed that the stepper
motors only delivered limited accuracy and showed unexpected irregularities even across a single
hatch line.
Galvo motors, even though limited in maximal deliverable speed by the micro controller, showed a
greater deal of regular scan tracks. The overall performance displayed homogeneous tendencies. The

resolution of the galvo motors, can be further increased by constructing a DAC with a higher bit rate
capable of delivering a signal ± 10 V, and thereby allowing a ﬁner incrementation of the galvoes.
Overall the galvoes proved to be a better solution for SLM.
5.3
Beam Characterisation
The beam characterization carried out in this project leaves a lot to be wished for, even though the
localization of the beam wait of the beam returns a greater understanding of the beam propagation. A
more complete characterisation should be carried out. Especially the actual waist diameter should
be recorded, this could be done by the knife-edge method, moving a knife edge trough the beam
and recording the power change as the knife-edge moves through. Allowing a comparison between
the theoretical beam waist and the actual beam waist. The method of measuring a Gaussian beam
diameter and algorithm is described in [39].
5.4
Scanner System Characterization
The characterisation of the two different scanner system proved that the pilot setup with stepper
motors, had a low resolution and introduced unpredicted irregularities. The circle experiments
displayed in Figure 4.9, displayed that the supposed straight line segments took a curvy shape at
speeds higher than 200 deg/min and in Figure 4.11 squares with supposed full density hatching was
engraved.
The result was irregular with an inhomogeneous hatching and some hatch lines even took a wavy
shape. This suggest that the stepper motors induce some vibrations to the mirrors, this is also seen at
higher speeds in the jitter test Figure 4.4. As the resolution is a product of micro stepping, described
insubsection 2.4.5, the results indicate an unreliability in precision of micro stepping.
Implementing the galvo system depicted another situation. The full implementation of a custom
ﬁrmware solution, limited the reliable execution to 2000 deg/min, therefore the jitter test was only
conducted to this level. The jitter introduced by the rotation of the mirrors was not noticeable in
Figure 4.10. The hatch line experiment, seen in Figure 4.11, displayed a homogeneous and full density
hatching across all scan speeds. Finally the circle experiment displayed in Figure 4.12, like the hatching
experiment showed no geometrical differences across the different speeds. The line segments making
up the circle represented straight lines as supposed.
As the experimental characterisation of the galvo stepper system, proved more reliable further tests
were conducted. The ﬁrst was a speed test, where the speed was found to be constant across the build
area along X and Y. A slightly lower speed was found when moving along a diagonal line, this was
addressed by a correction to the ﬁrmware.
Another experiment to ﬁnd the offset of the scanner system, compared to reference values, was
conducted. A pattern of circles was engraved, and center coordinates were compared to the reference
values, it showed that an offset was present at higher speeds. Generally it seems that the further along
the axis the hole is located, an increased offset is present, this suggests that each x and y coordinate
are a bit off. This was found to be true, and the planning ﬁrmware was altered. Another test was not
conducted, but should show a lower offset across different scan speeds.

5.5. Laser Melting
5.5
Laser Melting
The melting experiment carried out was done to test the system, and a energy density comparable to
industrial machines with the same powder was used. Slightly altering the power, was done to compare
the result. From these experiments it is clear that the powder recoating has to fully characterised and
automatised.
Manually laying out the layers without getting any positional feedback leaves the operator in the blind
if a step of the stepper motors enabling the recoating mechanism skips a step. It was possible to carry
out a single layer test, where different parameters was tested. This was done on the pilot setup with
stepper motors, and the result indicated a better process around 40 - 60 W.
The galvo setup was likewise used to carry out different melting of squares with 10 W to 60 W. All of
these was done at a speed of 1000 deg/min. Giving a energy density close to the one recommended
by EOS, presented in chapter 3. The melted parts displayed a coarse surface, and the lowest energy
densities failed to fully melt the layer, and therefore proper adhersion between layers and build plate
failed. Like the previous test a better result was qualitatively found just below 50 W.
Finally the DTU logo was build using 45 W and a scan speed of 1000 deg/min. The result had a coarse
surface and the density therefore assumed low. A large scale optimization of should be carried out.
With this experiment it is shown that complex geometries can be manufactured.
To optimize the melting process, ﬁrst positional feedback should be added to the power depositioning
system. This would allow in-situ veriﬁcation of the layer height of each individual layer. Next a full
characterisation should be carried out, mapping out the effect of different scan speeds and different
levels of power used.
The melting experiments carried out in this project, was done by ﬁrst ﬁnding an optimal power output
and keeping the scan velocity ﬁxed. In the future the use of FEM simulations should be included.
This would be a tool for bettering the performance, and also testing the inﬂuence of different scan
strategies.

6 Conclusion
To develop an experimental SLM scanner platform for powder bed fusion, knowledge was gathered
and the process of selectively fusing metal particles was researched. It was found that to create a
platform capable of returning the control to the operator and change the different parameters, a strictly
hardware solution was not adequate. The underlying planning and execution process had also to be
understood and controlled. Literature was reviewed to get a better understanding of the energy source,
build material, melting process and scan strategies.
During this project the focus was on the construction of the scanner system. The laser used was a
250 W CW/modulated laser, delivering a collimated beam. To align the laser beam properly to the
scanner system, a beam alignment mechanism was developed.
Initially a pilot setup was constructed, implementing custom made parts developed and reﬂective
mirrors was mounted onto stepper motors. An open source control solution was implemented, and
altered to ﬁt the purpose. Through testing, it became clear that the stepper motors introduced too
many vibrations and the outcome was irregular. Due to an unreliable process, the stepper motor
system proved incapable of delivering the resolution and the repeatability necessary for SLM.
An improved scanner system was developed using galvos to position the scan mirrors. To control the
galvo system, a custom ﬁrmware solution had to be implemented, allowing communication through
analogue signals. The galvo scanner system displayed homogeneous scan tracks and proved capable
of delivering reliable outputs.
During test, it was found that the scan velocity was limited to 1000 deg/min convertible to 125 mm/s,
by the execution time of the micro controller. The accuracy of the galvo scanner system was tested by
engraving onto steel plates, and showed promising traits.
Alongside the implementation of a galvo scanner system, the software platform enabling the process
was reﬁned. Leading to the development of a fully capable job planner, converting a CAD model into an
executable job ﬁle, containing different parameters decided in the software GUI. The execution of the
commands contained in the job ﬁle, was controlled by carefully designing a micro controller ﬁrmware
that allows interpretation and execution of the commands. To control the discrete movements of the
galvo system, Bresenham’s algorithm was used. The job ﬁle was communicated to the micro controller
through a custom gcode sender, giving the operator the option to send single line commands or run
entire job ﬁles via a GUI.
The galvo scanner platform enabled a reliable planning and execution, through experiments where
squares, circles and even complex geometries were engraved. The slicer and scanner system delivered
adequate geometrical results.
Melting experiments was carried out, and showed that the scanner platform was able to correctly move
the laser around the powder bed. A challenge was manually recoating and the results therefore varied
in quality. The issue of rough surfaces and enabling fully dense layers should be further investigated.

7 Outlook
The perspective of an open SLM experimental platform is many. Having the option to change the laser
source, and the optical elements allows for easily altering the setup to meet other demands. Using the
setup to melt different powders with different parameters is easily done by using the graphical user
interface. Test and veriﬁcation of current theory within the ﬁeld of laser melting should therefore be
possible.
To test the signiﬁcance of different scan strategies, the literature reviewed about fusing processes and
scan strategies should be carefully considered. The result could be a slicer, capable of predicting and
controlling the properties of the ﬁnal part.
Understanding the process of solidiﬁcation and the consequence of different parameters, another
feature might be added to the slicer. Full control over the parameters along the scanning, allow the
implementation of selectively choosing what parts of the ﬁnal product should contain a speciﬁc
micro structure. This should be possible by using simulation to ﬁgure out what temperature the
metal has to be reheated to, and for how long. Thereby selective segregating elements into the metal,
allowing control of differentiating the material properties on different locations of the same part. The
micro structure should be investigated by methods similar to the ones described in 1.1.3. Here the
different etching elements and preparation techniques suitable is described. Additionally a hardness
measurement should reveal difference of micro structure.
Further development of the depositioning system should be carried out. The primary issues addressed
should be adding positional feedback to the system. This would enable a much more reliable recoating
process, and therefore ensure homogeneous layers.
If an increase in scan speed is desired, the choice of micro controller should be revisited.
