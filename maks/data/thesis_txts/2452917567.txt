Abstract 
Trombe wall is a solar heating technology that can capture and store solar energy in 
the thermal mass of the wall material. The Trombe wall concept once became popular 
in the 70’s but it is rarely used in today’s architecture. This project concerns the 
energy simulation of a fan-assisted Trombe wall using Energyplus. A parametric 
analysis was performed for the parameters required to design a Trombe wall. A case 
study of a conceptual house using Trombe wall was presented and was simulated using 
the weather data for Copenhagen (Denmark) and Harbin (China), to evaluate on the 
heating performance of the Trombe wall and the possibility for reduction in insulation 
thickness. The results of the parametric analysis showed that the most influential 
parameters for Trombe wall design are the orientation, effective area of the absorber, 
conductivity and solar absorptance. Following the parametric analysis, a concept for a 
new low energy single storey house of 100 m2 was proposed. This house concept 
implemented a fan-assisted Trombe wall on the southern facade. The house was 
simulated in Copenhagen, Denmark and Harbin, China. The simulation results showed 
that, Trombe wall could realise reduction in heating demand in both localities. As a 
result, the insulation thickness could be reduced from 380 mm to 130 mm in the case 
of Copenhagen, and 380 mm to 100 mm in the case of Harbin.

Chapter 1 
Introduction 
In today’s building industry, new buildings are required to meet energy regulations 
whose purpose is to lower energy consumption in the building sector. In the EU, many 
countries have made goals to make their buildings up to zero energy status. These are 
measures taken to address reduction of fossil fuel consumption. Meanwhile, many 
countries have set off towards 100% renewable energy. For example, Denmark aims to 
become fossil fuel independent by 2050 [1]. 
In the Nordic, keeping warm during cold winter takes the priority in building design. In 
order to reduce the need for heating, the most common and established approach 
taken by designers is to insulate the building envelope to minimise conduction heat 
loss. This approach may have been influenced by the modern ultra-low energy building 
concept, such as the German Passivhaus Standard which has gained popularity since 
its origin in 1990 [2]. However, it can also be argued that, the “go-to” construction for 
the Nordic dwellings has become the three-block composite wall shown in Figure 1(a), 
consisting of two blocks of masonry and concrete, with a thick insulation in between. 
For example, a design guidebook called “Very low energy housing in North European 
Countries” suggests using very thick 380mm of insulation [3]. Is this the only possibility 
of achieving low energy buildings in cold climates and consume more and more 
insulation materials?

In Japan, winter is approached in a totally different way. A typical traditional Japanese 
house is built of mostly timber and other lightweight materials. As illustrated in Figure 
1(b), instead of adding a block of insulation to the wall, they have adopted “layers” to 
control the indoor climate. This is achieved by using multiple sliding doors to create 
“buffer zone” around the house, as opposed to a permanent wall. These layers can be 
closed to keep warm or removed at one’s will, like a piece of clothing. As a result, 
Japanese houses have acquired the ability to adapt its envelope to the seasonal and 
daily changes in the occupants’ thermal needs.  
From the two examples above, we can distinguish the contrasting approaches 
associated with two different cultures – the Nordic approach of “conserving” energy 
by insulation, and the Japanese approach of “adapting” to the specific needs of 
occupants, thereby minimising the “use” of energy.

1.2 Motivation and Objectives 
This thesis project concerns Trombe wall as an alternative energy saving technology 
to the conventional insulation technology used in the Nordic.  
The goal of this project is to investigate the potential of Trombe wall to realise the 
low energy building concept pursued by today’s building industry. During the research, 
limitations in application of Trombe wall will be identified and taken into 
consideration. Key design parameters of Trombe wall will be identified to allow 
performance optimisation. The last objective is to evaluate whether it is possible to 
reduce the amount of insulation. 
1.2 Research Questions

Background and Literature Review 
2.1.1 Trombe Wall and its Origin 
The Trombe wall concept was first presented and patented by Edward Morse in 1881 
(Figure 2) under the title “Warming and ventilating apartments by the sun's rays”. It 
takes its name from a French engineer, Felix Trombe who popularized it in 
collaboration with an architect Jacques Michel [6]. 
         
 
Figure 2: Original patent “Warming and ventilating apartments by the sun's rays”. Illustrations 
by Edward Morse [7] 
The concept of solar heating wall inspired the Trombe-Michel pair to build the first 
modern house to implement Trombe wall in 1967 in Odeillo, France [8]. As seen in 
Figure 3, the whole south-facing façade was made a Trombe wall to capture as much 
solar radiation as possible.  
==PAGE:12==
6 
 
 
Figure 3: Solar house in Odeillo. A detached house with Trombe wall covering the whole 
southern façade. Designed by Jacques Michel, built in 1967 in Odeillo, France [9] 
A Trombe wall is classified as a passive solar heating wall that requires zero running 
cost [10]. As illustrated in Figure 4, a Trombe wall consists of an absorber, usually a 
material with high thermal mass e.g. concrete or brick, and a glazing covering the 
absorber. This creates a greenhouse effect within the air gap between the glazing and 
the absorber, also known as the channel. The outer surface of the absorber may be 
coated with dark paint to enhance absorption and storage of solar radiation. The wall 
has vents at the top and bottom to allow some of the stored heat to be transferred by 
an airstream created by convection in the channel. A small portion of convection also 
occurs on the room-side surface of the absorber. Most of the solar energy absorbed is 
transferred by convection and radiation from Trombe wall to the room, but a portion 
is lost through the glazing to the exterior at night. Night insulation of the glazing can 
reduce this loss [11]. 
==PAGE:13==
7 
 
 
Figure 4: Working principle of Trombe wall. Illustration elaborated from [6] 
According to Medici [9] who conducted a literature study of Trombe wall in 
architectural periodicals of the 70’s, experimentation of Trombe wall architecture 
attracted much attention due to the emergence of a new movement in environmental 
architecture triggered by the international oil crisis. However, as the prices of fossil 
fuels decreased in the 80’s, it was used less often and temporarily made redundant. 
2.1.4 Limitations of Trombe Wall 
One of the main problems with utilising a passive Trombe wall for space heating is the 
uncertainty of the heating capacity since the solar intensity is inconsistent and 
periodical. Therefore any change in solar intensity could cause temperature 
fluctuations [12].  
The material used for the wall, usually concrete or bricks, have low thermal resistance. 
When solar intensity is insufficient to maintain the heat flux balance between the 
indoor to the outdoor, it results in excessive heat loss from the building [8]. This makes 
it difficult to comply with the low U-values current building standards require. 
During winter, at night or cloudy day, when the wall temperature is colder than the 
indoor temperature, reverse air circulation can occur also known as the inverse

Trombe wall performance relies on convection heat transfer. Thus, the design and 
dimensions of the channel and the inlet and outlet vents in the wall influences the 
convection process inside the Trombe wall. As a result, it affects the overall heating 
performance [12]. 
Overheating can occur when the solar intensity is excessive, especially during summer; 
however, it can also occur during heating season if the climatic conditions meet [14]. 
Another issue in Trombe wall application is the low aesthetic value associated with the 
technology. The reason for this is speculated that, many people are not favourable of 
the dark-matt surface covering a large part of a façade [10]. 
2.1.5 Modified Trombe Wall Concepts 
Some studies have been dedicated to overcoming the limitations mentioned previously. 
This includes using multiple Trombe wall in alternating angle (Zigzag Trombe wall) 
[15], using insulated wall (composite TW) [13], [16], using phase changing material for 
heat storage (PCM Trombe wall) [17], and using photovoltaic cells instead of glazing to 
generate electricity (PV Trombe wall) [18].  
In particular, the fan assisted composite Trombe wall seems to be promising as it may 
be able to address the issue with low thermal resistance, luck of control of heating, 
reverse air circulation and control over air flow. Some problems remain such as summer 
overheating and aesthetic value which are worth investigating. However, these topics 
were not considered further in this project as the scope was set to be in the heating 
application.

2.1.2 EU Energy Policies and Low-Energy Building Concepts 
Today, the agenda of reducing fossil fuel consumption is of ever-increasing importance 
both for climate change and sustainability reasons.  
The building sector is the largest energy consumer in Europe, which accounts for 
approximately 40% of primary energy and 36% of greenhouse emissions [19]. 
 
Figure 5:  Final energy consumption statistics by end-use in 2010 [20] 
Residential sector in the EU consume the highest amount of energy for space heating. 
As seen in Figure 5, space heating of residential buildings accounts for 66% of final 
energy consumption in the EU. This is proportionally the highest when compared to 
the US and China. Even though this figure is an average of countries in a wide spectrum 
of climate, Belussi et al. argues that it reflects the high heating energy consumption 
habits of the EU countries [20].  
The strategy for reducing the energy consumption within the building sector was 
addressed at the European level in the recast of the Energy Performance of Buildings 
Directive (EPBD) of 2018. It specified that all new buildings shall be “nearly Zero Energy 
==PAGE:16==
10 
 
Buildings (nZEB)” from 31st  December 2020 [21]. Although the quantitative definition 
of “nearly ZEB” differs by country [19], the concept of zero energy building can be 
defined as a building with greatly reduced energy demand that allows the energy 
demand to be balanced by an equivalent generation of electricity (or other energy 
carriers) from renewable sources [22]. 
In 2016, ZEBRA 20201 published a report that presented their research in market uptake 
of nZEB across Europe from 2014 to 2016 [23].  One of the key findings of their research 
was that, in order for the EU to commit to limiting global warming well below 2°C 
scenario and the related targets, the building sector will be required to reduce its CO2 
emission by 80-95% by 2050. The report also points out that the heating systems 
installed in the next 10 years will still be in operation in 2050. Therefore, to realise 
the decarbonation goals for 2050, it requires an absolute phase out of new fossil 
heating systems within the next 5-10 years[23]. Given the information above, an 
efficient and low energy heating technology is highly sought after. 
While the ZEB concept was implemented in the EU energy policies fairly recently, an 
ultra-insulated building concept known as Passivhaus has gained popularity since its 
origin in 1990 as a way of reducing energy consumption and providing ultra-low energy 
and zero carbon dwellings [2].  With approximately 60,000 completed projects 
worldwide (as of 2016), the Passivhaus design is rapidly spreading around the world 
[24].  
Central to this approach is the reduction of space heating demand through minimising 
thermal transmission and ventilation losses. The Passivhaus Standard requires that all

2.1.3 Trombe wall for Zero Energy Building 
The potential of utilising Trombe wall lies in reducing heating demand and storing the 
solar energy in the thermal mass of material. 
In their review articles, Sadineni et al.[26], Martinopoulos [27] and Belussi et al. [20] 
raise Trombe wall as a strategy for reducing heating energy in buildings. The unique 
energy storing ability of Trombe wall amongst other solar heating strategy is pointed 
                                            
2 IEE – Intelligent Energy Europe

out by Vanaga et al. [28] who states that one of important aspects in low energy and 
zero energy buildings is the use of solar energy and ability to accumulate it. Moreover, 
Aelenei et al. [29].states that passive approaches play a fundamental role in addressing 
ZEB design as they not only affect the loads put on the buildings’ mechanical systems, 
but indirectly the strive for renewable energy generation. 
According to Kristjansdottir et al., zero-energy building concept is increasingly 
superseded by the zero-emission building concept, in which the balance is measured 
in terms of greenhouse gas emissions [30]. Central to the Zero-emission concept is that 
the energy generation must counterbalance the embodied emissions from materials. 
Therefore, as argued by Moschetti et al. [31], moving from a zero-energy building to a 
zero-emission building requires not only an effort on energy-related measures but also 
a holistic approach on how to reduce the embodied emissions of the materials during 
the entire life cycle. 
Given the above information, if it is possible to reduce the amount of insulation by 
using Trombe wall, it can contribute towards reducing not only energy consumption, 
but the embodied energy of the building by reducing the insulation material.

Chapter 4 
Method 
4.1 Methodology 
The initial phase of the analysis was the model testing. The benchmark case used in 
this research for comparison of space heating by Trombe wall was created by Dong et 
al. [32]. This journal article presents experimental data of air and surface 
temperatures recorded from a test apparatus consisting of a novel designed Trombe 
wall and a test room, along with thermal efficiency derived from the measurements. 
As some of the room components and the physical properties of the materials used to 
construct the test apparatus were vaguely described, they were assumed and referred 
to typical constructions described in an industry technical guidebook. The result of 
model testing will be presented as part of the method. 
Next, a parametric analysis of Trombe wall was conducted using the same modelling 
method. The aim of this analysis was to identify the key parameters that should be 
taken into consideration for designing a Trombe wall. For this, a local sensitivity 
analysis was performed using a model of a small office room.  
The final phase of the research consisted of a case study through a design proposal of 
a Trombe wall and simulations using the weather data from Nordic climate and 
Japanese climate. The goal of this case study was to test a hypothetical extreme 
situation to investigate whether a reduction in insulation was possible in cold climates 
by utilising Trombe wall. Copenhagen (Denmark) and Harbin (China) were selected as 
test locations.

These options were evaluated based on applicability in early stage of building design. 
CFD would produce the most detailed results, and arguably the most accurate out of 
all. It could also be used to analyse the air movement inside the air channel and room 
etc. However, CFD was discarded as dynamic CFD simulations require details that may 
not be known at early phase of building design.  
Analytical method, for example, using formulae presented by Abdeen et al. [33], would 
require solving matrices. Computer programming languages for mathematical problems 
such as Matlab or Numpy would be employed for this purpose. The issue with this 
method was these tools provide code-based approach, which would be difficult to 
apply as a design tool.  
The heat balance method, which takes advantages of simulation packages such as 
EnergyPlus, IDA ICE and IES VE. The benefit of using these packages is that they offer 
3D visualisation and the ability to communicate with other computer aided design tools 
(CAD) and BIM tools like of Revit. Above all, heat balance method was chosen as the 
analysis method.

4.2.4 Modelling of Trombe wall 
Trombe wall was modelled according to the EnergyPlus reference [39]. A very narrow 
zone placed to the external wall surface of a room represents a Trombe wall, which is 
separated by an interzone partition. The depth of the Trombe zone corresponds to the 
size of the channel width. 
 
Figure 8: An example of a room with Trombe wall modelled during this project. The blue 
elements represent the Trombe wall attached to the external wall surface. 
==PAGE:25==
19 
 
For solving the energy balance between the Trombe wall and room zones, the Zone 
Cross Mixing component was used to emulate the air exchange between the two zones. 
This script has an energy effect on both the source and the receiving zone, thus 
maintaining both the air mass and energy balances in the two zones [40]. The user can 
specify the air exchange rate, which allows parametric run of air exchange rate later 
in the project. 
 
Figure 9: An example of zone cross mixing command used in EnergyPlus during simulation 
A drawback of using this syntax was that it only allowed one Trombe wall per zone. In 
cases where there were two Trombe wall in one room, this was overcome by dividing 
the room into subzones partitioned by an “air wall” and allowing free air exchange 
between them. The resulting data from two subzones was averaged to account for the 
subtle difference between the subzones.

was a compromise made after weighing the benefit of smaller time step e.g. 30min or 
15min, and the time taken for each simulation. The climatic data from the weather 
file came in hourly data. Therefore, it had to be interpolated if we were to do 
simulations with smaller time steps. Since the main interest in the analysis was the

Whilst there are agreements between the two plots (a) and (b), there are also 
dissimilarities between them. First, it is apparent that ΔT is greater in simulation than 
experiment, which indicates an overestimation of the heating effect of Trombe wall. 
A number of reasons can be considered as to what caused overestimation. First is the 
constant air flow rate. In the experiment, the rate of air exchange was determined by 
the buoyancy effect of the air inside the Trombe wall. Instead, buoyancy is replaced 
by air mixing at a constant flow rate, which is then used to solve the energy balance 
between room and Trombe zones.  
Second, when the contrast rooms are compared to each other, plot (a) sees higher 
increase in temperature during the day. The most significant heat gain for contrast 
room was the solar gain through the window. Thus, this discrepancy was expected to 
arise from the difference in the lower solar intensity in the input weather data, and 
the difference in the optical property of window glass. 
Third, it is apparent in Figure 14(b) that contrast room temperature rises before test 
room starts warming up (shown as ring in the figure). This was caused by the air mixing 
schedule. In the experiment, the vents between the two rooms were opened during 
6am-6pm every day. In the simulation, the mixing schedule was created using the same 
time period. However, because the mixing was forced, the cold air inside Trombe wall 
was forcefully mixed in the morning. As a result, the room temperature dropped after 
the mixing has started until the Trombe zone temperature has caught up with room 
temperature.

4.3.4 Conclusion of Model Validation 
The model was able to capture the temperature profile of Trombe wall and predict 
the room temperature with tendency of overestimation. It was decided that the model 
could be used for further analysis, but with a tendency for overestimation. The model 
may be used in practical design exercises, but its use may be limited to initial solar 
potential assessment, unless further fine tuning is done.   
Efficiency calculation was not very accurate. In order to validate the accuracy for 
efficiency, the accurate modelling of construction would be required. Using Honeybee 
was the limiting factor in this issue. The model does not represent buoyancy effect, 
however, it can be applied for a fan assisted type.

4.5 Phase 3: Case Study of a Conceptual House using Trombe Wall 
The final phase of this project comprised of a simulation of a hypothetical house in 
two locations, namely Copenhagen, Denmark and Harbin, China. Copenhagen 
represents a cold climate with low sunshine hours during winter. Harbin, on the other 
hand represents a cold climate with higher sunshine hours. One house was made 
without Trombe wall, and another was made with Trombe wall. The aim of this case 
study was to compare the heating demand of a house using an fan-assisted Trombe 
wall, along with reducing the insulation thickness of a wall construction.  
4.5.1 House Concept Description 
A 100 m2 house with single storey was created. This house consists of minimum details 
to represent an extreme case. The aim was to eliminate the influence of occupancy 
and other environmental factors. The house was designed based on the 
recommendation for U-values given in the design guide book produced by NorthPass 
project [3]. The external wall of the house was made of a “conventional” ultra-
insulated wall construction with U-value of 0.08, as shown in Figure 17.

Chapter 6 
Discussion 
This chapter has been divided into two parts, the first is dedicated to the discussion 
about the parametric analysis and the second concerns the result of the case study. 
Finally, the last section provides the overall discussion of Trombe wall.  
Discussion about parametric analysis 
From the parametric analysis, we can identify four key parameters in Trombe wall 
design, which are its orientation, effective area, conductivity of the absorber and solar 
absorptance of the absorber surface. Based on the assumption of local sensitivity 
analysis method, the four parameters are independent of each other. It was also found 
that any of these parameters possessed optimum points.  
Another finding was regarding the air flow rate. Figure 20 can be utilised as the 
performance curve of a fan driven Trombe wall across the heating season. Despite the 
lack of information regarding the instantaneous power of Trombe wall, which was not 
part of this study, it would still be valid to state that the information from Figure 20 
can be used to understand the performance of Trombe wall in application. For 
example, a Trombe wall can adapt to a small heating load by reducing the air flow. 
However, it cannot adapt to the need for a higher heating load than the maximum 
heating capacity determined by its size.  Therefore, the area of the Trombe wall needs 
to be determined according the yearly heating demand of the room desired for heating. 
The analysis of conductivity, density and specific heat was aimed to reveal their 
relation to the Trombe wall performance. It showed that the ideal material would have 
the smallest of all three properties, which suggests that a lightweight material with 
==PAGE:58==
52 
 
low specific heat and low thermal conductivity would be an ideal absorber. This is an 
opposing evidence to the conventional Trombe wall using a heavyweight material to 
store thermal energy. This may be explained by how test was set up. The choice of 
performance indicator was chosen as the heating energy for the whole heating period, 
which neglects the effect of energy storage. The test was set up in such a way that 
the absorber that can contribute to the immediate heating would perform better, 
because the heavy materials may be able to store heat for longer time, but the 
released energy into the room would be less. Therefore, it would not contribute 
towards the overall heating energy of Trombe wall.  
In order to realise the “adaptive” concept the Trombe wall must be able to store the 
solar energy until the occupant decides to use the energy. Investigation of this concept 
was attempted through the parametric analysis of materials. However, it was not 
possible to deduce any vital information about thermal storage from the parametric 
analysis. 
Discussion about case study 
In the case study, the aim was to compare the energy saving potential of a house 
concept with a fan-assisted Trombe wall. The house was simulated in two cold climates 
with different solar profiles. Copenhagen represented a location with little sunshine 
hours in winter, and Harbin represented a location with longer sunshine hours. This 
case study was not aimed to model a realistic situation, but rather a new concept for 
a low energy house. The house was designed to emulate an extreme case to find the 
lower boundary potential of Trombe wall. There were no internal gains and windows 
so to eliminate the heating demand reduction by people and solar gain through the 
window. It was anticipated that the result would illustrate the performance of Trombe 
wall in the worst design case of low energy building design.  
==PAGE:59==
53 
 
It was found that the Trombe wall house performed better in Harbin than Copenhagen. 
This was evident from the percentage of heating energy covered by Trombe wall 
(Figure 29). This is attributable to the abundance of solar radiation in Harbin during 
winter.  
The results of energy simulation illustrated that Trombe wall can in fact, reduce 
insulation thickness if the same amount of heating demand was to be set. And as a 
result, a wall construction with higher U-value may be used instead. This can achieve 
a new design of a low energy house that uses less construction material, which is a 
step forward towards the zero emission concept. 
While the Trombe wall concept house gave a positive result, the amount of reduction 
of a real house is subject to the heat loss of the house such as window. Therefore, it 
is not possible to estimate the real effect of and would be difficult to get a direct 
estimation. On the other hand, the concept of a new Trombe wall house created this 
project is worth further investigation.  
Overall discussion of Trombe wall and its future  
What was not considered in this project, and the main challenges of Trombe wall 
application are the environmental factors of the cold climate. In most places with cold 
climate, the snow is a problem because Trombe wall must always be exposed to the 
sun. Also, the glazing of the Trombe wall must be cleaned to avoid deposition of dust, 
which lowers the heating performance. This would make it difficult to apply in a tall 
building where frequent cleaning of Trombe wall is difficult. Also, there must not be 
any object that can shade the Trombe wall. This is a challenge in urban situations. The 
southern façade of the building must be used for Trombe wall, as shown in the 
parametric analysis. This is another challenge in urban situation as there is not so much 
freedom as to decide the orientation of a building, because of the limited cite area.  
==PAGE:60==
54 
 
The fact that the state energy policies identifying zero energy building enhances the 
use of active system such as the fan-assisted Trombe wall because, as the grid 
electricity generation becomes 100% or close to 100% renewable, the energy 
consumption in the electricity form will have less impact on the environment and is 
more easily justifiable. However, we must establish a way to accurately take a holistic 
approach to evaluate the cost of Trombe wall in terms of embodied carbon.  
In other relation to other new technologies, the fan-assisted Trombe wall concept may 
be integrated with the IoT technology. The solar energy stored in the Trombe wall can 
be measured with a temperature sensor which can be monitored by the central building 
management system. This can offer an on-demand heating for the occupants. This 
linked with smart technology such as machine learning and artificial intelligence 
algorithm to optimise the heat available in the Trombe wall may improve the operation 
to minimise the energy used by the fan.

Chapter 7 
Conclusion 
This project concerned the energy simulation of a fan-assisted Trombe wall using 
Energyplus. A parametric analysis was performed including the parameters required to 
design a Trombe wall. A case study of a conceptual house using Trombe wall was 
simulated to evaluate on the heating performance of the Trombe wall. 
The results of the parametric analysis showed that the most influential parameters for 
Trombe wall design are the orientation, effective area of the absorber, conductivity 
and solar absorptance.  
A concept for a new low energy single storey house of 100 m2 was proposed. This house 
concept implemented a fan-assisted Trombe wall on the southern facade. The house 
was simulated in Copenhagen, Denmark and Harbin, China. The simulation results 
showed that, Trombe wall can realise reduction in heating demand in both localities. 
As a result, the insulation thickness could be reduced from 380 mm to 130 mm in the 
case of Copenhagen, and 380 mm to 100 mm in the case of Harbin.
