Abstract 
Purpose: To support the improvement of current recycling systems in 
Denmark, this study investigates public-private partnerships between 
municipalities’ waste management departments and plastic packaging 
companies, in order to close the loop of specific plastic products, that 
currently end up in household plastic waste. 
Methodology: The study is based on two systematical literature reviews 
and 
two 
case 
studies, 
which 
are 
synthesised 
into 
specific 
recommendations for a framework to be developed. The framework is 
tested through theoretical simulation.  
Findings: Based on 32 studies, 11 frameworks, 20 drivers and 11 barriers, 
the study concludes in a framework addressed at Danish municipalities 
to identify and implement a value chain design for closed-loop recycling.  
Research limitations: The study is limited to a Danish context, and with 
post-consumer plastic waste as the circulating resource. Furthermore, 
the study calls for further testing in real-life settings.

Introduction 
Plastic is a versatile material in many aspects; it can easily be processed into solids, fibres, 
films, foams and coatings. They are durable but lightweight and low-cost (Callister & 
Rethwisch, 2015, Chapter 15). As a result, 380 million metric tonnes of plastics are produced 
annually on a global scale. Since its invention the accumulated production is estimated to be 
almost 8 billion metric tonnes (Geyer, Jambeck, & Law, 2017), which is more than the total 
weight of all human beings to ever walk this planet1. 
Plastic is produced from crude oil which is extracted from beneath the Earth’s crust where it 
has been capsulated for millions of years. The crude oil acts as a carbon storage, as does 
plastic products, but when released to the atmosphere through incineration it creates the 
imbalance we today know as the greenhouse effect (Center for International Environmental 
Law, Hamilton, Feit, Muffett, & Kelso, 2019). The main reason for the greenhouse effect is the 
increased concentration of greenhouse gasses (GHG), which is the highest in 650.000 years 
and are largely man-made (IPCC, 2007).  
In 2015 the plastic sector was responsible for 4% of the global GHG emissions (Zheng & Suh, 
2019), and by 2050 this number is estimated to rise to 15% if the current consumption trend 
continues (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2019).  
Of the total GHG emssions from plastics, 61% accounts for the raw material production, 30% 
for the conversion process from resin to products and 9% from the end-of-life (EoL) 
treatment (Zheng & Suh, 2019) as illustrated in Figure 2.

Conclusions 
• 
The global plastic sector is responsible for 4% of all GHG emissions and is expected to 
reach 15% in 30 years. 
• 
The majority of the GHG emissions are emitted in the production phase of the plastic 
resin. 
• 
Incineration is the most CO2 emitting EoL alternative compared to landfilling and 
recycling. 
1.2 Recycling as the Best Alternative 
Incineration is described to cause “extremely high emissions and is the primary driver of 
emissions from plastic waste management” (Center for International Environmental Law et 
al., 2019, p. 2), which is also evident in Figure 2.  
Landfilling on the other hand is the least CO2-emitting method, as it does not directly release 
the stored carbon to the atmosphere, but causes other environmental risks in the local area 
like micro plastic generation, groundwater contamination, loss of biodiversity, attraction of 
rodents (Christensen & Fruergaard, 2011, Chapter 10.2), and similar to incineration, it 
encourages the persistent need for crude oil for virgin plastic production.  
The recycling process does create some emissions but theoretically results in negative GHG 
emissions, as the recycling output displaces virgin plastic production thus avoiding the 
production emissions resulting in a factor 3.6 lower climate impacts (Center for International 
Environmental Law et al., 2019, p. 63).  
Conclusions 
Recycling is the least carbon intensive EoL alternative, as it evades incineration and avoids 
the new plastic production stage, which are two significant processes in emission 
reduction.

1.3 Plastic Treatment on a global Scale 
Figure 3 shows that landfilling is the most prevalent EoL treatment worldwide (Zheng & Suh, 
2019). Zooming into EU and Denmark, indicates that landfilling is being replaced by 
incineration, and in Denmark, where landfilling is not customary, the majority of resources 
are incinerated. Keeping in mind the high carbon intensity of plastic incineration, Denmark 
is releasing great amounts of GHGs. However, the low share of landfilling means a healthier 
local, natural environment. 
 
 
Figure 3: overview of plastic EoL treatment worldwide (Zheng & Suh, 2019), EU (Dakofa, 2018) and DK 
(McKinsey&Company & Innovation Fund Denmark, 2019). 
The Danish Parliament enacted in December 2019, that Denmark must cut its GHG emissions 
by 70% by 2030 (compared to 1990-levels). To reach this goal, the Danish Council on Climate 
Change (2020) have published a report recommending cost-effective policy solutions that will 
support the GHG reduction. Here, the Danish waste management sector is pointed out to 
having the potential to account for one twelfth of the total GHG reduction and the council 
claims that the challenge is the low quality of recycled plastic. 
• 
Incineration and landfilling encourages the persistent need for new raw material 
production. 
• 
Recycling is the most effective EoL treatment to reduce both GHG emissions and 
impact on local environment. 
 
1.4 Challenges in The Danish PCPW Recycling System  
Post-consumer plastic waste (PCPW) make up almost 60% of the plastic waste generated in 
Denmark (Miljøstyrelsen, 2020) and consist mainly of packaging products (Hahladakis & 
Iacovidou, 2019). The fraction originate from the households and is consequently collected 
by the municipalities.  
The system and stakeholders for recycling PCPW vary case by case. In Denmark, 
municipalities use different contracted Material Recycling Facilities (MRF), thus setting 
==PAGE:12==
 
Research Clarification - Introduction 
 
 
Page 5 of 118 
different sorting criteria depending on the MRFs. The illustration below shows a desired life 
cycle of PCPW in Copenhagen Municipality (Christensen & Fruergaard, 2011, p. 226; DAKOFA, 
n.d.). 
 
Figure 4: the waste management system for PCPW in Denmark 
However, in reality the output material’s quality from the MRF is much lower than desired 
and less than half will in reality become new products (Dakofa, 2018). The low quality of the 
fraction causes it to hold a negative market value, meaning that the municipalities pay up to 
3.000 DKK/tonne to dispose it to MRFs (Miljøstyrelsen, 2020). The source of the problem lie 
both in various non-polymer contaminations (like bio waste or sand) and polymer cross-
contaminations (like mixing HDPE and PET polymertypes) in the MRF processes (Eriksen, 
2019) and the lack of design for recyclability in the packaging industry (The Ellen MacArthur 
Foundation, 2016).  
The poor material quality cause uncertainty regarding the technical characteristics of the 
recycled resins, which is a problem in products with strict specifications in the material 
selection, like food packaging that has strict requirements related to the material’s barrier 
against ultra violet (UV) radioation, oxygen and liquids (Barlow & Morgan, 2013). 
==PAGE:13==
 
Research Clarification - Introduction 
 
 
Page 6 of 118 
Thus, when recycling PCPW it often become solid, low strength, products like fences, outdoor 
furniture, and plastic lumber (Christensen & Fruergaard, 2011, p. 224), hence the recycled 
plastic does not displace the need for virgin plastic resins in the production phase of new 
packaging products and the loop for packaging is not considered closed. 
Evidently, there is a need for an improved recycling system in Denmark, but the economic 
incentive for investing in recycling facilities’ is insignificant due to the low and stable prices 
of virgin plastic (Nordic Council of Ministers, 2018). Thus, there is a limited market demand 
because the price of recycled plastic is relatively high compared to its low quality. And the 
lack of demand for recycled plastic increase the prices further. For example, recycled HDPE 
from industrial recycling systems (often cut-aways from production) can be sold for 1.600 
DKK/tonne. In contrast, mixed recycled plastics from PCPW is only estimated around 200 
DKK/tonne (Miljøstyrelsen, 2020). 
Conclusions 
• 
PCPW make up the majority of the plastic waste in Denmark, and consist mainly of 
packaging products. 
• 
Poor material quality of PCPW causes it to have a negative market value and hinders 
investment in the sector.  
• 
The low quality of the material hinders it to displace the material for the production 
of the same consumer products.  
 
A HOLE IN THE SYSTEM 
A recent report shows that from Vestforbrænding, the waste management company in the 
western and northern municipalities surrounding Copenhagen, only 31% of the collected 
plastic fraction is proven to be recycled (Andersen, 2019). Thus, the majority of the plastic is 
sorted out throughout the whole value chain (as depicted in Figure 4) and consequently 
incinerated or discarded and it is complex to track the waste after it leaves the German 
recycler, where some plastic is sold to production facilities, some is incinerated, and some is 
exported to other countries. A mini documentary from 2019 showed that some of the Danish 
plastic waste can be found dumped in Malaysia (TV2, 2019). The realisation of the poor 
recycling system is demotivating the citizens and decrease their waste sorting efforts as 
scepticism towards politics and institutions is found to be associated with less availability to 
start a recycling behaviour (Scafuto, 2018). 
• 
Lack of control over the value chain reduces the actual recycling significantly. 
• 
Negative storytelling about plastic recycling decreases consumers’ sorting efforts.  
 
1.5 Value Chains and their stakeholders 
The Danish Environmental Protection Agency (Miljøstyrelsen, 2020) have in a recent study 
concluded that apart from development of recycling technologies, value chain (VC) 
collaboration of both private and public sector, is the most crucial prerequisite to improve 
==PAGE:14==
 
Research Clarification - Introduction 
 
 
Page 7 of 118 
the market for recycled plastic. This is partly due to of a fragmented VC, together with the 
absence of transparency and traceability (Hahladakis & Iacovidou, 2018). 
The VC collaboration will promote that collection, sorting and recycling is tailored to the 
needs in the downstream market. Vice versa, requirements from the recycling processes will 
be incorporated in design of new products. Concretely, sorting facilities can specialise in 
specific groups of polymers which will improve economy and quality of the recycling 
(Miljøstyrelsen, 2020). 
Such collaboration includes multiple stakeholders across the whole VC and require 
identification of the many factors to include and engage with, along with investigation of 
barriers and how they are overcome and the facilitation of the practical steps along the way 
(European Commission, 2014). 
Public-private partnerships (PPPs) are a way of incorporating both private and public 
partners to achieve a common goal. However, they require specific governance methods to 
be applied. If successful, they are praised for being a method for reaching better value for 
money, more innovation, better quality of results and sustainable performance (Warsen, 
Greve, Klijn, & Koppenjan, 2018).  
• 
Collaboration across the whole value chain is needed to improve collection, sorting 
and recycling of plastic. 
• 
Value chain collaboration require further research on how to engage with 
stakeholders, the barriers to overcome and the steps needed to facilitate the 
collaboration. 
• 
Public-private partnerships include partners from both public and private industry 
and is a means of reaching higher quality of the delivered service for the common 
benefit of all stakeholders.  
1.6 The Gap to Solve the Described Problem  
To solve the current challenges in the Danish recycling system for PCPW, more waste must 
be diverted from the incineration plants to recycling facilities. However, the low quality of 
the recycled material is a barrier for the demand of recycled plastic, leading to an immature 
recycling market. The current system has many holes due to lack of control over the value 
chain, which is why collaboration across the whole VC is required to reach a circular system 
that delivers high-quality recyclate to incentivise the industry to purchase more recycled 
plastic. 
The preceding research clarification has identified a gap in the knowledge realm of recycling 
systems, circular markets and value chain collaboration between public and private sector. 
A tool is needed to assist the development of collaboration in circular value chains to close 
this gap and to solve the identified problems in the Danish recycling system.

1.7 
Project Scope 
This research study investigates public-private partnerships between municipalities’ waste 
management departments and plastic packaging companies, in order to close the loop of 
specific plastic products, that currently end in household plastic waste. 
The study concludes in a framework addressed at Danish municipalities to identify and 
implement a value chain design for closed-loop recycling through public-private 
partnerships. 
PROBLEM STATEMENT 
“How can municipalities identify and engage in impactful public-
private partnerships (PPPs) with the packaging industry, to foster 
closed-loop recycling of plastic household waste?” 
Sub-questions 
• 
How can experience from existing recycling-related PPPs and the investigation of 
potential PPPs support decision making in future projects for circular economy (CE)? 
• 
What are the benefits for municipalities and their strategic partners, when entering 
these partnerships and what are the challenges? 
• 
How can a guide be created to support development and evaluation of partnerships 
between municipalities and the industry to foster closed loop recycling?

2.2 Literature Review Methodology 
In the research clarification phase an initial literature review is conducted to find evidence 
for the specific problem. The unsystematical approach shows limited, if not entirely absent, 
access to academic literature to the chosen topic; the combination of plastic recycling, 
circular economy value chains and public-private partnerships.  
Therefore, a systematic review (SR) is performed in order to gather and evaluate the 
available evidence in the different research fields using the methodology of Biolchini, Mian, 
Natali & Travassos (2007). The approach follows a structured protocol to collect and evaluate 
the literature in a replicable process, thus the research is less likely to include subjective bias 
from the researcher. 
The literature is synthesised in a meta-analysis, where the individual studies (PPP and CEVC) 
are compatibilized and merged to form one larger study.

and Result Analysis. Throughout the SR process, the findings are packaged and described in

2.3 Case Study Methodology 
Data from real cases are applied to this research in order to evaluate the knowledge found 
in the systematic literature reviews and to extract drivers/barriers of the selected cases. This 
is done similarly to an observational study, however the data is extracted from online 
accessible documents and supporting interviews to understand the gaps and findings from 
the documents (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009, p. 268).  
 
Figure 7: Overview of the steps in case studies 
The methodology used for the case studies follows guidelines from Hancock & Algozzine 
(2006). First, the desired outcomes must be identified; what questions do I want to find 
answers for in this case study. Second, what information do I need in order to answer that 
and from what sources. Third, the execution and lastly the analysis and discussion of results. 
OBJECTIVE OF THE CASE STUDIES 
The purpose of the case studies is to compliment the academic literature and fill out gaps and 
questions that have arisen during the literature reviews. Furthermore, they provide a 
practical perspective to the theoretical methodologies. Thus, the case studies seek to 
investigate and find answers in factors including; the organisation/system, the background 
of the project, the outcomes of the project, and/or the related drivers and barriers. These are 
formulated as research questions initially in the case study. 
DOCUMENTS COLLECTION AND RELIABILITY 
Academic studies on the specific cases are limited, hence “grey” literature (news articles, 
press releases and websites) is identified and analysed for the case studies. The documents 
are mainly internet sources, that often varies in quality and reliability. Therefore, at least 3 
different sources are identified for each of the cases in order to gain more representative 
data to cover the investigation. Data from the case company’s own website is used, but cannot

2.4 Framework Design Methodology 
The design stage of the project follows two design thinking methodologies; The Double 
Diamond and The Squiggle (Design Council UK, 2004; Newman, n.d.). The two models are 
relatively paradoxical but complement each other where the other fails. One being 
structured in its approach to design and rather related to the DRM methodology applied 
overall in this research project, while the other design model acknowledges the unorganised 
journey a design process often entails. 
THE DOUBLE DIAMOND 
 
Figure 8: The Double Diamond Design Process. Adapted from (Design Council UK, 2004) 
The Double Diamond process developed by Design Council UK (2004), is divided in four main 
stages that alternates between diverging and converging thinking. Based on personal 
experience with the methodology, this tool is a suitable guideline for a design process. 
However, it can also cause some confusion in the design team, as you often realise, that no 
process is that simple.  
==PAGE:23==
 
Research Clarification - Methodology 
 
 
Page 16 of 118 
 
Figure 9: Merge of DRM and Double Diamond 
First in the ‘Discover’ stage the design challenge is understood, rather than assumed. The 
researcher dives into the problem area and unfolds it along the way. In this project, the 
discovery phase includes the literature reviews and case studies, thus overlapping with the 
DRM methodology. 
Secondly, the ‘Define’ stage narrows down the findings through selecting a path for the 
design. The requirements here should be condensed into specific requirements that 
seemingly leave no room for innovation. In this project, the define stage is a synthesis and its 
specific framework recommendations. 
Third comes the ‘Develop’ stage where the solution space is diverging once again. Multiple 
directions are investigated and in this project this stage includes an ongoing development of 
framework designs. 
Lastly, the ‘Deliver’ stage starts to narrow down into one specific design solution which is 
then developed. In this project, the ‘Deliver’ stage is the graphical development of the 
framework illustrations and the written guide to assist the framework.  
THE DESIGN SQUIGGLE  
To complement the structured process model above, a contrasting model, The Squiggle, is 
added (Newman, n.d.). The model can, similarly to the Double Diamond, be applied to 
describe the whole design process, as it includes a research, concept and design phase and 
the focus narrows down along the way, as seen in Figure 10.

CEVC CO-DESIGN FOUNDATIONS (BLOMSMA ET AL., 2019) 
Blomsma, Pigosso and McAloone (2019) explore the foundations for a framework in CEVCs 
to be developed. It builds upon three theoretical perspectives, including resource-based view 
(RBV), resource dependency theory (RDT) and actor-network theory (ANT).  
The RBV seeks to understand how a focal organisation can create value through their internal 
resources; including assets like system, facilities, knowledge and materials.  
RDT is the holistic view of how the focal company is dependent on its suppliers and 
purchasers throughout its integrated ecosystem. Investigation of this helps to reduce 
uncertainty and risk by establishing collaborations between stakeholders in the network. 
ANT brings in an understanding of the interconnectedness of system actors, by drawing 
boundaries and investigating alignments and connections between the actors in the network.  
 
Figure 12: Circular Economy Value Chain framework.  
Adapted from (Blomsma et al., 2019) 
The theoretical framework in  is based on RBV, RDT and ANT. It is not integrated with specific 
industries or technical areas, which makes it applicable in many value chain cases, including 
PCPW. The framework process starts in the focal company with an RBV approach where CE 
opportunities in waste resources are identified and investigated. Subsequently, the shared 
value in the network is analysed, including trends, technologies, consumer behaviour, chain 
dynamics that hinder or accelerate CE and dyad, like supplier-company and company-

PLASTIC GREEN SUPPLY CHAIN MANAGEMENT FRAMEWORK (DESHMUKH & 
BORADE, 2019) 
Deshmukh and Borade’s (2019) framework for green supply chain management (GSCM) for 
small scale plastic processors is based on empirical data from Indian plastic companies. It 
addresses 19 KPIs to measure the performance of GSCMs within the focal company and 
implements a practical plan-do-check-adjust approach to each step in the framework. 
Interestingly, this methodology applies KPIs that usually are used to measure performance 
ad hoc or ex-post, but here they are involved in the planning process. The given KPIs are 
prioritised within the firm to support gap identification for the future development of the 
GSCM.

BARRIERS EXTRACTION 
Internal Motivation 
Circular initiatives and transformation must be approved and supported by top 
management. Internal strategies for CE are seen to increase the company’s comparative 
advantage (Gaur & Mani, 2018). If top management admits the benefits of a circular 
approach, then this commitment can be the most reliable driver for circular transformation 
within the organisation, partly because it motivates the individuals partaking the 
implementation (Bevilacqua et al., 2014).  
Human Resources 
Inadequate human resources with competencies in CE is a crucial problem for the 
transformation, which often results in a misunderstood attitude towards environmental 
issues (Bevilacqua et al., 2014). 
Economic Factors 
LCAs and consultancy in sustainable development are considered to be expensive activities, 
but many investments in CE and resource efficiency show financial returns over reasonable 
==PAGE:35==
 
Descriptive Study 1 - Systematic Review 1: Circular Economy Value Chains 
 
 
Page 28 of 118 
periods (Bevilacqua et al., 2014). Investment in environmental consultancy will, over time, 
strengthen the brand image and increase the company’s overall profit (Gaur & Mani, 2018).  
Stakeholder Communication 
Cross-functional coordination is crucial because CE and sustainable design overlap many 
knowledge fields; therefore it involves support from different functional areas internally and 
externally (Bevilacqua et al., 2014). Supply chains are complex systems, and even more so if 
stakeholders furthermore try to address environmental perspectives too, thus, collaboration 
must be effectively managed (Thaba, 2017). External stakeholders can be motivated by 
continuously showing progress like cost savings, carbon or energy reductions (Deshmukh & 
Borade, 2019).  
DRIVERS EXTRACTION 
Market Competition 
Remanufacturing and recirculation of resources are likely to decrease the effect of 
competition from other companies (Gaur & Mani, 2018).  
Economic Factors 
CEVCs require stronger supplier collaboration, which often leads to a stronger bargaining 
position. Thus, pricing and material management are more efficient (Thaba, 2017).  
Regulatory Mechanisms 
Proactive CEVC design and management will reduce the cost of regulatory compliance (Gaur 
& Mani, 2018). 
Demand 
Due to the environmental focus, the downstream stakeholders (customers, retailers, end-
users) start demanding sustainable products and information about the environmental 
performance of these (Bevilacqua et al., 2014). A new green portfolio will allow the firm to 
tap into new customer segments, thus increasing the market share (Gaur & Mani, 2018). 
Return of Resources 
Financial incentives for returning the used products will increase the quality and quantity of 
resources. Consumers’ accessibility and proximity influences the likelihood of returning 
resources (Gaur & Mani, 2018). 
Media Pressure 
Stakeholder collaboration will reduce the impact of coverage in media, general public and 
NGOs (Gaur & Mani, 2018).

GOVERNANCE ISSUES IN PPPS: A THEORETICAL PERSPECTIVE (DOLLA & LAISHRAM, 
2019) 
Dolla and Laishram suggest in their study of PPPs in municipal solid waste management that 
to understand and drive such PPPs network theory must be introduced. They apply 
networked governance theory, where the understanding of the partnership is that it is self-
regulating and self-organising. As a result, municipalities should not try to develop and 
control the whole value chain, but rather organising and incentivise it, i.e. bring together the 
segments of the value chain. The contracts to support the PPPs should be open-ended and 
based on tacit knowledge. The study concludes that due to a literature gap, a framework for 
networked governance of PPPs using this approach should be a priority, including 
conducting case studies based on such a framework.

This study develops a conceptual framework to understand and integrate sustainability into 
the procurement process of PPPs. The framework is then applied to analyse 25 case studies 
of existing PPPs. This study’s perception of PPPs is limited to the delivery of infrastructure 
services through long-term contracts, thus not directly applicable to the study of circular 
value chains.  
The sustainability foundation is inspired by the triple bottom line approach (3Ps: people, 
planet, profit), but the framework has also integrated indicators for transformative impact. 
Governance Instruments to stimulate sustainability: the procurer (often the public 
stakeholder) can use either formal or informal governance instruments. The formal is top-
down control mechanisms formulated as KPIs in the contract. The informal governance

PPP stages: design, build, finance, operate, maintain (DBFOM). Some PPPs only include some

sustainability criteria are challenging to measure due to their qualitative context, thus often 
left out, as there is no simple way to formalise the requirements in legal contracts as the 
procurer risk lawsuits by the losing consortia. Furthermore, the weighting of sustainability 
criteria is often relatively low in the award process. Thus, some bidders can leave out 
sustainability focus and still win the tender if providing a competitive price. The study 
emphasises a practice where a minimum score for each sustainability criteria is set. In those 
cases, the bidders are forced to include sustainability results in their bid to win it. Another 
related practise is to set a target price for the service; thus, all bidders are not competing on 
price but quality delivery in their biddings.

This study examines industrial symbiosis, previously referred to as CEVCs, promoted in PPPs 
in the region of TEDA, China. Benefits and challenges are discussed based on literature in this 
context. They find that governments or public institutions, hence their role as a network hub 
between many stakeholders, are the main force and thus should initiate and facilitate these 
CEVC PPPs. In TEDA, an NGO has set up an information platform where companies can 
exchange and transfer resources within the symbiosis network. Furthermore, some NGOs 
can certify products, services or organisations, to diminish distrust from users and other 
stakeholders. Various NGOs possess the power to link and support the application of such 
PPPs and should not be neglected in engaging continuous policy dialogue.  
Benefits of CEVC PPPs include the coordination between stakeholders that would generally 
be hesitant to engage in partnerships with external companies and NGOs. This can create, 
apart from new business opportunities, also political voices in sustainable production 
systems. 
Law and regulation in solid waste management is a barrier for many of these CEVCs, as they 
can be challenging to overcome and change. However, the bigger network of the CEVC paves 
the way for power emergence to change the regulation for the better.

PPPS – FOR AND AGAINST (YESCOMBE, 2007, CHAPTER 2) 
Some political concerns that may arise when establishing PPPs are identified and described 
by Yescombe in his book about financial and political perspectives in PPPs. The reasons for 
political debate include:  
• 
Private profit at the public’s expense. Thus, there must be a strong separation of 
regular public-sector procurements and actual PPPs. Also, it is essential to formulate the 
PPP so that the private participants do not make unrealistic high profits on the PPP. The 
business models’ revenues should be somewhat relatable to any B2B partnership.  
• 
Poor operating standards. There may be a debate on whether private partners would 
prioritise revenue over safety and quality. This concern strengthens the importance of a 
well-formulated contract and control. 
• 
Erosion of working conditions. Outsourcing some services to the private sector might 
cost some public workers their jobs. This loss of employment is a pitfall the public should 
be aware of or perhaps ensure that these workers are employed within the contract of 
the PPP. 
Generally speaking, the transparency conditions that public sectors typically work under, 
must be equally applied to the private partner as well to avoid political concerns and debate.

BARRIERS EXTRACTION 
The complexity of Large-scale Systems 
Public infrastructure projects in PPPs often entail large and complex systems like municipal 
solid waste management or electric vehicle charging infrastructure. These complex systems 
with long-term nature are challenging to govern (Dolla & Laishram, 2019; Qi et al., 2009; 
Zhang et al., 2019) 
Sustainability Barriers in Tender Processes 
The tender bidding phase is often financially focussed, thus diminishing the attention to 
sustainability. Furthermore, the sustainability performance is often complex to quantify and 
measure; thus it is qualitatively deliberated (Hueskes et al., 2017; Spraul & Thaler, 2019). 
Lack of Innovation Capacity in Public Sector 
Municipalities lack the competencies to conceptualise, and the management style often 
inhibits innovation, which leads to outdated or conventional projects in the initial tender 
process. (Ahmed & Ali, 2006). 
DRIVER EXTRACTION 
Sustainability Performance 
PPPs are praised for being a reliable model for building projects that have high sustainability 
performance due to the control from public entities and access to highly skilled technical 
people from the private sector (Qi et al., 2009; Spraul & Thaler, 2019). 
Access to Innovation Capacity 
The public sector can through PPPs tap into innovation processes, new technology and 
improved management from the private stakeholders in the project, thus leading to more 
impactful outcomes (Qi et al., 2009) 
Budget Benefits 
Public infrastructure in private sector collaboration broadens the access to funding through 
private capital, that can tap into both domestic and international funds. Thus, mitigating the 
municipalities economic burden by spreading it out during the lifetime of the partnership, 
instead of having to pay the cost upfront. This distribution of costs is beneficial in public 
budgets that often work on an annual timeframe (Yescombe, 2007; Zhang et al., 2019). 
Consequently, costs are reduced and stabilised over time, and the private sector ensures high 
work performance and cost-efficiency (Qi et al., 2009).

5.1 Introduction to case 
Copenhagen Municipality has initiated a project to increase circular economy of PCPW of 
meat and food trays. The project called “Partnership for Circular Food Trays” was initiated 
in 2019 and is a collaboration between many stakeholders both up- and downstream of the 
value chain of meat trays in Denmark.  
This specific case is selected due to the very direct relevance to this research project, because 
it views the municipality as the main stakeholder and initiator, PCPW is the circulating 
resource, it includes collaborations both up- and downstream the value chain, and it is a 
recycling project that close the loop rather than downcycling the resources. In fact, the case 
was an inspiration for this research project. 
In this case study the expected outcome is to understand the system and its stakeholders, the 
background for the project and which specific drivers/barriers that are related to the 
development of such project. This leads to the research questions that will be answered in 
this study: 
RQ1 
Which driver and barriers related to the partnership has been found? 
RQ2 
How and by whom was the project initiated? 
RQ3 
How is the CEVC system designed and how does the different stakeholders 
contribute to the system? 
RQ4 
What are the stakeholders’ benefits from the partnership? 
RQ5 
How does regulation impact the partnership?

5.2 Process of the case study 
The case study adheres to the methodology described in section 2.3. First, to give an overview 
of the partnership, the system design is described using public sources directly from the 
partnership initiator, Copenhagen Municipality and Amager Ressource Center (ARC). The 
system description will partly answer RQ3 and provide the initial understanding for further 
investigation of the case. 
Following the system description and illustration, the case study then dives deeper into RQ3 
and RQ4 and the other three research questions through document analysis. Further 
unanswered questions and gaps in the findings are elaborated through interviews with key 
participants. 
Lastly, all findings are listed, interpreted and concluded in relation to the research questions. 
5.3 The System Design 
The system stakeholder setup is extracted from a press release from all stakeholders (Amager 
Ressourcecenter, 2020), a press release from Danish Crown (Danish Crown, 2019), a press 
release from Copenhagen Municipality (Københavns Kommune, 2019) and a news article 
from Danish Radio (Danmarks Radio, 2019). 
The waste management company ARC that operates on behalf of Copenhagen Municipality, 
collects plastic waste from the citizens and transports it to their transloading station. Image 
recognition technologies developed by IHP Systems sort out PET meat trays from the total 
plastic fraction. The meat trays are baled (compressed) and shipped to the recycling facility 
4PET in the Netherlands, where they are washed and recycled into new pellets. The pellets 
are then transported to Faerch, a plastic converter in Western Denmark, where they 
reprocess the pellets into new meat trays. The meat trays are then transported to meat 
producer, Danish Crown, where it is used as packaging for new foods. The packaged foods 
are then sold in the supermarket chains REMA 1000 and Coop in Copenhagen. After use, it 
is the expectation that the citizens of Copenhagen sorts the waste into their designated 
recycling bins and the material loops within the same partnership.

SYSTEM INITIATION 
All statements identified in the documents that relate to the initiation of the deposit system 
are presented in Appendix M. 
In 1918, it became illegal to break glass bottles due to resource scarcity during World War I 
and in order to reduce energy consumption for production of new bottles. This was done to 
make sure that all bottles would be washed and reused by the breweries. In 1922 the 
breweries in Denmark initiated local collaborations on transport infrastructure to distribute 
the reusable bottles to the rightful brewery (Carlsberg, 2018). 
==PAGE:64==
 
Descriptive Study 1 - Case Study 2: Bottle Deposit System in Denmark 
 
 
Page 57 of 118 
In 1942, during World War II, Denmark was in resource shortage once again. A non-
compulsory national agreement leadled to the design of “the standard beer bottle” between 
the breweries (Carlsberg, 2018).  
This collaboration with washable glass bottles later added washable plastic bottles and the 
collaboration operated until the turn of the millennium, when EU legislation pushed 
Denmark to abolish the ban of non-refillable bottles and cans (as mentioned in section 
0.681853). Denmark had to introduce aluminium cans and at the same time, the government 
also introduced the deposit system to support recycling of the non-refillable containers. On 
the initiative of the Ministry for Environment, the breweries formed a not-for-profit 
organisation named Dansk Retursystem in 2002, which was granted the monopoly to operate 
the Danish Bottle Deposit System (Anker Andersen, 2019).

• 
Resource rationing during WWI initiated an informal collaboration between 
breweries in Denmark to distribute glass bottles between them to save transportation 
and to ensure the bottles were reused. 
• 
During WWII resource scarcity again drove the development of a standard beer bottle,

brewery to brewery.

The introduction of non-refillable bottles and cans that require recycling after every

THE CIRCULAR SYSTEM 
Statements related to the circular system are presented in Appendix K and are adapted to the 
actor-network flow chart in Figure 24. 
After filling beverage on the bottles, the beverage producer/importer transport the products 
to supermarkets, cafés and restaurants. Consumers buy the products and when they have 
consumed it the bottles are returned to the restaurant or in reverse vending machines in 
supermarkets. Big supermarkets have facilities to compress the bottles/cans, while glass is 
stored separately. From the retailers, Dansk Retursystem collects the empty bottles/cans and 
transport them to one of their two national facilities. Here they are registered, counted and 
sorted. Based on the counting, Dansk Retursystem refunds the deposit, that the supermarkets 
have paid back to the consumers (Anker Andersen, 2019). The materials are sorted in plastic, 
metal and glass. Plastic and metal is compressed and transported to other countries for 
recycling. Glass is transported to recycling facilities within Denmark (Dansk Retursystem, 
2020a).

The Circulating Material 
The bottle or aluminium can consumers are drinking from, possibly contain recycled 
material but not necessarily (Dansk Retursystem, 2020a). However, in this recycling system, 
the resources are kept in a closed loop. The high recyclate quality is ensured through efficient 
sorting and collaboration throughout the whole value chain of producers, importers, 
consumers and recycling facilities (Dansk Retursystem, 2020a).  
Economic Flows 
Beverage producers and importers can only sell in Denmark if they charge the additional 
deposit fee related to the selected container size. Once a month the producers must pay that 
share of deposits to Dansk Retursystem, but they will recollect that money when the retailers 
purchase the goods. All producers and importers must also pay a packaging fee for each 
deposit bottle they market. This is non-refundable and works like an environmental tax 
(Anker Andersen, 2019). 
Dansk Retursystem is funded through sign-up fees from producers, logistics fees and 
collection fees (Anker Andersen, 2019). Also, deposits from the approximately 10% bottles 
that are never returned are earned by Dansk Retursystem. 
Supermarkets receive a fee for handling the reverse vending machines. But also the 
supermarkets pay Dansk Retursystem a fee for the administration (Anker Andersen, 2019).

Statements related to the stakeholders’ benefits in the system are presented in Appendix L. 
The Danish bottle deposit system annually saves the environment 114.000 tons of CO2 
(Carlsberg, 2018), which is a benefit for all involved stakeholders. For the Danish government 
the CO2 savings contribute to the ongoing climate and recycling goals that are imposed on 
national and international level. 
From the consumers’ perspective, the system has tripled the available marketed beverage 
range; meaning that the introduction of the system lead to more varied products on the 
shelves (Anker Andersen, 2019). The 6000 reverse vending machines make it accessible for 
Danish consumers to easily return the bottles to be part of a robust recycling system.  
The supermarkets keep customers to their stores, as the reverse vending machines print a 
voucher that is often used in the store. Also, they are paid by Dansk Retursystem for 
collecting, sorting and handling the reverse vending machines (Carlsberg, 2018).  
The beverage producers have access to cheaper bottles produced by recycled material. But 
also pays an administrational fee to Dansk Retursystem for handling the post-consumer 
bottles. However, the more efficient the system becomes the cheaper the recycled resource 
and the cheaper administrational fee (Dansk Retursystem, 2020a). 
Dansk Retursystem are not-for-profit and cover their expenses from sign-up fees and 
administrational fees. The deposits from the 8% bottles that are never returned and thus 
never reimbursed to the consumers, are kept by Dansk Retursystem and spent on improving 
the reverse vending machines and funding social and environmental programmes (Anker 
Andersen, 2019).

All producers and importers of beverages must sign up for and use the system provided by

The Main Stakeholders 
Successful CEPPVCs require the collaboration of multiple stakeholders to reach beneficial 
impact financially and environmentally. The 7 Driving Forces for closed-loop supply chains 
by Gaur & Mani (2018) are mainly stakeholder related and include; internal drivers in the 
focal company, regulation from government, consumers as consumers, consumers as 
suppliers, NGOs and media, and competitors. The remaining driver is business opportunities, 
which is significantly implicated by the six stakeholders, as mentioned above. 
Correspondingly, the views of Bevilacqua et al. (2014) on sustainable VCs encompass 
equivalent stakeholders; however, this model is developed for sustainable design, not 
necessarily circular design, making it moderately complicated to apply on closed loops. Still, 
the model provides additional factors that are relevant to consider. Supplementary 
stakeholders include academia and consultants, along with various types of information 
flows that are indicated between the actors. 
Supporting Stakeholders 
The addition of environmental consultancy, general consultancy, and academic support in 
Bevilacqua et al.’s (2014) model proves applicable to the development of technology or 
innovation CEVCs, as the diverse competencies required may not exist internally within the 
firm. Concerning the network of case study 1, outsourcing tasks to consultancies is prevalent 
and has been used for data collection and competent technical feedback in the waste 
management system of Copenhagen (COWI, 2017). The addition of consumer and 
environmental organisations in the model from Bevilacqua et al. (2014) indicates 
furthermore the implications from indirect stakeholders and how they may influence the 
system. From case study 2 for instance, in 2008 the Danish Consumer Organisation 
(Forbrugerrådet) and the industry organisation for Danish breweries led the consultation for 
a governmental bill that would double the bottle deposit values (Folketinget, 2008). Thus, to 
predict the fluctuation in opinions on the system, leaders behind a CEPPVC should identify 
groups or organisations for relevant stakeholders to incorporate their viewpoints early on.

Criteria for Selecting Partners in Public Procurement 
Large scale partnerships initiated by public institutions must undergo a tender process to 
select a suitable private partner. Many criteria are evaluated, and often in light of financial

some technology-specific measures must be called for in the tender process. These include 
experience in the innovation field, technical competencies, and design plan for the project.

Resource Base View and Resource Dependency Theory 
As suggested by Blomsma et al. (2019) RBV and RDT are two appropriate theories to identify 
and organise stakeholders in a CEVC. Using RBV, the focal stakeholder is analysed in the 
perspective of what resources and assets they possess and how that can generate value thus 
revenue. Then using RDT the links in the VC are identified holistically, through questioning 
what is the organisation depending on to achieve the goals defined in RBV. Continuing this 
approach, one can analyse up- and downstream to identify the RBV and RDT of each 
stakeholder.

for sustainable packaging but also want to stay proactive with the future implementation of

recycled packaging, thus linking their RDT to producers like Faerch. Consumers respond to

People Participation 
Ahmed & Ali (2006) conclude in PPP management study that using facilitation agencies to 
include users and collaborators in the development stage leads to significantly improved 
access, insights and participation. An agency should be consulted or hired as municipalities 
often lack the capacity or human resources to do so themselves. However, the agencies often 
lack the technical knowledge of the specific system. In contrast to the benefits of user 
participation, Bhan and Doorneweert (2014) conclude that user workshops might cause 
assumptions to drive decision making and abstract solutions. But if successful, the systems 
are more empathic and holistic.

•

7.3 First steps 
A starting point for developing CEPPVCs in waste management is to know the size and 
composition of the waste fraction, understand its origin and impacts (Serageldin et al., 1995). 
Then awareness must be created for the implicated system stakeholders, including citizens, 
policymakers and private industry. Existing governmental regulation along with industry 
culture and standards must be investigated, firstly to be compliant but also to stay proactive 
with coming industry trends and regulation in government’s pipeline (Spraul & Thaler, 2019). 
Keeping compatible with these factors does not only protects against lawsuits but also 
improves trust, shared understanding and communication between the stakeholders of the 
system.  
Self-regulation in the food packaging industry 
In case study 1, it is found that the project was initially driven by industry self-regulation, 
where producers decided to “claim” PET as the food-related polymer to comply with safety

regulations for food packaging. Selecting one polymer for food packaging and not using it for 
other purposes, makes it easier to control and comply with the law that states that the 
recycled plastic for food packaging must also originate from food packaging in its previous 
use case. Furthermore, the project is run as a pilot to be proactive with the implementation 
of EPR regulation in Denmark. 
Regulation and crisis to initiate 
Case study 2 shows that government regulation and industry self-regulation was a primary 
initiator for the bottle deposit system. The equally strong powers in government and industry 
have influenced each other over time, driving both governmental regulation and the 
industry organisation of the bottle system. Moreover, two turning points in the establishment 
of the bottle systems were driven by WWI and WWII and resource scarcity. The growing 
focus on resource scarcity and energy overconsumption today during the climate crisis is 
similar to war times driving innovation, technology and regulation. 
Recommendations for framework development 
• 
Measure the size of the waste fraction, know its origin and impacts. 
• 
Investigate existing regulation to comply with and how to be proactive with future 
legislation. 
• 
Understand industry self-regulation (standards) to stay compliant with, and to 
increase trust and shared understanding with private stakeholders. 
7.4 Developing a partnership 
The framework developed by Blomsma et al. (2019) illustrates that the development of the 
CEVC starts internally in the focal company and over time, require more participation from 
co-designers. When the waste resource is identified, an exploration of opportunities for 
reprocessing the resource can start. In the case of municipal PCPW, it is desired to close the 
loop. Thus, the investigation step is redundant as the waste should be directed back to its 
original form through reprocessing. Subsequently, more stakeholders are involved, and 
alignment of interests start along with the investigation of how the circular goal can be 
reached while all stakeholders benefit. However, the earlier other stakeholders are 
introduced, the higher probability of reaching sustainability on a broader level (people, 
planet, profit), as the perspectives of the diverse stakeholders expand the scope of the project 
(Hueskes et al., 2017). Deshmukh and Borade (2019) suggest that KPIs used by the 
stakeholders to monitor performance during operation are employed early on to align 
interests and reach mutual benefits in the development phase. 
Case study 1 reveals how stakeholder development is a continuous journey that changes 
along the way. The project was initiated with political assistance from the NGO, Plastic 
Change, who are still a close partner of the project, but not considered a direct stakeholder 
any longer. Initially, the partnership counted Copenhagen Municipality and Rema 1000 but 
evolved to include Danish Crown and Coop as well.  
==PAGE:77==
 
Prescriptive Study - Synthesis of Case Studies and Literature Reviews 
 
 
Page 70 of 118 
Starting with the focal company and working your way up and down the value chain is 
suggested by Jain et al. (2018). Strategic alliances, information systems and the overall plan 
are connecting the whole value chain. At the same time, each stakeholder (suppliers and 
customers) form an individual strategic plan to make and deliver or purchase and dispose of 
the circulating resource. 
The procurer or initiator of the project can decide a formal governance strategy monitored 
through KPIs or informal governance based on mutual trust and interdependence, which is 
recommended for networks (Hueskes et al., 2017). By introducing networked governance 
theory to the approach, the partnership is perceived as self-organising, thus self-governed 
(Dolla & Laishram, 2019). As a result, municipalities should not try to develop and control the 
whole value chain, but instead organising and incentivise it, i.e. bring together the segments 
of the value chain. 
Recommendations for framework development 
• 
Start developing from the viewpoint of the focal company with a waste resource and 
include more stakeholders over time as the system evolves.  
• 
Investigate what technical and infrastructural processes are needed to recycle the 
waste back into its original form and quality. 
• 
Introduce partners along the way. 
• 
Discussing the alignment of interests between the partners requires stakeholders to 
look into their internal KPIs to ensure that the partnership is beneficial for all. 
• 
For circular and networked PPPs, it is recommended to use an informal governing 
strategy to foster trust and collaboration between the stakeholders. 
7.5 Prevent Political Commotion 
PPPs are often subject to much media attention and political debate, as taxpayers’ money 
funds them. Some consequences to be aware of are the idea that a private company is 
profiting at the public’s expense and companies prioritise revenue over quality and safety 
(Yescombe, 2007, Chapter 2). These situations are averted through making sure the private 
profit is equal to any comparable B2B transaction, and adequate control and governance 
ensures quality and safety.  
Recommendations for framework development 
• 
Ensure that the profit for private partners is equal to market values 
• 
Enforce quality and safety in the service delivered by the private partner through 
governance control. 
7.6 Sustainable PPPs 
Sustainability is a central focus of this research project, and circular economy is a means of 
reaching more sustainable production systems. Berrone et al. (2019) present a framework to 
evaluate the sustainability performance of PPPs based on six factors; (1) engagement of 
stakeholders, (2) access, (3) scalability and replicability, (4) inclusiveness, (5) economic 
==PAGE:78==
 
Prescriptive Study - Synthesis of Case Studies and Literature Reviews

impact, and (6) resilience and environment. However, the perspectives in the framework can 
also be applied for ex-ante development of sustainable PPPs. Berrone et al. consider 
sustainability as an optional input or a missing link in the current PPP development.  
In contrast, Cheng et al. (2020) regard the new generation PPPs as a driver for sustainability 
and not vice versa. The driving force behind the PPP development is sustainability, and the 
outcome is environmental infrastructure. As per the framework, the sustainability-oriented 
PPPs require user-participation, and the processes are founded on the triple bottom line; 
people, planet, profit. 
Sustainability requirements must be thoroughly described and quantified in the tender 
process, thus priming the mindsets of the bidders and becoming an aspect that bidders 
compete on (Hueskes et al., 2017). These requirements must have a minimum limit along 
with their weighing in comparison to other criteria like price. The minimum level of 
sustainability performance negates the chance of a bidding winner to have zero 
sustainability performance but the most competitive price.  
Recommendations for framework development 
• 
Look into Hueskes et al. (2017) to find a full list of sustainability KPIs. 
• 
Actively add quantifiable sustainability KPIs to the PPP tender advert and set 
minimum levels for each of them.  
• 
Promote user-participation to increase social sustainability. 
7.7 Theoretical basis 
The combination of RBV and RDT proves helpful in understanding value chains and the 
interconnectedness between the stakeholder. However, particular limitations within these 
theories include that RBV does not clarify what value specifically entails and only focusses 
on value creation within the focal organisation and leaves out shared ownership and shared 
risk (Blomsma et al., 2019). Furthermore, RBV is a static theory, that does not allow 
understanding of changes in the “rules of the game” within an industry. RDT, on the other 
hand, seeks to understand the broader context of a value chain by bundling companies and 
resources, which makes the holistic perception notably simplistic. Power and influence (Gaur 
& Mani, 2018) from stakeholders outside the value chain are neglected, which leads to a lack 
of consideration of essential factors.  
ANT is the missing puzzle that enables the RBV and RDT to be synthesised into a three-
dimensional theory that can be linked to the topic of CEVCs and PPPs, by including relevant 
external stakeholders and their influence on the system (Bevilacqua et al., 2014, p. 271) 
Applying a VC methodology of the SCOR model (Jain et al., 2018) overlaps with the RBV and 
RDT, as the ‘plan’ and ‘deliver’ steps are resource depended while ‘make’ is compatible with 
the value creation of RBV.  
Inspired by the four theoretical corners of the framework developed by Keivanpour et al. 
(2015), RBV and RDT cannot stand alone in the perception of PPPs. A technical view on the 
value chain must also be incorporated to include the correct internal and external factors 
==PAGE:79==
 
Prescriptive Study - Synthesis of Case Studies and Literature Reviews 
 
 
Page 72 of 118 
influenced by the technical details of polymer recycling. The additional theoretical corners 
include sustainability in a broader context. Thus, inspired by Hueskes et al. (2017) the triple 
bottom line approach to sustainability is included in the theoretical basis, to foster 
sustainability for all. 
Recommendations for framework development 
• 
RBV and RDT are applied to understand the role of each individual stakeholder and 
their link to the adjacent in the VC.  
• 
ANT includes internal and external factors (actors in a broader sense), to understand 
all the externalities that influence the VC. 
• 
The SCOR model and its view on connectedness of the stakeholders, and the 
representation of a VC is applied to simplify the overview of the VC. 
• 
A technical foundation (polymer recycling context) is equally significant in the 
creation of such framework.  
• 
The Triple Bottom Line (people, planet, profit) is applied to shed light on diverse 
sustainability challenges.

9.1 Introduction 
This framework offers an approach for municipalities to scan and establish closed loop value 
chains of post-consumer plastic waste in collaboration with the industry. Rather than 
recycling all plastic waste in one fraction, specific products are targeted and separated from 
the waste stream, then diverted back into the material supply of that same product. 
The framework is based on analytical data from systematical literature reviews and case 
studies, which provides a step-by-step guide supported with drivers, barriers and 
recommendations that supports the decision-making in the development process. 
The framework results in a public-private partnership that can be initiated by a municipality 
or a group of municipalities to increase power and impact. Though the framework assumes 
ignition from the public sector, it can also happen on the initiative of the private sector. In 
that case, the framework needs some adaption, which is not accounted for in its first version.

Throughout this framework guide, each step is related to a graphical representation, as seen 
in Figure 34. The framework illustration consists of multiple jigsaw puzzles that together 
form a circular value chain. The connectors of the puzzle pieces represents the 
supply/demand relation between the implicated stakeholders. The whole circle exemplifies 
the life cycle of the circulating product including all the stakeholders it journeys through 
from cradle to cradle.

KNOWING THE MUNICIPAL RECYCLING SYSTEM 
In order to overcome the barriers and leverage on the aforementioned drivers, first you need 
to find answers on the following questions 
What are the sorting criteria for the plastic waste? 
Why: In the investigation of the plastic waste, it is useful to know exactly how the 
citizens are instructed to sort the plastic. This has an impact on the access to plastic 
and whether it consists of rigid plastic only or also includes plastic films. Furthermore, 
it is relevant to know how long the sorting criteria have existed. This has an impact 
on how accustomed the citizens are to sort the waste, which influences how much and 
how well the plastic is sorted. 
How: The sorting criteria are easy to investigate. They can often be found at the 
municipality’s website. If your municipality currently do not offer plastic recycling it 
is likely to change, as the Danish Government is introducing a nationwide recycling 
scheme fully implemented by 2030.  
What is the journey of the plastic waste? 
Why: Often municipal waste trucks have multiple compartments and require a 
reloading station where similar fractions are baled up and transported to their 
respective recycling companies. You will need to decide whether it is possible to 
isolate the selected product in this station, or if it needs to be done by the recycling

Step 5: The Missing Links in a Circular Value Chain 
Now, we will turn back to the visual illustration of the stakeholders in the value chain. Notice 
how each puzzle piece have an input, a process (the cogs), and an output. 
 
Figure 39: Framework step 5 - understanding input, process and output 
Input: each stakeholder will receive the circulating product in various forms. Examples of 
input forms include a retail product, a waste fraction, a granulated plastic, plastic pellets, etc. 
Obviously, each stakeholder have many other inputs, but mainly the circulating resource is 
important for the overall value chain development. 
Process: the stakeholders’ roles in the value chain are to transform inputs to outputs, which 
is done through their internal processes. Some common stakeholder examples are given 
below: 
• 
Recyclers receive waste fractions and through washing, sorting and granulating 
processes, they resell recycled plastic pellets downstream. 
• 
Converters purchase plastic pellets and through thermo-shaping processes turning them 
into products like containers, films, bags, etc that can be sold to make profit. 
• 
Producers of consumer goods purchase plastic packaging from converters, which they 
wrap or fill their products in, before selling it to retailers like wholesale businesses. 
• 
Retailers and wholesalers purchase, distribute and sell the products until it has been 
distributed down to the consumers.

• 
Consumers purchase and consume the product before expectedly sorting the waste in 
the designated recycling bin. 
• 
The municipality collects the waste and distribute it to recycling companies, whom 
generally are paid to handle the waste. 
Output: after the resource has been processed by the individual stakeholders, it is either sold 
or sorted as waste.  
From recyclers down the value chain to consumers, the circulating resource is being sold 
with profit.  
Consumers pay a fee to the municipality in exchange for the waste management service.

9.2 Limitations to the framework 
The framework is developed on the basis of circular value chains and public-private 
partnerships with respect to recirculate post-consumer plastic waste. Thus, other waste 
categories are not applicable to the framework. Furthermore, the system is seen in a Danish 
context, with typical stakeholders from the Danish recycling system and national regulation. 
Without framework reiteration in a foreign context, the framework cannot be directly 
applied for circular value chains in other countries. The applicability of the framework is to 
support stakeholder collaboration and establishment of the circular value chains. Thus, it is 
limited from the further steps of contracting, tender processes and governing strategies. 
However, some identified recommendations for these are included. Lastly, the framework is 
based on literature studies and case studies, and as it is newly developed, it is yet to be tested 
in realistic settings.

11 Discussion of the Framework 
With the overall scope and objective of the research project in mind, the methodology, 
process and results are critically assessed. 
The framework presented in section 9 provides an approach for municipalities to scan and 
establish closed loop VCs of PCPW in collaboration with the industry. It is intended to support 
the holistic improvements to the current recycling system, which is necessary to increase the 
quality of the output, thus encouraging a new market demand for recycled resources. If 
successful, the framework has assisted the initiator to include the suitable stakeholders, 
overcome identified barriers and elaborate on relevant factors.  
11.1 Necessity of external Factors 
The framework developed by Bevilacqua et al. (2014), includes internal and external 
stakeholders. However, Bevilacqua et al. have given the exact type of stakeholder influence 
in advance; environmental information, lack of environmental information, and consultancy 
information. These information flows might be the most significant, but the approach limits 
the investigation of other influences. From the descriptive study 1, other identified factors 
include regulation, political power and media attention. The latter impose significant impact 
on the system, e.g. when negative media attention about the Danish recycling system was 
seen to increase scepticism towards recycling, thus decreasing the recycling efforts by the 
citizens. Many unforeseen aspects might impose the same significance to the value chain, 
some of which are covered in the developed framework. More will eventually arise, when 
the framework is tested in reality. 
11.2 Sustainability Aspects are not Default in Literature 
Interestingly, the literature review of CEVCs provided profound sustainability attention in 
relation to circular economy. But due the studies’ heavy focus on circularity, most of which 
left out other crucial sustainability perspectives, including consideration to human capital 
and economic sustainability. PPP literature on the other hand, seems to have undergone 
more holistic sustainability considerations, which contributed to the inclusion of the triple 
bottom line approach and diverse sustainability KPIs in the framework. 
11.3 Technical Foundation 
During the synthesis phase, I realised that technical polymer knowledge is equally important 
in the establishment of VCs in the plastic industry. Due to time constraints and the need to 
move forward to the development phase, polymer theory was not thoroughly included. As a 
mechanical engineer, I possess technical knowledge on polymer processing, which I relied 
on to fill this gap. As a result, I might be biased on deciding which polymer factors are 
relevant to describe and which are not. A more systematical approach, would diminish my 
personal bias and likely benefit the overall research, especially for non-technical readers. 
As highlighted in the research clarification, GHG emissions can be significantly reduced by 
improving the current recycling efficiency, and PCPW is pointed out to be responsible of a 
==PAGE:116==

twelfth of this contribution. Perhaps, if the framework was developed in a more generic and 
flexible manner, it could tackle other material streams like electronics, bio or metal waste. 
This would require the substitution of polymer theory with another technical foundation. 
Further research on the framework, could extend the applicability to support closed loop 
recycling in other industries and minimise their respective GHG emissions. 
11.4 Value Chains in B2B Contexts 
One can imagine that waste within one private company could circulate into a VC, where 
another company uses that as input. In that case, the resource is likely not post-consumer, 
the partnership is not public-private and the VC in probably not closed-loop, thus the 
adaptation and use of this framework in such context would lead to significant uncertainties. 
Yet, the framework does include multiple B2B links in the VC, such as recycler to converter, 
converter to producer, and producer to retailer, but the presence of a municipal waste 
management stakeholder is essential for the closed loop to be successful. 
11.5 Deduction to other Countries 
The framework is based on a Danish context, with Danish regulatory requirements and the 
standard Danish system for municipal waste management, including the sorting 
requirements, the fragmented value chain and market barriers. As the users of the 
framework are encouraged to investigate external factors themselves, the framework could 
potentially be applied to other countries with similar recycling systems. However, many of 
the barriers to overcome and drivers to leverage on, must be evaluated according to the 
translated region.  
11.6 Increased Focus on Recycling in Denmark 
The Danish government recently announced, that all 98 municipalities must from 2021 
initiate the development of systems for PCPW recycling and have it fully implemented by 
2025. Along with the introduction of packaging EPR the same year, there will be a significant 
need for knowledge and guidance for establishing robust and efficient VCs for PCPW. The 
framework presented in this study is partly developed on learnings from Copenhagen, where 
citizens have been sorting waste since 2013. From this framework, non-recycling 
municipalities can leapfrog and establish high-quality recycling collaborations from the very 
beginning. 
11.7 Communication Abstract Theories 
In consideration of diverse and potentially non-academic readers, the framework is 
communicated in a simple way that makes it comprehendible. This led to challenges in the 
development phase, as I needed to focus the extensive synthesis and abstract theories into a 
simpler wording. This was possible with support of the graphical representation. An example 
is the synthesis of RBV, RDT and the SCOR model, all extracted from SRs. To communicate 
these abstract theories, I translated them to the input-process-output approach described in 
step 5 in the framework and illustrated in Figure 49. In extension, I provided examples of 
simple input-process-output analysis of common stakeholders in the VC.

12 Conclusions 
This study investigates public-private partnerships between municipalities’ waste 
management departments and plastic packaging companies, in order to close the loop of 
specific plastic products, that currently end up in household plastic waste. The study 
concludes in a framework addressed at Danish municipalities to identify and implement a 
value chain design for closed-loop recycling. 
Plastic and GHG Emissions 
The plastic industry is currently responsible for 4% of the global GHG emissions and is 
expected to reach 15% by 2050. The vast majority of these emissions are emitted in the 
production of the plastic resin. In the EoL treatment of plastic, incineration is the most 
polluting alternative but also the most prevalent in EU and Denmark. Switching over to 
recycling, decreases this impact by a factor 3.6 as it displaces the emission-heavy plastic 
production stage. 
Current National Status on Recycling 
The present status on recycling, indicates that 18% of plastic world-wide is recycled, while 
the number is 30% and 41% for EU and Denmark respectively. However, less than half of this 
will in reality become new products, as much of the plastic is discarding throughout the value 
chain. In Denmark, recycling of Post-Consumer Plastic Waste (PCPW) is pointed out by the 
government to potentially decrease the waste sector’s emissions by a twelfth, which is 
prioritised to reach the national goal of 70% GHG reduction by 2030.  
The Recycling System in Copenhagen Municipality 
The PCPW recycling system in Copenhagen Municipality starts in the households where 
citizens source-separate the waste in designated bins. Municipal garbage trucks curb-side 
collect the bins and transport it to a transloading station, where a bigger fraction is 
consolidated from multiple trucks. The plastic fraction is compressed into dense bales and 
loaded on trucks that transport it to the contracted Material Recovery Facility (MRF) in either 
Sweden or Germany. Here, non-polymer items are removed, and the plastic is separated into 
various polymer type fractions like LDPE, HDPE, PP, and PET, that are granulated and 
pelletised. The pellets are sold to converters that use the plastic in their production.  
The quality of the recycled plastic is so low, that the MRFs are paid to take the waste 
resources, and their purchasers use and downcycle it in low quality products. The low quality 
of the plastic inhibits it to displace its original use case and the low value prevent investments 
in optimising the recycling system. The lack of control over the value chain is shown to 
reduce the recycling significantly. 
Opportunities for Improving the Recycling System 
Public-private partnerships (PPPs) and collaboration across the whole value chain are 
needed to increase collection, sorting and recycling of plastic. However, such collaboration

requires further research, which have been addressed in this study and results in a 
framework for such partnerships to be established.  
Methodology 
Systematical literature reviews are conducted on the topics of CEVCs and PPPs, along with 
two case studies of circular value chain partnerships. In total, 32 studies have been analysed 
and extracted, leading to 13 frameworks, 20 drivers and 11 barriers that are synthesised into 
specific recommendations for successful circular value chains to be established. The 
theoretical foundation is a synthesis of RBV, RDT, the triple bottom line and the SCOR model. 
The synthesised recommendations are applied in the framework development phase, to 
design a framework that supports municipalities in developing CEPPVCs, to recirculate 
targeted products from the PCPW stream. The framework is tested through simulation in a 
theoretical case, and the learnings led to a reiteration of the framework. 
The main problem statement is answered in the final presentation of the framework 
“How can municipalities identify and engage in impactful public-
private partnerships (PPPs) with the packaging industry, to foster 
closed-loop recycling of plastic household waste?” 
The final framework consists of a 10-step guide on how to identify and select producers, 
converters, and recyclers to assist in the reprocessing of PCPW and benefit from the shared 
advantages that incentivise the mutual collaboration. A theoretical approach called ‘input-
process-output’ is presented to understand and identify additional stakeholders than the 
ones mentioned above. External factors that influence the system are investigated, so the 
stakeholders can act accordingly to these externalities. Barriers are identified and the 
framework guide allows the stakeholders to overcome these. Recommendations for product 
redesign are put forward for the producers and converters to adapt the targeted product to 
the new system requirements. Lastly, sustainability KPIs are presented for inspiration to 
ensure a holistic approach to sustainability in the value chain development. 
12.2 Limitations in the process  
From the research clarification, a literature gap was identified, pin-pointing the necessity of 
two comprehensive literature reviews, that in combination, would cover this gap and 
support answering the problem statement. Thus, due to the comprehensiveness, these 
systematical reviews were more time consuming than initially planned. 
Throughout this study, it has been a challenge to engage with external stakeholders. 
Paradoxically, since the framework concludes and emphasises the importance of stakeholder 
inclusion. However, early in the project clarification, the research study was planned 
according to the CoViD-19 lockdown, thus leaving no time for fieldwork. As a result, the 
framework lacks real-life testing and evaluation, which is a critical factor for the success of 
the outcome. On the contrary, the comprehensive analytical research has provided a strong 
theoretical background for the further improvement of such frameworks.

Closing Considerations - Conclusions

Combining Design Research Methodology and the Double Diamond did result in some 
overlaps; however, the two methodologies were compatible and complimented each other. 
The DRM provided a structured overview of the overall study, while the Double Diamond 
added the notion of divergent and convergent thinking, which was helpful in the design 
phase. The addition of The Squiggle methodology to the design phase, was merely used 
descriptively rather than prescriptively. Yet, it served a useful purpose of explaining the 
process the framework development, and why it can be challenging to document such 
processes. 
12.3 Further Work 
The framework application is limited from other waste fractions than plastic, B2B value 
chains, or the application in other countries. To overcome these limitations, further research 
and testing must be conducted and reiterated in the study.  
Communicating abstract and technical data was shown to be challenging, thus the 
framework would be significantly improved if communicated through a booklet, video or 
interactive interface. 
Lastly, the lack of user testing is the biggest uncertainty regarding the success of the 
framework. Therefore, the framework should be tested with real users in either a simulated 
or real-life case.

4PET recycling facility 
o The 4PET facility is located in The Netherlands. 
o Faerch is currently not considering a local 4PET in Denmark, as the PCPW 
market [post-consumer plastic waste] is still too small. But there might be 
a business case for the combined Scandinavian countries.  
o Faerch does not strictly buy their own PET back through 4PET. They also 
buy rPET from other resellers when they buy raw materials. 
o The process for trays is slightly different from bottles. The multi material 
film must be removed. 
• 
Why PET is a great material 
o rPET is a good substitute for virgin PET. If the system is there, the rPET is 
cheaper. This is why Faerch has been using rPET long before it was a 
branding opportunity. Actually, using rPET was a secret for many 
packaging manufacturers previously, because it used to signal poor 
quality. Now it is a branding opportunity. 
o PET is a great material for recycling and food packaging.  
o Polyolefins (PE and PP) are degrading through the recycling process.  
==PAGE:145==

o Also, polyolefins are different on a chemical level as a result of the 
specific process that shaped them. Eg. PE that has been extruded can be 
different from PE that was injection molded. PET on the other hand is 
homogeneous.  
o The raw material of rPET is the exact same in all of Europe. 
o Where PET is mostly used in food packaging products, polyolefins are 
used in a broad context of products. Therefore, it is difficult to predict the 
additives in the material. 
o There is approximately 3 kg PET bottles per 1 kg PET trays.

Recycling is nothing new. In production facilities, scrap polymer has always

facility.

A History of Resource Rationing 
• 
In 1918 it became illegal to break a glass bottle, due to resource scarcity during 
World War I and in order to reduce energy consumption (in new production). This 
was done to make sure that all bottles would be washed and reused. In 1922 the 
breweries in Denmark started making local collaborations on transport 
infrastructure to share the reusable bottles. (Carlsberg, 2018) 
• 
In 1942 during World War II, Denmark was in resource shortage once again. A 
non-compulsory national agreement lead to the design of “the standard beer 
bottle” between the breweries. (Carlsberg, 2018) 
EU Competition and Aluminium Cans 
• 
Non-refillable beverage containers was very regulated for environmental reasons, 
and the use of aluminium cans was completely banned in Denmark, as Denmark 
used washable plastic and glass bottles instead. The ban of aluminium cans 
violated EU competition laws, so Denmark had to introduce aluminium cans. At 
the same time, the government also introduced the deposit system to support the 
aluminium cans. until the introduction of Dansk Retursystem in 2002. (Anker 
Andersen, 2019) 
• 
The new regulations were enacted in 2000 (Reloop Platform & CM Consulting, 
2016) and implemented in 2002 (Reloop Platform & CM Consulting, 2016) when 
Dansk Retursystem A/S was established on the initiative of the Ministry of the 
Environment to implement, administer and operate the deposit/refund system in 
Denmark. (Anker Andersen, 2019).
