Abstract
The background of this thesis focuses on the important role injection moulding has in mass-
producing plastic components.
This process relies heavily on efficient and effective cooling
systems to ensure both product quality and environmental efficiency. The objective of the
thesis was to design and implement a Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) digital twin of
Mouldflo’s Flow Monitoring System (FMS). This system is used to observe various properties
of the coolant injected into the mould. For the CFD simulation, the software STAR CCM+ is
used. This software offers a flexible solution for solving fluid and solid continuum mechanics
problems. This software handles heat transfer and turbulent fluid dynamics simultaneously.
The project uses two benchmarks for validations to ensure the accuracy of the simulations.
Both benchmarks were validated to a satisfactory level and were subsequently integrated into
the final digital twin.
The report concludes that the digital twin accurately replicates the FMS. However, while the
FMS continuously monitors fluid properties, the digital twin is restricted to operating for a
single cycle. The digital twin compensates for this by providing significantly more data than
the FMS. For example, while the FMS offers data on coolant properties at the manifold, the
digital twin can provide this information directly within the mould. This makes the digital twin
a reliable tool for optimising cooling processes in injection moulding, though certain aspects
still require further investigation. Its practical benefits include the potential to reduce produc-
tion cycle times, enhance product quality, and contribute to more sustainable manufacturing
practices.

Chapter1
Introduction
Injection moulding is a manufacturing process that allows for mass production of plastic com-
ponents with precision and consistency. The original injection moulding production process was
developed in the late 1800s, but the environmental impact was not a major factor considered
back then. The process has thus faced large criticism regarding its huge energy consumption.
Other criticisms of the process include the environmental impact of creating virgin plastics from
fossil fuels and the disposal of single-use products into landfills. Injection moulding businesses
is also, part of the manufacturing sector which contributes to 20% of global carbon emissions
due to the use of hydraulic oil and energy-intensive machines. However, these machines are
moderately being replaced with more efficient electric machines. On the other hand injection
moulding also offers certain environmental benefits. Compared to other moulding processes,
it is more energy efficient and produces less waste.[1] By optimising the injection moulding
process, manufacturers can further minimise waste from defective parts, reducing the need to
re-run cycles or rework deformed parts to meet quality standards. These corrective measures,
would otherwise consume additional energy and increase the carbon footprint.[2]
An example of an injection moulding machine is illustrated in Figure 1.1 and 1.2 where the
first step is filling the Hopper with the plastic granules. The plastic will then start filling the
barrel containing a reciprocating screw with 3 zones of heating bands. These zones contain the
following: 1st zone - granules, 2nd zone - transitioning and the 3rd zone - melted. As the screw
rotates it pushes the melting granules forward until ready to be pushed through the nozzle and
into the mould cavity. The mould which is designed with cooling channels will be cooling the
hot plastic until it has reached a temperature where it can be ejected without damaging the
component’s quality.[3]

The process to ensure the desired level of quality includes filling, holding, applying pressure,

An efficient cooling system is therefore crucial for successful injection moulding. Properly de-
signed cooling systems can reduce production time and costs while ensuring uniform cooling
in each cycle, which prevents distortion of the plastic products. Unfortunately, a poorly con-
structed cooling system will have the opposite effect. According to experiments, the heat that
enters the injection mould from the melt is roughly distributed in two parts, 5% of which is
transferred to the atmosphere through radiation and convection, and the remaining 95% is
conducted from the melt to the mould.[6] With 95% being conducted from the melt to the
mould, it is highlighted that controlling the temperature of the melt is essential to controlling
the injection moulding process.
1.1
Flow Monitering System
To allow producers to investigate their moulding processes, monitoring of critical parameters
is essential. For this purpose the Flow Monitoring System (FMS) was developed by Mouldflo.
This system effectively observes critical parameters essential for injection moulding, such as
temperature, pressure, and flow rate within the cooling system. This system provides essential
data used to evaluate production performance, enabling users to optimize production efficiency,
reduce energy consumption, and protect moulds from unnecessary wear or damage.
The FMS is highly versatile and easy to install, making it compatible with almost any type
of injection moulding machine. To accommodate various production requirements, the sys-
tem supports various types of manifolds, which are pipes designed to either distribute flow
from a single inlet to multiple outlets or converge flow from multiple inlets into one outlet.
These manifolds can be electronically connected to provide the necessary number of cooling
channels. These manifolds are available in configurations with 2, 4, 8, or 12 channels, offering
flexibility for different scales of operation. They are manufactured using either aluminum or
stainless steel, with the material selection depending on the required flow rate and operating
temperature. This flexibility allows manufacturers to expand cooling capacity by simply adding
manifolds as production demands increase. The FMS automatically identifies newly connected
manifolds and displays them on an accompanying touch panel screen for seamless integration
and control. Figure 1.4 and Figure 1.5 shows an 8-zone manifold and the FMS touch panel
interface, respectively.
Figure 1.4: 8-zone Manifold[7].
Figure 1.5:
Flow monitoring system touch
panel[7].

Manifolds are designed for straightforward placement on injection moulding machines and are
equipped with advanced sensors developed by Grundfos for accurate flow, pressure and tem-
perature measurement. The sensors are strategically placed to measure pressure in both the
inlet and outlet manifolds. This setup enables adjustments to the pump settings that drive
the coolant flow. Temperature and flow sensors are located on the manifold that receives the
coolant after it has passed through the mould. These sensors monitor whether the fluid re-
turns with the expected velocity, otherwise could indicate potential blockages in the mould.
Additionally, the temperature readings can reveal if the fluid returns hotter than expected,
suggesting a possible break in the system. Such a break could cause the heated polymer to
leak, warranting immediate investigation and corrective action.
The flow sensors utilize the vortex principle, as illustrated in Figure 1.6 and the pressure
sensors use the Wheatstone bridge.
The specifics about how these sensors operate can be
found in Grundfos data booklets [8]. The flow measuring system consists of a flow pipe with
an integrated bluff body and a differential pressure sensor. When fluid passes the bluff body,
it generates a series of alternating vortices on either side which is displayed in Figure 1.6.
These vortices propagate downstream, causing periodic pressure variations that are detected
by the sensor. The frequency of these variations is directly proportional to the volume flow rate
through the pipe. This design provides precise measurements for the volumetric flow, making
it easier to monitor and adjust cooling system performance.
Figure 1.6: Schematic of the vortex principle. The pipe (1), the bluff body (2) and the fluid
flow (3)[8].
The output from these sensors are flow rate in liters per minute.
This unit is commonly
used in industrial use because it aligns with the internationally recognized KV-value, which
measures the flow capacity of valves and regulators [9]. Most of the manifolds are also equipped
with relative pressure sensors which primarily consist of a chip that converts pressure into
electrical signals. The pressure difference between ambient air and the system causes the chip
to deform, altering the resistance in the strain gauges of a Wheatstone bridge. This resistance
change is converted into an analog output signal. Sensors with temperature measurement also
convert liquid temperature into electrical signals, which are then linearized and compensated
for temperature variations.[8] This adaptability, combined with the system’s robust design and
user-friendly interface, ensures that the FMS are an indispensable tool for optimizing injection
moulding processes.

2.1
Injection mould
In CFD simulations, especially those involving heat transfer, the accurate modelling of material
properties is critical. These properties gives the thermal behaviour of the system and directly
influence the accuracy of the simulation. This is relevant in injection moulding becuase heat
transfer dictates the cycle times, product quality, and overall process efficiency. Therefore, a
comprehensive understanding of the materials involved in the process is essential.
Mould materials typically used are of the following categories: steel, aluminium, and beryllium-
copper alloys.
Steel moulds, although more expensive, are usually preferred due to their high durability. Hard-
ened steel moulds, which undergo heat treatment after machining, excel in wear resistance and
have a long lifespan. Aluminum moulds, in contrast, are substantially cheaper but less suitable
for high-volume production or parts with tight dimensional tolerances. However, aluminium
moulds can still be the more economical choice if it is for producing 10,000 to 100,000 parts.
Beryllium-copper alloys are sometimes used in areas of the mould if heat removal is required to
be fast. This feature can reduce cycle times and improve the aesthetic quality of moulded parts.
However, the use of beryllium raises concerns about health risks and environmental impact.
Beryllium is a known carcinogen, linked to lung cancer and other respiratory diseases, and its
extraction is both costly and environmentally damaging due to its non-renewable nature.[10]

Thermal Transfer
In injection moulding, effective heat management is critical to ensuring the quality and efficiency
of the final product. The heat transfer is facilitated by conduction, convection, and radiation
between the mould, plastic part and cooling channels. This is the key focus of the simulation,
in regards to heat transfer, as only minimal heat loss occurs from the tubes and manifolds to
the surroundings before and after the coolant reaches the mould, due to effective insulation.
Thus the basic aspects of heat transfer will be evaluated in this part of the chapter to give the
reader an understanding of the heat transfer occurring in the injection moulding process. This
part of the chapter is written based on the Heat Transfer section in ”Simcenter STAR-CCM+
2310 - User Guide” [13]
Heat conduction is the transfer of internal energy from a region of higher temperature to a
region of lower temperature through interactions between adjacent particles in the material.
This process occurs as energy is exchanged between more energetic particles and less energetic
particles. Using Fourier’s law for conduction, provides a way of calculating the local heat flux
shown in Eq 2.3.1. It is here given in its differential form so it considers the heat transfer locally
instead of the whole material.
q′′ = k∇T
(2.3.1)
Here q′′ is the local heat flux vector, k is the thermal conductivity of the material and ∇T is
the temperature difference within the material. The main goal of simulating conductive heat
transfer is to calculate the temperature distribution within a medium while accounting for the
boundary conditions applied. This process utilizes the First Law of Thermodynamics, which
ensures energy conservation during transformations between forms. Combined with Fourier’s
law, it produces the Heat Diffusion Equation, a second-order partial differential equation that
models temperature fields. This equation can represent steady-state conditions, where temper-
ature remains spatially constant over time, or transient conditions, where temperature changes
with both space and time, as observed in metal quenching.
Then there is the heat transfer due to convection which is the transfer of heat through the move-
ment of fluids. Convective heat transfer involves two main mechanisms: diffusion, where energy
is transferred through random molecular motion, and advection, where energy is transferred
through the bulk motion of the fluid. Both mechanisms contribute to heat transfer to or from
a solid. Near the surface, where fluid velocity is low, diffusion dominates. At the solid-fluid
interface, where the fluid has no relative motion, heat transfer occurs entirely through diffu-
sion. Thus at the boundary layer between the coolant and the mould diffusion is the primary

channel pathway plays a crucial role in enhancing the cooling effect, the question remains: how

it is first essential to evaluate how general fluid behaviour changes with flow speed and why this

variation significantly affects heat transfer. The coolant can exhibit three types of flow: laminar
at low velocities, turbulent at high velocities, or transitional between these states. Laminar flow
is smooth and streamlined, while turbulent flow is chaotic and irregular, as shown in Figure
2.5.
Figure 2.5: Laminar vs Turbulent flow profiles [15].
In laminar flow, the heat has to be conducted through various layers of coolant to reach the
center of the channel. Since water and other coolants are poor conductors of heat, then heat
transfer is very inefficient.[16]
The chaotic behaviour in turbulent flow disrupts the boundary layers that form near the mould
walls, allowing cooler liquid to come into contact with the hot walls more frequently. The
increased fluid mixing ensures that the temperature reduction is more uniformly distributed
throughout the coolant, rather than being confined to the boundary layer. As a result, heat is
transferred more efficiently across the entire fluid, rather than primarily at the interface with
the wall.

3.3
Flow solvers
In this part of the chapter, it will be evaluated how STAR CCM+ solves the conservation
equations. STAR CCM+ have two types of solvers to solve the conservation equations, these
two solvers are the segregated solver and coupled solver.
The coupled method solves the equations simultaneously across the entire flow domain. This
algorithm yields more robust and accurate solutions for compressible flow, particularly in the
presence of shocks. One of its advantages is that the number of iterations required to solve a
flow problem remains independent of the mesh size, making it more efficient for simulations
that involve highly refined meshes. The segregated algorithm solves the governing equations
separated into smaller, independent sub-problems. Each sub-problem is solved separately, and
the results are later combined to form the overall flow solution. The correlation between the
momentum and continuity equations is achieved with a predictor-corrector approach. This
solver also uses less memory than the coupled solver, making it suitable for simulations with
larger meshes or limited computational resources. However, with a large mesh size the number
of iterations that the segregated algorithm requires for convergence also increases, making it
less efficient for highly refined meshes. This algorithm is also most effective for flows which
are mildly compressible and have low Rayleigh number natural convection and therefore is not
suitable for shock-capturing, high Mach number, and high Rayleigh-number applications [18].
One of the key components of a segregated computational fluid dynamics solver is the pressure-
velocity coupling algorithm, which is used to model the behaviour of fluids in motion accu-
rately. Two of the most commonly utilized schemes for pressure-velocity coupling are PISO
and SIMPLE. The PISO (Pressure-Implicit with Splitting of Operators) algorithm is an iter-
ative method used to solve the Navier-Stokes equations for incompressible flows. It is based
on an alternating-direction implicit (ADI) scheme, which allows for the decoupling of the pres-
sure and velocity equations. This decoupling results in a more efficient and accurate solution
20 of 75
==PAGE:34==
than other methods, making it a popular choice for modelling fluid flow behaviour. The SIM-
PLE (Semi-Implicit Method for Pressure-Linked Equations) algorithm is another method for
pressure-velocity coupling. It directly couples the pressure and velocity equations, thus en-
suring mass conservation and minimising numerical errors during calculations. This scheme is
especially useful for modelling complex flows where accuracy is critical. The choice between
these two algorithms depends on the specific flow problem being modelled in the simulation.
In general, PISO is preferred for steady-state problems, while SIMPLE is better suited for
unsteady, turbulent flows.[19]
An extension of the SIMPLE method is the SIMPLEC which was introduced in version 2302
of STAR CCM+ and stands for “SIMPLE Consistent”.
SIMPLEC, is a refinement of the
SIMPLE algorithm. Both schemes follow similar steps, but they differ in how they handle the
pressure correction equation. In SIMPLE, velocity corrections at a cell are expressed in terms of
pressure correction, assuming negligible velocity corrections at neighboring cells. The pressure
correction equation is then derived by taking the divergence of this velocity correction equation
and applying continuity.
SIMPLEC modifies this by assuming that velocity corrections at
neighboring cells are equal to those at the central cell. This adjustment results in a revised
pressure correction equation that is more consistent. Another key distinction is how under-
relaxation is handled. In SIMPLEC, pressure under-relaxation is implicitly incorporated into
the equation based on time-step size and velocity under-relaxation. This eliminates the need
for explicit pressure under-relaxation, effectively setting the under-relaxation factor to 1. This
ensures deeper convergence within each timestep. As a general fact the SIMPLEC has been
shown to either run faster or be more accurate compared to the SIMPLE scheme.[20]
Based on the solver’s most preferred uses as well as the knowledge from previous sections of
the flow being turbulent to most effectively produce high heat transfer the segregated solver
is used. From the assitance website AUTODESK Moldflow Communicator [16] the Reynolds
number for injection mould cooling should be between 4000 and 10,000 for testing. Therefore,
the segregated solver is the first choice for this level of turbulence, ensuring a balance between
computational efficiency and accuracy.
If problems occur with the simulation the coupled
solver is the alternative choice. The mould, plastic component and coolant should experience
temperature changes over time, thus the flow is unsteady. This makes the SIMPLE algorithm
the optimal selection for this simulation, with its enhanced version, SIMPLEC, also being a
potential option. An illustration of the solver hierarchy is shown in Figure 3.2.

3.4
Turbulence modelling
After determining the appropriate flow solver for solving the conservation equations, it is crucial
to evaluate how the assumed turbulent flow will be modelled. This step is essential because
turbulent flow is significantly more complex and difficult to predict than laminar flow, which
exhibits smooth and orderly characteristics.
Unlike laminar flow, turbulent flow is chaotic
22 of 75
==PAGE:36==
and involves fluctuations at multiple scales, making its behaviour challenging to represent
accurately. In this section, we will explore the various approaches for modelling turbulent flow,
considering factors such as the choice of turbulence models, their assumptions, and the specific
conditions under which each model provides the most reliable results.
This section of turbulence modelling will be based on Computational Fluid Dynamics: Princi-
ples and Applications[21], Lecture Notes for Course no. 41315 [22], Fluid Mechanics [23] and
cfd-online [24]. Here the possible approaches to solve the turbulence are: Reynolds Averaged
Navier-Stokes (RANS). Large Eddy Simulation (LES) and Detached Eddy Simulation (DES).
These turbulence modelling methods are displayed in Figure 3.3. The LES and DES methods
simulate turbulence by directly resolving the large-scale turbulent structures while modelling
the smaller-scale motions. However, since DES and LES are too computationally demanding,
these approaches will not be further evaluated in this project. Additionally, the flow within
injection mould cooling channels is typically more stable and can often be considered fully de-
veloped, especially for relatively simple channel geometries. Turbulence in such flows tends to
be less anisotropic and lacks the large-scale separation or swirling motions that would justify
the need for LES or DES.

4.2.2
Boundary Conditions
In the manifolds there are inserted sensors which measure flow values between 1 and 18 L/min.
Therefore the pressure difference between the outlet and the pump had the following values: 0.1
bar, 0.2 bar, 0.5 bar and 0.7 bar. This is done to ensure that the flow rates do not go so high that
the sensors would be damaged. Although the test setup uses a pump to control flow by applying
pressure, in STAR-CCM+, velocity is typically used as the primary variable for calculations
rather than pressure. This preference stems from velocity’s direct relationship with the Navier-
Stokes equations, which govern fluid motion by describing how momentum is conserved under
the influence of forces and pressure gradients. Velocity is inherently governed by the balance of
inertial, viscous, and external forces, making it more straightforward to compute. In contrast,
pressure serves as a constraint variable without its own governing equation, being determined
indirectly to enforce mass conservation through the continuity equation.
To address this, the simulation is set up by first calculating an initial velocity based on the total
flow rate (L/min) provided by the sensor. Using this velocity at the inlets, the corresponding
pressure difference is calculated. The resulting velocities at the individual channels are then
plotted as a function of the pressure difference. Just like in the previous simulation, the outlet
42 of 75
==PAGE:56==
is set as a pressure outlet to minimize the possibility of backflow. With boundary conditions
set, the next step is to construct the physics continua before running the simulations.
4.2.3
Physics Continua
There are two main differences in the physics continuas between this test-rig benchmark and
the Conjunction heat transfer benchmark. The first is that due to the insulated tubes, minimal
heat loss occurs between the TCU and the moulds, making heat transfer negligible and not
evaluated in this benchmark. As a result, the focus is solely on fluid dynamics, with no solid
components included in the analysis. The second main difference is that the state is steady as
once the flow has settled the velocities are assumed to be relatively constant until the pump is
turned off or the valves begin to close.
Since these benchmarks were created to evaluate the feasibility of integrating them into the final
digital twin, it was crucial that the physics continua could be combined. The fluid continuum
was therefore designed with the intention of being compatible with the heat transfer benchmark.
As a result, the k-ω turbulence model was selected. If it is seen that splitting the digital twin
into smaller components is more practical, other turbulence models might be more appropriate.
However, as this project aims to replicate the entire FMS system within a single simulation,
such an approach was not attempted. With the simulation setup complete, it is important
to perform a convergence study prior to running the simulation to ensure the reliability and
accuracy of the results.

Discussion
The aim of this report was to implement the FMS system for injection moulding in CFD and
validate its reliability and capability. This was accomplished, however lack in the experimental
data during validation were found and this require further investigation. For the experimental
data the biggest dilemma was the lack of access to an actual injection moulding process inte-
grated with the FMS system. Instead, this was substituted with two separate benchmarks one
for heat transfer and one for flow rates. The results were promising for these two benchmarks,
but not without uncertainty. The primary uncertainty does not lie with the simulation but with
the lack of details in experimental data gathered. The heat transfer experiment only had data
when the polymer had reduced from 230◦C to 52.1◦C. To compensate for this a Matlab code
was generated for how the temperature of the polymer will have changed based on filling time.
So while the experimental data over heat transfer was validated, the simplification might not
be sufficient for simulating actual injection moulding production which is the objective with
the digital twin.
Another limit with the experimental data was lacking description of coolant geometry. It was
not described how close the coolant channels was to the polymer, which will greatly affect the
heat transfer rate. Currently all the data given had been used, but the distance of the coolant
channels was trial and error to see if the simulation data could correctly replicate the experi-
mental data. While it was promising that the behaviour of the heat transfer was consistent each
time. If the channels were positioned closer to or farther from the polymer, the discrepancy
between the experimental and simulation data would increase.
For the simulation conducted on the test rig, the discrepancy between the experimental results
and the simulation was larger compared to the CHT benchmark. However, the discrepancy
remained within an acceptable range. The convergence study revealed that using a smaller
base size did give a small margin of reduction in velocity, which would decrease the overall
discrepancy.
However, the computational demands would be far too demanding.
Another
possibility for reducing the discrepancy could be to use the Reynold Stress model to solve
the unknown turbulence variables. Because even though k-ω have correction terms added to
handle the limitations with the Boussinesq approximations, maybe it does not entirely handle
the limit in this simulation. For the test-rig experimental data, a source of uncertainty comes
from the pressure sensors in the manifolds.
These sensors evaluate pressure based on the
difference between the manifold pressure and atmospheric pressure. Whereas in STAR CCM+
the pressure is evaluated as the force divided by the area. To account for this area difference
between the experimental and simulation results, the simulated pressure was multiplied by a
58 of 75
==PAGE:72==
factor of 5. This seemed to work fine with the test-rig simulations, but attempted with the
digital twin the pressure difference seemed quite high despite lower velocity inlet compared
to the benchmark. This underlines further the need to have more experimental validation,
especially with the FMS integrated to a injection moulding process.
For the digital twin, the flow had become much more complex which caused the flow residuals
not to converge completely and in turn required a much lower CFL value to give accurate
results. This yielded much more computational power which made it difficult to simulate a
whole injection moulding process. Therefore if the flow does not become more complex in other
mould designs, then it would be wise to divide the digital twin into the benchmarks. Thus
heat transfer would be evaluated alone in the mould and the FMS would be divided into two
where one gives the required data for the inlet to the mould and the second is the outlet. This
is important because the digital twin example demonstrated how increasing the fluid velocity
affects the cooling process of the mould. However, it is uncertain whether this impacts the
cooling of the polymer. This is because the heat transfer between the polymer and the mould
is kept constant. This is shown in Figure 2.6 where increasing the coolant velocity did not
enhance the cooling due to mould heat extraction limitations.
Since the TCU can control both the velocity and temperature of the coolant, the digital twin
was used to evaluate how coolant temperature impacts cooling efficiency. It was observed that
cooler coolant lowers the mould’s temperature more effectively than warmer coolant. However,
the power required by the TCU to cool the coolant, compared to simply increasing its velocity,
remains unknown. The digital twin can help determine the optimal balance between cooling
and velocity settings for the TCU, provided these power details are obtained.
But the big question remained: does faster mould cooling necessarily lead to quicker polymer
cooling? This was explored using the simulation setup from the CHT benchmark, as it runs
faster than the digital twin. The results showed that increasing coolant velocity improved poly-
mer cooling, but this must be combined with test-rig benchmark data to assess the associated
power consumption. By addressing these areas, future research can expand the applicability
and impact of CFD digital twins in manufacturing and beyond. By addressing these concerns,
future research can increase the reliability of digital twin results. This, in turn, can improve
manufacturing efficiency and contribute to a broader global impact.

Chapter7
Conclusion
When addressing the problem statement, ”Can the FMS be implemented as a CFD digital twin,
using STAR CCM+?” it is concluded that it can be. The digital twin can extract the same data
from the injection moulding as the FMS and more. The drawback for the digital twin however is
that it can only simulate one cycle where the FMS observes the coolant properties continuously.
As a result, the digital twin is invaluable for identifying optimal operating conditions, while
the FMS is crucial for ensuring these conditions are consistently maintained during production.
Thus the optimal setup is to actually have both implemented in the injection moulding process.
Other key differences between the FMS and the digital twin are present. Notably, while the
FMS measures temperature, mass flow rate, and pressure within the manifolds, the digital
twin expands on this by providing these measurements directly within the mould itself. This
extension is crucial because manufacturers are primarily concerned with the Reynolds number
in the mould, as it directly indicates the optimal cooling conditions for the polymer. Using
only the FMS system, simulations are required to estimate the optimal Reynolds number in the
mould for efficient cooling. Additional calculations are then needed to determine the required
mass flow rate from the manifold channels. In contrast, the digital twin not only enables users
to identify the optimal cooling conditions but also directly provides the corresponding settings
to achieve them. This streamlined functionality enhances the ability to optimize the injection
mould cooling process effectively.
This approach reduces the need for extensive physical testing during production, minimizing the
frequency of reworking or discarding defective polymer products. However, a current limitation
of the digital twin lies in its inability to account for the rapid cooling of the polymer during
the filling stage. This phenomenon may be influenced by factors such as the air within the
pressure-closed mould or the higher heat transfer coefficient of the polymer while in its fluid
phase compared to its solid phase.
To develop a functional digital twin that replicates Mouldflo’s Flow Monitoring System two
benchmarks are used for validation. Both benchmarks produced satisfactory results, though
there remains room for improvement. With these simulations successfully validated, they pro-
vided a reliable foundation for the digital twin’s results. The integration of these simulations led
to the creation of the digital twin. However, the computational power required was significantly
greater than anticipated. As a result, dividing the digital twin into three separate simulations
is recommended as a more efficient approach unless additional computational resources become
available. The digital twin enables comprehensive design evaluations and the analysis of vari-
ous parameters that influence mould cooling. While previous CFD tools have been utilized for
60 of 75
==PAGE:74==
similar purposes, this digital twin stands apart by integrating these simulations with a detailed
assessment of the operational requirements needed to achieve optimal cooling rates.
Furture Work
To ensure the accuracy of the digital twin, validation using an injection moulding process in-
tegrated with the FMS system is necessary. This is particularly important because certain
assumptions were made in the development of the digital twin. The primary assumption is
regarding the polymer’s initial temperature at the start of the cooling process. Therefore one
potential improvement would involve simulating the filling process using volume fraction meth-
ods, which could help address this assumption directly. Currently, this aspect is approximated
through Matlab code, but incorporating a more detailed simulation approach could enhance
the model’s precision and reliability.
