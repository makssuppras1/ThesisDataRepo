Abstract
The main focus of this project is to make an experimental and theoretical study of hydro-
dynamical bearings. The study will focus on classifying the behaviour with statical and
dynamical loading based on numerical predictions to Reynolds equation using the ﬁnite
diﬀerence (FD) method.
Through Reynolds equation the pressure distribution surrounding the journal is determ-
ined, but from the basic equation negative pressures are predicted which have to be taken
care of. To do so, diﬀerent cavitation models are tested and then compared to each other
both using the FD code and a commercial software (COMSOL). The comparison is mainly
made from the predicted position at diﬀerent static loads (locus curve) and the eccentricity
needed to reach this load. Furthermore, the size, position and shape of the resulting cavit-
ated area is investigated. The study shows that the predictions vary signiﬁcantly between
the methods.
The FD model is used to predict the journal orbit when the bearing is imposed to a
dynamic load. Furthermore, comparison between results obtained with the present code and
results from an article [1] on the same subject is conducted and good agreement is obtained.
Experiments have been conducted on the existing Cavitation Test-Rig (CTR), located at
the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) in Lyngby on a brass bushing. Sensors on the
test-rig measure the position of the shaft and the load on the bearing continuously, both at
static and dynamic loads, with various operating conditions. Thus, locus curves and orbit
plots can be obtained, which are then compared to the numerical predictions with equivalent
conditions and parameters. Diﬃculties with the way the test-rig was constructed and being
unable to reach a suﬃciently high dynamic load amplitude at higher frequencies limited
the possibilities of experimental work. The locus curve obtained showed that the behaviour
of the system was only predictable when applying load in one direction. This forced the
dynamical experiments to be done with load cycles remaining in one direction. Despite this,
orbit plots were obtained and compared to the numerical predictions and a coherence was
observed. The size and position of the orbits was similar, but because of the limited load
amplitude more interesting load cycles were not achievable and no further studies were made.
An identical bushing made out of Polymethylmethacrylat (PMMA, plexiglas) has been
fabricated but no experiments were conducted with it, though a numerical implementation

Chapter 1.
The journal bearing
The journal bearing is one of the most simple type of bearings as it consists only of a shaft
(the journal) and a bushing (the bearing) seperated by a lubricant ﬁlm. This lubricant ﬁlm
is pumped into the bearing through a feed inlet, with the purpose of overcoming friction
forces between the journal and bearing, as well as cooling. Despite their simplicity, journal
bearings have a huge load carrying capacity compared to rolling bearings. These are some of
the reasons why they are widely used in machinery and particularly in heavy-duty, marine
diesel engines, but also in steam turbine rotors in power stations and propeller shafts in
ships [2].
In recent years, regulations of emissions on ships and rising fuel prices have pushed the
industry to try to limit the fuel consumption. A common action is to lower the rotational
speed of the engine. To keep the same eﬀect of the motor, a higher torque is required which
is achieved by a bigger pressure leading to a bigger load on the bearings. Unfortunately
lowering the rotational speed and applying a higher load worsens the performance of the
bearing, making it more prone to cavitation.
1.1. Bearing geometry
Figure 1.1a and 1.1b show the the journal bearing from a front view (xy-plane) and side
view (xz-plane), respectively. The stationary member, the bearing bush, has its centre at
OB, and the rotating member, the bearing journal, has its centre at OJ attached to the
shaft. The bore diameter of the journal bush is deﬁned as

Reynolds equation
A bearing can be seen as a viscous pump where a pressure is generated in a thin ﬁlm. The
ﬁlm (with normalized thickness, ¯h) seperates two surfaces moving relative to each other. If
the bearing is designed so that the ﬂuid is squeezed to the sides due to a converging geometry,
a pressure distribution in the ﬁlm occurs. A simpliﬁcation of the principle is illustrated in
Figure 2.1. Letting a unit ﬂow with a constant density and viscosity going between the
two surfaces (Figure 2.1, in positive x-direction), the velocity distribution (illustrated as
triangular shapes), and hence the volume ﬂow, consists of two components [2]. The ﬁrst
one (the Couette ﬂow) due to the velocity of the two surfaces, and the second one (the
Poiseuille ﬂow) due to the rising or falling pressure distribution.

Chapter 4.
Cavitation
Prior to this chapter cavitation has only been mentioned brieﬂy in relation to the half
Sommerfeld solution, where the negative pressure region is suppressed. The cavitation phe-
nomenon will now be explained further. Since the negative pressure is not physically possible,
a few models will be implemented in Reynolds equation. In general, cavitation occurs as
a result of pressure reduction and is a formation of a gaseous phase in a liquid. In ﬂuid
ﬁlm bearings two types of cavitation are encountered: gaseous cavitation which is relatively
innocuous and vapour cavitation causing critical damages on bearing surfaces [6, p. 215].
4.1. Gaseous cavitation
Typical lubricating oils contain 10 % of the volume of dissolved gas when saturated with
water [6, p. 215] which is diﬃcult to avoid. In case of a pressure reduction, usually below
atmospheric saturation pressure, the dissolved air is released in the liquid as cavity bubbles.
For statically loaded journal bearings operating at steady-state conditions the cavitation
commonly takes place in the divergent section of the oil ﬁlm.
When the pressurised oil reaches the divergent section, after passing the minimum ﬁlm
thickness, the oil from the ﬁlm gap cannot occupy the whole clearance space causing rupture
in the oil that reaches the divergent zone. Commonly, the oil ruptures into so-called tongues
or ﬁnger-type cavities (see Figure 5.4) [6]. The dissolved air then expands and develops
free bubbles as the ambient pressure drops toward absolute zero. Finally, as the cavities,
containing low pressure air, reaches the converging region of the bearing, a gradual increase
in pressure cause them to diminish in size and lastly redissolve in the oil. A process with no
damaging eﬀect [6, p. 215].
4.2. Vapour cavitation
Vapour cavitation occurs when the pressure in the liquid drops toward vapour pressure
causing the oil to start boiling even at low temperature.
A typical example of vapour

Chapter 5.
Dynamic Modelling
In this chapter the center position of the shaft will be modelled when the bearing is dynam-
ically loaded. The curve that the shaft follows is known as the journal orbit.
Prior to this chapter, the pressure distribution, and hence the ﬁlm forces, have been
computed from an eccentricity ratio (as a direct problem), and in a local coordinate system
([0; 2π]).
This means that, if the ﬁlm forces have to balance a certain static load, the
eccentricity ratio has to be adjusted using trial and error until the criteria is satisﬁed which
is diﬃcult and tedious. Nor is it possible to for example ﬁx the position of an inlet globally
with the current code. Instead, an indirect method is used where the eccentricity ratio and
attitude angle is iterated until the static forces are balanced.
As mentioned, the pressure distribution has been computed in a local coordinate system
but will now be expressed in a global coordinate system, which will include the attitude
angle. Referring to Figure 2.2, a new global angle going from the negative x-axis, θ′ = θ +ϕ,
is introduced. Moreover, the eccentricity ratio is split into a ¯x- and ¯y-component. The ﬁlm
thickness (Equation 1.3) in dimensionless form then becomes
¯h = 1 + ε cos θ = 1 + ¯x cos θ′ + ¯y sin θ′
(5.1)
where ¯x = ε cos ϕ and ¯y = ε sin ϕ

6.1. introduction
COMSOL Multiphysics is a FEM (Finite Element Method) based software for solving coupled
diﬀerential equations. It includes several diﬀerent physics interfaces, including thin-ﬁlm ﬂow,
structural mechanics and PDE (Partial Diﬀerential Equations), which is utilized in this pro-
ject. The software allows coupling the before mentioned physics with each other even on
diﬀerent domains and geometries.
The simulation is based on a COMSOL tutorial for Journal bearings with cavitation6.
The governing equation is Reynolds equation (Equation 2.1) with an addition of Elrod and
Adams cavitation model which is explained in section 4.5.
As discussed in section 4.6, the results may vary when using diﬀerent cavitation mod-
els. The following section will only consider Elrod and Adams cavitation model, but the
same overall tendencies are present using half Sommerfeld. Half Sommerfeld is not a build
in cavitation model in COMSOL, but can be expressed as the following, using functions
COMSOL can use:
pHS = p · sign(p) + 1
2
(6.1)
Where p is the array containing the pressure in each node, and pHS is the Sommerfeld
pressure containing no negative values.
6.2. Rigid bearing
The study is set up as a thin-ﬁlm ﬂow, which is used to solve the Reynold equation in a
narrow channel that is represented by a surface. The geometry is simply a surface with
the dimensions of the inside of the bushing on the test-rig, and the parameters showed in
Table 5.2. The meshed geometry can be seen in Figure 6.1a. The mesh type is Free-Quad
with the element size set to the predeﬁned COMSOL parameter extra ﬁne. To make sure
6https://www.comsol.com/model/journal-bearing-with-cavitation-19717
53
==PAGE:74==
Chapter 6. COMSOL simulations
the mesh size is suﬃcient the pressure distribution without a cavitation model has been
compared with the numerical results and they are identical. The mesh could be reduced
to less points, but as the computational time has not been an issue, the standard mesh is
kept. Just like in the FD model, the boundaries in the axial ends of the bearing are set
to p = 0. The ﬁlm thickness is deﬁned from Equation 1.3, by deﬁning the angle along the
circumference as θ = Arctan(y/x). Furthermore the components of the journal velocity are
deﬁned as u = −ωR sin θ and v = ωR cos θ. The forces are obtained by integrating the
pressure of each nodal point in the x and y direction. The resulting force and the attitude
angle is ﬁnally determined from Equation 2.6 and 5.2 respectively. appendix C describes the
practical set-up as well as the study extensions used.
6.2.1. Inlet
In order to include inlets, the geometry is slightly changed by adding a "hole" in the surface
corresponding with the outer dimensions of the inlet on the cavitation rig bearing, see Fig-
ure 6.1c. The boundary condition is set to a constant pressure with the magnitude of the
supply pressure, 0.2 bar.
The resulting force is determined from the ﬁlm thickness, which at diﬀerent loads and inlet
conditions will shift the attitude angle. It is desired that the load will always be perpendicular
to the inlet position, as this is the case at the test-rig, or at least in a ﬁxed position. This
is achieved by adding an optimization module to the study that makes a correction of the
attitude angle. To shift the attitude angle the expression for the ﬁlm thickness is changed
to allow for the correction θcorr.
h(θ) = C(1 + ε cos (θ + θcorr))
The optimization tries to minimize abs(ϕ) by changing θcorr. The method used is Nelder–Mead
[14] which is a built-in feature in COMSOL. This ensures that the resulting load is applied
in a constant angle relative to the inlet.
6.2.2. Load driven
Similar to the attitude angle, COMSOL computes the resulting load from a speciﬁed ec-
centricity ratio (as a direct problem).

7.1.2. Superposition
The limitations of the aforementioned models are that they treat the deformation locally in
each node, not coupling the deformation and rigidity of the neighbouring nodes. In order to
take care of the coupling between the nodes, a partial FE method is used. A superposition
method can be used as the deformation of the bearing is linear with the load. This is done
by applying a unit load to all grid-points sequentially, on locations corresponding to the FD
mesh (101x51) on the bushing. The unit load is distributed to a small area surrounding the
node with the size of an element in the FD grid. This is visualized on Figure 7.2 where the
blue square indicates the area around the node (the black dot). From each unit pressure
the deformation is calculated with COMSOL in all other points on the mesh. By taking
advantage of the symmetry of the bushing it is not necessary to calculate the deformation
in all points, but merely one half row in the axial direction, see Figure 7.2. This means
that the inlet is not considered in this analysis as the deformation of the surrounding points
would be diﬀerent, breaking the rotational symmetry in that area.
The desired outcome of the study is the deformation of all nodes from loads anywhere on
the bushing. As the mesh does not align with the mapped mesh of the Finite Volume study
(see Figure 7.3), the deformation must be interpolated to do so (Matlab code is attached
in E.4)
As the bearing geometry is symmetrical along the axial direction, the deformation ﬁeld is
simply ﬂipped for the points on the opposing side (L/2 to L). The deformation from loading
the bushing at other angles are similarly obtained because of the rotational symmetry. This
results in 51 matrices (one for every point in the z-direction) with similar size as the FD
model (51x101). An example of the interpolated deformation from the COMSOL study is

8.1.2. Loading
The loading on the bearing is imposed by hydraulic cylinder. The load is controlled through
a servo valve controlled by the same LabVIEW program. The loading can be generated as
constant (static) or sinusoidal (dynamic). Due to the fact that it is driven by a hydraulic
system, there are some restrictions on the loads. Statical loads up to 7000 N can be ap-
plied in both directions, but dynamically the amplitude depends on the frequency.
The
load amplitude is limited by the ﬂow delivered by the pump (this is further explained in
D.5), which currently is not large enough to generate loading as high as wished for higher
frequencies. The range can be increased by ﬁtting a pump that can deliver a higher ﬂow, or
a bigger accumulator. However the latter will only allow for a higher amplitude in a limited
time depending on the size, as the accumulator will drain whilst the dynamic loading is
applied. The hydraulic system is further explained and visualized with a hydraulic diagram
in section D.5.
8.1.3. Distance Probes
The distance probes have to be calibrated when "looking at" the shaft, as diﬀerent materials
might change the output signal. This makes calibration diﬃcult since the test-rig only allows
the shaft to be moved vertically by imposing a load. Thus the calibration in the x-direction
has been made from measuring the position in the top of the bushing and the bottom. In
the y-direction this was not possible as the test-rig is not able to apply a load horizontally.
The probes have a linear conversion from volt to distance, and this is used to calibrate
the horizontal direction too. An average of the proportional constant at each side is used,
and the oﬀset is added by adjusting the mean position to be zero in the locus curve. The
calibration is further explained in section D.2.

8.2.2. Dynamic
When a periodic load cycle is applied to a journal bearing the orbit of the shaft will converge

The same rotational speed of the shaft (500 RPM) is used for all experiments, but the
frequency of the applied loading will be changed. As the loading is controlled indirectly
through a servo valve directing hydraulic ﬂuid to the piston, it is diﬃcult to create a perfect

Chapter 9.
Discussion
The investigation of cavitation showed that the cavitation model has a notably eﬀect on the
journal position. Reynolds boundary condition and half Sommerfeld are simpliﬁed in the
way that they do not enforce mass conservation. Even though Reynolds boundary condition
predicts the cavitation zone more speciﬁcally compared to the half-Sommerfeld solution (the
angle changes in the axial direction), the model only focuses on the pressure gradient at the
cavitation angle. Furthermore, it was noticed that an abrupt change in the pressure gradient
was diﬃcult to avoid with the present Reynolds boundary condition model when the amount
of grid points was reduced. Thus, if a more realistic result (ﬂow continuity at cavitation
angle) is wanted with a more coarse mesh, an improvement of the present model is necessary.
One solution could be to reduce the grid interval from [0; 2π] to [0; θcav]. This would neglect
the trivial grid points and improve the eﬃciency of the grid. Reynolds boundary condition
gives the best approach to zero pressure when the length-diameter relationship is above a
certain value. Furthermore, it does only focus on the pressure gradient in the circumferential
direction.
Even though the dynamic model showed good agreement when comparing it to a reference,
the ﬁrst order Euler method was used to estimate journal position and velocity. To obtain
more accurate predictions a model with higher order of accuracy (e.g. 4th order Runga-
Kutta) would be necessary.
In this report COMSOL has mostly been used to validate and compare the FD model,
both with respect to the pressure ﬁelds, but also deformation. Though it is possible to model
the dynamic behaviour of the system, or go more in depth with the eﬀect of inlets (position,
shapes etc.). Furthermore COMSOL could even easily be used to quantify the eﬀect of the
shaft and bushing being non-aligned.
Experimentally the project did not yield the consistent results, especially not with applying
a load in the opposite direction to what was shown in the experimental section. Overall the
test-rig had some problems that were not solvable within this project.
Had there been
more time and resources some changes would have been worth trying out. For once, the
hydraulic system was underpowered and not able to give a high load amplitude at higher

Chapter 10.
Conclusion
One of the major parts of the project was to set up a model to numerical predict pressures
from Reynolds equation using the FD method. The model was validated by comparing it with
analytical solutions to Reynolds equation implemented on two simpliﬁed bearing geometries;
the narrow and long bearing. Both comparisons yielded acceptable RMS errors. The model
was then implemented in two cavitation models; the half-Sommerfeld and Reynolds boundary
condition. These two were compared with a model (AJB-model), read from an article, where
mass conservation is enforced. From the comparison, it can be concluded that the method
used to predict the cavitation angle θcav has a notably eﬀect on the journal position.
Reynolds boundary condition was chosen as the main cavitation model and was imple-
mented in a model predicting the journal orbit when imposing dynamic loads. The model
was compared to what was obtained in an article where bearing characteristics, load data,
and resulting journal orbits (both numerical and experimental results) were given. The orbit
comparison showed good agreement.
Experimental the results were not as good as wanted. Though it was possible to produce
a locus curve, with a load ranging from positive to negative direction.
The locus curve
did not match the numerically predicted shape perfectly, but showed the tendency in one
direction. The way the test-rig is constructed makes it diﬃcult to calibrate the horizontal
distance probes which can be part of the reason that the horizontal movement is not as big
as estimated.
The dynamical behaviour of the system observed on the test-rig showed similar tendencies
as the static. The orbit of the bearing was only achievable when the load was applied in
one direction excluding alternating load, which would be interesting as it represents many
machines using journal bearings. For what it is, the results from the one-directional ex-
citation showed that it was possible to some degree to predict the position of the orbit,
and size of the orbit with the current set up. A load cycle of 5 Hz proved to be the best
compromise between loading frequency and amplitude of the load. This yielded very similar
experimentally measured orbits to the numerically predicted.

Chapter 11.
Future work
As outlined in the discussion diﬀerent improvements could be interesting to implement in the
numerical models. A cavitation model enforcing mass conservation could be utilized to see
the eﬀect on journal position both for static and dynamic load cases. Numerical predictions
could be improved to achieve higher accuracy - both regarding the FD derivatives and journal
position and velocity.
The test-rig has to be ﬁxed in order to get reliable results, and a bigger hydraulic system
ﬁtted in order to impose a bigger amplitude on the load cycles at higher frequencies. Chan-
ging the brass bushing to plexiglas would give the opportunity to see inside the bushing when
it is running, making it possible to observe the formation of cavitation zones in the ﬂuid
ﬁlm. Though the change of material yields some challenges. The main factors are: Thermal
expansion coeﬃcient is higher for plexiglas as well as its heat transfer is lower. However, the
Young’s modulus of plexiglas is lower yielding a higher deformation of the bushing when the
pressure builds up. In the deformation section this has already been taken care of numeric-
ally, and testing a plexiglas bushing would be an exiting next step to this. The test-rig has
temperature probes attached to the inlet and outlet of the bearing. This enables measuring
the heat transfer which is also possible through the FD model and in COMSOL.

paring the pressure distribution to that of the FD model without cavitations, which yielded
practically identical pressure distributions. Changing the mesh size (maximum element size
± 0.001) did not change the results, so the standard mesh size was kept. As of the conver-

D.1. Eksperimental procedure
Previous to all tests the following start procedure is used:
1. Supply oil pump is turned on
2. Suction pump is turned on
3. The ball valve is adjusted so the inlet pressure is about 0.5 bar.

6. LabVIEW is started, and parameters for study is speciﬁed.
Loading and data collecting is handled by the LabVIEW program whereas the angular
velocity of the axis and the inlet pressure is controlled from physical dials/valves.
D.2. Sensors/actuators and calibration
Distance probes
The distance probes used for measuring the position of the axis are of the type Megitt TQ
401. They measure accurately within 0.2 to 2 mm with a sensitivity of 8mV/µm. Within
this range the relation between the voltage and distance is linear, hence only two measuring
points is needed for calibration (More is added to make the calibration more accurate). The
sensors are sensitive to what the material it looks on, so it is not possible to calibrate them
using feeler blades or other tools of diﬀerent materials. It is also hard to control the position
in the shaft with enough accuracy to do calibration. The only points that can be obtained
117

with reasonably accuracy is when the shaft is resting on the bushing. With the clearance
being known, the maximum distance the shaft can move is known (2c). By applying a force
in the two directions (vertically) a number of times and noting the lowest and highest current,
these can be assumed to be at -c and c. As it is not possible to do the same in the horizontal
direction as we are not able to apply a suﬃcient force in this direction (and gravity will pull
the shaft down), the same conversion as from the vertical is used. To minimize the error
this will introduce, the distance of the distance probes are adjusted so that they are all the
same distance from the shaft. This is done by using feeler gauges of diﬀerent sizes for the
horizontal and vertical probes. The adjustments are made with the shaft being in rest, hence
making the wanted distance from axis to the probe in the vertical direction 2c + 0.5mm and
in the horizontal direction c + 0.5mm.

The pressure transducers is of the type HBM P8AP-225 with a range from 0 to 50 bar. They

D.6. LabVIEW
D.6. LabVIEW
The LabVIEW Program is designed to gather the data from the test rig. It samples the
data from all the sensor at a rate of 1700 samples pr. second and stores them as a .csv ﬁle
for post processing. In addition to the data collecting, control of the servo valve controlling
the force on the bearing, has been implemented whereas all other parameters are adjusted
manually with physical buttons (angular velocity, inlet pressure ect.). The control of the
servo valve has been made with three main settings.
• Constant force, where a constant voltage can be speciﬁed leading to a constant force.
• Sinusoidal force, with a speciﬁed frequency.
• Sinusoidal force, where the frequency matches the angular velocity of the shaft.
The interface shown in Figure D.3 allows real-time monitoring, saving the data, as well as
controlling the force applied to the bushing.
The program that underlies the interface is a block diagram, shown in Figure D.3. As
written on the ﬁgure, the diagram can be divided into 6 sections
1. Conﬁguring I/O modules and assigning channels.
The input signal is speciﬁed as well as the Voltage range or similar of each of the
sensors. The name of the value and the unit are speciﬁed too.
2. Creating and preparing ﬁle for logging
A .csv (table) ﬁle is created with the spaces allocated all of the measurements speciﬁed
in the workspace.
3. Deﬁning sample speed and initialize data collection
The sample rate is deﬁned, and continuous samples is chosen to be able to log data as
long as wanted/required for each experiment.
4. Reading data, applying calibration, writing to ﬁle, display on front pannel
This all takes place within a while loop, and runs continuous from the program is
started till it is terminated. The data is read from the drives at the speciﬁed sample
rate and is send to the calibration. Here the signal (voltage or current) is converted to
a suitable unit through an equation taken from the data sheet of the sensor, or through
a manual calibration process. Within the while loop, there is a if loop. This initializes
writing the data to the already created .csv ﬁle as well as directing the signals out
to the gauges displaying the values on the front panel. When the data-logging is not
switched on, the if loop just passes through the signal.
