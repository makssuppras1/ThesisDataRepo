ABSTRACT 
Sailing routes connecting the Baltic Sea and the North Sea experience increasing amounts of shipping 
traffic, the majority sails through Øresund. Shipping has several environmental impacts divided into 
physical impacts, air emissions, and discharges to water.  
The aim of this study was to use data fusion of acoustic and optics data to investigate the effect of 
shipping in the sailing channel in Øresund.  
This was done by mapping and quantifying seabottom features including sediment types and 
vegetation. Two localities in the Øresund (i.e., Flinterenden and Drogden) have been mapped at high 
resolution merging acoustic and optic data and comparing localities to determine differences between 
the seabed outside of the sailing channels and the seabed inside. 
The results demonstrate that both sailing channels have very limited vegetation compared to the areas 
outside them. Specifically, video inspections and a derived proxy for bottom classification show sharp 
transitions between vegetated areas and a bare seabottom landscape. Although firm relationships 
between heavy cargo ship traffic and seabottom abrasion could not be obtained it is concluded that 
the lack of vegetation inside the sailing channel is most likely caused by shipping activities also 
because other types of bottom disturbing activities, like trawling, are prohibited in the Øresund. There 
is potential for the method used in this study to be further developed to improve and automatize 
seabottom mapping and habitat type classifications.  
 
Keywords: Data fusion, Seabottom characterization, Shipping impact, Acoustics, Ground truth, 
Towfish, Øresund, Shipping channel, Dredging.

1 INTRODUCTION 
The ocean covers more than 70 % of the surface of the earth and is an important component of the 
Earth System, enabling human life as it produces most of the oxygen we need, works as a carbon 
sink, and provides food security. Furthermore, the ocean provides habitats for a variety of underwater 
species, populations, and communities. Recently the ocean space is subject to an increasing interest 
both regarding offshore construction such as artificial energy islands, artificial city development 
islands, offshore windfarms, oil platforms as well as new technology such as floating windfarms and 
floating solar panels. The oceans also serve as the foundation for much of the worlds’ economy, 
supporting sectors from recreational purposes, to fisheries, to international shipping (Jägerbrand et 
al., 2019; Díaz & Guedes Soares, 2020; Elshafei et al., 2021; Energistyrelsen, 2023).  
These initiatives have an impact on the marine ecosystems, however not a lot is known about the 
location and abundance of different marine ecosystems. In Denmark, only habitat types inside some 
of the Natura 2000-areas are mapped (Det marine Danmarkskort, 2023). This makes it difficult to 
estimate how much habitat is potentially lost in connection with construction, shipping channels, 
bottom trawling, etc. Considering the interest in loss of habitats and biodiversity on land, underwater 
habitat destruction and alterations in species composition should also be of equal interest. In 2008, 
The EU introduced the marine strategy framework directive (MFSD) which provides 11 descriptors 
to map and monitor the status of the marine environment to ensure all member states reach Good 
Environmental Status (GES). The 11 descriptors are (1) biodiversity, (2) non-indigenous species, (3) 
commercial fish and shellfish, (4) food webs, (5) eutrophication, (6) sea-floor integrity, (7) 
hydrographical conditions, (8) contaminants, (9) contaminants in seafood, (10) marine litter, and (11) 
energy incl. underwater noise (European commission, 2023).  
  
1.1 Baltic Sea and Øresund 
Danish waters are located in between the North Sea and the Baltic Sea. The Baltic Sea is connected 
through three straits: Lillebælt, Storebælt, and Øresund See Figure 1.1. The Baltic Sea is a semi 
enclosed waterbody with low salinity and hence brackish water. It has been shaped by the last ice age 
and human influence through deforestation and agriculture (Reckermann et al., 2022). The catchment 
area is around 420,000 km2 which means inflow of freshwater surpasses the inflow of salt water 
(HELCOM, 2018A). The salinity gradient is strong with high salinity in the outer part and low salinity 
in the inner part. This salinity gradient creates very different habitats and therefore the Baltic Sea is

Øresund is one of the three Danish straits connecting the brackish Baltic Sea water with the salty 
North Sea water and hence one of the areas with the strongest salinity gradient. The salinity gradient 
is both vertical and horizontal with high salinity water on the bottom and to the north (28-35 PSU) 
whereas surface water and more southern has a lower salinity (8-10 PSU) (Lumborg, 2005; Hansen 
et al., 2012). The hydrographic conditions with brackish water flowing out to the North Sea and a 
counter current of high saline water flowing into the Baltic Sea is typical for an estuary (Petersen et 
al., 2018). Øresund has a very strong halocline separating the two water masses at 10-15 m depth 
(Petersen et al., 2018). Øresund is up to 35 m deep, however only 5-10 m in the study area (Skippo). 
Both Drogden and Flinterenden sailing channels are dredged to 8 m depth to ensure shipping traffic 
can pass but are still too shallow to allow the high saline water to pass, therefore, this only occurs 
during storm surges (Meesenburg, 2014; Petersen et al., 2018). As biodiversity in estuaries changes 
with salinity as shown in Figure 1.2 (Whitfield et al., 2012; Telesh et al., 2013), it is apparent that 
there is a lower diversity of species including macroalgae and angiosperms as salinity decreases into 
the Baltic Sea (Petersen et al., 2016). Furthermore, the water below the stratified layer has lower

1.2 Pressures  
The pressures Øresund faces are based on human activities that either directly or indirectly cause loss, 
damage, or change to the marine environment. These pressures are among others caused by offshore 
and coastal construction, fishing, chemical and noise pollution, marine traffic, eutrophication, and 
climate change (Petersen et al., 2018). 
Offshore construction changes the marine habitat by taking up space, changing the natural current 
through Øresund, and affecting bottom features including hydrology and substrate. Construction 
includes offshore windfarms, The Øresund fixed link, and artificial islands like the future coming 
Lynetteholm. Furthermore, due to these constructions there is both dumping of materials and 
extraction of raw materials in several areas in the Danish part of Øresund (Søfartsstyrelsen, 2023A). 
The areas in which raw materials were extracted has left behind oxygen limited dead zones since the 
large holes formed, are around 15 m deeper than the surrounding areas and therefore experiences 
limited mixing and hence inflow of oxygen rich water (Bredsdorff, 2022). Today extraction of raw 
materials is done over a larger area to avoid holes. The suction pipes create craters that are 1.5 m 
wide and 0.5 m deep as they are dragged across the seafloor extracting raw materials (Petersen, 2021).

Since trawling has been prohibited in Øresund since 1932, the seabed is not disturbed from fishing in 
the same way as most other coastal areas (Øresundsakvariet, 2023; DTU Aqua, May 2023).  
  
The Baltic Sea experiences nutrient enrichment which can cause eutrophication defined as large 
organic matter increase production in marine ecosystems (Kaiser et al., 2011). So when nutrient 
loading causes algae bloom, the microalgae then block the sunlight, so the macroalgae at the bottom 
cannot survive. Furthermore, the microalgae have a very short lifespan, and as they die, they become 
dead organic matter on the seafloor, which demands oxygen for degradation. This can cause oxygen 
depletion, especially in areas with limited inflow of oxygen-rich water (Kaiser et al., 2011; Murray 
et al., 2019). Furthermore, as mentioned earlier the Baltic Sea has limited vertical mixing due to a 
permanent halocline which means that the bottom water in the deeper parts are already low in oxygen 
(Mackenzie et al., 2000; Petersen et al., 2018). The severity of stratification and eutrophication is 
expected to get reinforced with climate change (Petersen et al., 2018). Eutrophication is linked to 
agriculture and densely populated areas. In Øresund eutrophication is determined by nutrient levels 
in the Baltic Sea as well as sewage treatment plants along the Danish and Swedish coastline (Petersen 
et al., 2018). Better treatment plants and management of fertilizers in agriculture has lowered the 
amount of nutrients in Øresund, which have reduced the occurrence of severe oxygen depletion 
(Petersen et al., 2018) although Øresund still experiences moderate oxygen depletion (Hansen & 
Rytter, 2022A). 
  
1.3 Biology 
Despite low oxygen levels, the biology in Øresund is considered unique, in a Danish context, due to 
the trawling ban and the salinity conditions (Petersen et al., 2018; Øresundsakvariet, 2023). The 
benthic fauna community distributions are determined by depth, currents, salinity and substrate 
(Petersen et al. 2018). In areas with stronger currents and harder substrate the filter feeders including 
horse mussels (Modiolus modiolus) and blue mussels (Mytilus edulis) dominate (Petersen et al., 
2018). These filter feeders are defined as keystone species because they form mussel beds that provide 
habitat for multitude of other species (Koivisto & Westerbom, 2010).   
Similar to macro fauna index in lakes, marine bottom fauna can be used to assess the ecological status. 
In Denmark DKI-index (Danish Quality Index – index) gives the area a value between 0 (no bottom 
fauna) and 1 (highest status; maximal diversity) to evaluate the ecological status (Hansen & Josefson, 
2013). When the environment is disturbed the macro fauna species composition changes towards 
==PAGE:10==

more disturbance tolerant species (Petersen et al., 2018). The disturbances could be low oxygen 
levels, low or high salinity, high or low temperature, pollution, eutrophication, or combined effects 
(Laine, 2003; Zillén et al., 2008; Broman et al., 2019; Liénart et al., 2020).  
  
Eelgrass beds are present at up to 6 m depth (Miljøstyrelsen, 2023A). The depth at which eelgrass 
beds occur are a measure of water quality (Petersen et al., 2018). Eelgrass contributes to carbon 
sequestration, serve both as spawning grounds, and nurseries and as habitats for marine species 
(Petersen et al., 2018). A study aiming to value the ecosystem services from eelgrass beds along the 
Swedish west coast found that in 20 years one hectare of eelgrass in this area can produce 626 kg of 
cod (gadoid) and 7535 individuals of wrasse (Labridae) while sequesting 98.6 tons carbon and 466 
kg nitrogen more than an area without vegetation (Cole & Moksnes, 2016; Petersen et al., 2018). 
Based on the study a 10 % eelgrass bed loss in Øresund would in 50 years mean a sequestration loss 
of 294,000 tons of carbon and 1,400 tons nitrogen along with 1,900 tons of cod (Petersen et al. 2018). 
This indicates that an increase in water quality has ecological, climatic, and economic benefits.  
  
1.4 Shipping in Øresund 
Shipping is one of the main human activities in the Øresund with potential large impacts on the marine 
environment. On average there are 2000 ships passing through the Baltic Sea every day (Reckermann 
et al., 2022). The shipping route to and from Saint Petersburg through the Danish straits is the busiest 
in the Baltic Sea. The shipping traffic in Øresund is split into two shipping channels, one shipping 
channel is going west of Saltholm along the trench Drogden and another route is going under the 
Øresund bridge, east of Saltholm along the trench Flinterenden as shown in Figure 1.3.  
Nautical charts show that there is dredging in the shipping channel under the Øresund bridge (Kort 
& Matrikelstyrelsen, 2007; Skippo). However, according to Sjöfartsverket in Sweden who manage 
the dredging, the channel has only been dredged twice since the bridge was built in the year 2000. 
Although the Swedish authorities did not reveal when the last dredging occurred, they check every 
3-5 years, and said it is time to do it again soon (pers. Comm. Sjøfartsverket 2023).

1.4.1 Shipping impacts  
Environmental impacts from shipping vessels can be divided into three categories: Discharge to the 
water, air emissions and physical impact as shown in Figure 1.4. Each category includes several 
factors. The physical impacts include noise, artificial lights, collision with wildlife, shoreline erosion 
and resuspension of sediments as well as ship grounding and sinking (Jägerbrand et al., 2019). Air 
emissions include greenhouse gases, sulphur oxides, nitrogen oxides, particulate matter, volatile 
organic compounds, and ozone-depleting substances (Jägerbrand et al., 2019). The discharges to the 
water from above include biocides, oil, chemicals, invasive species, bacteria, and nutrients 
(Jägerbrand et al., 2019).

A study on human impact of the Baltic Sea looked at how shipping impacts other aspects of the 
marine environment. The study found that shipping affects: Climate change, coastal processes, 
acidification, the marine ecosystem, introduction of invasive species, airbourne nitrogen, fisheries, 
water pollution, marine litter, and coastal management (Reckermann et al., 2022). Furthermore, it is 
unknown how much underwater noise from shipping impacts marine life (Reckermann et al., 2022).  
  
1.5 Mapping the seafloor 
Mapping of the seafloor is essential for any decision making in construction, monitoring, offensive 
and defensive military operations, environmental impact, extraction, and conservation purposes. 
Without investigating the seafloor, it is impossible to know the effect of human activities and the 
location of ecologically important areas to protect (Brown et al., 2011). 
The marine strategy framework directive has seafloor integrity as a descriptor. This means that sea 
floor integrity cannot be deteriorated, and it must be in good ecological status. Furthermore, there has 
recently been an increased interest on marine conservation. The UN has decided that all member 
states must protect 30 % of both terrestrial and marine areas, whereas 10 % of the area should be 
==PAGE:13==

DTU 
Spring 2023

strictly protected (UN environment programme, 2022). However marine protected areas are only 
advantageous if the protected areas are of conservation value and the level of protection is high 
enough (Lester et al., 2009). Furthermore, the increase in offshore construction demands a better 
characterisation of the seafloor to accompany the high demand for offshore windfarms while also 
protecting areas of ecological importance. Additionally, the previously mentioned impacts of human 
activity, such as shipping, are only speculation until the seafloor is investigated and monitored.  
  
Characterization of the seafloor is performed by dividing the seafloor into habitat types, however, the 
different habitat types are not agreed on. In Danish waters the ocean habitat can be divided into 
different types decided by the European Environment Agency, EUNIS (European Nature Information 
System). However, HELCOM, MSFD (Marine strategy framework directive) and most studies uses 
their own, different, definitions (Calvert et al., 2015) which makes comparison challenging. 
According to the definition by the European Environment Agency Denmark has eight different types 
of protected marine habitats, each characterized by a number code: “1110: Sandbanks which are 
slightly covered by sea water all the time”, “1130: estuaries”, “1140: mudflats and sandflats not 
covered by seawater at low tide”, “1150: Coastal lagoons”, “1160: Large shallow inlets and bays”, 
“1170: Reefs” , “1180: Submarine structures made by leaking gases”, and “8330 Submerged or 
partially submerged sea caves” (European Commission, 2013). These are mapped inside some of the 
Natura 2000-areas in Danish waters (Miljøstyrelsen, 2023B). EUNIS 2019 habitat classification will 
be used in this study (EMODNET, 2023). The Øresund area has the habitat types visualized in Figure 
1.5 and described in Table 1.1. The habitat types are named after what is dominant, for instance the 
MB43 Baltic infralittoral mixed sediment has less than 90 % of a specific substrate type, so it is a 
mix of different substrate types (European Environmental Agency, May 2023). The data for this study 
is collected just north of the Øresund bridge which is marked as “rock or other hard substate” however 
there is also “sand” and “biogenic substrate” in the area Figure 1.6.

1.5.1 Methods to map benthic habitat types  
The first attempts to study the seafloor and the benthic habitat were made using primitive grabbing 
devices to get samples. Today the methods are more advanced, including sediment cores, trawling, 
grabs, but also optical data from videos and photos. Although these new methods are improved, they 
only offer information about the sampled area and not surrounding areas (Brown et al., 2011).  
The bathymetry can give an indication of the species present. To find the bathymetry, the seafloor 
has been mapped from satellites, however the resolution is course and covers only a few kilometres 
and for most purposes a finer resolution is needed (Lecours et al., 2016). The bathymetry of the sea 
floor affects the currents and hence the environment. Therefore, bathymetry is important for models 
of both water movement and species distribution (Lecours et al., 2016). To obtain a finer scale 
bathymetry map a common approach is to use acoustic data combined with ground truthing data.  
  
Mapping of the seafloor using acoustic methods is a well understood method in marine geology. The 
first habitat maps were produced by completing in situ sampling of the seafloor in different geological 
areas. However, habitat boundaries are more gradual than geological boundaries, therefore, habitat 
maps are often presented with gradual boundaries instead of distinct boundaries (Brown et al., 2011). 
Ground truthing, either by visualizing with photos or video, studying with sediment cores, or 
performing grabs or trawling, is therefore necessary. Combining ground truthing data with acoustic 
data provides the necessary biological characteristics for habitat mapping (Brown et al., 2011). This 
is especially important for displaying the biodiversity (Vanreusel et al., 2009).  
There are five types of sonar/echosounders: Sub-bottom profile, side-scan, multi-beam, split-beam 
and single-beam. The sub-bottom profile is used for investigating what is below the sediment surface, 
side-scan sonar is used to estimate the height of underwater objects, multi-beam echosounders allows 
bathymetry to be inferred, split-beam echosounders are used for target tracking and the single-beam 
echosounders are used for bottom detection (Schultz et al., 2013; Palomer et al., 2016; Bhardwaj et 
al., 2020; Kim & Park, 2022; Ona & Nielsen, 2022).  
  
There are three general approaches to studying the benthic habitat, considering (1) one species, (2) a 
whole community or merely using (3) environmental abiotic data (Brown et al., 2011).  
(1) In single species habitat mapping, the niche of the species of interest is mapped. The 
environmental data corresponding to the niche of the species. Biogenic reefs structures can 
successfully be mapped using acoustics as their backscatter signatures are specific enough to map 
their distribution. Motile species are more challenging and demand multiple surveys across different 
==PAGE:17==
Anne Mette Rasborg 
DTU 
Spring 2023 
 
Page 17 of 80 
seasons. This approach does not take species interaction into account due to a lack of data which 
makes the interpretation of results challenging. Generally, this method works well for biogenic reef 
structures, sessile or slow-moving species (Brown et al., 2011). (2) In benthic community mapping 
the habitat and the species in it, also known as the biotope, are identified. There is controversy on the 
meaning of the concept biotope, as well as the classification of each of them. This approach takes the 
gradual change in species composition into account since the species distribution is not as distinct as 
the geological change. Several studies have been conducted using this method (Brown et al.,2011). 
(3) “Abiotic” habitat mapping is when the environmentally similar and different regions and not the 
species are of interest. It is called abiotic because due to the lack of knowledge in species-environment 
interactions, it is not known how the environmental differences or similarities will affect the species 
and communities. This method provides information that is beneficial for management decisions 
(Brown et al.,2011).  
  
1.6 Hypotheses and aim 
Preliminary studies showed that the seabed inside the sailing channel Flinterenden in Øresund 
appeared different from outside of the sailing channel. There are several hypotheses as to why the 
sailing channel is different from the rest of Øresund. In this study some of the physical hypotheses 
were investigated. These hypotheses include: 
1.     Sediment resuspension caused by the vessel propellers, which then suffocates the vegetation as 
it resettles.  
2.     The sailing channel is deep, so it is more susceptible to oxygen or light deficit.  
3.     The sailing channel is dredged, which digs away the vegetation. 
4.     The sailing channel Flinterenden, (which loosely translated means rocky) consists mostly of 
rocky substrate that is unfavourable to some species of vegetation. 
5.     There are stronger currents in the deep sailing channel which washes vegetation away and disturb 
the settlement of new algae. 
  
The aim of this study is to use data fusion of acoustic and optics data to investigate the effect of 
shipping in the sailing channel in Øresund.

2 MATERIAL AND METHOD 
The study was conducted using a literature search and analysis of acoustics and optical data collected 
prior to the study (September 2022) as well as acoustic and optical data collected during the study.  
As mentioned in the introduction, optics is a method of ground truthing. In this study the optics data 
is video, which allows for more information to be captured than images. Other types of ground 
truthing can be sediment cores or grabs however that was not used in this study. Ground truthing is 
an important tool in combination with acoustics. A study with random high and low backscatter 
strength in an area found with the optical ground truthing that there was bacterial mats and outcrops 
in the given area (Asada et al., 2022). Ground truthing data is essential for providing biological and 
biodiversity characteristics for habitat mapping (Vanreusel et al., 2009; Brown et al., 2011).  
  
Underwater acoustic technology development began with Leonardo Da Vinci but only gained 
meaningful traction after the sinking of Titanic, after which interest in detecting large underwater 
structures arose (Medwin & Clay, 1998). By 1935, acoustic methods were used to detect the distance 
to the seafloor and even to find schools of fish (Medwin & Clay, 1998). Acoustics theory investigates 
the properties and behavior of sound, depending on the properties of the medium. Underwater 
acoustics depend on the properties of the water, and it strongly depends on water density which drives 
changes in the speed of sound and is affected by temperature, salinity, and depth. Temperature and 
salinity may change in some areas, especially with seasonal and sometimes even daily (Jensen et al., 
2011). The Øresund may experience major changes in salinity (Petersen et al., 2018) and temperature 
throughout the year. Since the water close to the surface can vary a lot in temperature and salinity, 
which affects the sound properties of the echosounder as it moves across this surface layer these 
changes can cause some variation in the sonar performance (Jensen et al., 2011).

Acoustics can either be active or passive. Passive acoustics relies on listening for sound emanating 
from the environment. To study specific phenomena the major parameter to be adjusted is the 
frequency which can be varied depending on the different phenomena wished to investigate. For 
instance, the amount of rainfall at sea, and even the size of the raindrops, can be monitored with 
passive acoustics (Medwin & Clay, 1998). Furthermore, migration patterns of whales and their habits 
can be determined. For instance, the critically endangered Baltic Proper harbour porpoise (Phocoena 
phocoena) is monitored using survey stations throughout the Baltic Sea (Amundin et al., 2022). This 
information is important for conservation purposes (Medwin & Clay, 1998; Amundin et al., 2022). 
Moreover, some fish schools can be detected using passive acoustics as they release gas from their 
swim bladder (Hahn & Thomas, 2009). 
Active acoustics is when sound is actively produced and transmitted, information can then be inferred 
through the received echo. The sound will be backscattered, which means that the sound will be send 
back, as it encounters organisms, objects, or density changes (Benoit-Bird & Lawson, 2016). The 
intensity of the backscatter holds information about the source of the backscatter. The time difference 
between transmitting and receiving can be converted to distance when the speed of sound of the 
medium is known (Benoit-Bird & Lawson, 2016). The output of acoustics depends on several 
environmental factors and seafloor features. For instance, hard seafloors reflect sound almost twice 
as fast and with twice as much angle compared to soft bottom (Hovem & Dong, 2019). This study 
uses active acoustics.  
Acoustics are also known from marine mammals who can detect objects and communicate, now it is 
also used for classification and surveillance (Hovem & Dong, 2019).  
 There are five typical types of active sonars (Lecours et al., 2016; Jones et al., 2019):  
-       Single-beam echosounder.  
-       Split-beam echosounder 
-       Multi-beam echosounder 
-       Side-scan sonar 
-       Sub-bottom profiler 
Single-beam echosounders transmit a single sound beam while split-beam echosounders transmit 
three to four single-beams which gives the 3D position of the target (Benoit-Bird & Lawson, 2016). 
Multi-beam echosounders transmits hundreds of sound beams, therefore multi-beams have a wider 
coverage of the water mass and seafloor compared to the narrower coverage of the single-beam 
(Lecours et al., 2016). Furthermore, through a method called “beam-forming” it is possible to 
==PAGE:20==
Anne Mette Rasborg 
DTU 
Spring 2023 
 
Page 20 of 80 
determine 2D or even 3D positional information based on the phase angle between each of the beam 
return signals, and prior knowledge of the structure of the individual beam transducers (Wang et al., 
2023). Since there are many more beams overlapping, outlier removal can be used to improve the 
robustness of 3D positioning estimation of targets, making multi-beam much more accurate than split-
beam for target tracking. 
The side-scan sonar sends acoustic signals out from both sides of the transducer body and is used to 
characterize the structure and characteristics of the seafloor. Bright regions reflect more sound which 
means they are raised or hard surfaces, whereas darker regions reflect less sound, therefore, these are 
depressed or soft surfaces (Lecours et al., 2016).  
The sub-bottom profiler can provide an acoustic profile of materials under the bottom surface layer 
(Jones et al., 2019) it is mainly used for geological and geophysical applications. 
The advanced types of active sonar are becoming more affordable and therefore used for more 
purposes, like multi-beam sonar for mapping of seafloor characteristics, however single-beam is still 
standard on ships (Lecours et al., 2016) and therefore this study could be widely applicable. 
Furthermore, single-beam echosounders provide the information needed for this survey and the 
required processing is less when compared to other sonars. Therefore, this study uses data collected 
single-beam echosounders. 
 
When generating a model of the seafloor bathymetry over a larger area by using single-beam 
echosounders, the transect lines are very narrow and the area between transect lines must be inferred 
through interpoliation. The distances between the transect lines are important to minimize to avoid 
missing features of the seafloor (Lecours et al., 2016). This uncertainty can be dealt with by 
computing an uncertainty map (Lecours et al., 2016).  It can be compared with shining a pencil torch 
light down onto the seabottom and receiving a 1D measurement (acoustic signal as it varies with 
time). With split-beam a single 3D signal is received, and with multi-beam a swath of 3D signals is 
received. A study combining split-beam and multibeam found that the combination of the two 
improved species classification significantly (David et al., 2024). 
  
Acoustic data can be used to produce maps of the benthic habitat. There are two overall strategies 
when producing a map of the benthic habitat: 1) Supervised uses labelled data to interpret acoustic 
data and find habitat types and 2) unsupervised segments the acoustic signal based on similarities 
before assigning a habitat type (Calvert et al., 2015). Brown et al., (2011) proposed three strategies: 
==PAGE:21==
Anne Mette Rasborg 
DTU 
Spring 2023 
 
Page 21 of 80 
abiotic surrogates (unsupervised), assemble first, predict later (unsupervised) and predict first, 
assemble later (supervised).  
In the unsupervised strategy, the acoustic data is segmented first and then it is classified into habitat 
type using observations from ground-truth, which can be either camera or sampling. This strategy 
generally first creates clusters using methods like data clustering and pattern recognition and then 
assign the habitat type to each cluster whenever the map pixels and the ground-truth data co-occur 
(Calvert et al., 2015). The supervised strategy classifies the habitat type from ground-truth data first 
and then interprets the acoustic data. Here the ground-truth data is looked at by an expert to create 
the habitat type signature from the acoustic data, these can then be used to classify areas that have 
acoustic data but no ground-truth (Calvert et al., 2015).  
A study comparing supervised and unsupervised classifications concluded that supervised 
classification varies considerably depending on the type of data (backscatter etc.) used whereas 
unsupervised performance was not affected by type of data. Supervised accuracy depended on the 
generation of accuracy statistics. Unsupervised works well in coarse substrata but is not optimal for 
soft substrata. (Calvert et al., 2015).  
For benthic habitat and seabed characterisation, single-, split-, and multi-beam echosounders as well 
as side-scan sonars and sub bottom profilers are often used to acquire acoustic data, whereas ground-
truth data typically is a type of photo, video, or physical sample. Video often being the most practical 
since sampling cannot be done everywhere, and cameras configured for video capture sample much 
faster than cameras configured for image capture.  Although it is a trade off since there is a loss in 
image quality due to video compression algorithms (Calvert et al., 2015). The ground-truth data is 
then segmented into the different types of habitats based on the substrate and the biota as mentioned 
in the introduction. Studies do not necessarily use the same classification of habitats (Calvert et al., 
2015) which makes them difficult to compare.  
  
Today there are four methods used in studies of marine geomorphometry:  
(1) Satellite radar altimetry, which has good coverage but comparatively imprecise measurements.  
(2) Optical remote sensing, which is cheaper than other methods but can only be used in shallow 
water. 
(3) Active acoustics, which can easily be installed on a range of ocean operated vehicles and is getting 
cheaper to use.

the speed of light, the distance to the target can be calculated. Although it is limited to shallow water

2.3.1.2 The Acoustic equipment  
For data collection a Kongsberg Simrad EK80 was used. It has a frequency range between 30 and 
500 kHz, a pulse duration between 64 and 2048 ms, the pulse forms can either be continuous-wave 
(CW), which is one tone or frequency-modulated (FM) which contains more tones and hence more 
data, the transducer has the option between split-beam and single-beam (Kongsberg, 2019). 
The pulse duration is usually changed along with other parameters when there is a change in the sonar 
configuration. However, if the system is automatic, it will change the pulse duration with the depth. 
A study comparing long, medium, and short pulse duration found that short wave duration gives a 
more precise range performance however it is very sensitive to noise whereas longer wave duration 
deals better with noise but also gives a less clear range performance (Clarke, 2018).  
The pulse forms continuous-wave and frequency-modulated differ in performance. The frequency-
modulated will receive more signals, however in shallow water the signal can get distorted and 
provide a faulty visualisation (Clarke, 2018). 
The transducer used for data collection is a combi-split transducer ES38-18/200-18C, the single-beam 
works at 200 kHz with a frequency range of 190-230 kHz and the split-beam works at 38 kHz with a 
frequency range of 35-45 kHz. Both have a beamwidth of 18 degrees (Kongsberg, 2023). The ping 
rate was set to 4 pings/s.  
The frequency ranges determine how much sound is transmitted and hence how much will return. So, 
the 200 kHz will show plankton whereas the 38 kHz will not (Benoit-Bird & Lawson, 2016). 38 kHz 
was used for the bathymetry and bottom classification since the bottom is clear and appears less noisy 
compared to 200 kHz.

2.4.1.3 Geopositioning:  
The towfish was not located next to the GPS. Therefore, calculations had to be made to estimate the 
actual position of the towfish. To determine the distance between the towfish GPS and the towfish, 
the position of the boat had to be converted to a UTM projection which is a Cartesian coordinate 
system measured in Northings and Eastings (m). UTM stands for Universal Transverse Mercator and 
is a coordinate grid system that divides the globe into 60 zones. Each zone is a two-dimensional 
(northern, eastern) coordinate system which makes distance measurements more precise (Usgs, 
2017). This only works over short distances, since the surface of the earth is approximating a flat 
plane. If the distances between points a small (i.e. much less than the width of the zone), then the 
error is small. If the distance is large or crossing over several zones, then one should use great circle 
distances.  
To convert the towfish GPS position to UTM position the latitude, longitude from the towfish GPS, 
theta and depth from the towfish was used to approximate a rigid body transformation between 
Havørred’s position and the towbody. The latitude and longitude determined the UTM zone (33 N), 
and the zone then determined an ellipsoid which was used to set up the correct UTM. The longitude 
and latitude were then projected onto the UTM coordinate system using the projfwd function in 
matlab to get easting and northing values, down (NED) vector Inertial Frame (SNAME, 1950). NED 
was also written NE0, because the depth is set to zero, so the UTM plane is a constant. The position 
of the towfish relative to the towfish GPS was calculated using a front, right, down (FRD) vector or

2.4.3. Ground truthing: optical data and mosaic  
2.4.3.1 Visualisation of sailing channel:  
To visualize the difference between the inside and the outside of the sailing channel, a mosaic was 
constructed from the ground-truth data. A mosaic is several images stitched together by an algorithm 
==PAGE:34==
Anne Mette Rasborg 
DTU 
Spring 2023 
 
Page 34 of 80 
and can be used to track changes in the species distribution (Li et al., 2019). There are two overall 
approaches to creating the mosaics: Feature-based and featureless, which is also called direct based 
(Li et al., 2019; Thompson et al., 2023).  
For Flinterenden a mosaicking program developed by Professor emeritus Bo Lundgren was used 
(following the method in Lundgren, 2023). This approach was a featureless or direct based method, 
which means that the pixels of the frames are used directly. The frames are stitched together under 
the assumption that the individual frames have a large overlap in position, rotation, and scale (Li et 
al., 2019). A limitation for this method is moving objects and a sensitivity to poor light conditions 
(Li et al., 2019; Thompson et al., 2023). To accommodate these limitations and assumptions the 
program creates several short mosaics of one minute instead of a longer one where the motion 
displacement would be more likely to change (Lundgren, 2023).  
For Drogden the Python script Mosaic.py by Fletcher Thompson was used (following the method in 
Thompson et al., 2023). This approach was feature-based method which means the algorithm 
detects features in the frames and calculates how the frame should be transformed or “Warped” to 
fit with the previous frame (see Figure 2.6). Feature-based method is limited when there is a lack of 
distinct features, which is the case in Figure 3.18. A fundamental limitation in mosaicking is the 
rotation of the towfish in relation to the seabed. If the camera pitches upwards, the final mosaic will 
become smaller, like a pyramid, whereas a downwards camera pitch, will make the final mosaic 
larger, like an upside-down pyramid (Figure 2.7). If the camera is rolled to either side, the final 
mosaic will take the shape of a spiral (Thompson et al., 2023). The data on the pitch and roll of the 
towfish could remove this limitation however this was not done because it is beyond the scope for 
this study.

2.4.3.4 Colour correction method: 
Using video as ground truth can be challenging if the true colour is too distorted due to a lack of light. 
Light attenuates in water depending on depth and turbidity (Quartino et al., 2013) therefore, the video 
was colour adjusted. After some testing it was found that the matlab functions imadjust and 
chromadapt worked well for colour correcting a video. Imadjust increase the contrast of an image 
by changing the intensity values, furthermore, imadjust saturates 1 % of both top and bottom pixel 
values by default (Mathworks 2023). Using Chromadapt on RGB images will modify the colour 
balance. For Chromadapt a threshold is set on the lower colour which was set to 0.9 meaning cutting 
out the lowest 90 % of colour as well as the top 5 % of the white.  
To avoid that the order of the two commands has an influence, it was done twice, one with imadjust 
followed by chromadapt and then chromadapt followed by imadjust.  
Drogen video could not be colour corrected as the script made a blue ring in the center surrounded 
by red (Figure: 2.10) and due to time limitation the grey scale video was used as “colour neutral”.

Merging data between acoustic and optical systems, it was found that the GPS track from the raw

Horizontal Dilution of Precision or the amount of available satellites (Figure: 3.2 and Figure: 3.3). A 
GPS which is a Global Navigation Satellite System (GNSS) can determine the 3D position of a 
receiver on earth’s surface by measuring the distance to a minimum of 4 satellites (Catania et al., 
2020). GPS quality is dependent on the dilution of precision, the satellites visible and positioning 
accuracy (Rychlicki et al., 2020). A study comparing different GPS receivers conclude that GPS 
receivers cannot be compared without a specific utility software, and they found that there are 
differences between both types and devices of the same type of GPS receiver (Rychlicki et al., 2020). 
So, the observed difference between the two GPS’s could be due to this (Figure 3.2, and 3.3). The 
Horizontal Dilution of Precision (HDoP), which is an indicator of precision and accuracy of the GPS 
position is usually around 2.5 m inaccuracy, but it varies between GPS’s and the number of available 
satellites (Specht, 2022). Here the sensitivity of the antenna is important and since the acoustic GPS 
is more expensive, it might have more sensitive materials. Although the acoustic GPS was placed on 
the deck of the boat whereas the video GPS was placed on top of the wheelhouse which could result 
in less interference. The speed during data collection from the acoustic GPS and the towfish varies 
with up to one knot (see Figure 3.2 and Figure 3.3).

 4 DISCUSSION 
4.1 Hypotheses 
As part of the preliminary study it was found on google earth that the lack of vegetation in 
Flinterenden sailing channel was visible from satellites. The rest of Øresund appears dark green while 
the sailing channel appears light green as if the bare sea bottom was visible. This was quickly rejected 
since the google earth desktop version revealed a ship at the end of the light green trail, so this was 
in fact just the ship wake (Frontpage Figure). Although not from space, the seabed does appear 
different inside vs outside of the sailing channel. To explain the lack of vegetation inside the sailing 
channel, 5 possible hypotheses was proposed: 1) sediment resuspension, 2) light limitation due to 
depth, 3) dredging, 4) sediment uninhabitable for vegetation, and 5) currents. 
  
1 Sediment resuspension caused by the vessel propellers, which then suffocates the vegetation as it 
resettles.  
Sediment resuspension from vessels could be causing the lack of vegetation since the wake from 
vessels make enough turbulence to be visible on google earth. Whether the wake caused sediment 
resuspension or only surface bubbles were meant to be studied by crossing the sailing channel right 
==PAGE:60==
Anne Mette Rasborg 
DTU 
Spring 2023 
 
Page 60 of 80 
after a large vessel has passed through. This was not possible since the towfish experienced technical 
difficulties. But disturbance of the water column were observed on the ships echosounder in the wake 
from large vessels, which is common (Pers. Comm. Captain Thomas Møller).  
The resuspension of sediments is among other factors influenced by the vessel speed, size, and under-
keel clearance as well as the sediment grain size (Byrnes & Dunn, 2020). Resuspension of bottom 
sediments can suffocate vegetation both by increasing turbidity of the water and by blocking access 
of sunlight, uptake of nutrients as well as limiting available space (Schoellhamer, 1996; Eriksson & 
Johansson, 2005). Furthermore, the resuspended sediments can contain nutrients and toxins 
(Dzialowski et al., 2008; Byrnes & Dunn, 2020). Studies conducted at 8 m depth show that 
sedimentation limits growth of macroalgae and furthermore an increase in tolerance is seen in species 
with longer or continuous reproductive period compared to species with a shorter reproductive period 
linked to a seasonal cue (Eriksson & Johansson, 2005).  
Moving vessels make depression waves, also known as squats (Gourlay, 2008; Rapaglia et al., 2015). 
This happens when ships move to shallow water and the water underneath the hull starts moving 
faster to compensate for the lack of space. Then the ship gets pressed further down into the water. 
These can potentially stress the bottom especially with vessels like the ones that can be observed on 
the AIS website “marine traffic”. On a day in May, a passenger ship with a 7 m draught had a speed 
of 13 knots in Flinterenden. In Drogden a crude oil tanker of the same depth had a speed of 9.2 knots. 
Both sailing channels have a depth of 8 m. Propellers can make thrust waves and the vessel itself can 
make depression waves, and both can cause resuspension of bottom sediments which can impact the 
benthic community (Byrnes & Dunn, 2020). Therefore, it is likely that the sediment resuspension 
affects the vegetation. However, to confirm this hypothesis studies in deeper sailing channels without 
sediment resuspension must be conducted.   
  
2 The sailing channel is deep, so it is more susceptible to oxygen or light deficit.  
The depth of the sailing channel limiting oxygen and/or light was also suggested as a hypothesis. The 
NOVANA report showed that there was between moderate oxygen deficit (between 2 and 4 mg/L) 
and no oxygen deficit in Øresund in September 2022 (Hansen & Rytter, 2022B) however the 
measuring station closest to the study area did not measure during the period (Miljøstyrelsen, 2022). 
Furthermore, the area with reported oxygen deficit had deeper water than the study area, so it is 
expected that there was not oxygen deficit in the sailing channel.

Light is necessary for vegetation to undertake photosynthesis. Light attenuation through seawater 
increases with depth as the light get absorbed and scattered through the water column following Beer-
Lamberts law (Al-Zhrani et al., 2021). And it is affected by the amount of suspended particulate 
material in the water column (Doron et al., 2007). However, the transect crossed an area deeper than 
11 m and the ground truth data showed vegetation and other studies in the Baltic Sea observed well-
developed macroalgae at 16.5 m depth (Eriksson & Johansson, 2005), so light deficit due to depth 
can be rejected.  
  
3 The sailing channel is being dredged, which digs away the vegetation 
A study in the English Channel found that impacted sites had a less diverse community of 
macrofaunal species compared to unimpacted sites (Birchenough et al., 2010). They further found a 
difference in fauna composition depending on the extraction method by comparing trailer and suction 
dredged sites Figure 4.1(Birchenough et al., 2010). This indicates that the sediment is unstable, 
especially in the suction dredged sites (Birchenough et al., 2010). The study found that mussels thrive 
in the dredged pits (Birchenough et al., 2010) which corresponds with what looks like mussels in the 
sailing channel (Figure 3.16, 3.18). There are several categories of dredgers including mechanical 
dredgers and hydraulic dredgers (Agarwal, 2021). Mechanical dredgers mostly resemble a floating 
excavator. Hydraulic dredgers use a pumping system to remove materials, the power of the pump 
determines the size of the material (Agarwal, 2021). Figure 4.1 show impact of two types of hydraulic 
dredgers. A dredging project for a sailing channels in Danish waters plan to use hydraulic dredgers 
for removal of sand and silt and mechanical dredgers for removal of clay (Miljøministeriet, 2023). 
So, it can be assumed that the Swedish authorities also use multiple dredging methods for 
Flinterenden.

Dredging of the sailing channel would potentially remove all the vegetation both directly by digging 
it away and indirectly with sediment resuspension coverage. Even though it is unknown when the 
sailing channel was dredged last, it must be several years ago since the Swedish authorities intend to 
do it again soon (Pers. Comm. Sjøfartsverket). In the acoustic data from Flinterenden the bottom 
appeared steady (Figure 3.6). When dredging does not make the bottom unsteady, it does not have 
long term effects on vegetation since underwater vegetation have a variety of reproduction strategies 
that can be asexual, sexual, or fragmentation with either continuous or yearly reproduction periods 
(Eriksson & Johansson, 2005; Sand-Jensen et al., 2006). Furthermore, studies suggest dredging can 
be beneficial for vegetation if timed with the reproductive period (Combroux et al., 2002). Therefore, 
dredging is rejected as the reason for the lack of vegetation this long after dredging activities.

4.2 Results and methods  
The study took place in Øresund in Flinterenden sailing channel. Using both acoustic data and ground 
truthing data in the form of video which was data fused based on GPS position. Drogden sailing 
channel was studies to compare the results from Flinterenden to a similar sailing channel. The seabed 
appeared different inside Flinterenden sailing channel compared to the outside (Figure 3.15, 3.16, 
and 3.10). There appeared to be a gradual decline in vegetation from the crossing towards the middle. 
So, the middle part had the least vegetation and there was no clear transition line. Flinterenden sailing 
channel is approximately 500 m wide, whereas Drogden sailing channel is approximately 300 m wide 
(Skippo). The middle of the sailing channel is the part that was being dredged, however the middle 
of the sailing channel is also where the vessels prefer to sail when there is no other traffic. However, 
this gradual effect is not shown in the results due to limitations of the method, furthermore the colour 
adapted ground truth data, did not show an increase in luminescence inside the sailing channel at all. 
The luminescence was meant to be a measure for sand and hence lack of vegetation. This was done 
by detecting the white pixels based on a suitable threshold value (Figure 2.9) and then visualizing the 
amount of white. However, the percentage of white pixels could include the light haze in some frames 
as well as mussels with white shells. Whereas dark fog and mussels with dark shells like blue mussels 
would classify as vegetation. This could bias the data since there appeared to be blue mussel beds 
(Mytilus edulis) in the sailing channel (Figure 3.16), furthermore blue mussel spat was found in the 
area in May and there are biogenic habitats in the area (Figure 1.6). Increasing the threshold removes 
more of the haze but also more of the smaller sandy parts compared to a lower threshold. 
Drogden sailing channel appeared similar to Flinterenden sailing channel although not as much sand 
(luminescence) was detected in Drogden. This could be caused by the method not being suitable with 
the new camera. The color correction method was not possible on the Drogden data, because of an 
unknown color flaw (Figure 2.10) instead the luminescence was repeated on one of the grayscale side 
cameras (Figure 3.14). There is a slight increase in the percentage of white pixels in the middle of the 
sailing channel, however there is also spikes in luminescence before entering the sailing channel, so 
this method was not successful in quantifying of lack of vegetation. Furthermore, only absence of 
vegetation was accounted for in this method, not which type of vegetation, which could have been 
interesting.  
The ground truthing data shows that there are most red and brown algae outside the sailing channel 
in Flinterenden whereas Drogden is dominated by green algae outside of the sailing channel (Figure

3.17). Different algae have different preferences for several factors including substrate, light, salinity, 
pH, water currents, herbivores, and nutrients (Quartino et al., 2001; Sand-Jensen et al., 2006; Singh 
& Singh 2015) however species composition and distribution depends on the tolerance of species and

changes with salinity (Telesh et al., 2013). Salinity in Øresund varies between 8 and 35 PSU with 
higher salinity in the bottom water flowing southwards and lower salinity in the surface water flowing

even closer, there are areas with lower backscatter just above the bottom. This could be algae, further 
studies should investigate the target strength and the ground truthing, since mapping the seafloor 
would be expanded greatly if all vessels collected data on the biological bottom characteristics. 
Although acoustic studies have limitations since using different data processing and collection 
methods, will reach different results (McGonigle et al., 2010). Furthermore, classifying a benthic 
habitat is challenging because the definition of habitat does not work on different spatial scales. For 
instance, a rock on a sandy bottom is a habitat for several species of macroalgae, while the sand 
underneath is a habitat for lugworms (Brown et al., 2011). Terrestrial habitats often get characterized 
by the dominant plant species whereas marine habitats are not only determined by spatial structure 
but also environmental and hydrographic parameters (Brown et al., 2011). Biogenic structures do 
however give an indication of the environmental parameters (Brown et al., 2011) since they are 
limited by a variety of environmental factors (Kaiser et al., 2011).  
4.3 Vegetation 
Underwater vegetation plays a huge part in the global oxygen production and carbon sequestration. 
It has been assumed that most marine carbon sequestration was done by the complex root systems of 
angiosperms like seagrass meadows, salt marshes, and mangrove forests. However, a study shows 
that macroalgae, despite not having a complex root systems, since they grow on rocks, also contribute. 
The study found that macroalgae export 44 % of their primary production and 25 % of that is 
==PAGE:67==
Anne Mette Rasborg 
DTU 
Spring 2023 
 
Page 67 of 80 
sequested. In Danish waters it is sequested in the sediment (Krause-Jensen & Duarte, 2016; Ortega 
et al., 2019). Furthermore, macroalgae is a source of protein, polysaccharides, fatty acids, and 
minerals and can help restore food security, either by direct human ingestion or as feed for livestock 
(Øverland et al., 2018; Wade et al., 2020). And since macroalgae are a sustainable, renewable source 
of biomass, it can be used in biofuel to combat fossil fuels (John & Anisha, 2011).  
Vegetation, especially seagrass meadows provide food as well as protection from predators for 
juvenile fish and other species (Whitfield, 2017). An important part of fish nursery areas is 
connectivity among habitats (Whitfield, 2017). In Queensland, Australia 30 - 70 % of the nearshore 
fisheries production can be explained by connectivity between mangroves and salt marshes 
(Meynecke et al., 2008). Øresund provides a unique ecosystem in Danish waters since bottom 
disturbing fishing methods like trawling has been prohibited since 1932 (Øresundsakvariet, 2023). 
Therefore, the eelgrass meadows and macroalgae are more likely to be connected in Øresund 
compared to other areas.  
Although macroalgae has good qualities regarding potential for carbon sequestration, food security, 
biofuel and refuge, too much macroalgae can cause oxygen depletion during degradation. In the Baltic 
Sea there are cases of trophic cascades where the decrease in larger predatory fish, which increases 
the number of small predatory fish, which decrease the number of invertebrate grazers, which causes 
an increase in macroalgae blooms (Eriksson et al., 2009). This effect is only increased due to 
eutrophication (Eriksson et al., 2009)  
  
4.4 Shipping impacts 
Shipping vessels have made several adjustments to improve their voyage. To avoid drag from a rough 
surface made by marine organisms settling and growing on the hull of the ship, the hull is painted 
with antifouling paint. The antifouling paint contains several biocides to be able to avoid biofouling. 
Shipping vessels have found a way to accommodate new regulations on emissions of sulphur oxides 
to the atmosphere by using scrubbers to clean out exhaustion gas by spraying with water, which then 
emits the sulphur oxides to the ocean instead of the atmosphere (Jägerbrand et al., 2019; Reckermann 
et al., 2022). Scrubber water is acidic and will therefore increase acidification (Reckermann et al. 
2022). In order to obtain the optimal depth/boyancy of the boat regardless of ballast, the vessels can 
either take in or release ballast water. Ballast water is transported far before it is released again which 
means that marine life gets transported to new areas and can become invasive species (Costello et al., 
2022). Bilge water is wastewater from the engine room which means this water can contain 
==PAGE:68==
Anne Mette Rasborg 
DTU 
Spring 2023 
 
Page 68 of 80 
everything that has been in the engine room. Since it can be unfavourable to have this water building 
up in the engine room, some vessels still have a so called “magic pipe” that lets the water out. 
However according to the legislation, the bilge water cannot be released to the marine environment 
if it contains too much oil. Other types of wastewater that is not favourable on board is wastewater 
from toilets and from cleaning live animal storage this is called black water. From the 1st of June 2023 
black water must be treated before it gets released to the Baltic Sea (Jalkanen et al. 2021). Water from 
showers, laundry and dishwasher is called grey water.  
Although these adjustments and solutions for large vessels are legal, they are not necessarily 
favourable for the environment. The impact of shipping can be divided into three groups, the ones 
mentioned are included in discharges to water, but there is also physical impacts and air emissions 
(Jägerbrand et al., 2019).  
The discharges to the water from above include biocides, oil, chemicals, invasive species, bacteria, 
and nutrients. These can be very persistent and harmful for the marine environment. For instance, 
anti-fouling paint used to contain tributyltin (TBT) and since it causes imposex in marine gastropods, 
it has been forbidden since 1991. It is however persistent, so it is still found in the sediment all over 
the Baltic Sea (Miljøstyrelsen, 2023C, HELCOM, 2018B). A recent study looked at contaminants 
from shipping in the Baltic Sea and found the highest discharge in harbours and in busy shipping 
channels (Maljutenko et al., 2021). They found that surface circulation and stratification is important 
for the dispersion of contaminants, but also that there is a difference depending on time of year having 
less dispersion in summer compared to spring and autumn and suggest the dispersion of contaminants 
is a problem for the marine life (Maljutenko et al., 2021). The study points out that both the amount 
of marine traffic and the weather conditions change from year to year however the shipping channels 
remain the same (Maljutenko et al., 2021). Which could increase the pressure on the marine 
environment in proximity to the shipping lanes. The study concludes that it is very difficult to monitor 
contaminants from ships since the dispersion happens very fast and the background levels of the 
contaminants makes it undetectable (Maljutenko et al., 2021).  
The physical impacts include noise, artificial lights, collision with wildlife, shoreline erosion and 
resuspension of sediments as well as ship grounding and sinking (Jägerbrand et al., 2019). Noise and 
artificial light are important factors in environmental impact assessments, especially for species that 
use light or noise for orientation or foraging however in Danish waters the sailing channels go through 
marine protected areas in several locations (Søfartsstyrelsen, 2023A). A study on the effect of boating 
on aquatic vegetation showed that species that thrive in turbid, nutrient rich water are more common 
==PAGE:69==
Anne Mette Rasborg 
DTU 
Spring 2023 
 
Page 69 of 80 
in coastal areas next to busy boating areas (Eriksson et al., 2004). Which indicated that the water 
quality near boating areas is not as good as aimed for. Wave energy causing coastal erosion is well 
known (Jägerbrand et al., 2019) but it is also able to uproot macroalgae (Gabel et al. 2017). The ship 
induced waves can change the species composition and abundance of macroalgae furthermore it also 
affects the growth rate of benthic invertebrates in shallow water as well as vulnerable life stages and 
growth in fish (Gabel et al., 2017). Common for several studies on shipping effects on ecosystems is 
that the wave energy focused on, is mainly horizontally away from the ship towards coastal systems 
and not vertically towards the seabed (Jägerbrand et al., 2019, Eriksson et al., 2004, Gabel et al., 
2017) although these so called squats from vessels and thrust waves from propellors have potential 
to impact the benthic community (Gourlay, 2008; Rapaglia et al., 2015; Byrnes & Dunn, 2020).  
Air emissions include greenhouse gases, sulphur oxides, nitrogen oxides, particulate matter, volatile 
organic compounds, and ozone-depleting substances (Jägerbrand et al., 2019). Shipping potentially 
has a net cooling effect since sulphur oxide and nitrogen oxide cool more than greenhouse gases 
warm (Jägerbrand et al., 2019). Although these gases are released in the air, they influence the water 
for instance nitrogen oxide causes acid rain in freshwater systems and acidification in marine systems 
furthermore it increases the amount of bioavailable nitrogen and therefore increases eutrophication 
moreover it can react with volatile organic compounds to form a precursor of ground level ozone 
(Jägerbrand et al., 2019).  
There are solutions being engineered to combat some of the environmental impacts. For instance, 
propellers with looped blades that are less loud and use less fuel compared to normal propellers 
(McFall-Johnsen, 2023). If the solutions should be taken into action it is important that they are cost 
effective although legal action is sometimes needed for green solutions in big industries.   
  
4.5 Climate change 
It is suggested that climate change will affect shipping. Changing climate conditions have clear 
impact on ships: weather including both visibility, wave height, storm frequency and severity (Lobeto 
et al., 2021), ice conditions, and sea level change (Reckermann et al., 2022). Furthermore, as the ice 
melts more shipping routes will be possible in the future. This can create bottlenecks for shipping 
however as sea level increases the narrow shipping channels are expected to expand (Reckermann et 
al., 2022). Climate change will also change precipitation, wind, and temperature patterns. 
Precipitation can be the strongest driver for biodiversity loss in the sound as it can change salinity, 
furthermore it can increase stratification (Meier & Kauker, 2003; Whitfield et al., 2012). Another 
==PAGE:70==

factor with climate change is the occurrence of more algae blooms, also toxic algae like blue-green 
algae (Petersen et al., 2018). Which can be detrimental to marine life and ocean-based aquaculture. 
  
4.6 Further studies  
To determine the reason for the lack of vegetation in the sailing channel, more studies must be 
conducted. The same data collection should be done in the same area where dredging is not done, in 
a deeper sailing channel with similar traffic, for instance Storebælt. Furthermore, samples of the 
sediment would be interesting to study the size of the sediment grains, biology, and toxicology to 
document the effect of pollution from discharges to the water.  
The method used for this study was the beginning to investigating the potential for using single-beam 
data to characterize the seabed habitat. It should be possible to use target strength to classify the 
bottom. If the data is precise enough, maybe even determine the type of vegetation.  
A study comparing multi-beam data and single-beam data in an area in Norway, found that single-
beam data from for instance fishing vessels can be used to mapping sediment and biotopes. It is not 
as good as multi-beam, but nevertheless there is more single-beam data already available (Elvenes et 
al., 2014). They used the supervised method, and they conclude that single beam data can be used 
where multibeam data is not possible although the maps will have lower resolution. However, the 
potential to get the distribution of vulnerable species worldwide is recognised (Elvenes et al., 2014). 
The distribution of for instance coral reefs or eel grass meadows are going to change with the 
changing climate so global monitoring is a huge advantage.  
  
5 CONCLUSION  
In conclusion, there was developed a method to fuse acoustic and optics data from Flinterenden, that 
was analyzed, and compared to similar data from Drogden to document the difference between the 
seabed outside of the sailing channels and the seabed inside the sailing channel. The method to 
quantify the vegetation cover, should be improved so it works on multiple video formats and to target 
presence of vegetation instead of absence of vegetation. Furthermore, the method should be expanded 
to target species of interest and hence make it possible to map the distribution of vulnerable species. 
Øresund has a very unique bottom since trawling has been banned for 70 years. In other areas where 
bottom disturbing activities are carried out, other activities could be causing the observed difference, 
but inside the sailing channels in Øresund, the disturbance is most likely caused by shipping traffic.
