Abstract
The problem of estimating the impulse response of a contrast agent moving into and
through tissue in the brain is analysed. In this thesis, Bayesian methods are used to
derive a posterior for a model of the experimentally found data. From this, methods
for estimating the impulse response, and speciﬁcally the perfusion into the tissue, are
developed and tested both in simulations, and on data from an MRI experiment. It was
found that in this Bayesian model, the estimate of the perfusion and the impulse response
are fairly reliable as long as measurements are made suﬃciently frequently compared to
how fast the impulse response changes.

Introduction

Introduction
Every year people get diagnosed with an illness of the brain such as brain tumors, en-
cephalitis, strokes and more. In order to be able to eﬀectively be able to deal with and
monitor problems in areas such as the brain without unnecessarily endangering the patient
through dangerous exploratory surgery, non-invasive methods using external scanners are
often required. These methods can give the data necessary to provide a diagnose for the
problem as well give critical information regarding the location and extend of a problem
needed for the creation of an appropriate plan for medicinal treatment and potentially
surgery.
In particular, an important feature of the brain is the blood-brain barrier, which is a
membrane used by the body to prevent dangerous molecules from entering the brain as
well as regulating the amount of nutrients the brain gets. Damage to this barrier may
pose serious danger to the patient, so being able to measure the transport of molecules
across this barrier is of interest on its own. Furthermore, many problems such as tumors
and alzheimers may be connected with damage to the barrier or otherwise modify the
distribution of chemicals such as nutrients across the brain. Accurately diagnosing prob-
lems within the brain will then include measuring the way that chemicals move through
it, which, in practice, can be done by measuring the perfusion. This is a numerical value
sometimes deﬁned as the volume of blood which enters a volume of tissue per unit of time.
An unusually high value of this may then indicate that there is undesired transport across
the barrier and consequently the presence of an underlying issue in the brain. For these
reasons, measurements of the perfusion can be highly relevant to an accurate diagnosis.
When measuring perfusion one needs to choose what you measure the perfusion of as
diﬀerent chemicals can travel through the brain very diﬀerently. For example, water ﬂows
through the blood-brain barrier easily, while sugar is only transported across the blood
brain barrier in limited amounts and so a large portion will enter and leave a volume of
brain tissue along with the blood. Then the brain tissue’s response to diﬀerent chemicals
can be measured by measuring the movement of another detectable chemical, called a
contrast agent, which behaves like the chemical of interest and can then be used as a
proxy measurement. In magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), the contrast agent such as
dimeglumine gadopentetate induces magnetic resonance in the nearby water molecules
which can then be measured, while in positron emission tomography (PET) the contrast
agent is radioactive and emits positrons which collide and annihilate electrons, releasing
two gamma rays in opposite directions which can be measured with light sensors, similar
to a CT scan.
For numerical purposes, the brain is modelled in a discretized manner such that it is
made up of a large amount of voxels. Then, at the beginning of an experiment, the contrast
agent is injected into the patient, either slowly or quickly as a bolus. The consequent
measurements from MRI or PET are turned into measurements of the concentrations
corresponding to each voxel in the brain.
Repeated measurements then give way to
concentration curves for the contrast agent in each voxel that are then analyzed. These
curves can be constructed diﬀerently. In MRI, the measurements are often with a constant
time between sampling and over a relatively short duration, for example for 15 minutes
after the contrast agent has been injected. In contrast, for PET, the measurements may

The goal of thesis is to investigate the numerical calculation of the impulse response
from equation (1) with a focus on calculating the perfusion from this. The problem will
be considered from three angles, computational eﬃciency, i.e. ﬁnding ways to calculate
estimates quickly, the quality of estimates, and the uncertainty of estimates. In chapter 2
we will elaborate on the forward model and discuss common ways of calculating r found in
literature, as well as how to extend them to be more considerate of physical concerns. This
will then motivate chapter 3 where we loosen model assumptions derive some theory about
the impulse response based on Bayesian calculations. In chapter 4 we will deﬁne basic
methods to simulate measurements from MRI and PET. Then in chapter 5 these methods
will be exploited to create the numerical methods necessary for solving the problem of
ﬁnding the impulse response as well as model parameters in practice. These methods will
be for doing point estimates of r of it as well as methods for ﬁnding or approximating
the full distribution which will be useful for uncertainty quantiﬁcation in the estimate
of the impulse response, broadly, but also speciﬁcally for the estimate of the perfusion.
In chapter 6 we will investigate how the derived methods work in various test scenarios.
Then in chapter 7 the methods will be deployed on data from real measurements from an
experiment with MRI. Finally, in chapter 8 the results of the thesis will be concluded.

A Bayesian approach
An obvious question in the Bayesian interpretation of equation (5) is what happens if
we try to take into account that the measurement of the kernel of equation (1), ca, is
itself a random variable. This is a relevant question as assuming no uncertainty in the
measurement of the kernel may lead numerical solution methods to give results ﬁtted to
errors in the approximation of the integral. One would then expect taking into account
the uncertainty in the kernel to give a kind of natural regularization for the problem,
hopefully leading to more stable estimators.

or scalars.

Actually ﬁnding the diﬀerential relatively easy for a function, as long as it is composed

derivatives [12, p. 163, 164].

Applying the model in practice
5
Applying the model in practice
To actually apply the ideas derived in section 3 we will need a number of methods. First,
we will want to calculate point estimates of r similar to the linear model in section 2 as
these will be useful for comparison. The ones we will look at are maximum likelihood
estimates (ML estimates) as well as maximum a posteriori estimates (MAP estimates).
These will be discussed in section 5.1. We will also to analyse the full posterior distribution
for the impulse response r as this will give us a good idea of how uncertainty in the impulse
response presents itself. Methods for estimating the full posterior will be discussed in
section 5.2. In order to give a relatively cheap estimate of the full distribution we will
also look at using the Laplace approximation in section 5.3. As several of these methods
will eﬃcient calculation of the gradient of the log posterior we will also discuss how to
implement these in section 5.4. Finally, some methods for estimating model parameters
will be discussed in section 5.5.
5.1

5.2
Markov chain monte carlo methods
Knowing the full distribution of the posterior p (r|˜ca, ˜ct) enables graphically plotting the
uncertainty in each variable, which is helpful when understanding a point estimate. It also
gives uncertainty quantiﬁcation, which can be used to give more informative estimates of
the impulse response function and the perfusion. For these reasons we would like to be
able to calculate, or at least estimate, the full distribution of the impulse response r. One
family of methods for doing this are the Markov chain Monte Carlo methods (MCMC)
[16]. Brieﬂy, in these methods we generate a Markov chain on the state space of r which
has the stationary distribution p (r|˜ca, ˜ct). By sampling from the stationary distribution
we can then get a set of samples which can be used to construct histograms and calculate
statistics of interest. The ﬁrst sample of r should be generated randomly. Note that
as only samples from the stationary distribution can be used, samples from the burn-in
phase need to be discarded.

5.2.3
Nonnegative sampling
Since we know the impulse response r cannot be negative, it might be a good idea to try
and enforce a nonnegativity constraint on r. We see that
p (r|˜ca, ˜ct, r ≥0) = p (r, r ≥0|˜ca, ˜ct,)
p (r ≥0|˜ca, ˜ct,)
= p (r ≥0|r, ˜ca, ˜ct,)
p (r ≥0|˜ca, ˜ct,) · p (r|˜ca, ˜ct) ,
where the fraction is a constant, with the numerator being 1 for r ≥0 and 0 otherwise,
and the denominator being some normalizing constant. Then adding a nonnegativity
constraint is equivalent to just renormalizing the posterior density p (r|˜ca, ˜ct) and setting
it to 0 if the constraint is violated. That leads to a number of ways to ensure nonnegative
samples of r. For example, using this new posterior density in MCMC would theoretically
work. However, this can severely complicate the sampling procedure. We know from
section 4 that we expect the impulse response to eventually approach zero as all contrast
agent should eventually leave the tissue. In reality this is complicated by the fact that,
depending on the contrast agent and the state of the tissue being observed, contrast agent
may stick around for quite a long time. Nonetheless, in general we should expect values of
r to be arbitrarily near zero. For that reason, sampling the way described in the previous
sections will almost always lead to some component of the proposal being negative, a
problem which becomes worse as r increases in dimension. Consequently, the acceptance
rate of proposals will either become extremely low, or the variance σ2
prop will be made too
small for the algorithm to explore the state space properly. In either case we would not
expect good results from MCMC. Alternatively, we can instead enforce r > 0 by using
the transformation r = ez and sampling from z. One issue with this is that the MAP
estimate of section 5.1 depends on the parameterization of r, though the MLE does not.
This lack of invariance with respect to the MAP estimate will further lead to potential
issues in section 5.3 which relies on this estimate. Furthermore, as we are most interested
in the maximum of r which describes the perfusion, and should be relatively far from zero,
it is questionable how much importance should be placed on this constraint. It is also
worth noting that due to the tendency of r to go to zero fast, even if the perfusion is very
small, in the log domain a positive solution will span many orders of magnitude. This
creates some modelling diﬃculties which will need to be addressed if a reparameterization
of r = ez is to be used. Some preliminary testing showed that it led to estimates of the
perfusion that were extremely unstable, so it is considered outside the scope of the thesis.

8
Conclusion
Methods to estimate the impulse response of the tissue were developed based on Bayesian
methods. This led to methods such as the MCMC method for quantifying uncertainty in
the estimate of the perfusion. A test of the Laplace approximation showed that this gave
a good description of the posterior distribution of the impulse response, at least in terms
of quantiles. However, as ﬁgure 9 showed, there are problems with the potential existance
extraneous maximum a posteriori estimates. It was argued that the MAP estimate should
be reliable, and in testing it was and testing showed this to be the case. Furthermore, the
method for estimating the hyperparameters of the prior were shown to work fairly well,
at least in the case of the Matérn kernel, which consistenly performed as well or better
than the squared exponential kernel. Furthermore, as ﬁgure 6 showed, if the time scale
of the change in the impulse response is too short relative to the sampling time, then the
estimate perfusion can be expected to be a lot more inaccurate than normal.

One of the major considerations when estimating the full impulse response rather than
just focusing on the maximum value is how to enforce a nonnegative constraint eﬀectively.

orders of magnitude, a good method will require nonstationary length scales and variances
in the prior as well as a nonstationary mean value.
