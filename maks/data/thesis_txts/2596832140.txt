Abstract
Synchronization of coupled phase oscillators is a prominent branch in nonlinear sciences. This phe-
nomenon occurs in large natural and artiﬁcial systems, from the harmonized dance of ﬁreﬂies to the
phase-locking of superconducting Josephson junction arrays. Their large number of dynamical units
have spurred the development of dimensional reduction techniques. In this report we depart from the
so-called Watanabe-Strogatz (WS) reduction for N identical oscillators with global sinusoidal coupling
that simpliﬁes the analysis to a (N −3)-parameter family of 3-dimensional manifolds. Then we refor-
mulate the time-dependent WS transformation as a conformal automorphism of the unit disk, widely
considered in complex analysis. The study of M¨obius transformations and specially Blaschke factors,
in close relation to hyperbolic geometry, reveals several interesting geometric and algebraic properties
of the reduced dynamics. Furthermore, we connect the celebrated Ott-Antonsen (OA) ansatz to Pois-
son kernels, or equivalently, wrapped Cauchy densities, and the reduced OA manifold to an invariant
2-dimensional Poisson submanifold. Thereupon we consider the paradigmatic Kuramoto-Sakaguchi
model and simulate the aforementioned reduced system for diﬀerent values of the phase-lag in the
coupling. Finally, we further extend the study to the intriguing case of two non-locally, symmetrically
coupled populations of identical oscillators, which exhibits chimera states with coexistence of coherence
and incoherence. The variation of the WS constants of motion, away from the uniform distribution,
will particularly unveil the emergence of breathing chimeras oﬀthe OA manifold, in analogy to the
unihemispheric sleep observed in some animals.
Keywords— Kuramoto-Sakaguchi model; Watanabe-Strogatz reduction; M¨obius transformations;
Hyperbolic geometry; Ott-Antonsen manifold; Breathing chimera

Introduction
“One of the pleasures of looking at the
world through mathematical eyes is that
you can see certain patterns that would
otherwise be hidden.”
Steven H. Strogatz
Collective synchronization is one of the most intriguing and frequent phenomena in nature and engi-
neering, ranging from the circadian rhythm in pacemaker cells and brain activity via neural networks
to the functioning of power grids and coupled laser arrays. Thus the study of self-organized synchro-
nization in large ensembles of interacting oscillators is a prominent branch in nonlinear science.
The paradigmatic Kuramoto model is the most popular one for the analysis of such large systems of
coupled phase oscillators, corresponding to the simplest case of uniform, all-to-all, purely sinusoidal
coupling. In chapter §1 we examine its mean-ﬁeld character through the Kuramoto order-parameter,
and then we derive the self-consistency condition from the transport equation by distinguishing be-
tween phase-locked and drifting oscillators in the continuum limit, as well as the critical coupling
strength from which partially synchronized states emerge, and ﬁnally we apply these results to the
Cauchy frequency density as done by Kuramoto [1975] in his earliest contribution to the ﬁeld.
With the aim of understanding the rich variety of dynamical scenarios exhibited by coupled phase
oscillators, and diminishing the computational costs for their simulation, modern dimensional reduc-
tion techniques have recently dealt with this challenge by smartly describing the mean-ﬁeld dynamics
with few macroscopic variables. The ﬁrst exact method was splendidly brought to light by Watanabe
and Strogatz [1994], closely connected to the work of Wiesenfeld and Swift [1995] on superconducting
Josephson junction arrays. Chapter §2 introduces the velocity ﬁeld for identical oscillators with global
sinusoidal coupling under consideration in Watanabe-Strogatz (WS) reduction, as well as the crucial
time-dependent WS transformation that relates the N oscillator phases with constants of motion via
just three macroscopic quantities. Then we review how the corresponding system of reduced equations
describes a (N −3)-parameter family of invariant 3-dimensional manifolds by properly applying three
additional constraints from the conversion of initial conditions (ICs) into the system constants.
The exceptionally simple collective dynamics of sinusoidally coupled systems led Marvel et al. [2009]
to recently reveal the action of a special M¨obius group as the responsible of this hidden structure.
In chapter §3 we reformulate WS reduced system in terms of M¨obius transformations, speciﬁcally
as conformal automorphisms of the unit disk D, also known as Blaschke factors. This alternative
description enables us to further apply the striking properties of such mappings from complex analysis
1
==PAGE:5==
and group theory in order to better understand the geometric and algebraic features of the reduced
dynamics, gathering the main results in Pikovsky and Rosenblum [2011] and Chen et al. [2017] as well.
In fact, we end the chapter by showing the close relation between Blaschke factors and hyperbolic
geometry in the Poincar´e disk that unfolds the gradient condition for Kuramoto-like phase models.
Another major breakthrough in the ﬁeld was carried out by Ott and Antonsen [2008] who discovered
a singular ansatz to analyze complicated phase oscillator continua under some regularity conditions.
In §4 we present the Ott-Antonsen (OA) ansatz by expanding the phase density as a complex Fourier
series and introducing the generalized Daido order-parameters.
The restriction of this method to
identical oscillators allows us to relate it with the aforementioned M¨obius reduced system in the
continuum limit, and we will show that this only happens for a uniform distribution of constants of
motion, which indeed unveils the nature of the OA ansatz as a Poisson kernel, corresponding to a
wrapped Cauchy distribution that is invariant under Blaschke factors. In diﬀerential-geometric terms
it deﬁnes the OA manifold as the 2-dimensional submanifold in the inﬁnite-dimensional space of phase
densities consisting of Poisson kernels, which in this case is not globally attracting as can be seen from
the ﬁnite-N continuum formalism recently proposed by Engelbrecht and Mirollo [2020].
In chapter §5 we put theory into practice by considering the Kuramoto-Sakaguchi (KS) model as an
extension of the original Kuramoto model that makes its dynamics richer just by allowing a phase-lag
in the coupling.
The associated reduced system from WS reduction using the M¨obius formalism,
in agreement with OA theory, simpliﬁes the analysis and shows the complementarity of its stability
domains for the synchronous and incoherent states. Furthermore, this averaged system serves as show-
ground to introduce the special case corresponding to a Hamiltonian vector ﬁeld by using Wirtinger
[1927] calculus, and supported by simulations in Matlab.
Finally, in §6 we further generalize the study to the Kuramoto-Okuda (KO) model, speciﬁcally to
two symmetrically coupled populations of identical phase oscillators with homogeneous phase-lag and
non-local coupling that decreases with distance, in line with Abrams et al. [2008] that presented
the ﬁrst solvable model for chimera states: a symmetry-breaking phenomenon where synchrony and
asynchrony coexist. Then we propose a selection of constants of motion that spread around the unit
circle in terms of a crowding parameter q that reveals an alternating synchronized-desynchronized
pattern between populations in the associated bifurcation diagram, showing the birth of breathing
chimeras oﬀthe reduced OA manifold and connecting it to the unique phenomenon of unihemispheric
sleep observed in some animals by Rattenborg et al. [2000].

1.1
Mean-ﬁeld character
The Kuramoto model was originally motivated by the natural phenomenon of collective synchroniza-
tion in which an enormous system of oscillators locks to a common frequency in the long run, as can
be seen in nature through the gathering of millions of ﬂashing ﬁreﬂies in harmonized dance in the
Great Smoky Mountains National Park in the United States, or the synchronized emergence of noisy
periodical cicadas exactly every 17 years in West Virginia.

coupled to the collective rhythm generated by the whole populations (in analogy to mean-ﬁeld ap-
proximation in physics), and using perturbation methods of averaging, showed that the long-term
dynamics for any system of N weakly coupled, nearly identical limit-cycle oscillators is given by
ordinary diﬀerential equations (ODEs) of the form:

the Kuramoto order-parameter for a stationary or steady solution, which is characterized by a constant

2.4
ICs and constants of motion
In order to simulate the WS reduced system (2.5), or equivalently (2.10), we have to be able to convert
any given initial conditions {θk(0)} in the N oscillator phases into a corresponding set of N constants
of motion ψk and 3 initial values γ(0), Θ(0), Ψ(0) for the WS variables. Since the WS transformation
(2.3) at t = 0 only deﬁnes N constraints on this set of N + 3 unknowns, there are several ways to
accomplish this conversion depending on the choice of the 3 additional constraints that get rid of such
multiplicity. A trivial and valid option is to impose all three of them on the WS reduced variables:
γ(0) = Θ(0) = Ψ(0) = 0, so that ψk = θk(0). However, a more natural dynamical choice is to deﬁne
them on the system constants {ψk} so that solution trajectories corresponding to diﬀerent initial
oscillator phases will evolve under the same ﬂow. In this way, the eﬀectively 3-dimensional ﬂow (2.5)
describes the reduced dynamics on the invariant manifold distinguished by the markers or constants of
motion {ψk}, of which N −3 are independent when imposing the three additional constraints on them.
Therefore, for N ≥3, the entire state space TN of phases {θk} can be foliated by a (N −3)-parameter
family of invariant 3-dimensional manifolds (leafs), each one governed by the dynamics of the reduced
system (2.5) and the WS transformation (2.3) determined by the constants of motion {ψk}.

M¨obius transformations are a special type of analytic functions in complex analysis, speciﬁcally the
rational complex functions of degree one, that have astonishing connections with geometry and group
theory: they are conformal automorphisms of the Riemann sphere, they send circles to circles, they
enjoy the symmetry principle, and they can be identiﬁed with matrices in the projective special linear
group, among other properties that we will investigate in what follows.
To begin with, we recall some notions from complex analysis (see e.g. Gamelin, 2001). Given an open,
connected domain D ⊂C of the complex plane, the class of holomorphic or complex diﬀerentiable
functions f : D →C is equivalent to the one of analytic functions in D. We say that f is conformal
at z0 ∈D if it preserves (oriented) angles, that is, if it maintains the angle of intersection of any pair
of smooth paths with non-vanishing tangent vectors at z0. Note that the local eﬀect of f on a path
is the multiplication of its tangent vector by the complex derivative f′(z0) at the point, that is, it
gets scaled by a factor |f′(z0)| and rotated by an angle arg(f′(z0)), as well as moving its base-point
to f(z0). Moreover, if we have that f is an analytic 1-to-1 function from D onto its image f(D), then
necessarily f′(z) ̸= 0 at every point, so f automatically becomes conformal everywhere. If such a map
exists, we say that D and f(D) are conformally equivalent (D ∼= f(D) for short), and particularly we
call f a conformal automorphism of D if it is a conformal one-to-one mapping of D onto itself.
In this regard, we ﬁnd the well-known Riemann mapping theorem: any simply connected domain
D ⊂C, not the whole C, is conformally equivalent to the open unit disk D = {z ∈C : |z| = 1}. In
fact, this result can be further generalized for Riemann surfaces (i.e. complex 1-dimensional analytic
manifolds with holomorphic transition functions) by the so-called uniformization theorem: any simply
connected Riemann surface is conformally equivalent to either the unit disk D, the complex plane C,
or the entire Riemann sphere ˆC. Here we interpret the Riemann sphere as the extended complex plane
ˆC = C ∪{∞}, identifying each point in the unit sphere S2 ⊂R3 with the corresponding one given by
the so-called stereographic projection, and the north pole with the extra point at inﬁnity, so that we
can deal with the problem of undeﬁned division by zero and non-compactness of the complex plane.
In this context, as shown in Milnor [2006], we can now classify the conformal automorphisms in any
simply connected domain D ⊂ˆC and their group structure Aut(D) under composition, as follows:
(1) If D = ˆC, the complex 3-dimensional Lie group Aut(ˆC) ∼= PSL2(C) consists of all M¨obius
transformations.
(2) If D ∼= C, the complex 2-dimensional Lie subgroup Aut(C) is equal to the group of all complex
aﬃne transformations, corresponding to M¨obius transformations that ﬁx the point at inﬁnity.
(3) If D ∼= D, the real 3-dimensional Lie group Aut(D) ∼= PSL2(R), having the topology of the open
solid torus ∂D × D, is made of Blaschke factors.

Kuramoto-Sakaguchi model
A few years after the revelation of the Kuramoto model that supposed a major breakthrough in the
ﬁeld of coupled oscillator networks, Sakaguchi and Kuramoto [1986] generalized it by considering a

dynamics of two coupled oscillator populations in the next chapter.

6.3
Chimera states
A particularly relevant synchronization phenomenon was discovered by Kuramoto and Battogtokh
[2002] in which a quasi-continuous array of identical oscillators could sharply split into a synchronized
and desynchronized subdomains. This fascinating symmetry-breaking anomaly was named chimera
state (from Greek mythology, a ﬁre-breathing monster with a lion’s head, a goat’s body, and a ser-
pent’s tail) by Abrams and Strogatz [2004], and deﬁned as a spatio-temporal pattern in a network
of non-locally coupled identical oscillators in which synchronous (phase and frequency locked) and
asynchronous (drifting) phase oscillators coexist. In fact, this intriguing behavior mirrors the uni-
hemispheric sleep seen in aquatic mammals, birds (even in mid-ﬂight, Rattenborg et al., 2016), and
reptiles: a unique state during which one half of the brain sleeps while the other remains awake, as
a trade-oﬀbetween sleep like a vital function and wakefulness as an anti-predator technique. This
essential conﬂict was discussed in Rattenborg et al. [2000], presenting the electroencephalograms
(EEG) recorded from a bottlenose dolphin’s cortex during unihemispheric slow-wave sleep, which
shows (desynchronized) electrical activity in the wake side while a high degree of synchrony among
the millions of neurons in the sleeping side.
The KO model (6.3) was presented by Abrams et al. [2008] as the simplest model that exhibits chimera
state, consisting of two symmetrically coupled oscillator populations with homogeneous phase-lag
and non-local coupling, where the self-coupling strength is a bit larger than the neighbor-coupling.
Their numerical and analytical results revealed that the desynchronized population may either show
a constant degree of coherence (stable chimera) or oscillating dynamics of the mean-ﬁeld parameter
(breathing chimera).
Some years later Laing [2012] observed that the chimera behavior could be
interchanged over time for two populations of oscillators with non-identical frequencies (alternating
chimera), as well as that for a suﬃciently high disorder or heterogeneity the macroscopic dynamic
performed chaotic behavior (desynchronized chimera, see also Bick et al., 2018).
These dynamical behaviors can be unveiled from the degree of coherence of the phase distributions
among both populations in the long run, as shown in Figure 6.2. We will be specially interested in
breathing chimeras which correspond to attracting limit-cycles, denoted either by the synchronization
pattern SD or DS, as we will study in next section in close relation to the selection of constants of
motions and their spread over the circle.

Conclusions
In the present thesis, the exact dimensional reduction techniques for coupled oscillators have been
studied, from Watanabe-Strogatz (WS) reduction to Ott-Antonsen (OA) theory, linked via the con-
formal automorphisms of the unit disk, widely used in complex analysis, as the underlying structure
of sinusoidally coupled systems of identical phase oscillators, recently revealed by Marvel et al. [2009].
One milestone in this report has been the development of a unifying notation in the ﬁeld, summarized
in Table 3.1, which collects the M¨obius formalisms introduced by Pikovsky and Rosenblum [2011] and
Chen et al. [2017], as well as their main results that have been rederived accordingly. Furthermore,
we have analyzed in more detail the geometric and algebraic properties of M¨obius transformations, we
have built the Blaschke factors from scratch in order to reveal their essential dynamics as composition
of geometric inversions in the Riemann sphere, and we have introduced the hyperbolic metric in the
Poincar´e disk which naturally arise for such transformations from the Schwarz-Pick lemma. In this
manner, it sheds some light upon the smart dimensional reduction into just three macroscopic variables
that was unveiled by Watanabe and Strogatz [1994], corresponding to the 3 degrees of freedom of the
Blaschke group, as well as the invariance of cross-ratios in connection to the constants of motion,
and the gradient and Hamiltonian conditions for the Kuramoto-like phase models (using Wirtinger
calculus) from the M¨obius reduced system (3.7), among others.
On the other hand, we have reexamined the reduction theory introduced by Ott and Antonsen [2008],
speciﬁcally for identical coupled oscillators in the continuum limit, in which case the dynamics gets
further reduced to a 2D system, and the Kuramoto order-parameter matches the M¨obius variable
b ∈D in (3.9), corresponding to a uniform distribution of constants of motions.
Then we have
shown that the OA ansatz indeed corresponds to a Poisson kernel, emphasizing its main features
and rearrangement as a wrapped Cauchy density. In addition, we have demonstrated that the OA
manifold (the 2-dimensional Poisson submanifold) is invariant under the dynamics governed by the
Blaschke factor (3.34), and we have outlined the main setup recently proposed by Engelbrecht and
Mirollo [2020] to systematically study the dynamics oﬀthe OA manifold.
Finally, we have applied the reduction theory developed throughout this report into the paradigmatic
Kuramoto-Sakaguchi model (5.1), as an extension of the Kuramoto model, by including a phase-lag α
in the coupling. Our simulations in Matlab show the long-term dynamics of the mean-ﬁeld variables,
as well as the hyperbolic Hamiltonian vector ﬁeld in the unit disk for α = π
2 , closely related to Joseph-
son junction arrays. We have further generalized our results to the Kuramoto-Okuda model (6.1),
introducing the M¨obius formalism for multiple populations, and simulating two symmetrically cou-
pled groups of identical oscillators with homogeneous phase-lag and non-local coupling as in Abrams
et al. [2008], which already enables the presence of chimera states as symmetry-breaking solutions.
To conclude, we have proposed a novel variation of constants of motion (6.8) in terms of a crowding
parameter q that exhibits breathing chimera states oﬀthe OA manifold, as well as an alternating
behavior among both populations that resembles the unihemispheric sleep observed in some animals.
