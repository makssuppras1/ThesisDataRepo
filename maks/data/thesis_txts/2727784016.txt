Abstract
In recent years mobile robots have been developing fast. They are becoming more pop-
ular in the industry as we go further by giving them the hard and repetitive work that
humans do for example in logistics, healthcare, agriculture, construction, cleaning, etc.
But when it comes to teaching students how mobile robots work, there are many educa-
tional robots around where each uses a different platform and is capable of doing tasks
differently. In this project, we are going to discuss 5 different mobile robot platforms
used in education which are: Robobot, SMR, TurtleBot, pi-top Robotics Kit, and Ros-
bot mini. We are going to be looking into each one’s size, price, maintainability, and
capability and rank them accordingly to decide which is mainly good and appropriate
for helping in teaching the next generation that chooses to go to a field where mobile
robots are going to be used. Also figuring out what is the capability of each one and
how hard it is to be able to add components to it like sensors, robot arm, etc.

1.3
Purpose
When talking about robots 3 places are being used in human daily life by having robots in
industry, education, or as a toy. The difference is that a toy is just an object that is used
primarily to provide entertainment. Industrial robots are one that has been developed
to automate intensive production tasks such as those required by a constantly moving
assembly line.
Educational robots can serve various purposes in enhancing learning
experiences. They can promote active engagement, problem-solving, and collaboration
among students as active learning tools. Here comes the ink between education and
industry where education plays a vital role in preparing individuals with the necessary
knowledge, skills, and competencies needed to succeed in the workforce. On the other
hand, the industry provides the practical application and real-world context for the
theories and concepts learned in educational institutions. Here comes the role of how
to provide the fundamental knowledge for the upcoming generation that is interested in
pursuing a career in the robot industry which will need the necessary experience that
the educational system can provide in order to proceed in such a career. That’s where
robot educational platforms come in order to provide the necessary information needed
to be able to engage and give the possibility to students to pursue such career.

2.5
SLAM
SLAM (simultaneous localization and mapping) is a method used for autonomous vehi-
cles that lets you build a map and localize your vehicle on that map at the same time.
SLAM algorithms allow the vehicle to map out unknown environments. Engineers use
map information to carry out tasks such as path planning and obstacle avoidance.
When considering a home robot vacuum, utilizing SLAM technology becomes crucial.
Without SLAM, the robot would simply move around randomly within a room, lead-
ing to incomplete floor cleaning. Moreover, this approach uses excessive power, so the
battery will run out more quickly. However, robots equipped with SLAM can leverage
various data sources, such as wheel revolutions, camera input, and other imaging sensors,
to accurately determine the required movement. This process, known as localization, en-
ables the robot to eﬀiciently navigate the space as shown in Figure 2.1.

2.7
Localization
Robot localization is the process of determining where a mobile robot is located with
respect to its environment. Localization is one of the most fundamental competencies
required by an autonomous robot as the knowledge of the robot’s own location is an
essential precursor to making decisions about future actions. In a typical robot local-
ization scenario, a map of the environment is available and the robot is equipped with
sensors that observe the environment as well as monitor its own motion. The localization
problem then becomes one of estimating the robot’s position and orientation within the
map using information gathered from these sensors.
For more information these resources are available: [Seb06b; Nol97]

2.8
Navigation
Robot navigation means the robot’s ability to determine its own position in its frame of
reference and then to plan a path toward some goal location. In order to navigate in its
environment, the robot or any other mobility device requires representation, i.e. a map
of the environment and the ability to interpret that representation.
The costmap for robot navigation arises from the combination of the robot’s pose,
estimated from the odometry data (encoders) and the IMU sensor, the perception of
objects and obstacles in the environment using the distance sensors, and the occupancy
grid map obtained from the SLAM technique.
These sources of information provide as output a joint measurement of obstacle areas,
probable collisions, and the movable area for the robot. There is a global costmap and a
local one. The global one accounts for the navigation path using the fixed map obtained
through SLAM, while the local version allows the robot to deal with the fine-grained
details of its immediate environment to move around obstacles.
After establishing the costmap (local and global) it is necessary to do path planning
in order to enable the robot to navigate around in the designated environment. Path
planning lets the robot find the shortest and most obstacle-free path from the start to
the goal state. The path can be a set of states (position and/or orientation) or waypoints.
Path planning requires a map of the environment along with start and goal states as
input. Some popular categories of path planning algorithms include [MAT]:
• Grid-based search algorithms
• Sampling-based search algorithms
• Trajectory optimization algorithms
In order to be able to navigate it’s necessary to have SLAM, mapping, and localiza-
tion in order to achieve the navigation process successfully. This process can help in
understanding how to achieve moving a robot through an environment from position A
to B and the process that is needed to achieve a successful navigation process.

3.3
SLAM
SLAM stands for Simultaneous Localization And Mapping. It is a process by which a
robot can build a map of its environment while simultaneously estimating its position
within it. ROS provides well-developed packages that can help in the completion of this
task.
GMapping SLAM:[Wikc]
• Advantages:
– Fast and eﬀicient for small to medium-sized environments.
– Uses a grid-based approach to create a 2D occupancy map.
– Has been widely used in many robotics applications.
– Can be used for both mapping and localization.
• Disadvantages
– Requires odometry data for robot movement.
– May not be robust to sensor noise.
– Limited scalability for large-scale environments.
Hector SLAM:[Wikd]
• Advantages:
– Eﬀicient and robust for mapping large-scale environments.
– Uses scan matching to create a 2D occupancy grid map.
– Can work with different sensor configurations, including LiDAR, sonar, and
RGB-D sensors.
– Can be used for both mapping and localization.
• Disadvantages:
– Requires a powerful computational system to run eﬀiciently.
– May have lower accuracy compared to other SLAM methods.
– Limited ability to handle dynamic environments.
During the process of SLAM the robots were controlled manually in the environment in
order to establish the map that the robot will be operating in.

3.3.2
Localization
AMCL (Adaptive Monte Carlo Localization) is a localization algorithm used in robotics
to estimate the position and orientation (pose) of a robot within its environment. It
is particularly useful when the robot needs to localize itself in an environment based
on sensor measurements, such as with a mobile robot equipped with sensors like laser
scanners or cameras.[Wika] [Doc]
The AMCL algorithm employs the following steps:
• Initialization: The algorithm begins by initializing the particles with random poses
within the environment.
• Motion Update: As the robot moves, the particles are updated based on the
robot’s odometry information. This step helps estimate the new potential poses
of the robot based on its expected motion.
• Sensor Update: When sensor measurements are received, the particles are updated
and re-weighted based on the likelihood of the measured sensor data given each
particle’s pose. This step helps align the particles with the actual robot pose by
assigning higher weights to particles that better match the sensor measurements.

3.4.2
Path Planning
Mainly for navigation 3 path planners have been used:
DWA (Dynamic Window Approach):[Wikb]
• Reactive motion planning algorithm.
• Suitable for robots operating in dynamic and uncertain environments.
• Considers robot’s kinematic constraints, environment, and dynamic capabilities.
• Evaluates potential motion states within a ”dynamic window.”
• Selects the best trajectory that maximizes a given objective.
• Known for simplicity and real-time performance.
TEB (Timed Elastic Band):[ROSb]
• Local trajectory optimization technique.
• Used for path planning and obstacle avoidance in robotics.
• Considers robot’s dynamic and kinematic constraints, obstacle shape and location.
• Formulates path planning as an optimization task.
• Uses an elastic band representation to encode the feasible and collision-free space.
• Adjusts band’s width and timing to generate smooth and collision-free trajectories.
Dijkstra’s algorithm:[ROSa]
• Guarantees to find the shortest path from a single source to all other nodes in a
weighted graph.
• It operates by iteratively selecting the node with the shortest distance from the
source and relaxing its neighboring nodes.
• Uses a priority queue to select the node with the smallest distance eﬀiciently.
• It explores the graph in a breadth-first manner, meaning it considers all possible
paths from the source before moving to the next node.
• Works on both directed and undirected graphs and can handle graphs with negative
edge weights as long as there are no negative cycles.
More information about path planning is provided: [MIT; Rob]

information is needed which is the centroid coordinate of the ball in the image frame
and the radius of the ball. In order to extract that information cv2.minEnclosingCircle
function that is used to find the minimum enclosing circle for a set of points. This
function returns the center and radius of the detected ball. In Figure 3.16, you can see

4.1 Introduction

The SMR, Turtlebot3 burger, Robobot, and Pi-Top use a differential drive steering
mechanism that allows the robot to move in all directions as shown in Figure 4.2. It
consists of two drive wheels attached at the right and left side of the robot’s base, each
connected to a motor. Those two motors are responsible for moving the robot and for
steering. This system even allows a robot to pivot in its place.
Figure 4.2: Differential Drive
As for the Rosbot mini it uses Ackermann steering which works by having the steering
axis connected to the moving link of the steering mechanism, which is at the center. The
moving link changes the direction of the wheels by transferring motion to the link that is
attached to the wheels. This is how Ackermann’s steering kinematics works. It is referred
to as kinematics because Ackermann’s principle of steering doesn’t get influenced by
any external forces. It involves only the relative motion between force links and doesn’t
involve the study of the effect of forces. The Ackermann steering geometry is designed
in such a way that the two front wheels are always aligned toward the common center
of the turning radius as shown in Figure 4.3.
Figure 4.3: Ackerman Steering

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
In conclusion, working with five different types of robots was a pleasure. Analyzing
each robot’s specifications, software, and hardware, as well as operating them, proved to
be an interesting experience. Throughout the process, several tests were conducted to
assess their operational performance. Each robot underwent modifications to enhance
its original design by adding new components. Working with these robots was both
interesting and challenging, as each one operates differently.
In my opinion, for educational purposes, the SMR has the upper hand with its
beginner-friendly platform that facilitates newcomers in learning how to operate a robot.
Additionally, it can perform advanced tasks, making it suitable for different types of
educational purposes. However, its downside is its large size. On the other hand, the
turtlebot3 burger is a small-sized robot, which was enjoyable to operate, but it requires
prior knowledge of ROS for effective use. The size of the robot makes it suitable for
maneuvering in tight areas like homes and tracks.
Among these robots, the most interesting robot that was enjoyable to operate was
the Rosbot mini, because of its challenging mechanism (Ackermann Steering) which
forced my potential to learn how to operate it. Although it may not be the best choice
for educational purposes, it was the most interesting robot to operate, pushing me to
learn and solve complex tasks.
In summary, each robot has its strengths and limitations, needing different skill lev-
els. The experience of working with these robots doesn’t only provide an understanding
of robotics but has also provided valuable insights into real-world challenges. As technol-
ogy continues to advance, the knowledge gained from this research will hopefully prove
beneficial in the future of the educational system.
