Abstract
Advancements in digital assistive technologies have brought forth innovative solu-
tions to enhance the reading experience for individuals with central vision loss. Our
research explores the design and evaluation of interactive reading interfaces, lever-
aging eye-tracking data to understand the impact of four kinds of typographical
interventions on readability. We developed an adaptive e-reader platform capable
of triggering typographical adjustments, aimed at improving the reading experience
without causing significant disruptions to the user. Through an experiment involv-
ing eye-tracking technology, we analysed the effects of the four interventions on the
user’s reading behaviour. Our findings suggest that careful typographical changes
can lead to increased reading speed and comfort. We also compare the four interven-
tions in terms of effectiveness, disruption and user acceptance, and assess the overall
results. The study contributes valuable insights into creating user-centric and inclu-
sive digital reading environments, with potential applications in developing assistive
technologies for individuals with visual impairments.

1.1
Motivation
Central vision loss, often attributed to conditions such as age-related macular de-
generation1, can significantly impede reading abilities, resulting in notable decreases
in reading speed and comprehension levels. To address these challenges, assistive
technologies emerge as invaluable aids in enhancing the quality of life for individ-
uals with visual impairments. The continuous evolution of information technology
provides multiple opportunities for innovative solutions meant to address the unique
needs of this group. Therefore, these advancements offer promising chances for en-
abling individuals with visual impairments to engage more actively in their daily
activities and social interactions.
Our motivation lies in the desire to address the nuanced challenges faced by individ-
uals with central vision loss, especially when it comes to consuming digital content.
While existing assistive technologies provide valuable support, they often lack tai-
lored solutions for the diverse reading needs of this demographic. Thus, our research
aims to fill this gap by focusing on the design and evaluation of interactive read-
ing experiences informed by eye-tracking data. By exploring various intervention
strategies, including sudden changes, suggestive designs, and progressive changes,

1.2
State of the art
An Assistive Technology (AT) is any item, piece of equipment, software program,
or product system that is used to increase, maintain, or improve the functional
capabilities of persons with disabilities [2].
Assistive technologies play a crucial role in empowering individuals with disabilities
by providing them with tools and solutions to overcome barriers in various aspects
of their lives. In the context of visual impairments, ATs aim to enhance accessibility,
independence and inclusion for individuals with vision loss. This section provides
an overview of the state of the art in assistive technologies, with a specific focus on
digital ATs designed to support reading experiences.
To gather the information presented in this section, we conducted a comprehensive
search using mostly general Google searches. We wanted to get an overview of the
most common assisting technologies on the market, which are mostly integrated
into existing devices. After finding out about the different kinds of ATs, we tried to
cover some examples in the different types. To ensure extensive coverage, we went
on to examine the products from major market players, specifically focusing on the

Digital assistive technologies, in particular, leverage the power of digital platforms,
software applications and computing devices to provide tailored solutions for indi-
viduals with disabilities. These technologies combine advancements in areas such as
human-computer interaction, computer vision and natural language processing to
create accessible and inclusive digital experiences. Some examples of digital assistive
technologies include closed captioning, screen magnifying technology, speech input
software and alternative input devices such as keyboards, head pointers, joysticks
and motion or eye-tracking devices, as well as screen readers and refreshable Braille
displays.
One of the most popular AT software solutions for people with visual impairment
are screen readers. A screen reader is a form of software program that allows indi-
viduals to read the content on a computer screen with a voice synthesizer or braille
display. The screen reader is the interface between the user and the computer op-
erating system and its applications. There are many brands and kinds of screen
readers, making them quite accessible to all users. For instance, one of the most
popular and widely used screen readers is JAWS (Job Access With Speech) [3] by
Freedom Scientific. JAWS is compatible with Windows operating systems and offers
comprehensive support for various applications, including web browsers, productiv-
ity software and more. It is a well-established screen reader that has been widely
adopted and integrated into digital systems.
Another prominent screen reader is VoiceOver [4], developed by Apple. VoiceOver
is integrated into macOS, iOS, and iPadOS, making it readily available on Apple
devices without the need for additional software installation. It provides a seamless
and accessible experience for individuals with visual impairments, allowing them to
navigate and interact with apps, documents, and websites using gestures, keyboard
commands or braille displays.
For Android devices, Google offers TalkBack [5] as the built-in screen reader. Talk-
back provides spoken and tactile feedback using gestures, enabling users to effectively

use Android devices and interact with various applications.
Furthermore, there are also open-source screen readers available, such as NVDA

As we can see, screen readers are a technology that is widely integrated into existing

Another popular example of assistive software for vision loss are screen magnifiers.
Screen magnification software enables individuals with low vision to enlarge text,
images and other on-screen content, making it easier for them to read and navigate.
This form of assistive technology offers a range of magnification levels, often up to 20
times the original size, to accommodate various visual impairments. Additionally, it
provides additional features such as colour enhancements and contrast adjustments,
enhancing accessibility for individuals with specific visual needs.
Among the popular screen magnification software options are ZoomText [7] and
Windows Magnifier [8] . These programs cater to different levels of visual impair-
ments and can be customised to meet the specific needs of individual users. By
using these tools, users with low vision gain increased confidence in navigating and
interacting with digital content. These software solutions empower individuals by
providing them with the ability to access and engage with digital materials more
effectively.

1.3.1
Central vision loss effects on reading
In their study “Reading performance and central field loss” [9], E. Kanonidou pro-

When it comes to intervention types, we identified two categories of interventions:
forced or imposed interventions and suggested or prompted interventions.
Forced or imposed interventions refer to changes in typography that are imple-
mented on the e-reader without prior consent or interaction from the user. In this
approach, the system would automatically apply modifications to the typography
without warning the user. These interventions are typically aimed at improving
readability or accessibility without requiring direct input from the reader. While
forced interventions can be effective in addressing the accessibility issues, they may
also lead to user frustration or resistance if the changes are not aligned with the
user’s preferences or needs.
On the other hand, suggested or prompted interventions would involve presenting
users with options or recommendations for modifying typography based on their

the modifications made. We decided to choose this one, from the group that
appears in the top-left corner of image 5.
2. Notification: The notification intervention offered a subtle way to suggest
typography changes to users without imposing immediate modifications. This
was by far the most non-intrusive and soft intervention, which we thought
would be valuable to evaluate with users later on.
It is an example of a
suggested and non-intrusive intervention that we wanted to test out.
3. Pop-up: While slightly more imposing and intrusive than the notification, the
popup intervention provided a clear and direct communication of suggested ty-
pography changes. We anticipated that this intervention would appeal to users
who prefer explicit guidance and appreciate concise information presented in a
prominent manner. Since we had considered two interventions with a pop-up,
where the only difference was the position of the dialog, we decided to count
it as one and to later on conduct an A/B test in order to find the best design
for it.
4. Gradual Change after Page Turn: This intervention offered a balanced
approach between forced and suggested modifications, introducing gradual
typography changes after the user transitions to the next page, which was
less intrusive than the gradual change after time. By incorporating changes
seamlessly into the reading process, we aimed to minimize disruption while
still effectively enhance readability over time.
As it can be seen, we overall tried to choose four interventions that had different
levels of freedom and intrusiveness, so one in each quadrant of our diagram, in order
to get a sense of each type, and understand what users prefer.

5.1
Research question and goal
The primary goal of this experiment was to enhance the usability of digital reading
interfaces for individuals with central vision loss by investigating the impact of four
different interventions on their reading experience. These interventions consisted of
forced or suggested changes on the typography of the e-reader. More specifically, we
used eye-tracking technology and data analysis in order to find out which interven-
tion is the least intrusive and best for the user that is reading a book on the digital
e-reader prototype we implemented.
Concretely, the experiment will help us achieve the objectives set in section 1.4,
determining which of the interventions are the least disruptive, while being effective
and approved by users.

Experiment design and setup
The general idea for this experiment was to have people come in and sit in front

The experiment was conducted at the eye-tracking lab in DTU Compute by our-

5.2.3
Experiment design
When it comes to the type of experiment, we decided to conduct a within-subjects
study. This choice was due to the fact that there is a lower variation between the
conditions for each participant, since they would all get the same text with the
same intervention. Moreover, the amount of participants needed for this type of
experiment is usually smaller. We established the following variables:
• Independent variable: the type of intervention.
• Dependent variables:
– Reading performance metrics. We measured the total reading time, the
reading speed before the intervention, the intervention time, the reading
speed after the intervention and the reaction time, which was the time it
took for participants to see the blinking bell from the moment we showed
it on the screen (only for the notification intervention). Additionally, we
also obtained answers to the comprehension questions for every text.
– Eye-tracking data: the Tobii stream outlet consisting of data about times-
tamps, gaze coordinates, gaze origin, gaze points, pupil diameter and
validity flags.
An example of all of the available data is available in
Appendix C.
– User feedback. The users’ answers from the form, which included the
perceived level of disruptiveness of each intervention, the difficulty to go
back to reading after it, their preferred intervention type and any other
comments.
• Control variables: the texts they read, the environment for the experiment,
the initial typography on each text, the typography after the intervention and
a calibrated eye-tracker for each user.
• Random variables: the gender of the participants, their level of fatigue and
the order of the texts-interventions.

5.2.4
Data collection
The data collection for this experiment was designed to capture a comprehensive
set of metrics to evaluate the impact of the different interventions on the reading
experience, such as the dependent variables mentioned in the previous section. Our
data collection strategy encompassed two main areas: eye-tracking data and reading
performance metrics, and user feedback.
Eye-tracking data and timestamps
Throughout the experiment, we used the eye tracker to continuously measure the
participants’ gaze positions on the screen, which were recorded every 11103 microsec-
onds. Furthermore, we also logged other timestamps metrics. As it was mentioned
earlier, we asked the participants to press the space key the moment they started
and finished reading, and we saved this input and its timestamp in a file. In the
same way, we were also saving the timestamps of when we showed each intervention,
since these were also triggered by keys on our keyboard.
This eye-tracking data, together with the different timestamps, would later provide
us with insights into how participants’ reading patterns were affected by the inter-
ventions as well as their reading speeds, allowing us to analyse the disruptions in
their reading flow.
User feedback
As we mentioned in the experiment set up, and in order to ensure participants were
actively engaged with the texts and to evaluate their comprehension, we asked three
multiple-choice questions after each text. These questions were designed to test key
details and ensure that participants were not merely skimming through the text.
The accuracy of their responses would help us evaluate the correlation between text
comprehension and reading behaviours.
Finally, participants also provided qualitative feedback on their reading experience.
The qualitative questions were about:

The test aimed to assess the following aspects:
• Clarity of use for the overall reader platform.
• Design of typography settings and clarity of their usage.
• Design and intuitiveness of interventions.
• Comparison of relevance and disturbance levels for all interventions.
• Identification of any major concerns with the reader or interventions.

to the application and its purpose, emphasizing its aim to improve readability for
visually impaired individuals through automatic typography adjustments.
Users

functionality while thinking out loud to provide immediate feedback. They then
read several paragraphs of text.

Once we had implemented the second screen, we had the interface needed to receive

between the Python script and the React application.
The solution we found, in order to do so, was using a WebSocket. A WebSocket
is a communication protocol that enables interactive, real-time communication be-
tween a client (such as a web browser, like our React app) and a server over a
single, long-lived connection. Unlike traditional HTTP requests, which are one-way
and require the client to always ask for updates, WebSockets allow for full-duplex
communication, meaning that data can flow in both directions simultaneously.
In order to facilitate this real-live communication, we first needed to adapt the
Python script to establish a WebSocket connection, acting as the server. This in-
volved several key steps, including setting up asynchronous functions to manage the
communication and integrating these with our existing code.

functions: receive_messages and send_messages. The send_messages function
continuously sends gaze data to the React app, while the receive_messages func-

Pupil diameter measures
After the initial analysis focused on the gaze points and reading times, we wanted
to explore more opportunities and decided to look into the pupil diameter values
offered by the Tobii eye-tracker data stream.
According to "The pupils"[30] from the "Clinical Methods: The History, Physical,
and Laboratory Examinations", the normal pupil size in adults varies from 2 to
4 mm in diameter in bright light. Our recorded values also fall into this interval.
==PAGE:104==
7.1. Quantitative Results
91
The pupils are also generally equal in size. During reading, pupil dilation is often
a response to increased cognitive load and mental effort or increased attention and
interest while pupil constriction could mean lower cognitive effort and becoming
calm, relaxed or disinterested.
The light conditions were consistent in the room during each experiment and there-
fore there are no external factors affecting pupil diameter measurements. However,
we are aware that the luminance conditions could have varied slightly, which might
affect the data we obtained. Therefore, we assume that pupil dilation or constric-
tion are mainly driven by changes in cognitive load, but can also be influenced by
unpredictable slight luminance variations.
We filtered the data points to use for analysis, taking only those that fell within the
actual reading time, and where both the left and right pupil diameters were marked
as valid (left_pupil_diameter and left_pupil_diameter both as 1).
As we wanted to see the evolution of the pupil size over time while reading, we plotted
the diameter against the reading time from start_timestamp to end_timestamp.
The most interesting aspect to explore was how the pupil sizes evolve during the
intervention time, compared to the rest of reading. This could be very relevant in
understanding the impact of each intervention on the reading patterns.

Discussion
In this chapter, we analyse the outcomes of our study and discuss their implications
in the context of our research objectives and the results we have seen in the previous
chapter.
E-reader and Interventions Design
The first objective we established in the scope of our project was to develop and
test an adaptive e-reader platform capable of delivering real-time interventions to
enhance readability for users with central vision loss. This process began with a
detailed exploration of existing technologies in adaptive typography devices and an
introduction to the existing project and prior work. The implementation phase of
our prototype involved the integration of a web application as our digital reading
platform and a robust eye-tracking system, crucial for collecting real-time data on
users’ reading behaviours. This is explained in depth in chapter 6. We also inte-
grated the interventions that could be triggered while users were reading, modifying
typography to enhance the readability. The seamless communication between these
components facilitated our experimental approach, enabling precise tracking of in-
tervention effectiveness and user responses.
Furthermore, we conducted three iterations throughout the development of our pro-
totype, each of them focusing on some implementation that was followed by the
102
==PAGE:116==
103
validation of our changes. The first iteration, as described in section 6.2, focused
on the design and integration of the interventions in the existing reading platform,
which was validated through a Thinking out Loud evaluation and A/B test. Based
on the feedback we obtained, we were able to iterate and adjust our prototype
accordingly, adding further functionalities and expanding the back-end and data
storage system of our project, which was crucial in order to conduct the main ex-
periment. The experiment of this project, which is described in chapter 5, allowed
us to thoroughly test our e-reader platform together with the interventions, which
would enable us to obtain data and results to support our other objectives.
Interventions’ impact on reading behaviour
Our second objective was to evaluate the impact of typographical adjustments on
users’ reading behaviour, focusing on reading speed and comprehension. The find-
ings from our data analysis reveal interesting insights into how these interventions
influenced reading dynamics.
Across all four intervention experiments the data consistently demonstrates an in-
crease in reading speed following typographical adjustments, as seen in Figures 53
and 54. This upward trend suggests improved fluidity and ease of reading after
the typography changes were implemented. This is what we were expecting since
we intentionally made the new typography more comfortable and easier to read.
Specifically, users exhibited higher reading speeds post-intervention compared to
pre-intervention phases. This outcome underscores the effectiveness of typography
modifications in enhancing reading efficiency.
Analysis of pupil diameter data revealed a consistent trend where pupils tended to
constrict after typographical adjustments (Figures 59, 61 and 65). Smaller pupil
diameters typically indicate reduced cognitive load and increased comfort during
reading tasks. The observed decrease in pupil size post-intervention further supports
the idea that the revised typography contributed to a more comfortable and fluid
reading experience for users.
This is also supported by the findings of Beymer,
Russell, and Orton in People and Computers XXII [16] about larger fonts reducing

cognitive load and eye strain.
These results highlight a clear correlation between typography changes and an im-
proved reading flow, supported by quantitative data.
However, the analysis of accuracy did not show any significant insights into the
effectiveness of typographical interventions on reading behaviour (Figure 56).
Minimize User Disruption
Another of the key goals of our study was to identify interventions that suggest or
present typographical changes in a way that causes minimal intrusiveness and distur-
bances to the user’s reading behaviour. Our findings indicated significant differences
in how each intervention was perceived in terms of disruption. The Undo and Popup
interventions were rated as highly disruptive, both qualitatively and quantitatively.
As shown in Table 6, Undo had the highest disruption scores, confirmed by users’
feedback that highlighted its intrusiveness and the difficulty in resuming reading
post-intervention. This was not surprising since several participants had already
mentioned it in our first validation test.
In contrast, the Notification intervention was found to be the least disruptive. This
was intentional as from the beginning we considered the Notification as the most
subtle in our two-dimensional overview in Figure 5.
However, most amount of
users never clicked on the notification, which means they did not see the actual
intervention, so it makes sense that users would find it non-disruptive.
The Gradual intervention, while initially disruptive due to the significant changes in
text layout, became less intrusive as users adapted to its predictable nature. This
aligns with our expectation that an intervention’s predictability can mitigate its
perceived intrusiveness over time.
Maximize Effectiveness and Acceptance
Finally, the effectiveness and acceptance of the interventions were also central to our
study, as stated in our fourth objective 1.4. Our analysis showed a clear preference
==PAGE:118==
105
for the Gradual intervention, which was favored by 39.13% of participants (Table 8).
Despite its initial higher intervention time, users appreciated its subtleness, which
minimized abrupt changes and allowed for smoother transitions.
The Popup intervention, while effective in enforcing typographical changes, was
slightly less favored due to its higher intrusiveness. However, it was still preferred
by many users, indicating its potential utility in situations where immediate changes
are critical. The Notification intervention, although rated high for user acceptance,
was less effective because users rarely acted on it, suggesting a need for a more
compelling design to prompt user interaction without causing disruption. Lastly,
the Undo intervention, while scoring high on effectiveness due to its enforcement,
was highly disliked by users, since it surprised them and they often found it too
intrusive and interrupting.

8.1. Limitations
107
8.1
Limitations
In our study, in both the experiment we conducted and the implemented prototype,
there are several limitations that need to be acknowledged, as they may impact the
applicability of our findings.
Participant demographics
When we designed this experiment, we were aware of certain limitations, particularly
concerning participant recruitment. This was previously mentioned in chapter 5,
highlighting the fact that our sample consisted predominantly of young university
students from DTU, all with ages ranging from 18 to 29 years old. This narrow age
range and the lack of participants with central vision loss may limit the applicability
of our results to the broader population of individuals with vision impairments. We
made the assumption that if the interventions were disruptive to younger, non-CVL
individuals, they would similarly affect those with central vision loss, though this
assumption requires further validation, and it could be seen as a potential weakness,
since it does not fully account for the specific challenges faced by individuals with
central vision loss.
Additionally, and while 43.47% of our participants did declare that they have some
type of vision condition and these do provide some variation in visual acuity among
participants, they do not fully represent the target population of individuals with
central vision loss.
Lost reading position after intervention
Another significant limitation identified from our previous tests was the challenge
of accurately determining the user’s reading position on the screen once the typo-
graphical change had been applied. To address this, we considered highlighting the
area where the user’s gaze had been located just before the intervention.
However, we encountered several challenges that prevented us from adding this
feature to our solution. One major issue was the difficulty of implementation, since
==PAGE:121==
108
Chapter 8. Discussion
this required accurately identifying the word or sentence at the user’s gaze position,
and finding that again when the typography change had been applied. But it was
especially due to a time concern, that we could not spend extra implementation time,
so this feature of helping users easily find their reading spot after an intervention
had to be disregarded and removed from our scope.
This is something we were aware throughout the experiment, but had to consider
as a limitation.

Chapter 9
Final Iteration and Future Work
Despite the positive outcomes achieved through our interventions, a significant chal-
lenge remained: users often struggled to relocate their reading position after a ty-
pographical change was implemented. This issue was notably highlighted during
our eye-tracking experiments, where participants frequently lost their place in the
text following an intervention, especially during the Gradual intervention. Address-
ing this challenge became a key focus for our final iteration, as discussed in this
chapter.
Given the complexities of implementation and time constraints, we were unable to
integrate a real-time solution within the current system. Instead, we proposed a con-
ceptual solution using a high-fidelity Figma prototype, which we tested afterwards
in order to validate our design.
The proposed solution mainly involves highlighting the location where the user had
been reading before the Popup intervention, or before the page turned in the case
of the Gradual intervention. This highlight, as observed in images 67 and 68 below,
for the Gradual and the Popup interventions respectively, would remain visible for
8 seconds following the typography change, providing users with a clear reference
point to resume reading, but making it disappear later to avoid longer distractions.

To validate this proposed solution, we conducted a Thinking out Loud test with six

assess the intuitiveness, clarity, and overall impact of the proposed improvements.

Test results
All six participants provided positive feedback regarding the improvements and the
highlight of the text. They appreciated the shade of yellow we had used, describing
it as subtle but noticeable. They also thought that the duration of the highlight—8
seconds—was appropriate, providing sufficient time to locate their previous reading
position without causing long-term distraction.
Three out of the six participants mentioned that, for the Gradual intervention, the
first time they turned the page, they initially reread the text at the beginning, the

one that was repeated. However, their eyes quickly moved to the highlighted part
and they understood the purpose of it. After this initial adjustment, during the
subsequent page turns, they immediately focused on the highlighted area without
rereading the beginning text, indicating a quick adaptation to the new feature.
This feedback suggests that the highlight feature effectively addresses the issue of
users losing their reading position after an intervention, thereby enhancing the read-

and the duration was deemed adequate by users. This final iteration demonstrates
an improvement in the usability of the e-reader platform, enhancing readability for

These findings indicate that, with further development and refinement, the high-
lighted text feature could be successfully integrated into the e-reader platform to
significantly improve user experience, especially for individuals with central vision
loss. Future iterations could further explore optimizing the highlight’s colour and
duration, as well as automating the feature for seamless functionality.
9.2
Future work
There are several opportunities for future work that we have considered for the
whole project in order to enhance the effectiveness and user experience of the inter-
ventions tested in this study. By addressing these areas, we can further improve the
performance and user satisfaction of our interventions, contributing to a better and
more comfortable reading experience.
Refining the Gradual and Popup Interventions
As we have seen in this chapter, some more implementation work is needed to solve
and refine the issues with losing the place of reading after the intervention and the
repeated lines in the Gradual intervention. Implementing a solution to highlight the
user’s reading position before and after the intervention as suggested in the previous
sections could mitigate this issue by providing a clear reference point for users to
==PAGE:125==
112

resume reading, reducing disruption and enhancing the overall reading experience.
This solution would make the Gradual intervention fully well rounded and non-
disruptive. The same improvement could also be applied to the other three inter-
ventions, especially the Popup intervention, as it was tested in our Figma prototype,
in order to combat its high disruptiveness.
Enhancing the Notification Intervention
Future work could additionally focus on making the Notification intervention more
salient and engaging to ensure that more users notice it and interact with it. One
approach could be to make the notification more persistent and slightly more in-
trusive, such as adding a more perturbing blinking or other visual cues to draw
attention. While this might increase the intervention’s intrusiveness, it would likely
improve its effectiveness significantly. Testing various options to enhance the salience
of the Notification intervention could help find the right balance between visibility
and user acceptance. In an additional experiment it could then be proved if these
improvements can indeed increase the user interaction with the intervention.
Leveraging AI
Another promising direction for future research is the use of artificial intelligence and
machine learning to analyse reading patterns in real-time. By monitoring how users
read, AI algorithms could automatically trigger the appropriate intervention at the
optimal and least intrusive moment, without manual input. This approach could
significantly enhance the user experience by ensuring that interventions are both
timely and relevant. For example, AI could detect when a user is struggling with a
particular section and trigger a helpful intervention to improve comprehension and
reading flow.

Chapter 10
Conclusion
Central vision loss can have notable negative effects on reading abilities, posing a
challenge for affected individuals in their reading and everyday tasks. The advance-
ments in digital assistive technologies have the potential to address the unique needs
of this group, enabling them to fulfill daily activities more comfortably and improve
their quality of life.
Motivated by the desire to address the specific challenges faced by individuals with
central vision loss in consuming digital content, our research aimed to design an e-
reader displaying interactive reading adjustments and assess their impact informed
by eye-tracking data.
After designing and implementing the e-reader prototype with four different inter-
ventions, we went through a series of experiments using eye-tracking technology, in
order to determine their effects on reading behaviour. Our findings demonstrated
that typography changes can significantly improve reading performance and com-
fort, while not observing correlations between the reading speed and comprehension
levels. Out of the four interventions, the Gradual one, despite some drawbacks,
emerged as the most promising approach, with high user acceptance and effective-
ness. However, issues such as disruption due to repeated lines need to be addressed.
The implications of our findings suggest that well designed typographical adjust-
113

114

ments can improve digital reading experiences.
Although our study has several
strengths, including extensive data, detailed data analysis and diverse intervention
types, it also has limitations mostly due to our experiment participants not being
part of the main target group we want to address.
In summary, our study contributes valuable insights into the effects of typographical
adjustments on reading behaviour for individuals with central vision loss, specifically
on the evolution of reading speeds impacted by interventions, and the evolution
of pupil diameter during reading. Furthermore, we assess the effectiveness, user
acceptance and disruption of the four interventions, identifying the most favorable
one and suggesting further improvements.
