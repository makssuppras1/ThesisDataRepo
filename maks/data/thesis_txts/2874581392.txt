ABSTRACT 
 
Wind Resource Assessment for Comodoro Rivadavia City, Patagonia, Argentina, using WAsP 
and the Global Wind Atlas 
 
An extraordinary wind resource is available in Patagonia Argentina where only 750 MW of installed capacity was developed 
by the end of 2018. Existing records show outstanding capacity factor values higher than 60%. Regulatory framework and 
incentives were modified during the last years in order to accelerate the development of renewable energy projects in 
Argentina, but other infrastructure limitations, such as electric grid, are a constrain to transport the produced electricity to 
the main consumption centers. For example, Buenos Aires city is located 1600 km from Comodoro Rivadavia. 
Comodoro Rivadavia is one of the most populated cities in Patagonia. Its main economic activity is oil production which is 
linked directly to the electricity demand in the area. The possible synergy between wind farms and oil fields here is based 
on the existing energy demand that is supplied mainly by produced energy in other regions of Argentina, transported by a 
high-voltage electric line of 500 KV. Additionally, Oil companies have the rights over extended areas due to oil exploitation 
agreements. This can simplify and accelerate wind projects developments in their areas. In addition, some big oil companies 
were obligated by the recent regulatory framework to consume at least 8% of its electricity from renewable energy sources 
by the end of 2017, reinforcing this synergy. 
In this project Manantiales Behr (99 MW) and Diadema (27.45 MW) wind farms are modelled in WAsP in order to understand 
the resources in the area. Manantiales Behr and Diadema wind farms belong to YPF and Capex (oil companies) respectively 
and are located in the same concession areas where oil is produced. Public information databases, including Global Wind 
Atlas (GWA), are used to build the WAsP Projects. Roughness sensitivity is performed in order to quantify the impact of this 
variable. Because of this analysis it was possible to identity that the roughness data imported from GWA database must be 
modified because roughness lengths are too high compared to the actual surface characteristics. In addition, WAsP’s IBZ 
and CFD models are run for both wind farms. The orography is very different in both windfarms even though they are located 
very close to each other. The Annual Energy Production (AEP) results are compared with actual production energy data. 
The vector maps with the lowest roughness values achieves the best matches. In addition, a new wind farm (extension of 
Manantiales Behr) is modelled.  
With this understanding of the resources it is possible to calculate the installed capacity that must be developed in order to 
supply the local energy demand from the oilfields. This resulted in 7 additional wind farms like Manantiales Behr which is 
totally achievable in the area around Comodoro Rivadavia.  
This study describes and quantifies a realistic and relevant case of the synergy that can exist between the oil industry and 
wind energy projects while transitioning from fossil fuels to renewable energy.

1 INTRODUCTION 
The wind energy installed capacity in the world has reached the value of 596 GW at the end of 2018. The leading countries 
are China (217 GW), United States (96 GW) and Germany (59 GW) (1). 
Although wind projects development has been accelerating during the last years in Argentina, the installed capacity remains 
low, only 750 MW by the end of 2018. 
Regulatory framework has been modified since 2015 to promote electricity generation from renewable energy sources. 
National laws were promulgated setting ambitious targets for this purpose. The government launched bidding processes to 
accelerate investment in this matter, setting boundaries for the financial framework which includes available funding and 
agreements for sell prices. 
The two main areas of interest where wind energy projects were developed (and are planned to be developed) are South 
of Buenos Aires Province and Patagonia region.  
Existing records show the outstanding capacity factor values obtained in the wind farms located in the Patagonia region. 
These values could be higher than 60%. This extraordinary wind resource may not be exploited to its full potential due to 
the fact that electric grid infrastructure is a constrain.  
Comodoro Rivadavia is one of the most populated cities in Patagonia. The main economic activity of the area is oil 
production, for that reason the city is known as the “national oil capital”. The electricity demand of the area is linked directly 
to this economic activity. 
1.1  OBJECTIVES 
 
In this project: 
- 
Two existing wind farms will be modelled in WAsP in order to understand the resources in the area. Public 
information databases will be used to build the WAsP Projects. The results will be compared to actual production 
energy data.  
- 
A new wind farm will be modelled in the area.  
- 
An estimation of the installed capacity needed to supply the electricity demand from oil fields will be done.

2.1 ARGENTINA WIND RESOURCES 
 
Argentina has a long tradition of using wind energy. It is estimated that even today the plains of the pampas have the largest 
concentration of farm windmills in the world, with over 400 000 in place. Wind power has certainly played an important role 
in the agricultural development of the country. 
 
Figure 1 - Wind power density map in Argentina 
The current exploitation of wind energy is not proportionate with its enormous potential. It has been estimated that 
Patagonia’s sustainable wind potential represents tens of times that contained in the whole of Argentina’s unsustainable 
annual crude oil production. Furthermore, it is not just the southern extremity of the country that possesses favorable 
conditions for the installation of wind farms (Figure 1). Numerous suitable areas exist in almost the whole of the Río Negro 
and Neuquén provinces, in various upland and coastal zones of Buenos Aires province, and in many other localities in the 
interior (2).

Argentina recently opened its electricity market to incorporate wide-scale clean energy and is currently one of the most

2.2 ELECTRICITY SECTOR IN ARGENTINA  
 
The institutional framework of the electricity sector in Argentina is defined by three main government entities, the Ministry 
of Treasury, the National Electricity Regulator (ENRE) and the wholesale electricity market operator Compañía 
Administradora del Mercado Mayorista Eléctrico (better known as CAMMESA). The Ministry and its Secretaria de Energia 
Electrica (SEE) design the national energy policy and set the broad regulatory framework. ENRE is an autonomous entity 
responsible for regulating and supervising the functioning of the national electricity market. It grants grid access licenses 
and supervises compliance of generation, transmission and distribution entities with safety, quality, technical and 
environmental standards set in the regulatory framework and the license agreements. Argentina is a federal country, so 
provinces also have the legal capacity to regulate energy issues in their jurisdictions, implementing their own laws, 
regulations and support policies. Provincial energy laws and regulations cannot contradict the national regulatory framework 
but could build on it. Provincial electricity regulators (or ENRESP under their Spanish acronym) regulate the electricity 
activity in their respective provincial regulatory frameworks. As the wholesale energy market administrator, CAMMESA 
coordinates dispatch operations, determines wholesale prices, manages transactions in the national interconnected system 
==PAGE:12==
10 
 
(Sistema Argentino de Interconexión, SADI), and acts as off-taker in certain power purchase agreements (PPAs). The board 
of directors includes equal representation of all market participants, including the Government of Argentina, the generation 
companies, transmission and distribution utilities and large power consumers.  
All electricity transactions in the Argentine electricity sector are conducted through a wholesale electricity market which acts 
as a clearing house for electricity trading. Set up in 1992, the wholesale electricity market in Argentina is organized as a 
competitive market, including a spot market and a term market (or contracts market). Prices on the spot market are 
established on an hourly basis as a function of marginal cost of production measured at the system's load center. On the 
term market, quantities, prices and contractual conditions are agreed upon directly between sellers and buyers. Generators 
declare their marginal costs semiannually. CAMMESA uses the semiannual marginal cost declarations from thermal and 
hydro generators to determine seasonal dispatch schedules to minimize energy cost in the spot market. A stabilization fund, 
managed by CAMMESA, was designed to stabilize prices for end users. Financed from the difference between the regulated 
and spot prices, the fund is used when spot prices exceed the regulated price and replenished when the regulated price 
exceeds the spot price. The Figure 3 -shows how the wholesale electricity market in Argentina was designed to work.  
 
 
Figure 3 - Schematic diagram of the wholesale electricity market in Argentina

Transmission companies are responsible for the operation and maintenance of their networks, but not for expanding the 
grid. Thus, expansion of transmission capacity is mainly driven by its users. For example, to connect to the grid a renewable 
project developer must seek a permit from the local transmission system operator, which issues a preliminary decision for 
evaluation by CAMMESA. If approved by CAMMESA, the decision is forwarded to ENRE, which issues a final decision on 
grid access, followed by a five-day waiting period for contestations. The developer pays for the line that connects the plant 
to the connection point. Argentina’s grid is currently operating at near capacity as electricity demand in recent years has 
increased (Figure 4) at a quicker pace than generation (Figure 2), transmission and distribution capacities. Spare 
transmission capacity that existed before the 2016 and 2017 renewable energy and thermal energy auctions has already 
been taken.

2.3 RENEWABLE ENERGY FRAMEWORK 
2.3.1 
Law 26 190 and Law 27.191 
 
These laws are legal regulations on national promotion for the use of renewable energy sources to generate electric power.  
Law 26,190 was passed on December 6th, 2006 and enacted on December 27th, 2006 (5). The aim of these legal regulations 
was to declare of public interest the generation of electric power from sources of renewable energy, in order to supply the 
electricity public service and to conduct researches and manufacture equipment for the development of technology. The 
aim of these legal regulations is to reach a contribution of sources of renewable energy equal to eight percent (8 %) of the 
total national consumption of electric power by December 31st, 2017. 
Law 26,190 was amended by Law 27,191. This law was passed on September 23rd, 2015, enacted on October 15th, 2015 
and published in the Official Gazette on October 21st, 2015 (6).The aim of these legal regulations was to reach a contribution 
of sources of renewable energy equal to eight per cent (8 %) of the total national consumption of electric power by December 
31st, 2017. For this purpose, each liable party shall reach a minimum contribution of sources of renewable energy equal to 
8 percent of the total own consumption of electric power by December 31st, 2017 and shall reach a minimum contribution 
of sources of renewable energy equal to twenty per cent (20 %) of the total own consumption of electric power by December 
31st, 2025.  
Major Users of the Wholesale Electric Market and Huge Demands Users which are Customers of the Distribution Public 
Service Providers or Customers of Distributors, whose power demands are equivalent to or higher than three hundred 
kilowatts (300 kW), shall duly and individually fulfill the aims set forth in the previous section. For this purpose, they may 
generate energy by themselves or buy energy derived from different generation renewable sources by means of an 
agreement, in order to comply with the provisions that were set in this law. They may buy the energy to the producer, to a 
distributor acquiring it on its behalf from a generator, to a marketer, or they can directly buy it to CAMMESA under the 
regulations that the Enforcement Authority set forth for such purpose. 
In case of failing to fulfill the consumption liabilities on the percentage share of renewable electric power generation 
mentioned, Major Users of the Wholesale Electric Market and Huge Demands Users which are Customers of Distribution 
Public Service Providers or Customers of Distributors shall pay the deficit percentage at a price equivalent to the Electric 
Power Variable Cost of Production corresponding to the generation whose fuel source is imported gasoil.

2.3.2 
The RenovAr Program 
 
The RenovAr auction program has the leading part in achieving Argentina’s objective of 20% renewables in electricity 
consumption by 2025. It was launched in May 2016 and has so far completed three bidding rounds (round 1, round 1.5 and 
round 2), awarding 147 projects with a combined capacity of 4,466 MW at an average sale price of 54.72 USD/MWh. The 
public tendering mechanism involves a mix of incentives and guarantees and was designed in a way that seeks to overcome 
some of the issues that hindered previous attempts by the country to foster renewable energy development. 
An earlier auction effort, the GENREN program, was launched in 2009 with the aim to contract 1 GW of renewable power 
but only a small part of the 895 MW awarded got constructed as developers had difficulties obtaining financing.  
A key element of the new regime is the Fund for the Development of Renewable Energies (Fondo para el Desarrollo de las 
Energias Renovables, FODER), which was created to help mitigate risks and attract the much-needed investments. FODER 
is a public trust fund set up to provide guarantees and financing. The fund, run by Argentina’s Investment and Foreign Trade 
Bank (Banco de Inversión y Comercio Exterior, BICE), provides two type of guarantees to RenovAr winners -- a liquidity 
guarantee, which ensures that project owners are paid for the electricity they deliver to the grid, and a solvency guarantee, 
designed to reduce country risk. The solvency guarantee allows project companies to exercise a put option under certain 
circumstances and transfer their assets to FODER in exchange for cash compensation. An additional optional guarantee 
was available to RenovAr bidders. The World Bank provided a USD-480-million guarantee to backstop the government’s 
failure to fund FODER when it needs to pay a put price. This guarantee backed projects in the first two tenders, Rounds 1 
and 1.5, of the RenovAr program. In March 2018, the World Bank approved another USD-250-million guarantee to support 
Round 2. The RenovAr program has so far attracted strong investor interest, which led to significant oversubscription of the 
bidding rounds. Bidders compete to sign 20- year power purchase agreements (PPAs) with CAMMESA, which acts as off-
taker on behalf of distribution utilities and wholesale market large users. Strike prices are denominated in USD and adjusted 
annually (7) (8). Round 3 of the RenovAr program was launched in November 2018 and the winners will be announces in 
July 2019 (9) (10).  
From the 147 projects of the 3 rounds that were adjudicated (capacity of 4,466.5 MW), 34 are wind energy projects (capacity 
of 2,466.2 MW) (Figure 5). The average sale price of these projects is 50.07 USD/MWh. The two regions where most of the 
projects are located are Buenos Aires Province (13 projects with a capacity of 1,011.0 MW) and the Patagonia region (11 
projects with a capacity of 740.7 MW). Most of the projects are still under construction.

2.4 COMODORO RIVADAVIA  
 
Comodoro Rivadavia is located in Chubut Province, Patagonia. This is one of the windiest zones in Argentina. Although the 
Patagonia has the lower populational density of the country, Comodoro Rivadavia is one of the most populated cities in the 
region (175.000 inhabitants in 2010). Comodoro Rivadavia is located in the shoreline of Golfo San Jorge. The San Jorge 
Gulf (Golfo San Jorge) is a bay in southern Patagonia, Argentina. It is an ocean basin opening to the Atlantic. Its shoreline 
spans Chubut and Santa Cruz province. The gulf measures approximately 142 miles (229 km) at its mouth and covers 
approximately 39 square kilometers (Figure 6).  
The discovery of oil in Argentina occurred in 1907 when a drilling rig which was looking for water discovered oil instead. 
The Argentine oil industry started in Comodoro Rivadavia and was facilitated by the 1886 National Mining Code. This code 
established that the oil fields belonged to the State, and that they could also be exploited by the private sector by concession. 
Nearly 40,000 oil wells have been drilled since then. The oil fields and its concession areas can be seen in Figure 7. The 
city evolved around this industry, and even today when this panorama has changed substantially, it is still called the 
"National Oil Capital." 
The oil fields electricity consumption is stable. There are no peaks during the day neither valleys during the night because 
the pumps in the wells (Electro submergible pumps and Sucker rod pumps) work 24/7. The electricity consumption has a 
direct relationship with the liquid that is produced by the wells (oil and water). This means that there is local stable demand 
in the area.  
The energy for the oil fields is supplied by two main sources: 
- 
Electricity produced in the same oil fields by burning gas in generators (associated gas from the oil production 
and/or gas that could be bought from suppliers that transport their gas through a gas pipeline that crosses the 
region); 
- 
Electricity transported through electric lines of 500 KV and 132 KV (Figure 8) (11).

2.5 RESEARCH OBJECTIVES  
 
The objectives of this project are to study: 
- 
Two existing wind farms in the surrounding areas of Comodoro Rivadavia; 
- 
The development of a new wind farm in the same area; 
- 
The possible synergy between wind farms and oil fields in the area. 
Facts that support the possible synergy are: 
- 
Proven wind resources available in the area; 
- 
Proven stable electricity consumption in the oil fields in the area; 
- 
Possible limitations to transport the electricity produced by future wind energy projects to the main consumption 
centers; 
- 
Oil companies have the rights over extended areas due to the oil exploitation agreements with the local government.  
- 
Regulation framework that forces big companies to consume renewable energy; 
- 
Manantiales Behr wind farm and Diadema wind farm belong to YPF and Capex (oil companies) respectively and 
are located in the same concession areas where oil is produced.

3 METHODOLOGY  
In this section, the methodology to model Manantiales Behr and Diadema wind farms (Figure 10 and Figure 11) in the area 
near Comodoro Rivadavia and a new theoretical development in the same region is discussed.  
 
 
Figure 10 - Manantiales Behr and Diadema wind farms methodology workflow 
 
 
Figure 11 - WAsP methodology – How it is applied to this study.  
 
==PAGE:19==

In order to create WAsP projects for the wind farms, data collection will be carried out, as WAsP is a series of modules that 
need input parameters. 
Existing data coming from different sources such as databases, archives, and newspapers articles will be collected and 
used as input data for modelling and analysis. Global Wind Atlas (GWA) will be used as the source to obtain a Generalized 
Wind Climate (GWC) (12) and the roughness and elevation maps of the areas of interest. In addition, Google Earth Pro (13) 
will be used as a primary tool to locate existing wind turbines and/or delimit the minimum extension of the maps that will be 
used. Manufactures catalogs will provide information about the power curves of the wind turbines. Archival data regarding 
turbine model and turbine height comes from public sources such as official documents posted on the company's web site 
and local newspapers publications. All this information is relevant in order to create a project in WAsP for each wind farm. 
Once the project is created, it will be possible to analyze it, run sensibility cases and arrive at conclusions. Wind farms 
energy production per month is available in CAMMESA database (14). This information will be used for comparison 
purposes.  
3.1 GENERALIZED WIND CLIMATE 
As wind observations are not available, a possible solution is to obtain modelled data from Global Wind Atlas (GWA), which 
is a good approximation. 
In GWA web page it is possible to identify the areas of interest inside a rectangle or polygon. Once the area is selected, the 
Wind Atlas file (.lib) will be downloaded and opened in WAsP to obtain the Generalized Wind Climate (GWC). Then it will 
be possible to work with the GWC while building the project.  
3.2 WIND TURBINES COORDINATES 
The selected wind farms location will be identified in a map where all the renewable energy projects in Argentina are shown 
(Figure 12) (15). Then, in Google Earth Pro each wind turbine location will be identified. When taken from Google Earth 
Pro, the coordinates of each wind turbine will be in the Latitude-Longitude system of coordinates. In order to upload these 
coordinates in WAsP, it will be necessary to transform them to the proper map projection (UTM-Proj.-N.hemisph., Zone 19). 
For this purpose, the Geo-Projection Transformer tool will be used (Figure 13). Once the coordinates are transformed; each 
wind turbine location will be uploaded in WAsP. 
3.3 ROUGHNESS AND ELEVATION MAPS 
To obtain the roughness and elevation maps, first the area of it will be determined. For that Google Earth Pro will be used. 
A radius of at least 150 turbine heights (rule of thumb) will be considered from the wind turbines in the extreme corners of 
the wind farm. 
General rule of thumb for (land cover) map size is: distance from any calculating site to border of map = 100 × {calculation 
height} + 50% (safety margin).  
This way, the circle areas that must be contained inside the map in order to guarantee it is reliable because all the influence 
of terrain features are being captured. The top-right and the base-left corners of the map area will be identified in Latitude-
Longitude system of coordinates (leaving the mentioned circles inside). With these set of coordinates (no coordinates 
transformation is needed) it is possible to use WAsP Map Editor tool to import the Elevation and Roughness maps from 
GWA Database. Roughness values can be adjusted taking into account photos of the area and satellite images and tables 
with correlation of type of environment with roughness classes. The selected map of each area will be imported in the WAsP 
project.  
3.4 WIND TURBINE POWER CURVE AND HEIGHTS 
The first step will be to identify the model and height of the wind turbines for each wind farm according public information 
like company's web site and local newspapers publications. After that, the power curves of the identified wind turbines will 
be searched in the available information from their manufactures’ websites and catalogues. Each power curve will be 
modeled in WAsP Turbine Editor tool and saved in a file (.wtg). This file will be uploaded in WAsP.

Some additional cases will be built removing or adding turbine clusters to see the impact on wake losses.  
For the grid, two rules of thumb are taken into account. First, apply a grid resolution better than 70% of the height of interest. 
Second, Max 1000 x 1000 nodes.

depends on selecting an area with the lower values of roughness if possible. Another difference is that the proportional 
wake loss is lower than the old cluster. This can be improved by the layout of the new cluster.

The RIX layer represents ruggedness index and is an objective measure of the steepness or ruggedness of the terrain. 
Large RIX values will lead to large errors in the flow modeling, most likely leading to an overestimation of mean wind speeds 
on ridges and hilltops (12). In Figure 52 it is possible to see that RIX values are significant in some areas where turbines 
are located, mainly in the area of the New Cluster. Because of that CDF modeling is performed.

5 IMPLICATIONS OF THE WIND RESOURCES IN THE AREA 
In this section, the energy that is consumed by the oilfields near Comodoro Rivadavia is quantified. Additionally, the 
equivalent installed capacity to supply that demand will be calculated.  
Manantiales Behr produces 16% of YPF Energy demand in the Wholesale Electricity Market (16). Considering the 60% of 
capacity factor average of Manantiales Behr (installed capacity 99 MW), it means that YPF consumes 371.25 MW. 
According to public information from the Subsecretary of hydrocarbons and fuels (25), the current amount of liquids 
produced by YPF in all the country is 15.33 Millions of m3/month (April 2019). The total amount of liquids produced in Golfo 
San Jorge Basin (Comodoro Rivadavia Area) is 17.56 Millions of m3/month (April 2019). Extrapolating, the total consumption 
of the oil fields in Golfo San Jorge is about 425 MW. 
This production was constant during the las 10 years from public data (Figure 55). This should be framed in Argentina’s 
context, where the electricity demand has been raising during the las years, as it was explained previously. It is possible to 
extrapolate this demand will remain, at least, constant for the next 20 years. So, with 7 wind farms of 100 MW (like 
Manantiales Behr) with a capacity factor of 60%, it would be possible to provide the 100% of the energy to the oil fields 
need.  
As these wind farms electricity production will have valleys and peaks, it would be necessary to connect them to the high 
voltage lines (500 KV to Buenos Aires) when the generation is higher than installed capacity, but also to take power for the 
oil field when going through valleys (spot market). Additionally, it would be necessary to provide regulatory framework in 
order to measure the consumed energy from the wind farms by the oil fields in order to comply with the renewable energy 
targets.

6 DISCUSSION 
The objective of this section is to understand the limitations of the applied methodology. In addition, the required information 
for future studies in the area is described.  
The main limitations of this study were linked to the reliability of the available data and data privacy.  
There is a meteorological station near the wind farms, next to Comodoro Rivadavia Airport. From this site, wind speed and 
wind direction measurements at 10 m height and 1-hour frequency were available in a public databased (Meteorological 
National Service). These observations were discarded as they were not suitable for the study that was being performed.  
Due to data privacy, it was not possible to collect information from the wind farms, even though both companies, YPF Luz 
and Hychico, were contacted. Consequently, it was not possible to work with observations. This was an important constraint 
of the study.  
Due to the lack of observations, Global Wind Atlas (GWA) was used as the primary source of data for the analysis. 
Nevertheless, this database has also shown limitations concerning roughness and GWC resolution when studying a specific 
wind farm.  
Regarding roughness, roughness length in the GWA has spatial resolution of 300m. This was derived from the GlobCover 
2009 land cover map that has a 10 arc-second (300 m) resolution. The data was converted to the GWA tiles, but the 
resolution remained the same. When importing the roughness maps form GWA, high values of 1.5 m were identified but 
there is no evidence, based on satellite images and photos form the area, that those values were accurate. Consequently, 
roughness sensitivities were performed, which were key when performing the calculations showing a significant impact on 
the results. 
Regarding GWC, GWA 2.0 was specifically designed to give an overview of wind power potential and planning and 
modelling on national, regional and global scales. It can be used to identify potential high-wind areas for wind power 
generation virtually anywhere in the world and perform preliminary calculations. However, it cannot be used for precise 
prediction of specific wind turbines and wind farms.  
Nevertheless, this study could replicate the exceptional conditions of the wind resources in the area. 
With more data, it would also be possible to continue the analysis using WAsP Engineering (fatigue loads, extreme loads 
and siting) and WAT (Site suitability for assessment of wind farms) software. In a follow up study, it would be possible to: 
 
Calculate extreme winds and determinate the appropriate wind turbines class for the area 
 
Estimate “other losses” that could affect the capacity factor 
 
Test different layouts of Manantiales Behr New Cluster  
In order to continue studying the wind resources potential in the Patagonia area, the next steps will be necessary: 
 
Obtain representative wind measurements / observations 
o 
Installing a met-mast 
o 
Getting in touch with meteorological national service to see how these measurements could be taken as 
they already have a met station in the area.  
o 
Alliance with operators of the existing wind farms and/or oil fields that could be interested in developing 
new wind farms in their concession areas.  
 
On the site mapping process to determine appropriate roughness values; 
 
Analysis of the electric grid in the region to identify constrains and opportunities.  
 
Analysis of the electricity demand by field to look for collaboration opportunities (one wind farm could provide energy 
for many neighboring oil fields).

7 CONCLUSIONS 
The synergy between oil companies and wind energy projects near Comodoro Rivadavia is based on the fact that the oil 
companies have the rights over extended areas where the wind resources and the electricity demand co-exist. In addition, 
regulatory framework forces big companies (like YPF) to consume renewable energy. Manantiales Behr and Diadema wind 
farms are a good example of this synergy and of the transition from an economy based on fossil fuels to renewable energy.  
Manantiales Behr (99 MW) and Diadema (27.45 MW) wind farms were modelled in WAsP. Public information databases, 
including Global Wind Atlas, were used as input for the WAsP Projects.  
Roughness sensitivity was performed to quantify the impact of this variable in the Annual Energy Production (AEP) as the 
original GWA roughness map did not correlate with actual data like photos of the wind farms and Google street view. Three 
additional roughness maps were built using the WAsP Map Editor Tool for each wind farm.  
WAsP’s IBZ and CFD models were run for both wind farms. For Manantiales Behr, both models have similar results as the 
terrain is very flat (up to 2% AEP difference). On the other hand, Diadema wind turbines are at the top of hills, so CFD is 
the most representative method to be used. The results of the two models were significantly different due to IBZ model 
overestimated the AEP (up to 14% AEP difference). The AEP results were compared with actual production energy data. 
The modified vector maps with the lowest roughness values (maximum roughness length values of 0.1 m) resulted in the 
most representative ones.  
Diadema wind farm´s WAsP projects were re-calculated taking off the “old” cluster of 7 wind turbines E-44 (height 45 m). 
Even though the old cluster is located to the West of the new cluster (height 78 m), and the predominate wind direction is 
West, the new cluster has no significant wake loss associated to the old one (less than 0.5% increase in the capacity factor).  
A new wind farm of 99 MW was built using WAsP. The selected location for it was in Manantiales Behr oil field concession 
area, next to the existing wind farm (towards the East). The results showed similar AEP and Capacity factor values for this 
cluster to the existing wind farm. The selection of the area and the layout is crucial while trying to optimize the AEP. It was 
not possible to continue with this analysis. For future studies, it would be necessary to count with wind observations and an 
“on the site” roughness map.  
Even though the resolution of the public information was not good enough to be used for accurate prediction of specific 
wind turbines, this study could reproduce, at a local level, the outstanding conditions of the wind resources in the area. 
It was possible to calculate the installed capacity needed to supply the local oilfields energy demand. This resulted in 7 
additional wind farms like Manantiales Behr which is totally achievable in the area around Comodoro Rivadavia due to its 
extension.  
The future of wind energy projects looks quite promising in Patagonia region. The development of new wind farms to supply 
local demand from the oilfields is a good approach to overcome the current electric grid restriction, while waiting for new 
infrastructure to be built in order to increase transportation capacity. The results of this study could be useful to raise the 
interest of oil companies in the matter and, for that purpose, could be shared locally during workshops or seminars of the 
oil industry.
