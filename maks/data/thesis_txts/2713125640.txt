Abstract
High temperature heat pumps can accommodate the huge need for process heating be­
tween 100 °C and 300 °C in the industry in a very efficient manner by utilizing excess heat
and matching the available heat source to the heat demand. Supercritical CO2 can be
employed as the working fluid of the high temperature heat pumps due to its favourable
thermophysical properties and an expander can replace the throttling valve to recover
expansion power and to enhance the cycle performance.
In this thesis, conducted in collaboration with the Danish Technological Institute (DTI),
a reverse Brayton cycle with an internal heat exchanger was modified and optimized in
EES. Two applications were simulated: the spray dryer and the autoclave. Both of these
cases exhibit large temperature glides, which is an important feature since they have the
potential to match well with a high temperature supercritical CO2 heat pump and minimize
irreversibilities. It was determined that the highest possible low pressure and the lowest
possible superheating are optimal for the cycle’s COP. An ideal expander yielded a 67.7%
and a 72.3% increase in the spray dryer and autoclave cycle’s COP compared to that of
a traditional cycle with a throttling valve. The COP values achieved were 2.23 and 2.05
respectively.
A sensitivity analysis with respect to the isentropic efficiency of the expander, with the isen­
tropic efficiency of the compressor and the effectiveness of the internal heat exchanger
as parameters, was conducted. It was found that when the isentropic efficiency of the
expander increases, the pressure ratio and the net work consumed in the cycle decrease
and the COP increases. The increase of the other parameters also improved the cycle.
From the various expander technologies (rotary vane, screw, scroll, piston, turboexpander)
explored through the literature and the market, only the scroll expander (isentropic effi­
ciency of 83%) could match the modeled cycle (pressure ratio, inlet temperature) and it
provided the best results (COP = 2.039 and 1.799 respectively). The commercial semi­
hermetic scroll expanders of Air Squared can be used in parallel to match the power output
of the modeled cycle if some of their parameters that don’t fit are disregarded.

Introduction
The utilization of energy is essential for improving the quality of life in the whole world, as
well as for the economic and social development of every country. Energy is defined as the
ability to produce work and can be encountered in many forms such as chemical, thermal,
electrical, mechanical, gravitational, nuclear, radiant, acoustic, and kinetic. Energy can be
stored, converted, and enhanced, depending on the application. The sources of energy
can be fossil fuels (oil, coal, natural gas, shale oil, etc.), renewable (biomass, geothermal,
solar, hydroelectric, wind, marine, etc.) and fissile (uranium), etc.
1.1
Energy situation
Global energy demand is growing as the world population and the development of the
economy grow. It looks like this energy demand will continue to grow, but conventional
sources of power generation will be depleted over time. They have already become dan­
gerous to the environment, and they are directly related to global warming. Due to the
gradual depletion of stocks of coal, oil and gas, nuclear energy is considered as a possible
alternative, but due to its risk issues, its relatively high costs, as well as its technological
monopolies, it is not an affordable option for most countries. So, the renewable energy
sources are preferred as alternative and long­term sources, while being environmentally
friendly and not posing risks to human health.
Although global energy consumption, as well as resource consumption, are growing at
a slower pace in recent years, it is expected to increase in the coming decades. The
United States maintain a significant volume of total energy demand, but the relative share
of consumption is declining over time, as it is expected to be 19.8% in 2050, compared to
24.1% in 2010. [1] Japan maintains a very high percentage of total oil demand, which was
around 31.5% in 2010, while it is expected to have decreased to 27.7% in 2030 and to
24.8% in 2050. [1] Global energy consumption is expected to increase significantly from
13.6 billion tonnes of equivalent oil (TOE) in 2010 to 44.6 billion TOE by 2050. [1] Because
of the important and growing China’s role in the global energy market, global demand of
carbon is expected to increase from 3.6 billion TOE in 2010 to 12.9 billion TOE in 2050,
while global energy demand for coal will also increase from 26.5% in 2010 to 28.9% in
2050. [1] Energy consumption tends to increase from 524 quadrillion Btu in 2010, to 630
quadrillion Btu in 2020 and 820 quadrillion Btu in 2040. [1] 10% of the world population
exploits 90% of the available fossil fuels, the gradual depletion of which makes careful
and sustainable energy consumption an urgent issue for all countries. [1]
1.2
Environmental impact of global energy consumption
The rapid increase in energy consumption results in an increase in emissions from com­
bustion products. The combustion products, and especially CO2 , accumulate in the at­
mosphere, while in addition, the nitrogen and sulfur oxides released pose a very impor­
tant environmental issue. Μethane emissions contribute greatly to the greenhouse effect
alongside CO2.
From the beginning of the industrial revolution onwards, there has been one continu­
ous increase in anthropogenic CO2 emissions. After 2000 there was an acceleration of
the CO2 emission rate, which is directly linked to strong economic growth in China and
other Asian countries, which are increasing demand for electricity produced by fossil fuels
and for diesel cars and trucks. In 2008, humans emitted almost 30 billion tons of CO2,

and even though emissions of this magnitude are unsustainable, the amount has now
increased further. [1]
Global CO2 emissions related to the energy sector tend to increase from 31.2 billion metric
tonnes in 2010, at 36.4 metric tons in 2020 and 45.5 metric tons in 2040. [1] In 2003, China
emitted about 3.5 Gt of CO2, while the corresponding US emissions were 5.8 Gt, but by
2010 China had increased emissions at 8.95 Gt , while the corresponding US emissions
fell to 5.25 Gt, despite the fact that China’s per capita emissions are still 2.5 times lower
than those in the US. [1]
It is, therefore, obvious that the capture of CO2 produced by human activities could con­
tribute significantly to reducing the emissions of CO2 to the atmosphere. The role of the
forest sector for the capture of CO2 has long been recognized by scientists and policy­
makers, but CO2 capture is not limited to the forestry sector. Using CO2 as a working fluid
in refrigeration systems and heat pumps is a possible solution.
1.3
Heating and refrigeration as part of the energy
consumption
Due to their size and penetration, the way in which heating and cooling are produced and
consumed has a significant impact on the EU economy and on its ability to achieve the
goals it has set. Heating and cooling in buildings, businesses and industry consume about
half of the energy produced and used in EU. [2] In addition, they are projected to remain

Heat generation technologies range from small decentralized applications, such as gas

Heating and cooling cannot be economically transferred in large distances, therefore they

1.4
Waste heat
Waste heat is heat created as a result of accomplishing work by a machine or other
energy­consuming activities. Based on the principles of thermodynamics, all these opera­
tions produce some waste heat, which has a lower exergy than the original energy source.
Human activities, natural processes, and living organisms are all sources of waste heat.
For example, incandescent light bulbs get heated, refrigerators heat the room air, and an
internal combustion engine produces high­temperature exhaust gases.

1.5
Heat pumps
A heat pump is a device that transfers thermal energy from one place to another, most
commonly between an enclosed environment and the outdoors. The energy is trans­
ported from the outdoors into the building when it is utilized to heat an indoor space. By
moving heat from the inside of a building to the outdoors, a heat pump may also serve
as an air conditioner which cools the indoor space. Heat pumps can play a crucial part in
energy transition and climate change mitigation due to their high efficiency and the rising
percentage of renewable, fossil­free production of electricity.
The refrigeration cycle is employed for the operation of a heat pump. A refrigerant at
ambient temperature is compressed in the heating mode. The refrigerant becomes heated
as a result. This thermal energy may be used, for instance, to heat a building’s interior
(heat sink). The refrigerant is then decompressed. Part of its thermal energy is lost and
returns to the environment in a lower temperature and pressure than before, and it can
now absorb the ambient energy from the heat source (water, air, ground, etc.). Then, the
cycle is completed, and it can be repeated. The cycle needs to consume electricity to run
the compressor and any pumps that might be used.
Coefficient of performance (COP) demonstrates the efficiency of a heat pump. For heat
pumps COP is defined as the amount of heat provided to the heat sink via the condenser
(or gas cooler) divided by the amount of work consumed by the compressor. A heat
pump is more efficient and consumes less energy when COP increases. Heat pumps are
normally much more energy efficient than simple electrical resistance heaters for space
heating. The way that electricity production is made dictates the carbon footprint of heat
pumps, as they use electricity to power their compressors.

Supercritical CO2 and reverse Brayton cycle
Interest in using CO2 as a refrigerant is growing rapidly in recent years, thanks to its
favourable thermophysical properties, non­flammability, non­toxicity, low cost, and negli­
gible global warming potential (GWP=1). Above its critical temperature (304.13 K, 31.0
°C) and critical pressure (7.3773 MPa, 73.8 bar), it acts as a supercritical fluid. Due to the
low critical temperature, the operation of the cycle in supercritical conditions is frequent.
The reverse Brayton cycle can be employed for the analysis of supercritical CO2 high
temperature heat pumps. By reversing the gas turbine Brayton cycle, a reverse Brayton
cycle creates a cooling effect: a gas is compressed, cooled, and then expanded. Due to
the expansion temperature being low, this gas can be utilized to cool an enclosure either
directly or through a heat exchanger. A reverse Brayton cycle consists of an adiabatic
(ideally isentropic) compression, an (ideally) isobaric heat rejection, an adiabatic (ideally
isentropic) expansion, and an (ideally) isobaric heat addition. This cycle can also be called
the Bell Coleman cycle or the gas refrigeration cycle. The reverse Brayton cycle is broadly
used in jet aircrafts for air conditioning systems using air from the engine compressors.

1.8
Throttling valves vs expanders
Throttling (or expansion) valves are used predominantly in refrigeration systems and heat
pumps. With a throttling operation they reduce the refrigerant’s condensing pressure (high
pressure) to the evaporating pressure (low pressure). The throttling valve’s narrow hole,
or orifice, lowers the refrigerant’s pressure to the point where it vaporizes (flash/partial
evaporation). The refrigerant entering the evaporator from the throttling valve is at low
pressure, low temperature, and partly liquid and vapor. Another purpose of the throttling
valves is to match the load characteristics with the equipment by regulating the liquid­
refrigerant flow to the evaporator. The pace at which the refrigerant enters the cooling
coil is proportional to the rate at which the liquid refrigerant in the coil evaporates. As
one might assume, the amount is determined by the amount of heat extracted from the
refrigerated area.
In the case of the throttling valve the process is not isentropic, thus it is very irreversible.

When the refrigerant enters the evaporator, its entropy is increased compared to when
it leaves the condenser and enters in the throttling valve. This irreversibility is offset by
the fact that throttling valves are very simple devices with a very simple purpose, and
as a result their cost is very low. Furthermore, they significantly reduce the system’s
weight, they make no noise, and they need very little or no maintenance. Also, if an
expander was used instead of a throttling valve in a typical vapor compression cycle, its
work when moving the refrigerant from a saturated liquid phase to a low­quality two­phase
liquid/vapor state compared to the energy consumed by the compressor would be so small
that it wouldn’t justify its high cost. Moreover, due to the two­phase flow and speed of the
expanding fluid, traditional expanders like turbines are not only lower in capacity than the
compressor, but they also function at a poor efficiency. The two­phase flow expanders
must also run at a different speed than the compressor for maximum efficiency. [9] These
are the reasons that throttling valves are preferred in the common applications of the
refrigeration cycle.
However, for supercritical CO2 cycles, where the refrigerant is expanded in the gas phase,
the issues regarding the liquid­vapor region disappear. Also, for cycles with a big tem­
perature lift (also large pressure difference), the work produced in the expander can be
significant and may offset the higher cost compared to a throttling valve. The contribution
to the performance improvement of the cycle could be substantial by using an expander
to recover partially the large throttling losses. According to experiments on a supercritical
CO2 cycle, there was a 7.4% COP improvement with the use of an expander instead of
a throttling valve and it could be 20.5% with a reasonable reduction of the mechanical
losses and 34.5% without any mechanical losses (theoretical cycle). [10]

2.1
Components of the heat pump
Heat pumps have the same components as a refrigeration system, but they function in
the opposite direction. The evaporator in a refrigerator and an air conditioner performs
the most essential function of chilling or freezing a space, whereas the condenser in a
heat pump serves the most significant duty of heating a space. A schematic of a basic
heat pump configuration can be seen in Fig. 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Schematic of a basic heat pump configuration [12]
2.1.1
Compressor
The compressor is one of the four basic components of a vapor compression refrigeration
system. Its role in the vapor compression refrigeration cycle is the increase in pressure
of the vapor refrigerant coming out of the evaporator. In the ideal compressor, saturated
mixture enters and superheated steam exits. With an ideal compression, the path followed
is this of constant entropy. Depending on the operating principles of compressors, they
are divided into two different types.
• The positive displacement compressor where the vapor refrigerant enters the suc­
tion side and is geometrically trapped. The reduction in the volume of trapped re­
frigerant completes the compression process.
• The aerodynamic, or simply dynamic, in which the pressure of the vapor refriger­
ant increases due to high speed of rotation that the vapor achieves while internally
centrifugal force is produced.
2.1.2
Condenser (or gas cooler)
The condenser takes the superheated steam from the compressor and first cools it to
the condensing temperature, then it condenses it. The condenser must remove from the
system all the heat absorbed by the evaporator as well as the compression heat from the
compressor. To achieve this, the condenser must have a sufficient surface area for heat
transfer to take place. In the case of a supercritical cycle, a gas cooler is used, and its
purpose is to reject heat from the supercritical fluid. No condensation occurs in that case.

==PAGE:21==
In addition to sufficient surface area for heat transfer, the condenser must have sufficient
internal volume for the superheated refrigerant from the compressor. Moreover, the vol­
ume should be sufficient to separate the fluid from the vapor. If the volume for separation
is very small, the condenser is filled with liquid refrigerant. With this condition, the volume
available for cooling of superheated steam reduces and the flow of steam to the condenser
drops.
Depending on how they are cooled, condensers can be distinguished in three types:
• In air­cooled condensers, which are cooled by air circulation.
• In water­cooled condensers, which are cooled by water circulation.
• In evaporative condensers, which are cooled with simultaneous water and air circu­
lation.
2.1.3
Expansion valve
Expansion or choke devices, or expansion valves, aim at regulating the necessary quan­
tity of liquid refrigerant flowing to the evaporator and changing the thermodynamic char­
acteristics of the refrigerant, so that from a high­pressure fluid at the condenser outlet it
is converted to a low­pressure fluid. In modern refrigeration facilities the following types
of automatic expansion valves are used:
• Capillary tube.
• Expansion valve with float control on the high side pressure.
• Expansion valve with float control on the low side pressure.
• Thermal expansion valve, also known as constant overheating valve or thermostatic
expansion valve.
• Constant pressure valve.
• Electronic expansion valve.
2.1.4
Evaporator (or gas heater)
While the evaporator in an air conditioner, for instance, is placed within the room, the evap­
orator in a heat pump is located outside the room and exposed to a low temperature. The
evaporator, like the condenser, is built of copper coil. The low pressure, low temperature
refrigerant enters the evaporator coil, causing the coil’s temperature to drop dramatically,
becoming even lower than the ambient temperature. The refrigerant within the evaporator
has a lower temperature than the ambient temperature, therefore it absorbs heat from the
environment. The blower or fan blows atmospheric air over the evaporator, releasing heat
and heating the refrigerant. The temperature of the refrigerant rises as it absorbs heat
from the ambient air, but its pressure stays constant, and it is completely transformed to a
gaseous state. In the case of a supercritical cycle, a gas heater is used, and its purpose
is to provide heat to the supercritical fluid. No evaporation occurs in that case.
Evaporators are divided into two types, depending on the fluid they cool, i.e. air or liquids,
as follows:
• Air­cooled evaporators, i.e. evaporators that cool the air.
• Water­cooled evaporators, i.e. evaporators that cool liquids.

2.1.5
Refrigerants
The refrigerant, in addition to the four essential components of the heat pump, is an equally
significant component. The refrigerant used in the heat pump cycle is like that used in re­
frigerators and air conditioners. Chlorofluorohydrocarbons are among the most regularly
utilized refrigerants, however a variety of other alternative refrigerants have also been
developed.
Non­flammable, non­toxic, non­corrosive, with no ozone depletion potential or global warm­
ing potential are properties that an ideal refrigerant would have. It should ideally be nat­
ural, well­studied, and have a low impact on the environment. It should also have a boil­
ing point that is slightly lower than the target temperature (although this can be adjusted
by adjusting the pressure), a relatively high density in gaseous form (which can also be
adjusted by adjusting the pressure), a moderate density in liquid form, a high critical tem­
perature, and a high heat of vaporization. Extremely high pressures should be avoided
wherever possible. Newer refrigerants solve the issue of chlorofluorocarbons’ (CFC) harm
to the ozone layer and hydrochlorofluorocarbons’ (HCFC) contribution to climate change,
although some raise concerns about toxicity and/or flammability.
Heat pumps using HCFCs cause the same environmental problems that refrigerators and
air­conditioners using HCFCs cause. HCFCs tend to deplete the ozone layer in the atmo­
sphere and contribute to the greenhouse effect. The direct emissions of refrigerant from
heat pumps, or other systems using a refrigerant, are due to the weakness to design and
create a hermetically sealed circuit, which results in a percentage of the refrigerant es­
caping to the environment. In many large systems, the amount of refrigerant estimated to
be released into the atmosphere each year sometimes exceeds 20% of its total quantity.
The refrigerant that leaks has a double effect on climate change. It affects it directly,
due to the high global warming potential (GWP) that it often has, as well as indirectly,
as the leaks reduce the efficiency of the system and, therefore, increase the electricity
consumption. In addition, a small but important role is played by the effects of the actions
needed for maintenance and replenishment of the leaking refrigerant through mobilization
of the competent workshops. The GWP of a refrigerant is one measure of how much a
particular mass of a refrigerant is estimated to contribute to global warming. It is a relative
scale, which compares the gas in question with that of the same mass of CO2 (of which
GWP is by definition equal to 1). The GWP calculation is done for one specific period,
which must be stated together with its value.
The ozone depletion potential (ODP) of a chemical substance is the amount of ozone layer
deterioration it may cause, with the ODP of trichlorofluoromethane (R­11 or CFC­11) set
at 1.0. The molecular structure of a material can be used to calculate ODP. The ODPs of
chlorofluorocarbons are generally equivalent to 1. Because of the more vigorous bromine
reaction with ozone, brominated compounds generally have higher ODPs in the range
of 5–15. Hydrochlorofluorocarbons have ODPs ranging from 0.005 to 0.2, owing to the
presence of hydrogen, which causes them to react quickly in the troposphere, lowering
their chances of reaching the stratosphere, where the ozone layer is present. Because
hydrofluorocarbons (HFC) have no chlorine, their ODP is nearly zero.

2.2.2
Spray dryer
Spray drying is a method of rapidly drying a liquid or slurry into a dry powder with a gas
of high temperature. Pharmaceuticals, foods and materials that require highly uniform,
tiny particle size, favor this technique of drying. Usually, the heat drying medium is air
or another high temperature gas. The large temperature difference (glide) of the heat
drying medium is the reason that the spray dryer can be a potentially good candidate
for a supercritical CO2 high temperature heat pump. Sometimes, nitrogen is employed if
the liquid is a flammable solvent such as ethanol or the product is oxygen sensitive. [16]
To disseminate the liquid or slurry into a regulated drop size spray, spray dryers employ
a spray nozzle. Rotary disk and single­fluid high pressure swirl nozzles are the most
prevalent. The drop sizes are usually between 100 and 200 μm in diameter and the dry
powder is most of the times free flowing. [17]
Single effect spray dryers are the most frequent form of spray drying. At the top of the
chamber, there is a single source of drying air, and it is blasted in the same direction as the
liquid being sprayed (co­current). A fine powder is created, but sometimes it may produce
dust and have a weak flow. Multiple effect spray dryers have been designed to overcome
these issues, which dry the liquid in two stages, the first being the same as in the single
effect and the second with an integrated static bed. The purpose of the bed is to cluster

2.4.1
Turboexpander
A turbine is a velocity expander device that converts the kinetic energy of a fluid (such as
steam, water, combustion gases or air) into the device’s rotating motion. A basic turbine
is made up of several blades (steel is now one of the most popular materials) that enable
fluid to flow into the turbine and drive the blades. While the fluid runs through, these
blades rotate, trapping part of the energy as rotational motion. Fluid passing through a
turbine loses kinetic energy and leaves with less energy than when it entered. [26] The
design of a radial inflow turbine can be seen in Fig. 2.6.
A turbine used in a heat pump has no difference from a steam turbine. Turbines utilized
in a heat pump, however, have certain unique characteristics due to the substantial dif­
ference in thermal physical properties between refrigerants and steam:
• Turbines utilized in a heat pump have a smaller enthalpy loss and a higher expansion
ratio than steam for a given temperature difference. [26]
• Organic fluids have a higher density and lower specific volume than water, allowing
for smaller flow passages and overall turbine dimensions.
• Due to the compact layouts and smaller sizes of organic turbines, it is necessary to
pay attention to the overspeed problem of a turbine during load shedding.
• Some refrigerants are flammable, explosive, or costly, thus safety and economics
must be considered.
One­stage axial turbines are often employed in systems with high flow rates and low
pressure ratios, but one­stage radial inflow turbines are suited for systems with lower flow
rates but higher pressure ratios, making them appealing candidates for high temperature
heat pumps. Radial inflow turbines have certain advantages:

• In comparison to axial turbines, radial inflow turbines are less susceptible to blade
profile imperfections, allowing high efficiency to be maintained as size lowers.
• As the blades are connected to the hub, radial inflow turbines are easier to produce
than axial turbines.
• As a result of the increased stiffness, the system’s rotor dynamic stability is also
improved.

2.4.2
Screw expander
The screw expander is made up of helical rotors with a clearance of roughly 50 μm and
is categorized as single or twin­screw. The construction is basic, with medium noise,
medium frictional and leakage losses and a quite high cost. Screw expanders have a
built­in volume ratio of 2 to 8 and a pressure ratio of 2 to 10. [28] Screw expanders have
recorded power outputs ranging from 1.5 kW to 1 MW, indicating that they have unique
properties for a wide range of sizes and capacity when compared to other volumetric
expanders. Isentropic efficiency has been observed to range from 20% to 70%. [28]
They work well for a temperature of 190°C and a pressure of 1.6 MPa. Typical single and
twin screw expander designs can be seen in Fig. 2.7.

Figure 2.7: Single and twin screw expander designs [29]
They have greater rotating speeds, which may render a reduction gearbox essential. Un­
synchronized or synchronized screw expanders are available. The rotors and bearings of
the unsynchronized screw expander require lubrication. The lubrication of an unsynchro­
nized screw expander necessitates an external loop for oil circulation, making the heat
recovery system more complex and preventing complete oil extraction from the operation.
Lubrication, nonetheless, significantly reduces leaks and frictional losses. Synchronized
screw expanders have a simple structure and do not require lubrication, but they have
higher friction and leakage losses. [28]
Pressure losses during suction and discharge, non­isentropic expansion induced by fric­
tion, irreversible loss during blowdown or blowback of working fluids, and expander inter­
nal leakage losses are all significant losses in screw expanders. The rotor profile is vital
in reducing leakage and frictional losses, as well as the efficiency of the expander. In
1990, the newly designed ”N” profile rotor, which can be seen in Fig. 2.8, was created at
City University London, UK , with little internal leakage and reduced frictional loss owing
to pure rolling motion between rotors. [28]
The main benefit of screw expanders over other expanders is that they can function as
compressors and expanders interchangeably with just one set of rotors. The bearing
forces associated with pressure distribution throughout the rotor render it unstable for
higher pressure ratio applications. The suction and discharge ports of the compressor
and expander can be properly positioned to reduce this instability. [28]

2.4.3
Scroll expander
The scroll expander is a positive displacement machine with a defined volume ratio built
in. It is comprised of two spirals: an orbiting and a fixed scroll. Fig. 2.9 shows the working
principles and the main components of a typical scroll expander. The built­in volume ratio
of scroll expanders is 1.5–5, with a maximum reported expander power of 12 kW and
an isentropic efficiency of 83%. [28] They can endure an operating pressure of 8.2 MPa
and a temperature of 180 °C. The usual volume ratios are between 1.5 and 4, however,
the expanders can be used in series for greater volume ratios. [28] Scroll compressors
generally have a pressure ratio of less than 10, however, in expander mode a pressure
ratio of 15 is recorded. [28]
Figure 2.9: The processes and the components of scroll expander [28]
There are two types of scroll expanders: compliant scroll and kinematically constrained
scroll. [28] The lubrication of the compliant scroll is required since it is not confined and
will cause severe wear if not lubricated. If properly lubricated, the compliant scroll may
significantly minimize leakage losses. The use of an external lubrication loop is generally
essential. The shape of the expander, the working fluid and the operating conditions
influence its performance. [28]
The design or built­in volume ratio is where volumetric expanders achieve the best perfor­
mance. Under expansion occurs when the expander’s internal volume ratio is less than
the system’s nominal volume ratio. Over expansion takes place when the expander’s in­
ternal volume ratio is greater than the system’s specified volume ratio. The performance

of the expander is undermined by both under and over expansion. Both phenomena can
be seen in Fig. 2.10. [28]

Scroll expanders are subjected to axial strains on thrust bearings when used in CO2 re­
frigeration cycles at high pressures. Oil­film pressure on shaft bearings forces the orbiting
scroll onto the stationary scroll, reducing axial stress on the bearings and radial leakage.
The over­expansion prevents the orbiting scroll from separating, and the axial leakage
channel is eliminated, thus improving volumetric efficiency. [28]
2.4.4
Piston expander
Small­scale CHP systems, waste heat recovery from internal combustion engines, and
refrigeration cycles all employ piston expanders. The maximum documented isentropic
efficiency is 76%, however the stated efficiency of expanders in most available literature is
less than 50%.[28] The piston expanders can withstand pressures of up to 9 MPa and tem­
peratures of 380–560 °C. They rotate at a lower speed than vane and screw expanders,
between 600 to 2000 rpm.[28] Because of the decreased speed, there is no need for a
speed reduction gearbox, thus the generator and the expander are directly attached. Ow­
ing to the greater internal volume ratio, piston expanders may be used for high pressure
ratios, notably in the transcritical or supercritical CO2 cycle. The internal volume ratio of
piston expanders ranges from 6 to 14. [28]

piston expanders, as seen in Fig. 2.11. The radial piston expander and the axial piston

In comparison to other positive displacement expanders, the piston expander has a struc­

expanders), lubrication is required for their smooth operation. In a reciprocating piston
expander, friction exists between the piston rings, the piston, and the cylinder wall. The
reciprocating piston expander is less appealing than other volumetric expanders because
of manufacturing complexity, poor breathing characteristics, torque pulsation, and high
cost. [28]
2.4.5
Rotary vane expander
Rotary vane expanders feature a straightforward construction, cheap cost, high torque,
great volumetric efficiency, and the ability to operate at high temperatures and pressures.
They have a built­in volume ratio of 2–8 and can withstand pressures of up to 8 MPa and
temperatures of up to 150 °C. The maximum documented isentropic and volumetric effi­
ciency for a vane expander is 71% with an expander power generation ranging from a few
watts to 2.2 kW. Low isentropic efficiency and power are linked to expander leakage and
frictional losses. They rotate at a modest speed of 1500–3000 rpm and can be connected
directly to the generator. Fig. 2.12 shows the working principle and the components of
a typical rotary vane expander. Another type of vane expanders is the revolving vane
expander.
Figure 2.12: Working principle (left) and components (right) of a vane expander [33], [34]
Leakage and, as a result, expander performance are significantly influenced by the ma­
terial of the vanes of the expander and its geometry. The number of vanes, inlet angles,
and port width to stator length ratio may all be adjusted to minimize power loss at the
expander’s inlet and outlet ports for given operating conditions. The power output of
an expander is determined by the placement of the input port in relation to the working
chamber, the expander’s shape and dimensions, operating circumstances, and the work­
ing fluid. The expander maximizes power for a certain value of chamber closing edge
angle, which can be selected during the design process. [28]

2.4.6
Ejector
An ejector can be an alternative for the expansion of the high­pressure refrigerant after the
condenser (or gas cooler). Ejectors may be found in literature also as vacuum ejectors,
eductors, surface jet pumps or velocity spools.
According to Bernoulli’s Principle, when a fluid’s velocity increases, its pressure decreases,
and vice versa. An ejector takes advantage of that principle by accelerating a fluid with
high pressure (the ‘’motive’’) through a nozzle. The result is that its pressure energy is
turned into velocity and around the nozzle tip the pressure is decreased (suction chamber
of the ejector). The suction fluid connected to the ejector side­inlet is entrained/sucked
into the ejector and mixed with the motive in the diffuser where their pressure is regained
as a result of the diverging geometry and the velocity reduction. It is important to note that
as a consequence, the discharge pressure is somewhere in the middle of the motive and
suction pressures. The motive stream (refrigerant after the condenser/gas cooler) has its
pressure decreased, whereas the suction stream has its pressure increased. A simple
drawing of an ejector can be seen in Fig. 2.13.

Optimization
After having set up the cycle, the next step is to find where the cycle yields the highest
COP. The cycle is decided to have two independent variables, which are the low pressure
(P5) and the superheating in state point 5 (δT SH,5) , whose minimum value is set at 5.5 °C,
which means that state point 5 must always be at least 5.5 °C away from the saturation
curve. This is a measure that ensures that the cycle will enter the saturation curve under
no circumstances.

In EES there are various optimization methods, like the Variable Metric method and the

two independent variables mentioned above in parametric tables.
As mentioned earlier and seen in Table 3.2, the cycle is run for a set of 5 different values
of the expander’s isentropic efficiency ηis,exp (0, 0.4, 0.7, 0.85, 1), 2 different values of
the compressor’s isentropic efficiency ηis,comp (0.6, 0.8) and 3 different values of the in­

efficiency are selected as such in order to cover the whole range of efficiencies between
an expansion valve (ηis,exp = 0) and an ideal expander (ηis,exp = 1) with a special focus
around 0.85 due to the fact that as mentioned in literature review this is the highest re­
ported value for experimental expanders. The 2 values of the compressor’s efficiency
serve the purpose of showing what happens for lower and higher groups of values of
compressor efficiencies found in literature and commercial applications.
Last but not least, the cycle runs without any errors or warnings through wide ranges of
the two independent variables for an internal heat exchanger’s effectiveness of ηihx = 0.3,
however higher values of effectiveness are tested to understand how the cycle reacts.
For ηihx = 0.6 there is of course an improvement in performance, but for ηihx = 0.7, the
cycle seems to reach its limit as the model provides valid results for much smaller ranges.
Overall, when combining the above parameters, 30 different scenarios are investigated
for each of the two cases, namely spray drying and autoclave, therefore 60 scenarios in
total, to obtain a clear view of the cycle’s behaviour and limitations and where it exhibits
the best performance.

4.1
General behaviour of the supercritical CO2 cycle
The following figures show the behaviour of the temperature lines and derivative of specific
entropy lines with respect to the specific enthalpy and pressure for CO2. As it can be seen
in Fig. 4.1a, the increase of the pressure leads to temperature lines that get further away
from each other. That means that for the same temperature difference there is a greater
enthalpy difference for higher pressure levels. Therefore, the heat rejection in the sink
through the gas cooler of the heat pump is higher.
However, as it can be seen in Fig. 4.1b, the increase of the pressure leads to lower deriva­
tive of specific entropy values. That means that the work required from the compressor
is higher for higher pressures, since the isentropic lines get flatter. Also, the work pro­
duced in the expander increases for higher pressures for the same reason. The isentropic
lines in the area that the compressor works are always flatter than the ones where the ex­
pander works, therefore the work consumed in the compressor will always be higher than
the work produced in the expander. Both the compressor work and the expander work
increase for higher pressures, but it’s not clear which increases faster, and therefore if
their difference, the net work, increases or decreases, especially if the heat supplied to
the sink is fixed.
Thus, it’s not clear if for higher pressures the performance of the cycle improves, since
the heat duty of the gas cooler increases, but it’s not certain if the net work also increases.
If that’s the case, then it’s a matter of which of the two increases faster as the pressure
increases. The model will help determine that. The influence of the low pressure, as
well as the superheating of state point 5, in the performance of the cycle will be explored
further in this section.
Also, it should be noted that in Fig. 4.1b it can be seen that for lower specific enthalpies the
derivative of specific entropy receives greater values. This is an indication that for lower
values of superheating the required work from the compressor is lower, which enhances
the performance of the cycle.

After running the cycle for a wide range of values of the low pressure (P5) and the super­

For the case of spray drying the cycle’s model in EES can run with zero or few errors
between 3500 kPa and 4800 kPa for the low pressure level (in state point 5). As the
pressure gets higher and approaches 4800 kPa, then more errors appear and the valid
results can reach lower superheating in state point 5. For instance, while for 3500 kPa the
cycle runs for every value of superheating between 5.5 °C and 27 °C, as it can be seen
in Table 4.1, when the cycle is investigated for 4800 kPa, then it can run without errors
only between 5.5 °C and 20.3 °C for the scenario ηis,exp = 1 / ηis,comp = 0.8 / ηihx = 0.3.
For the similar scenario of ηis,exp = 1 / ηis,comp = 0.8 / ηihx = 0.7 the cycle runs between 7
°C and 27 °C for 3500 kPa, and between 9.1 °C and 22.2 °C for 4800 kPa, which shows
that for higher ηihx values, the cycle runs for bigger minimum and maximum superheating
values. The range of the state point 5’s superheating possible for each of the scenario can
be seen in Table 4.1 for the spray drying case and in Table 4.2 for the autoclave case. It
must be noted that, given that in the autoclave case, the source and sink inlets and outlets
have lower temperatures than the spray drying case, it’s expected that the cycle’s model
will run for lower pressure levels. That’s proven by the fact that the cycle runs without
errors between 1500 kPa and 3100 kPa.
In the same tables the maximum COP for every scenario can be seen, as well as the
low pressure and the superheating for which it occurs. In the case of spray drying the
maximum COP is 2.228 and it occurs for 4800 kPa and 5.5 °C for the scenario where
ηis,exp = 1 / ηis,comp = 0.8 / ηihx = 0.6. The reason that the maximum COP for the similar
scenario where ηihx = 0.7 is 2.22, thus lower than for ηihx = 0.6 (which is counterintuitive),
is that it occurs for higher superheating for a smaller low pressure (8.379 °C and 4556
kPa). The reason behind this is that the low superheating can’t be reached for 4800 kPa,
thus a bigger COP than 2.22 doesn’t appear. In the case of autoclave the maximum COP
is 2.109 and it occurs for 2940 kPa and 5.5 °C for the scenario where ηis,exp = 1 / ηis,comp
= 0.8 / ηihx = 0.7. In general, the highest COPs occur for the highest low pressure and
lowest superheating, with some exceptions. Thus, almost exclusively the highest COPs
occur for a low pressure of 4800 kPa in the case of spray drying and 3100 kPa in the case
of autoclave and for a superheating of 5.5 °C.

4.3.1
Summary
To sum up, when an expander is implemented instead of a throttling valve, therefore when
ηis,exp increases, a general cycle improvement occurs. Increasing ηis,exp from 0 to 1 yields
a 67.7% increase in the COP of the spray drying case and a 72.3% increase in the COP
of the autoclave case (scenario: ηihx = 0.6 / ηis,comp = 0.8). ηCar and ηLor also have a
significant increase. As ηis,exp increases, ˙W exp increases faster than ˙W comp, thus ˙W net
decreases. Moreover, the mass flow rate, thus also the volume flow rate, increases due to
the increased work in the compressor and the expander, and as a result, VHC decreases.
The low pressure remains almost constant, but the high pressure decreases, thus the
pressure ratio also decreases.

5
Discussion
5.1
Key findings
One key finding of this thesis is that for supercritical CO2 cycles a high value of the low
level pressure is preferable as the COP of the cycle increases. This fact is not clear just
from the p­h diagrams for various temperatures and derivatives of specific entropy as both
the heat duty in the sink and the work in the compressor increase simultaneously from
increasing the low pressure. However, when running the model for the various scenarios
it is clear that higher values of the low pressure led to enhanced performances. The
pressure ratio also remains around 2.5 for higher values of the low pressure, while it
reaches values above 3 for lower values.
Another key finding of this thesis is that low superheating is important to keep the perfor­
mance of the cycle at high levels. This can be explained by the fact that a smaller enthalpy
difference is required for a given entropy difference, thus minimizing the required work in
the compressor. Therefore, the cycle of the heat pump must be as close to the saturation
curve as possible, without any state point entering it of course.
Overall, higher low pressures and lower superheating result in higher COPs for the heat
pump cycle. Nevertheless, in the case of spray drying the effect of the low pressure is
less prevalent since the temperature lift between source and sink is great and a change in
the low pressure impacts less the total pressure lift, which is correlated to the temperature
lift. In this case, the effect of superheating takes over and affects the COP greatly. The
opposite is true in the autoclave case where the temperature lift is medium and the low
pressure effect is more prevalent.
It was expected before the model simulations were carried out that higher isentropic ef­
ficiencies of the expander and the compressor and of the effectiveness of the internal
heat exchanger would lead to a better performance of the cycle. All the scenarios that
are examined verify this hypothesis. Indeed, the COP of the cycle increases and the net
work required decreases for both the spray drying and the autoclave case. The Carnot
efficiency and the Lorenz efficiency also increase. It is clear that the replacement of the
throttling valve with an expander greatly improves the cycle performance.
Even though the internal heat exchanger clearly increases the COP of the cycle and helps
broaden the operating range, when its effectiveness approaches ηihx = 0.7, the cycle
struggles to provide valid results because the pinch point temperature difference gets
violated. Therefore, the effectiveness of ηihx = 0.6 is selected for further investigations as
it provides good performance and valid results for a far wider range.
When the spray drying and the autoclave case are compared, all the performance indi­
cators, like COP and ηLor, achieved in the spray drying case are generally higher. The
temperature lift, thus also the specific enthalpy difference, in the sink is smaller compared
to that of the spray drying. Therefore, for the same heat rejected in the gas cooler, the
mass flow rate (and the volume flow rate) is higher in the case of the autoclave. This
leads to higher work extracted from the expander. However, the work required from the
compressor is also higher, thus the net work is higher. This leads to a lower COP for the
autoclave case since the heat rejected in the gas cooler is the same. VHC is also lower
for the autoclave case since the volume flow rate is higher.

The investigation of the various expander technologies in the model showed that there can
be more feasible matches for the autoclave case, which works in lower sink and source
temperatures, because the experimental expanders found in literature also operate to
relatively lower inlet temperatures and pressures. However, there can also be matches
for the spray drying case, which generally yields better performances, if it’s assumed that
the small mismatch between the inlet temperatures and inlet pressures of the experimental
expander and the cycle is of small importance. Especially, the scroll expander technology
seems to provide the best performance, while having a pressure ratio close to the one of
the model. There are commercial expanders, like those of Air Squared, that can potentially
be used for that purpose. Furthermore, piston expanders were also found to achieve
high performances and they are the most ubiquitous technology in literature. For vane
expanders, screw expanders and turboexpanders the performance is not as high, at least
based on the findings in literature so far, and it could be argued that these technologies
are not mature yet.
5.2
Constraints
For the model of the high temperature heat pump with an internal heat exchanger there
are clear limitations for the range of the low pressure. In the case of spray drying the lower
and upper limits are 3500 kPa and 4800 kPa and in the case of the autoclave those are
1500 kPa and 3100 kPa. These limits remain approximately the same for every scenario
of varying ηis,exp, ηis,comp and ηihx values.
Those limitations are imposed by the temperatures of the source and the sink, as well as
the existence of the internal heat exchanger. Lower or higher pressures are not achiev­
able due to the fact that the pinch point difference in the internal heat exchanger is violated,
especially when the cycle operates with lower superheating values. Also, pressures out­
side these limits are impossible due to the inclination of the compressor and expander
curves. When the expander and compressor efficiencies are fixed, then the specific en­
tropy inclinations in the logp­h diagram are also fixed, meaning that there is a predefined
range within which the low pressure can move before the compressor raises the pressure
to the high level imposed by the sink side temperatures.
As far as superheating is concerned, the cycle is run for values higher than 5.5 °C to avoid
state points entering the saturation curve. It is observed that for higher low pressures the
superheating range is constrained and can reach lower operating values. When ηihx is
increased, the cycle runs for greater minimum and maximum superheating values. The
constraints above are imposed by the pinch point temperature difference of the internal
heat exchanger.
Regarding the expander technologies, the experimental expanders found in literature
were scarce for the needed conditions of the supercritical CO2 cycle and the commer­
cial expanders were mostly not fit for CO2. From those commercial expanders that it
was declared explicitly that they can handle CO2, important data was missing because
the manufacturers didn’t disclose all the necessary information. In general, many ex­
panders had some parameters that matched with the model and some that didn’t, thus
no expander could fit perfectly. High temperature supercritical CO2 heat pumps are still a
very new concept and the market has not yet extensively developed expanders for these
applications.
5.3
Future work
This thesis was of course limited in time and not every possible aspect was investigated.
So, further studies are needed in the future to delve deeper into the possibilities of the

6
Conclusions
High temperature heat pumps working with supercritical CO2 can be employed for indus­
trial applications like the spray dryer and the autoclave that operate with large temperature
glides. Using an expander instead of a throttling valve to contribute to the work required
to compress the working fluid results in an enhanced performance of the cycle.
In this thesis, conducted in collaboration with the Danish Technological Institute (DTI),
an EES model of a reverse Brayton cycle was modified and optimized for the cases of
the spray dryer and the autoclave. It was found that the highest COPs occured for the
highest possible values of the low pressure of the cycle and for the lowest possible values
of superheating. Multiple scenarios were tested and it was determined that the cycle yields
a COP increase of 67.7%, reaching 2.23, for the case of the spray dryer and an increase
of 72.3%, reaching 2.05, for the case of the autoclave, when an ideal expander replaces
the throttling valve.
The increase of the isentropic efficiency of the expander led to the decrease of the pres­
sure ratio and the net work consumed in the cycle, thus the increase of the COP. The
increase of the isentropic efficiency of the compressor and the effectiveness of the in­
ternal heat exchanger also contributed to the improvement of the COP. Expectedly, the
specific entropy inclinations in the logp­h diagram, the sink and source temperatures and
the pinch point temperature difference imposed limitations to the operation of the cycle
expressed by the low pressure range and the superheating range, but the achieved per­
formance was nonetheless satisfactory.
There are many different expander technologies (rotary vane, screw, scroll, piston, tur­
boexpander) but few can operate properly in the supercritical CO2 reverse Brayton cycle.
The findings in the literature and in the market were scarce due to the relatively new con­
cept of expanders implemented in high temperature heat pumps and many of them are
not designed to use CO2 as working fluid. Nevertheless, the scroll expander with an isen­
tropic efficiency of 83% can achieve a COP of 2.039 and 1.799 for the spray dryer and
autoclave case respectively, while also maintaining a proper pressure ratio of 2.2. The
commercial semi­hermetic scroll expanders of Air Squared can employ CO2 and they can
match the cycle as far as power output is concerned if multiple expanders are used in
parallel, however there is a mismatch for some parameters.

High temperature supercritical CO2 heat pumps are ideal for process heating supply (100 °C to
300 °C) for industrial applications with large temperature glides. Taking advantage of the great
pressure difference of the cycle, it makes sense to use expanders instead of throttling valves to
recover expansion power and enhance the cycle performance.
This thesis reviewed and compared the available expander technologies (rotary vane, screw, scroll,
piston, turboexpander) in the literature and in the market. An EES model of a reverse Brayton cycle
was modified and optimized and it was found that the cycle yields optimal results when the low
pressure receives its highest possible value and the superheating receives its lowest possible value.
The spray dryer and the autoclave were the industrial applications that were simulated. An ideal
expander replacing a throttling valve yielded a 67.7% and a 72.3% increase in the cycle’s COP,
which rose to 2.23 and 2.05 respectively. The scroll expander (isentropic efficiency of 83%) could
match the modeled cycle and exhibited the best performance yielding a COP of 2.039 and 1.799
respectively. A commercial semi­hermetic scroll expander that could potentially be used for the
supercritical CO2 cycle was found.
