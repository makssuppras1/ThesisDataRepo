Abstract
The moving surface of a speaker, as any membrane-like mechanical system, can
present a modal behaviour around speciﬁc frequencies.
These resonances aﬀect the
acoustic output and directivity of a speaker, due to phase cancellation between the dif-
ferent areas of the diaphragm, while being almost invisible on impedance measurements,
who are usually used to characterise a speaker. This study focuses on extracting the me-
chanical part of the loudspeaker impedance, where the modal behaviour appears much
more clearly. The resonances appearing on this derived impedance are compared with
the mechanical behaviour of the loudspeaker diaphragm at the corresponding frequen-
cies, which is observed through a velocity scan over the whole surface. The validity of a
previously developed lumped element model including this modal behaviour is discussed
in regard to the results of the extraction and scans.

Introduction
Up to a certain frequency, the moving parts of a loudspeaker behave as a ﬂat piston:
the vibration of the coil, cone, dust cap and inner part of the surround have the same
amplitude and phase. However, at higher frequencies, several mechanical phenomena
induce a more complex behaviour for these elements. The general trend is a shrinking
of the moving surface. But this variation is not steady: the diﬀerent areas of the di-
aphragm go through resonances and anti-resonances in the process, and can get out of
phase from each other. This surface diﬀerentiation almost always follows a rotational
symmetry, which allows to consider the system to be one-dimensional.
Indeed, the surface of the driver can be in that regard seen as a one-dimensional me-
chanical transmission line: the coil, close to the centre of the area, induces movement
one one extremity of the cone. That vibration propagates outwards along the cone,
to the surround, which is in turn glued to the basket on its outer extremity.
This
closed-end one dimensional mechanical system can induces standing waves at certain
frequencies, which are the cause of these variations in phase and amplitude. This kind
of phenomenon greatly inﬂuences the acoustic output, because of sudden changes in the
moving area or of phase cancellation.
An other goal of this study is to relate these modes to the impedance of the speaker,
and study them through this data. An impedance measurement is the easiest and most
common way to characterise a speaker, and is the basis of most loudspeaker models.
However, the observation of mechanical modes through the driver impedance is a com-
plicated process. The impedance of a loudspeaker in free air can be separated into two
components, the electrical and mechanical impedances, which characterise the behaviour
of the speaker in those respective domains and depend on its electromagnetic and me-
chanical properties respectively. In high frequency, the electrical impedance completely
dominates the mechanical one. Therefore, the mechanical modes that occur in this fre-
quency range and are visible on the mechanical impedance are masked on the curve of
the total driver impedance by the electrical component. A major step of our study will
therefore be to ﬁnd a precise and repeatable way to extract the mechanical part of the
impedance from impedance measurements.

1.1.1
Analogies and basic model
The main challenge when studying the behaviour of loudspeakers is that it is determined
by the interaction between electric, magnetic, mechanic and acoustic phenomena. The
main method used to get rid of this issue is the use of equivalent electric circuits. Indeed,
the fundamental equations of mechanics and acoustics can be identiﬁed to the equations
of simple electric circuits if we identify parameters like pressure, velocity or force to
voltage and current.
We will in this study mainly take interest in the mechanical system. The cone is put
in motion by the coil, close to its centre, and is held by the surround and spider, that
act as springs, as presented on ﬁgure 1.1. This system can be assimilated, as a ﬁrst
approximation, to a simple mass-spring-damper system.
This simpliﬁed mechanical system can be represented, as it was introduced above, as
a simple RLC analogous circuit, where the current would represent the velocity of the
membrane, and the voltage represents forces applied on it. By using such analogies, the
complete driver system can be represented by the trio of interacting circuits presented
on ﬁgure 1.2, extracted from Marshall-Leach (2010). This model is the most standard
and simple representation of a loudspeaker.
In our study, however, we will not take interest in the acoustical part of the model, as all
the drivers will be studied in free air. In that case, the acoustic load on the speaker can
be simpliﬁed to an air mass that will be added to the mechanical mass of the membrane
in the mechanical circuit, making the use of the third circuit unnecessary.
This simpliﬁcation allows us to integrate the mechanical part of the model into the
electrical analogous circuit to sum up the whole model into a single circuit, by using the
mobility analogy for the mechanical part of the system. This transformation requires
to adjust the components of the mechanical impedance to respect the equations of the
system. Their respective impedances are inverted and they go from being in series to

1.1.2
Adjusted models
The model presented above therefore represents the speaker as an association of a resis-
tive inductance driving a simple second order mechanical system, and is an eﬃcient way
to identify the mechanical and electrical characteristics of the system and understand
the basic behaviour of a certain driver. However, our study will rely a lot on impedance
ﬁtting, which means adjusting the diﬀerent coeﬃcients of a certain analogous circuit to
have the resulting impedance ﬁt the measured impedance of a driver as best as possible
on a certain frequency range. This simple model does not oﬀer a satisfactory ﬁtting
for most drivers. Recent research in the ﬁeld has been focusing a lot on ﬁnding small
adjustments that could be made to both the electrical and mechanical parts of this
analogous circuit in order to achieve a better ﬁtting, by including some more complex
physical phenomena in the model.
Several models were studied in Thorborg et al. (2007), Thorborg and Unruh (2008)
and Thorborg and Futtrup (2011) for the blocked impedance. Their similarity is the
introduction of a semi-inductance, a component of impedance proportional to √jω, in
order to model the eﬀect of eddy current and skin eﬀect in the magnet system, as well
as to achieve a better impedance ﬁtting. The model that was advised in the last of these
papers as a compromise between precision and simplicity included this semi-inductance
in parallel with a resistance and inductance, as presented on ﬁgure 1.4.

1.2
Radial modes
As explained in the introduction, at low frequencies, the loudspeaker diaphragm can be
regarded as a piston. However, past this frequency range, its behaviour starts diverging
from that simpliﬁed model. A new approach was developed by K.Thorborg in an un-
published paper, which was to consider the diaphragm as a one dimensional mechanical
transmission line, formed by the cone and surround. These two elements are placed is
series and are glued to the basket on one side, while a vibration is input by the coil on
the other. This vibration propagates along the transmission line with a certain velocity,
up to the ﬁxed end, where it reﬂected. Standing waves can appear at certain frequen-
cies for this system, because of the reﬂection on the ﬁxed end. Their characteristics will
depend on the dimensions and stiﬀness of the cone and surround.
5
==PAGE:14==
These modes appear in the motional impedance as an S-shaped deviation from the -
6dB/octave asymptote in the high frequencies. The coil velocity will go through an
anti-resonance, followed by a resonance, for each of these modes. This shape is illus-
trated on ﬁgure 1.5, where the ﬁrst mode, in particular, appears very strongly on the
derived motional impedance.
The corresponding simulated motional impedance ob-
tained with the model presented on ﬁgure 1.4 is presented on the same ﬁgure. It does
not include the mechanical modes, as we can observe in the high frequencies.
Figure 1.5: Simulated and derived blocked and motional impedances of a 6.5" midrange speaker
The ﬁgure also illustrates the separation of the two parts of the impedance: for both the
blocked and motional impedances, we have the derived impedance, which is calculated
by subtracting the simulated version of the other part of the impedance to the total
measured one, that is plotted together with the simulated impedance. We can also ob-
serve the inﬂuence of the mechanical modes on the total impedance: the ﬁrst resonance
induces a small bump on the total impedance, while the rest of them are invisible, due
to both a weaker induced resonance and a larger diﬀerence between the blocked and
motional impedances in their respective frequency areas.
A tentative extended analogous circuit was proposed by K.Thorborg to include the ﬁrst
modes. Mechanically, the model was based on splitting the mass of the cone and sur-
round into several masses placed in series, which once again relies on the idea of a
mechanical transmission line from the coil, through these successive areas of the cone.
This splitting allows the diﬀerent areas of the cone to be driven diﬀerently and have dif-
ferent velocities in the model, which is the main limit of the standard lumped elements
model regarding the phenomena studied here.
6
==PAGE:15==
Figure 1.6 presents the concept of this new mechanical model: the mass of the moving
parts MMD is split into two masses M1 and M2. The ﬁrst one represents the central
area of the cone, and is directly put in motion by the coil. It is connected via a damped
spring to the other part of the mass, that represents the outer part of the cone and
surround. The latter is itself connected to the ﬁxed basket, or mechanical ground, by
the surround. The mass separation is not necessarily between the cone and surround
themselves: the location of the separation will depend on where the resonances appear,
and not on the separation between the diﬀerent elements of the diaphragm.
Figure 1.6: Mechanical transmission line of the cone and surround in the case of a two elements
mass separation
The corresponding equivalent circuit proposed by K.Thorborg to include the ﬁrst mode,
is presented on ﬁgure 1.7. As a reminder, the mass, which was modelled by an induc-
tance in the mechanical analogous circuit, therefore became the inductance CMES in
the electrical impedance model. Splitting the cone into two parts in the model therefore
means that CMES is separated into CMES0 and CMES1. The ﬁrst one represents the
inner part of the cone. It replaces CMES in the circuit, as it is the mass that is directly
put in motion by the coil, and is linked to the second through a mechanical compliance,
which on the circuit becomes the inductance LCES. Frequency dependent damping is
also added to this sub-system through ωΛES1, to model the losses.
Figure 1.7: Extended equivalent circuit including the ﬁrst resonance
7
==PAGE:16==
This model, however, does not correspond exactly to the transposition of the mechanical
system of ﬁgure 1.6. To be physically correct, LCES and ωΛES1 should be placed in
parallel, and an other set of spring and damper should be placed in parallel to CMES
to model the inﬂuence of the surround on the outer mass. However, simulations carried
by K.Thorborg revealed that the behaviour of the model presented is almost identical
to the more complicated, correct model, and presents a major advantage: the changes
made on the circuit to include the ﬁrst resonance are reduced to one branch of the
circuit, whose resonance frequency can easily be calculated from the values of LCES and
CMES.
Moreover, the simpliﬁcations allow this model to be easily be expanded to include the
following resonances, by adding new identical branches for each mode we want to model.
Each new added mass must be subtracted to CMES0, for the total mass of the system to
stay constant, and the main resonance of the system to stay unchanged on the simulated
motional impedance.

Chapter 2
Fitting algorithm
2.1
Goal
A Matlab algorithm was designed to adjust the values of the diﬀerent parameters of a
given analogous circuit based on the impedance of a speaker. The goal was to use one
of the models presented in part 1, and numerically adjust the values of the electrical
components of the model to have the simulated impedance ﬁt the measured impedance
curve as best as possible.
There are two main goals for this process.
• The ﬁrst is to extract the mechanical part of the speaker impedance. Our goal is to
work only from the total measured impedance, but the inﬂuence of the mechanical
modes is only visible on the mechanical part.
Achieving a good ﬁtting of the
impedance curve using those models allows us to separate the blocked and motional
parts of our simulated impedance. If the ﬁtting of the total impedance is good
enough, we can assume this separation to be close to reality. This means that by
subtracting the simulated blocked impedance to the measured total impedance, we
can get a good approximation of the speaker’s motional impedance. This process
will be key when it comes to understanding the results of our measurements. This
makes the blocked impedance the most critical for our adjustment, as the extracted
motional impedance depends on it.
• The second goal is of course to validate the models we are using, and especially
the part related to the mechanical modes, on diﬀerent sets of data by achieving a
good ﬁtting on the three parts of the impedance: blocked, motional and modal.
2.2
Principle
The parameters are subdivided into groups that can be adjusted separately because of
their inﬂuence on diﬀerent frequency ranges of the impedance. This separation is needed
9
==PAGE:18==
because of the large number of elements to adjust: each new parameter that is adjusted
at once adds a dimension to the optimisation problem, and past a certain number the
function is not eﬃcient. First, RE is adjusted alone, as it is the only parameter inﬂuenc-
ing the impedance at the very low frequencies. Then the rest of the blocked impedance
part is adjusted at once, as it shapes the impedance in the high frequencies.
Then
the motional part of the circuit is adjusted, as it inﬂuences the behaviour around the
main resonance of the speaker. Finally, the modal parameters are adjusted branch by
branch to ﬁt the high frequency resonances visible on the extracted motional impedance.
For each of those steps, the adjustment is realised through three functions.
• The ﬁrst one calculates the blocked, motional and total impedances given by the
model we are working on for a certain set of parameters.
• The second one is the error function. It calculates the diﬀerence between the sim-
ulated curve and the measured one by integrating the relative diﬀerence between
the two over the frequency range of interest, as presented on equation 2.1.

• The last function uses a matlab optimisation functions to minimise this error by

impedance is to be used: we can try to match the total simulated impedance to the

2.3
Steps
2.3.1
Blocked impedance and regular part of the motional impedance
As explained in section 2.2, the parameters are separated into 4 diﬀerent groups that
are adjusted separately. However, each of these groups can not be adjusted only once,
10
==PAGE:19==
one after the other. For each adjustment, the impedance is calculated using either the
previously adjusted values of the other parameters, or the Klippel coeﬃcients for those
that have not been adjusted yet. And even if those diﬀerent groups mostly inﬂuence
diﬀerent frequency ranges, the modiﬁcation of one group of parameters can still inﬂu-
ence the result of the ﬁtting for another group, which explains the need for an iterative
process.
The ﬁrst parameter to be adjusted is RE, because of its independence. Because it is the
one and only parameter to inﬂuence the very low frequencies, it can be adjusted at the
very beginning and not be touched again.
The second set of parameters is the rest of the blocked impedance. Indeed, the high
frequencies is the range where the Klippel ﬁtting is usually the worst, because of the
inadequacy of the model used. The changes in the simulated impedance induced by the
ﬁrst adjustment of this group of parameters are massive, which is why it is realised as
early as possible. These ﬁrst two steps will be achieved by ﬁtting the total simulated
impedance to the total measured one.
We then take interest in the motional impedance. The 4 main parameters are adjusted,
followed by the branch corresponding to the ﬁrst mode. Both these steps are realised by
ﬁtting the simulated motional impedance to the derived motional impedance, which is
obtained by subtracting the previously simulated blocked impedance to the total mea-
sured impedance.
Once this ﬁrst set of adjustments is realised, a new iteration is conducted.
Indeed,
the main parts of the blocked and motional impedance interact together around the
main resonance, where they successively take over in their contribution to the total
impedance. The existence of those grey areas where both parts are about equally im-
portant mean that changing one will inﬂuence the result of our ﬁtting for the other.
Only the adjustment of RE and of the modes past the ﬁrst one can be realised inde-
pendently because the former inﬂuences a very distinct frequency range and the latter
only inﬂuences the motional impedance in the high frequencies, where its contribution
to the total impedance is invisible, therefore not inﬂuencing the ﬁtting of the blocked
impedance in this area.
Therefore, a second adjustment of the blocked impedance (RE excluded) and of the
regular part of the motional impedance are conducted, in the same order. The eﬀect of
this ﬁrst series of adjustments is presented on ﬁgure 2.1.

but by extracting the imaginary part of the derived motional impedance and deducing
from it the inﬂuence of CMES, in order to isolate LCES. λ is deduced from this curve
thanks to its deﬁning equation 1.2.

rived motional impedance tended, on most of the speakers studied, to drop in the high

• First, a phase lower than −90◦means that the real part of the motional impedance

• Secondly, the model we use, because of its physical adequacy, can not go below
this value, and therefore the ﬁtting of the modes becomes impossible, due to the
phase of the simulated and derived motional impedances being too far apart.

of the derived and simulated motional impedances. We can clearly see the phase drop
in the high frequencies of the derived impedance, together with an unexpected raise of
the magnitude.
To tackle this issue, a new adjustment of the blocked impedance was realised, with
diﬀerent criteria and limiting conditions.
2.3.4
New adjustment of the blocked impedance with phase condition
A new approach had to be developed for this last adjustment of the blocked impedance
parameters. Indeed, the ﬁtting based the total impedance in the high frequencies was
usually giving great results when looking at the total impedance, but the extraction of

the motional impedance was still not precise enough above a few kilohertz, particularly
on the phase.
Therefore, this new adjustment would be realised through the derived motional impedance.
In that purpose, the simulated motional impedance obtained after the last step is
stored and use as a goal for this new adjustment. Instead of ﬁtting the total simu-
lated impedance on the total measured impedance, as was done in the other adjustment
of the blocked impedance, the derived motional impedance is directly calculated during
the process and ﬁtted on the previously simulated motional impedance.
The goal of this approach is to bring the phase back up to coherent values in the high
frequencies. The motional impedance simulated in the previous part of the process was
usually ﬁtting very well up to the ﬁrst mode, around one or two kilohertz, and can
therefore be considered a better approximation of the real motional impedance of the
speaker above these frequencies than the derived motional impedance, whose behaviour
is not physically correct.
However, if the results were better after this new adjustment, this change of paradigm
was not suﬃcient: the parameters were, in most cases, almost unchanged on this step.
Therefore, a more strict condition was added in the ﬁtting function: the phase of the

==PAGE:23==
derived motional impedance has to stay above a set value φmin up to a set frequency
flim. Those two parameters can be adjusted depending on the frequency range of the
modes studied and on how eﬀective the ﬁtting is on a particular driver. Implementing
this condition for the whole frequency range was usually too harsh of a condition and led
to poor results, which is why the limit had to be adapted to each case. flim only needs
to be higher than the resonances we are studying, in the limits of good functioning.
2.3.5
Last steps
Once this ﬁnal main adjustment is realised, the whole motional impedance is adjusted
once again to account for the changes in the derived motional impedance induced by
the previous change.

4.2.2
Observation of the ﬁrst resonance
To understand what happens mechanically in the membrane around this ﬁrst resonance,
the results of the scan for both these speakers is presented on ﬁgures 4.8 and 4.9, from
500 to 1200Hz, every 100Hz. The reading of these ﬁgures was explained in part 3.3.
The behaviour that we observe on the scan around this ﬁrst resonance is very similar
in both cases, with only a small shift in frequency. It represents, with small variations,
what will be observed around this ﬁrst mode for most loudspeakers.
A few hundred hertz before the resonance, we can still observe the piston mode, with
a moving surface that includes all the dust cap, cone, and most of the surround. All
the elements are moving with similar amplitudes and phases in that case. When getting
close to the resonance frequency, the amplitude of the central part will quickly decrease,
while only a small ring, that is here situated around the connection between the cone
and surround, will keep a relatively constant amplitude of displacement. The position
of this resonating ring relatively to the diﬀerent elements can vary from driver to driver
and will be discussed in part 5.2. However, the phase will stay constant on the moving
surface until the minimum displacement is almost reached for the central part. That
point also corresponds to the minimum on the motional impedance, and happens around
900Hz for the M1 speaker, and 800Hz for the S1.
Once that point is about to be reached, a decoupling of the centre and outer parts starts
occurring. Two distinctly separated areas will start to move out of phase. The limit
between the two is a circle of slightly smaller radius than the maximal amplitude ring.
The phase shift between the two areas will increase to reach 180◦around 1100Hz in the
case of the M1 driver, which is close to the frequency of the maximum in the motional
impedance.
That phase diﬀerentiation coincides with an increase of the displacement of the centre
part again. In this area, the amplitude of the displacement will increase uniformly until
it reaches the amplitude of the outer circle. However, a small ring between the two will
stay at low displacement. This ring corresponds to the position of the phase inversion.
As the inner and outer parts are now out of phase by 180◦, this small area between the
two will have almost no displacement.
The S1 speaker displays the same behaviour, with only a small shift in frequency, that
was also observable on the motional impedance. Moreover, the diﬀerence in amplitude
at the resonance between the central area and the ring of maximum displacement is
lesser than in the ﬁrst case. This can be related to the motional impedance as well,
where the resonance is less sharp for the S1 driver.

Chapter 5
Discussion
5.1
Success of the algorithm
The development of the ﬁtting algorithm had several goals. The ﬁrst was to develop
a tool allowing the numerical adjustment of the parameters of a certain loudspeaker
equivalent circuit by curve ﬁtting. The main tool currently used in that purpose is the
Klippel system, whose main defect is its lack of adaptability to model variation. There-
fore, the aim was to develop a tool that could be tweaked to work with commonly used
loudspeaker models, and who was just as automatic and autonomous as the Klippel
system.
In that regard, the algorithm was a success. The ﬁtting of the total impedance was very
precise on the whole frequency range on all the examples studied and the ﬁtting process
was made completely automated and adaptive to model variations.
The second goal was a precise extraction of the motional impedance. The success of
this extraction can be judged on each example by the frequency until which the derived
motional impedance considered correct. This frequency can be deduced by looking both
at the magnitude and phase of this derived motional impedance. The magnitude usu-
ally follows a -6 dB/octave asymptote, from which small deviations are observed due to
the mechanical resonances. But after a certain frequency, the magnitude would start
increasing due to this lack of precision. On the phase of the derived impedance, the
frequency limit could simply be extracted by looking where the curve would drop below
the physical limit of −90◦.
On most examples, this frequency limit was situated between 2 and 5kHz. That was
enough to always observe and study the ﬁrst resonance, but the extraction of the fol-
lowing modes depended on their frequency and on the eﬃciency of the extraction on
that particular set of data.
The third goal was the validation of the model including mechanical modes. In that
regard, the limitations did not come from the ﬁtting of the resonances itself or from the
44
==PAGE:53==
model, but from the extraction of the motional impedance. The resonances that were
correctly extracted on both the magnitude and phase were correctly ﬁtted. However,
the drop in phase in the derived motional impedance was happening quite early on most
cases and prevented a correct ﬁtting. However, the results allow to validate the model
in terms of impedance ﬁtting for the ﬁrst mode. The general validation of the model
will be discussed in section 5.2.
5.2
Validity of the model
5.2.1
Splitting of the moving mass
The model used for the resonances was based on a splitting of the mechanical mass of
the system into several masses linked by compliances. Which physically means than in-
stead of having a uniform behaviour, as in the piston range, the surface of the membrane
is split in diﬀerent circular areas which will be driven and behave diﬀerently. The link
between this model and what was observed on the scans will be discussed in this section.
The ﬁrst observation is that the scans did reveal a separation between several circular
surface areas of the membrane in terms of behaviour. Each resonance corresponds to
a new ring whose movement starts to diﬀer from the central area, both in terms of
amplitude and phase. The phase decoupling was the easiest to identify: around the
resonance frequency, the inner and outer parts start to get slowly out of phase until
they reach a complete phase opposition.
Higher order modes will present the same
behaviour, with break-ups getting successively closer to the centre. Which means after
the ﬁrst few resonances, the outer part of the membrane will present a succession of
small rings in phase opposition. The variations in amplitude are slightly more complex,
and are described in section 4.2.2. But even though the behaviour is less uniform than
for the phase when looking at the outer part of the surface, with the apparition of a
thinner resonating ring and the superposition of the modal behaviour with the general
shrinking of the moving surface, the idea of a mass splitting is still very much applicable
to the observable phenomena.

5.3
Limits of the model
The principle of the model applies to the transmission line formed by the cone and
surround. However, two elements of the moving parts of a speaker are not included and
might also be subject to break-ups: the dust cap and the spider. The case of those two
elements, how they diﬀer from the previously studied elements and how their mechanical
behaviour could be studied or included in a model will be discussed here.
The dust cap is the most problematic because of the fact that it is part of the actual
surface of the driver and therefore has a much larger inﬂuence on the acoustic output.
The reason it might have to be considered diﬀerently than the rest of the surface is the
mechanical assembling of the system: while the coil, cone and surround are mechanically
place in series, by being glued in succession along the mechanical transmission line, the
dust cap is not placed between two of those elements, but in parallel to the cone.
The spider, on the other hand, is placed in parallel of the whole system. Its mechanical
behaviour was never intended to be part of the model.
However, its inﬂuence was
sometimes visible on the motional impedance. For example, a spider mode is visible as
a minor resonance around 600Hz on the derived motional impedance of the 6.5" speaker
with stiﬀsurround, visible on ﬁgure 4.13. It can be seen on this ﬁgure that the ﬁtting can
be inﬂuenced by this resonance. Moreover, the identiﬁcation of a spider mode was easy
in that particular case because it appears way before the ﬁrst membrane resonance, in a
frequency range where the scan was showing a piston-like behaviour. However, through
the diﬀerent examples studied, several resonances visible on the motional impedance
could not be identiﬁed to any phenomenon visible on the scans, and could thus be spider
modes. When those resonances appear higher in frequency, in the usual frequency range
of the second or third membrane mode, it is much harder to identify it as a spider mode,
and the analysis of the mechanical behaviour of the loudspeaker becomes much harder.
The identiﬁcation of spider resonances, as well as their inclusion in the current analogous
circuit, would complement greatly and be facilitated by this study. Indeed, those two
categories of resonances can overlap and are based on similar mechanical phenomena,
but their inclusion in the mechanical system should be diﬀerent because of the layout
of the mechanical system. The link also comes from the fact that the spider resonances
appears in the motional impedance, similarly to the membrane resonances. The main
48
==PAGE:57==
ones are usually situated below a few kilohertz, where the motional impedance extraction
developed here is eﬃcient, making this algorithm a great tool for their study. Finally,
the scans realised here allowed us to clearly reveal which resonances were appearing on
the surface of the speaker, allowing to identify the resonances that were visible on the
motional impedance but not on the scans as potential spider modes.
5.4
Future use of the algorithm and model
As it was explained in section 5.1, the main goal of the algorithm was to develop an
eﬃcient curve ﬁtting tool that was adaptable to model changes. In this aspect the goal
was achieved, which means that the tool could be used for other studies. The validation
of new models, that focus on other aspects of the loudspeaker behaviour than what was
studied here could make use of the ﬁtting process. The ﬁrst aspect that comes to mind
is the study of spider resonances, as it was hinted in the previous section. More gener-
ally, the separation of the blocked and motional parts of the impedance of a speaker are
relevant to many of the current research topics.
The second use of the algorithm is the numerical information it provides on the reso-
nances when the ﬁtting is correct. This was largely discussed in section 5.2, but could be
pushed further. The scans that were conducted here allowed to study the link between
the numerical mass separation in the model and the location of the break-up for the
ﬁrst resonance. This means that an approximate localisation of the ﬁrst break-up can
be deduced from the analysis of the impedance only on any driver. This information is
extremely valuable, to understand the mechanical behaviour of the driver but also to
anticipate the eﬀect of this break-up on the driver response at diﬀerent distances and
angles.
Moreover, some tendencies were revealed about the inﬂuence of the materials and ge-
ometry of the drivers on the location and frequency of the resonances. But the fact that
the ﬁtting was made automatic could allow a more industrial and numerical study on
the link between the mechanical properties of the membrane and the characteristics of
the resonances (frequency, sharpness, location). The ﬁnal goal would be to link numer-
ically the mechanical characteristics of the cone and surround to the properties of the
resonances, to anticipate and limit their inﬂuence on the speaker response.

Conclusion
The main goal of this study was to study the mechanical resonances of loudspeaker
diaphragms and test the physical adequacy, ﬁtting capabilities and use of the lumped
parameters model proposed by K.Thorborg to include the inﬂuence of these resonances.
The aim of this model was to relate the mechanical behaviour of the diaphragm to
the driver impedance. For that, a tool had to be developed to extract the motional
impedance from the total measured impedance, and more generally to adjust the pa-
rameters of a certain model through curve ﬁtting on the impedance.
The ﬁtting process had a frequency limitation due to the extreme level diﬀerence be-
tween the total and motional impedances in high frequencies, increasing greatly the
precision of the ﬁtting required for a proper extraction. That limitation only allowed us
to thoroughly study the ﬁrst and main resonance of the diaphragm on all examples. The
ﬁtting of the modes was good when the extraction allowed it, and some links could be
observed between the separation of masses induced by the model and the localisation of
the resonances observed on the scans. However a thorough numerical study of the model
adequacy could not be conducted, due to the diﬃculty of separating the contributions
of the diﬀerent elements to the total mass.
The scans also revealed that the amplitude and phase variations around the resonances
are always the same for resonances situated on the cone or surround. Therefore, the
general mechanical behaviour can be deduced from the impedance, thanks to these ob-
servations and to the information on the resonances localisation provided by the ﬁtting.
However, such information can only be extracted if the cone and surround resonances
can be separated from the spider and dust cap resonances.
Future work should therefore focus on two main points. The ﬁrst is the identiﬁcation
and inclusion in the model of spider resonances, who can not be considered in the same
way than the diaphragm resonances due to the spider being mechanically placed in par-
allel to the rest of the system. Indeed, these resonances are in many cases situated in
the same frequency range than diaphragm resonances, making their study complemen-
tary. The second focus point would be a more numerical study of the accuracy of the
mass separation provided by the model, which would require destructive measurements
on speakers, but would provide very reliable and precise information on the resonances
localisation if the model is found to be correct.
