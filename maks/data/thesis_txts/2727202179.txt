Abstract
Manual annotation of objects in images is a crucial part of creating computer vision
datasets. The process, however, is tedious and time-consuming. Tools such as inter-
active segmentation models try to address this problem by allowing users to annotate
objects in images by clicking on them. Despite these efforts, there remains a demand
for more eﬀicient and effective annotation methods. This thesis proposes a frame-
work for suggesting clicking locations to users when using an interactive segmentation
model. By reducing the time spent examining objects and avoiding suboptimal click
locations, the proposed framework increases annotation eﬀiciency. For the creation of
this framework, we propose a pipeline composed of a reinforcement learning agent and
an interactive environment. The purpose of the pipeline is to simulate the iterative
process of generating object masks through agent-guided clicks. Experiments were
conducted in two distinct scenarios: one involving a lack of initial information and
the other where a single initial click was provided. Performance evaluation employed
the Intersection over Union (IoU) metric, measuring the similarity between generated
and ground truth masks. In the first scenario, the model produced by the pipeline
surpassed the random baseline by 22.8%, and by 4% in the second scenario. When the
model was restricted to the most important regions in images, it also outperformed a
baseline that simulated human annotator behavior in both scenarios. These results
are promising and underscore that the proposed framework has the potential to ex-
pedite dataset creation, enhance annotation quality, and ultimately advance research
in the field.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Image annotation plays a pivotal role in various computer vision applications, includ-
ing object recognition, image retrieval, and scene understanding. The availability of
annotated datasets is essential for advancing research in these fields. However, the
manual creation of annotated datasets is a laborious and error-prone process, imped-
ing the progress of new discoveries. Furthermore, human subjectivity can introduce
inconsistencies in the annotations, creating a need for tools that can automate and
enhance the eﬀiciency of the annotation process, leading to increased speed, accuracy,
and cost-effectiveness.
Addressing the limitations of human labor in annotation tasks, various interactive
tools have been developed to assist annotators. Using these tools, annotators can
simply click on the objects they want to annotate. While using these methods provides
a boost in eﬀiciency, there is still a need to go beyond what is possible today and
make annotation even faster and more accurate.
To maximize eﬀiciency when using these tools, reinforcement learning emerges
as a promising technique, through which an agent can learn from its environment
via trial-and-error interactions. This thesis explores the application of reinforcement
learning to streamline the process of image annotation. We propose the development
of a pipeline that trains an agent to provide real-time suggestions to users while they
utilize interactive segmentation tools. The primary objective is to reduce annotation
time and minimize unnecessary decision-making, allowing annotators to focus on
critical aspects of the annotation process.
This thesis delves into the intricate details of building the proposed framework.
We explore the various aspects of training a reinforcement learning agent to optimize
image annotation, encompassing the model’s development and the design of an in-
teractive environment through which the model is trained. The resulting pipeline
serves as a crucial tool for enhancing annotation processes, bridging the gap between
manual annotation and automated assistance, ultimately enhancing the annotation
process.
Through rigorous experimentation and meticulous evaluations, we investigate the
performance and capabilities of the developed pipeline. The insights gained shed light
on the effectiveness of reinforcement learning in guiding user annotation, leading to
improved outcomes in terms of accuracy and eﬀiciency. Additionally, we thoroughly
discuss the limitations of the framework and present potential directions for future
research, aiming to refine and expand its capabilities.

2.1
Convolutional neural networks
Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) are networks that were developed and de-
signed specifically for processing two-dimensional data such as images and video.
They are a crucial tool in the realm of computer vision and have been used exten-
sively for a wide variety of tasks and have led to significant advances [25] [20]. CNNs
are composed of hierarchically structured interconnected neurons for processing data,
where each layer is responsible for capturing more abstract features than the last. In
the first layer, basic features such as edges are detected, while more complex and less
concrete patterns are captured in the later layers, such as textures or shapes.

2.1.1
Convolutional layers
CNNs use a process called convolution to learn and extract the relevant features
without needing additional programming overhead. Convolution allows for filters to
be applied to overlapping regions of a given image, thus producing a feature map that
focuses on particular features. The way convolutions work is the following: First, a
matrix with a set of learnable parameters is defined. This matrix is called a kernel,
and it is spatially smaller than the image it will be applied to.
When a forward
pass happens, the kernel is applied in the top left corner of the image and produces
the dot product between the image and the kernel. Then, the kernel slides across
the image and is applied in every step it takes. The length of the kernel’s step is
called stride. After being applied across the entire length and height of the image,
a two-dimensional representation of the image is produced called an activation map.
Because the kernel is comparatively smaller than the image, fewer parameters need
to be stored. Therefore, it is much less memory intensive than having to store the
thousands of pixels that a typical image might be composed of. Oftentimes, after the
convolution, an activation layer is introduced to add non-linearity to the network.
Figure 2.2: Application of the convolution kernel
2.1.2
Pooling layers
Afterward, dimensionality reduction is applied via the usage of pooling layers to
allow for higher eﬀiciency [45].
There are several functions that can be used to
achieve this. The more typical ones are average pooling and max pooling. In max
pooling, the maximum out of a neighborhood of pixels is kept. In average pooling,
the neighborhood of pixels is replaced by their average.

In addition to these core layers, CNNs can also incorporate other types of layers,
such as normalization layers and dropout layers, to improve their performance and
robustness [41] [14]. Finally, the last layers of CNNs are typically fully connected
layers, similar to those found in typical neural networks, used for tasks such as clas-
sification. This entire process allows these final fully connected layers to be applied
only to the most relevant features for the task at hand.
2.1.3
Residual networks (ResNets)
Residual networks are deep neural networks that leverage convolutional layers and
were developed to address problems that arose in other deep neural networks[11] [8]
[36] [30]. Specifically, ResNets deal with the problem of degradation, which occurs
when increasing the depth of neural networks and causes performance drops. The
main idea behind ResNets is the utilization of skip connections, which can allow the
network to learn residual mappings. By the term residual mappings, we are referring
to the difference between a desired output and the currently produced output. Skip
connections on the other hand are simply shortcuts that allow connections to bypass
layers and thus connect the inputs and outputs of a block. What this means is, that
instead of approximating an underlying mapping H(x) where x is the input, we can
instead approximate the residual function F(x) = H(x) −x. The original function
can then be represented as F(x) + x. Both forms should be able to asymptotically
approximate the desired functions, but the authors show that it is easier to optimize
the residual mapping than to optimize the original. This allows for easier flow of in-
formation to deeper layers. This strategy allowed for sharp increases in the depth of
ResNets over plain networks, allowing them to better capture complex data. There-
fore, ResNets have been a staple in computer vision and remain a popular choice as
image encoders.

2.2
Reinforcement learning
Reinforcement learning (RL) is a method where an agent is trained to make decisions
in a given environment to achieve a specific goal [42]. When using RL, the agent
attempts to explore the possible actions it can take in an environment and find the
best ones. It follows a process of trial and error to eventually create a policy on what
its actions should be based on the state of the environment. To encourage the agent to
take the actions that lead to a desirable outcome, the agent is typically rewarded when
making an action that leads them closer to the overall goal. The agent is otherwise
punished for taking actions that are not leading to the desired outcome. Over time,
the idea is that the agent will figure out which actions lead to higher rewards. It will
therefore adjust its policy to follow those, while avoiding actions that lead to lesser
rewards or punishments. This approach aims to ultimately craft an optimal policy
for navigating the environment.

ronment through its actions based on the states and rewards it receives from the
environment.

rewards or punishments based on the actions taken by the agent.
• State (S): the state is a snapshot of the environment with which the agent will
interact in each of its steps. Based on the state that is given by the environment
to the agent and the agent’s chosen action, the environment will change and
produce a new state for the agent.
• Action (A): The decision made by the agent based on the current state of the
environment and the reward it receives. The action will lead to a new state
based on how the environment reacts to it.
• Reward (R): After the agent interacts with the state of the environment by
choosing an action, feedback is given to the agent. This feedback can be positive
or negative and represents the reward the agent achieved by choosing the action
it did.
• Policy (π): The policy is a map connecting states and actions. Based on that
map, when the agent comes across a given state, it will in turn look at this map
to see which action corresponds to this state. The policy changes based on the
agent’s rewards to better map rewarding actions to states.
The choice of reward structure specifically is a vital part of the way RL algorithms
work [39]. The rewards and punishments need to be carefully designed to provide the
agent with the correct incentives [12]. If, for example, the rewards are too sparse, the
agent might struggle to learn due to a lack of feedback. If, on the other hand, they
are too dense and specific, the agent might struggle to generalize.
We can look at an example to describe the concepts stated above. An example of
an RL problem could be that of a robot traversing a maze. In this case, the maze is
the environment, and the goal is reaching the end. Let’s assume that the actions the
robot can do are moving forward, left, right, and backward. In the first few attempts,
the robot will not make it far into the maze before getting stuck, and thus receive a
low reward. As it is exploring, it will inevitably take some correct choices by chance
and make it further. In these attempts, it will receive a comparatively larger reward
than before. Then, its policy will update, and it will learn to avoid the choices that
lead to dead ends. Eventually, by this process of gradually finding better paths while
also following the ones that are tried and true, it will reach the end. As mentioned
before, the reward structure is important. Let’s assume that the agent is rewarded
based on reaching checkpoints in the maze. If the checkpoints are too far apart, the
agent will have trouble learning how to reach from one to the other [1]. In conclusion,
it is crucial to craft a reward structure that allows the agent to learn eﬀiciently.

Policy and value function
The policy π is the way the agent maps optimal actions given a state so that it

2.2.2
Exploration and exploitation
Another important concept to understand relating to reinforcement learning is the
exploration–exploitation problem. [42] [27]
==PAGE:19==
2.2 Reinforcement learning
9
• Exploration is when the agent is trying out actions whose outcomes are un-
certain. Thus, they might bring about better results than the current policy of
the agent, or they may be worse than the results that could be obtained if the
agent simply followed its policy to pick actions.
• Exploitation is when the agent follows its policy and chooses actions that
already knows will lead to favorable outcomes.
If the agent starts “exploiting” its policy very early on, it will not have explored
enough and may end up using a suboptimal policy that does not lead to the best
results. On the other hand, if the agent constantly explores, it will never converge.
Therefore, it is crucial to strike a balance between exploration and exploitation. That
way, the agent explores enough not to choose a suboptimal policy, but after a suﬀi-
cient amount of exploration starts exploiting the policy that it learned while exploring.
Therefore, it is preferable that the agent starts by initially favoring exploration. This
can ensure that it will gather information and experience different actions and out-
comes. As more and more knowledge is acquired, the balance needs to shift so that
the agent relies more on learned favorable actions.
2.2.3
Markov decision process
Reinforcement learning problems can be mathematically modeled as Markov decision
processes (MDPs) using the terms clarified above [16].
An MDP is defined by a
tuple (S, A, T, R, γ), where S is the set of possible states, and A is the set of possible
actions. T is the transition function that defines the probability of transitioning from
one state to another given an action.
R is the reward function that maps state-
action pairs to real-valued rewards, and γ is the discount factor that determines the
importance of future rewards.
An MDP necessitates the existence of the Markov
property, which says that a present state encompasses all the information that is
necessary to determine a future state given an action, without having to look at past
states. Therefore, the current state can be used to evaluate the next actions without
depending on past states and actions. This property is useful as it allows us to focus
on the current state and action without having to keep track of the entire history
of past states.
Without this property, it would be very diﬀicult to scale our RL
algorithm to environments with large state spaces.

achieved by leveraging the deep learning algorithm’s abilities to recognize patterns
and represent complex data. Therefore, the need to explicitly engineer features in
the data is eliminated.
Being able to automatically learn from raw data without
the need for modifications allows for training on large amounts of data. Therefore,

2.2.5
Policy gradient algorithms
One way to solve reinforcement learning problems is the policy gradient approach.
The goal of this approach is to directly model and optimize the policy [43]. To do so,
the policy is typically parametrized using a neural network that outputs a probability
distribution over possible actions.
πθ(a|s) = softmax(fθ(s)⊤ga)
(2.3)
In this formula, πθ(a|s) is the probability of taking action a in state s, parameter-
ized by a neural network with weights θ. The function fθ(s) represents the hidden
layer(s) of the neural network, which takes in the state s as input and outputs a
feature vector. The vector ga represents a parameter vector for each action a. The
dot product fθ(s)⊤ga represents the similarity between the feature vector and the pa-
rameter vector for the given action. Finally, the softmax function is applied to obtain
a probability distribution over actions. Then, the performance of the parametrized

What this formula is representing is the expectation over states and actions. It

actions in the given state. By maximizing this objective function through gradient

that ultimately lead to higher cumulative rewards.
This approach is contrasted with value-based approaches. In a value-based ap-
proach, the idea is to minimize the loss between the predicted produced by the value
function and the target value [26]. Therefore, in these types of approaches, the policy
is more implicit as it is generated from the value function. A significant advantage
that policy gradient algorithms have is their ability to learn stochastic policies. This
can be understood with the following example. Consider the case of an agent con-

A deterministic policy would always either close or open the grip of the arm, de-
pending on the state of the arm and the tube. However, there might be noise that
is introduced or some other sort of uncertainty in the environment. In this case, a
policy gradient algorithm that has a probability distribution over the actions would
be better. That is because it might choose to grip with a probability of 0.8 and not
grip with a probability of 0.2. Therefore, it would be better suited to adapt to any
uncertainty in the environment.

Image annotation
As mentioned, image annotation is the process by which objects in an image are
labeled to be used in machine learning models. The traditional way of annotating
images is to use human labor. Typically, a worker has to manually click multiple
times to create the outline of an object in the image in order to annotate it. This
way of annotating images has several challenges associated with it [37]. One of the
reasons that it can be diﬀicult to reliably annotate large sets of images is the levels of
expertise required, depending on the task. For example, such a challenge arises if the
images to be annotated are a set of MRI scans that could include regions of medical
interest. In this case, the annotator would have to be adequately trained in medicine
to be able to make judgments about where the annotation begins and ends and what
it depicts [6]. Even for tasks that do not require advanced knowledge, annotating
large sets of images is time-consuming. For example, in the Cityscapes dataset, 1.5
hours were spent per image on average for a set of 5000 fine pixel-level annotations
[9]. For the creation of the COCO dataset, over 70,000 worker hours were utilized.
It has also been shown that untrained annotators can jeopardize the quality of the
annotated dataset, which in turn could make it unsuitable for use. When making the
COCO dataset, it was necessary to spend time and resources training and filtering

Due to the increasing demand for methods to assist in making the image anno-

the dog in the image. The resulting pixel-level mask provides important information
regarding the boundaries and shape of the object that was segmented in the image.
Models are typically trained to produce masks as described in the example above.

SimpleClick model
SimpleClick is a model used for interactive image segmentation [24].
SimpleClick
works by accepting as input an RGB image, one or multiple positive and negative
clicks, and optionally a previous mask. It then produces a new mask based on that
information. It works by using an architecture of three main components. A backbone
consisting of a vision transformer (ViT) generates a feature map of the image. Then,
a set of convolutional layers is applied to the last feature map from the backbone,
where features are combined. Finally, the feature pyramid is passed into a lightweight
segmentation head consisting of MLP layers. There, a segmentation probability map
is produced, which is then upsampled to the original resolution.
User clicks are
incorporated into the backbone and encoded in a two-channel disk map. Additionally,
the previous mask is concatenated with the click map. This concatenated map then
undergoes processing and is afterward combined with the image through element-wise
addition before being processed by the backbone.

As mentioned in 3.1, when the PPO network chooses an action, that action is fed to the
segmentation model along with the original image, and a new mask is produced. To

of the center of each square in the grid represent the potential coordinates of a click.
One of those coordinates can then be chosen, and a click occurs as explained in 3.3.1.
To see how this happens exactly, we can look at an example. Let’s assume that the
action space of the agent is 64. That means that the agent has 64 possible clicks
that it can do. We can imagine an 8×8 grid over the image and the 64 actions each
correspond to the coordinates of the central pixel in each box created by the grid as
shown in Figure 3.4. After the action is chosen by the PPO network, the coordinates
of the action along with its value (positive or negative) are fed to the segmentation
model. The segmentation model uses that information, along with the previous mask
and the image, to produce a mask.

3.4
PPO Network
The PPO model consists of three components, the shared layers, the Actor, and the
Critic. As shared layers, the encoder presented in section 3.2 is used to provide a
feature representation of the mask and image pairs. The reasons why both actor
and critic receive the image’s feature representation via the same pre-trained encoder
architecture are multiple. One of them is consistency in input. By having the same
network providing the feature representation, we can be sure that both the Actor and
the Critic have access to the same interpretation of the original image. This keeps
their understanding of the data in strict alignment and thus allows their respective
learning processes to be more coherent. Additionally, having the Actor and Critic
share these layers allows for more eﬀicient training, as the number of parameters
is significantly lower than if each had its own network producing a representation
for the images in question. Therefore, choosing to have shared layers between the
Actor and Critic was established early on and was used consistently throughout the
development process. The architecture of the Actor and Critic networks themselves
is the following:
• Actor: The Actor network consists of two Linear layers.
The first Linear
layer has input size x, where x is the same value as the length of the feature
representation produced by the Image encoder and output size y. It is followed
by a ReLU activation function, which introduces non-linearity to the network.
The output of the ReLU activation is then passed through another Linear layer
with input size y and output size corresponding to the action space.
• Critic: The Critic network is designed with two Linear layers. The first Linear
layer’s input size is x similar to the Actor network and the output size is y.
Following the Linear layer, a ReLU activation function is applied to introduce
non-linearity. The output of the ReLU activation is then fed into another Linear
==PAGE:35==

layer with input size y and output size 1, representing the predicted value or
expected return.
3.4.1
Performing a rollout
After having the trained encoder in place and a means of getting image and mask
pairs, the logic for performing a rollout was set up to have all the necessary conditions
for running the experiments with the entire pipeline. A data loader is created when
training the model, to provide it with the training and validation data. Then, con-
secutive rollouts happen, where based on the state, the model makes choices about
which actions should be taken.
In each rollout, a single image and mask pair is used.
A forward pass occurs
through the PPO model to obtain action probabilities and value predictions. During
the forward pass through the PPO model, the input state st is processed to generate
action logits, which represent the unnormalized scores for each possible action. These
action logits can be denoted as zt, where zt,i represents the action logit for action i at
time step t. The action probabilities, denoted as π(at|st), are obtained by applying a
softmax function to the action logits zt, which normalizes the logits and transforms
them into a probability distribution over the actions:
πrollout(at|st) = softmax(zt) =

where N represents the total number of possible actions. The softmax function
ensures that the action probabilities sum up to 1, allowing the agent to select an
action based on the probabilities generated by the PPO model. Based on the action
chosen, the click coordinates are calculated and fed into the Segmentation model
along with the image and any initial mask. The Segmentation model produces a new
mask, and the Intersection over Union (IoU) is calculated between this new mask and
the ground truth mask, which serves as the reward. The IoU can be computed using
the following expression:
IoU = Area of overlap between the predicted mask and ground truth mask
Area of union between the predicted mask and ground truth mask
In a single rollout, this process of feeding the Agent the previous state consisting
of the image and the previous mask is repeated a number of times. After finishing
a rollout, the advantages are calculated by subtracting the values predicted by the
Critic network from the discounted rewards. The advantages can be expressed as:
advantages = discounted rewards −values
where the discounted rewards are obtained using a discount factor γ and the
rewards obtained at each time step. To further facilitate training stability, the advan-

3.4.2
Training the Actor network
The Actor network is trained using the states that were observed in the rollout, the
actions taken, the action probabilities produced during each forward pass, and the
predicted values. Every time the Actor is trained, multiple training iterations occur.
In every training iteration, an observation is passed through the Actor network, result-
ing in a set of action logits zt from which log probabilities log(π(at|st)) are computed.
The policy ratio is then calculated by exponentiating the difference between the new
log probabilities and the log probabilities produced by the rollout:
policy ratio = exp(log(π(at|st)) −log(πrollout(at|st)))
Clipping is then applied to the policy ratio to ensure it stays within a certain range.
clipped ratio = clip(policy ratio, 1 −ϵ, 1 + ϵ)
Where ϵ represents the clipping parameter. For our tests, the clipping parameter is
set at 0,2. Two loss terms are calculated: clipped loss and full loss.
clipped loss = clipped ratio × A
full loss = policy ratio × A
These terms are obtained by multiplying the respective ratios with the normalized
advantages.
The clipped loss ensures that the policy update is within a certain
range to prevent overly large updates.
The full loss is the unconstrained version.
The minimum between these two losses is calculated, and that is the term used for
computing the gradients.
policy loss = −min(clipped loss, full loss)
The parameters of the network are then updated using the computed gradients to
adjust the network weights. Finally, the KL divergence is computed between the new
log probabilities and the ones from the rollout and if the divergence is higher than a
predefined threshold, the training is terminated.
3.4.3

After the Actor network is trained, the Critic network follows. It is trained using
the observed states and the corresponding returns (cumulative rewards). Similar to

In each training iteration, an observation is passed through the Critic network. The
Critic network then produces predicted values for the given observations. The differ-
ence between the predicted values and the actual returns is calculated and squared.
This difference represents the error between the predicted values and the true returns.
The squared differences are then averaged to obtain the value loss, which quantifies
the discrepancy between the predicted and actual returns:

where V is the predicted values and R is the rewards from the rollout. Backpropa-

3.4.4
Training both Actor and Critic
In the first stages of training, the Actor and Critic were trained separately. However,
after experimentation, we discovered that the network was capable of learning better
when they were instead trained at the same time. To do so, we followed the same
processes as in 3.4.2 and 3.4.3 where the policy loss and the value loss are calculated,
and then the total loss is calculated by summing them:
total loss = policy loss + value loss × c1 −entropy × c2
where c1 and c2 are coeﬀicients and entropy refers to the uncertainty or random-
ness in the policy distribution and is used to encourage exploration and prevent the
policy from becoming too deterministic.
Backpropagation is then performed and the weights of both Actor and Critic
networks are simultaneously calculated.
By training the Actor and Critic together, we aimed to achieve more compatibility.
As both components are trained simultaneously, they can adapt together and maintain
consistency. If the actor and critic are trained independently, it’s possible for them
to diverge or have conflicting updates, which can hinder learning.

capture information from both sources during training. Subsequently, a training loop

was compared with the IoU score of the corresponding mask concatenated with the
image. This comparison enabled the model to learn the correlation between the input
image and the mask quality, essentially training it on a regression task to predict the
IoU score based on the given image and mask. Following the training process, the
modified ResNet-18 model demonstrated promising results. It exhibited the ability to
accurately predict the IoU score for a given image, indicating its successful learning
of the relationship between image characteristics and mask quality.

4.4.3
Improved Dataset
Based on the results thus far, the model created was able to have an impressively
good performance on the dataset we provided and with the first initialization methods.
However, solving the problem of predicting optimal clicks for an object placed in the
middle of a small cropped image may not be robust enough. When talking about
real-life scenarios, the user that is localizing the object may draw a large box around
it without the object necessarily being in the middle. This led to the idea of creating
a dataset that would be more diﬀicult to train on.
To produce this dataset, the TACO dataset was used as a starting point. Similar
to the previous dataset, the process for producing the improved dataset is to start
by considering the bounding box of the objects in TACO images. Then, the boxes
are expanded in all directions by 50% to create a much larger cropped image than in
the original scenario. Additionally, after being expanded, the new bounding box was
displaced so that the object of interest would no longer be exactly in the middle. In
this way, a more realistic scenario is set up where it is not enough for the model to
learn that clicking in the middle will always be the optimal choice.

4.4.4
Action space baselines
One hyperparameter that has not been expanded on until now is the one concerning
the action space.
In all past experiments, the action space that the model could
explore was composed of 64 actions. In images that are produced from an object’s
bounding box with a small expansion, that may not be an issue, but that is not
necessarily the case with the new dataset. Especially when it comes to focusing on
small details in the image, having a larger action space can allow the agent to correct
the produced masks with a higher degree of accuracy. Therefore, to establish whether
in our case it would make a sizeable difference, we compared two oracle algorithms,
one with action space 64 and one with action space 256. The difference between the
two would establish whether exploring a higher action space could improve the model
or not.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
In this project, the goal was to produce an end-to-end pipeline for the creation of
a reinforcement learning agent to assist in making image annotation more eﬀicient
and effective. Initial experimentation using SimpleClick did not yield successful re-
sults, even in the simplest case of training on a single image. Exploration of the
underperformance’s causes led to replacing SimpleClick with SAM, a more modern
segmentation model. The increase in performance after this change shows the impor-
tance of this component in the pipeline. After incorporating SAM the pipeline was
able to solve the single image training problem and performed effectively when using
the first dataset preprocessing method.
We then explored the performance of the pipeline in a more diﬀicult and poten-
tially more realistic scenario, by switching to an improved dataset that would prove
more challenging. On this new dataset, we first investigated how two important pa-
rameters would affect performance. First was the action space, as in theory having
more actions available would allow the agent to use clicks that would target details
and small deviations between generated and ground truth mask. In practice, however,
the results between an oracle with an action space of 64 and those of an oracle uti-
lizing an action space of 256 were almost identical. An increase in action space does
not therefore automatically cause an increase in the highest possible IoU. It could
also be the result of how the action space is currently being considered. An action
space that is the result of dividing the image into a grid does not put any actual focus
on the region of interest, which would be the region where there is high uncertainty
from the model. The second parameter we explored was how much information from
initialization leads to higher performance. We considered two scenarios, one in which
no initial information is used and another in which the user was allowed to place the
first click and use that information to make suggestions afterward.
To conduct these experiments, we considered three baselines, the Oracle baseline,
the random baseline, and the simulated user baseline.
From the results of these
experiments, we can conclude that the model does learn to predict clicks that lead to
IoUs higher than those of the random baseline. While our model fails to perform as
well as the simulated user baseline, when selecting from the possible valid actions, the
model outperforms the simulated user, especially in the scenario where initialization
is used. These results are promising, as they show that with further modifications
and improvements, the proposed framework can produce a model capable of taking
the next step in improving interactive annotation tools.

CHAPTER 6
Future Work
Based on the conclusions drawn from the previous section, there are several avenues
for future research on the topic of eﬀicient annotation with reinforcement learning
that align with our findings.
One improvement that could be considered is the investigation of alternative ap-
proaches for choosing the action space. As we discussed in Chapter 5, there might be
better ways to determine the action points that could further enhance the eﬀiciency
of our annotation process. One idea would be to leverage information from the seg-
mentation model about uncertainty. When a mask is created, there are pixels that
the model is certain should be included in the mask or not, but there are others it
is unsure about, which creates areas of uncertainty in the image. By focusing our
actions on areas of high uncertainty, rather than spreading them uniformly through-
out the image, we could potentially improve both the accuracy and eﬀiciency of our
annotation pipeline. This hypothesis is concretely supported by the results of our
model when it considers only the list of valid actions, as demonstrated in 4.4.7.
Another path for future work is to evaluate the method against human annotators.
While our method surpassed random baseline approaches in both experimental setups
(refer to Sections 4.4.6 and 4.4.7), and was also compared to a simulated user baseline,
we haven’t yet tested its performance against real human annotators. It would be
a natural next step to gather a group of annotators and establish a baseline by
comparing their results on the same datasets. This comparison will help us better
gauge the effectiveness of our pipeline in comparison to human annotation. Moreover,
we can also measure the speed at which human annotators can segment dataset
objects using our model and compare it to their performance without using our system.
This evaluation will provide valuable insights into the eﬀiciency gains enabled by our
proposed pipeline.
By delving into these future research directions, we can further refine and optimize
our eﬀicient image annotation techniques based on reinforcement learning. Explor-
ing alternative action space selection methods and conducting thorough evaluations
against human annotators will not only enhance our pipeline’s performance but also
validate its real-world benefits.
