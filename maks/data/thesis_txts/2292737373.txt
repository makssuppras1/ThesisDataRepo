Abstract 
 
A silicon drift detector capable of detecting X-ray radiation originating from astronomical objects in the 
energy range 2-20 keV has been proposed as the third DTUSat project. The proposal is known as Cube-
WATCH. The purpose of this master thesis is to optimize a previously developed process flow and cor-
rect errors on photomasks with the aim of completing the fabrication of a CubeWATCH detector proto-
type. Subsequent to the fabrication, the functionality should be investigated. Detector characteristics 
and performance should be evaluated, if possible. In addition, theoretical studies are to be carried out in 
regard to technological features of the drift detector, and the astrophysical purpose. The efficiency and 
expected sensitivity of the detector are in this connection estimated. 
The applied methods include experimental and analytical research. The process flow optimization covers 
several different process entries, and entails considerations, discussions, and justifications of process 
choices and theoretical parameters. The optimization includes extensive testing work in cleanroom 
facilities. The prototype is then fabricated in the cleanroom using the optimized process flow and its 
functionality is afterwards investigated through relevant laboratory measurements. The theoretical 
studies are based on analytical work and calculations.  
The known errors on the photomasks are corrected and new masks are fabricated. An optimized process 
flow enabling the fabrication of the CubeWATCH silicon drift detector is created and documented. Based 
on the optimized process flow, a functioning detector prototype is fabricated with acceptable structure 
dimensions and alignment, though undesired defects are present. Experiences are made throughout the 
fabrication process in regard to the photomask design and the optimized process. Remarks are made to 
the design and possible solutions are suggested. The theoretical studies of the drift detector technology 
include comparisons of detector types and imaging devices. The studies of the astrophysical purpose 
include estimations of the efficiency and solid angle of the CubeWATCH detector. The estimates are 
used to calculate count rates as a function of energy for the background and typical bright sources. The 
count rates are then used to estimate the detector sensitivity as a function of time for two energy 
bands.

Resume (DK) 
 
Dansk titel: ”Silicium baseret driftdetektor til røntgenastrofysik”. 
En silicium baseret drift detektor, som er i stand til at detektere røntgenstråling fra astronomiske objek-
ter i energiområdet 2-20 keV, er blevet foreslået som det tredje DTUSat projekt. Forslaget er kendt som 
CubeWATCH. Formålet med dette kandidatspeciale er at optimere en tidligere udviklet procesfølge og 
rette kendte fejl i fotomaskedesignet med det formål at færdiggøre fremstillingen af en CubeWATCH 
detektor prototype. Funktionaliteten af prototypen skal herefter undersøges. Ydeevne og detektoregen-
skaber skal, hvis det er muligt, vurderes. Yderligere formål inkluderer teoretiske undersøgelser vedrø-
rende teknologiske forhold og fordele ved drift detektoren samt undersøgelser af det astrofysiske for-
mål. Herunder estimeres detektorens effektivitet og dens forventede følsomhed. 
De anvendte metoder omfatter eksperimentelt praktisk arbejde og analytiske undersøgelser. Optime-
ringen af procesfølgen omhandler flere forskellige procesemner og inkluderer begrundede overvejelser 
af procesvalg og teoretiske parametre. Optimeringen inkluderer omfattende fabrikationstest i renrum. 
Prototypen fremstilles derefter i renrummet ved anvendelse af den optimerede procesfølge, og dens 
funktionalitet undersøges gennem relevante laboratoriemålinger. De teoretiske undersøgelser er base-
ret på analytisk arbejde og beregninger. 
De kendte fejl på fotomaskerne er rettet. En optimeret procesfølge til fremstillingen af CubeWATCH 
detektoren er udarbejdet og dokumenteret. En fungerende prototype detektor er fremstillet ud fra den 
optimerede procesfølge. Strukturerne er acceptabelt tilpasset og dimensioneret. Uønskede defekter er 
imidlertid observeret. Der er redegjort for erfaringer i relation til problemerne med fotomaskedesignet 
og procesfølgen – mulige løsninger er foreslået. De teoretiske studier af detektorteknologien inkluderer 
sammenligninger af detektortyper og billeddannelsesudsyr. Undersøgelserne af det astrofysiske formål 
omfatter estimeringer af effektiviteten og rumvinklen af CubeWATCH detektoren. Estimaterne er brugt 
til at beregne tællehastigheder for typiske kilder samt baggrunden. Tællehastighederne er derefter 
brugt til at estimere detektorens følsomhed som funktion af tiden for to energibånd.

This master thesis is prepared by Trine Holm Christensen in the period 1 September 2014 – 16 February 
2015. The thesis concludes the student’s master degree in Earth and Space Physics and Engineering at 
the Technological University of Denmark (DTU) and is prepared at the DTU Space institute. The thesis 
constitutes 32.5 ECTS points.

esses. I also thank Filip Sandborg-Olsen for his help with the CleWin software and the correction of 
photomasks. In addition, I would like thank Erik Vilain Thomsen from DTU Nanotech for his assistance 
and guidance in regard to the functionality tests of the detector. Finally, I thank Lone Christensen and 
Carsten Munch Holm for their assistance regarding proofreading of the report.

1 Introduction 
Space research has been of interest for several centuries. While the terrestrial study of space carried out 
by astronomers with telescopes dates back several centuries, the physical in-situ exploration of space 
has only been possible in the last decades. The early exploration of space is often connected to the geo-
political Cold War. The space race between the United States and the Soviet Union resulted in the first 
artificial object to orbit the Earth and the landing of humans on the Moon. The acquired option of plac-
ing satellites in space gave rise to new scientific research; the atmosphere of the Earth is only transpar-
ent for certain energy bands, and is thus opaque to most wavelengths. The only allowed bands are the 
visible light and radio waves, so instruments designed to detect other wavelengths must be taken into 
space. The advances of space technology in recent decades have ensured exciting and important scien-
tific results. 
The placement of satellites in space enabled scientists to study specific celestial objects. In addition, 
discoveries were also made serendipitously. Satellites were deployed by the United States to ensure 
that nuclear weapons were not tested in space. Since nuclear explosions produce intense bursts of X-
rays and gamma-rays the satellites had detectors designed for high energy radiation. When in space, the 
satellites detected flashes of gamma-radiation not associated with nuclear weapon testing. Today, these 
transient flashes are known as gamma ray bursts. It has been found that most bursts are followed by an 
afterglow emitted at X-rays. Although the existence of gamma ray bursts have been known for some 
decades, they are still objects of investigation. 
Several spacecraft missions have studied the variability of X-ray sources. Among those was the European 
Retrievable Carrier (EURECA), which was launched in 1992. On board was the Wide Angle Telescope for 
Cosmic Hard X-rays (WATCH) instrument that was developed at the Danish Space Research Institute, 
mainly as a gamma ray burst detector. The instrument was sensitive in the 6-150 keV energy range. The 
total field of view covered a quarter of the sky. During operations, known X-ray sources were monitored 
and a number of new X-ray transients were discovered. 
The Technical University of Denmark (DTU) has a project known as DTUSat, which involves student-built 
satellites. The satellites should comply with the CubeSat specification of which the most significant are 
the overall size and mass: 10x10x10 cm3 and 1 kg. The third DTUSat project may concern the observa-
tion of X-rays emitted during the afterglow of gamma ray bursts. The proposal is known as CubeWATCH, 
and utilizes a silicon drift detector for the investigation. Parts of the design are based on the original 
WATCH instrument.

2.1 Gamma ray bursts 
Due to the threat of nuclear weapons in the 1960s, the United States and the Soviet Union signed a 
treaty prohibiting the testing of such weapons in the Earth’s atmosphere and in space. To assure that 
the treaty was not violated, the United States deployed a number of satellites for monitoring near-Earth 
space. The detectors on the satellites were designed to detect high energy radiation, since nuclear ex-
plosions produce intense bursts of X-ray, neutrons and gamma-rays. After 3 years of operation, the 
satellite system had detected 17 bursts of gamma-rays with energies in the range 0.2-1.5 MeV, and 
information regarding the location of the sources were obtained. The bursts originated from different 
locations and had varying duration – none of the bursts were of terrestrial origin (one originated from 
the Sun) [Seward and Charles, 2010, Chap. 17]. Confirmations of the new astrophysical phenomenon 
were provided the following year by the Russian Ioffe Institute [Aptekar et al., 2013]. The cause and 
origin of the intense burst known as gamma ray bursts (GRB) were unknown for many years despite 
several theories and intense observational efforts, to which many countries contributed [Fishman, 
2013], [Lund, 2013], [Aptekar et al., 2013]. 
In 1980, Mazets et al. (1980) published information on the GRBs detected in the KONUS experiment. The 
distribution over the celestial sphere in galactic coordinates of GRB sources determined in the KONUS 
experiment is shown in Figure 2.1. The source locations are seen to be distributed in a random way 
across the sky without any sign of anisotropy. Earlier data had indicated a weak concentration toward 
the galactic plane, however, with the increased amount of data, the trend had disappeared. 
 
Figure 2.1: Distribution of gamma-burst over the celestial sphere in galactic coordinates [Mazets et al., 1980].

With the launch of the Compton Gamma Ray Observatory spacecraft, a large number of GRBs was locat-
ed using the Burst and Transient Source Experiment (BATSE) instrument. The results showed the same 
distribution trend as found by the KONOS experimental results. The distribution of the source locations 
on the sky was found to be completely uniform, as illustrated in Figure 2.2. It was thus determined that 
GRBs originate at cosmological distances [Seward and Charles, 2010, Chap. 17], [Fishman, 2013]. For 
these bursts to be visible across such great distances, they must be among the most energetic objects in 
the universe.

latitude and longitude [Fishman, 2013]. 
The data from KONUS and BATSE revealed two types of events: short GRBs with duration ranging from 
0.03-3 sec and long GRBs with duration 7-400 sec. It has furthermore been found that most GRBs are 
followed by an afterglow emitted at longer wavelength; the burst must involve high velocity ejection of 
material, which will interact with the ambient medium causing heated material that will radiate energy 
for a period of time [Seward and Charles, 2010, Chap. 17], [Aptekar et al., 2013]. 
2.1.1 
Detection and localization of GRB 
Detailed studies of GRBs require a precise location of the source. In order to detect the X-rays emitted 
during the afterglow and obtain the source position, an X-ray detector with position resolution is neces-
sary. An imaging device, which is well suited for this purpose, is the rotation modulation collimator 
(RMC). The RMC requires a detector with only one dimensional position determination; the silicon drift 
detector fulfills this requirement. 
In the original concept, the RMC consists of two grids with parallel strips that co-rotate in front of an X-
ray detector. The grids have the same strip pitch and are separated by a fixed distance. The direction of 
the incoming radiation can be derived from the modulation of the detected signal as a function of the 
==PAGE:31==
29 of 153 
rotation phase of the grids [Brandt, 1994, Chap. 2]. An illustration of the original RMC can be seen in 
Figure 2.3. The RMC principle was employed on the WATCH instrument. In the WATCH design, the exit 
grid is removed and the detector is replaced by two different strip detector materials in an interleaved 
grid configuration, as seen in Figure 2.4. The WATCH one-grid configuration was designed to improve 
localization of the fluctuating GRBs [Lund, 1981]. An advantage of the RMC, compared to other imaging 
systems, is in regard to satellite stabilization. The RMC only needs one axis stabilization, which will be 
the rotation axis [Dallilar and Lund, 2013].

2.1.2 
X-ray bursts 
Like the original WATCH instrument, the CubeWATCH project proposal concerns the detection of 
gamma ray bursts. However, where the WATCH instrument was sensitive in the 6-150 keV energy range, 
the CubeWATCH detector should be able to detect radiation in the energy range 2-20 keV. Due to the 
lower energy threshold of CubeWATCH, it will be able to detect the so-called X-ray bursts, which are 
also of astrophysical interest. 
In the 1970s, it was discovered that some objects in the sky emit sudden and powerful bursts of X-rays. 
The light curve evolution of such sources show that the sources emit X-rays at a constant low level until 
there suddenly is an abrupt increase in the X-ray intensity followed by a gradual decline (a burst typically 
lasts for 20 sec). Sources that behave in this fashion are called X-ray bursters. X-ray bursters are believed 
to involve neutron stars in binary systems, where the stars are engaged in mass transfer. Most of the gas 
transferred from the companion star is hydrogen, which is then compressed against the surface of the 
neutron star. Due to the high temperatures and pressures in the accreting layer, the hydrogen is con-
verted into helium. At a certain point, helium fusion ignites explosively and heats the surface, which 
then emits X-rays, but the emission ceases within a few tens of seconds when the surface cools. X-ray 
bursts are thus thermonuclear explosions in the surface layers of the neutron star accreting hydro-
gen/helium from the envelope of the companion star. After a burst has ended, new hydrogen flows 
onto the neutron star and the process starts over. X-ray bursters will therefore emit bursts in an interval 
of hours or days [Freedman et al., 2011, Chap. 21].

2.2 Semiconductor diode detector 
Semiconductor diode detectors use a solid medium for the detection of radiation, and are therefore also 
referred to as solid state detectors. Solids can be classified into three groups based on their electric 
conductivity: insulators, semiconductors, and conductors. The periodic lattice of crystalline materials 
establish energy bands for electrons that exist within the solid. The energy of an electron must be con-
fined to one of these energy bands [Knoll, 2000, Chap. 11]. The electrical properties of a solid material 
depend on the population of electrons in the allowed energy bands. Current will flow easily in a conduc-
tor, since the energy bands are not separated. The energy bands in insulators and semiconductors are 
separated by band gaps of forbidden energies; the gap between the valence band and the conduction 
band is large for an insulator and its conductivity is 
therefore very low. The band gap is smaller in a 
semiconductor; an illustration of the relevant bands 
in semiconductors is seen in Figure 2.5. At ordinary 
temperatures, the electrons are bound in the crystal-
line lattice and are said to have an energy in the 
valence band. Electrons that have gained sufficient 
energy to break loose from their respective locations 
in the valence band are called conduction electrons 
and their energy lies in a higher conduction band. 
Conduction electrons can move around in the crystal 
and can therefore carry electrical currents. In addi-
tion to the electron created in the otherwise empty 
conduction band, the excitation process also leaves a 
vacancy, called a hole, in the otherwise full valence 
band. Together, the two are called an electron-hole pair. The electron-hole pair can be moved under the 
influence of an electric field. The hole, which represent a positive charge, will move in the opposite 
direction of the electron. Semiconductor materials such as silicon or germanium crystals are often used 
as detection mediums. 
When ionizing radiation pass through a semiconductor, energy may be transferred to the electrons of 
which most are bound in the valence band. If the transferred energy is sufficient, an electron may move 
into the conduction band thereby creating an electron-hole pair. Under the influence of an applied elec-
tric field, the electrons and holes will move toward electrodes, resulting in a current pulse. By integrat-
ing the current pulse, the induced charge can be measured and serves to determine the energy depos-
ited by the incident radiation [Knoll, 2000, Chap. 11]. The signal charge can be quite small, so the detec-
tor signal must be amplified. This is achieved by connecting the read out channels to a preamplifier. 
The electrical properties of a semiconductor can be altered by adding controlled amounts of a dopant 
atom – also referred to as impurity doping. When adding a group V element like phosphorus, only four 
of the five valence electrons will be part of the covalent bonding with the silicon atoms, thereby leaving 
a fifth weakly bound electron, as seen in Figure 2.6. The fifth valence electron is referred to as a donor 
electron. The energy required to elevate the donor electron into the conduction band is considerably 
less than that for the electrons in the covalent bonding. If the donor electron is elevated into the con-
duction band, it can move through the crystal. In addition, it leaves behind a positively charged phos-
Figure 2.5: Band structure for electron energies in 
semiconductors [Knoll, 2000, Chap. 11, p. 354]. 
==PAGE:33==
31 of 153 
phorus ion. Thus, a group V atom donates an electron to the conduction band without creating a hole in 
the valence band. This type of impurity atom is called a donor impurity atom. The resulting material is 
called an n-type semiconductor (excess of mobile negative charges). If a group III element such as boron 
is added instead, all of its three valence electrons is part of the covalent bonding, and one covalent 
bonding position will consequently be empty. In order to fill the vacant position, other valence electron 
positions will become vacated, as illustrated in Figure 2.7. These positions can be thought of as holes in 
the semiconductor material. The hole can move through the crystal and generate an electric current, 
while the negatively charged boron atom is fixed in the crystal. Thus, a group III atom accepts an elec-
tron from the valence band thereby leaving a hole, without generating electrons in the conduction 
band. This type of impurity atom is called an acceptor impurity atom. The resulting material is called a p-
type semiconductor (excess of mobile positive charges). [Neamen, 2012, Chap. 4]. 
 
Figure 2.6: Two-dimensional representation of the silicon lattice doped with a phosphorus atom. 
 
Figure 2.7: Two-dimensional representation of the silicon lattice doped with a boron atom. 
When an n-type semiconductor is joined to a p-type semiconductor, a p-n junction is created. The p-n 
junction diode is rectifying thus only allowing current to flow in one direction. Most semiconductor 
detectors contain at least one p-n junction diode. In semiconductor diode detectors, the p-n junctions 
are so called one-sided junctions where one of the doped areas have much higher concentration than 
the other; heavily and lightly doped materials are, respectively, often given a special “+” or “-” notation. 
If, for example, the acceptor atom concentration is much higher than the donor atom concentration, the 
junction is referred to as a p+-n junction. 
If no external voltage is applied to a p-n junction, the junction is in thermal equilibrium, and a potential 
difference is formed across the junction; the potential is called the built-in potential. Since areas of op-
==PAGE:34==
32 of 153 
posite doping are joined, electrons and holes will diffuse into regions with lower concentrations leaving 
behind a region of charged atoms – this region is depleted of mobile carriers. The accumulated space 
charge will create an electric field that counteracts the diffusion. The region is called the space charge 
region or depletion region [Lutz, 1999, Chap. 5]. The basic p-n junction with the space charge region is 
illustrated in Figure 2.8 along with the electric field and the diffusion forces.  
When an external voltage potential is applied to the doped areas it affects the potential drop across the 
depletion region and therefore its width. Under reverse bias, where the p-type regions are connected to 
negative voltage and the n-type regions are positive, the potential drop across the depletion region 
increases. The increase is due to the fact that the holes in the p-type semiconductor and the electrons in 
the n-type semiconductor are pulled away from the junction, thus increasing the width of the space 
charge region, which represents the sensitive detector volume. The width of the depletion region de-
pends on the wafer material, type and resistivity, and the sum of the built-in voltage potential and the 
applied biased voltage. The width will increase with increasing reverse bias voltage. In a one-sided junc-
tion, the depletion region will primarily extend into the lighter doped side. 
 
Figure 2.8: The basic structure of the p-n junction with space charge region, the electric field, 
and the forces acting on the charged carriers [Neamen, 2012, Chap. 7]. 
 
2.2.1 
Silicon drift detector 
The silicon drift detectors (SDD) are solid state X-ray detectors. Like other X-ray detectors, it measures 
the energy of incoming radiation by the amount of ionization it produces in the detection material. A 
unique electrode configuration is applied in the SDD: several p+-n junctions are formed in a strip array 
on both surfaces of a wafer, and point-like anodes are created on one surface [Gatti and Rehak, 1984], 
[Knoll, 2000, Chap. 13]. When p+-n junctions are placed on both sides of a wafer, there will be two space 
==PAGE:35==
33 of 153 
charge regions separated by the undepleted bulk at low voltages. This will cause the detector to be 
partially depleted, as seen in Figure 2.9a. At high enough voltages, the space charge regions forming at 
both wafer sides will touch each other, and it is thus possible to obtain a parabolic potential well with 
minimum in the center of the wafer. The detector is then fully depleted, see Figure 2.9b. Electrons will 
assemble at the potential minimum and move in the direction of the n+ anode, while holes drift in the 
electric field toward the p+ cathodes. [Lutz, 1999, Chap. 6]. The p+-n junctions in an SDD are reverse 
biased until the detector is fully depleted. The structure of a linear drift detector is seen in Figure 2.10. 
 
Figure 2.9: Depletion region and potential profiles for (a) partially 
depleted and (b) fully depleted silicon wafer [Gatti and Rehak, 1984].

Under the influence of the applied electric field, the electron cloud is transported parallel to the surface 
by the potential gradient between the cathode strips, and collected at anodes near the surface, while 
the created holes will be attracted to the nearest p+ drift electrodes. The electrode structure on each 
side of the wafer is for the large part identically designed and follows the same potential distribution, 
thus assuring a symmetrical parabolic potential distribution across the wafer. However, the symmetry of 
the biased cathodes gets interrupted in the collection zone, where a few cathodes on the back side act 
as push-up electrodes; they are biased in a way to deflect the drift channel upwards to the anodes, see 
Figure 2.11. Electrons created by the ionizing radiation are so confined within the electric potential well 
and are drawn to a potential minimum near the center of the wafer – the anode line. During the drift, 
the electron cloud preserves the information relative to the position and the delivered energy of the 
radiation crossing the detector. The signal on the anodes are read out using amplifiers. 
 
Figure 2.11: Conceptual illustration of the potential well under the 
influence of an applied electric field and push-up electrodes.  
In addition to the drift motion of the charge carriers due to the external electric field, another transpor-
tation occurs: diffusion by concentration gradients. The width of the diffusion cloud increases with time. 
Diffusion spread of the electron cloud can compromise the measured signal if there is too much and it 
will therefore be of interest to force the generated electrons together as they drift toward the anodes.

2.3.3 
Photolithography 
The photolithography process involves a number of sequential steps, with the aim of transferring a pat-
tern onto a substrate using light. The wafer is uniformly spin coated with a substance called photoresist, 
which is a light sensitive material. After coating, a softbake/prebake is performed as a way of drying the 
photoresist by removing excess solvent. When applying the photoresist, the user will have to define a 
number of coating parameters in a recipe. These parameters include the wafer thickness, resist type, 
and resist thickness. The baking temperature and time are also associated with the recipe. Photoresist 
can be either positive or negative depending on how it reacts during exposure to ultra violet (UV) light. 
When positive photoresist is exposed, the exposed part of the resist becomes soluble. Alternatively, 
when negative resist is used, the unexposed part becomes soluble [Geng, 2005, Chap. 9]. WS2013 de-
cided to use positive photoresist for the development of the SDD due to unwanted swelling tendencies 
associated with the negative photoresist [WS2013, p. 39]. 
Before applying the photoresist, a surface preparation step is performed to promote adhesion of the 
resist. The preparation step involves a 30 sec buffered hydrofluoric acid (BHF) dip followed by a rinse. 
However, in the case where the photoresist is to be deposited on a wafer surface coated with SiO2, this 
is not an option, since the BHF will etch the SiO2. Instead, the preparation step involves a dehydration 
bake of the wafer at a temperature of 150 °C. In order to improve the adhesion between the substrate 
and the photoresist material, an adhesion promoter called hexamethyldisilaxzane (HMDS) is applied 
[Geng, 2005, Chap. 9].  
The next step in the photolithography process involves alignment of the wafer and photomask followed 
by exposure with ultra violet light. A photomask is a plate with transparencies that allow the ultra violet 
light to shine through in a defined pattern. A photomask can be either bright field (BF) or dark field (DF). 
The BF photomask contains a copy of the pattern to be transferred; the pattern will block the ultra violet 
light during the exposure of the photoresist – BF photomasks are commonly used for creating structures 
of a deposited material, like aluminium. Conversely, the DF photomask contains a negative image of the 
==PAGE:39==

pattern to be transferred – they are commonly used when creating openings in a deposited layer, like

sound is switched on during fine the bath. The wafers are lastly rinsed in running water for 5 min

tone bath, since metals are not allowed in the rough and fine acetone baths. The wafers are placed in 
the bath for 4-5 min and then rinsed in running water for 5 min. 
2.3.4 
Etching 
Etching can be done with either wet chemistry or dry etch equipment. A number of methods and 
equipment exist for etching processes. The ones relevant for the development of the SDD are reactive 
ion etch (RIE), deep reactive ion etch (DRIE), wet silicon oxide etch using BHF, and wet aluminium etch. 
When etching is performed, the user will have to know the etch rates in order to obtain the etching 
time. Except when etching crystalline materials, the wet etching process is isotropic, meaning that the 
etch rate is equal in all directions [Geng, 2005, Chap. 11]. Isotropic etch will therefore lead to undercut-
ting beneath the masking layer. Dry anisotropic etches can therefore often be advantageous when etch-
ing small and narrow features or when undercutting is unacceptable.  
The dry RIE process uses chemically reactive plasma for the etching of the deposited material. The 
plasma is generated by an electromagnetic field under low pressure. Since the reactive ions are mostly 
vertically delivered to the wafer, the RIE is able to produce very anisotropic etch profiles. The DRIE proc-
ess used by the Danchip DRIE-Pegasus equipment is a modified version of the RIE. It is a dry etch that 
uses the Bosch process, which alternates repeatedly between two steps: plasma etch and deposition of 
a passivation layer. Together, the two steps constitutes an 8 sec cycle – the default values at Danchip 
are a 5 sec etch and 3 sec deposition. In the deposition step, a C4F8 plasma is formed and a coating is 
created on all surfaces thereby protecting the sidewalls in the following etch step. The etch step involves 
an ion bombardment that first removes the passivation layer on the surfaces directly exposed to the 
ions. When the bottom of the layer is opened, the etch itself begins [Labmanager, 2011].  
Wet silicon oxide etch can be performed in a number of ways. For the fabrication of the SDD, the etch-
ing is done using BHF, which has a more stable etch rate than HF. When etching narrow structures it can 
be advantageous to use BHF with wetting agent and also wet the sample in water for a few minutes 
before etching [Labadviser, 2014b].

2.3.5 
Impurity doping 
The fabrication of semiconductors requires a change in the dopant concentration in some areas in order 
to change the electrical properties and create local junctions of a desired polarity. The process is known 
as impurity doping and involves an introduction of controlled amounts of impurity dopants into the 
semiconductor. In the case where the dopant should affect only a portion of the substrate, the remain-
der of the surface must be masked to exclude the dopant. The key approaches of impurity doping are 
diffusion and ion implantation.  
Ion implantation is done by exposing areas of the wafer to a bombardment of ions. Ions are thus im-
planted into the silicon wafer in a layer just below the surface. The doping concentration will have its 
peak inside the semiconductor; the distribution is determined mainly by the ion mass and implanted-ion 
energy [May and Sze, 2004, Chap. 7]. The acceleration of the ions determines the penetration depth and 
the exposure time determines the dosage [Geng, 2005]. 
Diffusion implantation is performed by either gaseous or nongaseous diffusion. During gaseous diffu-
sion, the wafer is exposed to a high-temperature environment of dopant vapor. Nongaseous diffusion is 
done by pre-depositing a dopant on the surface and then driving the dopant into the wafer through 
diffusion by high-temperature processing. The dopant will diffuse into the wafer at sufficiently high 
temperatures because the dopant migrates from the dopant-rich surface to the dopant-poor interior. 
The doping concentration will decrease monotonically from the surface and the distribution is deter-
mined mainly by the temperature and diffusion time [May and Sze, 2004, Chap. 6]. Diffusion implanta-
tion proceeds equally in all directions at the edge of the masking window, causing the dopants to spread 
laterally under the mask as seen in Figure 2.12a [Ruska, 1987, Chap. 3]. As opposed to the isotropic 
dopant profile obtained through diffusion, an anisotropic profile, see Figure 2.12b, is obtained through 
ion implantation.

by the exposure time and dosage; this is not an option when using diffusion implantation.

2.3.6 
Deposition 
In addition to the growth of the silicon dioxide layer and the photoresist coating, the deposition proc-
esses used for the fabrication of the SDD include physical vapor deposition (PVD) and two chemical 
vapor deposition (CVD) technologies: low pressure chemical vapor deposition (LPCVD) and plasma en-
hanced chemical vapor deposition (PECVD). Both processes are used for the deposition of thin films 
onto the wafer. In CVD processes, reactions are most commonly activated by thermal energy or radio 
frequency; reactive gasses pass over the wafer and adsorb onto the surface where they react and form a 
film. PVD is a vacuum deposition process for metals [Geng, 2005, Chap. 13-14]. 
For the SDD fabrication, the LPCVD is used for the deposition of the polycrystalline silicon (poly-Si) used 
for the voltage divider. The user will have to select a process recipe and parameters, which include the 
temperature, pressure, gas flow and doping agent. The PECVD is used for the boron silica glass and the 
passivation oxide layer. For the deposition of the aluminium for the electrodes and connect traces, a 
PVD is employed using the Wordentec and Alcatel equipment.

2.4 CubeWATCH prototype 
The detector and its surrounding circuit are fabricated on a 4" silicon wafer. The sensitive detector area 
is approximately 5x5 cm2. The electrode structure consists of anodes and parallel drift cathodes. Both 
electrode types consist of an aluminium electrode on the outside of the insulating SiO2 layer and an 
impurity doped internal electrode beneath the insulation layer – the silicon is n+ doped beneath the 
anodes and p+ doped under the drift cathodes. The connection between the two structures is estab-
lished through openings, in the SiO2 layer, known as vias. The electrode structure is symmetrical on the 
two surfaces of the wafer, apart from the anode line region. There are 472 drift cathodes; 236 on each 
side of the anode line. In addition to the collection anodes and drift cathodes, a guard electrode sur-
rounds the circuit and the sensitive area. The detector is furthermore equipped with an integrated high 
voltage (HV) divider that generates the biases for the drift cathodes and the guard region. The HV divid-
ers are placed outside the sensitive area.

The fact that the field is strong enough to achieve full depletion, means that a positive space charge 
exists everywhere within the sensitive area. The positive space charge creates an internal electric field 
directed such that the holes created during the absorption of external X-ray photons will drift toward 
the surface of the wafer, while the electrons drift parallel to the surface in the middle of the wafer. 
Under the influence of the applied external electric field, the electrons drift toward the anode line 
where they are pushed toward the anodes by the push-up electrodes on the back side. The electrons are 
collected by the anodes, each of which are wire-bonded to their own preamplifier. The HV dividers con-
trol the drift field by providing the necessary voltage levels to the drift cathodes and conductor traces, 
which connect the voltage divider and the cathodes. 
The prototype is equipped with three different types of drift channels as an experiment in regard to 
diffusion spread of the electron cloud and electron focusing. One of the channel groups does not have 
any focusing while the other two have different levels. There are 18 drift channels in each group adding 
to a total of 54 channels (and anodes). The charge cloud has a Gaussian shape with a width that de-

achieve the best energy resolution, the charge cloud should preferentially be collected by a single anode

been chosen with this aim, thereby ensuring an uncompromised output signal.

3.3 Process flow and experimental work 
A chronological process flow for the fabrication of the SDD was developed and presented by WS2013. 
The process flow is divided into 11 sections and each step has its own number allowing for easy step 
referral. The flow is furthermore provided with a color key for easy orientation regarding process type.  
WS2013 noted that the process flow was a guideline and that changes would have to be made. During 
their experimental work, they carried out parts of the process and performed relevant measurements. 
The work included oxide growth, and fabrication of the front side alignment marks and the voltage di-
vider structures. Changes were made to the process flow, based on their practical experience; these will 
be considered in the optimization process and possibly included in the optimized process flow described 
in section 5.

5 Process flow optimization 
This section will describe the optimization of the process flow created by WS2013. First, the considera-
tions regarding the optimization are accounted for and discussed. The first subsection also includes 
descriptions of preliminary test work and the obtained results. The optimized process flow is created 
from the optimization considerations and results, and utilized in the following prototype fabrication. 
Second, the structure of the optimized process flow is described and justified. 
The optimized process flow is located in Appendix B. 
5.1 Process considerations  
To ensure that the bulk silicon is easily depleted while using a reasonable electric field, it is desirable to 
have wafers of low doping. For this purpose, 12 n-type double side polished (DSP) wafers with high re-
sistivity (≥ 10000 Ω-cm) have been obtained along with test wafers with higher doping. The test wafers 
will be used in the preliminary fabrication tests. The high resistivity wafers and test wafers have a thick-
ness of 500-550 µm and 350 µm, respectively. 
In the original process flow, the RIE was specified as etching method in several process steps. However, 
as mentioned in section 3.3, the RIE method was not actually used during the experimental work per-
formed by WS2013. They obtained fine results using alternative etch methods. For this reason, and the 
fact that the clean RIE equipment is no longer available in the Danchip cleanroom, the RIE process will 
not be used in the optimized process flow. 
5.1.1 
Cleanliness 
Cleanliness is an important issue during the fabrication of the drift detector. Small imperfections on the 
wafer and the layers can easily result in an unusable device, and the cleanliness should therefore be as 
high as possible throughout the fabrication process. For this reason, supplementary cleaning steps are 
added to the process flow in addition to the mandatory RCA cleans already present. These cleaning 
steps involve a 7-up clean and are carried out after the removal of photoresist unless an RCA clean is 
planned. However, the 7-up clean cannot be performed when aluminium is present on the wafer, since 
the aluminium will be etched in the acid. When performing etches with the DRIE-Pegasus, it is possible 
to perform O2 cleans of the machine. In order to comply with the requirement of cleanliness, a 3 min 
clean is performed before using the equipment and 1 min cleans are carried out between each wafer 
processing as described in section 5.1.6. Furthermore, a generally high level of cleanliness is practiced 
throughout the fabrication process. This especially concerns the handling of the wafers. The clean han-
dling of wafers include: closing wafer boxes when not immediately used, not talking around wafers, and 
avoiding objects (other wafers, tools, arms, head, etc.) over wafers. Moreover, when wafers and 
photomasks have not been used for a while, a 7-up clean is performed. Cleaning with air is done during 
photolithography steps and when otherwise deemed necessary. 
The cleanliness is also a particularly important issue during the photolithography steps. In several steps, 
where the subsequent etch is a BHF oxide etch, photoresist is spun on both sides of the wafer in order 
to protect the other side during the later etch. WS2013 split up the etching process and performed pho-
tolithography and etch on first the front side and then the back side. In relation to the optimization of 
the process flow, it was considered to do the photolithography steps consecutively and then etch on 
both sides simultaneously. The choice of splitting up the process relates to the desire to protect the 
==PAGE:58==

Photolithography steps 
A large fraction of the steps throughout the fabrication process involve photolithography, and the pro-
cedures that are involved do not differ very much. A pretreatment involving either HMDS or BHF is car-
ried out first, in order to promote the adhesion between photoresist and substrate. When applying the 
photoresist with the SSE Spinner, two recipes are used depending on whether the resist is applied on 
one side or both sides – a proximity bake is carried out for the latter instead of a contact bake so that 
the resist does not come in direct contact with the hotplate. The recipes are: 
 
_DHC_100mm_AZ5214E_1,5µm 
_DHC_100mm_AZ5214E_1,5µm_proxbake 
The recipes specify the size of the wafer (100 mm), the resist type (AZ5214E), and resist thickness (1.5 
µm). Associated with the recipes are baking temperature and time. For the first recipe, the baking tem-
perature is 90 °C. For the proximity bake, the temperature is 95 °C due to the increased distance result-
ing from the proximity parameter. In both cases, the baking time is 90 sec. 
In the fabrication steps, the second proximity recipe is used when resist is spun on both sides; this is 
done in the case where the photoresist acts as a protective layer during etch. The critical wafer side 
should be processed last, in order to avoid equipment handling of the critical side’s photoresist. It is 
important to note that when etch is performed with the DRIE-Pegasus equipment, resist cannot be pre-
sent on the back side – the machine will not be able to function correctly and wafers with resist on the 
back side are therefore forbidden. 
The alignment and exposure procedure is optimized for the Aligner-6inch equipment. Parameters are 
set by the user prior to the alignment. These are set in recipes; the only difference between the em-
ployed recipes is whether it is top side or back side alignment. The contact mode of the aligner equip-

57 of 153 
ment is in all cases set to proximity contact – this differs from the previous choice of hard contact em-
ployed by WS2013. Although proximity contact has a poorer resolution than contact printing, it has 
been chosen since it generates far less defects. Additional masking parameters include the separation 
and proximity distance, which are both set to 20 µm. Furthermore, the exposure time is set. Tests were 
conducted with 3.0 sec and 2.5 sec, however, the values lead to overexposure. An optimal exposure 
time was found to be 2.0 sec. The exposure time is the same for all photolithography steps following 
coating with the SSE spinner, since the resist type and thickness are the same. After the exposure, the 
development is done by placing the wafers in a developer at a temperature of 22 °C ± 1 °C with agita-

throughout the fabrication process. The wafers are rinsed for 3 min after the development and then 
spun dry. The described development can be done in either the manual developer baths Developer-1 
and Developer-2, or in the Developer-6inch bench, which is an automated developer bath. The Develop-
er-6inch is generally used throughout the photolithography process steps and excellent results have 
been obtained.

tone strip, as described in section 2.3.3. 
5.1.3 
Alignment marks 
The alignments marks on the front side of the high resistivity wafers have already been fabricated by 
WS2013; as described in section 3.3, they etched the 300 nm SiO2 in BHF and the bulk silicon was etched 
with the DRIE-Pegasus to ensure visibility throughout the process. Since several layers are fabricated on 
both sides of the wafers it is preferable to have alignment marks on both sides, and the optimized proc-
ess flow will therefore include fabrication of alignment marks on the back side. It was initially found that 
the etch into the bulk silicon is redundant in reference to ensuring visibility; the etch of the 300 nm SiO2 
would have been enough. However, since subsequent process step now include stripping the oxide 
layer, see section 5.1.4, it is necessary to have the alignment marks etched into the wafer surface. 
The alignment marks on the front side establish the reference coordinate system for all later photomask 
processes. They are only aligned relative to the physical geometry of the wafer. The front side alignment 
marks are fabricated using the top side alignment technique. Since all features on the back side will have 
to be aligned to the ones on the front side, the alignment marks on the back side are fabricated using 
the back side alignment technique (in which they are aligned to the front side). This method involves an 
intermediate crosshair adjustment step between the photomask and substrate adjustment steps, where 
crosshairs are adjusted to the marks on the photomask. When the substrate is loaded, the crosshairs are 
adjusted to the marks on the bottom-side of the substrate. The mask is afterwards aligned to the cross-
hairs, and the exposure is then carried out on the top side, which is now aligned to the wafer’s front 
side. 
It would have been preferable to etch the alignment marks into the silicon wafer before growing the 
insulation layer, in order to avoid the difficulties associated with the combination of etching methods – 
as described in section 3.3, WS2013 experienced oxide defects on the back side of the wafer after the 
BHF etch. For the upcoming fabrication it will fortunately be possible to etch the alignment marks on the 
back side of the wafers after the stripping of the oxide mask used for the front side phosphorus implan-
tation, see section 5.1.4.

5.1.4 
Impurity doping 
Junctions are to be created in the semiconductor by impurity doping, where phosphorus and boron 
dopants are implanted to create the n+-n- junctions and the rectifying p+-n- junctions, respectively. The 
impurity doping includes the implantation of phosphorus in anode areas on the front side of the wafer 
and boron in the cathode areas on both sides. It was originally planned to perform the doping by ion 
implantations, but since no ion implantation facility is available at Danchip, the diffusion implantation 
was chosen instead. This optimization section will therefore include extensive preliminary test work. 
The diffusion doping procedure involves several thermal process steps, and the phosphorus diffusion 
should therefore be carried out before the boron diffusion, since boron is more susceptible to thermal 
processes. The full thermal budget for the SDD production is discussed in section 5.1.9. 
The phosphorus doping is done by gaseous diffusion in the Phosphorus Predep (A4) furnace; it is not 
anneal afterwards, since several thermal process steps follow. The boron doping is done by nongaseous 
diffusion. Boron doped silica glass (BSG) is pre-deposited on both sides of the wafer in the PECVD2, after 
which the boron is driven into the silicon during an annealing in the Anneal-Oxide (C1) furnace. Since 
only parts of the substrate should be doped, the remainder surface must be masked to exclude the 
dopant. The existing oxide insulation layer is used as masking material for the phosphorus diffusion. It 
will be stripped in BHF after the doping and a new oxide layer is grown as masking material for the bo-
ron diffusion. This oxide layer will also be stripped after the boron doping and a new insulation layer will 
then be grown. Photolithography steps are carried out prior to the diffusion processes to open the oxide 
mask layers so a direct contact can be created between the dopant sources and the silicon wafer. The 
associated etch is done in BHF with wetting agent for 4.5 min as a result of a SiO2 etch rate of 75-80 
nm/min and the  300 nm layer thickness. The etching time is obtained by using the slowest rate and

adding 30 sec.4 The wafers are wetted in water for a few minutes before etching. The wafers are rinsed 
for 5 min after etching and then spun dry.

10 µm. Secondly, the junction depth in the cathode areas affects the detection capabilities of the detec-
tor in regard to radiation energy. The detector should be capable of detecting X-ray radiation in the 
energy range 2-20 keV, and a large junction depth will cause the lower energies to go undetected. These 
factors should be kept in mind during the optimization.

5.1.6 
Voltage divider 
The material for the HV divider has been chosen to be boron doped poly-Si with a thickness of  300 nm. 
This thickness differs from the one chosen by WS2013 – they had a layer thickness of 1000 nm. How-
ever, it has been found that a smaller layer thickness is sufficient. The poly-Si is deposited using LPCVD. 
WS2013 did not investigate the parameters associated with poly-Si doping, and it will therefore be the 
main purpose of this optimization section and extensive preliminary test work will be carried out. 
In order to deplete the detector, a voltage of approximately 1000 V is applied over the voltage divider. 
The detector is defined as a low power detector. There is an upper limit to the amount of power used 
for the satellite payload and a fraction of this will go to the SDD. It is therefore desirable to have a low 
current in the HV divider indicating a high resistance. The resistivity and hence sheet resistance of doped 
poly-Si depends on the dopant concentration, which is controlled by the dopant gas flow during deposi-
tion. Higher flow incorporates more dopant into the poly-Si and leads to lower resistivity [Doering and 
Nishi, 2008, Chap. 13]. The B2H6 gas flow is set to a default value of 7 sccm. Deposition test carried out 
with Danchip test wafers were performed using the default value and an increased gas flow of 30 sccm. 
The resulting sheet resistance values were around a few kΩ and within acceptable ranges, however, a 
large variation in the measurements was noticed. It was therefore decided to anneal all the wafers at 
950 °C for 1 hour in the Anneal-Bond (C3) furnace. The procedure lead to a much lower and acceptable 
variation, but it also lead to significantly reduced sheet resistance values. The sheet resistance was 
found to vary somewhat across the wafer. It was subsequently found that a more homogeneous sheet 
resistance across the wafer was obtained when an amorphous silicon5 was created at a lower tempera-
ture using the LPCVD process. 
Due to the lower temperature, the deposition rate is reduced to  4 nm/min calling for a deposition time 
of 75 min in order to obtain a thickness of 300 nm. The wafers are annealed after the LPCVD process, 
thereby creating the desired polycrystalline silicon.

5.1.7 
Aluminium features 
The aluminium features consist of the anodes, cathodes and conductor traces. They are all fabricated 
together, since they are made from the same material. Aluminium has been chosen, since it has high 
electrical conductivity and low X-ray absorption. Previous attempts of the aluminium deposition and 
following etch have been unsatisfactory and have not resulted in desired step coverage, see section 
3.3.1. It has been proved difficult to get a good electrical contact across the edge of the poly-Si HV di-
vider, where the aluminium traces overlap the poly-Si. An alternative method is therefore investigated 
along with the original method. Extensive preliminary test work will therefore be carried out. 
5.1.7.1 
Original method 
With the original method, the aluminium is deposited using the Wordentec equipment. The deposition 
can be done by either electron beam (E-beam) evaporation or by sputtering. The process temperature is 
in both cases less than 80 °C, but the deposition rates differ significantly; the deposition rate for the 
evaporation is 1-1.5 nm/sec, while the sputtering rate is up to  0.25 nm/sec depending on process 
parameters. Witthøft and Sandborg-Olsen (2014) chose to deposit the aluminium using the sputtering 
technique due to the importance of step coverage. It should be noted that they deposited 500 nm of 
aluminium while having a poly-Si voltage divider with a thickness of 1000 nm. They did not achieve the 
desired step coverage. The poly-Si layer thickness has now been reduced to 300 nm. A layer thickness of 
500 nm aluminium should therefore be sufficient for coverage.  
Since the test wafers have a poly-Si layer of approximately 360 nm, an aluminium layer thickness of 600 
nm is deposited using the Wordentec equipment and the E-beam evaporation technique. Following the 
execution of the photolithography steps, the aluminium features are etched using wet aluminium etch. 
Etching is done with the solution: H2O:H3PO4 (1:2) at 50 °C. The technical specifications in Labmanager 
(2013b) specify that it is recommended to bake the wafer on a hotplate at 120 °C for 2 min or in an oven 
at 120 °C for 30 min prior to etching, in order to prevent huge undercut or delamination of the resist. 
The hotplate cannot be used when photoresist is present on the back side. With the original method, 
the aluminium is deposited on first the front side, which is then processed and etched. Subsequent to 
the deposition of aluminium on the back side, photoresist is deposited on both wafer sides, in order to 
protect the front side during the upcoming etch. The wafers are therefore baked on a hotplate at 120 °C 
for 100 sec during the front side processing and in an oven during the back side processing. For the 
method test, the aluminium is only deposited on the front side, which is then processed and etched. The 
photoresist cannot be stripped in the rough and fine acetone bath, since metals are not allowed in 
them. Instead, the resist is stripped in the lift-off acetone bath for 4-5 min. 
The nominal wet aluminium etch rate is  100 nm/min for pure aluminium, indicating an etching time of 
6 min. As mentioned in section 3.3.1, the etching rate was found to be lower than the theoretical values.

5.1.8 
Passivation oxide and bond holes 
The final device is protected by a passivation layer that ensures minimum penetration of impurities into 
the electric circuit. The passivation layer is deposited using PECVD. The layer consists of SiO2 deposited 
on both sides of the wafer, at a temperature of 300 °C. The use of plasma permits the reactions to take 
place at lower temperatures. This is an advantage, since it prevents melting and de-shaping of the alu-
minium structures; the melting point of aluminium is 660.32 °C. The deposition is done with PECVD3, 
because it allows for small amounts of metal to be placed on the substrate. A uniform silicon oxide layer 
is achieved with the LFSiO recipe, which has a deposition rate of  75 nm/min. These parameters were 
also found by WS2013. 
WS2013 proposed a layer thickness of 300 nm, thereby having a deposition time of 4 min per wafer 
side. However, since several layer structures are larger than 300 nm, it has been decided to increase the 
layer thickness of the passivation layer. The thickest structure is the aluminium, which is 500 nm, for 
which reason the passivation layer thickness is increased to 600 nm. It should, though, be noted that the 
poly-Si for the HV divider and the aluminium connect traces overlap on small areas, leading to a com-
bined layer thickness of  800 nm. This might result in uncovered sidewalls. The deposition time is calcu-
lated to be 8 min. 
Following the deposition of the passivation layer, bond holes are created through it. The bond holes are 
created in a photolithography process and etched in BHF with wetting agent. The etching time of the 
PECVD-SiO2 is unknown, which is why tests are performed. The etch rate is found to be approximately 
225 nm/min. In previous etching steps, an additional 30 sec are added to the etching time, however, 
since it is of interest to protect the layers underneath the passivation layer, only 5 sec are added. The 
large decrease in the additional etching time is due to the fact that the oxide insulation layer and the 
aluminium features underneath the passivation layer are both susceptible to the BHF etch. The etch rate 
of thermal-SiO2 is 75-80 nm/min and a 4:1 etch relationship is found with aluminium. 
The bond holes through the passivation layer will thus be etched for 2.75 min. The wafers are wetted in 
water prior to etching. The wafers are rinsed for 5 min afterwards and then spun dry.

The reasons for the chosen order of the sections in the process flow is rather obvious. The sequence is

should occur before the boron implantation, since boron diffusion is more susceptible to thermal proc-

prior to the voltage divider but after the implantation, since the vias are connection points through the 
insulation layer between the implants and the poly-Si voltage divider. Then, the HV divider is fabricated. 
Since the conductor traces are going to overlap the HV divider structure, it will have to be fabricated 
prior to the aluminium features. This leads to the sixth and seventh section, which involves the fabrica-
tion of electrode vias, and the electrodes and the conductor traces, which are fabricated together, since 
they are made from the same material. Again, the vias are created first, since they are connection points 
through the insulation layer between the implants and the aluminium. The eighth section involves the 
growth of the passivation layer. Since the purpose of layer is to protect the final device, it is obviously 
fabricated last. The ninth section concerns the fabrication of bond holes in the passivation layer.

6.1 Defects and their criticality 
It has previously been described how it is a requirement that all circuit elements of the detector are 
operational. Defects and flaws can influence the detection system, which is why the wafers are visually 
inspected in a microscope throughout the fabrication process. The severity of system defects are cate-
gorized in three groups: mild and undisruptive, distorting, and severe and disruptive. The defects can 
either be interruptions or connections between adjacent features.  
Connections between boron p+ implants will lead to short circuits. If only a few biased features are con-
nected, the connection can be accepted, and the result will be a distorted potential distribution inside 
the wafer. However, if several biased features are connected, the potential distribution will become too 
distorted and result in a disrupted system. The same conditions apply in relation to the aluminium fea-
tures. Interruptions in the aluminium or p+ implants can to some extend be accepted, since the electrical 
system will still have contact through the vias. However, if a via point is located in an interrupted section 
of the p+ implants, contact will be created between the overlaying material (aluminium or poly-Si) and 
the bulk silicon. This contact will not be rectifying and the defect can thus lead to a disrupted system 
depending on the conductivity of the materials.

6.11 Design and process remarks 
During the fabrication of the detector, aspects regarding the design were found to cause problems. 
Throughout the photolithography alignment steps, it became obvious that the design of the alignment 
==PAGE:89==
87 of 153 
marks areas could be improved. As previously described, visibility through dark field photomasks was a 
problem. The problem could be solved by adding a large square around the alignment marks on dark 
field photomasks. Furthermore, it was in general troublesome to locate the alignment marks on the 
photomasks due to their small coverage area. On the alignment mask, long lines were employed in the 
design on three sides of the marks – this feature should be utilized on all the photomasks. In addition, it 
would be helpful to increase the number of lines and create arrows on them to aid the navigation. 
When spinning photoresist on the wafers, irregularities around the logo was occasionally observed – 
resist was missing across lines of the logo. The logo should possibly be removed or redesigned, though 
some features must be present, in order to distinguish between front and back side – it should be possi-
ble to cover the features with a 2" wafer. 
An additional element, which should be mentioned in regard to the photomasks design, is the oversight 
of adding cutting lines – when the final device has been fabricated on the 4" wafer, it should be cut out 
to fit test-evaluation equipment. The lack of cutting lines makes the process unnecessarily difficult, 
which is why cutting lines should be added to alignment photomask. The placement of cutting lines is 
indicated in Figure 6.30. 
In order to investigate the resulting doping profiles of the implanted junctions, an additional test struc-
ture should be created. The test structure should be two large identifiable areas, which should be boron 
and phosphorus doped, respectively. After the wafer is cut out to fit the test-evaluation equipment and 
the test structure is separated from the detector, spectrometry measurements can be performed on the 
doped areas, and doping profiles can thus be obtained.

In addition to the above described fabrication-step-problems, the design could also be optimized in 
relation to the defect issues. The size of the critical area, where occurring defects will distort the system, 
is quite large compared to the sensitive detector area. As previously described, defects causing system 
short circuits were observed after the photolithography implantation steps. The distorting implantation 
defects were primarily located under the connect traces and the voltage divider. Since implants at these 
locations are designed as a way of representing the electrical field on the surface they are not necessary 
in relation to a functioning detector and it could therefore be considered to remove them from the 
design. This would result in a smaller critical area, where defects will distort or disrupt the system. How-
ever, since implants at these locations do serve an electrically related purpose, it could be of interest to 
remove only part of them thereby increasing the gap between adjacent implants. It should be noted 
that if the underlying p+ implants and associated via points are removed from the design, it will not be 
possible to create the electrical contact from below as described in section 5.1.7.3. However, the con-
tact method is merely an auxiliary feature, since the design is made with the intention of creating con-
tact directly between the aluminium and poly-Si and it has been proven possible to establish electrical 
contact in the desired way. 
Following the completed fabrication of the prototype, elements of the process flow were found to need 
further optimization. The fabrication of the insulation layer is described in section 6.4. The desired and 
expected oxide layer thickness was 300 nm, however, the resulting thickness was only 260 nm. The 
oxide growth was carried out in the Anneal-Oxide (C1) furnace at 1050 °C for 30 min. In comparison, the 
thickness of the wet oxide mask grown in the Phosphorus Drive-in (A3) furnace at 1050 °C for 30 min 
was 305 nm. The deposition rate in the C1 furnace is thus less than the one in the A3 furnace, although 
the temperature and time are the same. In order to obtain a thicker insulation layer, the process time in 
step #36 should be increased to 35 min. The increase is documented in the optimized process flow in 
Appendix B. The applied time is documented in the applied process flow in Appendix C. 
As mentioned in section 6.7, the SSE Spinner coating equipment was responsible for creating a flawed 
resist-protection-layer on the non-critical wafer side, which then lead to unwanted oxide defects. An 
effort should possibly be made to optimize the protection of the non-critical wafer side. Solutions to the 
problem could be a highly increased resist thickness or applying blue tape onto to the flawed resist-
protection-layer. In any case, it is important to examine the non-critical process side as well as the criti-
cal process side throughout the fabrication process. 
In addition to the remarks regarding the photomask design and the optimized process flow, remarks 
should also be made to the fabrication process itself. It is a known fact that the fabrication process is 
long and time consuming, though altogether manageable. However, due to equipment booking, mal-
functions, and breakdowns additional waiting time has to be taken into account. An appropriate amount 
of time, where delays were taken into account, was scheduled for the CubeWATCH prototype fabrica-
tion. However, during the fabrication of the prototype an equipment was out of use for approximately 
three weeks, which lead to a significant delay. Nevertheless, since the original schedule was made for 
the two-batch (of three wafers) approach as well as half days in the cleanroom, the fabrication was 
completed in time by changing the two-batch process to a one-batch-and-a-test-wafer process and by 
altering the amount of time spent in cleanroom each day. In connection with time delay, it should also 
be noted that iterations are sometimes necessary. If the photolithography result on an exposed and 
developed wafer is unacceptable, the resist will have to be stripped and the wafer should afterwards be 
cleaned. The photolithography steps should be repeated, starting with the HMDS pretreatment. Al-

Resistance measurements across the sensitive area seemed to indicate that the entire area was electri-
cally conductive. One of the wafers was cut into several pieces in order to investigate and measure dif-
ferent regions separately. The regions and cutting lines are illustrated in Figure 7.6.

Possible causes to the apparent problem are identified, considered, and possibly further investigated. 
The causes and associated considerations are described in the following paragraphs. 
Contact between adjacent aluminium cathodes 
The aluminium features are visually inspected and found acceptable. It is very unlikely that the problem 
is caused by contact between adjacent aluminium cathodes. 
Contact between aluminium and bulk silicon 
Direct contact between the aluminium and the bulk silicon will cause a short circuit from below, because 
electrons can be transferred from the aluminium to the bulk. As previously described, several oxide 
defects were detected through visible inspection, and contact is thus made between the aluminium and 
the bulk. However, it is unlikely that contact between the aluminium and bulk is the sole cause of the 
apparent problem. 
Diode malfunction: non-rectifying 
If the p+ boron implants do not create the desired rectifying junctions, electrons might be able to travel 
from the aluminium to the bulk. The diode function has, however, already been verified in section 7.1. 
Similar measurements are carried out between anodes and cathodes and similar results are obtained. 
The p-n junctions are rectifying. 
Malfunction of insulation layer 
There is an offset between the aluminium features and the implants, as mentioned in section 3.1, page 
45. If the oxide insulation layer between the doped wafer and the aluminium does not insulate as ex-
pected, it will cause the entire cathode and anode area to short circuit. However, the fact that an elec-
trical resistance could not be measured between the p+ cathodes and the n+ anodes in both directions 
show that the insulation layer does work as intended. 
Contact between boron implants 
The cause is examined through resistance measurements on the cut-up wafer. Measurements are car-
ried out in a cathode region without via points and a cathode region with one row of vias. The meas-
urements on the cathode region without any vias correspond with the expected, however, the meas-
urements on the region with a single via row do not. The resistance between adjacent cathodes on the 
latter region is very small – the presence of vias do thus appear to create contact between the cathodes. 
The apparent problem is therefore induced by the p+ boron implants, assuming that a contact is some-
how created between them. During the fabrication of the boron implants, the doped areas were visible 
after the growth of the insulation layer. The features were in general well defined and accepted, so if 
contact is established, it is most likely within the substrate. 
The problem site has been isolated to the wafer interior and the boron doped areas. The edges of a 
piece of the cut-up wafer6 are examined with a scanning electron microscope (SEM), where the conduc-
tion abilities of the different areas might be visible as different nuances. A SEM image is seen in Figure 
7.7. The images do, unfortunately, not provide any information about the doped areas. The aluminium 
cathodes and the oxide insulation layer are distinguishable, however, the doped areas are not. The SEM 
images do thus not provide any new information to the investigation of the apparent problem.

8 Technological features of the drift detector 
This section describes the theoretical studies regarding technological features of the drift detector. First, 
the advantages related to detector type is addressed. The properties of the drift detector are compared 
to the alternative strip detector. Second, the possibilities regarding the imaging device are discussed. 
The chosen RMC technique is compared to the coded aperture mask. Third, theoretical calculations are 
carried out for the voltage divider resistance in reference to the potential progression. 
8.1 Detector type 
Semiconductor diode detectors are applied in many radiation detection applications due their ad-
vantages compared to the equivalent gas-filled detectors. The detector dimensions can be kept smaller 
because solid densities are much greater than that of a gas. Furthermore, for a given energy deposit a 
much larger number of carriers will be generated in semiconductor materials than with any other com-
mon detector type [Knoll, 2000]. When the semiconductor detector should be able to sense the position 
of interaction of the incident radiation together with its energy, a position-sensitive detector is neces-
sary. There are several position sensitive semiconductor detectors available, in which the electrodes are 
segmented into strips, pads or pixels. The silicon strip detector have a series of narrow parallel strips 
fabricated on the surface. The detector is used for widespread applications in the tracking of high-
energy particles and imaging of X-rays. The principle is much like that of the drift detector, however, 
both the cathodes and the anodes are designed as long strips. As a result of their large length, the 
readout strips will have a significant capacitance. 
An important factor of a detector is the signal to noise ratio. The noise in a detector system depends on 
a number of parameters: geometry of the detector, the biasing scheme, the readout electronics, etc. 
The detector capacity at the input of a charge sensitive preamplifier is often the dominant noise source 
in the system.  
The silicon drift detector with linear geometry is designed with parallel drift cathodes and point-like 
anodes. Unlike the long strip anode used in the silicon strip detector, with capacitance of 10-20 pF, the 
point-like anodes in the drift detector ensure a low capacitance (10-100 fF) [Spieler, 2005, Chap. 1]. It is 
therefore possible to design the preamplifiers with very low noise. The drift detector will thus have a 
better energy resolution and a lower energy threshold for the X-ray photons than can be achieved with 
conventional silicon strip detectors. As previously mentioned, the CubeWATCH detector should be ca-
pable of detecting X-ray radiation in the energy range 2-20 keV. 
The lower energy threshold of the silicon drift detector is a great advantage in the X-ray astronomy field. 
The detectors can be designed to include the low energies in the interval from 1-2 keV to 5-6 keV; this 
implies a large increase in the number of photons that can be registered from cosmic sources. Also, 
several of the atomic element lines that are important to the understanding of X-ray emission from 
astronomical objects, are located in this low energy band. For instance is it of particular interest to be 
able to detect the energy of iron’s characteristic radiation at 6.7 keV, since iron is present in many X-ray 
sources. In order to obtain a continuous spectrum around 6 keV, it is necessary to detect energies at 3-4 
keV – this is possible with the drift detector. The lower energy threshold of the drift detector is also very 
advantageous when investigating soft X-rays sources.

8.2 Imaging device 
A widely used imaging system is the coded aperture mask, in which a pinhole camera is used as a modu-
lation imager. Since only a small fraction of the light passes through the pinhole, the coded mask con-
tains numerous holes in a specific pattern. The principle is illustrated in Figure 8.1. The imaging system 
has a coded mask in front of a detection device, as illustrated in Figure 8.2. Images from multiple aper-
tures will then overlap at the detector. The image can be reconstructed by using an algorithm depend-
ent on the aperture configuration – the pattern in the detector is correlated with the known pattern in 
the mask and the two patterns match only in the direction to the source [Seward and Charles, 2010, 
Chap. 3]. 
  
Figure 8.1: Coded mask principle. 
 
Figure 8.2: Coded aperture mask imaging system. 
Another imaging system is the previously described RMC, see section 2.1.1. It was described how the 
RMC requires a detector with only one dimensional position determination and that the silicon drift 
detector fulfills this requirement. The images obtained through coded mask are much better than those 
from the RMC technique. The RMC has, nevertheless, been chosen as imaging system for the silicon drift 
detector for the following reasons: The coded mask needs several hundred signal lines, while the RMC 
only needs two signal lines – the RMC will thus require much less electrical power, which is desirable for 
the CubeWATCH system. Furthermore, a coded mask system must be mounted on a three-axis stabilized 
spacecraft system, whereas the RMC is designed to be placed on the simpler spin-stabilized spacecraft 
system. The three-axis system is much more costly than the spin-stabilized system; for a student satel-
lite project it is desirable to keep the cost at a minimum.

9.1 Astrophysical X-ray sources 
There are several astronomical objects that emit X-rays in the energy range 2-20 keV in addition to the

The strongest X-ray source in the solar system is the Sun. During solar flare occurrences, the X-ray flux 
from the Sun is greatly increased. If CubeWATCH was pointing toward the Sun during solar flares, the 
flare would clearly be visible in the observed signal.  
Scorpius X-1 and Cygnus X-1 are two sources in our galaxy that will stand out in the 2-20 keV energy 
band of CubeWATCH. Scorpius X-1 has a very soft spectrum. It is a neutron star in a low mass X-ray bina-
ry system and the strongest persistent X-ray source below 10 keV. The source will cause high count rates 
at lower energies and will thus be a disturbance in the detection of weaker sources, when it is in the 
field of view. Cygnus X-1 belongs to a high mass X-ray binary system with a blue supergiant as compan-
ion. It has for long been considered a black hole candidate [Brandt, 1994]. Cygnus X-1 exhibits two spec-
tral states: the hard-low state and the soft-high state. The soft X-ray spectrum in the soft-high state is 
believed to originate from an accretion disk around the black hole. Cygnus X-1 is, however, most of the 
time in the low-hard state and its spectrum is thus very hard like several other black hole candidates 
[Shaposhnikov and Titarchuk, 2006].

higher energy band, while Scorpius is brightest in the lower energy band and hardly visible in the higher 
band. Both sources will, though, be visible in the CubeWATCH 2-20 keV energy band and their spectra

9.2 Background components 
In addition to the sources that stand out in the sky, there are also background components. These in-
clude the cosmic diffuse X-rays, cosmic ray particles, and trapped radiation. 
The energy density in the X-ray band is dominated by a cosmic diffuse radiation known as the diffuse X-
ray background (XRB). For a detector with a large field of view like CubeWATCH, most of the detected X-
ray flux will come from the XRB. The X-ray band of the XRB spectrum can be split into several parts 
where different components dominate the observed intensity. An empirical fit to the XRB spectrum 
from 3 keV to 6 Mev has been obtained [Fabian and Barcons, 1992]. The spectrum can from 3 keV to 60

The second important background component is the corpuscular cosmic radiation. The cosmic ray parti-
cles are high energy radiation that strikes the Earth from space. The particles mainly originate outside 
the solar system but solar energetic particles associated with solar flares and other energetic solar 
events also contribute to the particle background. The particles that originate outside the solar system 
are referred to as galactic cosmic rays. The flux of the incoming rays at the Earth’s upper atmosphere 
depends on the energy of the cosmic rays, the solar wind, and the Earth’s magnetic field. As a result of 
the high energy of the cosmic ray, interaction with the detector system will cause dead time in the elec-
tronics, making observation impossible until the system has recovered. The cosmic rays will thus not 
contribute to the detected signal like the diffuse XRB, but instead interrupt the detection over a period 
of time.

terms of source intensity in units of Crab is found in the two energy bands by comparing the count rates. 
Cygnus X-1 has an intensity of 20 % that of the Crab Nebula in the low energy band. In the high energy 
band the value is 32 %. This indicates that a source with an intensity of 200 mCrab can be detected with 
10σ sensitivity after 6.25 min in the 2-6 keV energy band and that a source of 320 mCrab can be detect-
ed with 10σ sensitivity after 9 min for 6-20 keV. 
The calculated sensitivities for the two known sources serve as an expression for the sensitivity of the 
CubeWATCH detector. The result is compared to the original WATCH instrument, on which the detector 
system is based. The system was sensitive in the 6-150 keV energy range and had an effective area of 
approximately 45 cm2 [Lund, 2013]. The WATCH-EURECA count rates for the Crab Nebula in the 6-8 keV 
and 8-11 keV energy bands were 10.35 ± 0.14 and 7.05 ± 0.07 respectively. However, on average the 
detector only registers half of the count due to blocking by the modulation grid [Brandt, 1994]. The 
sensitivity of the original WATCH instrument in these energy bands is thus slightly higher than for 
CubeWATCH. However, the overall sensitivity in the 2-20 keV energy band will be higher for Cube-
WATCH, since it is able to detect radiation below the WATCH lower limit of 6 keV.

10 Conclusion 
The master thesis has addressed the development and fabrication of the CubeWATCH silicon drift detec-
tor for detection of X-ray radiation originating from astronomical objects in the energy range 2-20 keV. 
Part of the thesis work was based on previous work carried out in a theoretical introductory project and 
a subsequent bachelor thesis, where photomasks and the process flow necessary to produce a proto-
type detector had been created. The purpose of the master thesis included an optimization of the proc-
ess flow and correction of errors on the photomasks with the aim of completing the fabrication of a 
detector prototype. The functionality of the fabricated prototype should afterwards be investigated. The 
detector characteristics and performance should then be evaluated, if possible. In addition, theoretical 
studies of the astrophysical purpose and the technological advantages of the drift detector should be 
carried out, and the sensitivity of the detector should be estimated. 
The known errors on the photomasks were corrected and new masks were fabricated. 
In regard to the process flow optimization, a high level of cleanliness was to be incorporated. Cleaning 
steps were therefore added to the process flow after the resist removal steps, unless an RCA clean was 
planned. The wafer handling was kept clean, and equipment and wafers were cleaned with air when 
necessary. The photolithography steps in the process flow are generally the same, and were optimized 
for a proximity contact exposure on the Aligner-6inch equipment. The first part of the process flow in-
cludes the fabrication of alignment marks. They were initially planned to be fabricated on the front side 
after the growing of the oxide layer, however, in the optimized process flow they are fabricated on both 
sides before the oxide layer is grown. The optimization of the process flow furthermore entailed the 
method of impurity doping. Extensive optimization was in this connection necessary; the doping was 
originally planned to be done by ion implantations, but since no ion implantation facility is available at 
Danchip, the diffusion implantation was chosen instead. The phosphorus doping was done by gaseous 
diffusion while the boron doping was done by non-gaseous diffusion. Boron doped silica glass was de-
posited on the wafers and the boron was then driven in by thermal processes. Based on parameters 
used by Thomsen (2014), acceptable doping results were obtained. Doping was also an issue in the op-
timization of the voltage divider fabrication steps along with uniformity of the deposited poly-Si. The 
voltage divider should have a high and uniform resistance; extensive optimization and testing was per-
formed in order to comply with both requirements. The sheet resistance obtained in the preliminary 
work was  0.2 kΩ/sq. The uniformity across the wafer was high and a maximum variation of 15 Ω/sq 
was observed. The sheet resistance is acceptable, but lower than desired, which is why further optimiza-
tion should possibly be carried out to increase the value. The fabrication of the aluminium features was 
also optimized. Originally, the aluminium was to be etched in a wet aluminium etch. The method had, 
however, previously caused problems, which is why the alternative lift-off process was tested. The re-
sults were vastly improved and electrical contact was established between the poly-Si and the alumin-
ium. The lift-off process was therefore chosen for the optimized process flow. 
The prototype fabrication was carried out using the optimized process flow. In general, the fabrication 
processes went well. The structures were well aligned to each other and the dimensions were accept-
able, although some were slightly overexposed/underexposed despite consistent exposure time. A volt-
age divider sheet resistance of 0.275 kΩ/sq was obtained, and the uniformity across the wafer was very 
high with only a few ohms variation. Defects of different severity were present on the critical areas of 
the wafers – the most severe defects had occurred due to flawed protection resist on the non-critical 
==PAGE:128==
126 of 153 
fabrication side. The flaws in the protection resist were created by the coating equipment. Additional 
processing, which was not included in the process flow, was carried out to rectify unsuccessful fabrica-
tion result. The additional processing provided successful results. 
Throughout the fabrication process, experiences were made in regard to the photomask design and 
optimized process. Even though the process flow had been optimized prior to the fabrication, elements 
were found to need further optimization. The alignment process were at times troublesome, since the 
alignment marks could be hard to locate due to their small coverage area. In addition, visibility through 
the dark field masks was a problem, since the open areas were also quite small. Both problems could be 
solved by redesigning the alignment areas on the photomasks.  
Investigations of the prototype functionality were then carried out. The first investigations entailed 
resistance measurements on test structures. The obtained measurements were used to calculate the 
sheet resistance of impurity doped areas, knowing the length and width values of the test structures. 
Statistical calculations were used to evaluate the results. The calculated data were in general accept-
able, most having low standard deviations and comparable mean values. The test structure measure-
ments showed that the sheet resistance of the boron doped areas is within an interval of 30-40 Ω/sq, 
while the sheet resistance of the phosphorus doped areas is around 7 Ω/sq. The values are comparable 
to what was expected. The second part of the functionality investigations concerned the sensitive area. 
Previous resistance measurements across the sensitive area suggested that the entire area was electri-
cally conductive, which is why investigations of possible short circuit causes were carried out. The ap-
parent problem were found to be induced by the p+ boron implants, and it was assumed that a contact 
was somehow created between them. Further investigations were carried out through current-voltage 
characteristics of the junction diodes.  -  characteristics were obtained for different p-n and p-n-p junc-
tions. All characteristics and associated calculations complied with what was theoretically expected. The 
 -  characteristics showed that the implants are not connected, and that light had affected the previous 
resistance measurements. The assumed problem did not exist, and the detector components were 
found to function correctly, thus verifying the detector functionality. 
Theoretical studies of technological features of the drift detector were carried out. The silicon drift de-
tector was compared to the silicon strip detector, and the applied RMC technique was compared to the 
coded aperture mask in reference to choice of imaging device. Resistance calculations were carried out 
for the voltage divider in regard to the potential progression. Assuming a theoretical scenario with linear 
potential progression, the maximum acceptable resistance change was found to be 8 %. It was further-
more found that the local resistances can vary much more than 8 %, assuming a scenario where the 
local resistances vary with a random number – local resistance changes were repeatedly found to be 
acceptable at 25 %; variations at 50 % were also acceptable in some cases. 
Theoretical studies of the astrophysical purpose were also carried out. The efficiency and solid angle of 
the CubeWATCH detector were estimated. The thickness of the implants and the dead layers on the 
surface were taken into account when estimating the efficiency. The influence of the dead layers and 
the implants were clearly seen at lower energies; photon energies around 2 keV are inefficiently detect-
ed. The solid angle and the efficiency were used to calculate count rates as a function of energy for the 
background and typical bright sources. The count rates were then used to estimate the detector sensi-
tivity as a function of time for two energy bands.

11 Future work 
Evaluation of the functioning detector prototype should be conducted through performance test with 
an X-ray source. The detector characteristics and the influence of the wavy cathodes on the lateral diffu-
sion spread of the charge cloud should be determined in order to find the optimal cathode shape. 
The optimization of the voltage divider fabrication entailed investigations and optimization of the sheet 
resistance. An acceptable and highly uniform value was obtained, however, it was not as high as desired. 
Future work should therefore possibly include optimization of the process steps, with the aim of increas-
ing the voltage divider sheet resistance while maintaining the achieved uniformity – changes should be 
incorporated into the optimized process flow. 
Additional process flow optimization could be necessary. Defects were observed on the critical areas of 
the detector – the defects were found to be created by the coating equipment on the non-critical wafer 
side during coating of the critical side. Future work should possibly investigate how to optimize the pro-
tection of non-critical wafer side. In this connection, it might be of interest to investigate the advan-
tages, disadvantages, and limitations of dry etch methods. 
Remarks have been made to the photomask design based on experiences made throughout the fabrica-
tion process, and improved and additional design features have been recommended. Future work 
should involve redesign of photomask with incorporation of these features. The features were described 
in section 6.11 and include the following: 
- 
Guiding lines with arrows for alignment marks on all photomasks 
- 
Large squares around alignment marks for visibility (on dark field photomasks) 
- 
Cutting lines 
- 
Test structure for determination of doping level 
Optional features: 
- 
Remove or redesign logo on alignment mask – some features must be present in order to dis-
tinguish between wafer sides (it should be possible to cover the features with a 2" wafer during 
exposure of the back side) 
- 
Remove part of the implants under the connect traces and the voltage divider along with asso-
ciated via points 
The redesign of the photomasks should furthermore include adjustment of cathode shape, that is, when 
the optimal shape have been determined. 
When new photomasks have been produced with the adjusted cathode shape and the additional rec-
ommended features, a functional CubeWATCH detector should be fabricated.
