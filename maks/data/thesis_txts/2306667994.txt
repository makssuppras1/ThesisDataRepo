Physiological and behavioral response among Atlantic cod (Gadus
morhua) to the usage of an underwater time of ﬂight image acquisition
system based on LiDAR technology
by Simon JARNIT
This thesis investigated if exposure to the laser light source of the under-
water time of ﬂight (UTOFIA) system is stressful to Atlantic cod (Gadus
morhua) and brown crab (Cancer pagurus). This was done through a set of
controlled laboratory experiments where heart rate (fH) was measured as
an indicator of stress in ten free-swimming cod (tank dimensions: 8.5 x
2.25 meters, water height 1.5 m), and behavioural responses were inves-
tigated using 2D video sequences to detect spatial distribution and swim
behaviour. Behavioural response of brown crab was additionally evaluated
as observations of altered movement patterns when positioned at 0.5, 1 and
1.5m from the UTOFIA laser. Individual, average baseline fH of cod ranged
within the normal range reported for cod at similar temperatures and there
was no signiﬁcant change in mean fH of cod as a response to the UTOFIA
laser, nor did the laser cause any avoidance reaction. The average swim-
ming speeds were highly comparable when the laser was off and on, and
were similar to values reported for cod in the ﬁeld. The brown crab had no
observable change in movement in response to the laser exposure. It was
concluded that no adverse effects were observed in the tested species.

Introduction
1.1
The challenge
It is currently challenging to obtain high quality underwater images. There
are different reasons to why this is e.g. small particles suspended in the
water create a turbid environment which is a limiting factor to the quality
of the imaging, it is dark and distant. The natural solution to avoid the
issues with darkness and available ambient light is to use an illumination
source, but with an active light source the particles create backscatter at a
close range resulting in a degradation of the image contrast. When a light
beam enters seawater it is attenuated by absorption, resulting in less illumi-
nation for targets that are further away from the source. The attenuation of
the light beam is dependent on suspended and dissolved particulate mat-
ter, and scattering and attenuation of pure water [1, 2, 3]. This attenuation
makes it even more difﬁcult to obtain images at a close range of high quality
when it is night or the object of interest is in deep water.
The Horizon 2020 project has the objective to develop a new generation
of underwater imaging system to overcome these challenges. The under-
water time of ﬂight image acquisition system (UTOFIA) concept is to make
a new class of underwater imaging system to advance various mariculture
activities.
1.2
State-of-the-art underwater imaging and UTOFIA
Today, there is a large variety of camera systems with different advantages
to one another. At close range, camera systems with active illumination are
often preferred, however, such systems have short imaging range limited
to few meters or centimetres dependent on water clarity and the sensitivity
of the camera [3, 4]. In harbours where there is often high turbidity, the
backscatter prevents the optical camera in receiving high-quality images,
thereby the inspection time and difﬁculty related to manoeuvring is in-
creased. Both real-time image processing and controllable polarization are
methods used to limit and reduce the backscatter at the cost of increasing
noise of the image [4, 5]. Acoustical imaging sensors, such as sonar is often
used in low visibility conditions at greater depths. The majority of sonars

creates 2D images, often based on multi-beam technology that allows for vi-
sualization of static and moving objects without interference from turbidity
[6]. The range for sonars is usually between 10-200 meters, but with very
low resolution, making them unsuitable for e.g. species recognition. There-
fore, sonars are primarily used for navigation. In addition to 2D sonars,
there are also 3D sonars with the obvious difference in the amount of di-
mensional imaging. In comparison to 2D acoustic methods like multi-beam
and sector scanning sonars, the 3D systems are able to image targets in 3D
with no physical scanning as it ensoniﬁes larger areas at a time. As an ex-
ample, the Echoscope by Coda Octopus uses 128 x 128 beams providing
good 3D data, but with room for improvement resolution wise [7, 8]. An-
other system is the laser line or sync scanner, which is based on scanning
the object of interest with repeated illumination. A relatively small part of
the object is scanned at a time i.e. a line or point until the whole object
is processed. This is time-consuming and not suitable for moving objects
like e.g. marine wildlife, but rather limited to high precision surveys of
underwater structures. New promising techniques are developed to avoid
backscattered light and improve the contrast by having a range-gated re-
ceiver and a pulsed source. The idea of having a range-gated receiver with
a pulsed source is synchronizing the receiver with the reﬂected light from
the object or target. When the light is backscattered the receiver is disabled
in order to remove this negative effect on the images.

1.2.1
The niche
UTOFIA uses range-gating as it can be used to obtain 3D data; it increases
the range factor by 2-3 times in comparison to standard cameras and depth
information is attainable by having the receiver open at different time in-
tervals. UTOFIA is using a light detection and ranging (LiDAR) sensor to
increase performance and reduce the size and complexity of the system.
UTOFIA is targeted at some of the before mentioned issues by entering
a niche between short-range/high-resolution optical camera systems and
long-range/low-resolution sonar systems.
1.3
Applications
1.3.1
Fisheries management
There are various negative anthropogenic activities related to the status of
our oceans, such as pollution, intense ﬁshing, eutrophication and the intro-
duction of new species, all of which have led to alterations in the marine
ecosystems. In order to secure that people in the future can make a living
from the ocean, we need to know how to balance different and conﬂicting
goals, by obtaining new analytical approaches and gather data for robust
assessment [9]. The Common Fisheries Policy (CFP) aims to
"ensure exploitation of living aquatic resources that provides
sustainable economic, environmental and social conditions".
In order to reach such a prestigious goal, it is a prerequisite to survey the
ocean, stressing the need for obtaining reliable imaging data. Several ﬁsh
stocks are for example, being fully or overexploited, and there are concerns
about bycatch and discarding which may undermine the measures in ﬁsh-
eries management [10, 11]. Still, people are eating more ﬁsh products than
ever and the supply of ﬁsh as food is increasing at an annual average rate
of 3.2% [10]. This demand and development are caused by a growing hu-
man population (expected to reach 9.6 billion people in 2050) combined
with technological advances and simpliﬁcations within the capture, pro-
cessing, distribution and sale chain [10]. UTOFIA aims to provide new fea-
tures to support the implementation of both the CFP and marine strategy
framework directive (MSFD), as the system can provide high quality and
real time 3D data to aid in e.g. stock assessment, recognition of marine
life and size information. With time UTOFIA aims to enable ﬁshermen to
pre-screen the targeted species before landing, an innovative approach that
would help to relief unwanted catches which is highly relevant in relation
to the recent EU discard ban.

1.3.2
Marine litter
In Europe, the MSFD has set up a framework to achieve Good Environ-
mental Status (GES) by 2020 for their marine waters. There are currently 11
descriptors being evaluated to achieve GES. The 10th descriptor is targeted
at marine litter. GES is achieved for the descriptor if
"properties and quantities of marine litter do not cause harm
to the coastal and marine environment".
Not only is it important to address this matter for the sake of the environ-
ment, but it also causes economic losses for ﬁshing, shipping and tourism.
A recent study revealed that for 86% of Scottish vessels surveyed, marine
litter had a negative impact on the number of catches. Furthermore, it was
estimated that marine litter costs the Scottish ﬂeet between 11.7 and 13 mil-
lion Euro on average per year ≈5 % of the total revenue for affected ﬁsh-
eries [12, 13]. Approximately 1% of the total revenue for affected ﬁsheries in
Europe, is lost because of this issue [14]. Marine litter consists primarily of
plastics and recent studies have shown that there is a continuous increase in
the global annual production of 299 million tonnes. Other studies have es-
timated that approximately 10 % of all plastic debris ends up in the oceans
where it accumulates [15]. The attention towards marine litter is increasing
and at the 7th Environment Action Programme it was agreed that there is a
need for an EU-wide;
"quantitative reduction headline target for marine litter, sup-
ported by source-based measures and taking into account ma-
rine strategies established by Member States".
It is clear that there is a need to assess and monitor marine litter in order
to reach such targets. UTOFIA will with its increased detection and 3D
imaging be able to inspect larger areas than what is currently possible and
provide volumetric estimates of the pollution.
1.3.3
Aquaculture
There is as earlier mentioned a need to meet the demand for ﬁsh in the
following years and the European Commission (EC) are emphasising this
with both the Blue Growth Strategy and the Strategic Guidelines for a sus-
tainable development of European aquaculture [16]. In Europe there is def-
initely room for further growth in offshore aquaculture and different areas
within basins alike the North Sea are highly potential due to the productiv-
ity and hydrodynamic features [17]. UTOFIA can be used in this sector for
inspection of sea cages, structures, ﬁsh monitoring and disease status.

1.3.4
Underwater surveillance
Underwater imaging systems are usually aboard an undersea platform.
Dependent on the target or objective of the research different platforms
are selected e.g. autonomous underwater vehicles (AUV), towed-bodies
and remotely operated underwater vehicles (ROV). Today, ROVs are all-
important in underwater surveillance for different sectors e.g. mapping
related to marine science and assessment, oil and gas, infrastructural devel-
opment and maintenance in regards to leakages and ageing infrastructure.
Expenditures related to ROVs are estimated to increase from 1.39 billion
Euro in 2013 to 2.09 billion Euro in 2017, a growth rate of approximately
11% [18, 19]. ROVs are often used under challenging and sometimes haz-
ardous conditions like well killing in case of a blow-out [20]. Inspection
time, safety and reliability are crucial in such a scenario, and with time,
these activities will take place at deeper waters with higher requirements
to the tolerance of the equipment and quality of the imaging [20]. Under
such circumstances the UTOFIA system can assist marine scientist in e.g.
quantiﬁcation and detection of Norway lobster stocks and their burrows.
In conclusion, the UTOFIA system aims to improve the cost efﬁciency,
sampling capacity, range and image quality of underwater surveillance which
is a shared interest in all parts of the maritime industry. There are therefore
plenty of areas of interest where the system can prove its usefulness e.g.
sea ﬂoor and pipeline inspection, stock assessment at offshore ﬁsh farms
and maintenance of infrastructure. It is expected that marine science and
ocean discovery will beneﬁt from UTOFIA as well, as it eases species recog-
nition, provides growth assessment and 3D data suitable for understanding
interactions between and among species.
1.4
The project
Potentially UTOFIA will have a wide range of application and end users,
but before this ﬁnal stage is reached, it is important to test the performance
of the system under different scenarios to uncover any unwanted effects.
In this thesis, experiments were conducted to test whether the light source
from the laser had any impact on either the physiology or behaviour of
two commercially important species, representing demersal ﬁsh and crus-
taceans. More speciﬁcally, the species chosen for the experiment were At-
lantic cod (Gadus morhua) and brown crab (Cancer pagurus). Cod are widely
distributed and found in e.g. the North Atlantic shelf waters, Disco Bay,
Labrador Sea, Bay of Biscay and the Baltic Sea. Using cod, was not only
based on commercial interest and distribution, but also, that previous stud-
ies have shown that it adapts and acclimatizes to captivity relatively quickly.

Initially Norway lobster (Nephrops norwegicus) was chosen as the represen-
tative crustaceans and to be collected live from a research vessel in Katte-
gat. The decision of using lobsters was rejected, as studies and observations
have shown that light causes retinal breakdown, making lobsters unsuit-
able for this experiment [21]. Brown crab was chosen as a substitute as
they inhabit the same habitat as Norway lobster and because the National
Aquarium Denmark, Den Blå Planet, could provide specimens for the ex-
periment.
When ﬁsh are met with a real threat or in this case, perceived, it elicits
a stress response, which is a natural way to enhance chances of survival or
avoiding the stimuli. Stress response in ﬁshes has been categorized into pri-
mary, secondary and tertiary responses. The primary response is caused by
the perceived stimulus, which initiates the release and elevation of both cat-
echolamine hormones and cortisol into the blood circulation of the ﬁsh [22,
23, 24, 25]. Catecholamine hormones are released from chromafﬁn tissue
situated in the head kidney and the adrenergic nerve ending in teleosts [22].
Additionally, a cascade activation in the hypothalamus-pituitary-interrenal
axis leads to the release of cortisol [22, 24]. These neuroendocrine responses
lead to variety of physiological alterations that changes the metabolism
from being in an anabolic state to a catabolic state, which in turn should
enhance changes of survival [23, 24]. The secondary response encompasses
physiologically changes including increased heart rate (fH), stroke volume,
respiratory capacity and gill blood ﬂow [23, 25]. Also biochemical changes
are related to the secondary response, as the stress hormones lead to the
activation of different metabolic pathways which results in alterations in
the chemistry of the blood and the hematology of the ﬁsh that can lead to
e.g. hyperglycemia, hyperlactatemia and a reduction of the blood coagu-
lation time. Lastly, the tertiary response is related to whole animal- and
population-level changes to stress e.g. increased metabolism, reduction in
growth and diversity [22, 23, 24]. Retrieving data on stress response can of-
ten be problematic, as most disturbances can affect the measurements [24].
Even the set-up of the experiment and the procedures involved can be in-
ﬂuential to the stress level of the ﬁsh. Measuring the levels of cortisol in the
blood is often used in studies related to stress in ﬁsh [24, 26, 27]. The ﬁsh
need sedation before a blood sample can be retrieved from e.g. the caudal
vein or artery. When choosing blood cortisol levels as a stress indicator it
is important to be aware of the timing of sampling. The procedure from
netting to taking the blood sample should be within minutes. Using cor-
tisol as a measure of stress in this experimental set-up would be difﬁcult
because of the dimensions of the tank, ﬁg. 2.1, as we wanted the ﬁsh to
behave as natural as possible with the opportunity of avoiding the laser
light source. Therefore the method of using blood cortisol level was found
==PAGE:12==
Chapter 1. Introduction
7
unsuitable. An alternate approach of assessing stress was selected, focus-
ing on fH instead. As mentioned above, even minor stimuli can have an
effect on the fH. Also changes in the environment e.g. oxygen, temperature
and salinity, can inﬂuence the fH of cod [28, 29, 30]. Traditionally, hard-
wiring of the animal was the main way of achieving fH data. Hardwiring
is invasive in the sense that the ﬁsh at all time is connected to the receiving
equipment resulting in limited natural behaviour. Technological advances
provide new ways of receiving data, without the subject being hardwired,
making the behaviour more natural. Aquatic telemetry technology and bio-
logging has been under a constant development for several decades [31, 32].
The technique of telemetry is to give the individual of interest an electronic
device i.e. a tag or transmitter, that transmit the data to a logging or relay-
receiving station [31, 32]. Dependent on the sensor of the device different
data is obtained e.g. temperature, pressure, ﬁsh activity and fH [32]. In this
thesis bio-logging, which is a method that relies on storing data on a device
for later retrieval, was preferred. The reason for this choice was that there
was no need for a transmission of the data, as the experiment was carried
out in an enclosed tank why the ﬁsh (and hence the tag) could be retrieved
after the experiments. Based on the fH, the recovery time from surgical im-
plantation of the implants was also investigated. Lastly, the fH was used
to examine the recovery time after a max stress test, which was conducted
after the experimental period. The aim of the max stress test was to elicit
a maximum fH, which in turn could be used as an upper reference in the
perspectivation of a potential increase of fH due to the laser light source.
Light is crucial for many species of ﬁsh that are visually well-developed
for e.g. orientation, avoidance and foraging [33, 34]. Studies have shown
that white ﬁsh react to strobe light, with increased swimming speed and
avoidance behaviour from the source [34]. Other studies shows that ﬁsh
respond differently towards artiﬁcial light, dependent on e.g. intensity and
wavelength [33]. The aim of this study was to investigate any behavioural
response to the light source from the laser, by examining recorded 2D video
sequences. The sequences were analysed with the software LabTrack, to
track and detect patterns in the distribution of the ﬁsh and their swim
speed. Likewise the behavioural response to the laser was examined for
the crabs.

Materials and methods
2.1
Fish holding
42 adult cod of both sexes were caught by local ﬁshermen with gillnets
in Øresund (55◦38’40.7"N, 12◦39’26.2"E), January the 12th 2016 and trans-
ferred to National Aquarium Denmark, Den Blå Planet, Kastrup, Denmark.
The cod were maintained in two indoor tanks, where one served as both
the acclimatization and experimental tank with 35 of the caught cod. The
remaining 7 were kept as a control group for other purposes than the one
in this thesis. The acclimatization lasted from the 12th to the 27th of Jan-
uary and during the period the cod where fed with a diet consisting of blue
mussels (Mytilus edulis). Setting up the experimental conditions and testing
took place from the 25th of January to the 3rd of February. The experiment
period were performed from the 4th to the 12th of February and the feeding
was discontinued from the 2nd as it would affect the fH [35].

The acclimatization and experimental tank was crafted in stainless steel
with dimensions of 8.5 x 2.25 x 2 meters, ﬁg. 2.1. The tank was supplied
with aerated seawater at about 10◦C with a volumetric ﬂow rate of 100

vealed by the dark blue coloured background of the tank. One circular area

with a diameter of approximately half a meter was apparent close to the
inlet of supplied seawater. At the centre of the tank 4 small boulders were
spread and a larger rock was lying adjacent to the UTOFIA set-up. Initially
the water level of the tank was higher than the subsequent and re-evaluated
1.3 meters. The decision of changing the water level was done before the

were transferred to the tank, a single one avoided detection and remained
in the tank throughout the experiment. As it was ﬁrst observed during the

natural environment and that any removal would disturb the cod. During
daytime (8:00-16:00) the tank was illuminated by 6 ﬂuorescent lamps placed
above the tank to provide artiﬁcial lighting. A holistic management of the
water quality was controlled by the staff at the aquarium to ensure that pa-
rameters such as temperature, salinity, water level and oxygen remained
unaltered.
2.2
UTOFIA placement and control
In this thesis the research prototype UTOFIA ("system 0") was used. During
the set-up to the experiment, the UTOFIA system was lowered and centred
into one of the ends of the tank by a crane, ﬁg. 2.1. The crane was mod-
iﬁed for the purpose of this study, as it had the legs reversed to suit the
surroundings. The legs were equipped with sandbags whilst placing the
UTOFIA system in the tank, as it otherwise would tip over despite of its
2 ton capacity. The UTOFIA system was lowered manually into the water
with the two rope pulley system of the crane. Each leg of the UTOFIA sys-
tem had a rope permanently attached to the crane to secure the option of
removal in any thinkable emergency situation. One end of a cable was at-
tached to the back of the UTOFIA system, whereas the other was connected
to a control panel located next to the tank. The panel had three switches for
heating of the system, the camera and the laser. Before starting any emis-
sion of the laser the system had to reach 16◦C, which was done with the
heating switch. As the temperature was reached the switch was turned off,
and afterwards both the camera and laser was switched on for use with a
emission intensity of 100%. A diffuser was placed in front of the laser at
all time to accommodate the provided security guideline, which is due to
the fact that the visible green light that the laser emit has a wavelength of
532nm with a pulse energy of 1-3 mJ and a pulse repetition frequency be-
tween 100 to 1000 Hz. The panel was connected to a laptop that had the
software MATLAB (MATLAB 8.4, The MathWorks Inc., Natick, MA, 2014)

installed. A customized script designated for the UTOFIA set-up was exe-
cuted in MATLAB. Default conﬁgurations and settings was used to control
the entire UTOFIA system including abilities of turning the laser on and to
stream live 3D video footage. Lastly, the UTOFIA system had an integrated
sensor to detect any leakage or condensation within the system. The sensor
was connected to an alarm system that had to be checked as a daily routine.
2.3
Physiological analysis - Atlantic cod (G. morhua)
2.3.1
Ethics
All experiments and observations were following the guidelines set by the
Federation of Laboratory Animal Science Associations (FELASA) and the
laws by the directive 2010/63/EU on the Protection of Animals used for
Scientiﬁc Purposes. All experiments in this thesis were approved by an

2.3.2
Used animals
A total of 10 adult cod of both sexes with a mean weight of 1695g (± 192 s.d.)
and a mean length of 56cm (± 4 s.d.) were selected from the experimental
tank to investigate the physiological and behavioural response to the laser
light source.
2.3.3
Programming the DST-milli HRT
On the 27th of January, prior to surgically implanting fH loggers, they were
programmed with the software Mercury (STAR-ODDI, Gardabaer, Iceland)
and the associated Communication Box. The fH sampling frequency was
set to ’100Hz Fish [6s]’. During the 7 days post-surgical recovery period
fH was logged every 10 minutes, and after every measurement a buffer ﬁle
was saved. fH was logged every ﬁve minutes at night (17:00-09:00) and
every minute during day (09:00-17:00). A total of 480 and 192 samplings
were collected daily for day and night time, respectively.
2.3.4
Surgical and instrumentation procedure
On the 28th of January, surgery was performed on 10 cod to implant the
logging DST milli-HRT. The cod were individually anaesthetized by mov-
ing them from the experimental tank to an adjacent 40 L PVC box equipped
with a lid. The box contained 25 L of seawater from the experimental tank
with a 150 mg L−1 dissolved dosage of Tricaine methanesulfonate (MS222).
As the individual cod became anaesthetised i.e. loss of equilibrium and un-
responsive to manual stimulation, it was moved to a surgical box. The box
==PAGE:16==

11
was custom-made to match the anatomy and ﬁtted with a water-soaked
foam cushion to hold the cod in place whilst the surgical procedure was
ongoing, ﬁg. 2.2. A silicone tube was inserted into the mouth of the cod to
continuously ﬂush the gills with cooled aerated seawater containing 75 mg
L−1 MS222 at a rate of 3 L min−1, to ensure that the cod remained anaes-
thetized during surgery and obtained sufﬁcient oxygen.

A midventral 15-20 mm incision was made approximately 20 mm pos-
terior to the pectoral ﬁns. The DST milli-HRT was inserted into the body
cavity and placed in close proximity to the pericardium. A suture knot
was used to anchor the logger to the body wall and afterwards the inci-
sion was closed with 3-4 suture knots. The stitched area was treated with a
paste containing antiseptics, Bacibact Powder (Orion Pharma, Finland) and
Pevaryl R⃝1 % (McNeil Sweden ab). A coloured plastic bead was sutured to
the skin in front of the dorsal ﬁn to identify operated ﬁsh. A subcutaneous
injection containing analgesia 0.4 mg kg−1 (Butorphanol, Turbogesic Vet)
and antibiotics (10 mg kg−1 Enfroﬂoxacin, Baytril Vet) was given. Aseptic
technique was used to prevent the spread of infection. Upon completion of
surgery, the ﬁsh were relocated to the experimental tank for recovery from
anaesthesia. The ﬁsh were manually moved through the water to create a
passage/ﬂow of water across the gills until the ﬁsh resumed active ventila-
tion, ﬁg. 2.2. One operated individual, died on the 30th from jumping out
of the tank, section 2.1.
2.3.5
Experimental procedure
The UTOFIA system was turned on at 13:00 for 10 minutes during the ﬁrst
ﬁve days of the experimental period.
On the 6th day of the experimental period, a new set-up in the tank was
created to elicit the maximum fH. The water level in the tank was lowered to
1 meter from 11:00-15:00, and the ﬁsh were herded down to one end of the
tank where a ﬂoating plastic grid served as a barrier. The grid was strapped
==PAGE:17==
Chapter 2. Materials and methods
12
and mounted to two poles which kept it in place during the procedure. The
area was approximately 1/4 of the tank. At 13:20 two persons equipped
with nets and waders, got into the experimental tank to chase the ﬁsh for 3
minutes. The operated ﬁsh with coloured beads were thereafter netted one
by one, and held out of the water for 1 minute, the whole procedure lasting
from 13:26 to 13:53. Fish that had been held out of the water for 1 minute
were moved to the other 3/4 of the experimental tank for recovery.
On the 10th of February, ﬁsh with implanted loggers were netted one
by one, and killed with an overdose of MS222, followed by a sharp blow
to the head. Before the loggers were retrieved a check-up of the surgical
wounds were conducted and it showed perfect signs of healing without
any infections. All fH data from the 10 implants were successfully obtained
and stored in the Mercury software, albeit the one mentioned in 2.1 only
had data up until the 29th of January.

2.3.6
Heart rate validation - Quality index and buffer ﬁles
Two methods are used in Mercury to validate the obtained fH measure-
ments i.e. the quality index and the buffer ﬁles. All sampled measurement
made by the implanted loggers were graded with a quality index (QI) by a
two-stepped algorithm[36] in the software Mercury, where:
• 0 = Great
• 1 = Good
• 2 = Fair
• 3 = Poor
==PAGE:18==
Chapter 2. Materials and methods
13
The sampled data during the experimental phase [4/2 09:00 - 9/2 11:00]
were used to create a graph that compared the QI frequency of [0-1]-values
against [2-3]-values for the individual cod. The buffer ﬁles mentioned in
2.3.3 contained a chart with the electro cardiogram (ECG) of the ﬁsh, and it
was visually inspected to verify that the loggers had picked up the electrical
signal from the heart.
2.3.7
Recovery from surgery
Post-surgical recovery time was measured for every individual during the
recovery period [28/1 09:00 - 04/2 09:00]. The criterion for full recovery was
three consecutive measurements below or equal to the mean fH at day dur-
ing the experimental period [4/2 09:00 - 9/2 11:00]. The baseline used for
this analysis only consisted of QI [0-1]-values. The data analysis was done
in the software R: A language and environment for statistical computing
[37].
2.3.8
Heart rate comparison at day and night
A comparison of the fH of the individual cod during day and night was con-
ducted in R. The data used were compiled QI [0-1]-values during day and
night over the experimental period [4/2 09:00 - 9/2 11:00]. The results were
illustrated with density plots, overlaid histograms and box plots. Lastly,
a Welch two sample T-test was done to test the hypothesis that mean fH
during day and night were not different.
2.3.9
Mixed effects model
A linear mixed effects model was created to evaluate the possible effect of
laser exposure on fH, by using the lme4 and nlme package in the statis-
tics software R[37]. Six different models were created and evaluated based
on AIC, BIC and logLik. The models had treatment and day as ﬁxed ef-
fects and different random effects e.g. day, ﬁsh and a combination thereof.
Residual plots were created to reveal potential deviations from normality
and homoscedasticity. The data that was used for the selected model were
QI [0-1]-values from the experimental period and it only consisted of mea-
surements 10 minutes before, during and after the laser was turned on.
2.3.10
Recovery from max stress test
Alike section 2.3.7, a measured recovery time was done for every individual
following the max stress test mentioned in section 2.3.5. The criterion for as-
sumed recovery was three consecutive measurements below or equal to the
mean fH at day during the experimental period [4/2 09:00 - 9/2 11:00]. The
==PAGE:19==

baseline used for this analysis, which was conducted in R, only consisted
of QI [0-1]-values.
2.4
Behavioural analysis - Atlantic cod (G. morhua)
2.4.1
Camera set-up
5 GoPro Hero+3 (Woodman Laboratories Inc., CA, USA) cameras provided
the 2D video sequences for the behavioural analysis. The 5 cameras were
ﬁtted with recharged batteries and an additional BacPac to extend opera-
tion time on a daily basis. After conﬁrmation of battery level, the cameras
were synchronized to a single Wi-Fi remote and mounted with a handlebar
casing to wooden sticks. The sticks were then, in random order, placed in-
between the ﬂuorescent lamps above the tank at 10:00. The cameras were
turned on and off with the Wi-Fi remote for the recording sessions at 11:30-
11:45, 12:30-13:40 and 14:40-14:55. The idea of mounting the camera hours
before the sessions were to ensure that it had no disturbing effect on the cod.
The cameras were removed subsequent to the last session and the record-
ings were stored on an USB device for later analysis. Two steel frames of 2
x 1 meter were lowered into the bottom of the tank for later calibration in
regards to the analysis.
Next to the UTOFIA system, a stereo camera was placed. The purpose
of having the stereo camera was to record 3D trajectories of the cod in the
water. The data from the stereo camera was not included in this thesis, but
it is necessary to mention as it took up space within the tank.
2.4.2
Preparing the sequences for LabTrack
A total of 125 sequences were recorded over the experimental period. One
would expect a sum of 75 i.e. 5 cameras x 3 sessions x 5 days, but the
GoPro cameras have an automatic cut off length of 26 minutes, meaning
that the second session was cut in three parts. All of the sequences were
imported into GoPro Studio to remove ﬁsheye and to change the ﬁle format
to AVI. The converted ﬁles were then loaded into the software MediaCoder
to change the video BitRate to 16K Kbps and the video compression format
to MJPEG for successive video frames. Audio was disabled for a faster
process and the container setting were set to remain in AVI format.
2.4.3
LabTrack
As the ﬁles were converted they were loaded into LabTrack (LabTrack v.4.0,
BIORAS, Kvistgård, Denmark) for motility, distribution and swim behaviour
analysis of the cod. The software can simultaneously detect and track move-
ment of different objects between the frames making it ideal for this set-up.
==PAGE:20==
Chapter 2. Materials and methods
15
The sequences were labelled and calibrated with a known actual line length.
The process in the threshold menu was manually changed to ’Min bkgnd
subtract’, as this is the setting that works best for moving objects against
an uneven background. The minimum and maximum threshold was set to
20 and 40 as it improved the contrast between the background and the cod,
respectively. Also, the tracking settings were manually changed to 35 and
225 PX for minimum and maximum, respectively. The search radius was
changed to 20 PX. The schedule was set to ﬁt the longest sequence i.e. 1564
seconds and the settings were saved. In total 25 setting ﬁles were created,
this was necessary to secure correct calibration, as the GoPro cameras were
not in the exact same position from day to day. The ﬁles were executed with
their speciﬁc settings ﬁle and stored in a database.
2.4.4
Distribution
The processed ﬁles from LabTrack contained information of explicit (X,Z)
coordinates of the tracked ﬁsh, the time of the tracking, area and length.
The analysed data was divided into three time categories 10 minutes before,
during and after the laser was turned on for each of the ﬁve days. The
categories were then extracted and executed with a script in MATLAB. The
script constructed a numerical grid summarizing the number of tracked
individuals per point. The output from MATLAB were a series of ’heat
maps’. Each camera had a ﬁeld of view of the tank, in which, it recorded
tracking of all the cod before, during and after the laser was on for each day.
The scale ranged from 0-300 trackings, with a colour range going from deep
blue (few trackings) to red (many trackings). The ’heat maps’ were visually
inspected to evaluate distribution patterns.
2.4.5
Swim speed
A query was written in cooperation with BIORAS for LabTrack. The ﬁle
was loaded into LabTrack and executed with the 125 sequences. The query
used the position coordinates of the tracked subject to estimate average
swim speed (cm s−1) by measuring the distance between two following
points i.e. (x1,z1) and (x2,z2), and by dividing the distance with time. The
data were then exported to MATLAB to create a histogram of swimming
speed whilst the laser was on and off to compare the potential response to
the laser source.

3.1.4
Mixed effects model
A total of six different mixed models were constructed to describe the rela-
tionship between fH beats min−1 and operation of the laser:
1. HR.mod2 = HR ∼Treatment · Day + (Day|Fish)
2. HR.mod3 = HR ∼Treatment · Day + (1|Fish).
3. HR.mod4 = HR ∼Treatment · Day + (1|Day).
4. HR.mod5 = HR ∼Treatment · Day
5. HR.mod6 = HR ∼Treatment · Day, correlation =
corCompSymm(form=∼TreatDay|Fish)
6. HR.mod7 = HR ∼Treatment · Day + (1|FishDay)
All the six models had treatment and day as ﬁxed effects (with an inter-
action term), where treatment was divided into three levels, before, during
and after the laser was on. With the exception of model 5 (HR.mod5), all the
models were assigned random effects i.e. individual ﬁsh and experimental
day. Based on AIC, BIC and logLik (table 3.3), model 7 (HR.mod7) was
chosen. The random effect in the model was for every combination of ﬁsh
and day, with different assigned intercept. No deviations from normality or
homoscedasticity were observed by visual inspection of the residual plots,
Appendix D. The p-values obtained from the F-test revealed no statistically
signiﬁcant differences in mean fH beats min−1 between the treatments i.e.
wether the laser was on or off (p=.8749), whereas there was statistically sig-
niﬁcant differences in the mean fH beats min−1 between days (p=.0346) and
between the interaction of treatment and day (p=.0224), table 3.4 and Ap-
pendix D for more details. In other words, the model revealed that there
was no change in mean fH as a response to the UTOFIA laser being turned
on, yet the mean fH varied between the days.

i.e. neither did the ﬁsh go closer or further away from the UTOFIA system
and there was no observable change in distribution, ﬁg. 3.10.

3.3
Behavioural results - Brown crab (C. pagurus)
Initially, when just placed into the experimental aquarium, some of the
crabs showed aggressive behaviour towards the other individuals. How-
ever, during the acclimatization period the crabs would settle in a corner
each and burrow their bodies slightly down in the sand. Here the crabs
would stay motionless during the experiment, regardless of the UTOFIA
system being turned on. Neither did the distance to the UTOFIA system
alter the behaviour of the crabs.

Discussion
The present study shows that there was no detectable change in mean fH of
cod as a response to the UTOFIA laser, nor did the laser cause any avoid-
ance reaction, as evaluated by the distribution of ﬁsh within the tank and
their swimming speeds. Additionally, irrespectively of their distance to the
laser the crabs stayed motionless regardless of the laser being on.
All fH loggers worked according to expectations. However, as seen in the
frequency ﬁgure, 3.1, and ECG ﬁles of Appendix A, the signal strength, i.e.
how well the electrodes in the logger picked up the electrical signal from the
heart, varied between ﬁsh. This is despite the fact that placement of the in-
serted DST-Milli HRT loggers were done in accordance to a previous study
on rainbow trout (Oncorhynchus mykiss) aiming to optimize signal strength,
as both position and rotation inﬂuences this [38]. The loggers are relatively
newly developed, which is the reason why there currently exist no guide-
lines in terms of choosing which QI values to work with [36]. In this thesis,
[0-1]-values were preferred for the analysis, to secure a high quality of the
measurements. One could argue that the standard would improve by solely
working with QI 0, but it would be at the cost of number of measurements
in the dataset.
For the present thesis, a full post-surgical recovery was assumed when
three consecutive fH measurements were below or equal to the average
baseline fH during the experimental period. This resulted in an average
post-surgical recovery period of 45 hours and 10 min for the nine ﬁsh, with
two ﬁsh (ID159 and ID172) taking considerable longer than the average
with 78 hours and 40 min and 76 hours, respectively. A study on time of re-
covery in coho salmon (Oncorhynchus kisutch) after release from a simulated
seine capture was conducted using implanted ECG biologgers, in which re-
covery was considered when fH returned within the 99% conﬁdence inter-
val of the individual baseline [39]. Other studies investigating excess post-
hypoxic oxygen consumption (EPHOC), considered termination of EPHOC
to be when two consecutive oxygen consumption rate measurements were
below the standard metabolic rate +5% [40, 41]. Had post-surgical recov-
ery time in the present study been based on two consecutive measurement
of fH +5%, then the average recovery time would have been 25 hours and
==PAGE:34==
Chapter 4. Discussion
29
38 minutes. The two ’slow recovery ﬁsh’ (i.e. ID159 and ID172) would
still recover later than the other ﬁsh, with an average recovery time of 38
hours and 45 minutes. This illustrates that one should carefully consider
how to determine end of recovery periods, as this may strongly inﬂuence
the results and that a standardized way of interpreting fH data in regards to
recovery time from e.g. surgery would beneﬁt future studies. In many ex-
perimental studies on ﬁsh, the individual is usually left for between 24 and
48 hours to recover from surgery before an experiment is initiated [42, 43].
More solid results on required post-surgical recovery periods, which may
also be species speciﬁc, would thus provide stronger guidelines on what
recovery period is more appropriate.
The mean fH during day varied between 27 and 34 beats min−1, whereas
mean fH during night ranged between 26 and 37 beats min−1. Previous
studies on cod found a similar basal fH of 28.3 ± 0.53 beats min−1 after a
week of post-surgical recovery for resting ﬁsh at the same temperature [44].
On the contrary, the present maximum fH obtained following the max stress
test (ranging between 67 and 100 beats min−1) were higher than previously
found ≈40-60 beats min−1 [30, 44]. The before mentioned studies obtained
maximum fH through a critical swimming speed protocol i.e. the tested
ﬁsh were hardwired and forced to swim in a conﬁned area against a wa-
ter current. One study evaluated the comparability of data obtained using
different techniques to elicit maximum metabolic rate for cod, by compar-
ing different studies and found that there was a tendency for similar or
higher maximal oxygen consumption following a chase protocol than dur-
ing a critical swimming speed protocol [45]. As the metabolic rate correlates
with fH in cod, the chase and air exposure protocol used in the present thesis
arguably elicited a higher maximum fH than those methods used earlier [30,
44]. Interestingly, an even higher fH than the one following the max stress
test was obtained for six of the nine ﬁsh during the post-surgical recovery
period. This may be because ﬁsh were fed during this period (Feb 1st and
2nd, ﬁg. 3.4). Feeding elicits an increase in fH in ﬁsh [24, 25, 35], which then
may add to the increase in fH already seen during the post-surgical recovery
period. This is also why the ﬁsh were not fed two days prior to and during
the experimental phase, as it would have elicited an increase of the fH.
Individual fH varied between experimental days (p=.0346). The output
revealed that the intercept gradually became more negative between days
i.e. the average fH was lowered each day, Appendix D. A potential expla-
nation for this could be the lack of feeding, however one cannot exclude
that the cod were not fully recovered from surgery when the experimental
period began.
Generally, the ﬁsh had a preference of staying close to the UTOFIA sys-
tem and the adjacent stereoscopic set-up i.e. a large number of trackings
==PAGE:35==
Chapter 4. Discussion
30
were observed there. This was irrespectively of the laser being turned on or
not, so the distribution of ﬁsh within the tank did not change in response
to the UTOFIA system. Cod are known to aggregate close to artiﬁcial reefs,
most likely to increase their chances of survival by being sheltered from
predators and currents, and this could potentially explain the observed ten-
dency of the experimental cod [46, 47]. There were certain challenges in
regards to the set-up and data analysis leading to the ﬁnalized ’heat maps’.
Using GoPro cameras was successful, and when placing the cameras above
the tank, care was taken to minimize disturbance to the ﬁsh. And indeed,
the ﬁsh did not show any immediate behavioural or physiological response
to this. However, for future studies it would be beneﬁcial to consider a dif-
ferent type of camera as it was time consuming to prepare the sequences
for analysis e.g. removal of ’ﬁsh eye’ effect and 26min cut pr. sequence. In
addition, the longevity of the battery was reduced by the usage of WiFi (be-
ing turned on during the day), and the surrounding temperature i.e. colder
climate reduces longevity. Having the cameras in a ﬁxed position with a
constant power supply would reduce the amount of time spent on calibra-
tion, as a single settings ﬁle in LabTrack would apply for one single cam-
era capturing different sequences. The software LabTrack provided good
tracking on the free swimming cod, but the artiﬁcial light by the ﬂuorescent
lamps above the experimental tank backscattered on the water surface in
such a manner that it at times was tracked as movement. This problem was
solved by manual removal of areas with backscattering from the dataset,
Appendix F. Tracking close to the water inlet proved to be difﬁcult, as the
contrast was different from the rest of the experimental tank due to the con-
stant lack of sand on the bottom in the area (i.e. sand was removed by the
inﬂowing water). This explains the lack of tracking from 18-20 on the x-axis
of the third camera, Appendix F. Slowly moving individuals moving close
to the bottom had a tendency not to be tracked, however these instances
constituted only a very minor part of the total tracking and were thus con-
sidered to constitute an insigniﬁcant bias.
The average swim speeds of cod were 34 cm −1 (± 5 s.d.) when the laser
was on and 35 −1 (± 6 s.d.) when the laser was off. In comparison, an in
situ study found that cod with a length between 55-60 cm (i.e. highly com-
parable to the present cod) would swim with a mean speed of 38.9 −1 [48].
This suggests that the cod used in this thesis had adjusted to the experi-
mental tank and that the behaviour was close to what one would expect in
the ﬁeld.
The crabs that were used in this thesis had been exposed to artiﬁcial
light over a longer period (many months). Most likely, light-induced dam-
age to the photoreceptor of crustaceans depend on a large number of vari-
ables, but when the damage is done it is often irreversible [49]. Hence it
==PAGE:36==
31
cannot be ruled out that the lack of response to the UTOFIA laser seen for
the crabs in the present study could be because they have damaged pho-
toreceptors and hence to not see the light. In a future study it is advised to
investigate photoceptor and behavioural response to the UTOFIA system
in crabs that are not adapted to artiﬁcial light.
In conclusion, the present initial testing of the UTOFIA prototype was
promising, as no adverse effects were observed in the tested species. The
next step will be to move from the mesocosmos that was created in this the-
sis and apply the UTOFIA system under ﬁeld conditions. As ﬁsh respond
differently to wavelengths and indensity of light [33] it is here important to
observe if there is any change in behaviour of an species in contact with the
system.
