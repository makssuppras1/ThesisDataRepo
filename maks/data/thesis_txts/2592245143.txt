Summary
When a computational tool is intended to accurately infer some unkown quality of
biological data, which can be non homologous to the data which the inference is based
on, then it is necessary to ensure that its performance is measured on data which is
non homologous to the data which the inference is based on. Current approaches to
homology separation rely on approximations to speed up computation. If a stringent
homology separation is required, then they lack the needed sensitivity. I propose an
approach which is both stringent and designed throughout to accommodate modern
approaches to inference performance evaluations, namely nested cross-validation.
GPI-anchored proteins can be essential to the normal function of eukaryotes. The
experimental assays required to detect and verify their presence are costly and time
consuming. Computational methods, which can accurately imitate the experimental
processes, are needed to expedite and support eukaryotic cell function research. Over
a decade has passed since the last GPI predictor was published, a decade of advances
in computational power and increased availability of data sources. I present NetGPI,
a state-of-the-art GPI predictor, based on recurrent neural networks and an attention
mechanism. NetGPI is superior to previous methods at predicting the presence of
GPI-anchoring signal sequences, and at approximating the ω-site within a ±1 window.
The nucleus is the defining feature of eukaryotic cells. It hosts the genetic material
and regulates gene expression. These gene expressions begin as transcribed messenger
RNA which travels out of the nucleus, where it is translated into polypeptides. Many
of the polypeptides then make their way back to the nucleus, where they can perform
a variety of functions. Understanding the proteins that serve the nucleus is crucial to
further understand the nucleus and causal relationships within it. Few proteins have
been experimentally verified and no tools exist which provide reliable predictions for
subnuclear protein function. NuDeep is a subnuclear localization predictor, based on a
residual convolutional neural network architecture. Promising results were observed.
However, stringent homology seperation of the dataset impacted the performance.
Research into nuclear and subnuclear localization with deep learning is still ongoing.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In my thesis I present my research into predictive localization of eukaryotic proteins.
Proteins are a macromolecule (large molecule), made up of one or more chains of
organic chemical compounds called amino acids. These chains are called polypeptides.
They are sequenced into alphabetical character sequences for computation, where a
specific character represents a specific amino acid.
There are two general approaches to using computational methods for predictive
localization of proteins. One involves the discrimination of the presence of a certain
expression or signalling mechanism in a given sequence. This can involve predicting
the position or placement of the expression within the sequence itself. The other
involves labelling polypeptides with a localization. Both approaches, as well as the
expression placement, involve supervised labelling or classification.
This thesis specifically involves the documentation of two different localization
problems and the development of a novel prediction solution for each.
The first
problem is the localization of proteins on a cell’s surface, via a modification called
GPI-anchoring. The second is the sublocalization of proteins within the nucleus of
eukaryotic cells. Furthermore, the thesis also discusses current tools and approaches
to dataset preperation and issues with them. I propose an approach to dataset preper-
ation which is either free of these issues or agnostic to them.
In chapter two, I introduce the biological background and motivation. I begin
by presenting proteins and their synthesis in eukaryotic cells. Then I describe how
proteins are localized or sorted to their intended place, where I devote a section on
GPI-anchoring and a section on nuclear sublocalization.
In chapter three, I present the datasets employed, a seperate set of polypeptide se-
quences is used in the development of each solution. Then I describe how polypeptide
sequence datasets are prepared and split into training and testing subsets. I review
the current approaches to dataset preperation and present issues with the quality of
preperation when they are employed. Finally I show how the quality of preperation
can be measured and propose an alternative approach.
In chapter four, I introduce the theory behind the methods of sequence labelling
with deep learning that are employed. Specifically, the long-short-term-memory ar-
chitecture (LSTM)[HS97], the residual convolution architecture (rCNN) [He+15], dis-
tributed representations [Mik+13] and the Bahdanau attention mechanism.
In chapter five, I introduce GPI prediction, where the objective is to decide if a
protein sequence includes a signalling sequence, which signals for the GPI-anchoring

2.1
Eukaryotic proteins
2.1.1
Proteins
Proteins are the most abundant and diverse macromolecules (large molecule) in living
organisms. They are diverse both in shape and size as well as in function. Proteins
are made up of one or more chains of organic chemical compounds called amino acids.
These chains are called polypeptides and the bond that links the amino acids to-
gether is called a peptide bond. Genetic information of a gene is transcribed into
messenger RNA (mRNA, described in section 2.1.3) which provides the structure of
a protein to be synthesized.
All cells contain Ribosomes, which translate mRNA
into polypeptides. There are 20 standard amino acids with a genetic encoding in
all living cells, which are used to actualize the genetic information. The ribosomes
assemble the polypeptides step by step, starting at a well defined starting signal,
also called the N-terminus, and ending at a well defined end signal, also called the
carboxyl-terminus or C-terminus. Proteins often undergo modifications during and
after synthesis. Certain chemical groups or moiety can be attached and the polypep-
tides can be shortened, or cross-linked. Modifications performed after synthesis are
called post-translational modifications, while modifications performed during synthe-
sis are called co-translational modifications [Har00, Section 1.2, Section 4.2].
2.1.2
Eukaryotes
Organisms are divided into three main categories called domains. Two, Bacteria and
Archaea, are considered prokaryotes.
The third domain, Eukaryotes, differs from
prokaryotes mainly in three ways. Firstly, eukaryotic cells are two orders of mag-
nitude larger, secondly, when they are multicellular, the different cells within the
organism can serve very different functions. Lastly, their cells contain a nucleus and
other specialized substructures, all of which are referred to as membrane-bound or-
ganelles. The common eukaryotic organelles are; the nucleus, endoplasmic reticulum,

golgi apparatus, mitochondria and vacuoles. Chloroplasts are organelles which per-
form photosynthesis and are common to the plant subclade of eukaryotic organisms.
Lysosome are organelles common to cells of the animal subclade, where they serve to
break down obsolete and unwanted materials. Peroxisomes, which can generate heat
and usable components from fatty acids, as well as degrade various toxic compounds,
exist in almost all animal cells and many plant cells. These membrane-bound or-
ganelles have a plasma membrane separating their interior (often called the lumen)
from the exterior(the cytosol) [Har00, Chapter 5].
2.1.3
Eukaryotic protein synthesis
The largest organelle, the nucleus, contains and produces within it the nucleic acids,
deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) which stores the genetic information and ribonucleic
acids (RNA) which control and facilitate the actualization of the genetic information.
It also produces ribosomes in a nuclear substructure called the nucleolus. While the
DNA is contained within the nucleus, the ribosomes travel to the cytosol and the
RNA can travel to the cytosol or stay inside the nucleus, depending on its function.
The nucleus has both an inner and outer membrane and in many cells the outer
membrane extends into another organelle, the endoplasmic reticulum (ER).
Two organelles, mitochondria and chloroplasts, contain both genetic material and
ribosomes, which allows them to produce some of their own proteins.
All other
proteins are translated from mRNA produced in the nucleus.
The translation of
these proteins begins in the cytosol. The translated gene expression contains a signal
for the destination of the protein. Some signals signal to the translating ribosome
to move to the ER and continue the synthesis into the ER lumen. This is called
co-translational targeting. Proteins without a co-translational targeting signal are
synthesized completely in the cytosol. Afterwards the proteins remain there or are
directed towards the ER, mitochondrion, chloroplast, peroxisome or nucleus by a
post-translational targeting signal.
The endoplasmic reticulum is composed of layers of interconnected membrane
enclosed tubes and sacs, called cisternae.
It is divided into two parts.
A rough
part, where some ribosomes attach themselves as part of protein synthesis of certain
proteins, and a soft part where phospholipids and fatty acids are synthesized. These
ribosomes attach to the outside of the rough ER and synthesise the proteins into
its lumen. Many polypeptides are still inserted into the membrane of the ER after
the synthesis or translation is completed. Most of the proteins initially destined for
the rough ER are then transported in membrane-bounded vesicles, budding of the
organelle membrane, to the lumen of the Golgi apparatus, also known as the Golgi
complex.
The Golgi complex is usually located near the nucleus. It has three regions, a cis
region which faces the ER, a medial region in the middle and a trans region, facing
away from the ER. The Golgi is composed of layers of elongated and flattened sacks
that are built up from incoming transport vesicles on the cis side as new proteins

arrive. As proteins leave for their destination on the trans side, they again dissipate
into transport vesicles. Each region has specific enzymes, which can bud back, or be
left behind in a sense, as the layers move from the cis to the median and the median

2.1.4
Protein sorting for the secretory pathway
Proteins designated to be secreted out of the cytoplasm through the cell membrane,
are designated to follow the so called secretory pathway. The same goes for proteins
intended for the organelles of the secretory pathway, the cell membrane, as well as
the lysosome. The transcribed genetic information of these proteins usually includes
an ER signal sequence at the N-terminus, called the signal peptide, which directs
the translating ribosome to attach to the endoplasmic reticulum during synthesis.
After translation is complete they then move in vesicles to the cis side of the golgi
apparatus. Certain proteins intended for the ER, travel to the Golgi for modification,
then return back. After travelling in layers (cisternae), through the regions of the
Golgi apparatus and recieving any necessary modification, the proteins intended for
secretion, the plasma membrane or cell surface, and the lysosome, exit on the other
side into the trans-Golgi reticulum. There proteins are sorted according to signal and
directed towards their final destination. [Har00, Chapter 17, Section 17.3]
2.1.5
Protein sorting for the nucleus
The two membranes of the nucleus form together what is called a nuclear envelope.
This envelop has multiple protein structures called nuclear pore complexes, which al-
lows for the transport of molecules in and out of the nucleus. Many smaller molecules
can enter and exit the nucleus through these pores without the need for a specific sig-
nalling moiety granting them entry or exit. Proteins however, do need such a signal.
There are two known classes of such signals, which are formed by specific amino acid
sequences. These are the nuclear-localization signals (NLS) which signal the entry of
proteins into the nucleus and the nuclear-export signal (NES) which signals the exit.
Proteins have either a NLS or both a NLS and a NES. The proteins which have both,
shuttle between the nucleus and cytosol, whereas those which only have a NLS will
be restricted to it, after entry.
[Har00, Section 11.4]
2.2
GPI-anchoring, localizing proteins on the plasma
membrane
Proteins intended for the cell’s plasma membrane, are left attached to the membrane
of the ER after translation, in the same manner as they later are attached to the
==PAGE:16==
6
2 Localization in eukaryotic proteins
cell’s membrane. As they move along the secretory pathway, they remain attached to
the phospholipid membrane material that transports them. Parts of the polypeptide
which will be inside the cytoplasm will be on the cytosol side of the ER membrane,
Golgi apparatus or transport vesicle. Parts which will be exposed to the outside of
the cell, the exoplasm, will be in the lumen. When the transporting vesicle merges
with the plasma membrane of the cell, the protein becomes attached to the membrane
in the correct orientation, instead of secreting completely out into the exoplasm.
In many cases, the cell surface proteins require a special membrane attachment, a
glycosylphosphatidylinositol anchor (GPI-anchor). These proteins are co-translationally
directed towards the ER, by the N-terminal signal peptide, where the synthesis is
finished. A hydrophobic signal sequence at the C-terminus, of the fully translated
protein, signals for a GPI transamidase to cleave the peptide bond downstream of the
GPI-anchoring site, replacing the hydrophobic stretch with a GPI-anchor, anchored
to the ER membrane.
This attachment site is called the ω-site, the hydrophobic
region begins in position ω + 10[OM07].
GPI-anchored cell surface proteins are vital to multiple surface processes and
interactions. Various post-translational modifications performed along the secretory
pathway, prepare both the GPI-anchored protein and GPI-anchor for its designated
purpose on the cell surface [Har00, Section 17.5] [Kin16].
GPI-anchored cell surface proteins are vital to the function of eukaryotic organ-
isms, where they constitute between 10-20% of proteins targeted for the plasma mem-
brane. In mammals they are known to be vital for embryonic development[OM07].
The deficiency of GPI-anchored proteins can lead to diseases such as paroxysmal noc-
turnal hemoglobinuria, where the deficiency is aquired through a somatic mutation
and leads to the destruction of red blood cells, blood clots and bone-marrow deficiency
[Par96; San+19; Kaw+96]. GPI-anchors are crucial for many pathogenic fungi and
parasitic protozoans, where they not only facilitate cell surface interaction, but also
are a part of the cell wall structure and maintenance [Kom+18].
2.3
Subnuclear localization, the nucleus and its
substructure.
Section 2.1.5, describes how proteins are sorted for the nucleus, the nuclear enve-
lope and nuclear pore complex. Once proteins have entered the nucleus, there are
multiple localizations and a plethora of functions to perform.
The main localiza-
tions to mention, are the nucleolus, which is an organelle within the nucleus, where
most ribosomal RNA is synthesized. The area outside the nucleolus, but inside the
nucleus, is called the nucleoplasm and the network called the nuclear matrix gives
the nucleus, structural support. Other localizations include the chromatin, which
is a complex of DNA and protein, which forms chromosomes.
Chromosomes are
packaged DNA, which are dispersed throughout the nucleoplasm when a cell is not
dividing [Har00, Section 5.4, Section 11.4]. Telomeres are regions at the end of each

chromosome, which protect the ends of chromosomes from deterioration. Centromere
are specialized sub-sequences of chromosomes, which are the assembly site of kineto-
chores, a protein structure important for cell division. Nuclear speckles, Cajal bodies
and Promyelocytic leukemia bodies are found throughout the nucleoplasm. Nuclear
speckles are small membraneless organelles, proteins in speckles serve as reservoirs

The GPI-anchor dataset

3.1.3
Sequence homology
Biological homology refers to shared evolutionary ancestry of any pair of biological
entities, their resemblance through the transference of information [Wag89]. Sequence
homology, similarily refers to likeness due to transference of information and can be
either orthologous or paralogous. Orthologous genes are genes of different species,
which share an ancestral gene in the last common ancestor. Paralogous genes are genes
which are related via duplication. Likewise, sections of genetic material, which store
==PAGE:21==
3.1 The data
11
or expresses genetic information, as well as peptide sections, which are the produce
of genetic expression, are orthologous or paralogous, when they store, express or are
the product of, orthologous or paralogous information [Koo05]. This means that the
homology of gene expressions in the form of polypeptides translates to homology
of the amino acid sequences used in the development of computational tools. The
distinction between orthologs and paralogs will not be continued here.
When a computational tool, or prediction model, is intended to accurately infer
some unkown quality of its input, it is necessary to ensure that its performance is
measured on samples which are as different from the samples it bases its inference on,
as it is expected it will encounter when it is used. The more similar the samples which
the tool uses for inference are, to samples used to establish the quality of the inference,
the likelier it becomes that the quality of the inference will be overestimated.
3.1.4
Measuring pairwise sequence similarity
It is not trivial to discover the true homology of two proteins. We can nonetheless,
make the assumption that structural similarity implies homology and infer the chance
of homology from their structural similarities [Pea13].
In this chapter we will explore two measures. The global sequence percent identity,
which is a measure of the likeness of two sequences, and the local expectation value,
which is a statistical estimate of homology between subsections of sequence pairs,
where the homologous portion can be any subsection of each sequence.
The global percent identity is the more ubiquitous measure, the rule of thumb
being that any two sequences over 30% global identity should be, until proven other-
wise, assumed to be homologous [Pea13]. It is measured by aligning together portions
of each sequence as they are seen to best fit together and adding gaps to each where
they least fit together until the extent of both sequences has been covered, this is
known as a global alignment. The identity is then the percentage of amino-acids in
the longer sequence, which could be aligned to the same amino-acid in the shorter
one. The Needleman-Wunch algorithm is a dynamic programming algorithm which
finds the optimal global alignment, given the choice of parameters [Nee69].
Local sequence alignments are segments of two sequences which can be aligned.
The same algorithm can be used, with minor adjustments. The local expectation
value reports the number of times that any similarity found could be there by chance,
in a supplied dataset. This means that a reported expectation value of one indicates
that any similarity could be by complete chance, given that the dataset allows for an
accurate statistical estimate. The highest reported expectation value for a supplied
dataset is always the dataset size [Pea13]. The Smith-Waterman algorithm is a modi-
fication of the Needleman-Wunch algorithm, which finds the optimal local alignment,
given the choice of parameters [Smi69].
The FASTA36 package provides the program ggsearch36 for global alignments and
the program ssearch36 for local alignments [Pea16].

BLAST, Basic Local Alignment Search Tool, is available as a web service and as
a standalone package for local execution. It provides the program blastp for pro-
tein local alignments. BLAST is a heuristic approximation of the Smith-Waterman
algorithm [Alt90].
At least two readily available packages exist that cluster sequences, both protein
and nucleotide, based on inferred homology, CD-HIT and MMSeqs2. There was a homol-
ogy clustering program included in the standalone BLAST package called BLASTCLUST,
which is available in older versions.
CD-HIT compares selects a subset of proteins as cluster representatives which are
used to evaluate potential membership. First Cd-HIT orders sequences by decreasing
length. Then it iteratively selects the longest sequence without an assigned cluster,
checks if there exists a cluster with a similar enough representative, if there is no
cluster which the sequence can be assigned to, then the sequence becomes the repre-
sentative of a new cluster. CD-HIT uses short word filters as identity estimates, where
a certain number of di-, tri, deca- and pentapeptides are expected in a sequence of
a certain size. If an unassigned protein shares a greater number of short words or
k-mers with a cluster representative than expected, then they are assumed to pass
the identity threshold and an alignment is performed to confirm it. If it is confirmed
then they are clustered together, otherwise the unassigned protein finds another home.
CD-HIT requires as input a set of sequences, an identity threshold and short word size
[LJG01; LG06]. CD-HIT provides cd-hit which clusters down to a threshold of 40%
identity, then it provides the PERL program psi-cd-hit.pl to cluster further.The
psi-cd-hit.pl program uses the BLAST package for alignment.
MMSeqs2 suite of solutions for sequence similarity searching and clustering and is
available as a standalone package. It provides mmseqs cluster for clustering. It has
three stages of pairwise alignment measure. The first stage is a short word matching
stage, which unlike CD-HIT has a larger k = 7 word size and finds similar k-mers in-
stead of exact matches. The second stage is vectorized ungapped alignment. The third
stage is gapped Smith-Waterman alignment [SS17]. The mmseqs cluster program
has three clustering modes. Like CD-HIT, the default mode uses a representative
sample for each cluster, however the representative is chosen differently. Iteratively,
it creates a cluster from the sequence with most alignments above a chosen threshold,
along with the sequences whose similarity is above the threshold. These sequences
then form a new cluster and are no longer regarded for clustering.
Various BLAST programs were once made available along with the blastall pro-
gram, now however, the standalone package that is supported is called BLAST+. In-
cluded with blastall, but not in the BLAST+ package, is the clustering program
BLASTCLUST. BLASTCLUST is a score-based single-linkage clustering program, it uses
BLAST for pairwise alignments.

3.2
Sequence homology partitioning
Here, sequence homology partitioning refers to the reduction of similarity between
sequences used to evaluate a prediction model and used to develop or train the same
prediction model, such that their similarity does not imply homology. This has been
attempted in multiple ways and the chosen procedure will be context dependant.
[needs references!]
3.2.1
A conventional approach
One approach employed is to use either BLASTCLUST, CD-HIT or MMSeqs2 to cluster
sequences by homology.
Then group together the resulting clusters into sets for
training and evaluation. DeepLoc[AA+17] uses CD-HIT to cluster sequences to a 30%
identity threshold. The clusters are then grouped, or partitioned, together with the
goal of having a balanced label composition in each partition. DeepLoc uses four
partitions for training and performance validation during development and one as
a held-out test set.
SignalP 5.0 labels every sequence by an organism group and
type[AA+19], it utilizes CD-HIT to cluster sequences to a 20% identity threshold.
Then the clusters are again clusters with goal of having a balanced label composition,
here however, both sets of labels are considered. The first rendition of NetGPI[Gí+19]
uses a comparable approach. However, due to the lack of experimental data, the
CD-HIT clusters with experimentally verified GPI-anchoring signal sequences are first
grouped together to form the basis of a held-out test set.
3.2.2
The issue
All three homology clustering solutions employ various approximations, both in how
they cluster and in how they measure homology. The approximations are intended
to speed up the processing, as the time complexity of Needleman-Wunch and Smith-
Waterman is O(n2) when all pairwise alignments within a dataset of size n are needed.
Likewise, the time complexity of exhaustive clustering algorithms can be expected to
be O(n2) or O(n3). One issue with these approaches is, that the quality of the approx-
imations is not transparent. I examine the potential quality concessions made, when
clustering the GPI-anchoring dataset, using these three clustering methods. Using
the FASTA36 package, I obtain the Needleman-Wunch (NW) global identities, with
ggsearch36, and Smith-Waterman (SW) local expectation values (E values) with
ssearch36. Using the BLAST+ package, I obtain BLAST E values, with blastp.
The three implementations, ggsearch36, ssearch36 and blastp do not return the
same sets of pairwise alignments. The ggsearch36 program accepts an E value thresh-
old, even if it is set much higher than the size of the dataset, it does not necessarily
return a pairwise alignment. The ggsearch36 program does not return all pairwise
NW alignments.

3.2.3
Solving the issue
The end goal is to split a dataset into useful homology partitioned subsets, which
can be used to train and test a prediction model, using either cross-validation with a
held-out test set or nested cross-validation, described further in chapter [REF]. This
means that the partitioning should simultaneously reduce the similarity between par-
titions, such that homology would not be expected, and maximize the similarity of
the composition of partitions, such that, excluding sequence similarity, each is as
representative of the dataset as the others. In general, this means that we want each
partition to be a comparable portion of the dataset. For Sequence labelling we want
a comparable portion of samples with each label, in each partition. Furthermore, as
in the case of SignalP, there can be input labels that influence the prediction, which
we want a comparable portion of, in each partition.
To solve the end goal, a new tool Graph-Part is developed where the end goal is
considered throughout. Graph-Part uses any real-valued distance metric to separate
a set of labelled entity identifiers, S, labelled with labels from L, into a desired num-
ber of subsets or partitions, p, at a desired distance threshold, t, using a set, D, of
pairwise distances between the identifiers. Each entity identifier in S can also be
labelled with a secondary binary label, which can be used to prioritize identifiers.
Graph-Part approaches the partitioning in three phases.
In the first phase, the dataset is clustered using restricted nearest neighbour clus-
tering. Each entity identifier in S is initially regarded as a separate, or singleton,
cluster. Then D is ordered in ascending order and iterated over to connect pairs of
clusters together and form a larger cluster. If a connection between clusters would
result in a cluster whose size exceeds the expected partition size, ⌈|S|/p⌉, then the
connection is not made. If a connection between clusters would result in a cluster
having more entity identifiers labelled with any l ∈L than expected, ⌈|Sl|/p⌉, where
Sl is the set of entity identifiers labelled with l, then the connection is not made. If
a pairwise distance exceeds the threshold, then the procedure is stopped.
The GPI-anchoring dataset, described in section 3.1.1, is partitioned using Graph-Part
with three distance measures: the Needleman-Wunch (NW) identity, Smith-Waterman
(SW) expectation values, and Blast expectation values. The NW identity is trans-
formed so that 0 represents 100% identity and 1 represents 0% identity. The chosen
number of partitions is 5 and the threshold is set to 30% identity or 0.1 E value. The
set S contains a protein accession (AC) number for each protein in the dataset and
each accession number is labelled either ’Anchored’ or ’Not-Anchored’. Furthermore,
the proteins which have an experimentally verified annotation, for the GPI-anchoring
signal sequence, are labelled with a secondary priority label. Clustering with NW
identities results in 1299 clusters, of which 850 are singletons. The shortest distance
between any two clusters, measured with NW identity, is 31.8%. Measured with SW
E values it is, 7.1e−21 and with BLAST E values, 2.51e−42. Out of 3618 proteins,

The third and last phase is the removal phase. Now entity identifiers, which are closer
to entity identifiers in other partitions than the threshold allows, are removed from
S, as well as all associated pairwise distances from D. We let i = 1, then, until there
is no pairwise distance in D, between entity identifiers in different partitions, the
following procedure is repeated: For each entity identifier, u, the number of connec-
tions below the threshold, to entity identifiers in each partition is found. If u is more
connected to entity identifiers in another partition than its own, then u is moved to
that partition. In either case, the number of connections to other partitions, which
are below the threshold is noted. When all u in S have been covered, the c log10(i)+1
entity identifiers with most connections below the threshold, to other partitions, are
removed, where c is the number of entity identifiers with any connection below the
threshold, to other partitions. If c > 0 then we let i = i + 1 and repeat. If there are
any entity identifiers with priority labelling, then the removal procedure is first per-
formed, without removing any priority labelled entity identifiers, until the conditions
are met for all non priority labelled entity identifiers. The removal procedure is then
repeated without this restriction.

4.1
Feedforward neural networks
Feedforward networks are the first and simplest type of neural networks. They form
the basis for many commercial applications and are a stepping stone to other impor-
tant models such as RNN and CNN. Their goal is to approximate some function f ∗
by defining a mapping from an input variable x to an output variable y; y = f(x; θ)
and learn the parameters θ that give the best function approximation.
The term feedforward originates in the way information flows from the input nodes,
through the hidden nodes and finally to the output nodes. This process is known as
the forward pass of the network. The term network stems from the fact that they
typically involve the composition of various different functions, forming a chain struc-
ture such as f(x) = f (3)(f (2)(f (1)(x))). f (1) is called the first layer of the network,
which is the input layer. f (2) is called the second layer of the network, which is the
(first) hidden layer, and so on. The final layer of the network is called the output
layer, which in this case is f (3).
When a feedforward network is trained its learning algorithm must decide how to
use the layers of the network to best approximate the function f ∗that will produce
the desired output y. As each example x is accompanied by a label y ≈f ∗(x), the
training examples specify that the output layer must produce a value that is close
to y at each example x. This however, does not directly affect the hidden layers of
the network, but rather the learning algorithm must decide how they may be used to
best implement an approximation of f ∗[GBC16].
Applying FNNs to sequence labelling tasks, such as this protein prediction task, has
two limitations. Firstly, because the connections of FNNs do not form cycles, mean-
ing that the output only depends on the current input, not any past or future inputs,
the network is not able to capture the inherent spatial information about the order
of the amino acids in a sequence. Secondly, the network requires the input vectors to
be of equal size. This is problematic because the input vectors are protein sequences,
which vary greatly in length. There are two ways to overcome this limitation but both
will result in information loss. One solution is to truncate the protein sequences to a
fixed size vector and the other is to transform them into a vector of general features.

4.2
Neural network training
In most cases the algorithm used for training a neural network centers around using
the gradient to descend the cost function. Therefore, the process of designing and
training neural networks involves many of the same steps as required when training a
traditional machine learning algorithm with gradient descent. This includes choosing
the optimizer, the cost function as well as the form of the output units.
4.2.1
Optimization
Deep learning algorithms usually involve the task of either maximizing or minimizing
some function f(x) by altering x, which is referred to as optimization. When the goal
is minimize some function, the function is typically called the cost function or error
function, and when the goal is to maximize it is called the objective function. Given
==PAGE:39==
4.2 Neural network training
29
a function y = f(x), where x and y are real numbers, we can compute the functions
derivative f ′(x) to get the slope of the function f(x) at point x which specifies how
to scale a small change in the input to obtain a small change in the output.
This information can be used as a guide for minimizing the function as it informs
us on how we can obtain an improvement in y by changing x. When minimizing the
goal is then to find the point that obtains the absolute lowest value of f(x), called
the global minimum. However, in deep learning, we usually settle for finding a very
low value of f. This is because the global minimum is unlikely to be found as the
functions being minimized usually has many local minima that are not globally op-
timal. Training a deep learning model with gradient-based learning algorithm is an
iterative process and most optimization algorithms are strongly affected by the choice
of parameter initialization. The initialization can choice can determine how fast the
algorithm converges to an acceptable solution, or if it even converges at all. Typically
all the weights in the model are initialized to values drawn randomly from a Gaussian
or uniform distribution and the biases are set to heuristically chosen constants for
each unit in the network.
The optimization algorithm that is generally used for training a deep neural network
is stochastic gradient descent or its variants. One popular variant is the Adaptive Mo-
ment Estimation (ADAM) optimizer which was designed specifically for training deep
neural networks. It is an adaptive learning rate optimization algorithm, so it uses
separate learning rates for each parameter of the model which it adapts as learning
proceeds. Since the cost function is not equally sensitive to all directions in parameter
space, ADAM is a big improvement from the stochastic gradient descent which uses
the same learning rate for all weights of the network which is not adapted as learning
proceeds [GBC16].

4.2.3
Output units
The choice of activation function for the output units is tightly coupled with the
choice of cost function. The specific choice depends on the task at hand but it is
a requirement that the activation function is differentiable if the network is to be
trained using the gradient descent algorithm. If the activation function saturates it
can cause the gradient of the cost function to saturate. Saturated functions make the
gradient become very small which is problematic because when the gradient of the
cost function is small it cannot act as a guide for the learning algorithm.

log function undoes the exp function, involved in many activation functions, which
saturates when its arguments are negative. A wide variety of activation functions ex-

used for the units of the output layer as well as the units of the hidden layers. The

4.3
Recurrent neural networks
Recurrent Neural Networks, although conceptualized in the mid 20th century and
popularized with the long short-term memory network publication in 1997 [HS97],
prove to be very capable with enough computational power and have been effectively
utilized for protein sequence learning [Jur+17].
Recurrent neural networks are an extension of feedforward networks that allow cyclic
connections. These networks have been designed to process sequential data. By shar-
ing the same weights across several positions in the sequence the RNN can be applied
to sequences of various lengths and generalize across them. The recurrent connections
allow outputs of preceding positions to influence the output of the current position,
referred to as the state of the network.

The standard approach of encoding categorical data for inference, is known as one-hot

category. Distributed representations, also known as embeddings, are likewise used to
encode categorical values. However, the dimension of the category representations is
not beholden to k. Instead the representation is real valued and trainable. This means
that a neural network can learn semantic relationships between the subcategories.

5.1
Motivation
As described in chapter 2.2, GPI-anchored proteins can be essential to the normal
function of eukaryotes and their detection is relevant to further understanding of
eukrayotic cell function[MR04]. However, the experimental assays required to detect
and verify their presence are costly and time consuming. Computational methods
which are able to accurately approximate the experimental processes, can reduce the
time and cost of experimentation, guide prioritization, expedite research, provide new
insight and add support for current evidence.
There exist, at least, three publicly available methods, with a corresponding peer
reviewed publication, for the prediction of the presence of GPI-anchoring signals and
the position of the ω-site, where the GPI-anchor would be attached. The most recent
of which, PredGPI [PMC08], was published in 2008.
With a decade of advances
in computational power and in deep learning approaches which utilize it, as well as
increased availability of relevant data, there now exists an opportunity for a novel
approach.

5.3
Objective
The objective of GPI prediction is twofold. First, to determine if a protein sequence
includes a GPI-anchoring signal sequence.
Second, if the signal is assumed to be
present, to determine the position of the ω-site. The two tasks are combined and
carried out as one. This is accomplished by adding a placeholder at the end of each
protein sequence, to represent lack of confidence in potential ω-sites. Then the task
becomes one of maximizing the probability of a position in the sequence, also known
as pointing[VFJ15]. Moving forward, the placeholder position will be referred to as
the sentinel. If the sentinel position is the most probable, the GPI-anchoring signal
is determined not to be present[Mer+16; McC+18].

model hyperparameter combinations and to test the performance of the final selection.
In this case, it means that a total of 20 models are trained. For each partition, the
four other partitions are used for validation, where three partitions are used to train
a model and one to validate the performance. Every possible combination of training
and validation sets are used during model selection. This means that, for each parti-
tion in isolation, there are four models trained with the same hyperparameters. To
compare the performance of different combinations of hyperparameters, within the
validation group, the average performance of the four models, is compared with the
average performance using other hyperparameter combinations.
The SIGOPT platform is used for model selection. The SIGOPT optimization engine
provides suggestions for hyperparameters based on inference from the performance
of other hyperparameter combinations, measured with any real valued measure. The
user supplies the values and specifies if the value should be minimized or maximized.
Each of the five sets of four models was trained with at least 200 hyperparameter
combinations. The hyperparameters to tune are: The size of the distributed repre-
sentation (d), the LSTM cell hidden representation (d′), the number of LSTM layers,
the LSTM dropout, the attention hidden representation (d′′), the batch size, and the
optimizer’s learning rate, learning rate decay and weight decay.
Each epoch, after the first, the learning rate is updated by multiplying it with the
learning rate decay.
A model is then trained for each cross-validation split, on a shortlist of hyperpa-
rameter combinations, shown in table 5.1. Each combination was executed 30 times
and the model with the highest validation performance kept. The final 20 models are
shown in table A.1 and their validation performance in table A.2, in the appendix.
Each set of four models is used as an ensemble predictor for the respective test
partition. The logarithm of the probability distribution of each of the four models
is averaged and used as an ensemble prediction. The test results are gathered with
five, four model ensembles. However, the final product is a 20 model ensemble. The
logarithm will emphasize confident models and vocal disagreement between confident
models will present as modality in the combined probability distribution.
The neural network is trained with stochastic gradient descent using the Adam opti-
mizer [KB14]. The models are implemented with the PyTorch deep learning frame-
work [Pas+17].

5.4.3
Model performance evaluation
Two performance measures are used to assess the performance of the model. The
Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC) is used to assess the GPI-anchoring signal
presence discrimination and the F1 score is used to assess the ω-site position predic-
tion. Both are described in chapter 4.5.
The self evaluation during training is the Cross Entropy Loss and the Cross En-
tropy Loss is the sole influence on the gradient descent optimization. However, the
task is two-fold and the usefulness of an ω-site position prediction is predicated on
the quality of the GPI-anchoring signal sequence presence prediction. Therefore a
performance heuristic was devised, to use both as a stopping criteria and for perfor-
mance comparison.
The heuristic is a composition of both the MCC and the F1 score.
Here the F1
score is calculated with a tolerance of two positions from the annotated ω-site. This
flexibility is allowed since only 50 out of the 1002 positive samples have experimen-
tal evidence for the ω-site annotation. The MCC is weighed twice as important, to
emphasize the importance of the GPI-anchoring signal presence prediction over the
ω-site position prediction.

5.5.4
Likelihood & correctness
NetGPI returns the probability distribution, of the ω-site positioning, over the last
100 amino-acid in a protein sequence and the sentinel. During training the position
of highest likelihood is regarded as the predicted position and negative samples have
been coded as the sentinel, with no further regard given to discrimination between
negative and positive samples. Therefore, during training, can any position be re-
garded as a the predicted position even if it has, what seems to be, low likelihood. In
this report and for mass throughput analysis, the interpretation of what position and
what likelihood is regarded is positive and negative is kept inline with the modelling
and the position of highest likelihood is used for presence discrimination. However, in
a practical setting, where samples are examined on a case by case basis, the examiner
can judge the probability distribution itself. The highest false negative likelihood is
0.987, with 75% of the correctly predicted negative above that likelihood. The lowest
true negative likelihood is 0.196. Out of the four false negative predictions, three are
Aspartic acid ω-sites (two experimentally verified) where no probability is assigned

The objective of NuDeep is to predict the localization of proteins within the nucleus
of eukaryotic cells. As covered briefly in chapter 3.2. The dataset contains proteins
labelled with 13 different labels, 11 of which are subnuclear localizations, then there
are samples labelled as non nuclear proteins and samples labelled as shuttling pro-
teins. For a detailed introduction and overview of the methodology, see the NuDeep
manuscript, which is included in chapter 6.4.
6.1
Initial model details
As described in the NuDeep manuscript in chapter 6.4, the initial model design is
built around a residual convolutional neural network (rCNN) structure Specifically,
the arrangement of convolutional layers is structured by designing a common building
block, where an arrangement of rCNN layers is kept, but certain hyperparameter
variations are allowed. The structural arrangement employed by NuDeep is inspired
by the Inception network, as detailed in 6.4.
After two consecutive blocks there
is a max pooling layer, the global maximum of the convolutional features is then
transformed by a two layer feed forward network (FNN), into a logit vector of length
13. Softmax transforms the logit vector into a probability distribution, where the
the highest probability denotes the predicted label. The model input is the protein
sequence encoded by the Blosum62 matrix.

6.1.1
Initial model selection
The dataset is clustered using CD-HIT, to 20% identity and 0.1 E value, then the clus-
ters are partitioned for balanced label composition, see 3.1.4 for further detail. The
sequences are truncated, such that at maximum, the first 400 and last 400 are kept,
whereas sequences longer than 800, loose amino acid in the middle of the sequence,
shorter sequences are padded with 0 at the C-terminus. The initial model does not
attempt to predict the no nuclear proteins.
Five-fold nested cross-validation is used for generalized estimate of the performance
of potential model hyperparameter combinations. There are 20 models trained. Each
of the 5 partitions will be considered a held-out test set, where each of the remaining
partitions is considered a validation set and the other three considered the training
set. Training NuDeep is more time consuming than NetGPI and concurrently compar-
ing multiple hyperparameter combinations is not viable on the infrastructure utilized.
Instead a single set of hyperparameter is used for all 20 models and the validation
performance average, used to guide development.
The neural network is trained with stochastic gradient descent using the Adam op-
timizer [KB14], the cost function is the Cross Entropy Loss. The models are imple-
mented with the PyTorch deep learning framework and their performance is measured
using the K-category correlation coefficent (RK), also known as the Gorodkin. It is
an extension of the Matthews Correlation Coefficent to multiple (> 2) categories.
The validation RK is calculated using only samples with an experimentally verified
annotation for their labelling. The validation RK is also used for early stopping.

Initial model hyperparameters

(16×2×4 = 128). For the second block, the base output channel size increases to 32.
The FFN hidden representation size is 64. It has dropout between the layers, which
is set to 50%.

Abstract
Motivation: The nucleus is highly organised and contains several functionally distinct compartments,
including the nuclear matrix, chromosomal localised proteins and several membrane-less organelles.
For some of these compartments, only a handful of proteins have been reliably identiﬁed, and no
bioinformatical method can reliably predict the subnuclear localisation for these proteins.
Results: Here, we report the development of a predictor, NudeeP, that predict the subnuclear localisation
of a protein with higher accuracy than earlier methods. NudeeP is based on a convolutional neural
network, similar to what has been used in other sequence-based predictors. When trained on the
few experimentally veriﬁed proteins with subnuclear localisations, the performance is similar to earlier
methods. However, we show that discrimination can be signiﬁcantly improved by training NudeeP using
a dataset that is augmented by adding highly homologous protein to the experimentally veriﬁed proteins.
The fraction correct predictions is increased from 18% to 51% by this procedure.

1 Introduction
The eukaryotic cell is deﬁned by the nucleus that contains
the chromosomes and is separated from the cytoplasm
where proteins are synthesised. Therefore, proteins need
to be transported from the cytoplasm to the nucleus via
the nuclear pore complex (NPC). In addition, proteins
are exported from the nucleus to the cytoplasm also
utilisingthenuclearporecomplexandinteractionwithother
proteins (Fu et al., 2018).
The transport through the NPC is regulated by the
presence of signals in a protein sequence, including the
Nuclear Localisation Signal (NLS) (Freitas and Cunha,
2009), which controls the import, and the Nuclear Export
Signals (NES) (Lande et al., 2007) that governs the export.
Proteins can contain both signals and thereby shufﬂe
between the nucleus and the cytoplasm,
Eukaryoticcellscontainseveralsubcellularcompartments,
including mitochondria and the ER/Golgi system, separated
by membranes, but lately, compartments not separated
by lipid membranes has also been identiﬁed.
These
membraneless organelles (MLOs) often exhibit self-
organising properties and are present both in the cytoplasm
and in the nucleus. The formation of these is not well
understood.
Within the nucleus, membraneless nuclear bodies exist.
These can be divided into several different types, including
Cajal bodies, splicing speckles, PML nuclear bodies (PML-
NBs), and other droplets (Gomes and Shorter, 2019; Sawyer
et al., 2018). These bodies separate the nucleus into
distinct functional units where proteins and RNA assemble
into a liquid-like subcellular compartments (Anderson and
Kedersha, 2006; Weber and Brangwynne, 2012; Berchtold
et al., 2018). These compartments contain speciﬁc RNAs,
intrinsically disordered and multivalent hub proteins. The
functional reason for their formation is still unclear, and
different theories have been formulated during the last
decade. In general, it is believed that these bodies are likely
to be important for regulation of gene expression.
For
many
years,
imaging
(Uhlen
et
al.,
2010)
and puriﬁcation-based methods have been the most
used experimental approaches to determine subcellular
localisation (Christoforou et al., 2014; Breckels et al.,

2013). Unfortunately, experimental methods are still not
100% accurate and are expensive and time-consuming.
Therefore, the subnuclear localisation of many proteins is
still unknown.
Computational methods for the prediction of subcellular
localisation have existed for decades (Nakai and Horton,
1999). Their accuracy varies substantially between different
organelles; for instance, the identiﬁcation of signal peptides
is accurate while the prediction of peroxisomal proteins
is poor (Almagro Armenteros et al., 2017). Only a few
methods have been developed that predicts the subnuclear
localisation of proteins, and their accuracy is far from
perfect (Littmann et al., 2019; Kumar et al., 2014). The
performance is quite likely affected that only a handful of
unrelated proteins in some subnuclear localisations have
been identiﬁed.
Subcellular localisation methods can roughly be divided
intotwotypes, machinelearningmethods(AlmagroArmenteros
et al., 2017) and methods based on homology with proteins
of known subcellular localisation. Some methods combine
both approaches (Goldberg et al., 2014). State of the art
methods for subnuclear predictions combines both machine
learning and homology transfer. The advantage of machine
learning methods can be that they can also detect novel
proteins - not related to previously classiﬁed subnuclear
proteins. However, the performance of machine learning
methods can be lower.
With the rise of deep learning, it has become apparent
that convolution neural network (CNN) is one of the best
alternatives to ﬁnd motif(s) and to explore features of
biological sequences. Their extraordinary ability to work
with sequence data and model short-range relationships
between amino acids make them the most suitable approach
to explore new features of biological sequences, including
motifs regulating the association of proteins with nuclear
membraneless organelle proteins. Today, methods using
CNNs to predict the subcellular localisation of proteins
exist (Almagro Armenteros et al., 2017). However, to the
best of our knowledge, no CNN based method has been
developed to predict the association with membraneless
organelles nor subnuclear localisations.
One limitation of deep learning methods is that they
need a large number of experimentally veriﬁed data points
for training. Life science has undergone a revolution
during the last few decades by the introduction of high-
throughput methods that generate a vast amount of data. In
particular, the number of known sequences has increased
exponentially. However, for many biological problems,
including accurate subnuclear determination of proteins,
the generation of reliable data is still time-consuming and
expensive, limiting the use of deep learning methods.
Therefore, the number of proteins with experimentally
identiﬁed subnuclear localisation is meagre. However,
nuclear proteins are identiﬁed by large scale experiments,
without reliable subnuclear annotations. In addition there
exist many orthologous proteins to the experimentally
identiﬁed proteins in other eukaryotic proteomes.
It is well known that in general proteins that are
homologs,
in particular,
orthologs,
perform similar
functions and have similar properties. In general, the more
similar two protein sequences are, the more likely it is that
they share a functional similarity. However, the exact cutoff
dependsontheparticularfunctionandtheproteins. Thisfact
has been used to improve prediction methods by extracting
information from multiple sequence alignments (Rost and
Sander, 1993).
The standard methodology to use homology is by
creating a “proﬁle” describing the amino acid preferences
and conservation for each position of a protein. Although
proﬁle methods increase the information for each protein,
it does not increase the size of the dataset. Here, we
examine an alternative method to use homologous proteins.
We assume that proteins with a high similarity share
the function, in this case, sub-nuclear localisation, with
the experimentally studied proteins. Then we augment
the dataset by adding homologous proteins to the
experimentally veriﬁed proteins. Given the massive number
of sequences available, this can increase the dataset with
several orders of magnitude, enabling the use of deep
learning methods. The ﬁnal performance is then only tested
on the initial dataset.
In this study, we assemble a new protein dataset
with up to date annotations for sub-nuclear and nucleo-
cytoplasmic shuttling proteins that we use to train a novel
algorithm based on deep neural networks. We also create
an augmented dataset where we assume that proteins with
high similarity to a protein in the original dataset have the
same sub-nuclear localisation. Then, we developed NudeeP,
a new deep learning method able to accurately twelve
subnuclear classes including chromosome, nucleoplasm,
nucleo-cytoplasmic shuttling, nuclear membrane, nuclear
pore complex, PML body, Nucleus speckle, Cajal body,
nuclear matrix, nucleoli, other nuclear, and non-nuclear
proteins. We show that using the standard dataset, the
performance of NudeeP is similar to state of the art methods,
but when we use the augmented dataset, it is signiﬁcantly
improved. In contrast with other methods predicting sub-
nuclear localisations, NudeeP uses only sequence features
as inputs.

Training of NudeeP is done using ﬁve-fold nested cross-
validation with approximately the same number of proteins
in each of the ﬁnal 12 categories, using either the initial or
augmented dataset.
NudeeP was trained and optimised using ﬁve-fold nested
cross-validation with approximately the same number of
proteins in each of the ﬁnal 12 categories, see Table 1. Four
groups were used to train/validate the model and to identify
the best set of hyperparameters, while the ﬁfth group that
was kept out of the optimisation and only used to asses the
performance. This procedure was repeated using all ﬁve
subsets as the test set. The advantage of this approach is
that we obtain an unbiased test set performance on the whole
dataset at the expense of having to train 5x4=20 models.

LocNuclei (Littmann et al., 2019) uses Support Vector
Machines (SVMs) to distinguish between 13 distinct sub-
nuclear localisation and traveller proteins, see Table 2. As
for LocTree3 (Goldberg et al., 2014), LocNuclei combined
homology-based inference and machine learning-based de
novo predictions. The annotation is transferred if a similar
protein with experimentally known localisation is available;
otherwise, the SVM-based prediction is returned.
SubNucPred is a method for prediction of sub-nuclear
localisation of proteins using two different approaches. At
ﬁrst, the query sequence is checked for the presence or
absence of unique Pfam domains. If no unique domains are
found, it uses amino acid composition as input for an SVM
model (Kumar et al., 2014). SubNucPred can predict up
to 10 nuclear localisations including Centromere, Nuclear
Envelope, Nuclear Pore Complex, Chromosome, Nuclear
Matrix, Nucleoplasm, Nuclear Speckles, Nucleolus, PML
body and Telomere. Importantly, SubNucPred is not
able to predict proteins that shuttle between Nucleus and
Cytoplasm.

network similar to what has been used in other studies. We
examined several architectures, but in the end, this quite
simple architecture performed as well as any of the other

4 Conclusions
Here,
we introduce NudeeP a deep neural network
architecture able to predict sub-nuclear and nucleo-
cytoplasmic transport proteins. The performance of NudeeP
is comparable to state of the art when trained on
experimentally veriﬁed poteins.
However,
in contrast
to other methods,
NudeeP does not directly transfer
information from homologous proteins. i.e. it should be
better at classiﬁcation proteins that are not related to the
well studies proteins.
Next,
we show that using an augmented dataset
starting from experimentally veriﬁed proteins signiﬁcantly
improved the classiﬁcation. The fraction correctly predicted

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
In this thesis I have presented my research into predictive localization of eukaryotic
proteins. My research shows that current approaches to homology separation, which
are built on multiple layers of approximations, lack the sensitivity needed for the
problem space. I propose an approach which is thorough, and is designed with the
end goal in mind, having comparable, but homology separated subsets, suitable for
cross-validation or nested cross-validation.
I introduce the work of Gíslason et al., a novel, state-of-the-art GPI-anchoring
signal sequence predictor named NetGPI. NetGPI improves upon current methods.
However, the comparison is problematic as the existing experimentally verified data,
overlaps with the training sets of current methods.
Furthermore, the existing ex-
perimentally verified data is not representative of all eukaryotes, rather it is domi-
nated by model organism samples. Utmost care has been taken to develop a model
which generalizes well to non homologous proteins. Furthermore, it has been shown
that NetGPI is able to learn the nature of GPI-anchoring signal sequences. How-
ever, it is still not 100% accurate, especially w.r.t. the placement of the ω-site and
could benefit from more variety in available experimentally verified data.
There-
fore I suggest that care should be taken when these predictors are used as experi-
mental aids. NetGPI provides the probability distribution over the last 100 amino
acids and the sentinel.
The probability distribution on false negative predictions
shows that false negatives can have a probability mass around a potential ω-site,
but also that the probability mass can be completely misplaced and even almost
non-existant, with all probability mass on the sentinel.
A preprint of a previous
rendition of NetGPI, which used CD-HIT for homology separation, can be found on
bioRxiv [Gí+19].
A web service which implements that rendition is available at
https://services.healthtech.dtu.dk/service.php?NetGPI-1.0.
Lastly I cover my involvement in the development of a novel sub-nuclear predictor
named NuDeep. The methodology initially shows promise. However, unlike NetGPI,
the performance of the method is impacted once careful homology partitioning is used
and the objective, model or dataset will have to change.
