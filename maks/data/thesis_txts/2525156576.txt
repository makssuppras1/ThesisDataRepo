Abstract
Seaweeds are gaining an increasing interest as a functional food product. Although drying
is regarded as the preferred method for seaweeds conservation, it is known that it can
aﬀect their bioactive compounds. In the present study, the nutritional composition and
the antioxidant activity of Saccharina latissima were investigated over the drying time,
for a vertical (V) and a horizontal (H) arrangement method of the sugar kelp blades,
under the same conditions (T = 7 - 13 °C, RH 32 - 78%) on a commercial scale. Also, the
two drying methods were compared to each other. It turned out that most of the bioactive
compounds showed no variations (ANOVA; p > 0.05) during none of the drying methods.
The only macronutrient that dramatically decreased was the water (to V, H: 13.3% wet
weight). The protein content (V: 12.7%, H: 11.5% dry matter; DM) and its EAA/AA
ratio (V, H: 0.44), as well as the lipid content (V: 3.4%, H: 3.7% DM) and the fatty
acids composition (saturated; V: 31.3, H: 29.5% FAME, mono-unsaturated; V: 17.7, H:
16.9% FAME, polyunsaturated; V: 36.6, H: 40.4% FAME) remained constant. The total
carbohydrates slightly decreased during the vertical drying (to V: 45.7%, H: 44.2% DM),
due to a concurrent increase of the ash (to V: 26.5%, H: 31.4% DM). Fucoxanthin stayed
also stable (V, H: 882.7 µg/ g DM), like iodine (V: 2.0 mg/ g DM). The total phenolic
content (V: 1.1, H: 1.3 mg gallic acid equivalents/ g DM) and the free radical scavenging
activity (V: 3.4, H: 2.9 mg DPPH/ mL) stayed intact. No signiﬁcant variations were
detected between the two drying methods, apart from the drying rate that was 2.4 times
higher by the vertical drying, and the protein quality, which was higher at the ﬁnal end
products of the horizontal drying (EAA/AA; 12.5% higher, EAA score; 59.0% higher).
Both gave products of a great fatty acid proﬁle (n-6/ n-3; V: 1.2, H: 1.4). All ﬁnal end
products could be labelled as “high in protein”, “low in saturated fat”, “with no added
sugars”, but “high in iodine”, which should be considered by sensitive individuals, due
to the potential negative consequences on human health. Only the horizontally dried
ﬁnal product could be claimed to be “low in fat”. The requirements of the (EU) No
1169/2011 regulation lead to a nutrition declaration that make the end products more
attractive to the consumers, yet misleading because of the protein content (V; declaration:
17.1, scientiﬁc: 12.5 g/ 100 g). The overall recommendation would be a 23-hour vertical
drying to be used for an eﬃcient production of a high-quality product.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In western societies, seaweeds have no tradition of being a signiﬁcant food source [12].
However, their use as a food product and as a functional ingredient has gained increasing
interest over the past decades. This recent trend is endorsed by the nutritional and health
beneﬁts of seaweeds, due to the high content of essential nutrients [13] [14]. Also, the
cultivation of seaweed is regarded as a great opportunity to reduce agricultural land use
and related environmental burdens [13], in the framework of a more sustainable future.
Currently, the majority of seaweed production is aimed at their use in agriculture as
fertilisers and animal feed, and for producing hydrocolloids, such as alginate, agar-agar
and carrageenan [15]. The abundance of bioactive substances though, as well as the rich
and unique ﬂavours in many species, make seaweeds an attractive raw material in the
food sector [14]. In particular, certain seaweeds contain considerable quantities of lipids,
protein, carbohydrates, antioxidants and minerals, but the nutrient content can vary with
species, geography, location, season, water temperature, salinity, and light intensity [16]
[17]. Saccharina latissima (sugar kelp) is an edible species, which is accepted as a non-
novel food in EU [18], and so it is suitable for consumption and further commercialisation.
Drying is considered as the preferred method for stabilising the seaweeds biomass for
transportation and long-term storage [12]. Nonetheless, the eﬀect of drying on the bio-
chemical composition, antioxidant activity and nutritional value of seaweed biomass raises
a question [19].
1
==PAGE:24==
1.1
Aim of the Study
The aim of this study is to investigate any variations in:
• the nutritional composition; ash, iodine, protein, lipid, carbohydrate and pigments
content,
• the amino acids, fatty acids and pigments proﬁle,
• the quality of the protein and lipid content and
• the antioxidant activity of sugar kelp
during drying in a dehumidiﬁed ambient of 10.8 °C. When vertically hanged, the drying
lasts for 29 hours, whereas when horizontally laid, it lasts for 42 hours. It is therefore
aimed to:
• compare the above variations between two diﬀerent ways (vertically and horizon-
tally) of placing the sugar kelp blades to dry.
Also:
• the nutrition declarations and the nutrition claims of the ﬁnal products are intended
to be identiﬁed and compared.
The end-all, is to:
• track down the most eﬃcient drying method, so that a high-quality product and
its optimised production to be achieved.
1.2
Hypothesis
In the present study, the drying conditions in question are rather gentle, as there is no
applied heat, forced convection, vacuum, nor mechanical stress, but only dehumidifying.
Therefore, it is hypothesised that during drying:
• the crude protein, lipid, carbohydrate and ash content stay constant
2
==PAGE:25==
However, the drying causes continuous removal of water which creates room for oxygen
to access internally the seaweed matrix. Also, an overall long exposure (29 or 42 hours)
of the seaweeds to oxygen takes place. Consequently, the proteins, the lipids, but also
the pigments are put at risk of oxidation. Thus, it is expected that during drying:
• the water content decreases,
• protein and lipid quality deteriorates,
• the content of chlorophyll a, chlorophyll c, and β-carotene is reduced (but fucoxan-
thin is constant), and
• the antioxidant activity weakens.
Elevated concentrations of iodine in the ﬁnal end products can be a barrier to their
commercialisation, due to its negative health eﬀects on sensitive individuals [12].
In
particular, S. latissima is reported to bioaccumulate considerable amounts of iodine [14].
Nevertheless, iodine is a volatile halogen, so it is hypothesised that during drying:
• the iodine content decreases.
The way of placing the seaweeds (vertically or horizontally) to dry allows diﬀerent physico-
chemical, enzymatic, and microbial processes to occur. Water can be more easily removed
from vertically hanged blades, and drip to the ground. Therefore, it is expected that:
• the vertically dried seaweeds have a higher drying rate,
• the vertically dried end products have diﬀerent antioxidant activity, amino acid and
fatty acid composition, and thence,
• diﬀerent protein and lipid quality attributes than horizontally dried blades.
In addition, it is hypothesised that the nutritional composition of the end products will
permit them to be:
• labelled with nutrition claims, possibly for the high protein content.
Finally, with regards to the rather long lasting drying, it is expected that:
• a shorter drying period can be suggested, without compromising the bioactives
content, nor the quality of the ﬁnal dried S. latissima product.

Chapter 2
Theoretical Background
2.1
Seaweeds: uses and value
Algae are photosynthetic organisms and they have reproductive simple structures. They
constitute 25 - 30,000 species, with a wide variety of forms, sizes, colours and composition,
and can exist from unicellular microscopic organisms (microalgae) to multicellular of great
size (macroalgae) [20] [21]. The term “seaweed” is rather a popular term used to describe
the large marine algae [21]. Seaweeds are generally reside in the littoral zone and they
are classiﬁed into three major groups: the green algae (Chlorophyta, Chlorophyceae), the
brown algae (Heterokontophyta, Phaeophyceae), and the red algae (Rhodophyta) [17]
[21].
The world production of seaweeds reached 32.9 million tonnes in 2017, 97% of which
was harvested from aquaculture [22], and is increasing at a steady rate of 8% every year
[17]. Seaweeds have many uses; they are consumed as food in several cultures, notably in
China and Japan [13], and they are used in medicine for the treatment of iodine deﬁciency
and for intestinal disorders [21].
They also provide many ingredients to the food or
pharmaceutical industries such as hydrocolloids, pigments, vitamins, minerals, as well as
prebiotic substances in the form of complex carbohydrates and phlorotannins. Seaweeds
are often used as organic fertilisers and biostimulants, since they are usually rich in
potassium but poor in nitrogen and phosphorus, and some species such as Gracilaria and
Ulva are suitable for bioremediation [21]. This means that their cultivation could alleviate
eutrophication problems due to aquaculture or to wastewater discharges. Seaweeds are
even good substrates for generating biogas. Moreover, seaweed meal, which is produced
5
==PAGE:28==
by drying and milling, and extracts of particular seaweed species are used as animal feed
[21].
Focusing on their utilisation as food, recent interest among consumers to avoid chemical
additives and preservatives in their diet has led to recognition of seaweed as a source of
natural and healthy food. An already established worldwide market is around hydrocol-
loids as ingredients, such as agar, alginates and carageenan, from brown and red seaweeds
[23]. On the top of this, reports on the nutritional value of seaweeds have contributed
to a growing demand for seaweed food products [17]. It is proved that seaweeds are a
rich source of vitamins, minerals, antioxidants, omega-3 fatty acids, dietary ﬁbres and
secondary metabolites, such as fucoxanthin [12].
In fact, due to the complex habitats where they grow, they are exposed to extreme con-
ditions (eg. changes of salinity, temperature, nutrients, and UV-vis irradiation) in which
they must adapt rapidly to survive, producing a great variety of secondary metabolites,
which cannot be found in other organisms [20]. However, the chemical composition and
nutritional content of seaweeds can vary, depending on multiple factors like species, geo-
graphical location, season, water temperature, salinity, and light intensity. The bioactive
compounds found in seaweeds have been documented to exhibit various biological and
health-promoting activities, like anticancer, antitumor, antiviral, antiinﬂammatory and
antiangiogenic eﬀects [17]. Therefore, seaweeds are increasingly recognised as versatile
whole foods, supported by health food trends and the use of local and natural ingredients
[12].
2.1.1
Brown Seaweeds & Saccharina latissima
Brown algae have lower nutritional value compared to red and green algae, due to their
lower protein and higher mineral content. Nonetheless, brown algae contain a remarkable
number of bioactive compounds [21]. Also, due to their larger size (up to 35 – 45 m in
length for some kelp species) and ease of harvesting, brown seaweeds have been more
studied and are more exploited than the other algae types. They live mainly in shallow
waters or on shoreline rocks, and they have very ﬂexible stems that allow them to with-
stand the constant pounding of the waves. They are extremely variable in shape, and
some of the most common species include Laminaria, Saccharina and Sargassum [21].
6
==PAGE:29==
Species of Saccharina are mostly harvested in Asian countries, some parts of Europe,
and in coastal North America [17]. Large-scale seaweed cultivation in Europe largely
focuses on kelp species (Laminariaceae), especially on Saccharina latissima, because of
its phytochemical content and ability to achieve high biomass yields in a short time [12].
Saccharina latissima (S. latissima) is also known as sugar kelp, due to its sweet taste.
It grows in sheltered waters on rocky substrata and mainly in cold-temperate waters, its
frond grows fast in length from November to April, in weight in the summer, and it lives
for 2 to 4 years [17] [21] [24]. The morphology of S. latissima can be seen in Figure 2.1
and it consists of a basal holdfast, a ﬁrm cylindrical stipe and a single, undivided and
smooth laminated blade, which can grow up to about four meters long [17].
Figure 2.1: Fresh Saccharina latissima. Picture by Jennifer Steen Booher [1].
2.2
Water
Water is the most abundant molecule in food and is a fundamental ingredient to support
life. Water determines the physical, chemical and microbiological stability of foods, and
that is why many food processes involve the addition or removal of water [23].
Fresh seaweeds contain considerable amount of water (70 - 90%) [21], which makes them
susceptible to enzymatic deterioration, lipid oxidation, and microbial attack [17]. Al-
though there are several alternatives of removing water (e.g. salting, freezing), drying
7
==PAGE:30==
is the preferred method for stabilising seaweed biomass for maintaining product quality,
long-term storage and ensuring the safety of the consumer [17] [12]. This can be achieved
by minimising the ﬁnal moisture content of dried seaweed to less than 20% on a dry
basis, so that the water activity is approximately 0.6, limiting the amount of free water
available for all life forms (bacteria, mold, yeast) in the food, increasing its safety and
extending its shelf life [17].
2.3
Drying
Drying is a process during which water is removed from a material by evaporation (by a
stream of air or diﬀusion), sublimation, or an osmotic process. When there is use of air,
it is called convective drying, whereas in vacuum drying predominates the diﬀusion, and
in freeze drying prevails the sublimation [2].
Food drying is the removal of water from a food product. During drying of solid food,
water (in the form of moisture) moves from the internal regions of the product to the
surrounding air [3].
This occurs by a simultaneous process of heat (conduction) and
mass transfer (diﬀusion) [2], as it is demonstrated in Figure 2.2 (A). Free water near the
surface of the product is easily removed by evaporation, as it moves by gaseous diﬀusion
to the surrounding environment. At the same time, capillary forces draw water from
more internal regions to the empty porous spaces near the surface [3], as it is illustrated
in Figure 2.2 (B).
The moving force for both the gas and liquid diﬀusion is the water activity gradient;
water moves from regions of high to low water gradient [2] [3]. However, two resistances
to water transport occur; internal and external mass transfer resistances. The relationship
between them aﬀects the course of drying [2]. A typical plot of the water content versus
the drying time is given in Figure 2.3.
8
==PAGE:31==
Figure 2.2: A; Heat and mass transfer [2], and B; Movement of water from product interior
to the surface [3] during solid food drying.
The rate of drying is found by diﬀerentiating this curve. The plot of the drying rate
versus the water content (or drying time) shows that the rate of drying changes during
the course of drying (Figure 2.4). Sometimes, there is a short initial lag phase (A-B), in
which the drying increases or decreases [3].
Figure 2.3: Water content (g) versus drying time (h) [3].
If the external resistance is larger than or equal to the internal resistance, the transfer of
water to the surface of the solid is constant, and the drying is performed in a constant
rate (B-C). On the contrary, if the internal resistance is larger than the external, less
9
==PAGE:32==
water is transferred to the surface at a time. As a consequence, the ﬂux of evaporated
water decreases with time, and the drying is performed at a falling rate (C-D and D-E)
[2]. Thus, the internal mass transfer resistance determines the drying rate and the time
that the process lasts [3].
Figure 2.4: Drying rate versus water content [3]. A-B; lag phase, B-C; constant rate, C-E;
falling rate.
2.3.1
Drying of Seaweed
Sun drying, freeze drying, heat pump drying [17], oven drying [25] and vacuum drying [26]
are the most common techniques employed in seaweed studies. On an industrial scale,
these methods are widely applied, while Dansk Tang uses an adsorption dehumidiﬁer,
without applying heat, forced convection, nor vacuum.
The drying method can highly aﬀect the functional properties, bioactive compounds,
volatile compounds (aroma, ﬂavor), and antioxidant activity in the ﬁnished seaweed prod-
uct, depending on the disintegration of the food matrix during the process [17]. At the
same time, there are other constraints when deciding which drying technique should be
applied.
These refer to the energy requirement, the capital costs and the capability
for large-scale operations. For instance, freeze drying can yield better quality products,
but it is very energy consuming, its operating costs are high, and it is unproﬁtable to
scale-up the production [17]. On the contrary, oven drying is faster, its equipment and
operation cost is lower, while its capacity is much bigger. The use of high temperature
10
==PAGE:33==
though, caused greater nutrient loss and lower physico-chemical properties of seaweed
[25]. Therefore, when choosing the most appropriate drying method for seaweeds, both
the product quality and ﬁnancial factors should be considered.
When the method is decided, there are some factors that can aﬀect the ﬁnal product
quality. These are the drying time, in relation with the temperature and the humidity
of the ambient air, but also the harvest time of the fresh material [17] [27].
During
drying, the free water attached to the solid matrix of food is removed, creating an empty
space and stress at a cellular level, leading to material shrinkage [27]. Consecutively, the
drying time aﬀects the textural properties and the shelf-life of a food product [27]. It
has been reported that when the air drying temperature increases, while the humidity
decreases, the drying rate is higher, and the ﬁnal water content of the food item is lower
[17]. Also, that drying at temperature lower than 50 °C and humidity less than 25% is
suitable in terms of the processing cost, functional properties, and preservation of the
bioactive compounds in dried sugar kelp [17]. Dried blades of S. latissima can be seen
in Figure 2.5. Eﬀects of seasonal harvesting time are very signiﬁcant for the bioactive
compounds content of S. latissima samples [17], as well as for their antioxidant activity
[28], as it will be described later.
Figure 2.5: Dried blades of Saccharina latissima. Picture by Jonas Drotner Mouritsen [4].
2.4
Ash
Ash refers to the inorganic residue remaining after the combustion or complete acid-
facilitated oxidation of organic matter in food. Its content represents the total mineral
content in foods and, when foods are high in particular minerals, ash content can be
11
==PAGE:34==
important under a nutritional, toxicological, and food quality point of view [29]. This
implies that, even though ash represents a fundamental part of the nutritional value of
a food product, its amount should be taken into consideration, as the excess of certain
minerals can have adverse health eﬀects [30]. That is why EFSA has set tolerable upper
intake levels [30].
2.4.1
Ash in Seaweed
Seaweeds have strong bioadsortive and bioaccumulative capacities [31], so the ash content
can be much higher than it is in terrestrial biomass. In land vegetables it only accounts
for 1 – 10% of dry matter, compared to around 30% of dry solids in kelp species [32], in
which the ash is the second most abundant component after carbohydrates [12].
Generally, brown seaweed is highly rich in ash content which can account for over 50%
of its dry weight [32]. Its content can vary though, depending on the seaweed species,
composition of their cell walls, geographical origins, physiological stress, pH, the salinity
of water and other environmental changes, their method of mineralization [12], but also
on the season of harvesting [33]. The mineral content found in the sugar kelp is higher
than any other terrestrial plant and the highest among the seaweeds [12]. In particular,
the two metals that are strongly linked to the ash content of S. latissima seemed to
be K (26 - 120 g/ kg dry solids) and Na (12 - 48 g/ kg dry solids) [31]. Among the
micronutrients, iodine was reported to have the highest concentration (up to 6.6 g/ kg
of dry solids) [14]. Speciﬁcally, the highest values of ash in S. latissima from the inner
Danish waters were observed in January, whereas the lowest in July [33]. The values in
a dried seaweed product can vary also depending on the drying method applied. It is
reported that the faster rate in oven drying preserved the ash content of three Sargassum
species better than the freeze drying [25].
2.5
Iodine
Iodine is a trace element that is found in some foods, either naturally or by fortiﬁcation,
and available as a dietary supplement. It is an essential component of the thyroid hor-
mones which regulate many important biochemical reactions, including protein synthesis
and enzymatic activity, and are crucial factors of metabolic activity [34]. Iodine deﬁciency
12
==PAGE:35==
in the diet of people in several areas of the world is a usual phenomenon, since it is not
present in suﬃcient amounts in most of the foods (a few mg/ 100 g), resulting in goitre
[35]. Because of this, some countries have even implemented salt iodization programs, in
order to reduce the prevalence of iodine deﬁciency [34].
2.5.1
Iodine in Seaweed
However, iodine is abundant in most seaweeds, nominating them as an ideal natural
source of this mineral. Brown seaweeds can generally bioaccumulate many elements and
are a rich source of magnesium, copper, iron, and iodine as well as other scarcer elements
[36]. In particular, S. latissima was reported to contain extremely high iodine content
(up to 6.6 mg/ g of dry matter [14], while EFSA has suggested an upper limit for adults
of 0.6 mg/ day [30]) which, on the other side, could have negative consequences on human
health. Especially in sensitive individuals, if large amounts are ingested often and for an
extended period of time. This can lead to a negative impact on its nutritional value but,
luckily, these levels can be reduced by processing [12].
It has been reported that freeze drying can lower the iodine content more than air drying
(3.0 and 5.9 mg/g of dry weight, respectively) [12]. As might be expected, the mineral
composition varies according to the species, as well as many other factors such as sea-
sonal, environmental, geographical and physiological variations [37]. Truly, studies on
the iodine content of the species A. esculenta, P. palmata and Ulva intestinalis reported
no diﬀerences in the iodine content between freeze drying and air drying [12]. Regard-
ing seasonal variations, the highest concentration of iodine in S. latissima cultivated in
Scotland, was reported in August (4.8 mg/ g) whereas the lowest in October (2.8 mg/
g). Beyond that, S. latissima seemed to contain 80% more metal ions, compared to L.
hyperborea and L. digitata [32].
2.6
Proteins
Proteins are polymers of peptides which, in their turn, are polymers of 21 diﬀerent amino
acids (AA) linked together by peptide bonds. Peptide bonds are formed when the car-
boxylic acid of one amino acid and the ammonia group of another react, producing one
molecule of water [38], as demonstrated in Figure 2.6. Amino acids consist of elements
13
==PAGE:36==
like hydrogen, carbon, oxygen, sulphur and nitrogen, which is the most distinctive ele-
ment in proteins [39]. Proteins can range from a few amino acids joined together to some
thousands of amino acids in large proteins [38].
Figure 2.6: Formation of a peptide bond between two amino acids and production of one
molecule of water [5].
Although proteins are an abundant component of both animal and plant products, at-
tention should be paid on the quality. This is because there are nine amino acids that
humans cannot synthesise, and these are phenylalanine, valine, threonine, tryptophan,
methionine, leucine, isoleucine, lysine, and histidine [38]. They are called essential amino
acids (EAA) and their amount present in a protein, as well as their availability, is a way to
determine its nutritional quality. Especially in proteins of vegetable origin, there is often
a deﬁciency of one or more of the essential amino acids (usually methionine, lysine and
tryptophan) and, since in many parts of the world the diet is based on plant products,
the quality of the proteins are of a high importance [38] [40]. In the recent years, three of
the essential amino acids, the branched chain amino acids (BCAA) (leucine LEU, valine
VAL and isoleucine ILE), have gained popularity due to their contribution to muscle pro-
tein synthesis, and a multi-million dollar industry of nutritional supplements has grown
around it [41]. In parallel, two particular amino acids, the glutamic acid (GLU) and the
aspartic acid (ASP), have been associated with the umami taste which is the ﬁfth basic
taste and describes the sensation of deliciousness [4].
A way to estimate the quality of the protein under a nutritional aspect of view, is by the
ratio of the EAA/AA. As another way to describe the quality, the essential amino acid
score should be calculated, which takes into consideration the protein requirements that
meet the metabolic needs for humans, and have been published by WHO, FAO and UNU
[42] [43]. As an overall, the quality of protein for human consumption depends both on
the proﬁle of the amino acids and the digestibility of the proteins [43].

2.6.1
Protein in Seaweed
When it comes to seaweed, the protein fraction varies with the species. As a rule, its
concentration is low in brown seaweed (up to 24% of dry weight in Undaria), in contrast
to the green (maximum 44% in Ulva spp.)
and red (up to 44% in Porphyra tenera)
seaweed where it is higher. However, brown seaweed proteins have also been reported as
a rich source of essential amino acids like threonine, valine, leucine and lysine, but also
glycine and alanine [37]. It is worth to mention that the protein in algae contains all the
essential amino acids, and in concentrations higher than in terrestrial plants, even when
they are detected as limiting amino acids (i.e. essential amino acids present in the lowest
amount compared to human requirements) [37]. Their levels remain high despite the
ﬂuctuation in their concentration throughout the year [44]. In respect to the digestibility
of the seaweed proteins, the in vivo digestibility is not well documented. However, it
is known that brown seaweed contains high content of phenolic compounds, as well as
dietary ﬁbres, which act as antinutritional factors by limiting the digestibility of the
proteins, lowering their uptake [38] [12].
Seasonal variation in the protein content of S. latissima has been observed by many
studies. One of them showed that it was higher in February - March (13 – 14% of dry
weight), and lower during July - September (5 - 8% of dry weight) [45]. Another showed
that it was higher in November (9.7 – 10.8% of dry weight) and lower in May – July
(1.3 – 1.7% of dry weight) [33]. So, the protein content varies not only according to the
species and the season, but also to other factors, such as the geographical location, and
the environmental conditions [17]. Consequently, these variations bring to ﬂuctuations of
the EAA/AA ratio, the EAA score and the identiﬁcation of the limiting amino acid [33]
[43]. Nevertheless, histidine seems to be the limiting amino acid in most cases in these
studies. When drying of S. latissima occurs, the protein content seems not to change
by the applied drying conditions (type, temperature, humidity), but also the relative
amounts of individual amino acids remains constant [17] [12].
2.6.2
How to Determine the Protein Content
Protein content can be determined either directly or indirectly, and several methods have
been developed, based on diﬀerent analytical principles. The direct protein determination
15
==PAGE:38==
is done by analysing the amino acid residues. The indirect method includes the deter-
mination of the nitrogen content, or chemical reactions of functional groups within the
protein [46]. Each method seems to have its own advantages and disadvantages, leading
to an inevitable compromise on the ﬁnal results.
The direct method requires the step of the amino acids hydrolysis. This can cause par-
tial (methionine and serine) or full degradation (cysteine, tryptophan) of some amino
acids, and the fact that there is one and only hydrolysis step cannot assure the complete
hydrolysis of certain amino acids without the destruction of others [47]. Therefore, the
amino acid analysis may underestimate the protein content, and it remains a method that
requires a high ﬁnancial budget. Nonetheless, it is considered to be the most accurate
way of determining protein [47].
On the other hand, the indirect method of determining the total nitrogen content is
simple and more aﬀordable. In this method, the total nitrogen is translated into protein
by a conversion factor. The traditional and most commonly used conversion factor of
6.25 is based on an assumption that, ﬁrstly, the nitrogen content in proteins is 16% and,
secondly, that all nitrogen in foods belongs to the proteins [46]. However, it is known that
the relative content varies between diﬀerent amino acids, but also that each protein is
made of a diﬀerent amino acids composition. What is more, there is a wide range of other
compounds, like nitrate, ammonia, urea, nucleic acids, free amino acids, chlorophylls and
alkaloids that contain nitrogen [46], yet it is included in the calculation for the protein
content. Thus, the universal conversion factor of 6.25 can either underestimate or, in most
cases, overestimate the protein content, aﬀecting also the ﬁnancial value of the product.
Even though these errors are acknowledged by FAO, the determination of protein based
on total nitrogen content is considered acceptable, but the use of 6.25 as conversion
factor should be used only when the speciﬁc factor is not known [42]. Nevertheless, the
European Parliament and the Council of the European Union have set the Regulation
(EU) No 1169/2011, which requires the catholic use of the 6.25 factor on the labelling of
all food products [48].
In fact, all plant-based sources, including macroalgae, can accumulate non-protein ni-
trogenous compounds, like chlorophyll, nucleic acids, free amino acids and inorganic
nitrogen (e.g. nitrate, nitrite and ammonia), so the inclusion of nitrogen in the conver-
sion calculation can lead to an over-estimate of protein contents in seaweeds [47]. Several
16
==PAGE:39==
studies have calculated species-speciﬁc nitrogen-to-protein conversion factors, but gener-
ally for seaweed, a default factor of 5 is recommended to be used, after a comprehensive
study on several species, geographic regions, and a range of nitrogen contents [47]. As
for S. latissima, the nitrogen-to-protein conversion factor has been calculated to be 2.0
at the Faeroe Islands [43], 3.9 in the inner Danish waters [49], 3.7 at other regions of
Denmark [50], and 5.3 in Scotland [32]. Despite the variations of the factor according
to the geographical regions, the diﬀerences between the months of harvesting of fresh S.
latissima were either signiﬁcant (low in winter, higher in summer) [50], or not [43]. The
drying method applied did not seem to aﬀect the nitrogen-to-protein conversion factor,
though [12].
2.7
Lipids & Fatty Acids Proﬁle
Lipids are substances that, in general, are soluble in organic solvents like ether and chlo-
roform, but are with restrain soluble in water [51]. They have many important functions
in vivo and in food where, most commonly, they are found as triglycerides grouped as
fats and oils [52]. However, the terms lipids, fats, and oils are often used interchangeably
[51]. Triglycerides have a glycerol backbone with three fatty acids attached through ester
bonds, as it can be seen in Figure 2.7. Fatty acids consist of hydrocarbon chains with a
carboxylic acid at the end of the molecule [52]. The chain length and the saturation level
of this hydrocarbon chain determine the physical and biological properties of lipids. For
instance, unsaturated lipids can undergo oxidation reactions, the products of which may
make the fat-containing foods inappropriate for consumption [52].
Figure 2.7: Formation of a triglyceride (c), by esteriﬁcation of one glycerol molecule (a) with
three fatty acids (b) [6].
17
==PAGE:40==
Despite that, polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFAs) are fundamental nutrients which can-
not, or only to a lesser extent, be synthesised by humans. Thus, they must be directly in-
gested by the diet [53]. PUFAs can be classiﬁed into two groups, based on their metabolic
connections; the omega-3 (n-3) and omega-6 (n-6). The ﬁrst are usually provided by ﬁsh
and vegetable sources, while the second are ingested mainly via vegetable oil [53].
Two PUFAs that cannot be synthesised by humans are α-linolenic acid (ALA) (C18:3,
n-3) and linoleic acid (LA) (C18:2, n-6). Within the body, the ALA is converted (even
restrictedly) to eicosapentaenoic acid (EPA, C20:5, n-3) and docosahexaenoic acid (DHA,
C22:6, n-3) (seen in Figure 2.8), while the LA is converted to arachidonic acid (ARA)
[53].
The balance of n-3 and n-6 in the diet is health-promoting, as n-3 have been
reported to exhibit anti-inﬂammatory and antioxidant activity, to prevent breast cancer
and, especially EPA and DHA, are major components of brain cells and crucial for proper
development and functioning of the brain and the nervous system. On the other side, most
n-6 fatty acids can prevent inﬂammation and tumour growth [53]. Most importantly, the
ratio n-6/ n-3 of the dietary intake should be taken into consideration, which is currently
recommended by WHO to be lower than 10 [16].

Chemical structures of eicosapentanoic acid (EPA) and docosahexanoic acid
(DHA) [7].
2.7.1
Lipid Content & PUFAs in Seaweed
Seaweeds contain only small amounts of lipids (1 – 5% of dry matter), but the majority
of those lipids are long-chain polyunsaturated n-3 and n-6 fatty acids [21]. Traditionally,
the major source of these fatty acids, like ARA, EPA and DHA, is ﬁsh oil. However, it is
supposed that the original source of these PUFAs is not the ﬁsh itself, but marine algae
and other dietary sources of it [53]. Indeed, it is proven that seaweed, speciﬁcally, contain
fatty acids of linear chains, even number of carbon atoms, and one or more double bonds
18
==PAGE:41==
[16]. Although the lipid content in seaweed is lower than that of other marine sources, it
is reported that the level of n-3 and n-6 PUFAs of seaweed is higher than the one of land
vegetables, and they are especially a good source of EPA, which could even reach 50% of
the total fatty acids [37] [44] [54].
Regarding brown seaweed, high concentrations of oleic acid (C18:1, n-9) with 4.1 – 20.9%,
and ALA with 3.6–13.8% of the total fatty acid content have been observed. When it
comes to EPA though, brown seaweeds contain lower concentrations at 5.9 – 13.6% of
the total fatty acids, compared to red algae (48.0 – 51.0%) [44].
In S. latissima, which has a low lipid content (0.8 - 2% of dry weight [12]), the ratio n-6/
n-3 is found to be 0.8 - 1.3, while it is recorded that the EPA is at 5.4 – 16% of the total
fatty acid content, deviation which can account for variations in the season, geographic
area, salinity of the water and other environmental factors [37] [54]. For example, in the
inner Danish waters, S. latissima contained high concentrations of EPA in July (13.5%)
and low in January (4.7%), a trend which was observed also for the DHA. As it was
expected, variations like these in the concentrations of the PUFAs aﬀected the ratio n-6/
n-3 as well [54]. On its advantage, the high proportions of n-3 PUFAs at n-6 PUFAs’
expense in brown seaweed make this ratio nutritionally ideal [44].
The lipid content of S. latissima seemed to be insusceptible to several drying methods,
like freeze drying, sun drying, and even to a range of diﬀerent temperatures and humidity
levels [17]. The same conclusion was reached by a study on seaweeds of Sargassum spp.,
dried by sun, oven, and freeze drying. However, there were signiﬁcant diﬀerences in the
fatty acids composition among the drying methods, as freeze dried seaweed samples had
higher levels of PUFAs than the sun and oven dried samples [55].
2.8
Carbohydrates
Technically, carbohydrates are hydrates of carbon with the empirical formula Cm(H2O)n.
They can be divided into three chemical groups, according to their molecular size (clas-
siﬁcation by FAO/WHO [56]): monosaccharides (degree of polymerization; DP 1 - 2),
oligosaccharides (DP 3 - 9), and polysaccharides (DP > 9) [23]. In plants, a broad range
of monosaccharides and oligosaccharides are found, as well as storage polysaccharides
such as starch, and structural polysaccharides such as cellulose and hemicellulose. Gums
19
==PAGE:42==
are a varied group of polysaccharides obtained from plants, seaweeds, and microorgan-
isms. Gums (e.g. agar, alginates and carageenan in seaweed) are widely used in the food
industry as gelling, stabilizing, and suspending agents, due to their hydrophilicity which
enables them to be combined with water to form viscous solutions or gels, even at low
concentrations [23].
Carbohydrates can also be sorted by the chemical structure and their ease to be ab-
sorbed and digested. Based on this, the digestible carbohydrates can be converted into
monosaccharides, which are absorbed and provide metabolic energy [57]. In humans,
only the polymers of starch can follow this pathway. All the other polysaccharides are
non-digestible, and they can be divided into soluble and insoluble.
In terrestrial plants, dietary ﬁbre consist of lignin and other non-digestible, non-absorbed
substances [57]. Its consumption is proven to be health-promoting, since it supports the
growth and protection of the beneﬁcial intestinal ﬂora and, in combination with high
glycemic load foods, it reduces the overall glycemic response, preventing diabetes. Also,
it protects against obesity and reduces the risk of colon cancer [37] [44]. It is also used as
ingredients, due to its ability to modify important functional properties of food products,
even by being added at low levels [57].
2.8.1
Carbohydrates in Seaweed
Seaweeds contain large amounts of polysaccharides (up to 76% of dry weight) [37], most
of which are not digestible by the human gastrointestinal tract and, therefore, they are
dietary ﬁbres [44]. The representative carbohydrates in brown algae consist of fucoidan
(which is actually found embedded in the cell wall and inter-cellular spaces, and is asso-
ciated with protection against the eﬀects of dehydration during low tide [58]), laminarin
(β-1.3-glucan), cellulose, alginates, and mannitol [44]. The structural polysaccharides are
thought to physically support the thallus in water [37]. Speciﬁcally, in S. latissima the
content of the total polysaccharides can range from 38 - 61% of dry weight, depending on
the season [37], but also on the geographical region of harvesting [12]. However, the dry-
ing method (freeze drying versus air drying) and the temperature applied during the air
drying of the samples, did not seem to signiﬁcantly aﬀect the total carbohydrate content
[12].

The basic carbohydrate proﬁle of S. latissima consists of alginic acid at 17 - 33%, mannitol
at 2 - 19%, laminarin up to 33% and cellulose at 4.9 - 9% of dry weight, and glucose at

2.9
Pigments
Colour is an important attribute of food products, as it plays a major role in the con-
sumer’s acceptability, but it also provides information about any chemical changes, indi-
cating the overall quality. Processing, storage and preparation may change the colour of
the food, which can be often undesirable by the consumers [59]. Therefore, in most cases,
preserving the natural colours of a food product is a critical objective [59]. Pigments,
substances that give colour to food, like carotenoids and anthocyanins, can be isolated
and added to food products in order to provide or enhance their colour [59].
Nowadays, due to the high customer requirements, together with the trend for “clean
labels”, there is a strong preference for naturally produced pigments, rather than synthe-
sised [60]. Natural pigments, like β-carotene, riboﬂavin or anthocyanins act not only as
colourants, but also as nutrients and even as nutraceuticals [59]. It is a fact that some of
the pigments participate in metabolic processes, while some others work as antioxidants
and protect sensitive biomolecules from oxidative damage. So, these compounds conduct
a plethora of key functions in nature [61].
2.9.1
Pigments in Seaweed
By deﬁnition, the three categories into which macroalgae is classiﬁed (brown, green and
red seaweeds) are based on a wide range of pigments that absorb light for photosynthesis
[17]. So, among the other previously mentioned bioactive compounds, seaweeds are a rich
source of pigments, whose composition and concentrations are of scientiﬁc and industrial
interest.
In brown seaweeds, the most important pigments are found to be chlorophyll a (which
actively performs photosynthesis and converts CO2 and water into carbohydrates and
oxygen with the use of light energy [58]) and accessory pigments like chlorophyll c, β-
carotene, as well as other carotenoids, such as fucoxanthin. The latter is the predominant
pigment in brown seaweeds [17].
21
==PAGE:44==
Chlorophylls are green lipid-soluble pigments found in all algae, higher plants or cyanobac-
teria and perform photosynthesis [37].
They are tetrapyrrole pigments in which the
porphyrin ring is in the dihydro form, and the central metal atom is magnesium [59].
Chlorophylls exist in diﬀerent forms (e.g., chlorophylls a, b, c1, c2, and d), depending
on subtle distinctions in either the porphyrin ring itself or in the side chain groups. For
instance, chlorophylls a (seen in Figure 2.9) and b diﬀer only in the substituent group
on the C7 of the molecule, as chlorophyll a has a -CH3, whereas chlorophyll b has a
-CH=O [61]. When the magnesium is removed, which can be done by exposure to acid
and heat treatment, pheophytins a and b arise which have an olive-brown colour [59] [61].
In general, the stability of the chlorophylls can be changed by exposure to light, air, heat,
and extreme pH. Some of their health-promoting eﬀects are their ability to rebuild the
bloodstream and to deactivate toxins in the body, their antibacterial properties, as well
as their capability to promote fertility, due to their high Mg content [61].
Figure 2.9: Structure of Chlorophyll a [8].
Carotenoids are also fat or oil soluble, and heat stable substances [61]. They are tetrater-
penes and one way to categorise them is into carotenes, which are hydrocarbons, and
xanthophylls (e.g. lutein, astaxanthin, diadinoxanthin and zeaxanthin), which contain
one or more oxygen molecules via hydroxylation of the carotens by hydrolases [37] [59]
[61]. Carotenoids are the most widespread pigments in nature, and they are found in all
algae, higher plants and many photosynthetic bacteria [37]. They are responsible for the
bright red, yellow and orange hues. In fact, the more conjugated double bonds present
in the molecule, the more the hue will become more red [59]. They may occur in the free
form, like fucoxanthin, or in protein complexes [61]. They work as light energy harvesters,
but also as antioxidants by inactivating reactive oxygen species formed by exposure to
the light and air [37]. In this way, not only they protect the membranes and the tissues
22
==PAGE:45==
from oxidative damage, but consequently they also prevent or minimise serious illnesses
in humans such as cancer, heart disease, and nutritional blindness [61]. Also, they in-
hibit the growth of unhealthy cells and some of them, like a-carotene, β-carotene (seen
in Figure 2.10) and zeaxanthin, function as vitamers [37] [61]. This means that in the
body, they can be converted into retinol, known as vitamin A, which is essential for our
vision [62]. Carotenoids are reported to be insigniﬁcantly aﬀected by food processing,
and they show stability against extreme temperatures. However, since they are highly
unsaturated, oxygen and light constitute the major factors in their breakdown [59].

Regarding the variations in the concentrations between the most interesting pigments of
seaweeds during drying, it has been reported that a high temperature, together with high
levels of humidity can lead to higher concentrations of fucoxanthin, but a degradation of
chlorophyll a [17]. A higher amount of fucoxanthin content was observed also by another
study, where seaweeds of Undaria pinnatiﬁda were exposed to high temperatures (190
°C) [63]. When it comes to seasonal variations, fucoxanthin content showed a higher
production during May, when the water temperature was low, than in July [17]. Among
drying methods (air and freeze drying), diﬀerences between the colour of S. latissima
have been observed, without analysing each individual pigment though [12].
2.9.2
About Fucoxanthin
Fucoxanthin (seen in Figure 2.11) is a xanthophyll and it has unique biological properties
due to its molecular structure, where an unusual allenic bond, a 5,6-monoepoxide and a
conjugated carbonyl group are found and are responsible for the ability of fucoxanthin
to act as an antioxidant [64] [37]. It has attracted considerable interest, also because of
other health-promoting eﬀects in humans, like anti-inﬂammatory, anticancer, anti-obese
and antidiabetic activities, to name some of them. Although it has been shown that the
bioavailability of fucoxanthin in brown seaweeds is low in humans, it has been supposed

2.10
Antioxidant Activity
Oxidation is the transfer of electrons from one atom to another. It represents a fun-
damental part of aerobic life and our metabolism, since oxygen is the ultimate electron
acceptor in the electron ﬂow system [66]. However, the electron ﬂow can become uncou-
pled, generating free radicals and initiating the oxidation. Examples of oxygen-centred
free radicals include superoxide (O2-·), hydroxyl (HO·), peroxyl (ROO·), alkoxyl (RO·)
and nitric oxide (NO·), and they are known as reactive oxygen species. If these free rad-
icals are not eﬀectively scavenged, they can stimulate free-radical chain reactions, and
cause damages to cellular biomolecules (i.e. proteins, lipids and nucleic acids). Subse-
quently, in the body they can lead to disease conditions, such as ageing, cancer, heart
diseases, arteriosclerosis and many other [66].
On the other hand, lipid and protein oxidation can cause important quality deterioration
during processing and storage of food products.
The oxidative degradation of lipids
and proteins (peroxidation) in raw or processed food is not only responsible for loss of
nutritional value, but also for alterations in the appearance, and the sensory quality of
food [67] [68].
An antioxidant is a molecule that is able to inhibit the oxidation of other molecules,
by scavenging free radicals, as demonstrated in Figure 2.12. In terms of food, they can
increase the shelf life by retarding the process of lipid and protein peroxidation, but also
24
==PAGE:47==
protect the human body from free radicals and their eﬀects [66]. Antioxidants can be
either synthetic or natural. Until now, many synthetic antioxidants such as butylated
hydroxytoluene (BHT), butylated hydroxyanisole (BHA), and ethylenediaminetetraacetic
acid (EDTA) have been widely used in diﬀerent food products. However, due to their
potential health risks and toxicity, their use as food additives has been restricted [69].
Therefore, nowadays there is a great interest for replacing synthetic antioxidants with
natural and safe ingredients, especially from plant origin [67].
Figure 2.12: Mechanism of antioxidants against free radicals [10].
2.10.1
Antioxidants in Seaweed
Many eﬀective antioxidant compounds have been isolated and identiﬁed from diﬀerent
kinds of seaweeds, including phenolic compounds, tocopherols, polysaccharides, carotenoid
pigments such as fucoxanthin, β-carotene and astaxanthin, and peptides [67] [58]. Being
photosynthetic organisms, seaweeds are exposed to both light and high oxygen concen-
trations, which induces the formation of free radicals and other oxidative reagents. Due
to this, they should be able to generate the necessary compounds to protect themselves
against oxidation [20].
Speciﬁcally, many species of brown seaweeds constitute rich sources in natural antiox-
idants and many studies have been done on the antioxidant eﬃcacy of their bioactive
25
==PAGE:48==
compounds. Among them, extracts of Fucus vesiculosus were added in ﬁsh-oil-enriched
foods [70], extracts from selected species of seaweeds from Danish coast were evaluated
based on their phenolic compounds and antioxidant activities [67], and seasonal variations
in the antioxidant content and activity of the sugar kelp S. latissima were studied [28].
These studies were not only based on extracts from fresh seaweed, but their antioxidant
activity was tested either on other food products, or for any variations around the year.
It was observed that the antioxidant activity of S. latissima was higher in November and
lower in September [28].
When it comes to a dried seaweed product, it was reported that the antioxidant activity
was 5 to 10 times lower than in the fresh kelp. Also, it was proved that the antioxidant
activity can be highly aﬀected by the drying method applied [17]. Therefore, studies
have been also done on the antioxidant activity of S. latissima which was dried using
diﬀerent drying methods (sun drying, freeze drying, and heat pump-based drying) at air
temperatures of 30, 50, and 70 °C. It was concluded that the antioxidant activity was
better preserved in temperatures below 50 °C, while the values representing it decreased
with an increase of temperature and humidity [17].

Biomass Harvesting & Site Description
The brown kelp species S. latissima was wild harvested in March 2019, by the company
Dansk Tang at the Nykøbing bay (55°54’27.7"N 11°40’28.4"E), located in northern Odsh-

3.2
Drying Methods & Parameters
Two drying methods currently employed by Dansk Tang were tested and compared.
In the Vertical method (V), the freshly collected seaweeds were hanged manually on
ropes and kept upright throughout the drying process, while in the Horizontal (H), the
freshly collected seaweeds, from the same batch as from the V, were laid down on a food
certiﬁed metal grid. Both methods were performed at the same time and in the same
indoor facility, which consisted of a tent inside a larger room. It was equipped with an

phase) which contained the chloroform and the lipids was ﬁltered down into pyrex bottles.

in the fume hood for the chloroform to evaporate. Thereafter, the beakers were placed
in the oven (105 °C) for 2 hours, they were cooled down and weighed. The percentage

3.11
Pigments Analysis
The composition of the pigments was evaluated according to the indications of Bidigare,
Heukelem and Trees (2005) [81] on the analysis of algal pigments by High-Performance
Liquid Chromatography (HPLC). The parameters of the analysis were based on the study
of Honold, Jacobsen, Jónsdóttir, Kristinsson and Hermund (2016) [82]. The pigments
extraction was performed in duplicate, and the HPLC analysis was made as single deter-
mination. Brieﬂy, 100 mg of each freeze dried sample were weighed in a centrifuge tube,
and 3 mL of methanol (100%) containing BHT (0.025 µg/ mL) were added. The tubes
were placed in ice and sonicated in dark for 15 min. Then, the samples were centrifuged
at 5,000 rpm for 10 min at 18 °C.
As a result, two phases were formed, from which the upper was collected in a new tube.
The same process was repeated other two times, so that the extraction was performed in
three cycles. At the last time of extraction, 4 mL of methanol with BHT was added to the
pellet, so that the ﬁnal volume of the extract was 10 mL. The supernatants were pooled,
and 1 mL of each replicate was transferred to HPLC brown vials. Pigment analysis was
performed on an Agilent 1100 Series HPLC (Agilent Technology, CA, USA) equipped
with a diode array detector. Separation was obtained on an ZORBAX Eclipse XDB-C8
column from Agilent with a linear gradient consisting of A: buﬀer (0.028 M tetra butyl
ammonium acetate in water, pH = 6.5) and B: methanol 100%. The gradient program
started at at 5% B increasing to 95% B in 27 min and held for 7 min at 95%, further
increase to 100% in 1 min where it was held for 3 min, returned to 5% B in 2 min and
held there for 6 min. Injection volume was 100 µL, and the ﬂow rate was 0.9 mL/min at
60 °C.
Pigments were detected by ﬂuorescence at 440 nm. Methanol (100%) containing BHT
(0.025 µg/ mL) was used as internal standard to correct any volume errors, and it was
detected at the wavelength of 222 nm. The peaks were manually integrated and the
pigments were identiﬁed by comparing the retention time of the peaks with those of a
standard mix (DHI Laboratory Products - Hørsholm, Denmark). Calibration curves of
the identiﬁed pigments were constructed by diluted aliquots of the same standard. When
the areas of the peaks (Fucoxanthin, Chlorophyll a) did not fall within the range of the ar-
eas used for the calibration curve, the extracts were appropriately diluted, and the HPLC

analysis was performed in the same manner. Peaks of certain pigments (Chlorophyll C2,
Pheophytin a) that are not of considerable interest, whose areas did not belong in the
range of areas of the calibration curves, were not taken into account for the calculations.
The results are expressed as µg/ g DM.
3.12
Antioxidant Activity
3.12.1
Extraction of Antioxidants
The extraction was performed according to Safafar et al. [83], but with some modiﬁcations
made by Marinho et al. [28], and adjusted on the needs of the present samples. The
extractant was chosen based on the study of Wang et al. [84]. In a few words, 0.2 g of
freeze dried seaweed were dispersed in 5 mL of extraction solvent ethanol/water (80:20,
v/v) and shaken vigorously for 30 s. Then, the tubes were placed in sonication bath in
the dark and at room temperature for 30 min. Thereafter, the samples were centrifuged
at 2,160 *g for 10 min and the supernatants were collected and placed in new tubes. The
pellets, instead, were resuspended and the extraction process was repeated once more.
The collected supernatants were pooled, and the solvent was evaporated under rotary
evaporation (SpeedDry Vacuum Concentrator RVC 2 - 33 CDplus). The evaporation
lasted for 5.5 h under 50 mbar and at 30 °C for the removal of the ethanol, and for 1
h under 35 mbar and at 30 °C for the removal of the water. These parameters were
decided according to the plot of vapour pressure versus temperature, given in Figure B.2
in Appendix B. When the extracts were completely dried, they were kept at -80 °C until
further analysis.

Discussion
5.1
Variations during Vertical and Horizontal Drying
A general observation about the variation of the nutritional composition and the antiox-
idant activity during the drying, is that the concentration of most of the components of
S. latissima did not signiﬁcantly vary.
5.1.1
Water Content during Drying
As hypothesised, a nutrient that dramatically decreased was the water, which showed a
particular steep reduction after 13 hours of vertical drying. By the vertical method the
drying lasts for 29 hours (with a 13-hour constant rate phase), while by the horizontal
method it lasts for 42 hours (with a 37-hour constant rate phase), resulting both in a
water content of approximately 13.25% (wet weight). In the study of Sappati et al. (2018)
[17], in order to reduce the water content of S. latissima to this level, the seaweeds were
sun dried for 3.5 hours under 30 °C and 25% humidity. During the drying of the present
study, the average air temperature was 10.8 ± 0.8 °C, and the relative humidity at the
beginning was 78% and dropped down to 32% at the end. As it was expected, and as
it was supported in the above cited study [17], the lower drying temperature and high
relative air humidity decrease the drying rate of dried sugar kelp. The same behaviour
was observed by Stévant et al. (2018) [12], when the water content was detected to be
at 4% after 2 hours of air-drying at 70 °C. Therefore, the drying methods applied by
Dansk Tang are more time consuming, due to the lower temperature and higher relative
67
==PAGE:90==
air humidity of the drying facility. In any case, it was reported by Gallagher et al. (2018)
[88] that kelp is hard to dry, probably due to the high alginate content which creates its
gel-like nature, and strongly binds water.
In the vertical drying, it could be identiﬁed a falling rate phase (V18 - V29). This implies
that the internal resistance was larger than the external, so less water was transferred
to the surface [2]. Diﬀusion of vapour became more prominent, but more energy was
needed to transfer liquid water into vapour [3]. Speciﬁcally, it was noted that the water
content reduced only by 14% during the last 6 hours of vertical drying. So, in this case,
the drying period could be reduced to 23 hours. In the horizontal drying, the drying
rate kept on being constant until the end of the process (H42). This indicates that the
external resistance was continually larger than or equal to the internal resistance [2].
5.1.2
Ash Content during Drying
The ash content changed only during the vertical drying, by increasing after 13 hours of
drying, and reducing in the next 5 hours to levels (30.80% DM) that are still higher than
the initial (24.27% DM). This increase was not expected, and could have been detected
due to biological variations between the samples. During the horizontal drying the ash
content showed no variation (approx. 32.40% DM), as hypothesised. When seaweeds of
S. latissima were air dried (at 25 °C for 7 h), or freeze dried (for 20 h) by Stévant et
al. (2018) [12], the ash content was detected to be approximately 44.6% DM, which is
higher than the value determined in the present study. In the study of Sappati et al.
(2018) [17], where the seaweeds were freeze-, sun-, or air dried at temperatures of 30 - 70
°C, the ash content was reported to range from 22.85 to 33.03% DM, which is similar to
the ash content of the samples under investigation in the current study. However, except
for the drying methods and the conditions that diﬀered between all these studies, the
geographical region and the season of the seaweed cultivation were also diﬀerent, and
may have aﬀected the ash content of the samples.
5.1.3
Iodine Content during Drying
Although it was reported by Stévant et al. (2018) [12] that the iodine content can reduce
by processing, in the present study not only it stayed constant during the vertical drying,
68
==PAGE:91==
but it also increased during the horizontal, rejecting the initial hypothesis and showing
even no correlation to the ash content variations during time. Still, the values at the
last drying points (V: 2.08, H: 2.62 mg/ g DM), were lower than the value (3.98 mg/
g DM) reported by Schiener et al. (2014) [32] for fresh S. latissima harvested at the
same period in Scotland. They were also lower than the values of freeze dried (3.0 mg/
g DM) and air dried at 70 °C (4.9 mg/ g DM) [12], given that at this temperature was
detected the minimal concentration among the rest air drying temperatures. Neither at
this point should be ignored the variation of the iodine content between geographical
regions [37], but also between the life stages of the sugar kelp. It was observed by Roleda
et al. (2018) [74] that the juvenile S. latissima contained 4.0 mg/ g DM iodine, while
the adult sugar kelp contained 2.9 mg/ g DM. This biological variability between the
samples could explain the higher values at the end of the horizontal drying, since samples
from the initial point of this method were not analysed, but supposed to be the same
as the ones from the vertical method. Therefore, it was not feasible to conﬁrm this rise.
However, an analytical error, a contamination, or an analysis sensitivity issue could also
have occurred, as it was conﬁrmed for one of the H42 triplicates as well, resulting in its
exclusion from the calculations.
5.1.4
Protein Content & Amino Acids Composition during Drying
The protein content, as determined by the sum of the total amino acids, remained also
stable during the drying, by both methods. During the vertical drying, it was around
12.73% DM, and during the horizontal drying at around 11.52% DM. These values were
higher than the ones reported by Stévant et al. (2018) [12] on air and freeze dried S.
latissima, harvested in May in Norway; since the protein content was detected to be at
around 7.28% DM, no matter the drying method, it can be supposed that this diﬀerence
is due to regional diﬀerences of harvesting the raw material, depending on the nutrient
levels in the ambient surroundings. Indeed, also fresh kelp harvested in May and studied
by Marinho et al. (2015a) [33], contained just 4.5-5.2% DM. So, given that the ﬁnal
protein content is uncorrelated to the drying method, not only the protein content of the
Dansk Tang seaweed is higher than from other cultivation sites, but its drying methods
can preserve it until the ﬁnal product.
Apart from the amount of protein, also the amino acid composition of interest (umami
69
==PAGE:92==
ﬂavour; GLU and ASP, BCAA; VAL, LEU and ILE) seemed to remain stable over the
drying. It should be stated though, that according to Mouritsen et al. (2012) [4], S.
latissima is a poor umami source, especially when compared to the red seaweed Palmaria
palmata. With regard to the protein quality, the total amount of EAA stayed stable
during both the vertical and the horizontal drying, overturning the initial hypothesis. In
particular, at all the drying points there were nine EAAs detected (except tryptophan
which was degraded during the acid hydrolysis), conﬁrming seaweed as a source of most
EAA, but also drying as a mild method of processing.
The EAA/AA ratio was determined to be stable and at around 0.44 during both the
drying methods, and a similar value of 0.4 was reported for S. latissima, air dried at
diﬀerent temperatures [12]. It has been reported that the drying conditions do not aﬀect
the EAA score [12], but in the present study was conﬁrmed that it was not aﬀected nor
by the drying time.
The protein quality was also evaluated by the total EAA score, and levels equal to 100%
would indicate that the content of EAA matched the requirement pattern (mg AA/ g pro-
tein requirement) for the selected age group (3–10 years old) deﬁned by WHO/FAO/UNU
[42]. The ﬁnal EAA scores were 59.64% after 29 hours of vertical, with histidine being
the limiting amino acid throughout the process, and 94.84% after 42 hours of horizontal
drying, with histidine, but also methionine as limiting amino acids for at least the last

et al. (2019) [43] to be the most common limiting amino acid of S. latissima. Also in the
current study, the score of methionine, whenever it was identiﬁed as the limiting amino
acid, was just a few units lower than the one of histidine.
The crude protein content was constant (V: 15.7, H: 16.7% DM) during drying, as ex-
pected. Concerning the N-to-protein conversion factor, it did not show variations during
the vertical drying (around 4.11), nor during the horizontal drying (around 3.52). Thus,
the conversion factor is uncorrelated to the drying time. Similar values (3.97) were re-
ported for air and freeze dried S. latissima [12], but lower (2.7) for fresh biomass [43],
harvested both at the same period as the seaweed of the present study. Therefore, the
deviation can be explained due to the diﬀerent geographical regions of cultivation, and
so the nitrogen levels in the ambient surroundings.

Lipid Content & Fatty Acids Composition during Drying
The lipid content remained also stable during the vertical (approx. 3.38% DM) and the

for convective-, freeze-, and sun dried S. latissima [17]. Also lower values (1.6 - 3.0%

these samples were harvested at the same period, the diﬀerence can be supported by the

was steady during the drying, as it was expected, can support that the lipids presented

converting into volatile oxidised compounds due to the drying process [17].
Regarding the composition of fatty acids, the amount of total SFA, MUFA, and PUFA
remained also stable during the vertical (31.33, 17.73, and 36.56% FAME, respectively)
and the horizontal (29.50, 16.87, and 40.36% FAME, respectively) drying. Similar values
(SFA around 30% FAME, MUFA around 18% FAME, and PUFA around 42% FAME)
were reported for fresh S. latissima, harvested at the same period from the inner Danish
waters [54]. The relatively high proportion of PUFA indicate a good quality of the S.
latissima lipids, and that the drying methods applied by Dansk Tang are able to preserve
it.
The lipid quality in a wider context was maintained, contrarily to the hypothesis. More
speciﬁcally, the total n-3 and n-6 PUFAs did not vary during the drying. The sum of EPA
+ DHA, which are two health-promoting n-3 PUFAs, was detected to be around 7.81%
FAME by both of the drying methods, which was lower than the value reported for the
fresh S. latissima (10.34% FAME) harvested also in spring in Denmark [54]. However,
since the values during the drying did not change, the deviation can be explained by the
diﬀerent geographical origin of the raw material.
Regarding the n-3 and n-6 PUFAs, the proportion of ALA (n-3), for instance, stayed
at 7.23% FAME during the vertical drying, and at 7.80% FAME during the horizontal.
These values are around three times higher than the proportion of ALA in regard to
the total FAME in salmon (2.46% FAME) [89], which is a popular source of n-3 PUFAs
[90]. The LA (n-6), in its turn, remained at 6.04% FAME during both the vertical and
the horizontal drying, a value which is 1.56 times higher than the one in salmon (3.86%

FAME) [89]. Regardless of the three times more lipid content in salmon than in the
present dried samples, it is worth mentioning that the drying methods of Dansk Tang on
seaweeds of S. latissima are able to maintain two particularly important PUFAs which

Emphasising on the n-6/ n-3 ratio, which is recommended by WHO [16] to be lower than

Carbohydrate Content during Drying
The carbohydrate content seemed to decrease during the vertical drying, by 11% until the
end of the process, which stands against the hypothesis. During the horizontal drying, it
exhibited a constant concentration at around 44.17% DM. The values at the ﬁnal drying
points fell in the range of the carbohydrate content reported by Holdt and Kraan (2011)
[37] for fresh S. latissima (38 - 61% DM), and they were higher than the ones detected
for air and freeze dried (around 26.42% DM, independently of the drying method and
conditions) seaweeds of the same species and harvested also in spring [12]. Nevertheless,
the carbohydrate content is supposed to vary among geographical regions [12]. Since the
determination of the total carbohydrates was done by the diﬀerence of the total mass
and the rest of the macronutrients (Equation 3.5) in the present study, the reduction of
carbohydrates during the vertical drying was due to the concurrent increase of the ash
content, as it was discussed before. This correlation was also observed by Sappati et al.
(2018) [17], where it was reported that the carbohydrate content of the sugar kelp was
linearly dependent with the negative slope on the ash content.

5.1.8
Antioxidant Activity during Drying
When it comes to the antioxidant activity (TPC and DPPH radical scavenging activ-
ity), there were no ﬂuctuations in the values during the vertical drying, nor during the
horizontal. This stands against its hypothesised decline.
The TPC stayed around 1.1 mg GAE/ g DM during the vertical drying, and 1.3 mg GAE/
g DM during the horizontal. It is reported that although brown seaweeds contain large
amount of phenolic compounds, which are also known for their antioxidant activities, they
are highly heat sensitive. Thus, their chemical activity can be modiﬁed to a large degree
due to the applied processing conditions [17]. However, in a study by Farvin and Jacobsen
(2013) [67] that was made on the phenolic compounds of ethanolic extracts from fresh S.
latissima seaweeds, the values of the GAE reached up to 0.35 mg/ g DM. But when it was
determined on dried samples by Sappati et al. (2018) [17], the highest phenolic activity
was exhibited by freeze dried seaweeds, and this reached a concentration of 0.69 mg GAE/
g DM. Nonetheless, it is also supported in the literature that the extraction solvent [28]
[67], the seasonality [28], but also the drying method and the conditions applied have an
eﬀect on the TPC. Consequently, it is hard to compare the values of the present study
to the ones of other raw material, extraction methods and drying conditions. It has been
suggested that drying below 50 °C, and at low relative humidity (around 25%) [17], can
preserve adequately the phenolic compounds, which could be the reason why the drying
by Dansk Tang (10.8 ± 0.8 °C) results in higher values of TPC than in the other studies.
Neither the DPPH radical scavenging activity exhibited any variations during the vertical,
nor during the horizontal drying. During the vertical drying it was detected to be at
around 3.39 mg/ mL, while during the horizontal it was approximately 2.86 mg/ mL.
In a study by Marinho et al. (2019) [28] on the variation of the antioxidant activity of
S. latissima from Denmark under a seasonal perspective, the maximum value of DPPH
radical scavenging activity was reported to be 2.81 mg/ mL (in May), which is close to
the values of the present study, keeping in mind that the harvesting took place also in
spring. However, this result referred to the fresh biomass of S. latissima, so it can be
supported that the drying by Dansk Tang adequately maintains the radical scavenging
activity of the fresh kelp. Truly, other drying methods like convective drying were tested
for alterations of the DPPH radical scavenging activity, and it was concluded that the

higher the temperature and the relative humidity, the lower the antioxidant content [17].
The highest value was detected at 20.60 mg/ mL, after drying at 70 °C and 50% relative
humidity (kelp harvested in May in Maine, USA), indicating a weaker scavenging activity
of radicals [17]. Notwithstanding, it has been reported that also the extraction solvent can
aﬀect the results of the inhibition [67], making the comparison of the results complicated.
Focusing on the drying applied by Dansk Tang, what is interesting is that the ﬁnal
products exhibited no deterioration of the antioxidant content overall, compared to the
fresh kelp. And this could be supported by the fact that the drying air temperature was
relatively low (10.8 ± 0.8 °C), even though the relative humidity was quite high (45%
after 18 h). Also, the drying temperature was not much higher than the average sea
temperature in March (2.4 °C [71]), which means that the seaweeds were not exposed
to a considerably higher temperature by their transfer to the drying facility. What is
more, the seaweeds were not exposed to the sun light during drying, limiting another
threatening factor against the preservation of the antioxidant activity of seaweeds. So,
the given drying methods can be regarded as “gentle” to the antioxidant components of
the S. latissima.
Concerning the Fe2+ chelating ability of the extracts, the protocol that was used to
perform this analysis was based on the principle that excess of iron (Fe2+) is added to an
aliquot of extracts with diﬀerent concentrations, which is bound by antioxidants present
in the sample. Then, the Fe2+ that was not bound, but stayed free in the solution, forms
a pink colour complex with ferrozine, which is photometrically determined at 562 nm.
So, the higher the antioxidant content in the sample, the lower the absorbance.
What was observed during the analysis on the samples of the current study, is that
the measured absorbance was too high, resulting in a low inhibition of 10 - 40% (see
Equation 3.7). For the inhibition to fall in the range of the calibration curve, including
the value of 50% for the EC50 to be found, the absorbance had to reduce. This means that
the concentration of the extracts in the aliquot had to decrease (since the extracts had a
strong green colour), and the amount of the pink Fe2+ - ferrozine complex to be lower,
as well. In this framework, it has been tried both to reduce the concentrations of the
extracts aliquot (to lighten the green colour), and of the ferrous chloride, the ferrozine
and the extract (to lighten the pink colour) in the microtiter plate.
For the results
to be satisfactory, it had to be achieved the perfect ﬁt of concentrations of both the

extracts and all the reagents of the analysis, something that opens too many possibilities
of combinations, and was not reached during this project.

other study by Farvin and Jacobsen (2013) [67] on ethanolic and aqueous extracts of

the seasonality and the geographical region of harvesting can aﬀect the Fe2+ chelating
ability of the seaweeds. Another supposition could be that during the long storage of the
samples from their harvesting until the analysis, the ability of chelating the Fe2+ ions

5.2
Vertically VS Horizontally Dried End Products
The second part of the discussion is about the comparison of the vertical to the horizontal
drying method. To do so, the values of each nutrient at the last point of every drying
were considered (V29 and H42), regarding the samples at the end of the drying processes
as ﬁnal products.
As a general remark, it was observed that there were no signiﬁcant diﬀerences between the
vertical and the horizontal drying, in terms of their eﬀect on the nutritional composition
and the antioxidant activity of S. latissima. More speciﬁcally, the water, the ash and the
protein (both TAA-H2O and crude) content, as well as the TPC and the DPPH radical
scavenging activity were detected at the same levels between the two drying methods.
The fact that the antioxidant activity was insigniﬁcantly diﬀerent between the products
of the two methods, rejects the initial assumption for their dissimilarity.
The rate that the vertically hanged seaweeds have been loosing their water content (4.51
g/ 100 g/ hour) was 2.4 times higher, though, being aligned with the hypothesis. The
drying rate has been reported to be dependent on the drying temperature, the moisture
diﬀusion coeﬃcient, the diﬀerence in partial pressure of water vapour in the food product
and the surroundings, the air velocity, the material thickness, the surface area and the
phase transition [27].
In this case, since the samples of both methods had the same
physiological characteristics (individual blade thickness and surface area), and they were
exposed to the same surrounding conditions (air temperature and relative humidity, no
convection), the diﬀerence in their drying rate probably relied on the way of placing
the seaweed blades. This means that the horizontally placed seaweeds had less total
76
==PAGE:99==
surface exposed to the air, as it is possible that the blades would have overlapped one
another, leaving no space between them. In contrast, when the seaweeds were vertically
hanged, not only it is more feasible that air could have reached both sides of each blade
(promoting diﬀusion), but also the gravity could make some of the water drip to the
ground (accelerating the capillary forces to drag water from the interior to the surface of
the seaweed). Thus, the way of placing the seaweeds aﬀected the relationship between
the internal and external mass transfer resistances, and so the drying rate and time.
When it comes to the composition of their proteins, the amount of GLU was 1.35 times
higher at the end of the vertical drying, leading to the supposition that the umami
ﬂavour in the vertically dried ﬁnal products would be stronger. Due to the particular
taste quality of glutamic acid, which gives the feeling of deliciousness [4], it is possible that
the vertical drying results in products more desirable to the consumers. Despite this, S.
latissima is regarded as a poor source of umami ﬂavour anyway [4], so it is very probable
that the above diﬀerence would not be perceived by the consumers. This distinction in
the ﬁnal GLU concentration was the only one conﬁrming the hypothesised diﬀerent AA
composition between vertically and horizontally dried seaweeds.
The total EAA diﬀered between the ﬁnal products of the two drying methods, as the
horizontal drying gave a 1.13 times higher amount than the vertical. This diﬀerence
agrees with the hypothesis for diﬀerent quality attributes of the two ﬁnal products, yet it
could have been detected because of biological variations between the samples. However,
if the vertical drying lasted for 18 hours instead of 29, the total EAA would have been at
the same levels. That diﬀerence caused a deviation in the ratio of EAA/AA as well, even
though the ratio of the horizontally dried seaweeds was just 12.5% times higher. This
deviation could have been nulliﬁed by ending the vertical drying 11 hours earlier.
The rest of the bioactive compounds, including the lipids (together with the fatty acids
content, composition, and quality parameters that were assumed to be diﬀerent), the
chlorophylls, the carotenoids, and the carbohydrates, were detected to have no signiﬁcant
diﬀerences between the vertically and the horizontally dried seaweeds. The iodine content
was higher when the sugar kelp was horizontally dried, but a possible explanation was
given above and, based on this, not a high importance should be given in this deviation.
The labelling is the one that reﬂects the value of the product to the consumers. So,
the nutrition declaration and claims that each drying method introduced into the ﬁnal
77
==PAGE:100==
products deserve some attention, as they can have a ﬁnancial impact to Dansk Tang.
The vertically dried seaweeds seemed to be poorer in crude protein (20.6%), but without
compromising the claim of “high in protein”, which was expected to be adopted. They
are richer in fat (3.4%) though, at a level that deprives them of being “low in fat”. But
still, they can be claimed as “low in saturated fat”, which possibly attracts more the
conscious consumers.
Attention should be paid to the iodine content (181 mg/ 100 g) which was evaluated
as “high”, due to the negative health eﬀects that its excessive consumption can have.
Although there is currently no EU-regulation of maximum iodine concentration in food
products, EFSA has suggested an upper intake level for adults of 0.6 mg/ day [30].
According to this, only up to 0.3 g of dried S. latissima should be daily consumed,
assuming that it would be the only source of iodine in the diet. In France, the Expert
Committee on Human Nutrition has suggested a consumption not higher than 0.5 mg
I/ day [30], making the consumption of this species even more restricted. WHO has
suggested a provisional maximal tolerable daily intake of 1 mg/ day from all sources,
equivalent to 17 µg/ kg body weight [30]. Therefore, even though the commercialising of
these products is not legally limited in Europe, for the moment at least, awareness should
be raised especially to the vulnerable groups.
From a critical point of view, the diﬀerence between the scientiﬁc protein content, as it
was determined during this study (V: 12.5%), and the crude protein content that must
be declared according to (EU) No 1169/2011 [48] (V: 20.6%), should be pointed out. It
has been stated multiple times in the literature that the N-to-protein factor of seaweeds
is much lower than the factor of 6.25 that the legislation [48] requires to be universally
used. And this is because seaweeds typically contain high concentrations of non-protein
nitrogen [47]. The speciﬁc N-to-protein factor of this study (V: 4.65 and H: 3.31) falls in
the range of factors (2.0 - 5.3) that have been reported for the fresh biomass of this species
by Schiener et al. (2014) [32], Bak et al. (2019) [43], Nielsen et al. (2016) [49], and Manns
et al. (2017) [50]. Nevertheless, the fact that the regulation endorses the use of the 6.25
factor which is much higher than the actual species-speciﬁc conversion factor, leads to
an overestimated protein content of the seaweeds product. For the horizontally dried S.
latissima in this case, the scientiﬁc protein content was almost two times lower than the
declared protein. Consequently, the energy value that should be labelled was 1.2 times
78
==PAGE:101==
higher than the actual energy value. Hypothetically, if the legislation did not require
the use of the 6.25 factor, the use of the real amount of protein and energy value on the
labelling of Dansk Tang could make these products seem nutritionally inferior to similar
products of companies that, due to lack of knowledge, would use the recommended factor
of 6.25. Yet, the vertically dried seaweeds could keep the claim of being “high in protein”,
whereas the horizontally dried not. So on the one hand, the regulatory requirements lead
to a misleading labelling, but on the other hand, make the product more attractive to
the consumers.
5.3
Vertical VS Horizontal Drying
Taking all the above into consideration, it can be concluded that the vertical method
should be preferred by Dansk Tang, as it is the most appropriate drying method of S.
latissima. The main reason is that it gives products with similar nutritional composition
and antioxidant activity as the horizontal method, but it is much faster. In fact, the
drying rate of the vertical drying was more than twice higher than the one of the horizontal
drying. Also, the vertical drying occupies less space in the drying facility, giving the
opportunity for an easier scaling-up of the production.
Even more, it can be recommended that the vertical method can be stopped after 23
hours, i.e. six hours earlier than what is empirically done. By this modiﬁcation, there
would be no sacriﬁce in the nutritional composition and the antioxidant activity of the
products, but instead, the process would be even more eﬃcient.
Finally, the step of
re-moisturising the dried seaweeds could be skipped, as during these last six hours of
drying, there was only a slight decrease of the water content. Therefore, the 23-hour
vertical drying can be regarded as the optimal drying method of S. latissima in this
set-up, optimising the production of a high-quality product.

Chapter 6
Conclusions
The eﬀect of the vertical and horizontal drying applied by Dansk Tang on the nutritional
composition and antioxidant activity of S. latissima seaweeds was investigated in this
study. The variation of the fundamental bioactive compounds was studied during each
drying method, and the two methods were then compared to one another.
As a conclusion, the water content expectantly reduced from approximately 87.3% to
13.3% wet weight, by both drying methods. The ash content was unpredictably increased,
but slightly, and only by the vertical drying (V0: 24.3% DM, V29: 26.53% DM), and
consequently the carbohydrate content decreased (V0: 51.1% DM, V29: 45.7% DM).
Unexpectedly, the iodine content did not decrease, but stayed constant during the vertical
drying (V29: 2.1 mg/ g DM). Even more surprisingly, at the end of the horizontal drying,
it increased to 2.6 mg/ g DM, which requires further investigation.
The rest of the
bioactive compounds evaluated during the drying stayed statistically stable (ANOVA;
p > 0.05). The crude protein content remained constant during the drying (V: 15.7%
DM, H: 16.7% DM), as expected. All nine EAAs were detected at every sampling point
during both drying processes, and the EAA/AA ratio remained at around 0.44 in both
cases, overturning the initial hypothesis on the deterioration of the protein quality. At
the end of the vertical drying, the EAA score was 59.6% (HIS), while at the end of the
horizontal drying it was 94.8% (MET). The N-to-protein conversion factor was identiﬁed
to be around 4.1 (V), and 3.5 (H), with no signiﬁcant variations over time. It was anyway
much lower than the universal factor of 6.25, set by the regulation (EU) No 1169/2011,
leading to important deviations between the real nutrition declaration and claims, and
81
==PAGE:104==
the ones that must be declared on the labelling of the ﬁnal products. The later include
the claims of “high in protein” and “low in saturated fat”, among others.
The lipid
content was measured to be steady as well (V: 3.4% DM, H: 3.7% DM), verifying the
starting hypothesis. However, the quality of these lipids, evaluated by the FA proﬁle, was
proved to act against the initial supposition, by exhibiting no deterioration. The total
SFA, MUFA, PUFA, as well as the n-6/n-3 ratio and the sum of EPA + DHA stayed
stable during both drying methods. In addition, fucoxanthin was predictably constant
at around 882.7 µg/ g DM (V, H). The antioxidant activity surprisingly exhibited no
alterations (TPC; V: 1.1 mg GAE/ g DM, H: 1.3 mg GAE/ g DM, DPPH; V: 3.4 mg/
mL, H: 2.9 mg/ mL).
Comparing the values of the above bioactive compounds at the last point of each drying
method, the only expected deviation was observed at the protein composition and quality,
speciﬁcally in the total EAA. However, the amount in the horizontally dried seaweeds was
only 1.13 times higher. The diﬀerence in the iodine content is not a liable distinction. The
negligible deviations in the nutrient content, together with the 2.4 times higher drying
rate of the vertical method, leads to the ﬁnal conclusion that the vertical drying (ideally
of 23 h) should be preferred by Dansk Tang, for the eﬃcient production of a high-end
product.

Chapter 7
Further Development
For issues that have arisen through the present study, it is recommended for the antioxi-
dant activity to be investigated more, by applying methods such as the Ferric Reducing
Antioxidant Power Assay (FRAP).
It is also important for the iodine content to be conﬁrmed or rejected the observed
increase during the horizontal drying. Therefore, the H1 samples should be analysed,
and concerned as the initial samples of the drying.
From a business point of view, it is essential for Dansk Tang to perceive the economic
impact that the implementation of a 23-hour vertical drying would have, against the
29-hour vertical, or even the 42-hour horizontal drying. This requires a ﬁnancial study
on the electrical requirements of the adsorption dehumidiﬁer, as well as data about the
cultivation capacity and the revenue of Dansk Tang products.
As a recommendation for future studies on the scientiﬁc ﬁeld, it is proposed to be per-
formed a carbohydrate and a vitamins analysis over the time of the present commercial
drying. Also, the microbiological safety is suggested to be tested for any variations during
the drying, as well as to be compared for vertically and horizontally dried S. latissima
seaweeds.
Moreover, a sensory and textural analysis could take place, for samples dried by the
vertical and the horizontal method, both to conﬁrm the stronger umami ﬂavour of the
vertically dried seaweeds, but also to identify any variations in the taste and textural
attributes that could aﬀect the preference of the consumers.
