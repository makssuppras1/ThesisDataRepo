Abstract 
In collaboration with Team Danmark, the purpose of this Master’s thesis was to gain a deeper 
understanding of the dynamics between a bat and a ball in the context of table tennis. To 
limit the scope, the focus was mainly directed on the outer rubber layer of a table tennis bat. 
A highly experimental approach was applied, where two leading and different types of rubbers 
were examined; a European (EU) rubber and a Chinese (CH) rubber. The general hypothesis 
encountered was related to the superiority of CH rubber in terms of spin performance. Hence, 
the aim was to verify and quantify this difference in performance along with other metrics.  
 
Initially, a preliminary experiment was conducted with elite table tennis players in order to 
verify the difference in spin performance. By examining the serve stroke utilizing cameras, it 
was evidenced that the CH rubber on average displayed 28.7% better spin performance.  
The first part of the project thus characterized the mechanical properties and the three 
relevant key performance metrics (KPM) of the two bat rubbers; spin ratio, CoR and throw 
angle. In terms of the mechanical properties, the results concluded that the CH rubber was 
twice as elastic as the EU rubber, had a lower Shore A hardness and was significantly tackier 
than the EU rubber. In the KPM experiment, no evidential difference was found in the spin 
performance amongst the specimen, while the EU bat rubber on average displayed 20% better 
rebound capabilities. Furthermore, the CH bat rubber indicated 58% lower throw angle than 
the EU rubber flagging the possibility of performing a more attacking and forceful forehand 
stroke. With a tackier surface, the CH bat rubber was expected to impart more spin due to 
increased grip, however, this was not obtained. Thus, the hypothesis of a tacky CH rubber 
imparts more spin was rejected in the conducted experiment, perhaps highlighting the need of 
a dynamic experiment comprising the bat movement. Nevertheless, the better rebound 
capabilities of the EU bat rubber was in accordance with expectations due to a softer foam, 
which is capable of storing more elastic energy.  
 
The second part of the project characterized the physical and chemical properties of rubbers, 
where various analyses were performed; extraction, DSC, TGA, and FTIR. It was observed 
that the CH rubber consisted of two separate layers in contrast to the single-layered EU rubber. 
Extraction results showed greater weight loss of EU rubber, indicating the application of 
volatile compounds. DSC results showed identical Tg amongst the rubbers as well as the 
presence of a plasticizer in the EU rubber. TGA and FTIR concluded that both the EU rubber 
and the CH rubber were composed of NR and BR as expected, however, with a greater portion 
of NR in the CH rubber.  
Inspecting the two-layered CH rubber, it was similarly found that the top layer had a 
higher level of NR compared to the low layer. Furthermore, the tests concluded that the surface 
tackiness of CH rubbers is not a result of surface treatment, but rather a high NR level, which 
possibly is combined with tackifying resins.

1 Introduction 
With technologies emerging, allowing performance advantages for table tennis players, the 
purpose of this study is to develop a deeper understanding of the bat-ball interaction in order 
to reengineer one of the best table tennis rubbers on the market. The interplay between a ball 
and a rubber is crucial for elite players to perform spectacular strokes, which highly depends 
on the properties of the bat surface, also known as the rubber. Thus, in collaboration with 
Team Danmark, the desired outcome of this project lies in developing an enhanced rubber that 
competes with international benchmarks and ultimately creates better conditions for the 
Danish players. In order to do so, leading rubbers on the market will be examined closely - 
especially the Chinese rubbers have gained tremendous attention due to their superior spin 
performance capabilities [Emratthich, 2019]. In fact, the Chinese players have dominated the 
world top 10 rankings for almost a decade. Therefore, it is obvious to use a leading Chinese 
rubber as a baseline along with a leading European rubber to thoroughly understand the ball-
rubber interaction. For example, this includes the examination of rubber characteristics that 
affect the crucial strokes on parameters such as spin, velocity and ball trajectory.  
By using elements of reverse engineering, the goal is to identify relevant parameters of 
the rubbers that can be optimized and applied in the reengineering process of a superior rubber. 
Commercialization opportunities will furthermore occur if such a rubber is achieved due to the 
massive market of amateur players that are willing to invest in the best rubbers available.  
 
Hence, the main elements of this project are:  
 
1) Initial verification of spin performance difference between EU and CH rubber and 
examination of the spectacular forehand stroke in collaboration with elite Danish 
players from Team Danmark.  
2) Mechanical characterization of EU and CH rubber through experiments.  
3) Chemical characterization of the EU and CH rubber through experiments.  
4) Production of new rubber prototypes based on obtained findings (if sufficient time).  
 
The motivation for this study lies in the commercialization of table tennis rubbers, once the 
recipe for a superior rubber is discovered. The project would in parallel terms also be able to 
provide the Danish athletes with the best equipment to compete at larger events. A Gantt 
chart was used as a timeline for the project in order to manage relevant milestones and for 
completing tasks on time (see Appendix A.4).

1.1 Problem definition 
1.1.1 Problem Statement  
Worldwide competition in table tennis is tough with small differences determining the outcome 
between winners and losers. Knowing the importance of sports equipment, the CH and EU 
table tennis rubbers are amongst the leaders, enhancing the in-game performance. Hence, by 
understanding and identifying relevant parameters in the ball-rubber interaction for these 
rubbers, one can analyze and thereby recreate a rubber that competes at the highest level.  
1.1.2 Hypotheses 
This thesis encounters several hypotheses that are tested throughout the project: 
 
1. CH rubber is tackier than EU rubber due to physical observations, while the EU foam 
is softer than CH due to manufacturers ratings.  
2. EU bat rubber has better rebound capabilities due to softer foam. 
3. CH bat rubber has better spin capabilities due to increased rubber tackiness. 
These are either accepted or falsified by the end of this project.  
1.1.3 Context 
A table tennis bat consist of three main pieces; a blade, a foam and a rubber (see Figure 1.1). 
The blade is the fundamental layer made of wood, followed by a foam layer for impact 
absorption and speed. Finally, the outer rubber layer provides ball control and spin. The 
foam+rubber layers together will be described as bat rubber throughout this report. Moreover, 
a rubber can be found as pimples-in or pimples-out as a result of specific playing styles in the 
game, i.e. attacking or defensive.  
The game of table tennis is governed by ITTF rules that limits the bat rubber in 
thickness as it may not exceed a total of 4mm, including foam and adhesives. Similarly, the 
rubber must be made of a non-cellular rubber material and the foam of a cellular rubber 
material. The surface color must also be uniform red or black [“Technical Leaflet T4”, 2019]. 
Bat rubbers that meet the requirements are approved with a ITTF identification number and 
added to a long list of approved bat rubbers on the ITTF website [“List of authorized..”, 2019].

2 Literature Review  
2.1 KPM in Table Tennis  
In the context of the game, the bat rubber performance can be broken into a set of key 
performance metrics (KPM). The metrics are crucial for any elite table tennis player that seeks 
a competitive edge as well as for aligning their playing style with the bat rubber type. For 
instance, players can either have an attacking, defensive or balanced playing style in which 
they need to choose a bat rubber accordingly [Butterfly, 2019]. The KPM related to the bat 
rubber have, in fact, already been closely examined in several studies [Rinaldi et al., 2016; 
Rinaldi et al., 2019; Kawazoe et al., 2003; Blank et al., 2017; Varenberg et al., 2012], and can 
be compiled into the following three groups:  
 
● SR, which is the spin ratio between outgoing and ingoing ball spin. Spin, in general, is a 
fundamental element in the game of table tennis. Thus, the amount of spin applied to a 
ball makes it harder for the opponent to control the ball, resulting in the more the spin, 
the better the stroke [Varenberg et al., 2014]. Mathematically, spin is described as angular 
velocity, 𝜔 [𝑟𝑝𝑠]. Finally, the SR is written in the equation form: 
 
 
𝑆𝑅= 𝜔,
𝜔-.
 
(1) 
 
Ultimately, the aim is to achieve the largest SR as possible from an attacking stroke. The 
SR has previously been studied by Allen et al. [2012] in the case of tennis balls. Here, 
negative SR was obtained due to a change in spin direction subsequent to impact. 
According to multiple studies, the amount of spin on a stroke is highly related to the 
friction, tackiness and grip of the rubber surface [Varenberg et al., 2012; Liu et al., 2014]. 
It was furthermore shown by Rinaldi et al. [2019] that SR varies with different ingoing 
angles of the ball. Larger ingoing angles resulted in smaller SR, while further experiments 
concluded that negative SR decreased in magnitude as the ingoing spin increased. 
Although the performed studies were thorough, the authors only took backspin into 
account leaving topspin behind, which is applied more frequently in an attacking playing 
style [Lodziak, 2019].  
Chinese rubbers have a tendency of containing significant tacky surfaces [Letts, 
2018; Emratthich, 2018]. Tackiness refers to a material’s ability to stick to a solid surface 
when slight pressure is applied [“Rheological Testing of Tack”, 2015]. In the context of 
table tennis, tackiness thus increases the bonding between ball and bat, resulting in a 
firmer grip that allows the ball to be imparted with significant spin. A tribological study 
conducted by Varenberg et al. [2012] presented a scientific method in characterizing the 
frictional behavior of rubber surfaces. In total, 8 different rubbers were examined; 4

mentioning that the specimen examined in the study only included pimples-out rubbers. 
Many, if not all, elite players utilize a pimple-in rubber for better mechanical properties 
according to their aggressive playing style. Thus, the aforementioned study is not 
representative for an aggressive playing style, which is the point of interest in this project. 
A rheological approach in measuring the tackiness of PSA has also been pursued using a 
probe test. Here, the maximum tackiness was defined as the longest separation time 
[“Rheological Testing of Tack”, 2015]. However, this study pursued a general approach in 
determining the tackiness of strong adhesives such as tape and did not associate to any 
sport. Although these studies introduce the importance of tackiness in relation to spin, 
the field still lacks serious literature regarding this topic - especially on how to obtain and 
vary the tackiness phenomena on a table tennis rubber.

ingoing and outgoing ball velocity. An elite player is often looking to return the ball with 
the greatest speed possible in order to trouble the opponent. Physically, speed is related 
to the rebound capabilities of bat rubber. This key performance metric is known as linear 
Coefficient of Restitution:

The metric is unitless, positive and always below 1 (assuming no ball spin) due to loss of 
energy during impact [Kawazoe et al., 2003]. Hence, the outgoing velocity will always be 
smaller than the incoming velocity as well as a larger CoR reflects greater speed 
performance of a bat rubber. However, when the incoming ball spin is present, the CoR is 
affected by rotational energy that translates into translational energy. In such case, the 
CoR can be seen to exceed the value of 1.  
Rinaldi et al. [2016] evaluated the speed performance of bat rubbers using CoR 
and concluded that CoR decreased as the ingoing ball velocities increased. Furthermore, 
the metric attained its minimum when rubber+foam were combined as opposed to the 
foam alone. Generally, the findings of the aforementioned study were in agreement with 
those of Rinaldi et al. [2019] and Kawazoe et al. [2003], and are presented in Figure 2.2.

A rather advanced approach was pursued by Kawazoe et al. [2003], who, based on an 
impact study, predicted the CoR in relation to the distance from the grip end of the bat. 
Here, it was found that the rebound effect is optimum at a distance of 16 cm before 
eventually decreasing due to the mass distribution of the bat. This outlines the fact that 
the test region for CoR on the bat must remain identical during experiments for the sake 
of comparison, otherwise the metric would vary. Further predictions were made on impact 
force, rubber-ball deformation and contact time stating that; rubber deformation is slightly 
non-linear from an ingoing velocity of 20 m/s, and; contact time decreases marginally as 
ingoing velocity increases. As these parameters only are predicted, experimental 
verification is still needed. The aforementioned study is further complemented by the work 
of Liu et al. [2014], shedding light on rubber-ball interaction and stating that contact time 
is in reverse proportion with the hitting force (speed), if assumed constant impulse. The 
impulse is generated during contact and is defined as the product of hitting force and 
contact time, which is equal to the momentum created by waving the bat. Thus, the 
contact time decreases as hitting force increases and vice versa.

● Throw Angle (θ) is a metric corresponding to the angle difference between ingoing and 
outgoing ball trajectory [Rinaldi et al., 2019]. The metric is denoted θ and is illustrated in 
Figure 2.3. This metric defines the behavior of the bat rubber in terms of ball trajectories

In a recent study of Rinaldi et al. [2019], the throw angle was considered in addition to

and negative ingoing angles need to be pursued in order to fully understand and verify the 
behavior of this KPM.  
 
Concluding this subsection, a rather revolutionary study was conducted in determining the

the KPM [Blank et al., 2017]. Intriguingly, the tests showed a general precision of 75%-87% 
compared to the true values obtained from high-speed cameras. Although the accomplished 
work exhibited promising methodological development, it was the very first in literature and 
comprised several assumptions and simplifications. Nevertheless, utilizing sensors to calculate 
KPM in the future will undoubtedly be a more convenient approach.  
2.2 Table Tennis Rubber   
As table tennis has become an increasingly popular sport, the technology behind it has 
advanced. This includes the rubber, which is the element in contact with the ball. In the 1920s, 
European players experimented with rubber skins on the wooden blades in order to increase 
spin, speed, and control [Chen, 2016]. However, as of today, the rubber consists of a highly 
engineered mix containing several compounds, which influence its performance. Thus, the 
research of chemical investigation of rubber is of great importance.  
 
Rubber Compounds 
Modern rubbers typically consist of a mix of polyisoprene i.e. natural rubber (NR), styrene-
butadiene rubber (SBR) and/or butadiene rubber (BR) along with a variety of fillers and 
resins. NR is often used, due to its better mechanical properties regarding elasticity and tensile 
strength. In contrast to NR, which is commonly derived from the Hevea Brasiliensis tree, the 
SBR and BR are synthetic rubbers. The blend ratio of NR and synthetic rubbers in a table 
tennis rubber varies depending on the desired rubber properties. Zhang et al. [2014] stated an 
80/20 NR/SBR blend to be popular in table tennis rubbers when aimed at increasing the 
tensile strength. Typically, the addition of synthetic rubber to NR is performed to retain the 
tensile, shear and elastic properties of the NR as well as to decrease costs [Liu et al., 2014]. 
==PAGE:19==

too much SBR or BR will simply weaken the rubber. When comparing the results of multiple 
studies (Table 2.1), the 82/18 NR/SBR mix found by Zhang et al. [2014] achieved a tensile 
modulus of 25.7 MPa with a shore hardness of 65A, while a 75/25 NR/BR mix presented by 
Liu et.al. [2014] only achieved 18.2 MPa with a shore hardness of 59.5A.

Tackiness of Rubber 
Tackiness is a crucial element in rubbers as it enables a player to create spin and control the 
ball. When described by elite players, tack is the ability of a rubber to grip or stick onto the 
ball. Varenberg et al. [2012] furthermore describe the stickiness (tackiness) as being the main 
discriminating factor between different pimples-in rubbers. Chemically, tack can be added to 
a rubber either by using tackifying resins, plasticizers or a rubber with a high natural tack - 
such as NR [“Rubber Adhesives”, 2017]. Tackifying resins are defined as low-molecular-weight 
compounds (oligomers) that are added to the blend. Tackifiers dissolve in the polymer matrix 
to enhance the base polymer structure, which, besides improving tack and peel adhesion, also 
affects the glass transition temperature, Tg. The Tg of tackifiers is typically higher than of 
base polymers, e.g. NR [“Tackifiers”, 2018].  
Fujita et. al [1998] investigated the use of resins in Pressure Sensitive Adhesives (PSA) 
that can be adhered several times without losing tack, and which adhere fast under light 
pressure without any heating. PSA´s are in many ways similar to how tacky table tennis 
rubbers work as the adhesion occurs fast and under light pressure when impacted by the ball. 
PSA that is based on NR is widely used, although NR alone requires tackifying resins to obtain 
the desired tack (e.g. aliphatic or aromatic hydrocarbons, polyterpenes, rosin derivatives). 
Fujita et. al [1998] examined 7 different tackifiers in NR blends on a 90/10 ratio, where the 
blends were added to a polyethylene terephthalate (PET) ﬁlm. Here, the tack was measured 
through a probe test on a Polyken Tack Tester. The tackifiers showed to increase the Tg of 
all the samples, and the best results were seen on the miscible tackifiers, such as the aliphatic 
based resins (ESCOREZ 1102) and the polyterpene resins (Clearon K-4090). The immiscible 
tackifiers (Polypale, Kristalex 1120) provided lower tack and dual Tg´s were observable.  
 
 
 
==PAGE:20==

Fillers/Additives 
Polymer compound materials have several advantages including high strength, low weight, 
good resilience and superior chemical resistance [Chen, 2016]. In addition to rubber compounds, 
one can add fillers/additives. Commonly used are inorganic fillers such as carbon black (CB) 
or calcium carbonate (CC). The amount of these fillers needed to achieve optimal strength is 
highly dependent on the reaction between NR and BR (or SBR), however, an excessive amount 
of fillers also decrease the mechanical properties as concluded in the study of Burgaz et. al 
[2019]. 
The aforementioned study focuses mainly on tensile strength, shear modulus and 
elongation at break rather than the friction, adhesive properties (tackiness) and CoR as 
assessment criteria. Thus, this leaves room for a modern study to focus on the rubber 
performance in a table tennis context, examining properties of spin creation, tackiness or CoR. 
However, the studies in this section are still helpful in identifying the common components in  
table tennis rubbers and products with similar architecture such as NR, SBR, BR, CB, and 
tackifying resins.  
2.3 Chemical Characterization of Vulcanized Rubbers 
Identification of blend ratio and the compounds of rubber samples is important in 
understanding the performance of a rubber. Even the slightest change in blend ratio or 
additives, such as oils or carbon black, can have noticeable impacts on the performance. Here, 
two papers were investigated to understand the commonly used techniques for quantifying and 
qualifying blend ratios and rubber compositions. The papers mainly describe the level of 
precision of the techniques in binary and ternary rubber compositions.  
Fernández-Berridi et al. [2006] and Lee et al. [2007] compare the techniques as a mean 
to analyze and quantify tires dedicated for recycling. Alike table tennis rubbers, tires are 
typically made of NR/BR/SBR mix. Thus, the studies on these can be associated and related 
to the studies of table tennis rubbers. The three main techniques are:  
 
Differential Scanning Calorimetry (DSC) 
This technique detects the exothermic and endothermic processes, which appear at a controlled  
heating or cooling rate of a material. The method can be used in determining the glass 
transition temperature, Tg,  the melting temperature, Tm, and the crystallization temperature, 
Tc, of a material. The measuring range is usually within -70°C to +600°C [“Termisk 
karakterisering”, 2019]. 
  
==PAGE:21==
13 
 
Figure 2.4: (left) DSC curves of BR, SBR and NR, and (right) DSC curves of a NR/SBR 
blend with increasing SBR ratio [Lee et. al., 2007] 
Lee et. al. [2007] used DSC to characterize the thermal degradation of elastomers, where 11 
samples with various blend compositions, ranging from 0 to 100% NR (10% increase) were 
analyzed in terms of Tg. Vulcanized BR, NR and SBR were analyzed as well. The study 
showed that Tg of NR, BR, and SBR is distinct and possible to identify and separate. In a 
rubber blend, the Tg will be affected by the compounds and the ratio of the mix. In the study, 
the Tg (midpoint) of NR is identified to be -66°C, -107°C for (high 1,4-cis) BR and -51°C for 
SBR, as seen in Figure 2.4 (left). The impact of the blend ratio can furthermore be seen in 
Figure 2.4 (right), where the Tg increase as a function of SBR ratio. If the polymer components 
are immiscible, the curves will exhibit two steps, as seen in the binary blends of the same 
figure.  
It is important to notice that the Tg of a blend can vary depending on the chemical 
structure of the rubber. An example would be cis-1,4-butadiene and a trans-1,4-butadiene with 
Tg´s of -108°C and -83°C, respectively  [“Steric Arrangement”, 2017]. Both components are 
popular in BR. 
 
Thermogravimetric Analysis (TGA)  
This technique measures the change in weight as a function of temperature. TGA is suitable 
for quantitative analysis determination of compositions, volatile components or the portion of 
inorganic materials. The measuring range is usually within 30°C-1100°C [“Termisk 
karakterisering”, 2019]. To avoid oxidation, pyrolization of inorganic compounds and to achieve 
a stable curve, nitrogen (N2) can be used. In atmospheric air, inorganic compounds, such as 
carbon black, are pyrolyzed as well. 
TGA analyses were carried out by Fernández-Berridi et al. [2006] and Lee et al. [2007] 
with the aim of determining the precision of the technique in relation to quantifying and 
qualifying the blend ratio in NR/SBR and NR/SBR/BR rubbers. By observing the weight loss 
of the samples and the temperatures in which they occurred, they were able to estimate the 
blend ratio of the samples, while successfully identifying the rubber components. Generally, in 
==PAGE:22==
14 
an N2 atmosphere, NR has a decomposition peak temperature of roughly 350-370°C, while 
synthetic rubbers (SBR, BR) have a peak temperature of roughly 420-450°C.  
In order to quantify the level of carbon black in the aforementioned studies, the 
atmosphere was cooled to 400°C and changed from N2 to air. This change of atmosphere 
allowed the inorganic compounds to pyrolyze.  
 
  
 
Figure 2.5: (left) Weight loss and derivative weight loss curves of a NR/SBR blend, and 
(right) Calibrated data used to identify rubber blends [Fernández-Berridi et al. 2006] 
In the study of Fernández-Berridi et. al [2006], the temperature at 70% weight loss was used 
to identify the blend ratio of a binary NR/SBR blend within an accuracy of 5% (see Figure 
2.5, left). To obtain this, 13 samples with known compositions were analyzed in order to create 
a calibration curve and to calculate the blend ratio of 2 samples with unknown compositions 
(see Figure 2.5, right). A similar technique was used by Lee et al. [2007], however, with less 
accuracy, as the ternary NR/BR/SBR blends tested were too hard to interpret. Thus, TGA is 
useful for making a quick qualification of the compounds by observing the temperatures at 
which weight losses occur. Both studies also added that additional tests, such as FTIR,  are 
needed to obtain detailed results. Quantifying, on the other hand, requires samples with known 
compositions. Here, the samples need to be a binary blend and not a ternary blend.  
 
Fourier-Transform Infrared Spectroscopy (FTIR)  
FTIR is a common technique that uses infrared light to analyze the absorbance, reflection, and 
transmittance of light through a material at a predetermined light spectrum. FTIR is effective 
in identifying characteristic infrared (spectrum) bands and peaks related to the molecular 
vibration in the components. Infrared Spectroscopy implies the fact that the frequency of the 
absorbed radiation is equal to the vibrational frequency of the molecules in the mid-infrared 
region (4000cm−1 - 400 cm−1). Thus, able to identify characteristic bands of molecules. For a 
FTIR device, the raw data recorded is Fourier Transformed into a spectrum. FTIR is often 
used as an analytical chemical method for qualitatively identifying a material. Here, the 
spectrum of a material is compared with spectra of reference materials accessed through a 
database [“Termisk Karakterisering”, 2019]. 
==PAGE:23==
15 
Fernández-Berridi et. al [2006] and Lee et. al [2007] used FTIR to qualify and quantify 
the rubber blends in their studies. Both studies used characteristic peaks and bands in the 
“fingerprint” region (1400-650 cm−1) to identify components such as styrene, 1,2-butadiene, and 
isoprene (see Figure 2.6). A software was utilized to identify peaks in their studies and 
compared the spectrum results with an appropriate spectra library.  
 
 
Figure 2.6: (left) FTIR transmittance spectrum of NR, and (right) FTIR absorbance 
spectrum of a NR/SBR blend.   
It can be concluded that using TGA, FTIR and partially DSC, it is possible to detailly identify 
components within a rubber composition. In order to successfully quantify the components and 
blend ratios, samples with known compositions are needed to calibrate and validate the 
methods.

4.2.4 Discussion 
Tackiness 
A pattern is seen amongst the CH rubbers at compression forces of 20N and 40N, where the 
CH B rubber is seen to be tackiest followed by CH R2 as the second tackiest and CH R1 as 
the least tacky. The tack of CH B is observed to be 23.8% higher than the CH R2 on average, 
while the CH R2 has 32.5% higher tack than CH R1 on average.  
Between the compression forces of 20N and 40N, the tackiness readings are seen to 
increase with appx. 28% on average, stating that the larger the pressure, the stronger the 
adhesive bonding. This was in accordance with the set hypothesis of pressure sensitive rubber. 
Technically, this can be explained by the increased contact area when compressing the rubber, 
enabling the rubber to cling onto a larger surface of the ball. As the CH R2 is newer, it is also 
seen to be tackier than the CH R1, which might have deteriorated in properties over time as 
table tennis rubbers do [Lodziak, 2016]. Ideally, this should have been the case for old CH B 
too, however, this flags a difference in properties between a red and black CH rubber. In fact, 
top Chinese players are known to use black rubber on their forehand stroke [Letts, 2018] unlike 
every other elite player and this might explain the reason - it is tackier, enabling for more spin.  
The accuracy of the applied load cell used was ±0.050N, which validates the tack 
measurements of CH rubber as they are well above this limit. The EU rubber, however, showed 
negligible readings regarding the tackiness (-0.0025N). As this was well below the accuracy, 
the tack of the EU rubber was evaluated as invalid and assumed zero. 
  
Hardness 
In terms of hardness, the CH R rubber is interestingly seen to be 6A softer than the EU rubber, 
while the opposite conclusion prevails regarding the foam, which was expected. Here, a 
difference of 10A was spotted between the CH and EU foam, emphasizing a significant softer 
foam of the EU bat rubber. When foam+rubber is layered together (i.e. bat rubber), it is still 
seen for the EU sample to be 6A softer, indicating that the foam is the largest influencer on 
hardness. Lastly, CH B is seen to have softer rubber than CH R1 and CH R2, indicating a 
difference in the rubber compositions that could be addressed during the chemical 
characterization experiments in Chapter 5.  
 
==PAGE:38==
30 
Elasticity 
A clear difference is seen in the elastic properties of the two rubbers, where the CH rubber is 
80% more elastic than the EU rubber. Similarly, the CH rubber shows 63% greater strength 
compared to the EU as well as being capable of straining more before eventually breaking. 
However, it is worth mentioning the measured difference in the thicknesses of the rubber 
samples, where the CH rubber was 0.14 mm thicker. This difference is mainly related to the 
separation process of the rubber from the foam using a scalpel, where the pimples might had 
been slightly cut resulting in an uneven surface. Nonetheless, the thickness difference is 
evaluated as indecisive to override the obtained conclusions of this experiment.  
4.2.5 Conclusions 
● The CH rubber is tackier, softer and twice as elastic as the EU rubber. However, the 
CH foam is significantly harder than the EU foam. All of these results align with the 
presented hypotheses, except for the CH rubber being softer than EU rubber.  
● The black CH B rubber is tackier than both of the red CH R1 and CH R2 rubbers. 
Specifically, it is 23.8% tackier than CH R2. Amongst the two red rubbers, the CH R2 
rubber is 32.5% tackier than CH R1. Finally, the tack of EU rubber was immeasurable. 
4.3 KPM Experiment  
This main experiment was inspired by Rinaldi et. al [2019], who studied the case of foam and 
foam+rubber, respectively. Different from that study, the case here was the examination of 
two independent bat rubbers, namely the EU and CH bat rubber. Thus, some inspiration and 
knowledge were extracted from the study in conducting this experiment. The idea of this 
experiment is to capture the bat-ball impact using a high-speed camera in order to determine 
the three relevant KPM; SR, CoR and throw angle 𝛳.   
 
Based on the findings of mechanical properties experiments, the hypotheses for this KPM 
experiment are:  
 
1) The EU rubber exerts more speed i.e. larger CoR due to a softer bat rubber 
2) The CH rubber exhibits better spin performance i.e. higher SR due to tackiness

4.4 Discussion  
From Table 4.5 it is noted that a small amount of spin (2.49 rps) was in fact imparted at 0 
SS. The numerical ingoing spin difference between 3 SS and 6 SS is furthermore 8.69 rps, 
although one could have imagined a larger difference for comparison purposes. Ingoing 
velocities varied too due to ball machine settings, however, it would not affect CoR or SR as 
they are ratio based. Ideally, an advanced ball machine would have been desired for this 
experiment, informing the user numerical values of spin and velocity in advance. Nonetheless, 
the utilized machine provided accurate results and the three SS still create a firm baseline for 
comparison.  
 
CoR  
The EU rubber clearly indicates better rebound capabilities than CH R1 and CH R2 with 
larger CoR irrespective of 𝛼 and SS. Especially at an orthogonal angle with no spin, a 
significant difference of 16.98% is found between EU and CH R2. The results are expected as 
the foam of the EU rubber is softer than the one of CH rubbers. Softer foam is more elastic 
and thus capable of restoring more energy back onto the ball, as evidenced in Equation (5). 
Furthermore, the results are in accordance with the manufacturer’s performance ratings of the 
two rubbers (see Appendix F). It is also observed that the CoR exceeds the value 1 at 3 SS 
and 6 SS, which is explained by the rotational energy of the incoming ball being translated 
into translational energy.  
At the fixed bat angle, 𝛼=-30°, the results are furthermore seen to be located relatively 
identical across the three SS with CH R1 as the second-best performer. This is interesting as 
CH R1 was expected to deteriorate in properties over time compared to CH R2, although the 
difference at an orthogonal angle with 6 SS is rather negligible. However, as the foam is the 
major influencer on the CoR, the results flag that the foam perhaps do not deteriorate as much 
as the rubber. Nonetheless, the results from 0 SS and 3 SS definitively confirm that CH R1 
has better rebound capabilities than CH R2. On average, the CH R1 performs 15-20% better 
than CH R2.  
 
SR 
Surprisingly, no major difference in spin performance is observed amongst the three different 
rubbers. Inspecting the case of 3 SS and 6 SS, the results are located rather similar indicating 
that CH rubber generally does not impart more spin than an EU rubber. The hypothesis of a 
CH rubber imparts more spin onto the ball due to higher tack is therefore falsified in relation 
to the conducted KPM experiment. One can furthermore argue that the influence of bat 
movement is crucial as the preliminary experiment clearly evidenced a difference in favor of 
CH rubber. Thus, it could be intriguing to perform a KPM experiment with the stroke 
movement of a bat and subsequently inspect the results to observe any difference. Nevertheless, 
amongst the CH rubbers, it is seen that the CH R2 rubber generally displays slightly better 
spin capabilities with 10-15% on average. This might be explained by the deterioration of the 
CH R1 rubber surface over time.  
==PAGE:46==
38 
Large values of SR were achieved at 0 SS, especially at tilted bat angles. This was 
expected as the rubber grips onto the ball at tilted angles resulting in tremendous spin being 
imparted on the ball in either direction. Moreover, a negative SR is noticed at 𝛼=-30° and 
𝛼=0°, hinting that the spin direction changed subsequent to impact.  
Generally, it can also be concluded that the SR slightly decreases as the ingoing spin 
increases. Recalling Equation (1), this finding makes sense as these are in reverse proportional. 
Finally, higher SR is achieved at a closed bat angle compared to an open bat angle. A possible 
reason for this is that the ball skits onto the surface at an open bat angle, thus it has lesser 
contact with the rubber.  
 
Throw Angle  
A noticeable trend is observed regarding the 𝛳, where the EU rubber clearly exhibits larger 𝛳 
regardless of bat angles and amount of ingoing spin. On the other hand, the 𝛳 is lowest for 
CH R2 at the forehand stroke of 𝛼=-30°, while the CH R1 generally has slightly lower 𝛳 at 
the two other bat angles. This trend is seen in both 3 SS and 6 SS flagging that the tacky 
rubber surface comes greatly into play at closed bat surfaces rather than open. The difference 
between CH R2 and EU rubber is calculated to be 58.5% on average at the forehand stroke, 
while the difference between CH R2 and CH R1 is 30% on average under the same case.  
The tendency of lower throw angles for CH rubbers indicates that during a forehand 
stroke, the CH rubber allows the player to attack the ball more (exert more force) knowing 
that the ball has a greater chance to hit the opponent's side of the table in comparison to the 
EU rubber. Increased ball attack will ideally result in more ball spin and thus also affect the 
ball flight in a down-going direction due to aerodynamics. The lower throw angles also mean 
that the stroking player can attack the ball when the ball is further down in the bounce i.e. 
closer to the ground. This is obviously a big advantage for an attacking player in a real-life 
game scenario.  
 
Uncertainties  
It was observed that the ingoing ball trajectory was not completely horizontal at 0 SS and had 
a slightly upgoing angle of 2-3°. This could be explained by the misalignment of the wheels 
inside the ball machine questioning the quality of the device. One can, therefore, not compare 
the numerical throw angle values with other spin settings as it would be misleading. However, 
the EU and CH rubber could still be compared mutually.  
Many uncertainties in this experiment could be linked to the manual measuring in the 
post-processing using the Tracker software. The biggest uncertainty was, however, related to 
the manual measurements of the spin as the spin axis was not always parallel with the camera, 
causing difficulties in measuring accurate spin. Especially at outgoing spin, the spin axis was 
often three-dimensional as a result of different impact locations on the bat. In addition, the 
ball machine was observed to fire the ball in a non-straight path, once again questioning the 
precision and quality of the device. Hence, the 22 intersections points of the ball were not 
enough to fully cover the three-dimensional spin axis of the ball. Generally, the distribution of 
==PAGE:47==
39 
data did not show a large dispersion in values indicating that the measurements were fairly 
accurate.  
 
One assumption in this experiment was that the bat angle was precisely fixed at the 
chosen bat angles, while in reality there is some variation. This variation was measured to be 
in the range of ±1°. Although the variation does not seem much, the actual impact still remains 
unknown, thus contributing to slightly imprecise measurements. Another assumption was the 
identical impact point on the bat, which was not successfully achieved. The CoR values might 
therefore have varied depending on the impact point as found in the literature review, hence 
contributing to imprecise values of this particular KPM. Finally, external factors, such as 
humidity and temperature, might also have contributed to additional uncertainties.  
4.5 Conclusions  
● The EU rubber gives a player the ability to exert more speed on the ball compared to 
the CH rubbers. At 𝛼=0° with no spin, a difference of 16.98% was found between EU 
and CH R2. It is in accordance with expectations, and the hypothesis of a softer foam 
provides more speed for the EU rubber has therefore been accepted. 
● There is no significant difference in spin performance between the EU and CH rubbers 
in the conducted experiment. However, between the CH rubbers, the CH R2 rubber is 
seen to perform 15-20% better on average. The hypothesis of the CH rubber imparts 
more spin onto the ball due to higher tack is therefore falsified in the conducted 
experiment.  
● The 𝛳 is 58.5% lower for CH R2 rubber than EU rubber and 30% lower than CH R1 
at the forehand stroke, 𝛼=-30°. Generally, the 𝛳 is larger for the EU rubber. In a game 
scenario, lower throw angles of CH rubbers will allow the player to attack more (exert 
more force) and thus impart more spin on the ball.  
● The KPM experiment consisted of several uncertainties that could have been addressed 
with the availability of better equipment and resources. Nonetheless, the obtained data 
are still evaluated to be valid.

5 Chemical Characterization of Rubber 
This section consists of 4 methods to characterize and analyze the rubber compounds. The 
utilized techniques are TGA, DSC, FTIR and an extraction experiment, which were explained 
in Chapter 2. The purpose of these methods is to obtain data related to the chemical 
composition of the rubbers, in order to characterize and quantify the compounds. This 
knowledge can furthermore be used to reengineer a rubber and be helpful in explaining the 
mechanical behavior observed in the mechanical experiments.  
 
The following hypotheses were set in the chemical characterization experiments: 
  
● The NR is dominant in all rubber samples, while synthetic rubber will be present too.  
● The tack on the CH rubbers is applied through a surface treatment process.  
 
The main focus of the upcoming chemical experiments is to identify differences between the 
EU and CH samples. However, the techniques were also applied in examining the top and low 
layers of the CH rubbers that was discovered earlier in the project (Section 4.1). The layers 
were separated and analyzed individually to identify any differences and will be denoted CH 
R2-T and CH R2-L in this report. Based on the indications from the mechanical 
characterization, the CH B rubber was similarly analyzed in the FTIR and DSC analyses to 
spot any major differences between a red and black CH rubber.  
5.1   Extraction Analysis 
An extraction experiment was initiated to analyze the weight loss of the rubber samples after 
volatile compounds were extracted using benzine, and to observe the impact on the tacky 
surface on the CH samples. The method was chosen, as it is a common way to extract 
plasticizers and other volatile compounds from vulcanized rubber [Harada, 2016].  
In total, 3x3 samples of 100mg ± 5 mg were prepared and weighed before and after the 
extraction. The samples were weighed using a RADWAG PS 750.R2 precision scale. The 
experiment was performed with air-tight containers in room temperature surroundings. 
 
The following procedure was applied: 
 
1. Weigh the sample masses individually before the experiment and note it down.  
2. Place the samples in a sealed ceramic container and soak it in benzine. 
3. Replace the liquid every 12 hours for a total of 4 times.  
4. Let the samples dry at room temperature for 12 hours at the end of the experiment. 
5. Weigh the sample masses individually after the experiment and note it down.

5.5 Discussion 
The results of the DSC, TGA, FTIR and extraction experiment indicate differences in the 
composition of the rubbers, although the dominating compounds are identical. All the samples 
feature NR and BR with similar Tg and pyrolization peaks. In the FTIR analysis, no 
characteristic peaks related to styrene (698 cm-1) was observed. This confirms that the 
synthetic rubber in the samples is identified as BR and not SBR.  
 
The high portion of NR found in the samples is in accordance with expectations and the studies 
of Zhang et. al [2006] and Liu et. al [2007], as this provides better mechanical properties. 
==PAGE:59==
51 
Furthermore, based solely on the weight loss observed at 360-370°C in the TGA (N2), the CH 
R2 samples had the largest weight loss at roughly 58%, while the EU samples had a slightly 
lower weight loss of 50%. This indicates that the CH R2 sample is more likely to have a higher 
concentration of NR than the EU sample. In the case of the TGA performed in atmospheric 
air, the CH samples had a 12% weight loss of carbon black, while the EU sample had a 10% 
weight loss. 
 
Although the NR seems dominant, the results are insufficient to precisely determine 
the blend ratios as no rubbers with known blend ratios were tested as reference. A rubber with 
known blend ratio acts as calibration and helps in estimating the blend ratio relatively, as 
approached in the literature. To obtain a rubber with a known blend ratio, one can either self-
produce the mix or buy a rubber with a known blend ratio. However, none of these two options 
were possible in the conducted experiment due to lack of competencies and insufficient time.  
 
EU Samples 
Inspecting the DSC curves of the EU sample, an endothermic peak at -20.52°C and an 
exothermic peak at -50°C is found. This can be explained by a plasticizer, which has a Tm=-
20.52°C and Tc=-50°C. It is furthermore confirmed by 16.6% and 23% weight loss in the 
extraction and TGA experiments, respectively. In contrast, the weight loss, Tm and Tc are 
not observable for the CH samples, stating a difference. The use of plasticizer indicates the 
presence of a hard rubber - potentially a synthetic rubber - that demanded softening in order 
to obtain desired mechanical properties. In fact, this was backed by the physical observation 
of a brittle and harder EU sample at the end of the extraction experiment.  
The FTIR results of the EU sample showed a distinct presence of NR and BR as the 
peak intensities on the spectra were strong and noticeable. Moreover, the presence of NR 
seemed to be lower in EU sample compared to CH samples, which was in accordance with the 
TGA results. 
 
CH Samples 
In the DSC analysis, no major differences were observed in the Tg amongst the CH samples, 
while the TGA results stated a 30% difference in the ratio between the second and third weight 
loss for CH R2 and CH B at an identical temperature range. This indicates that BR level in 
the CH B sample is higher than in that of CH R2 sample, however, it does not align with the 
increased CH B tack observed in the tack test. Thus, the findings could also be related to 
production variations, degradation, color pigmentation, fillers or simply different blend ratios. 
Lastly, FTIR results did not display any differences in the composition of the two rubbers, 
although it did flag a difference in the intensity of the peaks and thus the ratio of the 
compounds.  
Between the CH R1 and CH 2 sample, the FTIR results were fairly similar with 
identical peaks and negligible difference in absorption, indicating that these rubber 
compositions are close to similar.  
 
 
==PAGE:60==
52 
Top and Low Layer 
TGA results on the top and lower layers of the CH R2 sample show a 353% difference in ratio 
between the second and third weight loss for CH R2-T and CH R2-L. This indicates a large 
difference in the level of NR on the top layer compared to lower layer, resulting in increased 
tackiness. Also, the TGA results flag that there are no components that selectively degas at 
low temperatures, which indicates that the tacky top layer is not a result of a surface 
treatment. The FTIR results highlight a minor difference in the compositions as both the CH 
R1-T and CH R2-T show peaks related to zinc stearate in contrast to the lower layers. 
However, zinc stearate is of less importance and does not affect the mechanical properties or 
the tackiness as it is used for mold release purposes and is non-sticky. Apart from this, the 
FTIR results do once again indicate a difference in blend ratios of the components due to 
varying peak intensities. 
 
 
Tackiness 
Considering the tacky properties of the CH top layers, minor traces of tackifying resins 
(aliphatic hydrocarbons, polyterpene resin) were found in the FTIR spectra. While the DSC 
analysis displayed a negligible 1°C difference between the Tg of the top and low layers, a small 
amount of tackifying resin could still have been added to obtain a small, yet measurable tack. 
As described in the literature review, it is common to use NR based PSA. With this in mind, 
it is likely that a similar technique was used on the top layer. 
 
The combined results highly suggest that the tack is rather a product of the rubber composition 
and not a surface treatment. In case of a surface treatment, a volatile compound added to the 
surface would have been extracted in the extraction experiment, and pyrolyzed at low 
temperatures in the TGA. This was not obtained.  
5.6 Conclusions 
 
● The NR is dominant in all the rubbers. The CH rubber feature a higher level of NR 
compared to the EU rubber. Synthetic rubber was similarly observed and identified as 
BR during the FTIR analysis. The results are in accordance with the expectations and 
the hypothesis of NR dominant rubbers. Another difference was identified between the 
EU and CH rubber. This difference was related to a plasticizer in the EU rubber that 
could be noticed in the extraction, DSC and TGA experiments.  
  
● The tack of the CH rubber is not a result of a surface treatment process, but rather a 
product of tackifying resins mixed with NR. Traces of the tackifying resins aliphatic 
hydrocarbons and polyterpene resin were found in the applied FTIR software. Thus, 
these findings reject the hypothesis of the tack is applied through surface treatment.  
 
==PAGE:61==
53 
● The examination of the multi-layered CH rubbers indicates a higher level of NR in the 
top layer than the low layer, resulting in increased tack. Furthermore, the top layer 
consists of zinc stearate on the surface, which is a mold release agent, and therefore 
does not affect the mechanical properties or tack. Finally, varying peak intensities in 
the FTIR spectra flag that different blend ratios were applied in the two compositions.  
 
● There are minor differences in the composition of the CH R1 and CH B rubbers. The 
DSC showed very similar properties regarding the Tg. The TGA and FTIR indicated 
a higher level of BR in the CH B rubber, however, this did not align with the findings 
of the mechanical tack test. It is furthermore concluded that the CH R1 and CH R2 
rubbers are very similar in composition and thus any differences found in the 
composition are due to the degradation of the rubber.

6 Future Work 
Based on the findings of this study, further research is necessary to fully uncover the topic. 
Mechanically, a dynamic experiment, consisting of controlled forehand stroke bat movements, 
could be interesting in investigating any spin performance differences between EU and CH 
rubber. Especially, as the preliminary experiment stated a difference in favor of CH rubber, 
while the KPM experiment did not. Thus, to evaluate the influence of bat movement i.e. larger 
momentum during the collision between bat and ball, one can set up such an experiment. 
Furthermore, the findings indicate that the combination of foam+rubber is perhaps more 
crucial than anticipated, which makes inspection of the foam another interesting aspect. 
Similarly, the influence of pimples (e.g. size, density, thickness) could be investigated in future 
work to eliminate any major influential factors on the KPM.  
In simulation terms, it should also be considered implementing the obtained data in a 
pre-existing numerical model (FEM) in order to achieve a precise bat-ball impact. If the impact 
can be simulated precisely, one could use this in evaluating the influence of relevant variables 
related to the rubber surface.  
Finally, a so-called ‘blind test’ experiment could be carried out in collaboration with 
Team Danmark, where the elite players would be handed different random rubbers without 
knowing what is what. Some of the rubbers would be EU rubbers treated with tackifying 
instances on the surface, while others would be normal CH rubbers. The purpose of this 
experiment would be to qualitatively indicate any differences between EU and CH rubber as 
well as to estimate the psychological effect of tackiness. In other words, whether or not it is 
the tackiness that defines a difference for the elite players.  
 
In chemical terms, it could be intriguing to dig into the composition of the foam layers in order 
to evaluate its influence and to gain a better understanding. However, more importantly, it is 
desired to entirely clarify what imposes the tackiness component in the CH rubber as the 
findings of this project devalue the possibility of surface treatment. By continuously applying 
a scientific method, one could eventually explain the root source of tackiness. This could, for 
instance, be tackifiers or simply the great amount of NR that generically is tackier than SBR. 
In relation to this, the double layer of the CH rubber could be further inspected to obtain new 
findings regarding the difference between these.  
 
A more thorough examination of the rubbers utilizing the FTIR method could also be 
helpful in fully determining the active components in the compositions. For the tacky CH 
rubbers, it could be useful to include known FTIR results of pure NR as a baseline for visually 
indicating the additional components within the rubbers.  
 
A difference was similarly spotted between the red and black CH rubber in terms of 
tackiness, but also during the weight loss in the TGA test and the absorbance in the FTIR 
test. This concluded a difference in the rubber compositions, thus, for research purposes, it 
would be exciting to further investigate the differences between these in terms of the exact 
blend ratio as they both are labeled with identical model numbers from the manufacturer. For 
this, samples with a known NR/BR blend ratio would be needed.

7 Reflection 
Reflecting upon this project, it was a great experience for us to work within the field of sports 
technology and in collaboration with Team Danmark. This project has been greatly 
experimental compared to our previous projects, which turned out to be thriving rather than 
demotivating. However, the numerous experiments did require us to get acquainted with new 
methods and approaches, which at times made it difficult to obtain a solid theoretical 
foundation, especially regarding the chemical characterization experiments. Neither of us have 
a chemical or polymer background, which led to major dependency on our supervisors in terms 
of applicable methods, interpretation and equipment accessibility. A scientific method was 
applied throughout the chemical characterization, where an analysis was chosen based on the 
findings of previous analyses. With our mentioned backgrounds, this made the preparation for 
each analysis less thorough – for instance, we had to perform same test over several occasions 
for different samples. Ideally, as half of this project was based around chemistry and polymer, 
an additional team member with such a background would have been valuable in the 
completion of this thesis.  
 
Although, severe difficulties were met during the chemical characterization, it provided 
us with new learning outcomes, such as enabling us to independently characterize a material 
in terms of chemical analyses. This will be highly useful in the future when working across 
multiple engineering domains as we did in this project.  
 
Reflecting upon the outcome, we did not achieve the desired outcome of a testable rubber 
prototype. Although, this was optimistic, the obtained knowledge from the experiments did 
not fully qualify us to set up specific requirements for an enhanced rubber. However, it did 
provide an unique and strong in depth knowledge of table tennis rubbers, and eliminated 
multiple theories, such as surface treated tack. We believe that in order to set up requirements 
for an enhanced rubber, the obtained knowledge in this project should be linked up with rubber 
experts. Thus, through co-creation, a rubber prototype could be made, which could be 
subjected to the same experiments as in this project.  
While it was intended to involve Danish elite players in the process of developing a 
rubber, this was not achieved due to the proportion of the project. In fact, we did not initiate 
any development process as a result of obtaining a fair understanding of the complex rubber.  
 
Finally, this project would have been more compendious if additional CH R1 rubber material 
was available as this would have excluded the purchase and use of CH R2. It would have 
removed any uncertainties in the difference between the two rubbers, resulting in fewer samples 
and fewer tests required. On the contrary, the examination of the ideally identical bat rubbers 
gave us an opportunity to assess the similarities and flag any crucial findings related to the 
manufacturing of table tennis rubbers.
