Abstract 
Human papillomavirus (HPV) is a common sexually transmitted virus, that infects the epithelial cell 
of uterine cervix in women. Persistent HPV infection, especially type 16 and 18, is the main cause 
of cervical cancer, which can develop over a course of 10-20 years from initially encountering the 
virus. The development of cervical cancer happens due to the oncogenic activity of the viral proteins 
E6 and E7 and is a gradual progression from cervical intraepithelial neoplasia (CIN) to invasive 
cancer. Although most individuals are able to clear the HPV infection, and sometimes CIN, 
themselves, cancer incidences reveal that some individuals are incapable of raising a sufficient 
immune response. 
 
In this project, we sought to investigate the immune system’s general response towards HPV-driven 
late-stage CIN3 and cervical cancer and its recognition of cancer-specific HPV epitopes. By using 
multicolor flow cytometry, we were able to show that cervical cancer patients display a high level of 
immune infiltration in the tumor and that a large proportion of the CD8 T cells show signs of 
exhaustion. These characteristics were limited to the cancer patients and correlated to the 
progression of the disease. No significant difference in inflammatory of exhaustive phenotypes were 
observed between healthy individuals and CIN3 patients, suggesting that the anti-tumor immune 
response is not yet manifested in these chronically infected individuals. By using a novel technology 
for high-throughput screening of epitope recognition, we were additionally able to detect specific 
CD8 T cell recognition of HPV epitopes from E2, E6 and E7 from both HPV type 16 and 18. 
Recognition of E2 was most prevalent, and similar epitopes were detected across multiple 
individuals, suggesting immunogenic epitopes of clinical relevance. Furthermore, using the same 
technique we also showed that local immune checkpoint inhibition with anti-PD-L1 positively affects 
the number of HPV epitopes being recognized in cervical cancer patients.

1 Introduction 
 
1.1 The immune system at a glance 
The immune system is the body’s own defense against foreign pathogens and malignant cells, and 
it is divided into the rapid and unspecific innate immune response and the slow acting but antigen-
specific adaptive immune response. The innate and adaptive immune systems are highly 
codependent, and both rely on the activity of immune cells that originates from hematopoietic stem 
cells in the bone marrow. The hematopoietic stem cells differentiate into either the common lymphoid 
progenitor cells or myeloid stem cells [1]. B cells, T cells and natural killer (NK) cells originate from 
the lymphoid progenitor cells, while granulocytes (neutrophils, eosinophils, basophils, mast cells) 
and monocytes originate from the myeloid stem cells [2], [3].  
 
The innate immune system comprises cells of the myeloid lineage as well as the lymphoid NK-cells 
and is the first defense against. The innate immune system initiates rapid inflammatory responses 
upon recognizing pathogen-associated molecular patterns and damage-associated molecular 
patterns that are related to infection, loss of tissue integrity, cell death or cancer [4]. The innate 
immune system is without memory function but is central for the activation of the adaptive immune 
response [5]. 
 
B and T cells comprise the adaptive immune system and can recognize antigens with their variable 
antigen-specific receptors. B cells express B cell receptors which recognize foreign antigens and 
upon activation, the B cells proliferate and differentiate into antibody-secreting plasma cells needed 
to fight infections [6]. On the other hand, T cells are mediators of the cellular adaptive immunity and 
are important for the immune system’s ability to fight both infections and cancer [7]. 
 
1.1.1 Myeloid cells 
Monocytes circulate in the peripheral blood, and are divided into classical, intermediate and non-
classical monocytes which all play a key role in immune responses [8]. Classical monocytes are 
most prevalent type and is characterized by surface expression of CD14.  Intermediate monocytes 
are characterized by their co-expression of CD14 and CD16 and non-classical monocytes are 
identified by their CD16 expression and low CD14 expression. The classical and intermediate 
monocytes are considered proinflammatory cells given their expression of cytokines like IL-6, IL-1b 
and tumor necrosis factor a (TNFa), whereas the non-classical monocytes express higher levels of 
==PAGE:13==
 
 
12 
anti-inflammatory cytokine like IL-10 and are involved in tissue repair [9], [10]. Upon inflammation, 
monocytes are recruited to the tissue and differentiate into phagocytic macrophages and the myeloid 
dendritic cells (mDC) [11]. Due to their antigen presenting function, monocytes, macrophages and 
DCs all express the major histocompatibility complex class II (MHC-II) [1]. 
 
DCs are a heterogeneous population of antigen presenting cells which also covers the lymphoid 
progenitor-derived plasmacytoid DCs (pDC) [12]. The mDCs are characterized by their expression 
of CD33 and CD11b and are specialized at taking up antigens and presenting them to naïve T cells 
for activation in the lymph nodes [13]. The pDCs are characterized as CD11c-, CD123+ and are 
known to be producing type I interferons (IFN-I) during viral infection [12]. 
 
1.1.2 T cells 
T cells are divided into cytotoxic T cells and helper T cells dependent on their expression of the 
surface receptors CD8 and CD4 [14]. The cytotoxic CD8 T cells interact with the major 
histocompatibility complex class I (MHC-I) molecules that are expressed on all nucleated cells, 
whereas the helper CD4 T cells interact with MHC-II molecules which are only expressed on antigen 
presenting cells. The helper CD4 T cells play a role in the activation of B cells and the CD8 T cells. 
When the CD8 T cells recognize their cognate antigen presented on the MHC-I of an infected or 
malignant cell, they can kill the target cell by different mechanisms; They can mediate apoptosis 
directly by releasing secretory granules of perforin and granzymes, and expression of the Fas-ligand 
or indirectly by releasing cytokines TNFa and interferon-g (IFN-g) [15]. 
 
Before becoming effective cytotoxic or helper cells, the T cells need to be activated. T cell precursors 
mature into naïve T cells in the thymus and migrate to the lymph nodes where they encounter 
antigens that are presented on MHC molecules of the antigen presenting cells. When a T cell 
receptor recognize a specific antigen in combination with co-stimulatory signals, the T cell proliferate 
and differentiate into different subsets. Recent studies suggest that the fate of memory T cells (TM), 
effector T cells (TE), and exhausted T cells is defined during the differentiation of T cells from naïve 
cells, however, the entire mechanism is not fully understood [16].  Based on the expression of 
CD45RA and CCR7 (a homing molecule to the secondary lymphoid organ), T cells are commonly 
divided into naïve, central memory (TCM), effector memory (TEM), and  terminally differentiated 
effector memory (TEMRA) T cells  [17], [18]. Additionally, early T cell subsets are also characterized 
by their expression of the costimulatory molecule CD27, which is gradually lost in the terminally 
differentiated T cells [19]. Conversely, the terminally differentiated T cells express CD57, which is a 
known marker of replicative senescence [20].

1.1.3 Antigen recognition by T cells 
The conventional T cell antigen receptor (TCR) is made up of a protein complex that includes the a 
and b chains. The ab subunits are associated with the CD3 complex responsible for signal 
transduction upon activation [21]. The ab chains show a resemblance to the immunoglobulin Fab 
fragment by having a constant region and a variable region made up of a variable (V), diversity (D), 
joining (J) domains [1]. 
 
In order to ensure cell-mediated immunity against various targets, a broad T cell specificity is 
needed. Indeed, the TCR repertoire is extremely diverse due to somatic gene rearrangements as 
well as the following combination of the a and b chains into a unique heterodimeric TCR with the 
ability to recognize specific antigens. When a T cell is under development in the thymus, the V(D)J 
and constant regions of each chain are combined by somatic recombination and further 
diversification is obtained by random insertion and deletions where the V(D)J segments are joined. 
Each chain in the TCR contain three complimentary determining regions in the variable region, which 
are hypervariable loop structures that govern the peptide-MHC recognition [21].  
 
The ability to perform somatic combination and heterodimeric paring of TCR ab chains, the number 
of distinctive TCRs will theoretically exceed 1015, though the actual number in each individual has 
been estimated to be 106-108 [22], [23].  Apart from recognizing a specific epitope, the TCR is also 
required to recognize the MHC molecule it is bound to. Hence, when undergoing thymic selection, T 
cells must recognize the MHC of the individual. 
 
1.1.4 MHC class I molecules  
MHC-I molecules are expressed on the surface of all nucleated cells and enables them to display 
fragments of peptides which are processed inside the cells. The MHC-I molecule comprises a three-
domain a chain which is anchored to the cell membrane and binds non-covalently to b2 microglobulin 
[1]. The membrane-distal a1 and a2 domains fold into a single peptide-binding groove closed in both 
ends, which restricts the peptide length to of 8-11 amino acids [24]. Peptides that are presented on 
MHC-I originates from the cytosolic compartment and are initially processed before being presented 
on the cell surface. Proteasomal degradation of intracellular proteins into smaller fragments is a 
continuing process in all cells, and peptides are subsequently transported into the endoplasmic 
reticulum [1]. Here, the peptides are loaded into the peptide-binding grove before the complex is 
transported to the cell surface. 
 
==PAGE:15==
 
 
14 
I humans, the MHC is also called human leucocyte antigen (HLA). The MHC-I a chain is expressed 
in three different alleles (HLA-A, HLA-B, and HLA-C), which are all highly polymorphic [25]. Thus, 
an individual can express up six different class I HLA types. Each individual HLA type carry different 
ability to bind different antigens, which is governed by the anchoring residues in the binding groove. 
 
1.2 Cancer immunology 
When the immune system fights cancer, it is the innate immune cells residing in the tissue that 
initiates the response. Upon activation from the tumor microenvironment (TME), the tissue resident 
macrophages kill and phagocytose cancer cells, and recruits additional immune cells by secretion 
of cytokines [26]. NK cells will kill cancer cells due to deficient MHC-I presentation, which is a 
common immune-escape mechanism [27]. The tissue resident DCs phagocytose dying cancer cells 
and begin to migrate to the tumor-draining lymph nodes and cross-present the internalized cancer 
epitopes on MHC-I to CD8 T cells. The CD8 T cells that recognize the epitopes become activated 
effector cells, divide, and enters the circulatory system until reaching the site of the cancer cells 
where they can eliminate the malignant cells [4].  
 
1.2.1 Tumor antigens 
The peptides that are presented on MHC-I of normal cells are self-antigens from the host proteome, 
which are not recognized by mature T cells. However, in a virally infected cell or a cancer cell, the 
MHC-bound peptides are dissimilar to self-antigens and mark the cells for destruction when 
recognized by cytotoxic CD8 T cells. The antigens displayed on cancer cells, are generally divided 
into tumor-antigens of high and low tumor specificity [7]. Antigens of high tumor specificity include 
antigens whose amino acid sequence is derived from somatic mutations in the cancer cell. The 
mutation enhances either binding affinity to the MHC molecule, or to a TCR and thereby give rise to 
a new antigen (neoantigen) different from the host’s self-antigens. Another type of highly tumor-
specific antigen are antigens derived from oncogenic viruses whose viral proteins are more dissimilar 
to self-antigens compared to the neoantigens. Moreover, oncoviral antigens are also shared 
between patients, which makes them interesting targets for immunotherapy. Similarly, antigens of 
low tumor specificity are also shared between patients, but are not exclusively expressed in cancer 
cells, but are for example inherent proteins that are overexpressed in the cell and subsequently 
presented on MHC-I more frequently, which can activate an immune response [28].

1.2.2 Immune suppression in the tumor microenvironment 
Although the immune system is able to recognize and eliminate cancer cells, some cancer cells that 
are not eliminated can instead escape the immune surveillance and develop a tumor. Cancer cells 
employ different escape mechanisms such as hiding their MHC-I antigen presentation to avoid T cell 
recognition, expressing co-inhibitory molecules such as programmed death-ligand 1 (PD-L1) or 
cytotoxic T-lymphocyte-associated protein 4 (CTLA-4), or by inducing an immunosuppressive 
microenvironment [29]. The TME is made up of the immune cells, stromal cells, extracellular matrix, 
signaling molecules, as well as lymphatic and vascular vessels surrounding and infiltrating the tumor. 
The tumor interacts closely with the TME, which collectively influence the tumor progression [30]. 
Cytokines and growth factors secreted by the tumor itself largely regulates the immune cells and can 
recruit immunosuppressive cells. Thus, anti-inflammatory cytokines and metabolites are in excess 
and can hinder effector T cells in infiltrating the tumor, also known as a “cold tumor”. On the other 
hand, “hot” tumors are highly inflammatory and characterized by high infiltration level of effector CD8 
T cells [31]. Although, tumors are known to develop in chronically inflamed tissue, “hot” tumors are 
still more disposed to respond well to immunotherapy [32], [33]. 
 
1.2.3 T cell exhaustion 
In chronic infection and cancer, T cells are persistently exposed to antigens and chronic stimulation, 
which causes them to become ‘exhausted’ [34]. T cell exhaustion is thought to be an evolutionary 
mechanism which enables the cells to adapt to the chronic antigen stimulation without causing 
immunopathology [16]. Compared to the effector or memory T cells, exhausted T cells (TEX) show 
less effector function such as decreased secretion of cytokines, high expression of inhibitory 
receptors, less proliferative capacity and an altered transcription profile caused by the transcriptional 
factors; TOX and T cell factor 1 (TCF-1). Specifically, a progenitor exhausted T cell subset (TPEX) 
has recently been defined by expression of programmed cell death-1 (PD-1) and TCF-1 (figure 1.1) 
[34]. This progenitor is capable of self-renewal and is believed to be responsible for the response 
obtained by immune checkpoint blockade therapy [35]. The progenitor gives rise to the terminally 
exhausted T cells that are defined by markers such as PD-1+, TCF-1-, Eomes+, CD39+. Currently, 
it is discussed whether TEX arise from the memory or effector T cells, or directly from the naïve T 
cells upon antigen stimulation [16]. However, studies in mice have shown that TEX are not derived 
from effector cells, and that TCF-1 expression drives T cell exhaustion at an early stage in chronic 
infection [36].

1.2.4 Myeloid derived suppressor cells 
The differentiation of myeloid progenitor cells into DCs, macrophages and granulocytes is impaired 
during chronic inflammation, which is characteristic for the TME. This lack of differentiation leads to 
an accumulation of immature myeloid derived cells that have immunosuppressive capacities. These 
cells are known as myeloid derived suppressor cells (MDSC) and their presence in tumors is 
associated with poor treatment outcomes in patients [38]. The MDSCs exist in two main 
subpopulations. The monocytic M-MDSC and the polymorphonuclear PMN-MDSCs which are 
phenotypically closely related with monocytes and the granulocytic cells respectively. In humans, 
the M-MDSCs are described as CD14+, HLA-DR-, CD11b+, CD33+, CD15- and PMN-MDSC as 
CD14-, HLA-DR-, CD11b+, CD33+, CD15+ [39]. The TME harbors many inflammatory factors that 
contribute to the recruitment of MDSCs, including IL-6, IL-1b and colony-stimulating factors [40]. 
Various immunosuppressive functions of the MDSCs have been reported; by secretion of IL-10 and 
TGF-b, the MDSCs induce the generation of immunosuppressive regulatory T cells and M2 
macrophages. They are also involved in impairment of T cells homing to the site of inflammation, by 
facilitating downregulation of L-selectin and are involved in impairment T cell activity by expression 
of IL-10, TGFb and production of reactive oxygen and nitrogen species [40], [41]. Thus, targeting 
MDSC may improve the immune response against cancer. 
 
1.3 Cervical cancer 
Cervical cancer is the fourth most common cancer type in women. Globally, the disease gives rise 
to more than 560,000 new incidences and more than 310,000 deaths every year [42]. In the Nordics 
alone, more than 1,300 cases of cervical cancer are detected each year, besides the many 
precancerous lesions  that require surgical  intervention [43]. The primary cause of cervical cancer 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
17 
is human papillomavirus (HPV) infection, as DNA sequences from the high-risk types are found in 
virtually all incidences [44]. Although vaccines for protection against high-risk HPVs have been 
launched, cervical cancer remain a great challenge for women with persistent chronic infection 
whose immune systems are not able to combat the viral infection. 
 
1.3.1 Human papillomavirus 
HPVs are a group of viruses that infect human squamous epithelia. More than 200 different 
genotypes have so far been identified, of which twelve types (16, 18, 31, 33, 35, 39, 45, 51, 52, 56, 
58 and 59) are carcinogenic and associated with cancer of the cervix, male and female genitalia and 
oropharynx, and are thus characterized as high-risk HPV types [45]. Other HPV types are classified 
as being low-risk and can cause benign diseases such as genital warts. Especially HPV type 16 and 
18 are highly associated with cervical cancer and account for approximately 70% of cervical cancer 
incidences [44], [46]. 
 
HPVs are non-enveloped DNA viruses with similarly structured genomes. The genomes contain 8 
kb of doubled stranded DNA which is divided into two primary domains. Early genes (E1, E2, E4, 
E5, E6, and E7) that take part in the replication and transcription processes are located in the early 
region (E). The late region (L) encodes the major and minor capsid proteins (L1 and L2) necessary 
for the assembly of new virions [44].  
 
Infectious cycle 
Skin-skin contact is required for the transmission of HPV; hence the main route of genital infection 
is through sexual contact [44]. In women, the HPV life cycle starts in the stratified squamous epithelia 
of the uterine cervix. Initially, the virus infects the basal cells of the epithelial layer that are located 
on the basal membrane. The basal cells are capable of proliferating and will develop into mature 
epithelial cells and move upwards towards the lumen of the genital tract, where they are eventually 
shed. Small abrasions in the epithelial layer compromises the barrier and gives HPV access to 
adhering on the basal membrane, from where it infects the basal cells once the lesion repairs [45]. 
When infecting the basal cells, the HPV genome is kept at a low copy number and express only low 
levels of protein. The early genes are initially expressed at low levels to maintain autonomous 
replication of the genome [47]. Initiation of late gene expression requires signals related to the 
terminal differentiation of the epithelial cells. When the epithelial cells start to differentiate, the viral 
genomes reach thousands of copies per cell and to express late genes. Thus, the capsid proteins 
are expressed when the epithelial cells have reached the upper part of the stratified cell layer. Here, 
the virions assemble and are released from the external surface (figure 1.2) [48]. By employing this 
==PAGE:19==
 
 
18 
mechanism of viral replication, HPV avoid early exposure to the immune system [49]. The delayed 
expression of viral proteins and assembly makes it easier to evade T cell and antibody responses. 
From the time of infection, the infectious cycle takes approximately 2-3 weeks, corresponding to the 
time required for a basal cells to terminally differentiate [44].

1.3.2 Cervical intraepithelial neoplasia and cancer 
The cervix is the lower part of the uterus that extends into the vagina. The cervical canal in the 
interior part of the cervix and is divided into two regions: The endocervix and the ectocervix (figure 
1.3) [50]. The endocervix is the part closest to the uterus and is lined by mucus-producing columnar 
epithelial cells. Below, the ectocervix is lined with stratified squamous epithelia that continues into 
the vagina. The endocervix and ectocervix meet in the squamocolumnar junction, also known as the 
transformation zone, which is specifically susceptible to HPV infection [51].  
 
 
Figure 1.3: Anatomy of the uterine cervix.  
==PAGE:20==
 
 
19 
 
The HPV early proteins E6 and E7 that are expressed in the host cells during infection are known to 
be oncoproteins. Their role is linked to inhibiting the act of the tumor suppressors p53 and Rb, by 
targeting them for degradation. With this, E6 and E7 promote proliferation of the epithelial cells and 
reduces apoptosis. Thus, persistent infection can lead to an accumulation of DNA damages that can 
result in dysplastic transformations of the cells, also denoted as cervical intraepithelial neoplasia 
(CIN) [47].  
 
CIN is divided into different stages dependent on how much of the epithelium is affected and 
correlates with increasing risk of the neoplasia developing into cancer [44]. Grade 1 involves the 
lower one-third of the epithelium, grade 2 involves two-thirds, and grade 3 involves almost the entire 
thickness of the epithelial layer. Development of CIN2/3 is almost excluded to high-risk HPV infection 
and the timespan from detecting a high-risk HPV infection until this stage is 3-5 years [52]. 
Progression from dysplasia to invasive cervical cancer is however limited to 30-40% of CIN3 cases 
and can take additional 10-20 years to develop [53], [54]. When a tumor develops, the cancerous 
cells escapes the basement membrane and invade the cervical tissue, and from here it can spread 
to neighboring urogenital tissue and via the lymphatic and circulatory system, the cancer cells can 
spread and form metastases in other areas of the body.  
 
More than 80% of women will acquire an HPV infection throughout their life-time [55]. However, most 
HPV infections and low-grade CIN are transient and resolve due to cell-mediated immunity and 
sometimes an additional antibody response to the major capsid protein L1 [44]. In fact, 90% of all 
HPV infections cannot be detected 1-2 years after of the acquisition, either because of viral clearance 
by the immune system or because of viral latency [56]. Nevertheless, this is not a guarantee against 
future HPV infection, since latent HPV virus can be reactivated and immunity from re-infection in 
virally cleared individuals not guaranteed [56]. Within the group of women infected with high-risk 
HPV, 27% have persistent infections, that can still be detected two years after the first positive test. 
Persistent HPV infection is a factor that increases the risk of pre-cancerous lesions to develop [56], 
[57].  
 
In Denmark, women aged 23-59 years are offered a liquid-based cytology (LBC) test to screen for 
cervical intraepithelial neoplasia and cervical cancer every 3-5 years [58]. The test is done using a 
brush, that collects cells from the transformation zone which are then examined with computer-aided 
microscopy [59]. In the case of low-grade CIN1/2, the patient will receive additional follow-up 
screening, whereas high-grade CIN3 will be treated with a conization procedure, which entails

surgical removal of the transformation zone and part of the endocervix. Cervical cancer is confirmed 
by a biopsy, and is treated by surgery, radiation or chemotherapy depending on the advancement 
of the cancer. 
 
1.3.3 Mucosal immunity of the cervix 
The cervix is composed of two dominant tissues that are separated by the basement membrane in 
both the endo- and ectocervix regions. One is the epithelial cells that reside on top of the basement 
membrane, the other is stromal cells that are located underneath. The stromal tissue consists of 
extracellular matrix with a mixture of fibroblasts, immune cells scattered in between and a layer of 
smooth muscle cells [60]. The complexity of the immune response is orchestrated by the immune 
cells in collaboration with the epithelial cells that primarily act as the first line of defense protecting 
the reproductive tract against pathogens with their physical barrier, mucus production, and excretion 
of antimicrobial peptides [61]. Moreover, the epithelial cells aid the recruitment of immune cells upon 
recognition of infectious agents through their inherent expression of various pattern recognition 
receptors and secretion of cytokines [60]. Hence, the underlying tissue resident immune cells initiate 
innate and subsequent adaptive immune responses. 
 
HPV infection and cervical cancer is located in the transformation zone that lies interspaced between 
the endo- and ectocervix. Thus, this zone is considered to be an immunocompromised area [44]. 
The immune response in this area of the reproductive tract is complicated by the apparent need for 
protection against infections while simultaneously having to tolerate the presence of commensal 
microbes [62]. 
 
Being more exposed to the external environment, the lower part of the reproductive tract is largely 
associated with the presence of microbes, which is distinct from the upper part that is regarded 
“sterile”. Thus, the ecto- and endocervix are characterized by different distribution of immune cell 
subsets [63]. For instance, the ectocervix has a higher density of macrophages, T cells and B cells 
than the endocervix. Generally, T cells are more abundant in the cervix than B cells, which is 
consistent with other mucosal tissues [62]. Specific CD8 T cell recognition of HPV proteins E2, E6 
and E7 has previously been reported in HPV16 and HPV18 infected individuals [64]. Despite the 
presence of such HPV-specific T cells, the immune system regularly fails at eliminating tumor growth, 
suggesting an immunosuppressive TME in cervical cancer patients.

2 Aims 
 
Cervical cancer develops after many years of persistent infection from high-risk HPV types. The 
state of chronic infection enables the epithelial cells of the cervix to transform into neoplasia and in 
some cases cancer. In this project, the overall goal was to investigate and evaluate the immune 
response towards HPV-driven CIN3 and cervical cancer, and to investigate how possible changes 
in the immune compartments correlate with the progression of the disease. 
 
Aim 1: Immunophenotype analysis 
In order to achieve this overall goal, the first aim of the project was focused on investigating the 
alteration in immune compartment of patients diagnosed with CIN3 or cervical cancer compared to 
healthy women. In doing so, different sample material (blood, biopsy and liquid-based cytology) was 
analyzed with multicolor flow cytometry, which also provided a deeper insight into the immune 
comparison of different sample material. 
 
Aim 2: T cell recognition 
The aim of second part of the project was to investigate which HPV-derived epitopes are recognized 
by the host’s CD8 T cells. Moreover, we wanted to investigate if T cell recognition is linked to the 
disease progression, by analyzing blood samples from the same cohort. T cell recognition was 
evaluated by using high-throughput DNA-barcoded multimerized MHC molecules bound to specific 
epitopes derived from the HPV proteome.

3 Materials and methods 
This section describes the methods used for obtaining the results presented in this thesis. Part of 
the experimental work was mostly done before the initiation of the master’s project, namely sample 
collection and cell isolation, epitope prediction and generation of the multimer library.  
 
3.1 Patient material 
Peripheral blood samples, cervical biopsies and liquid-based cytology (LBC) samples were collected 
from a cohort of Danish women including healthy donors, patients diagnosed with CIN3 and patients 
diagnosed with cervical cancer at various disease stages. Healthy donor samples were collected at 
Aleris-Hamlet Private Hospital, Denmark, from women who underwent hysterectomy surgery for 
reasons unrelated to HPV. Healthy donors with a history of cervical dysplasia were excluded from 
the study. CIN3 patient samples were collected at Hvidovre Hospital, Denmark, and cancer patient 
sample were collected at Odense University Hospital, Denmark. From all donors and patients, both 
blood samples, liquid-based cytology samples and biopsies were collected. No cancer patient had 
received any cancer therapy prior to the sample collection. Patients were excluded from the study if 
they were on immunosuppressive medication or received treatment for other cancer types.  
 
3.1.1 Blood samples 
Peripheral blood samples were collected in 10 ml Vacutainer heparin tubes and kept at room 
temperature (RT) up to 6 hours before cell isolation. To separate the buffy coat from the erythrocytes, 
the blood samples were poured into 50 ml blood separation filter tubes (Falcon) saturated with 15 
ml Lymphoprep density gradient medium (STEMCELL, cat. 07851), and phosphate saline buffer 
(PBS) was added for a final volume of 50 ml. After centrifugation at 1000 G at RT for 10 min, the 
buffy coats were collected into 50 ml tubes, washed with PBS, centrifuged 1500 rpm at 4°C for 5 
min. Afterwards the cells were resuspended in 10 ml PBS, transferred to a new 15 ml tube and 
counted on a NucleoCounter (Chemometec).  
In order to determine HLA type, 2·106 cells in suspension per donor/patient was transferred to an 
Eppendorf tube and centrifuged at 10.000 G for 5 min. The supernatant was discarded, and the 
sample sent for HLA typing at IMGM Laboratories GmbH, Germany.  
After additional centrifugation of the remaining cell suspension, the cells were resuspended in 1 
ml of freezing media (fetal calf serum (FCS) + 10% dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) per vial of cells (5-
10·10^6 cells/vial) and distributed in cryotubes. The vials were slowly frozen down by 1°C/min in 
==PAGE:24==
 
 
23 
freezing boxes placed at -80°C. Next day, the vials were transferred to a -180°C nitrogen tank for 
long-term storage until used for analysis.  
 
3.1.2 Biopsies 
Biopsies from the transformation zone of the cervix were collected from each participant. The 
biopsies were minimum 2 x 2 mm and kept on ice in 7 ml digestion buffer (1:10 Hank’s Balanced 
Saline Solution (HBSS) 50X + 15 mM 4-(2-hydroxyethyl)-1-piperazineethanesulfonic acid (HEPES)) 
until cell isolation that was done the same day as the sample collection. Using scalpel and tweezer, 
the biopsies were cut into smaller pieces which were transferred to gentleMACS C-tubes and 
centrifuged for homogenization for 1 min using gentleMACS Dissociator (Miltenyi Biotec). To avoid 
cell clumping, 2.5 ml DNase I (Sigma-Aldrich) was added to the samples. After 1-hour incubation at 
37°C, the samples were further dissociated with gentleMACS for 1 min. The suspension was then 
filtered through a cell strainer, centrifuged at 1500 rpm, in RT for 5 min, resuspended in PBS and 
counted. From this step the cells were treated as described for the blood samples. 
 
3.1.3 Liquid-based cytology samples 
Liquid-based cytology (LBC) samples were collected from each participant using the Cervex-Brush 
(Rovers) to collect cells from both the endo- and ectocervix. The brushes were kept on ice in 15 ml 
digestion buffer (1:10 HBSS 50X + 15 mM HEPES) until cell isolation that was done the same day 
as the sample collection. To isolate the cells, the brushes were rinsed, and the suspension filtered 
through a cell strainer and treated the same way as described for the biopsies. 
 
3.1.4 HPV genotyping 
The HPV statuses of all healthy donors and patients enrolled in the study were analyzed from 
additional LBC samples. These brushes were kept at RT in 10 ml SurePath Collection Vials (BD). 
Samples were sent to Department of Pathology, Hvidovre Hospital, Denmark, for HPV typing with 
Anyplex II HPV28 detection real-time PCR or BD Max DNA extraction, which can detect 19 high-
risk and 9 low-risk HPV types.

3.3 Detection of antigen-specific T cells  
Several MHC-based technologies for detection of antigen-specific T cells has been developed, 
mainly utilizing tetramerization of peptide-MHC (pMHC) molecules for sufficient stabilization between 
the low affinity TCR-pMHC interaction. These complexes have been detected via a combination of 
fluorescent or metal label [66]–[68]. However, the methods provide a limitation in the number of 
pMHC complexes that can be screened in parallel. In this project, an epitope screening of HPV 
antigens was made using a novel method that was developed in the group. By multimerizing pMHC 
molecules on polysaccharide backbones, the avidity of the interaction with the T cell increases. 
Additionally, the method enables high-throughput screening of more than 1000 pMHC specificities 
in parallel by adding unique DNA labels (“barcodes”) to each pMHC specificity (figure 3.1) [69].

sorted, and afterwards, the DNA barcodes are amplified and sequenced. This provides specific information 
about the epitopes and HLA-types that was recognized by the T cells.

3.3.3 DNA barcodes 
Unique double-stranded DNA barcodes had previously been designed in the group by combination 
of partly complimentary single-stranded oligos (A and B). After annealing and elongation of an A and 
B oligo, a single DNA barcode contains: 
• 
Two 25mer sequences, one from each oligo, which combined allows recognition of the 
specific AxBy barcode.  
• 
Biotin (B) for conjugation to the dextran backbone 
• 
A forward primer region (FR) and a reverse primer region (RR) 
• 
An annealing region (AR) 
• 
Two unique molecular identifiers (UMIs) which vary within the group of otherwise identical 
oligos. UMIs are needed for clonal reduction, i.e., to determine the number of DNA barcodes 
originating from a single TCR-pMHC interaction after DNA amplification.  
By combining A and B oligos with different 25mer sequences, a large library of (AxBy) DNA barcode 
can be generated. The DNA barcodes serve as unique identifiers for each pMHC specificity and are 
conjugated to the dextran backbone via a biotin-streptavidin interaction.

annealed and elongated to generate a large library of unique barcodes. Figure adapted from Bentzen 
et al. (2016) [69].

control epitope library) and conjugated to streptavidin. Initially, the plated DNA barcodes were 
thawed on ice and briefly centrifuged. DNA barcodes (final concentration of 17.8 mM) were mixed 
with dextran backbone (final concentration of 35 mM) in a 384-well plate and incubated 30 min at 
4°C.

3.3.5 UV-exchange of pMHC 
Recombinant monomeric MHC class I molecules were produced in E. coli and after purification they 
were refolded with UV-cleavable peptides (p*) specific to the HLA and biotinylated. In order to 
customize the pMHC library with HPV epitopes, a final concentration of 150 µM of each predicted 
peptide (Pepscan) was mixed with 75 µg/ml of their respected p*MHC complex in a 384-well plate. 
The plate was placed in 366 nm UV light for 1 hour at room temperature to exchange the UV-
cleavable peptide with the predicted epitopes (pMHC). 
 
3.3.6 Dextran-MHC assembly 
The exchanged pMHC monomers were multimerized by conjugation to the DNA barcode and 
fluorochrome-labelled dextran backbones. After centrifugation of the monomers (3300 g, 4°C, 5 min), 
to avoid aggregates, the supernatants were mixed with the dextran backbones in the 384-well plate 
and incubated for 30 min at 4°C. Afterwards, 18.7% freezing buffer (barcode-cytometry buffer (BCB: 
PBS + 0.5% bovine serum albumin (BSA) + 100 µg/ml herring DNA + 2 mM EDTA) + D-Biotin + 5% 
glycerol) was added. The plate was incubated on ice for 20 min before storing at -20°C. 
 
3.3.7 Multimer staining 
The barcoded multimer plates were thawed on ice and centrifuged (3300 g, 4°C, 5 min). For each 
sample to be stained, 1.5 µl of each pMHC-multimer was collected and pooled in a reservoir pre-
saturated with PBS + 2% BSA. To sediment any aggregates, the multimers were transferred to an 
Eppendorf tube and centrifuged (10,000 g, 2 min). To increase the multimer concentration, the 
supernatant was transferred to centrifugal concentrators (Vivaspin6, 100kDa, Satorius) pre-
saturated with 2 % BSA solution and washed twice in PBS. The centrifugal concentrators were 
centrifuged (3000 g, 4°C) until a volume equivalent to 80 µl per staining was left in the column. The 
concentrated pool of multimers were transferred to an Eppendorf tube and centrifuged to sediment 
aggregates (10,000 g, 2 min) and transferred to another Eppendorf tube. 5 µl of the multimer pool 
was aliquoted for baseline samples and stored at -20°C. Meanwhile, cell samples were thawed in 
10 ml RPMI+10%FCS, plated in a 96-well plate and washed twice in BCB, leaving 20 µl BCB in each 
sample. 5 µl of 1µM dasatinib and 80 µl of the multimer pool was added to each cell sample in the 
96-well plate, which was then incubated at 37°C for 15 min. Addition of the protein kinase inhibitor 
dasatinib reduces the TCR internalization and optimizes the pMHC-multimer staining [75]. 
Subsequently, the cells were stained with antibodies as previously described for phenotype analysis.

3.3.9 DNA barcode amplification and purification 
DNA barcodes were amplified by polymerase chain reaction (PCR). A common reverse primer (Rx) 
was added to all samples, whereas the forward primers (Fx) added to each individual cell sample 
contained a specific sampleID in the the 5’-overhang, which enabled multiplexing of the samples 
when sequencing. The overhang in both Fx and Rx also contain an adapter sequence for the 
sequencing platform.

Barracoda data analysis 
The DNA barcode sequence data was analyzed by the Barracoda 1.8 server [76]. For each 
sequence read, Barracoda identifies the sequences of the forward primer, annealing region and 
reverse primer and filters out any read which does not contain at least two of these sequences. The 
quantity of each DNA barcode sequence is counted and assigned to each sample identified by the 
Fx sampleID. To avoid amplification bias, clonal reduction is also performed using UMI reads.

additional statistical analyses unless otherwise stated.

4 Results 
In order to analyze and compare the immune profile and T cell recognition in healthy women with 
that of CIN3 patients and cervical cancer patients, we enrolled a total of 35 women in the study. In 
total 10 healthy donors, 10 CIN3 patients and 15 cervical cancer patients were included.  
 
The age distribution differed among the three groups, where the youngest women were found among 
the CIN3 patients and the eldest among the cancer patients (figure 4.1a). However, this age 
difference is not surprising due to the fact that CIN3 is discovered early among Danish women who 
enter the national screening program, and that cervical cancer can take additional 10-20 years to 
develop [55]. 
 
Overall, 11 different types of HPV infection (i.e., 16, 18, 33, 39, 45, 51, 52, 53, 61, 70, 82) were 
detected among all participants (table 4.1). Out of these, only HPV type 53, 61 and 82 are not 
classified as high-risk types of HPV associated with cervical cancer, though type 53 and 61 are 
reported to be linked to CIN [77]. The main part of the healthy controls was tested negative for HPV, 
whereas the vast majority of CIN3 and cancer patients were positive (figure 4.1b). The prevalence 
of HPV infection was highest among the cancer patients, and among these 60% of were tested 
positive for HPV16. On the other hand, the HPV18 was only detected in a few of the CIN3 and cancer 
patients.

4.1 Immunophenotyping  
 
4.1.1 Myeloid phenotypes 
In order to evaluate the immunophenotypes of healthy women and compare it to those who has been 
diagnosed with CIN3 or cervical cancer, we initially wanted to analyze the frequencies of myeloid 
cells orchestrating the innate immune response present in blood and the cervical tissue. Thus, the 
cells were stained with antibodies targeting surface markers and analyzed using multicolor flow 
cytometry (table 3.2). By doing this, it was possible to identify subsets of monocytes (or 
macrophages), MDSCs, and DCs.  
 
Figure 4.2 shows the gating strategy that was employed in both blood and tissue samples. All 
myeloid subsets were defined as CD45+ and Lin- (CD3, CD19, CD56) to include leukocytes while 
excluding T cells, B cells and NK cells respectively. Monocytes were divided into classical (HLA-
DR+ CD14+ CD16- CD64+), intermediate (HLA-DR+ CD14+ CD16+) and non-classical monocytes 
(HLA-DR+ CD14- CD16+). DCs were defined as mDC (HLA-DR+ CD14- CD16- CD33+ CD11b+) 
and pDC (HLA-DR+ CD14- CD16- CD33- CD11b- CD123+, CD11c-). The MDSCs (including 
granulocytes) were defined as HLA-DR- CD14+/- CD33+ CD11b+ cells and divided into PMN-MDSC 
(CD15+) and M-MDSC (CD15-). 
 
==PAGE:38==
 
 
37 
 
Figure 4.2: General gating strategy for myeloid cells. The gating strategy was made to distinguish between 
M-MDSC, PMN-MDSC, pDC, mDC and classical, intermediate, and non-classical monocytes/macrophages. 
 
 
4.1.2 Myeloid cells in the blood 
Out of all immune cells in the peripheral blood (CD45+), the cancer patients had higher levels of both 
classical and intermediate monocytes compared to the CIN3 (statistically significant) and healthy 
groups (figure 4.3). Given their association with pro-inflammatory immune response, this suggests 
that the chronic inflammation has become systemic in the cancer patients.  
 
In the population of gated PMN-MDSCs we saw an increase within the cancer group compared to 
the CIN3 group (figure 4.3). Supposing these cells have immunosuppressive function, like true PMN-
MDSCs, there could be reason to assume that the cancer patients also harbor some 
immunosuppressive cells to counterbalance the pro-inflammatory anti-tumor environment. It is 
==PAGE:39==
 
 
38 
important to note that MDSCs are generally not present in healthy individuals. Still, we were able to 
detect MDSCs in in all groups. This could be an indication that our gated population might not only 
comprise true MDSC but also other myeloid cells that are phenotypically closely related like 
neutrophils, eosinophils, and basophils. Thus, we renamed the PMN-MDSC population PMN/PMN-
MDSC. In order to verify the nature of the gated MDSCs, further analysis is needed to elucidate their 
actual function and cytokine release profile [79].  
 
Among the DC populations, no significant difference was seen between the groups when looking at 
the frequency of all leucocytes (figure 4.3). However, when looking at the frequencies of DCs within 
the Lin- myeloid cells only, a clear difference was seen showing a significantly lower level of mDCs 
in the cancer patients and a significantly higher level of pDCs in the CIN3 group (appendix 8.2). The 
role of pDCs is largely linked to anti-viral immunity, because they produce large amounts of type I 
IFNs upon viral infection [80]. Thus, their presence in CIN3 patients indicates an ongoing anti-viral 
immune response, which is in consistency with the fact that these patients generally had current, 
active HPV infection.  
 
The gated myeloid subsets were also visualized by UMAP dimensionality reduction (figure 4.4). It 
was obvious that the CIN3 group differed from the other groups in regard to the PMN/PMN-MDSC. 
Here, fewer cells were present in the CIN3 group, which was in accordance with the trend of lower 
PMN/PMN-MDSC among the myeloid cells (figure 4.3). The gating strategy used for the myeloid 
cells did not entail all myeloid cells, which was also evident from the grey ungated areas in the UMAP 
plot (figure 4.4a). Additionally, the heatmaps also revealed than some variation of surface markers 
within the defined population, especially for the classical monocytes (figure 4.4c). 
 
In total, the circulating myeloid cells showed mainly a phenotypic difference between the CIN3 and 
cancer patients, suggesting a systemic anti-viral innate immune response in CIN3 patients and a 
pro-inflammatory immune profile in the cancer patients.

4.1.3 Myeloid cells in biopsies 
The immune profile of the investigated myeloid cells was somewhat different when looking at the 
cervical tissue. In contrast to the systemic circulation, tissue harbors both monocytes that have 
recently been recruited as well as monocyte-derived macrophages which are often very 
heterogeneous [81]. M1 macrophages develop in highly inflamed environments, where they secrete 
pro-inflammatory cytokines and assist in the anti-tumor immunity. M2 macrophages are oppositely 
involved in anti-inflammatory responses [82]. In order to accurately distinguish macrophages from 
monocytes, additional surface marker staining is required. Although not consistently, M1 has 
previously been defined by markers such as CD80+ and HLA-DRhigh and M2 sometimes as CD14+, 
CD206+, CD204+ [81]. In this project, we applied the same staining and gating strategy as in the 
blood, hence we cannot fully distinguish monocytes from macrophages or be certain of their pro/anti-
inflammatory immune function. Nevertheless, in contrast to the blood classical monocytes, we did 
observe a reduction in the percentage of the CD14+ CD16- monocytes/macrophages in the cervix 
of cancer patients. Moreover, CD14-CD16+ monocytes/macrophages were absent in the cervix of 
cancer patients.

4.1.5 T cell phenotypes 
In order to evaluate the immunophenotypes of healthy women and compare it to those who has been 
diagnosed with CIN3 or cervical cancer, we also wanted to analyze the T cell subsets present in 
blood, biopsy and LBC samples as well as the characteristic differentiation markers they express. T 
cells are important mediators of cancer elimination due to their cytotoxic properties and it is thus 
important to understand how their effector function is altered in chronic infection and cancer. 
 
 
Figure 4.5: General gating strategy for T cells used for blood, biopsy and cytology samples. 
 
To do this, the cells were stained with antibodies targeting surface and intracellular markers and 
analyzed using multicolor flow cytometry (table 3.1). After acquisition on the flow cytometer, the T 
cells were gated out as shown in figure 4.5. Both CD4 and CD8 T cells were divided into naïve 
(CCR7+ CD45RA+), TCM (CCR7+ CD45RA-), TEM (CCR7- CD45RA-) and TEMRA (CCR7- CD45RA+), 
and these subsets were afterwards analyzed for expression of differentiation markers; PD-1, TOX, 
CD39, CD103, Ki67, CD27, CD57, Eomes, GZMB, TCF-1. This panel was designed to mainly 
characterize the activation/exhaustion status of CD8+ T cells, however, the CD4 T cells were also 
analyzed (appendix 8.3) but were not the main focus of this project.  
==PAGE:45==
 
 
44 
 
4.1.6 CD8 T cells in the blood 
The first samples that were analyzed for their T cell phenotypes were the blood samples. Again, the 
same healthy donors (n=10), CIN3 patients (n=10) and cancer patients (n=15) were included in the 
analysis for comparison.  
 
For the CD8 T cells, all groups had a majority of TEM, with the highest level found in the cancer 
patients (figure 4.6a). This group also had fewer naïve CD8 T cells, but since aging is associated 
with loss of naïve T cells, it might be explained by the higher mean age in the cancer group [83]. 
Interestingly, the ratio between TEMRA and TEM cells was decreasing with disease progression from 
healthy individuals to pre-cancerous CIN3 and cancer, which indicates less terminally differentiated 
effector cells (figure 4.6b).  
 
Besides looking into the different T cell subsets, we wanted to evaluate each subset’s expression of 
differentiation markers related to T cell activation and exhaustion, since exhaustion is related to 
chronic infection and cancer, where persistent antigen stimulation occurs. One of the markers of 
interest was the immune checkpoint PD-1. Normally, PD-1 is not expressed in the naïve population 
of T cells [84]. Yet, in the CCR7+ CD45RA+ population of CD8 T cells, a considerable frequency of 
PD-1 expressing cells were found. This indicates that the gated “naïve” cells might not be truly naïve 
T cells but comprise other subsets. Thus, further gating might be necessary, such as CD27+, CD57- 
and PD-1-, which has previously been described to co-define naïve T cells [85]. Instead, PD-1 
expression in T cells is induced when the TCR is activated, and the expression decreases in the 
absence of TCR signaling [86]. Thus, PD-1 expression indicates activation of the T cells. PD-1 can 
also be an indication of exhaustion; When TCR signaling is persistent, like the case in chronic 
infection and cancer, PD-1 is constitutively expressed on the antigen-specific T cells to adapt to the 
chronic stimulation [86]. Interestingly, the group of CIN3 patients was found to harbor a higher 
frequency of CD8 T cells that were expressing PD-1 in the blood (figure 4.6c). This group of CD8 T 
cells also had a clear tendency of higher CD103 expression. The integrin CD103 is important for the 
T cells’ homing to the tissues, suggesting that CD8 T cells in the circulatory system are homing to 
the cervical tissue at the site of the HPV infection in the genital tract, which has previously been 
described [87]. 
 
The transcription factor TOX has been shown to be upregulated in tumor-specific T cells where it is 
a key regulator of other exhaustion markers [88], [89]. In contrast to PD-1 and CD103, TOX was 
found to be expressed more frequently in the blood of cancer patients’ CD8 T cells compared to the 
==PAGE:46==
 
 
45 
CIN3 group. The higher frequency of TOX indicates, that the CD8 T cells in the cancer patients have 
become exhausted due to the chronic antigen stimulation throughout the preceding HPV infection, 
and possibly these dysfunctional cells have mediated the disease to progress into cancer. While the 
lower frequency of TOX+ CD8 T cells in CIN3 group compare to cancer patients indicates activation 
from recent infection.    
 
We also visualized the T cell differentiation by using UMAP. The UMAP made it possible to visualize 
populations of cells that were clustered based on their relative co-expression of the parameters they 
were stained for. At the same time, it also visualized the presence of these populations within each 
group. Specifically, a group of CD8 T cells with likely cytotoxic effector function (TEM and TEMRA) were 
identified as they co-expressed CD57 and GZMB (figure 4.7a, c). However, no major difference in 
phenotype populations was observed between the healthy controls, or the patient groups (figure 
4.7b).

4.1.7 CD8 T cells in biopsies 
Next step was to perform similar phenotype analysis in tissue samples to compare T cells in the 
blood with those found in the tissue and infiltrating the tumors. Thus, biopsies that were collected 
from the transformation zone of the cervix were analyzed with multicolor flow cytometry. This time, 
additional markers CD39, TCF-1 and Eomes were included in the staining panel. Compared to the 
circulatory system, naïve cells were hardly present in the tissue. On the other hand, the vast majority 
of cells were classified as TEM, and a smaller population of TEMRA (figure 4.8a). The ratio between 
TEMRA and TEM were consistent between the three groups (figure 4.8b).  
 
The main noticeable differences between the three groups were the frequency of CD8 T cells that 
expressed TOX, CD39 and Eomes. All of these markers were expressed at significantly higher 
frequencies in the cancer group (figure 4.8c). Tumor infiltrating immune cells have shown to express 
CD39, which is an enzyme involved in adenosine metabolism [90]. CD39 is upregulated as a 
response to tissue damage, hypoxia, chronic inflammation and is a key marker defining the terminally 
differentiated TEX phenotype associated with an upregulation of inhibitory receptors such as PD-1 
[90], [91]. Furthermore, co-expression of CD103 and CD39 defines specific tumor-infiltrating 
exhausted CD8 T cells [92]. 
 
TOX is as previously described also an important factor for T cell dysfunction and was likewise 
significantly higher expressed in the cancer group, supporting the notion of tumor infiltrating 
exhausted CD8 T cells in abundance. Also, Eomes, a T-box transcription factor, was more frequent 
in the CD8 T cells from the cancer tissue, which is also an indication of exhaustion of tumor specific 
CD8 T cells [93]. Specifically, Eomes has shown to inhibit apoptosis of T cells with low antigen-
affinity in order to maintain clonal diversity. 
 
No particular difference between healthy controls and the CIN3 patients was observed in regard to 
frequencies of the differentiation markers that were tested. This was also evident from the UMAP 
visualizations of the T cells (figure 4.9). Interestingly, the UMAP plots revealed a population of CD8 
T cells that were classified as TEM cells. In particular, the cells had a relatively high expression of 
many of the markers they were stained for: PD-1+, CD39+, CD103+, CD27+, Ki67+, Eomesint, 
TOXint. Expression of CD57 however divided these cancer-associated exhausted cells into two 
populations: CD57- (cluster 1) and CD57+ (cluster 2), which likely defines two different exhausted 
phenotypes (appendix 8.4). The two populations resemble previously reported CD8 T cell 
phenotypes in lung cancer, which were also TCF-1 absent [94], [95]. The functional difference 
between the two exhausted populations remains unknown. From inspection of the UMAP, cluster 1 
==PAGE:50==
 
 
49 
and cluster 2 appeared to be mainly found in the cancer group (figure 4.8b). By gating out the 
clusters, we found this difference to be significant (figure 4.10a). Furthermore, the proportion of the 
CD57+ cluster 2 seemed to correlate to the disease severity in the cancer group (p=0.0403) (figure 
4.10b).

4.2 T cell recognition of HPV epitopes 
The second part of the projects was aimed at understanding the T cell immunity related to HPV 
infection and disease severity. Thus, we wanted to characterize the CD8 T cell recognition of HPV 
proteins, to find relevant immunogenic epitopes. This was achieved by using DNA-barcoded pMHC 
multimers, which enables screening of multiple epitopes in parallel [69]. Thus, a large library of 1161 
different pMHC specificities was generated with peptides derived from the two most high-risk HPV 
types, 16 and 18. From each of these viruses, the early proteins E2, E6 and E7 were chosen for the 
epitope screening, as they have previously been described as immunogenic [96], [97]. The epitopes 
that were included in the library were all predicted to bind to the 14 most common HLA-I in Northern 
Europe (figure 4.14). 
 
 
Figure 4.14: Prediction of potential HPV-derived T cell epitopes. The peptides are derived from the HPV 
proteins E2 E6 and E7 from HPV16 and HPV18 and predicted to bind to different HLA types. The total number 
of different predicted pMHC specificities was 1161. 
 
For this study, healthy women (n=4), CIN3 patients (n=6) and cancer patients (n=4) that were also 
included in the phenotype analysis were chosen for comparison. Blood samples from each 
participant was initially sent for HLA typing, and each participant were afterwards only screening for 
T cell responsiveness towards pMHC matching their HLA type. Besides the predicted HPV-epitopes, 
epitopes from common viruses (EBV, CMV, Influenza (FLU)) were included as controls, as T cell 
recognition is common towards these. 
 
PBMCs were used for screening the T cell recognition. The cells were stained with the pMHC 
multimers and a phenotype antibody panel in order to determine the specific phenotypes of the 
antigen-recognizing T cells. Cells with multimers attached were sorted using FACS and underwent 
subsequent analysis to determine the origins of the bound pMHCs.

4.2.1 Impact of local immunotherapy on T cell recognition  
Besides screening our cohort for T cell recognition, we also screened two additional patients 
diagnosed with cervical cancer, who were enrolled in a clinical trial testing the efficacy of immune 
checkpoint inhibition. This was done as part of a collaboration with a research group from Vrije 
Universiteit Amsterdam, who performed the trial and collected the samples. 
 
An upregulation of PD-L1 has been reported in cervical cancer in several studies, which could make 
immune checkpoint inhibition targeting PD-L1 a promising therapeutic approach in these patients 
[100], [101]. In this phase I trial, durvalumab, an anti-PD-L1 monoclonal antibody blocking the 
interaction with PD-1, was administered as a single dose according to the protocol described by 
Rotman et al. [102]. Durvalumab was administered intratumorally (i.t.) in order to target both CD8 T 
cells infiltrating the tumor and the tumor-draining lymph nodes. Blood samples were taken prior to 
the injection and after 2 and 4 weeks and after 3 months (figure 4.19). 2 weeks after the durvalumab 
administration, the patients underwent hysterectomy, where biopsies from both primary tumor (PT) 
and lymph nodes (LN) were collected. Isolated cells from these biopsies were subsequently 
underwent rapid expansion protocol (REP).  
 
 
 
Figure 4.19: Timeline of the clinical trial testing durvalumab in cervical cancer patients. PBMCs were 
collected at Day 0 (before treatment), week 2, week 4 and after 3 months. Biopsies from tumor and lymph 
node (LN) were collected 2 weeks after treatment.  
 
 
Studying these cancer patients would provide an insight into the effect of immunotherapeutic 
treatment on T cell recognition, how it alters over time as well as a comparison of recognition by T 
cells in the circulatory system and those infiltrating the tumor.  
 
Following the same procedure as previously, PBMC, tumor and lymph node samples from two 
patients were stained with DNA-barcoded pMHC multimers from the same library in order to screen 
recognition of E2, E6 and E7 from HPV16 and HPV18. In patient DU09, who had tested positive for 
HPV16, HPV recognition by CD8 T cells was detected already at the baseline (figure 4.20), similar

5 Discussion  
The overall goal of this project was to investigate the alteration of the immune system during the 
development and progression of cervical cancer from HPV infection. Through immunophenotyping 
and DNA barcoded pMHC-multimer screening for HPV-specific T cell recognition, we were able to 
discover phenotypes of activated/exhausted T cells in the cancer patients and find specific HPV 
epitopes that were recognized in HPV infected individuals. The implications of the main results will 
be further discussed in this section. 
 
5.1 Immune infiltration in cervical cancer 
The level of immune cells infiltrating a tumor as well as the level of inflammation in the TME is likely 
to influence the clinical outcome [32], [33], [40]. When looking at the frequencies of myeloid subsets 
in cervical cancer patients, no major skewing of immune phenotypes was observed compared to 
healthy donors. However, a main observation was the different level of PMN/PMN-MDSC cells 
present in the blood of CIN3 patients and cancer patients. Additionally, the blood samples indicated 
systemic pro-inflammatory monocytes in the cancer patients and upregulation of anti-viral pDCs in 
HPV-infected CIN3 patients. Without knowing the true effects of these differences, the results 
showed that there still is some detectable difference between CIN3 and cancer.  
 
In turn, we observed a high level of myeloid and lymphoid cells infiltrating the tissue of cervical cancer 
patients based on samples collected with minimally invasive cervical brushes. In total, this implies 
an ongoing immune response in these patients, with a high level of immune infiltration which 
generally implies inflammation and good outcome from immunotherapy [33]. Though, further 
analyses with additional phenotypic markers are required to fully elucidate the true functional 
implications. Moreover, unsupervised gating with FlowSOM might also assist in identifying more 
specific myeloid subsets [103]. 
 
5.2 T cell exhaustion in cervical cancer 
The cytotoxic CD8 T cells are of vital importance in the clearance of viral infection as well as in the 
killing of cancerous cells expressing cancer-epitopes [15]. Nevertheless, it has become evident that 
the reason for the immune systems failure to eliminate cancers is because of the altered T cell 
phenotype and function. The immunophenotype of exhausted T cell has been increasingly in focus 
in cancer patients. Thus, in this project phenotype markers that have previously been associated 
with exhaustion and tumor-specific CD8 T cells were subject to our interest. Here, two distinct 
==PAGE:67==
 
 
66 
phenotypes were identified to be significantly overrepresented in the tissue of cervical cancer 
patients. Although heterogenous for CD57, both phenotypes expressed PD-1, CD39, CD103, TOX, 
Eomes, Ki67, indicating late stage of activation and exhaustion. One of the most surprising findings 
was the fact that TCF-1 was not expressed in either of these populations, since this transcription 
factor has been highlighted as a key indicator of a progenitor exhausted phenotype with high 
proliferative capacity and ability to respond to immune checkpoint inhibition [16], [34]–[37]. One 
explanation could be that the exhausted phenotypes are terminally differentiated, hence no longer 
expressing TCF-1. Similar findings have previously been seen in lung cancer, but to our knowledge 
it has not previously been described in cervical cancer [94]. 
 
Nevertheless, in order to establish further evidence of the exhaustion phenotypes, additional 
characteristics could be done. Co-expression of multiple inhibitory receptors such as PD-1, TIM3, 
LAG3 and CTLA4 is associated to more severe exhaustion, which is why these could be future 
targets of interest [104]. Moreover, it would be interesting to further investigate the difference 
between the CD57+ and CD57- exhausted phenotypes in terms of functionality. Traditionally, CD57 
has been reported to define T cells as terminally differentiated senescent cells, but not necessarily 
as exhausted cells, because CD57+ CD8 T cells can also be highly cytotoxic [20], [105]. To 
investigate the question of functionality further, it could be relevant to perform a cell cytotoxicity 
assay, intracellular cytokine staining or even single cell RNA sequencing. 
 
Besides the fact that the exhausted phenotypes were more abundant in the cancer patients, the 
proportion of these cells also showed a tendency to correlate to the severity of the cancer. Surely a 
larger cohort of patients are required to validate this, but it is an indication that the dysfunctional 
exhausted phenotypes, in particularly the CD57+ population, play a role in the disease development, 
and correlates with higher cancer stage in untreated individuals with cervical cancer. However, the 
causal relation of these cells with cancer needs further investigation.  
 
The CIN3 patients have likely been infected with HPV for a prolonged period of time before the 
epithelial cells turned dysplastic, why the persisting HPV infection leading to the development of CIN 
is sometimes defined as a chronic infection. However, our results show that the exhausted 
phenotypes have not yet fully manifested in these patients, because no significant difference was 
observed between these patients and the healthy controls who were mainly HPV negative. Only few 
of the patients diagnosed with CIN3 displayed a phenotypic shift towards more activated/exhausted 
CD8 T cell phenotypes. Since it is not all CIN3 cases but rather 30-40% that progress into cancer if 
==PAGE:68==
 
 
67 
left untreated, the presence of the exhausted phenotypes could be a prognostic marker indicating 
who are not able to clear the infection and the neoplasia themselves [53]. 
 
Given the identification of similar CD8 T cell population exhibiting exhaustion in both biopsies and 
the cytology samples, it is presumably feasible to use the less invasive cytology brush for collecting 
immune cells from the cervix for analysis in the future. The systemic immune system does however 
not provide the same image of the immune profile in the tissue, showing the importance of tissue 
analysis in cancer patients. No apparent co-expression of activation/exhaustion markers was found 
in the PBMCs. Yet it cannot be completely excluded that a deeper analysis using unsupervised 
clustering analysis would provide additional information regarding minor phenotype differences. 
 
5.3 T cell recognition 
In regard to the barcoded pMHC-multimer screening, we expected to see a high level of T cell 
recognition of HPV epitopes. In contrast to the neoepitopes normally derived from tumor mutations, 
the viral epitopes are not similar to self-antigens, and are thus more likely to be recognized by T 
cells, as the T cells have not been negatively selected due to recognition of self (negative selection 
in thymus). 
 
In this study, we screened 4 healthy donors, 6 CIN3 patients and 4 cancer patients in total. In order 
to fully map the T cell recognition, we would ideally need to include a larger and more homogenous 
cohort, infected with the same types of HPV and expressing the same HLAs. In this way we could 
better compare the recognition from each HLA type. 
 
Nevertheless, the T cell recognition of HPV epitopes showed that the CD8 T cells from CIN3 and 
cancer patients had a tendency of recognizing more HPV-derived epitopes than healthy controls. 
This was also expected, since we had certainty that these patients were HPV positive. The healthy 
donors also showed some recognition of HPV epitopes, it is likely that some of them could have had 
previous, transient HPV infection. Vaccination against HPV is unlikely to have influenced the T cell 
recognition, since the vaccines target humoral immune responses against the major capsid protein 
L1 [106].  
 
Based on the current data we also saw a small tendency of more frequent T cell recognition of the 
E2 protein derived from HPV16, although not exclusively. Besides, patients who were not HPV 
positive for type 16 or 18, but positive for other genotypes of HPV also showed recognition of the 
==PAGE:69==
 
 
68 
screened epitopes. To elucidate this further, we would need to compare the sequence homology of 
the HPV types found in the cohort with the sequences of HPV16 and HPV18, to see if the sequences 
are identical. 
 
As an additional part of the project, we also wanted to do phenotyping of the multimer+ CD8 T cells 
by applying the same phenotype staining panel. However, we observed a large background signal 
in the PE channel disturbing the population of PE-labelled HPV-multimer+ CD8 T cells. Thus, we 
were not able to evaluate the phenotypic markers expressed by the antigen-specific T cells. If we 
were able to characterize the phenotype of the multimer+ CD8 T cells, we would expect them to 
show different phenotypes between the healthy controls, CIN3 and cancer patients. Particularly in 
the cancer patients, we would expect them to resemble the identified exhausted CD8 T cells 
phenotypes, since another study have found tumor-specific CD8 T cells to express high PD-1 and 
Ki67, no CD45RA, while being CD57 heterogeneous [94]. The HPV-specific CD8 T cells in the 
healthy and CIN3 group we would expect to be less exhausted in accordance with the phenotypes 
found in these groups. Since we were not able to detect any large phenotypic differences when 
analyzing PBMCs, this assumption might be more evident if analyzing samples from the cervical 
tissue. 
 
As previously mentioned, anti-PD-1/PD-L1 immunotherapy, which has shown promising clinical 
efficacy in various cancer types, is a potential strategy for cervical cancer patients, due to high PD-
L1 expression in the tumor [100], [101], [107]. Here, we showed that administration of durvalumab, 
an anti-PD-L1 monoclonal antibody, directly into the tumor, has a large effect on the circulating CD8 
T cells’ ability to recognize specific HPV epitopes after 3 months. The reason for this effect is 
unknown, but recent studies have shown that mDCs play an important role in anti-PD-L1 
immunotherapy, because blocking the PD-L1 signaling from the DCs enhances the priming and 
reactivation of T cells [108], [109]. This could maybe explain the effect of the anti-PD-L1 treatment. 
If the DCs located in the tumor-draining lymph nodes are more successful in T cell priming it would 
result in additional activated CD8 T cells targeting HPV. The number of HPV epitopes recognized by 
CD8 T cells after 2 weeks were equivalent in the blood, lymph node and tumor, even though we 
expected to find more tumor-specific T cells in the tumors and tumor-draining lymph nodes. Since 
only few HPV epitopes were recognized in the tumors and lymph nodes, we cannot exclude the 
impact of the rapid T cell expansion protocol on the potentially exhausted HPV-specific T cells. The 
rapid expansion of tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes entails T cell activation, which makes the cells 
proliferate. But this process can potentially skew the T cell repertoire depending on the proliferative 
==PAGE:70==
 
 
69 
capacity of the T cell clones [110]. Additionally, due to the clinical protocol, we were not able to 
compare the effect of the treatment in the tumor before and after treatment. 
 
Although we only screened two patients from the clinical trial, we did observe a small difference in 
epitope recognition between circulating and tumor or lymph node resident T cells. In both patients, 
a higher proportion of the predicted E2 was recognized, whereas the tumor and lymph node 
contained more E6 specific T cells. This might be a coincidence, but it could also indicate some 
functional differences in T cells with different specificities. Again, single cell analysis characterizing 
T cells specific to either of the proteins could be applied to investigate this question.  
 
5.4 Perspectives 
The discovery of exhausted CD8 T cells in cervical cancer was not surprising, since exhaustion is a 
common phenomenon in cancer due to the persistent antigen stimulation and inflammatory tumor 
microenvironment. Generally, exhaustion is not an inherent bad thing, as it is a mechanism 
developed to ensure that T cells can adapt to chronic antigen stimulation and prolong an immune 
response while simultaneously limiting immunopathology. The presence of a high number of innate 
and adaptive immune cells as well as a large proportion of exhausted T cells in cervical cancer 
indicates that there is a partially ongoing immune response towards the tumor. However, in order to 
eliminate the cancer, the immune system must elicit a more powerful cytotoxic response.  
 
Several reports have highlighted the high level of PD-L1 expressed in cervical tumors [100], [101], 
indicating a potential for anti-PD-1/PD-L1 immune checkpoint inhibition in these patients. Since other 
studies have pointed to the ability of progenitor exhausted T cells to respond to immune checkpoint 
inhibition, there might be a great potential in using this therapy in cervical cancer patients. 
Nonetheless, based on a lack of TCF-1 expression, the exhausted phenotypes we discovered in the 
cervical cancer patients, do not resemble the previously described TPEX with proliferative capacity 
that correlates with checkpoint inhibition efficacy [35], [37]. On that basis, it could be theorized that 
immune checkpoint inhibition such as anti-PD-1 does not work effectively in these patients. Thus, 
extended knowledge about the functionality of our exhausted T cells would be needed to elucidate 
the prospect of immune checkpoint inhibition. 
 
The findings from the epitope screening also verified that CD8 T cells are capable of recognizing 
HPV epitopes after infection. The findings from additional screenings will provide further insight to 
which epitopes are more immunogenic. In this study, we saw that some epitopes, especially derived 
==PAGE:71==
 
 
70 
from E2, were recognized in several individuals, and these could potentially be relevant in future 
vaccines or cell therapies.

6 Conclusion 
In this project, we wanted to evaluate the immune response involved in the progression from pre-
cancerous CIN3 to invasive cervical cancer. Based on the presented results, we showed that HPV-
related cervical cancer is highly related to a large degree of immune infiltration in the tumor, from 
both innate and adaptive immune cells, although the distribution of innate immune cells was not 
remarkably skewed. A large proportion of the immune cells infiltrating the tumors were characterized 
as exhausted CD8 T cells, due to their expression of known exhaustion markers. This makes the 
cytotoxic response towards the tumor less pronounced, but the presence of these exhausted cells 
could also be beneficial in eliminating the cancer, if reinvigorated by immunotherapy. The results 
also revealed that the younger CIN3 patients do not yet exhibit signs of chronic inflammation or T 
cell exhaustion, which also indicates a potential for immunotherapeutic intervention in these patients. 
We also showed that HPV infected individuals are capable of raising a CD8 T cell response towards 
HPV derived epitopes, and that some of these viral epitopes are recognized across multiple patients. 
Especially the E2 protein from HPV16 was capable of being recognized, also in individuals infected 
with different HPV types. Furthermore, the use local immune checkpoint inhibition does enhance the 
CD8 T cells’ ability to recognize HPV epitopes.
