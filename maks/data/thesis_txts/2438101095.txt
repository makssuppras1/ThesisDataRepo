Abstract Most of today’s large organization hol
ds operation all over the world. The people they employ need to be 
able to coordinate shared work between geographically distant areas. The main solution lies in 
Information Communication Technology medium which allows continuous and instant exchange of a 
wide ranges of information. The correct implementation of ICT medium can ensure good 
communication performance over space while maintaining sustainable work conditions. 
Among these technologies, telepresence robots promise to reproduce the presence of a person in 
different location from the one they physically attend. This technology could potentially improve the 
work of some of the manager leading remote teams. However, this is not always the case.  This master thesis addresses thi
For this purpose, organizational and communication literature is revised. Carayon (2009) work balance 
theory introduces the relation between individuals, task, workplace and work tools in organizational 
setting. Media Synchronicity Theory express the need to match communication media capabilities with 
intended communication processes (Dennis and Valacich, 1999).  
At the same time data are collected in four case studies in three large organizations. Each case study is 
constituted by a remote manager carrying communication tasks with his/her team in communication 
tasks. Data are collected in form of field notes, focus group interviews and observations. 
The data is combined with the revised theories to create a framework which highlights the key relation 
between the elements present in remote communication task. The framework is used to categorize 
telepresence robot user interaction data into the key work system relations. It iIs then specified if the 
different user interaction instances improve or hinders the fulfillment of the communication tasks. 
Finally, the analyzed data is synthetized into a decision support guideline. The first part of the guideline 
suggests which actions to take for evaluating the overall sustainability mobile robotic presence in distant 
management task. The second part of the guideline is aimed to identify unsuitable interface between 
telepresence robot and other element of the work system when communication is disrupted.

1 Introduction 
 “The danger of the past was that m
en became slaves. The danger of the future is th
 
-Eric Fromm 
When the German sociologist Eric Fromm (1955) wrote this sentence in the conclusion of his work The 
sane society he was reflecting on how members of modern societies are in danger of alienate themselves. 
If we reflect today on these words we can get scared by considering how technologies are becoming 
integral part of human life. Many people are excited by today’s technological innovation pace. Others, 
like Fromm, warn on the possibility to give away our humanity for the comfort provided by technologies. 
In any case, the change is real and it’s proceeding at increasingly high speed. Therefore, it becomes our 
right and duty to reflect on this transformation and on its consequences. 
This is even more important for these practicing the profession of engineers. We are the one drawing 
knowledge from different scientific fields, or making discovery ourselves, with the purpose of improving 
people lives. However, we should have also the moral duty to be critics of the science we introduce in the 
society to understand if we are really supporting humanity and the life on our planet. 
This sense of responsibility directed my attention to the increasing amount of interaction people have 
today with technologies. Some of these machines can perceive the outside world, communicate with each 
other and with us. Sometimes people accept to include these technologies in their lives because of the 
practical advantages they generate. However, this can become a problem when the choice of using a new 
technology doesn’t follow a reflection on how this influences our life.  
The same mindset can lead organizations, that are in the end a collection of people, to adopt ICT 
technologies on a wide scale. This is easily understandable since new tools allow faster and uninterrupted 
exchange and processing of information, resulting in more efficient processes and ultimately reduced 
costs. 
As consequences, many companies working with knowledge or information tries to gain competitive 
advantage by adopting several ICT. If on one side computers gathers, elaborate and exchange data on 
their own, many times they interact with humans. In other situations, technologies are used as 
==PAGE:11==

communication medium. In these instances, the communication between people is mediated by the 
computers. 
The fact that since some years is possible to purchase movable robot and use it as surrogate of our 
presence can still sound curious. Telepresence robot are machines controlled from someone using 
internet network. The person controlling the robot can see what the robot sees through a camera placed 
on it. The people looking at the robot can see the face of the person controlling it on a screen placed on 
the robot. The robot operator is visible on the screen and people can talk with her or him. The result is a 
new kind of presence that allows people to interact with others from distance and have the feeling of 
attending remote locations. It’s not fully known which are the circumstances in which 
the embodiment through robots can bring 
benefits. Technological medium provides communication capabilities, but communication, in the end, 
remains a human action. 
The possibility of being present somewhere else through a robotic surrogate rises some questions. Are 
robots starting to look more human or are the people which, interacting always more closely with 
technology, start looking as machines? Is the analysis of Fromm accurate or humanity will survive over 
its own creations? 
1.1 Research goal 
The introduction of new technologies can represent a source of disruption for many organizations. 
Telepresence robot differs from other mediums for its ambition of reproducing the feeling of human 
physical presence by using robotics. Telepresence devices allow a newel type of interaction compared to 
previous telecommunication technologies. This interaction is expected to differ significantly from the 
ones experienced with other mediums. 
Work environments are complex systems where people 
interact with each other and with physical and technological 
tools. Leavitt (1965) suggests that task, people, technologies 
and structure of organizations are interrelated. The 
modification of one or more elements affects the state of the 
others. In other words, the introduction of new technologies 
impact on the task, people and structure of the organization.  
Similarly, Carayon (2009) describes the context in which organization’s work takes place as work 
systems composed by:

• 
Environment, represented by the workplace layout. 
• 
Individuals, the people working in the organization.

Tasks, carried out in workplace by individuals.

• 
Organizational condition, policies adopted and actions taken by an organization to support its

The modification of one of these elements influence all the others. In this view, telepresence robots 
represent a possible technological tool that can be introduced in a work system with consequences on 
other elements. In other words, the use of telepresence robots can impact on the people and on the tasks 
characterizing the organizations which adopts it. 
Certainly, organizations would plan to introduce this technology only if it can give a positive 
contribution to their goals. This could happen if mobile robotic presence simplifies a task or improve its 
performance for some member of the organization. The organizational members that could benefit from 
the introduction of telepresence robot are people communicating from different geographical areas.  
Managers are the people in organization appointed to transform the organizational goals into plans and 
then actions. They do so by coordinating with other people in the organization and by deploying 
organizational resources. For this reason, I believe that the managers located remotely from teams they 
coordinate could be main beneficiary of telepresence robot technology. Some of these managers may 
improve the collaboration with their team and be more present by using telepresence robots.  
However, there are many work conditions that can make desirable or unsustainable the use of this 
technology. The introduction of mobile robotic telepresence for remote communication process can 
affect in different ways different work systems. According to Carayon (2009) these modification could 
eventually result in psychosocial, cognitive and physical loads. These loads can then have effect on employees’ stress 
level and well-being. Consequently, the adoption a new working tool must be 
considered together with the needs of the people interacting with it. 
The aim of this thesis is to investigate the impact of telepresence robot’s adoption by managers on 
the activities they perform with others from distance.  In particular, I aim to describe the effects 
on well-being of the manager as the one of the individuals of their team. For this purpose, I 
investigate the characteristics that shape the perceived usability and usefulness of this tool in 
different communication process. 
==PAGE:13==

Since managers and other members of geographically disperse teams are the potential beneficiary of the 
robot, they are the ones who evaluates it and decide if using it. In this way, the final adoption of 
telepresence robots can happen only if its users perceive it as something easy to use which help them 
doing their job (Venkatesh Viswanath ; Bala, 2008). The ease of use is the extent to which a person 
believes that using the system will be free of effort. The perceived usefulness is the extent to which a 
person believes that using the system can improve her or his job performance. These two elements 
impact on the perception of the possibility to fulfill the job by operating the robot instead of other means. 
These expectation of usefulness and ease of use of telepresence robot in remote management tasks 
constitutes the main evaluation metrics adopted by the users. Each interaction occurs in a specific 
context that must be considered to fully understand the phenomenon. Consequently, the first 
information to acknowledge is the organizational context of the companies which decide to adopt 
telepresence robot. In second place, it is worth to describe the internal processes of the remote team and 
the skillset and mindset of its members. The communication process in which the robot is used are 
defined in more detail and related to the capabilities offered by telepresence robots.  
1.2 Research themes 
In their review of virtual teams literature Gilson et. al. (2015) suggest to further investigate some themes 
related to this topic. Among these, it is mentioned the study of working behaviors with emergent 
technologies and well-being implications of distance work. These two topics are imbedded in the 
research goal of this study. However, the research goal calls for additional themes. Each theme covers a 
different aspect of the research goal. The themes suggest fields of knowledge to address for their 
understanding. These directed me toward the literature used for the theoretical framework of this thesis. 
1) Best task for telepresence robot 
The first theme expressed is the evaluation of managerial communication process that best exploits the 
robot capabilities. As every medium, the adoption of telepresence robots may be more beneficial in some 
situations compared to others. Positive or negative use outcomes are partially influenced by some extent 
by the nature of the activity performed through remote robotic presence. Each communication task has 
several characteristics that defines it. For instance, a task can be described by the number of participants, 
from its purpose and from the type of information exchanged. Consequently, the capabilities of 
telepresence robots could better serve in tasks with specific characteristics. I want to identify specific tasks’
 features that get most benefit in using telepresence robot. 
==PAGE:14==
 
 
13 
 
2) Manager expectation from telepresence robot 
The second theme addressed is the perception of telepresence robots by the remote managers. They can 
have some expectation about the impact robots could have on their work. In some cases, these 
expectations can be related to the possibility to improve performances or simplify communication tasks. 
In other instances, the managers can expect telepresence robot to increase their access to another 
location. Some others could have no specific expectations and just be curious to experiment a new 
working tool. Expectations can be precisely defined or be vaguer. Managers' initial considerations are 
then integrated with the experience from the interaction through telepresence robot. The objective of 
this theme is to identify the reasons that make more or less desirable working with telepresence robots. 
The expectations should then be compared with the feedback following the interaction. This comparison 
should indicate the extent to which the final adoption or rejection of telepresence robot as working tool 
is founded on prior ideas or actual experience. 
3) Mobile robotic presence as strain source 
Another theme is the strain generated by the use of telepresence robots in work environment. There is 
the possibility that the medium can complicate the work it should support. Day and colleagues (2010) suggest a range of I
malfunctions or intrusiveness. The use of telepresence robots could possibily generate some of these 
demands. For instance, device malfunctions can increase the demand of maintenance of the system or 
impede the manager to access and use the robot. Alternatively, some of the people interacting physically 
with the robot may feel monitored by the extended presence of their boss.  
The possibilities are several, the objective is not to list all the causes of stress related to the introduction 
of telepresence robots, but to find a way to identify and eventually prevent them.  
4) Social interaction and mobile robotic presence 
Each person relates in a unique way with the other. The mediation of a machine can change how people 
relate to each other. Each medium can convey different type of information while filtering others. Face to 
face communication is normally considered the most suitable medium for social interaction. Social 
presence is the degree of awareness of a person as a social entity. Short, Williams and Christie (1976) 
points out that different communication media provides a different degree of social presence. Social 
presence can be supported by the transmission of nonverbal clues in a communication. Telepresence 
robots allow the exchange of different type of information common to other ICT medium, plus more, 
deriving from movement. However, it’s clear that the quantity and quality of information made available 
in that way is lower than the one that may be exchanged between people physically present in a same 
==PAGE:15==
 
 
14 
 
place. As consequence, the degree of social presence made possible by telepresence robot is most likely 
lower than the one available in physical interaction. 
5) Remote leadership with telepresence robot 
The last theme investigates how the leadership style adopted by distant managers may be influences by 
the mediation of telepresence robots. Every manager has his/her own way to lead people and engage with 
them (Jones & George, 2013). Each managerial style differs widely in the way personal relationship are 
handled and how the power at the manager’s disposal is used. The Ohio State University Behavior Model 
classifies the orientation of the leadership style. This theory states that managers can be focused mainly 
on the relationship with people or in the fulfillment of tasks. When managers prioritize support and trust 
of the subordinates they engage in what is called consideration. This approach to leadership is possible 
when there is good esteem of collaborators and it involves not just communication but a good personal 
relationship as well. The main point is then to understand how the use of telepresence as medium can 
influence the way managers interact with theirs subordinates and how this can influence their leadership 
style. 
1.3 Telepresence robot 
The integration of video conference software and robotics made possible the invention of telepresence 
robots (or mobile robotic presence). The aim of this technology is to simulate physical presence of a user 
by transmitting her or his image on a screen placed on a robot remotely located. The person attending the 
location of the robot can see the face of the user displayed on the robot screen. The user controls 
remotely the motion of the robot through multi-platform software. The robot can move through space by 
standing on wheels that are moved by an electric engine. In this way, the user can navigate distant spaces 
through an internet-connected device and interact with people from distance.

a telepresence robot for a usability study in business activity. The robot was presented as a possible

first telepresence robot resulted in a bulky robot design and in some functionality issues. Although some technical issues s

In this study, I consider the interaction with two commercial 
telepresence robots: Double 2, from Double Robotics and

with the robot. The robot stands vertically on two wheels 
using accelerometer and gyroscope. These sensors adjust the 
acceleration of the wheels so to keep the robot body vertical. 
The iPad inserted in the support functions as input and output 
hardware for video transmission and transmit the commands to the

1.4 Scope 
The research themes define the focus of the research. However, the overall scope of the study still 
appears to be too wide to obtain result that may be interpreted. For this reason, I introduce some 
restrictions on the phenomenon studied which reduce the scope the research. The first limitation is the 
scale of the organization addressed.  
I decided to concentrate the study on large organizations (more than 1000 employees). The idea is that 
bigger scale of the companies may imply larger scale of internal operations that can be spread over 
different geographical areas. Consequently, in this context, the topic of distance management appears to 
me as more relevant than in smaller organizations.  
The second restriction regards the characteristics of staff working in the team. I focus mainly on teams 
composed by staff involved in knowledge work. This implies that the work performed in the team are of 
intellectual nature and it requires some specific kind of academic knowledge. I limit the study to 
knowledgeable tasks for the technical or conceptual nature of the information exchanged. This allows me 
to study both simple and information rich communications.

Fisher and Fisher (2000) describe in detail duties and good practices of distance management. They start 
by defining which is the role and the tasks remote managers have to accomplish to ensure effective team

output (i.e. product or service). The remote manager should take care of all the boundary tasks.

As every leader, a distance manager must be able to inspire and motivate hers or his subordinates. Fisher

team. They advise distant managers to avoid being autocratic or fully abdicate the power to the team. An 
autocratic approach could reduce the feeling of autonomy of the team, affecting negatively their 
motivation. On the other side, abdicating the power can confuse distant team members. The suggested 
approach is a balanced delegation of decision making power. As consequence, the manager must be 
accessible to team members when they need to reach him/her. At the same time, the distant manager 
should not be concerned about controlling the team. To allow the team to carry daily operation at best, 
the distance manager must clearly define the responsibilities inside the team, so to avoid overlapping 
duties or leave tasks uncovered. The distant manager doesn’t have to direct the team giving them orders. 
Rather, she or he should communicate the team the boundary conditions, the constrains inside which the 
team can operate autonomously for goal achievement. A necessary condition for effective operation of a

its possibility under the given conditions. Under this aspect, the relationship described by Fisher and 
Fisher recalls the assumption from theory Y of McGregor (1960).

By leading, managers articulate a clear organizational vision for the members of the organization (Jones

playing in achieving organizational goals. In this way leaders influence the actions of others for the

Organic leaders are recognized by a group of people who decides to willingly support their values. The

Daft and Lengel (1984) introduce the concept of information richness in the Media Richness Theory

Dennis and Valacich (1999) make a further distinction by describing mediums by how they serve to 
different communication process. For this purpose, they developed the Media Synchronicity Theory 
(MST). In this theory the achievement of the common understanding of the conveyed messages is the 
main goal of communications. Communication can be described by the process of conveyance and 
convergence. Conveyance is the transmission of new and diverse information that enables the receiver to 
create and revise a mental model of the situation. Convergence is the transmission of higher level 
abstraction to existing mental models. The objective of convergence is that the different persons 
involved in the communication agree on the meaning of the new information. These two process requires 
different mediums to be fully effective. Conveyance will be better achieved through asynchronous media. 
Asynchronous media transmit and receive messages at different times, usually in written form. The time

on a larger amount of information. Contrary, convergence processes are better facilitated by 
synchronous media. These mediums provide real time communication and therefore can deliver 
continuous feedback about understanding of the message. Telepresence robots are designed as a 
synchronous medium. Consequently, they can be used in convergence processes.

2.2.7 Knowledge work and wellbeing consequences 
The work systems considered in the case companies share some of the characteristic of individuals, tasks

Many scholars provide an exhaustive description of knowledge intensive firms. However, there is not a 
shared consensus about the which firms can be defined as knowledge intensive. Alvesson (2004) defines 
knowledge-intensive firms as the ones that grounds core activities on intellectual skill. Then he limits 
this group to Professional Service Firms and R&D companies. Hislop (2005) cites previous definitions of 
knowledge intensive firms that include also IT service companies, law firms, biotechnology companies,

The definition of what is called knowledge intensive work is interesting also because it helps to

Straining work conditions of knowledge intensive professions has been widely studied and described by 
different researches in the last thirty years. 
Knowledge work is usually carried out in projects. This structure allows good coordination between 
persons with different skills toward the accomplishment of unique tasks. As knowledge work is always 
subject to uncertainty and surprise, the course of a project depends by its context. As consequence, is not 
possible to exercise full control on the projects (Docherty, Kira, & Shani, 2009).  
Team members of knowledge intensive firms cope with challenging tasks in great autonomy. The high

motivation. On the other side, knowledge workers are expected to exhibit a series of behaviors and

customers generates a set of work demands. Work demands can range from work-related objectives to 
social rules for individuals (Docherty et al., 2009). Regarding work-objectives, insufficient control over 
the fulfillment of highly demanding tasks can be a driver for occurrence of stress and strain (Karasek, 
1998). 
Social rules and organizational culture can reduce the attention organizational members dedicate to 
strain issues. A study focused on knowledge-based firm in Denmark (Ipsen & Jensen, 2012) underlined 
how knowledge work can be a source of strain and how institutionalized and shared stress intervention 
strategies are neglected. In this context, stress arises from the combination of high performance 
expectation and lack of formal support. In the knowledge-intensive companies studied in this paper, 
organizational members consider stress as an inherent feature of the job. As consequence, 
countermeasures are considered a responsibility of the individuals. Problem may occur when an

3.7.1 Data analysis framework 
The research problem of this study it’s quite complex in its nature. The well-being of individuals can be 
understood in many ways and an extensive number of factors plays a role in it. Starting by using Carayon 
(2009) I describe the main elements of a work system and understand from which relations may arise 
cognitive loads. The work system model constitutes the base of the framework as it underlines the 
existing relations between different elements. 
I identify as relation or interface between two work system element all the events and phenomenon that 
arise from the interplays the two elements. These are more exhaustively exposed in the next section. The first work system
the stage of the observed phenomenon. The vision and the strategy shape the human, physical and 
intellectual asset of the organization. Therefore, the first step to analyze data is to define the 
characteristics of the teams studied and their relation to the main organization. For this purpose, I 
applied the revised version of the star model, presented in section 2.1.6. In this way I express how the 
people, processes, power relations and motivation are linked to the team goals. 
In second place, I provided a description of the workplaces. The workplace design can be considered as a 
part of organizational design. However, as the work system model underlines, the workplace plays an 
active role on the sustainability of work systems. 
After defining the organizational and physical features defining the case study, I focus my analysis on two 
other elements: the tasks exectuted and the technological tools. The tool is represented by all the devices 
used in the communication. The communication considered here involves two nodes. The first node is 
the office of Copenhagen where the local staff members physically interact with the robot. The second 
node is in the location of the remote manager. In the office of Copenhagen, the communication take 
place from one of the two telepresence robot models available for the project (i.e. Double 2 and PadBot 
U1). The manager remotely accesses the office of Copenhagen by using other ICT devices as PC, Tablet 
or smartphone. I keep track of the mediums used in the two nodes in each communication instance. I 
take this action to understand how the medium capabilities impacted on the communication. The 
capabilities included in the analysis are the one introduced by Dennis and Valacich (1999) plus the 
motion capabilities distinctive of telepresence robots. 
==PAGE:59==

The tasks considered are communication processes aimed to sustain formal process or informal 
information exchange. These communications were either in place before the study or has been ideated 
for the test. For the process ideated exclusively for the study, telepresence use outcomes are compared 
with the one of other media used in the routine communications of the distant team. In the assessment of 
existing processes, the outcomes are compared with those of arising from the media usually employed in 
the task.  
I consider some characteristic to describe each type of meeting. The first one is the meeting purpose. The 
meetings studied ranges from formal processes aimed for coordination and exchange of information to 
ones with no explicit formal intent mainly constituted by social interactions. Another characteristic of 
the meeting is the number of people involved and the possible occurrence of simultaneous 
communications. I consider the type of information exchanged for each meeting reported in the data. These can be constit
information as shared files. Other meeting characteristic and information are later deduced from the 
data. 
I adapted the media synchronicity theory (A.R. Dennis & Valacich, 1999) to assess the relationship 
between telepresence robot and a wide range of communication process. MST has been ideated to relate 
the performance of communication processes to the media being used in it. MST defines communication 
performance to the common understanding of the information exchanged. I extend the scope of MST by 
claiming that common understanding is the goal in formal meeting, while feeling of intimacy and 
immediateness that constitutes social presence (Short et al., 1976) are a more desirable outcome in social 
interactions. It is possible to describe which media capabilities introduced in MST allows a certain degree 
of social presence (Short et al., 1976) for different communications. Short et al. (1976) suggested that 
==PAGE:60==
 
 
59 
 
presence is expressed through a series of verbal and non-verbal clues. These clues can be described by 
MST as symbols encoded, transmitted and then decoded. If the transmission of these information does 
not encounter disruptions and the information are decoded accordingly to the pertecipants expectations 
individuals may experience social presence. Consequently, communication performance can be 
considered the outcome of formal communication processes while social presence will be the target of 
informal communications. However, we can consider each communication task as being constituted by a 
different mix of informational exchange and socials interactions. As consequence, the quality of the 
information and clues transmitted impacts on the quality of every communication assessed.  
 
Figure 22, Carayon (2009) work system elements in communication process as described in Media Synchronicity Theory 
(Dennis & Valacich, 1999) 
When the medium capabilities fit the purpose of the meeting there is a good communication 
performance (A.R. Dennis & Valacich, 1999). Differently from MST I don’t consider only the fit between 
medium, task and individuals but also the fit with the workplace. If all the elements fit, the performances 
are satisfactory. In this case the individuals may recognize the relevance of the tool for the job and the 
quality of the communication. Perceived job relevance and output quality increase the perceived 
usefulness of the tool (Venkatesh Viswanath ; Bala, 2008). In the Technology Acceptance Model, the 
perceived usefulness impacts on the behavioral intention toward the ICT tool, and therefore on its 
possible adoption.  
==PAGE:61==
 
 
60 
 
On the other hand, if the medium does not fit with the communication process the perceived outcome 
can be unsatisfying and negatively affect the perception of usability and usefulness of telepresence 
robots. 
Together with perceived usefulness, behavioral intention is determined also by the perceived ease of use 
of the ICT tool. The perceived ease of use of a technological system is influenced by its objective usability 
(Venkatesh Viswanath ; Bala, 2008). There are several reasons that can make an ICT tool difficult 
operate. The malfunctions can originate from failures in one of the devices used in the connection (i.e. 
the robot and the device used by the manager to access it) or by inadequate workplace’s IT infrastructure. 
The rise of ICT issues may decrease the objective usability and consequently the perceived ease of use. 
The inclusion of TAM in the data analysis framework tell, in conclusion, that when the tool is not 
perceived as useful or easy to use, it won’t be adopted. 
System malfunction or failures may have negative consequences for the people possibly responsibles for 
of the system maintenance. This is expressed by the last model I include in the framework: the demand 
control model of Karasek (1998). The model expresses how ICT issues reduce the time available for 
people to perform their core work tasks. The reduced time and the added demand for maintaining the 
system can be source of strain for individuals working with unstable ICT systems.

3.7.2 Work system interfaces 
The complete analysis framework expresses some of the factors that may hinders or ease the use of 
telepresence robots in workplaces. Telepresence robot may show a good fit or be have a poor interface with the rest of the
judgement of mobile robotic presence for distant management work. The model identifies the sources of 
missing fits between ICT medium, individuals, tasks and workplaces. If all the most important elements 
of the work system match with each other, I expect a good degree of communication performance and 
social presence. A match between two elements of the system occurs when the interface of these does not 
interfere with the communication. In other words, a model of telepresence robot matches with a work 
system if: 
• 
The capabilities of the robot reflect the requirements of the communication task (Robot-Task 
fit) 
• 
The layout of the workplace, object and people present in it allows the normal operation of the 
robot (Robot-Workplace fit) 
• 
All the people involved in the communication task feels comfortable in interacting with a 
telepresence robot (Robot-Individuals fit) 
• 
The system interface provides to remote managers has a cognitively likeable experience 
(Manager-Connected device fit)  
Robot-Workplace interface The workplace physical characteristics’ can facilitate or obstruct the no
rmal operation of telepresence 
robot. Usually workplaces are designed to accommodate people rather than robots. Even if telepresence 
robots are designed to move through space as physically present persons, most robot does not have 
articulation to do that in all work environments. Consequently, closed doors, steps and other object can 
become obstacles. At the same time the cognitive experience acquired through mobile robotic presence 
widely differs from the one provided by our senses. Consequently, otherwise unnoticed features of a 
workplace as light or sound may prevent the successful experience of the surrounding environment.  
Robot-Team relation 
Telepresence robot is a concept relatively new and has being adopted marginally compared to other ICT 
mediums. Consequently, most people are not accustomed with this technology. The encounter with a 
telepresence robot can generate different reactions in people. These reactions, together with use experience, shape the re
d. Inadequate reputation of 
telepresence robot among people may impede them to carry out some tasks with it. 
==PAGE:64==
 
 
63 
 
Manager-Connected device relation 
On the robot operator side, the experience is quite different. The manager who controls the robot does 
not interact physically with the robot, but experience the remote environment through its perspective 
interacting with another device. The devices used to control the robot range from personal computer, to 
smartphones and tablets. The manager experience of the remote office is to some extent affected by the 
device she/he uses to connect to the robot. Therefore, I consider for the analysis also the different 
capabilities of these devices. 
Robot-Task fit 
As mentioned before, the communications processes present in organization display different 
characteristics. At the same time telepresence robot, as other ICT medium, are defined by a limited set of 
capabilities. Media Synchronicity Theory (A.R. Dennis & Valacich, 1999) expresses the need to match the 
capability of the medium with the requirements of the communication tasks. For this reason, 
telepresence robot will perform differently in diverse task even if the other work system elements stay 
unchanged. The robot-task fit relation describes to all the combinations of task characteristics and robot 
capabilities which make easier or more complicate specific communication task.  
Technical issues 
When the medium works as supposed but does not fulfill the appointed tasks as desired, it verifies 
unfitting interface between telepresence robot and other work system elements. Technical issues occur 
when it is not possible to use the technology at all. Since ICT problems can compromise the usability and 
the adoption of a technology I am interested in identifying the causes of these failures. I divide possible 
technical issues by their origin. The first category is constituted by the issues arising from telepresence 
robot system. Telepresence robot functions through the use different piece of hardware and software 
component. The correct function of the tablet mounted on the robot, the charging system and the 
proprietary software are three possible sources of technical disruption related to the robot system. The 
other source of disruption is the internet network used for the connection. The access to the robot can 
denied by malfunction of internet network in one of the two nodes of the communication.  
Other work system relations 
The relations introduced so far describes the interface of work system elements with the new 
telecommunication system. However, I consider also the existing interfaces established between 
individuals, workplace and task. These relations represent the context in which telepresence robot is 
introduced. These system relations are usually designed to maximize team performance and guarantee 
the balance of the work system (Carayon, 2009). In other words, the right people are placed in the most 
suitable place with the most appropriate tool to accomplish a task. In established processes the work 
==PAGE:65==
 
 
64 
 
system is usually includes other ICT tools that can be substituted or integrated with telepresence robots. 
In the setting of this study the relation between existing work system elements are considered as given, 
but may eventually impact on the use of the robot.

4.4.1 Technical issues 
A diverse range of technical hindrance prevented or obstacle the interaction in all the case studies. Some 
of these where linked to the robot itself while other arose from the setup with the internet network. 
Software failures 
Each technical issue has different consequences and may require different corrective actions. Software 
failures can require a short time for troubleshooting before reacquiring access to the robot. In most of 
observed cases the failure happened on the software on the device mounted on the robot. In these 
instances, the manager lost the possibility to connect to the robot until a local team member took some 
action to restart the software. 
Network failures 
Similarly, network failures have constituted a barrier for the remote managers to log into the robots. In 
the experiment of this study, the failures were mostly due to the impossibility to the device mounted on 
the robot to remain logged into the Wi-Fi network of the case companies. 
It should be noted that often the robot did not stay logged to the guest network because of issues with the 
credential. Therefore, I consider that most of these failures could be avoided by applying permanent 
setup with the main internet network of the office. 
Sometimes the internet network impacted on the robot usability also during use. Reduced coverage or 
bandwidth slowed down the reaction time of the robot to the manager’s commands. This resulted in 
increased difficulty in operating the robot and reduced feeling of the remote space.

4.4.7 Acceptance 
All the remote managers who tested mobile robotic presence rejected the possibility to continue to use 
telepresence robot in their job. In these cases, the negative evaluations are linked to little compatibility 
of the tool with their duties. The managers justified the rejection of the new medium by stating how it use 
had little relevance for their task. The relevance can be related to the role the manager fulfills. The role in 
turn derives from one or more organizational goals. 
Consequently, it appeared that the main condition for adopting telepresence robot is that the manager 
finds it relevant for his/her role. It is not possible to define specific metrics for relevance on the base of 
data collected in this study. However, is safe to assume that most managers expect technologies to help 
them to achieve organizational goals. 
The choice of rejecting telepresence robot was supported also by the satisfaction in the use of established 
communication medium. These comparisons suggest that the choice to switch to a new system is 
evaluated in relation with the current situation. As consequence when managers evaluated ease of use 
and usefulness of telepresence robot they have done it in relation to the medium currently in use for the 
tasks. 
It must be noted that all four case studies involved knowledge workers carrying out intellectual work in 
office space. The team members and the managers were seldom involved in tasks taking place over an 
extended working area. In this way the managers exploited the movement capability of the telepresence 
robot only in few instances. Moreover, in the distance work investigated here, the requirement of 
presence of remote managers was extensively fulfilled by established mediums.

5 Conclusion 
In this report I investigated the social sustainability of the use of telepresence robot for distant 
management. For this purpose, I studied the interaction between four remote managers and their teams 
through mobile robotic presence. All the study cases involved knowledge workers (as defined in 2.2.7) of 
large organization carrying intellectual tasks in office spaces.  
Before starting collecting data, I reviewed the literature introducing issues arising from knowledge 
intensive work, disperse teams, communication processes and ICT use in organizations. I paid particular 
attention to the Work Balance Theory from Carayon (2009) as it highlights the relation between 
occupation and well-being. I integrated this model with the Media Synchronicity Theory (A.R. Dennis & 
Valacich, 1999) and other works to develop a framework describing the introduction of telepresence 
robot for managerial tasks. 
I applied this framework to analyze all the data collected in the case studies. The result points out all the 
events and situations that impacted on the perceived usability and usefulness of the tool. These instances 
where labeled as “enablers” when contributed to the balance of the work system and as “disenablers” 
when destabilized the work system. 
In this way I introduced an approach to identify which events and situations related to the introduction 
of mobile robotic presence can cause of strain in organizational members.  
5.1 Research themes review 
The research themes put the ground for the design of the methodology and the analysis of the data. After 
the analysis, the different themes revealed to have a different role in the phenomenon compared to my 
initial expectations. Themes initially considered marginal acquired more importance. At the same time, 
themes I expected to be central were downsized during the analysis. Here I evaluate how the role the 
research themes evolved on the light of the results of the study. 
5.1.1 Role of task relevance 
The relation between the relevance of a task and the intention to use a technology for its fulfillment it is 
expressed in the Technology Acceptance Model (Venkatesh Viswanath ; Bala, 2008). The second version 
of the framework (TAM2) theorizes that people base their assessment of the usefulness of a system on 
the base of their mental assessment of how the performance achieved by its use may contribute to the 
achievement of important organizational goals.

The relevance of the task for which telepresence is adopted constitutes the first research theme of this 
thesis. The data collected fully confirmed the key role of this topic. All the managers motivated their decision to reject tele
elevance with their work. I interpreted 
the relevance that a manager attributes to a task as its perceived importance compared to all the other 
tasks the manager is responsible for.  For this reason, I included in the analysis framework the perceived 
relevance of the tasks subject to the tests. 
However, I initially expected that the managers would have joined the experiment for testing the robot 
just in tasks they regarded as essential. My tacit assumption was that if I would have given the managers 
the possibility to choose the process where to test the robot they would have employed it on task highly relevant for them.
preferred to test the robot in processes marginal for their role or in task established solely for the 
experiment. 
5.1.2 Role of expectations 
I included the expectation of the remote managers on telepresence robot as research theme because I 
thought it could play a significant role in its evaluation. I asked to all the remote managers involved in 
this study which outcomes they expected from the use mobile robotic presence. Then I looked for a 
relation between the expectations expressed, the observed experiences and the final judgments of 
managers. 
Some of the managers joined this study with an experimental attitude. They did not have specific 
expectations on telepresence robots but just wanted to explore the impact of this technology on their 
job. The lack of functional expectations prevented me to find a link between what managers expected and 
how they evaluated the real experience with the robots.  
Other managers were aiming to be increase their access in remote places by using the robot. Increased 
access was pointed out as a possible way to better exercise a caring leadership style or add flexibility to 
remote communication process.

interviewed managers described their management style as based on values as trust, honesty and 
empowerment.  
This approach is extensively supported by leadership literature. These works recommended managers 
coordinating team members carrying high profile work on complex tasks to leave their subordinates with 
a high decision-making latitude. This job design choice is aimed to generate a feeling of ownership and 
responsibility that motivates the team members. 
Trustworthy management approach is recalled also by some literature on distance management. In this 
context, the empowerment of remote team members is recommendable for the same reason just 
mentioned but also necessary for the impossibility to have direct supervision. 
The data showed little or no connection between the teams’ perception of the manager leadership style 
and the use of a telepresence robot. Many team members reported that they didn’t perceived any change 
in their consideration of the remote managers in comparison with other mediums. 
The results point out that an established relation between team members and the manager is a necessary 
condition to communicate through telepresence robot. Open and caring behaviors of the manager 
presumably makes conversation easier. Accordingly, Media Synchronicity Theory states that individuals’ 
acquaintance to the task and to the other people involved positively contribute to the communication 
(Alan R Dennis, Fuller, & Valacich, 2008).  
5.2 Contribution to knowledge 
This thesis studies a complex phenomenon constituted by a wide range of interplaying factors. For this 
reason, the theoretical framework I applied covers a likewise wide range of subjects. The holistic human-
centered structure of the Balance Theory (Carayon, 2009) is combined with the communication scheme 
provided by the Media Synchronicity Theory (A.R. Dennis & Valacich, 1999). The Technology Acceptance 
Model completes the core of the analysis framework depicting the organizational members’ interaction 
and perception of the new technological tool. 
The result is broad scheme describing communication processes in organization.  
I applied the framework for the schematization of the work of four managers and their remote teams. 
The results provided a clear picture of each communication instance. It is impossible to generalize the 
findings of each meeting, but it is possible to apply the same approach to analyze this phenomenon in all 
its manifestation. 
==PAGE:105==
 
 
104 
 
As consequence, the main outcome of this work is not the scheme in itself but rather its application 
approach. The analysis method suggests to observe a complex sociotechnical problem (i.e. remote 
communication through a newly introduced medium) and look for relations between different elements 
of the system. In this way it is possible increase the objectivity of the evaluation of the new technology by 
dividing the effect of the interplaying factors. 
This type of analysis has stronger practical, rather than theoretical implications. Decision makers in 
organizations could apply a similar method to understand where the effects of the use of the new medium 
originates from. 
This study addresses some different themes already addressed in some academic work. However, few 
studies investigate how using a specific communication media impacts on the well-being of 
organizational members. Other communication literature focusses mostly on the functional outcomes 
of technologies. This work shed light on the evaluation of the technology on the base of human 
necessities.  
5.3 Study limitations 
The study has been scoped and limited according to the different research themes. In this section I 
review the boundary of the study and I further explore research possibilities on the phenomenon.  
5.3.1 Office work and other possibilities 
The persons I interacted with in this project were willing to test telepresence robot in their work for 
different reasons. However, others declined the proposal to join the research. In addition to foreseeable 
defections due to time and resource constrains, a potential partecipant company rejected the project 
invitation for the impossibility to find a viable use case. In addition, they augmented that they expected 
telepresence robot to bring less value to the company compared to the established ICT mediums. This 
was related to the static nature of the communication tasks conducted by the remote managers and 
teams of that organization. 
Some project participants suggested to investigate the usability of the robots in on-site environment 
(e.g. to inspect factories or other production facilities), so to better exploit the robot motion capabilities. 
This feedback pointed out, once again, the central role of the fit between the medium and the task, 
together with the perceived importance of task in the manager’s perspective.  
This study limited to a reduced range of remote management tasks. However, the framework I developed 
here could be applied for other types of industries and activity to validate it. 
==PAGE:106==
 
 
105 
 
5.3.2 Experiment duration and system exposure time The managers had the possibility to keep the robot f
the manager reported to have used the robot continuously over that period. The two cases in SAS 
institute took place in one day each. In SimCorp unexpected circumstances compromised the 
uninterrupted utilization of the robot for the intended process.  
As consequence, only in Nestlé the robot was used in several instances over an extended time frame. In 
this way, the participants of Nestlé were the only ones who could get accustomed to the system. 
Extended usage also allowed them to get a complete picture on how the system works. The managers of 
the other companies could not learn to use the system in the same way. In these companies the manager 
and the team in Copenhagen have based their opinion on one or two use instances. 
The frequency and duration of the time participants has been exposed to the system could have had an 
impact on their relationship with the robot. People who did not get the chance to get accustomed with 
the robot through repeated interactions based their feedbacks on their first impression of remote robotic 
presence. In other words, they did not have the time to adjust their first ideas and perceptions through 
prolonged use (Venkatesh Viswanath ; Bala, 2008). Some of the behaviors generated by the robot could 
then be reduced by more extended use of the system. It is possible that people sharing space with a robot 
over long period feels less amusement or anxiety by working with it. 
The second relation that could be mediated by extended exposition to the system is between the manager 
and the user interface. As every other system, learning to operate properly telepresence robots may 
require some time. It is possible that increased skills in maneuvering the robot can reduce the cognitive 
effort directed to this action. Less effort to control the robot can result in more attention available to the 
manager for core activities. 
5.3.3 Medium comparison 
Even if I reviewed communication and medium literature, the study does provide a systematic 
comparison of telepresence robots with other ICT mediums. However, some of the data compare the 
experience of remote robotic presence with previously used medium. It would have been interesting for 
me to observe and get data on the state of the work system prior the introduction of mobile robotic 
presence. Attending face to face or video conference process could have constituted a useful baseline for 
the assessment of the technology. However, this was difficult because of the time available for the master 
thesis project and the difficulty of negotiating access from multiple companies for an extended period. 
Additionally, some of the tested interactions have been possible solely through telepresence and were 
==PAGE:107==
 
 
106 
 
impossible or unpractical through existing mediums. At the same time, some processes were arranged 
solely for this study purpose. 
5.4 Suggestion for future research 
This research was an attempt to describe a complex phenomenon in few manifestations. The result 
indicates the possibility to consider holistically impact on well-being from the use of a medium. 
However, the analysis method and the approach I introduced requires for further validation. 
Similar investigation could take place in similar and different organizational environments. 
Some future research could focus on the use of telepresence robot by manager involved in dynamic 
coordination in work environment constituted by wider spaces. Such study could evaluate the potential 
benefit deriving by the movement capability of telepresence robots. Alternatively, it would be interesting 
to apply the framework for the study of medium adoption in small medium enterprises. 
These studies should possibly take place over a more extended period. More interaction with mobile robotic presence coul
 
Finally, there is room for studies centered on the organizations' expectation on telepresence robot. This 
kind of research may involve a larger number of remote manager and assess their possible functional 
expectations. In this case, the objective could be to identify industries where mobile robotic presence be 
more effective. 
6 Practical implication of the study 
The whole study originates from the necessity to study the well-being implication of the adoption of 
telepresence robots in organization. My personal believes directed my attention to the human-
technology fit problem.  
I try now, on the base of the results, to develop a guideline to support decision making in this area to 
provide a practical contribution to work design practices. 
The scheme used to try to reach such ambitious goal is the Double Diamond model from Design Council 
(designcouncil.org.uk, 2018). This model was introduced to illustrate the product design process in four 
phases. I adapted the model for the development of the guideline.   
The first phase is the study of the problem and the collection of information. In this report it corresponds 
with the investigation and study of all related literature and models presented in the section 2. This step 
was necessary to make sense of all the possible direction where to articulate the research themes. The 
==PAGE:108==
 
 
107 
 
second stage is the definition of the problem. At this point, I make sense of all the knowledge present in 
the literature to define a more specific focus. This activity was carried by selecting, on the base of my 
personal beliefs, the theories and models I considered most important. This choice ended up in the set of 
theories selected for the theoretical framework. 
The third phase of the double diamond is the development of possible solutions. In this work the solution 
range is represented by the data analysis framework and by the system relations it underlines. The 
framework is tested on the data. The framework is initially deduced from theory but is then adapted to 
the findings. The last stage is the delivery of the decision support guideline. 
 
Figure 42, Research process and solution proposal displayed in double diamond model 
The decision support guideline is the synthesis of the research findings in form of logically structured 
considerations. These may be used in organizations to help ensure socially sustainable use of 
telepresence robot in distance management activities. The guideline must be considered applicable in 
contexts similar to the one studied in this work. However, the model can be discussed and adapted to 
different use situations. 
The guideline is constituted by two phases. The first phase is aimed to assist remote managers in 
evaluating the possible sustainability of telepresence robots for given distant management task. The 
second phase has the objective to help managers to identify failing interface in the work system after 
introducing telepresence.  
==PAGE:109==
 
 
108 
 
 
Figure 43, Two phases of decision support guideline 
6.1 Decision-making procedure 
The key relation of the work system investigated in this study gives an indication of the main issues or 
opportunities in adopting mobile robotic presence. This insight can be used to assist remote managers in 
the evaluation of telepresence robot as possible communication tool for distant management processes. 
In this section I propose a set of steps to follow when considering utilizing mobile robotic presence for 
remote managerial activities. Each step is derived from a key relation of the analysis framework 
(introduced in section 3.7.2) and to the related results. The steps sequence forms a structured decision-
making procedure. The steps are ordered so to minimize the duration and organizational effort given to 
the make all the relevant considerations. 
 
Figure 44, Steps of the decision-making procedure and their source from theoretical framework 
Usefulness assessment 
The first step is the define the value that telepresence may bring into the organization. Consideration 
about the value precede all the other step as strong adoption arguments represent a necessary condition 
for continuing the whole process. In other words, if there is no value in adopting mobile robotic presence 
there is no use in continuing the evaluation. 
The initial problem is to define what the manager wants to achieve with telepresence robot. This mean to 
align the expected outcome of mobile robotic presence with the capabilities of the telepresence robots 
available on the market. This assessment should indicate if the desired outcome can be achieved by this 
technology. 
==PAGE:110==
 
 
109 
 If commercially available telepresence robots can’t serve the intended purpose then is necessary to 
consider other solutions. Otherwise, if at least one model of telepresence robot may serve the purpose, it 
is possible to continue the analysis. Now the decision maker should consider all the processes and tasks 
in which the remote managers could be embodied by the robot. Then the manager should express the 
relevance of the target tasks as the extent to which each process contributes to the achievement of 
organizational goals. 
If the telepresence robot can contribute to significant organizational goals it should be compared to the 
ICT medium currently adopted for the tasks. Here is necessary to evaluate how current ICTs perform in 
the task intended for the robot. In this way is possible to understand the possible improvements in 
replacing current medium with telepresence robot. 
If the analysis concludes that a telepresence robot may add value in some remote management process 
then it is possible to evaluate the cost of the available robots. 
Describe intended tasks 
If there are solid argumentations to adopt telepresence robot for a set of distance management tasks it is 
possible to make some consideration to ensure that the robot(s) will fit for the tasks. 
To ensure that, it is necessary to describe extensively the target communication processes. The first 
information is who are the participants and the way they interact.  
The decision maker should ensure that the people joining the meeting are accustomed with the situation. 
Is important to establish if the interested people have good interpersonal relation. In general 
telepresence robot should not be used to introduce new people. Exception can be made if the remote 
manager demonstrates to be socially confident in doing so. 
It must be considered the number of people attending in the robot site and the informative flow. 
Telepresence robot can be used with profit with maximum one speaker at the time. If the intended 
process involves only another person (more than the remote manager) the decision maker should access 
his or her attitude toward robots. That can be done by anticipating the personnel feedback phase 
introduced later. 
Special attention should be given to the type of information exchanged during the meeting and the 
eventual presence of other ICT medium for information exchange. Telepresence robot allows to convey 
natural symbols as voice and face expression. Consequently, is not recommended to use the robot in 
meeting involving exchange of more complex information. 
==PAGE:111==
 
 
110 
 
Other ICT tools can be used in meetings for the exchange of complex information or to give access to 
others remote attendants. The presence of other ICT tools for the exchange of information can 
compromise the sustainable use of telepresence robot if the information these provide are not accessible 
to the robot. 
The last information to consider is the amount of physical movement involved by the meeting. It should 
be considered the extent to which motion can assist the manager in joining the intended processes. 
Describe tasks workplaces 
All the processes in which the manager plans to use telepresence take place in specific work 
environments. It is important to ensure that a telepresence robot can fit into the work space where it is 
intended to be used. The whole area should have a strong and stable Wi-Fi coverage and should be free 
from physical obstructions. Most common obstacles are steps and doors but other physical objects as 
furniture or equipment in the work space could hinder the robot movements. Other inconvenience can 
be originated by the wrong amount of light or excessive ambient noise. Too much or to less light can blind 
the robot operator, while too much noise in the work area can prevent her/him to speak or listen to 
others. 
Telepresence robot choice 
The information gathered about the process and workplaces indicates which capabilities are preferable 
for the robot. This knowledge constitutes the base for the selection and purchasing process of a specific 
telepresence robot model from the market. 
The choice of the robot should consider also the devices supporting it. The presence of a charging dock is 
an essential element to guarantee to the remote manager autonomy. It should be taken in account the 
types of device that can be used to control and see through the robot. Different robot models can connect 
to a different range of devices. The devices used to access remote presence inevitably effect the whole 
experience and therefore should be taken carefully in account. 
Obviously, the robot should be chosen to match at best with the environment it is intended for. However, 
it should be considered that modification of the workplace can enhance the match as well. The extent of 
these modifications can be related to the organizational advantage deriving from mobile robotic 
presence adoption. Extensive amount of environmental obstructions can make undesirable to use of this 
technology. 
Personnel feedback, consider involved individuals 
Once ensured that all the technical aspects of the task fit with a telepresence robot model is time to 
consider the people that will have to interact with the robot. If the decision maker is someone else from 
==PAGE:112==
 
 
111 
 
the manager, the remote manager should be the first person to be heard. In any case, feedback from all 
the other people involved in the tasks should be asked. A good practice could be to introduce the people 
with the concept of telepresence robot through video or other material. Then the decision maker should 
make sure that all the people involved understand the robot functions. Afterwards he or she should 
carefully listen to their feedbacks. The staff response should suggest their attitude in collaborating with a 
colleague embodied in a robot. 
This step is placed at the end of the framework to give the possibility to the person(s) in charge of the 
evaluation process to provide to the interested people as much details as possible. This choice is made 
under the assumption that the involved people are quite prone to technological innovation.  
In case the decision makers believe that the staff will be satisfied with the change they can skip this step. 
However, this choice can increase the risk of later rejection of the new work tool. 
Contrary, when there are strong perplexities about possible people attitude the step can be anticipated 
just after the usefulness assessment. Anticipation of the feedbacks' gathering phase is strongly suggested 
whenever the robot is expected to be used in one-to-one communication. 
 
Get organizational sponsorship 
As every other change process, the introduction of mobile robotic presence must gain the support of 
relevant stakeholders in the organization. If support can be more easily obtained in presence of specific 
information this step can be delayed to the end of the procedure. At this point it should be possible to 
argument the relevance and the fit of the telepresence robot with the work system. 
If less information is sufficient to get sponsorship, this step can be anticipated before in the process. In 
any case the decision maker should seek for organizational support only after the usefulness assessment 
to present basic argumentations about the added value of the change initiative. 
6.2 Diagnostic method algorithm 
The second phase of decision support procedure can follow the first one or another telepresence 
adoption procedure. Its purpose is to assist managers in identifying the causes of the situations that 
impede telepresence robot to be used profitably. 
==PAGE:113==
 
 
112 
 
Use condition 
The diagnostic method algorithm can be used if two conditions are respected. First, the remote manager 
or someone else in the organization should have already assessed the usefulness of telepresence robot for 
the intended task. The added value for important tasks is a necessary condition for telepresence use. 
Therefore, it is unlikely for a remote manager to use a telepresence robot if she or he does not believe it 
adds any value to her/his job. 
The second condition is that not more than one telepresence robot is used simultaneously in the same 
process. As the algorithm is based on the data collected in this study it can be effectively applied only to 
evaluation in a similar setting. 
6.2.1 Use and logic 
The method can be used every time a remote manager experience issues in communicating through 
telepresence robot.  
The algorithm is formed by logical steps linked to each other. Each step represents a question aimed to 
screen a possible failure related to mobile robotic presence. For each question is selected one between 
two mutually exclusive answers. The chosen answer leads to a possible diagnosis of the failure or to 
another question of the algorithm. 
The first step of the algorithm is to check if the technical setup of the robot is working. The following 
steps of the algorithm verify the presence of other potential issues in order of complexity.  
This is the problem hierarchy following an increasing degree of complexity: 
• 
Technical issues, caused by failures in elements of the robot system and supporting 
infrastructure. 
• 
Workplace interface issues, caused by environmental obstruction of the robot operation 
• 
Information exchange disruption, caused by inadequate exchange of information 
• 
Human factors, related to human perception of communication medium 
 
==PAGE:114==
 
 
113 
 
 
 
 
Figure 45, Diagnostic method alorithim scheme 
==PAGE:115==
 
 
114 
 
6.2.2 Possible outcomes 
If the answer chosen leads to technical issue or a work system mismatch that can be adjusted, the 
algorithm suggests to do so and then it reiterates the method. 
If a later iteration of the algorithm does not end pointing to a technical issue or mismatch it means that 
the communication occurs properly. This outcome would imply that: 
• 
The remote manager can access autonomously the telepresence robot 
• 
The workplace characteristic does not impede the robot utilization. 
• 
The remote manager and the local team exchange and understand the desired information 
without disruptions. 
• 
All the people involved in the communication have a positive attitude toward telepresence robot. 
 
If the mismatch cannot be resolved the diagram indicates that an evaluation error has be done. Each 
evaluation error is linked with step of the decision-making procedure. In this case it is suggested to 
perform a second time the step of indicated. 
 
Figure 46, Possible outcome of one iteration of diagnostic method algorithm 
Since the algorithm is grounded on the data of this study its applicability should be considered tentative. 
It is possible that it fails in identifying some unrecorded issues. The application of the guideline in 
different contexts could allow to integrate new enablers, disenablers or technical issues.

Learning objectives 
The learning objectives has been expressed according to learning objectives norms published by DTU 
(Thorp Hansen, Rasmussen, & Nøhr Hinz, 2010). These has been acquired during the realization of this 
master thesis project: 
1) Investigate previous literature and theories as base of a solid theoretical framework. 
2) Understand the balance between people, technology and process in organizations. 
3) Design an appropriate research methodology to address the research question. 
4) Use different methods to acquire most relevant data for the study of the phenomenon. 
5) Combine the reviewed literature into a theoretical framework for the analysis of the 
data. 
6) Apply a systemic view to schematize complex setting combining social and technical 
elements 
7) Use coding to acquire qualitative data from interviews. 
8) Present findings under form of case study and develop cross case analysis. 
9) Provide a practical guideline to apply the findings to organizational design practice.
