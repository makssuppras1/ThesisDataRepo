Abstract
On the path toward decarbonization, hydrogen, and hydrogen-based fuels can play an important
role in abating the emission of sectors where electrification or alternative solutions are unavail-
able or difficult to implement. The European Commission issued two delegated acts to define
what can be considered renewable hydrogen. The additionality delegated act ensures that
hydrogen production is always joined to renewable electricity production. Because of its net-
zero commitments and advancements with renewables, Denmark’s ambitions are to become a
leader in this new hydrogen economy. This thesis investigates how the future landscape of the
Danish Power-to-Hydrogen industry could develop, evaluating the cost-effectiveness and the
production volumes of green hydrogen in Denmark. Furthermore, this work also explores the
feasibility of establishing a hydrogen backbone to connect the Danish producers to the German
market. Analyzing the cost associated with the export of hydrogen and the flow dynamics within
hydrogen pipelines this thesis offers a comprehensive analysis of the economic and technical
aspects tied to green hydrogen production in Denmark.

1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
There’s a call for more renewable resources to slow down climate change and try and limit
global warming to 1.5°C. In the energy sector, the focus has been electrification, shifting from
fossil fuels to electrons produced via renewable resources. This transition, however, does not
come without challenges. The volatile nature of wind and solar production poses a challenge
to their deployment as, to accommodate for the high fluctuations of renewable power, is neces-
sary to rethink the whole energy sector itself. To facilitate and further increase the integration
of renewables in the energy system there is a need for a sufficient and strong electricity infras-
tructure able to withstand the volatility of wind and solar. However, new interconnections are
not enough when it comes to large proportions of fluctuating electricity production, and sector
coupling is needed to balance the energy system. Therefore, the conversion of electricity into
other carbon-neutral energy carriers becomes of crucial importance to reach the goals of the
Paris Agreements. These conversion technologies that turn electricity into low-carbon synthetic
fuels are known as Power-to-X (PtX). PtX systems enable more efficient coupling of electricity
with other sectors reducing CO2 emissions in sectors where direct electrification might result
hard to achieve.
Denmark has been at the forefront when it comes to renewable energy and recently has started
to invest more and more in PtX technologies leading the deployment of new PtX systems for
the production of green fuels. At the European level, joint efforts are being made to put in place
policies and funds to create the right conditions for the deployment of these technologies. The
Hydrogen Backbone Council is working is building a hydrogen pipeline to connect European
countries and facilitate the trade of green molecules.
The motivation for the thesis project lies in exploring the role that Denmark will play in the hy-
drogen space. Evaluating the Danish hydrogen production both in terms of quantity produced
and form an economical point of view.
1.2
Denmark PtX strategy
Following the Paris Agreement, Denmark, with the Danish Climate Act Denmark has set a target
to reduce greenhouse gas emissions by 70% in 2030 and achieve climate neutrality latest in
2050. As a part of these goals, renewable energy in Denmark is set to cover 100% of electricity
and 55% of overall consumption.
Demanrk is already at the forefront of the energy transition thanks to the high share of wind
energy in the primary energy consumption which reached almost 54% in 2022. This number is
only set to increase with a fourfold increase of onshore wind and solar installed capacity and a
fivefold increase in offshore wind by 2030.
If Denmark succeeds in achieving these goals, offshore electricity production alone will be
enough to supply twice as much as the electricity demand of a fully electrified Danish soci-
ety. Power-to-X could then play an important role in the Danish energy system as a flexible
off-take of clean electricity allowing a more efficient integration in the energy system of all this
additional renewable capacity.
Following the expansion of renewable electricity projects, the Danish government has published
its Power-to-X strategy in 2021 setting a target of 6GW of PtX plants to be installed by 2030 [1].
Overall, all of these PtX projects are expected to reduce Danish CO2 emissions between 2.5
and 4.0 million tonnes by 2030 and help in the indirect electrification of those sectors that can
not operate on electricity such as the steel and chemical industry where hydrogen could play

At the core of PtX sits electrolysis, which uses electricity to split water into oxygen and hydro-
gen. This technology has been known for over 200 years but it is only now that it has gained
momentum as a way to reduce CO2 emissions by coupling the electrolyzers with green elec-
tricity enabling the production of carbon-neutral hydrogen which can then be used directly as a
chemical or to produce e-fuels such as ammonia and methanol.
However, before looking at its potential use cases as a way to decarbonize other sectors such
as heavy industry, chemicals, and seasonal storage is important to scale up the production of
low-emission hydrogen first.
In fact, in 2022, 95Mt of hydrogen was produced worldwide, of these 47% was produced from
natural gas, 27% from coal 22% as oil, and 4% from electricity. Considering renewables rep-
resented about 30% of the electricity share in 2022 the result is that less than 1% of the global
hydrogen output was produced from renewable energy [2].
Therefore, to get on track with the Net-Zero-Scenario a rapid scale-up of low-emission hydrogen
is needed. One of the most significant challenges associated with green hydrogen production
is the higher cost compared to its fossil fuel counterpart. However, with the decrease in renew-
able generation costs, the implementation of carbon taxes, and the improvement of electrolyzer
technology green hydrogen could reach cost parity with grey and brown hydrogen.
1.3

If on the supply side, hydrogen sees a rapid increase in momentum in Denmark the same
can not be said for the demand side where the main off-takers of green hydrogen are repre-
sented by German, Dutch, and Belgian industries. As a result, coupled with the development
of new electrolyzer capacity the interest in a hydrogen infrastructure has risen drastically, not

1.4
Thesis Objectives
The core objective behind this thesis project is to answer the following questions:
How will the hydrogen production landscape develop in Denmark and what role will the Danish
Hydrogen Backbone play in establishing the Danish PtH2 industry in the new hydrogen econ-
omy?
Based on stated national development plans and future potentials, the total volume and the
cost associated with green hydrogen production will be investigated through a techno-economic
analysis of the future Danish PtX sector.

Furthermore, building on the results of the techno-economic analysis, the cost of exporting
green hydrogen to Germany and the gas flow dynamics in the hydrogen pipelines will be ana-
lyzed through non-linear programming (NLP) optimization problems. The influence of different
modeling solutions regarding the gas flow dynamics in the pipelines and the role that the hy-
drogen storage facility in Lille Turop could play in the export of hydrogen are analyzed in three
different scenarios:
• Steady-State conditions: the volume of hydrogen exported through pipelines, based on
limited demand, is calculated assuming steady-state (ST) flow conditions within the pipes.
• Quasi-Dynamic conditions: the volume of hydrogen exported to Germany is calculated
considering the possibility of using the pipelines as short-term storage for hydrogen mod-
eling the gas flow under quasi-dynamic (QD) conditions.
• Quasi-Dynamic conditions with additional Storage: the volume exported through the Dan-
ish Hydrgeon Backbone is calculated by implementing the effect of a storage facility in
quasi-dynamic conditions.
The contributions of this project are listed as follows:
1. Investigate and give an overview of the future landscape of the future green hydrogen
industry in Denmark.
2. Assess the volume and the associated costs of exporting hydrogen to Germany through
pipelines.
3. Propose a NLP model to analyze the gas flow dynamics of the Danish Hydrogen Backbone
under different conditions.

Literature Review
2.1
Levelized Cost of Hydrogen
As hydrogen has gained more and more momentum over recent years, the research regarding
the whole chain related to green H2 production has also increased. Techno-Economic-Analysis
(TEA) has been widely used to estimate the costs associated with hydrogen production. Include
methods for economic analysis. In the general literature, it appears as generally understood that
there are three main parameters affecting the Levelized Cost of Hydrogen (LCOH) namely: the
electrolyzer Capital Expenditure (CAPEX), the cost and origin of electricity used to run the elec-
trolysis, and the Full Load Hours which measure the degree of utilization of the plant over a
year. According to previous studies the cost of electricity accounts for almost half of the final
LCOH with Capital Investment being the second largest share with 30% followed by the fixed
costs for operation and maintenance (O&M). Figure 2.1 shows the breakdown of the Levelized
Cost of Hydrogen.
Figure 2.1: Breadkwon of the Levelized Cost of Hydrogen [6].
Values for the cost of the electrolyzers cost are widely accepted, with AEC ranging from 500-
1400EUR/kWe for alkaline technology, 1100-1800EUR/kWe for PEM, and 2800-5500EUR/kWe
for SOEC with an expected decrease in the future due to the learning curve [7].
However, the electricity cost, influenced by a variety of factors, is the main cost driver. Different
approaches can be used when considering the value and origin of electricity depending on the
type of operation of the electrolyzers which can be islanding (using only renewable energy),
fully grid-connected, or a mix of the two.
Raab et. al developed a linear program to study the effect of the integration of a hydrogen
production plant into the electricity grid in different locations around the world, showing how
drawing electricity from the grid could increase the utilization factor for the individual units thus
reducing the LCOH to values within the range of 2.5EUR/kg-4.0EUR/kg [8]. In another study,
Panah et. al. studied the effect of scaling up hydrogen production on grid-connected plants.
Using a Monte Carlo simulation they studied how the electricity price influences the final cost
of hydrogen [9]. Using the Danish electricity market as the study case they showed how green

hydrogen could be already produced at 3EUR/kg using AEC and PEM electrolyzers. Addition-
ally, with the mass adoption of all three electrolyzer technologies, they forecast that the price of
hydrogen could drop below 2EUR/kg.
The market price of electricity depends on a wide range of parameters and, as seen in re-
cent years, it has proved hard to produce accurate forecasts for future hourly DA prices due
to the many uncertainties that surround the power market and its politics. Moreover, the grid-
connected configuration cannot ensure that the hydrogen produced is, in fact, renewables, and
even though guarantees of origin can be issued to the certificate of the source from which the
electricity is produced they cannot assure that the final electricity used by the electrolyzers is
100% renewable but rather a mix of the gird. Therefore, focusing on the production of actual
renewable hydrogen, several papers propose an alternative approach considering only off-grid
electrolyzers connected to RES. In this case, the cost of electricity used to calculate the LCOH
is the Levelized Cost Of Electricity (LCOE) of that specific renewable generation technology,
focusing more on the cost of electricity rather than its price.
Jassen et al. calculated the LCOH projections for hydrogen produced through off-grid systems
for 30 European countries [10]. Comparing hydrogen production using on- and offshore wind
and solar PV the paper calculates the LCOH as the sum of electricity cost, and operational and
annualized capital expenditures. Final results showed that hybrid configurations of onshore
wind and solar PV generally result in lower renewable LCOH due to the higher full load hours.
Out of the 30 different countries, the study pointed to Denmark as one of the countries with
the lowest LCOH for hydrogen production from Onshore and Offshore wind power with values
ranging from 5.1EUR/kg to 2.0EUR/kg for different RES in 2030 which falls to 1.9EUR/kg con-
sidering a hybrid combination of solar and onshore wind electricity.
In their paper, Zheng et. al analyze the economics of off-grid wind/hydrogen systems in seven
countries around the world comparing hydrogen production coupled with onshore and offshore
wind power involving a battery storage system to further reduce the hydrogen cost [11]. Having
defined the cost of hydrogen as the ratio of annualized capital costs and annual operational cost
to the total yearly hydrogen production the paper shows that, in Denmark, hydrogen production
coupled with onshore wind power represents the cheaper option with LCOH ranging from 2EU-
R/kg to 4EUR/kg for Danish H2 due to the lower Capital Investment of onshore wind turbines
compared to the offshore counterpart. Furthermore, the study shows that the implementation
of a battery as a mean to store electricity could reduce the final LOCH but not significantly.
The origin of the electricity used by the electrolyzers also defines if the hydrogen produced can
be counted as a Renewable Fuel Of Non-Biological Origin (RFNBO) according to the updated
Renewable Energy Directive (RED II) published by the European Commission [12]. The core
of the RED II package is the principle of additionality which states that the production of re-
newable electricity matches the electricity used for hydrogen production. Therefore hydrogen
producers have to use electricity from the same installation or through a renewables Power
Purchase Agreement (PPA). Moreover, to ensure that the hydrogen supplied comes from new,
rather than existing, renewable production plants the act rules that the installation from which
the renewable power for the electrolyzer is generated must have been in operation for less than
36 months before the electrolyzer. On top of that, the additional RES is to be located in the
same bidding zone as the electrolyzer or in zones where the Day-Ahead prices are equal to
or higher than the bidding zone where hydrogen is produced. From 1 January 2030, hydrogen
producers must follow a temporal correlation, meaning hydrogen must be produced during the
same one-hour period as the renewable electricity used. Beacuse of the additionally criteria
and to investigate the cost associated with green hydrogen production, the LOCE was taken as
reference metric in the economic calculations.

2.2
Gas Flow Modeling
Following the rising interest in hydrogen, studying how to transport the gas has also gained im-
portance. However, at the time of writing, little literature can be found regarding the hydrogen
backbone.
To better assess the socio-economic value of establishing a hydrogen infrastructure, Energinet
conducted a feasibility study on the Western Jutland Backbone [5]. The results showed how
building this new infrastructure could benefit the Danish electricity and PtX producers. In the
study, two different routings are analyzed the ”West Jutland backbone” and the “Central Jutland
Backbone”. Moreover, the operating conditions and capacity of the possible hydrogen infras-
tructure were studied, focusing on the impact of compressor stations on the total volume of H2
transported. The feasibility study estimated that an initial capital investment between DKK 10
and 22 billion. In this work, the ”West Jutland backbone” was used as a reference for the future
Danish Hydrogen Backbone and the impact of the storage solutions was analyzed.
In their paper: ”Flexibility of Integrated Power and Gas Systems: Modeling and Solution Choices
Matter”, Enrica R., Yannick W., and Jalal K. focus on the role of natural gas pipelines as short-
term storage (linepack) in providing flexibility to power systems [13]. The paper presents a com-
parison of different modeling solution choices. It highlights the difference between implementing
steady-state, quasi-dynamic, or dynamic conditions on this flexibility provision. It emphasizes
the need for an accurate representation of gas flow physics to precisely analyze the impact of
the linepack on gas networks and concludes with recommendations for the modeling choices
of the gas infrastructure. The models presented in this paper were taken as base models on
top of which the models presented in this thesis have been built.

3
Green Hydrogen Production
In this chapter, the future landscape of the PtX industry in Denmark will be presented. First,
focusing on the electrolyzers the operating principles of the single cells, and the different tech-
nologies available today for green hydrogen production will be analyzed and discussed in terms
of efficiencies and capital expenditure. Thereafter, broadening the perspective, the chapter will
touch upon the future forecasts for the Danish power system. The forecasts for the availability of
renewable power, electricity demand, and the development of the PtX industry will be presented
to introduce the main assumptions lying behind the results. To calculate the yearly volume of
green hydrogen produced in Denmark the hourly data available for renewable power production
and demand were used in a time-series analysis. Finally, the results of the techno-economic
analysis will be presented in terms of the volume of green hydrogen production and the cost
associated with it.
3.1
Danish PtX system
Since the Climate Agreement for Energy and Industry, etc. of 22 June 2020, the Danish gov-
ernment has been working on a national strategy for the Power-to-X (PtX) sector. The focus
has been on creating the framework conditions to facilitate the development of the industry to
achieve the objectives of the Danish Climate Act.
The Danish strategy for PtX is based on four main objectives that aim to the promotion of the
sector nationally and internationally:
• PtX must be able to contribute to the realization of the objectives in the Danish Climate
Act.
• Regulations and infrastructure must be in place to allow the PtX industry to operate on the
market terms.
• The integration between Power-to-X and the Danish energy system must be improved.
• Denmark must be able to export PtX products and technologies.
To reach these objectives the government has already invested 170 million EUR through a ten-
der to support the industrialization and upscaling of PtX production in Denmark and reduce the
costs associated with hydrogen production. Five projects, accounting for a total electrolysis
capacity of 180MW won the tender and will receive a fixed subsidy price over 10 years. At the
same time, following the goals set in the Danish Climate Act, to reach 4 to 6 GW of total in-
stalled capacity many more projects have been announced, some have already been deployed
others are still waiting for the final investment decision. A table, summarizing all the Danish PtX
projects planned is available in the Appendix at Table A.1. The data were collected from the
Brintbanchen, the Danish Energy Agency, and/or made available directly from the companies
through their portals.. In the table below, the five projects that won the Danish PtX tender are
presented.

However, it is important to note that, most of the planned PtX plants have not reached the final

At standard conditions, the electrolysis reaction is not favorable in thermodynamic terms. Thus
external energy is needed to carry out the reaction. In an electrolyzer cell, the energy needed
to drive forward the reaction is provided, through an external, circuit by electrons that are used
to reduce the water molecule.
An electrolyzer cell is comprised of two electrodes, a negative electrode (Cathode) where water
is reduced producing hydrogen gas, and a positive electrode (Anode) where water molecules
are oxidized producing oxygen gas. The two electrodes are typically immersed in an electrolyte
solution or a substance that facilitates the movement of ions between electrodes. To prevent
gas mixing and maintain the equilibrium at the electrodes a membrane is used as the separator.
Electrolyzer cells can be classified based on the nature of the electrolyte solution and the oper-
ational temperature. Currently, there are three different categories of electrolyzers.
Alkaline Electrolysis Cells (AEC) use an alkaline electrolyte to transport hydroxide ions and
operate in a temperature range between 60 - 100°C at a pressure range of 1 - 30 bar. Water is
consumed at the Cathode where the Hydrgoen Evolution Reaction (HER) occurs and Hydrogen
and Hydroxil ions are produced. The ions are transferred in the electrolyte and through the
separator to the anode where the oxygen evolution reaction occurs and Oxygen and Water
are produced. The two half-reactions happening at the electrodes are shown in the chemical
equation below:

For the electrolysis reaction to take place, a minimum voltage is required. This is also known as
reversible voltage (Vrev). For electrolyzer cells, this corresponds to 1.23V at standard conditions
(1 bar and 25°C). However, the cells operate always at voltages higher than the reversible
voltage because of the presence of multiple overpotentials caused by kinetics and resistive
effects. The operating voltage of the cell can then be defined as the sum of the contribution of
the reversible voltage and all the overpotentials, namely the activation overpotential (ηact), the
ohmic overpotential (ηΩ) and the concentration overpotential (ηconc), as shown in the equation
below.
Vcell = Vrev + (ηact + ηΩ+ ηconc)

the cell. The relation between voltage and current density in an electrolyzer cell is non-linear.

Currently, the Alkaline and Proton Membrane Exchange Cells are the most mature and com-
mercialized technologies. AEC and PEM both operate at low temperatures and have a fast
response time which allows them to be suitable for frequency regulation and grid services. Al-
kaline cells produce hydrogen with an efficiency of around 66% (LHV value) while PEM yields
a lower efficiency of around 58%. On top of that, PEM cells are more expensive than AEC
because of the catalyst material used as electrodes. Typically, PEM cells have a platinum cath-
ode and an iridium oxide anode which are more expensive and scarce materials compared to
the nickel-based electrodes in AEC. PEM cells are also more sensitive to impurities and the ex-
pected lifetime of a stack is around 20 years while, for AEC cells, the technical lifetime is around
25 years. Solid Oxide Cells is a novel technology that operates at higher temperatures, which
allows to production of hydrogen with an efficiency of around 77%, higher than both AEC and
PEM. However, SOEC presents durability issues as the stack is exposed to high temperatures
and the expected lifetime is as low as 10 years with no real commercial plant in operation yet.
Because of the lower costs and a higher technical lifetime AEC electrolyzers are currently the
most deployed technology and, at the end of 2022, accounted for 60% of the total electrolysis
installed capacity. PEM followed with around 30% while SOEC represented less than 1% of the
total installed capacity. Analyzing the data collected from the Brintbanchen, AEC appears as
the preferred choice for the electrolyzer cells of future Danish projects, with PEM being limited
by the higher costs and lower efficiency and SOEC not yet fully developed to a commercial
stage.

Renewbale Power Generation
The updated Renewable Energy Directive (RED II) by the European Commission dictates that
hydrogen qualifies as a Renewable Fuel Of Non-Biological Origin (RFNBO) only when produced
using electricity that aligns with the principle of additionality. This principle mandates that the
electricity utilized must be sourced from the same facility or a renewable Power Purchase Agree-
ment (PPA). Furthermore, the power installation must be relatively new, and operational for less
than 36 months prior to the electrolyzer’s use. Additionally, from 2030, a strict temporal match-
ing of hydrogen production to the generation of renewable electricity is required, save for certain
exceptions based on regional renewable energy prevalence or low electricity emission intensity.
Therefore, in order to consider the hydrogen produced through electrolysis actually green, the
electricity consumed by the plants has to be sourced from renewable installations. Because of
the additionality criterion, in this work, only power generated from wind and solar is considered

3.4
District Heating
Another sector interlinked with the development of the PtX industry is the District Heating sys-
tem. In Denmark, today, 64% of private households are connected to the district heating. In
total, the annual heat production from district heating amounts to 67 PJ in the six main areas
built around the largest cities and 53 PJ in the other 400 smaller and decentralized areas [20].
Waste heat can be handled through the district heating system, transporting energy through
heated or chilled water from a place of production to a place of consumption.
Alkaline and PEM electrolyzers operate at temperatures around 80°C and the heat produced
from the plants is well suited for integration into district heating.
As explained in section 3.2, for the electrolysis reaction to occur, a minimum voltage is required
to provide the necessary energy. The energy demand for the water-splitting reaction is given
by the enthalpy of the reaction (∆H), following Gibbs Free Energy equation:
∆H = ∆G + T∆S
(3.14)
At adiabatic conditions, the cell operates at the so-called thermo-neutral voltage (Vtn) defined
as :
Vtn = ∆H
zF
(3.15)
Where z is the number of electrons transferred in the electrochemical reaction (2) and F is the
Faraday constant. Because the cells operate at a voltage always higher heat is generated as
a function of the difference between the operating voltage and the thermo neutral voltage as
shown in the equation below:
Qgen = NI(Vcell −Vtn)
(3.16)
A part of this heat generated is lost via heat radiation and it must be continuously removed to limit
the temperature inside the cell stack and ensure the right operating conditions are maintained.
While almost 5% of the input energy is estimated as unrecoverable losses, the other portion of
the heat generated can be recovered and potentially used for district heating [21]. The amount
of heat losses recoverable varies based on the electrolyzer technology. For AEC the potential
heat recoverable amounts to 16.4% of the total input, whereas for PEM it reaches 26.4%. The

3.8
Discussion
The potential that lies in the future PtX sector in Denmark is vast. While technological ad-
vancements are promising, the integration with Denmark’s existing power system poses both
challenges and opportunities.
32
Unlocking Danish Green Hydrgoen Production
==PAGE:40==
Based on the projects announced and the national goals, in the span of five years, the total
capacity installed could increase fivefold and reach 6.5 GW in 2030. Compared to the current
demand for electricity, the share of consumption in 2030 could already top 20% of the total de-
mand. These numbers are destined to increase up to the point where the PtX sector alone will
represent more than 50% of the electricity demand in Denmark.
To achieve these figures, accurate planning will be required to integrate in the most efficient
way possible the electrolyzers to the power system.
To produce green hydrogen as defined by the European Union, the availability of cheap and
abundant renewable power will be crucial for the development of the PtX industry. In this analy-
sis, the Levelized Cost of Electricity was used as the cost figure for the electricity used as input
by the electrolyzers. This allows to analyze the LCOH solely from a cost perspective, without
taking into account the electricity markets and the fluctuations of the electricity price. Bearing
in mind the criteria of additionality, green hydrogen produced will be required to stipulate Power
Purchase Agreements (PPAs) with new and unsupported sustainable energy capacity. This
could result in a less volatile price for the electricity at values closer to the actual LCOE of the
new renewable energy plants installed. This considered, the LCOE represents a fair metric for
the input price of the electricity used by the electrolyzers without taking into account the high
volatility of the electricity markets that would lead to high uncertainties on the final LCOH as the
future of the markets is thoroughly unpredictable.
Because of the abundance of wind, offshore power will represent the highest share of electricity
generation in the future Danish power system. However, installations of offshore wind turbines
are associated with higher installation and maintenance costs. The LCOE of electricity gener-
ated offshore currently sits at more than 60 EUR/MWh, while electricity generated through solar
panels costs 47 EUR/MWh. Learning curves and economy of scale will contribute in bringing
both LOCEs down.
However offshore electricity will remain more expensive than the solar counterpart. Therefore
producing hydrogen in Denmark could be more expensive than in other countries where solar
is much more abundant.
In the beginning stages, producing green hydrogen in Denmark could cost up to 7 EUR/kg. This
value is on the higher end compared to the figures found in the literature and it much depends
on the availability of renewable power. Assuming only the electricity in excess will be used to
produce hydrogen could mean Full Load Hours in the range of 800 hours per year. This very
low capacity factor is one of the main reasons behind the high Levelized Cost of Hydrogen.
As for the LCOE, learning curves will play an important role in bringing the costs down and
closer to the current prices of grey hydrogen. However, the availability of renewable power will
have the highest impact on the final LCOH. As the Danish power system develops and more
green capacity is installed the expected FLH of the PtX sector in Denmark will increase leading
to lower operational costs. In 2030, producing green hydrogen could cost 3.9 EUR/kg, and, de-
veloping with time, the value could fall below 3 EUR/kg in 2040 reaching 2.75 EUR/kg in 2050.
The availability of renewable power could affect these figures significantly. The results of the
sensitivity analysis show the range between which the Danish Levelized Cost of Hydrogen will
lay. In a High RES scenario, the LCOH could already be as low as 4.8 EUR/kg in 2025 and
reach 3.1 EUR/kg in 2030. On the other hand, producing green hydrogen could cost up to 4.6
EUR/kg in 2030 assuming a scenario with scarce availability of green power. In this analysis,

is defined as the rate of the mass flow along the pipeline axis (x). This term is influenced by the

On the other hand, the conservation of momentum captures the dynamics of the gas flow, bal-
ancing the inertia of the gas with the pressure gradient force and resistive force due to friction.
In Equation 4.4, the inertia term indicates the momentum of the gas within the pipeline and it is
described as the rate of change of the mass flow (m) over time (t). The pressure gradient term
describes the main driving force in the gas flow and is expressed as the change in pressure (π)
along the pipeline length (x) multiplied by the area of the section of the pipeline (A). The force
derived from the pressure gradient is opposed by the frictional force, which is a function of the
frictional factor (λ), the speed of sound in the gas (c) the diameter of the pipeline (D), and the
ratio between the mass flow rate and the pressure in the pipeline.
Together, these two equations are fundamental for understanding and describing the gas flow in
pipelines. To solve the PDEs, numerical methods are used to transform the equations into alge-
braic expressions discretizing time and space. In particular, for this work, the spatial derivates
are discretized by dividing the pipelines into segments of the same length introducing auxiliary
nodes between the original pipelines. The nodes of the gas network are denoted as i and j and
the length of the pipeline segments is thus referred to as ∆xi,j.

Results

Discussion
As the potential hydrogen production in Denmark vastly exceeds the demand, developing a
solid infrastructure is crucial for Denmark in order to achieve the goals set in the Climate Act.

As seen in section 4.1, the cost associated with transporting the green hydrogen to Germany
decreases as the volume of hydrogen exported increases.
The Danish Hydrogen Backbone will be built to meet the future needs of the system. Invest-
ments of 1.32 billion euros are estimated for building the first hydrogen network in Denmark and
future-proof it. Therefore, in the beginning phase, as the utilization factor of the H2 infrastruc-
ture, calculated as the ratio between the volume of hydrogen exported and the total capacity of
the network, could be as low as 8% leading to a high transport cost. In 2030, as the Danish Hy-
drogen Backbone becomes operational, the additional cost of exporting hydrogen could reach
figures as high as 1 EUR/kg. This is a value that has to be considered when talking about the
possibility of transporting hydrogen to Germany as it represents a significant share of the total
Levelized Cost of Hydrogen. T However, as the PtX sector ramps up and additional green H2
is produced, the transport cost falls down to 0.1 EUR/kg. When this cost is achieved, exporting
hydrogen from the Danish PtH2 plants to the German industries will not represent a bottleneck
to the development of the Power-to-X sector in Denmark.
As the transport costs vastly depend on the volume of hydrogen exported, consideration of how
to maximize the flow rate through the pipelines and ensure that the demand is met will be crucial.
The storage facility in Lille Turop and the pipelines could act as short and long-term solutions to
store green H2 produced in excess at times when renewable power is higher. During low peaks
of wind and solar production, the stored gas could then be discharged and sent to Germany to
compensate for the low availability of renewable power used to produce hydrogen.

Conclusion and Further work
This master’s thesis explored the future landscape of the Danish PtH2 sector and the role that
Denmark could play in the new hydrogen economy. The Levelized Cost of Hydrogen and the
possibility of exporting it to Germany and the associated costs were assessed through a techno-
economic analysis and a NLP model.
Green Hydrogen Production
In the future Danish energy system, green hydrogen production will assume a pivotal role. Fol-
lowing the additionally criteria, the electricity consumption of the PtX sector could reach more
than 50% of the total consumption. A rapid upscale in electrolyzer installed capacity must be fol-
lowed by equal growth in renewable power generation to produce green hydrogen as RFNBO.
The production volume of green hydrogen is expected to hit 5 TWh by 2030 and could potentially
see a tenfold surge, reaching 50 TWh by 2050. The results of the techno-economic analysis
show that, in Denmark, producing hydrogen could cost up to 7 EUR/kg in the first stages with
the figure developing below 3.0 EUR/kg by 2040.
One of the main factors that influence the cost of producing renewable hydrogen is the availabil-
ity of cheap green electricity. Connecting the PtH2 plants to new renewable electricity generation
capacity through PPAs will be crucial to ensure that the electricity used is effectively green and
to limit the cost of electricity as close to the LCOE as possible. Abundant renewable power will
be key in ensuring a high capacity factor for the electrolyzers. Depending on the scenario, the
Levelized Cost of hydrogen could vary between a range of 2.5 EUR/kg in the beginning phase.
Indeed, with abundant renewable energy generation, the LOCH could be as low as 4.8 EUR/kg
in 2025 already. On the other hand, in a scarce green electricity supply scenario, producing
hydrogen could cost above 7 EUR/kg in 2025. Integrating the PtH2 plants to the district heating
system will result in additional revenues for the projects depending on the heat demand and the
size of the systems. This should be taken into account in the planning phase of the project to
build a more efficient and integrated energy system.
Green Hydrogen Export
As the volume of demand for hydrogen in Denmark will represent only a small fraction of the
total expected production volume, export will play a critical role in the development of the indus-
try. Analyzing the hydrogen infrastructure plan announced by Energinet for 2030, it becomes
evident how important the hydrogen backbone will be in the future of the Danish PtH2. With
Germany’s industries as the main off-takers of green hydrogen, cost-effectively transporting hy-
drogen will be crucial to establishing the role of Denmark as an export country in the hydrogen
market. With 360 km of pipelines, connecting electrolyzers starting from the storage facility in
Lille Turop and extending down to Flensborg in Germany, the Danish Hydrogen Infrastructure
will represent a pillar investment for the whole sector with a total export capacity of 55 TWh/y.
In the initial phase, because of the low production volume, the utilization factor of the infrastruc-
ture could result as low as 8% of the total capacity. As a consequence, in the first years, the
cost of exporting hydrogen to Germany could represent a significant share of the total amount.
In 2030, exporting hydrogen to Germany could cost 0.85 EUR/kg, which added to the LCOH will
result in a total hydrogen cost of 4.7 EUR/kg. The additional tariff to be paid in order to export
hydrogen to Germany could represent 17% of the total cost. As the sector develops, the volume
of hydrgoen exported increases and with that, the cost of transporting it falls. By 2040, before
the possible expansion of the infrastructure, transporting hydrogen through pipelines could cost

Storage of green hydrgoen could play an important role in reducing the cost of exporting hydro-
gen to Germany and ensuring that the maximum volume of gas is exported.
The model presented in this thesis showcases how different modeling solutions for the gas flow
dynamics can affect the total volume of hydrogen transported through the pipelines.
Shifting the pressure within the pipelines allows to store the hydrogen produced in excess within
the pipes and discharged at times when the supply alone is not sufficient to meet the demand.
The infrastructure itself could then be used as short-term storage. Exploiting the linepack po-
tential of the pipelines could lead to a demand curtailment 70% lower compared to steady-state
conditions.
Furthermore, the salt caverns in Lille Turop, with a total capacity of 2500 tonsH2 per facility,
could serve as another important storage solution. Implementing the behavior of the storage
facility in the Quasi-Dynamic model could reduce 50% of the total volume demand curtailed
based on the input data used in the models.
These storage possibilities are worthy of consideration when modeling the hydrogen flow be-
havior within the pipelines with the scope of maximizing the volume transported and therefore
reducing the transportation costs.
Further Work
While this thesis provides a solid overview of the future landscape of the hydrogen economy in
Denmark both from a production and transport side, much more can be done to investigate the
benefits and costs associated with green hydrogen production in Denmark.
The LCOE was the only metric used as electricity price in the techno-economic analysis in
chapter 3, however, the price at which PtH2 plants buy the electricity may be very different.
Predicting the development of the electricity price is always rich in uncertainties but is not trivial
when assessing the Levelized Cost of Hydrogen. Day-Ahead prices or PPAs could be used to
estimate the cost of producing green hydrogen in a more realistic manner.
Moreover, accounting for the different operating conditions of each hydrogen production facility
could lead to different outcomes in terms of the volume of hydrogen produced and operating
costs. More detailed models to simulate the operation of the electrolyzers can be implemented
to achieve accurate results and simulate the real working conditions of the plants.
For the analysis of the transport costs in chapter 4, additional data regarding the demand for
green hydrogen have to be considered. The willingness to buy green H2 will play a crucial role
in driving down the supply costs. Accounting for a more flexible demand, both in terms of price
and volume will represent the system in a more realistic scenario. As the hydrogen demand in
Denmark will be limited compare to the production volumes, monitoring the cost of producing
hydrogen and exporting it will be crucial in ensuring the competitiveness of Danish green hydro-
gen compared to other countries with different power systems and therefore different production
costs.
In the models presented in Table 4.2, compressors are not considered. However, this compo-
nent is necessary for the operation of the pipelines in quasi-dynamic conditions and could add
limitations to the model. Adding this component to the sets of variables and constraints will help
in studying a more truthful scenario.
Furthermore, considering the costs associated with the production of hydrogen but also the
costs of operating the pipelines and the storage facility could influence significantly the results
of the model. Investigating, what are the cost-benefits of operating the hydrogen backbone as
a means of short-term storage could provide a more in-depth analysis of the Danish Hydrgoen
Backbone. Considering different production costs for each PtH2 plant could also be valuable to
assess the impact that the H2 infrastructure could have in lowering the cost of exporting green
hydrogen to Germany.
