Abstract
In recent years, with the development of AR, VR, gaming, and the film industry, the gen­
eration and editing of diverse 3D assets have become an important research area. 3D mesh
texture generation has always been a significant research direction within the field of 3D
asset generation and editing. Historically, 3D asset editing has been a labor­intensive
task. To simplify the technical threshold and operational difficulty of 3D asset editing,
automatic 3D texture generation and editing based on text input have become a critical
topic in the 3D generation field. With the successful application of diffusion models in
text­to­image generation, using diffusion models for texture generation and editing in 3D
has become a research hotspot. The mainstream methods currently include using diffusion
models for texture inpainting and employing classifier­free guidance to achieve score dis­
tillation sampling for 3D texture generation. In this master thesis, we propose a novel
wavelet­based representation method based on the score distillation sampling method.
First, we validated the feasibility of our method on the 2D prompt­guided image edit­
ing task. Then, we proposed a wavelet­based tri­plane representation method and intro­
duced a global­local texture generation mechanism, demonstrating the advantages of our
method in 3D texture generation, especially for complex object textures. Finally, we ap­
plied the proposed method to 3D texture editing, achieving precise editing of specific high
or low frequencies. We present qualitative and quantitative experimental results compar­
ing our method with baseline methods in 2D editing tasks, 3D texture generation tasks,
and 3D texture editing tasks, proving that our method outperforms the current best score­
distillation­based 2D editing methods and significantly exceeds the performance of SDS
methods in 3D texture generation and editing. Finally, we discuss the limitations of our
method and future research directions.
Overall, this thesis proposes a novel wavelet­based image representation method and tri­
plane representation method, allowing for SDS optimization targeting specific frequencies
in both 2D and 3D. Our method introduces wavelets into the field of mesh texture repre­
sentation for the first time. I believe our findings will advance the development of 2D
image editing and 3D texture generation and editing.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Generating high­quality 3D content has always been a crucial task in computer vision,
especially for future digital media such as films, games, and AR/VR technologies. In the
past, 3D assets were mainly manually designed and textured in 3D modeling software
like Blender and Maya3D. These processes are not only labor­intensive but also demand
high requirements from designers, necessitating significant learning time and professional
expertise. Recent advancements in 2D content generation have seen significant success,
thanks to the emergence of generative artificial intelligence and diffusion models such as
Stable Diffusion [1], and Imagen [2]. Text­based image generation models now facilitate
high­quality, diverse, and user­controllable image synthesis.
One highly intuitive approach to asset generation in 3D involves leveraging diffusion mod­
els that have already been validated in 2D. With this idea, the demand to extend 2D content
generation into 3D space is growing rapidly, yet we still face numerous challenges. While
2D diffusion models are trained on extensive 2D data, 3D data is inherently scarce and
challenging to collect and annotate. Consequently, synthetic samples generated by 3D
generative models trained on 3D data exhibit limited quality and diversity in both struc­
ture and texture. Various methods have been proposed to address this issue in different
3D asset generation tasks, which we will discuss further below.
First, let’s introduce the current main research directions in 3D asset generation. Lever­
aging 2D diffusion priors for 3D asset generation has led to many diverse tasks in the
current research landscape. These tasks primarily include text­to­3D and text­guided 3D
texture generation. Text­to­3D not only focuses on generating high­quality textures for
objects but also on generating the geometric shapes of objects. Therefore, it often fails to
produce very high­quality 3D textures and geometric shapes. Moreover, 3D assets gen­
erated using this method tend to be memory­intensive, containing numerous vertices and
faces, making them difficult to edit and manipulate for subsequent asset operations. Con­
sequently, the 3D assets obtained using current methods cannot be effectively applied to
future applications. Hence, this paper focuses on another 3D asset generation task, namely
prompt­guided mesh texturization. In this task, we do not alter the geometric shape of the
input mesh but generate high­quality textures based on fixed mesh geometry priors and
support partial texture editing by users. Recently, many studies have used various meth­
ods to achieve high­quality texture generation, which can be categorized into two main
branches.
The first branch involves directly utilizing the outputs of 2D diffusion models conditioned

on depth or normals to obtain the corresponding textures from the camera’s viewpoint.
This is achieved by inverse projecting the mask from the corresponding viewpoint onto
the UV map. In 2023, Elad et al. and Dave et al. first introduced this approach in their
TEXTure [3] and Text2Tex [4] methods. They utilized depth as a condition to provide ge­
ometric information to the diffusion model and obtained rendered images, masks, and po­
sitional descriptions of prompts for the corresponding camera viewpoints. Subsequently,
they directly used depth­aware 2D diffusion to obtain the diffusion model results for the
corresponding camera viewpoints and projected the results back onto the UV map based on
the previously obtained mask. Through this method, these models can achieve the process
of mesh texturization relatively quickly and with high quality. However, these methods
also encounter several issues. Firstly, they use a large guidance scale during the diffusion
stage of texture generation and a smaller scale in subsequent optimization stages. This can
make it difficult to correct erroneous texture results generated in the diffusion stage during
the optimization stage, as demonstrated in the failure case shown in fig. 1.1a. Secondly,
when objects have many occluded faces or there is not much overlap between the results
of two camera renderings during the camera initialization process, the resulting texture
may exhibit Multi­face Janus problem [5] or inconsistencies between different faces of
the same object. Failure cases addressing this issue are shown in fig. 2.8.

Another branch involves attempting to utilize the powerful 2D diffusion priors to update
parameters in 3D, essentially distilling knowledge from pre­trained 2D diffusion mod­
els. Consequently, this method is referred to as Score Distillation Sampling (SDS) [6]
technique. In this project, we adopt a mature SDS method to achieve text­guided mesh
texturization and editing and aim to enhance existing SDS methods to address their short­
comings.

1.2
Objective
The objective of this project is to achieve text­guided 3D model texturization using Score
distillation sampling (SDS) while also proposing a novel SDS method based on frequency
domain research to facilitate partial texture editing in complex 3D scenes. This involves
the following sub­goals:
1. Prompt­guided 2D image partial editing. To identify the limitations of SDS in
certain complex scenarios, we initially employ SDS for texture generation and par­
tial editing in 2D. Since the SDS method in 2D aims to guide the image distribution
closer to the diffusion model results, and SDS in 3D similarly updates parameters
to progressively align the rendered 3D results with the diffusion results, there are
parallels between 2D and 3D. However, Vanilla SDS performs poorly in complex
scenes and character representations. Through experimentation, we observe that
SDS and its improved algorithm, Delta denoising score (DDS) [7], Contrastive de­
noising score (CDS) [8], struggle to preserve the details and colors effectively in

Background
Score distillation sampling (SDS) is an optimization method that applies pretrained 2D
diffusion model priors to guide tasks such as 2D image editing and 3D generation. In
essence, SDS utilizes probabilistic density distillation by minimizing the Kullback­Leibler
(KL) divergence between the distribution of rendered images obtained and the distribution
results from the pretrained diffusion model. This chapter will introduce the fundamental
principles of SDS, discuss the latest improvements in 2D image editing methods based
on SDS. Finally, I will outline major 3D texture generation methods, including recent
advancements like the Text2tex method, and how we apply SDS for texture generation.
2.1
Score distillation sampling

classifier­free guidance (CFG) [9] is. Suppose we already have a pretrained diffusion
model; we can naturally use the pretrained denoising network to predict the unconditional
score [10] from the noisy sample xt sampled from noise distribution P (xT) in timestep t.

2.2.1
Delta denoising score
In the previous section, we discussed the fundamental principles of SDS. While SDS has
shown remarkable effectiveness in generating 3D assets, it often results in outputs that are
blurry. This issue is particularly pronounced when using SDS for initializing and optimiz­
ing existing images, leading to significant blurriness in image elements, as illustrated in
fig. 2.2.
Figure 2.2: Use SDS for image editing
To address this issue, Amir and colleagues proposed a new diffusion­based scoring tech­
nique to optimize parameter models for image editing tasks. Unlike previous SDS meth­
ods, the Delta denoising score (DDS) method introduces additional reference image­text
pairs where the text matches the content of the image. Consequently, the output score rep­
resents the difference between the original SDS result and the reference result. In essence,
DDS estimates the direction of the noisy gradients introduced by SDS using the reference
image­text pair. This allows us to eliminate negative optimization directions associated
with this estimate, resulting in clearer gradient directions. Through this approach, Delta
Score is introduced to provide effective gradients that can modify parts of the image being
edited while preserving other parts without needing to provide or compute masks.

2.3
Prompt­guided 3D texture generation and editing
Currently, with the rapid development of AR, VR, and the swift rise of the gaming and
film industries, the demand for 3D assets is increasing significantly. Consequently, in the
field of computer vision, text­guided 3D asset generation has made great strides, thanks
to the introduction of methods like SDS. Texture generation for 3D assets is also a crucial
research topic within the realm of 3D asset generation. In this domain, mesh data is typ­
ically used to represent 3D objects because it can more effectively convey object details
and complex shapes compared to voxels and point clouds [18, 19, 20]. Therefore, in this
project, the texturing of 3D objects mainly focuses on texturing 3D meshes.
To enhance the efficiency of mesh texturing, and benefiting from the success of current
2D diffusion models, automatic 3D texturing has become a significant research topic.
The mainstream approach for automatic 3D mesh texturing is text­guided mesh texturing
using text as prompts. This research topic can be divided into two main methods. The first
method directly utilizes pretrained 2D diffusion models to achieve texture inpainting, and
the results of 2D inpainting are back­projected onto the UV map according to the mask [3,
4]. The second method employs a distillation­based approach, optimizing the parameters
of mesh textures using the successful SDS method in text­to­3D without altering the 3D
geometry [21, 22].

2.3.1
Inpainting­based texture generation
This method directly employs pretrained 2D diffusion models and uses an iterative paint­
ing approach to generate textures. To ensure that the 2D diffusion model incorporates
3D information, these methods leverage depth information. They use diffusion models
conditioned on depth to generate textures for areas lacking texture on the mesh, while si­
multaneously considering the object’s geometric information. In this section, we will take
the method Text2tex [4] as an example to introduce the basic implementation principles
of this approach, as well as some of its limitations and failure cases.
Figure 2.7: Text2tex method pipeline
The fig. 2.7 above illustrates the pipeline of the Text2tex method, which can be divided into
two parts: generation and refinement. The core of both parts is how to generate textures
by painting missing regions on the surface. In the generation phase, Text2tex applies
a pretrained depth­to­image diffusion model, using the input text and depth geometric
information as cues to generate high­quality images. The authors use an inpainting mask
to progressively texture the entire mesh. This inpainting mask serves as a cue to guide
the diffusion process, indicating which areas should be generated and which areas should
remain fixed. This mask, used as the denoising guidance strategy, can be represented as
eq. (2.14):
ˆzt = ˆzt ⊙M + zt ⊙(1 −M)
(2.14)
Where M is the generation mask, zt is the noised latent code, and ˆzt is the denoised la­
tent estimation. Using the aforementioned Depth2Image model, the Text2tex method can
paint high­quality textures for a mesh from a single view. They project the generated 2D
views onto the texture space with proper UV parameterization based on the viewpoint
v = (θ, φ, r), assuming the Y­axis is the up­axis in the world coordinate system, θ is the
azimuth angle with respect to the Z­axis, Φ is the elevation angle with the respect to the
XZ­plane, and r is the distance between the viewpoint and the origin. Similar to the pro­
cess shown in fig. 2.7, the Text2tex method renders each selected viewpoint to obtain the
mask and depth map, using the depth map as a condition with the Depth2Image diffusion

Figure 2.9: Failure cases of Text2tex for complex objects
From the fig. 2.9 above, two main issues can be observed. For complex scenes like houses
and pine trees, as well as complex objects with fine details such as characters, the Text2tex
method tends to have texture errors. The primary reason for this is related to the cumu­
lative errors I mentioned earlier. In the generation stage, Text2tex typically uses a larger
guidance strength for the New regions to perform denoising, while a relatively smaller
strength is used for the Update regions and the subsequent refinement stage. However,
in the Update and Keep regions, due to the smaller denoising strength, it is difficult for
the Text2tex method to make significant modifications and updates to the previously gen­
erated pixels. This leads to a problem where if an error occurs in the texture generation
for the New regions at any step, these textures are difficult to correct in subsequent steps.
This can potentially affect the texture generation results of the Update regions and other
New regions, leading to a small texture error causing increasingly larger impacts. Another
issue is that for objects with fine details, such as characters, the Text2tex method cannot
accurately capture detailed information, such as correctly generating the colors of the eyes
and mouth. The main reason for this is that Text2tex uses a Depth2Image model with the
condition being the depth obtained from rendering the mesh. When the viewpoints are far

Method
In this chapter, I will introduce the improvements we proposed for 2D image editing and
3D texture generation and editing. First, I will present the research and pipeline we devel­
oped for 2D texture generation before the midterm. Inspired by that method, we designed a
global­local updating strategy to ensure both texture consistency and detailed local texture
generation for 3D texture generation.
To implement this global­local update strategy and address the issue of losing detailed
information for complex objects in 3D texture generation, as well as to overcome the
limitations of SDS in partial texture editing and specific frequency editing, we proposed
a novel SDS method based on wavelet representation. We first introduced WaveDDS in
2D image editing to validate our hypothesis. Then, we proposed using a wavelet­based
tri­plane to represent 3D texture features. We conducted experiments in both 2D editing
and 3D texture generation and editing to demonstrate the effectiveness of our method.
Overall, this chapter will cover the following topics:
1. We achieve more detailed image texture generation on 2D images. Inspired by the
experimental results of this method, we will use a global­local update approach for
texture generation on meshes in subsequent 3D texture generation.
2. To verify the effectiveness of wavelet representation in SDS optimization for spe­
cific frequency updates, we propose a new method called WaveDDS, based on the
DDS method for 2D image editing. This method demonstrates the effectiveness of
wavelets in SDS optimization.
3. Based on the findings in 2D and the validation of the effectiveness of wavelet rep­
resentation, we designed a new mesh texture representation method for 3D. We val­
idated this method’s effectiveness in tasks such as texture generation and specific
frequency texture editing.

3.3.1
Design of mesh texture representation method
Modern Graphics Processing Units (GPUs) create 3D graphics using rasterization­based
rendering techniques. This involves projecting each face of a 3D mesh onto a 2D screen
and determining the color of the projected face, typically a triangle, by interpolating
the colors of its three vertices. The rendering process used by GPUs is inherently non­
differentiable. To address this issue, many differentiable renderers have been proposed
[35, 36]. In this paper, we use the Pytorch library nvdiffrast [37] as the differentiable
renderer. This method has been successfully used in [38] to render RGB images from
arbitrary camera views.

(3.6)

Where p is the camera pose, gΘ(·) is the differentiable rendering function, and x is the
rendered image. In the forward mesh rendering process, for each vertex v of the mesh, the
rasterizer maps it to the corresponding pixel on the 2D screen. This way, the pixel cor­
responding to the vertex can directly query the UV coordinates from the texture map to
obtain the texture. For the pixels in the area between two vertices, the rasterizer uses inter­
polation to determine the UV coordinates of these pixels and retrieves the corresponding
interpolated positions and textures from the texture map, thereby obtaining the correspond­
ing textures. This allows us to determine the color of each pixel on the 2D screen. In the
texture fitting process, we compute the loss from the rendered image of the target mesh
and the input mesh:
L = ∥x −xinput∥2

Then we can backpropagate the loss to optimize the feature encoder and RGB decoder.

4.3
Evaluation methods
Following the evaluation metrics used in the baseline papers we are comparing with, we
use CLIPScore [51], Structural Similarity Index Measure (SSIM) [52], and Learned Per­
ceptual Image Patch Similarity (LPIPS) [53] to primarily analyze the performance of our
method in 2D image and 3D texture generation and editing tasks. CLIPScore is mainly
used to evaluate the correlation between the image and the input text prompt, while SSIM
and LPIPS are used to assess the similarity between the generated image and the reference
image.
4.3.1
CLIPScore
CLIPScore is a method that uses the Contrastive Language­Image Pretraining (CLIP)
model [54] to calculate the semantic consistency between an image and a text prompt.
Through the CLIP model, CLIPScore maps the image and text into the same embedding
space and calculates the cosine similarity between them:
CLIP −S(c, v) = w ∗max(cos(c, v), 0)
(4.1)
Where they set w = 2.5 in the paper, c is the textual CLIP embedding for the candidate
caption, and n is the visual CLIP embedding for the image. The greater the similarity
between the image and the text prompt, the greater the cosine similarity between the image
embedding and the text embedding vectors. Thus, a higher CLIPScore indicates that the
image better matches the description of the prompt.
4.3.2
SSIM
SSIM, known as the Structural Similarity Index Measure, is an index used to measure the
structural similarity between two images of the same size. Unlike traditional metrics such
as Mean Squared Error (MSE) or Peak Signal­to­Noise Ratio (PSNR), SSIM focuses more
on the structural information of the images. It evaluates the similarity by comparing lumi­
nance, contrast, and structure, thus better aligning with human visual perception. Given
two images, x and y, their SSIM can be expressed as:
SSIM(x, y) = [l(x, y)]α · [c(x, y)]β · [s(x, y)]γ
(4.2)
Where α, β, and γ are set to 1 in the paper, I(x, y) is the luminance comparison, used to
measure the average luminance difference between the two images, c(x, y) is the contrast
comparison used to measure the contrast difference between the images, and s(x, y) is
structural comparison used to measure the local structural similarity of the images. They

Our basic parameter settings follow the DDS method source code parameters, and we use
the default mother wavelet db3 as the mother wavelet type. We made some modifications
to the iteration numbers, mainly increasing the iteration numbers based on different appli­
cations. In the DDS source code, the iteration numbers are set to 200, but in our method,
since we use wavelet coefficients to represent the image and restrict the update of certain
parameters, the optimization process requires more iterations to achieve better results.
Regarding the wavelet level, which is the number of times the wavelet decomposition is
performed, we set it according to different applications. For tasks that retain low frequen­
cies while changing high frequencies, we set a larger J to ensure that there are not too
many low­frequency coefficients restricting the update of high­frequency subbands. For
tasks that retain high frequencies and update low frequencies, we set a relatively smaller J
to ensure that there are enough low­frequency components that can be updated to change
the color of the target in the image.

Parameter settings
The main parameter settings in the Wavelet­based Tri­plane texture representation imple­

• Number of iterations: 4000 for SDS optimization, 1000 for texture fitting if we do
editing task
• Guidance scale for classifier­free guidance: 100
• Wavelet level, parameter J in section 3.3.1.2: 4 for high­frequency editing and 3 for
low­frequency editing
• Mother wavelet: coif4 wavelet
• Batch size during training: 1
• [t_min, t_max] for diffusion model: [25, 500] for ControlNet­based SDS, and [25,900]
for Vanilla SDS
• Learning rate: 0.01 for feature encoder and 0.001 for MLP color decoder
• Stable Diffusion model: stabilityai/stable­diffusion­2­1­base for SDS and runwayml/stable­
diffusion­v1­5 for ControlNet­based SDS
• Tri­plane setting: plane size = 256 × 256, feature dimensions = 64
• Optimizer: Adam

Listing 4.2: The wavelet representation for Tri­plane
52
Prompt­guided mesh texturization
==PAGE:65==
In listing 4.2, we use the same Pytorch wavelet library as in the 2D implementation to
perform wavelet transform and inverse transform. This Pytorch library, provided by [57],
supports gradient backpropagation. In the DWTEncoder class, we define the wavelet­
based Triplane. During the initialization phase, we use torch.nn.init.uniform_ to initialize
the three planes. This initialization method fills the input tensor with values sampled from
the continuous uniform distribution.
Next, we use the wavelet transform with coif4 as the mother wavelet to decompose the
initial planes, obtaining the corresponding high­frequency subbands and low­frequency
subbands. The choice of the mother wavelet follows the explanation in [46], balancing
performance and speed. After completing the wavelet decomposition, we parameterize
all the decomposed subbands and place them into grids.
In the forward method, we use idwt_transform to reconstruct the planes from the param­
eterized wavelet coefficients. In idwt_transform, we use all levels of wavelet coefficients
and the N­1 level wavelet coefficients to construct the fine and coarse planes. Then, in
forward, we project the coordinates x from the 3D space onto the three planes and use
F.grid_sample for interpolation to obtain the fine and coarse feature values feat and feat2.
Finally, we concatenate these two feature values to obtain the final feature. Similar to
the implementation in 2D, if we only want to update high­frequency texture, we can use
detach() to avoid the gradient back­propagate to the low subband like the yl in grids.

5
Discussion and conclusion
5.1
Conclusion
In this project, we proposed a novel wavelet­based representation method for mesh texture
generation and editing, applying wavelet transform frequency decomposition to specific
frequency update tasks for special tasks. In the chapter 1, we described the importance
and significance of texture generation in the 3D generation field, and we also introduced
several common methods for 3D texture generation. In the chapter 2, we delved into the
basic background knowledge related to this project, such as Score Distillation Sampling
(SDS) and its application in 2D image editing and 3D texture generation. Following this,
in the chapter 3, we first identified the issues encountered with applying SDS to 2D tex­
ture generation and proposed a new pipeline to address the problem of local information
loss. Using this solution as our intuition, we aimed to develop a similar method for 3D
that applies a global­local texture generation mechanism to generate textures for complex
objects such as human bodies. However, during the implementation process, we found
that adding the global­local texture generation mechanism to Vanilla SDS, although it
significantly improved texture detail, led to a loss of overall texture information such as
color perception when generating local textures. To address this issue, we proposed us­
ing a wavelet­based representation for the optimization target. Leveraging the frequency
decomposition characteristics of wavelet transform, we can select specific frequencies for
updating, ensuring both global color consistency and local detail generation. To imple­
ment this idea, we first conducted experiments in 2D. In section 3.2, we proposed a new
method called WaveDDS based on the DDS method, aiming to use wavelet representation
for the optimization target and select the parameters we need to update according to the
corresponding high/low­frequency editing tasks. We demonstrated in 2D that our method
outperforms the DDS and CDS baseline methods, validating the correctness of our idea,
we conducted a quantitative analysis in the previous six tasks, our method showed an av­
erage improvement of 44.15% in LPIPS compared to DDS, and an average improvement
of 34.20% compared to CDS. Subsequently, in section 3.3, we proposed a wavelet­based
tri­plane texture representation method in 3D. According to our proposed method, we can
generate high­frequency or low­frequency textures by selecting the corresponding wavelet
coefficients to update. Therefore, we combined this new representation method with the
global­local texture generation mechanism. This combination allows us to use global per­
ception to first generate low­frequency color textures during the global stage, and then
use local detail perception to generate high­frequency details during the local generation
stage while maintaining global color consistency. We validated the effectiveness of our
method on human objects, the CLIPScore for our method is 0.52% higher than the vanilla
Prompt­guided mesh texturization
63
==PAGE:76==
SDS method and 0.90% higher than SDS with global­local texture generation. Finally,
we applied our representation to partial texture editing tasks on meshes. We demonstrated
that our method is significantly superior to the SDS method in both low­frequency color
editing and high­frequency detail generation tasks, we conducted a quantitative evalua­
tion of the 3D texture editing tasks. In both high­frequency and low­frequency editing,
our method showed an average improvement of 30.85% in LPIPS and 11.18% in SSIM
compared to the SDS with global­local texture generation method, while maintaining a
similar CLIPScore.
5.2
Limitations and future work
Although our method has been validated in both 2D and 3D as having significant advan­
tages over previous baseline methods, there are still some limitations that can be addressed
in future research.
1. We have demonstrated that our proposed method can perform updates for specific
frequencies in score distillation­based methods. However, the score distillation method
we currently use for texture generation is the SDS method, which has been shown
to result in blurred final outcomes [58, 23]. Therefore, our texture generation qual­
ity is still not sufficiently realistic and tends to be blurry. To address this issue, we
can apply the latest state­of­the­art method, VSD [6], to texture generation tasks in
future research to achieve better texture quality. What’s more, the size of feature
planes is 256 × 256 × 64 in our project, which also causes the limitation in texture
quality, we also can try to play these parameters and choose the best size and feature
dimension for feature planes to get the best texture quality and highest resolution.
2. Due to time constraints in the project, we only conducted quantitative evaluations
of the generated results. However, since this is a visual task, the best approach
would be to generate more results and test on more objects, followed by a user
study. This is especially important for 3D texture generation tasks. For example,
in the quantitative analysis of texture generation tasks we previously presented, we
calculated CLIPScore for three methods. Although the SDS with global­local gen­
eration method produces higher quality textures, its CLIPScore is still lower than
Vanilla SDS. Therefore, for such tasks, it is crucial to let users directly judge which
method can produce the best texture quality. Additionally, we have only validated
the effectiveness of our proposed method and have not yet tuned parameters such
as wavelet level, mother wavelet, and iteration numbers. Therefore, future research
should include ablation studies to compare different wavelet levels, mother wavelets,
and iteration numbers.
3. Our current optimization strategy is the global­local texture generation strategy,

==PAGE:77==
which involves first generating global color information and then adding local de­
tails. Although our method better preserves global color, it still results in some in­
consistencies. However, if we perform another global update after the global­local
process, we can achieve more consistent colors but might lose some high­frequency
information fig. 4.15. To address this issue, future research should adopt a similar
update strategy used in our 2D texture generation algorithm 1, alternating between
global and local updates rather than a step­by­step approach.
