Abstract
This thesis aims to investigate the ﬁre dynamics within a ventilated cavity, by combining experimental testing
and computational simulations. It will investigate heat transfer, ﬂame spread and material behaviour during
various ﬁre sizes. The thesis is motivated by previous catastrophic events, and aims to study the ﬁres behaviour
in a small scale ventilated cavity system.
A controlled experiment was constructed to replicate a ventilated cavity using combustible timber studs and
non-combustible Promatect-H cladding. This setup allowed multiple tests by only replacing the timber studs.
Methane gas was let through a mass ﬂow controller, allowing for a controlled Heat Release Rate (HRR), with the
resulting ﬂames emitting from a sandburner. Measurements were obtained using a oxygen consumption calorime-
ter, combustion gas analyser, thin-skinned calorimeters (TSC), and thermocouples. Tests were conducted with
sandburner inputs of 10, 20, 30, and 40 kW HRR to investigate thermal and combustion behaviour.
Simultaneously replications of the experimental setup were conducted using the simulation tool Fire Dynamics
Simulator (FDS). The simulation results aligned with measured surface and gas temperatures, conﬁrming valid
simulation of these aspects. At higher heat release rates, the absence of pyrolysis modelling led to discrepancies.
Incident radiant heat ﬂux and near wall gas temperatures exhibited poor correlation, likely due to equipment
limitations and assumptions applied during TSC calibration.
The ﬁndings in this thesis, support how vertical ﬁre spread accelerates within ventilated cavities, and how it
is diﬃcult to gather reliable data within a small cavity space. While FDS simulations proved reliable at low
HRR, its limitation to account for combustible materiels ignition at higher HRR highlights modelling gaps. This
study shows how a combined approach where experimental and numerical methods complement one another to
evaluate ﬁre spread in a ventilated cavity.

Introduction
Modern building design today is heavily inﬂuenced by factors such as energy eﬃciency, sustainability, dura-
bility, pricing and ﬁre safety when it comes to materials and construction methodologies. Among the many
developments within the building industry, the facade has evolved from a simple wall to a multi-layered system.
The main beneﬁts from the ventilated faced is the ability to passively control thermal comfort and moisture
control with the building envelope. However, the ventilated facades also present a concern regarding ﬁre safety,
especially when it comes to ﬁre spread. Flames can rapidly accelerate through the ventilated cavities, due to
the so called ”chimney eﬀect”.
The importance for studying ﬁres spread within ventilated facades has been highlighted by previous events. The
Grenfell Tower ﬁre in London (2017) demonstrated how a ventilated facade, containing combustible material,
could spread from the fourth ﬂoor to the 23rd ﬂoor, in approximately 12 minutes, before inevitably engulﬁng the
entire building, and taking the lives of 72 people. Similar cases, such as Kopervik Church ﬁre (2010) highlight
the need for research into ﬁre spread in ventilated facades. Incidents as these, especially after Grenfell Tower,
has lead to an increase in ﬁre safety regulations.
While ventilated facades are constructed to improve the eﬃciency of the building envelope, their ﬁre safety
performance remains challenging. Materials, insulation types and the ventilations geometry, all play a signiﬁ-
cant roll in the development of the ﬁre. Examinating diﬀerent cavity conﬁgurations within facades, by doing
experimental testing and computational simulations is necessary, to evaluate the ﬁre safety performance of the
ventilated facades.
1.1
Importance of Usage of Ventilated Facades
Over the last couple of decades, building envelopes have evolved from simple homogeneous walls to light, multi-
layered systems, often including ventilated cavities. These ventilated cavities improve thermal performance,
moisture control, and overall building eﬃciency by allowing controlled airﬂow [1]. As sustainability and energy
eﬃciency is becoming increasingly more important for new buildings, so does the ventilated cavity.
One of the main beneﬁts of the ventilated facade cavity is the ability to dry and regulate moisture within
the insulation material behind the rain screen. A naturally ventilated facade creates a temperature diﬀerence
between the cladding panel and the air cavity behind it. This causes a variation in air density, allowing air to
ﬂow upwards within the cavity. This airﬂow helps transport heat and moisture out through the openings at
the top of the wall, reducing humidity and improving insulation eﬃciency. During summer, the temperature
rising inside the cavity further increases this eﬀect. During winter, the ventilation rate through the ventilated
facade decreases, but the ventilated facade balances the interior-facing wall temperature, which reduces the risk
of moisture from condensation.

1.2
Previous Incidents Involving Ventilated Facades
Grenfell Tower Fire (2017)
In June 2017, Grenfell Tower in London, England, stood in ﬂames, claiming 72 lives [3]. The ﬁre erupted on
the fourth ﬂoor due to a faulty refrigerator before the ﬁre broke out of a window and entered the building’s new
exterior cladding system [4, 5]. Grenfell Tower had been refurbished with an aluminium composite material
(ACM) rainscreen cladding that was mounted on the building facade with an air gap (ventilated cavity) and
combustible foam insulation behind it. Once ﬂames reached the cladding’s polyethylene core and the ventilated
cavity, the ﬁre spread extremely rapidly vertically up the tower’s exterior. The UK’s Public Inquiry found that
hot gases and ﬂames likely deformed a uPVC window frame, allowing ﬁre to penetrate into the cavity between
the cladding panels and the insulation [5]. The ﬁre quickly raced up the chimney-like gap, and covered the
tower’s exterior within minutes. The combustible AMC panels with plastic cores were the primary fuel loads
that caused the ﬁre to spread rapidly. Burning plastic droplets from the panels fell, creating additional ﬁre
spread within the cladding on the ﬂoors below [3].
The ventilated cavity greatly worsened the ﬂame spread, with the chimney eﬀect carrying ﬂames upward much
faster than an exterior ﬁre would normally spread [5, 6]. This case highlights the ﬂaws in ﬁre safety regulations
and enforcement, since the combination of a ventilated cavity and combustible cladding proved deadly.

Norwegian Church Fire (2010)
In May 2010, Kopervik Church in Karmøy, Norway (a 19th-century wooden church) was completely destroyed
by a ﬁre that started due to the use of a gas-powered weed burner near the building. Investigators found that
ﬂames from the weed burner ignited the exterior and spread into the church’s ventilated wall cavity, which
acted like a chimney. The ﬁre travelled rapidly through hidden cavities behind the wood cladding, making it
extremely diﬃcult to control. Despite the church having a ﬁre alarm with direct connection to the ﬁre brigade
and ﬁreﬁghters arriving within minutes, the ﬁre in the concealed spaces raced up to the roof. The church’s timber
structure and the open cavities (with only tar paper and wood between) allowed the ﬁre to spread. Within a
short time, the church was engulfed in ﬂames and ultimately burned to the ground. This case highlights how
even a small external ignition (in this case, a weed burner) can trigger a devastating blaze when ﬁre ﬁnds its
way into a ventilated cavity. [7]
Knowsley Heights – Liverpool, England (1991)
Knowsley Heights, an 11-story public housing tower, was part of an early over-cladding pilot project in the UK.
In 1991 it was re-clad with a rainscreen system consisting of 100 mm of mineral wool on the concrete walls, a 75
mm ventilated cavity, and resin-bonded exterior panels on an aluminium frame [8]. Shortly after completion,
arsonists ignited rubbish at the base of the building. The ﬁre shot up the gap between the new cladding and the
wall, causing panels to fall oﬀ. Flames travelled vertically inside the cavity and re-entered the building through
new window ﬁttings at every level [8]. A forensic report noted that the ﬁre scorched the cladding over the full
height of one side of the building, though fortunately it did not penetrate deeply into ﬂats [9]. Furthermore,
when improving the new windows, the window sizes had been decreased. The sides of the old window areas
were ﬁlled in with a ﬂake/strand board on wooden studs. The window ledges were coated steel. Where the
windows were set back into the building, the cladding angled around the corners, leaving a large gap behind it,
about 0.5 meters deep. The unusually large air gaps at the corners enhanced the chimney eﬀect [9].
Beijing Television Cultural Center (2009)
On February the 9th 2009, on the last day of the Chinese New Year, Beijing Television Cultural Center stood
ablaze. A ﬁrework display set oﬀby the neighbouring building ignited the roof of the Cultural Center[10]. The
ﬁre rapidly spread down the sides of the building, with the help of strong winds. Witnesses state that debris
scattered around the build site later ignited. The whole building was fully engulfed within 13 minutes. [11, 12]
The vertical ﬁre spread in the Beijing Television Cultural Center ﬁre was partly caused by vertical cavities in
the building’s facade, which were constructed of combustible insulation materials. Once ﬂames entered these
cavities, the combination of combustible surfaces and the stack eﬀect rapidly accelerated the ﬁre’s upward
movement. [13]

Importance of Studying Fire Spread in Ventilated Cavities through Simula-
tions & Experimental work
Since full-scale experiments can be both expensive and complex, computational ﬁre simulations tools, like NIST’s
Fire Dynamics Simulator (FDS), have become increasingly more important for understanding and predicating
potential ﬁre spread. These tools allow for changes in geometry, materials and ventilation ect. prior to any
physical experiments, allowing for multiple ”what-if” scenarios at a minimal cost, which would be costly or not

possible if done using physical tests. [14]
In a study done by Khoo et al. [15] simulations using FireFOAM was done for cavities and validated against
experiments. The simulations predicted ﬂame heights, ﬂow velocity and heat ﬂux within 20% accuracy of the
physical test. By achieving simulations results within an acceptable range of the physical tests, indicates that
well-validated models can be used to study ﬁre spread. Simulations can ultimately help identify dangerous
situations early in the design process.
While simulations provide valuable insight into ”what-ifs”, experimental ﬁre testing is necessary to validate
models and performance in real world conditions. Fire behaviour is complex, and even more so in ventilated
facades, and can be studied in diﬀerent ways, including medium-scale test such as in ISO 13785-1 [16], which
allows for screening of materials and conﬁgurations.
Medium-scale experiments help develop products and
designs, but it can not capture all aspects of full-scale facade behaviour. Thus full-scale testing is still the best
way to evaluate ﬁre safety performance.
1.4
Objectives of the Research
This thesis aims to investigate the ﬁre dynamics within a simple ventilated cavity, containing combustible
timber studs, through experimental analysis and simulations, using Fire Dynamics Simulations (FDS). It aims
to understand the development of the ﬁre, within the ventilated cavity, by examining heat transfer mechanisms,
temperature proﬁles and Heat Release Rates (HRR).
The objectives of this research are as follows:
1. To evaluate ﬂame spread, heat transfer mechanisms, and temperature development in a ventilated cavity
using controlled ﬁre experiments.
2. To simulate the ﬁre dynamics within the cavity using FDS and validate the numerical results against
experimental data.
3. To examine the interaction between combustible timber elements and non combustible cladding during
ﬁre exposure within a ventilated cavity.
4. To compare measured and simulated HRR, temperature proﬁles, and incident radiant heat ﬂux to assess
the accuracy and limitations of the current modelling approach.

Literature Review
Ventilated facades, while having beneﬁcial eﬀects on moisture, pose an increased risk during ﬁres due to the
chimney eﬀect, which accelerates ﬂame spread.
A ﬁres behaviour is aﬀected by ventilation, insulation and
cladding material. Fire safety varies widely across countries, using diﬀerent testing methods and ﬁre sources,
leading to inconsistencies. Despite international eﬀorts, a lack of standardized ﬁre data makes it diﬃcult to
develop ﬁre safety strategies. Addressing these gaps is crucial for improving ﬁre safety within new buildings.
2.1
Research on ventilated facade systems and ﬁre spread
Ventilated facade systems consist of an outer cladding separated from the building’s exterior wall by an air
gap, often to improve insulation and moisture control. This design, however, can create a chimney eﬀect in the
cavity that greatly accelerates vertical ﬁre spread [17]. During a ﬁre, ﬂames and hot gases can escape through
windows and enter facade cavities, racing upwards towards higher ﬂoors. Ventilated cavities can be one of the
fastest pathways for ﬁre to spread. The ﬁre can travel 5-10 times faster, independent of combustibility surfaces
within the cavities, than on the building’s exterior walls [18, 19].
Denmark’s BioFacades:UpHigh project in collaboration with DBI, have conducted a full scale experiment on a
three-storey wooden facade. The objective was to investigate how multi-storey buildings with ventilated wooden
facades can be safely constructed without relying on ﬁre impregnation or sprinklers and preventing ﬁre spread
between ﬂoors. The ﬁndings in this test revealed that the facade failed to meet the criterion of containing the
ﬁre within one ﬂoor for at least 60 minutes. The ﬁre spread beyond the ﬂame deﬂectors between the ﬂoors
within approximately 20 minutes. [20]
2.2
Role of ventilation, insulation, and materials in ﬁre behavior
A ﬁres behaviour in a ventilated facade is inﬂuenced by several aspects, including the air gaps, the insulation
type behind the cladding, and the combustibility of the facade materials.
Ventilation:
The open cavities within ventilated facades act as vertical shafts, intensifying the ﬁre spread.
Research shows that larger cavity sizes and greater ventilation airﬂow will lead to faster vertical ﬁre spread [17].
When ﬂames are conﬁned to a smaller space, it will become elongated into the cavity as it seeks oxygen and
fuel to support the combustion process [19].
Insulation:
Once the ﬁre enters the cavity, the surface of the insulation materials is exposed to the ﬁre
source. The type of insulation used behind the cladding will therefore have an eﬀect the ﬁres development
[19]. Non-combustible insulations, like mineral wool, are ﬁre resistant and do not contribute to the ﬁres growth.
Combustible insulations, such as polyethylene cores (used in Grenfell Tower), can ignite and fuel the ﬁres
development. In Denmark insulation materials with class D-s1,d0 or lower, must be protected with a covering
class K1 10 B-s1,d0 or a construction class EI/REI30 on both sides in order to be a pre-accepted solution [21,
22].
Facade Cladding:
Much like the choice in insulation material, cladding material can have an inﬂuence on
the ﬁres development. Non-combustible materials such as Gypsum, Fiber-Cement and Promatect-H, will not
contribute to ﬁre growth. Combustible materials, such as timber or polyethylene inside ACM, will fuel the ﬁre,

2.3
Regulatory codes and safety standards for facades
Buildings in Denmark are constructed in accordance with the Danish Building Regulations (BR18), including
§117 for facades. The facades main goal, in regards to ﬁres safety under this paragraph is to ensure that:
1. spreading of ﬁre in and on outer walls and roofs is limited;
2. in buildings consisting of more than one ﬁre safety unit, spreading of ﬁre does not occur between the
various ﬁre safety units via outer walls and roofs for the time necessary for evacuation and for rescue
personnel to carry out their work;
3. outer walls do not develop unacceptable levels of burning droplets and particles;
4. parts of the outer wall does not fall down and constitute a risk of injury to persons. [23]
Denmark follows the European ﬁre classiﬁcation system EN 13501 [24] for materials and building components
[25].
When building in Denmark, a the Usage Category is determined ﬁrst, which is based on whether the building’s
occupants are awake, mobile, familiar with the building. This chosen Usage Category then deﬁnes the Risk
Class, which considers additional factors such as building height, depth, number of occupants, and ﬁre load.
Based on the Risk Class and documentation, a Fire Class is determined. The Fire Class indicates the level of
expertise and competence required to document ﬁre safety. [25]
Buildings that comply with Risk Class 1-3 and also Fire Class 1-2 can be built using only pre-accepted solutions.
Buildings that fall out of this range, must have additional documentation such as comparative assessment,
calculation or special tests.
2.4
Current Gaps in Fire Safety Research for Facades
Facade Fire Testing and Standards:
One gap is the lack of universal accepted full-scale test methods and
evaluation for facade systems. Simple material testing following ISO 9705 [26] are not necessarily representative
of full scale multi-layered facade ﬁres. This means that materials with Euroclass B, which addresses the risk of
ﬂashover under a standard 300 kW ignition source, will not necessarily prevent vertical ﬁre spread in the facade
of ventilated cavity [21].
New Materials:
The growing interest and need to build with sustainable materials leads to another gap
in knowledge. Building facades with bio-based, such as timber or thatch etc. poses ﬁre risks, and additional
energy-saving solutions such as sunscreens and PV panels can increase the risk of ﬁre spread [27].
Facade
construction with materials meeting Euroclass B-s2,d0 requirements can be considered a pre-accepted solution
for ﬂoor heights up to 22 meters. Fire-impregnated wood can meet this requirement, but uncertainty regarding
its outdoor ﬁre durability over the past couple of years has signiﬁcantly diminished the market for timber facades
in Denmark, as manufacturers cannot provide guarantees and builders are reluctant to accept the risk. For this
reason, the project, Biofacades:Uphigh are investigating how new bio-based building materials, that are neither
ﬁre protected or equipped with sprinklers, can be used in low- to mid-rise buildings. [28]

Lack of Detailed Data:
One big challenge when it comes to mitigating and understanding when it comes to
ﬁre spread in facades is the lack of detail and standardized data on facade ﬁres. The data gap aﬀects everything
from research to regulations within diﬀerent countries. [29]
In 2023, there was no international database tracking ﬁres involving combustible exterior walls. Over the last
three decades, facade ﬁres in large buildings increased sevenfold, but researchers relied on media reports due to
the lack of oﬃcial data. [29]
Without detailed and standardized data base, it is hard to create eﬀective ﬁre prevention strategies. The lack
of information makes it diﬃcult to compare risks, testing methods, and regulations. A coordinated eﬀort to

Heat transfer occurs through three primary modes: conduction, convection, and radiation[36].
Conduction
Conduction is the transfer of heat within a stationary medium, driven by temperature diﬀerences, without any
bulk movement of the material. The rate at which heat is conducted is described by Fourier’s law, shown in

• Anisotropic materials: Conductivity varies with orientation (e.g., pinewood).

For many engineering applications, conduction is analyzed under either steady-state or transient conditions.
In steady-state conduction, the temperature distribution does not change with time, meaning that heat ﬂows
at a constant rate through the material. This is often applicable to structural components in prolonged ﬁre
exposure, where the system reaches thermal equilibrium.
Transient conduction occurs when temperature varies with time, such as during the initial stages of ﬁre exposure
when materials are heating up.
In some cases, internal temperature variations within a material can be simpliﬁed. When the Biot number
is small (Bi < 0.1), conduction within the material is signiﬁcantly faster than heat transfer at its surface.
This allows the use of a lumped thermal approximation, where temperature changes uniformly throughout the

Convection
Convection is the transfer of heat through the movement of a ﬂuid medium, driven by temperature diﬀerences,
enabling the transport of energy and combustion products.
Convection is categorized into two diﬀerent mechanism:
• Natural convection
• Forced convection
Natural convection occurs when temperature diﬀerences within a ﬂuid lead to density variations, inducing
buoyancy-driven ﬂow. In ﬁre scenarios, hot gases rise due to reduced density, entraining surrounding air and
sustaining upward motion.
Forced convection results from an external force, such as wind or mechanical ventilation.
Convective heat transfer is characterized by the development of boundary layers:
• Velocity Boundary Layer: The region where ﬂuid velocity transitions from the no-slip condition at the
surface to the free stream velocity.
• Thermal Boundary Layer: The region where temperature gradients drive heat transfer between the
surface and the bulk ﬂow.
The eﬃciency of convection is quantiﬁed using dimensionless numbers:
• Reynolds Number (Re): Determines whether ﬂow is laminar or turbulent.
• Prandtl Number (Pr): Inﬂuences boundary layer development.

3.3
Combustion of Timber Studs in Ventilated Facades
The combustion of timber occurs in three distinct stages.
1) Pyrolysis Wood undergoes thermal decomposition, releasing gases such as CO, CO2, H2, and CH4. This
reaction is endothermic, requiring external heat to initiate.
2) Gas Combustion The released gases mix with oxygen and combust in an exothermic reaction, generating
heat.
3) Timber Heating The heat from gas combustion transfers to the surrounding timber, further increasing its
temperature. This accelerates pyrolysis, sustaining the combustion process.

As pyrolysis progresses on the surface of the wood, it leaves behind a layer of charcoal, consisting primarily
of porous carbon.
This porous structure signiﬁcantly aﬀects the thermal behaviour of wood by acting as
an insulating layer with low thermal conductivity.
As a result, it slows down heat penetration and delays
the temperature rise in the underlying wood, extending the time before it reaches the charring threshold of
approximately 300°C (according to DS/EN 1995-1-2). The charring rate of wood is also aﬀected by wood’s
material properties, such as density and moisture content. Higher wood density results in lower air content,
which in turn decreases the charring rate. Additionally, higher moisture content increases the speciﬁc heat
capacity of the wood, further reducing the charring rate.

Preparation of Materials
Promatect-H
The Promatect-H boards, classiﬁed as K1/K2,10 cladding with a reaction to ﬁre rating of A1, were delivered
with dimensions of 1250 x 2500 mm and a thickness of 10 mm. Using a circular plunge saw, the full plate was
divided into two pieces: a front board (625 x 1500 mm) and a back board (625 x 1850 mm).
Following this, 15 round holes were cut into the back board, as per the pre-determined positions, which will be
detailed in the upcoming Section 4.7. A core drill with a diameter of 60 mm was used for this task.
Scandiboard
Scandiboards (Euroclass A1[43]), with dimensions of 1250 x 1250 mm and a thickness of 25 mm, were used to
create inert studs for the experiment. The boards were cut into 50 mm x 1250 mm strips. These strips were
stacked one atop the other to form a stud with dimensions of 50 x 50 mm and a height of 1500 mm, held together
by screws. The strips were arranged in a criss-cross pattern to minimize or avoid thermal bridges through the
entire stud.
Mineral Wool
Two mineral wool ”TF-Plade” plates (Euroclass A2-s1,d0 [44]) were cut into rectangular pieces, each no larger
than 400 x 500 mm. These pieces were then placed in an oven at 250 °C for 24 hours, as this temperature is
suﬃcient to release organic compounds from the material. [45]
After heating, the mineral wool was transported to the ﬁre lab and stored in an airtight container to prevent
moisture absorption. The material was then cut into circular disks with a diameter slightly larger than 60 mm.
These disks were pressed into the pre-cut holes in the Promatect-H board, ensuring a tight ﬁt and eliminating
air gaps between the insulation and the board.
Pinewood
Five pinewood studs, originally measuring 45 x 95 x 3300 mm, were ordered for the experiment. Using a table
saw, they were cut to the required dimensions of 45 x 50 x 1500 mm. The Euro-classiﬁcation for the pinewood
is class D, s1/s2, found in Hurley et al. [13, p. 1136]
After cutting, the studs were stored in the ﬁre lab to allow their moisture content to reach equilibrium with
the surrounding environment. Just before testing, a Wood Moisture Meter was used to measure the moisture
content at three diﬀerent positions along each stud: the bottom third, middle, and top third. Additionally, the
weight of each stud was recorded prior to the experiments.

combustion for arbitrary geometries. The small-scale ventilated facade cavity setup from the previous chapter
was thus modelled in FDS, shown in Figure 35, to eﬃciently test diﬀerent selected ﬁre scenarios and validate

Computational domain
Based on previous studies, dividing the 0.05 m cavity width by 10 was considered an appropriate choice for the
cell size, resulting in a uniform cell size of 0.005 m in the x, y, and z directions. This ensures a proper grid
resolution to capture ﬂow details such as velocity gradients and turbulence while preserving the accuracy of heat
transfer calculations. An additional mesh was included above the cavity in the simulation to prevent unwanted
boundary eﬀects, ensure proper ﬂow development, and minimize numerical errors. To optimize computational
eﬃciency, the cell size in this mesh was doubled in all directions, reducing the total number of cells by a factor
of 8.
To improve simulation eﬃciency, parallelization is applied using a hybrid approach that combines Message Pass-
ing Interface (MPI) and Open Multi-Processing (OpenMP). The meshes are divided into multiple submeshes,
each computed separately and in parallel using MPI, with necessary information exchanged between them as
required. Additionally, each mesh is assigned multiple OpenMP threads to run computations simultaneously.
To optimize the usage of the 128 available cores at DTU’s databar, the simulation was divided into 64 meshes,
with 2 OpenMP threads allocated per mesh. This [64,2] conﬁguration ensures full utilization of all available
cores. The simulation consists of a lower, central, and upper multi-mesh. The lower multi-mesh is divided into
eight submeshes in the x-direction and four in the y-direction, totaling 32 meshes. The grid resolution of each
mesh is 36000 cells, as shown in Figure 36.
Figure 36: Lower multi-mesh: 8x4x1, grid resolution: 36000
The central multi-mesh is divided into 2 submeshes in the x-direction and 10 in the z-direction, totaling 20
meshes, each with a grid resolution of 26460 cells. The upper multi-mesh is divided into 6 submeshes in the
x-direction and 2 in the y-direction, totaling 12 meshes, each consisting of 40000 cells. The total mesh count is

Parameter Tuning: Radiation and Pressure Solver
In cases where accurate radiation modeling is required, increasing
the number of radiation angles improves the precision of predic-
tions. The default setting in FDS is 100 radiation angles, with
an angle increment of 5 and a time step increment of 3. To en-
hance accuracy, it is recommended to increase the number of radiation angles to 300. However, this increases
computational time. To compensate, the angle increment can be adjusted to 6, and the time step increment
can be increased to 6 instead of 3 [47].
The pressure solver settings were adjusted based on ﬁndings from previous test simulations, which indicated that
the default setting of 10 iterations in FDS did not ensure convergence. To address this, the maximum number
of pressure iterations was increased to 100 to ensure proper convergence. Additionally, a velocity tolerance of
0.005 m/s was introduced. Due to variations in mesh grid resolution, the FFT-based solver cannot be used for
the global solution of the Poisson equation. Instead, it is applied locally in parallel within each mesh to solve
the pressure ﬁeld. The pressure ﬁeld must align at the boundaries between meshes, requiring multiple local
iterations. By introducing a velocity tolerance, the largest allowable error at these boundaries is deﬁned before
concluding the iterations. [48]

was utilized at intervals of 10 simulated seconds. This was necessary as each simulated second required ap-

Combustible Material Contribution to HRR
The HRR contribution from the timber studs was estimated using the recorded mass loss data, which previously
showed more mass loss than the before and after weight measurements. This data was chosen, since a mass loss
rate can be calculated, which would not be possible with only before and after weight measurements.
The mass loss rate was determined using a linear regression analysis. This provided the best-ﬁt slope representing
the timber’s mass loss rate. The selected data points for the regression are based on a moving range, ensuring
that enough information is maintained about the mass loss rate while avoiding clutter. The HRR of the timber
was then calculated using Equation (26).
˙Qtimber = ˙m · He
(26)
where He = 12.4 MJ kg−1 is the eﬀective heat of combustion of the timber, found in the SFPE Handbook [13].

An estimation of the timbers HRR, can be seen in Figure 44 above, and it can clearly be seen that the timbers

using mass loss data. To ensure a meaningful comparison, to the measured HRR, the timbers HRR values were
adjusted vertically on the plot using the shifting factor, n, as deﬁned in Equation (27). This adjustment aligns
the timber HRR with the total measured HRR.
˙Qtimber,shifted = ˙Qtimber + n · ˙Qavg
(27)
˙Qavg represents the average HRR of the sandburner when the mass ﬂow controller is set to 18 ln/min, based on
measurements from all three experiments. The target HRR for these experiments was 10 kW, however, actual
measurements using the oxygen consumption method showed an average HRR of 9.85 kW across the three tests,
as seen in Table 8.
n represents the scaling factor that adjusts the timber HRR to align with the measured HRR. As the target
HRR increases, so does n. When the target HRR is 10 kW, n = 1; at 20 kW, n = 2; at 30 kW, n = 3; and at
40 kW, n = 4. This stepwise increase ensures that the timber contribution is appropriately adjusted to match
the total HRR.

Discussion
The structure of this chapter follows a descending order of relevance, with the most inﬂuential factors and
sources of deviation in each subchapter addressed ﬁrst, followed by those deemed less signiﬁcant. The aim is to
highlight what likely had the greatest impact on the results, while at the same time not being able to assign a
numerical value to the error.
7.1
Sources of Errors and Uncertainty Factors
Experimental measurements naturally involve errors and uncertainties, often due to the test setup. Factors like
the environment, equipment precision, and material properties can also impact the accuracy of the data.
Sandburner - Uniform Fire Spread
The sandburner was used to ensure a controlled and uniform ﬁre spread. However, inconsistencies in the burner’s
sand distribution led to a ﬁre that was not uniformly distributed. This had a direct impact on the ﬁre spread,
with the left timber stud igniting faster than the right stud. This uneven ﬁre behaviour introduced localized
heating diﬀerences, aﬀecting the experimental results.
Construction & Setup
There were air gaps between the studs and Promatect-H boards, and between the hole cut in the Promatect-H
board and the mineral wool. Gaps within the test setup and any broken-oﬀconstruction materials could alter
heat transfer and airﬂow patterns.
These inconsistencies may have introduced localized cooling or heating
eﬀects, impacting ﬁre behaviour and the measured data.

Accuracy of thermocouple placement
Despite eﬀorts to achieve precise placement, perfect positioning was not always possible, though the thermo-
couples were placed as close to the intended locations as possible. These minor oﬀsets in positioning could have
an inﬂuence on the temperatures measured.
Moisture content of materials
Moisture content within the timber studs was measured to between 13% to 15% prior to testing. Unfortunately
it was not possible to get one water content measurement after the tests, as the equipment broke (No spare was
available on DTU). However from the measurement that was received, 14% was measured. All measurements
were taken on from the side of the timber stud that were away from the cavity.
Promatect-H boards was chosen as cladding material for these tests, as it was ﬁrst assumed it had little to
no moisture absorption. Later when reviewing the data sheet for the boards, it was discovered that the water
absorption was equal to 0.50 g/cm3, and that the typical water content was around 5% to 10%. Since the
Promatect-H boards likely contains moisture within this range, during the tests, it is wrongly assumed that
only timber’s combustion would contribute the to weight loss of the test setup, as water will evaporate from
the boards whenever the tests are ongoing. This suggests that, the calculations for both the Mass Retention of
Timber Studs and Timbers HRR Contribution, are overestimated. This also became apparent when weighing
the the timber studs separately after the tests, and they had lost less weight than the Mass Retention results
implied.
Scale accuracy for mass loss of test
The mass loss of pinewood was measured using a scale, which introduced potential sources of error due to its
accuracy limitations. The scale measured to the nearest 0.01 kg and lacked a data logger, requiring manual
recording of values. Small ﬂuctuations in readings, as well as environmental factors such as vibrations, could
aﬀect the precision of mass loss measurements. During the experiments, when reaching the 40 kW phase, the
screw clamps would come loose as the timber combusted and the setup contracted due to the heat, reducing
their grip. To avoid altering the weight on the scale, and for our safety, they were not tightened when the tests
were ongoing. One screw clamp fell oﬀduring each test. If it fell oﬀthe scale, it was placed back on the scale
but not reattached to the setup. Despite placing the screw clamps back on the scale, their detachment still
caused a change in the measured weight, altering the mass loss of the setup.

Thin Skin Calorimeter - Oxidation
The thin skin calorimeter measured heat ﬂux to assess ﬁre exposure. Steps were taken during calibration to
achieve uniform oxidation on all TSC surfaces. However, not all TSCs received a 100% coat of oxidation, and
some had small gaps where the copper material was exposed. During earlier calibration, signiﬁcant temperature
diﬀerences at given heat ﬂuxes between charred and uncharred surfaces were noted. The most oxidized TSCs
were selected for the experiments, but small deviations in oxidation rates could still have inﬂuenced the results.
Diﬀerent thickness of Thermocouples
The thermocouples used in the experiments varied in thickness, which could inﬂuence response times and
temperature readings. All thermocouples regardless of their thickness were of the type K.
External Surroundings
The experiment was conducted in a laboratory speciﬁcally designed for ﬁre testing. To maintain controlled
conditions, an exhaust hood operated at a constant ﬂow rate, ensuring minimal interference with the ﬁre.
Additionally, a window approximately 20 meters away was kept open to allow for diﬀuse airﬂow, while all closer
openings, such as windows, doors, and gates, remained closed to prevent unintended disturbances.
Mass Flow Controller
The mass ﬂow controller was calibrated using air, but methane was used as the fuel source in the experiment.
This required the application of a correction factor, meaning the actual mass ﬂow might have deviated from
the intended input. Additionally, the equipment had been used extensively by previous students, and debris or
contamination from prior experiments may have entered the controller. Such contamination could have aﬀected
the mass ﬂow rates, introducing inconsistencies in fuel delivery and impacting ﬁre behaviour.
Combustion Gas Analyser - Delay and Silica Stone Dryness
The combustion gas analyser was used to measure gas concentrations during the experiment. However, there
was a delay in response time, which could have resulted in slight inaccuracies in capturing real-time ﬂuctuations
in gas composition. Additionally, the silica stone within the analyser needed to be suﬃciently dry to function
correctly. If any residual moisture was present, it could have aﬀected gas readings, introducing another potential
source of measurement error.

7.2
Discussion of Results
It is important to note that although some solutions are suggested in the discussion, these could not be imple-
mented. This was due to ﬁnancial and time constraints within the scope of the thesis, as only a ﬁxed quantity of
materials and fuel was available, additionally the ﬁre laboratory had to be booked in advance, thereby limiting
the number of tests that could be conducted.
Heat Release Rate
The HRR diﬀers between the simulation and experimental results, as shown earlier in Figure 42. This is not
a mistake, as the simulations did not include a pyrolysis model for the timber studs, while in reality the wood
ignites at around 300 °C. This causes signiﬁcant diﬀerences between the ﬁre scenarios.
Average Experimental HRR:
As shown in Figure 42, for the 10 and 20 kW ﬁre phases, the average HRR
falls within the error range, with average values of 9.85 and 20.21 kW, respectively. In contrast, a shift is
observed for the 30 and 40 kW ﬁre phases, where the average HRR values are well above the target. As shown
in Table 8, the averages are 32.28 and 46.9 kW, respectively, falling outside the target range when considering
Mean ± SD, which results in signiﬁcant deviation between the simulation and experimental results.
This
deviation is attributed to the ignition of the timber studs, which contributed to the overall HRR during the
experiments, as well as the margin of error using oxygen consumption method.
Simulation relative to Experimental HRR and Boundary Conditions:
The simulations rose to their
respective target HRR almost instantly within a few seconds of initiation and remained at those values with
minor ﬂuctuations, as shown in Figure 55. As no pyrolysis model for the timber studs was implemented in
the simulations due to the complexity of accurately coding it, no additional HRR was observed. It was brieﬂy
considered to model the timber studs using multiple vertical layers with the ’BURN AWAY’ function in FDS,
but this approach was discarded as it would not reﬂect real conditions, neglecting the protective and insulating
eﬀects of the charring layer.
In contrast, the experiments were continuous, progressing from 10 kW to 20 kW and so forth up to 40 kW.
The pyrolysis of the wood contributed to the HRR through both sudden spikes, likely caused by the ignition of
new areas, and continuous combustion, which increased the overall HRR. Pyrolysis also altered the boundary
conditions, as the lower sections of the timber studs were entirely consumed at later points in the tests, as
shown in Table 11, allowing more air to enter the cavity from the sides and further increase the HRR due to
increased oxygen supply. Additionally, the loss of the lower parts of the studs allowed ﬂames to ignite the base
of the studs rather than only the inner surface facing the cavity. This phenomenon was brieﬂy observed, but
due to malfunctioning screw clamps that detached, the tests were stopped before further observations could be
made.

As mentioned earlier the moisture content of the Promatect-H boards was not taken into consideration when
calculating the mass retention of the timber studs.
This resulted in overestimations for the timbers HRR
contribution, as they are based on the recorded mass retention. An alternative approach to approximate the

In Figure 40, the HRR reached 3 kW at 3813 seconds during the post-logging period. Similarly, Figure 41 shows

When using the recorded mass loss data, the HHR rate of the timber studs is overestimated. However, this

Timber - Charing depth
A comparison between Test 2 and Test 3 is possible, as both were exposed to the same conditions and duration,
with a ﬁre size up to 40 kW throughout the experiment. In contrast, Test 1 was not exposed to these conditions,
making a direct comparison diﬃcult.
For Test 2 the water content of left and right stud were 12.6% and 13.3%, and their densities were 444.4 kg/m3
and 441.5 kg/m3, respectively. These measurements, result in an almost identical charring rate, of around 0.6
mm/min, when comparing to theoretical charring rate provided from Giuliani [49], seen in Figure 71.
However, the results showed that charring in Test 2 was deeper, especially on the left side, where it reached
4.5 cm in depth and completely burned through the bottom 10 cm of the timber stud, whereas charring on the
right side was only 1.7 cm over the same area. The lower water content, on the left stud in Test 2, could have
meant that the wood dried faster, which in turn accelerated the charring eﬀect. Furthermore, when looking at
the surface temperature presented in Figure 50, it gives perspective to why charring primarily occurred in the
left side. The surface temperature measured are constantly higher, towards the left during 10, 20, 30 kW HRR,
and a bit more evenly distributed during the 40 kW HRR phase, prompting more charring to occur here. It is
important to state here, that the same theoretical charring rates, only apply when the timber studs are exposed
to the same conditions, and since the tests show a higher temperature on the left, it is expected to have deeper
charring depths.

When comparing the right-side studs in Test 2 and Test 3, a steady decrease in charring depth can be observed
along the entire stud, with only small increases in certain areas. The slower theoretical charring rate could be
the reason why the right-side studs for Test 2 and Test 3 diﬀer in charring depth, with charring depth being
smaller in Test 3 and showing a more gradual decrease along the stud.

Practical setup limitations:
The gas temperature was measured using two thermocouples pierced through
the burned mineral wool, installed at the beginning of the experimental setup and not altered unless necessary.
Accurately measuring gas temperatures at 0.5 cm from the wall and 2.5 cm in the middle of the cavity proved

could easily shift along the cavity depth. This movement, combined with the presence of a thermal boundary
layer, introduced measurement errors, particularly in the cavity depth direction. As a result, some gas tem-
perature readings may not accurately reﬂect their intended near wall or middle cavity positions. Additionally,
verifying the thermocouple placement after sealing the cavity was not feasible due to obstructed visibility.

Calibration of TSC:
The calibration of the TSC was conducted under in-
correct conditions. During calibration, natural convection was present, with
cold air cooling the TSC. Since the calibration accounts for the combined
heat transfer from radiation, conduction, and convection, this aﬀected the
resulting C-factor. However, during the experiment inside the cavity, nat-
ural convection is replaced by forced convection due to external inﬂuences,
speciﬁcally, ﬁre plumes driving air movement upward through the cavity.
As a result, the C-factor equation used, shown in Equation (23), is based on
false assumptions, further reducing the accuracy of the measured incident
radiant heat ﬂux measured by the TSC to that of the real incident radiant
heat ﬂux.
Furthermore, the calibration of the TSC was conducted at 10, 20, 30, and
40 kW/m2 incident radiant heat ﬂux using the H-TRIS device, while the
calibration methodology referenced in Hidalgo et al. [40], ranged from 10
up to 90 kW/m2. The reason for not extending the calibration beyond 40

Measurement Uncertainties in Incident Radiant Heat Flux:
Using the TSC method according to
Hidalgo et al. [40], the incident radiant heat ﬂux is determined solely by two inputs: the copper’s surface
temperature (°C) and the near wall gas temperature (°C). It is important to note that accurately positioning
the thermocouples to measure the near wall temperature at precisely 0.5 cm from the surface is challenging.
At times, the thermocouples may be positioned deeper into the cavity, while in other instances they may be
closer to the mineral wool. Additionally, there is a risk that a thermocouple may shift during the experiment,
potentially being pulled into the mineral wool by its own weight or by interference from another thermocouple,
as shown in Figure 69a and excessively discussed earlier. This is particularly problematic if the thermocouple
becomes embedded in the mineral wool, as the gas temperature are then inaccurately measured or not measured
at all. Detecting such displacement is diﬃcult during the on going experiments. Since the incident radiant heat
ﬂux relies entirely on the accuracy of the surface and gas temperature measurements, any error in the input
can signiﬁcantly alter the resulting heat ﬂux values.
Although most of the deviation between the simulation and experimental results is considered attributed to
the experimental setup, the simulation also lacked a key variable. Both the speciﬁc heat capacity and thermal
conductivity of copper in the simulation were assigned ﬁxed values at ambient temperature, rather than tem-
perature dependent ramping values. This results in inaccurate steady state temperatures for the copper surface,
which in turn leads to an incorrect incident radiant heat ﬂux and further contributes to the deviation between
the simulation and experimental results.
Emissivity - Additional Oxidation & Assumption of values:
Emissivity is a critical factor in the
calculation of incident radiant heat ﬂux. The lower positioned TSC were likely to experience increased oxidation
during the experiments compared to the upper TSCs and thereby shifting the emissivity beyond what was present
after calibration. The ﬁrst timber studs test lasted 63 minutes, the second and third 83 minutes each, and the
initial inert test, though not discussed in detail, took 34 minutes. This results in a total of 263 additional
minutes, or 4 hours and 23 minutes, during which the copper surfaces were exposed to further oxidation,
altering their emissivity and consequently their absorptivity. In addition, the presence of combustible materials,
such as the pinewood, introduces the possibility of soot forming on the surfaces, further inﬂuencing emissivity.
Changes in emissivity signiﬁcantly aﬀect the C-factor, with potentially large deviations. Figure 73 illustrates
the inﬂuence of the emissivity value on the ﬁnal C-factor.

Positive feedback loop
Many of the variables in the experiment inﬂuence one another in ways that create positive feedback loops, as
brieﬂy mentioned earlier when discussing the left skewed gas temperature values, which were partly aﬀected by
uneven surface temperatures due to thermal boundary layers. In real world experimental setups with numerous
variables, it is diﬃcult to predict which factors may trigger such a loop. In this case, the feedback loop likely
began with the unevenly distributed sandburner. The ﬂame skewed to the left, leading to higher temperatures
at the cavity’s left entry, and raising the temperatures in the bottom left corner. When the timber studs in this
area crossed the ignition threshold of approximately 300 °C, around the 30 kW target ﬁre, part of the lower
left stud ignited, increasing the HRR. This added a ﬁre plume directly above the ignition point, raising the
temperature in the preheat zone δfc, as shown in Figure 2 and reinforcing the loop. At this point, the left side
was aﬀected by both the sandburner’s skewed output and the ignited stud, which together increased the surface
temperature. In turn, convection raised the near wall gas temperatures, increasing the chance of ignition for
the upper parts of the left stud, further raising the HRR.
To break such a feedback loop, one approach is to step back after several test repetitions, evaluate which
variables caused the largest errors, and address them before running an optimized test. This was not done in
the thesis due to budget constraints, as the need for repeated trials for statistical relevance was prioritized over
conducting three separate tests with modiﬁed setups that might not be directly comparable.

Conclusion and Key Findings
The research examined ﬁre dynamics within a ventilated facade cavity using both experimental analysis and
numerical simulations. The experimental work centred on ﬂame spread, heat transfer, and temperature devel-
opment in a controlled cavity conﬁguration, reﬂecting practical construction methods with combustible timber
studs and non-combustible cladding. Measurements of Heat Release Rate, incident radiant heat ﬂux, and sur-
face and gas temperatures enabled a comprehensive assessment of ﬁre behaviour under varying heat exposure
conditions.
The simulations conducted using FDS were calibrated and validated against experimental data. The results
showed strong agreement in surface and mid cavity gas temperature proﬁles and Heat Release Rate during
the steady state phases of the 10 and 20 kW target ﬁres. In the 30 and 40 kW cases, deviations occurred
due to the absence of ﬁre stud combustion in the simulations, which was present in the experiments. Near wall
temperature proﬁles and incident radiant heat ﬂux showed poor agreement with experimental data, invalidating
these aspects of the simulation. Nonetheless, the overall ﬁre dynamics were accurately reproduced, conﬁrming
the simulations applicability for evaluating ventilated facade performance.
The study demonstrated the signiﬁcance of ﬁre spread potential within ventilated facade cavities, as shown
through both experimental and simulation results. Larger ﬁres intensiﬁed the chimney eﬀect, leading to faster
vertical ﬂame spread. Measuring incident radiant heat ﬂux proved diﬃcult, highlighting the diﬃculty of ob-
taining reliable data in ﬁre experiments and the importance of measurement accuracy.
9
Future Work
This study provided valuable insight into the ﬁre dynamics within a ventilated cavity on a small scale. However,
several areas could still be further investigated, summarized in the list below:
• Future Work with Simulations
– Investigate pyrolysis of timber studs in FDS for charring, mass loss, and additional HRR.
– Simulate the increase in HRR (10, 20, 30, 40 kW) to better match the experiments.
– Simulate cavity with diﬀerent geometry, materials and fuels sources to allow for broader general use.
– Investigate variation in ignition sources locations and burning surface directions.
• Future Work with Experiments
– Perform additional tests to increase the available data.
– Scaling the experiment to full-scale to determine whether the small-scale data is applicable
– Track changes to emissivity of TSC using infrared thermometer.
– Investigate ﬁre behaviour using diﬀerent combustible and non-combustible materials.
– Design better ﬁxtures for TSC and thermocouples to avoid displacement and improve data.
These further investigations should aim to improve the accuracy, relevance and practicality for both the exper-
imental setup and simulations.
