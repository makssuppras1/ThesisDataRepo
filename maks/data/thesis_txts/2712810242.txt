Abstract 
Biodiversity loss and climate change represent two major transformations that not only affect human 
societies but also planetary ecosystems. In order to address both issues, a biodiversity protection 
concept is proposed thereafter, to finance the rehabilitation or the protection of a land area with the 
earnings of a wind farm installed on it. If feasible, this idea could add new local value to wind energy 
and allow wind farms to become a direct enabling factor for local biodiversity and natural beauty in 
addition to indirect effects through mitigating climate change. 
Background research is first carried out on the main key aspects of the proposed concept. It reveals 
that the existing scientific literature focuses on biodiversity loss and current measures, but rarely 
proposes ideas to combine protected areas with renewable energy generation. 
A feasibility study on two application cases is then led to challenge the concept. A South-African wind 
farm close to a national park and a Danish wind farm in a farming area are thus analysed in terms of 
economic performance, socio-environmental context and biodiversity regeneration potential. Results 
show that performance depends on the country economics as well as the land use characteristics in 
the studied area. 
Finally, some recommendations for future research work are provided, in particular on the need to 
create an integrated and legal framework for this concept and to focus on some specific countries 
where biodiversity and climate are strongly affected.

1. Introduction 
Over the past decades, the scientific community has been describing and publishing about a new 
global threat to societies: climate change. Numerous scientists have thus collaborated under groups 
such as the IPCC (Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change) and warned governments about the 
ongoing emergency, inciting them to address this phenomenon. For example, in 2021 and 2022, the 3 
working groups of the IPCC have released their respective contribution to the Sixth Assessment Report 
(AR6) (IPCC AR6, 2022). 
By transforming our ecosystems, climate change will affect the whole globe. Its effect on most species 
will be particularly severe. The IPCC notably states with “very high confidence” that it “would cause 
unavoidable increases in multiple climate hazards and present multiple risks to ecosystems and 
humans” (IPCC AR6 WGII, 2022). 
In this context, renewable energy development appears to stand as part of the solution to limit global 
warming to reasonable evolutions. It should be noted that historically, the expansion of renewable 
energy capacity started in the 1970s to replace oil in the electricity mix and thus decrease the 
dependence on oil producing countries (Sørensen, 1991). Even if this reason still stimulates renewable 
energy development, the main focus is now on climate change mitigation. 
For example, the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), created by the United Nations, include 
renewable energy development in “Goal 7: Affordable and Clean Energy” (SDG, 2022). One of its 
targets is thus “By 2030, increase substantially the share of renewable energy in the global energy 
mix”. 
Since 2012, the total capacity of renewables has more than doubled (IRENA, 2022), reaching 3064 GW 
in 2021. This evolution underlines the booming growth of the market and the interest of both public 
and private stakeholders. Indeed, recent action plans from governments feature propositions in that 
sense. Wind energy in particular has been put forward as an alternative to fossil fuels that contribute 
to climate change. Indeed, the emissions of wind turbines over their full life cycle are much lower than 
coal or gas plants.  
Since the first wind farm projects in the US during the 1970s (Thomas, 1980), industrial wind turbines 
have evolved and the global capacity has increased as well. Currently, a global 825 GW of wind plants 
is operational (IRENA, 2022) with 222 GW in Europe. Some countries have developed the sector a lot, 
for example Denmark or Scotland, and have now a significant percentage of their domestic energy 
production coming from it (IRENA, 2022).  
However, other countries such as the emerging ones have started to invest in renewable energy only 
recently. For instance, South-Africa created its first wind and solar power plants at the beginning of 
this century (Akinbami, 2021). 
 
Simultaneously, another crisis is taking place on the globe. In a few decades, with human expansion, 
the pressure on biodiversity has increased, inducing a dramatic decrease in the number of species and 
individuals (IPBES, 2019). This loss of wildlife is mainly caused by land use change because of human 
activity, with the artificialisation of soil and farmland creation. However, climate change has also 
accelerated biodiversity loss through different effects, by directly transforming ecosystems and 
indirectly modifying human needs. Indeed, indirect impacts include for example the decrease in

farmland productivity that creates a need for more farmland area. Preserving and regenerating 
natural areas is thus key to protect species and ecosystems. 
As wind farms are usually located in natural areas far from urban life, projects often have to cohabitate 
with biodiversity. Negative impacts of wind energy over local species are well documented, for 
example regarding habitat loss, disturbance or mortality. (May, 2021) 
Impacts are however different between onshore and offshore wind farms. They need to be assessed 
specifically to study how species behave with this modification of habitat.  
Until now, few research has been done to study how wind energy development and nature protection 
could benefit each other. In some areas, wind farm projects already finance local social or 
environmental improvements. This indicates that there are additional revenues that could benefit the 
local environment if they are allocated in an appropriate way. The idea of the present work is to 
explore the possibility of combining both aspects to generate global and local positive impacts. A wind 
farm has an economic and societal value that could be associated with a natural area in a common 
concept.  
 
In that respect, the main purpose of this thesis will be to answer the following questions: 
Is it feasible to finance the rehabilitation or the protection of a land area with the earnings of a wind 
farm installed on it?  
If so, how much land in the vicinity of the wind farm could be preserved by using this scheme and how 
could wind farms be optimised for the goal of biodiversity protection? 
 
The next chapter will elaborate on the underlying themes behind this concept: wind energy 
technology, community benefits, biodiversity impacts, ecosystem regeneration, economics of wind 
farms and protected areas, … 
The second chapter will provide a general methodology to frame the concept and assess potential 
areas. The third chapter will apply the previous framework to a concrete example in South Africa while 
the fourth and last chapter will discuss the results and the concept as a whole.

2. Foundation and theoretical background 
2.1 Reflection on the biodiversity protection concept 
 
 
Figure 1: The state of the planetary boundaries, Source: "Azote for Stockholm Resilience Centre, 
based on analysis in Persson et al 2022 and Steffen et al 2015" 
 
Climate change and biodiversity loss represent two major cataclysms for this planet and are closely 
interrelated. These assertions have been well described by science recently.  
One useful tool to analyse these phenomena is the planetary boundaries framework. In 2009, 
Rockström et al. (Rockström, 2009) defined nine environmental categories that represent the “safe 
operating space for humanity”: climate change, ocean acidification, stratospheric ozone depletion, 
interference with the global phosphorus and nitrogen cycles, rate of biodiversity loss, global 
freshwater use, land-system change, aerosol loading and chemical pollution. Each category is 
characterised by a threshold indicator that should not be exceeded to guarantee survival of humanity. 
When the planetary boundaries were defined in 2009, three of the nine thresholds were identified as 
exceeded: climate change, biodiversity loss and changes to the global nitrogen cycle. The two main 
limits that will be developed in this thesis were thus already transgressed at that time. Furthermore, 
two other studies on the planetary boundaries were published in 2015 and 2022. The first one (Steffen 
et al., 2015) quantified that land-system change was too important due to deforestation: a fourth 
boundary was thus exceeded. The aspect of land use change will also be dealt within the present work, 
as it is closely linked to biodiversity loss. The latest study from 2022 (Persson et al., 2022) stated that 
a fifth boundary is now exceeded, chemical pollution now enlarged as “novel entities” (see Figure 1).  
The definition of planetary boundaries underlines the need to address problems together as they are 
intertwined. Indeed, climate change and biodiversity are often addressed separately, without looking

at potential side-effects. For example, a common solution provided by governments to combat climate 
change is the development of renewable energies that can have environmental impacts. Therefore, 
acting on one aspect without considering the other may do more harm than anticipated. In order to 
avoid conflicts between different needs, it is thus essential to combine approaches and seek for the 
best response to all environmental matters. 
 
The link between wind energy and biodiversity is often analysed looking at negative connections. The 
literature on this aspect is well-developed and will be addressed in a following chapter (refer). 
However, little work has been done to describe and imagine positive synergies between renewable 
energy and biodiversity. This is partly why this thesis proposes a biodiversity protection concept that 
aims at having a positive impact on both biodiversity and climate change. It will henceforth referred 
to as “the concept”. 
 
The starting point behind this idea is that land cultivated by and for human activities, and in particular 
farmland, is a direct threat to biodiversity. On the one hand, areas untouched by humans need to be 
preserved and protected from further expansion. It represents the first scenario for the application of 
the concept. On the other hand, the creation of new natural land, instead of currently artificialised 
land, is crucial to restore biodiversity and its habitat. This is the second scenario.  
The protection or the rehabilitation of the land will be financed with the earnings of a wind farm 
located on the area or close to it. More precisely, it means that the project will need to be at least 
economically feasible, i.e. to repay the initial investment. The additional profit will then directly be 
used for ecosystem enhancement. 
 
The first scenario is more likely to be relevant in Southern-hemisphere countries where large natural 
areas still host rich biodiversity and are threatened by poaching. Nevertheless, it may also be applied 
in countries where national parks could use more funding for regular operation or specific 
conservation programs. 
The second scenario would correspond more to Northern-hemisphere countries, in particular Europe 
or the United States of America, where competition for land use is tough and a great amount of land 
has already been transformed. The objective would then be to regain space for biodiversity while 
producing energy.

2.2 Relevant aspects 
2.2.1 Wind turbine technology 
Bent Sørensen stated in 1991 (Sørensen, 1991) that for “most of human history renewable energy has 
been the only energy option available”. Indeed, wind energy was mastered by humans very early to 
travel on ships and produce flour in mills (Kaldellis, 2022). It is only recently that wind has been 
harnessed to generate electricity and provide new services to our societies. In the 19th and 20th 
century, fossil energy remained very competitive because of its accessibility, putting renewables out 
of the race until the oil crises in the 70s. This decade witnessed a profound transformation of the 
energy sector and national energy planning strategies, that notably benefitted the development of 
nuclear and renewable energy (Sørensen, 1991).  
In the beginning of the 1980s, the implementation of wind turbines accelerated with nominal 
capacities between 15 and 100 kW for a rotor diameter of around 15 meters (Figure 3).  
 
 
Figure 3: Evolution of turbine size between 1980 and 2020 (from (Kaldellis, 2022)) 
 
Before the mid-1990s, the turbine capacity was below 1 MW. In the following years, the sizes of the 
turbines continued to increase, reaching capacities of multiple MW. Recent developments include one 
commercial wind turbine of 14 MW from General Electric (GE, 2022) and a prototype of the same 
capacity for Siemens Gamesa (SG, 2022), commercialised in 2024. Additionally, Vestas and Mingyang 
Smart Energy propose wind turbine concepts for future years, of respectively 15 and 16 MW (Vestas, 
2022, MSE, 2022).  
Wind energy development has accelerated a lot this past decade. The global estimated capacity 
reached 825 GW in 2021 (IRENA, 2022) (Table 1), making this type of energy source one of the fastest 
growing. Onshore wind represents more than 93% of the sector’s capacity but offshore wind is also 
witnessing a tremendous growth.

Wind energy, compared to other energy sources such as gas, nuclear or hydro, is also variable and 
only partly predictable. (Kaldellis, 2022) These characteristics stem from the nature of the energy 
source, the wind, that fluctuates with time and location. It is notably reflected in the capacity factor 
of the wind turbine, i.e. the average power output over the year compared to the maximum rated 
power capacity. Onshore wind turbines typically have a capacity factor of 26% to 42%, depending on 
the region, whereas for offshore it is between 32% and 51% (IEA, 2019). In some countries, for 
example in Europe, offshore capacity factors are over 50% which makes offshore wind very 
competitive with other plants. 
The wind resource is mapped in a wind atlas that shows the available energy in each region. Having 
an accurate measurement of the mean speeds in a potential area for a project is thus crucial. 
Resources are abundant to numerically assess mean speeds but for the implementation of a project 
field measurements are also carried out. 
The general dynamics of wind speed are affected by three forces: the pressure gradient force, the 
Coriolis force and the friction force (Axaopoulos, 2022). The pressure gradient force exists between 
areas of different pressure and induces wind directions from high to low pressure zones. The Coriolis 
force is caused by the Earth’s rotation and makes the wind directions bend to the right in the Northern 
Hemisphere and to the left in the Southern Hemisphere. Finally, the friction force slows the wind near 
rough surfaces by energy dissipation. 
Additionally, wind speeds also tend to accelerate on up-slopes (Wiggs, 2013) and to be higher at the 
top of mountains (Bridgman, 1993). This is due to the topography that basically blocks a section of the 
flow and diverts it upwards, reinforcing upper flows. This can also be related to the Venturi effect 
inside a pipe with a constricted section. When the flow section is reduced, the speed increases. This 
effect is also verified by field studies that measured it (Wiggs, 2013)(Bridgman, 1993).

2.2.2 Public perception and community benefits 
The public perception of wind farms, as for many infrastructure projects, is really dependent on the 
country. For example, in countries such as Denmark where the development of wind energy is now a 
big part of the country’s history and identity, new projects tend to have local support (Johansen).  
However even in these areas where wind farms are well implanted, the debate over wind farms can 
nowadays become very intense. In Denmark, with the increase in the wind turbines size, resistance 
against projects is now common (Johansen, 2021). In Scotland, wind energy is the first electricity 
source, with 44.5% of the electricity generation in 2020 (SESH, 2022). However, Warren et al. (Warren, 
2009) mentions that there is also an “Energy or Environment” debate in this country that opposes 
defenders of the renewable energy development and environmentalists that criticise new projects for 
their potential impacts on the landscape. If some arguments employed by both sides are based on 
facts, for example job creation or bird mortality, the core reason of the debate lies in the conflicting 
values of stakeholders (Warren, 2009). The article also adds that “landscape aesthetics, community 
(dis)empowerment and the relative importance of global and local factors are perceptual, 
unquantifiable and shaped by personal world views”. It confirms that debates around wind farm 
projects are complex and should be considered as such. 
 
In other countries, the opinions on wind energy are less understood. This is notably the case in Ethiopia 
where Gebreslassie et al. (Gebreslassie, 2020) have led a survey on this subject.  Results of the study 
show that local populations are generally supportive of the projects but also mention other related 
issues. They estimate that there is a need for more prior consultation from the wind farm developers 
and for better compensation to local communities. The study also proposes some policy 
recommendations to improve public perception of new projects, such as developing public benefit 
packages. 
 
Public benefit packages are commonly part of a larger concept called community benefits.  
The definition of community benefits is however difficult to state: there are different versions 
proposed in the literature (Munday, 2011)(Rudolph, 2017). Indeed, Munday et al. (Munday, 2011) 
notably point out the “complex and contested nature of ‘community’”. The difficulty comes from the 
fact that the community is partly defined by the nature of the benefit. For example, benefits can be 
intended for the local population in the form of monetary compensation, but also for a whole region 
when they deal with local employment benefits of a wind farm project. In the United Kingdom, 
communities are nevertheless defined as “communities of locality” but no clear geographical 
boundaries are set to limit this definition (Munday, 2011).  
There is also a great variety of benefits provided by wind developers to communities. Munday et al. 
lists five main categories which are displayed in Table 2.

Conventional economic benefits are the most developed but other forms of community benefits can 
also be relevant. It is also necessary to understand who has the power in the process of community 
benefits arrangements. 
Kerr et al. (Kerr, 2017) thus proposes a typology of three categories: CSR (corporate social 
responsibility) benefit packages, benefits schemes and fair shares.  
With CSR benefit packages, the developer takes the initiative to allocate a part of the budget to social 
compensation, without any legal requirements. This is meant to help with the social acceptance of the 
project. Here the power is on the developer side (Kerr). 
Benefit schemes come from sectoral norms and the stakeholders expectations. They are standardised 
and constitute a first step towards local empowerment. 
Fair shares is the form of community benefits that allows local communities to participate in the 
project. It imposes a legal requirement on developers to open the ownership of the project. In this 
manner, communities receive payment proportionally to the profitability of the wind farm. 
 
Rudolph et al. (Rudolph, 2017) also proposes some recommendations for the implementation of 
community benefit schemes, in the case of offshore wind farms. The article suggests that these 
schemes should be adapted to each situation, i.e. to the characteristics of the project. An important 
aspect is thus to understand “interrelationship between all three dimensions (community, benefit, 
impacts)” (Rudolph, 2017) to provide the right model of benefits to the right people. 
 
In a way, the proposed concept in this thesis takes the idea of community benefits to the highest level 
by redistributing all benefits from the wind farm to the local environment. 
This should inspire the practical implementation of such a concept, as community benefits examples 
provide an already-existing frame for future developments of the concept. 
 
2.2.3 State of biodiversity 
The current state of biodiversity is alarming. This situation is well described by the global assessment 
reports of the IPBES (Intergovernmental science-policy Platform on Biodiversity and Ecosystem 
services) (IPBES, 2019). 
 
The loss of the global biodiversity of the planet also has an impact on human populations and their 
societies. Of the 18 categories of nature contributions to people as defined by the IPBES, only material 
ones with indicators like agricultural production, fish harvest and bioenergy production have increased 
since 1970. Conversely, 14 other contributions associated with the regulation of material production 
like soil organic carbon and pollinators have decreased, endangering future production (see Table 3). 
Indeed, production has already decreased by 23% on the global terrestrial area. As both climate 
change and biodiversity loss are large and interconnected problems… and the solutions for climate 
change can often have negative impacts on biodiversity themselves…., there is a need for a concept 
that addresses both problems simultaneously….

average abundance of native species in most terrestrial biomes. Around 1 million species are currently 
facing extinction. 
Moreover, the local variety of domesticated plants and animals used in agriculture decreases, which 
also decreases the future resilience of crops to climate change or pathogens.  
 
Declines in biodiversity have been and are driven by direct and indirect factors. Indeed, changes in 
land use, direct exploitation, climate change, pollution and invasive alien species all cause global 
declines in nature: a decline of 47% on average of natural ecosystems, a decline of 23% on average of 
biotic integrity in terrestrial communities, a decline in the biomass of wild mammals by 82%... This is 
the result of indirect factors related to societal values and behaviours such as human population 
dynamics, consumption patterns or trade.

2.2.4 Environmental and biodiversity impacts of wind farms 
Regarding the environmental impacts of wind energy technologies, the United Nations Economic 
Commission for Europe (UNECE) released a detailed report in 2021 (UNECE, 2021) that compares 
energy generation technologies using life-cycle impact assessment (LCIA) methodologies. The utilised 
environmental indicators are of midpoint characterisation, i.e. on biophysical phenomena caused by 
various emissions, and endpoint characterisation which represents the effects of the previous 
phenomena on ecosystems, humans and natural resources. Midpoint characterisation indicators 
include climate change, freshwater eutrophication, ionising radiation, human toxicity, land use, water 
resource depletion and mineral, fossil and renewable resource depletion. Endpoint characterisation 
indicators are presented for Europe with impacts on ecosystems and human health. Normalised and 
weighted scores are also aggregated for each energy technology.  
 
The UNECE report summarises in Figure 5 the results of the assessment for onshore wind in Europe. 
For almost all categories, the tower is the more impactful part for a wind energy project. The generator 
however has a relative impact share of almost 75% for minerals and metals due to the utilisation of 
copper in its fabrication. Additionally, blades only affect certain categories such as dissipated water or 
climate change because of their composition of glass fiber and plastic (UNECE, 2021). Overall, the 
constituent materials of the wind turbine are thus the major sources of impacts. In fact, the other

and metal requirements. These aspects are less connected to the subject of this thesis and will not be 
developed further.  
 
However, life-cycle land use is a relevant category regarding the studied concept. Indeed, as the 
objective is to protect or rehabilitate the largest area possible, it is important that the energy plant 
occupies the smallest area. It will thus maximise the efficiency of the concept. Land use is measured 
in points per kWh, with points representing the land quality. Each type of occupation (crop, urban 
zone, ...) corresponds to a land use characterisation factor, in points per m2a (m2-annum, i.e. 1 square 
metre occupied during 1 year). It is possible to aggregate the number of points for each kind of energy 
technology. In that respect, onshore wind stands among the most efficient technologies in terms of

Wind farms also have impacts over biodiversity, in particular bird populations and bats. This collateral 
effect is well developed in the scientific literature and regularly mentioned as one of the main 
disadvantages of wind energy technologies. 
One study was led in Norway to analyse the impacts of onshore wind farms over bird populations 
(May, 2021). A specific LCIA methodology allowed the authors to “evaluate collision, disturbance and 
habitat loss impacts”. These impacts are estimated with an indicator, the PDF/GWh (potentially 
disappeared fraction per GWh). The results pointed out that the main impacts are indirect habitat loss, 
disturbance, direct habitat loss and to a lesser extent collisions or barrier effects. Wind farms located 
near the coast are more likely to affect populations of birds such as seabirds or raptors. One key aspect 
is that larger farms with larger turbines have greater overall impacts but lesser impacts per GWh. The

conclusion of this study calls for more consideration of LCIA results in wind energy projects to reduce 
potential impacts as much as possible. 
Indeed, areas to be prioritised for wind farm development should also be mapped in terms of 
biodiversity impacts. Quantifying and minimising nature disturbance, combined with maximising 
economic outcome, could help to find a good compromise for both renewable energy development 
and nature protection, satisfying a majority of stakeholders. 
 
Some low-tech solutions have been imagined to reduce the collisions with avian populations, i.e. birds 
or bats. These collision events are partly due to a motion smear effect (May, 2020) that tricks the flying 
animal and prevents it from anticipating the actual movement of the blades. Painting one of the blades 
in black helps to reduce this effect by 70%, as estimated in test conditions (May, 2020). This would 
constitute an easy modification to set up, that could lead to significant improvements for protecting 
avian life. 
 
An idea to reduce impacts could also be to optimise the use of land. It would mean to maximise the 
power density instead of the usual economic optimisation approach which is to minimise the levelized 
cost of electricity. Christie et al. (Christie, 2012) investigates this problem by testing different 
configurations of wind turbine arrays, i.e. shapes and sizes of wind farms. The authors of the article 
find out that this power density optimisation approach can lead to very different results than the 
economic approach. An optimal situation is thus determined for hexagonal arrays: a wind farm of 61 
turbines, spaced by 5 turbine diameters, achieves the best power density. The article also mentions 
that “the ratio between the perimeter set-back of a wind farm and the spacing between individual 
turbines within it” is a key element to optimise the wind farm configuration. Indeed, if the set-back 
distance is small compared to the array spacing, small arrays will be favoured in terms of power 
density, and conversely for a greater set-back distance. 
 
Another key consideration for optimising the use of land, that also concerns the concept, is the relative 
position of the wind farm compared to the protected area. It is assumed that putting the wind farm 
close to the border of the area or just outside of it would reduce most impacts.  
 
2.2.5 Biodiversity regeneration 
There is a need for biodiversity protection and enhancement. The question of how to effectively put 
in place programs is therefore analysed in different articles of the relevant literature.  
Many different measures can be taken to improve conservation of endangered species including laws 
and regulations (Luther, 2021). Luther et al. notably mentions that it is important to develop 
“protected areas and site management”, but also that there are other impact actions to be done “such 
as species-level management or improved laws and policies”. 
 
Another key aspect is that conservation actions are very costly (Kerkvliet, 2007) and don't improve a 
lot the conservation status of concerned species. They only prevent species from declining. According 
to Kerkvliet, global spending on threatened and endangered species recovery programs “on existing 
nature reserves alone are an estimated $7 billion annually, but Balmford et al. (2002) estimate that 
“an effective, global reserve program on land and sea” would cost $51 billion annually”. 
 
However, cost isn’t the only issue. Such policies create a heavy burden on landowners because of the 
costs associated with the conservation of species and the renouncement to other profitable uses of 
their lands to preserve species in their territories. They can have the feeling to lose flexibility in the 
management of their land (Lien, 2019), a feeling that isn’t compensated by PES (Payment for

ecosystem services) that are meant to propose incentives to help them with preservation costs. In 
many cases, it doesn’t succeed. However, using private fundings instead of federal government 
fundings (and administration) increases the interest of landowners in participating to the incentive 
programs.  
 
Some actions could be more cost-effective than others as a study also showed in European forests 
(Petersen, 2015). Indeed, conservation actions in forests are demonstrated as more effective in the 
number of species protected per unit of money. 
 
Another current limitation to species conservation is the difficulty to find a method to recreate 
colonies. There are many uncertainties such as knowledge on natural ecosystems and the impact of 
harvesting on the initial colony. A method was developed using Adaptive Management (AM) to deal 
with these uncertainties in specific cases. There are not many examples of applications of this method 
to species conservation but it was shown as effective in at least two cases, with amphibians (Canessa, 
2019) and with woodland caribou (Serrouya, 2019).

developers will have to seek the most efficient measures. Benefitting the most species should be a 
goal but not at the cost of the quality of the taken actions.

Another key issue in Denmark is the creation and restoration of natural wetlands, which host rich 
biodiversity and constitute carbon sinks. Indeed, in the country the size of these areas has drastically 
diminished with the expansion of human activities (Graversgaard, 2021). Graversgaard et al. studied

points out that the main focus of Danish policies has been nitrogen reduction in the soils, to depollute

2.2.6 Environmental analysis tools 
Different tools exist to analyse environmental problems. For example, life-cycle analysis can be used 
to estimate the environmental impact of a product or a process. It has notably been used to compare 
wind energy technologies with other energy technologies (see section (Environmental and biodiversity 
impacts of wind farms)).  
Regarding climate change, another common indicator is the carbon footprint of a technology. It 
constitutes a subcategory of an LCA analysis. This indicator allows the calculation of the equivalent 
CO2 emissions of a technology and will be mentioned in sections 4.1.4 and 4.2.4. 
 
Some other tools can help to assess the relevance of a policy. 
The DPSIR framework is one of them. Based on the Stress-Response framework developed by Anthony 
Friend and David Rapport (Rapport, 1979) in 1979, it was formalised by the European Environment 
Agency (EEA) in 1995 (Gabrielsen, 1995). From this time, it “has developed from a tool to describe 
natural ecosystems under stress to a framework for describing human - environment interactions and 
the related information flows” (Gabrielsen, 1995). 
The framework is constituted of five different parts:  
• 
Driving forces represent the causes behind an environmental problem. They can be primary, 
for vital needs, secondary, when concerning non-vital individual needs, or macroeconomic, i.e 
related to societal needs.  
==PAGE:22==
22 
 
Feasibility of a biodiversity protection concept: Using wind farms for financing nature conservation in their vicinity,  
Master’s thesis, Pierre Maquet, DTU Wind Energy 
• 
Pressures describe the results of satisfying the previous mentioned needs. They are of three 
main types: excessive use of natural resources, changes in land use and emissions. 
• 
State characterises the current phenomena in an area.  
• 
Impacts are the changes in the state caused by the satisfaction of the needs. They constitute 
the last step in a chain of effects that modifies the state. 
• 
Responses are defined as actions taken to address the impacts.  
 
According to the EEA, the interesting aspect of the DPSIR framework is the links between the five 
parts. They help to understand the dynamics behind a problem and thus to get the full picture. 
Indicators are used to quantify, when possible, each part and each link of the framework. 
 
 
Figure 8: The DPSIR framework (from (Gabrielsen et al., 2003)) 
 
The DPSIR method is closely linked to the questions raised by the studied concept in this thesis. In 
section 3.4, it will thus be adapted to correspond to the concept and applied on a concrete example.  
 
A second tool is the stakeholder analysis. This approach has taken different forms in different 
application fields and this can lead to confusion on the purpose of this analysis (Reed, 2009). 
Conceptualised by Freeman in 1984 in the book “Strategic management: A stakeholder approach.” 
(Freeman, 1984), it was initially used in business management to “mobilise, neutralise or defeat 
stakeholders, to meet the strategic objectives of firms” (Reed, 2009). It was then enlarged to other 
sectors (such as natural resource management) with a paradigm shift: the analysis was sometimes 
seen as a way to “empower marginal stakeholders to influence decision-making processes” (Reed, 
2009).  
The main objective of the stakeholder analysis method is above all to identify the parties concerned 
by a specific problem or situation, as well as to classify them in function of their interests, significance 
and influences. By doing so, it is easier to understand the relationships that may lie between the 
different stakeholders but also to find the right strategy for communication and participation. 
 
For the identification of the stakeholders, three subcategories are designated: 
• 
Primary stakeholders are directly concerned by the problem.  
==PAGE:23==
23

Master’s thesis, Pierre Maquet, DTU Wind Energy 
• 
Secondary stakeholders are intermediaries that help the primary stakeholders to achieve their 
intentions or goals.  
• 
Tertiary stakeholders are the other entities that are not directly concerned by and not 
involved in the problem. They usually are politicians, governmental bodies or other public 
institutions.  
In order to classify the different stakeholders, a common-used method is to use influence-importance 
matrices (Rietbergen-McCracken, 1998) (Figure 9). They allow a clear visualisation of players’ 
characteristics.

Relationships between participants can also be mapped with Venn diagrams, i.e. a figure in which the 
context of the problem and each participant are represented by a circle that can overlap. Then with 
this kind of diagram, the interactions between all the stakeholders are explicit.

2.2.8 Economics of a wind farm    
A wind farm is first and foremost infrastructure.  
The costs associated to this asset can thus be splitted into capital expenditure (CAPEX) costs and 
operational expenditure (OPEX) costs. 
 
The capital costs include procurement, construction, grid connection, engineering costs and initial 
financial costs. Figure 12 presents a typical cost breakdown for an onshore wind farm in 2018. 
 
 
Figure 12: Cost breakdown for an onshore wind farm (from (Milborrow, 2012)) 
 
Additionally, there are also interests to repay. Nowadays, they tend to be low due to the fall of interest 
rates. Moreover, the typical ratio between loan and equity funding is currently 80% loan versus 20% 
equity (Milborrow, 2012). 
 
OPEX costs include annual fixed costs, i.e. depending on the capacity of the farm in kW, for 
maintenance labour and materials. There are almost no variable costs, depending on the annual 
production of the farm in MWh, because of the absence of consumables.  
 
Different indicators are used to assess the cost-effectiveness of a wind farm. Net present value (NPV) 
and interest rate of return (IRR) are tools to determine the feasibility. These calculations are also used 
in feasibility studies in other domains, which gives a universal value to the obtained results. 
The Net Present Value (NPV) is a method that estimates the current value of all future cash flows in a 
project, whether they are positive or negative, including the initial investment. Each cash flow is thus 
discounted so that its importance in the sum decreases as its distance in time increases.

at the opportunity cost of capital (or ‘discount rate’)”. It is notably used to compare different energy 
technologies. 
 
The weighted average cost of capital (WACC) is the average rate a company pays to finance a project 
(Duffy), for example a wind turbine. Common practice is to use it as the discount rate for a feasibility 
analysis. This will be the method in the following calculation of this thesis (see section (Methods)).

ecological impacts, are presented as potential solutions to combine both approaches. A thorough 
review of examples in the United Kingdom is thus done in this paper. It classifies all innovations in

three categories as eco-process, eco-organisational or eco-product and involves the whole lifetime of 
a wind farm project from turbine design to management practices. Innovations are also driven by 
different factors both from public and private institutions that need to be identified to make the sector 
evolve towards better respect for the wind turbine environment. 
 
Indeed, governments and public institutions are now also taking these aspects into consideration. As 
previously presented, organisations such as the IPCC and the IPBES are responsible of the analytical 
work. At the same time, executive, legislative and judiciary entities slowly take interest in the subject. 
In particular, the European Commission (EU Commission, 2021) recently mentioned the importance 
of addressing the climate and biodiversity crises in tandem. It is because there is a “complex interplay 
between climate change, species and ecosystems” that our society also needs to act on climate change 
to reduce impacts on biodiversity. According to the Commission, it also represents a possibility to 
create projects that will tackle multiple problems at once and simultaneously reduce the induced 
costs. 
 
Another side of the problem is the relationship between stakeholders in the area of the wind farm. 
They generally have different interests because of their nature and occupation. Different tools can be 
used to monitor the relationships between these entities, such as the environmental methodologies 
like the DPSIR and stakeholder analysis frameworks that are presented in another part (refer). 
Moreover, relationships in the shared area can benefit from a well-organised cooperation and a good 
communication. For example, communities of practice is an idea that could help people to 
communicate efficiently as well as taking into account the needs of stakeholders that do not speak, 
i.e. living and still nature. This kind of community can be put in place in the case of offshore wind 
farms, as it was notably implemented in the Netherlands (Steins, 2021). It is however important to 
note that these initiatives are often ethnocentric and only consider biodiversity needs from the human 
scope, when they are considered at all. 
 
Other analytical tools can be used to assess projects regarding multiple criteria. In section 2.1, the 
planetary boundaries were mentioned as supported by precise indicators on several environmental 
criteria. Over this concept, Raworth (cite) elaborated a new approach called the Doughnut. This idea 
associates planetary boundaries to other categories, dealing with social needs. A few of these are 
connected to the concept, such as “Energy”, “Networks” and “Income and work” that should be 
positively impacted, or “Food” that could be negatively impacted if the concept were to replace 
farmland.

3.1.2 Available data for feasibility studies 
In order to carry out the two case studies, different sources were used. As the concept covers many 
aspects, there were various sources. 
The discount rates used in this thesis are after-tax, nominal. 
For the South-African case study, wind turbine costs were retrieved from the base data of the 
Integrated Resource Plan (IRP) established by the government in 2015. 
The electricity prices are difficult to obtain as there is no open market such as the European spot 
market. Therefore, the primary source was the feed-in tariffs from the REIPPPP bid windows, and in 
particular Bid Window 5. From this, assumptions were made for the economic feasibility of the 
concept. 
The chosen value for the case study discount rate is 10%. This stems from a study that was done on 
the economic feasibility of small-scale wind turbines (Olatayo, 2018) and is confirmed by an Eskom 
publication. 
Regarding geographical data, the Protected Areas Register (PRA) (PRA, 2022) provided the location of 
the South-African national parks. The wind resource assessment was carried out with the Wind Atlas 
for South Africa (WASA) (WASA, 2022). Other maps were also used such as Environmental 
Geographical Information Systems (E-GIS) (PRA, 2022) for grid information or OpenStreetMap (OSM, 
2022) and Google Maps Satellite (GMS, 2022) for a better visualisation of the location. 
For Denmark, the costs of wind turbines were taken from the report “Technology data Generation of 
Electricity and district heating” by the Danish Energy Agency (DEA, 2022b). Forecasted electricity 
prices are also given by the same agency, until 2035 (DEA, 2022a).  
The discount rate used in the feasibility study is 2.5%. It is based on a recent report from AURES II 
named “Renewable energy financing conditions in Europe: survey and impact analysis” (AURES, 2021) 
that specifies discount rate ranges for the different European countries.  
For the Danish case study, the wind resource assessment was preliminarily done with the Global Wind 
Atlas (GWA, 2022)but as explained later in the Results section 4.2.3, it was not chosen ultimately as 
the source for the economic feasibility calculations.  
The implementation costs of environmental measures in Denmark were based on the studies 
presented in section 2.2.5. 
 
The availability of relevant data has been a crucial issue for this work. Indeed, for South-Africa, sources 
are often old and sometimes very difficult to find. In order to ensure the quality of future works on 
the concept, there will be a need to investigate data quality both in terms of precision and recentness.

3.2 Selection of the area 
3.2.1 General considerations 
Several criteria can be considered for the selection of the area. Xing et al. (Xing, 2021) notably suggests 
a “practical wind farm siting framework” that puts in relief five main criteria: wind speed, slope, 
distance from gridlines, distance from roads, distance from residential areas. 
As the biodiversity protection concept of the present study addresses the rehabilitation or the 
protection of a specific area, the nature of the surrounding ecosystem also has to be taken into 
account in the choice of the wind farm location. 
Depending on the scenario of the concept (see section 2.1), there are two corresponding possibilities 
for this criterion:  
- 
In the case of a rehabilitation of an area that is currently used for other human activities such 
as farming, the rehabilitated area and the financing wind farm can, in principle, be located 
anywhere in a country. It would however be recommended to implement the project in 
regions of intensive farming that endanger local biodiversity so that the effects of the concept 
on the region are maximal. 
- 
In the case of a reinforced protection for an already protected area such as a national park or 
a reserve, both the wind farm and the financed area have to be within the protected area or 
close to it. This limits the available locations for the application of the concept but also 
simplifies the approach and the selection.  
Other considerations include the proximity of high-voltage transmission lines to allow the connection 
of the wind farm to the network. 
In some countries, renewable development zones are already specified and thus represent guidelines 
for the implementation of new renewable energy generation. This is notably the case in South-Africa.

3.4 Analysis of the socio-environmental situation 
The general context of the socio-environmental situation in the case country was first assessed via the 
Doughnut framework (as presented in section 2.2.9).  
The environmental value of the concept was then assessed with the DPSIR analysis (presented in 
section 2.2.6). This helped to represent the underlying issues related to the project. 
The DPSIR framework (represented in Figure 8) has been used to infer a specific framework, with 
stated indicators, that can be used for the implementation of the concept. As recommended by the 
EEA report (Gabrielsen, 1995), special attention was paid to the links between the five categories 
(Driving forces, Pressures, State, Impacts and Response). 
 
The first step to carry out a DPSIR analysis is to define the ethical dilemma around the assessed project, 
here the concept.  
The identified context in the chosen country is the base on which this analysis should be constructed. 
Indeed, the general ethical dilemma was identified as: 
“Would the implementation of the proposed concept, i.e. the association of a wind farm to a 
preserved natural area, contribute to the mitigation of environmental impacts and to the 
improvement of the current state?” 
 
From this question, the DPSIR framework was adapted to the specific problem that concept seeks to 
address. For each element, important aspects were listed as well as some indicators that could be 
used to quantify them.

4.1.1 Context and projected concept

4.1.3 Economic feasibility 
As explained in the Methods part (section 3.1.2), the bid windows are good sources for the current

A first calculation was led with the bid price of the last winning bidder, i.e. 617 R/MWh. However, with

the Discussion part (section 5). The obtained NPV was -1 670 MZAR and the IRR 0.8%. 
The idea was then to estimate how much was needed to make the concept feasible in the chosen 
area. In this way, potential subsidies from the government to develop such a project can be quantified. 
Therefore, the minimum price that would make the wind farm feasible is 1 192 R/MWh, i.e 93% more 
than the bid price of the last winning bidder. The Net Present Value that was obtained for the South-
African case study is thus just above zero, as expected showing that the project is indeed economically 
feasible. The value is 15.6 MZAR (922 kEUR). The IRR value is also just above the discount rate used in 
the calculations (10%) with a value of 10.1%.

4.1.5 Potential measures to enhance biodiversity in the chosen area 
In order to identify relevant actions for biodiversity protection in the vicinity of the Karoo national 
park, three different endangered species were analysed with the IUCN Red List assessments that are 
publicly available online. 
 
The riverine rabbit (Bunolagus monticularis), also called the bushman rabbit or the bushman hare, is 
an endemic species of rabbit with a very limited living area in South-Africa. It is classified in the IUCN 
Red List as “Critically Endangered” as the total number of mature individuals is currently estimated 
between 157 and 207 (Collins, 2019). The geographic range of the riverine rabbit overlaps the 
boundaries of the Karoo national park which indicates that its presence is possible on protected area. 
The estimated area of occupancy is 2 943 km2 with possible occurrence outside this zone. The 
preferred habitat for the riverine rabbit is shrubland. According to the Red List assessment (Collins, 
2019), 40 to 60% of this rabbit’s habitat was reduced or fragmented between the 1930s and the 1970s 
because of the expansion of agricultural activity.  
Numerous threats have contributed to the decline of this species. The main one is the expansion of 
agricultural area that reduced the size of its habitat. This threat is assessed as affecting the whole 
==PAGE:50==
50 
 
Feasibility of a biodiversity protection concept: Using wind farms for financing nature conservation in their vicinity,  
Master’s thesis, Pierre Maquet, DTU Wind Energy 
population, slowly but with a significant impact on the populations. Hunting and trapping with 
intentional use is the second main threat: rabbits are hunted for sport or for food. This affects the 
population rapidly with a significant impact (Collins, 2019).  
Key actions to protect this species thus include the acquisition of land to preserve rabbit populations, 
the expansion of suitable habitat and the restoration of degraded critical areas (Collins, 2019). All 
these measures are compatible with the proposed concept in this thesis. Indeed, the benefits could 
be used to set up a restoration program in the park so that populations can settle more within the 
boundaries, and hopefully grow. Moreover, this would contribute the protection of the riverine rabbit 
against poaching. 
 
The black rhino (Diceros bicornis) is a species that lived in most Sub-Saharan countries before 
becoming extinct in most of them. (Emslie, 2020) Its habitat can be shrubland, savanna or desert. The 
population of black rhinos is currently increasing with 3 142 mature individuals in 2020. However, the 
assessment of the IUCN Red List is still “Critically Endangered” (Emslie, 2020). In South-Africa, the 
black rhino is extant and reintroduced, which means that it resides in the country. During the first part 
of the 20th century, the black rhino was the most numerous species of rhinos in the world. In 1960, 
the population was estimated to be 100 000 but during the 35 following years, it was reduced by 98% 
because of intense poaching. The main threat to the black rhino it still illegal hunting (Emslie). 
The current actions that are taken to prevent mortality is thus to concentrate populations in 
intensively protected areas such as sanctuaries (Emslie, 2020). This aspect would be relevant to the 
proposed concept. Indeed, the costs of conservation actions for the black rhino have increased with 
the recent growth of poaching. Providing new sources of financing would concretely help to protect 
this species. 
 
The lappet-faced vulture (Torgos tracheliotos) (Birdlife, 2021) is a raptor that lives in Southern Africa, 
Sub-Saharan Africa and the Arabian Peninsula. It is extinct in almost all South-Africa, and in particular 
in the Karoo national park. Its habitat is diverse and composed of forest, savanna, shrubland, grassland 
and desert. The IUCN Red List assessment of the lappet-faced vulture is “Endangered” because of the 
current decline of the population (6 500 mature individuals). The diet of this scavenger is mainly 
remains on carcasses, but it also hunts small animals such as reptiles (Birdlife, 2021).  
There are different major threats affecting the lappet-faced vulture populations. Accidental and 
deliberate poisoning notably contributed to the decline of local sub-groups: farmers use poison on 
carcasses for predator control. Other threats include nest disturbance by humans, less food availability 
because of vaccination campaigns on livestock, and use for traditional medicine (particularly in South-
Africa). Various conservation actions are proposed to preserve this species. Regulating the use of 
poison and sensitising farmers about its consequences on the ecosystem are two important measures 
mentioned by the IUCN Red List assessment (Birdlife, 2021). Another idea is to provide more food to 
the species by leaving carcasses and protect breeding sites so that the populations can grow. As the 
species has gone extinct in the Karoo national park, actions financed by the concept could include 
reintroduction and rehabilitation of breeding sites. Specific enforcement would also be needed to 
monitor and protect the vultures. Finally, benefits from the wind farm could be used to finance 
awareness campaigns in the Karoo region so that the pressures on vulture populations are reduced.

the Karoo region, that can be financed with the concept. Indeed, there are 1 101 species listed by the

5. Discussion 
The results of the two case studies demonstrated that the concept is economically feasible, at least in 
theory. Indeed, for each case, in South-Africa and in Denmark, there is an electricity price over which 
the wind farm generates benefits that can be allocated to environmental enhancement. However, this 
threshold depends on the studied country and the specific investment costs used in the feasibility 
assessment. 
In fact, a big challenge for the present work has been to find recent costs for wind farm projects in 
South-Africa. The data that is used in the calculations is from 2015. It was adjusted to consider the 
inflation between 2015 and 2022 but not for the decrease in the cost of wind energy. The fact that 
this effect was not considered in the analysis might partly explain why the electricity price that makes 
the wind farm feasible is much higher than the bids in the last South-African Bid Window. 
Additionally, the discount rate value that was used in the economic feasibility for the South-African 
case study is 10%. This value is high compared to the one used for Denmark (2.5%). A potential 
explanation would be that the financing risk is much higher in South-Africa than in other countries, for 
example in Europe. Indeed, investments for wind farms are made in euros whereas revenue from the 
energy plant is received in South-African Rands. Thus, the risk associated to the exchange rate is 
significant for developers and this is reflected in the discount rate. 
Moreover, in South-Africa, it is observed that the bid window system mainly considers the bid price 
and the social value of the project. The environmental value could be integrated as one of the criteria 
in the bid window. This would thus recognize officially the need to tackle climate change and 
biodiversity loss at the same time. Another idea could also be to create special PPAs that promote the 
environmental value of the wind farm project, in parallel to the current environmental system. In this 
manner, wind energy capacity with high environmental value would be created regularly. This could 
satisfy the different stakeholders and boost interest for the concept. 
Another important aspect for any feasibility study of wind farms is the quality of the wind resource 
data that is used in the calculations. For the Danish case study, the first source that was used was the 
Global Wind Atlas (GWA, 2022). The obtained capacity factor seemed very high. Therefore, the annual 
energy output from real projects in Denmark was used instead as an input. This allowed to have high-
quality data for the calculations.  
In order to study the concept in more detail, there would be a need for research on its potential 
practical implementation. Indeed, the layout of the wind farm and its relative location compared to 
the protected area might affect the generated benefits. It would be beneficial to search for the best 
configuration. With the knowledge gathered in the present work, it is supposed that minimising the 
area of the wind farm and putting it just outside the natural area would also minimise its 
environmental impacts as well as the implementation costs. Nevertheless, further research is needed 
on this topic.  
Furthermore, there would be a need for specific regulation regarding this kind of concept. This would 
help to define it legally and frame the required steps to pursue such a project.  
The question of ownership has not been developed a lot in this thesis. Nevertheless, this is a crucial 
aspect for the concrete implementation of the concept. Therefore, an idea could be to open the 
ownership to local populations. It would empower them by taking decisions for their local

6. Conclusions 
The purpose of the present work was to assess the feasibility of a biodiversity protection concept, 
using the benefits of an onshore wind farm to rehabilitate or preserve a defined area.  
A first part detailed the foundation and theoretical background of this concept while providing context 
elements. There are numerous related topics such as community benefits or biodiversity 
enhancement measures that needed to be explored. Therefore, key takings from this background 
research are the following: 
- 
The wind energy sector has been expanding rapidly over the past few decades. Turbine 
capacities and sizes have also increased. 
- 
Projects can create conflicts that can be difficult to solve because of underlying opposed 
opinions. Therefore, community benefits are a way to redistribute wind farm benefits to local 
populations and empower them.  
- 
Biodiversity is threatened globally because of climate change direct and indirect impacts. 
Land-use change also affects habitats, thus increasing the pressure on species. 
- 
Onshore wind has an environmental impact, particularly on birds and bats. However, among 
the different types of energy plants, it is the second less harmful technology.  
- 
Costs can be estimated for the conversion of human-managed land to a natural area. In 
Denmark, the least expensive measure is forest rehabilitation. 
- 
Different environmental assessment tools can be used to evaluate the efficiency of a policy. 
DPSIR and stakeholder analyses are two examples of these tools. 
- 
National parks can have different sources of financing, from tourism activities, externally 
financed projects or public grants. 
- 
Economics of a wind farm mainly depend on the initial investment, the annual fixed operation 
costs and the selling price of electricity. Some systems such as feed-in tariffs guarantee a 
constant revenue. 
- 
Climate change and biodiversity loss are interrelated. Some researchers and the European 
Commission thus underline the need for projects that tackle both issues together. 
The proposed concept aims at addressing the different points described previously.  
To demonstrate it, two case studies were conducted, in South-Africa and Denmark. The Danish 
example showed that a wind farm can generate sufficient yearly benefits to finance the rehabilitation 
of at least 900 hectares with a capacity of 100 MW. The concept is thus, in theory, economically 
feasible in this case. However, the South-African example highlighted that with high investment costs, 
the concept is less likely to be feasible. One key aspect is the data quality, for the wind resource and 
the costs used in the calculations. For the South-African case study, this did not allow to come to a 
definitive conclusion on the feasibility of the concept, which would need further investigation with 
recent data. Consequently, potential measures for protecting specific species were evaluated in 
qualitative terms. Three species, the riverine rabbit, the black rhino and the lappet-faced vulture, were 
reviewed to identify the main threats affecting them and impactful measures that could be 
implemented. 
Further research on the concept could investigate ownership schemes as well as the practical 
implementation of the wind farm and the natural area. Other case studies could also be relevant to 
confirm the relevance of the concept and provide high-quality data.
