Abstract
Objective behavioral quantification remains a critical challenge in preclinical neuroscience
research. Traditional methods like the Basso Mouse Scale (BMS) are limited in resolution
and sensitivity, failing to capture subtle but biologically meaningful aspects of motor be­
havior. To address this, we developed KineMatrix, a modular, open­source, Python­based
software platform designed to extract high­resolution kinematic features from rodent lo­
comotion data captured via pose estimation tools such as DeepLabCut.
KineMatrix integrates signal processing, dynamic gait segmentation, and biologically­
informed feature engineering within a user­friendly graphical interface. The platform en­
ables high­throughput analysis by automatically computing thousands of spatiotemporal
features, ranging from joint angles and trunk sway to interlimb coordination and step vari­
ability, while supporting batch processing, visualization, and result export. Its flexible
architecture supports both algorithmic and deep learning–based gait event detection.
Some aspects of data extraction, behavioral testing, and validation of the machine learn­
ing models were conducted by ChangHui Simone Lin as part of her MSc thesis, which fo­
cused on evaluating and applying the KineMatrix software in experimental settings. While
the full development and implementation of KineMatrix were carried out by the author, Si­
mone Lin contributed by testing the platform on diverse datasets and providing feedback
on its utility for detecting subtle motor abnormalities that are often missed by conventional
scoring methods. These findings underscore the platform’s potential to generate repro­
ducible, interpretable, and objective digital biomarkers of motor performance. Together,
these tools offer a scalable, transparent, and biologically grounded solution for advancing
behavioral quantification in translational neuroscience. The software will be made publicly
available at: https://github.com/Bellardita­Laboratory/KineMatrix.

Introduction and Background
1.1
Behavior and Motor Control: A Window into Neural
Computation
Behavior is the primary output of the nervous system. Every voluntary movement, from
simple locomotion to complex skilled tasks, reflects the coordinated interaction of neural
circuits that integrate sensory inputs, internal predictions, motor commands, and biome­
chanical constraints [1, 2, 3, 4]. Studying movement thus provides a privileged window
into brain function, circuit organization, and plasticity.
Motor behavior emerges from the dynamic interplay of distributed neural computations
across cortical, subcortical, brainstem, spinal, and peripheral systems [5]. These cir­
cuits are not strictly hierarchical, but instead operate as flexible feedback­driven networks
capable of rapid adaptation to both internal and external perturbations [6, 7]. The neu­
ral mechanisms that underlie movement involve descending control, local spinal pattern
generators, sensory feedback from proprioceptors and cutaneous inputs, and continuous
integration across multiple spatial and temporal scales [8, 9, 10].
Traditionally, neuroscience has approached the study of movement by analyzing neural
activity directly: recording spikes, field potentials, or imaging calcium dynamics in brain
regions involved in motor control. However, as these measurements typically sample
only a small fraction of neurons, they offer limited access to the full complexity of circuit
function [11].
In contrast, behavior itself represents the integrated output of entire neural populations
and their interactions with the body and environment. This makes motor behavior an
extremely rich, high­dimensional signal from which neural computations can be indirectly
inferred. In this sense, behavioral analysis serves as a form of reverse engineering: where
careful, quantitative dissection of motor patterns allows researchers to make inferences
about underlying neural dynamics, circuit organization, and control strategies [12, 13].
For example, variability in gait timing may reflect the stability of spinal pattern generators;
asymmetries in limb coordination may indicate disruptions in commissural or propriospinal
pathways; and fine adjustments in joint trajectories may reveal adaptive compensations
arising from supraspinal plasticity. By treating behavior not as a mere consequence of
neural activity but as a proxy for neural computation itself, kinematic analysis offers a
powerful, systems­level approach to investigate both healthy motor function and circuit
reorganization following injury or disease.

This thesis embraces this perspective by developing tools that aim to extract rich, biolog­
ically meaningful information from movement, enabling behavioral data to serve not only
as an endpoint measurement, but as a mechanistic probe into neural circuit function.
1.2
The Need for High­Resolution Behavioral Quantification
Historically, assessment of motor function has relied heavily on qualitative or semi­quantitative
scoring systems. In clinical practice, scales such as the Unified Parkinson’s Disease Rat­
ing Scale (UPDRS) capture gross motor symptoms, while in preclinical models, scoring
systems such as the Basso­Beattie­Bresnahan (BBB) and Basso Mouse Scale (BMS)
provide ordinal evaluations of hindlimb function [14, 15].
While these tools offer important benchmarks for evaluating disease progression or ther­
apeutic interventions, they remain fundamentally limited. Ordinal scores reduce complex
movement patterns to categorical outcomes, sacrificing sensitivity and resolution [16, 17].
Subtle changes in joint coordination, compensatory strategies, or motor variability may go
undetected, especially during early dysfunction or partial recovery.
Modern neuroscience increasingly emphasizes that variability itself is informative: move­
ment variability reflects both network stability and adaptive plasticity [16, 18, 19]. Fine­
grained behavioral quantification allows researchers to capture these dynamics, revealing
compensatory circuit recruitment, maladaptive strategies, or emerging functional reorga­
nization [20, 3].
To address these limitations, there is a growing demand for high­resolution, objective, and
reproducible behavioral metrics capable of capturing the complexity of motor function with
greater precision. Such metrics should not only detect subtle movement impairments that
may be overlooked by conventional assessments, but also quantify patterns of coordi­
nation and compensatory strategies employed during recovery. Moreover, they should
provide the sensitivity needed to track recovery trajectories over time, reflecting adapta­
tions at the circuit level. Ultimately, these advanced behavioral measures are essential
for enabling a mechanistic understanding of how specific neural interventions influence
motor control and functional outcomes.
1.3
Kinematics as a Bridge Between Neural Circuits and
Behavior
Kinematic analysis, the quantitative study of movement trajectories, joint angles, coor­
dination, and timing, offers a powerful framework to translate observable behavior into
functional readouts of underlying neural circuits [21, 5, 17].
Locomotor behavior arises from spinal central pattern generators (CPGs) that generate
rhythmic motor outputs, integrated with descending supraspinal control, sensory feed­

back, and biomechanical constraints [3, 22, 8]. Supraspinal systems, including brainstem
reticulospinal pathways, motor cortex, basal ganglia, cerebellum, and thalamus, modulate
ongoing locomotion and skilled voluntary movement [10, 2, 23].
Kinematic analysis allows this complex neural output to be captured across multiple levels:
• Limb­level kinematics: joint angles, swing height, step length, protraction­retraction
ratios.
• Interlimb coordination: phase relationships, gait symmetry, and coordination pat­
terns [24, 19].
• Whole­body balance: trunk and tail stabilization, postural adjustments, and center­

• Variability metrics: stride­to­stride variability, reflecting network stability or com­

By analyzing these parameters, kinematic approaches link circuit­level mechanisms to
functional output. This becomes particularly important in models of injury or disease,
where compensatory circuit recruitment and plasticity may restore partial function without
necessarily restoring normal patterns [16, 27].
1.4
Aim of This Thesis
The objective of this thesis is to develop, implement, and validate KineMatrix, a dedicated
software platform for high­resolution kinematic analysis of rodent locomotion. KineMatrix
is designed to extract fine­grained spatiotemporal features from motion data, enabling
precise quantification of locomotor behavior across both healthy and pathological condi­
tions.
By combining automated segmentation, biologically informed feature extraction, and user­
guided verification, KineMatrix addresses key challenges in behavioral neuroscience: de­
tecting subtle movement impairments, quantifying compensatory strategies, and linking
kinematic features to underlying neural mechanisms. Through its modular structure, Kine­
Matrix provides a flexible tool to support both preclinical research and mechanistic studies
of motor control, contributing to a deeper understanding of how neural circuits generate
and adapt movement. To promote transparency and reuse, the full source code of Kine­
Matrix will be made available at: https://github.com/Bellardita­Laboratory/KineMatrix

Design and Methods
2.1
Functional and Scientific Requirements
The goal of this project is to create a software application designed for automated kine­
matic analysis of mouse behavior. Using pose estimation data, the tool provides an intu­
itive interface to automate the identification and quantification of motor impairments and
recovery patterns through kinematic features extraction.
For such a software to become a useful tool for neuroscience, some minimal requirements
are needed:
• User friendliness: The application has to be usable by as many users as possi­
ble, and not just a select few who can understand engineering terms. As such, all
the design and parameters have to be easily understandable and usable by neu­
roscientists with no background in data processing. This translates into an intuitive
workflow with parameters rooted in biology.
• Automation: Processing a big amount of data quickly can only be achieved by re­
moving human intervention. Indeed, a person manually tuning parameters for each
experiment can takes hours, while a computer can do the same in seconds. As
such, to meet the goal of efficiency, it is necessary to automate as much of the
process as possible.
• Biologically relevant features: The extracted features need to be linked to biology.
This is done by matching the features with their corresponding behavior, functional
muscle groups or neuronal activation, giving a biological meaning to an otherwise
mathematical feature.
Another important point is the accuracy of the features calculated: because of the limita­
tions of the camera (resolution and frequency) as well as approximations of the tracking
of the mouse, errors will exist in the pose data. These errors will then be propagated and
result in inaccuracies in the calculated features. While it is necessary to estimate them,
minimizing them is not the main goal of this project. As such, inaccuracies are tolerable as
long as they are clearly displayed to the user and do not impede the ability to distinguish
between neurological disorders.
Finally, an important goal of this project is to be modular: we need to be able to add more
elements to the Graphical User Interface (GUI), modify the steps in the workflow or change
the calculated features. This means that the project is to develop not only a software, but
also a modular architecture and framework that allows for these modifications to be done.

2.2
Software Infrastructure and Technical Stack
2.2.1
Programming Language Selection
The choice of a programming language is crucial, each having different pros and cons.
We decided to develop KineMatrix using Python 3.12 for multiple reasons.
First, Python is a high­level language, which allows for rapid prototyping, making it easier
and faster to implement and test new ideas. Moreover, its interpreted nature eliminates
the need for compilation, which further accelerates development cycles.
Additionally, Python offers a vast ecosystem of libraries that are readily available and
particularly helpful for building components such as the GUI. The setup process is also
streamlined; a simple requirements.txt file is sufficient to get new collaborators up and
running with minimal effort.
Finally, Python’s support for object­oriented programming encourages modular design
through reusable classes and layered abstractions, promoting cleaner and more main­
tainable code.
2.2.2
Graphical User Interface Framework
In order to create the GUI for KineMatrix, we chose to use PySide6, a Python module that
provides access to the C++ library Qt. This choice was motivated by several advantages.
First, PySide6 is cross­platform, allowing the software to run seamlessly on both Windows
and Linux systems. It also follows an object­oriented design, where interfaces are built
using widgets and layouts, making the application modular and easy to modify. Moreover,
PySide6 supports subclassing of all base objects, giving us the flexibility to override default
behaviors and customize any part of the software as needed.
2.2.3
Data Handling and Input/Output Management
As detailed in Section 2.4, the software is required to load both Comma­Separated Values
(CSV) files and video data, while also exporting computed features in CSV format.
To handle the loading and manipulation of CSV files, we chose to use Pandas, a Python li­
brary that offers robust and efficient tools for data analysis and manipulation. Its high­level
data structures and wide range of functions make it particularly well­suited for handling
structured tabular data.
For video processing, we employed OpenCV­Python, a widely­used library that enables
efficient extraction of key information from video files, such as frame count and frames
per second. Additionally, it is used to verify video file integrity and to extract specific
frames, which are crucial for tasks such as manual landmark pointing, as discussed in
Subsection 3.8.2.

2.4
Input Data Acquisition and Preprocessing
Before starting the features extraction, it is necessary to understand how the data has
been obtained.
First, an experiment is run, from which video recordings of a mouse walking are gathered.
Then, the videos are preprocessed before finally being passed into a tracking software.
Here we use DeepLabCut [28, 29, 30], a software package by Mackenzie Mathis Lab for
animal pose estimation using deep learning.
We will now describe all of those steps in more detail.
2.4.1
Experimental Setup for Kinematic Data Collection
This study focuses on the analysis of mouse gait through the observation of locomotion in
a controlled environment. The experimental protocol involves recording multiple trials of
mice walking through a transparent corridor, designed to capture precise kinematic data
from different viewing angles.
The setup begins with the placement of a transparent corridor, which is uniformly illu­
minated to ensure high­quality visual data from both the side and ventral views. Two
cameras are then positioned accordingly: one beneath the corridor to obtain a ventral
(bottom­up) perspective, and the other laterally to capture a side view of the animal’s
movement. Following the preparation of the environment, a mouse is introduced at one
end of the corridor and allowed to walk to the opposite end while both cameras record

2.4.2
Terminology and Metadata Conventions
Terminology plays a critical role when manipulating data between different software. This
is especially true for KineMatrix, where the data is loaded and stored in a structured way.
Part of this structure being obtained from the loaded file name, it is necessary to under­
stand the meaning of each term and how the information is transmitted through these
filenames.
An important consideration is the direction in which the mouse walks during the experi­
ment. Since only a single side­view camera is used, the camera captures either the left
or the right side of the animal. Therefore, each recording must be labeled with the corre­
sponding side, denoted as either Left or Right in the filename. This distinction is essential,
as it determines which side of the mouse’s body is available for analysis. For example,
a run labeled ”Left” indicates that the left side of the mouse was visible to the side­view
camera, allowing detailed study of that side.

Each individual traversal of the corridor by a mouse is referred to as a Run. To enhance
the statistical reliability of the data, multiple runs are recorded for each mouse on both the
left and right sides.
To investigate the effects of specific conditions, such as a pathology, injury, or phar­
macological treatment, mice are grouped into experimental Groups, also referred to as
Datasets. These typically include at least one control group composed of healthy ani­
mals, along with one or more experimental groups subjected to the condition of interest.
This design enables robust statistical comparisons and facilitates the identification of sig­
nificant behavioral or biomechanical differences across groups.
All datasets belonging to the same study or experimental session are collectively orga­
nized into a Batch. The primary function of KineMatrix is to process all runs within a given
batch and enable comparisons between the different datasets it contains.
Following each experiment, videos from both the side and ventral cameras are gener­
ated. These video files are systematically named using a standardized convention that
encodes all relevant metadata, typically separated by underscores (_). A standard file­
name includes, at minimum, the following components:
BatchName_DatasetName_MouseName_SideAndRunNumber_SideviewOrVentralview
This naming structure ensures that the software can automatically parse and organize the
input data for analysis.
2.4.3
Video Preprocessing for Pose Estimation
Before being processed by DeepLabCut, the side and ventral views are merged into a
single video file, as seen Figure 2.2a. This process reduce storage requirements and
facilitate data transfer between the different tools involved in the analysis pipeline. More­
over, the speed of mouse locomotion makes it difficult to observe individual steps in real
time. To address this, the video is temporally down­sampled by reducing its frame rate,
effectively slowing down the playback without sacrificing critical information, as illustrated
in Figure 2.2b.

places it. As a result, the orientation of the coordinate system may differ across Runs,
leading to inconsistencies such as flipped axes or reversed directions (e.g., mouse walk­
ing left­to­right versus right­to­left). These discrepancies can significantly affect the in­

2.4.5
Data Format and Coordinate System Normalization
Following processing by DeepLabCut, the output consists of a CSV file containing the
tracked coordinates for each body point (or joint) across all video frames. Specifically, for
each joint and for every frame, the file includes the X and Y pixel coordinates as well as
a Likelihood value. This likelihood score reflects the confidence level of DeepLabCut in
the accuracy of the tracked position.
It is important to note that tracking errors may occur, particularly because the goal is to
label anatomical points on the skeleton, whereas the tracking is performed on the skin
surface, which is highly stretchable and deformable. As a result, the predicted point may
not always correspond precisely to the underlying skeletal structure. In such cases, the
likelihood value provides an estimate of the reliability of the detected position, especially
when the prediction appears shaky or displaced.

2.5
Step Event Segmentation and Detection
To enable the computation of certain kinematic features, it is necessary to segment each
run into individual steps. Therefore, a step detection mechanism was integrated into the
processing pipeline.
2.5.1
Overview of Gait Event Definitions
Accurate step detection in mice requires an understanding of their skeletal anatomy. This
anatomical context is essential in order to identify the joints most relevant to locomotion
and, consequently, the points that should be tracked.
Figure 2.3 presents a textbook X­ray image of a mouse skeleton, captured from both the

With the skeletal structure of the mouse in mind, it becomes possible to characterize
the notion of a step. In the absence of external perturbations and when walking at a
relatively constant speed, mouse locomotion exhibits cyclic and repetitive patterns. These
movement cycles can be decomposed into two primary phases, as illustrated in Figure 2.4.
The first is the stance phase, during which the paw remains in contact with the ground,
providing support and propulsion. The second is the swing phase, in which the paw is
lifted off the ground and the limb moves forward in preparation for the next stance.
A complete step is defined as the sequence comprising one stance phase followed by
one swing phase. Therefore, a step begins when the paw initially contacts the ground
and ends at the subsequent ground contact following the swing. This definition provides
a consistent framework for identifying and analyzing steps across different runs and ex­

2.5.2
Step Detection Methods
Given the importance of accurately identifying gait events, we developed and evaluated
four distinct approaches to step detection. These methods were designed to ensure flex­
ibility across various experimental conditions. Three of these approaches are fully or
partially automated and are described in the following sections. The fourth method is a
manual step annotation tool, integrated into the software, which allows the user to visually
inspect the tracking data and manually define step boundaries when automatic detection
proves unreliable. This combination of algorithmic and manual techniques ensures that
high­quality step segmentation can be achieved, even in challenging scenarios.
Algorithmic Detection via Threshold­Based Rules
Our first approach for detecting steps involved developing a rule­based algorithm de­
signed to identify the four key gait events described previously: stance start, swing start,
peak, and touchdown. This method relies on characteristic features of the paw’s motion
during walking, particularly in terms of velocity and acceleration.
Empirical observation of the paw trajectory reveals that at the moment of touchdown, the
paw transitions from motion to a sudden stop. This transition is expected to result in a
sharp decrease in speed and thus a pronounced negative peak in acceleration. From this,
we hypothesized that the touchdown event could be located by detecting local minima in
acceleration, or more precisely, by identifying zero­crossings in the jerk (the derivative of
Automated Kinematic Analysis for Behavioral Quantification in Preclinical Neuroscience Research
13
==PAGE:22==
acceleration).
Conversely, the start of the swing phase is typically characterized by a rapid increase in
the paw’s velocity as the limb accelerates away from the ground. Consequently, this event
is likely to correspond to a local maximum in the acceleration signal.
Initial implementations of this detection algorithm, however, yielded suboptimal results.
Raw tracking data proved to be too noisy for reliable event detection, with high­frequency
fluctuations masking the broader gait cycle patterns. To address this, we applied a low­
pass filter to the data, effectively removing high­frequency noise and retaining only the
slower, periodic motions associated with step cycles.
Additionally, geometric distortions introduced by the camera lens have to be compen­
sated, as it can introduce drift or curvature into the tracked trajectories. To do so, a moving
average was computed over the vertical (Y­axis) trajectory of the paw, and subtracted from
the original data. This process helped to flatten any curvature in the step trajectory and
focus the analysis on deviations due to actual limb movement, rather than lens­induced
artifacts.
After implementing the previously described pre­processing steps and exploring various
combinations of detection criteria, we arrived at an empirically optimized algorithm for step
detection.
1. Pre­process the data
2. Find all the peaks above a given peak threshold value
3. Use the x jerk and x acceleration to find all the possible stance, swings and touch­
down
4. Find the optimal combinations of stance, swing and touchdown between each peak
This selection is based on a scoring function that minimizes step height variation and either
the temporal or spatial distance between consecutive events, thereby favoring physiolog­
ically realistic gait cycles.
Although this algorithm defines a general framework for automatic step detection, we
extended its flexibility by incorporating multiple threshold values and tunable parameters.
These additions give users control over the detection process, enabling them to tailor the
algorithm to their dataset.

2.6.1
Categorization of Extracted Features
There are two types of features that can be calculated: time features and one­value
features. Time features correspond to features over time, meaning features taking a
specific value at each frame of the tracked data. For example, the velocity of a specific
body part has a value at each frame and as such is considered a time feature.
On the other hand, one­value features are features that describe multiple frames at once.
Step height is such a feature since it correspond to a single value for a whole step. The

KineMatrix
KineMatrix is a software tool designed for automated kinematic analysis and feature ex­
traction. It follows a structured, sequential workflow that guides the user from data selec­
tion and preparation to the final extraction of relevant features.
In this chapter, we first present the overall architecture of the software, detailing its mod­
ular components and how they interact to support the analysis pipeline. Following this,
we describe the framework developed to facilitate the creation, maintenance, and future
expansion of KineMatrix, highlighting key design decisions that ensure scalability, usabil­
ity, and reproducibility. Finally, we provide a comprehensive presentation of the software
itself, including its main functionalities, user interface, and typical usage scenarios. This
sets the foundation for understanding the capabilities and advantages of KineMatrix in the
context of automated gait analysis.

The complete analysis workflow implemented in KineMatrix is illustrated in Figure 3.1.

The user interface design of KineMatrix was inspired by that of DeepLabCut, a widely used

Each step in the pipeline corresponds to a specific tab within the application. To proceed
through the workflow, users are required to complete the associated actions within each

Error Handling and User Feedback
To support a modular and user­friendly software environment, a dedicated error manage­

A centralized method was implemented to display informative messages through popup

a distinct exception to pinpoint the exact nature of the issue. This approach allows users

experience.

The worker handles exception forwarding, user­triggered interruptions, and optional progress
bar updates. Input elements are automatically disabled during execution to prevent user
interference.
To use it, developers simply pass their computation function to a dedicated interface. The

Given the large­scale nature of datasets handled by KineMatrix, computational efficiency

3.4
General Performance Optimizations
To ensure efficient execution across a variety of computing environments, several general
optimization strategies were implemented throughout KineMatrix.
Parallelization
A core principle in the software design is to exploit available compu­
tational resources whenever possible. As such, multiprocessing is employed extensively
when performing operations on the full dataset. The multiprocessing framework described
in Section 3.3.3 is applied in nearly every step of the workflow that involves batch­wide
computation.
Data Retention
Another optimization guideline is to preserve previously computed data
wherever possible. Rather than recalculating intermediate results on every action, the
system retains data unless a change in relevant parameters requires it to be recomputed.
This behavior is managed by the BatchFlags class (see Section 3.2.2), which tracks the
state of each processing step and invalidates downstream results only when necessary.
This design minimizes redundant computation and significantly improves responsiveness
in large datasets.
Decoupled Tuning and Validation
Finally, to maintain interface responsiveness during
parameter adjustment, each tab separates the tuning phase from the validation phase.
While the user is adjusting parameters, computations are performed only on the currently
displayed subset of data. The full dataset is updated only once the user confirms their
choices. This decoupling ensures that real­time interaction remains fluid, even in large
and complex datasets.

3.10.5
Detected Step Validation
After the step events have been detected, it is essential to ensure their correctness. To this
end, KineMatrix supports two complementary validation strategies: automated validation
and user validation.
Automated Validation
The first level of validation is performed programmatically. This
process ensures that all detected step events are:
• Ordered chronologically
• Free of temporal overlaps
• Fall within the bounds of the tracking data.
Any step data failing to meet these criteria is automatically discarded.
An additional validation method, based on removing outliers in step height and step length,
was implemented and tested during development. However, this was later removed from
the final version. Automatically removing such outliers contradicts the software’s goal of
preserving potentially meaningful variability, especially in pathological populations such as
spinal cord injured (SCI) mice. Outlier steps may contain biologically relevant information,
and it is left to the discretion of the user to handle them during later analysis of the exported
features.

Feature Extraction and Analysis Tools
The extraction of kinematic features from the tracked data and detected steps is handled
by a dedicated Features class. This class is responsible for computing, storing, and
exporting the set of features described in Section 2.6.
Each instance of the Features class is associated with a single mouse and a specific limb
(either fore or hind). As such, every mouse has two corresponding Features instances:
one for forelimb data and one for hindlimb data. Each instance processes all available
Runs for that mouse and calculates both time features and aggregated one­value features
for the selected limb.
The computed feature data are then made accessible in the Feature Matrix tab of the
interface. From this tab, users can navigate to the Plot or PCA tabs for visual analysis,
either through direct plotting of specific features or via dimensionality reduction through
Principal Component Analysis (PCA).

4
Discussion
This thesis presents the design, implementation, and validation of KineMatrix, a modular
and automated software for high­resolution KineMatrix analysis of rodent locomotion. The
goal of this work was to bridge the gap between neural dysfunction and motor behavior by
providing tools to extract biologically meaningful features from motion data. Through care­
ful architecture, biologically informed algorithms, and advanced visualization, KineMatrix
provides a platform capable of identifying subtle movement impairments and quantifying
therapeutic outcomes in preclinical neuroscience.
Comparative Context from the Literature
Automated KineMatrix analysis has seen increasing interest as a means to overcome
the limitations of qualitative or ordinal behavioral scoring. For instance, studies using
LocoMouse [21] and CatWalk [37] [38] have demonstrated the value of precise gait mea­
surements in mouse models of motor dysfunction. These systems focus on extracting
parameters like stride length, stance duration, and swing time, which align with several
of the features computed by KineMatrix. However, KineMatrix offers several extensions
beyond these platforms: (i) it allows user­guided correction of step detection, (ii) sup­
ports a broader set of biologically grounded features, and (iii) is implemented as an open,
modular framework easily adaptable to new tasks.
In contrast to earlier tools that are constrained by fixed experimental setups or closed
software ecosystems, KineMatrix emphasizes modularity and adaptability. For example,
DeepLabCut [29] revolutionized pose tracking by enabling markerless tracking via deep
learning. While DeepLabCut outputs raw coordinates, KineMatrix leverages these outputs
to compute structured, interpretable features and integrates quality control mechanisms
like step verification tools.
Furthermore, recent work in neurological disease models (e.g.[39][14]) has emphasized
the importance of analyzing compensatory gait strategies and locomotor variability, par­
ticularly in the context of spinal cord injury and neurodegeneration. KineMatrix incorpo­
rates variability metrics (e.g., step height variance, angular variability), thus offering a
finer­grained view into adaptive or pathological circuit dynamics than classical scoring
systems.
A Modular Framework for Flexible and Scalable Analysis
KineMatrix was developed with modularity as a core design principle. Each step in the
workflow is encapsulated in dedicated modules. This structure allows for future exten­
Automated Kinematic Analysis for Behavioral Quantification in Preclinical Neuroscience Research
53
==PAGE:62==
sions to accommodate different species, movement tasks (e.g., treadmill walking, swim­
ming), or new sensor modalities (e.g., 3D cameras). Furthermore, the design facilitates
reproducibility and consistency in analysis, two factors critical for large­scale preclinical
research.
This modular philosophy stands in contrast to several existing gait analysis platforms,
which are often limited by rigid workflows or proprietary configurations. For example,
CatWalk XT provides a powerful hardware­software integration but is tightly bound to
a specific corridor setup, limiting its adaptability to new experimental paradigms. Simi­
larly, LocoMouse delivers high­precision tracking for freely walking mice, but its analytical
pipeline is largely tailored to specific kinematic metrics and experimental constraints.
KineMatrix, on the other hand, is constructed as an open, adaptable, and scriptable
platform that can evolve alongside research needs.
Its modular architecture enables
researchers to plug in different tracking sources (e.g., DeepLabCut, SLEAP), integrate
behavioral data from varied contexts, or test different gait detection algorithms without al­
tering the entire system. For instance, the step detection module can easily be replaced
with newer deep learning models or extended for species with different gait characteris­
tics.
This modularity also promotes collaborative development. Since each processing block
is self­contained, improvements made in one lab (e.g., a new balance index module)
can be easily shared and reused by others. Such open scientific software ecosystems
have proven their worth in platforms like Bonsai and SimBA, which encourage community­
driven innovation and flexibility. KineMatrix aims to be part of this ecosystem, offering
transparent and extensible code that supports scalable, cross­laboratory comparisons.
Performance and Resource Usage
A notable challenge encountered during development was the high RAM usage. Cur­
rently, all tracking data, step detections, and feature matrices are held in memory through­
out the session. While this architecture ensures rapid data access and processing, it re­
stricts usability on systems with limited memory resources. The addition of deep learning
models further compounds this challenge, as both the model and its intermediate outputs
occupy substantial memory.
A potential improvement would be to implement data streaming or chunk­based process­
ing. By loading only subsets of the dataset on demand, memory usage could be drastically
reduced, enabling KineMatrix to run with lower memory usage at the cost of performances.
Another performance­related design choice was to circumvent the slow startup time of
multiprocessing pools in Windows by instantiating a single pool at launch and maintaining
it for the session’s duration. While this strategy enhances speed for parallel feature extrac­

tion or computation tasks, it monopolizes CPU resources, potentially degrading system
responsiveness and limiting parallel activity outside the application.
Data Quality and Polynomial Interpolation
KineMatrix currently treats the x and y coordinates provided by pose estimation tools like
DeepLabCut as fully accurate, without incorporating the likelihood values that accompany
each prediction. These likelihood scores represent tracking confidence and can be critical
for identifying and correcting erroneous frame estimates.
One possible approach is used by the neuroinformatics­unit in movement, a Python tool­
box for analyzing animal body movement across time and space [40]. They incorporated
a threshold­based filtering strategy, where low­confidence coordinates are flagged and
corrected via polynomial interpolation, which could enhance data integrity. For example,
short gaps due to transient occlusion or tracking dropout could be smoothed, reducing arti­
ficial jumps in trajectories and preventing them from contaminating the extracted features.
This would be particularly valuable in studies focusing on fine­scale motion deviations,
such as tremor, rigidity, or paw misplacement.
3D Reconstruction Challenges
The integration of 3D reconstruction into KineMatrix remains exploratory and is constrained
by both technical and practical limitations. One challenge is that only a subset of body
parts is visible in both ventral and lateral views, reducing the number of landmarks usable
for stereo triangulation. Body parts such as the wrist or paw, which are often occluded in
one view, limit the fidelity of full­body 3D reconstructions.
Another constraint involves the calibration requirements. Accurate 3D reconstruction typ­
ically depends on parameters like focal length, baseline distance between cameras, and
pixel scaling. To reduce user burden, a semi­automated calibration method could be in­
troduced. For instance, if users mark two known points on the video frame and enter both
their real­world distance and the camera’s distance from the walkway, an approximate
focal length could be estimated using the formula:

track, dp is the distance between the two marks in pixels, and dm is their actual physical

Conclusion
This thesis presents the development and validation of KineMatrix, an open­source, mod­
ular platform for high­resolution kinematic analysis in preclinical neuroscience research.
Designed to overcome the limitations of conventional ordinal scoring systems such as the
BMS, KineMatrix provides a scalable and biologically grounded solution for extracting and
analyzing detailed locomotor features from rodent pose estimation data.
The software integrates automated preprocessing, flexible step detection (including both
algorithmic and deep learning­based methods), and biologically informed feature extrac­
tion within a structured and user­friendly graphical interface. These capabilities enable
researchers to quantify subtle motor impairments, characterize compensatory strategies,
and track recovery trajectories with a resolution that aligns with circuit­level mechanisms.
KineMatrix was developed in parallel with and is complemented by a companion thesis
authored by ChangHui Simone Lin, which focuses on the development and validation
of machine learning models for step detection and the integration of deep learning into
the gait analysis pipeline. While the current thesis emphasizes software architecture,
biological relevance, and user interaction, the companion work ensures that the analytical
backend is robust, validated, and interpretable within the biological context. Together,
the two theses offer a unified framework for transparent, automated, and reproducible
behavioral quantification in neuroscience.
In summary, KineMatrix provides a powerful tool for extracting rich, biologically meaningful
features from behavior, turning movement into a mechanistic window on neural function.
Its modular design ensures that it can be readily adapted to new experimental paradigms
and extended with novel computational approaches, supporting the ongoing integration
of engineering and neuroscience in preclinical research

A
Appendix A – Cross­Platform
Implementation Notes
The software was designed to run on both Windows and Linux systems. While the core
functionality remains consistent across platforms, several operating system­specific dif­
ferences were encountered during development. These differences primarily concern
multiprocessing behavior and GUI­related attributes.
A.1
Multiprocessing
A significant difference between Windows and Linux lies in how multiprocessing is handled
at runtime. On Windows, the creation of a multiprocessing pool can be noticeably slow.
This delay is due to the way Windows spawns new processes: By launching a fresh
Python interpreter and importing the necessary modules. Meanwhile, a Unix­like system
forks the current process, which is much faster. As a result, initializing a multiprocessing
pool on Windows can cause a substantial delay before the data processing even begins.
To resolve this, the multiprocessing pool is now created once at application startup and
reused throughout the session. This avoids repeated slow pool creation and ensures
consistent performance on Windows.
However, a performance difference at startup still exists: while the software opens imme­
diately on Linux, Window user will have to wait for the pool to be set up before being able
to use KineMatrix.
Another impact of this startup initialization method comes from the fact that the pool is
never closed until the software is closed. As such, since this method reserves all the
cores for KineMatrix, a significant impact on performance of other background tasks can
emerge, as explained in the Discussion section 4.
A.2
Platform­Specific Adjustments
The software has been tested and confirmed to work reliably on both Windows and Linux.
However, some minor platform­specific adjustments were required to ensure full function­
ality.
For example, on Linux, tool­tip display within the Qt­based GUI required explicitly set­
ting certain widget attributes. Specifically, the attributes WA_AlwaysShowToolTips and
WA_Hover had to be enabled for tool­tips to appear correctly. These changes were not
necessary on Windows, where the default GUI behavior already supported tool­tips as
intended.
